# Abolition Argues on Purely Philosophical Grounds

## The Point
Readers who know Lewis as a Christian apologist often assume *The Abolition of Man* argues from Christian premises to a Christian conclusion. It does not. Lewis defends only the **objectivity of value** — an anthropological claim about what makes us human — and explicitly brackets the question of whether that moral law has a "supernatural origin." [^1] He makes himself "prepared to make allies wherever he can find them," emulating Christ's "Whoever is not against us is for us" (Mark 9:40). [^2] This deliberately non-confessional framing is a major reason the book persuades such a broad readership: its argument "doesn't require its adherents to be signed-up members of a particular confession." [^3]

## Evidence
Lewis writes, "Though I myself am a Theist, and indeed a Christian, I am not here attempting any indirect argument for Theism." [^4] He puts the Bible "on a par with the Hindu Vedas," quotes "St. Paul in the same breath as Confucius," and brackets Christ's "Greater love hath no man" with Horace's *Dulce et decorum est*. [^5] The similarity he finds between Christian and non-Christian wisdom leads him not to "So much the worse for Christianity" but "So much the better for these other traditions." [^5] Ward stresses the framework is "resolutely non-religious (but not necessarily un-religious, still less anti-religious)." [^3] Lewis's own loyalties remain subtly discernible — he closes the appendix by citing John 12:24, "Unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies" — but "an apologetic for Christianity per se it is not." [^6] The critic A.D. Nuttall judged that by reaching "the sceptic by reason alone," Lewis "fought the fight with one hand tied behind his back"; Lewis would grant the point, holding that "the best must not be made an enemy of the good." [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Though I myself am a Theist, and indeed a Christian, I am not here attempting any indirect argument for Theism." [^4]

> "He does not care, as long as he is in line with the Great Tradition, which he sees shining everywhere." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint thought affirms a real moral law that all people can perceive by the Light of Christ "given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16), independent of formal church membership. Lewis's strategy — establishing a common moral ground accessible to honest humanists and "pious Pagans" alike before any sectarian appeal — parallels the LDS conviction that truth and "the Father of lights" illuminate every tradition, so that whatever is "virtuous, lovely, or of good report or praiseworthy" (Article of Faith 13) may be claimed wherever it is found. The argument is an invitation, not a fence.
