# The Resurrection as the Linchpin of Christianity

## The Point
The Resurrection is the hinge on which the whole four-point case turns. Once the New Testament is shown to be historically reliable, the decisive question is whether Jesus "really died and rose from the dead" — for if He did, "then it's not difficult to make the case that Jesus is God and the entire Bible is the Word of God."[^1] Turek treats the Resurrection not as a pious assumption but as the best historical explanation of how "scared, scattered, skeptical Jews" were transformed into the boldest peaceful missionary force in history.[^2] Paul himself, Turek notes, stakes everything on it: Christianity "is false unless the resurrection of Christ is an historical fact."[^3]

## Evidence
Turek's strongest converging lines all point at the Resurrection. The **creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3–8** — death, burial, resurrection, and appearances to fourteen named people plus five hundred others — predates AD 40 (even liberal scholars concede this), making it far too early to be legendary development.[^4] The **excruciating testimony** is central: eleven of the twelve apostles died as martyrs for proclaiming the Resurrection, and there is no record, Christian or non-Christian, of any apostle recanting.[^5] Crucially, they were not dying for a mere belief but for something they claimed to have *verified with their own senses* — "they saw Jesus; they talked with Jesus; they touched Jesus; they ate with Jesus."[^5] Turek invokes J. Warner Wallace's cold-case principle that conspiracies require a motive (money, sex, or power) — none of which the apostles had; as Jews, fabricating this new claim earned them only excommunication, torture, and death.[^6] The best explanation of their radical transformation is an **"impact event"** so powerful it changed everything: "that Jesus actually rose from the dead just as He predicted."[^2] Even hostile extrabiblical sources concede that the disciples *believed* He rose and were willing to die for it.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "The impact event was that Jesus actually rose from the dead just as He predicted." [^2]

> "They could have saved themselves by recanting, but they chose excruciating deaths instead. The apostles were firmly convinced Jesus had risen from the dead because they verified His resurrection with their own senses." [^5]

> "If the New Testament is historically reliable—if Jesus really died and rose from the dead—then it's not difficult to make the case that Jesus is God and the entire Bible is the Word of God." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The reality of the bodily Resurrection of Jesus Christ is at the very heart of Latter-day Saint faith — it is the foundation of the entire plan of salvation, and Saints affirm it with full conviction. The LDS witness of the Resurrection is, if anything, *expanded*: beyond the New Testament appearances, the Book of Mormon records the risen Christ ministering bodily to people in the Western Hemisphere (3 Nephi 11), and Joseph Smith and Sidney Rigdon testified, "this is the testimony, last of all, which we give of him: That he lives!" (D&C 76:22–24). Turek's argument from multiple, sensory, willing-to-die witnesses harmonizes with the LDS principle of establishing truth by witnesses — and Saints would say the chorus of witnesses to the living Christ did not fall silent with the apostles but continues through latter-day revelation.
