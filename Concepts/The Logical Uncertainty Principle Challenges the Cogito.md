# The Logical Uncertainty Principle Challenges the Cogito

## The Point
The narrator previews a forthcoming counter-position they call the **logical uncertainty principle**: the claim that *everything has a degree of uncertainty* — "including René Descartes' *I think therefore I am*." [^1] This stakes out the opposite of Descartes' foundationalism: where Descartes seeks one absolutely certain bedrock, this principle denies that even the cogito attains 100% certainty. (Stated here only as a teaser; the full argument is deferred to a future video.)

## Argument
1. **Stated as a teaser, not yet argued.** Within this video it is asserted, not defended: "I will argue for what I call the logical uncertainty principle, claiming that everything has a degree of uncertainty, including René Descartes' I think therefore I am." *(forward reference)* [^1]
2. **It targets the cogito's claimed status.** The principle directly contests the conclusion of [[The Cogito Is Self-Verifying Through the Act of Doubting]] — that rational self-awareness is an *undoubtable absolute certainty* — by extending uncertainty even to that supposed bedrock. *(scope: universal, including the foundation)* [^1]
3. **It inverts the foundationalist project.** [[Methodic Doubt Seeks an Undoubtable Foundation]] assumes that doubt terminates at something incorrigible; the logical uncertainty principle implies the regress never fully halts — every claim, including the terminus, retains some nonzero uncertainty. *(anti-foundationalist implication)* [^1]

**Steelman (in favor of the principle):** Any claim has to pass through fallible cognition — memory, the rules of inference, the assumption that "thinking things exist" — none of which is itself certified beyond doubt. If certainty about P requires certainty about the faculties used to reach P, and those faculties can never self-certify without circularity, then no belief reaches a clean 100%, and the honest assignment to even the cogito is "very high, not absolute." This mirrors the practical lesson drawn elsewhere in the course that knowledge is high confidence rather than absolute certainty (see [[Thoughtful Faith - How Do I Know What I Know (Epistemology)]]). **Steelman of the Cartesian reply:** the cogito is special precisely because it is *performatively* self-verifying — the act of entertaining the doubt instantiates the thing doubted — so it is not just very probable but immune to the demon scenario in a way no empirical claim is. The burden the principle must discharge is showing that immunity also fails.
