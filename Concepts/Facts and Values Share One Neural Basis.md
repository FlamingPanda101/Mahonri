# Facts and Values Share One Neural Basis

## The Point
This is the chapter's keystone claim for [[Sam Harris]]'s larger project. His fMRI data showed that **mathematical belief** (e.g., "2 + 6 + 8 = 16") and **ethical belief** (e.g., "It is good to let your children know that you love them") produced a *similar* pattern of brain activity — even though these were "perhaps the most dissimilar sets of stimuli" in the study.[^1] Harris infers that "the physiology of belief may be the same regardless of a proposition's content," and therefore that "the division between facts and values does not make much sense in terms of underlying brain function."[^1]

## Evidence
Belief was mediated largely by the MPFC, which Harris describes as "an anatomical bridge between reasoning and value," since even judging emotionally neutral propositions engaged regions strongly connected to the limbic system.[^1] He offers two strands: first, content-independence — "if, from the point of view of the brain, believing 'the sun is a star' is importantly similar to believing 'cruelty is wrong,' how can we say that scientific and ethical judgments have nothing in common?"[^2] Second, the *norms* of reasoning (consistency, freedom from bias) apply equally to factual and to moral beliefs, so "there is a deep analogy, if not identity, between the two domains."[^3] Harris is careful to distinguish this neuroscientific suggestion from his prior *philosophical* claim that values reduce to facts about well-being: the brain data do not prove the philosophy, but make the fact/value split "look suspicious."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Mathematical belief ... showed a similar pattern of activity to ethical belief ... This suggests that the physiology of belief may be the same regardless of a proposition's content." [^1]

> "If, from the point of view of the brain, believing 'the sun is a star' is importantly similar to believing 'cruelty is wrong,' how can we say that scientific and ethical judgments have nothing in common?" [^2]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the inference most in need of scrutiny, and Harris himself half-concedes it: shared neural correlates are not a demonstration that the logical is/ought gap is illusory. The fact/value distinction is a claim about *justification* — that no set of purely descriptive premises entails an evaluative conclusion without an evaluative premise — and a *category* claim cannot be settled by a *physiology* finding. That two beliefs "light up" the same region shows only that the brain encodes both as accepted propositions (precisely Harris's own definition of belief as acceptance); it says nothing about whether the *content* "cruelty is wrong" is true, or how it could be grounded. By parity, believing "the sun is a star" and believing "Sauron is evil" might share a neural signature, yet one is a fact about the cosmos and the other about fiction — sameness of the *believing* does not collapse differences in the *believed*. Harris still needs a prior evaluative frame to get from facts to values, the very move critics say he never earns (cf. [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]]). The Latter-day Saint can grant that fact and value are deeply unified — but ground that unity in a personal God whose own nature is both truthful and good, rather than in a shared brain region (cf. [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]], [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]).
