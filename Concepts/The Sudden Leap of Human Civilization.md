# The Sudden Leap of Human Civilization

## The Point
Not only is the human animal unlike every other creature — the *arrival* of that difference is abrupt in a way mere biology struggles to explain. [[Jacob Hansen]] notes that for most of the bajillions of years of natural history, and even for most of human history, our ancestors were essentially "doing animal stuff." Then, in what he calls "the blink of an eye," civilization erupted: in the last four or five thousand years a creature that had behaved like an animal was suddenly "flying to the Moon." The suddenness is the point. A smooth Darwinian story predicts gradualism; what we actually observe is a near-instantaneous explosion of language, art, science, and self-transcendence concentrated in one species at the very end of the timeline.

## Evidence
Hansen stresses the timescale twice. Against an atheist chronology of billions of years, the entire human cultural leap is compressed into "literally only the past four or five thousand years," an interval that, set against "the bajillions of years," amounts to a blink.[^1] To make the strangeness vivid he offers an analogy: imagine teaching a preschool for a hundred years.[^2] Every cohort of babies behaves like babies — "they poop, they cry, they do the little baby thing."[^3] Then, in your hundredth year, in the final minutes of class, one group "get together, build a rocket ship, and they fly to the moon."[^3] The rational reaction is astonishment — "those are weird babies... how did that happen?" — not the flat dismissal "no, no, they're just babies, they're just babies."[^3] The parallel is exact: insisting "human beings, we're just animals" in the face of so sudden and total a transformation is, Hansen suggests, as obtuse as calling the rocket-building babies ordinary.[^3]

This sharpens the companion argument that the human creature is the strangest animal that ever lived (see [[The Weirdest Animal That Ever Existed]]): there it is the *magnitude* of the difference; here it is the *speed* of its appearance. Both press the same conclusion — that the capacities driving the leap (abstract reason, cumulative culture, the urge to transcend mere survival) sit awkwardly inside a purely material account of mind (see [[The Mind Is Not the Brain]]).

## Best Quotes
> "it's literally only in the past four or five thousand years that all of a sudden, in the blink of an eye, we went from basically behaving like animals to flying to the Moon." [^1]

> "all of a sudden, in the last minutes of class, the children get together, build a rocket ship, and they fly to the moon... you'd go, 'what happened to those babies? those are weird babies.'" [^3]

> "then you got people that ran 'no, no, they're just babies, they're just babies'... 'human beings, we're just animals, we're just animals.'" [^3]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration offers a reading in which the human leap is not an unexplained fluke but the unfolding of a creature made for more than survival. Man and woman bear the image of God ([[Genesis 1.26-27]]; see [[Image of God - Patristic Interpretations]]), and Latter-day scripture frames mortal life as a probationary arena for beings of eternal stature ([[Abraham 3.25]]). If the human person carries a divine endowment, the sudden flowering of art, intellect, and aspiration is what we would expect once that endowment is given room to express itself — the awakening of children of God, not the random twitch of an especially clever ape. This also guards the dignity such a creature is owed: strip away the soul and its divine source, and the very ground of human worth erodes (see [[Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls]]).
