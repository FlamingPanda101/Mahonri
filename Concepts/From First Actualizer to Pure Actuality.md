# From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality

## The Point
Feser argues that a first actualizer cannot merely be the cause at the head of the queue; it must be *purely actual* — devoid of any potentiality whatsoever. The regress at issue is a regress of actualizers of the *existence* of things, so the first member must be able to actualize other things' existence without its own existence having to be actualized. [^1] If it had any potential needing actualization, then either something else actualizes it (in which case *that* is the real first cause) or some purely actual "part" does (in which case that part is the true first cause) — and any "mixture" answer simply restarts the vicious regress. [^2] Hence to acknowledge a first actualizer at all is to acknowledge a purely actual actualizer, and "thus an actualizer that has all the divine attributes which follow from being purely actual." [^3]

## Evidence
1. **The dilemma against a potential-bearing first cause.** Suppose the first actualizer has a potential that must be actualized for it to exist. What actualizes it? (a) Something else — then it was never first. (b) A purely actual *part* — then that part is first and the "potential part" is merely its first effect. (c) A part that is itself a mixture of act and potency — then what actualizes *that* part's potential? We are "back to a vicious regress and haven't reached a first actualizer after all." [^2]
2. **"First" means primary, not temporally prior.** A first cause is "one having underived or 'primary' causal power," not "the one which happens to be at the head of the queue." [^4] Even an infinitely long hierarchical series would still need a source of causal power outside it (see [[Why a Hierarchical Series Cannot Regress to Infinity]]).
3. **Reply: "anything actualizing must itself change."** The objection that an actualizer must undergo change (and so have potentiality) commits a *fallacy of accident*: in our experience actualizers change (your arm moves because it is itself moving), but it doesn't follow that *anything* that actualizes must change — just as "every U.S. president has been under seven feet tall" doesn't entail anyone who *could* be president must be. [^5] There is no essential connection between actualizing and being a mixture of act and potency.
4. **Not self-contradictory.** Unlike an "immortal mortal" (something that both dies and does not die), "a purely actual actualizer is something that actualizes other things without itself being actualized, and there is no self-contradiction in that idea." [^6] The actualizers of experience must themselves be actualized precisely because they are *limited* (an arm is actually here, only potentially there); a being with no potentiality has no such limitation. [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "If we acknowledge a first actualizer at all, we have to acknowledge thereby a purely actual actualizer. And thus we have to acknowledge an actualizer that has all the divine attributes which follow from being purely actual." [^3]

> "A 'first cause' is one having underived or 'primary' causal power, in contrast to those which have their causal power in only a derivative or 'secondary' way." [^4]

> "A purely actual actualizer is something that actualizes other things without itself being actualized, and there is no self-contradiction in that idea." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this is a powerful result: the chain of dependent existence cannot bottom out in another dependent thing, so there must be a self-sufficient ground — a real point of contact with the scriptural truth that all things depend on God ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). The divergence is at the term Feser reaches. *Pure actuality* means a being with no unrealized potential — and therefore one that cannot change, cannot acquire, cannot progress, and (later in the chapter) cannot be material. Restoration theology confesses an embodied, passible, progressing Father ([[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]) who is genuinely conditioned by co-eternal law ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). On the LDS reading, Feser's "first actualizer" need not be the unconditioned, potency-free absolute of Aristotle ([[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]); the argument can establish a *primary sustaining cause* without forcing the further, distinctly Hellenic identification of that cause with pure act ([[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]). In fairness, Feser's dilemma is tight on its own terms: if "actualizer of existence" is taken in his strict sense, a potency-bearing first member does collapse the regress. The LDS reply targets the prior framing — whether the existence of an eternally-existing being requires *actualization* at all.
