# Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection rebutted is that translations merely render the text neutrally. [[Dan McClellan]] argues that dogmatic translations *manipulate* the text to enforce **univocality** — the dogma that scripture is not allowed to disagree with itself. On Genesis 2:19, the move "is basically them saying... we have imposed this dogma of univocality. The text is not allowed to disagree. We're going to alter the text in translation to force it to agree... whether it likes it or not. And it doesn't."[^1] Translation is "the lowest hanging fruit when it comes to how we're going to manipulate this Bible into what we need it to be."[^2] See [[Genesis 2-19 Pluperfect Is a Test for a Dogmatic Translation]] and the existing [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Translation as the path of least resistance for dogma — rate: strong
Of the three judgments, McClellan says translation is where dogmatists "see the most wiggle room... to kind of curate a Bible that best serves their interest."[^2] Canon is usually fixed by tradition and manuscripts are inaccessible to most, but translation choices are continuous and low-visibility, so "you can just take the Bible and say, 'You're not allowed to say that, Bible. I tell you what you're allowed to say.'"[^3] **Steelman of the translator:** every translation necessarily makes interpretive decisions — total neutrality is impossible, and a translator who believes in inerrancy will reasonably prefer renderings consistent with it (the "analogy of faith"). So "dogmatic" choices are, from inside the tradition, principled. McClellan's reply: that principle is exactly the problem — it lets a *prior commitment* override the most natural reading of the source text (see [[Harmonization Overrides Critical Thinking - Dogma Over Data]]).

### 2. "Their Bible... crafted in their own image" — rate: moderate
McClellan's sharpest charge: with the NIV, ESV, and LSB, "that's people telling the Bible what it's going to be," so "it's their Bible in the sense that they have crafted it in their own image rather than... endorsed the Bible as it has come down to them."[^4] This connects translation manipulation to the episode's thesis: a curated Bible is the artifact of human agendas, not a received divine object. See [[There Is No Such Thing as The Bible]].

### 3. The agenda can run to politics — rate: moderate
McClellan extends the worry: the curated Bible "far too often these days involves... politics and the rights of others who are not a part of your tradition."[^5] This is asserted rather than demonstrated in the clip, so rate moderate — it is a motive-attribution that would need case studies to substantiate, though it aligns with the vault's [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]].
