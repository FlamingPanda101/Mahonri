# Childhood Formation as a Universal Gift Not Mormon-Specific

## The Point
**Critic's charge:** Raising a child into baptism imposes a "huge amount of religious pressure" at a young age and is uniquely harmful to Mormon children.[^1]

**Response:** Every parent forms a child before the child can consent — in sports, manners, citizenship, and morals — and that formation is a *gift* that transfers to whatever the child later becomes. Cardon Ellis's analogy: his father drilled him in basketball free throws; he never became a basketball player, yet kept the transferable lesson that "if you want to get good at something, you do the basics repetitively." Baptism is the LDS instance of a universal practice: a rite of passage that disciplines, forms identity, and — by the hosts' reading of the social-science literature — leaves children *better* adjusted, not worse. Singling out baptism while exempting all other childhood formation is special pleading (see [[The No-Consent Objection Proves Too Much]]).

## Evidence
1. **The basketball analogy.** Ellis was coached relentlessly in a sport he abandoned, but "an invaluable lesson was taught to me... do the basics repetitively every day until it's muscle memory." He carried it into media and podcasting. "Stripping your children of that is a tragedy."[^2] *Rate: an argument from analogy — illustrative rather than probative, but it neatly captures the transferability of early discipline.*
2. **Formation requires guardrails, not neutrality.** "Development happens as a response to stimuli... at some point you have to actually start introducing guardrails." Teaching a child not to hit a sibling is formation, not "manipulation" — and the same logic underwrites baptism.[^3] See [[The No-Consent Objection Proves Too Much]].
3. **Rites of passage and adjustment (as the hosts characterize the data).** "All the social science shows that religious children are better adjusted socially than non-religious children," and "the religion that has the most rites of passage" produces the best-adjusted children — with baptism as Mormonism's "very first rite of passage."[^4] *Rate: asserted live without citation; the general finding that religious involvement correlates with adolescent wellbeing has real support, but the strong "most rites of passage" claim is the hosts' gloss and is unverified in the episode.* Compare [[Positive Childhood Religious Experience Predicts Adult Retention]].
4. **The community gift.** Baptism enlists the child into "the family of the ward" — "you're part of the team now" — supplying belonging and contribution, not isolated performance for a "taskmaster."[^5] See [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]].
5. **The "or you just grew up" rejoinder.** To the ex-member narrative "I saw my first R-rated movie and realized everything I was taught was wrong," the hosts answer: "Or you just grew up" — maturation, not the unmasking of childhood "brainwashing," explains shifting views.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "If you want to get good at something, you need to do the basics repetitively every day until it's muscle memory... So stripping your children of that is a tragedy." [^2]

> "Children that are raised in a religion with specific rites of passage are generally far more well-adjusted. And in Mormonism, their very first rite of passage is what? It's baptism." [^4]

> "'I saw my first R-rated movie and I realized everything I was taught as a kid was wrong.' And it's like, 'Or you just grew up.'" [^6]

## LDS Connection
The Restored gospel makes baptism the first covenant rite of passage in a graded series leading to the temple, and lays on parents the explicit duty to teach children before 8 ([[D&C 68|D&C 68:25-27]]). Framing it as universal formation answers the "Mormon-specific harm" charge: every culture forms its young, and the LDS claim is that this particular formation — grounding a child's identity as a child of God within a covenant community — is protective rather than damaging (see [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]] and [[Positive Childhood Religious Experience Predicts Adult Retention]]).
