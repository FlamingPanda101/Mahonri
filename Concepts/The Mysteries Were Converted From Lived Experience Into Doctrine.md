# The Mysteries Were Converted From Lived Experience Into Doctrine

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]], relaying [[Dave Butler]], argues that the "great apostasy" accomplished something very specific: it **converted the mysteries from lived experience into philosophical proposition.**[^1] The ancient question was *participatory* — "Have you been through it? Have you moved through the drama and stood in the presence of God and come out transformed?" The councils replaced it with a *doctrinal* one — "Do you affirm the correct set of propositions about the nature of God?"[^1] Both matter in Christian theology, "but they're definitely not the same thing," and "losing the first while preserving the second is a pretty fundamental change in what the Gospel is *for*, not just a minor adjustment."[^1] This is why other Christian traditions practice nothing like the endowment: the *content* was removed (first by [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category|Josiah]], then by the councils that burned texts like the [[The Acts of John Records Jesus Instituting a Mystery Ritual|Acts of John]]), and what survived was a *creed* — a set of affirmations — rather than an *initiation*. The endowment, on this reading, preserves the older, participatory form, in which [[Matthew 13.10-11|the "mysteries of the kingdom"]] (*mystērion*) and Paul's "wisdom among the perfect" ([[1 Corinthians 2.6-7|teleioi]]) were *things you went through*, not merely things you believed.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The participatory/propositional contrast is textually grounded.** The Gospels and Paul use *mystērion*/*teleioi* language ([[Matthew 13.10-11|Matt 13:11]]; [[1 Corinthians 2.6-7|1 Cor 2:6-7]]), and the *Acts of John* depicts a literal participatory rite — so the claim that early Christianity had a *doing* dimension that later crystallized into a *believing* dimension has primary-text anchors. *Rating: the contrast is real and well-attested in the vault's wider apostasy cluster.* [^1]
2. **It explains an otherwise awkward fact.** If the endowment were a 19th-century invention, its *absence* from creedal Christianity is unremarkable; but if early Christianity was *participatory* and the councils made it *propositional*, the absence is exactly what the apostasy thesis predicts. *Rating: a coherent explanatory payoff.* [^1]
3. **It coheres with the vault's existing notes.** [[Teleios Means Initiate - You Will Be Endowed Not Be Flawless]] makes the same lexical case from a different source, and [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]] frames the rite as formative experience rather than doctrinal test. *Rating: cross-corroborated within the corpus.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic objects: (a) **the dichotomy is too clean** — early Christianity plainly cared about *both* right belief (Paul anathematizes false gospels, Gal 1) and right practice from the start, so "the councils swapped experience for doctrine" caricatures a development that was always both; (b) **the "mysteries" were not a hidden rite** — mainstream exegesis reads *mystērion* as God's once-hidden, now-openly-proclaimed plan of salvation (Eph 3:3–9), available to all believers, not an esoteric initiation, so there was no participatory secret for the councils to lose; (c) the **creeds answered real heresies** (Arianism, docetism) and codified the church's lived worship, so propositional precision was a *defense* of the experience, not its replacement. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is not that doctrine is *bad* or that early Christians ignored belief, but that the *center of gravity* shifted — from an ordinance-bound, transformative initiation (attested in the *Acts of John* and the First-Temple pattern) to a confession of propositions as the test of belonging; the endowment's distinctiveness is precisely that it *keeps the participatory pole* the creeds let lapse, and the apostasy claim is about *loss of that dimension and its priesthood authority*, not the absence of all continuity.
