# The Ocean as the Human Condition

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s allegory begins not with any church or worldview but with reality itself. Strip away every narrative humans tell, and "man is adrift in a sea without any definite purpose or direction, in an environment that can be both beautiful and awful." The ocean is the raw human condition: vast, beautiful, terrifying, lonely, and — crucially — *unjust*, "where chance alone rules your well-being or suffering." This is the shared starting point "bigger and more universal than any particular church or worldview," and any honest account of faith must begin by taking it seriously. The point is diagnostic: before defending a particular boat, Hansen establishes *why anyone needs a boat at all*. Faith traditions are the boats people have built since the dawn of time to keep their heads above water; to pretend the ocean is harmless is "either naive or heartless."

## Evidence
- The frame is deliberately pre-religious: "the most fundamental conversation we can have, the starting point for all seekers, is a conversation about reality itself... bigger and more universal than any particular church or worldview." The human condition "is the one thing all people share." [^1]
- Hansen grounds the metaphor in lived experience — years sailing and surfing, pulling three panicking people from Hawaiian riptides — to insist "the ocean is no joke" and "commands immense respect." [^2]
- The condition's defining feature is injustice: people "rightly ask why am I so lucky to enjoy the calm beautiful seas, or why are others so unlucky as to only endure a short miserable drowning." To miss "the perverse injustice of the human condition" is to be "either naive or heartless." [^3]
- This is *why* people build narratives: "since the dawn of time people have constructed boats, usually in the form of narratives, and they use these boats to navigate this metaphorical ocean... something to keep you from drowning, especially amid storms." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "The fundamental reality is the ocean in all its desolation, injustice, and vast drifting emptiness. It's where chance alone rules your well-being or suffering. When you consider this plight you understand why many believe mankind is in need of rescue." [^3]

> "When we strip away all of our narratives, man is adrift in a sea without any definite purpose or direction, in an environment that can be both beautiful and awful." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The "rescue" Hansen says mankind needs is precisely what the plan of salvation supplies. Where the bare ocean offers "chance alone" and "vast drifting emptiness," LDS theology answers with a purposeful cosmos: a premortal origin, a mortal probation with direction and a destination, and a Rescuer in Jesus Christ. The very injustice Hansen highlights — that some enjoy calm seas while others drown in storms — is a form of the problem of evil that LDS thought addresses through agency, premortal life, and an eternal scope in which mortal inequities are not the final word. Compare the related claim that without God the ocean has no shore at all in [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]], and the constructive answer in [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]].
