# Catholic Marriage History Undercuts the Polygamy Attack

#type/claim

## The Claim
Matt Fradd's "dubious character" reason charges that Joseph Smith "secretly practiced polygamy and even married and slept with teenage girls," which is "hard to reconcile with someone who claims to be God's chosen messenger," and that the New Testament permits only monogamy. Luke Hansen — explicitly answering a *Catholic* critic on Catholic terms — makes four counter-points:

1. **The NT does not clearly forbid polygamy.** [[Michael Heiser]]: "The New Testament says nothing directly about polygamy. Only a preference for monogamy is evident." [[Thomas Aquinas]] ("I hear Matt's a fan") and [[Martin Luther]] both acknowledged polygamy is allowed in at least some circumstances. The LDS view matches: monogamy is the norm and ideal, "but it may be permissible... like when directly commanded by God." [^1]

2. **The tu quoque on chosen messengers.** Fradd says polygamy is hard to square with being God's messenger — "and then he refers to the Bible where many of God's chosen messengers are polygamists." Abraham, Jacob, David: the very standard Fradd invokes indicts his own scriptures. "Of all people on Earth, Catholics should understand that God calls imperfect men." [^2]

3. **The "teenage" point, in Catholic-historical context.** The marriages with evidentiary support were to women **17 or older**; "still young, but it was a different time" with a far lower age of consent. Edward Peters (PhD, Catholic canon law, Catholic University of America): until **1917**, canon law "had considered basically anyone above the age of 12 capable of marriage," and the 1917 code only then raised it to 14 for girls. "Respectfully, Matt, maybe you should learn more about Catholic history on marriage teachings before commenting on Joseph Smith's." [^3]

4. **A papal counterexample.** Pope **Alexander VI** — the Vicar of Christ — "sired multiple illegitimate children while holding the papal office," and is "not the only pope with grave moral errors." If moral failure disqualifies, the office Fradd defends has its own ledger. [^2]

Hansen pairs this with a methodological plea: critique polygamy honestly, following non-LDS biographer [[John Turner]], who notes it "remains unclear whether Smith had sex with women who were already married to other men or his very young wives" — so don't "make claims beyond the evidence." See [[Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr]] and [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]]; contrast the predatory-pattern reading in [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Fradd)
1. **The conduct is real and grave.** Joseph did practice polygamy secretly, did marry teenagers, and did marry women already married to living men (polyandry). These are not fabrications, and a high holiness standard for a prophet is a reasonable expectation. *Rating: the factual core is conceded; the dispute is over judgment, not occurrence.*
2. **"Descriptive not prescriptive" is a serious reply.** Fradd's own rejoinder — OT polygamy is *described*, not *commanded*, and the NT moves toward monogamy — is a standard, defensible reading that Hansen must (and does) engage rather than dismiss. *Rating: a legitimate distinction; not obviously decisive either way.*
3. **Secrecy compounds the charge.** Beyond polygamy as such, the *concealment* (including from Emma and the public) is independently troubling and is not answered by the canon-law or papal points. *Rating: a real residue the tu quoque does not dissolve.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic notes the rebuttal is **almost entirely tu quoque**: bad popes and low historical ages of consent show *Catholics are inconsistent*, not that Joseph's conduct was *right* — "you do it too" cannot establish a prophet's holiness. Citing Aquinas/Luther as permitting polygamy "in some circumstances" elides that they meant rare hardship cases (e.g. Luther's Philip of Hesse counsel, which he urged be kept secret precisely because it was scandalous), not a divinely commanded program of dozens of sealings including to other men's wives. And "17 or older" still includes a 14-year-old in the Helen Mar Kimball case by most accounts, so the age framing is contestable. The Latter-day Saint reply is that the charge was *moral disqualification*, and the tu quoque is therefore on-point: it shows the disqualification standard, consistently applied, would unseat biblical prophets and Catholic popes alike — so the real question is theological warrant (was it commanded?), which [[Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr]] and [[Polygyny Was Normative but Wife-Sharing Was the Transgression]] take up, not bare moral revulsion. See also [[The Tu Quoque Deflection and the Burden of Proof]] on what this move can and cannot accomplish.
