# The Unevangelized Dilemma Refutes Cant-Know-Morality-Without-Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] catches the presuppositionalist in an internal contradiction over moral epistemology. [[Jeff Durbin]] holds, as a presuppositional point, that one **cannot know** a moral truth (e.g. "murder is wrong") with justified certainty *until God says so* in scripture — "Is it possible to know that murder is wrong without the Bible? ... No, it's not." But Durbin's *own* Reformed theology also holds that people who **never heard** of Christ are still justly condemned, because "there is a moral law that's written on everybody's hearts" (Romans 1–2). Carroll presses the dilemma: if the law written on the heart is sufficient to *condemn* the unevangelized, then humans evidently *can* know moral truth **without the canon** — which refutes the first claim. The two positions cannot both stand.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The set-piece dilemma (the lever).** "If we didn't have the Bible, would we know murder is wrong? If so, you'd be required to admit that we don't need the Bible to know moral truth. So, which is it?"[^3] The Reformed system needs natural law to be *epistemically accessible* (so the heathen are "without excuse," Romans 1:20) and simultaneously needs it to be *epistemically inaccessible* (so that certainty requires the Bible, securing the presuppositional argument). Carroll forces a choice between them.

2. **Durbin's answer collapses under his own appeal.** Asked "how do you know that murder is wrong?" Durbin answers "because God has spoken," then immediately affirms "the moral law, which is in Romans 1, has always been written on everybody's hearts... before the Bible was around."[^4] That concession is the admission Carroll wanted: knowledge of the moral law predates and is independent of the written canon, so "you can't know morality without the Bible" is false on Durbin's own terms.

3. **It dissolves the canon-as-precondition move.** The presuppositional argument tries to make the 66-book canon the *precondition* of all knowledge (moral and otherwise). The unevangelized doctrine grants a real, knowable, conscience-borne moral order *prior to* and *outside of* the text — exactly what the LDS view affirms (God writes truth on hearts directly; scripture records it but is not its ceiling). See [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]].

**Steelman of the presuppositionalist.** A careful Reformed apologist distinguishes *knowing that* murder is wrong (the unbeliever has this by general revelation, enough to be culpable) from having a *justified, warranted account* of *why* it is wrong (which, he argues, only the Christian worldview can supply). On that reading the two claims are not contradictory: the heathen has guilty *awareness* without philosophical *grounding*, so "you can't justify moral truth without God's revelation" survives even though bare awareness does not. Carroll's reply (consistent with [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]) is that this retreat concedes the practical point — moral knowledge is genuinely available apart from the *Bible* (Durbin keeps saying "Bible," not merely "God") — and that grounding-talk smuggles the canon back in as the unjustified ultimate standard the rest of the conversation is disputing.

## Best Quotes
> "So, Jeff, your position is that we can't know moral truth before we hear God say so. But isn't it also your position that people who have never heard of Jesus Christ in this life are still condemned to hell because God wrote the law on their hearts?"[^3]

> [Durbin] "Is it possible to know that murder is wrong without the Bible? Uh, no. It's not." ... [then] "there is a moral law that's written on everybody's hearts... before the Bible was around."[^4]
