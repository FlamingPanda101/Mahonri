# Pre-Columbian Horse Remains Found in Yucatan Caves and Cenotes

## The Claim

Beyond the much-discussed central-Mexico stratigraphy near San Luis Potosí (see [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism]]), there is an **older and independent corpus of horse remains from the Yucatán Peninsula** — recovered from caves and cenotes since the late 1800s — that points to horses in the Americas in pre-Columbian (and Book of Mormon-era) times. The remains went largely unremarked, [[Daniel Johnson]] argues, because the discipline either pre-judged them modern or declined to look. Caves and cenotes matter because, as [[Kirk Magleby]] notes, skeletal/osteo material survives far longer in a cave than in most surface environments.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. Mercer's *Hill Caves of Yucatan* — extinct horses misidentified as modern

Edward Mercer excavated Yucatán caves hoping to find traces of prehistoric habitation and published *The Hill Caves of Yucatan*. He recovered horse teeth and bones, but — applying "the prevailing thought of the day" — labeled them modern *Equus caballus*, i.e. assumed-European, and "left it at that." Later paleontologists reexamined the finds and identified them not as *E. caballus* but as **extinct New World species** — *Equus conversidens* and *E. mexicanus*.[^2]

### 2. Mayapán cenote — *Journal of Mammalogy* (1957)

The late-Classic Maya capital **Mayapán** (occupied c. AD 1020–1440) has horse remains recovered from one of its cenotes — sacred ceremonial water sources around which Yucatán cities were built. The material (curated by the Carnegie Institution) was written up in the *Journal of Mammalogy* in 1957, where the horse teeth and bone fragments were characterized as coming from a **pre-Maya** (hence pre-Columbian, pre-contact) time.[^3]

### 3. Loltún Cave and the stratigraphic association

The **Loltún Cave** system has produced horse and other megafauna remains; in a chamber called the Huechil Grotto, two excavations ("El Túnel," "El Toro") found Maya Classic and pre-Classic ceramics interbedded with **horse-bone fragments in the same layers** (plus bison bone deeper down) — "not where you'd expect the horse bones to be." Organic material near one horizon carbon-dated to **1805 BC ±150** — into the Jaredite window. Critics answer "stratigraphic mixing," but Johnson stresses the original authors were "really perplexed," not dismissive.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The mainstream reading remains coherent. (a) The carbon dates are on *associated* charcoal/shell, not on the horse bones themselves — so a date proves only that *something* in the layer is old, and a contaminating modern bone could intrude. (b) Cenotes and caves are notoriously *bad* sequenced contexts: water transport, collapse, and reuse mix material across millennia, so "in the same layer" is weaker than in a clean dig. (c) Re-identifying Mercer's teeth as *E. conversidens* actually concedes the standard story — those are Pleistocene species presumed extinct long before 600 BC, so their presence in a cave says nothing about Nephite-era *living* horses unless the dating is secure. (d) A genuine domestic-horse culture should leave abundant material culture (tack, stables, horses in Maya art used as mounts) that is essentially absent. The honest LDS position therefore treats this as corroborating texture, not proof — "trending," not resolved.
