# Martin Harris Was a Witness of Christ Not Only of the Plates

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues we systematically under-read [[Martin Harris]]: we remember him as scribe, funder, and witness *of the plates*, but "we don't tend to think of Martin Harris as a witness of Jesus Christ — that's exactly how we should think of Martin Harris." In Joseph Smith's earliest (1832) history, the Lord "appeared unto him in a vision," making Martin one of *three* founding figures — with Joseph and [[Oliver Cowdery]] — who saw the Savior. This reframes the witness motif from documentary (golden plates) to **Christological** (a personal vision of Christ), and supplies the heritage Bradley traces forward to apostle [[Dallin H. Oaks]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The 1832 history lists three Christ-visionaries in a row.** Joseph introduces his own First Vision ("the Lord opened the heavens upon me, and I saw the Lord"); then "a man by the name of Martin Harris became convinced of the vision… and the Lord appeared unto him in a vision"; then "the Lord appeared unto a young man by the name Oliver Cowdery." Bradley: "Count them… One, two, three. Three Witnesses" who "saw the Savior at the beginning of this work."[^1]
2. **Martin's New York City errand was Christ-commanded.** Per the 1832 text, after Martin gave Joseph $50, "the Lord appeared unto him in a vision and shewed unto him his marvelous work… and said the Lord had shown him that he must go to New York City with some of the characters." So the famous trip to [[Charles Anthon]] originated in a revelation *to Martin*, not merely Joseph's idea — Martin is a genuine coworker receiving independent revelation.[^2]
3. **This instantiates the law of witnesses.** Bradley ties it to D&C 6 / the biblical "in the mouth of two or three witnesses": the Restoration needed multiple independent witnesses of Christ, and Martin is one of them — distinct from, and prior to, his later role as a witness of the plates. (See [[The Law of Witnesses Establishes Truth]].)[^3]
4. **It grounds the apostolic-continuity argument.** An apostle is by definition "a witness of Jesus Christ" — "you can't just be an apostle in the abstract." Martin filled that witnessing role; his descendant Oaks fills it formally. (See [[The Harris Bloodline Connects the First Revelation to the 18th Prophet]].)[^4]
5. **Steelman of the critic.** A skeptic notes the 1832 account is autobiographical and the only source for Martin's and Oliver's "visions of Christ," that "vision" language can be flexible, and that this reads more into the source than later witness statements support. Bradley's reply is textual fidelity: he is reporting what Joseph's *earliest* history says, in parallel grammar for all three men, before later harmonization — and the claim is modest (Joseph regarded these three as having seen the Savior), not a fresh apologetic for the plates. The interpretive weight is the *grouping*; the data is the 1832 wording.

## Best Quotes

> "We don't tend to think of Martin Harris as a witness of Jesus Christ. That's exactly how we should think of Martin Harris. He appeared to him in a vision and told him. And the same thing with Oliver Cowdery."[^1]

> "How many of us have realized that he did that because Christ appeared to Martin Harris and told him to take the characters?"[^2]

> "An apostle is a messenger, an apostle is a witness. You can't just be an apostle in the abstract… An apostle's role is to be a witness of Jesus Christ."[^4]
