# The Fourfold Medieval Sense of Scripture

## The Claim

By the medieval period the patristic multiple-senses approach (see [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]]) hardened into the classic **fourfold sense**: the **literal**, the **allegorical**, the **moral**, and the **anagogical**. [^1] The anagogical sense — from the Greek for "leading up" — is "a method of mystical or spiritual interpretation... that looks beyond literal, allegorical, or moral meanings to discover a text's hidden reference to eternal life, heaven, or the afterlife." [^2] Whatever was salvific or christological ranked highest; the literal sense ranked lowest. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The christological sense overruled all others.** "If there were problematic parts of it you just had to find a way to read Christ into it... the christological sense overrules any other sense." [^4] McClellan illustrates with the LDS "where's Waldo" habit of finding Christ everywhere ("there's a rock... the rock is Christ"). [^5] **(Accurate characterization of medieval allegoresis and its modern echoes.)**
2. **Erotic poetry shows the system at work.** The [[Song of Songs 4.5|Song of Songs]] ("I love your breasts, church... they look like some deer") is read not as the erotic poetry it plainly is but christologically as Christ and the church. "The last thing they were going to do is champion the plain sense of this erotic poetry." [^6][^7] **(Strong — the allegorical reading of the Song is the dominant pre-modern interpretation.)**
3. **This located authority in the church, not the text.** Four open-ended layers of meaning meant *someone* had to adjudicate the right reading — and "well, the church is" the ultimate authority, "which endows the institution with an awful lot of authority." [^8] This sets up the Reformation backlash. See [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]]. **(Strong.)**
4. **Steelman.** A defender of allegory could argue the fourfold sense is hermeneutically richer and more pastorally fruitful than wooden literalism, and that the medievals still affirmed the literal as the foundation on which the others rest (Aquinas: the spiritual senses presuppose the literal). McClellan's point is comparative and historical: pre-modern Christianity granted *enormous* interpretive latitude, the very opposite of today's hard literalism. **(Fair — and consistent with the show's thesis that literalism is a modern invention.)**
