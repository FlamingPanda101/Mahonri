# Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Spalding theory holds that [[Solomon Spalding]] wrote a romance about Roman adventurers in the New World, that [[Sydney Rigdon]] obtained it and met [[Joseph Smith]] *before* the record shows, and that the Book of Mormon is really Spalding's manuscript repackaged.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Completely discredited among trained historians.** The theory "has been completely discredited by any good historian"; "among historians who have actual graduate training in history, there's nobody who takes that."[^2] *Rating: strong — scholarly consensus.*

2. **Born of the same biased project.** [[Philastus Hurlbut]] gathered the Conneaut, Ohio interviews to *establish* the Spalding theory at the same time he collected the [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem|anti-Smith statements]] — i.e., a commissioned, motivated effort.[^3] *Rating: strong — provenance undermines it.*

3. **Requires an unsupported Rigdon–Smith pre-acquaintance.** The theory depends on Sydney Rigdon meeting Joseph earlier than documented, "all this baloney" with no good evidentiary basis.[^4] *Rating: strong.*

4. **It survives only outside scholarship.** Mark laments it "still gets pushed today" in comment sections by people who are likely not historians: "it never died."[^5] *Rating: contextual — explains persistence despite refutation.*

5. **Steelman.** A defender of the theory might point to alleged stylistic parallels and witness statements claiming familiarity with Spalding's manuscript. The decisive counter is that the surviving Spalding manuscript bears no real resemblance to the Book of Mormon and the transmission chain (via Rigdon) is unevidenced — which is why no credentialed historian maintains it. Mark does not detail the parallels here, treating the consensus as settled.

## Best Quotes
