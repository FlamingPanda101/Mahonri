# Greco-Roman Jewish Creation View Was Material Substrate

#type/claim

## The Claim

The conventional view across Greco-Roman period Judaism — and the broader Hellenistic philosophical world — was that creation involved God (or the gods, or the Demiurge) ordering and imparting form to a pre-existent material substrate, not generating matter from absolute nothingness. The axiom *ex nihilo nihil fit* ("nothing comes from nothing"), first articulated by Parmenides and affirmed by Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, and Epicureans, was universally accepted. "Creation" meant transformation of existing stuff — imposing form, function, and order on chaotic, formless, passive matter that occupied the realm of "non-being" (τὸ μὴ ὄν). When God "called the non-existent into existence," this referred to moving things from the state of formless non-being to the state of formed being — not to creation from nothing.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Philosophical Background: *Ex Nihilo Nihil Fit*

The principle that "nothing comes from nothing" was a bedrock of Greco-Roman thought:

- **Parmenides (5th c. BCE):** "What is not" cannot be thought or spoken of; being is eternal and ungenerated. Nothing can come from nothing.
- **[[Plato]], *Timaeus*:** The Demiurge (divine craftsman) creates the world by looking to the eternal Forms and ordering the pre-existing "Receptacle" (χώρα / *chōra*) — chaotic, formless matter. Plato explicitly says the world "came into being" (γέγονεν), but from pre-existing principles, not from nothing.
- **[[Aristotle]], *Physics* 1.8–9:** Matter (ὕλη / *hylē*) is eternal. Creation is the actualization of potential in pre-existing matter, not generation from nothing. "Coming into being" is always from a substrate (ὑποκείμενον).
- **Stoicism:** Two eternal principles — active (God/Logos) and passive (matter). God permeates and organizes matter but does not create it.
- **Epicureanism:** Atoms are eternal; nothing comes from nothing, nothing returns to nothing.

McClellan summarizes: "The conventional wisdom of the day within the Platonic and Middle Platonic worldview... along with the Stoic didn't accept the notion that anything could come out of nothing."[^2]

### 2. Aristotle on "Non-Being" → "Being"

Aristotle's framework is crucial for understanding the language in 2 Maccabees and Philo:

> "Aristotle talks about the creation of things as a process of bringing them from non-being into being and then the destruction of things as a process of taking them from being to non-being. So this is par for the course when it comes to creation out of pre-existing material."[^3]

In Aristotle's *Physics* 1.8 (191a–b), "non-being" (τὸ μὴ ὄν) is the privation of form in matter — not the absence of matter. A statue "comes into being" from bronze; the bronze was "not a statue" (non-being qua statue) but it existed as bronze. This is the philosophical idiom in which Hellenistic Jewish texts operate.

### 3. Wisdom of Solomon 11:17 — The Explicit Statement

Perhaps the clearest statement of the Hellenistic Jewish view comes from the Wisdom of Solomon (c. 1st century BCE), roughly contemporary with 2 Maccabees:

> "For your all-powerful hand, which created the world out of formless matter (ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης / *ex amorphou hylēs*), did not lack the means to send upon them a multitude of bears or bold lions." ([[Wisdom 11.17|Wisdom 11:17]], NRSV)

This is the same authorial milieu as 2 Maccabees. The Greek phrase ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης directly echoes Plato's *Timaeus* and explicitly affirms creation from pre-existent formless matter. If 2 Maccabees 7:28 were teaching creation ex nihilo, it would contradict this clear statement from a closely related text.

### 4. Philo's Dualism

As detailed in [[Philo Did Not Endorse Creation Ex Nihilo]], Philo explicitly posits two eternal principles:

> "At the very least one principle of being must be active, the other passive, and that the active cause is the universal mind... The passive principle, on the other hand, is in itself lifeless and motionless, but when moved, shaped, and quickened by reason, is transformed into the most perfect master work, this world."[^4]

Philo read Genesis 1:2 (the "water, darkness, and chaos" — *tohu wa-bohu*) as depicting precisely this pre-existent passive matter. He believed "Moses had anticipated Plato in this matter."[^4]

### 5. Genesis 1:2 and the *Tohu Wa-Bohu*

The Hebrew Bible itself opens with a state of pre-existent chaos:

> "And the earth was without form, and void (תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ / *tohu wa-bohu*); and darkness was upon the face of the deep (תְּהוֹם / *tehom*). And the Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters." (Genesis 1:2, KJV)

The text does not say God created the *tohu wa-bohu* or the *tehom* — they are simply there when the narrative begins. God's creative acts in Genesis 1 are acts of separation, ordering, and filling — imposing form and function on pre-existent chaotic elements. This is fully consistent with the broader ANE cosmogonic pattern (cf. Enuma Elish, where Marduk creates the cosmos from Tiamat's defeated body).

### 6. "Non-Being" as Formlessness, Not Nothingness

The key to understanding the Hellenistic Jewish view is the concept of "non-being":

> "All of the world was created out of a material substrate that occupied the realm of non-being. And imposing form and function on that material substrate moved it from the realm of non-being to the realm of being. That is what the majority of scholars agree about this."[^5]

In this framework:
- **"Non-being" (τὸ μὴ ὄν)** = formless, chaotic, passive matter. It "is not" in the sense that it lacks form, identity, and function. It is "non-existent" with respect to being a cosmos.
- **"Being" (τὸ ὄν)** = matter that has received form, order, identity, and function from the divine craftsman.
- **"Creation"** = the process of moving matter from the realm of non-being to being by imposing form.

When 2 Maccabees 7:28 says God made the world "not out of things that exist" (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων), it means God did not make the world out of already-formed things — He made it out of the formless substrate of non-being. This is creation from pre-existent matter, not from nothing.

### 7. The New Testament Context

Even New Testament passages sometimes cited for creation ex nihilo make better sense within the material-substrate framework:

- **Hebrews 11:3:** "By faith we understand that the worlds were framed by the word of God, so that the things which are seen were not made of things which are visible." The contrast is visible/invisible, not being/non-being. "Invisible things" (μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων) — God formed the visible world from invisible material, consistent with the material-substrate view.
- **Romans 4:17:** Paul describes God as one "who gives life to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not exist." The context is Abraham's faith in God's promise of offspring — not cosmic creation. "Calling into existence" refers to God bringing about what was promised but not yet realized.

### 8. The Shift in the Late 2nd Century CE

The doctrine of creation ex nihilo, when it emerged through Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus, was a deliberate philosophical innovation — a break from the consensus, not a clarification of it. It was driven by specific apologetic needs (anti-Gnostic polemic, resurrection defense) and required rejecting the *ex nihilo nihil fit* axiom that had governed Greco-Roman thought for nearly a millennium.[^6]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A traditional view might argue: Genesis 1:1 ("In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth") describes the initial creation of matter itself, with verse 2 describing the state of that created matter before God ordered it. The Hebrew verb *bara* (בָּרָא) is used exclusively of divine creation and never takes a material complement — suggesting creation without pre-existing material. Furthermore, the philosophical "consensus" of the Greco-Roman world is precisely what biblical revelation challenges. The fact that later fathers articulated creation ex nihilo against that consensus shows they were recovering the Bible's own teaching, not inventing something new.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **_Bara_ does not entail creation from nothing.** The verb is used for God's creative acts within Genesis 1 that clearly involve pre-existing material (e.g., Genesis 1:21, God *bara* the sea creatures — from the waters that already existed; Genesis 1:27, God *bara* humanity — from the dust of the ground in Genesis 2:7). *Bara* means "to create" in the sense of bringing about something new through divine action, without specifying whether material was pre-existing.
2. **Genesis 1:1 is grammatically ambiguous.** It can be read as a temporal clause ("When God began to create the heavens and the earth...") or a summary title. Many scholars (e.g., [[E.A. Speiser]], *Anchor Bible Genesis*) argue the *tohu wa-bohu* of verse 2 is the starting condition, not something God created in verse 1 and then... left formless.
3. **The consensus is not just "the world's opinion."** The material-substrate view was held by the biblical authors' own intellectual descendants (Philo, the author of Wisdom, the Maccabean author) in the very period when these texts were being written. This is not pagan opposition — it's the view of Hellenistic Judaism itself.
4. **The development is historically traceable.** If creation ex nihilo were the Bible's own teaching, we would expect to find it articulated clearly somewhere in the pre-2nd-century Jewish or Christian corpus. Instead, we find Wisdom of Solomon explicitly teaching creation from formless matter, Philo teaching eternal passive matter, and 2 Maccabees using philosophical language consistent with the material-substrate view.
