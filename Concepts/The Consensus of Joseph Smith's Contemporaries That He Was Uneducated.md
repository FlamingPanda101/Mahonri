# The Consensus of Joseph Smith's Contemporaries That He Was Uneducated

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that a Book of Mormon of the demonstrated scale and complexity is beyond the documented capacities of the young Joseph — and that the evidence for this comes not from Latter-day Saints but from a *near-universal consensus of Joseph's own contemporaries*, friend, foe, and family alike, that he was barely educated. The probative weight is that the testimony is broad and largely *hostile*: people with every motive to dismiss the Book of Mormon nonetheless describe an author who, on their own account, lacked the faculties to produce it. Carroll pairs this with the production data — roughly 269,320 words across 6,852 sentences dictated in about 65 working days in a single draft — to argue that the natural-authorship explanation is the one that strains credulity.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The output is genuinely extraordinary.** By Carroll's count the text was produced by a 23-year-old in ~65 working days, one draft; Jesus Christ is named by some title nearly 4,000 times (about once every 1.7 verses); it spans 600 BC–AD 450, carries 26+ authors "each one identifiable by unique vocabulary and style," 170+ unique proper nouns, 77 major storylines, and 63+ sermons totaling 87,000+ words. Whatever its origin, the artifact is not trivial to account for.[^2] *Rating: a real explanandum* — even the critic [[Joseph Smith Did Not Author the Book of Mormon|Kyle Beshears concedes the complexity]] is hard to source to one young man.

2. **Hostile and family testimony converges.** Carroll stacks the contemporaries: Benjamin Pierce ("the Smith boys grew up without a desire for education... none of them ever went to school when they could get out of it"); Pomeroy Tucker, a skeptic, who called him "uneducated and ignorant"; his mother [[Lucy Mack Smith]] ("less inclined to study the books than any child we ever had"); his father-in-law [[Isaac Hale]], no friend of Joseph, who in 1825 saw "a careless young man who was not very well educated"; Orsamus Turner, who thought him a "cheat and a fraud" of "less than ordinary intellect"; [[W. W. Phelps]] ("very limited abilities and common learning"); Rev. John A. Clark ("utterly dull and destitute of genius"); Abel Chase ("very poor and uneducated and ignorant"); and even Eber Howe's hostile 1834 *Mormonism Unvailed*, which grants "the common advantages of education were... much neglected."[^3][^4] *Rating: the strongest form of the argument* — the witnesses are independent and mostly adversarial, so the convergence is hard to attribute to LDS bias.

3. **The skills-versus-record gap.** Carroll's framing question: enumerate the skills and knowledge required to *dictate* a long, multi-author, theologically dense book, then ask whether the documentary record shows Joseph possessed them. He argues the gap between requirement and record is the heart of the case, not a bare appeal to ignorance.[^5]

4. **Critics will not let you read it.** Carroll's tactical observation: opponents "caricature it... cherry-pick verses out of context" but "will not invite you to read it," because the Christ-saturated content (e.g. [[2 Nephi 2.8]]) undercuts the "demonic" framing on contact. The book itself is, for him, the decisive datum.[^6]

**Steelman of the critic.** The damaging admission is one Carroll reports against himself: after he laid out exactly this evidence to ~200 creedal Christians at a Ratio Christi conference, the audience *agreed* Joseph "could not have done this on his own" — and then concluded he "could have been demonically possessed."[^7] This is a coherent move for the critic: granting Joseph's incapacity does **not** force the LDS conclusion, because a third option (preternatural, demonic assistance) remains live — exactly the [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable|demonic explanation]] [[Trent Horn]] keeps on the table via [[Thomas Aquinas]]. The critic can thus accept every contemporary quotation and the entire word-count and still deny divine inspiration. Carroll's rejoinder is that this only relocates the question to whether the demonic premise is itself justified, and that the evidence's strength is shown precisely by the critic's retreat to "the devil made him do it." See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].

## Best Quotes
> "It was produced by a 23-year-old... in 65 working days in one draft. One draft. It contains 269,320 words comprising 6,852 sentences."[^2]

> "Whether friend or foe or family... the consensus among Joseph's contemporaries was that he was not the sharpest stick in the pile."[^4]

> "The evidence for Joseph Smith not being able to write the Book of Mormon is so strong they've resorted to the devil made him do it."[^7]

> "One thing you will see the critics do is not invite you to read the Book of Mormon. They will caricature it. They will cherry-pick verses out of context... but they will not invite you to read it."[^6]
