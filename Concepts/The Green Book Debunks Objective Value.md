# The Green Book Debunks Objective Value

## The Point
Lewis opens *The Abolition of Man* by dissecting a single sentence from a school textbook (King and Ketley's *The Control of Language*, which he pseudonymously calls "The Green Book"). Glossing Coleridge's reaction to a waterfall, the textbook tells children that when we call something "sublime" we "appear to be saying something very important about something: and actually we are only saying something about our own feelings."[^1] Lewis's central objection is to the word **"only."** The lesson quietly reduces every value-judgment to a mere report of the speaker's emotional state — and, crucially, it smuggles this contentious metaethical thesis into the mind of a boy who "thinks he is 'doing' his 'English prep' and has no notion that ethics, theology, and politics are all at stake."[^2]

## Evidence
Ward stresses that the schoolboy example is a rhetorical springboard, not Lewis's real target: "The Green Book is a specimen of a much larger problem."[^3] What inflames Lewis is the *insidious* "only": he "would not, of course, deny that our feelings may be relevant factors when we make evaluative statements. But to reduce evaluative statements in general to nothing more than statements of emotion on the part of the speaker, as Gaius and Titius appear to do, is excessive."[^4] The deeper danger is *concealment*: the textbook "introduces a highly contentious view without ever admitting it to be such... It smuggled it in without argument in a book on a slightly different subject, for children, without probably being even aware that it was controversial."[^5] Ward notes the **seamlessness of the normative** (Cuneo): once one is a subjectivist about aesthetic value, consistency forces subjectivism about *every* normative domain — morality, prudence, logic — so the harmless-looking lesson about waterfalls is in fact a solvent for ethics itself.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "We appear to be saying something very important about something: and actually we are only saying something about our own feelings." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The Green Book's lesson is the textbook ancestor of every modern metaethic that dissolves "X is wrong" into "I disapprove of X" — emotivism ([[Ethical Emotivism as O'Connor's Metaethic]]) and the well-being subjectivism behind [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] alike. Latter-day Saint scripture flatly denies the "only": moral perception is not a private feeling but a *response to real light*. "The Spirit of Christ is given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16), and that Light "enlighteneth every man... that hearkeneth to the voice" (D&C 84:46). When a child recognizes a noble act as noble, the Restoration says he is detecting a quality genuinely there, illumined by the Light of Christ — not merely emitting a feeling. The covert smuggling Lewis exposes is itself a moral failing: teaching children that their deepest valuations are illusions corrodes the conscience God planted in them (compare [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
