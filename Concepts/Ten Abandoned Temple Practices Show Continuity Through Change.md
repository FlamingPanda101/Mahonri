#type/claim

## The Claim

The Latter-day Saint temple has been continuously altered — practices have been added, modified, and quietly dropped across the Church's short history — yet none of these changes was treated as apostasy, because the discontinued items were forms and procedures, not the covenant core. [[Jonathan Stapley]]'s documented catalog of *lesser-known* discontinued practices (set apart from the controversial penalties/wording changes) is offered as proof that change in the temple is the historical norm and is doctrinally unremarkable: familiarity with today's form is "not always been done that way" and need not be "done that way in the future."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

The case is strongest when the critic's own framing — *"they keep changing the temple"* — is granted in full and then turned: the changes are real, numerous, and datable, which is exactly what a *living, revelation-led* institution should produce rather than a static one (the burden-shift of [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]).

1. **The list is concrete and verifiable, not hand-waved.** Each item carries a date and a mechanism, so it cannot be dismissed as apologetic vagueness: children's proxy baptisms ended ~1960 (David O. McKay's journal);[^1] eating fruit in the Nauvoo/Endowment-House Eden reenactment;[^2] baptisms *of healing* (Naaman model) phased out early 20th c. to free fonts for proxy work;[^3] healing blessings by men and/or women discontinued 1921;[^4] non-temple prayer circles (1851–1929) consolidated to the temple May 3, 1978;[^5] the temple choir and annex ended under George F. Richards;[^6] Disney *Fantasia* footage licensed for the 1955 creation film;[^7] early temple libraries;[^8] temple-consecrated oil sold at a gate shop;[^9] and women once sealed to multiple living men.[^10]

2. **The mechanisms are administrative, not doctrinal reversals.** The drivers Keystone names are font availability, standardization, and streamlining ("the choir wasn't essential, so it didn't make the cut")[^6] — the kind of policy housekeeping covered by [[Mistakes Versus Leading Astray]] and [[Conflating Anecdote and Culture With Doctrine Misrepresents the Church]]. A practice dropped *to make proxy ordinances more available* is the ordinance's priorities being clarified, not the ordinance being abandoned.

3. **It fits the restoration-as-adaptation model.** Per [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]], God meets people in their culture, so the endowment was always expected to carry adaptable forms around a stable covenant. Fruit-eating, a choir, a licensed film clip, and a gate-side oil shop are precisely the *forms* the model predicts will flex.

4. **The historiographical honesty is itself evidence.** The presenter repeatedly flags uncertainty — "this next one I have next to zero information on, so I apologize in advance" about temple libraries[^8] — and credits a named academic historian.[^11] An apologetic that volunteers its own evidentiary gaps is behaving like history, not propaganda.

**Steelman of the critic.** The strongest counter grants every date and still presses three points. First, *quantity cuts both ways*: a defender cannot celebrate the sheer number of changes as proof of vitality and simultaneously insist the temple transmits a fixed, anciently-rooted ceremony — if fruit, choirs, baptisms-of-healing, and the film can all go, the "ancient pattern" doing the apologetic work is thin. Second, *the form/core line is drawn after the fact*: the practices were not labeled "mere forms" while they were observed (children were really baptized for the dead; women were really sealed to two living men), so calling them peripheral *now* is a retroactive sort that the critic never agreed to. Third, *the catalog is curated*: Keystone deliberately brackets the genuinely controversial changes (penalties, washing/anointing wording, the lecture at the veil), so a list built to look benign should not be generalized into "all temple change is benign." The honest reply is narrow and holds: this source proves the *modest* thesis — that discontinuation of a temple practice is, by itself, historically routine and not evidence of apostasy (the lexical point of [[Apostasia Means Deliberate Rebellion Not Mere Change]]) — while the *covenant* content and the harder cases must be argued on their own merits, not smuggled in under a friendly list.
