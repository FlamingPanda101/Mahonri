# Hinckley I Dont Know That We Teach It

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection)

Critics cite [[Gordon B. Hinckley]]'s 1997 *Time* magazine answer about whether the church still holds that God the Father was once a man — "I don't know that we teach it, I don't know that we emphasize it" — as evidence either that Mormons hide their real beliefs or that the prophet himself did not know his own doctrine.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The full quote is candor, not evasion.** Hinckley said: "I don't know that we teach it. I don't know that we emphasize it. I understand the philosophical background behind it, but I don't know a lot about it, and I don't think others know a lot about it."[^2] [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] reads this as straightforward honesty about an esoteric teaching, not denial of it.
2. **"Spontaneous, so don't parse it too much."** Griffiths cautions that the answer was off the cuff and should not be over-read in either direction — neither as a doctrinal repudiation nor as a gaffe.[^3]
3. **The follow-up clarification.** Griffiths notes that at the next general conference Hinckley addressed members who had written him, saying in effect "I know the doctrine" — modeling the responsible move of stating what we know, what is important, and what we honestly do not know.[^4] This undercuts the "he denied it" reading.
4. **The honest unknowns.** Griffiths lists the genuine blanks: we do not know God's planet, whether he was a good or bad kid, whether there is a "Heavenly Grandpa." Admitting ignorance about specifics is, he argues, the intellectually responsible posture.[^5]
5. **Steelman of the critic.** A critic can fairly say that a living prophet publicly hedging on a founding prophet's signature teaching is itself significant: if the doctrine were secure and central, why the discomfort? The "esoteric / spontaneous" framing can look like damage control. The strongest critical version is not "Hinckley didn't know his doctrine" but "the church's own leadership treats King Follett as a liability." See [[Modern LDS Tensions on King Follett Discourse]] for that institutional ambivalence.
6. **Evidence rating.** The quotation is authentic and widely cited; Griffiths' irenic reading is plausible but partial — it explains the *tone* without resolving the *theological tension* the critic is pressing.

## Best Quotes

> "On whether his church still holds that God the Father was once a man, he sounded uncertain: 'I don't know that we teach it. I don't know that we emphasize it. I understand the philosophical background behind it, but I don't know a lot about it, and I don't think others know a lot about it.'"[^6]

> "President Hinckley's response there was spontaneous, so I don't want to parse it too much... but he's being honest in that we really don't know very much about it."[^7]
