# The First Shall Be Last Frames Western Social Progress
## The Point
Nearly every Western reform movement is animated by a distinctively Christian shape: the conviction that the weak, the poor, and the victim have a *claim* on society, and that history should bend toward a world where universal human dignity is realized. That moral grammar — "the first shall be last and the last shall be first," and "the power of victimhood" — is itself part of "the revolutionary inheritance of Christianity," ultimately rooted in the cross. Secular progressivism keeps this trajectory while disowning its origin.

## Evidence
1. **Victimhood as Christian inheritance.** "The power of victimhood is again something that is part of the revolutionary inheritance of Christianity... because that is the point of the crucifixion. Nobody in Caesar's world would have said, 'I'm a victim, therefore you have got to prioritize me.'"[^1] The elevation of the victim is counter-pagan.

2. **Rights of the poor inscribed in law.** Medieval canon lawyers shifted the duty-of-charity into a *right*: "up until then it had been thought that the rich have a duty to give to the poor, but these canon lawyers start to inscribe in the frameworks of law... that the poor and the weak have a right."[^2] (This is the legal birth Holland traces; cf. [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]].)

3. **A flame of social reformation.** "When Christianity instills the universal dignity of all mankind it lights a flame of social reformation... it frames the idea of social progress toward a society where universal human dignity is fully realized. It is this framing of social progress... that undergirds essentially all Western social movements."[^3] The very *direction* of progress is a Christian premise.

4. **The repeating archetype of revolution.** Because "at the heart of Christianity is the principle that the first will be last and the last shall be first," the West acquired "a kind of archetype of reformation, of revolution, that will repeat over and over again throughout European history."[^4] The papal reformation, the Reformation, and the Enlightenment are successive "ripple effects."

## LDS Connection
Scripture makes care for the marginalized a covenant obligation, not a cultural mood: King Benjamin ties salvation to imparting to the poor (Mosiah 4:16-26), and Zion is defined by having "no poor among them" (Moses 7:18). The Restoration thus owns the very "first shall be last" trajectory the video traces — but grounds it in covenant and the literal kinship of all people as God's children, so the impulse toward justice has a source that can sustain it once secular borrowed capital runs out.
