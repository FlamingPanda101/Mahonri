# The Early-Development Dilemma for Macroevolution

## The Point
To build a genuinely new body plan, a mutation must act on a gene that does its job **early** in development and controls the expression of many downstream genes.[^1] But mutations affecting early development and the body plan as a whole are essentially always lethal — Gelernter notes there are "total of no examples in the literature of mutations that affect early development and the body plan as a whole and are not fatal."[^1] This produces a **destructive dilemma**: mutations that arrive *late* in development cannot remake the organism (it is already built — at most "more lavish eyebrows"), while mutations that arrive *early* enough to matter destroy the organism, because "too many things downstream depend on those early cell divisions."[^2] Late, no good; early, no good.

## Evidence
1. **You must act early to change the plan.** Berlinski's farmyard illustration: to turn a sheep into a different creature you "have to get in there early before they start building them"; a small late change might give "purple wool" or "the wrong color hooves," but a change big enough to make a *different* creature is "almost certainly likely to be fatal."[^3]
2. **No non-fatal early-development mutations on record.** "Evidently there are total of no examples in the literature of mutations that affect early development and the body plan as a whole and are not fatal."[^1]
3. **The disjunction stated formally.** "If they come late in development they're not going to make a difference — the organism is already constructed... If they come early they can't make a difference because inevitably they destroy the organism. Too many things downstream depend on those early cell divisions."[^2]
4. **Science's duty to test guesses.** The panel frames this not as an attack on Darwin the man but as the ordinary scientific obligation: "scientists are paid for making guesses... but if scientists after the guess has been made don't do their job... then we are false to science."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "There are total of no examples in the literature of mutations that affect early development and the body plan as a whole and are not fatal." (Gelernter, quoting his essay) [^1]

> "Late, no good; early, no good." (Berlinski) [^2]

> "A mutation that is going to recreate the creature in such a way that it's a different creature is... almost certainly likely to be fatal." (Berlinski) [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is a technical objection to the *creative power of unguided mutation*, not a claim about the earth's age or about whether organisms share ancestry, and Latter-day Saint thought takes no fixed position on evolutionary mechanism. Its apologetic use is modest and should stay honest: if the very mutations capable of producing new body plans are the ones natural selection cannot preserve, the gap is more naturally filled by direction than by chance — consistent with an LDS picture of a God who organizes life through ordered processes rather than with creation *ex nihilo*.
