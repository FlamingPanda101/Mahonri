# The Court Analogy for the Godhead

## The Claim
The one "what" of the Godhead is best modeled as a **single corporate entity composed of multiple persons** — like a court. Three judges form one Supreme Court; we speak of "the court" with singular pronouns ("the court found him guilty"), and any one justice can speak *as* the court. So the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are three persons constituting one Godhead, spoken of in the singular without being one *being*.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Singular language for a plural body is ordinary usage
We routinely use a singular noun and singular verbs for a multi-member institution — "the Supreme Court ruled," even when only one justice wrote the opinion. This dissolves the force of "one God" singular language: oneness of reference does not require oneness of being. *(Strong as an analogy for *linguistic* usage; see steelman for its metaphysical limits.)* [^1]

### 2. A member can speak for the whole
On a court, one justice writes "the court's opinion" and speaks with the authority of the whole. This grounds the **divine-agency** reading: when the Son speaks or bears the divine name, he can say "I am the court" without being numerically identical to the other members. See [[Divine Agency and the Divine-Name Bearer]]. [^2]

### 3. It accommodates a chief justice (relational order)
A court has a chief justice; all justices are equal *as* members of the highest body, yet there is internal order. This maps onto **ontological equality with relational subordination** — the Father is "first" relationally while the three share the divine nature. See [[Ontological Equality With Relational Subordination]]. [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
[[Go With Jordan]] presses the decisive objection: **"A court is not a being. It is an institution."** A court is a *legal abstraction*, not a substance — there is no third entity "the court" over and above the three judges, whereas Trinitarians claim the one *ousia* is a real being, not a mere collective noun. So (the objection runs) the analogy actually concedes the LDS position is *three gods* loosely grouped, and a corporate noun cannot carry the metaphysical weight of "one God." Hansen's rejoinder is that scripture itself depicts God institutionally — the divine council, the Son at the Father's *right hand* (a king-with-right-hand-man image; [[Hebrews 1.3|Hebrews 1:3]]) — not as a single undifferentiated being, so the burden remains on the Trinitarian to show the "one being" claim *in the text*.

## Best Quotes
> "We can have a single entity — let's say there are three judges, and those three judges form a single court, and we will use singular pronouns to talk about the court... even if only one of them speaks." [^1]

> "On a court, there is a chief justice... not all of them are the chief justice, but any one of them that speaks can speak as if they are the court." [^3]

> "[Jordan:] A court is not a being. It is not a nature... It depicts it as an institution." [^4]
