# Isaiah 7:14 — Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated

## The Claim

Christian apologists (e.g., [[Tim Chaffy]] of Answers in Genesis) cite [[Isaiah 7.14|Isaiah 7:14]] as a prophecy of Jesus's virgin birth, reading the KJV's "a virgin shall conceive" as a messianic prediction fulfilled in [[Matthew 1.23|Matthew 1:23]].[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Alma means "young woman," not "virgin"

The Hebrew word _alma_ (עַלְמָה) means "a young woman of marriageable age" — it does not specify virginity. Most modern critical translations (NRSVUE, JPS Tanakh) use "young woman." The Septuagint (Greek translation, ~2nd century BCE) rendered it as _parthenos_ (παρθένος, "virgin"), and Matthew quoted the Septuagint, not the Hebrew.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (lexical). The semantic range of _alma_ is well-established in Hebrew lexicography; the Hebrew word for "virgin" specifically is _betulah_.

### 2. The grammar indicates a contemporary pregnancy, not a future one

The Hebrew uses an adjective meaning "pregnant" without a tense marker. The Septuagint supplies a future-tense "shall conceive." But the Hebrew adjective "overwhelmingly is used to refer to either a current existing pregnancy or an imminent pregnancy" — "is pregnant or is right about to be pregnant."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (grammatical). The dominant usage pattern of the adjective supports a present/imminent reading.

### 3. The prophetic context is 8th-century BCE, not messianic

Isaiah 7 is set during a crisis under [[King Ahaz]] (~735 BCE). A coalition of northern kingdoms threatened Judah. Isaiah pointed to a pregnant woman known to the king and prophesied: before her child grows up, those threatening kingdoms will be gone. The prophecy's function was to say "relax — those countries are not going to be countries anymore" — it was a politically timed sign, not a prediction about a Messiah 700+ years later.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (contextual). The surrounding narrative (Isaiah 7:1-17) explicitly addresses Ahaz's contemporary political crisis.

### 4. The child's name was to be Immanuel — Jesus was never called Immanuel

The prophecy says "she shall call his name Immanuel." Nobody in the New Testament ever calls Jesus "Immanuel." Matthew 1:23 changes the subject from the mother to an unnamed "they" ("they shall call his name Emmanuel"), which "sounds like the followers of Jesus will refer to him as if he were God among us" — shifting a name into a title.[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (textual comparison). Direct comparison of Isaiah 7:14 (MT/LXX) with Matthew 1:23 shows the subject and function were altered.

### 5. Matthew likely constructed the fulfillment

Matthew's author, working with the Septuagint's _parthenos_ rendering, "wrenched this out of context" — the Septuagint had already brought the text "a little closer" to his time by translating it as present-relevant, and "Matthew has just given it one additional step" to tie it to Jesus.[^6]

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (critical inference). This is the mainstream scholarly view, supported by the cumulative evidence above.

## Best Quote

> "The overwhelming majority of scholars will agree on this — this is a reference to a woman who is pregnant in the time period when the author ... knew about some woman who was pregnant and was like, 'Look, that woman right there, she's pregnant.' And then it goes on to say she will give birth to a son, which is not much of a prophecy because that frequently happens." — [[Dan McClellan]][^7]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- Apologists argue the Septuagint translators (pre-Christian Jewish scholars) chose _parthenos_ deliberately, suggesting they understood _alma_ to mean "virgin." [[Dan McClellan|McClellan]] notes that _parthenos_ had "semantic fuzziness" — it was used of Dinah _after_ her violation, so it didn't rigidly mean "virgin" either.[^8]
- Some argue Matthew's midrashic approach would have been understood as creative interpretation, not literal fulfillment. McClellan acknowledges Matthew follows "dynamic interpretive hermeneutic practice" but says the modern apologetic claim is that "this is absolutely historical."[^9]
- See also [[Virgin Birth Is a Later Development in the Jesus Tradition]].

## Second Voice — Dialogue Gospel Study (2026)

In his *Dialogue Gospel Study* session, [[Dan McClellan]] makes the same case in a Latter-day Saint setting and uses it as the **flagship example of translation bias**: Christian translations render *almah* as "a virgin will conceive," but the Hebrew means "young woman" and the verb form indicates she is **already pregnant**, so the oracle is a timeline to King [[King Ahaz|Ahaz]] — by the time the already-pregnant young woman gives birth and the child is old enough to know right from wrong, the rival northern kingdom will be destroyed (per the session summary).[^10] He frames this within the larger claim that the Christian Old Testament is systematically shaped to support Christian theology — see [[The Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament Are Not the Same Book]].
