# The Apostles Held Equal Power With Peter

## The Claim

[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that the early church — even *after* the Roman see came to be associated with Peter's authority in the third century — held that the other apostles possessed authority *equal* to Peter's, not subordinate to it. This is offered as patristic evidence for how to read the New Testament: the fathers "didn't read Peter as the top guy in a pyramid structure," and the reason is that "you just look at the New Testament and you don't find that idea." [^1]

The witnesses Ortlund stacks up:

- **[[Cyprian of Carthage]]** (mid-3rd c.), responding to Stephen of Rome's claim of Petrine authority, states the rest of the apostles "were also the same as was Peter, endowed with a like partnership both of honor and power." Cyprian also held that "Peter did not assert that he had rights of seniority and that therefore upstarts and latecomers ought to be obedient to him." Ortlund: "equal power... I regard that as a consensus or close to a consensus among the church fathers." [^2]
- **Isidore of Seville** (7th c.), showing the view "persists late into the patristic era": "the other apostles also became equal sharers with Peter in honor and authority." [^3]
- **[[John Chrysostom]]** on Acts 15: James, not Peter, "was invested with the chief rule"; "Peter indeed spoke more strongly, but James here more mildly, for thus it behooves one in high authority to leave what is unpleasant for others to say." [^4]

Ortlund's logical frame: the appearance of a "Petrine office" associated with Rome is itself a third-century development (with [[Irenaeus of Lyons|Irenaeus]] earlier crediting *both* Peter and Paul as founders of the Roman church); but even once that idea arrives, the fathers do not invest the Roman bishop with authority *over the others*. The "consensus of equal power" is thus a standing patristic obstacle to Vatican I's "hierarchical subordination and true obedience." [^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Catholic)

The Catholic reply has substantial purchase and should be presented fully:

1. **"Equal in honor and power" is about the apostolic *order*, not the *primacy*.** Catholic theology readily grants that all the apostles received the fullness of orders and the power to bind and loose — bishops are their successors in *that* sense. Cyprian's "like partnership" affirms the collegiality of the episcopate; it does not deny that one member (Peter / his Roman successor) holds a *primacy of jurisdiction* within the college. The two are compatible: a college of equals can still have a head.
2. **Cyprian himself wrote on the "Chair of Peter."** In *De unitate* Cyprian grounds the church's unity in the chair of Peter ("the primacy is given to Peter, that there might be shown one Church and one chair"). Catholics argue Ortlund leans on Cyprian's anti-Stephen polemic while underweighting Cyprian's own Petrine ecclesiology — Cyprian is a *contested*, not a clean anti-papal, witness. (Ortlund's counter, made elsewhere in the talk, is that Cyprian thinks *every* bishop sits in that chair.)
3. **Acts 15 and James.** James's summary speech ("my judgment") can be read as the *local* bishop of Jerusalem formulating the decree, while Peter supplies the doctrinal principle by revelation (the Cornelius precedent, Acts 10) — so James's prominence is administrative, not a refutation of Petrine primacy. Chrysostom calling James "chief" in Jerusalem is consistent with James being the resident bishop there. (Compare the LDS-framed treatment in [[Acts 15 Shows James Deciding Not Peter]].)
4. **Development and explicitation.** That a sharply-defined *jurisdictional* supremacy is articulated more fully in later centuries is, on the Catholic account, ordinary doctrinal development, not invention — the seed (Peter's primacy) is in the NT and the early fathers, the flower (Vatican I's precise definition) comes later.

The standing tension is *what the equal-power language proves*. Ortlund reads "endowed with a like partnership of honor and power," set explicitly *against* Roman claims of seniority, as denying precisely the supremacy Vatican I asserts. The Catholic reads it as affirming collegial equality of *order* while leaving a primacy of *jurisdiction* intact. The dispute turns on whether a "primacy of jurisdiction" that the fathers never describe in Vatican I's terms can be read back into their "equal power" statements — the same retrojection worry that animates Ortlund's whole historical case (see [[Catholic Doctrinal Development Is the Same Double Standard]]).

## LDS Relevance

This argument is congenial to the LDS position at the level of *conclusion* — the apostles were a college of co-equal authority, not subjects of a single supreme Peter — which resonates with the LDS model of the Quorum of the Twelve holding the keys collectively (see [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]]). But the LDS reading of Peter is more exalted than Ortlund's: Latter-day Saints generally affirm Peter as the *presiding* apostle and senior holder of the keys (the First-Presidency analogy applied to Peter, James, and John), so they would resist Ortlund's flattening of Peter to merely "first among genuine equals." The shared yield is negative — neither tradition grants Rome a supreme Petrine office — while the constructive pictures differ: Ortlund sees a permanently collegial, office-light apostolate; the LDS see a structured presiding order whose authority was lost in the apostasy and restored. Ortlund's patristic "equal power" data, taken strictly, would also pressure any claim that Peter held *unique* transmissible keys.
