#type/claim

# Naturalism Is a Short Step to Mormonism

## The Claim
The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] argues that Mormonism is "almost uniquely" easy to reach from naturalism, because Latter-day Saint metaphysics already shares so much with what many atheists believe: an eternal (uncreated) universe, rejection of creation *ex nihilo*, a finite/limited God, materialism (including "spirit matter"), and compatibility with evolution and even panpsychism. Converting from naturalism to Mormonism "requires minimal modification of your belief" — the "least renovating" move available.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A finite, non-ex-nihilo God (rate: strong, doctrinal).** "God is still the creator of the world, but he didn't create *ex nihilo*. He created the world out of pre-existing material," so "there are certain laws of nature that God just has no control over" — which, Green notes, "could help with the problem of evil." See [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]].[^2]

2. **Shared naturalist commitments (rate: moderate, comparative).** "They share a lot of beliefs with many atheists: they believe in an eternal universe ... they reject perfect-being theism." Green: his LDS friend Joseph Llewellyn "is plausibly more of a naturalist than I am," depending on how naturalism is defined.[^3]

3. **Trent Horn's "least renovating" observation (rate: moderate, cited).** Green relays [[Trent Horn]]'s remark (from his debate with [[Jacob Hansen]]) that he could see how a naturalist would be attracted to Mormonism because converting "almost requires minimal modification of your belief ... the least renovating you'd have to do to your worldview."[^4]

4. **Evolution is "built into" the view (rate: moderate).** "Evolution is almost built into their view," with a central emphasis on growth and change over time; this removes a major friction point that naturalists have with classical theism.[^5]

5. **Materialism and rational matter (rate: moderate, metaphysics).** Mormons are materialists who nonetheless posit "spirit matter" — a stratified materialism. Their first philosopher, [[Orson Pratt]], was a panpsychist (and "panagentialist"), drawing on Mormon ideas about intelligence, agency, and matter — so Mormonism is "uniquely well suited" to panpsychism. See [[LDS Panpsychism and the Intelligence Threshold]].[^6]

> This is corroborated by the broader pattern that independent philosophers keep reconstructing LDS-shaped theology when they follow the arguments — see [[Independent Philosophers Keep Reconstructing LDS Theology]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
"Short step" cuts both ways and is not an argument for *truth*. (a) Metaphysical proximity to naturalism explains *attraction*, not *evidence*: a worldview being a small edit from one's priors says nothing about whether its historical claims (gold plates, restored priesthood) are true. (b) Green himself does not take the step — he remains agnostic, holding that the **problem of evil** (especially [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism|animal suffering]]) still bites finite theism. (c) A finite God purchases a problem-of-evil advantage at the cost of classical-theist goods (aseity, ultimate sovereignty) that other Christians take as non-negotiable, so "minimal modification" understates what is surrendered. (d) "Naturalist Mormon" stretches *naturalism*: adding spirit matter, premortal intelligences, and divine embodiment is a substantive supernatural ontology, not bare naturalism. The point shows Mormonism is *philosophically intelligible* to a naturalist, not that it is true.

## Best Quotes

> "It's not as much of a leap, I guess, if you're a naturalist to go to Mormonism ... if you're going from naturalism to theism, this is like the least renovating you'd have to do to your worldview."[^4]

> "God is still the creator of the world, but he didn't create *ex nihilo*. He created the world out of pre-existing material. So there are certain laws of nature that God just has no control over."[^2]
