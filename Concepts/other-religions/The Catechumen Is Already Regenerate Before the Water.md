# The Catechumen Is Already Regenerate Before the Water

#type/claim

## The Claim
**[[Gavin Ortlund]]** argues that regeneration — being made alive to God — observably begins *at faith*, during catechesis, *before* baptism, so baptism cannot be the moment it happens. His evidence is twofold: (1) **Acts 10**, where Cornelius receives the Holy Spirit and speaks in tongues *prior* to baptism — and Ortlund claims this is the *pattern* in Acts, where one never sees the Spirit poured out or fruits of regeneration *at* the moment of baptism; and (2) **pastoral experience**: he repeatedly sees adult converts (he names a hypothetical "John," drawn out of witchcraft) who, during a months-long catechetical process, exhibit the full fruits of regeneration — conviction, faith, moral transformation, sometimes tongues — and asks how many "Corneliuses" he must see before concluding regeneration starts at faith and is merely *sealed* at baptism.[^1] **[[Trent Horn]]** concedes Cornelius but quarantines it as a unique exception (a "boundary case") and reinterprets "John" as not-yet-regenerate.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Cornelius receives the Spirit before baptism (Evidence: scriptural, decisive on its face).** In [[Acts 10|Acts 10]] the Holy Spirit falls and Cornelius speaks in tongues *before* water baptism — the one explicit NT case where the Spirit is described as temporally prior to the rite (Horn grants this is the only such case).[^2] Ortlund adds that *nowhere* in Acts is regeneration shown happening *at* baptism, making Cornelius consistent rather than anomalous.[^1] *Strength: a clear narrative datum that the order can be faith/Spirit → baptism.*

2. **The "John" case and the multiplication of Corneliuses (Evidence: experiential, cumulative).** Ortlund argues that if Cornelius can be an exception, and he keeps seeing converts make a "180" — "from the witchcraft to the speaking in tongues" — bearing genuine spiritual fruit before baptism, "why not see them like Cornelius?" He cannot, in conscience, tell such a person "you're not yet made alive to God."[^1] *Strength: presses the regenerationist to deny what looks like obvious Spirit-work, or to multiply "exceptions" until the rule erodes.*

3. **The Matthew 7 fruit-inference (Evidence: scriptural warrant for judging).** Jesus' "by their fruits you will recognize them" ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]]) licenses *reasonable* (not infallible) inference from a good tree's fruit to its root. Ortlund notes even Peter makes this inference in Acts 10 — from tongues to "they have received the Holy Spirit." The fruit Ortlund means is narrow: virtue *cultivated by the Spirit in response to the true gospel of the triune God*, not generic common-grace niceness.[^3] *Strength: grounds the experiential argument in dominical teaching and apostolic practice.*

4. **Two causes, one effect (Evidence: conceptual).** Since faith *and* baptism are both spoken of as saving, Ortlund finds it unstrange to say one is saved at faith — which "leads up to baptism where it is kind of formally expressed" — rather than forcing the new birth into the water itself.[^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view (Horn):** Cornelius is a genuine, singular **exception** — the only place in Acts where the Spirit precedes baptism — and its narrative *purpose* (per Acts 11) is precisely to break the kingdom open to Gentiles, showing they are fitting recipients of baptism without first becoming Jews. Exceptions do not nullify rules: Horn parallels the good thief (saved by Christ's word without baptism, indeed arguably without Chalcedonian faith) as an exception to salvation-*by-faith* that no Protestant treats as overturning the rule. Against the "John" argument, Horn is **skeptical of any reliable one-to-one correspondence** between visible behavior and the invisible work of the Spirit (see [[Actual Grace Is a Kick in the Pants Not Regeneration]]): the pre-baptismal change can be *actual grace* drawing the person, or a natural/psychological response to apprehending a great good — the same explanations that account for transformed Mormons and Buddhists, for the ~20% of Americans who are *former* Christians, and for "mediocre Christians" who remain regenerate yet show little fruit. On Horn's epistemology, divine revelation (Scripture, tradition, magisterium), not empirical observation of behavior, must fix *when* the invisible new birth occurs — "as high as the heavens are above the earth, so are your ways above mine" — and that revelation locates it at baptism. The pastoral pull of "John" is real, but it is, for Horn, an unreliable instrument for dating an invisible act.

## LDS Relevance
The Acts 10 pattern Ortlund presses — faith and a manifest change *before* the ordinance — coheres with the LDS ordering of the first principles and ordinances: *faith*, then *repentance*, then *baptism* (Article of Faith 4), where genuine conversion precedes and qualifies one for the rite. But Latter-day Saints diverge from Ortlund's conclusion: baptism by proper authority remains *necessary* for the remission of sins, not a dispensable seal on an already-finished regeneration (3 Nephi 11; Moroni 8). Cornelius is striking for the LDS reading too — the Spirit testifies and gifts can be given before baptism, yet Peter still commands the water (Acts 10:47–48), implying the ordinance accomplishes something the prior outpouring did not. Ortlund's "regeneration is complete at faith" would, if true, render the baptismal command (and a fortiori baptism for the dead) superfluous — exactly the inference the Restoration resists. See [[Catechetical Instruction Before Baptism]] and [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]].

## Best Quotes
> "How many times do I have to see this before I start to conclude it doesn't look like the baptism is the cause of regeneration — it just looks like that starts at faith and is sort of sealed and publicly expressed at baptism?" [^1]

> "When you see someone do a 180 — they go from the witchcraft to the speaking in tongues — why not see them like Cornelius?" [^4]

> "I can't say to John, 'you're not yet regenerated, just because the Holy Spirit's working at you in some way, but you're not yet made alive to God' — that would go against everything I understand about the Holy Spirit's work in drawing people to salvation." [^1]
