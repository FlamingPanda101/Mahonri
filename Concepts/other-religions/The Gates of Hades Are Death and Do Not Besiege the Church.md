# The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church

## The Claim

[[Jacob Hansen]]'s reframing of the standard Catholic proof-text. [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18]] — "the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" — is routinely read as promising that the institutional church can never be overcome and therefore can never apostatize. Hansen argues the Greek/Hebrew idiom means something narrower and defensive: "the gates of Hades" is "the gates of Sheol," i.e. **the power of death**. Gates do not attack; "gates lock things in." So the verse promises that **death will not imprison the church** — that the church will be *immortal* — not that a visible governing institution will enjoy unbroken succession. Christ fulfilled it by *bursting open the gates of death* in the resurrection. [^1]

Hansen marshals two authorities he expects to surprise a Catholic audience: the **Catholic Confraternity Bible Commentary** ("the whole expression... means the power of death. Christ is predicting that his church will be immortal"), and the Harvard-trained scholar [[Jack P. Lewis]] ("the gates of Hades is to be considered as a figure of speech for death, which cannot keep the Christian imprisoned... Matthew 16:18 should not be twisted into being considered a proof text" for the church's indefectibility). He adds a final twist: even granting the indefectibility reading, "the whole claim of our church is that the gates of hell *didn't* prevail" — because the church was *restored*. [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "gates" idiom is genuinely defensive (rating: strong; lexical)
"Gates of Sheol/Hades" as a stock Hebrew expression for the realm/power of death is well attested (cf. Isaiah 38:10, "the gates of the grave"), and a city's *gates* are defensive fortifications, not siege weapons — so the natural picture is death *failing to hold* the church captive, which fits a resurrection theme. This is the lexical backbone shared with [[Sheol Was the Undifferentiated Realm of All the Dead]]. [^1]

### 2. He cites the opponent's own tradition (rating: strong rhetorically; the Confraternity commentary)
By quoting a *Catholic* commentary to the effect that the verse means "Christ... will be immortal," Hansen removes the appearance of a self-serving "Mormon" gloss and shifts the burden: the institutional-perpetuity reading is not the only, or even the historic-Catholic, reading. [^1]

### 3. Independent scholarly support (rating: moderate; one specialist)
[[Jack P. Lewis]]'s study of the verse's interpretation explicitly cautions against using it as a proof-text for ecclesial indefectibility — a non-LDS academic voice converging on the death-not-institution reading. [^2]

### 4. The "even if" fallback (rating: moderate; internal to the LDS system)
Hansen's escape hatch: *if* the verse did guarantee the church against being overcome, the Restoration is precisely the claim that death/hell did **not** finally prevail — the church came back — so the verse is satisfied either way. [^2]

### 5. Steelman of the Catholic reply ([[Joe Heschmeyer]])
Heschmeyer largely *grants the lexical point* — he himself calls it a synecdoche for "Hades, death, the power of death" — and then argues it cuts the other way. If "the power of death" is what must not prevail, then a **1,700-year disappearance of the institutional church caused by the death of the apostles is the power of death prevailing**. He reinforces this with the Greek verb *katischyō* ("prevail"): it can denote even a *temporary* triumph — Scripture says Midian "prevailed against Israel" for five years (Judges 6) — so a centuries-long victory of death over the church is a fortiori "prevailing." On this reading the verse refutes the apostasy *because* it means death, not despite it. [^3]

### Steelman of Hansen's counter to that reply
The rejoinder presupposes that "the church" in view is the *institution* and that the death of the apostles *is* the church dying — both of which Hansen denies: on his account the **body of believers** (the church Christ promised to keep) never died, only the institutional fullness/priesthood authority was lost, so "death" never prevailed over the thing the verse actually protects. The dispute thus collapses back into [[The Institutional Church and the Body of Believers Are Two Senses of One Word]].

## Best Quotes

> "What's the purpose of a gate? Like, 'Oh no, the gate's going to get me.' Gates don't attack you. Gates lock things in. The proper understanding is the gates of death will not prevail against the church." — Hansen [^1]

> "In all these passages, the Hebrew for gates of hell is the gates of Sheol... the whole expression means the power of death. Christ is predicting that his church will be immortal." — Hansen quoting the Confraternity Bible Commentary [^1]

> "If you look at the Greek word being used for prevail, it includes even a temporary triumph... If the power of death prevailed over the institutional church for 1,700 years... then the gates of hell prevailed." — Heschmeyer [^3]
