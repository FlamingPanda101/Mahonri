# The Strongest Argument Grants the Most Premises

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]]'s original video called the demonic hypothesis "the strongest argument against Mormonism." [[Trent Horn]] agrees, but reinterprets *why* — and in doing so turns the compliment into an analysis of argumentative strength itself. **An argument is strongest, Horn says, when it grants the most premises of the opponent's position.** Conceding ground and *still* reaching your conclusion is more powerful than denying everything. His worked example is abortion: the *weak* pro-choice argument denies the fetus is human ("just a clump of cells"); the *strong* one grants full humanity and a right to life but denies a right to use the mother's body. Granting more makes the argument harder to dislodge.[^1][^2]

Applied to Mormonism: a *weak* anti-Mormon move ignores the chiasmus and stylometry; the *strong* move (the demonic hypothesis) **grants** all that sophistication and reattributes its source. That is why it is rhetorically the strongest available line — it doesn't have to fight the evidence.[^3]

But the same yardstick exposes the move's weakness, which becomes the heart of the dispute over Horn's debate syllogism.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman, then the reversibility problem)
**Steelman of Horn.** The "grant-the-premises" heuristic is a genuine and widely-recognized feature of strong argumentation (it is why steelmanning works): an argument that survives maximal concessions is more robust than one that depends on contested denials. Horn is right that conceding the Book of Mormon's literary sophistication and *still* explaining it (naturally or demonically) is dialectically stronger than pretending the sophistication isn't there.

**The reversibility problem (Carroll's counter).** Carroll's decisive reply targets the syllogism Horn used in his debate with [[Jacob Hansen]]:
> If Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired. Catholicism is true. Therefore the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired.

Carroll argues this is not *sound* — but crucially he refines what he means under cross-examination. Horn corrects him that "unsound" means a premise is *false*, and Carroll concedes the better word is **unproven**: the second premise ("Catholicism is true") "cannot be proven," so the syllogism can never do real work. The tell is **reversibility**: run modus tollens the other way and you get an equal-and-opposite syllogism with identical force — "Mormonism is true; therefore Catholicism is false." When an argument flips cleanly onto its user, both versions rest on unproven premises, and neither advances the ball. Carroll reads Horn's deployment as "damage control": *if I lose the Book of Mormon debate, it doesn't matter, because Catholicism is true.*[^4][^5][^6]

**Horn's rejoinder.** Horn does not deny the syllogism is reversible; he says *everyone* reasons this way — a Mormon presented with arguments for a rival faith will likewise say "but I know by the burning in the bosom that the Book of Mormon is true, so those are false." Presupposing one's overall worldview to assess a particular claim is universal and not by itself a vice. (Carroll's narrower complaint is venue-specific: fine to do privately, but bringing an unprovable premise to a *debate* as if it settles the matter "falls flat.")[^7][^8]

**Honest assessment.** Both insights are correct and they cut in opposite directions. Horn is right that granting premises is what makes the demonic line *rhetorically* strongest. Carroll is right that the *load-bearing* premise of Horn's syllogism (the truth of Catholicism, ultimately resting on the Church's authority) is exactly the thing left unproven in the debate — so the argument's strength is parasitic on a premise it imported rather than earned. The dispute therefore relocates, as both men acknowledge, to the **great-apostasy / authority** question: *did Christ's authority leave the earth after the apostles?* That, not the demonic label, is where the real adjudication has to happen.

## Best Quotes
> "Any argument is going to be the strongest when it grants the most premises of your opponent's position."[^1]

> "The weakest argument for abortion is to say a fetus is not human... The strongest argument for abortion says yeah, this is a human being with a right to life — it just doesn't have a right to use the mother's body. It's granting more premises."[^2]

> "It's not sound because the second premise cannot be proven... I could turn it back on you: Mormonism is true, therefore Catholicism is false. You could run modus tollens on my original argument and get the same thing."[^5]

> "I saw it as damage control... like you saying, if I lose this debate it doesn't matter, because Catholicism is true. When a syllogism can just be turned around on the person using it, it falls flat."[^6]
