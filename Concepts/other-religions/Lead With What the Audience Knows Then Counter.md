# Lead With What the Audience Knows Then Counter

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] describes an implicit rule he used throughout his book: first present the thing his evangelical readers already think they know, then supply the counter-evidence. He admits the witnesses section "broke" this rule by juxtaposing two anecdotes flatly, and explains why the rhetorical order matters for a dual audience. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The method stated.** "Typically what I want to do... is to first present the thing that evangelicals, if they're familiar with, already know. And then to provide the counter." [^1] *Rating: a sound pedagogy* for moving a resistant audience — start inside their frame, then expand it.

2. **The "exit door" is the familiar datum.** "The most common thing about the Book of Mormon witnesses that an evangelical would know is the fact that Martin Harris said he saw it with the spiritual eyes. Cuz that's the exit door." [^2] *Rating: accurate audience analysis;* it identifies the single fact evangelicals reach for and positions it to be answered.

3. **The steadfast witnesses are genuinely new information.** Beshears notes most evangelicals will learn for the first time that [[John Whitmer]] "literally saw them" and maintained that after excommunication — "baby's first." [^3] *Rating: strong;* it justifies why even an imperfectly balanced section advances the reader (cf. [[All Three Witnesses Upheld Their Testimony for Life]]).

4. **The dual-audience constraint.** Beshears must write for evangelicals without telling Latter-day Saints "let bygones be bygones... there's no differences between us." [^4] *Rating: moderate;* it links the method to his larger "hold the tension" project (see [[Holding the Tension Between Anti-Mormonism and Syncretism]]).

**Steelman of the critic.** [[David Snell]]'s pushback is that leading-with-the-familiar can produce a falsely balanced "one anecdote vs. one anecdote" structure that under-represents the evidence (here, omitting that all three witnesses stayed firm). The method is good only if the "counter" is proportionate to the actual evidence; Beshears concedes the witnesses section fell short and says his next book corrects it.

## Best Quotes
> "First present the thing that evangelicals, if they're familiar with, already know. And then to provide the counter." [^1]

> "The most common thing... an evangelical would know is the fact that Martin Harris said he saw it with the spiritual eyes. Cuz that's the exit door." [^2]

> "I know there's sections like this in this book, but like... baby's first. So evangelicals are really not familiar with this story." [^3]
