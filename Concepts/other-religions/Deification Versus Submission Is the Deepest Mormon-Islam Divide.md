# Deification Versus Submission Is the Deepest Mormon-Islam Divide

## The Claim

The sharpest contrast between the two faiths is the *telos* of the human being. Latter-day Saint theology teaches *apotheosis* — that humans (uncreated eternal intelligences) can progress to share, to a maximal degree, "the fullness of the mind and power and glory of the Father." Islam holds that the very idea of a human becoming divine or sharing God's nature is "entirely absent"; the human goal is faithful servanthood and submission (*islam*), rewarded by paradise as a gift. This is the doctrine on which the "white Islam" comparison most decisively fails.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. LDS deification: humans can become like God
"Latter-day Saints teach that we can be deified and become like God." Deification/*apotheosis* is "the belief that humans can progress and become like God," sharing "divine attributes to a maximal degree" — "sometimes called a robust deification where humans or eternal spirits are considered uncreated and the goal is to attain the fullness of the mind and power and glory of the father." It is "not merely an analogy but a literal potential for shared divinity through following Christ's path." See [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — robust deification of uncreated intelligences is the signature LDS distinctive.

### 2. Islam: the goal is servanthood, and deification is absent
In Islam, "while believers strive for closeness to God through devotion and righteous living, the concept of humans becoming gods or sharing the divine nature in the Latter-day Saint sense is entirely absent." The word *Islam* "comes from submission — to submit to the one God, Allah, and fulfilling humanity's role as servants and worshippers"; the reward is "paradise, a spiritual physical garden of delights experienced as a gift from Allah, not a state of becoming divine."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — Islam's framing of the human vocation as `'abd` (servant/worshipper), with deification as unthinkable, is a central commitment.

### 3. Salvation: a Messiah's grace vs. mercy for the devout
Latter-day Saints are saved "through the grace of Christ... the grace and merits and mercy of the holy Messiah." Islam "doesn't have a sort of salvific figure... a Messiah that they can look to for salvation. Rather, they see that Allah will save them if they are devoted enough to him and if they submit to him" — adhering to the five pillars.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — Islam's lack of a salvific-Messiah and its mercy-for-submission soteriology contrast sharply with LDS Christocentric grace.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A sympathetic reader can note real proximity: both faiths affirm that humans are made for *nearness* to God, both make righteous living and devotion central, and Islamic mystical (Sufi) traditions speak of *fana* (annihilation of self in God) and intimate union that can sound, from a distance, like a kind of divinization. One could argue the LDS "become like God" and the Sufi "lose yourself in God" are both refusals of the cold, distant deism the comparison's critics fear. The reply is that the *direction* is opposite: deification *elevates* the human toward the divine nature (same species, shared glory), whereas even the most intimate Islamic union *dissolves* the self before an utterly transcendent God whose nature can never be shared — to claim otherwise would be *shirk*, the gravest sin. A theology whose summit is "become a god" and one whose summit is "be the perfect servant of the one God who has no partner" are not the same religion wearing different clothes; they are opposite answers to what a human being is *for*. (For what the two traditions can nonetheless learn from each other, see [[Holy Envy - What Latter-day Saints Can Learn from Islam]].)
