# Filioque

## Overview
*Filioque* is Latin for "and the Son." It is a phrase the Western (Latin) church added to the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, so that the Holy Spirit was said to proceed "from the Father **and the Son**" rather than "from the Father" alone, as the original 381 creed read. The interpolation spread in the West from roughly the 6th-9th centuries and was eventually endorsed at Rome, despite the creed's text having been fixed by an ecumenical council. The East regarded this unilateral alteration of a conciliar creed as both procedurally illegitimate and theologically mistaken, and the dispute became the central doctrinal trigger of the 1054 East-West (Great) Schism.

## Apologetic relevance
The Filioque is a favorite piece of evidence that the creeds were humanly negotiated and editable rather than a fixed apostolic deposit: a supposedly settled, "infallible" creed was in fact rewritten centuries later, and that rewrite split Christendom in two. Latter-day Saint apologists use it to argue that creedal formulations of the Trinity developed and were contested over time rather than descending intact from the apostles, undercutting the claim that the Nicene creed is a neutral, immutable baseline for who counts as Christian. Eastern Orthodox apologists, from the other side of the same debate, point to it as proof that the Latin West tampered with an ecumenical creed without authority. Either way it shows the creed's wording was the product of regional politics, not unanimous revelation.
