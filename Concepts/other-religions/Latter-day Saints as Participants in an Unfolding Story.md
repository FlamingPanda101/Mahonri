# Latter-day Saints as Participants in an Unfolding Story

## The Claim
Developing his "theological history vs. historical theology" distinction, [[Kyle Beshears]] argues that Latter-day Saints experience their faith as a living story begun in 1820 and still unfolding, in which each member asks "where am I in this story?" — they are not merely recipients of a finished revelation but active participants in an ongoing one. [[David Snell]] affirms this maps onto the LDS belief in continuing restoration. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A history that is still being written.** Beshears: Latter-day Saints are "a theological history... a history that began in 1820 and has been unfolding to this present day and Latter-day Saints see themselves participating in as doctrine is revealed and developed and practiced." [^1] *Rating: strong as a phenomenological description;* it captures why continuing revelation and living prophets are structurally central, not peripheral.

2. **"Where am I in this story?"** "If we're in chapter 18, what character am I and where am I in that story?" — doctrine matters, "but it's less important than the story and the practices and the participation in it." [^1] *Rating: insightful;* it explains the LDS premium on personal/ancestral narrative over creedal abstraction.

3. **Snell's reframe as ongoing restoration.** Snell agrees: "Latter-day Saints, we believe in an ongoing restoration and because of that... we see ourselves not only as recipients of the story, but as active participants in the story as it continues to unfold," whereas Protestants feel more like "recipients of a story that happened." [^2] *Rating: strong;* the LDS insider confirms the evangelical outsider's read, a mark of an accurate characterization.

4. **The pioneer-ancestry evidence.** Beshears offers an anecdotal tell: ask a Latter-day Saint about the King Follett sermon's relation to Trinitarianism and you may get a shrug, but "tell me about your pioneer ancestry... they're about to spill a book on me." [^3] *Rating: moderate;* anecdotal but illustrative of where identity is anchored.

**Steelman of the doctrine-first view.** An evangelical could object that anchoring identity in an unfolding *story* risks subordinating fixed truth to institutional narrative — if the story keeps developing, doctrine becomes whatever the current chapter requires. Beshears himself frames this descriptively, not as an endorsement; and the LDS reply is that continuing revelation is precisely the claimed mechanism by which the story stays tethered to God rather than drifting (see [[Theological History vs Historical Theology]]).
