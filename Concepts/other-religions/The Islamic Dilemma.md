# The Islamic Dilemma

#type/claim

## The Claim
The "Islamic dilemma" is a Christian argument that the Qur'an's own statements about the Bible trap Islam in a fork with no good exit. As [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]] frames it: the Qur'an "speaks well of the previous scriptures of the Bible as revelation, as preserved revelation and authentic revelation," commanding Jews and Christians to judge by the [[Quran 5.47|Torah and Gospel]] — *while at the same time contradicting them* (denying Christ's crucifixion, his divine sonship, God as Father, and recasting the prophet-narratives).[^1] From this the dilemma is built:

> "If the Bible is true like the Quran says it is, then the Quran is false for contradicting the Bible. However, they might say, 'Well, the Bible's been corrupted.' Okay, fine. If the Bible's corrupted, then the Quran is false for saying that a corrupted book is true. See the dilemma? So either way you go, it's a dead end."[^2]

So the two horns are:
1. **The Bible is uncorrupted** → the Qur'an is false for contradicting a true revelation.
2. **The Bible is corrupted** → the Qur'an is false for vouching for a corrupted book as true.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Qur'an's repeated endorsement of the prior scriptures (Christian steelman).** Avery marshals a chain of texts: [[Quran 5.47|5:47]] ("let the people of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed in it"), [[Quran 5.43|5:43]] ("why do they come to you for judgment when they have the Torah?"), the *musaddiq* formula (the Qur'an "confirming what is with you"), and [[Quran 10.94|10:94]] (doubt is resolved by consulting "those who read the scripture before you").[^3] On their face these treat the Torah/Gospel of Muhammad's own day as accessible and authoritative — which is the load-bearing premise of horn 1. See [[The Quran's Testimony to the Previous Scriptures]].
2. **The "infighting" argument that the dilemma is not trivial.** Both hosts note that if there were one clean answer, Muslims would all converge on it; instead there are "five or six" responses across "two main categories," with *da'wah* figures publicly anathematizing one another over whether the Torah is textually corrupt.[^4] Avery analogizes to Trinitarian disagreement: serious people admit the Trinity is a *hard* topic and offer competing models, whereas defenders of Islam claim the dilemma is "easy" yet contradict each other — evidence (he argues) that it is in fact hard.[^5] *(This is a sociological argument about difficulty, not a logical disproof; a Muslim could reply that internal disagreement also besets every live Christian debate.)*
3. **Muslim steelman — the corruption is post-Muhammad and/or partial.** Several callers grant horn-2-style moves: the scriptures were *true in Muhammad's day* but corrupted *later* ("Abishek"), or only *parts* are corrupt while the rest stands ("S", the first caller). Avery's rejoinders: (a) if corruption is post-Muhammad, the 7th-century text the Qur'an endorsed still survives in earlier manuscripts, so the contradiction stands; (b) [[Quran 2.85|2:85]] forbids believing part of the scripture and rejecting the rest, so "partial confirmation" is not a Qur'anic option. See [[The Guardian and Criterion Reply to the Islamic Dilemma]].
4. **Muslim steelman — the Qur'an is the criterion, not the judged.** The most common reply on the stream: the Qur'an is *muhaymin* (guardian/criterion) over the prior books, so it adjudicates them rather than being adjudicated by them. Avery's counter is twofold — *muhaymin* means guardian/witness, "not a judge or decider," and no verse anywhere says "judge the Bible by the Qur'an," whereas [[Quran 10.94|10:94]] runs the dependence the other way.[^6] A fair Muslim rejoinder (not voiced clearly on-air): classical *tafsir* often reads *muhaymin* (Q 5:48) precisely as the Qur'an *guarding/superseding* prior scripture, so the lexical dispute is genuinely contested rather than settled in the Christian's favor.
