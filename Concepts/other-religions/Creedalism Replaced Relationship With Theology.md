# Creedalism Replaced Relationship With Theology

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Jacob Hansen]] and [[Blake Ostler]] argue that the post-apostolic creeds functioned as a *litmus test* — "who is the real Christian?" — and thereby *redefined* Christianity from a *relationship with Christ plus ethical living* into a set of *theological propositions*. This, they say, "misses the mark," because Jesus marked his disciples by *love*, not by correct metaphysics. The deeper problem with creedalism, Ostler adds, is that it is *not based on revelation*. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Creeds shifted the definition of "Christian."** After the apostles died, competing ideas proliferated; the creeds answered "who's the real Christian?" by *belief content*, turning the gospel of relationship into "theological considerations rather than... 'by this men shall know if you are my disciples, if you have love one to another.'" *Rating: a substantive historical-theological claim*; creeds did serve a boundary-marking function. [^3][^4]

2. **The real defect is the absence of revelation.** "The real problem with creedalism is that it's not based on revelation. If we're ever going to resolve these issues, it's not because we're so smart we can figure it out, it's because God... tells us." *Rating: the load-bearing LDS premise* — it routes the whole dispute to continuing revelation vs. *sola scriptura* + reason. [^5]

3. **Continuing revelation + prophetic fallibility absorb error.** Ostler offers Brigham Young's Adam-God teaching as a *prophet who "screwed up"* — handled by (a) an express doctrine of prophetic fallibility and (b) continuing revelation that "logically entails that we don't know it all already." *Rating: an internally coherent move* (cf. [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]), though critics see it as unfalsifiable. [^6][^7]

4. **The Bible is not self-interpreting.** "I've got a Bible and all I need is the Bible" still requires the claim "I've got the right interpretation"; the Bible "clearly is not self-interpreting." *Rating: a strong point* against naïve *sola scriptura* (cf. [[All Doctrine Requires Interpretation Rebuttal]], [[The Self-Authenticating Canon Refuted]]). [^8]

5. **Steelman of the creedal/Protestant view.** Creeds did not *replace* relationship; they *protected* it — guarding the apostolic gospel against Gnostic and Arian distortions so that the *true* Christ (and thus a true relationship) could be known. "Love one another" presupposes *which* Jesus you love (1 John itself polices Christology, 1 John 4:1-3). And creeds claim to *summarize* Scripture, the deposit of *prior* revelation, not human cleverness — so "not based on revelation" begs the question. The fallibility-plus-new-revelation model arguably trades one problem (interpretive uncertainty) for a larger one (no fixed standard at all). *Rating: a strong rebuttal* — the dilemma "relationship vs. theology" is a false dichotomy; right belief and right relationship are presented in the NT as intertwined, and Ostler himself agrees there was a real "ecclesiastical structure." [^9]

## Best Quotes
> "These creeds were formed originally as a litmus test for who is a Christian and who wasn't... it changed what was in the gospel, which is about relationship with Christ and ethical considerations, and it turns it into theological considerations." [^3]

> "The real problem with creedalism is that it's not based on revelation. If we're ever going to resolve these issues... it's because God, who really is smart, tells us." [^5]

> "Did Brigham Young screw up on Adam-God? Yes... we have an express doctrine of fallibility of prophets... and a doctrine of continuing revelation." [^6]

> "It clearly is not self-interpreting. That's nonsense. The Bible is a guide. We're doing the best we can to understand it, but undoubtedly at points we err." [^8]
