# Book of Enoch - Ethiopian Orthodox Preservation
#type/claim

## The Claim
After 1 Enoch fell out of the Jewish and Western/Orthodox canons by the 4th century, it survived as canonical scripture in only one living tradition: the **Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church**, which holds the most expansive biblical canon in Christendom. Geographically and ecclesiastically isolated from the North African and European councils that fixed the narrower canon, the Ethiopian church kept copying 1 Enoch in **Ge'ez (Ethiopic)** straight through the centuries — which is why the complete text of 1 Enoch survives *only* in Ge'ez, and why the Western rediscovery (via [[James Bruce (explorer)|James Bruce]]'s 1773 manuscripts) had to come *from Ethiopia*.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Sole complete-text survival.** While Greek fragments and Aramaic Qumran fragments preserve *parts* of 1 Enoch, the *full* book survives only in the Ethiopic version maintained by the Ethiopian Orthodox Church — making that tradition the indispensable transmission channel.
2. **Canonical, not merely archived.** 1 Enoch is not a curiosity in Ethiopia but part of the canon; the Tewahedo Church's "broader" canon (which also includes Jubilees and other texts) is described in the source as the most expansive in Christendom. The book was *used and venerated*, not just preserved on a shelf.
3. **Isolation explains divergence from the 4th-century consensus.** As noted in [[Book of Enoch - Exclusion from the Canon]], the canon-narrowing decisions (Athanasius's Festal Letter, 367; Hippo, 393; Carthage, 397) were North African/European; "the Ethiopian church, geographically isolated, was not party to these... decisions." So Ethiopia simply never executed the exclusion the rest of Christendom did.
4. **It is the source of the Western recovery.** The whole rediscovery chain (German 1801, English 1821, etc., per [[Book of Enoch - Western Rediscovery 17th-19th Centuries]]) begins with Ge'ez manuscripts Bruce obtained in Ethiopia — though McClellan flags those particular copies as "late" and divergent, so Ethiopian preservation guaranteed *survival* without guaranteeing a pristine *text*.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A confessional Western Christian can grant Ethiopian preservation while denying it confers authority: that one isolated, geographically peripheral church retained a book the rest of the church (Jewish, Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant) deliberately set aside is, on this view, evidence of canonical *eccentricity*, not of suppressed truth — the broader consensus is the more reliable guide. One might also note that the surviving Ethiopic is a translation-of-a-translation (Aramaic → Greek → Ge'ez), centuries removed from the original. The pro-Enoch rejoinder is that Qumran's Aramaic fragments independently confirm the book's pre-Christian antiquity, so Ethiopia preserved something genuinely ancient and formative that the majority canon lost — making the "eccentric outlier" the faithful custodian rather than the deviant.
