# The Sage as Exemplar Parallels the Imitation of Christ

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] identifies a fourth mark of Stoic ethics in the New Testament: the **sage as exemplar**. Stoic ethics holds up a model person to imitate (often Socrates); [[Philo of Alexandria]] held up Moses to introduce Judaism to the Greco-Roman mind; and Christians hold up Christ. The structure — "live as one who wishes to be [the exemplar]" — is the same; only the exemplar changes.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Imitate the sage (rated: textual).** [[Epictetus]]: "even if you are not yet Socrates, still you ought to live as one who wishes to be Socrates." Socrates is "the quintessential guy — the one who allowed himself to be killed unjustly" (the hemlock).[^1]
2. **The Christian parallel (rated: textual).** Ephesians 5: "walk in love, as Christ also hath loved us" — the same imitative structure with Christ as the model. "The Stoics would go with Socrates; Philo would use Moses; we use Christ."[^2]
3. **Philo as the precedent move (rated: comparison).** When [[Philo of Alexandria]] "is trying to introduce Judaism into the Greco-Roman mind," he supplies Moses as the exemplar — the same bridging strategy Thomassen attributes to the New Testament authors with Christ.[^3]

**Steelman of the critic.** Imitation of an exemplar is a near-universal feature of moral and religious traditions (Confucian sages, Buddhist arhats, Hebrew patriarchs), so its presence in both Stoicism and Christianity shows a common *form*, not borrowing. Moreover, the New Testament's "imitate Christ" is not merely ethical modeling but participatory and christological (being "in Christ," conformed to his death and resurrection) — a register absent from the Stoic sage. The parallel is real at the level of moral pedagogy; the claim of Stoic influence is weaker here than for the more distinctive marks (open ethic, agape vocabulary, natural law).

## Best Quotes
> "Even if you are not yet Socrates, still you ought to live as one who wishes to be Socrates… you need an example to model your life after."[^1]

> "The Stoics could go with Socrates; Philo would use Moses; but what do we use? We use Christ."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The sage-as-exemplar mark rounds out Thomassen's four-part case (with the open ethic, adverbial ethic, and natural law) that Stoic ethics is embedded in the New Testament, supporting [[Stoicism Was a Theoretical Preparatory Gospel]]. For Latter-day Saints the "imitation of Christ" exemplar is central (taking his name, following his example), and Thomassen's point is that the *structure* of holding up a model sage was a familiar Greco-Roman form the gospel could fill with Christ.
