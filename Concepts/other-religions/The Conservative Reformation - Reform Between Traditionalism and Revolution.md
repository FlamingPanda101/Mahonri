# The Conservative Reformation - Reform Between Traditionalism and Revolution

## The Claim
The magisterial Reformation was not a revolution but a *conservative* reform. Borrowing the framing of the American Lutheran theologian [[Charles Porterfield Krauth]] ("the conservative reformation"), Cooper argues there are errors on two opposite sides: on one hand *dead traditionalism* that idealizes the past and permits no change; on the other a *radical revolution* that says "everything is just change... we need to start over and every generation needs to go back and take a fresh look at the Bible." The magisterial Reformers (those "led by cities and princes") consciously steered between these — conserving "the truth of the past, the good of the past, what God has done," while correcting "practices that are aberrant" — whereas the radical reformers (the Anabaptists) embraced the revolutionary pole. On this telling the charge "the Reformers were revolutionaries" mistakes the Anabaptist minority for the Reformation proper.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Krauth's two-sided error.** Cooper credits the category to Charles Porterfield Krauth, who "speaks about the conservative reformation" and says "there are errors on two different sides of things." We must "conserve the good of the past... but on the other hand, we don't want to move toward this radical revolution that just says, well, everything is just change." [^1]
2. **The Anabaptists are the true revolutionaries — and they don't come from the Reformation.** "You see this kind of thing the Anabaptists were doing... this radical tossing away of the past." Ortlund adds that Anabaptism is "a late medieval spirituality grounded in German mysticism," i.e. it predates and is independent of Luther; the revolutionary impulse is "not really connected that much to the Protestant Reformation." Cooper draws "a pretty strong distinction between the magisterial reformation, which is that more conservative reformation, versus that more revolutionary attitude you might find among some of the Anabaptists." [^2]
3. **The revolutionary impulse is paralleled, not caused, in politics.** Cooper notes the same "start over every generation" instinct later echoed by [[Thomas Paine]], who argued a constitution "needs to be reworked with every generation" — situating radical revolution as a broader cultural current that ran *alongside* the Reformation rather than flowing *from* it. [^3]
4. **The Reformers' own self-understanding was conservative.** [[John Calvin]] writes to Cardinal [[Cardinal Sadoleto|Sadoleto]] that "our agreement with antiquity is far closer than yours, and we are only trying to renew that ancient form of the church that has been distorted by illiterate men and... almost destroyed by the Roman pontiff." [[Richard Müller]] is cited that "the doctrines of God, the Trinity, creation, providence, predestination, and the last things were taken over by the magisterial reformation virtually without alteration." [^4]

## LDS Relevance
This Protestant self-portrait — *renewal of an ancient form distorted over time*, explicitly **not** "the church died after the apostles and now there's a new Pentecost" — is precisely the claim a Latter-day Saint affirms is insufficient. The LDS position holds that the corruption was deep enough to require not merely conserving-and-correcting a surviving institution but a *restoration* of priesthood authority and ongoing revelation (see [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]] and [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]]). Where Krauth's spectrum runs from traditionalism to revolution with magisterial reform as the golden mean, the Restoration claims a fourth option the spectrum omits: God reopening the heavens with new prophetic authority — neither conserving Rome's hierarchy nor "just me and my Bible," but a renewed apostolic commission. The LDS [[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug|living canon]] also recovers the very "every generation takes a fresh look" openness Cooper here labels Anabaptist excess, but grounds it in prophetic office rather than individual reading.
