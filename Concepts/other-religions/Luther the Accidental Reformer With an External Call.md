# Luther the Accidental Reformer With an External Call

## The Claim
Luther was an "accidental reformer," not a self-appointed revolutionary. When he posted the 95 Theses he "had no intention of starting a new church"; he performed the ordinary academic act of a professor proposing theses for public disputation, and "nothing that Luther taught at this time went against any official church teaching." Crucially, Luther acted under an *external call*: his ecclesiastical superior [[Johann von Staupitz]] directed him to take a doctorate at Wittenberg, and that doctorate was a public commission "to teach the Word of God" — which is why later Lutheran scholastics always called him "Dr. Luther." The panel sets this deliberately against the figure who "just said I have got a burning in my bosom... and so the spirit is just telling me to go do this or that." Genuine, lasting reform "is not something that's manufactured... it arises out of circumstance."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The 95 Theses were an ordinary academic act.** "Luther had no intention of starting a new church... He did what was a very ordinary thing to do, which was if there was some kind of topic of theological debate, a professor would propose a series of theses and then you'd have some public debate about it." The abuse of indulgences was a live topic, and "nothing that Luther taught at this time went against any official church teaching. There was no ecumenical council that decreed any of these kinds of things about indulgences." [^1]
2. **The external call via Staupitz.** "Luther was called by his ecclesiastical superior Staupitz, Johann von Staupitz, to get a doctorate in Wittenberg, and he had no intentions of really doing that, but because he was asked to, he did." Luther "saw this as an external call... 'I've been given a call.' ... He was told to go teach theology... And so it was in all of that that Luther said, 'Okay, well, I now have been commissioned and I've taken a vow to teach the word of God.'" [^2]
3. **"Dr. Luther" as a marker of legitimate commission.** "Later Lutherans when they referred to him... they all just call him Dr. Luther. The reason for that is because they say he had a call. He was given a doctorate. He was asked to do this. He was prepared. He was trained." [^3]
4. **Explicit contrast with private spiritual impulse.** "He wasn't just a random person that just said I have got a burning in my bosom, and so the spirit is just telling me to go do this or that. He was going through the proper external kind of channels. And this happened kind of accidentally. And I think anytime you have real reform that is meaningful and lasting, it is not something that's manufactured... it arises out of circumstance." [^4]
5. **Not an isolated charismatic.** Luther "didn't come to all this all at once" — he reached imputed righteousness "through Melanchthon especially" and "much later," inside "a whole circle of people that were pastors and were professors in Wittenberg... dialoguing and discussing and praying through these issues all together." [^5]

## LDS Relevance
The "burning in my bosom" line is a direct, unmistakable jab at the Latter-day Saint experiential epistemology — the [[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]] promise and the [[Doctrine and Covenants 6.22-23|D&C 9 / D&C 6:22-23]] "burning in the bosom" as a witness of truth (developed in the vault at [[Burning in the Bosom vs Self-Evidencing Light]]). The panel's rhetorical move is to credential Luther by *institutional* call (doctorate, ecclesiastical superior, public commission) and to discredit the appeal to private spiritual confirmation by association with caricatured Mormon "feelings."

A Latter-day Saint can answer on two fronts. First, the dichotomy is false: Joseph Smith likewise claimed an *external*, even more direct call — angelic ordination and the conferral of priesthood keys ([[D&C 13]], [[D&C 27]]) — not merely a private feeling; "the burning in the bosom" is confirmatory, not the sole basis of authority. Second, the irony cuts back: Luther's "external call" was a *Roman Catholic* doctorate granted by an institution the panel elsewhere argues lacks divine authority to commission anyone (see [[Refusing the Papacy Is Protest Not Schism]]) — so the call legitimizing Luther flows from the very office whose authority the Reformation denies. The LDS claim is that a genuinely *new* divine commission (restored apostolic keys) is exactly what an apostate institution cannot supply from within — which is why a restoration, not an academic chair, was required. On the "man of his time" honesty the panel praises in Luther ("I didn't come to this all at once... through torture and prayer"), compare the vault's [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]] applied to LDS prophets.
