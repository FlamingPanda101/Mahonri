# Credobaptists Cannot Agree on the Age of Baptism

#type/objection

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] presses a *tu quoque* against credobaptists (who deny infant baptism for alleged lack of evidence): the practice they put in its place — baptizing *children* once they "profess faith" — has even *less* early historical evidence than infant baptism, and credobaptists cannot even agree among themselves on the minimum age. If thin evidence disqualifies infant baptism, it disqualifies child credobaptism a fortiori, leaving the credobaptist with a gruesome gap.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Wildly divergent ages among credobaptist leaders.** Trent assembles clips: Al Mohler would wait until "very close to adolescence" (10–12); Keith Foskey and Tom Buck endorse baptism at 7; Allie Beth Stuckey allows as young as 3; a guest on Todd Friel's *Wretched* recommends waiting "well into the teenage years." There is no agreed answer to "how old must a child be?" — the practice is improvised.[^1] *(Strong — direct quotations showing the incoherence.)*
2. **The 4th-century fathers did not baptize children.** [[David Wright]], in the very book Ortlund cited, found that apart from a questionable Augustine case he could identify no children "of seven or older who were baptized answering the questions for themselves"; the non-infant baptisms among the fathers "are all of adult years," the youngest being [[John Chrysostom]] at ~18. So the 4th-century fathers were neither infant-baptizers in those cases *nor* child-credobaptists — they baptized adults. The credobaptist cannot claim them.[^2] *(Strong — primary-scholarship datum that cuts against the credobaptist appropriation of the fathers.)*
3. **Historically credobaptists baptized only teens/adults.** Baptist pastor Caleb Morell's research shows 18th-century Baptists did not baptize children under 12 and rarely anyone under 18; through most of the 19th century candidates were mid-teens, "seldom younger than 13"; only by the mid-20th century did Southern Baptists begin baptizing many under 12. The modern young-child baptism is itself a recent innovation.[^3] *(Strong — historical evidence that credobaptist practice has drifted.)*
4. **The gruesome gap.** If baptism is delayed to the teens but children can *sin* by 7–8 (they are disciplined for wrongdoing) while being *incapable of the saving faith* required for baptism, then on the credobaptist's own premises there is a window in which a child can be damnably guilty yet unbaptizable — "a gruesome conclusion when it comes to their salvation." Infant baptism (or the LDS age-of-accountability resolution) avoids this; the credobaptist's improvised age does not.[^4] *(Strong internal reductio.)*

**Steelman of the credobaptist view.** The credobaptist replies that *baptism does not save*, so the "gruesome gap" dissolves: an unbaptized child who dies is safe by grace (most Baptists hold to the innocence of those below an age of understanding), and baptism is the believer's *public profession*, rightly timed to genuine, credible faith rather than a fixed age — disagreement about *when* faith is credible is pastoral prudence, not doctrinal incoherence. The lack of a single number reflects respect for individual spiritual maturity, not confusion. Trent's rejoinder is that this concedes his deeper point — that the timing of baptism is a *discipline* worked out over time, exactly as he says infant baptism is — and that severing baptism from salvific efficacy contradicts the patristic consensus on baptismal regeneration (see [[Baptismal Regeneration Is the Doctrine Infant Baptism Is the Discipline]]).

## Apologetic Engagement (LDS)
This argument is a gift to the Latter-day Saint, because the LDS tradition *solves the very gap Trent exposes* without infant baptism. The Restoration fixes a principled age of accountability — **eight years** (D&C 68:27; Moroni 8) — below which children are sinless and saved without baptism, and at which they become accountable and are baptized. So where the credobaptist flounders ("3? 7? 12? 18?") and the Catholic resolves it by baptizing infants to cover original guilt, the LDS answer needs *neither* improvisation *nor* infant baptism: there is no inherited guilt, children are whole until eight, and baptism follows accountability. Trent's critique of credobaptist arbitrariness thus doubles as an unintentional argument for a revealed, fixed threshold — see [[Eight Is a Developmental Threshold of Moral Accountability]].

## LDS Relevance
Directly showcases the explanatory power of the LDS age-of-accountability doctrine: it resolves the dilemma (sinful-but-unbaptizable children) that, by Trent's own demonstration, neither credobaptists nor (without original sin) anyone else can resolve cleanly.
