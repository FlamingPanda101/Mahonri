# The Quran Is a Controlled Transmission and the New Testament Is a Free Transmission

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Wes Huff]] (PhD candidate in New Testament textual criticism, U. of Toronto) argues that the New Testament and the Quran sit at opposite ends of a transmission spectrum, and that the difference *inverts* the popular intuition. The New Testament is a **free transmission** — copied without central control across the ancient world — which seems messy (many variants) but is precisely what lets scholars recover the original. The Quran is a **controlled transmission** — an orally memorized text standardized by [[Uthman]], who destroyed variant codices — which looks clean but leaves us unable to trace the text back behind that standardization to verify what [[Muhammad]] recited.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The NT spread beyond any central control.** Within the first few centuries copies of the NT documents existed across North Africa, the Middle East, Europe, and Asia. Huff: "no one person or group could have ever controlled the text at any one point in time," because copies "buried in the sands of North Africa" would contradict any attempted edit elsewhere. This is the definition of a free transmission — "not controlled by an external authority... widely copied without constraint." *Rating: this is mainstream textual-critical description; strong.*[^1]
2. **The Quran was oral, then standardized by destroying variants.** On the traditional account (Sahih al-Bukhari), the Quran was memorized by Muhammad's companions and not collected in full at his death. After reciters died at the battle of Yamama, Abu Bakr began collation; [[Uthman]] later "collects all of these copies, he standardizes the text, and he destroys the differing copies." Huff: "this is literally the difference between a controlled transmission and a free transmission." *Rating: faithful to the standard Islamic-history narrative; strong.*[^2]
3. **Companions disputed Uthman's text.** Huff cites the hadith record that companions (e.g. Ubayy ibn Ka'b, Ibn Mas'ud) had readings or whole chapters Uthman did not include, and that "a lot of Muhammad's own immediate followers said Uthman did not get it right." If the standard text was contested by those closest to the source, confidence that it preserves the original is reduced. *Rating: the hadith do record such disputes; the inference is reasonable.*[^2]
4. **Transparency asymmetry.** Anyone can buy a critical edition of the Greek NT (Nestle-Aland) with a full apparatus of variants; "we are not hiding anything." There is "no critical edition of the Quran" of comparable maturity — Quranic text-criticism is barely 50 years old. *Rating: accurate as of the 2020s; see [[Andy Bannister]] / Quran Gateway in [[Textual Multivocality and Tenacity Trace the New Testament to the Autographs]].*[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
Huff is careful and concedes the genuine point in the Muslim boast: the consonantal *rasm* of the Quran is remarkably stable, and he grants it is "a little bit unfair" to compare a 66-book library written over 1,600 years on three continents with a single book codified within ~25 years. His thesis is comparative, not absolute: the NT is *more recoverable*, not variant-free. The **strongest Muslim reply** is that a controlled transmission is a *feature* — divine preservation (Surah 15:9) realized through the *ummah* — and that the destroyed variants were minor (orthographic/dialectal, the "seven ahruf"), so Uthman standardized rather than corrupted. A fair rejoinder: that reply concedes Huff's factual core (variants existed and were suppressed); it relocates the dispute to *theology* (was the suppression divinely sanctioned?) rather than refuting the transmission history. Note also the limit of the argument: a more recoverable *text* is not the same as a *true* text — establishing what the autographs said does not by itself establish that they are inspired. Compare the related but distinct argument in [[Quranic Textual History Is Cleaner Than the Bible's]] (Daniel Peterson), which makes the same suppression point from the LDS side.

## LDS Relevance
For Latter-day Saints this argument is double-edged and worth handling with care. On one hand, it is a powerful tool *against Islam* and harmonizes with the LDS view that the Bible is generally reliable and that the Restoration confirms (not replaces) it. On the other hand, the very logic — "an authority that standardizes a text and destroys variants weakens our confidence in the original" — is a tool critics turn against the LDS scriptural tradition (e.g. revisions across editions of the Doctrine and Covenants, the JST). And the LDS "plain and precious things... taken away" thesis ([[1 Nephi 13.40|1 Nephi 13]]) actually depends on the *opposite* intuition from Huff's — that the biblical text *was* corrupted in transmission. The two can be reconciled (doctrinal/redactional loss in the Old Testament's formation vs. NT *copying* fidelity), but a Latter-day Saint deploying Huff should be aware the framework is not automatically friendly to every LDS claim. See [[The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument]] and [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]].
