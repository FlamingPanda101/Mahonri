# Baptismal Regeneration Breeds Ritualism That Eclipses Christ

#type/claim

## The Claim
**[[Gavin Ortlund]]** offers his deepest *pastoral* objection as an "invitation" rather than a knock-down argument: traditions that make baptism the *cause* of regeneration tend, at the street level, to breed **formalism** (or ritualism) — faith placed *in the rite* rather than in Christ. "Baptism saved me, so I'm good" displaces the living trust the rite was meant to point to. The whole purpose of the sacrament, Ortlund insists, is to *lift the eyes to Christ* — you see the water and think of how Christ washes sin away; you go under the dark water and rise, and feel union with the crucified-and-risen Lord (cf. [[Romans 6.3-4|Romans 6:3-4]], [[Galatians 3.27|Galatians 3:27]]). If we get "stuck" at the rite, we stop looking *through* it to Christ.[^1] **[[Trent Horn]]** can see the concern but is "not as concerned," and answers with a **symmetry**: every soteriology that gives assurance can be abused into presumption — Calvinist perseverance of the saints as much as, or more than, baptism — and the cure for ritualism is not to scrap rituals but to "move the heart with" them.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The formalism mechanism (Evidence: pastoral observation).** Ortlund clarifies the worry is *not* presumption-of-final-salvation per se but **ritualism**: "the object of faith is in baptism rather than in the God who works through baptism."[^2] If the rite itself is the cause, attention naturally fixes on having-done-the-rite. *Strength: names a real and observable failure mode of high-sacramental piety.*

2. **The sacrament's God-ward telos (Evidence: theological).** Baptism's symbolism is *designed* to direct faith to Christ — submersion and rising as death and resurrection with him. A view that terminates salvific weight *in* the act risks short-circuiting that telos.[^1] *Strength: argues from the rite's own intended meaning, not merely from abuse.*

3. **The "baptize the baby so nana is happy" case (Evidence: example, conceded by Horn).** Both grant the degenerate case — infant baptism done for family sentiment with no intent to instill faith. Horn agrees this is bad and notes the Catholic Church *withholds* infant baptism absent "a well-founded hope the child will be raised Catholic."[^3] *Strength: Horn's concession shows the abuse is real enough that the Church guards against it.*

**Steelman of the opposing view (Horn):** The ritualism worry proves too much, because it afflicts *every* tradition that offers any ground of assurance. Horn presses a pointed **symmetry**: a five-point Calvinist who holds *perseverance of the saints* is at least as exposed to false presumption — "I made my act of faith years ago and I can't lose my salvation" — and arguably more so, because baptism at least supplies an *objective*, external sign that walks a line between presumption and lack-of-assurance, whereas a remembered conversion can be doubted ("was I really saved when I was eight?"). Catholic theology, moreover, already *blocks* the magical reading: baptismal grace can be received yet bear no fruit if the person does not *cooperate* in faith, the baptismal liturgy explicitly *asks for faith* ("what do you seek for your child? Faith") and even includes an exorcism and charges to godparents — so the rite is structurally ordered *toward* living faith, not away from it. The deeper reply (Isaiah 55:8–9, also [[Actual Grace Is a Kick in the Pants Not Regeneration]]) is that a street-level abuse cannot overturn a doctrine grounded in revelation; humans "are ritualistic creatures," and the answer to ritualism is to *move the heart with* the ritual (getting your wife flowers can go cold too — that doesn't make it wrong), not to "throw the baby out with the bathwater" by discarding sacraments. On Horn's view the formalism charge is a *pastoral caution* both sides should heed, not evidence against baptismal regeneration.

## LDS Relevance
The formalism critique is a live intramural issue *within* the Restoration, not just a Protestant-vs-Catholic one. Latter-day Saint theology makes saving ordinances — baptism, confirmation, temple ordinances, baptism for the dead — *causally necessary*, which is precisely the structure Ortlund warns breeds ritualism; LDS leaders themselves repeatedly preach against trusting the *form* of an ordinance over a *converted heart* (e.g., warnings against going "through the motions," and Moroni 7's insistence that an act done grudgingly "profiteth him nothing"). Ortlund's "the rite should point you *through* it to Christ" lands as a caution Latter-day Saints can own, even while rejecting his conclusion that the ordinance is therefore non-causal. Horn's symmetry reply is also useful to the LDS apologist: the charge that "your sacramentalism breeds presumption" rebounds on *any* assurance-bearing system, including Protestant eternal-security. The Restoration's distinctive move is to bind the *objective* ordinance to *ongoing* covenant-keeping and personal revelation, aiming (like Horn) to walk between presumption and despair. See [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]].

## Best Quotes
> "The whole point [of the sacraments] is to point our eyes to Jesus Christ... and so traditions that think it's baptism that causes regeneration can put so much focus on the rite that if we get stuck there, we're not looking from it up to Christ." [^1]

> "If you believe in the perseverance of the saints, that just as much if not more so could lend itself to the same kind of presumption — 'I made my act of faith years ago and I can't lose my salvation.'" (Horn) [^4]

> "We have a bad habit as humans to become ritualistic... but that doesn't mean it's wrong to get your wife flowers — you just have to always move the heart with it." (Horn) [^5]
