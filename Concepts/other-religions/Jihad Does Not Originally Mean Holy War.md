# Jihad Does Not Originally Mean Holy War

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The popular Western equation "jihad = holy war = mandate to kill unbelievers everywhere" is, Peterson argues, neither the literal meaning of the word nor the original or majority juristic reading. The term has been narrowed in modern usage, then weaponized to paint all of Islam as inherently violent.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The Arabic word means striving/effort, not war.** "Jihad, by the way, doesn't even mean holy war. It means effort or striving. It's come to imply holy war."[^2] *(Strong — *jihad* (j-h-d) literally denotes exertion/struggle; the martial sense is a derived, context-dependent usage.)* Peterson notes it can mean building a better educational system for one's country — and offers an LDS analogy: 19th-century Latter-day Saints planting poplar trees and digging irrigation canals were "building the kingdom," a comparable kind of striving.[^2]
2. **Aggressive, indiscriminate warfare is not the original reading.** While some say "jihad is justified against unbelievers everywhere," Peterson responds: "I don't think that's the original reading."[^3] The classical jurists who codified the rules of warfare typically held it to be **defensive**, not aggressive: an Islamic country attacked has the right to defend itself; Muslims deprived of the right to worship have a right to rebel — "but it was not indiscriminate warfare against all the world."[^3] *(Moderate-to-strong — reflects a major strand of classical fiqh; the tradition is genuinely contested, which Peterson acknowledges.)*
3. **The "kill all non-Muslims" claim is empirically false.** "I've had some people even tell me, 'Well, Muslims believe that all non-Muslims should be killed.' It's flatly not true." Peterson's evidence: substantial Christian populations have survived in the Middle East from before Islam to the present — "why aren't they all dead if it's really a Muslim's duty to kill them all?"[^4] *(Strong — the continuous existence of Middle Eastern Christian communities is decisive against the universal-killing claim.)*
4. **Steelman.** A critic can rightly point out that violent passages *do* exist in the Quran and that extremists cite them sincerely (Peterson concedes both — see [[Islamic Violence Is Mostly Political Not Quranic]]). The honest position is that the texts are genuinely readable in more than one way; Peterson's claim is not that violent readings are impossible but that they are not the *original*, *literal*, or *majority-juristic* meaning — and that the survival of non-Muslim minorities falsifies the maximalist "kill them all" reading.
