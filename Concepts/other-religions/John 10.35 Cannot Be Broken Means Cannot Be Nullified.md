# John 10:35 — "Cannot Be Broken" Means "Cannot Be Nullified"

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[John 10.33|John 10]] is a flagship proof-text: Jesus says "the word of God came" to those called "gods," and "scripture cannot be broken" ([[Psalm 82.6|Ps 82:6]] quoted). Protestants read this as Jesus calling Scripture "the word of God" and affirming its inerrancy ("cannot be broken"). [[Trent Horn]] argues both inferences misread the passage.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Jesus calls it "your law," and distinguishes Scripture from the word of God — rate: strong
The line "I said you are gods" is Psalm 82:6, which **Jesus calls "your law" rather than "scripture."**[^1] More importantly, Jesus distinguishes the *scripture that records* the event from "the word of God itself, which was the prophetic message" delivered to the recipients.[^2] The "word of God" in v. 35 is the historical prophetic utterance *to* the "gods," not the Psalm text. So the verse does not equate Scripture with the word of God. See [[Word of God Means Prophecy Preaching or Christ Not Scripture]].

### 2. "Broken" (lyō) = "nullified," a legal status, not "inerrant" — rate: strong, scholar-anchored
Scholars [[John C. Poirier]] and Douglas Pharr are cited: the Greek term translated "broken" in v. 35 is better rendered **"nullified."** The passage concerns Scripture's *legal, binding, unrepealable* status — not freedom from error.[^3] Pharr: "Christ was not concerned here with the factual verity or accuracy of scripture, but with the authority of its voice and the binding nature of its testimony."[^3] That Jesus introduces his evidence as "your law" reinforces the *normative/legal* register. So "cannot be broken" is not a proof-text for inerrancy.

### 3. The argument structure: a fortiori from "gods" to Jesus — rate: strong
Jesus' actual point is *a fortiori*: if creatures could be called "gods," then he — "consecrated by the father" — can far more rightly receive the title Son of God.[^2] The debate with the Pharisees was not about the *truth* of "you are gods" but about its still-binding normative status that Jesus could appeal to.[^4] **Steelman of the Protestant:** "scripture cannot be broken" naturally reads as a high view of Scripture's reliability, and Jesus' easy appeal to a single phrase presupposes Scripture's authority as God's word. Horn grants the high view of Scripture's *authority* but denies it entails (a) inerrancy specifically or (b) the equation "Scripture = the word of God," since the "word of God" in the verse is the prophetic event behind the Psalm. Cf. the separate Christological reading at [[John 10-33 - Making Yourself God (Missing Article)]].

### 4. The decisive point: "word of God" is not used of Scripture here — rate: strong
"The most important element of John 10 is that the term word of God is not used to describe scripture. Jesus is referencing an event in history when God delivered a prophetic message… something that happened long before the writing of Psalm 82."[^4] The Psalm *records* the word of God; it is not itself called the word of God.
