#type/claim

# Stoicism Was a Theoretical Preparatory Gospel

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]]'s overarching thesis: **Stoic thought is embedded in the New Testament from the very beginning**, so the standard "Hellenization corrupted the originally pure church" account of the Great Apostasy mis-assigns the blame. Painting "the Greeks as the bad guys" is an error. Instead, Stoicism functioned as a **theoretical preparatory gospel** — "the very same as John the Baptist did for Christ" — a forerunner that prepared the Greco-Roman world to receive the gospel.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Greek thought is in the NT from the start (rated: the thesis).** Against the model where Hellenism is "the final nail in the coffin" after the apostles die, Thomassen argues "we find Greek thought embedded into the very fabric of the New Testament" — and gives multiple examples (John 1:1 Logos; agape; the open and adverbial ethics; natural law; the sage as exemplar; Philemon).[^1] See [[John 1-1 Deploys the Stoic Logos as a Bridge]], [[The Open Ethic - Stoicism Anticipated Love Your Enemies]].
2. **A bald assertion: Stoicism as forerunner (rated: explicitly the author's claim).** "I'm going to assert — this is a bald assertion on my part — that Stoicism is acting in a theoretical way the very same as John the Baptist did for Christ. It is a theoretical precursor for the gospel to be introduced."[^2]
3. **Why Christianity took root in the Roman/Greek sphere (rated: the argument).** Though the apostles went "all over the place" (India, Scythia, Egypt), Christianity "took root within the Roman sphere," where the dominant ethos was Stoic — "that's not insignificant."[^3]
4. **The Hellenization models are all post-apostasy (rated: framing).** Tertullian (reject Athens), Clement of Alexandria (quilt in the good bits), and Augustine (baptize neoplatonism) are "living in the ashes of the apostasy"; none appeals to new revelation. The apostasy, on this view, is marked not by Greek influence but by the *cessation of revelation*.[^4] See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]].
5. **Stoicism colored what was taught (rated: the caveat).** Thomassen grants the double edge: "Stoicism may have been the tool that helped the Greco-Roman world learn the gospel, but the gospel they learned may also have been colored by the Stoicism that was used to teach it" — so the thesis cuts toward the necessity of ongoing personal revelation, not toward endorsing Hellenization wholesale.[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** (a) The whole case rests on parallels that may reflect a shared **moral milieu** or convergent insight rather than literary dependence (Thomassen jokes about "plagiarism" precisely because dependence is hard to prove). (b) Several "Stoic" elements have strong **Jewish** roots (the creative Word; heart-over-sacrifice; love of the alien), so attributing them to Stoicism can overreach. (c) Treating Stoicism as a *providential forerunner* is a theological judgment, not a historical finding, and other scholars (and the traditional LDS narrative) still read **Hellenization as a corrupting force** on the doctrine of God (creation ex nihilo, divine impassibility, the unmoved mover) — see the vault's [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]] and [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]. The "embedded from the start" observation is defensible; the "preparatory gospel" verdict is an interpretive, contestable frame.

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
This reframes the Great Apostasy: the villain is not Greek philosophy per se but the loss of living revelation. It sits in productive tension with the vault's "Hellenization corrupted the concept of God" thread (e.g. [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]], [[Hellenization Was Not Corruption of an Original Pure Church]]) — Thomassen distinguishes Stoic *ethics* (a providential preparation) from later metaphysical corruptions. The same author elsewhere argues mainstream Christianity is *converging* back toward Restoration doctrines; here he argues Greek thought was a God-used bridge, parallel to how Freemasonry gave Joseph Smith a liturgical vocabulary.
