# The Godhead and Tawhid Are Theological Opposites, Not Parallels

## The Claim

At the most basic level — the doctrine of God — Latter-day Saint theology and Islam are not parallel but opposite. The LDS Godhead (three distinct, embodied divine persons who *organized* uncreated matter, with the Father as the head of a council of gods) would be "entirely blasphemous to Muslims," whose *tawhid* affirms one absolutely indivisible God who created from nothing. The "white Islam" label collapses precisely where the two faiths are furthest apart.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The LDS Godhead is a social plurality of embodied persons
In LDS theology "the Godhead refers to the three distinct and separate divine persons of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit," who "possess unique personalities, wills, and unique centers of consciousness having... distinct spatiotemporal identities," unified by "perfect shared purpose, love, and glory" — what some call social trinitarianism. The Father is "the god of all other gods or the head god or the god at the top of the council of gods." See [[The Godhead Is Not Polytheism]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — an accurate statement of the LDS distinctive (distinct persons, divine council).

### 2. LDS theology rejects creation from nothing
"Mormonism rejects creation from nothing... God is understood to have organized existing uncreated matter to form the world." This uncreated-matter premise underwrites deification (eternal intelligences). See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — organizing pre-existent matter is a core, well-documented LDS teaching.

### 3. Islamic *tawhid* is the direct contrary
"Islam is built on the foundation of rigorous and absolute monotheism" — "there is no God but Allah," an "indivisible unique being." Islam regards even classical-Christian Trinitarianism as "blasphemous and polytheism," and "the idea that God organized pre-existing matter or that humans can become divine is entirely foreign to the classic Islamic theology." See [[If You Want Strict Monotheism Be a Muslim]].[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — *tawhid* and Islam's rejection of the Trinity as *shirk* are central, uncontroversial Islamic commitments.

### 4. The contrast runs the wrong way for the analogy
The presenter's punchline inverts the label: "I might even... go as far as to argue that there is more theologically speaking similar between classical theism and Islam than LDSism and Islam." Absolute-monotheist creedal Christianity is the closer cousin to *tawhid* — so pinning "Islam" on the Latter-day Saints, of all Christians, is the least apt fit.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a defensible comparative judgment (classical theism's simple, immaterial, ex-nihilo God resembles *tawhid* more than the LDS embodied plurality does).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A defender of the comparison can say the relevant similarity was never the *content* of the doctrine of God but the *shape* of the movement: a prophet delivering monotheistic-sounding scripture against a backdrop of perceived idolatry. And both faiths do share a refusal of the Nicene homoousios Trinity — Muslims and Latter-day Saints alike reject "three persons in one substance," which a critic could spin as common ground. The reply is that agreeing on what to *deny* (the metaphysical Trinity) masks total disagreement on what to *affirm*: Islam denies the Trinity by collapsing God into one indivisible person and one uncreated Creator; Mormonism denies it by *expanding* into a plurality of embodied persons, a divine council, organized matter, and human deification. Two doctrines can share an enemy and still be opposites — which is exactly why a Muslim would find the Godhead blasphemous, not familiar.
