# Heinleins Razor - Start With Misunderstanding Not Malice

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] names a working rule for debate ethics: **"Heinlein's razor — don't attribute to malice what can be attributed to ignorance."**[^1] Applied to apologetic exchange, the principle yields a default ordering of charitable readings. When an opponent gets your view wrong, "our first instinct should just be: this person misunderstood me," rather than "this person misrepresented me."[^2] The distinction is load-bearing: a *misunderstanding* is innocent (ignorance, ambiguous language, the gap between "what people say versus what they mean"), whereas a *misrepresentation* is a moral charge implying deliberate distortion. Horn's rule is to reach for the innocent reading first and escalate to the culpable one only when warranted: "you may need to say they misrepresented me," but you start with "we are ignorant, or misunderstood, or hopped over something."[^3]

[[Gavin Ortlund]] supplies the parallel maxim from the love-side: First Corinthians 13 means "how you argue is part of the content you're trying to get across — if it's just... without love, it's a clanging gong or resounding cymbal; without love it's not just insufficient, it's counterproductive."[^4] Charity is therefore not a softening of the argument but a condition of its effectiveness. This is the same ethic operationalized in [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]] and the posture described in [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
- **The rule is bidirectional and self-disciplining.** Horn applies it to his own side first — naming classic *Catholic* misrepresentations of sola scriptura (e.g. the John 21 "not enough books in the world" retort, which "would be beyond what its defenders claim") — and asks "what would you like Catholics to stop saying because they haven't better understood it."[^5] The razor is a discipline imposed on oneself, not only a shield for one's own position.
- **It is calibrated, not naive.** Horn explicitly preserves the option to charge misrepresentation when the evidence supports it: "if your position is this, and what they said is this, and if their video gets five times as many views as yours, you know, you might need to point that out."[^6] The rule sets the *default* and the *order of inference*, not an absolute ban on ever attributing bad faith — which keeps it from collapsing into credulity.
- **Charity has limits, modeled on Christ.** Horn guards against turning charity into a refusal ever to be sharp: "there are times — I think of Christ with the Pharisees... he calls them a brood of vipers." He adds the Elijah-mocking-the-prophets-of-Baal precedent, but with a controlling proviso: mockery "is like salt, it's something that should be done sparingly; when you overdo it it sours everything," and the failure mode is making the exception "their modus operandi."[^7] So the razor coexists with occasional sharpness rather than forbidding it — the same tension between sharpness and love handled in [[Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue]].
- **A practical mechanism to break bad-faith spirals.** Horn pairs the razor with a procedural rule — "one rebuttal, then you get a dialogue" — precisely because face-to-face dialogue "is a great way" to "break the infinite cycle" of rebuttal-to-rebuttal that breeds suspicion of malice.[^8] The charitable default is reinforced by a format that surfaces genuine misunderstanding instead of letting it harden into assumed misrepresentation.

## Best Quotes
> "It's called Heinlein's razor, and it's just an adage: don't attribute to malice what can be attributed to ignorance." [^1]

> "We can be tempted to say like, oh, this person misrepresented me, when I think our first instinct should just be: this person misunderstood me." [^2]

> "How you argue is part of the content you're trying to get across. If it's just... without love, it's a clanging gong or resounding cymbal — without love it's not just insufficient, it's counterproductive." [^4]
