# Muhammad May Have Been a True Inspired Prophet

## The Claim
Peterson, a Latter-day Saint scholar of Islam, declines to rule out that Muhammad was a genuine, divinely inspired prophet. He titled his biography *Muhammad: Prophet of God* and, given the chance to soften it, chose not to — "because he might have been. I don't know, but he might have been. I don't rule it out."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The earliest call narrative reads like a theophany.** Peterson highlights an account in which Muhammad, meditating in a cave above Mecca, hears a voice three times — "Oh Muhammad, messenger of God" — then looks up and sees a figure "seated upon his throne." Peterson argues this is most naturally read as a vision of an enthroned **God**, not the angel Gabriel, since in the Quran God (not Gabriel) is associated with a throne. Later Islamic theology spiritualized this ("you can't see God").[^2] *(Moderate — a defensible reading of a specific hadith/sira tradition; the throne association is sound, but the identification is interpretive and not the standard Muslim reading.)*
2. **"Glowing" phenomena are reported of both Muhammad and Joseph Smith.** Some accounts say Muhammad would "glow" or get a different look on his face when receiving revelation — and "there are accounts about Joseph Smith that say the same thing," describing him glowing, "looking almost translucent." For Peterson this parallel is part of why he keeps the door open: "it's possible that he really was receiving revelation."[^3] *(Weak-to-moderate — anecdotal phenomenological parallels; suggestive, not probative.)*
3. **The fallibilist frame makes inspiration-with-error coherent.** Because [[Prophets Are Fallible Today|prophets are not infallible]], affirming Muhammad as inspired does not require endorsing everything he taught; Peterson simultaneously thinks Muhammad "got some things wrong."[^4] See [[Other True Prophets Do Not Undercut Joseph Smith]].
4. **Steelman (the limits).** Peterson is candid about the disanalogies that count against an over-strong claim: Muhammad was older (40) at his call, his career ran ~22 years, his revelations were private with no witnesses or artifacts (see [[Muhammad Had No Corroborating Witnesses or Physical Artifacts]]), and Islam's denial of Christ's deity is a real doctrinal problem (see [[Islam Denies the Deity and Divine Sonship of Christ]]). His position is genuinely agnostic — "I don't know" — not a confident endorsement.

## Best Quotes
> "I wrote a biography of Muhammad called *Muhammad, Prophet of God*. I didn't actually choose the title... I could complain. And then I thought, no, I don't think I will — because he might have been. I don't know, but he might have been. I don't rule it out." — [[Daniel C. Peterson|Daniel Peterson]] [^1]

> "I looked up and I saw him seated upon his throne. Now I think this is a vision of God... in the Quran, Gabriel the angel is never associated with a throne. God is associated with a throne." [^2]

> "There are accounts about Joseph Smith that say the same thing, that some people who were there when he received a revelation actually describe him glowing, looking almost translucent. There are some accounts that say that of Muhammad... well, I mean, it's possible." — [[Daniel C. Peterson|Daniel Peterson]] [^3]
