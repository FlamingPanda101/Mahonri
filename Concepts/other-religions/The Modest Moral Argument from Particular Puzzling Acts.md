# The Modest Moral Argument from Particular Puzzling Acts

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] proposes a deliberately *modest* form of the moral argument that differs in strategy from the usual one. The standard argument tries to explain *morality in general* ("you can't have objective morality without God") and repeatedly stalls, because the atheist simply declares the relevant moral fact **self-evident** or a **brute necessary truth** — "it's just always true that it's wrong to cause pain for pain's sake," like 2+2=4 — and feels no pressure to explain further.[^1] Horn's reframing: stop arguing about generic morality and instead **zero in on the *most* puzzling specific moral facts** that naturalism strains to explain, and argue only that **God is the best *explanation*** of them, not that morality is impossible without him.[^2]

Two features define the approach:[^2]
- **It is modest in ambition.** "It doesn't try to say it will prove beyond a shadow of a doubt that God exists." It claims God is the best explanation for "many strange features about morality rather than just the generic concept of morality itself."
- **It is an argument from poverty, not proof.** "In fact, it's more of an argument about the *moral poverty of secularism* that opens the door for God than a strict argument for God from morality." The work is done by *particular hard cases*, each chosen to defeat the easy naturalist escapes.

The hard cases Horn selects are the ones where every standard secular explanation runs out: the **intrinsic dignity of non-rational humans** (severely disabled people, infants, the deliberately-brainless) over more-rational animals (see [[The Brainless Organ-Farm Reductio Against the Consciousness Criterion]] and [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]]); and the wrongness of **painless, fully-consensual perverted sexual acts** (see [[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]]). The unifying point: "morality consists of much more than this single basic truth" about reducing suffering — and that *surplus* is exactly what we'd expect "if God made the universe according to a very specific design," loving human beings and ordering their most important powers.[^3] His closing instruction to apologists is the thesis in one line: focus on "atheism's difficulty in explaining the wrongness of very particular immoral acts… and how the wrongness of these acts is what we'd expect if God existed."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **It sidesteps the brute-fact stalemate (rate: strong as strategy).** Against the *generic* claim, the atheist can flatly assert a necessary moral truth and walk away. Horn's particular cases (deliberately producing brainless humans; painless bestiality) are engineered so that the *usual* naturalist machinery — harm, suffering, consent, social side-effects — is unavailable *by construction*, forcing the interlocutor either to bite an intolerable bullet or to posit an increasingly *ad hoc* set of brute truths. That is a real dialectical gain over the "you can't have morality without God" version Horn himself calls "Reddit-level."[^1]
2. **It concedes what is true to lower the cost of entry (rate: strong, rhetorical).** By granting "you can be good without God," "you can have morality without God [as agreed rules, like football]," and even that there *are* non-silly secular accounts, Horn removes the strawman objections and makes the remaining claim — *objective* morality's surplus features are best explained by design — harder to dismiss. The modesty is a feature: an argument for *best explanation* survives counterexamples that would sink an argument for *strict entailment*.[^2]
3. **It dovetails with the evolutionary-debunking point (rate: strong, supporting).** The cases are chosen precisely because they involve wrongs that cause *no* identifiable suffering or survival cost — so a survival-tuned, mindless evolutionary process has no reason to track them, yet we judge them clearly wrong. The "too coincidental" coherence between brute moral facts and human moral revulsion is itself the surplus the theist explains. See [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]].[^5]

### Steelman of the atheist

The naturalist has principled replies. (a) **Bite-the-bullet consistency:** some of Horn's "monstrous" cases are bitten precisely by the most rigorous secular ethicists (Jeff McMahan on newborn organ-harvest; Peter Singer and David Benatar on sex with animals/corpses) — which the atheist can present not as a *failure* of naturalism but as naturalism's *honesty* in following arguments past squeamishness, while the theist merely sanctifies a gut reaction. (b) **Brute moral realism is not ad hoc:** Erik Wielenberg argues necessary moral truths are no more mysterious than necessary *mathematical* truths, which the theist also accepts without a "lawgiver"; if "2+2=4" needs no God, neither need "gratuitously degrading a human being is wrong," even in pain-free cases. (c) **Best-explanation cuts both ways:** the atheist can say *design by a good God* is itself a costly, unparsimonious posit, and that "we evolved rich social emotions that overshoot bare survival" explains the surplus revulsion without any supernatural premise. (d) **The poverty charge proves too little:** even if naturalism's explanations "get strained" (as [[Joe Schmid]] grants), *strained* is not *false*; an inference to God requires showing the theistic explanation is *better*, not merely that the naturalist one is uncomfortable. Horn concedes the modesty: this opens a door, it does not compel a walk through it.

## Best Quotes

> "The best moral argument for God's existence focuses on being modest and zeroing in on the most puzzling moral facts to explain from a naturalist perspective." — Horn[^2]

> "It's more of an argument about the moral poverty of secularism that opens the door for God than a strict argument for God from morality." — Horn[^2]

> "Morality consists of much more than this single basic truth… [the puzzles make] perfect sense [if] God made the universe according to a very specific design." — Horn[^3]
