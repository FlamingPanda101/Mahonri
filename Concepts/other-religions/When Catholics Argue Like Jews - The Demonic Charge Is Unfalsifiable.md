# When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] grants that an LDS member may *privately* believe a rival religion is demonically inspired, but argues that deploying "this could be demonic" as a **public apologetic tool** against the Book of Mormon is illegitimate because it is **unfalsifiable** — and, worse, that it reenacts the exact reasoning by which first-century Jews dismissed Jesus. He proposes (half-jokingly) a book titled *When Catholics Argue Like Jews*: the Pharisees granted Jesus' miracles were real but reattributed them to the devil ([[Matthew 12.24-26]]; cf. John 8:48, where they call him demon-possessed). Carroll's charge is that [[Trent Horn]]'s "demonic explanation is on the table" move does structurally the same thing — it concedes the evidence (the chiasmus, the stylometry, the witnesses, the translation speed) and then neutralizes it by relocating the cause to Satan, a move no evidence could ever rebut.[^1][^2]

The claim has three prongs:
1. **Epistemic.** A demonic attribution is a "get out of jail free card" / "backup plan": *if we can't explain it, at least we can say it's demonic.* It only works if you presuppose your own worldview first — exactly the begging-the-question Carroll accuses Mark Driscoll of.[^3][^4]
2. **Symmetry.** The move is available to *everyone*: Islam could say it about Mormonism, Judaism about Christianity, Mormons about Catholic Eucharistic and Marian miracles. A weapon every side can fire is not a real argument.[^5]
3. **Moral hazard.** On a large platform the label dehumanizes and builds a wall (the "boy who cried wolf" effect Horn himself used against Steven Crowder's "the Pope is a communist"). Carroll ties it to the Michigan shooter, who believed Latter-day Saints were the antichrist — not blaming Horn, but warning that inflammatory-and-unfalsifiable labels carry real-world risk that true-but-inflammatory claims (e.g. "abortion is homicide") do not.[^6][^7]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)
**Steelman the Catholic "could be demonic" charge fairly.** Horn is *not* Driscoll. He explicitly refuses to *declare* Mormonism demonic; he keeps it as one live hypothesis among natural ones, and he insists the charge can be wielded responsibly (see the companion note [[Not Even the Devil Can Raise the Dead - The Aquinas Falsifiability Defense]]). His strongest point against Carroll's "unfalsifiable" framing: Jesus *himself* reasons about whether a demonic attribution coheres ("if Satan cast out Satan... how shall his kingdom stand?"), which shows demonic claims are *evaluable*, not immune. And there is a real disanalogy Horn presses: what makes Mormonism unlike Methodism or Baptism is that it advances *as a virtue* an "otherworldly encounter" at its founding — Joseph Smith's visitation — which, Horn argues, legitimately opens the question of *what kind* of supernatural element that was. A religion that stakes itself on an angelic visit invites the [[Galatians 1-8 - Angel Preaching Another Gospel]] question by its own design.[^8][^9]

**The LDS reply.** Carroll's rebuttal is that even a *coherent* demonic story is not the same as an *evidenced* one: "I understand the concept... but it's baseless. There's no evidence for that." The Pharisees' attribution was internally coherent too, and they were presupposing their own framework. The move's fatal tell is **reversibility**: Carroll notes that Horn's own syllogism ("If Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is false; Catholicism is true; therefore...") can be run in reverse with identical force ("Mormonism is true; therefore Catholicism is false") — a sign the premises are *unproven*, not that one side has won. An argument that flips cleanly onto its user is "damage control," not demonstration. (See the companion note on argument strength and reversibility.) Honest concession to Horn: Carroll *admits* he has "no great answer" to the Eucharistic flesh, the incorrupt hosts, Fatima, and the Marian apparitions — he just thinks "lazy" is a better word than "demonic" for explaining them away, because the label presupposes the conclusion.[^10][^11]

**Where the genuine debate lies.** Both men converge on a real distinction: a demonic explanation is *not* automatically illicit (both grant Satanism may be demonic — "if it's in the name"). The live question is the **threshold of evidence and the venue**. Carroll: maybe true, but keep it private; on a platform it does harm and proves nothing. Horn: keep it among competing hypotheses, used "with care," because some wonders exceed natural explanation while still falling short of God-only miracles. The disagreement is therefore less about logic than about *apologetic ethics* — what a responsible apologist may say publicly about a rival faith's supernatural claims.

## Best Quotes
> "This makes me want to write a book called *When Catholics Argue Like Jews*, because you have first-century Jews validating Jesus Christ's miracles — they're saying 'yeah, it is a miracle, but it's demonic.'"[^1]

> "It's kind of like a backup plan. Like, hey, if we can't explain it, at least we can say it's demonic. And that's... what the Jews did in the early centuries. So I just don't find it helpful in the apologetic world."[^3]

> "I understand the concept as you've explained it... but it's baseless. There's no evidence for that. It's kind of the circular reasoning — who established that rule?"[^11]

> "Why did the Jews kill Jesus, or what was one of their arguments? It's: he's possessed by a devil."[^4]
