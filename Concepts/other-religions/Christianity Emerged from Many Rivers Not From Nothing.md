# Christianity Emerged from Many Rivers Not From Nothing

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] qualifies his own "Christianity changed everything" thesis: Christianity did not come "from nothing." It emerged from a specific first-century Mediterranean matrix fed by many rivers — Jewish scripture, Greek philosophy, Persian moral dualism, and the Roman idea of universal order. The radical novelty (a crucified slave as universal God) is real, but it is a *recombination* and re-centering of inherited materials, not a creation ex nihilo of a culture.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The four rivers.** (a) The **Jewish** scriptural inheritance; (b) **Greek** philosophy — the influence of Plato and Aristotle is "immense," so much so that "a lot of theology is just footnotes to Plato"; (c) **Persian** moral dualism — rival principles of light/dark, truth/lies, absorbed into Hebrew scripture during the Babylonian exile (Cyrus is hailed as a messiah); (d) **Roman** universal order — *imperium sine fine*, a globalized Mediterranean of safe shipping lanes and roads that let [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] write to Galatians, Ephesians, Corinthians, Romans.[^1] *(Strong — standard history-of-ideas; the multi-causal account is mainstream.)*
2. **The Greek gods as "malign stepparents."** Holland (recalling his boyhood love of the charismatic, glamorous Greek gods over the Hebrew prophets) argues the philosophers — Plato through the Stoics — reacted against the *charismatic pettiness* and cruelty of gods like Athena (Prometheus chained, his liver eternally devoured) and located the true divine "beyond these stories." Christian theology then *adopts* that philosophical understanding of the divine and "bundles it into the package." The Greek gods are thus "the malign stepparents of Christianity" — defined by their rejection.[^2] *(A vivid framing of how Christian monotheism absorbed Greek philosophical theism.)*
3. **Even Paul's conversion draws on Euripides.** When the risen Christ asks Paul "why are you kicking against the pricks?" (Acts), the phrase comes from Euripides' *Bacchae* (the fate of those who reject Dionysus). Holland notes the clear resemblances between a god like Dionysus and Christ that "even Paul can recognize" — evidence the new revelation was articulated *through* the available cultural-mythic vocabulary.[^3] *(Illustrates the recombination thesis at the level of the founding texts.)*

**Steelman of a more exclusivist Christian view.** A believer might worry this dissolves Christianity into a syncretic stew — "just one more Hellenistic mystery religion." Holland blocks that reading: the *content* is genuinely revolutionary (the rivers flow *into* something new at "the molten heart of the gospel"), and the inversion of the cross has no real precedent. The "many rivers" point is about *seedbed*, not *essence* — analogous to how a believer can affirm both that God prepared the nations and that the gospel is *sui generis*. The note belongs in the apologetic toolkit precisely because it pre-empts the "copycat religion" objection by granting the borrowings while insisting on the novelty.

## LDS Relevance
The "many rivers" model resonates with the LDS reading of history as one of *preparation* — God scattering truth among the nations and philosophies (cf. Alma 29:8, God granting "unto all nations, of their own nation and tongue, to teach his word") ahead of fuller revelation. It also reframes the common anti-LDS "syncretism" charge (that the Restoration borrowed from masonry, folk magic, or 19th-century revivalism): if mainstream Christianity itself is a Spirit-guided recombination of Jewish, Greek, Persian, and Roman streams, then borrowing cultural forms is not by itself evidence of fraud. Compare [[The Dominion Thesis - Western Values Are Christianized]].
