# Inaccuracy as a Form of Disrespect

## The Claim
[[David Snell]] raises a case the "attack the person, not the idea" criterion does not obviously cover: an interlocutor who is *respectful in tone* but persistently *inaccurate* — who won't "do the homework" to represent beliefs correctly and keeps straw-manning. Both he and [[Kyle Beshears]] conclude that this refusal-to-be-accurate is itself a covert form of disrespect, which is why it feels like anti-Mormonism even without overt insult. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The puzzle: polite but wrong.** Snell describes opponents who are "being respectful but inaccurate. Even just historically. And sometimes they will not seem willing to do the homework in order to represent beliefs correctly." [^1] *Rating: a real gap* in the simple person/idea test — no name-calling, yet something is plainly off.

2. **Refusing to fact-check signals contempt.** Beshears reframes it: "it's not only that I'm unwilling to fact check myself, it's that I don't trust you and I don't respect you enough to do the homework." [^2] *Rating: strong;* it shows that misrepresentation, even when courteous, communicates that the other person is not worth getting right — so it collapses back into "attacking the person."

3. **They're no longer engaging facts.** Snell: "they're not actually engaging with facts anymore, are they? They're disrespecting you." [^3] *Rating: strong;* accuracy is recast as a duty owed to the *person*, not merely to the truth.

4. **Mostly unintentional, rooted in pride.** Beshears judges it "probably mostly unintentional... a condition of our fallenness and our selfishness and our pride." [^4] *Rating: moderate;* it keeps the charge charitable — diagnosing a vice rather than assuming malice — while still naming the failure.

5. **It cuts both ways.** Snell stresses "it goes for both sides... both sides of the aisle are guilty of this." [^5] *Rating: strong;* the symmetry mirrors [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea]].

**Steelman of the inaccurate party.** Honest mistakes happen; no one can master every nuance of another tradition, and demanding flawless accuracy could chill good-faith engagement. The concept answers this: the fault is not occasional error but *unwillingness to correct* once shown — the persistent refusal to do the homework, especially after being told. That refusal, not imperfection, is what reads as disrespect.
