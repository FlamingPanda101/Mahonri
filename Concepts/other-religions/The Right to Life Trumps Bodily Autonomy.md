# The Right to Life Trumps Bodily Autonomy
#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that when the right to life and the right to bodily autonomy genuinely conflict, the right to life is the weightier of the two: autonomy is a right to control one's body, but it does not extend to *killing* an innocent human being who is dependent on that body. Against [[Johnny (Atheist Analysis Debater)|Johnny]]'s bodily-autonomy case (framed through self-defense law and the famous "organ-sharing"/violinist parallel), Horn replies that the unborn is not an unjust aggressor and that withdrawing life support from a dependent person by lethal means is morally distinct from merely declining to *donate* an organ. Bodily autonomy is real but bounded; it cannot ground a right to directly end another's life.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Autonomy does not include a right to kill the innocent.** A right over one's own body is not a right to take an innocent third party's life. Even granting strong bodily autonomy, "my body, my choice" does not license, say, killing a born child who depends on me — and the unborn (per [[The Unborn Is a Human Organism Not a Blueprint]]) is a human organism, not a body part.
2. **The unborn is not an aggressor; self-defense analogies fail.** Johnny leans on self-defense law (Castle Doctrine, Stand Your Ground, an AOJP — ability/opportunity/jeopardy/preclusion — analysis). Horn's counter is that self-defense presupposes a *culpable or unjust threat*; the unborn, in the ordinary case, is an innocent, non-aggressing being whose mere presence and dependency cannot constitute the kind of attack that justifies lethal force.
3. **Killing vs. not-sustaining (the violinist/organ-sharing reply).** The Thomson-style "you may unplug the violinist" / refusing to share your organs intuition trades on a *failure to rescue*. Horn's distinction is that most abortions are not a passive disconnection but an *active killing*, and that parents already owe their offspring ordinary duties of care that strangers do not owe a violinist — so the analogy understates the relationship and overstates the parallel to organ donation.
4. **Triage is not license to kill (carried from the burning-lab case).** As in [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]], Horn insists that *choosing whom to save* (rescue triage) never implies *permission to slit throats* — so even cases where we wouldn't expend resources to save an embryo don't show we may actively destroy it.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The strongest pro-choice reply (Judith Jarvis Thomson) grants fetal personhood for argument and still defends abortion: no one has a right to *use* another's body without consent, so the woman may withdraw bodily support even if the fetus dies, just as you may unplug the violinist. Bodily integrity is a uniquely stringent right — we do not legally force even corpses to donate organs to save a life, so why force a living woman? And in pregnancies threatening the mother's life, autonomy and self-preservation align. Horn's rejoinder is the active-killing/positive-duty distinction above and the claim that gestation, unlike random conscription to a violinist, flows from the ordinary parental relationship to one's own offspring — but the debate's crux is whether "declining to sustain" accurately describes abortion, and whether bodily autonomy can ever be outweighed by another's right to life.
