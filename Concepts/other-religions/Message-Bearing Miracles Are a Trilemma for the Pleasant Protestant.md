# Message-Bearing Miracles Are a Trilemma for the Pleasant Protestant

#type/claim

## The Claim
In the same breath that [[Hayden Carroll]] calls the demonic charge "the strongest argument against Mormonism," [[Trent Horn]] flips it: the demonic charge is, he says, "the strongest argument *against Catholicism*" — and that is exactly why Catholic miracles are so probative. Horn's move is to **split miracles into two kinds**:

1. **Generic faith-healings** — a missionary or preacher heals someone dying of illness. Horn freely grants God can do this through *anyone*: Mormon missionaries, Protestants, even non-Christians (he cites [[Craig Keener]]'s research, noting most of Keener's catalogued cases are healings). A healing shows God's *benevolence*; it carries no denominational message.[^1]
2. **Message-bearing miracles** — the Eucharist transforming into incorrupt human flesh, a Marian apparition declaring herself the Immaculate Conception, Zeitoun, the Miracle of the Sun at Fatima (seen, he notes, by thousands including non-Catholics). These do not merely show God is kind; they **communicate a specific doctrinal content** that is *distinctively Catholic* — Eucharistic Real Presence, Marian dogma.[^2]

The second category, Horn argues, generates a **trilemma**, and it bites hardest on the *pleasant* Protestant (the one who grants Catholics are fellow Christians, "another branch of the tree"). Faced with a miracle that endorses unique Catholic Mariology, the pleasant Protestant must say one of three things: (a) it is **demonic** — but then he has become the *unpleasant* Protestant, conceding Catholicism is a satanic counterfeit, which contradicts his "fellow Christian" premise; (b) it is **God's message** — in which case God is endorsing Catholic doctrine and he should convert; or (c) it **didn't happen** — denying well-attested events. The unpleasant Protestant at least escapes consistently (it's the devil getting people to become "damned Catholics"), but the pleasant Protestant is squeezed.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
**Steelman of Horn.** The argument is sharper than a generic "look at our miracles," and several features give it real force:
1. **The healing/message cut is principled.** Horn is not cherry-picking; he *concedes the easy cases to his opponents*. By granting that God heals through Mormons and non-Christians, he isolates the only evidentially interesting class — miracles whose very content is doctrinal — and refuses to argue from anything weaker. This is the [[The Strongest Argument Grants the Most Premises|grant-the-premises]] method turned on his own side.
2. **It weaponizes the opponent's own charity.** The trilemma is structured so the *more* generous a Protestant is toward Catholics, the *worse* his position: charity ("you're my Christian brother") closes off the demonic exit and forces either conversion or denial. The unpleasant Protestant's consistency is purchased only by the ugly cost of calling a sister church satanic — which is the very [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable|"Catholics arguing like Jews"]] move under discussion, now aimed back at Protestants.
3. **It connects to the falsifiability defense.** A message-bearing miracle is precisely the kind of event that, on Horn's [[Not Even the Devil Can Raise the Dead - The Aquinas Falsifiability Defense|Aquinas criterion]], strains the demonic explanation: the devil might stage a wonder, but events that publicly and durably *teach* a true-claiming doctrine sit uneasily with "the father of lies merely deceiving." Horn folds these miracles into his "spiral argument" for the Church's authority — foundation at the Resurrection, confirmation across history by sacramental miracles, healings, and apparitions.[^4]
4. **Honest concession from the other side.** Carroll, to his credit, does not pretend to have an answer: "those are things that I don't necessarily have a great answer to. My Catholic fiancée would tell you that... those are the things that I struggle with." He still prefers "lazy" to "demonic" as the label, but he grants the evidential weight is real.[^5]

**Where an LDS or Protestant reply pushes back.** Carroll's standing rejoinder from the [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable|unfalsifiability]] note still applies in reverse: a *Mormon* could mount the symmetrical trilemma about the Book of Mormon's message-bearing production, and Horn would answer it with "magic trick, not miracle" — so the same skeptical resources Horn uses on Mormonism could, an opponent argues, be turned on Marian apparitions (natural psychology, mass suggestion, pious legend). The disanalogy Horn must defend is that *his* message-bearing events resist naturalization in a way the Book of Mormon's production (a text, always writable by *someone*) does not. Whether Fatima or a Eucharistic miracle truly clears that bar is the live question the trilemma pushes the Protestant toward — and Horn's recommendation to Carroll is exactly to "dive into those Catholic miracles, signs, and wonders."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "I actually think that's the strongest argument *against Catholicism*. Because... it creates a kind of trilemma."[^3]

> "When God does a miracle that has a message behind it for like really distinct Catholic theology — like a Eucharistic miracle, a Marian apparition — to me it seems like he's trying to communicate a very particular message about this particular church."[^2]

> "I agree that Protestants can do miracles too... most of those are faith healings. I do not limit God in what he can do."[^1]

> "On the record, those are things that I don't necessarily have a great answer to... but to label it as demonic, I think is — I was going to say dishonest, but maybe just it's lazy." — Hayden Carroll[^5]
