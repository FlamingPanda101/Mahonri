# Holy Envy — What Latter-day Saints Can Learn from Islam

## The Claim
Rather than treating Islam as the thing one must avoid being associated with, Peterson commends three Muslim practices as objects of **"holy envy"** — admirable disciplines Latter-day Saints can learn from: serious daily prayer, reverence for scripture, and religion as a whole-life, seven-day-a-week identity.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Disciplined, embodied prayer.** A devout Muslim prays five times daily, marking the time and using physical gestures (prostration, forehead to the ground as a token of absolute submission — *salat*, "liturgical" ritual prayer). There is also *du'a*, free invocation, available anytime. Peterson contrasts this with the LDS ideal of "praying continually," which he admits is easy to neglect: "It's really easy to go a whole long day and not have said a prayer at all." For a Muslim, "you're always either moving toward prayer or just finishing prayer."[^2] *(Strong — accurately describes salat and du'a; the self-critique is Peterson's pastoral application.)*
2. **Reverence for the physical scriptures.** Peterson recounts a Palestinian BYU student (named Castro Mohammad) unimpressed that Latter-day Saints put marked-up scriptures on the floor under classroom seats — in Islam the Quran is never placed under another book or on the floor, and worn copies are ritually disposed of, as a Jew disposes of a Torah scroll. Peterson's own Cairo-bound Quran was inscribed "let none touch it but the pure," reflecting the practice of ritual cleanliness before reading.[^3] *(Strong — these reverence practices and the Quran 56:79 allusion are accurate.)* He commends the reverence while noting one can overcorrect into treating scripture as too sacred to actually read.[^4]
3. **Religion as a seven-day-a-week identity.** Islam is "a religion with you every day": modest dress (especially for women) that signals devotion, dietary regulations that constantly remind you that you are "a little different." Peterson suggests this functional reminder is part of the value of the **Word of Wisdom** — declining a glass of wine repeatedly flags one's identity and even opens gospel conversations.[^5] *(Moderate — the functional-marker reading of the Word of Wisdom is Peterson's interpretive point, offered tentatively: "it may or may not be the intrinsic merits of the thing.")*
4. **Steelman / framing.** "Holy envy" is not syncretism — Peterson is clear elsewhere that Islam denies the deity of Christ and that he would not adopt these practices wholesale ("I don't think we ought to go that far"). The claim is that recognizing genuine spiritual goods in another tradition is consistent with, even demanded by, the LDS belief that God gives [[Early LDS Leaders Praised Muhammad as Inspired|partial light to many peoples]].

## Best Quotes
> "There are some things I think we can really learn from — the seriousness with which they take prayer. A devout Muslim prays five times daily... You can't forget that you're a devout Muslim because you're always either moving toward prayer or just finishing prayer." — [[Daniel C. Peterson|Daniel Peterson]] [^2]

> "In Islam, you treat the Quran with reverence. You never put it under another book. You never would put it on the floor. When it's worn out... it's ritually disposed of the way a Jew would dispose of a Torah scroll." [^3]

> "I like that it's a religion with you every day... It dominates dietary regulations. It's one of the reasons I think we have the Word of Wisdom — it just reminds you every once in a while you can't actually do that because you're a little different." — [[Daniel C. Peterson|Daniel Peterson]] [^5]
