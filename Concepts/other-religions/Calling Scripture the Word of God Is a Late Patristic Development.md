# Calling Scripture "the Word of God" Is a Late Patristic Development

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] surveys the church fathers and argues that the earliest of them call "the word of God" either the Christian *message* or *Christ himself* — not a set of written texts — and that they do not even call the NT "Scripture" before [[Justin Martyr]]. Explicit "Scripture = word of God" statements appear only gradually, becoming common among *later* fathers (e.g. [[Basil the Great]], 4th c.). Horn flags this section as "preliminary," so its conclusions are tentative.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Pre-Justin: word of God = the message or Christ — rate: moderate, self-described as preliminary
"Prior to Justin Martyr, the church fathers don't even call the New Testament writings scripture. They identified scripture with the Old Testament and the word of God with Christ himself."[^1] [[Clement of Rome]] says the apostles were "established in the word of God" (the Christian message); the [[Didache]] describes the word of God as a spoken reality about the faith; the [[Epistle of Barnabas]] warns "you shall not let the word of God issue from your lips with any kind of impurity."[^2] For these writers the word of God is like the *charisma* or the gospel — a message, not a corpus.

### 2. Ignatius and Justin: Christ and the Creator-Word — rate: moderate
[[Ignatius of Antioch]] wishes the Smyrnaeans happiness "through the immaculate spirit and word of God," probably a reference to Christ.[^2] [[Justin Martyr]] repeatedly uses "word of God" for the person who created the world (echoing [[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter]] 3:5 and [[Hebrews 11.3|Hebrews 11:3]]); where he may mean the Mosaic writings, "the meaning isn't clear."[^3]

### 3. Irenaeus and Tertullian: the Word produces and speaks through Scripture — rate: strong
[[Irenaeus of Lyons]] uses "word of God" for Christ in dozens of places ("the word of God made man"), and of the Eucharist says the bread "receives the word of God" (Christ).[^4] Crucially, Irenaeus says "the scriptures are indeed perfect since they were spoken by the word of God and his spirit" — i.e. Scripture is perfect *because the Word produced it*, not because Scripture *is* the Word.[^4] [[Tertullian]] likewise can call the book of Jeremiah "the word of God spoken to Jeremiah" (ambiguous: is the word, or contains the word), yet elsewhere distinguishes "the word of God, Christ" from the Old and New Testaments.[^5]

### 4. Basil and later fathers: the explicit identification emerges — rate: moderate
"Later church fathers like Basil the Great in the 4th century do offer clear statements identifying scripture as being the word of God. But this seems to be common more among later fathers than earlier ones."[^6] **Steelman of the Protestant:** the earliest fathers are terse and the corpus is fragmentary; absence of the explicit phrase may reflect occasional writing, not a different theology — Irenaeus already treats Scripture as divinely produced and authoritative, which is most of what the Protestant needs. Horn concedes the survey is "preliminary" and that results are "somewhat tentative," so this is an argument about *development of expression*, not proof that early Christians denied Scripture's divine origin. The payoff is at [[Scripture as Word of God Is Itself a Doctrine of Sacred Tradition]]: a *gradual recognition* implies the identification is a deliverance of tradition.
