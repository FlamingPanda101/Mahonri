# Peter's Respect Is Fear of God Not the Interlocutor

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] offers a linguistic reading of [[1 Peter 3.15|1 Peter 3:15]] ("give a defense for the hope that is in you, with gentleness and respect"). He argues the word translated "respect" is *phobos* (fear), and that in New Testament and Septuagint usage *phobos* is characteristically directed at *God* — so Peter is commanding the apologist to contend with gentleness toward the person and fear *of God*, not deference to the opponent. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Greek term is phobos, "fear."** Beshears: "the word for respect is phobos, which is fear." [^1] *Rating: lexically correct;* 1 Peter 3:15 reads *meta prautētos kai phobou* — "with gentleness and fear." Most translations render *phobos* here as "respect" or "reverence," which can obscure the fear sense.

2. **Phobos is God-directed in the biblical idiom.** "Very often in the New Testament and in the Greek the Septuagint of the Old Testament, phobos or fear is related to God." [^2] *Rating: strong;* "the fear of the LORD" (Hebrew *yirʾat YHWH*, LXX *phobos kyriou*) is a pervasive biblical category, supporting a God-ward reading. (Note: many commentators read the *phobos* in 3:15 as reverence toward God; the steelman below records that the referent is debated.)

3. **The practical upshot reframes the whole encounter.** "You contend for your faith in gentleness, kindness with the person and fear of God... Am I doing what is pleasing to you [God] in this moment?" [^2] *Rating: strong as application;* it makes the controlling question in any debate "is this pleasing to God?" rather than "am I winning?" — the direct antidote to victory-worship (see [[Victory as an Idol in Apologetics]]).

4. **It guards both gentleness and conviction.** Because gentleness is owed to the *person* while fear is owed to *God*, the verse forbids both contempt for the opponent and the cowardice of syncretism. [^3] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it coheres with Beshears's "hold the tension" method (see [[Holding the Tension Between Anti-Mormonism and Syncretism]]).

**Steelman of the standard rendering.** Many exegetes hold that the *phobos* in 1 Peter 3:15 *does* most naturally refer to reverence toward God (the immediately preceding verse, 3:14, cites Isaiah 8 on fearing God, not man), so Beshears's reading is well within mainstream scholarship rather than a novelty — but a minority do take it as respectful demeanor toward the questioner. Either way, the apologetic payoff (gentleness toward people, ultimate accountability to God) holds; Beshears's contribution is foregrounding the God-ward fear that the bland "respect" translation flattens.

## Best Quotes
> "The word for respect is phobos, which is fear. And very often... in the Septuagint of the Old Testament, phobos or fear is related to God." [^1]

> "You contend for your faith in gentleness, kindness with the person and fear of God. Respect for God. Like am I doing what is pleasing to you in this moment?" [^2]

> "If you take away God... and you replace it with victory, well, victory likes conflict." [^3]
