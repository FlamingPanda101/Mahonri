# The Clarity of Science vs Obscurity of Religion Is a False Dichotomy

## The Point
The common contrast between the bright "clarity of the sciences" and the murky "obscurity of religion" is, in [[Robert Barron|Bishop Barron]]'s words, "a false, phony dichotomy." The fantasy that the "light of reason" delivers crisp 100% certainty is an **Enlightenment fantasy**; in reality only a "narrow range" of things appear in that bright light, while the "most important truths" are known "in the twilight." Religion therefore does not occupy a uniquely murky epistemic category — it lives in the same twilight as nearly everything else humans know.

## Argument
1. There is a tendency to "distinguish sharply between the clarity of the sciences and then all this obscurity of religion." Barron resists this "right away." [^1]
2. Even the things we should know best are uncertain: how sure are you of "your wife's deepest convictions and beliefs"? Spouses "become more mysterious to each other as we move through life." [^1]
3. The picture of "100% epidictic certitude — the light of reason shines and then the things that I clearly see, that's what I believe" — is "an Enlightenment fantasy." "Almost none of life is like that." [^2]
4. The Enlightenment's own critics saw this — Barron names **Goethe** ("Gerta") and **Pascal** — observing that it is "such a narrow range of things that allow themselves to appear in that kind of brilliant bright light." [^2]
5. "Most of the most important truths that we know are known in the twilight; they're known in a fleeting way." [^2]
6. Therefore the clarity/obscurity split between science and religion is a "false, phony dichotomy." [^2]
7. **Steelman of the objector.** A defender of the dichotomy would say science earns its clarity precisely by its method (controlled, repeatable, intersubjective observation — see [[Stripping Subjectivity From the World Produces Nihilism]]), so the contrast tracks a real epistemic difference rather than an Enlightenment myth; appeals to "twilight" can license unfalsifiable belief. Barron's rejoinder is that the same probabilistic, tradition-borne, partly-obscure character pervades science and history too (see [[Knowledge Runs on Degrees of Probability Not Certitude]]), so the difference is one of degree, not a clean clarity/obscurity wall.

## Best Quotes

> "This fantasy of 100% epidictic certitude — that's an Enlightenment fantasy, that the light of reason shines and then the things that I clearly see, that's what I believe. Gosh, almost none of life is like that." [^1]

> "Go back to the critics of the Enlightenment — go back to people like Goethe and others, Pascal comes to mind — it's such a narrow range of things that allow themselves to appear in that kind of brilliant bright light. Most of the most important truths that we know are known in the twilight; they're known in a fleeting way." [^2]
