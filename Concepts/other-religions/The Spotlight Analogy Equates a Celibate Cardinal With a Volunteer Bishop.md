# The Spotlight Analogy Equates a Celibate Cardinal With a Volunteer Bishop

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Anti-Mormon coverage — led by [[Michael Rezendes]], the journalist behind the Catholic *Spotlight* exposé — equates the LDS [[Paul Adams]] case with the Catholic abuse scandal, implying the Church of Jesus Christ "has its own *Spotlight*." The objection is the *analogy itself*: that the two institutions' handling of abuse is "basically pretty much the exact same thing."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The two cases differ on every material axis (strong — the on-screen table)
The episode lays the cases side by side. *Spotlight* (Catholic): **paid clergy** abused **thousands** of children across dozens of criminal cases; administrators **discovered the extent** and **reassigned** the abusers to escape prosecution; the church paid millions in settlements and one diocese reached the largest U.S. bankruptcy settlement. Adams (LDS): **no clergy were involved at all** — only an **inactive lay member** who abused **his own two daughters**; the **parents lied** about the extent so leaders "never knew fully until years later"; the church **excommunicated** the abuser; the abuser and his wife were jailed; and the **Arizona Superior Court dismissed** the case against the church [^1]. See [[Abuse Is Less Likely in the Church Not More]].

### 2. The structural difference is the celibate solo priest vs. the married lay bishop (strong)
"Every good Mormon is just a lay Mormon, but every bad one is the freaking president, priest, and king." The Catholic predator was "a paid, trained, childless, lifetime celibate employed … cardinal"; the LDS bishop is an unpaid volunteer "soccer dad." The LDS safeguards have no Catholic analogue: "bishops must be married. Women staff half the leadership in a local ward. Interviews are always open to parents to attend. And clergy rotate every few years. There are no lifelong celibate single priests operating a solo diocese in the LDS Church" [^2]. See [[Abuse Is Less Likely in the Church Not More]].

### 3. The bishop was the helper, then recast as the villain (strong)
Adams confessed a single past incident; the bishop brought in the wife (hoping she would stop and report it), called the abuse helpline, and "advised the couple to call the police themselves" — they refused. Over two years both parents repeatedly assured him the abuse had ceased. Rezendes's headline, "Seven years of sex abuse: How Mormon officials let it happen," "upgraded" one confessed incident to "seven years of rape" and "made it President Nelson's fault," even though the bishop was the one urging reform [^3].

### 4. The method is a rhetorical formula, not a finding (strong)
The episode names the technique: blame the institution for any one member's sin; equate a lay volunteer with a trained celibate cardinal; "always make it about systems, institutions, procedures, so that the blame can crawl upwards"; "use loose language, eschew precision"; "focus on the emotional aspects"; "never investigate past the headline." The analogy survives only by "loosening the language" until two unlike things sound alike [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
The strongest opposing case: an institution *can* be culpable even when the abuser is a layman, if its procedures (a helpline that discouraged calling police; confidential excommunication) functionally shielded the abuse; "no clergy abused anyone" is not the same as "the institution handled it well"; and a child suffered for years regardless of the org-chart distinctions. A grieving family's lawsuit is not mere opportunism. This is a fair challenge — and Part 6 answers it not by denying institutional responsibility in principle but by the comparative facts (the bishop urged reporting; the parents lied; the abuser was excommunicated and jailed; the court dismissed the suit) and by the reporting dilemma the coverage omits (see [[The Reporting Catch-22 and the Johnson Case]]). The claim is narrow: the Adams case is *not* a *Spotlight*, and the equation requires suppressing the differences.

## Counter-objection: "all abuse is satanic, so this belongs with the SRA episodes"
The We Are the People Utah podcast uses "all abuse is satanic" to fold ordinary abuse cases back into the satanic-ritual-abuse narrative ("a Trojan horse for 'prophets are eating babies'"). The episode insists on the distinction: "there is a world of difference between abuse and satanic ritual abuse, neither of which is sanctioned by, protected by, or covered up by" the church [^5]. See [[Accusations Cannot Transmute Into Evidence]].
