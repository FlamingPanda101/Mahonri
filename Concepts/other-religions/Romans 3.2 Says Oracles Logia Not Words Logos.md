# Romans 3:2 Says "Oracles" (logia), Not "Words" (logos)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] cites [[Romans 3.2|Romans 3:2]] in the NIV — "the very words of God" — as evidence that Israel was entrusted with Scripture, i.e. that Scripture is the words of God.[^1] [[Trent Horn]] argues this leans on a translation choice that the Greek does not support.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Greek is logia, not logos — rate: strong (lexical)
"The Greek word in this passage is logia, not the more common logos or word. So most translations render it the oracles of God rather than the words of God."[^1] *Logia* denotes divine **oracles/utterances** — prophetic pronouncements — not "words" in the sense of a written corpus. The NIV's "words of God" is a defensible gloss but is the *outlier* that makes the verse sound like a statement about Scripture-as-text. See the broader lexical point at [[Word of God Means Prophecy Preaching or Christ Not Scripture]].

### 2. N.T. Wright: "oracles" are not equated with Scripture — rate: strong, scholar-anchored
[[N.T. Wright]] is quoted: "Nowhere else in early Christian writings are Israel's scripture designated as God's oracles. Israel was entrusted with what the gentile nations might have perceived as divine oracles."[^2] Wright thus distinguishes *oracles* (divine pronouncements/promises) from the category "Scripture." This blocks the inference oracles ⇒ "the Bible is the words of God."

### 3. A.T. Robertson: commands and promises, not the text — rate: moderate, scholar-anchored
Protestant Greek scholar [[A.T. Robertson]] is cited to the effect that *logia* here "may refer to the commands and promises of God rather than scripture itself."[^2] That Horn enlists a *Protestant* grammarian strengthens the point against a Protestant proof-text. **Steelman of Ortlund:** "oracles of God" were in fact *deposited in* Israel's Scriptures, so being entrusted with the oracles is functionally being entrusted with the Scriptures; the NIV gloss captures the practical referent. Horn's reply: even so, the verse predicates "oracles/words of God" of God's *utterances*, not of the written documents as such — so it cannot do the work of establishing the contested premise that the human writing *is* the word of God.
