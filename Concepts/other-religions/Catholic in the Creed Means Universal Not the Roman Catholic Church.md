# Catholic in the Creed Means Universal Not the Roman Catholic Church

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection (offered as the "low-hanging fruit" a Latter-day Saint might raise) is that the Creed makes you "basically pledge allegiance to the Catholic flag" by confessing "I believe in one holy Catholic Apostolic Church." [^1] A Latter-day Saint, the worry goes, cannot say that line.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Greek-lexical correction (the core)
"You know what the Greek says? 'In one holy apostolic catholicos faith.' In the Greek, little-c catholic, **catholicos**, just means universal." [^2] So the confession is not allegiance to the Roman institution but "I believe in one body of Christ around the whole world." [^3] **Rating: lexically correct** — Greek *katholikos* (καθολικός) means "universal/general," and the creedal "catholic" is the lowercase universal-church sense, not the proper-noun Roman Catholic Church.

### 2. The anachronism argument (strong)
"In the creed, you're not confessing allegiance to the Roman Catholic Church 'cause it didn't exist for another 700 years. You're not confessing allegiance to the Pope because ... papal supremacy, Petrine primacy in the Roman Catholic Western way wasn't understood for another 600 years." [^4] **Rating: historically substantive** — the developed doctrines of papal supremacy and the institutional Roman Catholic Church as later defined postdate 325, so reading "catholic" in 325 as "Roman Catholic" is anachronistic.

### 3. The reframed confession
The payoff: a Latter-day Saint *can* affirm "one holy catholic and apostolic church" because it means "one body of Christ ... around the whole world," not submission to Rome. [^3] The guest treats this as the easiest of the "three things" Mormons mistakenly stumble over in the Creed. [^5]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A Catholic apologist would respond that while *katholikos* lexically means "universal," the fathers (e.g. Ignatius, "where the bishop is, there is the catholic church") already attached it to a *visible, hierarchically-ordered, episcopally-governed* communion — not a vague worldwide aggregate of believers. "Apostolic" in the same clause implies apostolic *succession* and authoritative office. So affirming "one holy catholic and apostolic church" in its 4th-century ecclesial sense arguably commits one to a unified, bishop-led, apostolically-successive Church — a structure the LDS restoration narrative explicitly says had failed by then. The lexical point ("universal") is correct but does not, by itself, neutralize the ecclesiological content the early church loaded into the word.
