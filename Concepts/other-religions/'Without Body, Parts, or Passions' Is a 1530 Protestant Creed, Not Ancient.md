# 'Without Body, Parts, or Passions' Is a 1530 Protestant Creed, Not Ancient
#type/claim

## The Claim
The familiar credal formula that God is "without body, parts, or passions" is not an utterance of the apostles or the earliest church but a *Reformation-era confessional phrase*, traceable to the sixteenth century — its best-known wording appearing in the Protestant articles of 1530 onward and carried into the Thirty-Nine Articles (1563) and the Westminster Confession (1646). The claim is used in the vault to deny that *impassibility plus incorporeality* is the self-evidently "ancient" Christian doctrine it is often presented as; it is a particular, datable theological settlement. This matters because [[The Head-Chest-Belly Anatomy of the Soul|C.S. Lewis's anatomy of the soul]] and [[Thick and Clear Religion - Uniting Head and Belly|"thick and clear" religion]] dignify the trained *passions* and the *embodied* middle of the human person — close to a Latter-day Saint anthropology that refuses to treat passion and body as defects to be transcended.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The phrase is confessional, not patristic.** The exact triad "without body, parts, or passions" appears in the Reformed/Anglican confessional tradition — Article I of the Thirty-Nine Articles ("without body, parts, or passions") and Westminster Confession 2.1 — rather than in a creed of the first centuries. Its crystallized *form* is early-modern, even where the underlying ideas (divine simplicity, impassibility) have older philosophical roots.
2. **"Passions" meant more than emotion.** In the classical/scholastic vocabulary a *passion* is something one passively undergoes; to call God "without passions" was to deny that He is acted upon or changed by His creatures. The formula thus encodes a specific (Hellenically-shaped) doctrine of impassibility, not a neutral datum of revelation.
3. **It sits in tension with the embodied model of the soul.** Lewis (via [[After Humanity - Michael Ward|Michael Ward]]) locates the *definitively human* soul in the Chest — the seat of trained emotion / Magnanimity — neither pure intellect (the angelic Head) nor pure appetite (the animal Belly). A theology that makes the divine ideal "without parts or passions" pulls the other way, toward the purely "clear," disembodied God.
4. **LDS relevance.** The Restoration affirms an embodied God ("The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's," [[Doctrine and Covenants 130.22|D&C 130:22]]) and that "the spirit and the body are the soul of man" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 88.15|D&C 88:15]]). On this view the 1530 formula is a post-apostolic *innovation* that pushed Christendom away from the more anthropomorphic God of the Hebrew Bible — evidence, for the LDS reading, of doctrinal drift rather than apostolic continuity.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A traditional Christian responds that while the *phrasing* is sixteenth-century, the *doctrine* (divine simplicity, incorporeality, impassibility) is genuinely ancient: the Fathers (Irenaeus, Athanasius, Augustine, the Cappadocians) and even pre-Christian Jewish thinkers (Philo) affirm that God is spirit (John 4:24), invisible, and not composed of parts, and that scriptural "passions" ascribed to God are anthropopathic accommodations. On this view the confessions merely *summarize* the catholic consensus; dating the slogan to 1530 does not make the doctrine modern any more than dating "Trinity" or "homoousios" makes those concepts late. The disagreement is whether incorporeal impassibility is the inherited apostolic teaching or a Hellenistic overlay later codified.
