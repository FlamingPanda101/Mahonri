# Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud

## The Claim
Asked point-blank whether Joseph Smith was "an outright fraud," evangelical scholar [[Kyle Beshears]] — who does *not* accept Joseph as a prophet — answers no. He once would have said "Yeah, it's obvious. How could he not be? Angels, seer stones, gold plates, come on." But after immersing himself in the primary sources for his book, he concluded Joseph was "an extremely complicated man" who was genuinely sincere, even if Beshears cannot follow where that sincerity led. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Sincere desire for salvation in a hostile religious climate.** Beshears holds "zero doubt that the atmosphere of evangelical Christianity in the frontier in the early 19th century was not conducive for him to find salvation," and judges the young Joseph's critique of sectarian division "biting and true" — "we should... if there's one Lord, one faith, one baptism, why did we have this 13, 14-year-old kid not be able to find truth in these competing churches." [^3] *Rating: strong as an empathetic historical reading;* it grants the First Vision's framing problem (rival revivalist sects) without conceding the vision's truth.

2. **A pastoral side visible in the Nauvoo record.** Reading Joseph "visiting people during an epidemic, praying over them, mourning a woman who lost her child on Christmas Eve... counseling people during church trials, standing up for somebody when everybody else was pretty sure they were guilty," Beshears — himself a pastor — "immediately saw" a pastoral sincerity he "couldn't unsee." [^4] *Rating: strong;* it is grounded in Joseph's own journals rather than enemies' affidavits (cf. [[Read the Primary Sources Before Judging a Person]]).

3. **Sincerity increased rather than waned over time.** Against the standard "con man learns to exploit followers" arc, Beshears argues Joseph's "sincerity for his desire to see Christianity healed" did not diminish but "actually increases as he grows in this prophetic office." [^5] *Rating: moderate;* it is an impression from the sources, not a demonstrative proof, but it directly contradicts the calculating-fraud model.

4. **The remaining objection: first-person divine revelation.** Beshears does not whitewash Joseph. For Joseph "to produce revelations that are in the first person active voice of Jesus Christ... bothers me deeply," forcing him to "hold him at arm's length." [^6] *Rating: this is the honest limit of the concept* — sincerity is affirmed, prophethood denied (see [[Doctrine and Covenants Is Harder Than the Book of Mormon for Evangelicals]]).

**Steelman of the fraud thesis.** A critic (e.g., in the vein of [[Dan Vogel]]'s "pious fraud" reading) can grant Joseph's pastoral warmth yet insist that sincerity and deception coexist routinely in charismatic founders; warmth toward the flock is exactly what makes a movement cohere. Sincerity, on this view, is psychologically real but evidentially irrelevant to whether the plates and revelations were fabricated. Beshears partly concedes this by refusing to endorse the "prophet" verdict — his point is narrower: the cartoonish "outright con" is not what the documents show. See [[Refusing the Pious Fraud Category]].

## Best Quotes
> "At one point in my life I would have said yeah, it's obvious. How could he not be? Angels, seer stones, gold plates, come on. But in the writing process of this book I came to a different conclusion." [^1]

> "I met an extremely complicated man... I don't think Joseph Smith was an outright con, but in my conviction I don't think he was a prophet either. I think that Joseph Smith was sincere in his desire for salvation." [^2]

> "There's a sincerity and I even dare say like a pastoral side to him that I immediately saw as a pastor... I couldn't unsee that. So I... will never throw away this element of sincerity behind him. I just don't follow him in where his sincerity led him." [^4]
