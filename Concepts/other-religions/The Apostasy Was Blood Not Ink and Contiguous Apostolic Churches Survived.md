# The Apostasy Was Blood Not Ink and Contiguous Apostolic Churches Survived

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] reframes the Great Apostasy against a Rome-only "tunnel vision." His thesis: after Pentecost the apostles fanned out far beyond Jerusalem and Rome — **Thomas → India, Bartholomew → Armenia, Andrew → Scythia and Scotland, Matthew → Parthia** — planting churches across the world, most of which were destroyed not by doctrinal drift but by **conquest** (Huns, then the Islamic expansion of the 7th–10th centuries). [^1] "Mostly the apostasy wasn't ink, it was blood." Several of these **contiguous apostolic churches still survive** and are routinely disbelieved: **Armenia** (first Christian nation, founded by tradition through Bartholomew), the Indian **St Thomas** church (claiming descent from Thomas, citing an ancient Hebrew metal-plate inscription as evidence of an early presence), and the **Ethiopian Orthodox** church (preserver of much apocrypha, claimant of the Ark). [^2] The kicker for the apostasy debate: Rome's claim to be "the universal church" is in part a *survivor's* claim — "we were the ones who survived, so we get to say we're the only true one" — when in fact "there were twelve" original plantings, and Rome's later dominance owed less to doctrinal purity than to **England**: Rome reached England, England colonized the world, so the Western form of Christianity rode empire to global reach while Ethiopia and Bulgaria conquered no one. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The ancient non-Roman churches are real and genuinely old.** The Armenian Apostolic Church, the St Thomas Christians of India (Malankara/Syriac tradition), and the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church are historically attested, long-continuous communions with their own liturgies and canons — the existence-and-antiquity half of the claim is solid (cf. [[Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church]]). *Rating: strong as to existence; the *apostolic-founding* claims rest on later tradition.* [^2]
2. **"Catholic" originally meant *universal*, not *Roman*.** The vault's [[Catholic in the Creed Means Universal Not the Roman Catholic Church]] supports Barnes's point that Rome's exclusive "universal church" branding is a later appropriation, not a first-century given. *Rating: coheres with existing vault content.*
3. **Islamic conquest did erase much Eastern Christianity.** The collapse of once-flourishing churches across North Africa, the Levant, and Asia under conquest is historical fact, lending real substance to "the apostasy was blood." *Rating: historically grounded as a description of decline by conquest.* [^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The **apostle-founding traditions are mostly late apocryphal legend** — the very point the vault documents from the critical side in [[Apostolic Martyrdom Traditions Are Late Apocryphal Legend]]. That Armenia or India *has* an ancient church does not establish that *Bartholomew* or *Thomas* personally founded it; those attributions come from the same 2nd–5th-century apocryphal Acts scholars treat as non-historical. "Contiguous since an apostle" is precisely what cannot be demonstrated for most of them. And surviving-by-geography cuts both ways: it explains Rome's reach without vindicating *anyone's* unique truth-claim, which is awkward for an LDS Restoration argument too. A measured reply: Barnes's load-bearing claim is the modest, defensible one — that Christianity was *plural and global*, and that Rome's "only true church by survival" rhetoric is weak — not that every founding legend is fact; he treats the apocryphal Acts as preserving a real *pattern* (apostles dispersed worldwide) even where specific details are legendary. For the Latter-day Saint the payoff is that "apostasy" was largely violent overthrow of many plantings, not a tidy doctrinal corruption of one.

## Best Quotes
> "Mostly the apostasy wasn't ink, it was blood. The gospel was planted all over the world at different times and places, and it was usually destroyed by hostile nations." [^1]

> "The Roman Catholic claim to be the universal Christian church is kind of by default — 'we were the ones who survived, so we get to say we're the only true one.' Whoa, whoa, there were twelve." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The note repositions the Great Apostasy ([[Great Apostasy]]) as the *violent suppression of a worldwide apostolic church*, not merely the doctrinal decay of the Roman see — and uses the survival of disbelieved contiguous churches (Armenian, Indian, Ethiopian) to relativize Rome's "one true church" claim, a move structurally parallel to the LDS claim that authority was lost broadly and must be restored, not inherited from any one surviving institution.
