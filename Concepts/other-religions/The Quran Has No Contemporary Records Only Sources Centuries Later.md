# The Quran Has No Contemporary Records Only Sources Centuries Later

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that the gulf between the Quran's and the Book of Mormon's *origin evidence* dissolves the Muhammad–Joseph Smith parallel. On the Islamic side, tradition holds that the angel Gabriel dictated the Quran to Muhammad over ~23 years, which he recited to companions who memorized or wrote it; but the records that establish this tradition come **almost two centuries after Muhammad's death**. There are, Carroll says, *no* contemporary records — no eyewitness documents — that Muhammad recited the Quran word-for-word as it stands today.[^1] He recruits University of Chicago Islamicist [[Fred Donner]], who argues the "traditional Islamic origins narrative... which almost everyone accepted with considerable confidence, is far less secure than we once thought": the sources are literary chronicles and biographical dictionaries written **two or three centuries** after the events (8th–10th century), resting on "almost no actual documentation," and they conflict with the somewhat-earlier non-Muslim (mainly Christian) descriptions.[^2][^3]

Carroll's twist: this critique **boomerangs on Heschmeyer's own tradition**. A Roman Catholic who discounts the Quran for lacking contemporary documentation cannot consistently rest his faith on doctrines that likewise "have no historical evidence to support them" — Carroll names the supremacy of Rome, papal infallibility, and the Marian dogmas.[^4] The disanalogy with the Book of Mormon, by contrast, runs the other way: the Book of Mormon has a contemporary original/printer's manuscript and a cluster of named witnesses (see [[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]]).

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
1. **A credentialed, non-LDS source carries the weight.** Donner is a mainstream historian (PhD Princeton; *Muhammad and the Believers*, *Narratives of Islamic Origins*), not an apologist, and the "documentary gap" is a recognized problem in Islamic studies, not an LDS invention. Carroll even notes the later traditionist sources are "heavily debated, including by Catholics."[^1] *(Strong — this is the academic consensus that the *traditional* origins narrative is late and lightly documented, independent of any apologetic agenda.)*
2. **The tu quoque is structurally fair.** If "no contemporary documentation" is disqualifying for the Quran's transmission story, the same standard applied evenly indicts distinctively Catholic claims that postdate the apostolic age and lack first-century attestation. The point is not to prove those doctrines false but to deny Heschmeyer a double standard. *(Moderate — it is an *ad hominem / consistency* move; it embarrasses the objector without settling the truth of either tradition. See [[Heschmeyer Selectively Trusts the Islamic Tradition He Needs]].)*
3. **It sharpens, rather than abandons, the historical method.** Carroll is not anti-tradition wholesale; he is insisting that *contemporary* documentation is the gold standard ("historians like to work with documents... the most secure basis"), and that on that standard the Book of Mormon's case is *stronger*, not weaker, than the Quran's. *(Strong as stated — the asymmetry in surviving manuscripts and witnesses is the load-bearing claim of the whole video.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** (a) Donner's skepticism about the *traditional narrative* does not show the Quranic *text* is late — most scholars (Donner included) accept the Quran's consonantal skeleton as substantially early (7th-century), so "no contemporary records of the dictation event" is not the same as "the Quran is a centuries-later forgery." Carroll's framing risks overclaiming. (b) The Catholic boomerang cuts only if Catholic doctrines are validated the *same way* the Quran's transmission is — by a claimed chain of historical eyewitness dictation. A Catholic will answer that doctrines like papal primacy or the Marian dogmas are justified by *developing* apostolic tradition and the Church's living authority, not by a documentary chain, so the "no contemporary record" yardstick is a category mistake against them. (c) An even-handed skeptic can turn the documentary standard back on the gold plates too: the witness statements are real, but the plates themselves were not retained for examination, so "contemporary documentation" is doing contested work on both sides. The honest residue is that *every* tradition here asks for some trust beyond strict contemporary artifacts; the live question is whose evidentiary chain is least encumbered.
