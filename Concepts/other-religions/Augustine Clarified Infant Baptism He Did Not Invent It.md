# Augustine Clarified Infant Baptism He Did Not Invent It

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues the picture "only really changes with Augustine" — that Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings (c. 410) introduced the emphasis that infants must be baptized or be damned. [[Trent Horn]] strongly disagrees: Augustine *clarified and unified* a doctrine and discipline that already existed in the 3rd century; he did not originate it. The proof is that Augustine's *opponent*, [[Pelagius]], **also** affirmed infants must be baptized — so the belief was already universal, not an Augustinian innovation forged to "dunk on Pelagius."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Pelagius himself affirmed infant baptism — the killer datum.** Augustine reports Pelagius saying he "had never heard even an impious heretic" deny infants the sacrament of baptism, asking "who is so impious as to wish to exclude infants from the kingdom of heaven by forbidding them to be baptized?" Since Augustine and Pelagius agreed on *almost nothing else*, their agreement here shows infant baptism was the *universal* prior conviction of the church, not a position Augustine manufactured. Pelagius even appeals to the practice's *universality* as his ground.[^2] *(Decisive — an opponent's concession is the strongest possible evidence the belief predated the dispute.)*
2. **The Nicaea analogy: clarifying, not creating.** Trent: just as the Council of Nicaea "did not create the doctrine of Christ's divinity but helped create uniform expressions of it" in a church with "sloppy or even heretical views," so Augustine's formulations on infant baptism "helped unify different disciplines that had emerged" — "not a theological genesis." The disorder Augustine addressed (some delayed baptism, e.g. [[Gregory of Nazianzus]]) reflects an *inconsistently applied* discipline, not the *absence* of the doctrine.[^3] *(Strong — a well-chosen structural parallel.)*
3. **Heretics and Donatists weren't credobaptists either.** The Council of Carthage (419) treated infants baptized *by Donatist heretics* as validly baptized (not to be rebaptized) — showing even the Donatists practiced infant baptism. "Even heretics like the Donatists did not hold anything resembling a credobaptist position," contrary to later Baptist historical-revisionist claims.[^4] *(Strong — closes off the "some early group was credobaptist" escape.)*
4. **The 4th-century delays don't equal rejection.** [[Gregory of Nazianzus]], baptized as an adult, did *not* rebuke his parents (possibly out of filial honor) and did *not* recommend others delay; pressed why hurry when Jesus was baptized at 30, Gregory replied "Jesus was God, you weren't," and urged, "Have you an infant child? Do not let sin get any opportunity, but let him be sanctified from his childhood." A few prominent delayed baptisms (often from fear of post-baptismal sin) are not a "universal rejection" of the 3rd-century infant-baptism norm.[^5] *(Strong — turns a stock credobaptist example into a witness for infant baptism.)*

**Steelman of Ortlund's view.** Ortlund's more careful claim is not that Augustine invented *infant baptism* but that he decisively shifted its *theological meaning and stakes* — making it, via the doctrine of original sin and the damnation of unbaptized infants, *necessary for salvation* in a starker way than before, and giving the practice a rationale (inherited guilt) that earlier, more "innocence of children"–friendly fathers had not emphasized. On this reading Augustine is a genuine turning point in *why* infants are baptized and *what is at stake*, even if the bare practice predates him. Trent partly concedes the development of *expression* while denying any change in the underlying doctrine/discipline; the honest middle is that Augustine *systematized and hardened* an existing practice into a sharper soteriological necessity — neither pure invention (Pelagius refutes that) nor mere repetition.

## Apologetic Engagement (LDS)
This is the hinge for the Latter-day Saint. The whole patristic edifice Trent defends rests on **original sin** — infants are baptized because they bear inherited guilt needing washing ([[Psalm 51.5|Ps 51:5]]; Origen; Augustine). The Restoration cuts the root: there is no inherited guilt (2nd Article of Faith; Moroni 8; D&C 137:10), so infant baptism is not merely unnecessary but, in Mormon's words, "solemn mockery." Crucially, Trent's own evidence shows that as soon as the church universalized infant baptism, it did so *on original-sin grounds* — so an LDS reader can grant Trent's history (infant baptism is ancient, even pre-Augustine) while insisting the doctrine that drove it was already an error. The Pelagius–Augustine dispute thus marks, for Latter-day Saints, not the *invention* of infant baptism but the *entrenchment* of the false premise that necessitated it (see [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]]). Where the early fathers spoke of children's *innocence*, the Restoration vindicates that instinct against the original-guilt doctrine that overrode it.

## LDS Relevance
Locates the doctrinal error precisely: the antiquity of infant baptism is conceded, but its driving premise (original guilt) is exactly what Restoration scripture rejects — so the strength of Trent's historical case becomes, for the Latter-day Saint, a clear map of where and why the apostolic pattern was altered.
