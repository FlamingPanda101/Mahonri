# Personhood by Majority Vote Is a Dangerous Criterion
#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that if personhood — and therefore the right to life — is grounded in *collective human decision* ("we've decided" who counts) rather than in objective human nature, then rights become whatever the majority confers, and can be withdrawn from any group the majority chooses to exclude. When his interlocutor [[Johnny (Atheist Analysis Debater)|Johnny]] grounds the infant's right to life in the fact that "we value the human species" and "we've decided," Horn turns this into a reductio: a consensus criterion would have *validated* every historical atrocity in which the in-group voted some humans out of personhood (slaves, Jews under the Reich, the "savage"). A standard that licenses the strong to define away the rights of the weak is precisely the standard a theory of *equal* human rights must reject.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Consensus tracks power, not worth.** If "person" means "whoever we agree to count," then the unborn, the disabled, the enslaved, or any disfavored group lacks rights the moment consensus shifts. Horn's point is that this makes rights a function of majority sentiment rather than a protection *against* it — the opposite of what a right is for.
2. **The historical parity.** Horn presses that societies have repeatedly "decided" certain humans were non-persons: "150 years ago people didn't have emotional attachments to African slaves; that didn't prove they weren't human." A criterion that would have ratified slavery or genocide is disqualified by that fact alone.
3. **It collapses into might-makes-right.** Once Johnny concedes (under cross-examination) that he "lean[s] more towards nihilism" and that the rat lacks our rights only because "we value the human species," Horn shows the position has no principled floor — the same logic that excludes the fetus can exclude anyone the powerful disfavor.
4. **Contrast with the nature-based ground.** The claim is the negative mirror of [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]]: only a *non-conferred*, intrinsic ground (membership in the human/rational kind) yields rights that are genuinely *equal* and cannot be voted away.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Johnny's fair reply: *all* moral status may in fact be socially conferred — there is no mind-independent "personhood" written into biology, so even our treatment of the born is a human construction, and admitting this is honesty, not endorsement of atrocity. He can add that a sophisticated consensus view need not be bare majoritarianism: it can appeal to reflective equilibrium, reciprocity, or the interests of beings who can be harmed, which still excludes early embryos (no interests, no sentience) without licensing slavery (slaves obviously have interests). Horn's rejoinder is that any purely conferred status remains in principle revocable and so cannot do the work of an *inviolable* right — the disagreement is over whether objective, intrinsic human worth exists at all.
