# Baptismal Regeneration Is the Doctrine Infant Baptism Is the Discipline

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]]'s framing device for the whole infant-baptism debate is a distinction between **doctrine** (what the faithful must believe) and **discipline** (what the faithful are bound to do). *Baptismal regeneration* — that baptism really saves and communicates justifying grace — is a **doctrine** whose antiquity is "overwhelming." *Infant baptism* is a **discipline** that took time to be uniformly implemented. Trent's primary case is therefore **theological, not historical**: (1) baptism is for spiritual regeneration; (2) all people need spiritual regeneration; (3) therefore all who *can* be baptized should be — including infants. The history is secondary support, which reframes the entire dispute: even granting Gavin Ortlund's reading of the early silence, an *uneven discipline* is exactly what one expects.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Baptismal regeneration is conceded even by Baptist critics.** Trent stacks hostile witnesses: Baptist scholar [[George Beasley-Murray|G.R. Beasley-Murray]] wrote that treating baptism as "a purely symbolic rite must be pronounced not alone unsatisfactory but out of harmony with the New Testament itself" — admitting this "runs counter to the popular tradition of the denomination to which the writer belongs." Baptist pastor William Webster granted baptism is "one of the few teachings... for which there is a universal consent of the fathers"; even [[R.C. Sproul]] conceded it was "testified to early" with no early opposition.[^2] *(Strong — multiple admissions against denominational interest.)*
2. **The scriptural anchor.** Baptism "now saves us" ([[1 Peter 3.21|1 Pet 3:21]]); one must be "born of water and the Spirit" to enter the kingdom ([[John 3.5|John 3:5]]). Trent treats these as locating real saving efficacy in baptism (against a bare-symbol reading); contrast [[Gavin Ortlund]]'s metonymy reading in [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]].[^3] *(Contested but textually grounded.)*
3. **The doctrine/discipline split does real work.** Because doctrine can be uniform while disciplines "take time... to be fully implemented in harmony with said doctrine," the early unevenness on *infant* baptism is not evidence against it. Trent concedes "many people in the early church were inconsistent in applying this discipline" while insisting the underlying doctrine (all need regeneration) was settled — so the inference "infants too" was always available and was progressively realized.[^1] *(A genuinely useful analytic move that defuses many arguments-from-silence.)*
4. **The second premise targets the Church of Christ.** "All need regeneration, so baptize infants" is aimed at groups (e.g. the Churches of Christ — [[Everett Ferguson]]'s tradition) that affirm baptismal regeneration *yet* withhold infant baptism: Trent argues that position is internally unstable, since if baptism regenerates and all need regeneration, denying it to those who cannot yet profess faith requires extra justification.[^4] *(Sharp internal critique.)*

**Steelman of the credobaptist view.** [[Gavin Ortlund]] (and Baptists generally) reply that the New Testament consistently presents baptism as following *repentance and faith* (Acts 2:38; "believe and be baptized," Mark 16:16), so baptism is the believer's response, not an efficacious rite worked on a passive recipient — making infant baptism a category error regardless of how one rates regeneration. They can grant baptismal regeneration in *some* sense (baptism as the ordinary occasion of conversion-initiation) while denying it does anything *ex opere operato* to a non-believing infant. The disagreement is thus not only "doctrine vs. discipline" but *what baptism is for* — and the credobaptist says its God-intended object is the professing believer. Trent's rejoinder is the theological syllogism plus the claim (developed in the sibling notes) that the *historical* evidence, read against arguments from silence, favors an apostolic pedobaptist discipline anyway.

## Apologetic Engagement (LDS)
For Latter-day Saints this debate is instructive but lands on a *third* position: the Restoration affirms baptism is *necessary and salvific* (regeneration-adjacent: "born of water and of the Spirit," [[John 3.5|John 3:5]]; D&C 22) — siding with Trent and against bare symbolism — yet *rejects infant baptism outright* (Moroni 8 calls it "solemn mockery," because little children are whole and not capable of sin). So the LDS view splits the Catholic package: baptism saves, but infants neither need nor may receive it, because there is no inherited guilt to wash away. Thus the very doctrine/discipline machinery Trent uses to *defend* infant baptism, an LDS reader uses to *locate the error*: the church correctly retained a high view of baptism's efficacy while wrongly extending it to infants once the (mistaken) doctrine of original guilt took hold. See [[Augustine Clarified Infant Baptism He Did Not Invent It]] and [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]].

## LDS Relevance
This note clarifies *where exactly* Latter-day Saints agree and disagree with Catholic baptismal theology — agreeing on salvific efficacy (a point of contact with Trent against Evangelical symbolism) while rejecting infant baptism on the ground that there is no original guilt. It strengthens the LDS hand by showing the infant-baptism dispute is downstream of the original-sin dispute, which Restoration scripture resolves.
