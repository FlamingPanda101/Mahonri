# Tertullian's Opposition Presupposes an Established Practice

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim
[[Tertullian]] (c. AD 206) is the *only* prominent pre-Reformation Christian writer to recommend *against* infant baptism — which credobaptists cite as early support. [[Trent Horn]] argues the opposite: Tertullian's testimony actually presupposes that infant baptism was an *established, elaborate* practice in his day, and his reasons for delay were not credobaptist reasons (he affirmed baptismal regeneration and original guilt) but a fear of post-baptismal sin. By the 3rd century, [[Origen]], [[Cyprian of Carthage]], and [[Hippolytus of Rome]] attest infant baptism as the universal apostolic norm.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Tertullian affirmed exactly what credobaptists deny.** He held that baptism *regenerates* and that the soul is sinful from birth: "every soul, by reason of its birth, has its nature in Adam until it is born again in Christ... and because unclean, it is actively sinful." So his delay was *not* the modern credobaptist rationale (that infants are innocent and baptism merely symbolizes a prior conversion). He delayed to avoid *post-baptismal* sins he (after embracing the Montanist heresy) thought unforgivable — a prudential, idiosyncratic motive, not a theology of believer's-only baptism.[^2] *(Strong — Tertullian's own words undercut his usefulness to credobaptists.)*
2. **His argument presupposes an elaborate existing rite.** Tertullian objects to the *godparents'* burdensome duties — which only makes sense if infant baptism was already a developed institution with sponsors, not a novel emergency improvisation. Trent: this "speaks of the elaborate nature of infant baptism at this time," "something that would be unexpected for a practice that is novel and only done in emergencies."[^3] *(Strong — the shape of his objection is evidence for an entrenched practice.)*
3. **He never calls it heresy or novelty.** Tertullian is "quick to label other innovations as abominable heresies" (e.g. modalism), yet against infant baptism he offers only a *prudential* "prefer to wait" argument — never the denunciation of a novelty unconnected to the apostles. Ferguson's claim that Tertullian "would only have opposed it if it were novel" is undercut by Tertullian's later heretical (Montanist) trajectory, which Trent says drains that inference of force.[^4] *(Strong — the absence of a novelty-charge from a famous heresy-hunter is telling.)*
4. **The 3rd-century consensus is infant baptism as apostolic.** [[Origen]]: "the church received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism even to little children," because the apostles knew "in everyone was original sin's innate defilement which needed to be washed away." [[Cyprian of Carthage]]'s council rejected even *delaying* infant baptism to the 8th day (the circumcision analogy), judging "the mercy and grace of God is not to be refused to anyone born of man." [[Hippolytus of Rome]] gives instructions for baptizing infants who can't speak for themselves. The live 3rd-century debate was over *timing* (8th day?), not *whether* — proof the practice was already universal.[^5] *(Strong — multiple independent 3rd-century witnesses; the timing-only debate is decisive.)*

**Steelman of the credobaptist view.** Ferguson's counter (which Ortlund follows): Tertullian's very *existence* as an opponent shows infant baptism was contestable in c. 200 — a genuinely apostolic, universally-understood-as-mandatory practice would be hard to argue against at all, the way no one argued against baptizing adult converts. And Tertullian's stated reason (let sponsors not take on risk; let candidates "know Christ" first) at least *gestures* at a believer's-baptism intuition even if his theology differs. Moreover, that the 3rd-century writers had to *assert* apostolic origin ("the church received from the apostles") may indicate the claim was being established, not simply reported. Trent's rejoinder is that Tertullian opposes it *prudentially* (delay), not *in principle* (never baptize infants), and never as a novelty — and that the unanimous, geographically spread 3rd-century attestation, debating only the eighth-day question, is far better explained by apostolic origin than by a 2nd-century invention that somehow became universal and "apostolic" within a generation or two without controversy.

## Apologetic Engagement (LDS)
A Latter-day Saint reads this exchange as a window onto *when* the corruption set in — and finds it useful that the patristic record itself shows infant baptism *tied to original sin* (Origen, Tertullian, later Augustine). That is precisely the LDS diagnosis: infant baptism is the practical fruit of the false doctrine of inherited guilt. So the more successfully Trent demonstrates that 3rd-century Christians baptized infants *because* "in everyone was original sin's innate defilement," the more clearly he locates (from an LDS view) the theological wrong turn. The Restoration's Moroni 8 is, in effect, a direct answer to Origen's stated rationale: infants have no defilement to wash away. Note too that Tertullian is the early church's clearest voice that *something* about infant baptism warranted hesitation — a hesitation the Restoration vindicates on different grounds.

## LDS Relevance
Helps date and diagnose the rise of infant baptism: the patristic defenders ground it explicitly in original sin, which is exactly the doctrine Restoration scripture rejects — so the historical case for infant baptism's antiquity simultaneously strengthens the LDS case about *why* it arose and *where* it went wrong.
