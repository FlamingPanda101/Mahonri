# Homicide Is Not Always Murder - The Mens Rea Distinction

## The Claim

[[Joe Heschmeyer]] (the Catholic) concedes, against a questioner pressing him on abortion, that **homicide — the killing of an innocent human being — is not always *murder***, because *murder* (and culpable sin generally) requires **knowledge and intent** (the legal *mens rea*; theologically, "sin is an act of the will"). A bad act done in invincible ignorance, or by someone genuinely unable to know what they are doing, is a tragedy and a wrong, but not the *crime* of murder and not damning sin. This natural-law distinction does heavy work in the abortion exchange and ties directly into the broader debate principle that culpability scales with light received.[^1]

Heschmeyer's worked examples:[^2]
- A woman who kills her child while **sleepwalking**, believing the child is "covered in spiders," has caused a death but is "not guilty of the crime of murder."
- A woman so **mentally ill** she cannot tell her child from a demon is not, morally or legally, a murderer.
- By contrast, a woman who **knows** the unborn is a human life and aborts anyway *is* guilty: "someone who knows better and then does it anyway is guilty of murder."

The same machinery extends beyond abortion. Asked whether pre-Nicene Christians (or his own children) are damned for not holding Trinitarian orthodoxy, Heschmeyer answers that **heresy and apostasy, like any sin, must be "done knowingly and willingly"** to be damning — "my children are almost certainly Trinitarian heretics... I'm not too worried about it yet." Culpability is indexed to knowledge (Luke 12:48, "to whom much is given"), with the conscience and the "law written on the heart" ([[Romans 2.6-13|Romans 2]]) accusing or excusing on the day of judgment.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The act/culpability distinction is standard in natural-law ethics and Catholic moral theology (rate: strong, mainstream).** Catholic teaching distinguishes the *objective* gravity of an act from the *subjective* culpability of the agent; mortal sin requires grave matter **plus** full knowledge **plus** deliberate consent. Heschmeyer's "sin is an act of the will... doing a bad thing that you knew better and you chose to do" is a faithful summary. So "abortion is intrinsically gravely wrong" and "not every woman who aborts is a murderer" are *both* affirmable without contradiction.[^2]
2. **It is also ordinary criminal law (rate: strong, factual).** The homicide/murder distinction (murder vs. manslaughter vs. excusable homicide; the insanity and automatism defenses) is exactly the legal architecture Heschmeyer invokes — the sleepwalking-mother case mirrors real automatism acquittals. This lends the moral claim independent, secular plausibility.[^1]
3. **It rescues the conception view from the [[The IVF Reductio Against Conception-as-Ensoulment|IVF reductio]] (rate: this is the load-bearing function).** Heschmeyer can hold the embryo is a full person from conception *and* deny that every IVF mother or post-abortive woman is a murderer — because intent and knowledge may be absent. This blocks Hansen's "you'd have to jail the grandmothers" prong without surrendering the metaphysics of personhood. The distinction is thus the Catholic's primary defense against the reductio.[^4]

### Steelman of the LDS reply ([[Jacob Hansen]])

Hansen presses two objections:
- **Convergence cuts the other way.** Since Heschmeyer concedes abortion "isn't always murder," Hansen claims a shared conclusion reached more economically: on the LDS delayed-ensoulment view there is *no person yet* to murder, so one need not run everything through subjective culpability. "You yourself seem to be indicating that what they're doing isn't murder."[^5]
- **The IVF case is not ignorant.** Hansen resists letting the mens-rea move *fully* exonerate IVF: "I don't believe women who get IVF are mentally ill," and they "can be shown" the biological facts — so if conception really did make a person this obvious, the ignorance defense would be thin and most IVF *would* be culpable. (Heschmeyer's rejoinder: the *biological* question — when a distinct organism exists — was genuinely inaccessible for most of human history, so ignorance here is not like the visible-child cases.)[^6]

### Steelman of the Protestant note ([[Aaron Shafovaloff]])

Shafovaloff adds the evangelical coda that even a *genuine* murderer — one with full mens rea — is offered **immediate and complete forgiveness** in Christ (Psalm 51, the David paradigm), while still being justly liable to punishment by the state. This keeps the gravity of culpable killing intact while denying that culpability is the last word soteriologically.[^7]
