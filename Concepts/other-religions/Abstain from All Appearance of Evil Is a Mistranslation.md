# Abstain from All Appearance of Evil Is a Mistranslation

## The Claim

A common piece of folk ethics holds that a Christian must avoid any behavior that *someone else might misunderstand* to be wrong — that morally neutral acts become sinful if they merely look bad to an observer. The proof-text is the KJV of [[1 Thessalonians 5.22|1 Thessalonians 5:22]], "Abstain from all appearance of evil." [[Dan McClellan|McClellan]] argues this is a mistranslation: the verse says nothing about how acts appear to other people.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *eidos* means "kind/type," not "outward appearance" — here

The Greek word the KJV renders "appearance" is *eidos* (εἶδος). It carries both senses: it can mean the *form* or *outward appearance* of a thing, but it can also mean the *variety, kind, or type* of something. McClellan notes that scholars today "pretty unilaterally agree" the second sense is in view in this passage.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (lexical). The semantic range of *eidos* is well-established; the dispute is only over which sense fits 1 Thessalonians 5:22, and the scholarly verdict is "kind/type."

> **Caption note:** the auto-generated captions transcribe the Greek word as "ethos"; McClellan is in fact discussing *eidos* (εἶδος), the word actually present in 1 Thessalonians 5:22.

### 2. The corrected reading is "avoid every kind of evil"

Read with the "kind/type" sense, and in line with the majority of modern New Testament translations, the verse should read something like "avoid every kind of evil." It is telling the reader to steer clear of *all the different ways evil can be manifested* — not forbidding acts that aren't wrong simply because a bystander might misread them.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (translational). This matches mainstream modern renderings (e.g. "abstain from every form of evil," ESV/NRSVUE, where "form" = category).

## Best Quote

> "The Greek word here that is translated 'appearance' is [*eidos*], which can mean the form or the outward appearance. But it can also mean the variety, the kind, or the type of something ... this passage should actually read something like 'avoid every kind of evil.'" — [[Dan McClellan]][^4]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- A defender of the folk reading could appeal to the "outward appearance" sense of *eidos* as still lexically possible. McClellan's reply is that lexical possibility is not the question — the near-unanimous scholarly judgment is that the "kind/type" sense is what the author meant here.[^2]
- Same genre as [[Isaiah 7.14 Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated]]: a doctrine or ethic built on a single archaic-English word that does not bear the weight modern readers place on it.

## LDS Relevance

This is a clean, low-stakes illustration of the principle behind the eighth Article of Faith — that the Bible is the word of God "as far as it is translated correctly." A widely repeated rule of Christian conduct turns out to rest entirely on a 1611 rendering ("appearance") that the underlying Greek does not support; the modern reading ("kind") dissolves the rule. For Latter-day Saints this is useful in two directions: it warns against KJV-only literalism that treats a single English word as binding revelation, and it models the value of consulting the original languages and current scholarship rather than proof-texting an archaic translation. It does *not* impugn the KJV wholesale — only shows why "translated correctly" is a meaningful qualifier. Compare [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]] for the related point that translation is interpretive, not mechanical.
