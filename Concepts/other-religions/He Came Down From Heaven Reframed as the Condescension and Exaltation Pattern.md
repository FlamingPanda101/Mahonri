# He Came Down From Heaven Reframed as the Condescension and Exaltation Pattern

## The Claim
As the guest reaches the Creed's incarnation and ascension clauses, he reads them through two distinctly LDS lenses: (1) "he came down from heaven" = the **condescension of God** (a Book of Mormon theme), and (2) the ascension is paired with the LDS couplet **"As man is now, God once was; as God is now, man may become."** The argument: even these clauses are compatible with LDS exaltation theology once read carefully. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Condescension reading (uncontroversial)
"For us men and for our salvation, he came down from heaven. — Condescension of God, we're down with that." [^2] **Rating: solid** — the "condescension of God" (cf. 1 Nephi 11) maps naturally onto the incarnation clause; nothing LDS-specific is strained here, and it echoes Philippians 2's kenosis.

### 2. The Lorenzo Snow couplet, de-sensationalized (the notable move)
The guest invokes "As man is now, God once was; as God is now, man may become," then argues it is "often times taken out of context to be something sci-fi when in reality it is: as man is now, meaning mortal, God once was mortal; as God is now, ascended to heaven, man may become ascended to heaven." [^3] So he reads the couplet *minimally* — about mortality and ascension/exaltation — rather than the maximal "God was once a sinful man on another planet" caricature. **Rating: a genuine softening of the King Follett / Snow couplet**; defensible as one LDS reading, but it underplays the stronger metaphysical claims Joseph Smith made in the King Follett Discourse about God's own progression.

### 3. Ex materia creation aside (consistency note)
On "through him all things were made" the guest flags: "depends on which mean made. I'm an ex materia guy, not an ex nihilo guy, but that's fine." [^4] **Rating: a consistent LDS commitment** (creation from pre-existing matter) noted in passing; it does not contradict the clause, which is silent on *ex nihilo* vs *ex materia*.

### 4. "The Lord, the giver of life" = divinity, not a third co-equal Lord
On the Holy Spirit clause "the Lord, the giver of life," the guest says: "I wouldn't call the Holy Spirit the Lord. — Well, translation. It just means divinity ... preeminence." [^5] **Rating: a translation-softening** consistent with his pattern; the Greek *to kyrion* is read as a title of divine status rather than identity with Yahweh.

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A critic would note that reframing the Snow couplet as merely "mortal → ascended" empties it of the very content that makes it distinctively LDS and offensive to classical theism — namely that God the Father himself was once a man and progressed to Godhood (King Follett Discourse). The "ascension only" gloss is a defensible *devotional* minimization but is not what Lorenzo Snow's couplet, in its fuller doctrinal setting, has historically been taken to mean. Likewise, reading "he came down from heaven" as compatible with LDS exaltation ignores that the Creed's *subject* is the eternal, uncreated Son descending — not a being on a trajectory toward Godhood. The clauses are compatible only at the level of surface wording, not underlying metaphysics.
