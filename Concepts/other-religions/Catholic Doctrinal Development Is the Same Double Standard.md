# Catholic Doctrinal Development Is the Same Double Standard (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] argues the LDS Church repeatedly changes doctrine ("they change on everything... it's constantly in flux"), implying Catholicism, with its catechism and tradition, does not.[^1] The Ward Radio panel responds that every Catholic charge applies "on steroids" to Catholic history — and that, unlike Mormonism, Catholicism claims an *infallible*, unchangeable magisterium that nonetheless invented new dogmas.

## The Rebuttal

1. **Papal infallibility itself is a late invention (rated: strong, historical).** The doctrine that the pope speaks infallibly *ex cathedra* on faith and morals was formally defined only at Vatican I (1870); there was no one "claiming to be the infallible leader of the church... in the first centuries." Catholics "create it out of nowhere" and then say "this has been what we believe the whole time. And you say, 'Where?' They say, 'The tradition.'"[^2] The asymmetry is sharp: LDS *do not claim infallibility*, so doctrinal revision is principled (see [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]]); Catholics claim an unchangeable deposit yet demonstrably added dogmas.

2. **The Marian dogmas, purgatory, icon veneration (rated: moderate to strong).** Hansen lists doctrines "invented out of whole cloth by the papacy" that "none of these things existed in the early church" — the Marian dogmas (e.g., the Immaculate Conception, 1854; the Assumption, 1950), purgatory, and icon veneration.[^3] *Steelman:* John Henry Newman's *Essay on the Development of Christian Doctrine* gives Catholicism a sophisticated theory under which these are *organic developments* (implicit-to-explicit) rather than additions; the panel does not engage Newman, and a Catholic would say the LDS "restoration" framing is itself a development theory. The rebuttal's real force is the *double standard*, not a denial that development can be principled.

3. **The death penalty and the burning of heretics (rated: strong, documentary).** The Catechism today calls capital punishment "inadmissible," yet the Church "literally used to burn heretics," and the Council of Florence declared that pagans, Jews, heretics, and schismatics "will go into the eternal fire." That is "a 180-degree reversal" of the kind Heschmeyer accuses Mormonism of.[^4]

4. **Schism and succession (rated: strong, historical).** On the "succession crisis" charge: Catholicism produced the 50,000+ Christian denominations tracing to it, and the Western Schism's *three simultaneous popes* — a succession crisis far exceeding 1844.[^5] See [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]].

5. **Slavery (rated: strong, documentary).** Catholic orders — Jesuits, Franciscans — "ran large estates in Brazil, the Caribbean, and parts of North Africa," worked by enslaved Africans and indigenous people, and the Church "actively through their priesthood participated in the slave trade." The panel's recurring point: "Stick with the authority lane, bro... you don't have to try and defend everything."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "They just invent new doctrines and then they say, 'No, this is what we've always believed.' ... You say, 'Where in the tradition?' They're like, 'In the tradition.' And you're just like, 'What?'"[^2]

> "We don't even have an infallible standard. We're allowed to change. You're not. So the biggest thing in all this that drives me nuts is the double standard."[^4]

> "The moment a Mormon turns around and begins to look into your changing doctrines, they'll be blown away... they literally used to burn heretics."[^4]
