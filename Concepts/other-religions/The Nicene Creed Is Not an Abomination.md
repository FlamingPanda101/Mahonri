# The Nicene Creed Is Not an Abomination

#type/claim

## The Claim
The argument — advanced by the Orthocardia guest with [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] — is that Latter-day Saints have *nothing to disagree with* in the **original Nicene Creed of AD 325** once it is read in its own 4th-century context. The First Vision's rebuke that "all their creeds were an abomination" (Joseph Smith–History 1:19) is *not* aimed at the Nicene text Joseph would never have known, but at the *divisive sectarian dogmatism* of his Protestant New England — the wrangling "creedalism," not the ecumenical creed itself. Read in context, the 325 Creed even encodes the **monarchy of the Father** (subordination of the Son), which *cuts against* later co-equal Western Trinitarianism — so far from an abomination, the Creed of 325 is something a Latter-day Saint can largely *affirm*. The LDS quarrel is with the later *Athanasian* Creed, the *Westminster Confession*, and modern (21st-century) glosses on Nicene language.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Creed" meant something narrower in 1820s English.** Citing [[John W. Welch]], the guest notes that in Joseph Smith's idiom "creed" denoted "any system of principles which are believed and professed" — so the rebuke targets *sectarian, contention-producing* dogmatism, not the ecumenical councils. See [[The Creeds Were an Abomination Meant Sectarian Dogmatism Not the Ecumenical Creeds]]. *Rating: moderate — a defensible lexical/contextual reading; the phrase's reference is genuinely debated.*
2. **The 325 text encodes the monarchy of the Father.** "I believe in one God, the Father almighty" and "God from God... begotten of the Father before all ages" foreground the Father's primacy and the Son's derivation — a *subordinationist* grammar closer to the LDS Godhead than to later co-equal formulations. *Rating: moderate — many patristics scholars agree pre-Constantinopolitan Nicaea is less developed than later Trinitarianism, though "subordination" is contested.*
3. **The hard, distinctively un-LDS bits are *later*.** The Athanasian Creed's "incomprehensible/co-equal/without body parts or passions," and the *Filioque* ("and the Son," added centuries after 325), are post-325 developments — so the *specifically* objectionable creedal content is not in the 325 text. See [[The Nicene Creed Was Left Ambiguous Unlike the Athanasian Creed]] and the vault note that "without body, parts, or passions" is a 1530s Protestant formula, not ancient. *Rating: strong on dating — the Filioque and the Athanasian Creed are demonstrably later.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Critics on *both* sides push back. A traditional Christian notes the maneuver is selective: *homoousios* ("one substance") is the very heart of 325, and the LDS Godhead of three *separate* beings is not obviously compatible with it — the guest "doubles down" by extending *homoousios* to *all humanity*, which is a *re-interpretation*, not an affirmation, of what Nicaea meant. A Latter-day Saint critic notes the move sits awkwardly with the First Vision's plain language and with the vault's own historical line that the creeds were "forged in contention" (see [[St Nicholas Slapped Arius - The Creeds Were Forged in Contention]]) — if the creeds are products of politicized councils, "the 325 text is fine" concedes a lot. A measured reply: the claim is *narrow and contextual*, not a wholesale endorsement — it distinguishes (a) the *act of binding* creeds as tests of fellowship (the "abomination"), from (b) the *propositional content* of the 325 text read in its original subordinationist sense, much of which an LDS reader can accept. The honest core: "the creeds are an abomination" is best read as a critique of *creedalism and sectarian contention*, and the 325 Nicene text, in context, is far less objectionable to Latter-day Saints than the later Athanasian/Westminster developments.

## Best Quotes
> Latter-day Saints have nothing to disagree with in the Nicene Creed of 325 once it is read in its original 4th-century context — the quarrel is with the *later* Athanasian Creed and the modern interpretation, not the 325 text.

## LDS Relevance
This *softens* the standard "creeds = abomination" polemic into a *precise* one: the target is sectarian creedalism and the *later* hardening of Trinitarian dogma, not the 325 Nicene text as such. It coordinates a cluster of vault notes — [[The Creeds Were an Abomination Meant Sectarian Dogmatism Not the Ecumenical Creeds]], [[The Nicene Creed Was Left Ambiguous Unlike the Athanasian Creed]], [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]] — and stands in deliberate tension with the more polemical [[St Nicholas Slapped Arius - The Creeds Were Forged in Contention]], which uses the same history to *relativize* creedal authority. Use it to *narrow and steelman* the LDS position, not to claim the Creed is simply true.
