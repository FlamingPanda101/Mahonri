# The Argument from Silence Cuts Both Ways on Infant Baptism

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] grants [[Gavin Ortlund]]'s observation that the first ~150 years of Christian writing lack *explicit* references to infants being baptized — but argues the argument from silence cannot bear the credobaptist weight, because (a) the silence is explained by the demographics of the early church, (b) the same silence afflicts the *child* baptisms Baptists actually practice, and (c) the [[Didache]]'s adult-shaped baptismal instructions presuppose adult converts without thereby excluding infants ("ought implies can").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Demographic explanation of the silence.** Most apostles died before most Christians were born *into* Christian homes (rather than converting as adults), so early baptismal descriptions naturally address adult converts — the very population being catechized. The absence of infant-baptism descriptions is what you'd expect even if the practice existed.[^2] *(Strong — a real confound the bare silence ignores.)*
2. **"Ought implies can" defuses the Didache fasting rules.** Ortlund notes the Didache prescribes pre-baptismal *fasting*, which infants cannot do, as if baptism were adult-shaped. Trent replies with the moral principle most people grasp intuitively — you are only expected to do what you are able to do — so the Didache's failure to carve out an infant exception no more excludes infants than its silence on dying converts (who also lack a day to fast) excludes deathbed baptism. More tellingly, the Didache *does* prohibit the unbaptized from the Eucharist ("give not what is holy to the dogs") yet contains **no prohibition against baptizing anyone** — there is no class of persons it says cannot be baptized.[^3] *(Strong — turns the Didache from a credobaptist datum into a neutral-or-favorable one.)*
3. **The silence is symmetrical — Baptists' own practice is equally unattested.** [[Doug Wilson]] (a Baptist who came to accept infant baptism) and [[Jordan Cooper]] both note the NT contains no example of the *child* "walk-the-aisle, profess, then get baptized" practice modern Baptists follow either — Wilson's own childhood Christmas-Eve baptism at age 10 "is nothing about that in the New Testament." If silence refutes infant baptism, it equally refutes credobaptist child baptism. And the household-baptism texts (Acts 16) that Baptists invoke for "whole households professed" apply just as well to infants in those households.[^4] *(Strong tu quoque that neutralizes the silence argument.)*

**Steelman of the credobaptist view.** Ortlund's better argument is not bare silence but *positive shape*: the earliest baptismal accounts don't merely omit infants, they consistently describe a sequence of instruction → repentance → profession → baptism that *fits* adult converts and would have to be substantially modified for infants — and no early text shows that modification (no infant-specific rite) until later. The demographic reply explains missing *descriptions*, but a universal apostolic infant-baptism discipline should have left *some* early trace of how infants were incorporated, given how much the sources say about baptismal preparation. Trent's rejoinder is that such traces do appear by the early 3rd century (Hippolytus, Origen, Cyprian) as the *universal* norm — too quickly and unanimously to be a recent novelty — so the trajectory points back to apostolic origin; see [[Tertullian's Opposition Presupposes an Established Practice]].

## Apologetic Engagement (LDS)
The methodological lesson — that arguments from silence are weak and often symmetrical — is one Latter-day Saints deploy constantly (e.g. against "the Book of Mormon mentions X but archaeology is silent"). Here a Latter-day Saint can *agree with Trent's method while declining his conclusion*: the early silence indeed doesn't prove credobaptism, but neither does it establish apostolic *infant* baptism; the LDS read is that what the apostles instituted was believer's baptism by proper authority, and that infant baptism is a later corruption that arose once original-sin anxiety made it seem necessary (Moroni 8). The symmetrical-silence point actually *helps* the Restoration claim that the original pattern was obscured and later altered.

## LDS Relevance
Reinforces a transferable apologetic principle (silence cuts both ways) while showing the patristic baptism debate is genuinely under-determined by the earliest evidence — which is consistent with the LDS narrative of a gradual departure from the apostolic pattern rather than a clean continuity in either direction.
