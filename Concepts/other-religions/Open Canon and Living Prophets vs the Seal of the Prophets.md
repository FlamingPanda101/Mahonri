# Open Canon and Living Prophets vs the Seal of the Prophets

## The Claim

On revelation, canon, and agency, Latter-day Saints and Muslims diverge in structurally opposite directions. The Restoration affirms *continuing* revelation through *living* prophets, an *open* scriptural canon, and robust libertarian free agency; Islam affirms Muhammad as the *seal* (last) of the prophets, the Quran as the *final and unchangeable* revelation, and an ethos of *submission* to God's will. The "same religion" label cannot survive this contrast.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Latter-day Saints: continuing revelation through a living prophetic line
"Latter-day Saints believe in continuing revelation through living prophets. Joseph Smith is seen as a prophet in a continuing line of prophets and his successors also serve as God's messengers. This means that the scriptural canon is open" — new revelation can "expand the body of authoritative texts." See [[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — an open canon and a living-prophet succession are defining LDS commitments.

### 2. Islam: Muhammad as the seal, the Quran as final and closed
"Islam regards Muhammad as the seal of the prophets, signifying that he is the last prophet that received unique divine revelation embodied in the Quran. This is complete and final. No new prophets will come after him. And the Quran is the ultimate unchangeable guide for humanity."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — *khatam an-nabiyyin* (seal of the prophets) and Quranic finality are central Islamic doctrines.

### 3. Agency: libertarian free will vs. submission to the decree
LDS theology "strongly affirms a type of libertarian free will" — individuals "genuinely have the ability to choose otherwise even given all the circumstances," and "God cannot coerce or compel human choices." Islam, by contrast, while affirming human responsibility, "do[es] not delve into the philosophical intricacies of libertarian free will," and the frequent "*insha'Allah* (God willing)" reflects that "things happen according to God's will."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the LDS emphasis on libertarian agency is real; the characterization of Islam as comparatively more deterministic is a fair generalization (Islamic schools vary on *qadar*, which the presenter acknowledges).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can note that "prophets and revelation" *is* genuine shared ground — both traditions reject the cessationist idea that revelation ended with a closed ancient text, both venerate a founding prophet, and both have post-founding authoritative guidance (LDS living prophets; Islamic hadith and the scholarly *ulama*). One could argue the difference is one of degree (how *open* the channel stays) rather than kind. The reply is that the *structural* commitments point opposite ways: an open canon with ongoing prophetic addition is the formal contradictory of a sealed canon with a final prophet, and a theology built on the power to "choose otherwise" sits awkwardly beside one whose signature phrase defers every outcome to the divine decree. Sharing the *category* "prophetic religion" does not make two faiths the same any more than sharing "monotheism" does (cf. [[The Godhead and Tawhid Are Theological Opposites Not Parallels]]).
