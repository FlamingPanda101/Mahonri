# Actual Grace Is a Kick in the Pants Not Regeneration

#type/claim

## The Claim
**[[Trent Horn]]** answers the pastoral argument that converts are visibly regenerate *before* baptism (see [[The Catechumen Is Already Regenerate Before the Water]]) by deploying the Catholic distinction between **actual grace** and **sanctifying grace**. *Actual grace* is God moving a person *toward* seeking sanctifying grace — "a spiritual kick in the pants" (Carl Keating's phrase) — which produces real spiritual movement and even moral change *without regenerating* the person. *Sanctifying grace* is the regenerating life of God, conferred (normatively) at baptism. So the pre-baptismal transformation Ortlund observes is either actual grace drawing the catechumen, or a natural/psychological response to apprehending the gospel's goodness — not yet the new birth. The deeper claim is **epistemic skepticism about reading the invisible from the visible**: there is no reliable one-to-one correspondence between observed behavior and the Spirit's regenerating work, so behavior cannot date regeneration.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two-grace taxonomy (Evidence: theological framework).** Horn distinguishes *actual* grace (God "moving the person towards seeking his sanctifying grace") from *sanctifying* grace (regeneration itself). His own conversion at 17 illustrates actual grace: "spiritually dead, but God moved me towards" seeking baptism — "it doesn't regenerate you but it's a spiritual kick in the pants."[^1] *Strength: supplies a principled category for genuine pre-baptismal God-work that is not regeneration, so the data Ortlund cites need not entail his conclusion.*

2. **Natural/psychological explanation (Evidence: defeater for the fruit-inference).** Apprehending a great good (the gospel) can itself produce a natural change in the virtues — temperance, prudence, fortitude, even a kind of love and faith — by ordinary psychological means. Horn insists these explanations be included because they "account for a lot of other things."[^2] *Strength: shows visible virtue is overdetermined; it does not by itself prove the supernatural new birth.*

3. **Parallel cases of fruit *without* regeneration (Evidence: comparative).** Horn marshals counter-examples both men accept as *not* truly Christian regeneration yet exhibiting striking transformation: Mormons ("super duper nice people"), Buddhists with positive experiences, and converts who later **apostatize** — citing Pew's finding that ~20% of Americans are *former* Christians, many presumably once genuine.[^3] *Strength: if dramatic life-change can attach to false gospels and to people who fall away, change-before-baptism is a weak index of regeneration.*

4. **The mediocre Christian (Evidence: from the other direction).** Horn notes the very common "mediocre Christian" — regenerate, never apostate, but bearing little visible fruit. If regeneration can persist *without* fruit, then fruit and regeneration are not one-to-one, defeating the inference *from* fruit *to* regeneration.[^4] *Strength: breaks the biconditional Ortlund's argument needs.*

5. **Revelation over observation (Evidence: epistemic principle).** "I would say the act of spiritual regeneration is something so foreign to me... an invisible work of the Spirit in the heart of man, that my primary source of knowledge for how that happens must come from divine revelation" — and revelation (Scripture, tradition, magisterium) locates it at baptism. He invokes [[Isaiah 55.8-9|Isaiah 55:8-9]]: "as high as the heavens are above the earth, so are your ways above mine," refusing to let empirical correlation set theology.[^5] *Strength: relocates the question from sociology to revelation, where Horn thinks the regenerationist case is strong.*

**Steelman of the opposing view (Ortlund):** The actual/sanctifying-grace distinction risks becoming *unfalsifiable* — any pre-baptismal evidence of new life can be re-labeled "merely actual grace," so the theory is protected from the data rather than tested by it. Ortlund's reply is that he does *not* mean generic common-grace niceness (he grants Mormons and Buddhists that); he means virtue *cultivated by the Holy Spirit in response to the true triune gospel*, the very thing [[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]] tells us to read as a good tree's fruit, and the very inference Peter himself makes in Acts 10 ("they have received the Holy Spirit"). To call *that* a mere "kick in the pants" is, for Ortlund, to deny in theory what Scripture commands us to recognize in practice. And the apostasy/mediocre-Christian cases cut against a Calvinist's *assurance* but not against the narrower claim that, where the Spirit's gospel-fruit is genuinely present, the person is *already alive* to God. The dispute thus bottoms out in epistemology: Horn privileges revelation over observation and treats the visible as nearly opaque to the invisible; Ortlund treats Spirit-wrought, gospel-specific fruit as a divinely warranted (if fallible) window onto regeneration.

## LDS Relevance
The actual/sanctifying-grace machinery is foreign to LDS theology, which has no Augustinian-Thomistic created-grace taxonomy; the Restoration speaks instead of the *gift of the Holy Ghost* (conferred by laying on of hands *after* baptism) versus the *influence* of the Spirit that can strive with and convert a person beforehand (Moroni 10:4–5; the Spirit testifying to investigators). Functionally this maps onto Horn's distinction: a real, pre-baptismal divine drawing that is *not yet* the full covenant gift. But Horn's epistemic move — "the invisible new birth is so opaque that only the magisterium's revelation can date it" — runs against the LDS confidence that an *individual* may receive a personal, Spirit-borne witness of conversion (a "mighty change of heart," Mosiah 5:2; Alma 5:14) that is genuinely *known* to the convert. Where Horn distrusts the experiential and defers to ecclesial authority, the LDS model trusts personal revelation precisely where Horn does not. See [[Actual Grace Is a Kick in the Pants Not Regeneration|this argument]] alongside [[The Catechumen Is Already Regenerate Before the Water]].
