# The Guardian and Criterion Reply to the Islamic Dilemma

#type/claim

## The Claim
The most common Muslim response on the stream is that the Qur'an is a **guardian (*muhaymin*) and criterion** over the prior scriptures: a Muslim accepts whatever in the Torah/Gospel agrees with the Qur'an and rejects whatever conflicts (e.g. Jesus as Son of God). On this view there is no dilemma, because the Qur'an's affirmation of the Bible was never a blanket endorsement — it was an endorsement *as filtered through the Qur'an*.

> Caller: "Quran is a criterion over the Old Testament and New Testament. Whatever contradicts the Quran in the Bible, we don't have to take... We take only [the part that] does not contradict the Quran."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
**The Muslim case (steelman).**
1. *Muhaymin* in Q 5:48: the Qur'an is sent "confirming the scripture that came before it and as a *muhaymin* over it." Many classical commentators gloss *muhaymin* as guardian/witness/overseer — i.e. the Qur'an *preserves what is true and corrects what is false* in the prior books. That is a textual hook for selective acceptance.
2. The Qur'an explicitly denies specific biblical claims (God has no son; Jesus was not crucified), so the Qur'an clearly does *not* intend to ratify every sentence — which makes a filtering principle the most natural reading.
3. Callers repeatedly insist the *direction of testing* runs Qur'an → Bible: "We judge the Bible based on the Quran. What is truth in the Bible, we judge by the Quran."[^1]

**[[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]]'s rebuttals.**
1. **No verse states the principle.** Avery's central challenge, repeated to several callers: produce *one* verse that says the Qur'an only confirms *part* of the scripture, or that the Bible is to be judged *by* the Qur'an. "There's not one verse in all 114 chapters... where the Qur'an says that it is a criterion over the previous scriptures."[^2] The burden, he argues, is on the one asserting the filter.
2. ***Muhaymin* ≠ judge.** "A guardian is not a judge or decider over what is true... It guards them. It points back to them as the truth. It doesn't disqualify them in any way."[^3] *(This lexical claim is contestable — see the Muslim steelman above; classical tafsir often gives *muhaymin* exactly the corrective/superseding sense Avery denies. The dispute is genuinely about the semantic range of the word.)*
3. **[[Quran 2.85|2:85]] forbids picking and choosing.** This is Avery's strongest counter: 2:85 rebukes those who "believe in part of the scripture and reject the rest," promising them "disgrace in this life" and "the severest punishment." If selective belief is condemned, then the Qur'an affirms *all* of the prior scripture, not a Qur'an-approved subset — which collapses the filtering reply back into horn 1 of [[The Islamic Dilemma]].[^4]
4. **The 2:85 context fight.** The final caller ("S") concedes 2:85 forbids picking and choosing, then retreats to context — the verse rebukes Israelites for killing/expelling each other, so its "do you believe in part?" is a rhetorical reproach, "not a legal injunction." Avery presses that the verse still attaches *punishment* to selective belief, and ends the call when "S" attributes the punishment solely to the social sins.[^5] *(Here the Muslim has a real point about the verse's historical setting — 2:85 is addressed to a specific covenant-breaking generation — even if it does not obviously rescue the filtering principle. Both context and Avery's "all-or-nothing" inference are defensible readings.)*
