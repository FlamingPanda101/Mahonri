# Islam's Reformation Problem and the Literal Quran

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] argues Islam faces a distinctive structural obstacle to liberalizing reform: because the **Quran is the literal, final, uncreated word of God**, a Protestant-style "go back to the sources and strip away superstition" Reformation tends to produce *more* literalism, not less — and the most rigorous "reformers" (e.g. ISIS) read the text most literally. Muslims have nonetheless adapted, either by "Protestantizing" themselves (jihad-as-internal, "get on the right side of history") or by going fundamentalist — but the literal-Quran premise means there is no easy path to a liberal reformation.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Islam's modern trauma forced a recalibration.** Triumphant for most of its history (it "pulverized" the Sasanian Empire and dismembered the Roman East), Islam was shocked from Napoleon's 1798 invasion of Egypt onward to find the "despised Christian powers" dominant; by the 20th century most Muslim lands were under direct or indirect Christian hegemony. This demanded a rethinking of what Islam *is*.[^2] *(Strong — standard account of the colonial-era crisis in Muslim thought.)*
2. **Slavery as the test case.** The Ottoman Sultan was willing to contemplate banning the slave trade — though slavery was legitimized by Muhammad's own practice and mandated in Islamic scripture — *only* because he needed British and French help in the Crimean War, and he set his scholars to "work out a way" to square Islamic law with the new norm. Adaptation came under external pressure and required juristic ingenuity precisely because the scriptural sanction was explicit. See [[Christianity Has a Concept of the Secular Islam Does Not]].[^3] *(Concrete, documented example of the reform difficulty.)*
3. **Two adaptive strategies, both "Protestant."** (a) *Liberalizing*: "protestantize" — relocate religion to the heart, read "go crucify offenders" as "try to be kind to them," treat jihad as the internal struggle; (b) *Fundamentalist*: the mirror-image Protestant move of "interpret the scriptures literally — what does the Quran say? It's inherent, that's what we'll do." Holland notes neither classical Christians nor Muslims historically read scripture in a "brutally literal way"; literalism itself is a modern (originally Protestant, anti-Darwin) reflex now adopted by some Muslims, "and the consequence has been a kind of very brutal understanding of Islam," because the Quran, hadith, and life of Muhammad contain material that, read flatly, "sanctions quite a lot of bloodshed."[^4] *(A sharp, even-handed analysis — applies the same literalism critique to fundamentalist Protestants and fundamentalist Muslims alike.)*
4. **The reformation-is-structurally-hard point.** "You can't have a Reformation if this is the literal word of God and the last word of God — this is it." A Christian Reformation could reinterpret because Christians "have a very sophisticated, almost poetic understanding" of scripture and no single God-given legal corpus; the Quran's status as the verbatim final revelation closes off that move. ISIS-style ideologues *are* reformers in the back-to-the-sources sense — which is why they reject international law and human rights and re-endorse slavery "because it's in the Quran."[^5] *(The crux claim; genuinely illuminating about why "Islamic reformation" rhetoric misfires.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Muslim reformers and many scholars reply that Islam has a *rich* internal tradition of contextual interpretation (*maqasid al-sharia*, the objectives-of-the-law jurisprudence; centuries of *ijtihad*; the Mu'tazila's "created Quran" position) that licenses non-literal, purpose-driven readings without denying the text's divine status — so a liberalizing reform is structurally available, just contested. Holland concedes Muslims *have* adapted repeatedly and forecasts further accommodation; his claim is about *relative difficulty and friction*, not impossibility. The honest middle: the verbatim-revelation premise raises the cost of liberal reinterpretation compared to Christianity, but "no reformation possible" overstates it — the live question is which interpretive tradition (objectives-based vs. literalist) prevails under modern pressures.

## LDS Relevance
The contrast sharpens an LDS strength: continuing revelation. Because Latter-day Saints hold scripture is not the *final* word — the canon is open and a living prophet can extend or clarify doctrine (Articles of Faith 9; the 1978 revelation on priesthood; the 1890 Manifesto ending plural marriage) — the Church possesses exactly the adaptive mechanism Holland says a closed-canon, literal-final-revelation faith lacks. Where Islam must choose between literalism and unauthorized reinterpretation, the Restoration claims an *authorized* channel for development. This connects to the broader apologetic that a living magisterium/prophetic office resolves what textual literalism cannot.
