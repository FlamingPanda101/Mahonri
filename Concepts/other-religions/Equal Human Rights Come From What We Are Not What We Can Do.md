# Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] argues that the right to life is grounded in *what we are* — a human organism, a member of the human (rational) kind — and **not** in *what we can do*, such as think, feel pain, or survive on our own. Because functional abilities come in degrees and are unequally distributed, grounding rights in them yields *unequal* rights; only our shared biological human nature is genuinely equal among all of us, so only it can ground *equal* rights. This is the personhood core of the SLED-style pro-life case (Size, Level of development, Environment, Degree of dependency are all morally irrelevant), deployed here against the demand that the unborn "earn" personhood by some capacity.[^1]

The argument works by testing each proposed criterion of personhood to destruction:[^2]
- **Pain** → then humans who *can't* feel pain (some disabled people) aren't persons, and *any* animal that feels pain *is* one ("if you ate a slice of pepperoni pizza, you'd be an accomplice to murder").
- **Rational thought** → newborn infants can't do it either, so you'd have to grant a squirrel the same right to life as a baby (or deny the baby's).
- **Viability** → snakes and squirrels survive outside a uterus, so it tracks nothing about personhood; and "if Martians kidnapped us and put us on Mars where we're not viable," that wouldn't license killing us.

Horn's positive ground: "a newborn baby has more rights than a rat or a cow... not because it can think at a certain level — it can't think better than those animals — but because it's a member of our human community."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The parity arguments are internally consistent (rate: strong).** Each criterion is refuted by the same move — find a *born* human who lacks the trait, or a *non-human* who has it — exposing that the criterion either over-includes animals or under-includes vulnerable humans. To keep newborns, the disabled, and the comatose inside the moral community, one must locate value in *kind-membership*, which the unborn also possess. The infant-vs-rat test is the sharpest form: it forces the interlocutor to name what an infant has that a smarter animal lacks.[^2]
2. **It dovetails with the "human organism" datum (rate: strong, factual).** The argument presupposes the unborn *is* a human organism (not a blueprint or a clump), which Horn defends separately — see [[The Unborn Is a Human Organism Not a Blueprint]]. Granted that, "younger / smaller / more dependent" are exactly the SLED differences that are conceded to be irrelevant in all *born* cases.
3. **The burning-building / embryo-lab rejoinder (rate: a real limit on the opponent's reductio).** [[Johnny (Atheist Analysis Debater)|Johnny]] presses the famous case: in a fire, you'd save one child over five frozen embryos, *proving* (he says) we don't really equate them. Horn's reply is the load-bearing distinction: rescue-triage shows only that *we sometimes don't choose to save* certain humans — not that *we may kill them*. "If my wife was in that burning lab and there were five strangers, I'm going to save my wife. Does that mean I'd go slit the strangers' throats? Absolutely not." He inverts it: many would save five *pregnant* women over five non-pregnant women, sensing the "little human beings inside."[^4]

### Steelman of [[Johnny (Atheist Analysis Debater)|Johnny]]'s reply

Johnny does not concede. His fair points: (a) the connection from "is a human organism" to "has the right to life" is exactly what's in dispute — Horn *asserts* kind-membership grounds rights but (Johnny says) hasn't *shown* the "intrinsic connection," so the criterion-elimination merely relocates the question rather than answering it; (b) our differential treatment of animals and humans may itself be a *conferred* status rather than an intrinsic one (his consensus account — see [[Personhood by Majority Vote Is a Dangerous Criterion]]); (c) the burning-building intuition still shows the *felt* moral weight of an embryo is far lower than a child's, which a theory of strictly *equal* worth must explain away. Horn's rejoinder is that intuitions of *attachment* are unreliable guides to *worth* — "150 years ago people didn't have emotional attachments to African slaves; that didn't prove they weren't human."[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Our human rights don't come from what we can do, such as think or survive on our own. They come from what we are. And equal human rights can only be grounded in the one thing that is equal about all of us — our biological human nature, not our fleeting functional abilities." — Horn[^1]

> "Unless you're willing to say newborn babies have the same right to live as a squirrel — or basically no right to live — you can't use rational thought as a standard for personhood." — Horn[^2]

> "A newborn baby has more rights than a rat or a cow... not because it can think at a certain level — it can't think better than those animals — but because it's a member of our human community." — Horn[^3]
