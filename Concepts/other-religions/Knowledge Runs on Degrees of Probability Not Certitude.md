# Knowledge Runs on Degrees of Probability Not Certitude

## The Point
Almost everything human beings hold to be true is known only to **varying degrees of probability**, not to "epidictic certitude" (incorrigible, demonstrative certainty). Only a tiny class of things — "certain mathematical givens" — are known with that kind of certainty. This is offered by [[Robert Barron|Bishop Robert Barron]] (and adopted by [[Jacob Hansen]]) as an authority-witness that converges on the LDS "collective witness" / confidence-spectrum model of knowledge: religion is not "uniquely unsure," because *all* of our knowledge falls under the same probabilistic heading.

## Argument
1. There is a "handful" of things we know with epidictic certitude — e.g. certain mathematical givens. [^1]
2. The "extraordinary majority" of what we hold true is held instead "on the basis of degrees of probability." [^1]
3. This includes **all the sciences** and **all of history**: consider how much we "know" only because of a tradition we ourselves have not verified. [^1]
4. Worked example: how sure are you that Julius Caesar was killed on the Ides of March in 44 BC? Only "really the handful of sources we have from that ancient time, all of which could be deceptive," carried by a tradition that "might have changed the stories." [^2]
5. Therefore religion is not "uniquely unsure" — "the whole range of what we know really falls under that kind of heading." Treating religion as singularly uncertain (vs. clear, certain science) is a category mistake. [^2]
6. **Steelman of the objector.** A critic might say this over-levels the field: scientific claims are *intersubjectively testable and repeatable* in a way that the Ides-of-March or a religious witness is not, so "degrees of probability" still leaves science far more warranted than revelation, and the move risks special pleading for faith. Barron's reply (developed in [[The Clarity of Science vs Obscurity of Religion Is a False Dichotomy]]) is that the sharp clarity/obscurity line is itself an Enlightenment fantasy — even our most intimate and important knowledge (e.g. a spouse's deepest convictions) lives in that same probabilistic "twilight."

## Best Quotes

> "Practically everything we believe is to varying degrees of probability rather than epidictic certitude. There's a handful of things that we know with epidictic certitude — certain mathematical givens, etc. — but the extraordinary majority of those things that we hold to be true, we do so on the basis of degrees of probability, and that includes all the sciences." [^1]

> "How sure are you that Julius Caesar really was killed on the Ides of March in 44 BC? Well, yeah — based on really the handful of sources we have from that ancient time, all of which could be deceptive, etc., that were carried by a tradition that might have changed the stories." [^2]

> "Once you start down that path, everything we believe, everything we know, is always known to varying degrees of probability… so in a way it's trying to put religion in this 'oh, it's uniquely unsure' sort of thing — well, the whole range of what we know really falls under that kind of heading." [^2]
