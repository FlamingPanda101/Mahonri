# Name the True Church or Concede the Apostasy - the Negative's Burden

## The Claim

[[Jacob Hansen]]'s structural framing of the whole debate. An institutional apostasy *just is* "the lack of a true church on earth" — the absence of a continuously-existing institution holding the ultimate authority Peter held. So the burden cuts against Heschmeyer: "You have to point to an institution that exists, that has existed continually going all the way back to Peter and the apostles, that holds his same ultimate authority." If no such institution can be named, an institutional apostasy follows by default. Hence Hansen's recurring demand: *"point me to the true church."* He insists this is **not** a dodge of the apostasy question but its very logic — and that it forces Heschmeyer to defend the papacy whether he wants to or not, because the papacy is the only candidate institution claiming Petrine universal jurisdiction with continuous existence. [^1]

In cross-examination Hansen sharpens the point into a trap: he gets Heschmeyer to affirm that without the papacy one lacks "the fullness of the church," then argues this is *functionally identical* to the LDS claim that the fullness was lost and needed restoring. He also presses Heschmeyer to say *when* the Catholic Church began — extracting the answers "1965" (Vatican II) and "1870" (Vatican I's definition of papal infallibility), since infallible universal jurisdiction is "essential to the character of the Catholic Church" yet was only dogmatically defined then. The implied dilemma: either the essential institution is recent (so there *was* a gap), or the Catholic must locate continuous Petrine universal jurisdiction in the early centuries, which Hansen denies anyone can do. [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The definitional move is internally tight (rating: strong; granting the definition)
*If* one accepts Hansen's stipulated definition — apostasy = loss of the continuous institution holding Peter's universal jurisdiction — then the negation genuinely does require naming such an institution. The argument is valid given its premise; the contest is over the premise. See [[Roman Primacy Was a Later Claim Not First-Century]] for the historical case that no such universal-jurisdiction claim is early. [^1]

### 2. The "fullness of the church" concession (rating: strong rhetorically; Heschmeyer's own words)
Heschmeyer concedes that absent the papacy "you wouldn't have the fullness of the church," and that even valid Orthodox churches lack that fullness. Hansen pounces: that is the same shape as "the fullness was lost" — the LDS thesis stated in Catholic vocabulary. [^3]

### 3. The dating squeeze (rating: moderate; cross-examination pressure)
By tying the Catholic Church's *essence* to dogmas defined in 1870/1965, Hansen makes Heschmeyer choose between conceding institutional discontinuity and conceding that the essential papal claims are not demonstrably first-century. [^2]

### 4. It reframes who must prove what (rating: moderate; debate strategy)
The move lets Hansen avoid arguing the apostasy *from scripture* (he concedes he is "not making an argument for the apostasy from scripture... I'm making the argument from the plain history") and instead win by default if the continuous institution cannot be produced. See [[The Institutional Church and the Body of Believers Are Two Senses of One Word]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the Catholic reply ([[Joe Heschmeyer]])
Heschmeyer makes three counter-moves. **(a) Wrong debate.** Universal jurisdiction is a *papacy* question; one could be wrong about it and still affirm the church survived on earth — an Orthodox believer rejects the papacy yet rejects a Great Apostasy, so naming-the-papacy is neither necessary nor sufficient for Hansen's case. **(b) Institutions develop without restarting.** Dating "the Catholic Church" to 1870 because a dogma was defined then is like dating the United States to 2020 because the country changed; there is "institutional integrity back to 1776." A church can change teaching and even behavior and remain the same institution. **(c) Positive disproof suffices.** As the negative, he need not *locate* the true church to *disprove* the apostasy: if Daniel 2 and the indefectibility texts show the kingdom never fell, the apostasy is false regardless of which body is the true church — "if you want to approach me afterwards, I can give you some hints." See [[Daniel 2's Stone Kingdom Dates Indefectibility to the Roman Empire]]. [^5]

### Steelman of Hansen's counter to that reply
The "wrong debate" reply only works if "the fullness of the institutional church" can be preserved *without* the office Hansen targets — but Heschmeyer himself ties the fullness to the papacy, so by his own lights its loss would be an institutional apostasy by Hansen's definition. And "disproving without locating" still leaves the audience seeker — who wants to *join* the true church — with nowhere to go, which Hansen presents as the existential cash-value of the question.

## Best Quotes

> "If you don't have a continuous existence of a church on earth that goes all the way back to the apostles, then you have an institutional apostasy as defined by the church." — Hansen [^1]

> "You wouldn't have the fullness of the church without the papacy — which to me sounds like essentially what Latter-day Saints are claiming when we claim the fullness of the church was lost, but there still was a body of believers. How is that different from what I'm saying?" — Hansen [^3]

> "You're posing a counterfactual where I'm not a Catholic... I'm not intending to solve that second problem for you tonight. If you want to approach me afterwards, I can give you some hints." — Heschmeyer [^5]
