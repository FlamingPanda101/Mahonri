# Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea

## The Claim
Asked where the line falls between legitimate polemics and anti-Mormonism, [[Kyle Beshears]] gives a clean criterion: you cross the threshold "when you've left ideas and you've attacked the people and the person." Disagreeing with LDS doctrine is not anti-Mormon; disparaging Latter-day Saints as people is. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Doctrinal disagreement is not anti-Mormonism.** "It's not anti-Mormon for an evangelical to say I don't believe Joseph Smith was a prophet or I don't believe the Book of Mormon is scripture. Like those are just basic statements of facts about evangelicalism." [^1] *Rating: strong and fair-minded;* it protects honest disagreement from being labeled bigotry, defusing a common conversational trap.

2. **The threshold is denigrating texts and persons.** "It is anti-Mormon to denigrate those texts and the people... when you stopped engaging with the ideas or the history and you've started to just disparage the person." [^2] *Rating: strong;* it gives a usable, content-neutral diagnostic that both sides can apply to themselves.

3. **Polemics is legitimate when it keeps dignity.** Beshears affirms "I think if you're going to do polemics, which I think is a legitimate mode... but you have to have the dignity and the respect and some modicum of trust with your interlocutor or else what are you doing?" [^3] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it rescues robust disagreement from the false binary of either silence or contempt.

4. **It applies symmetrically.** Beshears insists the same standard binds Latter-day Saints toward evangelicals — "I see it on my side... and I've been attacked with it from y'all's side. And I think both of us need to just knock it off." [^4] *Rating: strong;* the symmetry is what makes it a principle rather than a partisan cudgel.

**Steelman of the harder-edged polemicist.** One could object that some ideas are so harmful that vigorously attacking the *movement* (not merely the proposition) is warranted, and that the person/idea line can be exploited to shield bad ideas from forceful critique. Beshears's reply is built into the criterion: forceful critique of *ideas and history* is fully permitted; only the slide into personal disparagement is ruled out. The line targets contempt, not intensity.
