# Islamic Violence Is Mostly Political Not Quranic

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Because Islam is "culturally associated with violence and terrorism," many assume Muhammad and the Quran are the *source* of modern Islamic terrorism — "guilty by association."[^1] Peterson grants the texts can be read to justify violence, but argues the dominant drivers of contemporary terrorism are political and desperate, not religious-doctrinal.

## The Rebuttal
1. **The texts can be read for violence — and Muhammad did become a warlord.** Peterson is candid: people who want violence "find passages in the Quran to justify their violence, and they're definitely there." After the *Hijra* (622) Muhammad became a political leader, and wars followed until his death (632); critics can point to him as a model warlord-prophet.[^2] *(Strong — the Medinan period and the difficulty of separating Islamic "church and state" are real, because Jesus held no political office but Muhammad did.)* This is the honest concession that makes the rebuttal credible.
2. **Empirical studies tie suicide terrorism to politics, not piety.** Peterson cites Robert Pape's book *Dying to Win*, which analyzed all then-available suicide-bombing cases and concluded the phenomenon "has almost nothing to do with the religion; it has to do with politics" — a desperate tactic of people who feel powerless.[^3] *(Strong — accurately represents Pape's central thesis on the strategic, occupation-driven logic of suicide terrorism.)* See [[Robert Pape]].
3. **Suicide bombing was not invented by Muslims.** It was pioneered by Marxist **Tamil Tigers** (Hindus) in Sri Lanka; "Muslims caught on fairly quickly that it was an effective thing," but the tactic's origin is secular-nationalist.[^4] *(Strong — the LTTE's pioneering, systematic use of suicide attacks is well documented.)* Analysis of Muslim bombers' own statements often shows non-religious motives.[^3]
4. **Steelman.** A critic can object that the existence of genuinely violent Quranic passages and a warlord founder means religion is at least a *contributing* and *legitimating* cause, even if politics is the trigger — and that Pape's thesis is itself contested by scholars who find ideology more salient. Peterson does not claim religion is *irrelevant*; he claims it is not the *primary* or *originating* cause, and that equating ordinary Muslims with terrorists is empirically and morally unjust — citing Egyptians who disavow Hamas and American Muslims who reject violence.[^5]
