# The Humanist-Reformation Apostasy Narrative Is Not Historically Supported

## The Point
Combs argues that the familiar "great apostasy" story — a pure primitive Church corrupted into a thousand-year "Dark Age" before being restored — is a *synthesized* product of fifteenth/sixteenth-century humanist periodization grafted onto Reformation polemic. It is not, he says, "supported by historical evidence." The model wrongly assumes time can be carved into hermetically sealed epochs in which one period contributes nothing to the next.

## Evidence
1. **The narrative's manufactured origin.** Renaissance humanists invented the tripartite scheme (classical / Dark Ages / modern). Protestant Reformers then adapted this periodization "for their own critique of the Roman Catholic Church," arguing the Church had fallen into darkness and needed restoration. Catholics responded in kind, casting the Reformers themselves as the origin of a great apostasy. [^1]
2. **Eusebian model repurposed.** Where Eusebius had narrated apostasy only as individual/small-group heresies overcome by a triumphant Church, the humanist/Reformation writers expanded the concept to cover whole *periods of time* — "triumph over apostate people but also over apostate periods of time." [^2]
3. **Proof-texting 2 Thessalonians.** Apologists turned to [[2 Thessalonians 2.3|2 Thessalonians 2:3]] ("except there come a falling away [Greek *apostasia*] first") for biblical warrant. The Presbyterian commentator Albert Barnes (whom Combs notes was "born in 1830") summarized that most Protestant commentators referred the passage "to the great apostasy under the Papacy," identifying the "man of sin" with the Pope. [^3]
4. **The scheme ignores continuity.** The narrative "ignores Christian teachings, art, and practices that arose in the medieval period and remain influential on Christianity today — including on Latter-day Saints," and it ignores evidence that "good Christians continually served each other and worshiped God throughout the history of Christianity." [^4]
5. **No evidence of mass corruption.** "While it is possible that there were some Christians who were corrupt or who wrote with ulterior motives, the idea of mass corruption and a unified effort … to reshape the earliest teachings of Christianity into something foreign and false is not supported by the extant ancient records." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Historians today recognize that the synthesized humanist/Reformation narrative of a dark age of apostasy, born in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, is not supported by historical evidence." [^4]

> "Most Protestant commentators have referred it to the great apostasy under the Papacy, and by the 'man of sin,' they suppose there is allusion to the Roman Pontiff, the Pope." (Albert Barnes, quoted) [^3]

## LDS Connection
Combs stresses that early Latter-day Saints "inherited from Protestants a great apostasy narrative," some even repeating the Protestant identification of the Pope as the "man of sin." Twentieth-century General Authorities (McConkie's revised *Mormon Doctrine*; Dallin H. Oaks) explicitly disavowed the anti-Catholic reading of 1 Nephi 14:10, treating the "church of the devil" as "any philosophy or organization that opposes belief in God" rather than a specific denomination. See [[Great Apostasy Is Not a Scriptural Term in LDS Standard Works]].
