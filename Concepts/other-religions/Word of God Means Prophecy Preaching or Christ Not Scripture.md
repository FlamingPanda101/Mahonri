# Word of God Means Prophecy, Preaching, or Christ — Not Scripture

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]], drawing on scholar [[John C. Poirier]], argues the biblical phrase **"word of God" / "word of the Lord"** has a stable, threefold-plus referent: (1) prophecy, (2) the apostolic preaching, (3) God's specific spoken utterances in salvation history, and (4) Christ himself. Applying it to the written Bible is "wholly out of keeping with how scripture itself uses that term."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Prophecy: "the word of the Lord came to…" — rate: strong
Poirier notes the convention's first sense is prophetic: "the word of the Lord came to Elijah the Tishbite saying…"[^1] The phrase introduces a divine *oracle* delivered to a prophet — a speech-act, not a document. This matches the OT data Horn catalogs at [[No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God]] (Nathan, Shemaiah, John the Baptist).

### 2. The apostolic preaching / the gospel — rate: strong
The second sense is the apostles' proclamation. [[1 Thessalonians 2.13|1 Thess 2:13]] is the paradigm: the *spoken* gospel received as God's word.[^2] Horn ties this to the patristic equation of "word of God" with the *charisma* / the gospel — a message about the faith, not a text. See [[Calling Scripture the Word of God Is a Late Patristic Development]].

### 3. God's salvation-history utterances and Christ himself — rate: strong
"Word of God" also names specific things God *spoke* (e.g. the Ten Commandments recited to Moses) and, climactically, the **Word made flesh** ([[John 1.14|John 1:14]]; cf. [[Revelation 12.4|Rev]] 19:13, where the rider's name "is called the Word of God").[^3] The Catechism is invoked: "through all the words of sacred scripture, God speaks only one single word, his one utterance… namely Jesus himself, the word made flesh."[^4] So even where Scripture conveys the word of God, the *Word* is ultimately Christ, not the ink.

### 4. Poirier's summary and the honest limit — rate: strong, scholar-anchored
Poirier: "The problem with referring to scripture as the word of God… is that that convention is wholly out of keeping with how scripture itself uses that term."[^1] **Steelman of the Protestant:** word-concepts can extend by metonymy — if the gospel preached is the word of God, and that same gospel is later inscripturated, the written form may legitimately inherit the title (Hebrews 4:12 arguably does this). Horn's reply is that the NT writers never actually make that extension explicit; where they seem to (Rom 3:2; Heb 4:12), the Greek or the context points elsewhere. See [[Romans 3.2 Says Oracles Logia Not Words Logos]].

## Best Quotes
> "The problem with referring to scripture as the word of God, of course, is that that convention is wholly out of keeping with how scripture itself uses that term." — John C. Poirier, as quoted[^1]

> "Scripture uses word of God or word of the Lord in two ways. As a reference to prophecy… or as a reference to the apostles' preaching. The phrase word of God never appears as a reference to scripture itself." — Poirier, as quoted[^1]

> "Through all the words of sacred scripture, God speaks only one single word, his one utterance, in whom he expresses himself completely, namely Jesus himself, the word made flesh." — Catechism, as quoted[^4]
