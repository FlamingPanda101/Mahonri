# Read the Primary Sources Before Judging a Person

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] argues that an honest historical judgment of Joseph Smith requires reading what Joseph *himself* wrote — his journals and personal correspondence — rather than relying only on what others said about him. He applied this rule deliberately: he spent six months reading "all of his journals and then every single document that he wrote that was correspondence" before drafting his chapter on Joseph. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The golden-rule test he set for himself.** Beshears asked: "If somebody in the future were writing about me and they were only taking other people's word for it, how would I feel about that? And I don't think I like that. But if I had left thousands of papers, journals, correspondence, documents that I had written, wouldn't I want a person to take that into deep consideration? The answer is yes." [^3] *Rating: strong as method;* it is the same do-unto-others principle that governs his whole approach (see [[Love of Neighbor Requires Understanding]]).

2. **He read across the spectrum, then went to the primary corpus.** He read [[Fawn Brodie]], [[Richard Bushman]], [[John Turner]]'s recent biography, and [[Dan Vogel]] — "whether you're a friend or a critic of the church" — and *then* turned to the Joseph Smith Papers, owning "every single volume." [^4][^2] *Rating: strong;* breadth of secondary reading plus primary immersion is exactly the standard academic historians ask for.

3. **The payoff was a more complicated, less cartoonish figure.** The primary-source immersion is what produced his "extremely complicated man" conclusion and the pastoral Nauvoo portrait — precisely the data the enemy-affidavit tradition omits. [^5] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it demonstrates the method's fruit (cf. [[Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud]]).

**Steelman of the skeptic.** A critic can grant the value of primary sources while warning that an author's own journals are self-serving curation — Joseph had every incentive to present himself sympathetically, so reading "his side" risks being charmed by a practiced self-presentation. The corrective is not to ignore the papers but to triangulate them against hostile and neutral sources alike — which Beshears claims to have done by reading Brodie, Vogel, Bushman, and Turner first.
