# Christianity Has a Concept of the Secular Islam Does Not

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] argues that the deepest, least-noticed Christian inheritance of the West is a **concept of the secular** — a neutral civic space distinct from religion — and that Islam structurally lacks it. This is why a confident secular liberalism keeps misreading Islam as "just another religion" it can absorb, and why Holland calls Islam "uniquely indigestible for a secular mindset." The secular is not a culture-neutral default that all peoples share; it is a specific, contingent Christian product that the West mistakes for the universal background of human life.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The genealogy of the secular: render unto Caesar → Augustine's two cities.** Holland traces the idea to Jesus's "render unto Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and unto God the things that are God's" ([[Matthew 22.21|Matt 22:21]]), institutionalized by [[Augustine]] (writing as Rome fell) into the doctrine of the *saeculum* — the doomed-to-pass span of fallen earthly order, set against the eternal *religio* of the city of God. Over the Middle Ages and into modernity this "two rival orders" idea mutates into a *neutral space* called "the secular." Hence the West has "church and state," never "synagogue and state" or "mosque and state."[^2] *(Strong — a credentialed historian's genealogy; the specifically Christian provenance of the church/state distinction is widely accepted.)*
2. **Islam is totalizing — God is manifested in everything you do.** Classically, Islam is "a totalizing way of leading your life": God-given law (*sharia*) governs every aspect of existence, so the notion of a value-neutral "safe space" where everyone meets as fellow citizens is alien. Muslims in the West are pressed into a "Procrustean bed" — forced to conceptualize what they belong to as a *religion* (a Christian category) when, classically, Islam is "much more than a religion."[^3] *(Strong as a description of classical Islamic political theology; Holland himself flags that individuals can and do separate the two in practice.)*
3. **Judaism had to be re-templated too.** The same pattern: post-French-Revolution Jews were told they could have "freedom of religion," but Jews "didn't have religion — they were a people"; to become French citizens they had to reconceive their tradition as "Judaism, a religion." The secular template is so dominant that even traditions with no native concept of "religion" must retrofit themselves to it.[^4] *(Supports the thesis that "religion-as-a-separable-sphere" is a Christian-secular construct, not a human universal.)*
4. **The arrogance of the secular default.** Holland's sharpest point: secular liberals assume "everyone has a sense of what the secular is" the way everyone knows what a tree or a dog is — but they don't. The belief that secular civilization is "such a broad tent that everyone can be brought into it" is itself a culturally specific (and arrogant) assumption, not a neutral fact. Islam is "at least as sophisticated a civilization" and "for most of its existence much more powerful," so there is no given that it should accommodate the liberal template.[^5] *(A genuinely challenging argument that cuts against a core liberal-secular self-image.)*

**Steelman of the opposing (secular-liberal) view.** A defender of liberal secularism replies that the secular *is* effectively universal-izable: plenty of majority-Muslim societies (Turkey under Atatürk, Indonesia, Tunisia) and millions of individual Muslims do operate a functional church/state distinction, so the "structural impossibility" is overstated — it is a contingent theological emphasis, not an iron law. Holland half-concedes this ("theologically speaking" Islam resists it, but individuals manage it) and even forecasts that Islam, like Judaism, will likely accommodate the host society over time. The disagreement is thus about *degree of friction*, not absolute incompatibility — but Holland's point stands that the friction is real, asymmetric, and routinely denied by secular optimists who treat their own framework as the neutral human baseline.

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration is itself a *post-secular* movement that complicates Holland's neat binary: it claims a totalizing covenant life (temple, law of consecration, a kingdom-of-God ecclesiology) within a fully modern, religiously plural, church-and-state republic — Latter-day Saints live the "two orders" tension Holland describes rather than dissolving it. More directly, Holland's thesis is a powerful apologetic resource: it shows the secular frame in which critics demand the Church "stay in its lane" is not a neutral arbiter but a particular Christian inheritance — so the believer need not concede that the secular public square is theologically empty ground. Compare the borrowed-capital argument in [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]].
