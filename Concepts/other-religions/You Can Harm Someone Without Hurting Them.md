# You Can Harm Someone Without Hurting Them

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] attacks the premise underneath the consciousness criterion: the student's view that "something is only a moral question if it affects someone's well-being," where well-being is glossed as the felt experience of pain or pleasure. Horn calls this "an overly truncated view of morality." His counter-thesis: you can **harm** someone — make him *worse off*, deprive him of a good he has a right to — *without* **hurting** him (causing any conscious pain). Because harm and felt pain come apart, the absence of a fetus's pain does not entail the absence of harm to the fetus, and the consciousness-grounds-all-morality move collapses.[^1]

His worked examples of painless harm:[^1]
- **The stolen gift card.** A classmate bragged about stealing a gift card off the present table at a rich girl's birthday, justifying it because "she'd never miss it and had plenty of money." He was right that she'd feel no pain over it — yet it is still theft and still wrong.
- **The unknown inheritance.** Defrauding someone of an inheritance they never knew existed: they feel no pain, but they are harmed by being "deprived of something they have a right to."
- **The deprivation of a future life.** "Just as aborting an unborn child may not hurt the child, but it deprives him of the life he has a right to." This is the **deprivation account** of the wrongness of killing: what is taken is the entire future the victim would have had, whether or not the victim is currently aware of it.

The structure is a counterexample-to-a-definition: the student offered "harm = damage to felt well-being" as if it were exhaustive; Horn produces cases everyone judges wrong where *no* felt well-being is touched, refuting the identity. Morality therefore includes *deprivation of rightful goods*, not merely *infliction of suffering*. See [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The intuitions are widely shared (rate: strong).** Almost everyone agrees the gift-card theft and the secret-inheritance fraud are wrong, *and* agrees the victims feel no pain. That combination is exactly what a pure well-being-as-felt-experience theory cannot accommodate, so the examples do real work against the *identity* of harm with felt suffering — paralleling the vault's [[The Knockdown Argument - Well-Being Is Not Identical to Moral Goodness]] against Harris.[^1]
2. **It generalizes beyond a single case (rate: strong).** Horn stresses "there's many other examples I can present" — theft, fraud, posthumous defamation, broken promises to the dead. The point is not one clever case but a *whole class* of painless wrongs, which makes the truncation charge against the consciousness criterion structural rather than anecdotal.[^1]
3. **It plugs directly into the deprivation account of killing's wrongness (rate: strong as analysis).** Marquis-style "future-like-ours" reasoning is the natural home of the abortion application: killing is wrong primarily because it deprives the victim of a valuable future, a deprivation that obtains independent of present consciousness. Horn's three examples build a ladder from trivial property up to the deprivation of an entire life. See [[The Brainless Organ-Farm Reductio Against the Consciousness Criterion]].

### Steelman of the student (Kai Bevon)

The student has resources to resist. (a) He can bite the bullet on the *grounding*: the gift-card and inheritance cases may still reduce to well-being once you widen the lens — they erode *trust*, set precedents that make *others* fearful, or frustrate *preferences* the victim actually has (even unfelt ones), so a sophisticated preference- or rule-utilitarian needn't concede a non-well-being harm. (b) He can deny the parity: a birthday-party victim is an *existing person with standing interests and rights*, whereas (on his view) a non-conscious embryo has no interests *yet* to be frustrated — "that zygote is not capable of suffering as far as we know" — so "deprivation of a future" presupposes a present subject who *has* that future as his, which is the very point in dispute. (c) The "right to the life" premise is doing the heavy lifting, and the student would say Horn has not *shown* the embryo has that right; he has illustrated that *rights-holders* can be harmed painlessly, then assumed the embryo is a rights-holder. So the examples refute the crudest version of his principle while leaving the refined version — harm = frustration of the interests of a *subject who has interests* — standing.[^2]
