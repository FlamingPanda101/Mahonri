# The Creeds Were an Abomination Meant Sectarian Dogmatism Not the Ecumenical Creeds

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common LDS reading (and the foil here) is that when Christ told Joseph Smith in the First Vision that "their creeds were an abomination," he meant the **ecumenical creeds** of Christendom — Nicaea, the Athanasian Creed, etc. — and that the Great Apostasy is located at the Council of Nicaea, where bishops "voted on the nature of God." The co-host states he had grown up believing exactly this: "their creeds were an abomination was actually the creeds that Jesus Christ was taking issue with the actual ... creeds. All of these councils that had been done to corrupt the doctrine of who he actually was." [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The 1820s lexical argument (the central move)
Citing **John W. Welch** ("Jack Welch ... the guy that discovered chiasmus"), the guest argues that in early-19th-century American English "the word creed ... referred rather to any system of principles which are believed and professed." [^2] On Welch's reading of Webster's 1828 dictionary, "creed" in Joseph Smith's culture did *not* denote the ecumenical creeds. **Rating: lexically plausible but selective** — Webster's 1828 does list a broad "summary of belief" sense, yet it also explicitly references "the creed of the apostles, Nicene creed," so the term was not innocent of the technical meaning even in 1828.

### 2. The biographical-context argument (moderate)
"Joseph Smith was a product of Northeastern American Protestantism," and had Christ used the word "creed," Joseph "would have understood it as the rigid sectarian dogmatism of Protestant New England ... not ... ecumenical creed, because there was very little Catholic or Orthodox influence. He didn't know what those were." [^3] So the "abomination" is *denominational division* — "the divisive denominational systems of Christendom of denominational divorce." [^4] **Rating: reasonable as cultural background, but speculative about Joseph's mental lexicon.**

### 3. The strategic payoff
This reinterpretation lets the guest both (a) preserve the First Vision's force against sectarianism and (b) free the LDS apologist to affirm the Nicene text. He hedges honestly: "I'm not saying that this couldn't have been referring to the Ecumenical Creeds." [^5] He still firmly rejects the Athanasian Creed and Westminster Confession as "certainly not in alignment with our theology ... cosmology or nature of God." [^6]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional LDS apologist (and many critics) will note that Joseph Smith's *own later* statements — e.g., the King Follett Discourse and D&C 130 on God's body — attack precisely the *metaphysical content* of creedal theism (immateriality, no parts and passions), not merely denominational squabbling. On that reading the "abomination" really is the substance of the creeds, and the guest's softening is a modern apologetic retreat that sits uneasily with Joseph's own anti-creedal trajectory. The guest partly grants this by still rejecting the Athanasian/Westminster metaphysics.

## Best Quotes
> "John Welch explains that the word creed in an early 19th century American English referred rather to any system of principles which are believed and professed." [^2]

> "When Christ told Joseph the creeds were an abomination, he rather meant that the divisive denominational systems of Christendom of denominational divorce were an abomination, not necessarily the Nicene Creed." [^4]

> "If Jesus Christ told him the word creed he would not have understood it as ecumenical creed. He would have understood it as the rigid sectarian dogmatism of Protestant New England." [^3]
