# Cracking Down on Public Dissent, Not Private Belief

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] argues that in [[Brigham Young]]'s day you would be "cracked down on for not believing in Adam-God," with your salvation threatened by the prophet — proof the doctrine was coercively binding. [[Luke Hansen]] rebuts that the church only disciplined **public opposition** to leaders, never **private disbelief** — and that most members never accepted Adam-God at all.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Most members never believed it (strong — 19th-c. primary witness)
[[T.B. Stenhouse]] (1873): "The mass of Mormonism does not believe the doctrine of the Adam deity, but of them all only one, [[Orson Pratt]], has dared to make a public protest." Hansen: it "definitely was not being brought from the top down... or even heavily emphasized upon the general membership" [^1].

### 2. Only public, "militant" dissent was punished (moderate — selective evidence)
Orson Pratt, a Quorum of the Twelve apostle, "taught publicly against the doctrine" for eight years and **remained in good standing**. When he finally submitted, Young told him: "you need to run new doctrine through me... before you teach it," but also "you are not perfect and neither am I" [^2]. Hansen's parallel to today: you can privately believe Joseph Smith didn't practice polygamy and stay a member; you're disciplined only if you "publicly teach" it [^3].

### 3. Brigham Young himself went soft on it (strong — primary, undercuts "binding")
By 1857 Young said: "Whether Adam is the person that we should consider our Father or not is considered a mystery by many... I do not care for one moment how that is... for in either case we are of one species" [^4]. Hansen maps a timeline: strong statements 1852–56, then years of distinguishing God from Adam, then a final 1877 revival in the Lecture at the Veil — "the death throes of the doctrine" [^5].

### Steelman (Heschmeyer's side)
Heschmeyer presses the tension hard: "It sounds like even the example you gave, he's not actually that free to oppose it. He's the only one free enough to stand up, and then he gets called to the carpet" [^6]. He concludes Pratt "was right... and he's being punished for trying to hold to what you would regard today as the true faith" — which Hansen **concedes** ("I think so, yes") [^7]. So even under "reliability," the system "can actually include cracking down on people for not believing false doctrine" [^7]. Hansen's only escape is to relabel it "cracking down on people for *publicly opposing*" leaders — an orthopraxy, not orthodoxy, offense.

## Best Quotes
> "The mass of Mormonism does not believe the doctrine of the Adam deity, but of them all only one, Orson Pratt, has dared to make a public protest." — T.B. Stenhouse (1873), quoted [^1]

> "It's not so much that you have to believe this doctrine, but it's a matter of you can't openly rebel against leaders of the church." — Luke Hansen [^3]

> "Pratt was right... and he's being punished for trying to hold to what you would regard today as the true faith. Is that right?" — Heschmeyer / "I think so, yes." — Hansen [^7]
