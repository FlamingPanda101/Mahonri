# Born of the Father Before All Ages Affirms Christs Preexistence and Subordination

## The Claim
The Nicene clauses "born of the Father before all ages" and "God from God, light from light, true God from true God" are read by the guest as affirming two things Mormons hold and critics deny them: (1) Christ's **preexistence**, and (2) the Son's **derivation from / subordination to** the Father. "Born of the Father before all ages ... that's a preexistence. They get mad at us for believing in a preexistence." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Before all ages" = preexistence (the headline)
The guest notes the co-host's observation that the Creed itself asserts the Son existed "before all ages," yet critics mock the LDS doctrine of a premortal existence. [^1] He concedes the orthodox gloss ("eternally begotten") but presses that the *language* of being "born/begotten of the Father" is generative and relational. **Rating: rhetorically fair on preexistence of the Son specifically; weaker as support for the LDS doctrine of a universal human premortal existence, which the Creed does not address.**

### 2. "God from God" = derivation (subordination)
"God from God. That is saying Christ is God from God, meaning Christ comes from God the Father." [^2] Combined with the *autotheos/deuterotheos* distinction (see [[I Believe in One God the Father Almighty Encodes the Monarchy of the Father]]), the guest reads the derivation clauses as encoding the Son's secondary, dependent status: "the early Christians believed in subordination." [^3] **Rating: the derivation language is genuine; the inference to ontological subordination is contested** — orthodoxy reads "God from God" as the Son receiving the *same* full deity from the Father, not a lesser one.

### 3. The "plowboy" simplicity objection (rated weak by the guest's co-host)
The co-host complains that "God from God, light from light, true God from true God" sounds like "some kind of like Eastern paradox" imported from "Hellenistic philosophy," contradicting the gospel's supposed plainness "preached to the plow boy." [^4] **Rating: a rhetorical/aesthetic objection, not an exegetical one** — it asserts complexity is suspect rather than showing the doctrine false.

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
The orthodox reading: "born of the Father before all ages" was crafted precisely to say the Son is **eternal and uncreated** — not a temporal pre-mortal birth like LDS spirit-birth. "Eternally begotten" means the begetting has no beginning; it is the anti-Arian denial that "there was a time when the Son was not." So the clause affirms the Son's *co-eternity*, the opposite of a beginning-having preexistence such as LDS spirit children possess. Reading it as ordinary preexistence collapses the very distinction the fathers built. The co-host even half-concedes this: "they don't believe Christ has no beginning ... but Christ is eternally proceeding." [^5]
