# Jerome Consistently Excluded the Deuterocanon

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that [[Jerome]] — "the greatest scholar of the early church" — held a stable, lifelong canon that **excluded** the deuterocanonical/apocryphal books, aligning with the later Protestant Old Testament. In his preface to Samuel and Kings, Jerome lists the Old Testament in the Jewish three-fold division (law, prophets, writings), defends a **22-book Hebrew canon**, and explicitly terms Judith, Tobit, and 1–2 Maccabees "apocryphal." Against the popular claim that Jerome "changed his mind" and later accepted these books, Ortlund cites scholar **Ed Gallagher**: "the available evidence indicates that Jerome consistently excluded the deuterocanonical books from the Christian canon of scripture." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Catholic reply
The Catholic case does not rest on Jerome; it concedes him as the famous outlier and argues he is **one dissenting voice against the broader stream** ([[Augustine]], the North African councils of Hippo and Carthage, later Florence and Trent). On this view: (a) Jerome nonetheless *translated* Tobit and Judith and *quoted* deuterocanonical books as scripture, suggesting practical acceptance; (b) he submitted his private scholarly judgment to the church's authority; and (c) one learned man's preference for the *Hebraica veritas* cannot overturn the consensus that the Spirit guided the church to the wider canon. The lone-exception framing is also how Catholic apologists like [[Trent Horn]] handle him (see [[Jerome]]).

### Ortlund's reply
- **The preface is explicit and early.** Jerome reaches the 22-book Hebrew canon and brands the additional books *apocrypha* — and Gallagher argues this was his view "very early in his career," so "Jerome's theory regarding the deuterocanonical did not substantially develop." [^2]
- **The translation argument cuts the other way.** Gallagher shows that Jerome's *prefaces to Tobit and Judith* demonstrate he did **not** regard them as canonical even as he translated them — translation is not endorsement. [^3]
- **The citation argument is answered by the two-tier distinction.** When Jerome cites Sirach as "scripture," his preface to the books of Solomon classifies Sirach/Ecclesiasticus and Wisdom as books the church "reads... for the strengthening of the people but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas" — tier-two, not canonical (see [[The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha]]). [^4]
- **His authority persisted to Trent.** Even 16th-century Catholic opponents of Luther — Cardinal Cajetan, Cardinal Ximenes — appealed to Jerome when excluding the deuterocanon, showing his canon remained a live Catholic option right up to Trent. [^5]

## LDS Relevance
Both Ortlund's Protestant view and the LDS view reject the notion of a single timeless, unanimously-fixed Christian canon — but they diverge sharply on the remedy. Ortlund uses Jerome to argue *backward* toward a **narrower, closed** Hebrew-aligned canon as the apostolic original; the LDS open-canon position treats the same patristic fluidity as evidence that canon boundaries are *humanly negotiated and re-openable by living revelation*. Jerome's lifelong dissent is, for the Latter-day Saint, one more datum that the "biblical canon" was never the seamless deposit either Catholics or Protestants need it to be — though Ortlund would insist the data point *toward* a definite shorter list, not toward an indefinitely expandable one.

## Best Quotes
> "The available evidence indicates that Jerome consistently excluded the deuterocanonical books from the Christian canon of scripture. There seems then to be no reason to think that Jerome ever adhered to an Old Testament canon different from the one he endorsed [in the preface to Samuel and Kings]." — Ed Gallagher, as quoted by Ortlund [^1]

> "Therefore just as the church also reads the books of Judith, Tobias, and the Maccabees but does not receive them among the canonical scriptures, so also one may read these two scrolls [Sirach, Wisdom] for the strengthening of the people but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas." — Jerome, preface to the books of Solomon, read by Ortlund [^4]
