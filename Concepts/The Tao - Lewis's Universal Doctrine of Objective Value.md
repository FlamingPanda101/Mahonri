# The Tao - Lewis's Universal Doctrine of Objective Value

## The Point
"The *Tao*" is Lewis's name for the single, objective doctrine of value that, he argues, underlies every great moral tradition.[^1] Ward stresses that Lewis adopts the Chinese term deliberately — not to privilege Eastern thought, but "to de-emphasize Western categories and remind his readers that moral reality is universal."[^2] The *Tao* is the body of self-evident moral first principles that human beings do not invent but discover, and apart from which no moral judgment of any kind is possible.[^3]

## Evidence
Ward draws out two claims Lewis makes about the *Tao*. First, it is **objective and discovered, not constructed**: "The Tao is not something that human beings simply make up; it is something they discover."[^4] Second, it is **universal**: Lewis quotes "a diverse chorus of voices" — Plato, Aristotle, Horace, Confucius, Jesus, St. Paul, Locke, Shelley and others — to show that "the human family is a real family, with certain core values in common."[^5] Lewis concedes that lumping together "the traditional moralities of East and West, the Christian, the Pagan, and the Jew" yields "many contradictions and some absurdities," but his thesis is not that all moralities coincide on every point — only "that they all derive from a single source, the universally accessible Tao, and all therefore agree in principle on the objectivity of moral value."[^6] Crucially, the *Tao* is also the only platform from which it can itself be reformed: progress is real, "but these improvements can only be made... from inside the Tao. Adjustments have to be organic, not surgical."[^7] To leave it is fatal: "As soon as one steps outside the Tao, one has stepped into the void."[^8] Even the subjectivist proves the point — it is "impossible to live in a logically consistent fashion holding to the belief that value is based on instinct"; in practice "we all act as if we did indeed recognise an objectively real ethical ecology, within which we morally live and breathe."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "The Tao is not something that human beings simply make up; it is something they discover." [^4]

> In practice "we all act as if we did indeed recognise an objectively real ethical ecology, within which we morally live and breathe, just as we physically live and breathe within the earth's atmosphere." [^9]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's *Tao* is a remarkably close philosophical cousin of the Latter-day Saint doctrine of the **Light of Christ**: a universal moral illumination given to all people in every nation, by which "every man" may "know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16-19). Latter-day Saint scripture, like Lewis, treats this law as **objective and eternal** — "the law by which all things are governed" (D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]) — not a human invention but a reality to be discovered and obeyed through moral **agency**. Lewis's catalogue of pagan, Jewish, and Christian moralists converging on one law also fits the LDS expectation that fragments of God's truth are scattered through all the world's cultures. This stands directly against the naturalist project of [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] and [[Science Can Determine Human Values]]: where Harris would derive value from measurable well-being, Lewis (and LDS thought) hold that the moral law is received from a transcendent source, and that "as soon as one steps outside" it, reasoned morality collapses — the self-refutation traced in [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]].
