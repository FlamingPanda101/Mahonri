# Shrinking Anachronism List as Evidence of Authenticity

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics have long argued that the Book of Mormon contains a list of anachronisms and historical impossibilities that prove it is a 19th-century fiction. The fraud theory makes a specific prediction: if [[Joseph Smith]] invented the text, it "should contain the errors that you would expect from someone inventing an ancient record without actually knowing ancient history," and for a long time critics claimed it did exactly that [^6].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Roper's 2019 survey of the whole criticism corpus.** [[Matt Roper]] (the one LDS scholar Arnold flags) went through every published criticism of the Book of Mormon's historical/archaeological claims since 1830 — over 1,000 publications — and found **141 of 205** claimed anachronisms *fully eliminated* by subsequent research, with another ~11% trending toward elimination. *Rating: a concrete, quantified, falsifiable claim; its force depends on Roper's classification standards, which a skeptic could contest.* [^7]
2. **"Eliminated" means external confirmation, not internal argument.** Arnold stresses the eliminations came from "non-LDS digs and secular published findings" confirming that things critics called impossible "actually existed in the ancient world" — "from outside, not from within." This pre-empts the "apologists just argued back" reply. *Rating: strong if accurate; the reader cannot audit each of the 141 from the video alone.* [^8]
3. **The trend direction is the real argument.** "Usually, when a fraud gets scrutinized over two centuries, the problems multiply. When an authentic record faces scrutiny, the problems shrink." The list is "70% shorter than it used to be." Arnold's inference is Bayesian: a shrinking error list is more probable under authenticity than under fabrication [^8].
4. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) A shrinking list can also reflect *post-hoc accommodation* — anachronisms can be reinterpreted, redefined, or quietly dropped without new digs. (b) Some "eliminations" rest on the mere *possibility* a thing existed somewhere in the ancient world, not on its presence in the relevant Mesoamerican time and place. (c) 64 of 205 remain unresolved by Roper's own count, and the most-cited problems (horses, steel, chariots, large populations, no confirmed Nephite site) are precisely the ones critics still lead with. Arnold acknowledges critics "get really quiet about the items that get crossed off" but does not enumerate which 64 remain [^8].

## Best Quotes
> "His 2019 analysis found 141 out of 205 claimed anachronisms fully eliminated by subsequent research. Another 11% were trending toward elimination." [^7]

> "It means that external archaeology, like non-LDS digs and secular published findings, confirmed that the thing critics called impossible actually existed in the ancient world. The eliminations came from outside, not from within." [^8]

> "Critics still leading with the archaeology argument are working from a list that's 70% shorter than it used to be. And they get really quiet about the items that get crossed off." [^8]
