# The Deification Publishing Boom Since 1950

## The Point
Deification — that humans can become gods — was the doctrine that most sharply marked Latter-day Saints as heretics ("Mormonism tells you that a man is our God"). [[Brian Thomassen]] argues it has quietly become a respectable subject of Christian theology in the West, measurable in a publishing boom and codified, of all places, in the Catholic Catechism. [[Joseph Smith]]'s position was blunt: "they are gods, even the sons of God" (D&C 76), and "God himself was once a man as we are now and is an exalted man."[^1] Where [[Augustine]] was "surprisingly tentative" in rejecting it,[^2] the modern Western church has reopened the question.

## Evidence
- **Paulsen's bibliography.** Citing [[David Paulsen]]'s 2006 survey, Thomassen reports "a current bibliography of articles, books, chapters in books and dissertations reveals 222 publications" on deification — "195, or 88%, were published since 1950," "104, nearly half of the total... since 1990," and at least 20 more from 2002–2006.[^3] The significance is that these works are "not that they're false" — "they're just exploring the idea, which means it is no longer a closed anathema."[^4]
- **It moved West.** The striking part is *where*: deification "existed in the eastern space to certain degrees," but "in the western space it was kind of rejected" — and now it is being seriously taken up there.[^5]
- **The 1993 Catholic Catechism.** The revised Catechism quotes [[Athanasius]]: "The only begotten Son of God, wanting to make us sharers in his divinity, assumed our nature so that he, made man, might make men gods."[^6] Thomassen notes the timing — added "in 1993... It wasn't there before"[^7] — and ties it to the established Eastern *theosis* tradition feeding back into Catholicism.
- **Eastern images of participation.** From the *Philokalia* he draws the image of three lamps whose light blends so "you can't make a distinction between the light and the lamps" (a oneness that "doesn't require the extinction of the three individual beings"), and *perichoresis* — Greek "to spin" — the Father, Son, and Spirit "intermingling and working together like a dance."[^8][^9]
- **The remaining gap, and Ostler's answer.** Catholic Answers stresses participation *in* rather than *becoming* God (the heated sword "takes on the heat but does not become the flame"). Thomassen counters with [[Blake Ostler]]'s fusion analogy: deification is like hydrogen fusing in the sun, "an adding to and an expansion of power and knowledge and light" — the creature "actually becomes a new thing."[^10][^11]

## Best Quotes
> "195, or 88%, were published since 1950. 104, nearly half of the total, were published since 1990... It's no longer anathema. It's no longer inimical. It is something to be considered." [^3]

> "The only begotten Son of God, wanting to make us sharers in his divinity, assumed our nature so that he, made man, might make men gods." — Catechism of the Catholic Church (1993), quoting Athanasius [^6]

## LDS Connection
This is the convergence-side, modern-historical companion to [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]] (which recovers the *ancient* substrate) and to [[Human Deification in Mormonism]]. Lorenzo Snow's couplet and Joseph's [[King Follett Discourse]] presuppose exactly the human-to-divine trajectory that critics called blasphemous and that the West is now reinvestigating. The crucial LDS distinctive that the convergence does *not* reach is captured in [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]]: mainstream "participation" theology (the sword warmed by fire, sharing God's *communicable* attributes only) still posits an "uncrossable line between creature and creator," whereas the LDS reading — and Ostler's fusion image — denies that line. So the honest framing is partial convergence: the question is reopened and the vocabulary shared, but the ontological gap remains the live dividing point.
