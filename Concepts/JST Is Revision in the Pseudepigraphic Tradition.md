# JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the Joseph Smith Translation (JST) is best understood not as a
*translation* but as a **revision** that belongs to the same ancient stream as Jewish
pseudepigraphic and Targumic literature. Joseph Smith was "doing a similar thing" to the
Genesis Apocryphon (Dead Sea Scrolls) and the Targums: repackaging an authoritative text to make
it more relevant, accessible, and resonant for a contemporary community — adding, expanding,
harmonizing, and removing uncomfortable material. "That's been going on for over 2,000 years. So
there's nothing new" — what was new was Smith's willingness to claim the prophetic authority to
do it.[^1] [[Benjamin Park]] adds the crucial twist: Smith's revisions function to **reassert**
biblical authority, not subvert it.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The JST is revision, not translation — rate: strong
McClellan: "the idea that it's a translation only works if you decide to take a more expansive
understanding of the notion of translation as any kind of transmission… Revision is much more
appropriate, much more accurate."[^2] Scholars have identified "between five to seven different
types of changes" — deleting italicized words, adding new material, harmonizing — "the kind of
stuff that a lot of people would love to have the authority to do but have never felt like they
had that authority or could never get away with it."[^3] This reframes the JST so that the
"surviving manuscript doesn't match an ancient original" objection misfires, paralleling
[[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]].

### 2. It belongs to a 2,000-year-old pseudepigraphic practice — rate: strong
The named analogues are the **Genesis Apocryphon** and **Targumic** retellings — texts that
"repackage the authoritative texts in a way that were more relevant to the contemporary community."
McClellan calls for "a lot more work… regarding the nature of his revision and the Book of Moses,
Book of Abraham as kind of within the same stream of tradition as a lot of pseudepigraphical work
in the past."[^1] This situates the LDS scriptural project inside, not outside, the history of
how scripture was actually produced — compare [[Book of Mormon as American Pseudepigrapha]].

### 3. The additions reassert biblical authority — rate: strong (Park)
Park's example: [[Thomas Paine]]'s *Age of Reason* (circulating in the Smith home — "or at least
thrown at Joseph Senior") popularized the Enlightenment critique that "Moses isn't even identified
as the author of Genesis." One of the first things the JST does is add a verse, "I, Moses, wrote
this record." "Some might see him challenging biblical authority, but another interpretation is
he's trying to *reassert* biblical authority" through modern prophecy.[^4] The JST is thus a
response to a hermeneutical crisis, not a rejection of the Bible. **Steelman of a critic:** Smith
*did* present the work as inspired correction of a corrupted text, so calling it "revision" still
concedes he was not recovering a lost ancient *Urtext*; McClellan grants exactly this — the value
is in repackaging for a community, not in textual recovery.

## Best Quotes

> "I think revision is much more appropriate, is much more accurate. And again, I think Joseph Smith was kind of returning to the kinds of things that went on before there was a closed canon."[^2]
