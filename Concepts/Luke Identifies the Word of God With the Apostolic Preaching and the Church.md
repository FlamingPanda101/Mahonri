# Luke Identifies the Word of God With the Apostolic Preaching and the Church

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] observes that the NT author who uses "word of God" most often is **Luke**, and in Luke–Acts the phrase "almost always refers to an undefined message about God or what God has revealed" — never to a written text, and at points even to the *growing church itself*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. In the Gospel, the word of God is the preached gospel — rate: strong
Luke records crowds expecting to *hear Jesus preach* the word of God, and Jesus redefining his family as "those that hear the word of God and keep it."[^1] Horn notes this cannot mean only those who heard the OT read; it must include those who heard the *orally proclaimed gospel* and responded.[^1] The referent is an event of hearing, not a scroll.

### 2. Kodell's three-stage development — rate: strong, scholar-anchored
Biblical scholar [[Jerome Kodell]] is quoted: in Acts, "the apostolic preaching about Jesus, the message of the whole Christ event, becomes the word of God. This is the third step… The word of God is first God's promise in the Old Testament, then the preaching of Jesus himself, and finally the preaching about Jesus."[^2] On this trajectory the term moves *toward* the apostolic kerygma — never landing on "a book."

### 3. "The word of God grew" — the word as the church — rate: strong, striking
On three occasions Luke says "the word of God grew," without tying it to revelation being given.[^3] Kodell: "after the resurrection the word of God is embedded in the Christian community. It lives in the church… Luke sees the word so bound up with the community life and witness that he can say the word of God grew when the church adds new members."[^3] A 1995 dissertation likewise concludes that in Acts the term applies "primarily to the apostolic period for the Christian proclamation rather than to Jesus's own message."[^4] **Steelman of the Protestant:** "the word grew" can be read as the *message* spreading (a metonymy for evangelistic success), which is compatible with that message later being written down as Scripture. Horn's point is narrower and holds even on that reading: Luke's usage never *equates* the word of God with a text, so it cannot be cited to prove "Scripture = word of God."

### 4. Why this matters for sola scriptura — rate: framing
If the NT's heaviest user of the phrase locates the "word of God" in preaching and the church's life, then the Protestant premise (the *written* Scripture is *the* word of God) lacks its most natural lexical home. The data instead fit the Catholic triad of Scripture, Tradition, and the living Church. See [[Scripture as Word of God Is Itself a Doctrine of Sacred Tradition]].
