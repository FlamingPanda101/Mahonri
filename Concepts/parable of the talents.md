# parable of the talents

The **parable of the talents** (Matthew 25:14–30; cf. Luke 19's parable of the minas) is Jesus's story of a master who entrusts differing sums ("talents," large units of money) to three servants before departing. Two invest and double what they were given and are commended as "good and faithful"; the third buries his single talent out of fear and is condemned for failing to put it to productive use. The parable is the standard biblical proof-text for an ethic of active **stewardship** — that resources held in trust are to be responsibly grown, not hoarded or left idle.

## Relevance

In the vault the parable supplies the moral frame for defending the Church's investment reserves (Ensign Peak) against critics. The Church's stated intent — to be "good stewards" who "make the most" of consecrated funds "in a socially responsible manner," screening out direct participation in sin industries — is cast as a *parable-of-the-talents posture*, a fiduciary stewardship ethic rather than a propaganda apparatus or hidden hoard. It thus anchors several rebuttals to the "profiting from war / lobbying / hidden wealth" charges.
