# Laban and Nephi Enact the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Christina Dymock]] calls the slaying of Laban (1 Nephi 4) "the key that unlocked" her seven-feasts thesis ([[Nephi Encoded the Seven Hebrew Feasts as the Outline of First Nephi]]): the otherwise-anomalous **casting of lots** maps the chapter onto the **Day of Atonement** (Yom Kippur), where the high priest casts lots over two identical goats. On her reading **Laman** is the **Azazel** goat — the lot falls on him, [[Laban]] accuses him as a *thief* (a sin he did not commit, since Nephi took the plates), and Laman is "cast out," later wandering the wilderness like the scapegoat driven out of the camp. **Nephi**, who *dons Laban's clothing and identity*, plays the high priest who slays the **la-Yahweh** goat while paradoxically *identifying* with the sacrifice — and whose Egyptian-etymology name "the fair one" pairs with **Laban** ("white one"), the color of the Yahweh goat. [^1] [^2] [^3]

> [!note] Distinct from the Johannine version
> The vault already documents the two-goats template applied to the **Passion in John** ([[Barabbas and Jesus Are the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]], via Barker/Butler). *This* note is the application to **1 Nephi 4** within Dymock's festal outline — a different text and a different (and more contested) cast assignment. The shared underlying ritual is Leviticus 16's two goats; the claims do not overlap.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Casting lots is the diagnostic anomaly.** Dymock had always skipped the casting of lots (1 Ne 4) as unimportant; recognizing that the high priest casts lots over the two Yom Kippur goats turned it into the structural key locating the chapter at the Day of Atonement. *Rating: the lots-detail is genuinely in the text and genuinely unusual; reading it as the goat-lot is the inference.* [^1]
2. **A sin Laman did not commit is laid on his head.** Laban falsely brands Laman a thief and casts him out — sin imputed to an innocent party who is then expelled to the wilderness, the defining action of the **scapegoat**. *Rating: a real structural rhyme with the scapegoat rite; the identification of Laman *as* Azazel is interpretive.* [^2]
3. **Nephi assumes the sacrifice's identity, as the high priest does.** On Yom Kippur the high priest offers a bull for himself and a goat for Yahweh and mingles their blood — officiant and offering bound together. Nephi literally puts on Laban's garments and identity while performing the killing, "expressing identity with the sacrifice." *Rating: an evocative parallel to the priest/victim merger; depends on the larger Yom Kippur framing being correct.* [^3]
4. **The name colors line up.** **Laban** means "white one" — the color associated with the Yahweh goat; **Nephi**, by the Egyptian etymology, means "the fair/beautiful one" (see [[Nephi Means Good and Fair - The Goodliness Inclusio]]). Dymock allows the two roles blur between Laman and Nephi. *Rating: suggestive onomastic color-coding; the blurring of who-plays-which goat is a weakness she concedes.* [^3] [^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic objects that (a) **casting lots was common** in ancient Israel for many decisions, so its appearance in 1 Nephi 4 need not invoke Yom Kippur at all — Dymock herself admits she long "skipped over it"; (b) the cast assignment is **unstable** — she wavers on whether the Yahweh goat is Nephi, Laban (the "white one"), or a "mixture of Laman and Nephi" — and a typology whose roles can be reassigned to fit is hard to falsify; (c) "Azazel = Satan / the wilderness scapegoat" leans on later Enochic tradition (the same contested move flagged in the Johannine note); and (d) Nephi disguising himself in Laban's clothes has a plain narrative motive (deceiving Zoram) that needs no ritual subtext. The reply is the *convergence within the festal sequence*: the Atonement reading is not free-floating but sits exactly where the seven-feasts order predicts (after the Days of Repentance, before Sukkot), and the lots + imputed-sin + expulsion + priest-identifies-with-victim cluster is more than any single coincidence.

## Best Quotes
> "When they brought in the two identical goats they would cast lots over their heads — which one was going to be the Yahweh goat and which one was going to be the Azazel goat… so he puts a sin upon Laman's head that Laman didn't commit. Who steals? Nephi takes the plates… and then Laman is cast out and eventually wanders in the wilderness just like the Azazel goat." [^2]

> "Nephi takes on Laban's identity by dressing himself, so that even though he is performing the sacrifice he's expressing identity with the sacrifice." [^3]
