# The Dual Potential of Human Nature as Moral Test

## The Point

Evolution did not make us simply "selfish" — it built into us a **dual potential** for both selfishness and altruism, aggression and cooperation. [[Samuel T. Wilkinson]] explains this through the biology of *levels of selection*: traits that win "within groups" (selfishness) are opposed by traits that make "altruistic groups beat selfish groups." Because we are genuinely pulled in both directions and possess free will (what LDS theology calls *agency*), Wilkinson concludes that "life is a test of character" — we are here to choose between the good and the evil that are both inherent within us. Human nature's contradictions are thus not a bug but the very condition that makes moral choice meaningful.

## Evidence

- **Turn "survival of the fittest" into a question.** The phrase is incomplete: "survival of the fittest *what*?" — the fittest individual, family, or group? Different levels of selection favor opposing social traits, so the same evolutionary process yields both selfishness and altruism. [^1]
- **Atheists notice the duality too.** Wilkinson quotes [[Sam Harris]]: "no creature matches us for the ability to cooperate, but also no creature matches us for sadistic cruelty." This "dual potential of human nature" is widely observed, not a theological invention. [^2]
- **The Auckland Islands shipwrecks.** In 1864 two crews wrecked near the same islands; Musgrave's "remarkably cohesive and altruistic group" all survived (the captain swam a wounded man to shore on his back), while the Raynal/Grafton-type "every man for himself" crew mostly died. The story dramatizes how group-level cooperation, not selfishness, drove survival. [^3]
- **"Everything else is commentary."** Wilkinson cites the biologists' summary: "selfishness beats altruism within groups, but altruistic groups beat selfish groups, and everything else is commentary" — calling it "almost like eternal truth." [^4]
- **Integration, not suppression.** Picking up Hansen's point, Wilkinson agrees the test may be to *integrate* these drives rightly (even aggression and the reproductive drive serve life) rather than to let either "run wild." [^5]
- **Free will makes it a test.** The duality matters only because we choose: "when you combine that with the sense that we have free will — what in religious terms we refer to as agency — it seems to be that life is a test of character." [^6] Compare [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]] and [[The Genetic-Determinism Argument Against Moral Responsibility]].

## Best Quotes

> "Selfishness beats altruism within groups, but altruistic groups beat selfish groups, and everything else is commentary... I feel like that is a principle of almost like eternal truth." [^4]

> "When you combine that with the sense that we have free will — what in religious terms we refer to as agency — it seems to be that life is a test of character, and we are... here to choose between the good and the evil that is inherent within us." [^6]

## LDS Connection

This maps almost directly onto the Latter-day Saint plan of salvation, in which mortality is a probationary "test" and moral agency is the central feature of the second estate. Lehi's teaching that there must be "opposition in all things" (2 Nephi 2) is mirrored in Wilkinson's claim that nature built genuine opposing dispositions into us; agency is meaningful precisely because both the "natural man" and the capacity for Christlike love are real options. The purpose of life, on both pictures, is the choosing — using agency to integrate and master our natures toward the good, which for Latter-day Saints is the path of eternal progression.
