# Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Inerrantists assert: **"There are no contradictions anywhere in the Bible."** When pressed on any specific pair of passages, they defend this by arguing that *as long as some reconciliation is not impossible*, the passages do not actually contradict. The implicit definition: a contradiction exists if and only if there is **no logically, humanly, or physically possible** way to harmonize the two propositions.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The defended claim is not the asserted claim (rated: strong)
[[Dan McClellan]] observes that almost no one will actually defend "there are no contradictions." Under pressure they defend a different, far weaker proposition: that it is *not entirely outside the realm of possibility* that there are none. The two claims differ enormously in strength — the first is a positive assertion about the text; the second is merely a statement that the negation has not been proven impossible. Treating the second as if it vindicated the first is a **bait-and-switch**.

### 2. "Not impossible" is too weak to function as evidence (rated: strong)
A standard that admits *any* unevidenced scenario — "doesn't matter how implausible, doesn't matter how improbable," only that it is not strictly impossible — is no standard at all. It does not distinguish the true from the false, because for ancient texts there is virtually nothing in the impossible category (see [[Interpretation Is Not Empirically Verifiable]]). This is the move dissected in [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]].

### 3. We use a higher standard everywhere else (rated: strong)
By the "not impossible" rule we could never call anything false. See [[The Horse in the Backyard Analogy]]: it is *physically possible* a stranger's claim about a horse in your yard is true, yet you can be fully confident it is false. Ordinary justified confidence does not require logical impossibility of the alternative; it requires the alternative to be unsupported by evidence — which is exactly the situation with most harmonizations.

### Steelman of the opposing view
The harmonizer's instinct is not worthless. The **principle of charity** in interpretation does counsel that we prefer a reading on which a text is coherent when the evidence genuinely underdetermines the question, and historians do reconstruct unattested-but-plausible scenarios. The strongest version of the inerrantist position is: "Where two readings are equally supported, prefer the one that coheres." The rebuttal is not that harmonization is *never* legitimate, but that it is being deployed asymmetrically — invoked to rescue a prior dogma rather than as a neutral tie-breaker, and stretched to scenarios with *no* supporting evidence rather than merely choosing between evidenced readings (see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]).

### Scholarly context
Mainstream biblical scholarship treats the relevant passages as the product of **multiple authors, sources, and redactional layers** (e.g., the documentary/source approach to the two creation accounts and the two interwoven flood narratives — see [[Genesis 7.2-3|Genesis 7:2-3]] and [[Genesis 7.8-9|Genesis 7:8-9]]). On that model, divergences are expected artifacts of composite composition, not puzzles requiring rescue. The burden is therefore on the harmonizer to show their reconstruction is *evidenced*, not merely *possible*.

## Best Quotes
> "People who assert that there are no contradictions anywhere in the Bible are really only prepared to defend the claim that it is not entirely outside the realm of possibility that there are no contradictions anywhere in the Bible." [^1]

> "A contradiction is only a contradiction if and only if there is no logically, humanly or physically possible way for two different propositions in the Bible to be harmonized or reconciled." [^2]

> "What that means is that they are at liberty to conjure up any and all possible scenarios that are not in evidence that can harmonize or reconcile the two different propositions. Doesn't matter how implausible, doesn't matter how improbable. All that matters is that it is not physically, logically, or humanly impossible." [^3]
