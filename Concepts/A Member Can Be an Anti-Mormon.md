#type/claim

# A Member Can Be an Anti-Mormon

## The Claim
The label "anti-Mormon" is not coextensive with "non-member" or "ex-Mormon." On [[Jacob Hansen]]'s conduct-based definition (see [[Anti-Mormon Is Public Intentional Repeated Erosion of Faith]]), what matters is the *activity*, not the membership records: "You can be a member of this church and be an anti-Mormon 100%." [^1] Conversely, a sincere outside critic like [[Steven Pynakker]] is *not* anti-Mormon, and most ex-Mormons are not either. Membership and animus are orthogonal axes. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Membership does not exempt you.** Asked whether progressive members count, Hansen answers: "If they are publicly and intentionally... repeatedly eroding fundamental truth claims of the church I would absolutely [say so]. You can be a member of this church and be an anti-Mormon 100%." [^1] He applies this to RFM, who "is still a member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints" yet, in Hansen's view, "should be excommunicated immediately for his own good" because the brethren's "clear definition" places him "in rank apostasy." [^3] *Rating: strong and internally consistent;* it is the direct corollary of defining the term by conduct rather than identity, and it blocks the move "I hold a temple recommend, therefore I can't be anti."

2. **Non-membership does not convict you.** The same criterion clears an honest evangelical: "I don't think that you're an anti-Mormon because you're not engaging in intentional continuous public attacks on the fundamental claims of the church." [^4] Disagreement, even total doctrinal disagreement, is not the trigger. *Rating: strong;* the symmetry (members can be in, outsiders can be out) is what keeps the definition from collapsing into tribalism.

3. **Most ex-Mormons are not anti-Mormons.** Hansen volunteers the limiting case against his own polemical reputation: "Are all ex-Mormons anti-Mormon? I mean, of course not... I think most ex-Mormons aren't anti-Mormons. Most people who leave the church I think actually generally look back on it and say it was a good thing." [^2] Pynakker presses the humane edge: there are "highly ethical, highly moral people... who are hurting still to this day," and they are not to be branded. [^5] *Rating: strong;* conceding that the *majority* of leavers fall outside the label disciplines the term and answers the worry that "anti-Mormon" is a cudgel against everyone who exits.

**Steelman of the critic.** One can object that "a member can be an anti-Mormon" is mostly a rhetorical device for delegitimizing internal reformers — that calling a temple-recommend-holding member "in rank apostasy" weaponizes the term against precisely the people trying to improve the institution. The reply built into the surrounding dialogue is the venue argument: members have ordained channels for dissent and the sustaining vote (see [[Reform Through Priesthood Channels Not Public Attack]]), so the charge attaches not to *being* a critical member but to running a sustained *public* campaign against the core claims while inside. The disputed premise remains whether "eroding fundamental claims" can be drawn without sweeping in good-faith reform.
