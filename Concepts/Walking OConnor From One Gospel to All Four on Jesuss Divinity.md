# Walking O'Connor From One Gospel to All Four on Jesus's Divinity

## The Point
This is [[Jacob Hansen]]'s cross-examination of [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] on the claim "Jesus never claimed to be God" — the only one of Hansen's Jubilee turns that is straight New Testament apologetics rather than philosophy of religion. Hansen runs a Socratic funnel: pin O'Connor to two premises he will grant — (a) the divinity of Jesus "developed very early," during the eyewitness period (Paul writing "decades after Jesus's death"), and (b) the four gospels are "the most reliable source that we have available" for what Jesus said and did — and then show those very gospels portraying Jesus doing and saying things "that no mortal man would do or say."[^1] The drama Hansen highlights is O'Connor's shifting position: he begins by saying "there's only one gospel that unambiguously portrays Jesus as himself claiming to be divine, and that's the Gospel of John," and by the end concedes "I'm happy to say that the synoptic authors were portraying Jesus as God."[^2]

## Evidence
- **The funnel premises.** O'Connor grants the tradition developed "fairly early," and that there is no "better source for knowing what Jesus actually claimed than the gospels" — "no, we don't."[^3] Rate: **a shrewd setup** — it forecloses the move of dismissing the gospels wholesale while accepting them as the historical baseline.
- **The "only John" claim and its reversal.** O'Connor first restricts unambiguous divinity to John ("the latest and most theologically motivated"), then, pressed on the synoptics, says "I'm happy to grant that the synoptic authors were trying to portray Jesus as God."[^2] Hansen replays the contradiction: "before it was John was the only gospel... and now he is saying that Matthew, Mark and Luke also portray him as God."
- **The synoptic data points Hansen marshals.** The blasphemy charge against Jesus; Jesus as the one who will *judge the entire world* ("it would have to be a very special kind of human"); worship (proskuneō) directed at Jesus; and the **virgin birth** — "is Jesus's birth portrayed as a normal human birth? No, not at all... so if he's not a man, what is he?"[^4] O'Connor's recurring escape is source-critical: the birth narratives are "later editions" (Luke's especially), so even granting the *portrayal* he need not grant the *history* (cf. [[Virgin Birth Is a Later Development in the Jesus Tradition]]).
- **O'Connor's *ego eimi* parry on John 8:58.** When Hansen leans on "before Abraham was, I am," O'Connor counters that the same Greek (*egō eimi*) appears one chapter later when the healed blind man says "I am [the man]," so it can be "just a way of identifying yourself."[^5] Rate: **a real linguistic point** that the divine-name reading of John 8:58 must reckon with — see [[John 8-58 - Before Abraham Was I Am (Divine Name Agency)]]. Hansen's reply is cumulative rather than lexical: it is the *constellation* (blasphemy + judgment + worship + virgin birth) across all four gospels, not one phrase, that carries the case.
- **O'Connor's bottom line — portrayal without trust.** He concedes the portrayal but withholds belief: "that's fine... you just don't trust those gospels. No — in the same way that I don't trust the legendary accounts surrounding the birth of Alexander the Great."[^6] Rate: **the honest atheist endgame** — Hansen has won the *exegetical* claim (the evangelists meant to present a divine Jesus) but not the *historical* one (that the portrayal is true). Both men implicitly agree the dispute has moved from "what do the texts say" to "are the texts reliable."

## Best Quotes
> "There's only one gospel that unambiguously portrays Jesus as himself claiming to be divine, and that's the Gospel of John." [^2]

> "Well again, I'm happy to say that the synoptic authors were portraying Jesus as God." [^2]

> "Is there a better, more reliable source for the historical Jesus than the gospels? No." [^3]

> "I don't trust those gospels... in the same way that I don't trust the legendary accounts surrounding the birth of Alexander the Great. I don't think that he was God either." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Hansen here argues a thoroughly *mainstream-Christian* high Christology — useful evidence that Latter-day Saints affirm the divinity of Christ from the New Testament, against the charge that they deny it. The specifically LDS angle surfaces elsewhere in the vault: Latter-day Saints read the gospels' divine-Jesus portrait through a Godhead (not Trinitarian) lens and through divine-name / agency frameworks rather than ontological simplicity. The contrast with the related note [[Jesus Never Claims Deity as Explicitly as the Messenger Does]] is instructive: that note presses how *implicit* the synoptic claims are, whereas here Hansen presses O'Connor to concede the synoptic authors nonetheless *intended* a divine portrayal.
