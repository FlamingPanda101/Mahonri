# The Angel of Yahweh Texts Are Editorial Interpolations

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the conflated identity of the messenger and God arises from **textual interpolation**: the theophany stories "originally had God themselves visiting Moses, visiting Jacob, visiting Hagar, visiting Gideon, visiting Manoah and his wife," and "a later editor put in *Malakh* [messenger] in a few places — not all the places — to muddy the waters and create this sense of a conflated identity." [^1] This redaction is why the messenger ends up saying "I am God."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Internal narrative seams.** In several theophanies the text oscillates between "the messenger of Yahweh" and "Yahweh/God" as the speaker (e.g., [[Scriptures/Exodus 3.6|Exodus 3]]: the *malʾak* appears in v.2 but God speaks in vv.4–6). McClellan reads this unevenness ("in a few places, not all the places") as the fingerprint of selective editorial insertion. *Rating: a recognized critical observation about the angel-of-Yahweh texts; the interpolation hypothesis is one scholarly explanation among others.* [^1]

2. **A named, paralleled set of cases.** He lists Moses, Jacob, Hagar, Gideon, and Manoah and his wife as theophanies later softened by inserting the messenger. *Rating: these are exactly the classic *malʾak YHWH* episodes, so the data set is appropriate; the claim that each originally lacked the messenger is a reconstruction, not a manuscript fact.* [^1] *Steelman of the traditional view:* the messenger/God alternation can be read as an intentional literary device for the *malʾak*'s representative identity rather than as later tampering, and there is no surviving manuscript witness to a messenger-free original.

3. **It fits a broader redaction-critical model.** The interpolation claim coheres with McClellan's wider view that editors reshaped Israel's theophany and divinity language over time. *Rating: consistent with his program; its strength depends on accepting source/redaction criticism of the Pentateuch.* [^1]

## Best Quotes
> "Those stories originally had God themselves visiting Moses, visiting Jacob, visiting Hagar, visiting Gideon, visiting Manoah and his wife, and a later editor put in Malakh in a few places — not all the places — to muddy the waters and create this sense of a conflated identity." [^1]

> "That's what's responsible for the messenger saying, 'I am God.' But this could make sense in light of the intuitive logic of divine images. You just had to imagine that the messenger was a sentient walking, talking, breathing, and eating divine image." [^2]
