# New Religious Movements — Sociological Categories

## The Weber-Troeltsch Typology

Max Weber and Ernst Troeltsch (early 20th c.) developed categories to
understand how religions evolve socially:

### Church
- Large, inclusive, low-tension with surrounding society
- Professional, educated clergy
- Membership by birth (to some degree)
- Examples: Roman Catholicism, mainline Protestant denominations

### Sect
- Smaller, high-tension, high-demand
- Voluntary membership (you choose to join and stay)
- Often lay-led or anti-professional-clergy
- Strong behavioral requirements and symbolic boundaries
- Examples: early Methodists, Amish, LDS (in many respects)

### Cult (Original Scholarly Sense)
- Troeltsch's residual category for groups that didn't fit church or sect
- Small, loosely organized, mystical, highly personal
- Focused on ritual and experiential practice
- What we'd now call New Age or metaphysical movements
- **Not pejorative** — purely descriptive

## How This Applies to LDS

### The Church-Sect Tension
Dawson: "Churches that become too inclusive and dilute their standards
too much fail ultimately. Sectarian religions are strong because they
have strong behavioral requirements and barriers."

The LDS Church navigates a tension:
- **Sectarian strengths**: High demands (Word of Wisdom, tithing, temple
  worthiness), distinctive identity markers (garments), boundaries.
- **Church-like maturity**: Global organization, professionalized
  (unpaid) clergy, established social presence.

### The Garment Example
Dawson, referencing a New York Times article on LDS garments: "If those
garments went away altogether, that would be one of the symbolic and
physical markers of being part of the church that would be gone.
Sociologically, it would probably weaken the church."

### The Temple Openness Tension
Cardon and Dawson discussed how LDS temples balance:
- **Inclusivity**: Open houses, published photos of interior rooms,
  greater transparency.
- **Distinctiveness**: Restricted access to dedicated spaces preserves
  sacred identity.

Dawson: "The trick is how do you balance these things? How do you become
more transparent and open and less divisive and more inclusive yet
maintain the integrity of your identity?"

### The Irony of Discomfort
Dawson: "Being uncomfortable about your faith is in part actually a
marker that probably you've got something worthwhile going on. If you can
just talk freely about every aspect of your faith on the subway to any
old buddy, then really in a sense it probably means there's nothing
particularly special or unique or distinctive about what you're
believing."

## Early Christianity as an NRM
Dawson's classroom framing: "Christianity started as a new religious
movement. You have one guy named Jesus, 12 guys around him, miracles.
From the perspective of a Roman citizen, it would look just as strange,
just as weird, just as outlandish" as any modern NRM.

Every world religion was once a new religious movement.

## Apologetic Application
- When accused of being a "cult," ask: "In what sociological sense?"
  The church-sect-cult typology is descriptive, not evaluative.
- The LDS Church is sociologically a **sect** (high-demand, voluntary,
  distinctive) trending toward **church** status — this trajectory
  mirrors early Christianity.
- Strong boundaries and distinctive practices are sociologically
  **normal and healthy** for religious vitality — not evidence of
  pathology.
