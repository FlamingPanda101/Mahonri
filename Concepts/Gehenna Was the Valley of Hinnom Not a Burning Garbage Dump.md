# Gehenna Was the Valley of Hinnom Not a Burning Garbage Dump

## The Claim

**Gehenna** — the New Testament word most often translated "hell" — was not a metaphysical realm but a literal place: the **Valley of the Son of Hinnom** (*Gay Ben Hinnom*), a real valley sweeping south and southeast around Mount Zion past the City of David. [^1] It became a symbol of wickedness and punishment because it was the site of **Tophet**, where pre-exilic kings "made their children to pass through the fire" (child sacrifice). [^2] McClellan flags as a **myth** the widespread claim that in Jesus's day Gehenna was a perpetually-burning garbage dump where refuse and criminals' bodies were thrown: there are "no archaeological or textual data that actually support that." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A real, locatable valley
Gehenna is the Greco-Roman-period form of Hebrew *Gay Ben Hinnom*. The valley runs around the south of Mount Zion, meeting the central valley and the Kidron Valley on either side of the City of David. McClellan: "I was just in that valley a couple weeks ago. It's a lovely park down there... Grass, green grass... a little concert venue." [^4]

### 2. Tophet and child sacrifice made it a byword for evil
In the Hebrew Bible, Tophet (in this valley) is where pre-exilic Israelite kings burned their children in sacrifice. To the exilic and post-exilic tradents curating these texts, the place was associated with "unspeakable evil." Because it combined wickedness, child sacrifice, and burning, it became "a symbolic location for wickedness and punishment" in the Hellenistic period. [^2]

### 3. The "perpetually burning landfill" claim is a late myth
A popular apologetic and homiletic claim holds that Gehenna was the city dump — fires always burning, bodies of criminals tossed in — so Jesus's "Gehenna" simply pointed to a familiar local horror. McClellan rebuts this directly: **no archaeological or textual evidence supports it**, and "we don't see anybody making those claims until many centuries after the time of Jesus." [^3]

### 4. Gehenna's punitive sense is borrowed, then literary
The shift from *valley* to *place of postmortem punishment* runs through the Hellenistic-period literature. The Book of Enoch references a deep, dark, cursed valley by Jerusalem where rebellious entities are reserved for punishment — "the first... suggestion that this is a place for eternal postmortem punishment." [^5] (See [[Book of Enoch - Origin of Hell and Judgment Imagery]].) The eternal-fire imagery itself traces back to [[Isaiah 66.24|Isaiah 66:24]] — "their worm shall not die, their fire shall not be quenched" — picked up in the Gospels' Gehenna sayings (e.g. Mark 9:48).

## LDS Relevance

The Gehenna myth is a textbook case of an emotionally vivid "biblical hell" that turns out to rest on no ancient evidence — a pattern Latter-day Saints can wield against the assumption that eternal-torment infernalism is plainly the New Testament's teaching. If the very word translated "hell" in the Gospels is really the name of a Jerusalem valley, freighted with later symbolism and later legend, then the literalist lake-of-fire reading is far less secure than supposed. This supports the LDS reframing of "hell" as largely figurative and remedial (compare [[English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell]], [[Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell - DC 76 and DC 18]]).
