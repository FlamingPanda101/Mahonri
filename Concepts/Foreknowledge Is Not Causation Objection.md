# Foreknowledge Is Not Causation Objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The most common creedal response to the predestination/creation-dilemma argument is: **divine foreknowledge is not divine causation.** "Just because God knows what's going to happen doesn't mean he caused it to happen." God can perfectly foreknow your free choices without determining them, so He is not responsible for your damnation — you are.[^1] [[Ben Kaiser]] presses the same point to Carroll: if foreknowledge isn't causation, then in the six-child analogy "the parents aren't creating the rejection any more than God is," so the child's choice stays free.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The argument does not require causation — only the avoidable creative choice — rate: strong.** [[Hayden Carroll]] grants the objection outright: "You don't need causation for the argument to work."[^3] The dilemma locates the *first moral act* not in the creature's rebellion but in God's decision to create a being He foreknows will be damned, when He was under no obligation and lost nothing by abstaining (see [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]]). Foreknowledge supplies the *knowledge* condition for moral responsibility; the free, avoidable act of creating supplies the *agency* condition. Causation of the sin itself is irrelevant. **Steelman of the objector:** the creedal theist can argue that creating libertarian agents is a distinct good whose value can justify the risk — i.e., God wills the *good of agency*, and the damnation is a foreseen-but-not-intended side effect (the doctrine of double effect). Carroll's reply: double effect requires the good to outweigh the evil and the evil not be a means; *eternal* torment with no redemptive payoff fails the proportionality test.
2. **[[Luke Hansen]] reframes the objection as a covert denial of sovereignty — rate: incisive.** When the Christian says "knowledge isn't causation," they are really affirming that the outcome is *not fully within God's creative control* — which is a limitation on premise #2 of [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]. So the objection, taken seriously, dissolves classical sovereignty rather than rescuing it.[^1]
3. **Kaiser's [[John 9.1-3|John 9:1-3]] appeal cuts the other way — rate: moderate.** Kaiser cites Jesus' answer that the man's blindness was caused by *neither his sin nor his parents'* as a model for separating suffering from sin/causation.[^2] This rebuts the idea that suffering implies prior guilt, but it does not touch the creation dilemma, which is about God's *upstream* choice to create at all — not about assigning blame for a particular affliction. The verse is more relevant to LDS pre-existence (see [[Pre-Existence Implied by John 9]]) than to the predestination problem.
