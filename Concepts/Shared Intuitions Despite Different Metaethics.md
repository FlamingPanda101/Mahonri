# Shared Intuitions Despite Different Metaethics

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]]: what the three interlocutors share when they recoil at [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21]] "is not a metaethical theory at all. We have quite different metaethical theories and very similar moral intuitions." Those shared intuitions "have a history behind them" — and that history is Christianization.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Convergent intuitions, divergent foundations.** A Christian moral realist (Scrivener), a Christian host (Brierley), and an atheist [[Ethical Emotivism as O'Connor's Metaethic|emotivist]] (O'Connor) all feel the same outrage at biblical slave-beating. The agreement is at the level of *intuition*, not *theory*.[^1] *(Strong observation — it relocates the debate from "whose metaethics is right" to "where do our shared intuitions come from," which is the actual historical question.)*

2. **O'Connor grants the historical shaping.** Asked if his outrage is "a consequence of the Christian culture in which you've been raised," and whether he'd feel differently born elsewhere: "I have no doubt."[^2] *(Major concession — see [[The Recoil from OT Violence Is Itself Christian]]. O'Connor concedes the *contingency* of his intuitions while denying it settles *uniqueness/necessity*.)*

3. **The grounding question is set aside.** Brierley wants to probe whether the Christian story is a *better grounding* than emotivism; O'Connor repeatedly defers this as tangential to the historical thesis. See [[Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls]].[^3] *(The participants agree to bracket the metaethical-grounding dispute in favor of the historical-causation dispute.)*

4. **Steelman of O'Connor.** That all three share Christianized intuitions does not prove the intuitions *could only* be Christian — it proves they were *shaped within* a Christian culture, which is the uncontroversial "influence" claim, not the contested "unthinkable without" claim. See [[Unthinkable Without Christianity vs Blossoming Thesis]].[^2] *(Fair — the shared-intuition point supports historical influence robustly but cannot, by itself, establish exclusivity.)*

## Best Quotes

> "What we three share at that point is not a metaethical theory at all. We have quite different metaethical theories and very similar moral intuitions."[^1]

> "Those moral intuitions have a history behind them — and that's why we're having the debate we're having."[^1]

> "I have no doubt." — O'Connor, on whether his moral emotions are a product of Christian culture[^2]
