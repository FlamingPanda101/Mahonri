# Belief as Acceptance - The Mental Act of Grasping a Proposition as True

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that the ordinary distinction between "belief" and "knowledge" is misleading: knowing that "George Washington was the first president" and believing the same sentence "amount to the same thing," differing only in degrees of certainty along a single spectrum.[^1] More fundamentally, belief is not a static thing stored in the mind but a **present-tense act of acceptance** — "the act of grasping, not the thing grasped."[^2] To believe a proposition is the *disposition to accept it as true*, and it is this acceptance ("Your car is being towed") that drives behavior and emotion.[^2]

## Evidence
Harris draws on the OED's second sense of belief — "mental acceptance of a proposition, statement, or fact as true, on the ground of authority or evidence" — and says this is "exactly what we are after," as opposed to sense 3 ("the thing believed").[^3] He argues beliefs are not stored as discrete propositions or structures in the brain: the claim "Starbucks does not sell plutonium" is one most people would wager on, yet they had almost certainly never entertained it before, so no pre-existing structure could correspond to it.[^4] Accepting it is "merely [to] put a name to the summation" of many present processes — episodic memory, semantic knowledge, inductive reasoning, assumptions about economic incentives.[^4] Belief in this sense — accepting linguistic propositions whose source seems reliable — is, Harris says, how we acquire "everything we know outside of our personal experience."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Knowing that George Washington was the first president of the United States and believing the statement 'George Washington was the first president of the United States' amount to the same thing." [^1]

> "'Belief,' therefore, can be thought of as a process taking place in the present; it is the act of grasping, not the thing grasped." [^2]

## Apologetic Engagement
Much of Harris's analysis is uncontroversial and even congenial to a Latter-day Saint epistemology: belief comes in degrees of confidence (cf. [[Know and Believe Mark Points on a Confidence Spectrum]]) and is an active reaching toward what seems real, not a brute datum. The point of friction is reductive. Harris's account is *normatively* sound only because it smuggles in non-natural items — truth, evidence, the *should* of accepting what the evidence warrants — that bare matter does not supply. If belief is just a neural disposition, why should one disposition be "valid" and another defective? That presupposes an objective standard of reason and truth to which minds are answerable. Theists argue such reliable, truth-tracking reason fits a mind made in the image of a rational Creator far better than blind evolutionary tuning for survival (the argument from reason; cf. [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]], [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]). A purely faith-versus-knowledge caricature of religious belief is also rebutted here: the Restored Gospel treats belief as an evidence-responsive act of acceptance that can grow into knowledge, not a separate, evidence-immune category (cf. [[Anatomy of Belief - Three Independent Conversations]]).
