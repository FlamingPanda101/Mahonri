# Comparative Anatomy and Homology as Evidence for Common Ancestry

## The Point

**Homology** — similarity between structures (such as bones) shared by different organisms *because they inherited them from a common ancestor* — is, [[Seth M. Bybee]] argues, the classic anatomical signature of evolution, and it applies straightforwardly to humans. If humans share a common ancestor with other vertebrates, their anatomy should be built on the same plan, and it is: the human arm contains the same set of bones as a bat wing, a whale flipper, and a bird wing. [^1] The prediction can be pushed further — our anatomy should resemble apes more than distant relatives, and the *hominin fossil record* (forms arising after the chimp–human split) should resemble us most of all — and that is exactly the pattern observed. [^2]

## Evidence

- **The tetrapod limb.** Comparing the human arm with even distant relatives (bats, whales, birds) reveals "the same set of arm bones," a homology conserved across vertebrates and best explained by common ancestry. [^1]
- **A graded pattern toward humans.** Anatomical similarity to humans is greater in the great apes than in distant relatives, and "sharpens and becomes stronger" in the hominin fossil record — showing observable transitions in foot, leg, pelvis, back, shoulders, skull, jaw, and brain toward modern traits (e.g. loss of arboreal features, upright posture). [^2]
- **Not just bones.** The same homology extends to tissues and organs (brain size and complexity) and to genetics. [^3]
- **Ontogeny.** Development reveals the same homologous structures across distantly related species: human embryos have a set of **gills** (built by the same genes that build gills in fish, later becoming the ear) and a **well-formed tail** (later reduced to the coccyx). [^4]
- **Vestigial traits.** Reduced grasping muscles (absent in ~10% of humans, present in all apes), the diminished appendix, the coccyx, and rare human tails are functional leftovers of ancestral structures, best explained as evolutionary remnants. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "If we compare the bones in the human arm with other living relatives—even distant relatives such as bats, whales, birds, and so forth—we should share the same set of arm bones, and we do... because we share a common ancestor with all other vertebrate animals." [^1]

> "Although uncommon, humans are sometimes born with tails, demonstrating that the genetics for this once fully functional structure are still present in our DNA." [^6]

## LDS Connection

For Bybee these homologies describe the assembly of the **physical body**, which Latter-day Saint thought can affirm was formed "from the dust" — i.e. from the materials and lineage of the physical creation — without surrendering the conviction that humanity was made on purpose and in God's image (see [[A Body from the Physical Creation Joined to a Premortal Spirit]]). The shared body plan does not, on his account, touch the questions of the spirit, of Adam and Eve, or of human worth before God, which lie outside what comparative anatomy can address. The same evolved attributes that anatomy traces — brain complexity especially — are, in his reading, part of what makes the body a fit dwelling for a spirit "with godlike potential." [^7]
