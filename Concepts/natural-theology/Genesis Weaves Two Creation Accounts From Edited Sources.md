# Genesis Weaves Two Creation Accounts From Edited Sources

## The Point

Genesis does not contain a single, unified creation account but two accounts woven together by a later inspired editor — the first running Genesis 1:1–2:3, the second beginning at 2:4. They differ in particulars (in the first, male and female are created simultaneously; in the second, the woman is formed after the man and after the animals; the seven "days" belong to the first, the Garden of Eden to the second). [^1] For Shannon this is not threatening: Latter-day Saints already understand scripture (the Book of Mormon, the JST, the Doctrine and Covenants) as inspired records compiled and redacted from earlier sources. Genesis "is an ancient record that draws on previous sources to produce an inspired record." [^2] Because there are two accounts, "neither presents a single authoritative statement on how humanity came into this world." [^3]

## Evidence

- Scholars identify two accounts joined by a redactor; Latter-day Saints, who "believe the Bible to be the word of God," can hold that editor to be inspired. [^1]
- The JST frames the creation as a third-person retelling of revelations given to Moses (note the aside to Joseph Smith in Moses 1:42), not a first-person Mosaic autograph; Hebrew did not exist as a language until centuries after Moses, so Moses could not have written Genesis in its present form. [^4]
- Just as Mormon compiled and glossed authentic material from Alma, "the five books of Moses, including Genesis, seem to have been composed and compiled from authentic material deriving from Moses and other earlier prophetic sources." [^2]
- Having two accounts reinforces reading Genesis "in its ancient context, as an ancient Israelite would," and reminds readers that the LDS Church is "not committed to a specific literal interpretation of Creation in Genesis." [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "Like the Book of Mormon, Genesis is an ancient record that draws on previous sources to produce an inspired record." [^2]

> "When we speak of the biblical perspective of Creation, we are not speaking of a single perspective with a single goal. . . . [Neither account] presents a single authoritative statement on how humanity came into this world." [^3]

## LDS Connection

A composite, edited Genesis is fully compatible with revelation: [[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin|editing and compiling sources is how scripture stays relevant in every dispensation]] (cf. A of F 1:8–9). Because no single creation narrative is authoritative on *mechanism*, the question of *how* humanity came to be is left open — leaving room for evolution.
