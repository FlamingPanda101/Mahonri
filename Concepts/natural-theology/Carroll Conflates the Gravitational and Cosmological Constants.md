# Carroll Conflates the Gravitational and Cosmological Constants

## The Claim
Atheists (Westbrook citing [[Sean Carroll]]) answer the gravitational-constant fine-tuning by replaying Carroll's debate clip: the naive "one part in 10^60" estimate for the early universe's expansion rate is wrong, and "if you ask the same question using the correct equations" of general relativity, "the probability is one" — almost all early-universe cosmologies have the right expansion rate. [[Trent Horn]] says he (and other physicists) cannot reproduce what Carroll means, and that the clip equivocates: Carroll is asked about **G**, the gravitational constant (the intrinsic strength of gravity between bodies, fixed by Newton in 1680), but answers about the **expansion rate**, which is governed by a *different* constant — the cosmological constant Λ (the strength of gravity in empty space). So Carroll "talks about the gravitational constant, then he turns to Sean Carroll to talk about the expansion rate." Horn cites physicist Michael Strauss saying the same thing, and notes Barnes and Lewis do not even *need* G to make the fine-tuning case.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong)** G and Λ are genuinely distinct quantities; conflating "the gravitational constant is fine-tuned" with "the expansion rate has probability one" is a real category slip, independently flagged by Michael Strauss, who "spent about an hour looking for any papers" giving an expansion rate of one and could not find them.[^1]
2. **(Strong, redundancy)** Even if G were granted as not-fine-tuned, the case stands: Barnes and Lewis omit G from their calculations entirely, so the argument does not hinge on it.[^2] See [[Luke Barnes]].
3. **(Strong, cosmological constant)** The cosmological constant Λ is, on all sides, the hardest case: changing it "by a mere one part in 10^120" makes the universe expand too fast or too slow. Non-theist physicists agree it is severe — Alexander Vilenkin calls it "the most notorious and perplexing case of fine-tuning in physics," Leonard Susskind says "statistically miraculous events" would otherwise be needed, and Lawrence Krauss calls it "perhaps the most profound unsolved fundamental problem in physics."[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A defender of Carroll could argue that *if* cosmic inflation occurred, it drives the mass-energy density to the flat (Ω≈1) value naturally, so the expansion-rate tuning is *explained dynamically* rather than being a brute coincidence — which is a substantive physical point, not mere equivocation. Krauss separately argues a cosmological constant of exactly zero would be "more natural" and more life-conducive, so the small nonzero value is not obviously evidence of design.

## Counter-objection
The inflation reply does not dissolve the problem; it relocates it. As Strauss notes, "inflation itself has to be finely tuned in order to do that — it has to turn on at the right time... turn off at the right time," so the tuning reappears one level down ([[The Multiverse Cannot Escape Prior Fine-Tuning]] makes the structurally identical point about ensembles). And Krauss's "zero is more natural" claim is doubly weak: there is no value the constant would "naturally" take (so "more natural" is undefined), and a recent paper argues a *small nonzero* Λ is in fact more conducive to life than zero. Most decisively, fine-tuning is about *narrowness amid a broad range*, so even if Λ could be *more* life-permitting at some other value, the fact that its actual value falls inside a ludicrously narrow life-permitting band — when it "should be much, much larger" — is exactly what cries out for explanation. The thermostat illustration: a dial settable from 70° to 10,000,000° that reads 80° is fine-tuned even though 70° would be more pleasant.[^3]

## LDS Connection
Purely physics-and-philosophy-of-science; no distinctively LDS content. Its vault value is to inoculate against a frequently-circulated "a real physicist debunked this" clip by locating the precise equivocation (G vs. Λ) and showing the cosmological-constant case is conceded as severe even by atheist physicists.
