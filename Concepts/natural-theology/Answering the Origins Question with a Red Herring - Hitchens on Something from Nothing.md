# Answering the Origins Question with a Red Herring - Hitchens on Something from Nothing

## The Point
Asked the metaphysical question "how do you get something from nothing without cause," [[Christopher Hitchens]] replied by pointing to stars and galaxies "blowing up and bursting into flame," then asking who authored *that* [^1]. [[Alex OConnor]] shows this is "much worse than" a *tu quoque*: it is a **red herring** — not "you can't explain it either," but "you can't explain this *other, unrelated* thing" [^2]. The question is how things *came into being*; Hitchens answers by noting that things *come to an end* — "it just doesn't answer the question" [^3]. O'Connor also flags the technical error: when stars explode, the matter still exists, merely rearranged, so this is not genuinely "something from nothing" and is "an inappropriate analogy" [^4]. And Hitchens, who bristled when *his* questions were answered with questions, here answers a question with a question — the very move he disliked [^5].

## Evidence
- O'Connor's pyramids analogy: it is as if, asked how the Egyptians built the pyramids, someone answered, "well, erosion will destroy them eventually, so they did a pretty poor job" — "that's not an answer to the question, totally unrelated" [^6].
- The self-defeating dilemma: Hitchens first mocks the religious view because the universe is "allowed to come to destruction"; then, shown the Christian belief that God *intervenes* before cosmic death (Judgment Day), he calls *that* "fatuous." O'Connor: "both the universe ending and not ending are ridiculous according to Hitchens" — leaving no coherent alternative [^7].
- This forces the issue back to the start: to claim a *better*, non-ridiculous account, "Hitchens... must address the question of how the universe could come into being without a designer. Notice... this is the very question we started with that Hitchens refused to even try to answer" [^8].

## Best Quotes
> "It would be a tu quoque fallacy if [the Christian] had said 'Hitchens can't explain how something comes from nothing' and Hitchens said 'yeah, well you can't explain it either.'... Instead Hitchens responds by saying 'yeah, well you can't explain this *other unrelated thing*.'" [^2]

> "Both the universe ending and not ending are ridiculous according to Hitchens... this whole conversation is a trainwreck." [^7]

## Apologetic Engagement
The cosmological/contingency arguments (see [[The Leibnizian Cosmological Argument from Contingency]]) ask why there is something rather than nothing and what sustains contingent being. O'Connor — again, an atheist — shows that one of the New Atheist movement's most quoted figures never engaged that question, swapping it for a rhetorically vivid but logically irrelevant observation about cosmic decay, and then ruling *both* horns of the only available dilemma "ridiculous" while offering no third option. That is a clean illustration of why "winning the room" is not "answering the argument" ([[Winning the Debate Is Not Winning the Argument]]). Two cautions, in fairness: (1) O'Connor is *not* endorsing the theistic conclusion — a serious atheist would actually engage the metaphysics (e.g., contest the causal premise, appeal to quantum cosmology or a brute fact), which is exactly what Hitchens failed to do; and (2) the apologist must not commit the mirror error of Hitchens' interlocutor — note that "what caused God?" is itself often a straw man ([[What Caused God - The Hume-Russell Straw Man]]), and the contingency argument must be made carefully, not as a slogan. The takeaway is that the origins question deserves a real metaphysical answer from *both* sides; a charismatic dodge is not one.
