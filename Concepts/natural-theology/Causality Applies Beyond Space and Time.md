# Causality Applies Beyond Space and Time

## The Point
Turek argues that atheists borrow the law of causality to do science and to build arguments, then attack it the moment a cause points to God.[^1] Two atheist evasions fail by their own standard. First, Krauss's claim that "every... physical effect... has a physical cause" is **self-refuting**: it would mean either Krauss's non-physical mind couldn't cause his physical book, or his mind is merely his brain — in which case his thoughts are determined "meat machine" reactions with no claim to truth.[^2] Second, the claim that causality "only applies to physical things in space-time" is false, because the premises and conclusions of arguments are immaterial yet causally related — "to deny causality beyond space and time would be to deny logic."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Science presupposes causality.** "At the foundation of science is the law of causality... Science is, at a minimum, a search for causes." If things can come into existence without causes, "how can anyone do science?" Krauss hedges — "all things that begin *may* have a cause" — only when the effect implies theism.[^1]
2. **The "all causes are physical" claim self-destructs.** If every physical effect needs a physical cause, then either (a) Krauss's mind is immaterial — contradicting the claim, since an immaterial mind produced his physical book; or (b) his mind just *is* his brain — in which case his book's contents are fully determined by physics and "we wouldn't have any reason to believe that anything in his book is true."[^2]
3. **Materialism dissolves reason.** "As meat machines completely determined by the laws of physics, we cannot reason; we can only react." Quoting Doug Wilson: "We are no different than a can of Coke fizzing." "To know you're just a robot, you'd have to be more than a robot."[^4]
4. **Causality is metaphysical, not merely physical.** Aristotle's distinction: Krauss disputes *material* causality (what a thing is made of); the radio-debate atheist disputes *efficient* causality (the primary source of an effect). But "true premises result in valid conclusions" — a causal relation among non-physical things. "If the law of causality only applied to physical things, then no argument would work because premises and conclusions are not physical things."[^3]
5. **Denying causality is self-defeating.** Ask any doubter, "What caused you to come to that conclusion?" Any evidence or reasoning marshaled against causality must itself *use* causality. "Any denial of the law of causality uses the law of causality."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "We are no different than a can of Coke fizzing." (Doug Wilson, quoted by Turek) [^4]

> "To know you're just a robot, you'd have to be more than a robot." [^4]

> "To deny causality beyond space and time would be to deny logic, which would be self-defeating and would negate our ability to argue anything." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Turek's defense of reason against materialist determinism aligns squarely with Latter-day Saint thought: if every thought were a physically determined reaction, moral agency and rational evaluation collapse — exactly the atheistic implication LDS apologists also press. The Restoration grounds genuine agency in the eternal, uncreated *intelligence* of each person ("intelligence... was not created or made," D&C 93:29; cf. [[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]]), so a Latter-day Saint embraces Turek's anti-determinism without needing his ex-nihilo metaphysics. One nuance: where Turek treats causality as a brute metaphysical law the cosmos *received* from God, LDS theology tends to view such law as co-eternal with God rather than authored from nothing by Him — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]].
