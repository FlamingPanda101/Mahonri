# Atheism Undercuts the Rationality Science Requires

## The Point
Against Dawkins' thesis that "science supports atheism, not Christianity," [[John Lennox]] argues the reverse: atheism undermines the very rationality on which science depends. Every scientist must "believe in the rational intelligibility of the universe before you can do any science at all" — yet "science doesn't give you that"; it is a presupposition science assumes but cannot establish.[^1] Worse, on [[Richard Dawkins|Dawkins]]' own naturalism human cognition is the product of "unguided evolution," so "it's unlikely that our view of the world is accurate." If a reductionist materialist holds that "my scientific theories are the result ultimately of the motions of atoms in my brain produced by an unguided random mindless process," then, Lennox asks, "why should I believe them?"[^2] This is the [[The Argument from Reason - Atheism Undermines Reason Itself|argument from reason]].

## Evidence
Lennox's image is a man "sitting on the branch of a tree cutting off the branch on which they're sitting": atheism "actually undercuts the scientific endeavor very seriously," a "fatal flaw."[^3] He sharpens it into a charge of incoherence: "An argument that purports to derive rationality from irrationality doesn't even rise in my opinion to the dignity of being an intelligible delusion. It is logically incoherent."[^4] Theism, by contrast, explains why science works at all — "the same God who created the universe is ultimately responsible for the human mind." Lennox notes that scientists "of the eminence of Eugene Wigner and Albert Einstein use the word faith" for their trust in the universe's intelligibility.[^5] He also accuses Dawkins of a category mistake: confusing *mechanism* with *agency*. Newton, on discovering the law of gravity, did not conclude "I've got a mechanism, therefore I don't need God"; his praise for God increased, because "the sophistication of the mechanism... is evidence for the sheer wonder of the creative genius of God."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "An argument that purports to derive rationality from irrationality doesn't even rise in my opinion to the dignity of being an intelligible delusion. It is logically incoherent." [^4]

> "You're making a category mistake because you're confusing mechanism with agency. We have a mechanism... therefore there's no need for an agent. I would suggest that the sophistication of the mechanism... is evidence for the sheer wonder of the creative genius of God." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology gives the argument from reason firm metaphysical footing because it denies that mind is reducible to matter. "The intelligence of spirits... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" ([[D&C 93]]:29), and "there is no such thing as immaterial matter; all spirit is matter" of a "more fine or pure" kind ([[D&C 131]]:7–8) — refined substance, not nonrational mechanism. Because human intelligence is uncreated and partakes of "the light of truth" that "was not created or made" (D&C 93:29), our reasoning is not the accidental byproduct of colliding atoms but a feature of beings who share in the intelligence of God (D&C 93:36). This grounds exactly the trustworthy rationality Lennox says atheism cannot supply. See also [[Theism Makes Science Possible]].
