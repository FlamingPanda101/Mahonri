# Personal Revelation Is the Most Legitimate Path to God

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Asked what would make him believe in God, [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] declines the usual atheist demand for a public miracle and instead affirms that "one of the most legitimate ways to come to believe in God, if not *the* most legitimate way, is through... personal experience and revelation" [^1]. He grants it is non-transferable — "it's not the kind of thing that's going to convince another person... that's why it's laughed out of the room in debates" — but insists "that's not supposed to convince anybody"; as far as it convinces *you*, "it's like the most legitimate way imaginable" [^2]. The narrator's claim: this is *precisely* the restored gospel's epistemology, which creedal apologetics, leaning on argument, lacks.

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence
1. **Arguments are the climate, not the cause.** The narrator's thesis: "rational arguments may maintain a climate in which belief may flourish, but it is a direct experience with God that ultimately convinces us" [^3]. Rating: **a strong epistemic distinction** echoing Plantinga's "properly basic" belief and Pascal's "reasons of the heart"; O'Connor (an agnostic) independently arriving at it gives the point unusual rhetorical weight.
2. **The elitism objection to natural theology.** O'Connor argues that if reading the *Blackwell Companion to Natural Theology* and parsing the Leibnizian cosmological argument were the route to God, then God would be "a pretty unfair administrator of his love" — gating salvation behind intellect, schooling, money, and leisure [^4][^5]. Rating: **forceful** — it is a moral argument *against* making philosophy the gateway, and it dovetails exactly with the LDS claim that revelation is available to every sincere seeker regardless of education.
3. **Scriptural anchor.** The Lord's words to [[Oliver Cowdery]]: "cast your mind upon the night that you cried unto me in your heart... Did I not speak peace to your mind concerning the matter?... What greater witness can you have than from God?" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 6.22-23|D&C 6:22-23]]) [^6]. Rating: **directly on point** — it makes the felt, internal witness the *highest* evidence, matching O'Connor's "most legitimate way."
4. **The experiential analogy.** "Just like how we can't know a song by somebody telling us about it, we can't know God until we experience him" [^7]. Rating: **apt illustration** of acquaintance-knowledge vs. propositional knowledge.
5. **Steelman the skeptic's worry.** The obvious counter (which O'Connor himself half-raises): an epistemology grounded in private, non-transferable experience is **unfalsifiable and indiscriminate** — Muslims, Hindus, and Latter-day Saints all report confirming spiritual experiences, so the method cannot adjudicate *between* religions and risks confirming whatever one already wants. It also makes religious knowledge immune to public scrutiny by design. The narrator partly concedes the discrimination problem and addresses it via the discernment-of-spirits discussion (see [[Praying to Know Truth and the Discernment-of-Spirits Objection]]). Honest tension: granting personal revelation as *the* path buys universality and humility at the cost of public verifiability and inter-religious arbitration.
