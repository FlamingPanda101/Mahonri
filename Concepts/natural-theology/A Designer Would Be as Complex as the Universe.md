# A Designer Would Be as Complex as the Universe

## The Point
**Objection:** Going back to David Hume and pressed hard by Richard Dawkins: a designer of the universe would be at least as complex as the universe it designs, so it would require a cause of its own — adding the designer merely multiplies what needs explaining, making a more parsimonious naturalistic account likelier.[^1] **Reply:** This objection only touches Paley-style "design arguments" that reason inductively from the *complexity* of artifacts to a designer; Feser's proofs are not of that kind at all.[^2] And the deepest point: Feser *agrees* with Hume and Dawkins that any complex cause would need a cause — which is exactly why his arguments conclude to something "absolutely simple or noncomposite," not complex.[^3]

## Evidence
- Paley / Intelligent Design arguments start from biological complexity (the eye, the bacterial flagellum), note the analogy to human artifacts, and infer a designer by probability. Feser's proofs "simply have nothing at all to do with 'design arguments' of this sort."[^2]
- His arguments do not appeal to "complexity" in the relevant sense: the Neo-Platonic proof begins merely from a thing's being *composite* — "a pebble or a drop of water or any other relatively simple object" works as a starting point, something a design theorist would never use.[^4]
- They concern hierarchically ordered causes operating "here and now," not a linear series tracing to a temporal origin like the origin of a species; and they aim at "strict metaphysical demonstration, not ... mere exercises in inductive or abductive reasoning." So objecting that some alternative is "more probable" or "more parsimonious" misses the point.[^5]
- Most importantly, "all of the arguments defended in this book would agree with Hume and Dawkins that a cause of the world which was itself complex would require a cause of its own. That is why they conclude that the ultimate explanation of things must be something absolutely simple."[^3] The God so reached has no parts to be assembled, and so is not "anything Hume, Dawkins, or any other atheist would posit as an alternative terminus."[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "All of the arguments defended in this book would agree with Hume and Dawkins that a cause of the world which was itself complex would require a cause of its own. That is why they conclude that the ultimate explanation of things must be something absolutely simple or noncomposite rather than complex." [^3]

> "A pebble or a drop of water or any other relatively simple object which in the normal course of things obviously comes about through natural, unintelligent causes would for the Neo-Platonist do as well as any other example as a starting point for the proof." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This reply is doubly useful to a Latter-day Saint apologist. First, it refuses the weak "god of the gaps" / Paley posture that critics love to attack and instead argues from metaphysical necessity (compare [[What a Proof of God Is - Demonstration vs God of the Gaps]] and [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]]). Second, it cleanly answers the schoolyard taunt "if everything needs a cause, what caused God?" by distinguishing what is composite/begins from what is uncaused. The classical-theist assumption that diverges from the Restoration is the *means* of the answer: Feser escapes the regress only by positing **divine simplicity** — God as utterly partless, with no real distinction even between His attributes (see [[The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One]] and [[The Argument from Composition - Whatever Has Parts Needs a Composer]]). LDS theology pictures God as an embodied, exalted Person, not a metaphysically simple essence, and locates the "stopping point" of explanation in eternal, uncreated realities rather than in absolute simplicity (see [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]). Be fair to Feser: his point that the terminus of explanation cannot itself be one more composite thing in need of assembly is a real insight the Saint can affirm — the disagreement is whether "absolutely simple" is the only way to be unassembled.
