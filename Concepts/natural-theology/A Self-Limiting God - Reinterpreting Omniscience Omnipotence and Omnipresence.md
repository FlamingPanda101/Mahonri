# A Self-Limiting God - Reinterpreting Omniscience, Omnipotence, and Omnipresence

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] holds that the three classical "omni" attributes "get us into trouble" and were "just imported from Christianity" into early LDS usage. He reinterprets each around a **self-limiting, embodied, agency-respecting God**: (1) **omniscience** — God need not know the future, because "if free will is real, then the future doesn't exist yet, and so there isn't any future to be known"; (2) **omnipotence** — God "is self-limiting, because if he's weeping then things aren't unfolding in the way that he would like... so he limits himself and allows agency"; (3) **omnipresence** — "God can't be omnipresent in the body," so it is "the spirit... as this element that fills the universe" that "effectively fulfills that function," not the Father's body.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The open-future argument — rate: a substantive philosophical position.** Givens finds "the argument very compelling that if free will is real then the future doesn't exist yet." This is open theism's core claim; the vault treats it sympathetically at [[Open Theism and a Self-Surpassing God]] and [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]], and the incompatibility premise at [[Foreknowledge and Free Will Are Logically Incompatible]]. He frames not-knowing-the-future as no defect, since there is no fact-of-the-matter to know.[^1]
2. **Weeping entails self-limitation — rate: a tight internal inference.** The argument ties back to [[Moses 7|Moses 7]]: a God who *weeps* over wickedness is a God for whom "things aren't unfolding in the way that he would like them to unfold." An impassible, all-determining God would have nothing to weep about. So divine grief *implies* a God who has limited his own power to make room for agency. This is the affective complement to the philosophical case.[^2]
3. **Omnipresence through the Spirit, not the body — rate: coherent within LDS metaphysics.** Because the Father has "a body of flesh and bones" ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]), he is spatially located; ubiquity is achieved by "the spirit of Christ as this element that fills the universe." This preserves both embodiment and cosmic presence. See [[God in Heaven With a Physical Body Implies a Physical Heaven]].[^2]
4. **Honest about the move — rate: candid.** Givens concedes the omni-terms "were used in the first year of the church," granting they are early LDS inheritances from creedal vocabulary rather than original Restoration coinages — then argues they should be qualified, not simply asserted.[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing (classical-theist) side:** Each reinterpretation purchases relevance at the price of perfection. Denying foreknowledge makes God a temporal learner who reacts to events he did not foresee — the "self-surpassing" deity of process theism, not the God of Isaiah 46 ("declaring the end from the beginning"); biblical prophecy presupposes detailed foreknowledge. A "self-limiting" omnipotence concedes God is not the actual ground of all being, and a spatially-located God who needs an auxiliary "element" to be present everywhere is, the classical theist says, a finite being inside the universe, not its transcendent Creator. The standard compatibilist reply is that exhaustive foreknowledge does not *cause* (and so does not negate) free acts (the vault's [[God's Knowledge Does Not Abolish Free Will]]).

**Counter-objection (Givens's / LDS rejoinder):** The compatibilist reply only saves foreknowledge by leaving the *future itself* fixed — and a fixed future is incompatible with libertarian agency regardless of whether God's knowledge "causes" it. Prophecy can be preserved as God's perfect knowledge of present trajectories and his power to act ("a master chess player"), not as reading a settled script. And "finite, located" is a feature: a God genuinely present *in* relationship, who weeps and responds, is more worthy of worship (see [[The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric]]) than an immutable Absolute who can neither learn nor be moved.

## Best Quotes
> "I don't think we believe in an omniscient God in the sense that we necessarily are required to believe that he knows the future. I find the argument very compelling that if free will is real, then the future doesn't exist yet, and so there isn't any future to be known."[^1]

> "There clearly seems to be a sense in which God is self-limiting, because if he's weeping then things aren't unfolding in the way that he would like them to unfold. So he limits himself and allows agency."[^2]

> "God can't be omnipresent in the body... so we fall back on the idea of the spirit of Christ as this element that fills the universe and effectively fulfills that function of omnipresence."[^2]
