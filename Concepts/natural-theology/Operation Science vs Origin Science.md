# Operation Science vs Origin Science

## The Point
Turek distinguishes two kinds of science: **operation (empirical) science**, which studies repeatable, observable processes in real time, and **origin (forensic) science**, which reconstructs unrepeatable historical events from the clues they left behind.[^1] The distinction matters because "science doesn't say anything, scientists do" — and a scientist's worldview affects origin questions far more than operation questions.[^2] Atheists and theists barely disagree about how electricity or engines work; they disagree sharply about the origin of the universe, of first life, and of new life-forms — because those are historical questions settled not by repeatable experiment but by interpretation, where prior philosophical commitments do the heavy lifting.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The two types defined.** "Operation or empirical science involve[s] repeatable events that you can observe in real time. Questions involving origin or forensic science involve historical events that cannot be repeated. For that reason, operation science is usually more certain than origin science."[^1] No one can rerun the origin of the first cell in a lab, just as no detective can resurrect victims and replay a murder — both require a forensic approach.[^4]
2. **Worldview drives the divergence.** Atheists and theists "are both looking at the same evidence. The reason they come to different conclusions is because they start with different philosophical assumptions derived from their worldviews."[^3] Turek illustrates with the O. J. Simpson trial: faced with the same blood evidence, jurors split dramatically along worldview lines — "a person's worldview can affect how he or she gathers and interprets evidence."[^5]
3. **Science is, minimally, a search for causes.** There is little consensus on a demarcation line for "science," but most agree with Francis Bacon that it is a search for causes — and there are only two kinds of efficient cause, "natural and nonnatural (i.e., intelligent)."[^6] Origin science, like archaeology, cryptology, and forensics, legitimately infers intelligent causes; a definition narrow enough to exclude them "even excludes evolution as science."[^7]
4. **The category confusion atheists exploit.** Figures like Krauss, Dawkins, and Atkins "use the prestige of operation science as cover for their guesses about how the world and life originated."[^8] Turek quotes John Lennox: "Statements by scientists are not necessarily statements of science."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "There are two types of science: operation science, which investigates repeatable questions, and origin science, which investigates historical questions." [^1]

> "Worldview differences between atheists and theists don't affect operation questions much — there is vast agreement there — but they do affect origin (historical) questions. That's where all the controversy exists." [^9]

## LDS Connection
This distinction is methodologically useful rather than doctrinally LDS, but it dovetails with a Restoration instinct that not all "science says" claims carry equal weight, and that questions of ultimate origins are open to a divine, intelligent cause. Latter-day Saint scripture frames creation as God *organizing* a cosmos governed by eternal law ([[D&C 88|D&C 88:6–13]]; D&C 130:20–21), and Alma's appeal that "all things denote there is a God... all the planets which move in their regular form do witness that there is a Supreme Creator" ([[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]]) is precisely an origin-science inference to an intelligent cause from observed order — see [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]. The same caution about smuggling philosophy under the banner of "science" appears in biblical studies via [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship]]. Keep the tie honest: operation/origin is a shared apologetic tool, not a uniquely LDS teaching.
