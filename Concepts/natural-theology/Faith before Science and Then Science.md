# Faith before Science and Then Science

## The Point

Ugo A. Perego — a population geneticist who became a seminary teacher, then a Native
American mtDNA researcher, then director of the Rome Institute of Religion — describes a
personal method for reconciling a scientific career with discipleship. When science (or
Church history, or doubt) generates conflict, he does not first try to win the technical
argument; he **recenters on a small set of fundamental questions** about God, Christ,
Joseph Smith, and the restored Church, settles his testimony there, and only then returns
to the science. Hence his ordering: *faith before science, and then science.*[^1] The
arc itself — seminary teacher → scientist → institute director, holding both
simultaneously — is the lived proof that the two vocations need not compete.

## Evidence

- **The fundamental-vs-marginal questions framework.** Perego adopts Elder Lawrence E.
  Corbridge's 2019 BYU devotional distinction between "primary" questions (Is there a God
  and is he our Father? Is Jesus the Son of God and Savior? Was Joseph Smith a prophet?
  Is the Church the kingdom of God?) and the "marginal questions" people "run in circles
  over." Settling the primary questions first "requires diligence, humility, faith, and
  patience."[^2]

- **The method tested under fire.** Reconstructing Joseph Smith's Y-chromosome profile
  (2002), Perego was drawn into the literature on Joseph Smith's plural marriage and was
  "quite disturbed" by sensationalized, biased accounts — "it felt like I was drinking mud
  from an old boot." His response was not to litigate the history first but to stop, spend
  days "reading scriptures, pondering, and pouring out my soul to the Lord," and ask about
  the reality of the First Vision. "The answer came... I felt at peace," and he could
  resume the work — which became a decades-long, fruitful research relationship with the
  Smith family.[^3]

- **Two paths, deliberately held together.** Perego spent roughly equal time in BYU
  buildings named for scientists (Widtsoe, Eyring) and for the Prophet (Joseph Smith);
  earned science degrees while taking *twice* the required religion courses plus a graduate
  minor in ancient scripture; and worked over a decade at the Sorenson Molecular Genealogy
  Foundation while volunteering as an institute instructor. He reports "equal joy and
  satisfaction, spiritually and intellectually," in both.[^4]

- **Intellectual humility as the posture.** Perego frames lack of knowledge as sometimes
  "an act of mercy" that preserves accountability, and quotes BYU–Idaho colleagues:
  "Revealed truth... rarely provides knowledge about how things work... While God could
  choose to reveal anything, He rarely reveals what we can discover on our own."[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Things that I have learned, studied, and researched, and things that I do not have an answer for... have gradually and steadily faded into the background in the face of my relationship with the Divine." [^1]

> "I had to reassess my primary questions. I had to reevaluate where I stood with my personal testimony of the Restoration." [^3]

> "Science is concerned with how we came to be, while faith provides answers about why we are here. Science and religion may be viewed as complementary sides of the same coin." [^6]

## LDS Connection

Perego embodies the volume's central claim that a faithful scientist need not choose. On
organic evolution he notes the Church affirms a historical Adam and Eve who entered a
covenant with God and whose spirits (with all humanity's) are children of heavenly
parents, while taking *no official position* on how their physical bodies were formed or
whether others lived before — leaving that to science and to future revelation
(D&C 101:32–34). The information gap means "there is no real need for conflict."[^6] His
"faith first" ordering is the individual-discipleship counterpart to BYU's institutional
charge to [[Teach the Best Science in the Light of the Gospel]] and to Porter's diagnosis
in [[The Real Faith Crisis Is Being Forced to Choose Between Faith and Science]]. On the
DNA question specifically, see [[Perego - DNA Cannot Settle Book of Mormon Historicity]].
