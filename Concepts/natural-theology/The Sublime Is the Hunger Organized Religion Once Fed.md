# The Sublime Is the Hunger Organized Religion Once Fed

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Robert Greene]] defines the **sublime** (after Longinus, Burke, and Kant) as an experience that takes a person *outside the "circle"* of cultural convention — a peculiar, intense *mix of pleasure and pain* at the threshold (Latin *sub-limen*, "up to the threshold" of the door of death). He argues that humans have a built-in *hunger* for this transcendence; that art and religion were originally *one* enterprise built to deliver it (cave paintings, temples, initiation rites); and that modern "algorithmic," reductive culture *starves* people of it — which is why they turn to drugs, psychedelics, and spectacle in search of a substitute.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The sublime is a real, recurring human structure (medium)
Greene roots the concept in a long philosophical lineage and describes its signature as contrary emotions held at once — Burke's safe-horror, the terror-and-awe of the Alps, Rudolf Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* (which O'Connor supplies). Neurologists, he notes, now study awe and the sublime as a distinctive brain state.[^1]

### 2. Art and religion were originally the same act (medium)
In the ancient world, Greene argues, there was no category "I'm an artist": temples, ziggurats, drama, sculpture, and the 30,000-year-old painted caves of France were built to *recreate a primal shock* — the sacred experienced as a heightened, sublime reality, not as decoration.[^2]

### 3. Modern culture starves and counterfeits the hunger (medium)
Greene contends people are "hungry for religion" but repelled by *organized* religion's perceived rigidity, and that consumer culture feeds them "little jolts" instead. He distinguishes the *false* sublime (porn, alcohol, video games, Marvel films) — addictive states done *to* you by someone else — from the *true* sublime, which must be *cultivated from within* by an act of will (meditation, attention).[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The naturalist reply: the sublime is fully explicable as evolved affect — awe likely evolved to handle vastness, threat, and social hierarchy, and its religious dress is a cultural by-product. That a feeling is *intense*, *universal*, and *hard to verbalize* says nothing about a transcendent referent; nostalgia for premodern "wholeness" romanticizes cultures that were also brutal and ignorant. The hunger is real, but it is a fact about *brains*, not evidence for the sacred.

## Counter-Objection
Greene's framing does not actually *argue* from the sublime to God — and that is the honest limit. But the convergence he and O'Connor describe (a deep, cross-cultural, ineluctable longing that secular consumer culture conspicuously *fails* to satisfy) at minimum reframes the "religion is a crutch" trope: the appetite *predates* and *outlasts* its modern denials, surfacing as drug-seeking and spectacle precisely where religion has withdrawn. A by-product story must still explain why the substitutes leave the hunger intact.

## Apologetic Engagement
Two secular thinkers conceding (a) that humans have a structural hunger for transcendence and (b) that reductive modernity cannot feed it is a useful natural-theology data point — a cousin of the apologetic argument from beauty/music to transcendence (see [[Music Conveys Transcendence Better Explained by Theism]]). The boundary to mark clearly: Greene's *prescription* is pagan/Zen self-cultivation and a critique of "sclerotic" organized religion that a Latter-day Saint will not simply adopt. The apologetic value is the *diagnosis* (a real, God-shaped hunger) rather than Greene's *remedy*.

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration locates this hunger in the literal divine kinship and pre-mortal origin of the human spirit — a homesickness for God's presence that the [[The Temple Endows Power to Build the Kingdom Now Not Only in the Afterlife|temple]] is built to answer through embodied ritual, exactly the "art-and-religion-were-one" instinct Greene describes (cf. [[Medieval Liturgy and Drama Enacted Divine Presence]], [[Worship Is a State of Being That Draws Heaven Down]]). Where Greene says the sublime must be cultivated from within by will, the Restoration says the deepest form is *covenantal* and *given* — drawn down, not merely self-generated.
