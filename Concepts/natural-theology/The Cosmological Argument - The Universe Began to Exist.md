# The Cosmological Argument - The Universe Began to Exist

## The Point
Turek opens the Causality chapter with a Kalam-style cosmological argument: (1) everything that has a beginning has a cause; (2) the universe had a beginning; (3) therefore the universe had a cause.[^1] It is now "uncontroversial among scientists" that the universe — space, time, and matter — began in a "big bang," so much so that even prominent atheists like Stephen Hawking and the agnostic cosmologist Alexander Vilenkin concede a beginning.[^2] The live question, Turek says, "isn't the beginning, but who or what caused the beginning."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **A philosophical argument predating modern science.** Medieval philosophers argued that "today never would have arrived if there were an infinite number of days before today"; since today is here, the past cannot be infinite and the universe must have begun.[^1]
2. **Scientific consensus on a beginning.** Turek cites Hawking ("Almost everyone now believes that the universe and time itself had a beginning at the big bang") and Vilenkin at Hawking's seventieth-birthday celebration ("All the evidence we have says that the universe had a beginning").[^2]
3. **The cause must transcend the effect.** If space, time, and matter began, "the cause must transcend space, time, and matter" — i.e., be spaceless, timeless, and immaterial; it must be enormously powerful to bring the universe into being, and a *personal agent*, because only an agent (not an impersonal law like gravity, which "blindly does the same thing over and over") can *choose* to create.[^4]
4. **Not a "God-of-the-gaps."** The inference rests on what we *do* know (the beginning of space, time, and matter), not on a gap in knowledge; the conclusion "flows logically from the evidence itself." Turek turns the charge around as a "natural law of the gaps fallacy" — faith that an undiscovered natural law will someday explain the beginning.[^5]
5. **Nature cannot be its own cause.** "Since nature had a beginning, nature can't be its own cause. The cause must be beyond nature, which is what we mean by the term 'supernatural.'" Searching for a *natural* cause of *all* nature is impossible in principle, "like me saying, 'Give me more time and I'll discover that I gave birth to my own mother.'"[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Almost everyone now believes that the universe and time itself had a beginning at the big bang." (Stephen Hawking, quoted by Turek) [^2]

> "All the evidence we have says that the universe had a beginning." (Alexander Vilenkin, quoted by Turek) [^2]

> "So we are left with a spaceless, timeless, immaterial, powerful, personal first cause. That sounds an awful lot like a theistic God." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The argument's central thrust — that the cosmos points beyond itself to a powerful, personal, transcendent Cause rather than to brute matter — is congenial to Latter-day Saint theism and useful against atheism. The honest divergence lies in *what* "create" means. Turek (pp.34-35) frames the cause as creating "the universe out of nothing" (*ex nihilo*); LDS scripture instead teaches that God *organized* eternal, uncreated matter and intelligences: "the elements are eternal" (D&C 93:29) and "intelligence... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" (D&C 93:29; cf. [[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]]). A Latter-day Saint can affirm premises (1) and (2) as a refutation of an eternal, self-existing cosmos while reading the "beginning" as the beginning of *this* organized universe, not the absolute origination of matter from nonbeing. See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]] and [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]].
