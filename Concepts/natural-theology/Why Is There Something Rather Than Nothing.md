# Why Is There Something Rather Than Nothing

## The Point
[[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Leibniz]]' contingency argument begins not from the universe's *beginning* but from its sheer *existence*. The foundational question is "Why is there something rather than nothing?" — which Leibniz called "the first question which should rightly be asked." [^1] The point is that the bare fact that *anything at all* exists is not self-explanatory and cries out for an explanation; the explanation Leibniz reaches is God. [^1] This reframes the cosmological project away from "what kicked off the Big Bang?" and toward the deeper demand of the [[The Principle of Sufficient Reason|principle of sufficient reason]]: that the existence of contingent reality as such must be accounted for, whether or not it had a temporal start.

## Evidence
- The question is posed as the natural, even *scientific* and *intuitive*, response to the universe — curiosity about why the universe exists "seems scientific - and intuitive." [^2]
- It cannot be dodged by sheer size or scale: enlarging the object in question never dissolves the demand for an explanation (see the [[The Shiny Sphere in the Woods Illustration|shiny-sphere illustration]]), so the universe's vastness is no excuse to stop asking. [^3]
- The question presses toward [[Contingent and Necessary Beings|the contingent/necessary distinction]]: because the something that exists (the universe) exists *contingently*, its existence rests on a non-contingent being "that cannot not exist." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "The first question which should rightly be asked is: Why is there something rather than nothing?" [^1]

> "Curiosity about the existence of the universe seems scientific - and intuitive!" [^2]

## LDS Connection
The question "why is there something rather than nothing?" is a shared weapon against the atheist's "the cosmos is just a brute fact" reply, and Latter-day Saints can press it as readily as classical theists. The honest divergence is over what counts as the final answer. For Craig and the classical tradition, the question terminates in a single necessary being who is the explanation of all else. The Restoration locates ultimacy differently: some realities — intelligence, matter, and law — are themselves eternal and uncreated, not brought into being from nothing (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]). So an LDS reading affirms the force of the question — contingent reality is not self-explanatory — while answering that the necessary, self-existent ground includes co-eternal realities rather than a lone metaphysically simple being.
