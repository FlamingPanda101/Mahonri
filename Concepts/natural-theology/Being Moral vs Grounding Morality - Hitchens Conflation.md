# Being Moral vs Grounding Morality - Hitchens Conflation

## The Point
The moral argument does not claim atheists *cannot be moral*; it asks how they *ground* or *justify* the morality they admittedly have. [[Alex OConnor]] shows that [[Christopher Hitchens]] systematically conflated these two questions [^1]. When asked "how do you determine right and wrong without God," Hitchens called the question "insulting" and "degrading," answering the (different) charge that an atheist couldn't *act* ethically [^2]. As O'Connor puts it: "the question was not how can a-theists be moral, but how can a-theists *ground* their morality. This is a subtle difference, but it's crucial" [^1]. Hitchens "is answering an objection that was never put to him... one that the religious rarely if ever actually asked" [^3]. The indignation ("how dare you suggest that I can't be moral without believing in God") is rhetorically powerful and logically beside the point.

## Evidence
- O'Connor's clarification of the theist's actual claim: "when the religious say that there cannot be such thing as morality without God, they're not saying that there cannot be... morality without *belief* in God... you can be an atheist and be moral, you just can't objectively *ground* your morality" [^3].
- Even when Hitchens grasps the real question, his answer is to *report* the moral instinct (parental love for children, Socrates' inner daemon, Adam Smith's "internal witness") rather than justify it [^4].
- O'Connor: "we're not interested in whether we have a moral instinct — of course we do, who doesn't know this — what we're interested in is how to *justify* the moral instinct, which Hitchens fails to do by simply appealing to the fact that the instinct exists" [^4].

## Best Quotes
> "The question was not how can a-theists be moral, but how can a-theists ground their morality. This is a subtle difference, but it's crucial." [^1]

> "He's answering an objection that was never put to him — one that the religious rarely if ever actually asked." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a textbook clarification an apologist can deploy verbatim — and it comes from an atheist, so it cannot be dismissed as Christian special pleading. The moral argument (see [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]], [[By What Standard - Morality Requires an Objective Standard]]) concedes from the outset that unbelievers are often deeply moral people; the question is *ontological grounding*, not personal virtue. O'Connor's diagnosis exactly parallels the standard reply to Sam Harris in [[Harris Describes Morality But Cannot Ground Concern for Others]]: *describing* that we have moral intuitions is not the same as *justifying* why they are binding or true. Be fair to O'Connor: he is not endorsing the theist's conclusion. He thinks the grounding question is legitimate and that Hitchens dodged it — but he would press the theist for *their* grounding too. The apologetic gain is narrow but real: the most popular atheist rebuttal ("I'm insulted — of course I'm moral!") is conceded, by an atheist, to miss the argument entirely.
