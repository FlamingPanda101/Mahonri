# Rejecting Biblical Inerrancy Solves the Moral Contradiction Problem

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] presses a classic atheist dilemma against Christianity: the *same* moral intuition that apologists invoke to argue toward God's existence is the intuition believers are then told to *suppress* when they open the Bible and read God commanding the slaughter of the [[Canaanites]] — "God is telling men to hack babies to death with their swords" — or subordinating women [^1]. His point: a religion cannot claim morally consistent grounding when "the first thing I see opening that book is something which contradicts the very moral intuition that I was supposed to use to get there in the first place" [^2]. The narrator concedes the force: "There are many examples of biblical contradictions, and anyone who pretends there are not is demonstrably wrong" [^3].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Reject inerrancy at the root.** The Latter-day Saint move is to deny the premise both atheist and creedal Christian share — that every line of the Bible is inerrant divine speech. "There is [a solution] if you reject biblical inerrancy. And we, as Latter-day Saints, do" [^3]. Evidence rating: **strong as a *consistency* solution** — it genuinely removes the contradiction by not asserting the offending verses are God's voice. It does not, however, prove the verses are uninspired; it shifts to criterion (point 3).
2. **Distinguish "the author thought God commanded it" from "God commanded it."** The narrator asks whether "the original author thought that God commanded it when in reality he did not" [^3] — locating error in the prophet's understanding, not in God. Rating: **plausible and historically modest**; it aligns with Joseph Smith's own statement that "a prophet was a prophet only when he was acting as such," but the video does not cite that backing.
3. **Use the collective prophetic witness as a filter.** Latter-day Saints "look at the overall message of God's prophets throughout time," identify "outliers that seem out of character," and may judge them uninspired [^4]. Worked example: the command for women to "keep silence in the churches" ([[1 Corinthians 14.34-35|1 Cor 14:34-35]]) is contradicted by New Testament female missionaries and by women teaching and prophesying ([[1 Corinthians 11.5|1 Cor 11:5]]), so it reads as outlier, not doctrine [^5]. Rating: **internally coherent** but depends on an independent canon (Book of Mormon, modern revelation) to define the "consistent message."
4. **Steelman the creedal/atheist side.** O'Connor anticipates the inerrantist's only honest escapes and rejects them as unavailable: the orthodox Christian "trusting" the NT cannot, like a critical scholar, simply say [[Paul]] didn't write the misogynistic passages [^1]. And the inerrantist would rightly object that a "pick the outliers" method risks letting the reader's modern morality, not God, become the canon-within-the-canon — exactly the danger the narrator pre-empts: "I'm not saying that each person has the right and authority to reject any of the LDS canon because they don't like it" [^6]. The LDS reply is that the filter is the *prophetic collective*, not private taste, and that the Bible remains "highly reliable" with "major themes... that mutually reinforce one another" [^6]. The unresolved tension: who adjudicates which prophet is the outlier remains a live question.
5. **Linguistic/contextual note.** The "silence" texts ([[1 Corinthians 14.34-35]], [[1 Timothy 2.11]]) are widely flagged by NT scholars (e.g., textual displacement of 1 Cor 14:34-35 in some manuscripts; disputed Pauline authorship of the Pastorals) — the very scholarly leverage O'Connor invokes for himself but says the inerrantist forfeits [^1].

## Best Quotes
> "The same moral intuition that I'm told I'm supposed to rely upon to prove the existence of God, is that same moral intuition that I'm supposed to ignore when I encounter the... less than enviable situations of the Canaanites." [^1]

> "The first thing I see opening that book is something which contradicts the very moral intuition that I was supposed to use to get there in the first place, that leaves me with some cause for concern." [^2]

> "There are many examples of biblical contradictions, and anyone who pretends there are not is demonstrably wrong. So, is there a solution to this issue? Well, there is if you reject biblical inerrancy. And we, as Latter-day Saints, do." [^3]

> "While we don't hold the Bible to be inerrant, we do hold it to be highly reliable. There are major themes and ideas that mutually reinforce one another... unlike many Christians, we don't have to defend every outlier in scripture as if it was inerrantly spoken by God himself." [^6]
