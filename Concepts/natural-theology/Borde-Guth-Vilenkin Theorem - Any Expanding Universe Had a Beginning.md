# Borde-Guth-Vilenkin Theorem - Any Expanding Universe Had a Beginning

## The Point
The second premise of the Kalam argument — *the universe began to exist* — faces a standard atheist escape: maybe the universe is past-eternal, or maybe our Big Bang is just one episode in a wider, beginningless multiverse. Craig's video answers that this escape was closed in 2003 by three leading cosmologists, **Arvind Borde, Alan Guth, and Alexander Vilenkin**, who "proved that any universe which has on average been expanding throughout its history cannot be eternal in the past, but must have an absolute beginning."[^1] The result is striking because it is largely independent of the detailed physics: it follows from the geometry of expansion itself, so it holds even where general relativity breaks down. As the video puts it, scientists "can no longer hide behind a past eternal universe. There is no escape. They have to face the problem of a cosmic beginning."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The theorem is general, not model-specific.** Earlier, an atheist could grant the standard Big Bang model yet hope some alternative model would restore an eternal past. The video notes that one alternative after another "failed to stand the test of time," and that BGV applies to "any adequate model": it "must have a beginning just like the standard model."[^3]
2. **It covers the multiverse.** The single most common contemporary route around a cosmic beginning is to embed our universe in an eternally inflating multiverse. The theorem "even applies to the multiverse, if there is such a thing," so positing more universes does not buy a beginningless past — the *ensemble* still has a past boundary.[^1]
3. **It strengthens, not replaces, the other lines of evidence.** BGV is presented as the capstone to the thermodynamic argument and the Big Bang discovery chain (Einstein → Friedmann/Lemaître → Hubble) — see [[Scientific Evidence That the Universe Began (Thermodynamics and the Big Bang)]]. Together they make it "quite plausible... that both premises of the argument are true," so the conclusion follows: "The universe has a cause."[^4]
4. **The cause must transcend spacetime.** Because the universe "can't cause itself," and because BGV pushes the beginning back to an absolute boundary of expanding spacetime, the cause "must be beyond the space-time universe" — spaceless, timeless, immaterial, uncaused, and "unimaginably powerful."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Three leading cosmologists, Arvind Borde, Alan Guth, and Alexander Vilenkin, proved that any universe which has on average been expanding throughout its history cannot be eternal in the past, but must have an absolute beginning." [^1]

> "This even applies to the multiverse, if there is such a thing. This means that scientists can no longer hide behind a past eternal universe. There is no escape." [^2]

## LDS Connection
For a Latter-day Saint, BGV is a useful *ally against atheism* — it undercuts the picture of a self-existing, beginningless cosmos that needs no Cause and leaves brute matter as the ultimate reality. A finite past for the expanding spacetime universe sits comfortably with the Restoration affirmation of a Creator who acts upon the cosmos. The honest caution is the same one that attends the whole Kalam project: the argument is *typically* deployed for creation **ex nihilo**, an absolute origination of being from non-being — and that conclusion LDS theology rejects. Restoration scripture teaches that God *organized* eternal, uncreated matter and intelligences: "the elements are eternal" and "intelligence... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" (D&C 93:29; cf. [[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]], [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). Importantly, BGV itself does **not** require ex nihilo: it establishes a past boundary for our *expanding spacetime*, not the non-existence of all matter, energy, or intelligence prior to that boundary. So a Latter-day Saint can affirm the theorem and the beginning of *this* organized universe while declining the metaphysical leap to creation out of nothing (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]).
