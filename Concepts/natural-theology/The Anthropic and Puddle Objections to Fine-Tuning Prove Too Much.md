# The Anthropic and Puddle Objections to Fine-Tuning Prove Too Much

## The Claim
Testify rebuts the two most popular naturalist replies to fine-tuning — the **anthropic objection** and the **puddle analogy** — by arguing both "prove too much." The anthropic objection says: because we exist, we couldn't observe a life-prohibiting universe, so our fine-tuned universe "wasn't really all that improbable at all." Testify answers with a **firing-squad** scenario: if your cousin's firing squad fires and you're still standing, "it's pretty clear this wasn't luck" — survival is "strong evidence that they intended to miss," not a reason to stop asking why you survived. The **puddle analogy** (Doug the puddle "fits" his hole because he molds to it) fails because, unlike a puddle, "life can't" survive just any universe — it "demands ridiculously specific conditions."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Firing-squad: survival is the thing to be explained (rating: strong; parity-of-reasoning).** "If they kept firing and kept missing, it's not luck — it's evidence that something else is going on." The anthropic "you can only observe it because you survived" move would absurdly apply here too, yet no one accepts it; so it fails for the universe by parity.[^1] This is the same move documented in [[The Anthropic Principle and the Firing-Squad Reply]].
2. **Puddle disanalogy: life is not infinitely pliable (rating: strong; disanalogy).** "Doug the puddle can survive any hole, but life can't without a universe that's precisely dialed in." The analogy presupposes exactly what fine-tuning denies — that observers would arise across a wide range of constants.[^2]
3. **"The objection that proves too much" (rating: moderate; meta-logical).** Testify's label captures the structure: an objection that, taken consistently, would dissolve *all* inferences from improbable-but-survived outcomes to intentional causes is too strong to be believed, since we routinely (and rightly) make such inferences (the firing squad, a rigged lottery).[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a standard and reasonably strong rebuttal: the anthropic principle (in its weak form) is a true *selection effect* — observers will of course find themselves in observer-permitting conditions — but as a *complete explanation* it commits the "old evidence"/sharpshooter error the firing squad exposes. The selection effect explains why *we* don't observe a dead universe; it does not explain why *there is* a life-permitting universe to be observed. The puddle analogy (Douglas Adams) is genuinely weaker than the firing-squad reply, because it smuggles in the assumption that life adapts to any physics, which is false. A sophisticated atheist will retreat from the naive anthropic move to the **multiverse** (so that a life-permitting universe somewhere is unsurprising) — which is why Testify treats the multiverse separately (see [[The Multiverse Reply to Fine-Tuning Relocates the Problem (Testify)]] and the vault's [[Multiverse as Ad-Hoc Escape from Fine-Tuning]]). As a Protestant source, the payoff is only that the anthropic deflation fails, leaving the design inference standing.

## LDS Relevance
The firing-squad reply is general natural theology and applies identically whatever one's doctrine of creation. For Latter-day Saints, the intuition it defends — that a life-permitting cosmos calls for explanation beyond "we're here, so never mind" — fits a universe deliberately *organized* for the flourishing and progression of God's children. The vault already houses this analogy from an LDS debate context ([[The Anthropic Principle and the Firing-Squad Reply]], Jacob Hansen); Testify supplies an independent Protestant statement of the same rebuttal, useful as corroborating, cross-traditional support.
