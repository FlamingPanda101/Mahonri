# The Warfare Model of Science and Religion Is a Myth

## The Point
The popular picture of science and religion locked in perpetual "warfare" is not history — it is a nineteenth-century invention. In a pastoral appeal "to the Latter-day Saint audience... from scientists who care," Jamie Jensen relays the testimony of two believing non-LDS scientists, Dr. Connie Bertka (a Unitarian Universalist geologist, past co-chair of the Smithsonian's Broader Social Impacts Committee) and Dr. Lee Meadows (a Presbyterian science educator), who together dismantle the conflict myth and assure Latter-day Saints they are not alone.[^1] Bertka notes that historical scientists and clergy "would have been puzzled by a question about reconciliation," and that the warfare thesis traces to "two mistaken individuals, John William Draper and Andrew Dickson White."[^2]

## Evidence
- **The history is cooperative, not combative.** Among the early church fathers "Augustinian logic reigned," using nature to illuminate scripture; only Tertullian opposed harmonizing faith and science, and his minority view was wrongly canonized as the norm by Draper and White.[^2] In the medieval period "it was within the monastic communities that classical manuscripts were collected and copied, leading to the preservation of the natural sciences."[^3]
- **Most scientists are not anti-religious.** In Ecklund's eight-nation study, the assumption that scientists embrace conflict "is wrong": in the U.S., 51% favored an *Independence* approach versus only 29% *Conflict*, and just 22% said learning science made them less religious. The lower religiosity of scientists is largely explained by their growing up in less-religious homes, not by science eroding belief.[^4] See [[Science Is Agnostic Not Atheistic]].
- **Both sides built the myth.** The caricature is "popularized by both the media's common portrayal of science and religion in conflict and the highly publicized views of some well-known scientists like Richard Dawkins."[^5]
- **Massive bridge-building is underway.** Jensen catalogs institutional efforts: Ian Barbour's four models (Conflict / Independence / Dialogue / Integration); the journal *Zygon* and IRAS (1954); the AAAS Dialogue on Science, Ethics, and Religion (1995); the Smithsonian's Hall of Human Origins Broader Social Impacts Committee; Meadows's *The Missing Link* and the NSF-funded LUDA project teaching evolution in Alabama; and the HHMI-funded reconciliation workshops at BYU.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Historians who have studied the relation between science and religion through time would remind us that many scientists and clergy from historical times would have been puzzled by a question about reconciliation." [^2]

> "Many people assume that scientists are antireligious, a caricature unfortunately popularized by both the media's common portrayal of science and religion in conflict and the highly publicized views of some well-known scientists like Richard Dawkins." [^5]

> "Renewed efforts by the scientific community... illustrate that the task of reconciliation between science and religion need not be ignored or delegated to the purview of religious institutions only." [^7]

## LDS Connection
For Latter-day Saints the chapter is doubly reassuring: not only is the warfare narrative historically false, but believing scientists of *other* faiths are doing exactly the reconciling work the Restoration would predict — because "all truth is part of the gospel of Jesus Christ." Meadows's hard-won insight that there are "multiple ways of knowing" is the same epistemology that lets a Saint hold revelation and laboratory side by side without panic. Jensen's closing invitation is explicitly ecumenical: "Let us, as Latter-day Saints, join hands with our fellow brothers and sisters of other religions... and, with our religious scientist friends, be an active part of this incredible effort."[^7] This is the historical-and-sociological backing for [[The False Dichotomy Between Evolution and Faith|the false dichotomy]] and the partner truth to [[The Clarity of Science vs Obscurity of Religion Is a False Dichotomy|the clarity/obscurity dichotomy]].
