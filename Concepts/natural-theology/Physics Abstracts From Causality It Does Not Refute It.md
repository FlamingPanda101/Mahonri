# Physics Abstracts From Causality It Does Not Refute It

## The Point
Feser's master reply to every science-based objection (Russell on causation, Newton's inertia, relativity's block universe, quantum indeterminism) is one move: modern physics deliberately *abstracts* from causality, capturing only what can be "mathematicized," so its silence about causes is an artifact of method, not a discovery about reality. [^1] "The absence of something in a representation of nature is not the same thing as a representation of its absence from nature." [^2] A black-and-white line drawing contains no color, but that tells us nothing about whether the scene was colored — "the colorlessness of the image is an artifact of the artist's method." [^2] Reasoning from physics' predictive success to the non-existence of causation is therefore "a very crude fallacy." [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Russell's "physics has no causes" proves too much.** If absence from the equations meant non-existence, we would have to eliminate "event," "law," and "explanation" too — terms that "do not themselves appear in the equations" yet are needed to do science at all. [^4] Russell's later self refutes his earlier self: physics leaves "the intrinsic character" of what it describes "completely unknown." [^5]
2. **Newton's inertia can't help.** The law "presupposes the existence of things like water molecules," so "it can hardly explain their existence." [^6] And a law of nature is just shorthand for "the way a thing will tend to operate given its nature" — so to invoke a law is not an alternative to explaining via the actualization of a potential; it relocates the question (what makes there be things with *that* nature?). [^7] Weisheipl: mathematical physics "must leave out of consideration the qualitative and causal content of nature... it can neither affirm nor deny their reality." [^1]
3. **Quantum mechanics, if anything, points toward Aristotle.** Causation need not be deterministic; a cause need only "make the effect intelligible," which a nondeterministic interpretation supplies. [^8] Radioactive decay is grounded in the *form or nature* of the atom (Pb²¹⁰ has a probability x of decaying because of what it is), so it has a formal cause and an efficient cause; "production" of the daughter atom "is a near-synonym for 'causation.'" [^9] Heisenberg even read quantum "statistical expectations" as "a 'potentia' in the sense of Aristotelian philosophy." [^10] Quantum theory approximates "potentiality without actuality"; the block universe approximates "actuality without potentiality" — each drops causation because each drops one half of it. [^11]
4. **The selective-skepticism point.** No one says engineering or medicine rest on "outdated science" because they ignore particle physics when discussing concrete foundations or herniated disks; the details are irrelevant to the point being made. To press them only against theistic examples (a desk holding a cup) is "sheer special pleading." [^12]

## Best Quotes
> "The absence of something in a representation of nature is not the same thing as a representation of its absence from nature." [^2]

> "It is the method that drains causality out of the world, with quantum mechanics being something like a limiting case." [^11]

> "However inertia is interpreted, it must be made compatible with the principle of causality, which captures deeper levels of reality than physics does or can." [^13]

## LDS Connection
This note is almost pure common cause with the Restoration against atheism: Feser dismantles the favorite naturalist claim that science has abolished causation (and with it, room for God). Latter-day Saint apologists can deploy the same "physics abstracts, it does not refute" reply, and the insistence that laws of nature are shorthand for how things behave "given their nature" actually resonates with an LDS metaphysics in which God works *with* the natures of co-eternal realities ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). The only caution is the destination, not the path: Feser is clearing the ground for a *purely actual* sustainer ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]), whereas the LDS thinker affirms a present sustaining cause without the timeless-immutable conclusion ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). There is no real LDS objection to this chapter-section itself — it is Feser at his most ecumenically useful against materialism; the divergence appears only when the recovered notion of causation is pressed onward into the doctrine of God.
