# Reconciliation Pedagogy Increases Evolution Acceptance Without Eroding Faith

## The Point
This is the empirical payoff of the whole reconciliation project: when evolution is taught with an explicit **reconciliation approach** — rather than a "conflict" framing or a "deficit model" that treats religious students as ignorant — religious students both *accept the science more* **and** *keep their faith*. Danny Ferguson, Ethan Tolman, Spencer Shumway, and Cassidy Shively (former student researchers in Jamie Jensen's BYU lab) report that across many studies "we saw no reduction in religiosity by improving evolution acceptance," and that interviewed students reported a *positive* effect on their testimonies.[^1] The finding matters because the usual alternatives backfire: the deficit model makes "students dig in their heels even further," and merely teaching the facts is "marginally successful" because worldviews don't bend to contradictory data alone.[^2]

## Evidence
- The approach follows Barnes and Brownell's **ReCCEE** (Religious Cultural Competence in Evolution Education) six practices: acknowledge perceived conflict; invite students to discuss their views; teach the nature of science and multiple ways of knowing; present non-atheistic, non-literalist options including God-guided evolution; showcase believing-scientist role models; and have the instructor share their own reconciliation story.[^3]
- **Robust across settings.** Acceptance rose in BYU classrooms, at other religious institutions, and even when taught in a *religion* class rather than a science class; a 15-minute live-animal aquarium presentation raised acceptance among aquarium patrons, and a 6-minute ReCCEE lesson raised it among public-university undergraduates.[^4]
- **Survey + essay data.** Students surveyed before and after a reconciliation module "almost universally increase their acceptance of evolution"; one student wrote, "I wasn't necessarily... at peace with things before, but... I am at peace with things now." Another: "It increased [my testimony]."[^5]
- **The "official neutrality" lever.** A recurring turning point in student essays is learning the Church "hasn't made a statement confirming or denying that evolution is real," which repeatedly moved students from skepticism toward openness.[^6] See [[Nothing Has Been Revealed Concerning Evolution]].
- The deficit model and bare-facts model are explicitly contrasted as the *ineffective* baselines the reconciliation approach outperforms.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "In all these instances, we saw no reduction in religiosity by improving evolution acceptance. We even interviewed past students... All interviewees indicated a positive effect." [^1]

> "A particularly harmful approach is to teach that the rejection of evolution is a product of some kind of deficit... Methods stemming from this approach are highly ineffective, often causing students to dig in their heels even further." [^2]

> "By helping them to reconcile their doubts and questions in issues pertaining to science and religion, BYU faculty are providing them with a template for seeking understanding in relation not only to evolution but to every other major question they may encounter." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The result reframes apologetics as *pedagogy*: how a difficult truth is taught determines whether the hearer keeps the faith. Teaching evolution (or any hard issue) as a forced choice — "the science or your testimony" — predictably loses students; teaching it inside a framework that honors the student's worldview keeps both. That is a transferable model for every "secondary question" a member will meet, which is why the authors call it "a template for... every other major question." It is the constructive complement to the warning that [[Forcing a Choice Between Science and Faith Drives People Out|forcing the choice drives people out]], and the data-side answer to [[Where Will You Go - Deconstruction Rarely Leads Somewhere Better|deconstruction that leads nowhere better]].
