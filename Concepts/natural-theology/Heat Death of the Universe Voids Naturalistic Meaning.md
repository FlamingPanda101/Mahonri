# Heat Death of the Universe Voids Naturalistic Meaning

## The Point

[[William Lane Craig]] argues that on atheism the *final* state of things drains all earlier states of any ultimate significance. Two horizons of annihilation are in view. First, the personal horizon: "if there's no God… when you die… you simply cease to exist," so a kind life and a cruel life reach "the same outcome: nothingness." Second, the cosmic horizon: "scientists predict that eventually the whole universe and mankind with it will die out," so "everything comes to nothing." If both the individual and the cosmos terminate in oblivion, then no act — even discovering a cure for cancer or saving a child — can carry *ultimate* meaning, however much immediate value it has. This is the temporal engine behind [[The Absurdity of Life Without God]].

## Evidence

- **The two lives, one end argument.** "One person lives a kind, generous, thoughtful life. Another lives a horrible, violent, selfish life. It doesn't matter — in both cases the outcome is the same: nothingness. So how can their life choices have any objective meaning?" [^1]
- **Conceding immediate value to isolate the ultimate.** Craig grants the interlocutor's example — "it's certainly meaningful if I discover a cure for cancer or save a child's life… I agree completely!" — then presses, "but atheism can't explain why." [^2] The move concedes *subjective* and *immediate* meaning precisely to show that *ultimate* meaning is what's missing.
- **The cosmic terminus.** "Scientists predict that eventually the whole universe and mankind with it will die out. So everything comes to nothing." [^3] The eventual heat death removes any final repository in which achievements could matter.
- **An atheist witness to the conclusion.** Craig cites Bertrand Russell's verdict that "we must build our lives on the firm foundation of despair," presenting cosmic futility as a conclusion drawn by atheists themselves, not only by their critics. [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "One person lives a kind, generous, thoughtful life. Another lives a horrible, violent, selfish life. It doesn't matter — in both cases the outcome is the same: nothingness." [^1]

## LDS Connection

The argument turns entirely on the premise that death is annihilation and the cosmos ends in oblivion — a premise Latter-day Saint doctrine denies at both horizons. At the personal horizon, the resurrection and the immortality of the soul mean a life's choices are never erased into "nothingness" but are gathered up in eternal life; "to be carnally-minded is death, and to be spiritually-minded is life eternal" (2 Nephi 9:39). At the cosmic horizon, eternal progression and the eternal nature of intelligence and matter mean reality is not headed for a final blank but toward exaltation and "eternal increase." Because the LDS cosmos has no terminus of pure nothingness, the kind life and the cruel life do *not* converge on the same end — which is exactly the asymmetry Craig says atheism cannot supply. See also the [[Monopoly Analogy for Lifes Purpose]], where treating the temporal "game board" as all there is mirrors the naturalist's collapse into [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]].
