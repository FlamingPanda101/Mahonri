# Attention Is Allocated by a Motivated Value Hierarchy

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] argues that perception is not value-neutral: what we attend to is selected by our motivations, which in turn flow from a pre-existing "hierarchy of values." The invisible-gorilla viewers focus on counting passes only because the instruction "taps into" something they already care about. Attention is therefore goal- and value-relative — perception is structured by what matters to the perceiver before anything is seen. [^1][^2]

## Argument
1. Peterson asks not just *what* viewers attend to but *why* they comply with the instruction to count passes at all. [^1]
2. One answer: the task reads as "a little cognitive test," tapping a standing value — "I want to be maybe a smart and competent person or… at least as smart and competent as everyone else." So "the instruction taps into a pre-existent value structure and then it's motivating." [^1]
3. A second answer: social compliance — the room has "an implicit story" placing the lecturer "at the top of the… hierarchy," and merely being present means one has "bought into that presupposition," making it "logical… to play along." [^2]
4. Once the task is motivationally significant, the effect on perception is decisive: "as soon as you play the game… you focus your very limited attentional resources precisely on what it is that you're supposed to do." [^2]
5. Conclusion: the rule for perception is that what falls outside the "motivated interpretive frame" is treated as irrelevant and screened out — "don't pay attention to anything that isn't directly relevant to the desired outcome." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "That taps into your hierarchy of values — part of your value is I want to be maybe a smart and competent person… and so the instruction taps into a pre-existent value structure and then it's motivating." [^1]

> "As soon as you play the game… you focus your very limited attentional resources precisely on what it is that you're supposed to do." [^2]

> "The rule for perception is don't pay attention to anything that isn't directly relevant to the desired outcome." [^3]
