# The Blatchford-Price Syllogism - No Adam No Atonement

#type/claim

## The Claim

The popular critic's argument — "if Adam and Eve weren't real, then there was no Fall; if no Fall, no need for atonement; if no atonement, no need for Jesus Christ; so what's the point of Christianity?" — is not a neutral logical truth but a *traceable historical inheritance* with a flawed pedigree. [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] locates its origin in the British atheist Robert Blatchford's 1903 book *God and My Neighbour*, shows it was rebutted almost immediately by Christian apologists, and notes the irony that it was then adopted by the *father of young-earth creationism* as an argument against evolution. Its survival on "ex-Mormon Reddit" and in anti-Christian polemic does not make it sound; the existence of brilliant, committed Christians who accept evolution refutes it "in practical terms."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **It has a datable atheist origin.** Spackman attributes the chain to [[Robert Blatchford]], "a British atheist" he calls "a fundamentalist atheist" for holding "very simplistic assumptions that don't even really go with his atheism." Blatchford's broader syllogism ran: a perfect God would inspire a perfect book and a perfect religion; Christianity and the Bible are not perfect; therefore God does not exist — and then, "evolution is real, therefore there's no Adam... no fall... no need for Jesus or atonement... we don't need churches."[^2]
- **It was answered at once by Christians.** "Pretty quickly G. K. Chesterton the Christian apologist in England is writing responses to Blatchford," as were pastors — both on the logic and on the other assumptions.[^3]
- **It was ironically co-opted by a young-earth creationist.** "An American guy who is a fundamentalist but on the Christian side picks up Blatchford's syllogism and thinks it works as an argument against evolution" — [[George McCready Price]], the Seventh-day Adventist "father of young earthism." The syllogism "gets separated from Blatchford's other assumptions" and "gets very popular," but "the logic is pretty flawed."[^4]
- **Counterexamples falsify it empirically.** "The existence of people who are deeply committed to Christianity, who are highly intelligent, who also accept evolution also shows in practical terms that it doesn't work" — Spackman names Francis Collins ("the guy who runs the National Institute of Health"), "various popes," [[C.S. Lewis]], and Billy Graham. "I don't think they were ignorant. I don't think they were milquetoast Christians" — they simply "didn't buy this logic."[^5]
- **You can grant a literal Adam and still reject it.** The argument is not rescued by conceding historicity: "You can have a fairly literal or historical reading of Genesis and Blatchford still be wrong."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "That logic is deeply flawed. And it actually goes back to a British atheist in 1902 or 3... He writes in his book called *God and My Neighbour*."[^2]

> "An American guy who is a fundamentalist but on the Christian side picks up Blatchford's syllogism and thinks it works as an argument against evolution... George McCready Price, the 7th Day Adventist, the father of young earthism. And this syllogism gets separated from Blatchford's other assumptions. It gets very popular. But the problem is the logic is pretty flawed."[^4]

> "You can have a fairly literal or historical reading of Genesis and Blatchford still be wrong."[^6]

## LDS Connection

The syllogism is the engine behind a common faith-crisis attack — "we get a lot of critics... saying if Adam and Eve weren't real then there wasn't a fall, then there was no need for atonement, no need for Jesus Christ." Spackman's genealogical move (it is a borrowed, rebutted, century-old argument, not a deduction from the gospel) defuses it without forcing a choice between scripture and science. It dovetails with the Restoration teaching that [[Nothing Has Been Revealed Concerning Evolution|the Church has no doctrine on the mechanism of creation]] and with the broader point that [[The Fall Was a Change of Degree or Scope Not an Absolute Reversal|the Fall need not be an absolute cosmic reversal]] for the atonement to be necessary — what is "at stake" is "the birth, death, and resurrection of Jesus," not the historicity of every garden detail.
