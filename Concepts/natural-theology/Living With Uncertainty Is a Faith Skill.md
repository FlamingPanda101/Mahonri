# Living With Uncertainty Is a Faith Skill

## The Point
Evolutionary biologist Jared Lee (Southern Virginia University) argues that the way to reconcile evolution and faith is not to resolve every question but to learn to *live with uncertainty* — and that doing so is a skill demanded by **both** faith and science.[^1] Uncertainty is built into each enterprise: faith deals in things "hoped for" and not yet seen (Heb. 11:1), and science "never proves something one hundred percent correct" but only sets up the next question.[^2] Crucially, uncertainty is **not doubt**. Doubt "indicates a potential loss of faith," whereas uncertainty simply names "the unknown that is inherent to faith."[^3] Treating an open question as if it were a faith-threat is the category error that drives needless crises.

## Evidence
- **Uncertainty is intrinsic to faith.** We have certainty that all will be resurrected, "however, exactly when and how this Resurrection will take place is uncertain." Silence of scripture on a topic "does not make that topic bad or evil" — e.g., the *New Era* answer on dinosaurs concedes there has been no revelation, yet "the scientific evidence says they existed."[^4]
- **Uncertainty is intrinsic to science.** One biologist described science as "more like the Three Stooges trying to move a piano than some heroic march to the truth." Data are incomplete, analyses inconclusive; resolution comes only from "a series of experiments and observations published by many people over a long period of time" — illustrated by the ever-lengthening scientific estimates for the age of the earth.[^2]
- **Three practices for living with uncertainty:** (1) have the humility to weigh new information critically; (2) distinguish primary from secondary gospel questions; (3) "recognize and be comfortable with leaving gospel questions unanswered," since "if we knew everything, there would be no need for faith."[^5] See [[Primary and Secondary Questions]].
- Henry Eyring is cited for the parallel: "religious truth comes forth 'line upon line, precept upon precept' in a process of sifting and winnowing similar to the one [known] so well in science."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The word doubt should not be considered similar to uncertainty since it indicates a potential loss of faith, while the other terms express the unknown that is inherent to faith." [^3]

> "Science never proves something one hundred percent correct. Instead, it sets up more questions that need to be addressed." [^2]

> "We must live by faith, because if we knew everything, there would be no need for faith." [^5]

> "To live with this uncertainty, we need an open heart and an open mind." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Lee's counsel reframes a perennial pastoral problem: members taught that a faithful person must *have all the answers* are set up to mistake an unanswered secondary question for a crisis of testimony. The Restoration actually models the opposite — revelation comes "line upon line" (D&C 9:8 invites us to "study it out"), the Lord has "purposely not revealed everything" (Articles of Faith 1:9), and Alma frames faith as an experiment that yields to knowledge only over time (Alma 32:33–34). Holding a question open in patience is therefore not weak faith but mature faith. This is the same posture as treating a faith crisis as a [[Renovation Not Demolition - Faith Crisis as Rebuilding From the Foundation|renovation rather than a demolition]], and it depends on the [[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think|epistemic humility]] that admits our current knowledge is partial.
