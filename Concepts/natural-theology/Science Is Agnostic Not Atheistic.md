# Science Is Agnostic Not Atheistic

## The Point
Jamie Jensen argues that science, as a way of knowing, "is an agnostic approach — there is no belief involved."[^1] Science cannot provide evidence that there is a God, nor that there is not one. It is therefore a category error — committed by both religious people and atheistic scientists — to treat science as atheistic.[^2] Questions about the supernatural (Does God exist? What is the purpose of life?) lie outside the realm of scientific study, which deals only with the tangible, shareable, and repeatable natural world.[^3] Recognizing this agnosticism dissolves the perceived threat: the two ways of knowing need not challenge one another.

## Evidence
- Jensen distinguishes scientific *acceptance/rejection* from religious *belief*: "we don't believe gravity to be true. More accurately, we accept, based on the evidence, that gravity exists."[^1] Framing a scientific theory as a "belief system" wrongly sets it up to rival the religious belief system.[^4]
- A survey of 1,000+ college students found 48% believed accepting evolution requires rejecting God — and "the more students thought that science had to be atheistic, the less willing they were to accept evolution."[^5]
- Jensen's restaurant conversation with a Catholic priest and a humanist about morality: whether morality evolved or was endowed by God is "irrelevant to my conviction that God is real," because that conviction rests on a different, nonscientific kind of evidence.[^6]
- Spiritual evidence is real but differs from scientific evidence: it is not necessarily tangible and not shareable, yet it is repeatable — Moroni's promise (Moro. 10:3–5) is framed as a genuine experiment with a clear prediction.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Science is not atheistic (as many religious people presume and many atheistic scientists attest); it is agnostic, plain and simple. Knowing whether God exists or not is not a question for science but a question of faith." [^2]

> "A correct understanding of the agnostic nature of science, incidentally, is one of the best ways to increase acceptance of evolution." [^8]

## LDS Connection
By fencing science to the natural world, Jensen protects faith from a "God of the gaps" collapse: if belief in God rests only on what science cannot yet explain, every advance erodes faith; but belief grounded in spiritual evidence — felt presence, answered prayer, the witness of the Holy Ghost — is untouched by scientific progress.[^9] President Russell M. Nelson is quoted that conflict between science and religion "only arises from an incomplete knowledge of either science or religion, or both."[^10] This maps onto the restored-gospel conviction that there are two complementary epistemologies — study and faith — that "ultimately... lead to one great whole."[^11]
