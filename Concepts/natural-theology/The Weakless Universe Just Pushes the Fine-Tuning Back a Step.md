# The Weakless Universe Just Pushes the Fine-Tuning Back a Step

## The Claim
Atheists (Westbrook) argue the electroweak force only *appears* fine-tuned if it is the lone value you may vary; citing a paper by Harnik, Kribs, and Perez, they claim a "weakless universe" — one with *no* weak force at all — can still be life-permitting if you adjust other parameters. [[Trent Horn]] answers that the paper's own language gives the game away: it requires "judicious parameter adjustment," i.e. you can drop the weak force only by *re-tuning all the other constants* — "so this just pushes the problem back one step." He adds that the authors underestimate how life-hostile such a universe is. Their paper concedes that without the weak force there is no ordinary radioactive decay, hence no molten planetary cores heated by uranium and thorium, hence "plate tectonics, volcanoes, geothermal heat would not exist" — yet they call this "anything more than a curiosity." Horn notes a molten core is needed to generate the magnetic field that shields life from radiation, which recent research holds necessary for life on a planet.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, self-undermining concession)** "Judicious parameter adjustment" *is* fine-tuning: removing one tuned parameter at the cost of tuning the rest does not eliminate the improbability, it redistributes it.[^1] This is the same logic as the inflation reply in [[Carroll Conflates the Gravitational and Cosmological Constants]].
2. **(Moderate, physical)** The downstream losses are real: a weakless universe forfeits the usual pathways to heavy elements, radioactive heating, molten cores, and thus planetary magnetic fields and radiation shielding.[^1]
3. **(Strong, literature state)** Per Barnes and Lewis, "only a handful of peer-reviewed papers have challenged the fine-tuning cases... and none defend the contention that most values of the constants and initial conditions of nature will permit the existence of life."[^2] See [[Luke Barnes]].

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The strongest pro-Harnik reading is that the *space* of life-permitting universes is larger and more multiply-realizable than single-parameter analyses suggest: if there are *many* combinations of constants that yield some viable chemistry, then the prior probability of "a life-permitting universe" is not as astronomically small as fixing-one-knob estimates imply. On this view, the weakless model is a proof of concept that life-permitting physics is not a razor-thin island but an archipelago.

## Counter-objection
An archipelago of life-permitting points scattered through an otherwise life-prohibiting space *still* leaves the total life-permitting volume vanishingly small relative to the whole parameter space — multiplying a tiny set of islands by a few does not change the order of magnitude of the improbability. And the weakless case in particular trades away features (radioactive heating, magnetic shielding) that the authors themselves admit, then waves off as a "curiosity"; that is an aesthetic judgment, not a demonstration that complex life could arise. The objection therefore concedes the structure of the fine-tuning argument (narrow life-permitting region) while disputing only its *measure* — and offers no calculation showing the measure is large. This mirrors the multiverse problem: every attempt to enlarge the life-permitting set imports fresh tuning to do so ([[The Multiverse Cannot Escape Prior Fine-Tuning]]).

## LDS Connection
General philosophy-of-physics with no distinctively LDS content. Its vault role is to answer the most technically-dressed atheist objection ("a viable universe with no weak force") by exposing the buried "adjust everything else" clause — keeping the burden on the critic to exhibit an *untuned* life-permitting cosmos.
