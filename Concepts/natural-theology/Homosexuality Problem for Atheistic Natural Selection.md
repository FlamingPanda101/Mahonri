# Homosexuality Problem for Atheistic Natural Selection

## The Claim

Within an atheistic, deterministic, natural-selection framework, homosexuality
presents an acute problem: if (a) homosexuality has a genetic or biological
component and (b) natural selection weeds out traits that reduce reproductive
success, then (c) homosexuality must be either a "genetic defect" that natural
selection is working to eliminate, or a choice. But atheists who deny free will
cannot consistently claim it's a choice. This forces the atheist into an
uncomfortable position that conflicts with the socially liberal values most
atheists hold. LDS theology avoids the problem by affirming free will: people
with same-sex attraction can exercise agency in how they respond to their
inclinations.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Natural Selection Logic (Argument weight: Secondary — Internal Critique)

[[Hayden Carroll]] engages an atheist student on ASU campus:

> "There's a lot of problems with natural selection though, right? Like natural
> selection doesn't always lead us to populating our genes... That causes a lot
> of problem for like homosexuality. Like do you think homosexuality is like a
> genetic like defection as an atheist?"[^1]

The logic:
1. Natural selection favors traits that promote reproductive success.
2. Exclusive homosexuality precludes direct biological reproduction.
3. Therefore, under natural selection, homosexuality would be selected
   *against*.
4. If it has a biological component, it's a "defect" in evolutionary terms.
5. If it's purely a choice, the atheist must affirm free will — which
   contradicts materialist determinism.

The atheist student's response: "I have no comment on that."[^1] — an
acknowledgment of the difficulty.

### 2. The Free Will Problem (Argument weight: Secondary — Internal Contradiction)

> "But you reject free will, too. So, natural selection is working to weed
> those people out. It's not a position you probably want to hold, but you have
> to as an atheist. Does that make sense?"[^2]

The bind for the atheist:
- **If homosexuality is biologically determined** → natural selection views it
  as a maladaptive trait being weeded out. This is a politically toxic
  conclusion that conflicts with LGBTQ+ affirmation.
- **If homosexuality is a choice** → you've affirmed free will, contradicting
  materialist determinism.
- **If you try to have it both ways (biological but not a "defect")** → you're
  making a value judgment that natural selection itself doesn't make. Natural
  selection has no opinion; it just selects for reproduction. A trait that
  precludes reproduction IS, in evolutionary terms, maladaptive.

### 3. The LDS Solution: Free Will (Argument weight: Primary — Doctrinal)

Carroll and [[David Butler]] present the LDS alternative:

> "What is our solve for that? Like in terms of... we don't look at it as
> natural selection. Our solve is free will. [People] have free will that they
> can actually choose."[^3]

The LDS framework:
- Same-sex attraction is an inclination, not an identity-determining destiny.
- Individuals with same-sex attraction can exercise agency — choosing to live
  according to their inclinations or choosing to live according to religious
  covenants.
- Carroll cites the example of [[Timber Harvard]], an LDS man who experiences
  same-sex attraction but is married to a woman in the temple and has
  children.[^4]
- The analogy: heterosexual married men are naturally inclined toward sexual
  variety, but they "restrain themselves to enter into the order of marriage
  that is ordained by God."[^5]

### 4. LDS Theology Provides a Unique Framework (Argument weight: Tertiary — Distinctiveness)

> "We are the only — I think — theists that I know of that believe we are
> eternal, as far [as] in Christendom."[^5]

The co-eternality doctrine is relevant here: because human spirits have always
existed and were not created *ex nihilo*, their fundamental identity is not
reducible to biology, genetics, or environmental conditioning. The "natural
man" (or woman) is a temporary condition, not the eternal self.

Butler adds that only LDS theology provides a *reason* why procreation matters
morally:

> "The Christian idea is that creation of a being is at conception — like
> there's no pre-earth life... at the end of the day, why is it a moral bad if
> we all [stop] having babies? But if God has, you know, many spirits that He
> wants to bring into this world and develop, then it's a moral bad. And you
> don't get that with the other Christian religions."[^6]

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: The "Gay Uncle" Hypothesis
**Steelman:** Evolutionary biology has proposed mechanisms by which
homosexuality could be adaptive at the *kin* level — the "gay uncle"
hypothesis suggests that homosexual individuals who invest resources in their
siblings' offspring increase the reproductive success of shared genes. This is
well-attested in other species and resolves the apparent evolutionary paradox
without calling homosexuality a "defect."

**Response:** This is a legitimate evolutionary counterargument that Carroll
doesn't engage. However: (a) The kin selection hypothesis remains contested
and doesn't account for *exclusive* homosexuality without any direct
reproduction. (b) More fundamentally, the kin selection defense still treats
homosexuality as a *trait to be explained away* in evolutionary terms — it
doesn't escape the framework where reproductive success is the only metric. (c)
The deeper problem is the determinism/agency tension — if everything is
biologically determined, moral categories collapse regardless of adaptive
explanations.

### Objection 2: Conflating "Is" and "Ought"
**Steelman:** Carroll conflates evolutionary description with moral evaluation.
Even if natural selection "weeds out" a trait, that says nothing about whether
it's morally good or bad. Many true things are evolutionarily maladaptive
(e.g., risking your life to save a stranger). The evolutionary "defect" label
is descriptive, not normative.

**Response:** Carroll would likely agree that evolution has no moral content —
but that's precisely the point. On atheism, there IS no moral content to
anything; "defect" is the only category naturalism provides. The atheist who
wants to affirm LGBTQ+ dignity is borrowing from a moral framework their
worldview cannot supply. Only a worldview with free will and objective moral
value can distinguish "this trait reduces reproduction" from "this person has
less dignity."

### Objection 3: The LDS Position Is Also Problematic
**Steelman:** Telling people with same-sex attraction that they can "choose" to
marry someone of the opposite sex is pastorally harmful and ignores the reality
that sexual orientation is not a simple choice. The LDS "solution" imposes
suffering on LGBTQ+ individuals by demanding they deny a fundamental aspect of
their identity.

**Response:** Carroll preempts this: "A lot of people hate him [Timber]
because they would say, 'Oh, you can't love your wife. You can't have a
fulfilling marriage because you're denying yourself of what your inclinations
are.'"[^4] Carroll's counter-analogy is the heterosexual married man who
"chooses not to sleep around with other women" despite natural inclination.[^5]
The LDS position treats sexual restraint within covenant bounds as universally
applicable, not singling out any orientation. Whether this analogy holds is
vigorously contested — but it's the position presented.
