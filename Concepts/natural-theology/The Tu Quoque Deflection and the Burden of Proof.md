# The Tu Quoque Deflection and the Burden of Proof

## The Point
[[Alex OConnor]] identifies a "fallacy that Hitchens would always commit" — *tu quoque* ("you too"): when shown a gap in his own position, [[Christopher Hitchens]] would reply that the religious cannot fill it either [^1]. O'Connor's rebuttal is decisive: "It may be hypocritical of somebody to criticize the mistake of another person while committing the mistake themselves, but this doesn't change the fact that a mistake has been made" [^1]. Challenged to ground morality, Hitchens "first brushes over the question... and then he says 'but my problem is that the religious can't do it either.' Okay, let's say they can't — that doesn't change the fact that you haven't done so either" [^2]. The deflection *feels* like a counter-strike and scores debate points, but it leaves the original question untouched.

## Evidence
- O'Connor anticipates the standard defense — that the burden of proof lies on the religious, not on Hitchens — and answers it on the merits: "Hitchens clearly says that he *does* believe in free will... that morality *does* exist... these are independent claims he is making that do require justification. He does have the burden of proof so long as he's making them" [^3].
- The free-will exchange shows the same pattern: to the claim that religious free will is incoherent, O'Connor replies, "okay, but that doesn't change the fact that *you* have also failed to do so, Mr. Hitchens" [^4].
- The smoking analogy: a smoker's advice not to smoke "doesn't make Hitchens' advice any less good just because he doesn't follow it himself" — hypocrisy and falsity are distinct [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "If someone asks 'how do you justify morality' and your response is 'well, you can't do it either,' you're committing a fallacy." [^2]

> "Hitchens clearly says that he does believe in free will... these are independent claims he is making that do require justification. He does have the burden of proof so long as he's making them." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
Two lessons, and a caution. First, the *tu quoque* exposure is a transferable tool: when an interlocutor responds to "how do you ground X?" with "well, you can't either," the honest move is to note that this concedes the point and merely spreads the problem around. Second, and more pointed for apologetics, O'Connor articulates the **burden-of-proof** principle that anchors arguments like [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]: the atheist who positively *affirms* objective morality and libertarian free will has made claims and therefore owes a justification — he cannot hide behind "the burden is on the theist." Caution, in fairness to O'Connor: he is not saying the theist's grounding succeeds. He would turn the same demand back on the apologist — *you* affirm objective morality, so *you* must ground it (the theist answers: in God's nature). The genuinely shared lesson is that whoever makes the positive claim must defend it; the rhetorical "you too" settles nothing.
