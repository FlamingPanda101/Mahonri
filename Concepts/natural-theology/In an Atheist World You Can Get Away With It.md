# In an Atheist World You Can Get Away With It

## The Point
If morality is just the well-being of conscious creatures, then the only reason not to harm others is the suffering it might bring back on you — and sometimes it won't. [[Jacob Hansen]] presses the decisive contrast: "In an atheist world you actually can get away with it... in our world you can never get away with it." A moral system that tells you to avoid bad acts because *you* will suffer for them assumes you can never escape the consequences. But in a godless universe, that assumption is false: "you can get away with stuff — like, people did get away with stuff."[^1]

## Evidence
Hansen takes Harris's own framework — wrongdoing is what moves a conscious creature toward suffering — and shows where it leaks. The system bottoms out in the premise that harming others harms yourself; remove that link and the obligation evaporates. History supplies the counterexample: people have inflicted enormous harm and died comfortably, never suffering for it. So the atheist must "make the assumption that you never can get away with anything in the world, and that's just not the case."[^2] This is the same fault line as [[Reason Can Justify Immoral Acts When You Can Get Away With Them]]: once self-interest is the only standard, immoral acts become *rational* whenever detection and blowback are unlikely. Naturalism cannot guarantee that wrongdoing returns to the wrongdoer, so it cannot guarantee a reason to be good.

## Best Quotes
> "In an atheist world you actually can get away with it, right — in our world you can never get away with it." [^1]

> "They have to make the assumption that you never can get away with anything in the world, and that's just not the case — like you can get away with stuff. People did get away with stuff." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The restored gospel closes the loop the atheist account leaves open. There is an ultimate justice: your well-being is genuinely connected to everyone else's, so "to harm others is to harm yourself" is not a hopeful slogan but a law of reality — "watch out, because you can't get away with anything."[^1] This is not the arbitrary punishment of divine-command theory; it is the built-in moral structure of a universe governed by eternal law, where breaking God's laws and wronging others reliably issues in suffering. Where naturalism makes "you can't escape consequences" a falsehood, the gospel makes it true, and so supplies the grounding for the obligation Harris's framework needs but cannot provide (see [[Harris Describes Morality But Cannot Ground Concern for Others]]).
