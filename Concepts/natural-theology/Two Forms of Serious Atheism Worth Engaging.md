# Two Forms of Serious Atheism Worth Engaging

## The Point
Charitable apologetics means refusing to live in an echo chamber that only beats up atheism's weakest arguments. [[Jacob Hansen]] grants that nine out of ten atheists hold "pretty weak arguments," but insists the serious work is engaging the *strongest* forms. He names two: the most serious is [[Friedrich Nietzsche]], who honestly concedes that without God morality is "all arbitrary, it's all just power, and it is what it is"; the other, which Hansen thinks ultimately falls apart, is [[Sam Harris]]'s attempt to give morality an objective basis in the well-being of conscious creatures.[^1] Picking the best version of the other side is the only way to test your own position — the same discipline of [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]].

## Evidence
Hansen distinguishes the two by the kind of strength each has. Nietzsche is strong by *consistency*: he doesn't flinch from the conclusion that meaning and morality are constructions of power. Harris is strong by *proximity*: of all the atheists trying to rescue objective morality, "he gets the closest."[^2] Hansen even calls himself "a big fan of Sam's work" and plays devil's advocate on Harris's behalf before responding — modeling engagement rather than caricature.[^2] The two represent the two live options for an atheist who wants to be intellectually honest: embrace the loss of objective value (Nietzsche) or try to reconstruct it on naturalistic terms (Harris).

## Best Quotes
> "There's two forms of atheism that I think are the most serious... the most serious atheism is like Friedrich Nietzsche — he just recognizes this and says yeah it's all arbitrary, it's all just power, and it is what it is." [^1]

> "Sam Harris is probably the most popular popularizer of trying to provide an objective basis to morality... I'm actually a big fan of Sam's work and I think he actually does — he gets the closest." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Honest doubt deserves an honest hearing, and Latter-day Saint apologetics is strongest when it engages atheism at its best rather than its worst. The two serious atheisms map onto what the Restoration uniquely supplies. Against Nietzsche, the gospel denies that value is mere power: every soul is a co-eternal intelligence of intrinsic worth, so meaning is built into reality rather than willed into being. Against Harris, the gospel grants what is right in his instinct — morality really does track flourishing and suffering — while supplying the obligation and grounding his naturalism cannot (see [[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]] and [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]). Charity toward the serious atheist is not a concession; it is confidence that the restored gospel can meet the strongest case.
