# Hilbert's Hotel - Why an Actual Infinite Cannot Exist

## The Claim
This is the first of [[Al-Ghazali]]'s two philosophical arguments for the Kalam's second premise (*the universe began to exist*), as presented in [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]'s animated explainer. [[Aristotle]] held the universe to be eternal in the past, but Al-Ghazali objected that "if the universe did not have a beginning, then the number of past events in the history of the universe is infinite," and "the existence of an actually infinite number of past events leads to absurdity. It's metaphysically impossible."[^1] The argument is therefore: (1) an *actual infinite* cannot exist in the real world; (2) a beginningless series of past events would be an actual infinite; (3) therefore the series of past events is not beginningless — the universe began. This note develops premise (1); the impossibility of *forming* an infinite by adding one event after another is treated in [[An Actual Infinite Cannot Be Formed by Successive Addition]].

The case for premise (1) is the thought experiment of the mathematician [[David Hilbert]]: a hotel with an *actually infinite* number of rooms, all occupied. Reflecting on what such a hotel could do reveals contradictions, showing that "an actually infinite number of things cannot exist in the real world."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A full infinite hotel can still accommodate a new guest (Rating: A — the core paradox).** Every one of the infinitely many rooms is occupied — "there's not a single vacancy." Yet when a new guest arrives, the manager shifts the occupant of room 1 to room 2, room 2 to room 3, "and so on to infinity," vacating room 1 for the newcomer "even though all the rooms were already full, and nobody has checked out."[^3] A hotel where *no* room is empty can nonetheless make room is already absurd.
2. **It can accommodate infinitely many new guests (Rating: A).** When an *infinity* of new guests arrives, the manager moves each occupant "into a room with a number twice that of his own room" (1→2, 2→4, 3→6, …). Since every number doubled is even, "all the odd numbered rooms become vacant, and the infinity of new guests gratefully checked in. And yet before they arrived, all the rooms were already full."[^4]
3. **Subtraction yields contradictions — infinity minus infinity (Rating: A — the decisive step).** Now run the guests *out*. If everyone in the odd rooms checks out, "an infinite number of people have left the hotel, and yet there are no fewer people in the hotel." But if instead everyone in rooms numbered 4 and higher checks out, "only three people are left," even though "exactly the same number of people left the hotel this time as when all the odd numbered guests checked out." So: "we have a contradiction. We subtract identical quantities from identical quantities, and get different answers."[^5] In transfinite arithmetic ∞ − ∞ is not well-defined; Craig's point is that *instantiating* such arithmetic in reality would yield genuine contradictions, which cannot obtain.
4. **The verdict (Rating: A — Hilbert's own conclusion).** "These absurdities show that an actually infinite number of things cannot exist in the real world." Hilbert himself: "The infinite is nowhere to be found in reality. It neither exists in nature, nor provides a legitimate basis for rational thought... The role that remains for the infinite to play is solely that of an idea."[^6]
5. **Application to the past (Rating: B — the inference to cosmology).** If a beginningless past is an actually infinite collection of events, it would be a real-world actual infinite of just the kind Hilbert's Hotel shows to be impossible. So "if Ghazali's two arguments are right, then the universe is not eternal in the past; it must have a beginning."[^7] This is the *philosophical* counterpart to the *scientific* arguments for a beginning in [[Scientific Evidence That the Universe Began (Thermodynamics and the Big Bang)]] and [[Borde-Guth-Vilenkin Theorem - Any Expanding Universe Had a Beginning]].

## Steelman the Critic
The argument has able critics, and Craig answers two objections in the video itself. (1) **Disanalogy: past events do not coexist.** A defender of the infinite past can say Hilbert's Hotel is irrelevant because its rooms are all *simultaneously actual*, whereas past events are gone — at most one event exists at a time, so there is never an actual infinite of *concrete things* lying around to generate paradoxes. Craig grants the structure of the objection but answers it by *modifying* the story: "Suppose the hotel has been under construction from eternity past, one room being added each year. How many rooms would there now be in the hotel? An actual infinite number. So if the past is infinite, that would imply that Hilbert's hotel could exist, which is absurd."[^8] (2) **Actual infinites do exist — namely numbers and other mathematical objects.** If the set of natural numbers is an actually infinite collection that really exists, premise (1) is simply false. Craig replies that this "presupposes that numbers really exist, but this is a highly disputed assumption that most philosophers reject" — i.e., it assumes mathematical *Platonism*, and anti-realist views (fictionalism, nominalism) deny that numbers are a mind-independent infinite *thing*.[^9] A further critic's move Craig does *not* address: one may grant that ∞ − ∞ is ill-defined yet deny that an infinite past requires performing any *subtraction* — the past just *is*, no guests check out of it — so the inverse-operation paradoxes (Evidence 3) may not transfer to a merely *successive* (rather than *manipulable*) infinite. This is why the second, independent argument from successive addition matters: it targets the past's *formation* directly rather than its cardinality. The honest assessment is that the actual-infinite argument is powerful but contested among philosophers of mathematics; its force depends on rejecting full-blooded Platonism and on holding that real-world instantiation of transfinite arithmetic would be genuinely contradictory rather than merely counterintuitive.

## LDS Connection
This argument is a strong *ally against atheism* for a Latter-day Saint, since its target is a self-existing, beginningless *physical* cosmos that needs no Creator — and it requires no commitment to creation *ex nihilo*. The data it establishes is the **finitude of the past series of cosmic events**, i.e., a beginning of *this* organized universe, which sits comfortably beside D&C 76 and the Big Bang. The one place a Latter-day Saint must read carefully is the leap from "no actual infinite of *events*" to "no beginningless reality of any kind." Restoration scripture teaches that intelligences and the elements are *eternal* and uncreated — "intelligence... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" (D&C 93:29) — which an objector might call a "completed infinite." But *eternal* (no beginning, no end) is not the same as a traversed *actual infinite of discrete past events*; the LDS claim is about the beginningless *being* of intelligence/matter, not about completing an infinite count by successive addition (see [[Infinite and Eternal Are Not the Same - The Regress Objection Rebutted]] and [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). So a Latter-day Saint can wield Hilbert's Hotel against an eternal *event-series* cosmos while maintaining the eternity of the substrate God organizes (cf. [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]).
