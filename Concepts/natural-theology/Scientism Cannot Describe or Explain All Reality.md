# Scientism Cannot Describe or Explain All Reality

## The Point
**Objection:** Science is the only genuine source of knowledge, and our best scientific theories make no reference to God — so natural theology can be dismissed as unscientific.[^1] This view is **scientism**, a key ingredient of the New Atheism. **Reply:** There are no good arguments for scientism and decisive arguments against it. Feser presses four: (1) it is self-defeating or else trivial; (2) the scientific method cannot in principle describe all of reality; (3) the "laws of nature" it invokes cannot be the ultimate explanation; and (4) its main supporting argument — the predictive success of physics — has no force.[^2]

## Evidence
- **(1) Self-defeating or trivial.** The claim "only scientific methods yield knowledge" is *not itself* a scientific claim and cannot be established scientifically; science rests on philosophical assumptions (an external world, that it is law-governed, that intellect and senses can track it) which science can't justify without arguing in a circle. To validate them requires an *extra*-scientific vantage point — whose very existence falsifies scientism.[^3] If the advocate then redefines "science" to include any rational inquiry, scientism becomes trivial and loses its power to dismiss natural theology: he'd have "no nonarbitrary reason for denying that natural theology could in principle count as a science."[^4]
- **(2) Physics is deliberately abstract and quantitative.** Physics studies only what can be captured mathematically — those aspects "susceptible of prediction and control" — so everything qualitative "necessarily falls through his methodological net."[^5] The aircraft-engineer parable: engineers abstract passengers down to average weight; it would be ludicrous to conclude humans have *no* features but weight, or that there's "no fact of the matter" about their meal preferences. Likewise the success of physics does not show the world has no features beyond those in a physics textbook — the method *stipulates* those features be left out "whether they are there or not."[^6] Bertrand Russell (no friend of natural theology) is quoted: physics gives "the logical structure of events" but leaves "completely unknown what is the intrinsic character of the events." Since there is "no such thing as a structure all by itself," physics itself *implies* there is more to reality than it can reveal.[^7]
- **(3) Laws of nature can't be ultimate.** Any appeal to laws (or Krauss-style "layers" of laws) just raises what a law *is*, how it has efficacy, and where it comes from — questions the scientific mode of explanation presupposes laws to answer and so cannot answer. On the Aristotelian view, "laws of nature" are shorthand for how a substance behaves given its nature, so laws *presuppose* the substances and give no comfort to scientism.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "To break out of this circle requires 'getting outside' of science altogether... But then the very existence of that extrascientific vantage point would falsify the claim that science alone gives us a rational means of investigating objective reality." [^3]

> "The success of physics doesn't show that the natural world has no features other than those described in a physics textbook. The reason qualitative features don't show up is not that the method has allowed us to discover that they aren't there but rather that the method has essentially stipulated that they be left out of the description whether they are there or not." [^6]

> "All that physics gives us is certain equations giving abstract properties of their changes. But as to what it is that changes, and what it changes from and to—as to this, physics is silent." (Bertrand Russell, quoted by Feser) [^7]

## LDS Connection
The refutation of scientism is fully shared ground: any theist resisting "only the lab gives knowledge" benefits from Feser's dilemma and from his point that physics, by design, brackets the qualitative and so cannot pronounce on what lies outside its net. Restoration epistemology is squarely anti-scientism — it commands seeking "learning, even by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118) and holds that truth is known "by the power of the Holy Ghost" as well as by reason and the senses (Moroni 10:5), pairing empirical inquiry with revealed knowledge rather than collapsing all knowing into one method. This mirrors the Turek-based [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting]] and [[Philosophy Is the Foundation of Science]]. The points are not distinctively classical-theist, so the divergence with LDS here is minimal; Feser's broader project of course aims this anti-scientism toward establishing the *classical* God, and on that downstream conclusion the Restoration engages his metaphysics critically (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]) — but the anti-scientism arguments themselves are a clean ally for LDS apologetics against materialist atheism.
