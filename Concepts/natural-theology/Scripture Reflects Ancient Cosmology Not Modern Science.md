# Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology, Not Modern Science

## The Point

The Bible assumes the cosmology of its ancient Near Eastern environment, not the cosmos of modern science. For the biblical authors the earth was a flat disk on foundational pillars, capped by a solid dome (the "firmament," Hebrew *raqia*, from a root meaning "to beat out" like a metal plate) that held back the "waters above," with cosmic seas surrounding the whole and the realm of the dead beneath. Because scripture was written within this worldview — not to correct it — it is a category error to read Genesis as a science textbook. As Greenwood puts it, "the Bible assumed the trappings of its environment." [^1] This is the assumption Nephi names when he says God speaks to people "according to their language, unto their understanding" (2 Ne. 31:3). [^2]

## Evidence

- The cosmos in scripture is three-tiered (heavens, earth, sea), the same structure found in Mesopotamian (*Atra-Hasis*) and Egyptian (the Cairo Hymn to Amun-Re) texts; Genesis 1 sorts animals into birds of the heavens, beasts of the earth, and fish of the sea. [^1]
- The earth is a flat disk (Dan. 4:10–11) on pillars (Job 38:4–6); stars are set in the firmament (Gen. 1:14) and the firmament has "windows" through which rain falls (2 Kgs. 7:2; Mal. 3:10). [^1]
- The Hebrew of Genesis 1 reflects this: *raqia* is a beaten-out solid dome separating the waters above from the waters below; *shamayim* means "sky," and *eretz* means "land/ground," not the globe — "the ancient Israelites did not view the world as a globe." [^3]
- The ancient Israelites "were not stupid"; they reasoned from observation (falling rain implies waters above). Their science was simply ancient science. [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "This is not to say that the Bible borrowed from the ancient Near East. Rather, it is better to say that the Bible assumed the trappings of its environment." [^1]

> "The authors and editors of Genesis were not twenty-first-century scientists, and we do them and ourselves a disservice if we expect twenty-first-century science from them." [^2]

> "The picture painted in Genesis 1 and 2 derives from cosmological and scientific perspectives different from modern ones. . . . The Creation accounts are not intended, either anciently or modernly, to serve as definitive scientific statements about the universe." [^4]

## LDS Connection

The Church is not committed to a literal reading of the Genesis creation accounts; President Kimball called the rib "figurative" and Elder Nelson read the "days" as indefinite "divisions of eternity." [^5] Reading scripture in its ancient context (2 Ne. 31:3; D&C 1:24) dissolves rather than creates conflict — President Nelson taught that "conflict only arises from an incomplete knowledge of either science or religion, or both." [^6]
