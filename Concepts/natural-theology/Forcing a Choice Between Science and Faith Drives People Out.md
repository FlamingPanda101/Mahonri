# Forcing a Choice Between Science and Faith Drives People Out

## The Point
When students are handed an "inescapable choice between science and their faith," some abandon religion and others reject good science — and both outcomes are avoidable losses created by the false dichotomy itself.[^1] The harm is documented from both directions: atheistic teachers who say religion "has no place in the classroom," *and* religious leaders who "actively preach against evolution," together convince young people that the two are at war.[^1] The pastoral stakes are made vivid by Lee Meadows, who as a teenager was told by his own church, "Son, people who ask those kinds of questions end up in Hell" — and so "stopped asking questions, which is often a tragic choice for a child to make."[^2]

## Evidence
- About 40% of Americans reject evolution outright for a young-earth account; the authors tie this climate to "the growing trend in our generation to leave organized religion."[^3]
- Students arrive having absorbed conflict from every direction — atheistic high-school teachers, teachers who dodge evolution as "just a theory," opinionated family, anti-evolution church leaders, and pop culture (Pokémon, *The Simpsons*, "monkey-to-man" memes).[^1]
- Faced with a forced choice, "some students decide to leave their religion... Other students choose their religious identity over the science, feeling like they need to reject the theory of evolution to be faithful."[^4]
- **Teachers are casualties too.** A Southern biology teacher told Meadows: "I would love to teach evolution. It's the right thing to do... But if I teach evolution in my community, people at my church will never speak to me again."[^2]
- Meadows's diagnosis: "The churches I was in forced a dichotomy between faith and science." His escape came only from understanding the nature of science — realizing there are "multiple ways of knowing."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Students feel that they are faced with a presumed dichotomy, an inescapable choice between science and their faith. Sadly, some students decide to leave their religion when they feel forced to choose." [^4]

> "Son, people who ask those kinds of questions end up in Hell." [^2]

> "I stopped asking questions, which is often a tragic choice for a child to make. I learned to keep all my questions inside." [^2]

> "By forcing this choice, we lose out on the incredible benefits offered by embracing both ways of knowing." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The chapter is a sustained warning against a *self-inflicted* exodus: when a community makes a secondary question (the mechanism of creation) into a loyalty test, it teaches its own children that honest inquiry is disloyalty — and some will leave rather than lie. President Nelson's principle cuts the other way: "All truth is part of the gospel of Jesus Christ. Whether truth comes from a scientific laboratory or by revelation from the Lord, it is compatible."[^7] The Restoration's command to [[Embrace All Truth From Any Source|seek truth from every source]] means a faithful Saint need never fear an honest fact — and leaders who imply otherwise risk "contributing to the very thing" they fear. The constructive alternative is documented in [[Reconciliation Pedagogy Increases Evolution Acceptance Without Eroding Faith|reconciliation pedagogy]]; the historical correction is that [[The Warfare Model of Science and Religion Is a Myth|the warfare model is a myth]].
