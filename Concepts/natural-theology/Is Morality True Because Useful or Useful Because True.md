# Is Morality True Because Useful or Useful Because True

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] constructs a dilemma for the atheist that deliberately mirrors the Euthyphro dilemma Christians face. He first restates Euthyphro — "is something good because God says so, or does God say so because it's good" — and gives the classical resolution: "something is good because God is good by his very nature, so everything he commands will be good."[^1] He then turns the same structure on the naturalist: **"is morality true because it's useful, or is it useful because it's true?"**[^2]

The dilemma bites on both horns. If **morality is true because it is useful**, then "there's all kinds of useful moralities to achieve social ends that we'd probably be pretty horrified by" — and Horn names the historical track record: "euthanasia for marginalized groups and incapacitated people, that's been done throughout history." Collapsing moral truth into usefulness surrenders ethics "to the mercy of utilitarianism."[^3] If instead **morality is useful because it is true**, then usefulness is downstream of a prior truth, and "we have to ask, well, what makes it true? And it's not the fact that it's useful to us. We have to look for a more prior explanation."[^4] Either way the evolutionary account fails to *ground* morality: it can at most explain how we came to *believe* moral truths, not what makes them true. This is the ontology-not-epistemology point developed in [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]] and [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
- **The math parallel disarms the evolutionary debunking move.** Horn concedes that we likely *did* evolve our moral beliefs — but denies that origin explains their truth, using mathematics as the disanalogy that breaks the debunker: "the reason we have mathematical skills is that our hunter-gatherer ancestors — if you're bad at counting tigers, you didn't last very long — but it doesn't follow that we invented mathematics. We did evolve to get better at it, but math is prior to what we evolved to figure out."[^5] So "Evolution caused [the belief]" does not entail "evolution made it true." Moral truth, like mathematical truth, can be discovered-by-evolved-creatures rather than created-by-evolution.
- **The second horn forces the grounding question the naturalist wants to avoid.** Saying morality is "useful because it's true" concedes there is a moral truth prior to and independent of human utility — at which point the naturalist owes "a more prior explanation" of what that truth consists in, and bare survival-usefulness cannot supply it.[^4] The dilemma thus functions as a pump toward a transcendent ground, paralleling the moral argument in [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]].
- **The first horn has a body count.** Horn does not argue abstractly that utilitarian grounding is *unappealing*; he points out that "true because useful" has historically licensed exactly the atrocities (euthanasia of the marginalized) that secular people themselves condemn — so the horn is not merely counterintuitive but is contradicted by the objector's own settled moral convictions.[^3]
- **Structural symmetry with the theist's own dilemma.** By first answering Euthyphro for the Christian (God is good *by nature*, a third option between arbitrariness and an external standard — see [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]) and then posing the parallel for the atheist, Horn neutralizes the tu-quoque: the Christian has a stable terminus in God's nature, whereas the naturalist's parallel dilemma has no comparable third option — usefulness is either the ground (utilitarian horror) or it is not (and then something prior must ground it). [[Gavin Ortlund|Ortlund]] calls it "a nice twist on the moral argument."[^6]
