# The Universe Had a Beginning - A Cause Beyond Matter and Time

## The Point
Meyer's **first discovery** is that twentieth-century astronomy and astrophysics established the material universe had a *definite beginning in time and space*.[^1] Joined to the principle of causality — everything that begins to exist must have a cause — a beginning of matter, space, time, and energy points to a cause that *cannot itself be material*, because there was no matter "to do the causing" before the beginning. The cause must therefore *transcend* the domains of matter, space, time, and energy.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The expanding universe (observation + theory).** Light from distant galaxies is *redshifted* (stretched to longer wavelengths), indicating the galaxies are receding and the universe is expanding outward in the forward direction of time.[^3] Einstein's 1915 general relativity adds that gravity is the curving of space by mass-energy.[^4]
2. **Hawking's singularity theorem.** Running the expansion backward, matter and energy grow more densely concentrated and space more tightly curved, until at a finite point in the past space becomes "infinitely curved" and "all physical reasoning becomes impossible." Paul Davies: beyond that extremity there would be "neither matter nor space nor time nor energy" — "they come into existence at that point."[^5]
3. **A material cause is excluded in principle.** "You can't explain the origin of the universe as the result of a material cause because it's matter and energy themselves that come into existence at that point, before which there was no matter to do the causing."[^2]
4. **The principle of causality.** "One of the basic principles of rationality is the principle of causality, that everything that begins to exist must have a cause. This is presupposed in every attempt we make to make sense of the world." So the beginning "must have a cause, and yet the cause cannot be material — it must transcend... matter, space, time, and energy."[^6]
5. **Attempts to dodge the beginning carry theological freight.** Meyer notes that theoretical-physics maneuvers to "circumvent the beginning" themselves "end up having... theological implications" — they do not cleanly avoid a transcendent cause.[^7]
6. **A convergent witness.** Nobel laureate Arno Penzias (co-discoverer of the cosmic background radiation, 1965, which sealed the Big Bang over the steady-state model): the cosmological evidence is "precisely what I would have predicted had I nothing to go on but the five books of Moses, the Psalms, and the Bible as a whole."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Everything that begins to exist must have a cause... and yet the cause cannot be material. It must transcend the domains of matter, space, time, and energy." [^2]

> "Before which there would be neither matter nor space nor time nor energy. They come into existence at that point." (Paul Davies, quoted by Meyer) [^5]

## LDS Connection
The argument's thrust — that the observable cosmos points beyond itself to a transcendent, non-material Cause rather than to self-existing matter — is congenial to LDS theism and a strong reply to materialism, and it resonates with Genesis 1:1, "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." The honest divergence is over *what* began and what "create" means. Meyer (with classical theism) reads the beginning as the absolute origination of matter from nonbeing (*ex nihilo*); LDS scripture teaches God *organized* eternal materials — "the elements are eternal" and "intelligence... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" (D&C 93:29, 33). A Latter-day Saint can wield premises (1)–(2) against an *eternal, self-existing* cosmos while reading "the beginning" as the beginning of *this* organized order rather than of all reality.
