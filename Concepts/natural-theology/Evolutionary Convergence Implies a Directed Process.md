# Evolutionary Convergence Implies a Directed Process

## The Point

Evolution is not an open-ended dice-roll: the same forms and functions recur independently across unrelated lineages — a phenomenon biologists call **convergence**. [[Samuel T. Wilkinson]], drawing on Cambridge paleobiologist [[Simon Conway Morris]], argues that this repeated re-discovery of the same solutions means evolution is "constrained to go in certain directions" by deeper chemical and physical laws. The "museum of all possible animals" is astronomically large, yet life occupies a vanishingly small, recurring corner of it — evidence of a process "seeded with inevitabilities" rather than pure randomness. This is the empirical backbone for the larger claim that an evolutionary cosmos can be directional and purposeful.

## Evidence

- **Convergence: the same answers, over and over.** Wilkinson highlights that eyes "have developed independently about 40 different times," and that when [[Richard Dawkins]] asked a colleague for "good ideas in biology that have evolved only once," the expert could name only "a handful." [^1] That a complex structure recurs independently "didn't have to, but it did." [^2]
- **The museum of all possible animals.** Borrowing Dawkins's thought experiment, Wilkinson notes the number of possible biological forms "is greater than the number of atoms in the known universe," yet actual life is "a tiny, infinitely small fraction" — and those few forms keep recurring. [^3]
- **The injection-molding analogy.** Like molten plastic that looks random up close but fills "a predetermined shape" of a mold, mutations may look random while "filling a predetermined form" — "there seems to be this mold that is inherent in the physical laws of the universe and evolution maybe kind of feeling out the edges of it." [^4]
- **The loaded-dice analogy (Hansen).** [[Jacob Hansen]] extends the point: rolling a "one" repeatedly signals "some constraint that is causing it to fall on one every time" — if outcomes keep landing in a narrow range, "it's not random." [^5]
- **Honest scope.** Wilkinson concedes randomness "may play some role" but "a comparatively small one"; he stops short of claiming proof of design, holding that the deeper laws "guiding the process" are "clearly not" themselves random. [^6]

## Best Quotes

> "Certain forms and functions have kind of recurred over and over and over and over again in evolution, such that there seems to be — in the words of Conway Morris — this is a process that is seeded with inevitabilities, and it's not just one random thing after another." [^7]

> "There seems to be this mold that is inherent in the physical laws of the universe, and evolution maybe kind of feeling out the edges of it." [^4]

## LDS Connection

Directionality in nature dovetails with the Latter-day Saint picture of a God who *organizes* pre-existing matter toward intended ends rather than producing it by random accident. Where classical design arguments reason from fine-tuned constants (compare [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]), convergence supplies a *biological* teleology: the "mold" of physical law channels life toward forms that make embodied, relational, agentive beings possible — exactly the kind of creatures the plan of salvation requires. Convergence thus furnishes a this-worldly hint that the cosmos is built to bring about persons capable of eternal progression.
