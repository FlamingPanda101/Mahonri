# The Argument from Reason - Atheism Undermines Reason Itself

## The Point
If materialistic atheism is true, then every thought is just the product of nonrational physical forces — atoms moving according to the laws of physics and chemistry. But if our reasoning is wholly determined by nonrational causes, there is no reason to trust that our beliefs are true, including the belief in atheism itself.[^1] Turek's central thesis is therefore radical: "The main point of this chapter is not to show that all arguments for atheism fail. The main point of this chapter is to show that all arguments for anything fail if atheism is true."[^2] Reason can only be trusted if minds are more than molecules in motion.

## Evidence
Turek leans on atheists' own admissions. Evolutionary biologist J. B. S. Haldane wrote: "If my mental processes are determined wholly by the motions of atoms in my brain, I have no reason to suppose that my beliefs are true... and hence I have no reason for supposing my brain to be composed of atoms."[^3] Francis Crick's "Astonishing Hypothesis" reduced "you," your joys, sorrows, identity, and free will to "no more than the behavior of a vast assembly of nerve cells."[^4] Turek presses the point: if that is true, then Crick's own book is also nothing but nerve-cell behavior, giving us no reason to believe it — "We're not really reasoning; we're merely reacting... Materialism refutes itself!"[^5] He enlists [[C.S. Lewis]]: "Unless human reasoning is valid no science can be true."[^6] Turek frames the move positively too: realism — the commonsense belief in a reality external to our minds — is best explained if "our minds work because they are made in the image of the Great Mind," designed by "the source of truth, reason, and reality."[^7] He notes the irony of [[Richard Dawkins]] headlining a "Reason Rally" while his worldview "actually makes reason impossible."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Since the nonrational laws of physics determine everything we think, there's no reason to trust our reason." [^1]

> "We're not free creatures—we're just molecular machines. We're not really reasoning; we're merely reacting... Materialism refutes itself!" [^5]

## LDS Connection
The argument from reason resonates deeply with Restoration anthropology, which denies that the mind is reducible to matter and roots human rationality in something eternal. Latter-day Saints teach that "the intelligence of spirits... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" ([[D&C 93]], v.29) and that "there is no such thing as immaterial matter; all spirit is matter" yet of "a more fine or pure" kind (D&C 131:7–8) — a refined substance, not nonrational mechanism. Because the human intelligence is uncreated and akin to the divine, our capacity to "arrive at true conclusions about the orderly external world"[^7] is not a fluke of colliding atoms but a feature of beings whose minds share in the light and intelligence of God (D&C 93:36). See also [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]].
