# Electrons in Love - The Psychophysical-Laws Objection Rebutted

## The Claim
[[Neil Sinhababu]] (Neil S. Babu) reframes the fine-tuning argument: what motivates it, he says, is not life-as-we-know-it but the existence of *conscious embodied agents*, whatever they are made of. He then argues that the probability of such agents may not be low, because if the universe's **psychophysical laws** (the laws linking physical states to consciousness) were different, even a hydrogen-only universe could host minds — protons and electrons that "have sensory experiences," "yearn to be together," and "fall in love." And a theist, he presses, cannot dismiss conscious electrons as metaphysically impossible while affirming non-physical minds (God): "theistic commitments to a non-physical God are incompatible with the view that... love between subatomic particles is so bizarre as to be metaphysically impossible." [[Trent Horn]] rebuts this.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (Horn's replies)
1. **(Strong, disanalogy)** Minds existing *apart from* bodies does not entail minds existing *within any* body. Horn's analogy: light can travel apart from a medium (vacuum), but it does *not* follow that light can travel through *any* medium — it cannot pass through lead. Likewise consciousness existing without a body (God, angels) does not show consciousness could inhere in a hydrogen atom.[^1]
2. **(Strong, theistic grounding)** On theism, God and angels are conscious "because of God's unique ability to sustain that state of affairs, not some natural psychophysical law" — so the theist owes no commitment to mind-friendly natural laws making electrons conscious.[^2] Robin Collins' point applies: a hydrogen atom lacks the "stable complexity" for a rational nature ([[Robin Collins]]).
3. **(Strong, even-if)** Even granting that conscious-electron universes *could* exist, our universe's complex biological consciousness is still absurdly rare given the constants, so it still demands explanation — John Leslie's red-wall/white-wall fly: a bullet striking the lone fly on the blank white wall (our solitary life-permitting universe under known laws) warrants a design inference regardless of the many flies on the red wall (universes with different psychophysical laws).[^3]
4. **(Moderate, value reply)** A world of "real love" involving sacrifice, hardship, and free self-locomotion is plausibly *better* than "trillions of Lovecraftian electrons" floating aimlessly for billions of years — a world with higher-quality moral goods, not merely more minimal ones — so God would have reason to prefer our kind of universe.[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
Sinhababu's sharpest point stands as a genuine challenge: the theist *does* hold that consciousness is substrate-independent in at least some cases (God is immaterial), so the theist cannot lean on "minds require brains" the way a naturalist can. If consciousness is not essentially tied to neural complexity, why think it is tied to *any* particular physical arrangement — why not electrons? The objection thus exploits a real tension in theistic philosophy of mind, and Horn's "God sustains it directly" reply risks looking ad hoc (consciousness is possible wherever God wills it, conveniently including us and excluding the cases that would defeat fine-tuning).

## Counter-objection
The light-through-lead disanalogy answers the tension precisely: substrate-*independence in some cases* is not substrate-*universality*. That consciousness can exist immaterially (God) or in suitably complex bodies (humans) is fully consistent with its being *impossible* in a hydrogen atom whose nature lacks the requisite complexity — just as light's medium-independence is consistent with its being blocked by lead. "God sustains conscious agents where their nature permits" is not ad hoc; it follows from the classical view that creaturely consciousness tracks a being's *form*, and a hydrogen atom's form is too simple. And crucially, Horn's "even if" concession seals the argument: grant Sinhababu his conscious electrons, and the John Leslie point still bites — *our* finely-tuned biological universe remains improbable under the known laws and still cries out for explanation, so the objection at most multiplies the kinds of life-permitting universes without removing the demand to explain *ours*.

## LDS Connection
This is technical philosophy of mind and natural theology with no distinctively LDS stake; both classical theists and Latter-day Saints can use Horn's replies. (A Latter-day Saint would add only that LDS metaphysics already treats intelligence/spirit as a fundamental, uncreated reality — [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] — which independently blocks the assumption that consciousness is a cheap by-product readily had from any matter under tweaked laws.)
