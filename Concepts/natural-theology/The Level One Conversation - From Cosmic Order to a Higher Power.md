# The Level One Conversation - From Cosmic Order to a Higher Power

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] organizes his apologetics into stacked "conversations," and the *level one conversation* is the case that leads to the conclusion **that there is a God**.[^1] It works not from scripture or revelation but from the bare features of reality — "Who am I? What is the purpose of all of this? Why is the universe here at all? Why is it organized the way that it is? Why do there seem to be regularities and patterns?"[^1] Grappling honestly with those questions, Hansen says, leads to the judgment that "the world is not just a bunch of matter and motion," which "at least open[s] the door to the idea of some sort of a higher power."[^2] Design and fine-tuning are the engine of this first conversation.

## Evidence
1. **It is a stack, not a leap.** Hansen breaks belief into three conversations that "build on each other" — "you can't really have conversation two unless you've had conversation one" — and level one is the foundation that concludes there is a God.[^1] This mirrors the four-level structure he lays out elsewhere; see [[The Four-Level Conversation Structure of Apologetics]] and [[Anatomy of Belief - Three Independent Conversations]].
2. **The questions are perennial, not parochial.** Hansen frames them as "deep profound philosophical questions" running "back to Plato and Aristotle and all the way back through Western history," and finds it "fascinating to see the way people have grappled with this."[^1] The argument is therefore meant to be common ground with any thoughtful person, not a sectarian appeal.
3. **Order and regularity are the data.** The decisive observation is that reality is *organized* — exhibiting "regularities and patterns" — rather than chaotic; this is what makes "matter and motion" an insufficient description and what the rest of the clip cashes out as specified complexity and fine-tuning.[^2] See [[Specified Complexity - The Cloud and the Statue]] and [[Chance, Necessity, or Design - The Fine-Tuning Trilemma]].
4. **The Fibonacci illustration.** Hansen's interviewer adds the example of the Fibonacci sequence "discovered by mathematicians" — "something so beautifully eloquent" that "it transcends the logic of a chaotic system," making it "seem impossible that this kind of complexity could just spring up out of nothing."[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "The level one conversation is this idea that leads you to the conclusion that there is a God... why is the universe here at all? Why is it organized the way that it is? Why do there seem to be regularities and patterns?" [^1]

> "The world is not just a... bunch of matter and motion, and that leads you to sort of this — at least opening the door to the idea of some sort of a higher power." [^2]

## LDS Connection
LDS theology is at home with a cosmos that is *organized* and *ordered* by a higher power — indeed the Book of Mormon stages this very argument when Alma tells Korihor that "all things denote there is a God... the planets which move in their regular form do witness that there is a supreme creator" ([[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]]; see [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]). Keep the tie honest: Restoration theology characteristically frames God as the *organizer* of an ordered, law-governed cosmos of eternal matter rather than as a creator *ex nihilo*, so "higher power" is read as the supreme Organizer-and-Orderer of reality. Hansen's level-one case is shared natural theology, the kind of common ground he elsewhere urges Latter-day Saints to recover.
