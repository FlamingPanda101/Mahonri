# Intelligent Design as Inference to the Best Explanation

## The Point
Turek argues that intelligent design uses the very same forensic, historical method Darwin used: examining clues left behind and drawing an "inference to the best explanation."[^1] The dispute between Darwinists and design theorists is therefore not about *evidence* — everyone studies the same evidence — but about a prior philosophical commitment over which *causes* are allowed onto the table.[^2] If one rules out intelligent causes before looking, one will never read the evidence correctly should an intelligence actually be responsible. And the definition of "science" that excludes design is itself a philosophical claim, not a scientific finding.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Same method as Darwin.** Origin questions are historical, so a scientist "can't go into the lab to repeat and observe the origin and history of life." One evaluates clues and infers the best explanation — exactly what archaeologists and detectives (who are doing "science") do.[^1] Meyer: neo-Darwinism and ID are "two different answers — formulated using a similar logic and method of reasoning — to the same question."[^4]
2. **A debate about interpretation, not data.** "Everyone is looking at the same evidence. This is a debate about how to interpret the evidence," which "involves philosophical commitments about what causes will be considered possible before looking at the evidence."[^2]
3. **The demarcation question is philosophical.** "How one defines 'science' is not science itself." Philosophy undergirds every natural science; declaring a theory outside one's "materialistic dogma" does not make it false. "The issue is truth — not whether something fits a materialistic definition of science."[^3]
4. **Unseen causes are routine in science.** Meyer notes physicists postulate forces, fields, and quarks; biochemists infer submicroscopic structures; psychologists discuss mental states; evolutionists invoke unobserved past mutations and vanished transitional forms. An unseen designer is "no more unscientific than postulating unobserved macroevolutionary steps" (Lennox).[^5]
5. **Critics substitute insult for argument.** In debate, Michael Ruse called Meyer's conclusion "religious," Peter Atkins "nonsense," and Peter Ward "crap" — but, as Meyer answered, "Crap" is not an argument; if Ward's reasoning were valid, we could not even infer that the Rosetta Stone was made by an intelligent agent rather than by wind and erosion.[^6]
6. **The "bad design" objection fails.** Claiming God would have given us "titanium bones" assumes we know the designer's intent, ignores that all design in a physical world involves trade-offs, and confuses a *preference* with the absence of design: "Different design is not no design!"[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "This is not a debate about evidence. Everyone is looking at the same evidence. This is a debate about how to interpret the evidence."[^2]

> "The fact that certain self-appointed priests of science assert that a theory is outside the bounds of their own materialistic dogma doesn't mean that the theory is false. The issue is truth."[^3]

## LDS Connection
Turek's complaint — that an *a priori* commitment to materialism forecloses an intelligent cause before the evidence is weighed — parallels the Latter-day Saint critique of methodological naturalism wherever it is treated as metaphysics rather than method (compare [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship]]). LDS natural theology likewise reasons from observable design to a Designer (see [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]). The honest caveat is that Turek's inference, taken alone, yields only "an intelligent cause with purpose"; it does not by itself establish the specific, embodied, organizing God of Restoration theology — a further step Turek concedes the biology alone cannot supply.[^8]
