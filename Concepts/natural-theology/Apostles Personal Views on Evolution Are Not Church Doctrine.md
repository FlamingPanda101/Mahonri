# Apostles Personal Views on Evolution Are Not Church Doctrine

## The Point
A favorite critic move is to quote a strongly anti-evolution Apostle — usually Joseph Fielding Smith or Orson F. Whitney — as if his opinion were *the Church's doctrine*. The history of the 1909/1925 statements **explodes this move**. During the same decades, the Quorum of the Twelve openly contained Apostles on **opposite sides**: Smith and Whitney rejected evolution; the scientist-Apostles James E. Talmage and John A. Widtsoe were tentatively open to it (old earth, long death, even human evolution). The First Presidency *knew* this and deliberately **kept the Church from adopting any of their individual views**. As Widtsoe himself cautioned the Twelve, Apostles "are bound to have individual views, but they should not be pressed as certainties." [^1]

## Evidence
1. **The Twelve held openly incompatible positions — by design.** Spackman: the First Presidency statements "kept the Church collectively from espousing or advocating any of the views of individual Apostles like Orson Whitney, Joseph Fielding Smith, and others (who rejected evolution) or the views of James E. Talmage, John A. Widtsoe, and others (who were tentatively open to evolution)." [^2]
2. **Widtsoe's own rule for apostolic opinion.** Writing to the Council of the Twelve in 1931: Apostles "are bound to have individual views, but they should not be pressed as certainties." This is a General Authority articulating the doctrine/opinion line *from the inside*. [^3]
3. **Pro-evolution leaders were never disciplined — they were promoted.** Talmage (geologist) was ordained an Apostle in 1911 and Widtsoe (chemist) in 1921 *after* the 1909 statement. Pro-evolution LDS scientists like Frederick Pack were given Church-published pamphlets distributed by the tens of thousands and put on the Sunday School general board; Adam S. Bennion, who taught "Evolution and Christian Faith" to CES teachers, became an Apostle in 1953. Teaching evolution in a Church setting was not treated as teaching against the Church. [^4]
4. **The pro- and anti- camps existed simultaneously in print.** Joseph Fielding Smith published vigorous anti-evolution pieces ("The Origin and Destiny of Man... the Great 'Miracle of Unbelief'") in the same era that Talmage and Widtsoe argued scientific fact should inform scriptural interpretation. Both ran in Church magazines; neither was the Church's official position. [^5]
5. **Talmage's interpretive rule cuts against treating any view as final.** Talmage: "We should be very careful in taking what we consider the one and only interpretation... and sweeping away as utterly wrong all accumulated knowledge that may seem to point to another interpretation. We have to recognize fact whether it be called scripture or science." [^6]
6. **The official record itself names the disagreement.** The Church's "Organic Evolution" history entry openly states that "Latter-day Saints and their leaders found themselves on both sides of this issue," naming Talmage and Widtsoe on one side and Joseph Fielding Smith on the other — and then cites the 1931 instruction to "leave... [it] to scientific research." [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "[Apostles] are bound to have individual views, but they should not be pressed as certainties." — John A. Widtsoe to the Council of the Twelve, 1931 [^3]

> "[The statements] kept the Church collectively from espousing or advocating any of the views of individual Apostles... who rejected evolution... or... who were tentatively open to evolution." [^2]

> "We should be very careful in taking what we consider the one and only interpretation... and sweeping away as utterly wrong all accumulated knowledge that may seem to point to another interpretation." — James E. Talmage [^6]

> "Latter-day Saints and their leaders found themselves on both sides of this issue." — Church History Topics, "Organic Evolution" [^7]

## LDS Connection
This is the evolution-specific application of the foundational principle that **[[Doctrine Is Defined by the Unified Voice of the Highest Authorities|doctrine is the unified, sustained voice of the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve]]** — not the private opinion of any single leader, however senior or forceful. It is reinforced by the recognition that **[[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine|prophets are fallible]]** and that there is a real difference between **[[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction|inspired, infallible, and ultimate]]** statements. The takeaway for apologetics: when a critic quotes Joseph Fielding Smith's anti-evolution writings as "what Mormons believe," the correct reply is that the Church *deliberately declined to make his view official* and tolerated the opposite view in his fellow Apostles at the same time. An Apostle's opinion on a scientific question is exactly that — an opinion that "should not be pressed as a certainty."
