# Winning the Debate Is Not Winning the Argument

## The Point
Atheist [[Alex OConnor]] argues that [[Christopher Hitchens]] habitually won *debates* without winning *arguments*: he "presented [bad arguments] so charmingly and convincingly that it's easy to be won over by them" [^1]. O'Connor names this directly — "presenting bad arguments cleverly is the definition of sophistry" — and insists that rhetorical victory and logical victory are two different things [^1]. His closing plea to fellow admirers is the thesis in miniature: "please don't think that just because he won the debate he also won the argument" [^2]. This is a structural critique of the entire New Atheist debating style, voiced from inside atheism: charm, wit, applause lines, and audience cues ("relying on his audience's tendency to go 'yeah, I don't need God'") can manufacture the *appearance* of having the upper hand "even when he definitely doesn't" [^3].

## Evidence
- O'Connor concedes Hitchens was "fierce and brilliant... as a journalist" but "in no way a serious philosopher" [^4].
- He diagnoses Hitchens' core technique as weaseling "out of actually answering serious questions by relying on quips, jokes, or other kinds of distractions" [^4].
- Hitchens himself admitted the practice: if a cheap point "gets a laugh I'm not above doing it... or I skip a stage in an argument... especially if it works" [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "Unfortunately, presenting bad arguments cleverly is the definition of sophistry." [^1]

> "Please don't let the man's aura blind you to his mistakes and faulty reasoning, and please don't think that just because he won the debate he also won the argument." [^2]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is one of the most useful concessions an apologist can cite, precisely because it is not partisan: a prominent atheist is conceding that the rhetorical force of figures like Hitchens outran their logical substance. It licenses a clean move in conversation — *grant* that the New Atheist was eloquent and persuasive, then ask the audience to separate the performance from the proof. Be fair, though: O'Connor's point cuts both ways. He explicitly warns himself against "only criticizing my opponents," and the same standard condemns *Christian* debaters who substitute one-liners and applause for grounded argument (cf. [[Victory as an Idol in Apologetics]]). The lesson is not "atheism loses" but "audience reaction is not truth-tracking" — a discipline both sides owe the question. It also models intellectual honesty: O'Connor remains an atheist and says so, which is exactly why the critique carries weight.
