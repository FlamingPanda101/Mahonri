# Motivated Reasoning and the Tides of Bias

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that human reasoning is shot through with systematic, predictable bias — yet this does **not** dissolve the distinction between justified and unjustified belief. People often acquire beliefs "for reasons that are more emotional and social than strictly cognitive" (wishful thinking, in-group loyalty, self-deception), so knowing *what* someone believes is not the same as knowing *how* they think.[^1] Crucially, "a bias is not merely a source of error; it is a reliable pattern of error," which means reason can be used to detect and correct its own failures.[^2]

## Evidence
Harris catalogs reasoning failures: arbitrary "anchoring" (estimates of San Francisco doctors shift with the last digits of one's Social Security number), and the finding that such errors are "systematic," correlated across tasks, and decrease as cognitive ability rises and with training.[^3] He cites John Jost's twelve-country study (23,000 subjects) linking political conservatism to dogmatism, inflexibility, death anxiety, and need for closure — while noting liberalism, too, has its biases (e.g., race effects in a trolley-style sacrifice study).[^4] Against the stronger conclusions of neurologists Robert Burton and Chris Frith — that conscious reasoning is mostly post-hoc rationalization "born of the confusion between imagination and memory," so "reason isn't going to bridge" worldview gaps — Harris counters that the unconscious basis of thought does not make reasoning post-hoc, any more than being unaware of the neurology of algebra means we never follow algebra's rules.[^5] He insists "the fact that reason must be rooted in our biology does not negate the principles of reason," citing Wittgenstein on how the contingent structure of our minds constrains, but does not abolish, logic.[^6] His witchcraft analogy is the rebuttal's edge: belief in witchcraft was once a "cultural universal" yet collapsed under reason, so religion's genetic tethering does not make it ineradicable.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "A bias is not merely a source of error; it is a reliable pattern of error. Every bias, therefore, reveals something about the structure of the human mind." [^2]

> "The fact that reason must be rooted in our biology does not negate the principles of reason." [^6]

## Apologetic Engagement
On the diagnosis, much common ground exists: scripture and the Restored Gospel likewise warn that the natural mind is prone to self-deception and motivated reasoning, and that humility before evidence and correction is a virtue (cf. [[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think]]). The deeper tension is that Harris wants two things at once — reason as a biologically rooted, bias-ridden organ, *and* reason as a reliable judge that can "bridge" worldviews and stand over its own errors. The theist presses: on naturalism, why trust a faculty selected for survival to track truth in domains evolution never "aimed" at, especially once you concede how systematically it misfires? Harris's confidence that reason transcends its biases is better underwritten if reason images a rational Creator than if it is the output of blind matter (the argument from reason; cf. [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]], [[Reason Is Emergent From Intuition]]). His witchcraft-religion parallel also equivocates: that *some* universal beliefs were false (witchcraft) does not show *this* universal belief (in God) is false — that would require an independent argument, not a sociological pattern. Notably, Harris's own demand that we follow "valid chains of evidence and argument" presupposes objective logical norms his materialism does not obviously fund (cf. [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]).
