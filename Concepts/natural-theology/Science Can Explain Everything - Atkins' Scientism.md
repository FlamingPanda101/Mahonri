# Science Can Explain Everything - Atkins' Scientism

## The Point
Atkins states the positive creed of **scientism** plainly: "if we can explain everything without having to invoke supernaturalism of any kind then we are showing... an extraordinary, wonderful aspect" of the human mind.[^1] He grounds this not in pessimism about religion but in a near-boundless optimism about the human brain — "capable of understanding seemingly without limit" — and concludes that to claim "there is an area where the human mind cannot penetrate and cannot give comprehensible explanation... is a denial of humanity."[^2] His method is parsimony ("simplest explanations are adequate explanations unless they can be shown to be deficient") and holism: science is "a reticulation of ideas," an interconnected network in which "no field of inquiry is an island."[^3] Meyer's reply is that this is an unargued metaphysical commitment, not a finding of science: deciding in advance that every question must have a materialistic answer guarantees a materialistic answer "but it may not in fact be the true explanation."[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The creed.** "I think that if we can explain everything without having to invoke supernaturalism of any kind then we are showing what a Christian should think of as an extraordinary, wonderful aspect of God's only image, man."[^1]
2. **Simplicity as a rule.** "My position is that simplest explanations are adequate explanations unless they can be shown to be deficient." He invokes Ockham's razor ("my good friend William of Ockham") as his starting point.[^5]
3. **Science as a network.** "Science is a reticulation of ideas, a whole network of ideas that hang together... cosmology depends upon properties of fundamental particles... no field of inquiry is an island." He uses this to resist treating the origin of life as a special "island" where intelligence may be invoked.[^3]
4. **The "context of inquiry" exchange.** Atkins asks why we don't "assign intelligence" to gravity or general relativity. Meyer answers that the *context of inquiry* differs: asking what nature "ordinarily does" excludes intelligence by the nature of the question, but asking "how did nature... arise in the first place" makes an intelligent cause "at least a logically possible answer."[^6]
5. **Mind reduced to matter.** Atkins grants that "mind is a reality" but insists "mind is material... an activity of the brain, and the brain is solely material" — closing the door Meyer wants left open to mind as an explanatory cause.[^7]
6. **Meyer's rebuttal — the question-begging move.** "If you decide that in advance then you'll get a materialistic explanation, but it may not in fact be the true explanation... the explanation the evidence is pointing to."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "For people like Steven and his colleagues to suggest that there is an area where the human mind cannot penetrate... I think that's a denial of humanity." (Atkins) [^2]

> "We don't want to have to... give up the ghost at one point and invoke the Holy Ghost instead." (Atkins) [^8]

> "If you decide that in advance then you'll get a materialistic explanation, but it may not in fact be the true explanation." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The refutation of scientism is fully shared ground for Latter-day Saints: a faith that commands learning "by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118) and that knows truth "by the power of the Holy Ghost" as well as by reason (Moroni 10:5) is squarely anti-scientism, refusing to collapse all knowing into the laboratory. Meyer's point — that "science can explain everything" is a philosophical presupposition smuggled in ahead of the evidence, not a result of science — is one any theist can press. (Atkins' confidence in the unlimited reach of the *unaided* human mind also sits uneasily beside the Restoration's emphasis on revelation as a distinct and necessary avenue to truth.) For the developed philosophical case, see [[Scientism Cannot Describe or Explain All Reality]] and [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting]].
