# The Shiny Sphere in the Woods Illustration

## The Point
The weakest-looking premise of the contingency argument is the first — "everything that exists has an explanation of its existence" — which an atheist may resist by saying of the universe, "It's just there, and that's all. No explanation needed." [^1] [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]'s reply is an intuition pump: imagine hiking and finding a shiny sphere lying on the ground. You would naturally ask how it came to be there, and think it absurd if a friend said, "There's no reason or explanation for it. Stop wondering. It just *is*!" [^2] The illustration shows that treating an existing thing as a brute, explanation-less fact is unreasonable — and, crucially, that this verdict does not change with the object's size. This is a popular-level support for the [[The Principle of Sufficient Reason|principle of sufficient reason]] and a companion to the more technical case in [[Why a Brute Fact Cannot Terminate Explanation]].

## Evidence
- The core move is the **scaling argument**: if the ball were larger it would still require an explanation, and "if the ball were the size of the universe, the change in its size wouldn't remove the need for an explanation." [^3] Mere magnitude is explanatorily irrelevant, so the universe's vastness gives no license to stop asking why it exists.
- The illustration anticipates the "but what about God?" rejoinder by pairing with [[Contingent and Necessary Beings|the contingent/necessary distinction]]: the demand for an explanation applies to contingent things (like the sphere, and like the universe), whereas a necessary being exists by its own nature and so is not in the same explanatory predicament. [^4]
- It reframes curiosity about the universe as continuous with ordinary and scientific reasoning — refusing explanation for the sphere (or the cosmos) is the anti-intellectual move, not the demand for one. [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "There's no reason or explanation for it. Stop wondering. It just IS!" [^2]

> "If the ball were the size of the universe, the change in its size wouldn't remove the need for an explanation." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The sphere illustration is a faith-friendly, broadly theistic tool: it targets the materialist's "the universe just *is*" exactly where Latter-day Saint apologists also resist it, insisting that contingent reality is intelligible and not a sheer brute fact (compare [[Why a Brute Fact Cannot Terminate Explanation]]). The honest divergence is again downstream, at the explanatory terminus. Craig's analogy drives toward a single necessary being as the explanation of all contingent reality. The Restoration agrees the cosmos demands explanation but holds that the self-existent, non-contingent layer of reality includes eternal, uncreated realities — intelligence, matter, and law — rather than a lone metaphysically simple God (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). The fair tension for the LDS side is that these eternal realities must themselves be necessary and intelligible rather than brute — a standard the sphere illustration would press on any view.
