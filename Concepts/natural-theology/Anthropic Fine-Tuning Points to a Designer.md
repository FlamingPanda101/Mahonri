# Anthropic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer

## The Point
Meyer argues that modern physics reveals design "in the very fabric of the universe": since the 1950s–60s physicists have recognized that the laws, fundamental constants, and initial conditions are finely tuned, against staggering odds, to make life possible.[^1] Because this fine-tuning exhibits exactly the marks that trigger a design inference — extreme improbability joined to functional specification — and because deliberately tuning a system toward a propitious end is the kind of thing intelligent agents characteristically do, intelligent design stands as the best explanation for cosmic fine-tuning.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Many independent, life-critical parameters.** Slight changes to the expansion rate of the universe, the speed of light, the masses of quarks, or the strength of gravity or electromagnetism would render life — even basic chemistry — impossible. Physicists call these "anthropic coincidences" and their convergence "the fine-tuning of the universe."[^3]
2. **Leading physicists read it as design.** Meyer quotes Paul Davies ("the impression of design is overwhelming") and Fred Hoyle ("a commonsense interpretation of the facts suggests that a superintellect has monkeyed with physics, as well as chemistry and biology").[^4]
3. **Fine-tuning shows the design signature.** It exhibits "precisely those features — extreme improbability and functional specification — that invariably trigger an awareness of, and justify an inference to, intelligent design."[^5]
4. **The design inference for physics parallels the one for biology.** The same logic that detects design in DNA's specified information applies to the specified physical parameters, making design "detectable in both the physical parameters of the universe and the information-bearing properties of life."[^2] See [[Anthropic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer#The Point]] alongside [[DNA as Digital Code - The Information Enigma]].

## LDS Connection
A finely tuned, life-permitting cosmos coheres easily with LDS theology's picture of a purposeful, ordering Creator. The honest caveat is the same as for Meyer's other arguments: he infers a generic designer (and ultimately a transcendent God), whereas the distinctive LDS claim is that God *organized* a cosmos of eternal materials toward life and exaltation rather than fine-tuning a universe created from nothing.
