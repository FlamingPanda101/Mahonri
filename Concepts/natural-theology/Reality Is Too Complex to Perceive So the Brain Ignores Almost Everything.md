# Reality Is Too Complex to Perceive So the Brain Ignores Almost Everything

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] argues that the foundational problem the brain solves is not how to *take in* reality but how to *filter it out*. Reality is layered and interconnected to the point of being "complex beyond comprehension," so a perceiving system that tried to register it all would be overwhelmed. Adaptive perception therefore requires ignoring "almost everything" — which makes selective blindness a built-in feature of cognition, not a defect. [^1] This sets the epistemic stage: whatever we "know" from perception is already a drastic, pre-filtered simplification.

## Argument
1. Reality has nested layers of staggering complexity — "subatomic… atomic… molecular… [organ-level] exceedingly complex structures… you as a totality… families… tribal groupings… Nations… all the way out to the limits of the cosmos." [^1]
2. The brain is the most complex item of all: it is "so much more complex than everything else in the universe that it's not even in the same category." Peterson cites [[Gerald Edelman]]'s estimate that "there are more connections… more patterns of connections in your brain than there are subatomic particles in the universe." [^1]
3. A system facing such complexity "ha[s] to simplify it down to the point where you can sort of think about one thing and do one thing," which requires screening "all of that out." [^2]
4. So the brain's standing question is "what can I ignore?" — and "the answer to that is well you need to ignore almost everything." [^2]
5. This is "a deadly deadly difficult problem" because relevance is not self-evident and can change suddenly: with "imperfect knowledge you may think something's irrelevant and it turns out to be of critical importance." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Reality is so complicated, it has so many layers and so many interconnected causal links that it's complex beyond comprehension." [^1]

> "There are more connections, more patterns of connections in your brain than there are subatomic particles in the universe." [^1]

> "Part of the problem your brain is always facing is what can I ignore — and the answer to that is well you need to ignore almost everything." [^2]
