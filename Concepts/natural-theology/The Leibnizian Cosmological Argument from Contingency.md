# The Leibnizian Cosmological Argument from Contingency

## The Point
Even if the series of contingent things regresses infinitely — a beginningless universe, an endless cycle of Big Bangs and Big Crunches, or a multiverse — the totality is not thereby explained. [^1] Following Leibniz and Samuel Clarke, Feser argues that even an infinite series of contingent things "is just as contingent as the individual contingent things that make it up," so the question "why does the series itself exist at all?" remains. [^2] David Hume thought that once each member has a prior member as its cause, everything is explained; Feser calls this "an illusion." [^3] Only a being that exists necessarily — of its own nature, depending on nothing else — can terminate the regress of explanation. [^4]

## Evidence
Feser develops the argument along two independent routes:

1. **The "whole series" route (Leibniz/Clarke).** Borrowing Leibniz's image: imagine an infinite series of geometry books, each copied from a prior one. We have an immediate cause for every book, yet have not explained why the content is *geometry* rather than Shakespeare or a repair manual. [^5] Likewise, even an infinitely old universe leaves unexplained why it consists of *these* kinds of contingent things, governed by *these* laws, rather than others. [^6] Positing a further *contingent* cause outside the series merely relocates the problem, generating a higher-order series that needs the same explanation — "passing the explanatory buck" *ad infinitum*. [^7]

2. **The "here and now" route.** Forget the series as a whole. Take any single contingent thing: since nothing in its nature entails its existence, we must ask why it persists in existence *at this very moment* rather than being annihilated — a question not answered by naming whatever generated it in the past. [^8] This requires a *simultaneous* sustaining cause; if that cause is contingent, the same question recurs, yielding a regress of simultaneous causes that can only terminate in a necessary being. [^9] This route is "reminiscent of the Aristotelian, Neo-Platonic, and Thomistic arguments" and complements them: contingent things require explanation because they are mixtures of actuality and potentiality, are composed of parts, and have essences distinct from their existence. [^10]

## Best Quotes
> "Even if there is an infinite series of contingent things, each caused by a previous one, there is still the question of why the series itself exists at all. For the series is just as contingent as the individual contingent things that make it up are. It could have failed to exist; so why doesn't it?" [^2]

> "Each time we will merely be passing the explanatory buck rather than explaining anything." [^7]

> "Nothing less than such a necessary being could possibly terminate the regress of explanation." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This is among the strongest natural-theology weapons against atheism: it blocks the move of treating the universe (or multiverse) as a self-sufficient brute fact, and shows that an infinite causal regress does not by itself answer "why are there any contingent things at all?" Latter-day Saints can wholeheartedly press this against materialist atheism. The honest classical-theism-vs-Restoration divergence appears at the terminus. Feser's regress ends in a single, absolutely simple, purely actual being that *is* subsistent existence. LDS metaphysics instead affirms eternal, uncreated realities (matter, intelligences, law) that are not created from nothing (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]] and [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). On the LDS view the "necessary," self-existent layer of reality is richer than a lone *actus purus*, and need not be metaphysically simple. Some LDS apologists also note the argument, by itself, yields *a* necessary ground but arguably not its uniqueness (see [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]]). Feser at full strength does derive uniqueness — but only by way of the divine-simplicity premises the Restoration questions (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]).
