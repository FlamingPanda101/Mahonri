# Veridical NDEs Resist Naturalistic Explanation

#type/claim

## The Claim
A **veridical** near-death experience is one in which the experiencer, while apparently unconscious or clinically dead, reports verifiable details they could not have perceived from the body's physical position — the folkloric "red shoe on the hospital roof," or (in Bruce Greyson's own first case) a stain the experiencer described on the intern's tie that she had no normal way to see. [[Alex OConnor|O'Connor]] flags these as the single hardest sub-class of NDE for naturalism: if even *some* are genuine, the mind seems to acquire information independent of the sense organs.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Greyson's first-hand case (medium, second-hand)
Greene relays Greyson's account: as a young intern he spilled sauce on his tie; later a patient who had attempted suicide and been unconscious described the stain and the circumstances in a way Greene says she "couldn't have made up or just reasoned her way into." That case is what launched Greyson's decades of NDE research.[^1]

### 2. The "red shoe" type recurs (medium)
O'Connor notes the classic pattern: the patient seems to float above the body, then above the building, perceives an object on the roof, is revived, and the object is found — a structure reported often enough that it "crops up quite a lot," even granting that some instances are later debunked as fabricated.[^2]

### 3. Both interlocutors refuse to dismiss it (framing)
Neither speaker is a believer; O'Connor calls it "probably the biggest subject I most commonly get asked about that I've paid the least attention to," yet says he has "heard one too many strange stories to just throw it all out." Greene, asked to explain it, candidly says he cannot and will not attempt it in his book. The refusal to explain-away is itself the datum.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic's strong reply: the *evidentially testable* veridical claims, when subjected to prospective controlled study (e.g. the AWARE studies, which placed hidden targets in resuscitation bays), have produced essentially **no** confirmed out-of-body perceptions. Anecdotal veridical reports are vulnerable to retrospective reconstruction, cueing (overhearing staff while semi-conscious), lucky inference, coincidence, and embellishment in the retelling — and the debunked cases prove the genre is porous. Absent prospective confirmation, veridical NDEs are striking stories, not evidence of disembodied perception.

## Counter-Objection
O'Connor's posture concedes the weakness of anecdote and the failure of some cases, but resists the inference that the *category* is closed: the prospective studies are few, underpowered, and hampered by how rarely cardiac-arrest survivors recall anything at all, so a null result is weak evidence of absence. The rational stance, he argues, is calibrated agnosticism — neither credulous acceptance nor confident dismissal — precisely because the best cases remain unexplained and the testing is immature.

## Apologetic Engagement
Veridical NDEs are the most *epistemically aggressive* anti-materialist datum, and therefore the one to handle with the most care. The apologetic payoff, if any survive scrutiny, is large (perception without sense organs implies a perceiver distinct from the body); but the evidential base is contested and the honest apologist should present it at O'Connor's altitude — as an unresolved anomaly that embarrasses confident materialism — rather than as proof. Overselling it invites the debunked-case rebuttal and damages credibility.

## LDS Relevance
If the spirit can perceive apart from the physical eyes, that comports with the LDS view of the spirit as the true seat of perception and agency, able to function when the body cannot (cf. [[Near-Death Experiences in the Brain-Dead Challenge Materialism]], [[The Mind Is Not the Brain]]). As with all NDE material, the Restoration treats this as *suggestive natural-theology corroboration*, never as a source of doctrine — the content of such experiences is culturally colored and not canonized.
