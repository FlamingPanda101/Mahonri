# Does the Cosmological Argument Presuppose the Ontological Argument - Kants Objection

## The Point
**Objection (Kant, repeated by Grayling):** The cosmological argument secretly presupposes the ontological argument. The ontological argument (in Descartes' version) says a supremely perfect being must exist because existence is a perfection — so God exists "by definition," like a bachelor being unmarried. Since the cosmological argument explains contingent things by a *necessary* being, it must be borrowing that "exists-by-definition" notion of necessity; and since the ontological argument is unsound (existence, Kant says, is not a real perfection), the cosmological argument falls with it.[^1] **Reply:** This conflates two different senses of necessity. The arguments do *not* claim God exists "by definition" (logical necessity); they claim He exists by *metaphysical* necessity — and that is a different notion to which Kant's critique simply does not apply.[^2]

## Evidence
- **The historical refutation is immediate.** Aquinas and most Thomists after him *explicitly reject* the ontological argument while affirming the cosmological one. So the alleged dependence cannot be a strict entailment — competent philosophers have consistently held the one without the other, and (per chapter 6) without inconsistency.[^3]
- **Grayling's fallback fails too.** He concedes one might mean a *metaphysically* necessary being — but dismisses it as "merely relative," i.e., a being necessary only *as a condition for* the contingent world, which wouldn't show it "could not have failed to exist."[^4]
- **This is a false dichotomy.** Metaphysical necessity can be spelled out without collapsing into either logical ("by definition") necessity *or* merely conditional necessity:[^5]
  - **Aristotelian:** contingency comes from being a mixture of act and potency; what is pure actuality, with no potentials that need or could be actualized, exists necessarily.
  - **Neo-Platonic:** contingency comes from being composite; what is absolutely simple has no parts that need or could be combined, and is for that reason necessary.
  - **Thomistic:** the first cause is necessary precisely because its essence *just is* existence — it neither needs nor could have existence "conjoined" to it (the very need that makes contingent things contingent).[^6]
- All three attribute to God a *more-than-conditional* necessity that is nonetheless not the *logical* necessity Kant targeted — so the objection misses.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "When arguments like those defended in this book claim that God exists of necessity, they are not claiming that he exists by definition. That is not the only notion of necessity there is, and it is not the notion to which they are appealing. But Kant's claim would have merit only if it were the notion of necessity to which they were appealing." [^2]

> "That one needs to do more than merely claim that the cosmological argument presupposes the ontological argument should be obvious enough from the fact that Aquinas, and most Thomists following him, explicitly reject the ontological argument while endorsing the cosmological." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The distinction Feser draws — between a God who exists "by definition" (logical necessity) and a God whose existence is grounded in the nature of reality (metaphysical necessity) — is useful to any theist resisting the Kantian dismissal of natural theology, and a Latter-day Saint can welcome the deflation of "you've just defined God into existence." The divergence is in *how* metaphysical necessity gets cashed out: each of Feser's three routes (pure actuality, absolute simplicity, essence-identical-to-existence) is a classical-theist attribute the Restoration contests, viewing them as a Hellenized reconstruction of the embodied God of scripture (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]). An LDS reader can grant Feser's logical point against Kant — necessity need not mean "true by definition" — while declining the specific *actus purus* metaphysics he uses to fill it in. The broader LDS framing locates ultimate, non-contingent reality in eternal matter, intelligences, and law rather than in a metaphysically simple being; cf. [[Contingent and Necessary Beings]] and [[Why the Necessary Being Must Be God Not the Universe]].
