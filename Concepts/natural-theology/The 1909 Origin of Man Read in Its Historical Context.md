# The 1909 Origin of Man Read in Its Historical Context

## The Point
The 1909 First Presidency statement "The Origin of Man" is the document critics most often cite to prove the Church is officially anti-evolution. But **read in context, it shows the opposite of a hard line** — it shows the First Presidency *editing out* the hard line. The draft was written by Elder Orson F. Whitney, a committed anti-evolutionist, and his draft contained sweeping anti-Darwinian declarations ("the doctrine of the fixity of species," no "ape for an ancestor," "this shuts out the evolutionary process completely"). **The First Presidency deleted all of that.** What survived affirms human divine origin without committing the Church against evolution as a mechanism. And the follow-up official letter of 1910 explicitly conceded that scripture had *not* answered whether man's body "evolved in natural processes... through the direction and power of God." [^1]

## Evidence
1. **Whitney's draft was a straw man.** Whitney sent it to chemist John A. Widtsoe (a future Apostle), who replied it was "an excellent statement of the scriptural view" but scientifically a caricature: *"Of course, it does not in any sense deal with the law of evolution or the theory of animal descent as believed by many evolutionists."* He warned it would "not after all answer the questions propounded by the evolutionist." [^2]
2. **The hardest language never made it in.** The deleted (italicized-in-draft) portions included: *"The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints holds to the doctrine of the fixity of species as against the evolution that disregards that doctrine"* and *"This shuts out the evolutionary process completely."* Spackman: "The First Presidency chose not to use Elder Whitney's strong language, which would have committed the Church to an explicit doctrinal position against evolution." [^3]
3. **Widtsoe's framing note captured the intent.** He recommended the statement be understood as made "in answer to numerous questions concerning the origin of man; and not to use the authority of the Church in settling one of the scientific issues of the day." [^4]
4. **The 1910 official follow-up letter is decisive.** Published in the *Improvement Era*'s "Priesthood Quorums' Table" — "the established source of definitive statements to the Church's quorums" — it stated that "the authentic statements of scripture, ancient and modern" did *not* answer "whether the mortal bodies of man evolved in natural processes to present perfection, through the direction and power of God," calling these "questions not fully answered in the revealed word of God." Spackman: "This letter shows that the 1909 statement did not eliminate the possibility of divinely guided evolution." [^5]
5. **What the statement actually does say.** It frames itself modestly — on "questions which, though not vital from a doctrinal standpoint, are closely connected with the fundamental principles of salvation" — and its real burden is the **literal divine sonship and embodied image of God**, anchored in the Brother of Jared's vision (Ether 3) and the First Vision, not in biology. [^6]
6. **It is a creature of its intellectual moment.** The statement's description of Genesis as a record of "the facts relating to man's origin" reflects the era's **Baconian "science = facts" framework and Scottish common-sense realism**, the same idiom as the 1910 Christmas message praising "real science" against "mere speculations of men." (See [[Reading Prophetic Statements in Their Era - The Eclipse of Darwinism]].) [^7]

> "Inquiries arise from time to time respecting the attitude of the Church... upon questions which, though not vital from a doctrinal standpoint, are closely connected with the fundamental principles of salvation." — 1909 "The Origin of Man" [^6]

## LDS Connection
This is a model case of **how to read an early-twentieth-century prophetic statement responsibly**. The 1909 statement is authoritative for what it *affirms* — that we are God's literal offspring, made in his image — but the editorial history demonstrates the First Presidency consciously **declining to make the Church's official voice an anti-evolution voice**, even when a prominent Apostle pressed them to. The distinction between *a leader's personal anti-evolution conviction* (Whitney's) and *the collective doctrinal output of the First Presidency* (the trimmed statement) is exactly the distinction at the heart of [[Apostles Personal Views on Evolution Are Not Church Doctrine|how Latter-day Saints distinguish doctrine from opinion]]. The lesson generalizes: official statements should be read for what they carefully say, in their historical idiom, not weaponized for what individual drafters wished they said.
