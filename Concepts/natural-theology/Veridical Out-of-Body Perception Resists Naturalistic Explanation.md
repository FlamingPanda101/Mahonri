# Veridical Out-of-Body Perception Resists Naturalistic Explanation

## The Point
The single hardest fact for every reductive theory is **veridical out-of-body perception**: cases in which a person, while clinically near death, accurately reports events they could not have perceived through the physical body — and which are later confirmed [^2]. Moody treats this independent corroboration as the linchpin that separates the NDE from dreams, hallucinations, drug effects, anoxia, and seizures [^2]. He is professionally cautious — confidentiality bars him from naming names — but states plainly that he "continue[s] to be baffled and amazed," and predicts any serious investigator will find the same strange corroboration [^2]. He reinforces the point by showing that the nearest neurological analogue, the autoscopic hallucination, has *no* accepted explanation of its own and thus explains nothing [^1].

## Evidence
1. Moody's culminating argument against the dream theory: "there is the fact that independent corroboration of a kind exists for certain of the reports of out-of-body episodes. Though commitments to others prevent me from giving names and identifying details, I have seen and heard enough to say that I continue to be baffled and amazed" [^2].
2. He generalizes it as a research prediction: "anyone looking into near-death experiences in an organized way is likely also to uncover such strange apparent corroboration... enough facts to make him wonder whether near-death experiences, far from being dreams, might not belong in a very different category indeed" [^2].
3. The OBE's neurological look-alike fails to explain it: autoscopic hallucinations differ sharply (the double is *alive*, talks, is seen chest-up; the NDE body is a silent, lifeless shell seen whole), and crucially "there is no explanation for autoscopic hallucinations, either" [^1].
4. (Later editions reinforce this evidential line: in the Afterword Moody commends Kenneth Ring and Sharon Cooper's *Mindsight*, recounting "astonishing cases of persons who, although blind, had apparent visual perceptions of their surroundings while out of their bodies during close calls with death.") [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Finally, there is the fact that independent corroboration of a kind exists for certain of the reports of out-of-body episodes.... I have seen and heard enough to say that I continue to be baffled and amazed." [^2]

> "...near-death experiences, far from being dreams, might not belong in a very different category indeed." [^2]

## LDS Connection
If a person accurately perceives the physical world from a vantage *outside* the body, then perception is not reducible to the brain's sensory organs — there is a perceiving self that can operate apart from the body. This is precisely the Restoration claim: the spirit is a real entity that can see and know while separated from the body, and the faithful "walk by faith, not by sight" precisely because the deeper reality is spiritual (2 Corinthians 5:7; Alma 40 on the spirit's conscious existence between death and resurrection). Veridical OBEs offer empirical friction against materialism and support for conscious post-mortal existence.
