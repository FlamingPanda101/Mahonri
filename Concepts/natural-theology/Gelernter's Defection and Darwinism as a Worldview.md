# Gelernter's Defection and Darwinism as a Worldview

## The Point
David Gelernter, a Yale computer scientist with no stake in religious apologetics, publicly gave up Darwinism after reading Meyer's *Darwin's Doubt* and Berlinski's *The Deniable Darwin* — calling Darwin's theory "beautiful" and saying it was "no joy" to abandon it.[^1] His defection is significant because of *who* makes it and *what happens next*: he reports that within academia challenging Darwinism brings not scientific debate but "bitter," "angry, outraged, violent rejection."[^2] The panel's diagnosis is that Darwinism has "passed beyond a scientific argument" to become "the basis of a worldview and an emergency religion."[^3] Grouped with Marx (origins of the future) and Freud (origins of guilt), Darwin supplies the materialist account of *where we came from*, which is why challenging it provokes a defense of identity rather than evidence.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **A reluctant, credentialed defector.** Gelernter's *Claremont Review* essay "Giving Up Darwin" reports that Meyer's book "convinced me that Darwin has failed"; on the panel he repeatedly affirms the theory's beauty and his regret at letting it go.[^1] He is candid about his limits — "I'm not a biologist... a book review is not the same as a book."[^5]
2. **The reaction is not scientific.** "You take your life in your hands to challenge it intellectually... they will destroy you if you challenge it."[^2] What he sees at "colleges across the West is nothing approaching free speech on this topic" but "a bitter, fundamental, angry, outraged, violent rejection which comes nowhere near scientific or intellectual discussion."[^2]
3. **ID is presented as science, not theology.** Berlinski insists Meyer's argument "is not a way to bring in a theological argument; it is a scientific approach," and Meyer agrees the case "isn't an interpretation or a deduction from the scriptural text" but "an inference from biological evidence."[^6] See [[Intelligent Design as a Scientific Inference]].
4. **Darwin as one leg of a materialist tripod.** With Marx and Freud, "they form the basis of a kind of comprehensive materialistic worldview... they answer all the same questions that traditional Judeo-Christian religion has addressed," which explains the "emotive reaction" to skeptics.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Darwinism has indeed passed beyond a scientific argument... you take your life in your hands to challenge it intellectually. Yes, they will destroy you if you challenge it." (Gelernter) [^2]

> "Darwinism is no longer just a scientific theory but the basis of a world view and an emergency religion for the many troubled souls who need one." (Gelernter, quoting his essay) [^3]

> "It certainly was no joy to conclude that Steve was right; it was no joy for me to give up a beautiful theory." (Gelernter) [^1]

## LDS Connection
Gelernter's account illuminates the *sociology* of the origins debate that Latter-day Saints, like other theists, encounter: opposition to design arguments is often defense of a materialist worldview rather than weighing of evidence. The distinction the panel insists on — that intelligent design is "an inference from biological evidence," not a deduction from scripture — is one an LDS apologist can adopt, keeping natural-theology arguments separate from revelation while leaving the mechanism of evolution open (the Church holds no fixed position on it). The honest claim is the inadequacy of *unguided* materialism, not a young-earth reading.
