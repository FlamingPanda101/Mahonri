# Teach the Best Science in the Light of the Gospel

## The Point

When BYU's Board of Trustees first authorized a formal evolution course (circa 1970),
the permission came with a charge that has guided the program ever since: teach the very
**best, most rigorous** course in evolutionary biology that can be had at any top
university — *but do so in the light of the gospel*.[^1] This is the operative model for
how a faithful institution handles a contested science. It rejects both options of the
false dilemma: it neither dilutes the science to protect belief nor sets the science
loose to corrode it. Whiting argues this "safe space" approach — rigorous science taught
honestly within a framework of faith — is precisely why BYU went, in a quarter century,
"from an institution that once widely distrusted evolution to one that has largely
accepted it."[^2]

## Evidence

- **The charge itself.** Duane Jeffrey recounts that when the formal evolution course
  was approved, the board's instruction was "to teach as scientifically rigorous and
  accurate a course in evolution as could be had at the best universities in the country
  but to teach it in the light of the gospel."[^1] Whiting: "We have done our best to
  follow this instruction."[^3]

- **The mechanism is a *safe space* for hard questions.** Whiting contrasts his own
  1980s undergraduate experience — feeling unsafe raising evolution with faculty or
  peers, having to "work out the reconciliation largely on my own" only after leaving for
  graduate school — with the deliberate environment BYU later built, where students can
  "ask difficult questions" and have them "answered honestly, with scientific rigor, and
  within the context of faith" *before* they graduate. He calls this the primary reason
  for the program's success and the change in student attitudes.[^4]

- **Heritage of the same conviction.** The charge echoes Spencer W. Kimball's vision for
  BYU education — subjects "perfumed lightly with the spirit of the gospel" — and the
  First Presidency's 1910 Christmas declaration that "our religion is not hostile to real
  science."[^5]

- **A transferable lesson.** Whiting explicitly generalizes: other "heart-wrenching and
  divisive topics" at the science–faith interface should be handled the same way. People
  leave when "forced to choose one over the other"; the remedy is to "provide safe spaces"
  for grappling, teach the best science, and "work together on a road to faithful
  reconciliation" — without pretending the science is simpler than it is.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "We are to teach the very best, most rigorous course in evolutionary biology but to do so in the light of the gospel." [^1]

> "Just as we provided a safe space to learn and discuss evolution at BYU, we need to provide opportunities for students to feel safe in grappling with their faith and with science, even though it may make some feel uncomfortable... We must teach the best science. We must provide safe spaces." [^6]

> "Reconciliation often does not mean entirely discarding one side to embrace the other." [^6]

## LDS Connection

This is faithful scholarship as institutional policy. It assumes — with the restored
gospel — that all truth can be "circumscribed into one great whole" (cf. D&C 88:118), so
the best secular science and the gospel cannot ultimately conflict. The model refuses the
forced choice diagnosed in [[The Real Faith Crisis Is Being Forced to Choose Between Faith and Science]],
mirrors how an individual believing scientist orders his commitments in
[[Faith before Science and Then Science]], and underwrites the documented results in
[[Evolutionary Biology at BYU Is an Academic Success Story]].
