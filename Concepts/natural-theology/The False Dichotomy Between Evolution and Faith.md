# The False Dichotomy Between Evolution and Faith

## The Point
Latter-day Saint students are too often handed a forced choice: accept evolution and abandon faith, or keep faith and reject the science. Jamie Jensen argues this dichotomy is "preached from both sides of the aisle" — by young-earth creationists who jettison science for a literalist Genesis, and by a vocal minority of atheistic scientists who weaponize science to preach atheism.[^1] The dichotomy is false because science is agnostic about God, and the Church has taken no doctrinal position on the mechanism of creation. Insisting on the choice carries a real faith cost: students taught to "ignore the science" are set up to lose their testimonies when they later meet the overwhelming evidence for evolution.[^2]

## Evidence
- BYU biology seniors score in the 99th percentile nationally on *knowledge* of evolution, yet *acceptance* varies widely — only 38% of students entering a nonmajors biology class accept human evolution (61% by the end), versus 81% of U.S. adults.[^3]
- A survey of 1,000+ college students found 48% believed that to accept evolution you must reject God — and the more atheistic students thought science was, the less willing they were to accept evolution.[^4]
- Jensen recounts a well-intentioned member who blamed youth secularization on the teaching of evolution and wanted schools to teach a literalist alternative "with absolutely no scientific evidence." She warns this "will not hold up to scientific scrutiny and may end up contributing to the very thing he is trying to prevent."[^2]
- Counter-examples to the dichotomy: notable believing scientists such as Francis Collins, former head of the NIH and leader of the Human Genome Project.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Like so many others, this individual has set up a false dichotomy between science and religion. This dichotomy is often preached from both sides of the aisle." [^1]

> "Accepting evolution as a valid scientific theory is among the legitimate, faithful choices a person can make while maintaining faith in the restored scriptural truths of God's creative work and plan." [^6]

> "But if learning about evolution puts your faith in jeopardy, choose your faith. Choose your faith until you can better understand the science (or until science can provide better explanations)." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The volume is published precisely to dissolve this dichotomy, showing that "accepting the scientific theory of organic evolution is not antithetical to wholeheartedly believing in the restored gospel."[^6] Because the Church holds that "nothing has been revealed concerning evolution," the mechanism of creation is open ground; a Latter-day Saint may accept evolution, hold an agnostic view, or affirm God-guided (theistic) evolution without contradicting revealed doctrine about God's purposeful creation and the Fall.[^8] The 1910 First Presidency counsel applies: "Diversity of opinion does not necessitate intolerance of spirit."[^9] Jensen's pastoral bottom line preserves the priority of faith — salvation does not depend on accepting evolution — while refusing to make the science an enemy of belief.
