# Dark Theology Erodes the Child-of-God Seatbelt

## The Claim
[[Cardon Ellis]] offers a pastoral/ethical reason the creation dilemma matters: "dark" theologies practically erode the conviction that every person is a child of God, removing a mental "seatbelt" against cruelty. Most Christians who really believe their fellow man is a child of God have an internal check — "if they're ever being cruel to somebody... I am being cruel to a fellow image-bearer of God." But theologies that deny universal divine kinship — hard Calvinism ("some of us actually weren't created children of God; Jesus didn't die for everybody"), materialist atheism ("you're a meat computer"), or "Mormons are atheists" polemics — loosen that seatbelt. Ellis says he only "aggressively fights a theology" when it stops people from being good Samaritans.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **It reframes doctrine by its behavioral fruit — rate: a values claim, defensible but not a logical disproof.** Ellis grounds the test in [[Luke 18.19|the parable of the Good Samaritan]] (wrong race, wrong creed, wrong town — yet the true neighbor), making *orthopraxy* the ultimate measure (see [[Orthopraxy over Orthodoxy]]).[^2] This is an argument from consequences, not a refutation of the doctrine's truth — a hard Calvinist could be perfectly charitable, and Ellis concedes he doesn't want to "misrepresent their faith." **Steelman of the Calvinist:** Reformed theology affirms *common grace* and the *imago Dei* in all people (Genesis 1:27 applies to everyone, elect or not), so the claim that Calvinism denies universal image-bearing is contestable; limited atonement concerns the *scope of redemption*, not whether the non-elect bear God's image. A Calvinist would say humility before a sovereign God produces *more* charity, not less.
2. **It connects to "why the debate matters" — rate: the stream's thesis.** Ellis ties the seatbelt point to why Trinity-vs-Godhead is worth fighting about: not to win formulations (LDS and creedal Trinity are "99% the same") but "to avoid the ravages of its worst implications" — *ex nihilo* producing "an entire class of second-class children of God... destined just for suffering."[^3] See [[Why the Trinity vs Godhead Debate Actually Matters]].
3. **The Anne Frank illustration — rate: emotionally forceful, philosophically the problem-of-hell again.** Ellis: it's "too far" to say Anne Frank "was literally just born to suffer and be tortured by the Third Reich" and that this "glorifies God." This restates [[Hell - For Gods Glory Defense Rebutted]] in the register of moral revulsion at a concrete case rather than abstract analogy.[^4] **Steelman:** the theist replies that ultimate justice/redemption in the next life reframes earthly suffering (the standard problem-of-evil theodicy) — but Ellis's point is that the *creation* dilemma is worse than the problem of evil because the person need never have been created at all (see [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]]).
4. **The "secure faith" corollary — rate: anecdotal.** Ellis cites the LDS rejection of a "zero-sum game" (you don't have to call other churches/miracles demonic) and Jared Halverson's line that "the people most secure in their faith are the least likely to lash out," contrasted with [[Mark Driscoll]]'s "demonic" rhetoric.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "There's this like seatbelt in their mind if they're ever being cruel to somebody that... I am being cruel to a fellow image-bearer of God... But it seems to me that these darker ideologies... whether it's atheism that just says you're a meat computer, or whether it's some reformed theology that says... some of us actually weren't created children of God." [^1]

> "If your theology and your dedication to it thereof is actually becoming a hindrance to you treating fellow wanderers on this earth as children of God, well, now I've got a problem with you... the ultimate test, I think, of God... is are we being good Samaritans?" [^6]

> "Anne Frank was literally just born to suffer and be tortured by the Third Reich. And that that somehow glorifies God... that's too far for me." [^4]
