# Reading Prophetic Statements in Their Era - The Eclipse of Darwinism

## The Point
Prophetic statements are given to real people in a real intellectual moment, and they often **borrow the era's vocabulary and assumptions** without that borrowing becoming binding doctrine. The 1909 and 1925 statements were written during the **"eclipse of Darwinism"** (roughly 1880s–1920s), when many working biologists doubted Darwin's *mechanism* (natural selection) even as evidence for evolution itself mounted — and laypeople wrongly concluded that "science had given up on evolution." The statements also breathe the air of **Baconian "science = facts" philosophy** and **Scottish common-sense realism**. Spotting that era-bound idiom is what lets us read the statements accurately: it explains *why* they sound the way they do, and it keeps us from mistaking period framing for eternal doctrine. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **The "eclipse of Darwinism" reshaped the whole debate.** During this period "many biologists believed that [Darwin's] theory of natural selection could not adequately account for organic evolution," which "misled many lay people into concluding that the scientific community had lost faith in organic evolution generally." Critiques of Darwin's *mechanism* were heard as critiques of evolution *itself*. [^2]
2. **The most influential LDS scientists were not biologists.** The Church's scientist-Apostles were a geologist (Talmage) and a chemist (Widtsoe); its consulted experts were geologists and paleontologists (Frederick Pack) — fields that accepted an old earth and "long death" but were one step removed from the live biological debate over human descent. The leadership's caution tracked the *state of the science as they encountered it*. [^3]
3. **Baconian science set the bar the statements use.** In the popular framework, only what was "measurable, quantifiable, and demonstrable" counted as science; "facts were scientific, but explanations of those facts never could be." Evolution, as a *theory*, was suspect by that standard. This is why the 1910 First Presidency Christmas message could say, "Our religion is not hostile to real science. That which is demonstrated, we accept with joy; but vain philosophy, human theory and mere speculations of men, we do not accept." [^4]
4. **Common-sense realism made everyone his own interpreter.** The dominant American philosophy held that God "revealed facts, both in scripture and in nature," accessible to ordinary common sense — so trained scientists making counterintuitive claims were viewed with suspicion ("apes did not birth humans; everyone knew that"). The 1909 statement's framing of Genesis as a record of "the facts relating to man's origin" reflects this. Even William Jennings Bryan voiced it: "The one beauty about the word of God is, it does not take an expert to understand it." [^5]
5. **The "warfare" myth distorted the framing — then and now.** The late-1800s "conflict hypothesis" portrayed science and religion as inevitably at war (propped up by historical falsehoods like the Columbus flat-earth myth). It is why people still "instinctively frame the evolution issue in terms of 'science versus religion'" — a framing the chapter calls "unhelpful." Most scientists of the era were Christians, on both sides; the real fault line was *how to interpret nature and scripture*, not science vs. faith. [^6]
6. **Reading-in-context yields the payoff.** Spackman's thesis: "the Church's official view of evolution in the early twentieth century was more cautious, tentative, and open than has often been assumed." The era-bound language is the evidence — once you see the Baconian/common-sense idiom and the eclipse-of-Darwinism backdrop, the statements read as *deliberately restrained*, not as a doctrinal anti-evolution decree. [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Our religion is not hostile to real science. That which is demonstrated, we accept with joy; but vain philosophy, human theory and mere speculations of men, we do not accept." — First Presidency, 1910 [^4]

> "It is unhelpful to frame the discussion in terms of science versus religion. It is better to speak of evolution supporters and evolution opponents with differing beliefs about the nature of science, the nature of faith, and where science leaves off and faith begins." [^6]

> "The Church's official view of evolution in the early twentieth century was more cautious, tentative, and open than has often been assumed." [^7]

> "Evolutionism is not Darwinism. There were many evolutionists, and indeed some Darwinians before Darwin." — James E. Talmage, 1890 lecture [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the methodological key to the entire evolution file — and a transferable principle. **Continuing revelation means prophetic statements are given in time, to a particular generation, in that generation's language** — which is precisely why the Church distinguishes the *enduring revealed core* (God created humans in his image, on purpose) from *era-conditioned framing* (Baconian "real science," "mere speculation"). Recognizing the idiom is not debunking the prophets; it is reading them honestly, the same charitable historical move applied in [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin|reading a leader as a man of his time]]. The reward is large: it dissolves the "science versus religion" frame the critic depends on, and it shows that what looked like an anti-evolution stance was in fact a careful, *open* one — consistent with the later, explicit [[What the Official Statements on Evolution Actually Say and Deliberately Do Not|"no official position" on the mechanism]].
