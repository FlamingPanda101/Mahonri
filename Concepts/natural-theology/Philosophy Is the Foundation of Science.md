# Philosophy Is the Foundation of Science

## The Point
Science cannot stand on its own. It rests on a set of immaterial, philosophical assumptions that science itself can neither prove nor produce: that truth exists and can be known, that nature is orderly and consistent, that effects have causes, that the past resembled the present, that our senses report a real world, that logic and math apply to matter, that we have free will to follow evidence, and that we ought to report results honestly.[^1] "You can't prove the tools of science... by running some kind of experiment. You have to assume those things are true in order to do the experiment!"[^2] Turek's point against the atheist is sharp: theism can supply and ground these preconditions; atheistic materialism cannot — yet the atheist uses them anyway.

## Evidence
1. **Turek's expanded list (after Craig's).** The foundations of science "are not made of or completely explained by molecules": truth exists and can be known; the laws of nature are orderly and consistent; effects have causes (causality); causes in the past were like those in the present (uniformity); our senses give accurate information (realism); the immaterial laws of logic and mathematics apply to the material world; we have free will to follow the evidence; we can make rational inferences from data; and "we should report our results accurately (objective moral values exist)."[^1]
2. **These are needed before, during, and after the experiment.** *Before:* scientists must frame philosophical rules (are intelligent or supernatural causes allowed?) — "those questions can't be answered by science; they are answered by scientists doing philosophy." *During:* they rely on orderly law, causality, and realism. *After:* deciding "what is good evidence... is not evidence itself — a philosophical value judgment must be made," and they must interpret it honestly.[^3]
3. **No philosophy-free science.** "While it's certainly true that you can use bad philosophy to do science, you can't use no philosophy."[^2] Turek's running example is Richard Dawkins, who "admits he uses materialistic philosophy to come to his scientific conclusions. That's why some of his conclusions are bad conclusions."[^4]
4. **Feser's metal-detector parable.** Edward Feser likens the scientism-committed atheist to a man who sweeps a beach with a metal detector and concludes there is no plastic or rubber anywhere — never noticing his own detector is made of plastic and rubber. Science "couldn't work without" the immaterial realities it depends on, so "atheists can't use science to deny that they exist."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "You can't prove the tools of science — such as the laws of logic, the law of causality, or the reliability of observation — by running some kind of experiment. You have to assume those things are true in order to do the experiment! That's why you can't do science without philosophy." [^2]

> "While it's certainly true that you can use bad philosophy to do science, you can't use no philosophy." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The claim that lawfulness, intelligibility, and objective moral truth undergird all inquiry resonates with Restoration scripture, which roots the cosmos in a divine intelligence: "the light of truth... is in all things, which giveth life to all things, which is the law by which all things are governed" ([[D&C 88|D&C 88:6–13]]), and "the glory of God is intelligence" ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:36]]). Latter-day Saint epistemology also pairs study with faith — "seek learning, even by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118) — affirming that empirical work is embedded in a larger, revelation-aware way of knowing rather than being the sole one. The preconditions Turek says science "borrows" are, on the LDS view, features of a reality God orders and within which He works.
