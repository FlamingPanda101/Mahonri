# Atheists Concede Fine-Tuning Is the Strongest Theistic Argument

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] opens his "argument atheists fear most" episode by documenting that a striking number of prominent atheists rank the fine-tuning (teleological) argument as the strongest — or at least most intriguing — case for God. This is offered not as proof but as a *meta*-observation: when sophisticated unbelievers single out one theistic argument as the one that "has to impress you," that argument deserves serious attention.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, hostile witnesses)** The montage: [[Richard Dawkins]] grants the physical constants are "getting close to a good argument"; [[Christopher Hitchens]] says "every one of us picks the fine-tuning one as the most intriguing"; the atheist YouTuber "genetically modified skeptic" places the teleological argument from fine-tuning in "S tier... where only the most effective arguments belong"; Rationality Rules bumps it to "A" tier as "compelling"; [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] and Joe Schmid rank it A because "the alternative explanations... often don't seem very plausible."[^1]
2. **(Moderate, defection cases)** Philosopher [[Philip Goff]], once an atheist, was moved by fine-tuning toward panpsychism and then a liberal Christianity (in his 2023 *Why? The Purpose of the Universe*); the fine-tuning kept him from "fully embracing an atheistic purposeless universe."[^2] [[Antony Flew]]'s late deism is the classic precedent.
3. **(Moderate, caveat)** Horn is careful: that atheists call it *top-tier* does not mean they find it *convincing* ("otherwise they wouldn't be atheists") — there are serious objections, handled elsewhere.[^3] See [[Chance, Necessity, or Design - The Fine-Tuning Trilemma]] and [[The Anthropic Principle and the Firing-Squad Reply]].

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
An atheist can grant the concession while denying it carries evidential weight: ranking fine-tuning "best of a bad lot" is consistent with thinking *every* theistic argument fails. [[Sean Carroll]]'s line captures it — fine-tuning "is the best argument that people have for the existence of God. But it's a terrible argument" — and he holds it is actually [[Fine-Tuning Is Evidence for Naturalism Not Theism|evidence for naturalism]]. So "even atheists rate it highly" is a rhetorical, not a logical, point: the strength of an argument is fixed by its premises, not by its reputation.

## Counter-objection
The concession is not load-bearing for the *conclusion*, but it is legitimately load-bearing for *attention and burden*: it rebuts the common dismissal that fine-tuning is a fringe god-of-the-gaps move, since the people making that dismissal are the same ones ranking it highest. Carroll's "best but terrible" needs its own argument (that fine-tuning favors naturalism), which the vault treats on its merits ([[Fine-Tuning Is Evidence for Naturalism Not Theism]], [[Theism Doesn't Predict the Universe We Observe]]) rather than letting the verdict ride on the slogan. So the concession does exactly what Horn claims — it earns the argument a serious hearing — without overclaiming.

## LDS Connection
General natural theology shared with Latter-day Saints, for whom a precisely ordered, life-permitting cosmos witnesses a "supreme creator" (Alma 30:44; [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]). No distinctively LDS content beyond the usual organization-vs-*ex nihilo* caveat.
