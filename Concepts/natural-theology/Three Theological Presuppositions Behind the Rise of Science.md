# Three Theological Presuppositions Behind the Rise of Science

## The Point
Before presenting his three discoveries, Meyer argues a historical thesis: **modern science arose uniquely in the Judeo-Christian West** (roughly 1300–1750, peaking 1500–1750) because Christianity supplied three "intellectual presuppositions" that made systematic empirical investigation rational.[^1] Many civilizations (Chinese, Islamic, Roman) had the *material* conditions for science and real technical achievements, yet the systematic method — isolating variables, mathematizing nature, testing — emerged only in the Christian West.[^2] Historians ask "why there, why then?" and locate the difference in *ideas*: (1) the **contingency** of nature, (2) its **intelligibility**, and (3) human **fallibility** requiring testing.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Contingency of nature → go and look.** Greek science assumed nature embodied a necessary intrinsic *logos*, so the most logical form must be the real one (e.g., planetary orbits "must" be perfect circles) — armchair deduction over observation. The Christian doctrine of a *free* Creator implied the order "could have been otherwise": God could have made gravity an inverse-cube rather than inverse-square law. So "the job of the natural philosopher was not to ask what God *must* have done, but what God *actually* did" (a near-paraphrase of Robert Boyle) — which forces *empirical observation*.[^4]
2. **Intelligibility of nature → confidence there is order to find.** Modern science "was inspired by the conviction that the universe is the product of a rational mind who designed it to be understood, and who designed the human mind to understand it." Kepler: "God created us after his own image so that we could share in his own thoughts." The shared rationality of Creator, cosmos, and human mind gave researchers confidence that nature's secrets were *there to be revealed*, motivating the hard work of experiment.[^5]
3. **Human fallibility → the necessity of testing.** The doctrines of creation and the *fall* were recovered together in the late-medieval/Reformation period. Because the fall affected our cognition, humans are "vulnerable to self-deception, flights of fancy, and jumping to conclusions," so we must *test* our theories against nature. Meyer credits Steve Fuller and Peter Harrison; this impulse toward testability and "systems of accountability" (the ancestor of peer review) is part of where scientific method came from.[^6]
4. **Belief as "science starter," not "science stopper."** Newton wrote a *design argument* (initial-condition fine-tuning for stable orbits) into the General Scholium of the *Principia* — "this most beautiful system... could only proceed from the counsel and dominion of an intelligent and powerful being." For Newton, Kepler, and Boyle, theology framed the whole inquiry: "belief in God was not a science stopper... it was the science starter."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Science in any modern form arose in Western civilization alone... because only the Christian West possessed the necessary intellectual presuppositions." (Ian Barbour, quoted by Meyer) [^1]

> "The job of the natural philosopher was not to ask what God must have done, but what God actually did." [^4]

> "Belief in God was not a science stopper for Sir Isaac Newton; it was the science starter." [^7]

## LDS Connection
This historical case undercuts the "science vs. religion" framing and instead grounds the lawful, intelligible cosmos that science assumes in a rational Creator — a thesis the Restoration welcomes warmly. LDS scripture casts intelligence and law as pervading and governing reality: "the light of truth... is in all things, which giveth life to all things, which is the law by which all things are governed" (D&C 88:13), and "the glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36). An LDS reader affirms all three presuppositions; the one nuance is that Meyer's "free Creator who could have made the laws otherwise" is a creator *ex nihilo*, whereas LDS thought casts God as organizing a cosmos of eternal law and matter He also works within. See [[Theism Makes Science Possible]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]. The contingency point also illuminates why an *a priori* [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship|methodological naturalism]] becomes question-begging when treated as metaphysics rather than method.
