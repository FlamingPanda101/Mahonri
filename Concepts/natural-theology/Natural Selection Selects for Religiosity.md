# Natural Selection Selects for Religiosity

## The Claim

If atheistic natural selection is true — that traits which promote
reproductive success are "selected for" — then belief in God is being
naturally selected FOR, because religious people have significantly higher
birth rates than atheists. Atheists reproduce below replacement rate
(~1.7 children vs. the ~2.1 needed for population stability), while religious
communities (LDS, Muslims, Catholics) have substantially more children. Under
the atheist's own framework, natural selection is "weeding out" atheism and
favoring religiosity. This creates a performative contradiction: the atheist
must affirm both that natural selection shapes populations AND that atheism
(true belief) is being selected against.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Atheist Birth Rates Are Below Replacement (Argument weight: Secondary — Demographic)

[[Hayden Carroll]] presents the demographic data:

> "What is the repopulation rate for atheists? Yeah, it's probably very low.
> It's no, I think it's like under 0.2 — which you need two, right? To
> repopulate for a family. 2.1. Yes. Over and they're at like 1.7."[^1]

The replacement fertility rate (sustaining population without immigration) is
approximately 2.1 children per woman. Atheists in developed nations consistently
fall well below this threshold. Meanwhile:

> "Latter-day Saints are having 12 kids. My dad has 11 siblings. Even Islam or
> Muslims are having lots of kids. Catholics are having lots of kids."[^2]

### 2. Natural Selection Logic Applied to Belief (Argument weight: Secondary — Internal Critique)

Carroll applies the atheist's own framework:[^2]

1. Natural selection favors traits that increase reproductive success.
2. Religious belief is a trait (whether genetic predisposition or cultural
   transmission).
3. Religious believers reproduce at higher rates than atheists.
4. Therefore, natural selection should be selecting FOR religiosity and AGAINST
   atheism.

> "So you have to say natural selection is selecting for those who believe in
> God — religiosity — because they're populating their genes more than the
> atheists. So it's actually better to believe in God, assuming the atheistic
> worldview."[^2]

Note: "Better" here means "more reproductively successful," not "more true."
But the argument creates an ironic tension: the worldview that claims to be the
most rational is, by its own evolutionary logic, being naturally deselected.

### 3. Parallel to Pascal's Wager (Argument weight: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

[[David Butler]] draws a parallel to [[Pascal's Wager]]: "It's like... when it
comes down to it, kind of illogical to choose against it — to choose against it
because the only possible way to have a positive outcome at the end of this
life... is to choose God."[^3]

The natural selection argument adds an *evolutionary* Pascal's wager: even
within an atheistic framework, belief in God is the reproductively successful
strategy.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Evolutionary Success ≠ Truth
**Steelman:** Natural selection selects for reproductive fitness, not truth. A
false belief that promotes reproduction (like religious belief, on the
atheist's view) would be selected for. This is exactly what atheists argue:
religion is a useful evolutionary adaptation or byproduct, not a true account
of reality. The fact that religious people have more children no more proves
God exists than the fact that rabbits have lots of offspring proves rabbits
have true beliefs.

**Response:** Carroll is not arguing that higher birth rates *prove* God
exists. The argument is an *internal critique* of the atheist's naturalistic
worldview: if natural selection shapes everything including beliefs, then the
atheist must acknowledge that their own worldview is being evolutionarily
outcompeted. This doesn't prove theism true, but it creates an ironic tension
for the atheist who claims to be on the side of reason and progress while
acknowledging that their belief system is a reproductive dead end.

### Objection 2: Birth Rates Are Cultural, Not Genetic
**Steelman:** Religious birth rates are a function of culture, not genetics.
Atheist children raised in religious households often become atheists, and vice
versa. Belief is not heritable in the genetic sense, so natural selection
doesn't operate on it directly. This weakens the "natural selection is weeding
out atheism" argument.

**Response:** Even if belief is culturally transmitted rather than genetic,
cultural evolution operates on analogous principles — successful cultural
traits propagate. The point remains: the belief-behavior package of theism
out-reproduces atheism, and under a naturalistic framework, reproductive
success is the ultimate metric of "fitness." Carroll would likely acknowledge
this is a rhetorical point rather than a logical proof.

### Objection 3: Correlation Is Not Causation
**Steelman:** Religious people have more children for socioeconomic and
cultural reasons (earlier marriage, community support, traditional gender
roles), not because religious belief per se causes higher fertility. As
societies develop, birth rates drop regardless of religious affiliation. The
data doesn't show that atheism *causes* low fertility.

**Response:** The causation question doesn't affect the core irony: whatever
the mechanism, the package of traits associated with religious belief
(including cultural practices that promote fertility) outcompetes atheism on
natural selection's own terms. If evolution is the blind watchmaker, it appears
to prefer theists.
