# Interpretive Assumptions Are the Glasses We Read Scripture Through

## The Point

Every reading of scripture is filtered through assumptions, the way light is filtered through eyeglasses. "Assumptions are like the lenses in our glasses; they shape our understanding of what scripture means." [^1] The danger is not in *having* glasses — that is "universal and normal," since "we all make assumptions when we interpret" — but in denying we wear them, or reading with a smudged or wrong prescription. [^1] Spackman's whole method follows from this: "our positions and interpretations are downstream of the assumptions we make; change the assumptions, and you change the resulting interpretation." [^2] Faithful, orthodox people can reasonably disagree about which assumptions to make, so the spiritual task is to "try to recognize the glasses we wear" — to surface, name, and evaluate our assumptions rather than operate in ignorance of them.

## Evidence

- The three assumptions Spackman isolates — concordism, perspicuity, and inerrancy — each, when inverted, generate different readings of 2 Nephi 2:22 and different answers on death before the Fall. [^3]
- Joseph Fielding Smith's "plain reading" assumed he was not interpreting at all, only "echoing the obvious and plain meaning" — which is itself an unexamined assumption: "If I am wrong, then the revelations are wrong—I have not placed private interpretation upon them." [^4]
- Even retaining concordism, *different* assumptions about Eden (Clark's death-in-the-garden, Ludlow's possible-reproduction reading) yield very different reconciliations of scripture and science — demonstrating "again the effect of differing assumptions and the importance of recognizing them." [^5]
- Elder Widtsoe acknowledged the same: "We set up assumptions, based upon our best knowledge, but can go no further." [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "When it comes to scripture, assumptions are like the lenses in our glasses; they shape our understanding of what scripture means. If our glasses are smudged—or we deny we're even wearing glasses!—it can skew our perceptions." [^1]

> "Our positions and interpretations are downstream of the assumptions we make; change the assumptions, and you change the resulting interpretation. . . . We must try to recognize the glasses we wear." [^2]

## LDS Connection

The "glasses" frame underwrites intellectual humility about doctrine: because every reading (including a leader's) rests on assumptions, no interpretation of the creation mechanism is exempt from re-examination. This is why [[No Death Before the Fall Is a Twentieth-Century Interpretation Not Settled Doctrine|"no death before the Fall"]] should be held as one assumption-laden reading among several, not as binding doctrine.
