# Near-Death Experiences in the Brain-Dead Challenge Materialism

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Robert Greene]] reports, from Bruce Greyson's *After* and related literature, that some near-death experiences occur in patients who are *clinically dead* — heart stopped, breathing stopped, no measurable brain function — yet who return with vivid, structured experiences sharing common themes (an oceanic sense of oneness, a panoramic life review). His inference: "If your brain is dead, not operating, but your mind is operating, that clearly indicates that the mind is not the brain."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Experiences during measured absence of brain function (medium, second-hand)
Greyson, a psychiatrist, has collected thousands of cases, some occurring while the patient was declared dead. That a richly organized conscious experience is reported from a window in which the cortex is non-functional is, on its face, anomalous for a model where the brain *is* the mind.[^1]

### 2. Common, recurring phenomenology (medium)
The "clichés" — oneness with everything, dissolution of the discriminating mind, the life review unfolding in compressed time — recur across thousands of independent reports and across cultures. Greene argues their universality and independent attestation are what make them "scientifically interesting," not dismissible as one person's hallucination.[^2]

### 3. A scientist's first-person case (medium)
Neuroanatomist Jill Bolte Taylor's hemorrhagic stroke — a trained scientist watching her own brain shut down region by region, perceiving raw "V1" pre-processed visual data and a felt dissolution of her body's boundaries — is offered as a credentialed, introspective datum about what happens as the filter fails.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The materialist replies that "clinically dead" is not "brain-inactive": EEG flatline at the scalp does not exclude deep or transient activity, and the experiences are almost certainly *constructed* during the gradual loss of, or the *return* to, function — not during true zero activity. Anoxia, hypercapnia, DMT release, and reperfusion can generate vivid imagery; the standardized phenomenology reflects shared neurochemistry and brain architecture; and *timestamping* the experience to the flatline window is notoriously unreliable. So NDEs, however moving, are not evidence of mind without brain.

## Counter-Objection
Greene and O'Connor concede much of this and avoid overclaiming — O'Connor stays "agnostic." But they press that (a) the *return*-construction story still owes an account of cases that include **veridical** perceptions impossible from the body's position (see [[Veridical NDEs Resist Naturalistic Explanation]]), and (b) the materialist's confidence outruns the evidence: dismissing thousands of structured, cross-culturally convergent reports as "just" neurochemistry is itself a metaphysical commitment, not a finding. The honest position, they argue, is that the data are unexplained, not explained away.

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a *foil* offering: two secular voices treating mind-without-functioning-brain as a live possibility. For the apologist its value is defeating the confident equation mind = brain (cf. [[Brain-Mind Correlation Is Not Causation - Mental States Have Causal Power]]), not proving the soul outright. The Restoration should resist baptizing NDE reports as doctrine — their *content* is shaped by the experiencer's culture (Greene endorses Greyson on this) and the Church makes no claim that NDEs deliver reliable theology. Their apologetic use is narrow and defensive: they make hard materialism look less obviously true.

## LDS Relevance
A spirit that can perceive and act while the body lies inert is precisely what LDS anthropology affirms — the spirit is the seat of the person and survives the body's failure (Alma 40:11; [[Doctrine and Covenants 88.15|D&C 88:15]]). The vault already gathers scriptural NDE-type ascents in [[NDE Parallels in the Bible]]; this note adds the contemporary clinical version as corroborating (not foundational) data, with the same caution applied in [[Moody's Tentative Conclusions on the NDE]].
