# Relative Non-Being vs Absolute Nothing — The Greek Distinction Copan and Craig Miss

#type/claim

## The Claim

The single most important distinction in the whole *creatio ex nihilo* debate — and the one [[Blake Ostler]] says [[Paul Copan]] and [[William Lane Craig]] fail to grasp — is the Greek difference between **relative non-being** and **absolute nothing**:

- **Relative non-being** — μὴ ὄν / ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος (*mē on* / *ek tou mē ontos*), using the negative **μή**. This denotes a formless material substrate that "is not" only in the sense that it has not yet received form, identity, or being. It is "no-thing" — not yet a thing — but it is *not* absolutely nothing. Platonists "called pre-existent matter 'the non-existent.'"[^1]
- **Absolute nothing** — οὐκ ὄν / ἐξ οὐκ ὄντος (*ouk on* / *ex ouk ontos*), using the negative **οὐκ** ("not in any way or mode"). This is what *creatio ex nihilo* actually requires.[^2]

Ostler's thesis: the biblical and pre-Nicene texts cited for *ex nihilo* almost all use the **μή** (relative-non-being) construction, which is the language of creation from a pre-existing substrate. Reading them as *ex nihilo* ignores a distinction the Greek-speaking authors themselves observed. "Copan and Craig simply fail to note this important distinction, and thus their exegesis is critically flawed."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Aristotle's usage.** Aristotle, *De Generatione Animalium*: "For generation is from non-existence (ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος) into being, and corruption from being back into non-existence." This describes a new animal generated from existing parents — relative non-being, a formless substrate with the *potential* to receive form, "not absolutely nothing but... not yet a thing."[^4]

2. **Henry Chadwick on Clement.** Chadwick observes that when Clement of Alexandria discusses creation in the *Stromata*, "the phrase he employs is *ek me ontos* not *ex ouk ontos*; that is to say, it is made not from that which is absolutely non-existent, but from relative non-being or unformed matter, so shadowy and vague that it cannot be said to have the status of 'being.'"[^5] See [[Clement of Alexandria]].

3. **Edwin Hatch on the Platonists.** For the Platonists, "the world was in its origin only potential being (τὸ μὴ ὄν)" — a "vast shapeless but plastic mass, to which the Creator gave form."[^6]

4. **The Shepherd of Hermas uses the μή construction.** The Shepherd says God "made from the non-existence into existence" using ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος — relative non-being — which "makes it fairly clear that God created what is from potential being, not from absolute nothing."[^7] By contrast, [[Theophilus of Antioch]] and Irenaeus, when they *do* mean creation from nothing, switch to ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων.[^8]

5. **It explains Romans 4:17, 2 Maccabees 7:28, 2 Enoch, Aristides, etc.** Once the distinction is seen, the whole catalogue of proof-texts collapses: each uses relative-non-being language and is fully consistent with *creatio ex materia*. See [[Romans 4.17 Calls Into Being Does Not Mean Ex Nihilo]], [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]], and [[Aristides of Athens]].

## Supporting Evidence (Copan & Craig, steelmanned)

Copan and Craig can reply that the μή/οὐκ distinction is not always observed rigorously in Koine Greek, that authors are sometimes loose, and that a text asserting God made "the all" / "everything" while calling God the sole uncreated, all-powerful Maker most naturally implies that matter too is among "the all" God made. On their reading, the burden runs the other way: if a writer believed in eternal uncreated matter co-existing with God, he should have said so, and the bare "non-being" idiom should not be pressed to *exclude* a prior creation of the substrate. They argue the distinction is a technical artifact later than many of the texts, so it cannot govern, e.g., a mother's words in 2 Maccabees.

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

The texts that *do* state how God created — Wisdom of Solomon ("from formless matter," ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης), [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]], [[Justin Martyr]] ("out of shapeless matter," ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης), [[Athenagoras of Athens]] (potter and clay) — use the language of *ordering a substrate* even while calling God all-powerful and sole Maker of "the all."[^9] So the inference "all-powerful + Maker of all ⟹ *ex nihilo*" is demonstrably invalid: the same vocabulary coexists with explicit *creatio ex materia*. And where the μή construction is used, it is the precise philosophical idiom of the surrounding culture, not a loose colloquialism.

## Best Quotes

> "In each case the phrase he employs is *ek me ontos* not *ex ouk ontos*... it is made not from that which is absolutely non-existent, but from relative non-being or unformed matter, so shadowy and vague that it cannot be said to have the status of 'being.'" — Henry Chadwick[^5]

> "Platonists called pre-existent matter 'the non-existent.'" — Jonathan Goldstein, quoted by Ostler[^1]

> "To say that God called to existence that which does not exist, as in Romans 4:17, actually assumes a preexisting substrate that God, by impressing form upon it, organizes into a thing that exists. Copan and Craig simply fail to note this important distinction." — Blake Ostler[^3]
