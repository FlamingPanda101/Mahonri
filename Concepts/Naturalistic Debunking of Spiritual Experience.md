# Naturalistic Debunking of Spiritual Experience

## The Point
A large block of Seth's opening targets "spiritual epistemology" — the practice of treating spiritual experiences as "evidence of something supernatural." His argument is that if these experiences all have adequate natural explanations, then they give no support to theism's picture of reality.[^1] He runs down a list — out-of-body experiences, near-death experiences, demons, revelation, healing, guidance, feelings of the Holy Ghost, and prophecy — assigning each a naturalistic cause, and ties the whole pattern to *overactive, "better safe than sorry" pattern recognition* that makes people "see things that aren't there."[^2] The LDS apologetic tradition contests several of these specifics (see [[Psychological Explanations of NDEs Rebutted]]) and, more fundamentally, argues that a debunked *cause* does not debunk the *content* of an experience — that spiritual experience is "the lens, not the picture." See [[Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture]].

## Evidence
1. **Item-by-item naturalization.** Out-of-body experiences arise from a malfunctioning vestibular system; NDEs from "very similar brain phenomena"; demons from sleep paralysis and epilepsy; revelation from hallucination and "cultural hallucinatory influences"; healing from placebo (and Seth claims prayed-for hospital patients don't "recover quicker"); gut "guidance" from the enteric nervous system, the gut's "second brain."[^3]
2. **Prayer as selection bias.** Seth's flagship case: a believer prays to find lost keys, finds them, and counts it as answered prayer, ignoring the "thousands if not millions of prayers unanswered." "Selection bias is when you narrow your focus to just one piece of evidence and you ignore everything else... if you take the one successful prayer and divide it by all the failed prayers it looks a lot more like a coincidence."[^4] Compare [[Motivated Reasoning and the Tides of Bias]].
3. **The Holy Ghost and failed prophecy.** Feelings of the Holy Ghost reduce to "the elevation emotion," and "the track record of theistic prophecy is not good" — Seth cites a database of patriarchal blessings promising recipients they would "live to see the second coming," all of which failed.[^5]
4. **The unifying mechanism: overactive pattern recognition.** Like hearing rustling bushes and inferring "a lion," an "overly sensitive pattern recognition" produces false positives: "if they're seeing things that aren't there, you're not understanding reality correctly."[^2] This is the same evolutionary "better safe than sorry" engine he uses to explain religion as such, reinforcing his [[Theism Is a God-of-the-Gaps Project Doomed by the Track Record|god-of-the-gaps thesis]].

## Best Quotes
> "If you take the one successful prayer and you divide it by all the failed prayers, it looks a lot more like a coincidence." (Seth) [^4]

> "Feelings of the Holy Ghost — we can reduce that to the elevation emotion." (Seth) [^5]

> "If you have overly sensitive pattern recognition you see things that aren't there... and if you're seeing things that aren't there, you're not understanding reality correctly." (Seth) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Seth's debunking aims squarely at the LDS reliance on personal revelation and the witness of the Spirit, so it meets a well-developed counter-literature. Latter-day Saint responses argue (a) that the naturalistic explanations are themselves contestable on the data — e.g., NDE features that resist purely psychological accounts (see [[Psychological Explanations of NDEs Rebutted]]) — and (b) that explaining the physiological *medium* of an experience does not show the experience is non-veridical, any more than describing the eye debunks sight: spiritual experience is "the lens, not the picture." See [[Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture]] and [[Do Not Argue Spiritual Experience to Be Valid]]. Notably, Hansen does not engage this block in the debate, keeping his case ontological rather than epistemological.
