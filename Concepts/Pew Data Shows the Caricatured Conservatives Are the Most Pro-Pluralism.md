# Pew Data Shows the Caricatured Conservatives Are the Most Pro-Pluralism

#type/claim

## The Claim
The [[Ward Radio]] hosts argue that the 2024 Pew Religious Landscape Study contains a buried irony: the very group "lampooned as the most conservative" turns out to be the *most pro-pluralism* on the survey's attitudinal questions.[^1] Latter-day Saints were found most likely, among the groups measured, to say that American society is *strengthened* because it is made up of people of many different religions including the non-religious; that one can be moral and have good values *without* believing in God; that humans have a God-given duty to protect and care for the earth, its plants and animals; and that religion does more good than harm, that science and religion are compatible, and that churches help the poor and strengthen community.[^2][^3] The hosts press the inversion explicitly: "the actual liberals who value 'diversity as our strength' are the Mormons" — and Pew separately reports them as "the least judgmental."[^4] The theological backstop they offer is that Latter-day Saints are "the non-hellists" and "the non-isolationists": because they reject the doctrine that the unevangelized are damned, they can sincerely say "we want more of your faith and his faith and her faith — we want it all."[^5]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The inversion is the argumentative payload (rated: strong as a debate point).** The claim's power is that it turns the standard caricature on its head using the critic's own preferred value. A group routinely cast as rigid, exclusivist, and conservative scores highest on precisely the cosmopolitan attitudes ("diversity is our strength," "you can be moral without God," "many religions strengthen society") that secular liberals treat as their own virtues.[^1] If the metric of tolerance is endorsing pluralism, the data names Latter-day Saints the most tolerant — an embarrassment to the caricature. This pairs with [[Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity]]: the same survey makes them both the most devout *and* the most pluralism-friendly.

2. **A theological mechanism, not just a poll result (rated: moderate).** The hosts ground the attitude in doctrine: because LDS soteriology does not consign the unevangelized to hell ("we don't even believe in sending people to hell, we don't like that"), members are freed to affirm other faiths as genuine goods rather than as rival roads to damnation.[^5] This connects the empirical finding to the vault's existing soteriology of inclusion — see [[God Can Lead People Through Other Religions]] and [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]] — and contrasts it with what the hosts call the "isolationist" *sola* framework that (on their telling) routes "everyone else" to hell. The distinctive contribution of *this* note is the **empirical** turn: it is not a fresh theological argument for inclusivism but the claim that survey data *confirms* LDS members actually hold the inclusive attitudes their theology implies.

3. **Same datum, secondhand sourcing (rated: caveat).** As with the devotional superlatives, the attitudinal findings are read off the same X thread summarizing Pew, never the published cross-tabs.[^2] "Most likely of the groups surveyed to say X" is a ranking that depends on which groups Pew bucketed and how each item was worded, so the specific superlatives should be checked against the report before being repeated as established fact. The authority of the source is C accordingly.

4. **Steelman of the critic (rated: strong — the inversion can be reread two ways).** A skeptic has two clean replies. (a) *Cheap pluralism for the secure.* It costs a confident, growing, high-fertility church little to celebrate "many religions," because its own truth-claims and birth rates are not in doubt; magnanimity toward rivals is easy from a position of strength, so the attitude may signal confidence rather than principled tolerance. (b) *Pluralism in attitude, exclusivism in claim.* The same hosts who tout the pro-diversity numbers spend the bulk of the episode arguing that creedal Christians are not really Christians and that the *sola* traditions are "isolationists" bound for incoherence — so the warm survey answers about "many religions strengthening society" coexist with a hard exclusive claim to *the* Restoration and *the* priesthood. A critic will say this is the very pattern [[The Diversity Test - What a Community Tolerates Reveals Its God|the diversity test]] probes: tolerance on civic adiaphora ("society is strengthened by many faiths") tells us little about whether the community tolerates diversity on *who God is* — where LDS theology is as exclusive as any. The hosts would reply that civic pluralism and theological conviction are simply not in tension: one can hold the fullness of truth and still rejoice in every increment of "faith and goodness" elsewhere ("more power to you" even to the atheist with good morals).[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "So the actual liberals who value, quote, 'diversity as our strength' are the Mormons that are lampooned as the most conservative ones... the most likely to say American society is strengthened because it's made up of people of many different religions including non-religious people."[^1]
