# Love Transcends Moral Obligation

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that genuine love is *not* a response to moral duty: "Love doesn't work within the constraints of some kind of moral ought." If I love you only because I am obligated to, "it wouldn't be love." This both answers the regress worry ("why *ought* I love?") and resists [[Jacob Hansen]]'s proposed *consequentialist* grounding of morality. [^1] See [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Duty-driven love is not love** — rate: phenomenologically strong. "If I only loved you because I felt obligated to do so... you're just bowing to some kind of external pressure." Telling one's wife "I only love you because I'm morally obligated to" would give her "every reason to believe I don't really love her at all." *(phenomenological)* [^2]
2. **Answer to the regress** — rate: a principled concession that grounding terminates. To Hansen's "isn't there a higher ought that grounds loving others?" Ostler replies that *every* grounding ends somewhere: "I ought to do it because God commanded me. Why ought I obey God? Because God is right. Why is God right? He just is. It ends somewhere." Love is the terminus: "Love is. It simply is. You can't make love be something it isn't." *(philosophical)* [^3]
3. **Against the consequentialist grounding** — rate: a sharp internal critique of Hansen's view. Hansen proposes that "good/bad" just orient an act between poles of ultimate suffering and ultimate flourishing. Ostler objects: "That's a consequentialist view... If you love me only because you want to avoid ultimate suffering, that's not love. That's all about you." Other-directedness is essential: "Do I love you because it will lead to *my* flourishing? No, I love you because it will lead to *your* flourishing." *(ethical theory)* [^4]
4. **Self-sacrifice as the test case** — rate: the decisive example. Ostler: you would "sacrifice yourself for your children even if it included great pain for you." Hansen tries to reabsorb this into self-interest ("I'd be more miserable alive knowing they died"); Ostler rejects the redescription: "I don't think you'd do it out of regard for yourself... you do it out of regard for them. Very different motivation." *(case)* [^5]

**Steelman of Hansen's consequentialism:** Hansen is defending a *eudaimonist* reading of the Christian paradox — "you find your life by losing it" ([[Luke 10.30-37|cf. the Gospel ethic]]): rightly ordered self-love and other-love converge, so that willing the good of the other *is* one's own deepest flourishing ("mutual glorification"). On this view Ostler's "pure altruism" and Hansen's "enlightened self-interest" may be extensionally identical — the loving act and the flourishing-producing act coincide. Ostler concedes the *outcome* ("it in fact does" benefit you) but insists the *motivation* must remain other-directed, or it ceases to be love; the dispute is finally about the order of explanation, not the behavior.

## Best Quotes
> "We don't love each other because we have a moral duty to love each other. Love doesn't work within the constraints of some kind of moral ought." [^1]

> "If I only loved you because I felt obligated to do so, I think you'd say... you're just bowing to some kind of external pressure." [^2]

> "It ends somewhere... Love is. It simply is. You can't make love be something that it isn't." [^3]

> "Do I love you because it will lead to my personal flourishing? No, I love you because it will lead to your personal flourishing." [^4]
