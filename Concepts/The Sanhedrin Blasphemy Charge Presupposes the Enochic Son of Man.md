# The Sanhedrin Blasphemy Charge Presupposes the Enochic Son of Man

#type/claim

## The Claim

The high priest's reaction at Jesus's trial — tearing his robes and declaring blasphemy — only makes sense if "Son of Man" carried the loaded meaning it has in [[1 Enoch]]'s Book of Parables: the pre-existent, divinely enthroned cosmic judge named before creation. If the phrase meant only a generic "human one," the verdict of capital blasphemy would be a wild overreaction by the most educated religious official in Jerusalem. The trial scene therefore has "a hole in it" without the Enochic background.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Jesus's Cosmic Use of the Title
Jesus uses "Son of Man" in three registers — his earthly ministry ("nowhere to lay his head," authority to forgive sins), his predicted suffering ("must suffer many things"), and the **cosmic** sense drawn from the Enochic tradition: "ye shall see the Son of Man sitting on the right hand of power and coming in the clouds of heaven."[^1] It is this third use that he invokes at the trial.

### 2. The Trial Exchange
Before the Sanhedrin — the 70-member supreme council under the high priest — Jesus is asked directly, "Are you the Messiah, the Son of the Blessed?" He answers, "I am, and ye shall see the Son of Man sitting on the right hand of power and coming in the clouds of heaven." The high priest immediately tears his robes; the council votes for execution.[^2]

### 3. The Load-Bearing Question: What Was Blasphemous?
> "What exactly was blasphemous about that? If Son of Man just meant a human being, a common poetic usage in Hebrew, tearing your clothes would be a significant overreaction."[^2]

The high priest was the most educated religious official in Jerusalem. Hearing "Son of Man seated at the right hand of power coming on the clouds," he heard a **specific claim**: identification with the pre-existent, divinely enthroned judge of [[1 Enoch]] 46–62 — the figure named before creation who sits on God's throne and before whom kings are destroyed.[^3]

### 4. Blasphemy in the Second Temple Sense
A human being claiming to *be* that figure is exactly what blasphemy means in the broader Second Temple sense — claiming divine prerogatives that aren't yours. Arnold's conclusion: "The reaction was not necessarily an overreaction. It was the only coherent response... to what was actually being claimed."[^3] This presupposes the Enochic frame of reference was live in the room — consistent with 1 Enoch's circulation at Qumran and citation in [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude 1:14–15]]. See [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]].

## Best Quotes

> "When he heard Son of Man seated at the right hand of power coming on the clouds, he heard a specific claim. He heard Jesus identify himself as the pre-existent, divinely enthroned cosmic judge from the Enochic tradition."[^3]

> "The entire crucifixion narrative has a hole in it without this background."[^3]
