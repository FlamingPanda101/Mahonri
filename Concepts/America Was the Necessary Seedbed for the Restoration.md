# America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration

## The Claim

Why did the Restoration of the gospel occur in the **Americas and not in France**, when both revolutions happened in the same century and a Frenchman ("a Jacques") could easily have boarded a boat? [[Brian Thomassen]]'s answer: only the American constitutional order embedded a **limiting principle** on government that carved out the protected space — religious liberty, individual conscience, the room "for the person to define themselves" — that the Restoration required. The American model is an Enlightenment one with a built-in restraint; the French model is a [[Rousseau Is the Anti-Enlightenment Lynchpin of the French Revolution|Rousseauian, utopian]] one with **no limiting principle**, which devoured religion and ended in imperial absolutism. America is "the seedbed of the Restoration."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Enlightenment model creates space; it does not guarantee outcomes
The American documents enshrine *negative rights* — "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness." The pursuit of happiness is **not a guarantee of happiness**; it "providing the space where now you can go and frame your life as you will… Maybe you'll succeed, maybe you won't. That will be on you." This limiting, space-creating character is exactly what allows free moral agency and, with it, the free reception of restored gospel claims. It mirrors [[D&C 101]]:77–78 — laws established "that every man may act… according to the moral agency."[^2]

> **Steelman:** That the early-Republic United States had unusually strong constitutional protections for religious free exercise and disestablishment (First Amendment) is historically uncontroversial, and the comparative point — that revolutionary France was overtly hostile to organized religion — is well grounded.

### 2. The French model destroyed the space for religion
Under the Revolution, "lots of clergymen are killed, lots of clergymen are forced to swear an oath of allegiance to the power of the state," and the trajectory ends in a "monarchic imperial system reborn" under [[Napoleon]]. France also had a long prior "history of the religiously non-conforming… just being killed" — the Cathars, and the Huguenots (St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre, 16th century), many of whom fled to England and Germany. No protected dissenting space → no "Jacques" could have done in France what Joseph Smith did in New York.[^3]

### 3. The contrast: Washington vs. Napoleon
[[George Washington]] relinquished power ("I retire from the great theater of action"); [[Napoleon]] declared "I am the throne" (1813), echoing Louis XIV's "I am the state." The American founders did not have to kill each other; the French revolutionaries did (factions rising and falling, killing their enemies). The presence or absence of the limiting principle is the whole difference.[^4]

> **Caution / honest assessment:** This is a *providential* reading of history, not a neutral causal claim — it presupposes that the Restoration is true and then asks why its setting was uniquely suitable. The argument also idealizes the American founding (slavery, which the [[Abraham Lincoln|Lincoln]]–Douglas material in the same talk concedes) and compresses a complex comparative history. As intellectual history it is a *plausibility* argument about preconditions, not a proof.

## Best Quotes

> "It is only within the American model that a government was set up that had a limiting principle that established the space that allows for religion. It allows for individual conscience."[^5]

> "I like to refer to it as the seedbed of the restoration… We're going to plant the seed in this soil that's ripe and ready to go and fertile."[^6]
