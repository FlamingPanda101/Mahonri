# 1 Corinthians 14 Was Not Written by Paul

## The Claim

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] argues that [[1 Corinthians 14.34-35|1 Cor 14:34-35]] ("Let your women keep silence in the churches") was **not written by Paul** — it is a later interpolation, originally a marginal gloss derived from [[1 Timothy 2.11|1 Tim 2:11]], that migrated into the body of the text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The internal contradiction.** Just three chapters earlier, [[1 Corinthians 11.5|1 Cor 11:5]] assumes women *do* pray and prophesy aloud. "Either Paul is contradicting himself within three chapters, or..."[^2] *(Strong — the tension is real and long-noted.)*

2. **The floating-text evidence.** O'Connor: "It's the only part of Paul's letters which move around in early manuscripts" — in the Western text-type, vv. 34-35 appear *after* v. 40. Displacement is a classic signature of an interpolated gloss. "There's a lot of really good scholarship on why that wasn't actually written by Paul."[^1] *(Strong — this is the well-known argument of Gordon Fee and others; the manuscript dislocation is genuine, though the interpolation thesis is contested.)*

3. **The apologetic irony.** O'Connor stresses that he, "a skeptic," can use criticism to *absolve* Paul of sexism, "whereas the Christian has to own them. It's in there. It's part of the scriptural canon."[^3] *(Effective — the interpolation thesis is more available to the critic than to the inerrantist, who must defend the received text.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener — the contextual reading.** Scrivener prefers harmonizing 14 with 11 via context: Corinthian worship was "an utter zoo" (tongues, drunkenness at communion), and wives were "speaking over their husbands as they preached," so "we'll sort that out at home." On this reading γυνή = "wife," and the silence is situational, not a blanket ban.[^4] O'Connor: "I disagree. I think the interpretation that makes sense is that Paul didn't write that verse."[^1] *(Scrivener's reading is exegetically defensible and avoids excising text; the dispute turns on whether the manuscript dislocation outweighs the contextual harmonization.)*

## Best Quotes

> "Paul didn't write that verse — it found its way into the scriptural canon as a mistake, because somebody had read 1 Timothy 2:11 and was making a note in the margin."[^1]

> "It's the only part of Paul's letters which move around in early manuscripts. It's really interesting."[^1]

> "I, as a skeptic, am able to absolve Paul... whereas the Christian has to own them."[^3]
