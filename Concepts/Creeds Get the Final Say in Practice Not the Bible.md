# Creeds Get the Final Say in Practice Not the Bible

## The Claim
[[Greg Matsen]] and [[Hayden Carroll]] argue that creedal Protestants profess *sola scriptura* — "I only believe the Bible's infallible" — yet their actual operating authority is the creed. The tell, Carroll says, is hermeneutical: when the creedal reading and "a plain reading of the text of the Bible itself" diverge over the Trinity, "they would prioritize the interpretation of the creeds." So the Bible nominally "gets the final say," but in disputed cases the creed does.[^1][^2] What makes this version distinctive — and fairer than the usual LDS jab — is that Carroll grants the Protestant the strongest form of their position before pressing it.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **Carroll states the Protestant's own best defense first.** He explicitly refuses the strawman: "to be fair to them, they would say... they wouldn't say that they only go to the Bible for salvific truth, but rather the Bible gets the final say. And their argument is just that the creeds align with the Bible." He even grants their *revisability* principle — "if a creed is wrong it should be changed."[^2] *Rating: this is the correct steelman* — confessional Protestantism (e.g., the Westminster tradition) does hold creeds as *norma normata*, subordinate and corrigible standards under Scripture as *norma normans*. Carroll's argument therefore lands only if the *content* really does diverge.

2. **The divergence is located, not merely asserted.** Carroll supplies a concrete test case: "show me where consubstantiation — *homoousios*, the same substance — between the Father, Son and Spirit is taught in the biblical text." The reply ("it says the Father and the Son and the Spirit are one") is met with the lexical point that "the word is not the same word that was imposed upon the text centuries later."[^3] *Rating: a real pressure point* — the technical term is admittedly post-biblical (see [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]], [[Consubstantial Is a Mickey Mouse English Translation of Homoousios]]); whether the *concept* is biblical is the live question.

3. **The [[John 17.20-23|John 17]] parallel exposes a non-consubstantial sense of "one."** Jesus prays "let them be one as we are one" for disciples who are "obviously not part of the Trinity," using oneness in a sense that cannot mean shared substance.[^4] So when a creedal reader takes the Father–Son "one" as consubstantiality, they are choosing the creed's sense over a sense the text itself models elsewhere. *Rating: forceful* — though it shows the text *underdetermines* the metaphysics rather than *excludes* the Trinity (cf. [[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality]]).

4. **Sola scriptura's structure is itself a creedal inheritance.** Matsen's framing: "your whole structure of belief with the Bible being supreme actually comes from the creeds" — the canon and its supremacy were defined by the councils, so the professed Bible-only authority is parasitic on conciliar tradition.[^5] *Rating: a genuine bite* against naive *sola scriptura*; see [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]] and [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon]].

5. **Steelman of the creedal reply.** A confessional Protestant answers that prioritizing the creedal reading is not preferring tradition *over* Scripture but preferring the *whole-canon* reading over a proof-text — "one" in John 10:30/17:11 is read with John 1:1, Hebrews 1:3, and Philippians 2:6, and a creed just summarizes that synthesis. The demand for the *word* "consubstantial" is a category error (no one demands the word "Trinity" or "incarnation" appear either); doctrines can be biblical without being verbatim. And the John 17 parallel cuts both ways: Trinitarians read the disciples' unity as *analogical* (union by grace) precisely because they already distinguish union-by-nature from union-by-grace. *Rating: strong* — so the claim reduces to a contest over whether the Trinity is the *best* synthesis of the canon, not a clean demonstration that creeds trump the Bible.
