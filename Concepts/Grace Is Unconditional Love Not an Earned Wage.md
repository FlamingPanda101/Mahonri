# Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common Reformed charge is that LDS soteriology makes salvation an *earned* transaction, whereas grace is God's free gift; relatedly, the Calvinist insists God has no obligation to save and may justly damn. [[Blake Ostler]] meets the point by *conceding* the gratuity of grace while redefining what grace is. [^1] See [[God's Wrath Is the Natural Consequence of Rejecting Love]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **God has no duty to save, because grace is a gift** — rate: a notable concession that disarms the objection. "God has no moral duty to save me, because it's a matter of grace. He gives it to me as a gift. He doesn't have a duty to give a gift." *(theological concession)* [^2]
2. **But he could not be loving while damning eternally** — rate: the key distinction. Having no *duty* to give a gift is compatible with love; *actively decreeing* someone to "suffer for eternity" is not. "He could not be loving while sending me to hell to suffer for eternity. Love is what it is. You can't redefine it." *(moral)* [^3]
3. **Grace = unconditional love** — rate: the constructive reframe ([[Ephesians 2.8-9|Eph 2:8-9]]). "We are accepted by God as a matter of grace. Let me give you another word for that: unconditional love. God accepts us as we are... just because we are who and what we are. We don't need to prove anything to him." *(definitional)* [^4]
4. **Trying to earn love misunderstands love** — rate: ties to [[Love Transcends Moral Obligation]]. "If I try to earn that kind of love, I'm showing I don't understand what love is. One cannot earn love. Love is given as a gift. It's freely given." *(conceptual)* [^5]

**Steelman of the Reformed critic:** The Calvinist replies that grace's gratuity is *precisely* why God may justly pass over the reprobate — if grace is unowed, withholding it from some is no injustice ([[Romans 11.6|Romans 9-11]]), and LDS talk of being "accepted as we are" risks cheap grace that bypasses substitutionary atonement and regeneration. A Protestant would also press [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25:23]] ("after all we can do") as evidence of works-grounded acceptance in LDS scripture. Ostler's rejoinder: unconditional acceptance is not antinomian — it is the *ground* from which transformation grows (see [[After All We Can Do Means Despite All We Can Do]]) — and a "grace" that coexists with decreeing billions to eternal torment is not the unconditional love the word names.
