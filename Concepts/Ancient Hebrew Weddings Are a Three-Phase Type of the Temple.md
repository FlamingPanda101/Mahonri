# Ancient Hebrew Weddings Are a Three-Phase Type of the Temple

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the ancient Hebrew wedding is a **deliberate type of the temple, the sealing ordinance, and the Plan of Salvation**, with [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]] as the bridegroom. Its three phases — *shidduch* (matchmaking), *erusin* (betrothal), and the wedding itself — parallel the three-part temple procession, and the whole apparatus (the father choosing the bride, the betrothal contract, the dowry, the year-long separation while the groom builds the bridal home, the night-time wedding feast) is replayed across scripture as a single covenant grammar. Understood this way, the temple's purpose is not merely "get sealed and check the box," but **entering a marriage relationship with Christ**; the marriage model "adds another layer of evidence for the Book of Mormon."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Three phases mirror the three-part temple

- **Shidduch (matchmaking).** The **father of the groom** selects the bride and negotiates the terms binding two families; the bride may accept or veto. Woodmansee notes God the Father chose Eve for [[Adam]] — "who selected the bride for Adam? God the Father" — and a servant (not Isaac) chose [[Rebekah]] for [[Isaac]] (Genesis 24).[^3]
- **Erusin (betrothal).** Signing the **ketubah** (written contract), washing in the *mikvah*, the dowry, and then a year of separation. See [[The Ketubah and the Jerusalem Window Depict Christ as the Marriage Covenant]].[^4]
- **The wedding.** The bridegroom returns by night, the bride is "lifted up" and carried to the father's house, and a seven-day feast follows.[^5]

Woodmansee maps these onto "that three-part procession through the temple" that hosts David Butler and Mike Day have detailed.[^6]

### 2. The pattern recurs across scripture

[[Isaac]] and [[Rebekah]] (Genesis 24) show the earliest form — a bride "called out of Babylon" by the father's servant, given gifts beforehand. Jacob's seven years for Rachel are the **bride price** he paid because he was penniless (himself a type of the bridegroom who "offered himself as the bride price"). Moses's ascent of Sinai follows the wedding steps — proposal (the Ten Commandments), washing and anointing, the veil — as well as the three-part temple.[^7]

> **Steelman:** Scripture itself figures the God-Israel and Christ-Church bond as marriage ([[Hosea 2.19-20|Hosea 2]]; Ephesians 5; Revelation 19/21), and the bridegroom/feast/bride motif is pervasive in Jesus's parables. That the temple and the sealing are *covenantal marriage* with Christ is the prophets' and the New Testament's own framing, here read back into the ritual mechanics — consonant with [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]].

### 3. It reframes the temple as relationship, not transaction

The payoff is pastoral: "the Covenant isn't just a piece of paper, it is a relationship," in which "the one who does everything... is the groom," and all the bride must do is "be loyal and true" — i.e. keep the commandments out of love. This dovetails with [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]] and [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]].[^8]

## Best Quotes

> "What our relationship with Jesus Christ looks like... it really has implications for our Temple covenants, for our Temple ceremonies, and I think it adds another layer of evidence for the Book of Mormon as well." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^1]

> "When we look at the three-part Temple we're also seeing the three-part marriage ceremony... the terms of the marriage given, and then the bride accepting the betrothal, and then the actual marriage." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^9]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The reading is avowedly typological and devotional; Woodmansee says she is "just skimming over the surface." Many "parallels" depend on reconstructions of ancient Jewish wedding custom that are themselves debated, and on [[Margaret Barker]]'s contested first-temple thesis. The vault's [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] flags exactly this risk: a long list of resemblances without textual controls proves little.
- A skeptic can grant the marriage *metaphor* throughout scripture while denying that the modern LDS temple ceremony is its lineal descendant rather than a 19th-century composition.
