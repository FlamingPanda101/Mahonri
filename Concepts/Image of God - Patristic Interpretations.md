# Image of God — Patristic Interpretations (Imago Dei)

## The Point
Early Christians agreed that humanity was made "in the image of God" (Genesis 1:26–27) but disagreed sharply on *what* the image is — the body, the soul/mind, the whole person, or moral virtue — and whether the "image" and the "likeness" referred to two distinct things. The Genesis text gave "fragmentary hints" rather than a systematic doctrine, so interpretation diverged. Notably, [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] located the image in the *body*, while [[Origen]] and [[Augustine]] located it in the soul/mind, explicitly denying the body could bear God's image. This diversity matters for Latter-day Saints because it shows the embodied-God reading was a live patristic option, not a modern invention (compare [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]]).

## Evidence
1. The foundational text is Genesis 1:26–27: "Let us make humankind in our image, according to our likeness." These verses place humans in an "intermediary role between God and creation" — linked upward to God (theomorphism) and downward as stewards over creation. Humans alone were created in God's image, unlike animals made "after their kind."[^1]
2. The text raised unresolved questions: "Should it be identified with the human mind or soul, the body, the whole person, or moral virtue? Does the image refer to humans or to Christ...? What is God's likeness? Is it synonymous with the image, or does the image and likeness each refer to unique characteristics?"[^2]
3. Many fathers held Christ to be *the* image of the invisible God (2 Corinthians 4:4; Colossians 1:15), so humans are made in the image of the Son. Irenaeus: humans are "created in the image and likeness of the Son."[^3]
4. **Irenaeus (body):** He identifies the human *body* as formed after the "image of God" — God "gave [Adam's] frame the outline of His own form that the visible appearance too should be godlike." This counters the Platonic view that the image lies only in the immaterial soul.[^4]
5. **Origen (soul, not body):** "We do not understand … this man indeed whom Scripture says was made 'according to the image of God' to be corporeal. For the form of the body does not contain the image of God."[^5] He reads Genesis 1:26–27 as the creation of the *inner* person, "invisible, incorporeal, incorruptible, and immortal."[^6]
6. **Augustine (the reasoning soul):** "the interior man, where reason and intellect reside" is the image of God; reason both makes humans superior to other creatures and is the vehicle of immortality.[^7]
7. **Image vs. likeness:** Both Irenaeus and Origen distinguish the two. Origen notes Genesis 1:27 says God made humans "in his image" but is "silent about the likeness" — the likeness is reserved for the consummation of the world.[^8]
8. **Women in the image:** Some fathers were ambiguous on whether women bore the image; John Chrysostom affirmed Eve's "equality of honor," noting God said "Let us make" of both.[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "We do not understand … this man indeed whom Scripture says was made 'according to the image of God' to be corporeal. For the form of the body does not contain the image of God." [^5]

> God "gave [Adam's] frame the outline of His own form that the visible appearance too should be godlike for it was as an image of God that man was fashioned and set on earth." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints affirm that God the Father has a tangible, glorified body (D&C 130:22) and that humans are physically made in that image (Moses 2:27). Irenaeus's reading — that the *body* itself images God — is a striking patristic precedent for the LDS position and stands against the Platonizing fathers. See [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]] and [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]].
