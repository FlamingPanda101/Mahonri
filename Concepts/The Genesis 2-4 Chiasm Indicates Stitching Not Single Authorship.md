# The Genesis 2:4 Chiasm Indicates Stitching Not Single Authorship

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The Word Room argues that Genesis 2:4 contains a chiasm (inverted parallelism / A-B-B'-A' structure): A "the heavens and the earth" / B "when they were created" / B' "in the day the Lord God made" / A' "the earth and the heavens." Because the verse is so deliberately patterned — even reversing the usual order "heavens and earth" to "earth and heavens" — they argue it must have been *composed as a unit by a single author*, and so cannot be the seam between two contradictory sources.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The chiasm is real, but its existence does not entail single authorship (decisive logical point).** McClellan grants "that is a clear chiastic structure," then identifies the false dichotomy: the apologist offers only two options — (a) one author wrote 2:4a and 2:4b together, or (b) two pre-existing sources coincidentally mirrored each other and an editor got lucky. "Those aren't the only two options."[^2]

2. **The consensus third option: deliberate redactional stitching.** As the five commentaries show, 2:4a "was composed *with* Genesis 2:4b in mind and in an effort to stitch the priestly creation account to the earlier non-priestly creation account."[^3] A redactor *crafting* 2:4a to chiastically interlock with the existing 2:4b produces exactly this pattern — the chiasm is *evidence of intentional joining of two sources*, not of unitary composition. Carr explicitly notes 2:4a "is bound to the material it introduces in 2:4b by inverse parallels... an A B C-prime B-prime A-prime pattern."[^4]

3. **Distinct literary fingerprints undercut single authorship (strong, linguistic).** Within the chiasm the two halves differ systematically:
   - 2:4a uses the **definite** "*the* heavens and *the* earth" (matching Genesis 1:1–2:3); 2:4b uses the **undetermined** "earth and heavens" with no article.[^5]
   - The **order reverses** (heavens→earth vs earth→heavens).
   - **Different verbs** for "create/make" (*bara* in 2:4a vs *asah*).
   - **Different divine names** across the surrounding units (Elohim in Genesis 1; Yahweh Elohim in Genesis 2).
   Carr: these features "would be unusual in a single textual unit."[^5] McClellan: "It's not just 'ooh look, chiasm.' It's also the literary qualities of one are entirely distinct from the literary qualities of the other."[^6]

4. **Rate the evidence: strong.** The chiasm argument trades on the unstated premise "patterned text ⇒ single author," which the redactional-bridge model directly defeats. The clustering of independent distinguishing features (article, order, verb, divine name) makes coincidental single authorship the *least* likely explanation. Compare the parallel debate over chiasmus in the Book of Mormon — see [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship]] and [[Pareidolia Objection to Chiasmus Finding]].

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A skilled single author could deliberately vary article, verb, and word order *for stylistic/poetic effect* within a chiasm — variation is a feature of good Hebrew style, not necessarily a source seam — and the divine-name shift is, on a literary reading, a theological signal of God's two roles (transcendent creator / immanent covenant Lord). McClellan's reply: the variations here align *systematically* with the independently-established boundary between the Priestly and non-Priestly material (same article-usage, verbs, and names that distinguish those sources throughout), so the simplest explanation is two sources joined, not one author varying.

## Best Quotes

> "Is it possible that these are two different lines from unrelated contradicting sources and the editor just put them together to make the pattern? Technically, yes... But what is more likely?"[^2]

> "Genesis 2:4a was composed with Genesis 2:4b in mind and in an effort to stitch the priestly creation account to the earlier non-priestly creation account."[^3]

> "It's not just 'ooh look chiasm.' It's also the literary qualities of one are entirely distinct from the literary qualities of the other, and the literary qualities of the other are consistent with the rest of the creation account that runs from Genesis 2:5 through to the end of Genesis 3."[^6]
