# Book of Moses Is a Posthumous Label Joseph Smith Never Gave

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that two of the most common things members say about the [[Book of Moses]] are anachronisms Joseph Smith never authorized: (1) that it is the "**Joseph Smith Translation**," and (2) that it is even called the "**Book of Moses**." Joseph "never said 'I'm going to re-translate Genesis' — that's wrong, he didn't do that"; the actual JST/inspired version came *later*. And "Joseph never called it the book of Moses. That's the thing. He never told us what it was." The titling and the assembly into a single book are the work of later editors and "the Mormon zeitgeist," not of Joseph. This is offered as the corrective groundwork for understanding the volume — and as the opening that makes his [[The Book of Moses May Reconstruct the Lost 116 Pages|116-pages theory]] possible.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The revelations came without titles
The 1830 revelations bore only generic descriptors — "a revelation, Joseph the seer, to the elders of the church" — "they came without titles." Barnes contrasts this with the lurid titles of genuine apocryphal forgeries ("the lost book of Adam's revelation from the serpent"): Joseph "wasn't trying to write fanfiction… He's just receiving light." The plainness of the descriptors is itself evidence he wasn't packaging a marketed book.[^4][^2]

### 2. The OT1 manuscript headings are bland and un-Mosaic
Per the Joseph Smith Papers, the original manuscript **OT1** carries three section headings, which Barnes reads off: "a revelation given to Joseph the Revelator" (≈ Moses 1:1–36); "revelation given to the elders of the church of Christ on the first book of Moses" (Moses 1:37 through ch. 4); and "the revelation **concerning Adam**" (Moses 5–8) — "such a lame title… not even *about* Adam or *on* Adam or *of* Adam. It's *concerning* Adam."[^5][^6] None of these is "the Book of Moses."

### 3. The name "Moses" is back-derived from the opening line
Where did "Book of Moses" come from? Barnes says even [[Bruce Porter]] is unsure who coined it. The best guess: the revelation opens "the vision that Moses had when he was on the mountain," and the content is Genesis-like (Moses being the purported author of Genesis) — "so no wonder they call it the book of Moses." The title is an inference from the text, applied later, not a designation Joseph gave.[^7]

### 4. The assembly and canonization were posthumous and communal
Joseph "never really published it… he never put them all three together and said 'this is the book of Moses.'" Instead [[Franklin D. Richards]] "in 1857 or something, long after Joseph is dead, digs up these old revelations and puts them in a missionary tract" for British missionaries; "then 30 years later we take it and canonize it officially." A mission president "called it the book of Moses as he was trying to annotate everything that Joseph Smith said." The title is "basically an invention of the Mormon zeitgeist."[^8][^9] *(This compilation history is documented and overlaps the [[Stephen Smoot|Steven Smoot]] account in [[The Pearl of Great Price Is the Most Unstable Volume of LDS Scripture]]; Barnes' distinctive twist is the normative claim that the **labels themselves are mistaken**, not merely that the volume evolved.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can turn the "Joseph never told us what it was" point against the apologist: if Joseph gave no account of these revelations' nature or source, that silence is at least as compatible with ordinary 19th-century pseudepigraphic composition as with ancient restoration — which is roughly [[Dan McClellan]]'s framing in [[JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition]]. Calling the popular labels wrong does not by itself establish what the texts *positively are*; it mainly clears the ground for Barnes' own (admittedly speculative) reconstruction. And the "JST" label is not pure error: Joseph *did* later fold this Genesis material into his Bible revision, so the Book of Moses and the JST of Genesis are genuinely entangled — the relationship is messier than "simply wrong."

## LDS Connection
This is the descriptive correction that the whole episode is built on. By detaching the texts from the labels "JST" and "Book of Moses," Barnes (a) aligns with the [[The Pearl of Great Price Is the Most Unstable Volume of LDS Scripture|instability thesis]] and the communal-compilation picture in [[Canonization Is a Community Process Not a Unilateral Prophetic Act]], and (b) opens conceptual space for reclassifying the texts as backward-looking restorations ([[Annidictions - Joseph Smiths Restorations Look Backward Not Forward]]) and possibly the recovered lost-116-pages content ([[The Book of Moses May Reconstruct the Lost 116 Pages]]).
