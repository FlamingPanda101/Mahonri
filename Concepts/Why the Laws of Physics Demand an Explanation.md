# Why the Laws of Physics Demand an Explanation

## The Point
Hansen argues that the very existence of *law* in nature — rather than random chaos — is evidence for a mind, and that historically this expectation is what made science possible. Science, he says, "emerges from a judeo-christian tradition... with the idea that the universe is not the capricious arbitrary whim of the gods but rather is the basis of order and law"; because early scientists "expected to find law in nature," they looked, "and what did they find? They found law."[^1] The deeper point is that order itself cries out for explanation: drilling down through biology to chemistry to physics, "what you find there is what you least expect — it isn't random chaos, it's something that looks designed."[^2] Seth's reply is that this rests on an *equivocation fallacy*: "the laws of physics are just patterns... just because we can calculate patterns doesn't necessitate some divine mind behind those patterns."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Science presupposed an ordered cosmos.** "Because they expected to find law in nature they began to study, and what did they find? They found that nature is indeed not arbitrary but that it is predicated on laws... historically that's why science got started."[^1] Compare [[Theism Makes Science Possible]].
2. **Order, not chaos, is the surprise.** "When you go down to the root... what you find there is what you least expect; it isn't random chaos, it's something that looks designed."[^2] Hansen presses the contrast visually with the pool table: "why doesn't it look like this? This is the way the universe should be — it shouldn't look something like this; it shouldn't be organized and orderly, yet that is what it is."[^4]
3. **Seth: 'law' equivocates.** "This argument fundamentally rests upon an equivocation fallacy... the laws of physics are just patterns; we go out into the world, we see the planets moving and we can calculate a pattern" — calculability does not entail a Lawgiver.[^3]
4. **Materialism is not the whole of reality (Hansen).** Hansen adds that even granting science's success with the physical, "are we to assume that... the reductionist materialist view of the world is the only valid way of looking at reality? It doesn't" exhaust reality — likening pure materialism to looking "with one eye closed."[^5] This connects to the broader [[The Argument from Reason - Atheism Undermines Reason Itself]].

## Best Quotes
> "Because they expected to find law in nature they began to study... and what did they find? They found law." (Hansen) [^1]

> "It shouldn't be organized and orderly, yet that is what it is... why is it not total chaos?" (Hansen) [^4]

> "The laws of physics are just patterns... just because we can calculate patterns doesn't necessitate some divine mind behind those patterns." (Seth) [^3]

## LDS Connection
The "law in nature points to a Lawgiver" argument fits the LDS conception of God as one who governs through and is even "constrained by eternal law," organizing an orderly cosmos rather than decreeing it ex nihilo by arbitrary fiat. The claim that materialism captures only part of reality also coheres with the LDS affirmation of an irreducible spiritual/metaphysical order — a point Seth himself concedes exists. See [[Theism Makes Science Possible]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]].
