# Pharaoh Also Hardens His Own Heart

## The Claim
McClellan notes — as a *point against* the wax/clay analogy, not for it — that "Pharaoh also hardens his own heart in the book of Exodus." He raises this precisely because it "would kind of undermine the notion that this hardening is just a product of God's glory shining on a heart that has been irresponsibly managed by its possessor." If hardening were an automatic effect of glory on a clay-filled heart, the text would not separately depict *Pharaoh himself* doing the hardening.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Two distinct agents in the text.** Exodus alternates between "the Lord hardened Pharaoh's heart" (e.g., [[Exodus 9.12|Exodus 9:12]]) and statements that Pharaoh hardened his own heart. The narrative does not collapse these into a single mechanical process.[^1] (Strong — the dual attribution is on the page.)
2. **It defeats the "automatic mechanism" reading.** "It's obviously not something that just happens when a human heart is exposed to God's glory." If it were a constant glory-effect, Pharaoh self-hardening would be redundant or nonsensical.[^2]
3. **It reinforces selectivity.** Because hardening can be done *by Pharaoh* or *by God* at different points, divine hardening is shown to be a discrete, chosen act — supporting [[God Explicitly Hardens Pharaohs Heart in Exodus]].

**Steelman.** The apologist could turn this verse to their advantage: "See — Pharaoh hardened himself *first*; God only later confirmed Pharaoh's chosen disposition, exactly as the clay-heart model predicts." This is the classic compatibilist harmonization and is textually arguable for the early plagues. **But** McClellan's narrower point survives: the very existence of a *separate* "Pharaoh hardens himself" agent proves hardening is not an automatic, uniform effect of divine glory — which is what the wax/clay analogy requires.
