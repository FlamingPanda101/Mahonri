# The Judas Counterexample

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues Jesus himself supplies a counterexample to omnibenevolence. In [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] (cf. [[John 17.12|John 17:12]], "son of perdition") Jesus says of Judas it "would have been better for that man if he had not been born." Hayden: "If going to hell was better for Judas than not being born... that statement would make zero sense" *unless* some outcomes are so catastrophic that non-existence is preferable for the individual. "Which raises the unavoidable question: why initiate the birth of Judas at all?"[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Scriptural anchor for the value premise** — rate: strong rhetorically. The dilemma needs the premise "some outcomes are worse than non-existence"; Jesus' own words supply it, so the affirmative cannot dismiss it as the skeptic's assumption. *(scriptural)* [^1] See the existing [[Matthew 26.24]] note, already used for [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]].
2. **Forces the creation question** — if non-existence would have been *better for Judas*, and God could have refrained, why create him? *(application of the dilemma)* [^2]
3. **Cross-exam pin** — Hayden gets Gavin to affirm hell is worse than heaven, that Judas-types are *human beings* (not angels), and that God was not *compelled* to create Judas — then asks what good Judas got out of it. Gavin: mystery. See [[Appeal to Prophetic Necessity Rebutted]] and [[Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good]]. *(elenctic)* [^3]

**Steelman of the affirmative:** (a) *Hyperbole/idiom* — "better not to have been born" may be a Semitic woe-formula expressing the gravity of betrayal rather than a literal axiological comparison of hell vs. non-existence (cf. Job 3, Eccl 4:3). (b) *Comparative not absolute* — it may mean "better for *him*" only relative to the enormity of *this sin*, not a metaphysical verdict that his creation was a net evil. (c) Gavin's actual reply: Judas's betrayal was *prophesied and necessary for the atonement* (see [[Appeal to Prophetic Necessity Rebutted]]), and Christ's descent into hell may even have reached such souls (he speculates "we don't know if Judas was among them"). Hayden's rejoinder: even granting necessity, that instrumentalizes Judas and never answers what good Judas himself received.

## Best Quotes
> "Even Jesus acknowledges this tension... Jesus calls Judas... a son of perdition, and that it would have been better for that man if he had not been born."[^1]

> "If God didn't have to create Judas and he knew not being born would have been better for Judas, then why did God create Judas?"[^2]

> "And how can you say that's willing the good for Judas? What did Judas get out of this? Why is this good for Judas?"[^3]
