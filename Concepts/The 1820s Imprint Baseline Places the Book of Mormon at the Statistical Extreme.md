# The 1820s Imprint Baseline Places the Book of Mormon at the Statistical Extreme

## The Claim

Instead of arguing the [[Book of Mormon]]'s improbability in the abstract, [[Matt Vallejo]] anchors two of his variables — author profile and book length — in hard data on what was actually being published in the United States during 1820–1829, the exact decade preceding the book. Drawing on an organization that tracks period imprints, he reports **~41,000 unique U.S. imprints** in that decade, and uses their distribution as the denominator against which [[Joseph Smith]] is an outlier on two axes at once: he belongs to the *least likely author class* yet produced one of the *longest works of the decade*.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The author-class distribution.** By source-of-author, ~35% of 1820s imprints came from clergy (deeply educated), ~30% from lawyers, and so on down — with farmers, artisans, and self-educated laymen, where Joseph would fall, comprising only about **5–7%** of titles. So before any other consideration, Joseph belongs to the bottom ~5% of the population that produces anything that gets printed at all.[^3] *Rating: a real and underused framing — it converts the familiar "uneducated farmer" point (see [[The Consensus of Joseph Smith's Contemporaries That He Was Uneducated]]) into a population base rate.*

2. **The length distribution.** Word counts of the ~41,000 imprints form a roughly normal distribution centered near **20,000–21,000 words** — most "imprints" were pamphlets (~50 pages), not novels. At ~265,000–270,000 words, the Book of Mormon sits at the far-right tail, which Vallejo places in the **top ~0.0001%** by length.[^4][^5] *Rating: the pamphlet-heavy shape of the era's output is accurate and rhetorically strong; the precise percentile is an estimate.*

3. **The two named comparanda.** The only 1820s imprint plausibly *longer* than the Book of Mormon is John Neal's *My Brother Jonathan* (est. 370,000–400,000 words) — and Neal was a lawyer, editor, and professional writer with two schoolteacher parents, i.e. exactly the profile one would predict for the decade's longest book, and older than Joseph. James Fenimore Cooper's *The Last of the Mohicans* (~150,000 words) came from a Yale-educated novelist who had already written several books. Joseph fits *neither* profile.[^6][^7] *Rating: the contrast cases are well chosen — they show that the era's other long-book authors had precisely the training and track record Joseph lacked.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Wrong reference class.** The 41,000-imprint pool is *all* American authors; Joseph was not a random draw from it but a scripturally-immersed religious founder dictating a sacred text — a tiny, non-representative sub-population for which base rates derived from the general pool may not apply. See [[The Book of Mormon Is Measured Against the Wrong Period]].
- **Length is partly an artifact of genre and dictation.** A revelatory/scriptural register that recycles biblical phrasing and parallelism can accrue length faster than original belletristic prose; raw word count may overstate the compositional difficulty relative to, say, Cooper's plotted novel.
- **Estimated counts are soft.** Vallejo concedes the *My Brother Jonathan* word count "is really hard to actually get" and must be estimated; the percentile claims (top 0.0001%) inherit that uncertainty.
- **Selecting two long books proves little on its own.** The comparison is suggestive, not decisive — it carries weight only as one conditioned input inside [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions|the larger combined argument]], not as a standalone proof.

## Best Quotes

> "35% of all books in the 1820s were written by clergy... You got 30% that are lawyers, and then down and down you go. Joseph would likely find himself in that farmers, artisan, self-educated layman."[^3]

> "The average word count for most imprints in the 1820s was about 20 to 21,000 words... that was the grand majority of those imprints — they were pamphlets, they weren't full-on novels."[^4]

> "He's in the bottom 5% of likely authors, but he's in the top 0.0001% in terms of book size production."[^5]
