# Anti-Mormonism United Skeptics and Evangelicals Against the LDS Church

## The Claim
The satanic panic persisted in Utah longer than anywhere else because anti-Mormonism collapsed the usual check on it. Elsewhere, skeptical rationalists balanced credulous evangelicals; in Utah, where the accused community VIPs were Latter-day Saint leaders, anti-Mormon atheists and anti-Mormon evangelicals made common cause and the skepticism that elsewhere ended the panic evaporated.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The normal skeptic/believer balance (strong — mechanism)
Nationally, "against the accusations were the rationalists. Skeptical atheists and secularists rolled their eyes at accusations of satanic possession." This skeptical pole usually deflated local panics [^1].

### 2. In Utah the VIPs were the church (strong)
"In Utah, your average business mogul or neighborhood VIP was a Latter-day Saint. The community leaders were also church leaders." So accusations aimed at high-profile figures became accusations against the Church [^2].

### 3. Shared hatred fused the camps (strong)
"When the accusations were directed at leaders of the LDS Church, suddenly the anti-Mormon evangelicals and the anti-Mormon atheists were fighting side by side. The typical skepticism evaporated." "Suspicion and hatred of the LDS Church united the most unlikely of foes" [^3].

### 4. A credentialed historian states the thesis (strong — [[Massimo Introvigne]])
Introvigne: "When, however, the Satanism scare hit Mormon country, rationalist and post-rationalist counter-Mormons did not divide along predictable lines … All evangelical counter-Mormons accepted the reality of the satanic conspiracy." The counter-cult movement, "firmly convinced of the existence of the devil, was quite slow in adding Satanism to its list of cults" — it "hated Mormons and Catholics more than actual self-professed card-carrying Satanists" [^4][^5].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A critic could argue that Utahns embracing the claims reflected sincere child-protection concern rather than anti-Mormon opportunism, and that attributing the panic's persistence to "hatred of the Church" is itself a defensive deflection that paints all critics as bigots. The rebuttal does not deny sincere concern existed; it isolates a structural anomaly — the documented disappearance of the *skeptical* counterweight specifically where the targets were LDS — and grounds it in a non-LDS sociologist's published analysis, not merely apologetic assertion. The claim is about why skepticism failed in Utah, not that every believer was a bigot.
