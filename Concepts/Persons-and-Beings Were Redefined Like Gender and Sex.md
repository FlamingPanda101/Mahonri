# Persons-and-Beings Were Redefined Like Gender and Sex

## The Claim
To make the Trinity "work," 5th-6th-century theology had to **drive a wedge between two previously-synonymous words** — "person" and "being." [[Chandler Henry|Chandler]] analogizes this to the modern separation of "gender" and "sex": take two synonyms, functionally split them, then re-read older texts through the new distinction. The maneuver is the same in form; the analogy is offered as illustration, not as a political broadside.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Person" and "being" had been synonymous
On [[Aristotle]]'s terms, to be your own person was to be your own being (see [[Being and Person Are the Same in Aristotle]]). Trinitarianism had to "start redefining words that had always been synonymous" so that three persons could be one being. *(Moderate-strong; the ousia/hypostasis split is indeed a deliberate technical divergence from ordinary usage.)* [^1]

### 2. Parallel: gender vs sex
The transgender movement, Chandler argues, gained ground fast by taking two synonyms — gender and sex — and "functionally separated them for their means," redefining "gender" as a social construct distinct from biological sex "even though those two had never been defined that way." The structural move (split synonyms → advance a framework) mirrors the Trinitarian split of person/being. *(Weak-to-moderate as evidence about the Trinity — it is an *analogy of form*, not proof; persuasiveness depends on accepting the contested gender claim. Flagged as rhetorically charged.)* [^2]

### 3. The re-reading move: reading the new distinction back into old texts
Just as activists (he claims) read a later gender/sex distinction back into the 1995 Family Proclamation, Trinitarians read the later being/person distinction back into the Shema and other texts that never carried it — anachronistic eisegesis. See [[Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge]], [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible]]. *(Moderate as it restates the anachronism argument; the analogy is the rhetorical packaging.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A Trinitarian (and many neutral observers) would object that the analogy is **tendentious and question-begging**: it presumes the gender/sex distinction is illegitimate in order to discredit the person/being distinction by association, but the two cases are not parallel in justification — the Cappadocians argued *philosophically* that *hypostasis* and *ousia* were never truly synonymous (the individual vs the kind), a distinction with independent grounding, not an arbitrary redefinition to win an argument. Coining precise technical vocabulary is normal in every discipline (cf. "mass" vs "weight" in physics) and does not entail bad faith or anachronism. The force of Chandler's point therefore rests entirely on whether the person/being distinction is principled or ad hoc — the very issue argued in [[Being and Person Are the Same in Aristotle]].

## Best Quotes
> "Trinitarianism in the 5th century and the 6th century basically had to change persons and beings... They had to start redefining words that had always been synonymous." [^1]

> "[The transgender movement] basically took two synonyms, gender and sex, and then functionally separated them for their means... even though those two had never been defined that way." [^2]

> "That is exactly what you're doing when you're reading back into the text things like the Shema — this sort of ontological oneness — when it was a statement of devotion and Yahweh alone." [^3]
