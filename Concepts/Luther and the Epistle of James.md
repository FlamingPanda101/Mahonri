# Martin Luther and the Epistle of James

## The Conflict
Luther's doctrine of *sola fide* (justification by faith alone) collided
directly with James 2:24: "A man is justified by works and not by faith alone."

## What Luther Actually Said

- **1522 Preface**: Called James "an epistle of straw" compared to the
  "true and noble" books of the NT.
- On the difficulty: "That epistle of James gives us much trouble. For the
  papists embrace it alone and leave out all the rest."
- The "stove" quote: "I almost feel like throwing Jimmy into the stove as
  the priest in Kalinberg did." (Referring to a priest who burned wooden
  apostle statues for warmth.)
- On authorship: "I maintain that some Jew wrote it who probably heard
  about Christian people but never encountered any."
- On content: "It contains not a syllable about Christ. Not once does it
  mention Christ except at the beginning."

## What Luther Did NOT Do
- He **did not** try to remove James from the canon.
- He **did not** deny it was scripture.
- He later moderated: admitted James "promulgates the laws of God."
- He "walked really close to that line, but he didn't cross it."

## Why It Matters
- Some Catholics claim Luther tried to remove James — this is historically
  inaccurate.
- But Luther's extreme frustration reveals how difficult James 2 is for
  *sola fide* — the Reformer himself recognized the tension.
