# Surah 10-94 Makes the Bible the Standard for the Quran

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]]'s favorite text in [[The Islamic Dilemma]] is [[Quran 10.94|Surah 10:94]]: "If you are in doubt about that which We have revealed to you [the Qur'an], then ask those who have been reading the scripture before you." He argues this verse *inverts the assumed authority order*: it makes the **previous scriptures the test of the Qur'an**, not the Qur'an the test of the previous scriptures.

> "So the Quran demonstrates that the Bible is the authority, is the standard of truth over the Quran, while the Muslims would have it the other way around... the Quran says no, it's the previous scriptures that are the supreme authority. Don't get it backwards."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The verse's internal logic (Christian steelman).** Avery walks a caller through it step by step and gets agreement at every node: (a) the doubt is *about the Qur'an*; (b) the remedy is to *check the prior scriptures*; (c) if the Qur'an lines up with them, Muhammad "will know the Quran is true and from Allah"; (d) therefore the contrapositive holds — if it does *not* line up, "he would see that the Quran is not from Allah." Conclusion: "the previous scriptures are the judge on whether or not the Quran is getting stuff right."[^2] This is the cleanest form of the argument: the verse makes the Bible the *epistemic criterion*.
2. **It rules out the "selective corruption" hypothesis.** Cameron adds a second-order point: if only *some* parts of the prior scriptures are reliable, why would Allah send Muhammad to *those scriptures* to remove doubt at all? "That seems to be problematic on the face of it." And if a defender restricts the confirmation to "only the stories of the prophets" (the "Daniel Haqiqatjou line"), then "go to those stories of the prophets — you're going to find a ton of stuff that conflicts with the Qur'an."[^3] Either way the verse cuts.
3. **The "yes to everything but the conclusion" pattern.** Avery's recurring complaint is that callers concede each premise yet deny the conclusion. He caricatures it: "Yes, it has feathers. Yes, it has wings. Yes, it lays eggs... but it's not a bird."[^4] *(This is a rhetorical charge of inconsistency, not itself a proof; a Muslim could deny one of the earlier premises — e.g. that "doubt" implies a genuine epistemic test rather than a rhetorical address to opponents.)*
4. **Muslim steelman / live counters.** Callers offer several: (a) Muhammad "wasn't doubting, but wasn't sure" — Avery replies that is what doubt *is*; (b) the verse only invites a *comparison* showing "alignment," not a verdict that the Bible *judges* the Qur'an; (c) the Qur'an is elsewhere the *criterion* (see [[The Guardian and Criterion Reply to the Islamic Dilemma]]). A stronger reply not pressed on-air: many *tafsir* read the address ("if *you* are in doubt") as rhetorical — directed at the *hearer/skeptic*, since several hadith have Muhammad affirm he had no doubt — which would blunt the claim that the verse installs the Bible as the standard. Avery pre-empts this: "Who cares? It's not the argument. What does Allah tell him to do *if* he did [doubt]? ... his standard is the previous scriptures."[^5]
