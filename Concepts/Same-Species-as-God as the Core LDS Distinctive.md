# Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive

## The Claim
If one had to name the single doctrine that most sharply distinguishes Latter-day Saint theology from Islam, mainstream Christianity, and Judaism alike, Peterson says it is the conviction that **humans are of the same species as God** — that God, angels, and humans lie on one continuum or spectrum. He half-jokingly calls this the "fundamental heresy" of Mormonism from those traditions' point of view.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The other monotheisms unite in denying shared species with God.** Islam holds we are created things ([[Islam Denies the Deity and Divine Sonship of Christ|Edison and the light bulb]]); mainstream Christianity holds we are God's children only by *adoption*, "a different species altogether"; Judaism agrees. "That is absolutely foreign to Islam, absolutely foreign to mainstream Christianity or Judaism."[^2] *(Strong — correctly identifies that creator-creature ontological distinction is shared across the three traditions.)*
2. **LDS theology treats divine sonship as ontological, not merely adoptive.** Against the adoption model, the LDS claim is that humanity and Deity are on "the same continuum or spectrum." Peterson calls it "a revolutionary thought in the gospel as we have it."[^1] *(Strong as a statement of distinctive LDS teaching on the nature of God and human deification.)*
3. **This reframes the whole Islam comparison.** Because the deepest LDS distinctive is shared-species deification — which Islam, Catholicism/Protestantism, and Judaism *all* reject equally — the supposed gulf between LDS thought and Islam (over Christ's deity) is no wider than the gulf between LDS thought and Nicene Christianity. The Muhammad comparison thus tells us little: every other monotheism is "foreign" on the point that matters most.[^2]
4. **Steelman.** Critics (Christian and Muslim alike) regard same-species deification as precisely the disqualifying error — a collapse of the creator-creature distinction they see as the heart of monotheism. Peterson does not argue the doctrine here; he simply names it as the real fault line, framing it as "revolutionary" rather than defending it. An LDS reply would invoke patristic *theosis* and texts on becoming "partakers of the divine nature," but that argument lies outside this source.
