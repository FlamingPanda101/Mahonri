# Embodied Risen Christ Strains the Trinity

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Why might a sincere Trinitarian clergyman hesitate to "definitely" affirm Jesus's *bodily* resurrection? The hosts propose a doctrinal (not faithless) reason: if the risen Christ has a body, and the Son is co-substantial (consubstantial) with the Father, but the Father is held to be bodiless spirit (per [[John 4.24|John 4:24]], "God is spirit"), then affirming an embodied Son seems to generate a contradiction within the shared divine nature. A scrupulous Trinitarian might therefore withhold "definitely." This argument is attributed to [[Jacob Hansen]].[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The dilemma stated (the LDS argument).** [[Jacob Hansen]]'s move: "If he has a body and he's co-substantial with the Father, but the Father doesn't have a body, then that's a total contradiction."[^1] The LDS resolution dissolves the tension by affirming that the Father *also* has a body of flesh and bone ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]) and that the Godhead is three distinct persons, not one substance. *Rating: strong as an internal critique of one-substance Trinitarianism; it is a live debate, not a knockdown.*

2. **Explains a "no" without imputing apostasy (moderate).** The hosts note many respondents may be lay or "over-enthusiastic 17-year-old youth pastor[s]" who half-know that "God is spirit and must be worshipped in spirit" and so doubt a *bodily* resurrection to avoid accidental heresy.[^2] This reframes some mainline "no" answers as confusion or scruple, partially conceding [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]] for this item.

3. **Historical pedigree (strong).** The hosts correctly observe this was a genuine second-century battleground: docetism and related christologies disputed whether Christ had real flesh while alive or after rising.[^3] [[Luke 24.32|Luke 24]] and [[John 20.17|John 20]] emphasize a tangible risen body ("handle me and see"), which orthodox writers wielded against docetists.

**Steelman of the Trinitarian reply.** Classical Trinitarianism is not actually trapped here. On Chalcedonian christology the *one person* of the Son subsists in *two natures*: the divine nature remains bodiless and impassible, while the assumed *human* nature is genuinely embodied and resurrected. Consubstantiality holds at the level of the *divine* nature, which the body never touches. So a well-catechized Trinitarian can affirm both a bodily risen Christ and a bodiless divine essence without contradiction — meaning Hansen's dilemma bites hardest against *naive* or *modalist* formulations, not the careful creedal one. The LDS counter is that the two-natures solution is itself an artifact of Greek metaphysics imported into Hebrew categories.

**Linguistic/scholarly context.** [[John 4.24|John 4:24]] reads *pneuma ho theos* — a zero-copula construction ("spirit [is] God") that the panel elsewhere warns is over-read by those who take it to mean God has *no* physical manifestation whatsoever.[^4] Whether "God is spirit" entails "God is bodiless" is exactly the contested exegetical hinge.
