# Nahom Names a Tribal Region Not the Altar Findspot

#type/claim

## The Claim

The Book of Mormon's Nahom ([[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34]]) is best identified with the **NHM tribal *region*** in northwest Yemen (around the Wadi Jawf), **not** with the specific temple site where the famous NHM votive altars were excavated. The altars do not prove their own findspot was called Nahom; they are inscriptional evidence that the *Nehem region — located elsewhere* — already existed and bore that name in Lehi's day. [[Neal Rappleye]] argues this distinction both corrects a common apologetic misuse and actually *strengthens* the correlation, because a sizable region is far easier for a passing caravan to enter than a single pinpoint site. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The altars name a region "west of Marib."** The votive altars were found at the Bar'an temple near Marib. The dedicant, Bi'athar, is called a "Nehemite," and the English (British Museum, 2000) and German museum catalogs — written by the scholars who excavated them, including excavation director Burkhard Vogt — gloss him as being "from the region of Nehem **west of Marib**." So the inscription points *away* from its own findspot toward the region where the Nehem tribe is still found today. [^2]

2. **Tribe and territory are conflated in Yemen.** Objection: "NHM is a tribe, not a place." Reply: in ancient South Arabian inscriptions the *nisba* (gentilic) ties a person's identity to their tribe *and its territory*; Rappleye cites Alessandra Avanzini to this effect, and notes one scholar even indexes tribal names under toponyms. Tribe and land are routinely interchangeable, so "from the tribe of Nehem" and "from the region of Nehem" are not rival readings but the same integrated reality. [^3]

3. **The region is substantial but still tiny against Arabia.** The modern Nehem administrative district / tribal territory is roughly 5,000 sq km; pre-Islamic Nehem may have straddled both sides of the Wadi Jawf. Against the ~1.25 million sq mile Arabian Peninsula, Rappleye's "back of the napkin" odds of blindly hitting an NHM region are about 1 in 600 — and he has found no other NHM place or tribe anywhere else in Arabia. [^4]

4. **A larger target raises, not lowers, plausibility.** Because Nephi's text only ever fixes a general *region* anyway (you cannot GPS the tomb of Ishmael from the text), the correct unit of comparison is region-to-region. A ~2,000–5,000 sq km Nehem is a much more plausible thing for Lehi's party to have passed through than a lone city or temple. [^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can press that this is a *retreat*: earlier popularizers did point to the altars/findspot as "Nahom," and shifting to "it's the region, not the site" looks like moving the goalposts after the spelling-and-distance problems surfaced. A region ~100 miles from the altars, several thousand sq km in size, is a broad target, and NHM is a common Semitic triliteral, so finding *some* NHM toponym near a long caravan route is not wildly improbable. Rappleye partly grants the first point — he agrees lay apologists (himself years ago) sometimes misstated it as a burial-site-named-Nahom — but answers that the peer-reviewed scholarship always located the *region* correctly, that the joint match of name + mandatory eastward turn + dating is what carries weight (see [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn]]), and that the burden is on the critic to produce a competing NHM anywhere else on the peninsula. He even concedes that *if* one insisted the altar temple itself had to be Nahom, there is in any case a sister temple with thousands of burials only one mile east — so the "no burials" sub-objection fails either way.

## Best Quotes

> "It's not saying the altars were found in the exact right place. It's saying that Nehem is in the exact right place." [^2]

> "Biathar was from the region of Nehem west of Marib. Now that's an important detail... it's telling us that the region to the west, where we find the Nehem tribe in early Islamic times — we still have Nehemites over there in that area — situated in the same place all the way back to the sixth or seventh century BC." [^2]

> "If anything, it being a larger region makes it more plausible, shall we say, that Lehi might have passed through it than just a tiny specific site." [^1]
