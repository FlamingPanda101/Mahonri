# The Conway-Kochen Free Will Theorem — Indeterminism Preserves Freedom

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]] calls this his "most crucial piece of
evidence for free will." Classical determinism pictured the universe as a wound
clock — [[Michio Kaku]]: "the universe is a clock... so what you're going to eat
10 years from now... has already been fixed."[^1] Jones argues physics has
overturned that picture. **First**, the Princeton mathematicians [[John Conway]]
and [[Simon Kochen]] proved the **Free Will Theorem**, which Jones states as:
"we can only have free will if we live in an idealist indeterministic universe
instead of a realist deterministic universe... Either we are free and the
universe is indeterministic, or there is no free will and the universe is
determined. There is no middle ground."[^2] **Second**, he claims the
experimental record now shows the actual universe is indeterministic: recent
experiments by [[Anton Zeilinger]] "demonstrated that intuitive features of
realism must be abandoned,"[^3] the 2007 violation of **Bell's inequality**
falsified hidden-variable theories so that "a particle's position is dependent on
the observer,"[^4] and in 2011 Brukner and Kofler showed this "even affects the
macro level."[^5]

The conclusion: "the actions of the observer cannot be determined by prior
causes... at the quantum level it is completely indeterministic and nothing is
certain unless an observer makes a measurement."[^6] Hence — via the
[[Werner Heisenberg|Heisenberg uncertainty principle]] and over
[[Albert Einstein|Einstein]]'s objection that "God doesn't play dice" — "no one
can determine your future events given your past history. There's always the
wild card."[^7] Free will, Jones says, is "preserved."[^8]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Free Will Theorem (Conway–Kochen, 2006/2009).** If experimenters have
   freedom in choosing measurement settings, the particles' responses are not a
   function of the prior state of the world. Jones reads this as a clean
   dichotomy: indeterministic-and-free vs. deterministic-and-unfree, with "no
   middle ground."[^2] **Rating: strong but interpretively loaded** — the theorem
   is a genuine peer-reviewed result, but its leap from particle indeterminism to
   human *free will* is named provocatively and is disputed by physicists (the
   theorem's "freedom" is a technical no-prior-determination condition, not
   agency). It also *assumes* experimenter freedom as a premise, which a
   superdeterminist denies.

2. **Experimental defeat of local hidden variables.** Bell-inequality violations
   (Aspect onward, and [[Anton Zeilinger]]'s photon experiments) rule out *local*
   hidden-variable theories — the determinist's best hope for restoring a fixed
   underlying reality. "In 2007, quantum physics said goodbye to reality."[^4]
   **Rating: solid on the physics** — local realism really is experimentally
   excluded; the contested step is again the inference to libertarian agency
   rather than merely to non-locality or contextuality.

3. **Macro-level reach (Brukner–Kofler, 2011).** Jones cites work showing
   observation-dependence "even affects the macro level,"[^5] resisting the reply
   that quantum indeterminacy washes out at human scales. **Rating: moderate** —
   the cited result concerns when macroscopic realism can fail, but whether
   ~10⁻³⁴-scale indeterminacy meaningfully propagates to neural decision-making is
   exactly the open question determinists press.

4. **Heisenberg uncertainty as the in-principle limit.** Because position and
   momentum cannot be jointly fixed, the future is not *in principle* computable
   from the present — removing the predictability classical determinism
   required.[^7] **Rating: established physics, modest philosophical payload** —
   unpredictability-in-principle is real, but it yields an *open* future, not by
   itself an *authored* one (see Counter-Objection).

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)

The strongest objection comes from a determinist who is *also* a critic of free
will on Jones's own terms — and it is the very point [[Sam Harris]] makes in
[[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]: **quantum randomness
is not freedom.** "If my decision was due to a random release of
neurotransmitters, how could the indeterminacy of the initiating event count as
*free will*?... This would obliterate any semblance of human agency." A choice
that is undetermined-because-random is no more *mine*, and no more praiseworthy
or blameworthy, than a choice fixed by prior causes — it is just noise inserted
into the causal stream. So even granting every line of physics Jones cites,
indeterminism at most defeats *determinism*; it does not by itself deliver
*agency*. The luck/sourcehood dilemma reappears: determined → not up to me;
random → not up to me either.

A second objection: the Free Will Theorem **presupposes** experimenter freedom
to derive particle "freedom," so it cannot be used as independent proof that
agents are free without circularity — and a superdeterminist simply rejects the
freedom premise.

## Counter-Objection

The steelman is correct that indeterminism is *necessary but not sufficient* for
libertarian freedom — and Jones overstates when he treats quantum results as
nearly proving free will. But it does real work: it **removes the in-principle
defeater** (a closed, computable future) that hard determinism leaned on, which
is why the same physics is also used in the vault to block a fixed cosmic
beginning in
[[Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow]]. The remaining gap — turning an *open* future into an *authored* one —
is precisely where Jones's case must be supplemented by **agent causation**: a
self that is neither compelled by prior causes nor reducible to random
micro-events. Materialism cannot supply such a self (its only ingredients are
caused matter and chance), which is why the Latter-day-Saint metaphysic of an
uncreated, co-eternal intelligence is offered as the missing term: the agent is
the primitive source that *uses* an indeterministic substrate without being
either determined or randomized by it (see
[[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]] and
[[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). On this reading
Jones's quantum argument and Harris's randomness objection are both right about
half the truth — and the disagreement is resolved one level up, in the
metaphysics of the agent.

## Best Quotes

> "We can only have free will if we live in an idealist indeterministic universe
> instead of a realist deterministic universe... Either we are free and the
> universe is indeterministic, or there is no free will and the universe is
> determined. There is no middle ground." [^2]

> "In 2007, quantum physics said goodbye to reality, as new experiments verified
> the violation of the famous theorem Bell's inequality, which demonstrated that
> hidden variable theories were in fact false and a particle's position is
> dependent on the observer." [^4]

> always the wild card. There's always the possibility of uncertainty in
