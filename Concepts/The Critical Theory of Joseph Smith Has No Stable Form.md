# The Critical Theory of Joseph Smith Has No Stable Form

## The Claim
Keystone's framing of the vaguest attack — "Joseph Smith made the Book of Mormon up all on his own" — is that the *critical* account of how he did it has never held a stable shape, and that this instability is itself evidence. The naturalistic theory has reversed direction across roughly 200 years: (1) at publication, critics called the book "simplistic trash, exactly what you'd expect" from someone of Joseph's limited education; (2) once its complexity was recognized, it became "a given that Joseph clearly didn't have the brains to write the book," so [[Sydney Rigdon]] supplied the theology and [[Solomon Spalding]] the history; (3) after that theory "came crashing down," critics returned to lone authorship — but now insisting Joseph "must have actually been a lot smarter and well read than his friends, family, and even foes claimed." The data never changed; only the story did. "The lack of a cohesive explanation that makes sense of all of the available evidence after almost 200 years strengthens my testimony."[^1]

This is distinct from the burden-of-proof point in [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]] (which shows each *single source* fails on word-count and access). Here the argument is *historiographical*: the oscillation of the theory between mutually exclusive portraits of the author — ignorant fool versus closeted genius — is the tell. A true explanation tends to converge as evidence accumulates; this one keeps swapping its central premise to dodge whichever fact is currently inconvenient.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The portrait of Joseph reversed itself (rated: strong, documented historiography).** The same fool→genius arc is independently traced from *Mormonism Unvailed* (1834) through Fawn Brodie's 1945 "religious genius" to the recent "he simply had the skills" claim (see [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]]). Two incompatible Josephs are required at different times to keep the naturalistic verdict fixed. *The verdict is held constant while its supporting premise is freely inverted.*
2. **Each pivot was forced by a collapse, not a discovery (rated: moderate-strong).** The shift to Rigdon/Spalding answered the recognition of the book's complexity; the return to lone authorship followed the [[Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin|Spalding manuscript surfacing in 1885]] and proving unrelated. The moves are reactive patches, not a research program homing in on a result.
3. **The "smarter than everyone said" premise contradicts the witnesses (rated: strong).** Recovering lone authorship requires positing a well-read, capable Joseph that his own intimates deny — [[David Whitmer]] and Emma described a man who "could hardly write legibly" and could not dictate a coherent letter (see [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]]). The latest theory survives only by overruling the people best placed to know.
4. **No cohesive synthesis after ~200 years (rated: the host's interpretive claim).** Keystone treats the persistent absence of one stable, evidence-fitting naturalistic account — despite enormous critical effort — as positive evidence for the prophetic claim. *Honest scope: this is an inference to the best explanation, not a proof; it raises the prior on the traditional account rather than demonstrating it.*

**Steelman of the critic.** The reframing has real force. (a) *Theories legitimately revise.* Science routinely discards failed models (Spalding) and refines its picture of a figure as new evidence arrives; that the early "ignorant fool" caricature was abandoned is intellectual *honesty*, not weakness — and a more capable Joseph is simply the better-supported reconstruction, not an ad hoc dodge. (b) *Both poles can be partly right across time:* a young man of modest formal schooling can still be naturally gifted, well-storied, and Bible-saturated, so "limited education" and "unusually able" are not strict contradictions. (c) *"No cohesive explanation" cuts both ways* — the believing account also leaves open questions (mechanics of translation, the seer stone, KJV stretches), so stalemate is not asymmetric evidence for either side. (d) The witnesses' "could hardly write" remarks are reminiscent and partly devotional, and describe penmanship and letter-writing, not oral narrative capacity. The strongest Latter-day Saint reply is to grant that theories may revise, but to press that these revisions *invert the load-bearing premise* (the author's very capacity) rather than refine a detail — and that the naturalist still owes one account that fits the dictation feat, the witnesses, the complexity, and the absence of a documented source, all at once.
