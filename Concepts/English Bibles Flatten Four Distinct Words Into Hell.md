# English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell

## The Claim

The single English word **"hell"** in most translations papers over several *distinct* original-language terms that meant different things to different authors in different periods. There is no one biblical word, and no one biblical concept, that "hell" cleanly translates. What modern readers picture as hell — eternal conscious torment in a lake of fire — is a *composite* assembled long after the biblical texts, drawing on at least four separate streams.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The terms collapsed under "hell"
McClellan distinguishes the underlying words English flattens:
- **Sheol** (Hebrew) — the grave / undifferentiated realm of all the dead, frequently rendered "hell," "grave," or "pit" in English Bibles. It carried no reward-or-punishment distinction. [^1] (See [[Sheol Was the Undifferentiated Realm of All the Dead]].)
- **Hades** (Greek) — the Greco-Roman underworld, whose concept "bleeds into" Judaism in the Hellenistic period. [^2]
- **Gehenna** (Greek transliteration of Hebrew *Gay Ben Hinnom*) — the Valley of the Son of Hinnom near Jerusalem, "in the New Testament... frequently translated 'hell' in English translations." [^3] (See [[Gehenna Was the Valley of Hinnom Not a Burning Garbage Dump]].)
- **Tartarus** — the Greek deep pit of punishment, the imagery behind the binding of rebellious angels (drawn into early Jewish texts like [[1 Enoch]]).

### 2. Each came from a different stream of tradition
McClellan stresses that hell "comes together from a number of different streams of traditions that begin inconsistently" — influenced by Greco-Roman Hades, by the Book of Enoch, "perhaps by Zoroastrianism," and later by [[Dante]] "and others that are giving us additional imagery." [^4] The modern "accumulated conceptual package" is a synthesis, not a single inherited term.

### 3. The flattening hides the diversity
Rendering Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, and Tartarus all as "hell" creates the false impression of one stable, biblical doctrine. In reality the New Testament itself reflects *all three* postmortem frameworks (annihilation; temporary torment then annihilation; eternal conscious torment) and never systematizes them; that did not happen "until well after the Bible has been completed." [^5] This is a translation-driven [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text|univocality]] imposed on a plural text.

## LDS Relevance

The lexical flattening is exactly the move the Latter-day Saint tradition resists. LDS scripture redeploys the word "hell" in *non*-infernalist senses — a temporary spirit prison from which most are eventually delivered, with only the sons of perdition suffering "outer darkness" (D&C 76; see [[Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell - DC 76 and DC 18]]). Recognizing that "hell" is a translator's umbrella over four distinct words removes the assumption that a single eternal-torment hell is *the* biblical teaching, and licenses the LDS reading in which postmortem states are graded and largely remedial (compare [[LDS Afterlife - Degrees of Glory]], [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]]).
