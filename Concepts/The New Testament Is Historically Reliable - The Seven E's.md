# The New Testament Is Historically Reliable - The Seven E's

## The Point
The fourth and most developed point of the four-point case asks whether the New Testament documents are historically reliable — not whether they are the inerrant "Word of God," but whether they "accurately tell us the basic facts about the historical person named Jesus of Nazareth."[^1] Turek first establishes that we possess an accurate *copy* (the text is reconstructable to more than 99% accuracy, with the disputed remainder affecting no doctrine), then offers seven lines of evidence — each beginning with "E" — that the writers were telling the *truth*: Early, Eyewitness, Elaborate, Embarrassing, Excruciating, Expected, and Extrabiblical testimony.[^2]

## Evidence
No single testimony is decisive, but together they form a cumulative case.[^3]
- **Early.** Most or all NT documents predate AD 70, since none mentions the Jewish war or the temple's destruction (which Jesus predicted), and several presuppose the temple still standing. Acts ends with Paul alive under house arrest (~AD 62), so Luke is earlier still; Paul quotes Luke in 1 Corinthians (AD 55–56); and the resurrection creed of 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 predates AD 40 — far too early to be legend.[^4]
- **Eyewitness.** Luke records 84 verified historical/eyewitness details in Acts 13–28 (Colin Hemer), names eight rulers correctly in Luke 3:1–2, and Sir William Ramsay (initially skeptical) judged Luke "along with the very greatest of historians." Over thirty NT figures are confirmed by non-Christian sources or archaeology — including the ossuary of Caiaphas.[^5]
- **Elaborate.** "Undesigned coincidences" (J. J. Blunt identified 60+): subtle, unplanned interlockings where one writer inadvertently answers a puzzle raised by another (e.g., Philip being from Bethsaida explains why Jesus asked *him* about food), a hallmark of independent eyewitnesses.[^6]
- **Embarrassing.** The writers record details no inventor would fabricate: the disciples as dim-witted cowards, Peter rebuked as "Satan," the women (not the men) as first witnesses, Jesus called demon-possessed and "out of his mind," and a Messiah crucified "under God's curse."[^7]
- **Excruciating.** Eleven of the twelve apostles died as martyrs for claiming the Resurrection and never recanted; they had verified it with their own senses. Contrasted with Islam, whose martyrs merely have faith.[^8]
- **Expected.** Old Testament messianic prophecies (esp. the Suffering Servant, Isaiah 52:13–53:12, attested before Christ by the Dead Sea Scrolls) fit only Jesus.[^9]
- **Extrabiblical.** Ten non-Christian sources within 150 years (Josephus, Tacitus, etc.) corroborate the storyline; Jesus is cited by 42 sources total — four times as many as the emperor Tiberius.[^10]
Turek closes by noting that apparent "contradictions" are minor *differences* of detail — exactly what authentic, independent eyewitness accounts produce (the Titanic survivors disagreed on details but all agreed the ship sank).[^11]

## Best Quotes
> "What did the Jewish New Testament writers have to gain by making up a new religion? By insisting the Resurrection occurred, they got excommunicated from the synagogue and then beaten, tortured, and killed. Last I checked that was not a list of perks!" [^12]

> "None of these testimonies alone is decisive, but together they comprise a powerful cumulative case that the New Testament writers were telling the truth." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints hold the Bible to be the word of God "as far as it is translated correctly" (Articles of Faith 1:8) and revere the New Testament as a true witness of Christ's life, death, and Resurrection — so Turek's defense of its historical reliability is welcome common ground. The "undesigned coincidences" argument from multiple independent witnesses also echoes a deeply LDS principle: that truth is established "in the mouth of two or three witnesses." The honest distinctive is that, for Saints, the New Testament's reliability does not *exhaust* the witnesses of Christ — the Book of Mormon stands as "Another Testament of Jesus Christ," an additional independent record. Turek's qualifier "as far as it is translated correctly" is essentially built into his own method (he reconstructs the text and brackets the <1% in question), a point of unexpected agreement on the difference between an inspired original and its transmission.
