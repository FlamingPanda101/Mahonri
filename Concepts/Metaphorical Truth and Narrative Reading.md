# Metaphorical Truth and Narrative Reading

## The Point

[[Jordan Peterson]] defends two linked ideas against [[Sam Harris]]'s proof-texting attack: (1) a complex *narrative* must be read holistically, not sentence-by-sentence, and (2) **metaphorical truth** — claims "literally false" that nonetheless produce better outcomes when acted upon, occupying an intermediate truth-space. Harris's rebuttal: the further you flee the literal text, the more "unconstrained" your interpretation becomes.

## Argument (steelman)

1. **Narrative is conditioned by its whole.** "It isn't reasonable to take a single sentence out of a coherent narrative and say it stands on its own." Like a film with a twist ending, the Bible's beginning "has to be read in light of the end." Peterson invokes Chekhov's gun: every element in a narrative is conditioned by the rest.[^1]

2. **The definition of metaphorical truth.** "Some things are literally false, but if you behave as if they were true you come out ahead of where you would if you behaved according to the fact that they are false" — so calling them merely "false" is incorrect.[^2]

3. **Harris's "tennis without the net" rebuttal.** "The more you get away from the literal, the more you are unconstrained by the text and you can just broadcast on it anything you want." Fundamentalism has an edge precisely because the literal reading is constrained; literary readings are "playing tennis without the net."[^3]

4. **Peterson's tu quoque.** The same objection applies to facts: "there's an infinite number of facts and an infinite number of potential interpretations," so tracking from fact to value is equally underdetermined — which connects to [[You Cannot Get From Facts to Values Without an Interpretive Structure]]. Harris denies the analogy: some interpretations are "more and less plausible."[^4]

5. **The limit case Peterson concedes.** Some sentences are "so blatant that you can't use context to paraphrase them" — e.g. stoning a non-virgin bride. Harris: "you don't have to read to the end of the book to know that it's wrong; you can get that from the paragraph."[^5]

6. **Where it lands.** Peterson reads Revelation "psychologically" — the apocalyptic structure of reality and the hero born at the darkest point — while disclaiming competence to make "fundamental metaphysical statements."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "Metaphorical truth is my argument that there are some things which are literally false, but if you behave as if they were true you come out ahead... to call them false is incorrect."[^2]

> "If you're going to go purely literary on any of these texts, you are on some level playing tennis without the net — you're unconstrained by the text and you can do with it more or less anything you want."[^3]
