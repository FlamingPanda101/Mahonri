# Naturalistic Explanations Cannot Account for Collective Sacrifice

## The Point
[[Jeremy Runnells]] and other critics argue that revelation "through feelings" is an "ineffective and unreliable method," and that spiritual experiences are really neurological or psychological artifacts. Fife concedes that any single such mechanism *might* explain *some* experiences — but argues the debunking collapses when applied to the *collective*: the persecuted, impoverished early Saints who built and abandoned temples, crossed the plains, and died for their testimony. Are we to believe all of that was confirmation bias and "elevated emotion"?

## Evidence
1. **The critics' toolkit, named.** Fife catalogs the secular explanations marshaled against testimony: the backfire effect, cognitive dissonance, confirmation bias, elevation emotion, frisson, intuition, the illusory truth effect, and the "God Helmet" temporal-lobe experiment.[^1]
2. **The collective problem.** Early Saints were "persecuted, mocked, tortured, murdered, and driven from state to state"; the Kirtland bank failed; Boggs issued an extermination order; the prophet was killed. Fife asks what gave them strength to press on — and whether anyone really endured all that "because of confirmation bias or elevated emotion."[^2]
3. **Temple-building against self-interest.** Destitute Saints finished the Nauvoo temple under [[Brigham Young]] *knowing* they would soon be expelled from Nauvoo — behavior Fife says the "cognitive dissonance" label cannot explain.[^3]
4. **Head and heart together.** Fife argues spiritual truth engages both "the heart (feeling) and the head (rational thought)," citing Jacob 7:11-12 (Jacob learns of Christ through scripture, experience, logic, and the Holy Ghost). He grants such experience is not transferable *proof* — "like witnessing the breaking of a world record without an adjudicator" — but insists it is valid.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "The longer I listened to critics, the more I saw them twisting themselves into knots to explain spiritual experiences away." [^2]

> "Religious experience is not proof, but it is valid. If I've experienced God and the gospel bears fruit, what more proof do I need?" [^4]

## LDS Connection
Fife's move mirrors the standard Latter-day Saint rejoinder that debunking the *medium* of an experience does not debunk its *content* — spiritual experience is "the lens, not the picture" (see [[Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture]]). Where critics isolate one finding (the unanswered prayer, the failed blessing — see [[Naturalistic Debunking of Spiritual Experience]]), Fife widens the frame to the aggregate of two centuries of costly devotion, arguing that no single neurological "misfire" scales to explain it.
