# Pure Rationality Makes Morality Selfish - Only God Connects All Well-Being

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that a *consistent* secular ethic of well-being collapses into egoism, and that only a God who unites everyone's well-being can rescue genuine other-regarding morality. He distances himself from "traditional utilitarianism" and the [[Sam Harris]]–style humanism that defines the good as "the greatest happiness for the greatest number," charging that it "simply ignore[s] the weaknesses in utilitarianism." [^1] The decisive weakness: "the only suffering or well-being you experience is your own," so coherent logic makes *your own* well-being "the thing of ultimate importance" — which "frames all of morality ultimately as selfish." [^2][^3] The "saving factor" is "a metaphysical force like God who connects the well-being of all," so that "you can never ultimately, on the eternal time scale, increase your well-being by harming others." [^4]

## Evidence
**The critique of humanist utilitarianism.** Utilitarianism "alone has no basis for saying that I should sacrifice my own well-being for the well-being of the group," and "the idea of inherent human rights [is] completely inconsistent with this philosophy, as the good of the many... trumps the rights of the few." [^1] Because each person experiences only their own well-being, "other people's well-being only matters to a rational person to the degree that it affects them" — "that's why the cancer diagnosis of a random person in a country far away does not cause you to lose sleep at night." [^2]

**The atheist witnesses.** Hansen marshals two consistent atheists to show this is where secular morality *honestly* leads. He quotes [[Ayn Rand]] at length — "happiness comes from making other people happy... let's aim at the day when people will not hear it anymore because it isn't true... you can allow only those whom you value, and if they contribute to your happiness" — and notes that "biologically this makes perfect sense, as our genetics are inherently only concerned with their own propagation." [^3] He then names [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]: this egoism "is the position of serious atheists like Nietzsche, as seen in his conception of the will to power, which essentially is a will to well-being." [^4] (Compare [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]].) Crucially, Hansen grants the egoist's logic is internally coherent — "if this purely rational view of morality makes you uncomfortable, it should" — but says "something in us cries out against this, and I believe this is rightly so." [^4]

**The theistic resolution.** A God of justice "connects the well-being of all," so harming another can never, on an eternal timeline, net you well-being; thus "your well-being is intimately connected with God and all of creation," and morality "becomes about proper relationship to God and creation." [^4] This is what scripture means by "wickedness never was happiness," why "we desire for those that we love to conform to the celestial order," and why "love is connected to righteousness... the force that connects all mankind together" — sealed by the high-priestly prayer that disciples "may be one" ([[John 17.20-23]]). [^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** The atheist can reply that Hansen has not refuted egoism — he has only added a celestial *reward structure* that makes altruism instrumentally pay off, which is itself a higher-order egoism ("be good so that *you* flourish eternally"). And the move from "something in us cries out against this" to "therefore God connects all well-being" is an appeal to moral intuition plus a posit, not an argument that such a God exists. The Latter-day Saint rejoinder is that the intuition is veridical data about the nature of being (see [[Moral Disagreement Is Really Disagreement About the Nature of Being]]), and that grounding morality in relational flourishing under God is more explanatory than leaving the universal cry against egoism unexplained (see [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Ultimately, the only suffering or well-being you experience is your own... your own well-being would be the thing of ultimate importance, because your own well-being is the only well-being that you experience." [^2]

> "If this purely rational view of morality makes you uncomfortable, it should, because it frames all of morality ultimately as selfish. But biologically this makes perfect sense, as our genetics are inherently only concerned with their own propagation." [^3]

> "The saving factor in this equation is when you posit a metaphysical force like God who connects the well-being of all... so you can never ultimately, on the eternal time scale, increase your well-being by harming others." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This is the heart of the video's restored-gospel payoff. Where secular well-being ethics terminates in rational egoism, the Latter-day Saint metaphysic of a just God who binds all beings together makes self-interest and love for others ultimately coincide: "wickedness never was happiness" (Alma 41:10) becomes a structural fact, not a platitude. The goal is "proper relationship to God and creation," and the telos is the oneness of [[John 17.20-23]] — "that they all may be one, as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee." It directly answers the open problem left by Part 1's well-being framework ([[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]): obligation toward *others* is grounded not in bare rationality but in a God who connects every person's well-being.
