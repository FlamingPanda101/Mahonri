# Relationship Not Payment - Atonement Rejects Retributive Justice

## The Claim
The polemical core of [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Ostler's Compassion Theory]] is that atonement is about entering a **relationship**, not making a **payment**. He explicitly "rejects the retributive notion of justice that demands that someone must suffer and pay a price in order for someone to be forgiven."[^1] In its place: "The demand of suffering and payment is replaced by the condition that one must repent and have a genuine change at the core of one's being — one's very heart — to meet the demands of justice."[^1] Decisively, [[Blake Ostler]] holds that **God can simply forgive**: "God can in fact forgive us without requiring that someone must first suffer."[^2] The whole apparatus of a price that *must* be paid to an offended Father or to "some Platonic ideal of justice" is dismantled.

This is the constructive contrary of the [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories|penal-substitution view]] most Latter-day Saints hold, and it is the same instinct (atonement is relational/consensual, not a coerced transaction) that the vault develops at [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]] — though Ostler presses the *opposite* horn: not that God is constrained to demand payment, but that no such demand exists, only the relational condition of repentance.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Demands of justice" reinterpreted as the Law of Restoration.** The Book of Mormon's "satisfied the demands of justice" texts ([[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:15]]; Mosiah 15:9) are not retributive: "The demands of justice are answered in Christ's atonement because we each receive what we freely choose — nothing could be more just than that."[^3] If we repent, Christ "willingly and lovingly accepts into his being what we would have suffered so that we don't have to"; if not, we suffer for our own sins — but this is consequence, not retribution (see [[The Law of Restoration as the Single Law of Justice]]). *(Strong: re-reads the very texts penal-substitution leans on.)*
2. **Probation, not immediate execution of justice.** Justice is met "by placing us on probation and allowing us time to repent rather than executing justice immediately," which is itself an act of mercy ([[Alma 42.22-25|Alma 42]]).[^4] *(Fair: keeps the Alma 42 justice/mercy tension while draining it of retribution.)*
3. **The suffering is redemptive, never pointless.** Because Christ's pain is directed wholly at healing relationships and "transforming our darkness into light," it is "maximally redemptive on the Compassion Theory" — by contrast with views that have him suffer to discharge an abstract debt (see [[The Pointless versus Redemptive Suffering Distinction]]).[^5] *(Fair: the redemptive/pointless distinction is what licenses any divine suffering at all on this model.)*
4. **Steelman of the retributivist / penal-substitution reply.** The substitution defender answers that justice is not an arbitrary "Platonic contraption" but rooted in God's own holy nature, so sin *must* be dealt with, not merely forgiven by fiat; and that the substitute is God himself freely giving his life, so no innocent is *coerced* (the self-giving-love framing at [[Substitutionary Atonement Is Not a License to Sin]]). Ostler's rejoinder is that if God can forgive directly — which scripture affirms — then any model making suffering a *precondition* of forgiveness fails the [[The Desiderata and Abelards Constraint for a Theory of Atonement|moral desideratum]] by punishing the innocent for the guilty (the *innocence principle*, which he extracts from [[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:11-12]] while rejecting that text's retributive premise).[^6] *(Honest: the disagreement reduces to whether justice is self-grounded in God's nature and therefore non-waivable — the same fault line the vault flags between classical and LDS theism.)*

## Best Quotes
> "The Compassion Theory rejects the retributive notion of justice that demands that someone must suffer and pay a price in order for someone to be forgiven."[^1]

> "He does not suffer to appease the wrath of a vengeful Father or to satisfy the unjust demands of some Platonic ideal of justice... God can in fact forgive us without requiring that someone must first suffer."[^2]

> "The demands of justice are answered in Christ's atonement because we each receive what we freely choose — nothing could be more just than that."[^3]
