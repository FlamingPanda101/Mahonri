# Divine Hiddenness Is a Gift That Sanctifies

## The Point
Jones reframes the whole problem: God's hiddenness "is really a gift. It is necessary to help change us appropriately."[^1] Because humans are fallen and self-absorbed, simply handing them propositional knowledge that God exists produces no meaningful change — Scripture's own forced encounters prove it. God therefore *first* convicts and humbles us, then reveals himself "not how we demand or when we demand, but in the way he knows best."[^2] Hiddenness is the patient, merciful method of a God who "cares more than we could know," not the silence of a God who does not care.[^3]

## Evidence
- **The father-of-triplets analogy.** A father, hideously burned so his face terrifies and his voice frightens, cannot reveal himself to his toddlers without traumatizing them, so he writes letters and sends messengers until they mature. One daughter grows disinterested; one grows bitter and decides the letters must be from an "imposter"; the third studies the letters, trusts his promises, and when she is mature enough his appearance no longer frightens her and they live together in happiness.[^4] "While we are currently spiritually immature, God stays physically hidden so we can grow in love and humility."[^5]
- **Forced revelation often does nothing.** Balaam (Numbers 22) received a direct revelation of El Shaddai yet "didn't repent or join the side of Israel"; the Pharisees watched Jesus cast out demons and were unmoved. So God has *already shown* that propositional proof alone "does not initiate a meaningful change."[^6]
- **Garcia: hiddenness develops perseverance.** Laura Garcia: "persons can only advance towards the goal of union with God by way of trials, darkness, and abandonment... God's hiddenness is thus the result of his merciful love, which develops love and perseverance in his disciples by withdrawing the signs of his presence from time to time."[^7]
- **The wrong desires problem (John 7).** When Jesus' unbelieving brothers challenged him to "show himself to the world," his reply was that the world already hates him — the desires are already wrong, so a public display would change nothing; instead, "who loves me... I will love him and manifest myself to him."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Divine hiddenness, if anything, is really a gift. It is necessary to help change us appropriately." [^1]

> "God is not hidden because he does not care, but because he cares more than we could know." [^3]

> "God's hiddenness is thus the result of his merciful love, which develops love and perseverance in his disciples by withdrawing the signs of his presence from time to time." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The analogy of gradual, age-appropriate revelation is deeply congenial to Restoration theology: mortality is a school in which the veil keeps God hidden precisely so that love and humility can be *grown* rather than imposed. The "letters and messengers" of the analogy map naturally onto scripture, prophets, and the quiet promptings of the Spirit that testify without compelling. And the soul-making frame — that trials and seasons of divine silence are the means by which disciples are refined — is the same logic the Restoration applies to suffering generally (compare [[The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil]]). Where a classical theist treats hiddenness as a pedagogical tolerance, the Latter-day Saint can go further: a probationary estate *requires* a measure of hiddenness, because a test you cannot fail is no test, and a faith that costs nothing refines no one.
