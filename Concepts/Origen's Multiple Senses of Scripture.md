# Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture

## The Claim

[[Origen]] (3rd c. CE) — alongside [[Augustine]] — represents the early church's "more thoughtful and more insightful approach" to scripture, which was emphatically **not** literalist. [^1] Origen taught several senses of a text: the **plain/historical/literal sense** ("what the words themselves seem to mean just on the surface") and deeper **moral, allegorical, and anagogical** senses. Crucially, he ranked the surface sense as "the least important or the least significant," holding that the moral and spiritual senses were "what the scriptures intended." [^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Origen treated problematic texts as non-literal.** Confronting the imprecatory [[Psalm 137.9|Psalm 137:9]] ("blessed are the ones who dash your babies against the rocks"), he refused the surface reading: the Babylonian babies are "your impure thoughts," to be dashed against the Rock (Christ). [^4][^5] See [[Psalm 137 Babylonian Babies as Allegory for Impure Thoughts]]. **(Well-attested; Origen's allegorical method is standard in patristics.)**
2. **He denied a literal six-day creation.** Origen held the six days "aren't literal 24-hour periods," noting (as a textual problem) that there was "evening and morning, day and night before the creation of the sun" in [[Genesis 1.5|Genesis 1]]. [^6] **(Strong — Origen's *De Principiis* explicitly questions the literal creation week.)**
3. **He tolerated gospel disagreement.** Origen "acknowledged, hey, you know, sometimes the gospels didn't agree with each other on certain things and you need to just relax and just chill." [^7] For Origen, surface discrepancies pointed *toward* a deeper spiritual meaning, not away from inspiration. **(Moderate as paraphrase; consistent with Origen's harmonization theory.)**
4. **Steelman of the literalist response.** A modern inerrantist regards Origen's allegory as exegetically undisciplined — license to make any text mean anything. McClellan's framing concedes the church later needed to constrain interpretation (hence the medieval fourfold scheme and the Reformation's turn to the text), but insists the early church's *default* was that "there is truth in the scriptures... but it might not be resting on the surface." [^8] The point is historical: flat literalism is the latecomer, not Origen. See [[Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention]]. **(Strong as a historical claim.)**

## Best Quotes

> "One of them was what he called the plain sense or the historical sense or the literal sense — what the words themselves seem to mean just on the surface." [^2]

> "He argued that the surface sense, the plain sense was like the least important or the least significant sense." [^3]

> "Origen was like, yeah, the whole six days of creation, those aren't literal 24-hour periods." [^6]
