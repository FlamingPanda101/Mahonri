# Irreformable Teachings Reformed — The Death Penalty Defeater

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]]'s fourth "decisive problem" with post-apostolic infallibility is that, once it arises, "it's overturned by the facts of later church history."[^1] His flagship case in cross-examination: **the death penalty.** Quoting [[Trent Horn]]'s co-author Ed Feser — "the traditional teaching on the legitimacy in principle of capital punishment clearly meets the criteria for being an irreformable part of the ordinary magisterium" — Ortlund notes the 2018 catechism revision under Pope Francis now calls the death penalty "**inadmissible**... an attack on the inviolability and dignity of the person." His burden: "why should I accept your church's magisterium as infallible when it so manifestly reverses a supposedly irreformable teaching?... What good is having a purportedly infallible magisterium if its irreformable teachings get reformed?"[^2] He runs a parallel case on **"no salvation outside the church"** — Florence's *ex cathedra*-grade bull (pagans, Jews, heretics, schismatics "cannot share eternal life... unless joined to the Catholic Church before death") versus Vatican II's inclusion of Jews and Muslims in the plan of salvation.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

**Steelman (Ortlund / Protestant):**
- **It is a falsification test the magisterium appears to fail.** An infallible, *irreformable* teaching that is later *reformed* is, on its face, a contradiction — and the death-penalty and Florence cases are the cleanest available specimens. This is the historical analogue of the [[A Fallible List of Infallible Teachings - The Symmetry of Uncertainty|fallible-list]] problem: not just *what is* infallible, but whether the allegedly infallible *changed*.
- **Other catalogued reversals.** Ortlund lists Nicaea II on icon veneration vs the earlier church, and the medieval reading of "no salvation outside the church" vs its later softening, as further instances "you don't need a PhD to see."[^1]
- **Authority that can reverse "irreformable" teaching cannot deliver the certainty it promises** — so the supposed Catholic advantage over sola scriptura's interpretive flux evaporates.

**Steelman (Horn / Catholic counter):**
- **A contradiction requires *two* infallible statements; neither here is infallible.** Horn's structural reply: "not every teaching act of the magisterium is infallible — the vast majority are not, and this is one of them." The 2018 revision is *not* an exercise of infallibility (Feser would agree), and "there are theologians who say [the death penalty] was never *infallibly defined*, it was just long practiced." You need the original teaching infallible *and* the revision infallible to disprove papal infallibility — and you have neither.[^4]
- **Development vs reversal.** The death penalty was permitted "to preserve the social order"; what changed is "a development in understanding how to apply it and when," not the principle itself — analogous to doctrinal development, not contradiction.
- **The Protestant Bible faces the identical dilemma.** Horn's tu quoque: an atheist asks Ortlund "is slavery evil? — then what about Leviticus permitting it?" If the Protestant can resolve the tension between a Bible that "commands the ability to pass on slaves perpetually" and "slavery is evil," then "the Catholic Church can do the same thing in our application of the death penalty." The "apparent contradiction" move Protestants use for Scripture is available to Catholics for tradition. See [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]].
- **Even if it worked, it does not prove sola scriptura.** Horn's repeated firewall: "even if you were correct that papal infallibility is false, there are other options — Orthodox — that would still not prove sola scriptura is true. It'd be like arguing whether you should be a Republican and I bring up a point about being a Democrat."[^5]

## LDS relevance
The defeater is **friendly to the LDS claim of continuing revelation and unfriendly to *both* debaters' fixed systems.** Where Horn must defend that no *irreformable* teaching truly changed, and Ortlund must defend a *closed* deposit, the Restoration frames the very phenomenon Ortlund documents — the church's teaching genuinely *developing and correcting over time* (death penalty, icons, the scope of salvation) — as evidence that God *still speaks* and *updates* through living prophets (cf. [[Seven Seals and the Age of the Earth Show Continuing Revelation]] and [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]). Notably, Ortlund's own closing praises a church "always reforming" (*semper reformanda*) as "the glory of Protestantism" — the LDS reply is that a *reforming* church needs *living revelatory authority* to reform *toward*, which neither sola scriptura nor a magisterium that forbids reversing itself can supply.
