# John 1:1 — "And the Word Was God" (Qualitative Theos)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[John 1.1|John 1:1c]] — "and the Word was God" — proves Jesus IS the God of Israel, identical in being to the Father.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Greek grammar is qualitative, not definite
In the third clause — *kai theos en ho logos* — the word *theos* (God) appears **without the definite article and before the verb**. In Greek copular sentences (X is Y), when the predicate noun precedes the verb and lacks the article, it is **qualitative** — it describes the *nature or quality* of the subject, not its *identity*.[^1]

If John meant to identify the Word as *the* God of Israel (the one mentioned in clause 2 with the article *ton theon*), it would read *ho theos en ho logos*. It does not, and "there is no manuscript that is thought to be anywhere near original that says that."[^2]

### 2. Even conservative scholars concede this
[[Daniel Wallace]], a prominent evangelical Greek grammarian, acknowledges *theos* here must be qualitative, not definite. He then argues this is still Trinitarian by asserting Jesus is "the second person of the Trinity" rather than encapsulating all that God is.[^3] But this rescue requires importing post-Nicene assumptions ([[Consubstantiality|consubstantiality]], persons vs. being) that the author of John did not possess.[^4]

### 3. The best translation: "the Word was deity" or "the Word was divine"
McClellan argues "the best translation would probably be the word was deity" — meaning Jesus possesses the qualities that define deity, not that Jesus IS the God of Israel.[^5] The New World Translation's "the Word was a god" is within the realm of plausibility, though McClellan does not endorse the NWT generally.

### 4. This is the academic consensus
McClellan calls this "the academic consensus of Greek grammarians" — the same grammarians Trinitarians appeal to for other points agree that John 1:1c is NOT identifying Jesus as the very God of Israel.[^6]

## Best Quote
> "What the author of the Gospel of John is saying is that Jesus was deity, Jesus was divine. They're trying to get close to saying that Jesus was God without saying Jesus was God, because in this time period Jesus had some kind of very special relationship to God that they hadn't really figured out how to articulate yet." [^7]

## Counter-arguments
- Trinitarians argue Wallace's path: qualitative *theos* still supports the Trinity when read through the lens of consubstantiality. Response: that lens didn't exist when John was written.
- Some argue Colwell's Rule (a definite predicate noun before the verb can drop the article) makes *theos* definite. Response: Colwell's Rule is disputed and even if valid, it allows definiteness — it doesn't require it; context determines.
