# The Bull of Jacob Shows Yahweh Was Depicted as a Bull

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues that ancient Israel widely regarded a **bull** as a legitimate image of their national God **Yahweh**. The case combines (a) the divine title ***Abir Yaakov*** — "the **Bull** of Jacob" — in [[Genesis 49.25|Genesis 49]] and elsewhere; (b) the **Kuntillet Ajrud** drawings of a bull-headed figure and a calf suckling; and (c) the biblical golden-calf / Jeroboam episodes, which make sense only if a bull-image was *recognized* as Yahweh, not a random foreign god. This places Yahweh as the virile **son** figure of the divine family (the Ugaritic son, Baal, has bull/storm imagery).

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Bull of Jacob/Israel" is a divine title (rating: moderate; lexical)
The KJV "mighty God of Jacob" (Gen 49:24) renders *Abir Yaakov* with "God" *italicized* (inserted); *Abir* "probably means the **bull**." The title also appears in Second Isaiah and the Psalms, and *Abir Israel* ("Bull of Israel") in Isaiah 1 — all referring to Yahweh, "the shepherd, the stone of Israel." [^1] See [[Genesis 49 Names Three Distinct Gods in Jacobs Blessing]].

### 2. Kuntillet Ajrud iconography (rating: moderate; archaeological, interpretation debated)
At **Kuntillet Ajrud**, *pithoi* (large storage-jar sherds) bear images that "seem to be pictures of Yahweh," both as a **bull-headed man** and as a **calf suckling from its mother's udder.** "There are a lot of representations of Yahweh as a bull, in the Old Testament and outside." [^2] Butler also flags possible bull-iconography on **Solomon's throne**: a word the Masoretes vocalized *ogol* ("rounded," 1 Kings 10) could be *egel*, "a **calf**," i.e. a bull's head over the throne-back. [^3]

### 3. The golden calf and Jeroboam presuppose a known bull-image of Yahweh (rating: strong as an argument-from-coherence)
When Aaron presents the calf saying "here is your God that brought you out of Egypt" ([[Exodus 32|Exodus 32]]), it is "difficult to imagine the Israelites would just go after some random god"; far more likely "they knew that a bull was an image of their God." Likewise **Jeroboam**, to stop pilgrimage to Jerusalem, set up **bull idols** in the north — which only works as competition if the bull was a *recognized* symbol of the same God worshipped at Jerusalem. [^4] This sharpens, rather than excuses, the sin: Moses invited everyone *up the mountain* to meet Yahweh, and they "settled for this lower form" — Yahweh-worship done wrong. [^5] Compare [[Golden Calf and Bronze Serpent as Idolatry Tests]].

### 4. Fits the divine-family son (rating: moderate; LDS application)
The Ugaritic son **Baal** ("the rider on the storm," martial champion) carries storm/bull associations; a bull-imaged Yahweh slots into the same family-of-gods grammar that Butler reconstructs from Ugarit ([[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]]), supporting a Father (El) – Mother (Asherah/Shaddai) – Son structure.

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Hebrew Bible's narrators present the golden calf and Jeroboam's bulls as **apostasy**, full stop — evidence that bull-worship was a Canaanite corruption Israel was meant to reject, not authentic Yahwism. *Abir* is traditionally rendered "Mighty One," a metaphor of strength (a bull connoting power) rather than a claim that Yahweh *was* a bull, and the Kuntillet Ajrud figures are disputed (some identify the bull-headed image with Bes, not Yahweh). On this reading the iconographic data reflect popular syncretism the prophets condemned. Butler's reply: the *Abir* title sits in canonical blessing-poetry, the calf/Jeroboam stories only have polemical force if the audience already linked the bull with Yahweh, and aniconism was a *later* development (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]).
