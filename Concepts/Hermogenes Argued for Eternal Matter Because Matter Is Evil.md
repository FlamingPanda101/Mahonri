# Hermogenes Argued for Eternal Matter Because Matter Is Evil

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Hermogenes]] — a Christian teacher writing around the end of the second century, *after* [[Tatian]] and [[Theophilus of Antioch]] had formulated *creatio ex nihilo* — defended the older view that **matter is eternal and uncreated**. His argument, preserved in [[Tertullian]]'s hostile *Adversus Hermogenem*, is a **theodicy**: since matter is the source of evil and a good God would not make anything evil, matter cannot be something God created; it must be eternal.[^1]

[[Blake Ostler]] highlights Hermogenes for two reasons. First, he shows *creatio ex materia* was still a living, argued Christian position at the very end of the second century — *ex nihilo* had not yet "won." Second, and more pointedly, Hermogenes reasoned from the **same Middle Platonic premises** as Theophilus yet reached the **opposite conclusion**, demonstrating that those premises do not entail *ex nihilo*; they merely frame a dilemma. See [[Theophilus Arguments for Creation Ex Nihilo]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The argument as Tertullian reports it.** "But we find evil things made by him, although not by choice or will. Because if they were made by his choice or will, he would have made something inconsistent or unworthy of himself. What he does not make by his choice, must be understood to be made by the fault of another thing: from matter without doubt."[^2] The logic: (a) evil exists in the world; (b) a good God does not will/make evil; (c) therefore the evil traces to an independent factor — pre-existing, uncreated matter.

2. **He shares the Platonists' premise that matter is evil.** By Hermogenes' time "all parties discussing the issue were working from Middle Platonic assumptions about God and matter."[^3] The premise "matter is evil/recalcitrant" is exactly the Middle Platonic premise [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]] also held — and which Theophilus exploited from the other direction.

3. **Same premises, opposite conclusion.** Theophilus kept the Platonic premises and made matter *created* (to protect God's sovereignty and uniqueness); Hermogenes kept them and made matter *eternal* (to protect God's goodness). This fork is Ostler's evidence that the late-second-century debate was an *intramural Platonic* dispute over how to handle matter, not a clash between revelation and philosophy.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (the *ex nihilo* side, steelmanned)

Tertullian's reply (which Ostler reports as winning the day) is that Hermogenes' eternal matter is fatal to monotheism: a substance "coequal with Himself" that God did not make would be a second uncreated principle, so God "could not be Lord of" it, and could not be "Almighty" if his power did not extend to producing all things from nothing.[^5] On this view Hermogenes solves the problem of evil only by surrendering divine sovereignty and omnipotence — too high a price — and the cleaner solution is to deny matter is intrinsically evil at all (the eventual orthodox move via Augustine: evil as privation, not substance).

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

The Latter-day Saint escape is to **reject the shared Platonic premise** that uncreated matter must be either evil or a rival to God. Ostler argues that on the LDS view "matter is entirely subject to God for any expression of its causal and lawlike properties" — neither inherently evil (so Hermogenes' theodicy premise drops) nor a competing sovereign (so Tertullian's omnipotence objection drops).[^6] Both Theophilus and Hermogenes lose their footing because both stand on Middle Platonic ground. The whole dilemma — create matter *ex nihilo* and own its evil, or leave it eternal and lose sovereignty — is an artifact of importing the Greek concept of matter, and dissolves once that concept is abandoned. This connects to the broader LDS critique that *ex nihilo* sharpens the problem of evil unbearably. See [[Problem of Hell and Creation]] and [[The Creation Dilemma]].

## Best Quotes

> "What he does not make by his choice, must be understood to be made by the fault of another thing: from matter without doubt." — Hermogenes, in Tertullian, *Adversus Hermogenem* 2.5, quoted by Ostler[^2]

> "He cannot say that it was as its Lord that God made use of matter for the work of creating the world, for He could not be Lord of a substance which was coequal with Himself." — Tertullian against Hermogenes, quoted by Ostler[^5]
