# Moroni-Calvin Parallel — The Self-Authentication Gambit

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Both [[John Calvin]]'s "feel it in your bones" canon test and [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]]'s "burning bosom" test ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]]) claim that a subjective interior confirmation establishes a scripture's divine origin. [[Trent Horn]] and [[Joe Heschmeyer]] argue these are "the same move, the same trick... the same gambit" — and that the gambit is manipulative and unbiblical [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Both make confirmation contingent on the seeker's worthiness — rate: strong
Moroni promises that if you ask "with a sincere heart, with real intent, having faith in Christ," God will manifest the truth; Calvin says the *elect* in whom the Spirit works will recognize Scripture. In both, the test only "works" for the chosen one. This creates "a really strong impetus as a seeker to say, oh yeah, that's got to be right. The emperor must have clothes, because if he doesn't, then I'm not one of the righteous ones" [^1]. **Steelman:** a believer may reply that grace genuinely does illumine the heart, and unbelief genuinely can flow from a disordered will — the experience need not be a "trick." The hosts grant the move "maybe that's too harsh," but the structural objection (below) stands regardless of motive.

### 2. It is unfalsifiable / circular — rate: strong
"How do you know that you're the elect? Well, scripture gives these signs and I fulfill them. So I'm among the elect, and so I know that this is scripture" — a circle. Doubt is reinterpreted as evidence of the *doubter's* defect, not of the claim's falsity, so the test can never fail. See [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture]] [^2].

### 3. It chills honest inquiry — rate: strong
In both systems, even *asking* "where did the Bible (or the Book of Mormon) come from?" "calls into question your good faith or your elect status," deterring deeper probing [^3].

### 4. Scripture itself rejects the move — rate: strong
"Scripture never makes this manipulative move. It doesn't say, if you're really one of the good ones, you'll know this is all true." On the contrary, [[Jeremiah]] warns "the heart is deceitful above all things" ([[Jeremiah 17.9|Jeremiah 17:9]]) — so a deep inner feeling "is not a terribly great predictor of X being true" [^4]. See [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]].
