# The Tree Sweetens the Bitter Waters So the River Is Both Love and Filth
#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that the puzzling double identity of the river/water in Lehi's and Nephi's tree-of-life vision — called "the love of God" in one place ([[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11:25]]) yet "the filthiness... the depths of hell" in another ([[1 Nephi 15|1 Nephi 15:27]]) — is resolved by the **Marah** episode in [[Exodus 15.23-25|Exodus 15]], where the Lord shows Moses a *tree* (Hebrew *ets*) that turns bitter water sweet. On this reading, the *same* water is sweet in the tree's presence and bitter in its absence: near the tree of life (the Asherah / divine Mother) the river is the love of God; cut off from the tree it becomes the filthy depths of hell. The water's moral valence is not a contradiction but a function of *proximity to the tree*.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Marah pattern (Exodus 15:23-25).** At Marah the waters were too bitter to drink; "the Lord shewed [Moses] a tree, which when he had cast into the waters, the waters were made sweet." The mechanism — a *tree* sweetening *bitter water* — is exactly the variable Butler imports to explain the river.
2. **Nephi's two labels are about presence vs. absence of the tree.** Nephi glosses the water as "the love of God" when describing those who reach and partake of the tree, but as "filthiness" and "the depths of hell" when describing those swept away from it. The single body of water reads opposite ways depending on whether the tree is present, just as Marah's water did.
3. **Coheres with the tree-as-Asherah reading.** This is a corollary of [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]] and of [[Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah]] (where Butler reads Isaiah 5:20's "bitter for sweet" as cultic vandalism = *removing the tree*): if the tree is the divine Mother and the source of "living water," then her presence sweetens and her removal embitters. The Exodus 15 tree thus links the Book of Mormon vision to a First-Temple complex of tree, water, and life.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A critic notes that the standard reading already explains the "contradiction" without any Asherah machinery: Nephi simply uses the river *symbolically two ways* — as the love of God for the faithful and as a polluted current of worldliness sweeping the wicked toward hell — a common prophetic flexibility, not a hidden Exodus 15 cipher. Lehi's account ([[1 Nephi 8|1 Nephi 8]]) calls the water "filthy" plainly; the "love of God" label in [[1 Nephi 11|1 Nephi 11]] attaches to the *tree*, not unambiguously to the river, so reading both onto one body of water may over-systematize the imagery. Butler himself frames these temple readings as hypotheses "deep in the shadows," not demonstrable exegesis. The reading's strength is its economy — one Exodus motif harmonizing two otherwise jarring labels — but it remains a devotional inference rather than a proven intertext.
