# Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Luke Hansen]] turns a Catholic admission into a **tu quoque**: if [[Trent Horn]] and [[Pope Benedict XVI]] say the early Old Testament "affirms henotheism" as a temporary stage of **divine pedagogy**, then Catholics already accept inspired texts containing immature views of God — so they can't disqualify [[Brigham Young]] for the same. [[Joe Heschmeyer]] rebuts that "henotheism" equivocates, and Isaiah 43 actually denies any equal gods.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Trent Horn's "affirm" (strong — Catholic primary, used against Catholics)
Hansen quotes Horn (on Benedict XVI and the "dark passages"): "the earliest parts of the Old Testament **affirm**... henotheism, the belief that there are many gods but only one God should be worshipped, Yahweh," citing **[[Psalm 97.9|Psalm 97:9]]** ("Thou art exalted above all other gods") [^1]. Hansen leans on the word *affirm*. Heschmeyer: "the Old Testament is infallible... we wouldn't think the Old Testament's making error on that" [^2] — so he must read Horn as *incompleteness*, not error.

### 2. "Depends what you mean by henotheism" (strong — Heschmeyer's disambiguation)
Heschmeyer's three-category framework for "other gods": (1) literally **non-existent** idols; (2) real but **subordinate** spiritual beings (demons, angels — citing **[[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Corinthians 8]]** and child sacrifice "to demons"); (3) real and **equal** to God. Categories 1 and 2 are biblical; category 3 is not [^3]. This is where his controversial "Mormon gods aren't incompatible with atheism" remark came from — disembodied powerful beings are a question of *materialism*, not *theism* [^4].

### 3. Isaiah 43 and the "boast" reading (moderate — contested exegesis)
Hansen invokes [[Michael Heiser]]: **[[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]]** ("I am, and there are no gods besides me") reuses a city-boast formula (Nineveh/Babylon: "I am, and there are none besides me") — so it's a "soft affirmation" of henotheism, not ontological denial [^5]. Heschmeyer answers that in Isaiah 43 God boasts **to Israel**, not to gods: "before me no God was formed nor shall there be any after me" denies any comparable being [^6]. See [[Isaiah 43-10 - No God Formed Before Me Rebutted]].

### Steelman / common ground
Both grant a "divine pedagogy" (slavery not abolished at once; **[[Hebrews 1.1-2|Hebrews 1]]** "in many and various ways"; the **[[Jonah 3.4|book of Jonah]]** weaning Israel off a local-deity God). The disagreement is whether Israelite henotheism was God *leading them up from* error (Heschmeyer: "despite, not because of, God's revelation") or scripture *affirming* it (Hansen) [^7].
