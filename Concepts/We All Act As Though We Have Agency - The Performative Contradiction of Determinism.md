# We All Act As Though We Have Agency — The Performative Contradiction of Determinism

## The Point
Even the determinist cannot *live* his determinism. In a striking concession, the critic admits: "we all act as though we have agency even if some of us understand that that agency is extremely limited or non-existent," and that "we use terms in ways that doesn't actually reflect what's really going on, but it's the best we have in the language that we use." [^1] This is a performative contradiction: the very act of asserting, persuading, deliberating, and recommending that we "deal with" the absence of agency presupposes agents who can weigh reasons and revise beliefs. If "you ultimately don't make choices," then the determinist did not *choose* his conclusion and his listener cannot *choose* to accept it — the argument is just one more caused event, not a rational insight with a claim on us. A position that must be *contradicted in the very act of stating it* is self-undermining; the universal, unshakeable practice of treating ourselves and others as agents is itself evidence that the agency-eliminating theory is false.

## Evidence
- **The lived datum.** "We all act as though we have agency even if some of us understand that that agency is extremely limited or non-existent." [^1]
- **Language betrays the theory.** "We use terms in ways that doesn't actually reflect what's really going on, but it's the best we have." [^1] The admission that our entire moral and rational vocabulary is built around agency — and is indispensable — is a heavy cost for the view that agency is illusory.
- **The argument is addressed to choosers.** The whole exchange urges the interlocutor to *reconsider*, *deconstruct*, and *deal with* reality — speech-acts that only make sense among agents who can do otherwise (cf. the same self-defeat in [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]).

## LDS Connection
The Restoration treats the universal experience of agency not as a useful fiction but as a report of reality: agency is foundational to personhood, "that every man may act in doctrine and principle... according to the moral agency which I have given unto him, that every man may be accountable for his own sins in the day of judgment" (D&C 101:78). Lehi grounds this in the structure of being itself — without genuine choice "there is no existence" of moral order; men must be "agents unto themselves" and "free to choose" (2 Nephi 2:27; cf. [[2 Nephi 2.8]]). The performative contradiction is exactly what we should expect if [[D&C 93|D&C 93:29–30]] is right that intelligence "act[s] for itself" — the determinist's inability to stop *behaving* as an agent is the truth leaking through the theory. Where materialism makes agency an illusion to be explained away ([[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]), the LDS view says we cannot escape acting as agents because we *are* agents; and since responsibility rides on that agency ([[Moral Responsibility Presupposes Genuine Agency]]), the lived contradiction is itself a quiet argument for moral accountability ([[Alma 41.3-7]]).
