# Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] offers a pastoral method for anyone reexamining their faith: **build the positive case first**, then test it against the critics. He pictures belief as a balance scale — "we can fill each side of the scale with reasons to doubt or reasons to believe" — and warns that if a person never seeks "the best reasons to believe," they "will not be ready to handle the doubts."[^1] Only after the positive case is genuinely understood should one "go ahead and examine what the critics have to say," approaching the test "from a position of strength" rather than from a half-built structure.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Order the inquiry: positive case before critique.** "When approaching this issue, we should start with the positive case. Start by looking for things that you can believe in. Start with God."[^3] Hansen lists the thinkers who filled his own scale — Aquinas, Pascal, Plantinga, Craig, Lewis, Chesterton, [[Jordan Peterson]] — and challenges the reader to be able to summarize their arguments before declaring belief untenable.[^3]

2. **The scale reframes the "shelf."** Doubts are real and remain on the scale, but "when we start with a positive case we approach from a position of strength," and the challenges "pale in comparison to the reasons for belief, especially when we place our spiritual experiences on the scale."[^4] Properly weighted, objections "become like puzzles that you've not quite sorted out rather than intellectual deal breakers."[^5]

3. **The earthquake must come — but second.** Hansen is emphatic that this is not hiding from critics: "Am I saying we should just ignore the critics? No, of course not."[^6] "Eventually our building must be exposed to an earthquake — but it would be crazy to do that without a fully developed foundation."[^2] This sequencing is the practical corollary of treating faith crisis as a [[Renovation Not Demolition - Faith Crisis as Rebuilding From the Foundation]].

4. **Knowing the strongest case requires genuine engagement.** The discipline assumes one has actually understood the best arguments on each side, not strawmen — the same charity captured in [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]]. Hansen's wager: a reader who has truly mastered the positive case will find even a strong critique like [[Jeremy Runnells]]'s CES Letter unable to "pull down your belief in God and Jesus."[^7]

5. **It tracks the level structure.** Because the positive case is built bottom-up (God → Jesus → Restoration), the method presupposes knowing "where you are" on the levels and meeting others where they are. See [[The Four-Level Conversation Structure of Apologetics]].

## Best Quotes
> "It's like we are all faced with a scale, and we can fill each side of the scale with reasons to doubt or reasons to believe. There are reasons to doubt. But if we don't seek deeply the best reasons to believe... then we will not be ready to handle the doubts." [^1]

> "Once we have really put in the legwork and understand the best arguments on the positive side, we can go ahead and examine what the critics have to say... But when we start with a positive case, we approach from a position of strength." [^2]

> "When you consider all the things in the positive case, suddenly the challenges to faith become much smaller. Yes, they're still there... but they are put into proper perspective. They become like puzzles that you've not quite sorted out rather than intellectual deal breakers." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The "scale" is a restoration-friendly model of testimony as a *preponderance* of converging witnesses rather than a single unbreakable proof — the spiritual confirmation promised in Moroni's promise is placed on the scale alongside rational evidence, not set against it. Starting with the positive case echoes Alma's counsel to first plant and nurture the seed and observe whether it is "good" before judging it (see [[Alma 32.27-43]]); the swelling of the seed is positive evidence weighed before the verdict. It also reframes "doubt your doubts before you doubt your faith" as a procedural rule about *order of operations*: secure the foundation, then let the earthquake test it.
