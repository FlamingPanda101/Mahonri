# The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line

## The Claim

Because *everybody* — ancient and modern — concedes the Bible contains poetry, metaphor, and allegory, the real interpretive question is never "literal vs. not." It is *where to set the threshold* between literal and figurative. "Really what we're looking for is where we're going to put the threshold between this is literal versus this is figurative." [^1] Layered on top is a second line: "what you have to believe... this you have to believe it this way." [^2] The threshold is not given by the text; it is chosen by the reader's tradition.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The threshold moves across history.** Origen/Augustine put it far toward the figurative (the surface sense was least important); the Reformers nudged it toward the text but still allowed metaphor, poetry, and figurative cosmology; modern fundamentalists shoved it hard toward the literal. [^3][^4] The very mobility of the line shows it is a human setting, not a textual datum. See [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]]. **(Strong.)**
2. **Genre cases prove the point.** The [[Song of Songs 4.5|Song of Songs]] is erotic poetry no one reads flatly; imprecatory psalms are not read as infanticide commands. Conceding these is conceding that the literal/figurative threshold is unavoidable. **(Strong.)**
3. **The Chicago Statement smuggles the threshold back in.** By defining "literal" to "take account of all figures of speech and literary forms," Article XV reintroduces the very line it claims to have removed. See [[Chicago Statement on Inerrancy Redefines Literal]]. **(Strong.)**
4. **No principled, text-internal stopping point.** Once the line is a choice, contradictions get resolved by demoting one text to "figurative" (see [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]]) and flat-earthers extend the literal further than the framers wanted (see [[Literalism Produces Flat-Earthers as Its Logical End]]). The threshold is set by dogma in both directions. **(Strong.)**
5. **Steelman.** A hermeneut would say the threshold is not arbitrary: authorial intent, genre conventions, and ancient Near Eastern context give principled grounds for classifying a passage as poetry, idiom, or report. McClellan would largely agree about *method* — historical-critical genre analysis is exactly his tool — but argue that inerrantists invoke "genre" selectively, as a rescue device triggered by doctrinal embarrassment rather than by consistent application of context. **(Fair — the disagreement is about consistency of application, not the legitimacy of genre judgments.)**
