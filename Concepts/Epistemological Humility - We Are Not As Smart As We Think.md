# Epistemological Humility — We Are Not As Smart As We Think

## The Point

The epistemic payoff of [[Jonathan Haidt]]'s interview, and its tie to the Thoughtful Faith Level 1 unit on epistemology. Haidt urges "moral humility" or "epistemological humility": the realization that "as individuals we're actually not all that smart on our own ... not as smart as we think we are." Individual reason is weak and biased (the [[The Rider and the Elephant Model of the Mind|rider]] is small); genuine knowledge and wisdom emerge from *systems* of people configured correctly — like neurons in a brain, or buyers and sellers in a market. [^1]

## Argument

1. **Individuals are not as smart as they think.** "As individuals we're actually not all that smart on our own — we're certainly not as smart as we think we are as individuals." Overconfidence in one's own reasoning is the default error. [^1]
2. **Genius is systemic, not individual.** "When you put us together in a system in the right way, amazing stuff happens ... we're all basically neurons, and neurons aren't really all that smart, and ... our genius comes from getting the system right." Knowledge is a property of well-structured collectives. [^1][^2]
3. **Markets as the model.** Libertarians "are the main people who understand the brilliance of a market when there's no central mind." Distributed systems can aggregate information and preferences no single mind could. [^2]
4. **Systems can fail.** The same logic diagnoses dysfunction: "the American system right now is just not working very well" — markets aggregate well, but the political system fails to aggregate preferences or information, worsened as "we're getting more emotionally polarized, we hate each other more," with rising anger and voting "against something." [^3]
5. **The practical prescription.** "A little more epistemological humility from all of us ... would make our democracy work a little better." Humility is offered as a civic and epistemic remedy, paired with the conversational practice of acknowledgement (see [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]]). [^4]
6. **Apologetic relevance.** For the Thoughtful Faith epistemology unit, the note cuts in (at least) two directions: it warns against overconfidence in one's private reasoning and validates trusting knowledge that emerges from a community/tradition rather than the lone individual; the LDS reader may map "getting the system right" onto a covenant community or prophetic-council model, while a critic could press that the same humility should chasten *any* group's certainty, including a church's. The note records Haidt's position neutrally; he does not himself draw a religious conclusion.

## Best Quotes

> "Ultimately ... you can call it moral humility or you can call it epistemological humility — it's the realization that as individuals we're actually not all that smart on our own; we're certainly not as smart as we think we are as individuals." [^1]

> "When you put us together in a system in the right way, amazing stuff happens ... we're all basically neurons, and neurons aren't really all that smart ... our genius comes from getting the system right." [^1]

> "A little more epistemological humility from all of us ... I think would make our democracy work a little better." [^4]
