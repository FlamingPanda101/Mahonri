# The Six-Child Analogy for the Creation Dilemma

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]]'s second intuition pump (chapter one of *The Creation Dilemma*), framed personally for [[Cardon Ellis]]: you and your spouse already have five children and have fulfilled the command to "multiply and replenish." You ask God whether to have a sixth. God appears and says: you may have the child, it is not sinful, you are under no obligation — but "I know the future, that child will knowingly reject my Son... and your child will go to hell. Permanent hell." Then He leaves. The question: **why would you ever have that kid?** Carroll argues that an all-good, all-loving agent would not — and God the Father is in exactly that position (all-powerful, unbound, foreknowing, creating from nothing, without the child's consent).[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The analogy isolates avoidable, foreknown, gratuitous damnation — rate: strong as a parallel.** Like the button (see [[The Button Thought Experiment for the Problem of Hell]]), it removes obligation ("you've already multiplied and replenished") and stake ("you lose nothing"), so the only remaining factor is the foreknown eternal-torment outcome.[^1] The parental frame is rhetorically sharper than the button because parenthood is already an act of love, making the refusal to create feel like the *more* loving choice.
2. **The "how bad is hell?" hinge — rate: this is where the analogy lives or dies.** Ellis immediately notes the analogy only works if hell is genuinely terrible: "if it's a slap on the wrist... have the kid, but if hell is... weeping and wailing, gnashing of teeth, torturous." Carroll agrees and flags **watering down hell** as one of the creedal "solves" he rejects.[^2] He further grants the "deprivation" model (hell as mere separation from God, not active torture) but argues hell is *still worse than heaven*, so the dilemma survives even on the gentlest creedal conception.[^3] See [[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]]. **Steelman of the theist:** a defender can argue (a) the value of a free, image-bearing person existing outweighs the risk, or (b) per *deprivation* + free-will models, the child *chooses* its end, so the parent who lovingly grants agency is not culpable — the analogy "smuggles in" a fixed ending the real parent cannot actually foresee with certainty. Carroll's reply: God *can* foresee it with certainty, which is exactly the disanalogy that makes God's case *worse*, not better, than the parent's.
3. **The "free will" deflection is anticipated — rate: handled.** When the panel says "but it's their choices," Carroll redirects to the parent's prior knowledge and avoidable choice (see [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]]). He sharpens it elsewhere with the lava-pool/wood-chipper analogy: you would *restrain* a friend headed for self-destruction, so "letting them" is not love (see [[Free Will Defense Fails - The Lava Pool Analogy]]).

## Best Quotes
> "You may choose to have another child. It will not be sinful... but if you do, I'm telling you, I know the future, that child will knowingly reject my son... and your child will go to hell. Permanent hell... And then he leaves. And you look at your spouse, and the question I have for you... is why would you ever have that kid?" [^1]

> "If hell wasn't a bad place... have the kid, you know, but if hell is what... they think this — weeping and wailing, gnashing of teeth, torturous place... that's one of the solves to the creation dilemma is they kind of water down hell." [^2]

> "Should you create a being when you don't have to, knowing that it will end up in eternal conscious torment? And my argument is that if you are all good and if you are all loving, that you cannot do that." [^4]
