# Each Generation's Power Over Nature Is Power Over Posterity

## The Point
Lewis sharpens his analysis by adding **Time** to the picture: those who write on social matters, he complains, "have not yet learned to imitate the physicists by always including Time among the dimensions."[^1] Once time is included, "Man's conquest of Nature" is revealed to be largely the power of *the present generation over future generations* — and, if that generation is also the one "most emancipated from tradition," its power reaches backward too, cancelling the influence of its predecessors.[^2] Each new technology of control (contraception used as selective breeding, propaganda, conditioning) lets one generation make its descendants "what one generation, for its own reasons, may choose to prefer."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward, drawing on William Brandt, explains the rhetorical architecture: Lewis "extends time forward" and then "extends it backward," so that the generation which finally conquers Nature stands as a tiny temporal slice exercising dominion over all who come before and after.[^2] Lewis's image is that of the Fates: humanity will have "taken the thread of life out of the hand of Clotho," the goddess who spun each mortal's thread.[^4] Ward notes John Finnis's complementary reading: what Lewis exposes is "what happens when procreation is taken out of marital intercourse; procreation becomes reproduction, a form of production entailing the radical maker-product, master-slave relationship of total domination."[^5] The same point is echoed in later bioethics: Leon Kass, cited by Ward, warns that "a society that allows cloning... has tacitly assented to the conversion of procreation into manufacture and to the treatment of children as purely the projects of our will."[^6] Crucially, the later generations have **no "concurring voice"**: they are "denied existence" or selectively bred without consent, made to order by progenitors they can never answer back to.[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Each new power won by man is a power over man as well... in reality... the power of earlier generations over later ones." (Lewis, as Ward presents the argument) [^2]

> "A society that allows cloning, whether it knows it or not, has tacitly assented to the conversion of procreation into manufacture and to the treatment of children as purely the projects of our will." — Leon Kass, quoted by Ward [^6]

## LDS Connection
The reduction of future persons to "projects of our will," bred "without their concurring voice," stands in sharp contrast to the Latter-day Saint understanding of the human family. Spirits are not raw material manufactured to a designer's specification but **children of Heavenly Parents** whose premortal agency was honored in the very plan of salvation (cf. Abraham 3:22-26; Moses 4:3). Where Lewis's Conditioners deny posterity any "voice," LDS theology holds that each soul comes already endowed with eternal identity and the capacity to choose. The doctrine that we are answerable to past *and* future generations also harmonizes with the Restoration's emphasis on sealing the hearts of the children to the fathers (Malachi 4:5-6; D&C 2) — a turning toward posterity in covenant love rather than in technological domination.
