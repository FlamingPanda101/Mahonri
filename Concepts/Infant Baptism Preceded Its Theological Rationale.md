## The Point
It is difficult to identify exactly when or why the practice of baptizing infants began. The historical record suggests the *practice* of infant baptism preceded any formal *theological rationale* for it: when Augustine articulated his doctrine of original sin (early fifth century), it was well after infant baptism had become widespread, and Augustine appealed to the existing practice to support his doctrine — not the other way around.

## Evidence
1. Christian sources of the first two centuries make no explicit mention of infant baptism; several second-century writers held that little children are innocent.
2. The earliest unmistakable reference comes from Tertullian (ca. AD 206), who is aware of the practice and opposes it, yet implies that Mark 10:14 ("Suffer the little children to come unto me") motivated Christians to baptize their infants.
3. Other scriptural motivations: the biblical precedent of circumcising male children eight days after birth (Genesis 17:12); John 3:5 (necessity of being born of water and Spirit); and Psalm 51:5 ("I was born guilty").
4. Irenaeus (AD 180) wrote that Christ "came to save all by means of himself — all ... who by him are born again to God — infants, children, adolescents, young people, and old people."
5. High infant mortality and the belief that baptism was required for the kingdom made urgent baptism understandable, especially for sickly infants.
6. Infant baptism was probably less common than adult baptism, since baptismal liturgies assume candidates who can act for themselves; parents spoke as proxies for infants.
