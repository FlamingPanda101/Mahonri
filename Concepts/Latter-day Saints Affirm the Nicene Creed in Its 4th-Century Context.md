# Latter-day Saints Affirm the Nicene Creed in Its 4th-Century Context

## The Claim
The guest argues that the standard LDS posture of treating *all* the creeds as the "abomination" of the Great Apostasy is intellectually sloppy. Properly distinguished, the **Nicene Creed of 325** contains nothing a Latter-day Saint must reject; the genuine LDS objections lie with the later *Athanasian* Creed, the *Westminster Confession*, and the *modern interpretation* of Nicene vocabulary — not the 325 text. The thesis: "theologically we disagree with nothing in it." [^1]

## The Rebuttal

This is an internal LDS recalibration rather than an attack on a critic, so the "rebuttal" here is the guest's positive case plus its weak points.

### 1. The line-by-line walk-through (the rhetorical core)
The guest reads the USCCB English text clause by clause, inviting his co-host to "stop me if I say anything you don't believe," and claims each line is acceptable once contextualized: "born of the Father before all ages" = preexistence; "God from God, light from light" = the Son derived from the Father; "homoousios" = same essence; "begotten not made" = a translational nuance; "he came down from heaven" = the condescension of God; the resurrection and ascension clauses = uncontroversial. [^2] Only three items get flagged — *consubstantial* (a translation quibble), the *Filioque* ("and the Son"), and "the Holy Spirit, the Lord" — and each is dissolved by appeal to original Greek or to a later interpolation. **Rating: rhetorically effective, evidentially mixed** — the move depends entirely on substituting subordinationist 4th-century readings for the creed's own intended catholic sense.

### 2. The Creed is dogmatically "soft" (moderate)
The guest claims "the Nicene Creed was specifically left ambiguous because there was so much diversity in thought of the fathers that were at that council," in contrast with the rigidly dogmatic Athanasian Creed and Westminster Confession. [^3] He grants that the Creed should *not* function as a "dogmatic litmus test" to damn outsiders, but insists that "as you can see, there is nothing in the Nicene Creed that us as Mormons disagree with." [^4] **Rating: partly historical** — Nicaea (325) was indeed less metaphysically elaborated than later confessions, but it was explicitly framed *against* Arius with anathemas, i.e. it was a litmus test by design.

### 3. Distinguishing the creeds (the load-bearing distinction)
"Do I think that we believe in the Athanasian Creed? Do I think we believe in the Westminster Confession? Absolutely not. ... We have no issue with the Nicene Creed." [^5] The later "Athanasian metaphysics" of divine simplicity and co-equality, he stresses, came "100 200 300 years later" and "had nothing to do with Athanasius," who "had been dead for 100 years." [^6]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing (orthodox) view
A Nicene Trinitarian would respond that the guest has *not* affirmed the Creed but a homonym of it. The fathers at Nicaea coined *homoousios* precisely to **exclude** the subordinationist and tritheistic readings the guest imports; "God from God, true God from true God" is anti-Arian language asserting the Son shares the *one* divine being, not a derived second-rank deity. To "affirm" the Creed while denying co-equality, divine simplicity, and numerical unity of essence is, on the orthodox view, to deny what every clause was written to secure. The guest concedes the scholars he cites (Branson, Bercot) "are not Mormon" and "would take issue with other beliefs that we had" [^7] — an admission that he is harvesting historical observations against their authors' own conclusions.
