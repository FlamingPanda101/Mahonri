# Isogesis - Reading Metaphysical Monotheism Back Into the Text

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A sophisticated Trinitarian move: grant that the original authors lacked the concepts, but argue that "with later understanding and reasoning and new thought categories we can go back" and see they were *trying* to convey a multi-personal single substance — "like a child expressing something it can't fully express." [[Blake Ostler]] calls this *isogesis* (eisegesis): reading later metaphysics back into texts that lack it. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Importing later categories is dishonest with the text (rating: strong; methodological)
"What you're trying to do is make sense of a text in light of your understanding by importing back into it your understanding. It's called isogesis. It's an irresponsible, dishonest way to deal with the text." The text "stands and says what it says within the context in which it was written." [^2]

> "the text stands and says what it says within the context in which it was written... importing back into it your version of scriptural inerrancy is an illicit move." [^2]

### 2. Scriptural uniformity is the hidden premise (rating: strong; internal critique)
The apologist "needs a single view" because of "the inerrancy and plenary/verbal inspiration of the Bible," so they force "a consistent point of view among all writers." But "there's a diversity of points of view." Assuming uniformity to read the Trinity in is illicit. See [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]]. [^3]

### 3. The "let us create" data resists it (rating: moderate; textual)
Genesis 1's "let us make" addresses a divine council, not a multi-personal monad; "they want to drag in all these assumptions, use a term monotheism that isn't a biblical term, and then assume when they read these texts that their use implies all of these things." See [[Genesis 1.24-26|Genesis 1:24-26]]. [^4]

### 4. Two legitimate "language games" — keep them distinct (rating: strong; meta-method)
Ostler distinguishes (a) *scholarly* reading (honest with the text on its own terms) from (b) appeal to *further revelation* (which can disclose more, as Christians read Christ into Isaiah's Immanuel). He is willing to accept the Trinity *if* it is offered as revelation — "it would be foolish for me not to listen" to God. But the Trinity "is never expressed in a fuller revelation. There is no text that says the three divine persons are one substance. That's a scholarly creation." See [[Trinity Is a Scholarly Creation Not a Revealed Doctrine]]. [^5]

> "It was a fuller revelation, not a fuller council of scholars getting together to cogitate about it." [^6]

### 5. "I can see it's there" is not an argument (rating: moderate; rhetorical)
"Traditional Christians don't claim any further revelation... They're just telling you, 'I'm so smart, I figured it all out.'" When the contradictions are pointed out, the reply "Oh, but I can see that it's there" merely imposes "your own worldview onto a text that doesn't have that worldview." [^7]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian replies this is *not* eisegesis but *theological development*: the Church reads the whole canon synthetically under the Spirit's guidance (the regula fidei), recognizing that earlier texts are fulfilled and clarified by later revelation and by Christ himself — exactly the move LDS readers make in seeing Restoration doctrine latent in scripture. They argue Ostler's sharp "text on its own terms" vs. "revelation" dichotomy is itself a modern, historicist hermeneutic. Ostler's counter: development is fine *if* grounded in revelation, but the consubstantial Trinity is grounded in *philosophy* (Platonism) and *conciliar reasoning*, not in any revealed text—so it fails the very standard the developer claims. See [[All Doctrine Requires Interpretation Rebuttal]] and [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].

## Best Quotes

> "It's called isogesis. It's an irresponsible, dishonest way to deal with the text." [^2]

> "if you want to deal with it in a scholarly manner, let's do that language game... If what you want to say is, I've had revelation... then let's go with that." [^5]

> "you're just imposing your own worldview onto a text that doesn't have that worldview." [^7]
