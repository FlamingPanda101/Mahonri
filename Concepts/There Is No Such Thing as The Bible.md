# There Is No Such Thing as The Bible

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the definite article in "*the* Bible" is illusory: "there is no such thing as *the* Bible. There are Bibles."[^1] Different groups can each point to a collection of texts that legitimately functions as a Bible, and **no group can authoritatively, objectively, or universally declare its own collection to be *the* Bible** and the others wrong. When you call something "my Bible," you are only speaking for yourself or your group — you have either participated in or accepted others' answers to three undecidable questions (canon, manuscripts, translation).[^2] McClellan notes the irony that he opens every social-media video as "a scholar of *the* Bible and religion" — the phrase is a colloquial convenience that dissolves under scrutiny.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The phenomenon is descriptive, not provocative — rate: strong
This is not a fringe rhetorical stunt but a restatement of an uncontroversial fact in textual and canon scholarship: there is no single object "the Bible." There are canons that differ (Samaritan, Jewish, Protestant, Catholic, Orthodox, Ethiopian Tewahedo), textual traditions that differ (Masoretic, Septuagint, Dead Sea Scrolls, Textus Receptus, critical text), and thousands of translations. Each is a curated artifact. **Steelman of the believer:** a confessional Christian can reply that "the Bible" denotes the *divinely intended* canon-text-meaning even if humans dispute its edges — the disagreement is epistemic (we argue about which one) not ontological (there still is a true one). McClellan's reply is that no one can adjudicate that intended object without circularly privileging their own tradition; see [[The Canon Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

### 2. "When you speak for your group, you can say 'this is our Bible'" — rate: strong
McClellan concedes the legitimate move: "Now, when you're speaking for your group, yeah, you can say, this is what we like... This is *our* Bible. It's not *the* Bible."[^2] The error is universalizing a group preference into an objective fact. This parallels the vault's broader critique of presupposition-smuggling in apologetics (see [[Putting Dogma Over Data]]).

### 3. Even original-language readers consume a curated text — rate: strong
Even Greek Orthodox believers who speak Greek read the Septuagint in *translation* (Demotic or Katharevousa), and even Hebrew/Greek readers must choose a manuscript base.[^4] The "just read the original" escape hatch does not recover a single objective Bible; it merely relocates the same three choices. See [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

## Best Quotes
> "There is no such thing as *the* Bible... There are Bibles, and when you say this is my Bible, that's because either you have participated in or you have just accepted the decisions of others."[^2]

> "None of us can say, 'That's the wrong Bible' — as definitively, authoritatively."[^5]

> "There's no such thing as the Bible. Hi everybody, I'm Dan McClellan. I'm a scholar of the Bible and religion."[^3]
