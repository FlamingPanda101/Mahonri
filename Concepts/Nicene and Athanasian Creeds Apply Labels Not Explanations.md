# Nicene and Athanasian Creeds Apply Labels Not Explanations

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Trinitarian objection is that the Nicene (325) and Athanasian creeds faithfully clarify the New Testament's teaching about God — "three persons, one substance." Butler and Day rebut: the creeds do not *explain* anything; they apply a borrowed philosophical vocabulary to the New Testament and, when pressed, retreat to "it's a mystery," which is not an explanation but an admission of unintelligibility.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The creed names a problem without solving it (rating: strong; logical)
Butler: "Since you then just say 'I can't explain it,' you haven't actually explained anything. You've just applied the language to it... saying we have the same *ousia* doesn't actually mean anything." Calling [[John 10.30|"I and the Father are one"]] "same substance, different persons" relabels the data without resolving how Jesus has a body and the Father does not yet they are "the same being." [^1]

### 2. The Athanasian Creed reads like a committee statement (rating: moderate; rhetorical)
After Day reads the Athanasian Creed's cascade ("the Father uncreated, the Son uncreated... and yet not three uncreateds but one"), Butler quips it "reads like a corporate mission statement written by a committee... how do we say something that sounds profound but actually says nothing." Day grants it is incomprehensible unless you know it is simply hammering at [[Arius]]. [^2]

### 3. It contradicts itself on modalism (rating: moderate; internal tension)
The councils explicitly *reject* modalism (one person in three modes) — because that would mean the Father suffered on the cross and Jesus prayed to himself — yet the creed's "three are one" language pushes toward exactly the "hey Dad, it's me, you" picture the councils condemn. Day shows the resulting LDS memes line up with the creed's words while the councils disown them. [^3]

### 4. McConkie's verdict (rating: moderate; LDS authority)
[[Joseph McConkie]]: the creed "is not found in any gospel. Neither is the Athanasian. It derives from no utterance of Christ nor from the words of any of his apostles. It directly contradicts the plain language of the New Testament and cannot be expressed in scriptural language... Its best defense is the admission that it is a mystery and as such is indefensible." [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The orthodox reply: "mystery" is not obscurantism but appropriate epistemic humility before an infinite God — the creeds set the *boundaries* of orthodox speech (Jesus is fully God, fully man, not a creature, not the Father) rather than claiming to comprehend the divine essence. The Greek vocabulary (*homoousios*, *hypostasis*) was the precision tool needed to exclude real errors (Arianism, modalism), and [[John 17.20-23|John 17]]'s "that they may be one" shows the unity language is Jesus's own. Butler grants the creeds were "providing a vocabulary so people who want to appear educated can join the church," but insists a vocabulary that terminates in "mystery" explains nothing.
