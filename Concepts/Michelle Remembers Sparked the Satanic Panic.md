# Michelle Remembers Sparked the Satanic Panic

## The Claim
The 1980 book *Michelle Remembers*, by psychiatrist [[Lawrence Pazder]] and his patient (later wife) [[Michelle Smith]], single-handedly launched the satanic panic. It popularized "recovered memory therapy" and broadcast lurid satanic-ritual-abuse claims that turned out to be entirely uncorroborated — yet none of the debunking facts were known at the time, so the public believed it.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The book's claims (strong; documented contents)
Via recovered memory therapy, Smith "remembered" being locked in cages, sexually assaulted, forced into rituals, witnessing human sacrifices, being smeared with infant blood and dismembered body parts, an 81-day torture session of screaming, fires, and blood, and encounters with supernatural beings [^1].

### 2. No corroboration whatsoever (strong; the core rebuttal)
"No evidence has ever been found to corroborate Smith's memories." An 81-day ritual left "no trace, no sights, no sounds, not even a suspicious buildup of unopened mail"; no neighbor heard a graveyard ritual; during the 8 alleged captive years Smith was proven enrolled in and attending school; no one was reported missing [^2][^3]. Pazder first blamed the **Church of Satan** but withdrew the accusation when they threatened to sue for defamation [^2].

### 3. The conveniently unfalsifiable target (strong)
Smith "carefully managed to only implicate her mother by name" — a mother who had died before publication — and the book omits her sisters who should have been present, any police investigation, or documentation of an alleged car crash [^2][^3]. "But none of these facts were known at the time" [^4].

### 4. Media amplification (strong)
The book hit just as the talk-show era erupted; Smith and Pazder appeared on Oprah, Donahue, and Geraldo Rivera, whose devil-worship special reached **20 million homes** [^5]. See [[The Satanic Panic Was Driven by Financial and Reputational Incentives]].

### Steelman
A defender might say trauma victims often cannot corroborate childhood abuse and that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. The rebuttal: an 81-day public ritual and years of "captivity" make *specific, checkable* predictions (missing persons, mail, school absence) that all failed — this is not ordinary unverifiable trauma but actively falsified claims.

## Best Quotes
> "Despite all of the lurid details in the book *Michelle Remembers*, no evidence has ever been found to corroborate Smith's memories." [^2]

> "A ritual of fully 81 days left no trace, no sights, no sounds, not even a suspicious buildup of unopened mail." [^3]

> "In the end, Smith and Pazder divorced their spouses and married each other, pocketing millions of dollars in royalties and book deals without ever proving one case of abuse." [^6]
