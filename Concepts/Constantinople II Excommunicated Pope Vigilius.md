# Constantinople II Excommunicated Pope Vigilius

## The Claim

[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that papal *supremacy* as defined at Vatican I is incompatible with how the early church's ecumenical councils actually behaved — and the Second Council of Constantinople (553), the Fifth Ecumenical Council, is his "granular" test case. Two layers:

**The general pattern.** *Lumen Gentium* (Vatican II) makes it "the prerogative of the Roman pontiff to convoke these councils, to preside over them, and to confirm them." Yet "none of the ecumenical councils in the early church were ever convoked or presided over by a Roman bishop. In every case, it was the emperor." At Constantinople I the Roman bishop was absent and sent no legates. So the Catholic must hold that the pope possessed this prerogative but "simply never exercised it for the first seven ecumenical councils" — even when Arianism was "taking over the church." Ortlund: "What's the point of the pope having such a prerogative only to never use it?" [^1]

**The decisive episode.** This is "not just an argument from silence" because the councils *positively* assert authority over the pope. Emperor Justinian I sought condemnation of the Three Chapters (three persons and their writings) to reconcile non-Chalcedonians. [[Pope Vigilius]] resisted, issuing a solemn **First Constitutum** forbidding anyone "with ecclesiastical dignity and rank" to condemn the Three Chapters "contrary to what we have declared." Per the Catholic scholar Richard Price, Vigilius "could not have stated more unambiguously that his decree was final and left no room for discussion." [^2]

The council condemned the Three Chapters anyway, and disciplined Vigilius: they removed his name from the diptychs (read aloud in the liturgy), declaring it "alien to Christians" — "functionally excommunicating him in his person, though not in his office." Their stated rationale invoked Matthew 18:20 ("where two or three are gathered"): "no individual therefore could forestall the decision of the universal church." Ortlund's summary: "Conciliar authority trumps papal authority. That's their explicit argumentation." Vigilius then capitulated, issuing a **Second Constitutum** that condemned the Three Chapters — reversing himself. Price marvels that "two of the lengthiest contributions arguing for diametrically opposed positions are written by the same person," each "claiming to give the final and definitive ruling." [^3]

Ortlund grants one escape for *infallibility* — Vigilius was under "major duress," so an infallibilist can quarantine the episode. But *supremacy* cannot be salvaged: "it's very difficult to see how you can affirm papal supremacy when the early church is functioning like this... the other bishops, when they're gathered in council, regard themselves to have authority over and apart from the Pope." [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Catholic)

The Catholic case that 553 is survivable — even confirmatory — is real:

1. **The council's decrees became valid only when the pope confirmed them.** Vigilius's Second Constitutum *did* ratify the condemnation, and Constantinople II is reckoned ecumenical *because* the Roman see ultimately received it. On the Catholic reading this shows the system working: a council's definitions attain universal binding force through papal confirmation, exactly as *Lumen Gentium* describes. As one Catholic historian (quoted by Ortlund) notes, unlike the rejected Second Council of Ephesus ("Robber Synod"), Constantinople II "over time... was accepted and even recognized as valid by the Pope."
2. **Vigilius was coerced.** He was physically in Justinian's Constantinople under imperial pressure; his vacillation reflects duress, not the free, deliberate exercise of the *Petrine* office. The infallibility charism attaches to *ex cathedra* definitions of faith and morals issued freely — not to a disciplinary spat over the orthodoxy of long-dead authors conducted under threat.
3. **The Three Chapters quarrel was disciplinary/factual, not a definition of dogma.** Condemning particular writings to placate the miaphysites is a matter of church governance and historical judgment; it is not the kind of solemn doctrinal definition Vatican I's infallibility covers, so a papal reversal here embarrasses no defined dogma.
4. **Emperors convoked councils as a contingency of Christendom.** In a church fused to the Roman state, only the emperor commanded the logistics to summon the world's bishops; imperial convocation reflects civil power, not a denial that doctrinal *confirmation* belonged to Rome. Papal legates were present at and shaped several "imperial" councils (e.g. Chalcedon's reception of Leo's Tome).

The unresolved core: Ortlund's reply is that the bishops did not merely *act* without the pope — they *theorized* their authority as superior to his, in writing, at an ecumenical council, and brought a sitting pope under discipline. Subsequent papal "confirmation" of a verdict the pope had first solemnly forbidden, then accepted under pressure, looks (to Ortlund) less like supremacy vindicated than like supremacy retrofitted onto the record. The Catholic holds that final reception by Rome is what counts; Ortlund holds that the *episode itself* displays a conciliarism Vatican I cannot accommodate.

## LDS Relevance

For Latter-day Saints the Vigilius affair is doubly useful but also double-edged. It independently corroborates the LDS thesis that no divinely-protected supreme office governed the post-apostolic church — a council excommunicating a pope, and a pope contradicting himself "for all time" twice, is hard to square with either Roman primacy *or* an unbroken line of authoritative successors. It thus reinforces [[Roman Primacy Was a Later Claim Not First-Century]] and the apostasy narrative. But the same evidence cuts against any model (LDS included) that locates indefectible *priesthood* authority in the sixth-century episcopate: if the councils were already this disordered, the LDS would say it illustrates authority *already departed* (see [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]]), whereas Ortlund would say it simply shows the office was never the divinely-instituted thing Rome claims. Both reject Rome; they disagree on whether anything authoritative was *there to lose*.
