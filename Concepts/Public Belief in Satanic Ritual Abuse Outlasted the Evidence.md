# Public Belief in Satanic Ritual Abuse Outlasted the Evidence

## The Claim
Even as investigators concluded the ritual-abuse cases were baseless, public belief in satanic ritual abuse peaked — a gap between evidence and conviction that let the panic persist for decades.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Belief at its zenith in 1992 (strong — polling)
A 1992 Deseret News poll found 90% of Utahns thought satanic ritual abuse of children was real, "even the sacrifice of babies" — at the very moment investigators had concluded otherwise [^1].

### 2. Sobering-up lagged the public (moderate)
The video notes "it would be years before the sobering up would reach the public," even though law enforcement and prosecutors had privately recognized the cases' collapse much earlier [^2].

### 3. A 40-year echo (moderate — framing)
The panic "would echo and last for 40 more years," with the leaked Glenn L. Pace memo becoming a lasting fixture of the Utah religious landscape [^3].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
One might argue that widespread belief reflected genuine public concern for children rather than irrationality, and that skepticism can itself become a fashion that dismisses real victims. But the specific belief polled — organized satanic abuse including infant sacrifice — was precisely what the funded crime unit failed to substantiate in ~99% of cases, making the 90% figure a measure of hysteria's durability, not of hidden crime.
