# A Historian's Definition of Doctrine vs the Believer's

## The Claim
Historian [[Benjamin Park]] argues that the perennial question — "was Adam-God ever *doctrine*?" — gets opposite answers depending on **which definition of "doctrine" you use**, and that both answers can be defensible at once. The *historian's* definition: doctrine is whatever is "officially taught, promulgated... captured in periodicals and perhaps even captured in the temple." By that yardstick "it's rather clear that Adam-God doctrine became an official teaching even if it was disputed by some within the faith." The *believer's* definition adds a durability test: a doctrine is "not just something that's officially taught... but something that lasts over time" — and because Adam-God did not last, the believer can say it was never truly the church's doctrine. Park, "trying to capture the believer's perspective," grants both, then offers the precise historian's verdict: Adam-God "was never fully canonized," though "the concept of canonization is a bit more complicated and layered and porous than what is typically depicted" [^1].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The historian's bar is met by the public record (strong — descriptive).** Adam-God was taught repeatedly, printed in periodicals "especially in Europe," and integrated into the St. George temple endowment via the [[Lecture at the Veil Required Adam-God|lecture at the veil]]. On the "officially taught and promulgated" definition, that record settles the matter: it *was* official teaching for a period. Park insists this is true "even if it was disputed by some within the faith" — internal dissent does not retroactively make a promulgated teaching non-doctrine for the historian [^2].

2. **The believer's durability test is principled, not ad hoc (moderate — the steelman of faith).** The faithful claim is that doctrine, properly speaking, is what "lasts over time"; transient teaching that the church later sets aside was never doctrine in the binding sense. Park does not treat this as special pleading — he frames it as a genuinely different, internally coherent definition. This is the same instinct behind the modern LDS appeal to a "fallible prophet" who can teach error privately; see the canonization-process version in [[Was Adam-God Official Binding Teaching]] [^3].

3. **"Canonization" itself is porous (supporting — the historian's caveat).** Park's verdict that Adam-God "was never fully canonized" comes with a warning against treating canonization as a clean binary. Standard works, temple text, periodicals, and prophetic sermons occupy overlapping tiers of authority, so "doctrine" is layered rather than on/off. This cuts both ways: it blocks the critic's "it was canon" overclaim *and* the apologist's "it was never anything" underclaim [^4].

### Steelman of the critic
A critic can press that the two-definitions move risks letting the church "have it both ways": when convenient, point to durability to deny Adam-God was ever doctrine; when defending continuity (the [[The Church Kept Most of Adam-God Rejecting Only Specific Tenets|kept/rejected table]]), point to the promulgated record to claim most of it survived. Park's own framing answers this only by candor — he concedes the historian's bar *was* met, which is why he says the honest faithful position is not "Young never taught it" (a denial later leaders made) but "Young taught it and it did not last." The instability he documents — Young "muddled," with "wiggle room" by 1857 — is itself evidence that even the promulgating prophet did not hold it with canonical fixity.

## Best Quotes
> "From a historian, doctrine is something that's officially taught, promulgated... captured in periodicals and perhaps even captured in the temple, which is certainly the case in Adam-God doctrine." — Benjamin Park [^2]

> "From a believer's perspective... a doctrine is not just something that's officially taught and promulgated and accepted, but something that lasts over time. And so they would say that because Adam-God doctrine did not last, it was not official doctrine." — Benjamin Park [^1]

> "It is fair to say that the Adam-God teaching was never fully canonized. Though again the concept of canonization is a bit more complicated and layered and porous than what is typically depicted." — Benjamin Park [^4]
