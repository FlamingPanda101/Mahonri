# Fawn Brodie Popularized the View of the Hebrews Theory

## The Claim
The *View of the Hebrews* plagiarism theory is a relatively modern criticism whose popularity traces almost entirely to [[Fawn Brodie]]'s 1945 biography of Joseph Smith, *No Man Knows My History*. Earlier seeds (a 1902 debate claim; environmentalist writers like Isaac W. Riley and Edward Meyer) existed, but Brodie "memorialized" the theory and made it "a staple argument" repeated by later critics down to the [[CES Letter]].[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A traceable genealogy of the charge (rated: strong, documented).** The first direct accusation comes in 1902, when a Campbellite (Disciples of Christ) debater told an RLDS elder that Joseph "must have plagiarized" from *View of the Hebrews* and similar books.[^2] A bizarre 1887 account from an unnamed grandson of Ethan Smith merged the [[Solomon Spalding]] theory with Ethan Smith but is "not a very credible report."[^3]
2. **Brodie is the hinge (rated: strong, documented).** Brodie devoted "two or three pages" to *View of the Hebrews* and wrote that "although a direct dependence cannot be proven, the striking parallels cannot be a coincidence." After 1945 "anti-Mormon publications ... all just like are citing Brodie."[^4] Notably she "curb-stomps the Spalding theory" as incredible — and *replaces* it with *View of the Hebrews*.
3. **Modern critics piggyback on Brodie (rated: moderate, inferential).** Today's TikTok/YouTube arguments are "most of the time ... just piggybacking off of Fawn Brodie."[^5] *Steelman:* Brodie was a serious, research-driven biographer (and a former Latter-day Saint who knew the milieu), so "popularizer" should not imply "fabricator" — she may have surfaced a real signal. Smoot's reply is that even Brodie conceded "direct dependence cannot be proven," and the parallels she leaned on dissolve under analysis (see [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]]).

## Best Quotes

> "As with most things in this world, our problems start with Fawn Brodie because she like popularized this idea."[^4]

> "She says things like, 'Although a direct dependence cannot be proven, the striking parallels cannot be a coincidence.'"[^4]

> "She kind of memorializes it, it kind of becomes a staple argument now for origins of the Book of Mormon ... most of the time they're just like piggybacking off of Fawn Brodie."[^5]
