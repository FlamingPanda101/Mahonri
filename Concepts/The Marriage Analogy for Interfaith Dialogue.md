# The Marriage Analogy for Interfaith Dialogue

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] illustrates the right balance of interfaith engagement with a courtship analogy: a couple who only ever discuss what they *disagree* on will break up early, but a couple who only ever discuss what they *agree* on will marry a stranger and rupture at the first real disagreement. Healthy dialogue, like a healthy relationship, requires both. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Grounded in the greatest commandment.** Beshears names the book's central call as loving God (from the Shema, [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deuteronomy 6:4]]) and neighbor — and "to love my neighbor as myself means I have to understand who my neighbor really is," not "a caricature you have in your head." [^1] *Rating: strong;* it roots the method in scripture, not mere etiquette (see [[Love of Neighbor Requires Understanding]]).

2. **The disagreement-only pitfall.** A couple whose every date is an argument "call it quits and walk away... pretty early"; constant "here's why you're not Christian" conversation will "fizzle out." [^2] *Rating: strong analogy* for relentless polemic.

3. **The twin agreement-only pitfall.** A couple who only ever say "you too? I love that" rush to marry, then discover real differences (he uses the toilet-paper-direction joke) and rupture — "only agreement... you've created a relationship with a person that you don't actually know." [^3] *Rating: strong;* it diagnoses syncretism as a *relational* failure, not just a doctrinal one — it "cheapens it."

4. **Both errors prevent real knowledge.** "Only disagreement prevented you from developing relationship and only agreement... you've created a relationship with a person that you don't actually know." [^3] *Rating: strong;* the analogy delivers Beshears's "hold the tension" thesis in memorable form (see [[Holding the Tension Between Anti-Mormonism and Syncretism]]).

**Steelman of the disagreement-first apologist.** One could argue that with eternal truth at stake, soft-pedaling disagreement to "build relationship" risks false comfort, and that the marriage analogy could license endless rapport-building that never confronts error. Beshears guards this flank by insisting *both* must happen — he explicitly rejects "casseroles for each other," kumbaya, and forgetting differences as "equally displeasing." The analogy is for balance, not avoidance.

## Best Quotes
> "For me to love my neighbor as myself means I have to understand who my neighbor really is in order to love them. Are you really loving somebody if it's a caricature you have in your head?" [^1]

> "Imagine that couple sitting down and the only thing they ever talk about is 'you too? I love that.'... they rush and they get married and then comes the first day they discover they actually prefer the toilet paper to be switched in the opposite direction." [^3]

> "Only disagreement prevented you from developing relationship and only agreement... you've created a relationship with a person that you don't actually know." [^3]
