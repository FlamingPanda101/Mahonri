# Abolition Foresaw the Post-Truth World

## The Point
Ward presents *Abolition*'s most striking legacy as its prophetic diagnosis of a culture that has abandoned objective value. Following Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (1981) — which makes "in its critique of emotivism… identical" a case to Lewis's — Ward shows how, once belief in objective value evaporates, moral disagreements become **incommensurable**: "there is no common yardstick against which diverse perspectives can be reckoned." [^1] The result is a "post-truth world" in which "the question of truth, as such, is relegated to a second- or third-order issue: the real question is one of power." [^2] "Post-truth" was the Oxford Dictionaries' Word of the Year in 2016, defined by the very head/heart split Lewis attacked. [^3]

## Evidence
Lewis judged the modern rupture "the greatest of all divisions in the history of the West," running "somewhere between us and *Persuasion*" — the Austen novel whose title names what we have lost, since to persuade "presupposes a common medium of discourse in which both judgements and desires can be shared." [^4] Without it, MacIntyre observes, "protest becomes a distinctive moral feature of the modern age and… indignation a predominant modern emotion," because "the facts of incommensurability ensure that protestors can never win an argument" and "can never lose an argument either" — so protest "is characteristically addressed to those who already share the protestors' premises." [^5] As objective value drains away and "the public square is evacuated of practical reason," moral discourse "increasingly resembles a war zone" of propaganda and "animal instincts," where change comes only by mobilizing "a sufficiently large contingent of like-minded protestors." [^2] The post-truth definition itself — "circumstances in which objective facts are less influential… than appeals to emotion and personal belief" — perpetuates the split "between the head with its facts and the belly with its emotions," and "for as long as [they] remain disconnected… the relevance of *The Abolition of Man* will continue." [^3] Ward notes the same diagnosis appears, almost contemporaneously, in Adorno and Horkheimer's *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1944), which one reader said *Abolition* anticipated "in a shorter, soberer and less dialectical way." [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The question of truth, as such, is relegated to a second- or third-order issue: the real question is one of power. And thus we arrive at our post-truth world." [^2]

> "For as long as the head with its facts and the belly with its emotions remain disconnected from each other, even opposed to each other, the relevance of The Abolition of Man will continue." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's warning that a culture without shared objective value collapses into a contest of raw power — and ultimately threatens to remake (and so abolish) human nature itself — sharpens the modern relevance of a high view of the human person. Where transhumanist and reductive projects would treat humanity as raw material to be re-engineered, Latter-day Saint doctrine insists on humanity's eternal, divine nature and destiny: men and women are children of God, agents "to act for themselves and not to be acted upon" (2 Nephi 2:26), endowed with a real moral sense that no conditioning regime may legitimately overwrite. The recovery of objective moral law is, on this view, the recovery of what it means to be human — and the safeguard against being "abolished."
