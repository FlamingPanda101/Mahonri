# Inattentional Blindness Shows We Are Blind to the Unattended

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] argues that perception is "narrowed… far more than anybody ever guessed." Using [[Daniel Simons]]'s "monkey business / invisible gorilla" experiment, he shows that people absorbed in a task fail to see large, obvious, unexpected events in plain sight. The naïve theory — that anomalies automatically capture attention — is empirically false, which has direct epistemic consequences: we cannot assume we perceive even striking features of a scene we are looking straight at. [^1][^2]

## Argument
1. Sensory hardware already narrows us: vision registers only "a very small little slice of the whole electromagnetic spectrum," and we lack senses other animals have (e.g., platypus electroreception). [^1]
2. But the deeper limit is attentional. In Simons's video, viewers count passes by players in white; about half of first-time viewers fail to notice a gorilla walking through, a player leaving, and the curtain changing color. [^2][^3]
3. Even *primed* viewers — told something odd will happen — still miss one or more changes, "and they're not really trivial things," e.g., "the disappearance of a person" and the whole background changing color. [^4]
4. The pre-experiment hypothesis was that "if something anomalous or unexpected happened your attention would be automatically devoted towards it." The result showed "that's just wrong." [^5]
5. Corrected statement of perception: "you're not very conscious of the environment and radical things can happen and you won't notice them unless they interfere with what you're doing." [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "We're actually narrowed in what we can perceive far more than anybody ever guessed." [^1]

> "For people who haven't seen or heard about a video like this before, about half miss the gorilla." [^3]

> "Radical things can happen and you won't notice them unless they interfere with what you're doing." [^6]
