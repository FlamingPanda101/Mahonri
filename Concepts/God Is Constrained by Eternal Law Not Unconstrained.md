# God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
In the fine-tuning exchange, [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] pins [[Francis Collins]] on omnipotence. Collins agrees the life-permitting constants act "as a constraint on God's creative power," then repeatedly retreats: God "chooses to be" constrained [^1]. O'Connor sharpens it: *could* God have made an equally interesting universe with a stronger gravitational constant, avoiding a Big Crunch "somehow," "because God is God"? Or is He genuinely bound by the constant? [^2]. He escalates to the logical limit — are the constants like the laws of logic, such that even God cannot make "2 + 2 = 5"? [^3]. Collins, "like most Christians, is reluctant to say that God is bound by anything" [^4].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The LDS position bites the bullet O'Connor exposes.** Where creedal Christianity equivocates ("He chooses to be constrained"), the restored gospel affirms that God *is* genuinely constrained "by what we call eternal law" [^4]. Omnipotence is redefined: "Our notion when we say God is all powerful is simply to say that God can do all things which are possible to do" [^4]. Rating: **philosophically clean** — this is the standard analytic move (omnipotence ≠ ability to do the logically impossible), here extended from logic to metaphysical/moral law.
2. **Collins's own concession already grants the principle.** Collins admits that with only gravity altered, "we would have had a collapse... Even God couldn't make that happen" [^5]. The narrator's point: Collins has *already* conceded a constrained God for physics, and is merely refusing to name it. Rating: **strong** — it is a fair reading of the transcript; Collins says "even God couldn't" while denying God is constrained.
3. **The 2+2=5 analogy is the hinge.** O'Connor asks whether the constants are brute metaphysical necessities like logical laws [^3]. The LDS answer is essentially *yes* — eternal law is co-eternal with God, not authored by Him. Rating: **coherent within LDS metaphysics** (Joseph Smith taught matter and intelligence are uncreated/eternal), though it is a *substantive metaphysical commitment*, not a neutral deduction.
4. **Scriptural anchor — Alma 42.** Alma teaches that "the work of justice could not be destroyed. If so, God would cease to be God" ([[Alma 42.22-25|Alma 42:22-25]]) — explicitly subordinating God's action to an eternal principle He cannot violate without ceasing to be God [^6]. Rating: **directly on point**; this is the strongest textual support and the radical claim "God would cease to be God" is repeated four times in the cited passage.
5. **Steelman the classical-theist side.** Classical theism's strongest reply: a God constrained by external eternal law is not the absolute, self-existent ground of all being (aseity) — He becomes one more contingent inhabitant of a law-governed cosmos, raising the regress "what grounds the eternal law?" Collins's "He chooses to be" is an attempt to preserve sovereignty *and* fine-tuning. The LDS trade-off is explicit: it sacrifices absolute aseity to gain a coherent answer to the problem of evil and the logic of the Atonement (see [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]]). Honest tension: this is a genuine fork in theism, not a knockdown — the cost is a smaller, conditioned God.

## Best Quotes
> "And so that acts as a constraint on God's creative power... once he's made that choice that he wants this universe... He can't make that interesting universe but by this very specific value of the constant of gravity." [^1]

> "Could he have created the world as interesting as it is today with a stronger force of gravity?... Or is he constrained by the constant of gravity in what he can create?" [^2]

> "It's the Latter-day Saint position that God is constrained in some sense by what we call eternal law. Our notion when we say God is all powerful is simply to say that God can do all things which are possible to do." [^4]

> "If you're going to stick with the laws we've got and just change one constant, gravity, and expect that to end up with complexity, it won't work. Even for God. Even God couldn't make that happen." [^5]
