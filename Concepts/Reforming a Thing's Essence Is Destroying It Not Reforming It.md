#type/claim

# Reforming a Thing's Essence Is Destroying It Not Reforming It

## The Claim
[[Steven Pynakker]] repeatedly defends critics like [[John Dehlin]] as would-be *reformers* — Martin Luther figures who "want to stay and reform" rather than destroy. [[Jacob Hansen]] answers with an argument from **essential characteristics**: every thing has properties without which it would not be that thing, and to alter *those* is not to reform the thing but to destroy it and replace it with something else. If a critic wants the church to stop claiming Joseph Smith was a prophet, he wants "thing A" turned into "thing B" — and "if you change it to thing B, thing A no longer exists." So Dehlin, whatever his manner, "does want to destroy the church"; the honest move is to "just own it." [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Identity is fixed by essential, not accidental, characteristics.** "Things have, in philosophy, essential characteristics. They're the things that if they didn't have that characteristic, they wouldn't be that thing." [^3] Reforming accidental features is genuine reform; reforming essential ones "would not be a reform — that would be a destruction of what the church is." [^3] *Rating: strong as a conceptual distinction;* it is a clean application of essence/accident metaphysics and it reframes "destroy" from an emotional accusation into a definitional claim.

2. **The transform-to-B formulation.** "If you take something and you transform it into something that it is not, you have destroyed the thing that originally existed... John wants to change the church from thing A to thing B. And if you change it to thing B, thing A no longer exists." [^1] The targeted essentials are concrete: "the church should no longer claim that Joseph Smith was a prophet of God." [^4] *Rating: strong;* by naming a specific candidate-essential (Joseph's prophetic call), Hansen makes the argument testable rather than hand-wavy.

3. **The Martin Luther / Catholic-pope analogy.** Pynakker says Luther "wanted to stay and reform" Catholicism; Hansen replies that Luther ultimately rejected the *essential* Catholic claim — papal supremacy: "If the Roman Catholic Church doesn't have a pope, then it's not Roman Catholicism anymore. It's something different." [^5] Luther was therefore not reforming Catholicism but leaving it for something else. *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* historically arguable (Pynakker notes Luther was excommunicated and wanted to stay), but it does isolate the principle: a "reform" that negates a defining claim is a departure, whatever the reformer's stated wish.

4. **The polite-destroyer.** Hansen's sharpest line: Dehlin "just doesn't want to be mean about it. And that's fine. Like just own it. Say I think that this thing is false and falsehood should not be propagated. Why are you so afraid of just saying that?" [^6] This connects to [[The Say-Versus-Do Disconnect in Critics]]: the demand is for the critic's stated self-description to match the logical upshot of his project. *Rating: moderate;* rhetorically forceful, though it assumes the critic shares Hansen's binary that wanting fundamental change *entails* believing the institution should not exist.

**Steelman of the critic.** Pynakker presses the strongest reply (66:00–72:00, 133–145): churches *can and do* reform features that once looked essential and survive as recognizably themselves — the Protestant Reformation "held on to the good parts" while purging abuses; even Latter-day Saint practice has changed substantially (the priesthood ban, polygamy) while members still call it the same church. Whether a given claim (Joseph as prophet) is truly *essential* or merely *currently central* is contestable, and labeling reform "destruction" can be "dangerous language" that recasts sincere members as enemies. Hansen's defense is the essence/accident line itself — *some* changes really are identity-ending — plus his insistence that he is asking only for honest labeling, not silencing. The live question is who gets to designate which claims are essential.
