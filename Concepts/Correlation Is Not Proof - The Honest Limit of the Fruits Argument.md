# Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument

## The Point
The "by their fruits" argument from sociology is genuinely suggestive but has an honest ceiling: aggregate, state-level correlations between LDS concentration and good family outcomes cannot by themselves establish **causation**, and even a real causal benefit would not prove the gospel's **truth-claims**. The episode itself models this candor in a couple of moments — and several confounds it raises (or glosses) are worth keeping on the table.

## Evidence
1. **The hosts concede a confound on-air.** Pressed on whether mountains and scenery explain Utah's numbers, a host volunteers the inconvenient direction too: "elevation correlates to higher suicide rates, which is something you have to remember ... if we were just being honest."[^1] This is the right instinct — the same elevation/rurality/gun-access confounds catalogued under [[The Fruits of the Church - Do Critics Have a Better Program]] cut both ways.

2. **Selection, not just causation.** The hosts' own "it's family-friendly, so family-minded people move here" mechanism is a *selection* effect: people who already prioritize stable families relocate to Utah/Idaho.[^2] That can inflate a state's family-structure score without the gospel having caused those families — a sorting story, not a treatment effect.

3. **Secondhand, uncited numbers.** Much of the supporting data (the "Clint Teppel" rankings, the infidelity/divorce percentages, "best state four years running") is recited from memory without citation and not verified in the episode. Aggregated and confounded figures repeated this way are exactly where [[Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion|the fruits argument]] is most vulnerable to pushback.

4. **The is/ought-style ceiling.** Even granting that practicing the gospel *causes* better family outcomes, that establishes the program "works" pragmatically — it does not establish that Joseph Smith was a prophet or that the Book of Mormon is historical. Good fruit is *consistent with* truth and shifts the burden (see [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]]), but a high-demand community of almost any creed can produce strong families. The honest claim is the modest one: the fruit is healthy, which is what a true tree *would* produce — not a proof that it *is* the tree.

## Best Quotes
> "Actually, elevation correlates to higher suicide rates, which is something you have to remember ... if we were just being honest." [^1]

> "It's not because everyone in the state is a Latter-day Saint, it's because Latter-day Saints, when they're involved in the structure of society, they build a place that supports everybody." [^2]

> "It's just so cool to see that the fruits are so apparent ... above any other state." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This note is the methodological guardrail for [[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]], [[The Law of Chastity as a Family-Stability Engine]], and [[Fruits Means Outcomes Not Right Belief in the Trinity]]. It keeps the "by their fruits" case ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]]) honest: the fruit metaphor was always an *indicator*, not a syllogism, and the strongest version of the argument concedes correlation-vs-causation and pairs the data with the spiritual witness rather than leaning on statistics alone — the same balance modeled in [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]].
