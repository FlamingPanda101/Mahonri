# Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)

## The Claim

Quoting [[David Litwa]]'s *We Are Being Transformed: Deification in Paul's Soteriology*, [[Dan McClellan]] dismantles the standard objection that humans cannot be deified because, unlike the Creator, they lack the **power to create**. Litwa's thesis: in the first-century Greco-Roman *and* Jewish worlds, divinity was defined by **immortality and (super-human) power**, *not* by the power to create. Most gods of the ancient Mediterranean — including all the Olympians — were not creators, yet were unproblematically called gods. Therefore the existence of **non-creating gods** and even **created gods** was "fairly staple theology" until the late-antique rise of *creatio ex nihilo* (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]) and a philosophically strict monotheism in the 4th century CE. This is McClellan functioning as **support** for LDS deification: the objection that "only the uncreated Creator can be God, so exalted humans cannot be gods" assumes a post-biblical definition of divinity that the biblical and early-Jewish texts did not share.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The two defining divine qualities were immortality and power — not creation.** Litwa: "Immortality and power. These are… the basic divine qualities of the Jewish God Adonai as well as the God Christ… Having these qualities in the ancient mind made one a god because a god is an immortal being with superhuman power. The power to create was not essential to godhood in the ancient world."[^1] *Rating: strong* — it locates the definition of "god" in the period's own categories rather than in later creedal metaphysics.

2. **Most gods were not creators, so creation cannot be the criterion.** "None of the Olympian gods were creators in this sense. In fact, most gods in the ancient Mediterranean basin were not considered to be creators."[^2] *Rating: strong* — a straightforward comparative-religion observation that defeats the "creator-only" definition by counterexample.

3. **The Bible itself names *theoi / elohim* who did not create.** Litwa points to gods "in Jewish writings who had nothing to do with creation," and McClellan reads the supporting NT case [[John 10.34-36|John 10:34-36]] ("I said, you are gods," Psalm 82 in Jesus' mouth) as language that "cannot just be swept aside as a figure of speech… in light of modern monotheistic categories."[^3] *Rating: strong* — the deifying language is treated as data to be explained, not explained away.

4. **Created gods were normal pre-Nicene theology — even for Christ.** "Up until the fourth century, Christians such as Arius asserted that Christ himself was both a god and a creature," basing it on Proverbs 8:22 ("the Lord created me… the beginning of his ways"). Litwa concludes: "The idea of created or generate gods was not, it seems, unusual before the 4th century CE."[^4] *Rating: strong* — ties the abstract point to a concrete, well-documented Christian position ([[Arius]]) and a specific proof text.

5. **The shift is datable and external.** The move away from this picture is driven by "the rising salience of a more philosophically strict idea of monotheism, where only one entity can be called God," plus the turn from the Septuagint's *ektisen me* ("created me") to the Hebrew "begat me" in Proverbs 8:22.[^5] *Rating: strong* — the change is a tradition-historical development, not a recovery of the original sense (cross-reference [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A classical theist answers that the *Jewish* conception was always the exception Litwa half-concedes: Deutero-Isaiah makes Yahweh's power to create *central* to his claim to deity, so creation-as-criterion is not a late import but a prophetic datum, and the "other gods" of the Hebrew Bible are precisely *idols and nothings* (cf. the anti-idol reading of [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]]), not genuine deities of the same kind. On this view "created gods" in pagan or Arian sources prove only what pagans and heretics thought, not what the canonical authors taught; and the firm Creator/creature line that the Nicene tradition drew is the biblical line, not a Greek overlay. McClellan/Litwa's reply (given in the video): Deutero-Isaiah cannot be made "the rule and standard for all Jewish thought for all future time," the Hebrew Bible nowhere actually states that no other gods *exist*, and the deifying texts (Psalm 82; John 10) are canonical, not heretical — so the strict line is the renegotiation, not the baseline.

## LDS Relevance

This is one of the strongest scholarly supports in the cluster for the LDS doctrine of [[Human Deification in Mormonism|deification]], because it neutralizes the objection that *most* directly targets the LDS view: "an exalted human would be a *created* being, and a creature can never be a true God." Litwa's data say that **created, non-creating gods were ordinary ancient theology** — exactly the conceptual space LDS exaltation occupies, where God and humans are the [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis|same kind at different stages]] and divinity is participatory and graded rather than defined by *ex nihilo* creative omnipotence. It dovetails with the vault's argument that [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE|creatio ex nihilo is a late-2nd-century innovation]] and that [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum|ancient Judaism affirmed a divine spectrum]]. Caveat (the honest fault line): Litwa is describing *ancient categories of divinity*, not endorsing the LDS same-species ontology; he would not say the Father is an exalted man. The LDS gain is that the *definition of "god"* the critic uses to exclude exalted humans is itself post-biblical — not that Litwa has signed the King Follett Discourse.
