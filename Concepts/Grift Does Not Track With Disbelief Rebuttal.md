# Grift Does Not Track With Disbelief Rebuttal

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The episode's provocative title calls it "the hollow faith of Pastor **Grift**." But "grift" means cynically pursuing money while pretending to believe. [[Jonah Barnes]] himself rebuts the framing: **disbelief and money-seeking do not track together** in the data. The clergy who believe the *least* (mainline, Catholic) are comparatively poorly paid and do much of the on-the-ground charity, while the clergy most accused of monetization (Evangelical / non-denominational pop-ups) believe the *most*.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Belief is highest where monetization is most alleged (strong/internal-consistency).** Evangelical and non-denominational churches — often accused of "only thriving in wealthy suburbs" — score to the 90th percentile on bodily resurrection, hell, and miraculous healing.[^1] If they are profiting, they are at least "believing their own tea," which is the opposite of a grift.[^2] *Rating: strong on the show's own data.*

2. **Low-belief clergy are cheap (moderate).** Catholic priests reportedly earn ~$30–60k (some figures as low as ~$15k); Methodist ministers ~$48–75k.[^3] A Catholic priest who only 23% believes in Adam and Eve "isn't costing our society so much because they're so cheap."[^4] Low pay is hard to square with "grift."

3. **Low-belief traditions do the heavy charitable lifting (moderate).** Barnes credits Catholics and mainline Protestants (alongside Latter-day Saints) with doing "all of the heavy lifting of charity in North America" — soup kitchens, homeless missions.[^5] Sacrificial service is evidence against cynical self-enrichment.

4. **Reframe accepted: the real problem is sincerity, not fraud.** The hosts converge on a better title — "It's disappointing how much America's pastors actually don't believe" — replacing the accusation of *fraud* (grift) with a lament about *sincere doubt* among the well-paid.[^6] *Rating: this is the panel's negotiated conclusion, not a hard data finding.*

**Steelman of the "grift" framing (defended by Jonah).** Jonah partially re-defends the title: the *mainline* tradition, where disbelief is highest, still "stress[es] tithing," has "their own economy," and carries pension burdens "bankrupting their churches." So a pastor who is lukewarm about the resurrection yet draws a salary and pension from a flock he is supposed to be shepherding can fairly be said to be extracting value disproportionate to his conviction — even if he is not a flamboyant prosperity-gospel huckster.[^7]

**Context.** The disagreement turns on the *definition of grift*. The skeptical host uses the cynical-fraud sense (money + feigned belief), under which the data exonerate most clergy. Jonah drifts toward a broader sense (drawing a living from a vocation one only half-believes), under which mainline clergy remain culpable. Both can be right because they are measuring different things.

## Best Quotes

> "It looks like from this data that the ones that are monetizing it the most are also the ones that believe the most." [^1]

> "At least the ones that are pushing the most amount of money... at least they're believing their own tea." [^2]

> "Catholics and Mormons... do all of the heavy lifting of charity in North America." [^5]

> "It wasn't people. It was pastors... there's people in the pews who have stronger, deeper beliefs than the guy they're paying." [^6]
