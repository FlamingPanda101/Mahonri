# Joseph Smith Lacked the Metallurgical Skill and Time

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Even granting the materials, the objection assumes [[Joseph Smith]] *could* make
witness-consistent plates. The rebuttal: there is no evidence he had any
metalworking skill, and no time in his documented life when he could have done the
months-to-years of secret labor required.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. No evidence of metalworking skill

Joseph was a farmer. The only craft connection offered by critics is that his
father was a cooper (barrels with metal bands) — which the panel dismisses as
nowhere near the skill needed to roll thin sheets, cut, shape, and finely engrave
hundreds of plates.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (absence of skill evidence plus the implausibility of the cooper link).

### 2. Experts who *have* done it say it's extremely hard

Metallurgist [[David Baird]], who built a replica, stated making consistent thin
pages, cutting, shaping, and engraving them "would have been extremely difficult,"
that it would have taken Joseph "quite a while," and that there is no evidence
Joseph had the required skills. People doing it part-time have taken several
years.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (testimony from someone who actually performed the task).

### 3. No time in the historical record

A full-time effort would have required Joseph to vanish from farm work for months,
or secretly labor for years — and the record contains no trace of such absence.
By contrast, the *translation* of the Book of Mormon is heavily documented. The
"secret kiln/cave in the hill, once a year" theories are mocked as ad hoc.[^4]

### 4. The aging/oxidation step adds even more skill

Beyond making the plates, a forger would need to artificially age/oxidize the
surface (cf. the acid-treated [[Kinderhook plates]], which were far smaller and
simpler), compounding the required expertise.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The honest concession: it is not strictly *impossible*. The panel explicitly
refuses to say "no" outright — "is it hypothetically possible? Yes" — to avoid
mirroring [[Trent Horns Word-Count Argument Uses Wrong Parameters|Trent Horn's
overreach]] in the other direction. A determined skeptic could posit a hidden
apprenticeship and undocumented absences. The argument is therefore about
*plausibility / best explanation*, not metaphysical impossibility — leading
directly to [[Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates]].

## Best Quotes

> "Do you have any evidence that Joseph Smith had any metalworking skills at all? I mean, he was a farmer."[^2]

> "If Joseph Smith was to make his gold plates, it would have taken him quite a while. I don't think there's any evidence that he had any of the skills required to do that." — [[David Baird]][^3]

> "We would have seen something in the historical record to say... Joseph Smith wasn't doing his farm work and was hiding out for all this time... And yet we don't see that."[^4]
