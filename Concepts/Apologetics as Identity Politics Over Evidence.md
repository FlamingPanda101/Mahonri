# Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence

## The Claim

Dan McClellan argues that the apologetic enterprise is fundamentally NOT about discovering truth or even defending truth — it is about **defending identity markers** that are central to practitioners' social identities. When evidence conflicts with those identity markers, identity politics overrules evidence. "The apologetic endeavor is not about finding out what's true. It's not about defending what's true. It's not about what's most likely. It is about defending dogmas and making the people who accept those dogmas feel justified and validated in accepting those dogmas." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence — The Identity-Politics Model

### 1. Identity Markers Are Central to Social Identity

McClellan argues that for many Christians, biblical inerrancy is not just a theological position — it is an **identity marker**: "There are social identities that are very important to them that have as one of their identity markers, one of their lines in the sand, the notion that the Bible cannot contradict itself." [^2] An identity marker is not a belief you hold; it is a boundary that defines who is "in" and who is "out" of your community. To abandon the marker is to risk social exclusion.

### 2. When Identity Markers Become Central, They Override Evidence

"When those social identities become important enough, then that identity marker becomes central to your worldview, and you will fight tooth and nail in defense of that identity marker. Not because there is evidence or any data or any reason or any logic behind it, but because it's central to your identity politics." [^2]

The mechanism McClellan describes is:
1. A community has an identity marker (e.g., "the Bible is inerrant")
2. That marker becomes essential to belonging
3. Evidence challenging the marker triggers an identity-threat response, not an evidence-evaluation response
4. The person defends the marker using whatever arguments are available, regardless of quality
5. Because the real goal is identity preservation, not truth-seeking

### 3. Evidence Cannot Change Minds Governed by Identity

McClellan makes a stark claim about the limits of evidence: "If you cannot accept it's a contradiction, no amount of argumentation is going to change your mind. You're only arguing because you're trying to defend your worldview and your identity politics." [^3] This is not a claim that the apologist is stupid or insincere — it is a claim about the psychological function of the arguments. They serve an identity-protection purpose, not a truth-discovery purpose.

### 4. The Direction of Influence: Identity → Argument, Not Argument → Identity

The standard apologetic self-conception is: "I have examined the evidence and concluded the Bible is inerrant; therefore, when I encounter apparent contradictions, I seek harmonizations."

McClellan inverts this: "I am socially identified as someone for whom the Bible is inerrant. Therefore, when I encounter apparent contradictions, I MUST seek harmonizations — any harmonization — because the alternative (admitting contradiction) would threaten my identity."

This is why "you're not going to find actual engagement with the data or with the scholarship. You're going to find rationalizations that are overwhelmingly weak, inadequate, and fallacious." [^4] The rationalizations are not meant to convince outsiders; they are meant to reassure insiders that the identity marker still holds.

### 5. The Dogma-Over-Data Motto

McClellan's trademark phrase "Data Over Dogma" (the name of his podcast) encapsulates his counter-approach. His critique is compressed into the formulation: "Within apologetics, the identity politics are in charge — not the data. It is putting dogma over data." [^5]

## The Psychological Mechanism

This analysis draws (implicitly) on well-established concepts in social psychology:
- **Motivated reasoning:** People process information in ways that protect pre-existing beliefs central to their identity
- **Cognitive dissonance:** When evidence conflicts with identity-central beliefs, the dissonance is resolved in favor of the identity, not the evidence
- **Social identity theory:** Group membership is maintained partly through shared beliefs; threats to those beliefs are experienced as threats to group belonging

McClellan's contribution is applying this framework specifically to apologetics as a practice — showing that what presents itself as rational defense of faith is actually identity-protection behavior.

## The Implication for Debate

If McClellan is correct, debating apologists on the evidence is largely futile. The contradiction isn't being denied because the evidence is insufficient — it's being denied because admitting it would threaten social identity. The apologist is not evaluating arguments; they are defending a boundary. This explains the phenomenon of apologists accepting "laughable arguments" that "just doesn't fit" the text — they are not evaluating fit; they are seeking permission to continue believing what their identity requires them to believe.

## Relevance to LDS Apologetics

While McClellan's critique is directed at conservative Protestant inerrancy apologetics, the identity-politics model is a general framework that could be applied to ANY tradition's apologetics — including LDS apologetics. The question it raises: when do LDS apologetic arguments serve truth-discovery, and when do they serve identity-protection? This is a question the library should hold honestly.
