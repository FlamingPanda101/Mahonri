#type/claim

# Dated Anti-Mormon Books Refute the Rebranding Charge

## The Claim

A staple of modern online criticism — pushed, the hosts say, by figures like David Laddings and Josh Bensons — is that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has recently **"rebranded"**: that "you guys are saying Jesus is God… you believe in Christ and all this stuff" only lately, to sound mainstream-Christian.[^1] The [[Ward Radio]] hosts turn the critics' own back-catalog against them. Reading **anti-Mormon books decade by decade**, they show that hostile authors already attributed these same beliefs to Latter-day Saints generations ago — so the supposed "new" Christ-language is documented in the polemical literature itself, long before any alleged PR pivot. The point is concrete and citable rather than abstract; it gives the burden-of-proof argument (see [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]) a paper trail.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. A 1997 critic conceded "nothing of substance has changed"
[[Mark J. Cares]]' *Speaking the Truth in Love to Mormons* (2nd ed., 1997) quotes the 1997 General Conference to argue that **"nothing of substance has changed in their teachings"** — they still teach obedience to the living prophet, still speak highly of Joseph Smith, still teach redeeming the dead.[^2] The hosts note the irony: Cares meant it as an *attack* (the LDS haven't really reformed), but it doubles as a hostile witness that the doctrine the critics now call a recent "rebrand" was already on the record — and stable — in 1997.[^2]

### 2. A 1998 "Dictionary of Mormonism" (by a critic) already affirmed LDS Christology
The hosts read a 1998 *Dictionary of Mormonism* — written by an evangelical critic — whose own entries state that "the Mormon definition of accepting Jesus is believing that Jesus lives and is divine," and that although Mormons hold the Son "inferior to the Father," they affirm he is *divine*.[^3] "Here we are almost 30 years later," the hosts say, with critics claiming "Mormons don't think Jesus is God" — a claim the critics' *own* reference work refuted in 1998.[^3] The "inferior to the Father" wording is itself just **subordinationism**, the early-church position of Justin Martyr, Clement, and Origen (see [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]]).

### 3. The same dictionary concedes the "Godhead" is the LDS term
Its *Trinity* entry says "Mormons do not believe in the Trinity… it's the Godhead." The hosts answer that even the *Oxford Dictionary* defines the Trinity as "the Christian **godhead** as one in three persons" — so the critic's wedge word is itself standard Christian vocabulary, and "one God, three divine persons" is something Latter-day Saints "have no problem with" (see [[The Godhead Is Not Polytheism]]).[^4]

### 4. A 1961 book attacks LDS for *already* "rebranding"
Reading *Mormonism Now and Then* (1961), the hosts point out it accuses the Church of having rebranded — in **1961** — which means whatever terminological shifts a critic wants to call "the rebrand" were being complained about more than sixty years ago, not invented for the internet age.[^5] (The hosts add the obvious tu quoque: "Has Protestantism rebranded at all in the last 60 years?")[^6] The deeper LDS point is that terminology develops in every living tradition without the underlying claims contradicting — see [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]].

### Steelman (the critic's side)
A critic can fairly reply that *emphasis* genuinely shifted: 20th-century correlation, the 1995 "Living Christ" testimony, and the 2018 push to retire the nickname "Mormon" did foreground Christ-centered language and downplay distinctives like the King Follett discourse. That is a real change in public presentation. The hosts' rebuttal does not deny shifts in emphasis; it denies the stronger claim that the *substance* of LDS Christ-belief is a recent fabrication — and it grounds that denial in the dated testimony of hostile authors who described the same beliefs in 1961, 1997, and 1998.

## Best Quotes
> "Even he says that the Mormon definition of accepting Jesus is believing that Jesus lives and is divine. And here we are almost 30 years later — 'Mormons don't think Jesus is God.'"[^3]

> "For all of the rebranding claims, this guy in 1997 says nothing of substance has changed in their teachings."[^2]

> "This is from 1961, and this guy is saying Mormonism rebranded. Has Protestantism rebranded at all in the last 60 years?"[^6]
