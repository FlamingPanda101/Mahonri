# Luke Could Have Omitted the Flight to Egypt (Apologetic Objection)

## The Objection

Apologists argue that Luke's failure to mention the flight to Egypt and the slaughter of the innocents doesn't mean it didn't happen — it simply "doesn't say that it doesn't preclude the possibility that the slaughter of the innocents happened and they went into Egypt. It just doesn't mention it." Luke may have made an editorial choice to leave it out.[^1]

## Why It Fails

### 1. It's "pretty criminal neglect" for a careful historian

McClellan's argument: "One needs to use the editorial mind when crafting an interesting story." The slaughter of innocents by a king and a flight to a foreign country is a "juicy" and "strong" detail — exactly the kind of dramatic event an ancient historian would include if he knew about it. Omitting it while including temple rituals is "pretty profound authorial neglect."[^2]

### 2. Chronologically, it doesn't fit

Matthew's timeline places the slaughter up to two years after Jesus's birth (Herod asks when the star appeared, then kills all boys two and under) — meaning the family was still in Bethlehem. Luke's timeline has them returning to Nazareth shortly after Jesus's birth and then "visiting Jerusalem at the Passover every year." This "does not allow for them to flee to Egypt until Herod dies" and "suggests they were free to come back once a year" — incompatible with being fugitives.[^3]

### 3. Luke is internally consistent without Egypt

Luke 2:39 says "when they had finished everything required by the law of the Lord, they returned to Galilee, to their own town of Nazareth." There is no gap in Luke's narrative where the flight to Egypt could be inserted. The sequence is: birth → temple → return to Nazareth. Inserting a two-year stay in Bethlehem, flight to Egypt, and years in exile would break Luke's narrative flow entirely.[^4]

## Best Quote

> "My argument would be that is pretty criminal neglect to leave out that detail. It's a pretty strong detail, too. That's juicy. That's a juicy part of the story." — [[Dan McClellan]][^5]
