# Self-Authenticating Canon — Feel It in Your Bones

## The Claim
[[John Calvin]]'s case (Institutes I.7) that Scripture authenticates itself, requiring no decree of the church: "How shall we be persuaded that [Scripture] came from God without recurring to a decree of the church? It is just the same as if it were asked, how shall we learn to distinguish light from darkness, white from black, sweet from bitter? Scripture bears upon the face of it as clear evidence of its truth as white and black do of their color." Elsewhere in the same chapter Calvin says we know it because "we feel a divine energy living and breathing in it." The believer, guided by the Holy Spirit, can simply *see* that Scripture is God's word — so no infallible church or tradition is needed to establish the canon [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. It is self-evidently a "hippie-dippie" feelings test — rate: rhetorical setup
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] notes the irony that Calvin — "the first 'facts don't care about your feelings' guy," who tells objectors their feelings can't overturn double predestination — abandons that rigor exactly here, resting canon-recognition on "you just feel it in your bones" [^2]. **Steelman of Calvin:** Calvin does not mean raw sentiment; he means the *internal testimony of the Holy Spirit* (testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum), a theological category, not mere emotion. The hosts' "surfing/hippie" framing underplays this. But the substantive objections below survive the steelman.

### 2. It is empirically false — believers contradict each other — rate: strong
If every Spirit-led Christian just *knows* the canon as easily as black from white, the disagreements should not exist. Yet Calvin cited **Baruch** as Scripture (modern Protestants reject it), [[Martin Luther]] argued four NT books did not belong, and Protestants, Catholics, and the early Christians all differ. "Somebody's feelings are wrong." See [[Reformers Disagree With One Another on the Canon]] [^3].

### 3. It fails the "beautiful and profound" test — rate: strong
Self-authentication implies one opens the Bible and is so struck by every page's beauty that divine authorship is obvious. But Scripture contains "long census collections" (e.g. genealogies in [[Numbers 6|Numbers]]) that no one finds self-evidently divine; **2 Peter** even admits Paul is "hard to understand" ([[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter 3:15-16]]). Most Christians believe the Bible is inspired because *someone told them*, not from unmediated encounter [^4].

### 4. Scripture itself never teaches this method — rate: strong
Nowhere does the Bible say "this is how you'll know which books belong." By contrast, the Qur'an (Surah 17, al-Isra') and the Book of Mormon ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]]) *do* propose a beauty/feeling test — so the method, if valid, would validate rival scriptures. And [[Jeremiah]] 17:9 warns "the heart is deceitful above all things," so a deep inner conviction is no reliable predictor of truth. See [[Moroni-Calvin Parallel - The Self-Authentication Gambit]] [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "When he comes to this, he literally says... 'we feel a divine energy living and breathing in it.' And it's like, when did he take up surfing? This is the most hippie-dippy kind of argument for how we know the canon. We feel it in our bones." [^2]

> "It's no more difficult to tell which books are in the Bible as telling black from white. Okay, well, why are so many of you disagreeing and contradicting one another? It doesn't make sense. Those two things can't both be true." [^3]

> "It is rarely because you open a Bible and you read it and are convinced, yes, every single book, page and word in this collection is so beautiful and profound it must have God as its source... there's other passages that are just long census collections." [^4]

> "Scripture itself, you get nothing in scripture that says, this is how we're going to know which books belong in the Bible." [^5]
