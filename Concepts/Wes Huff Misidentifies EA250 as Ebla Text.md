# Wes Huff Misidentifies EA250 as Ebla Text

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Being Rebutted)

In his tweet on creation ex nihilo, Wes Huff cited "EA 250" as evidence from the Ebla archives, quoting: *"Lord of heaven and earth, the earth was not, you created it. The light of day was not, you created it. The morning light you had not yet made exist."*[^1]

## The Rebuttal

"EA 250" is not an Ebla tablet. The prefix "EA" designates the **Amarna tablets** — a collection of diplomatic correspondence from 14th-century BCE Egypt (Tell el-Amarna) — and has nothing whatsoever to do with the Ebla archives (Tell Mardikh, Syria, 3rd millennium BCE). Huff has confused two entirely distinct archaeological corpora separated by roughly a millennium. The correct designation for the Ebla tablet he intended to cite is **TM.75.G1682**.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. EA = Amarna, Not Ebla

Dan McClellan immediately flags the error:

> "EA is the designation, the prefix designation for the Amarna tablets. It has nothing to do with the Ebla archives. So Wes has misunderstood what kind of text he's looking at here. And EA250 has absolutely nothing whatsoever to do with creation."[^2]

The **Amarna letters** (EA 1–382) are Akkadian-language diplomatic correspondence between Egypt and its Levantine vassals and neighboring powers from the 14th century BCE. They discuss politics, trade, marriage alliances, and military matters — not cosmogony. EA 250 specifically is a letter from the ruler of Shechem to the Pharaoh.[^2]

### 2. The Correct Designation: TM.75.G1682

The text Huff intended to cite is from the **Ebla archives** (Tell Mardikh, Syria, c. 2500–2250 BCE). The specific tablet is:

> "We're actually looking for an entirely different text that goes by TM.75.G1682."[^2]

- **TM** = Tell Mardikh (the modern site name for Ebla)
- **75** = 1975 (the year of discovery)
- **G** = the archive room designation
- **1682** = the tablet inventory number

A smaller duplicate tablet covering column 1 with variants is designated TM.75.G2500.[^3]

### 3. The Error Signals Deeper Problems

McClellan uses this error as a diagnostic:

> "Right off the bat, Wes is showing one, that he doesn't know the difference between EA and Amarna, and two, that he's just pulling uncritically from more than 50-year-old translations that have been widely panned and widely criticized by scholars as overly apologetic."[^4]

The misidentification is not a trivial typo — it reveals that Huff is not working from primary epigraphic knowledge but is relying on secondhand apologetic sources (likely Norman Geisler)[^5] without verifying the underlying scholarship.

### 4. The Ripple Effect

Because Huff didn't know the correct tablet designation, he couldn't have checked:
- The actual epigraphic publication history of TM.75.G1682
- Alfonso Archi's refutation of Pettinato's reading
- The scholarly consensus that the text is "quite incomprehensible" beyond its opening words[^3]
- The broader rejection of Pettinato's apologetic Ebla-to-Bible project

The error is not isolated — it's a symptom of uncritical reliance on apologetic secondary sources rather than engagement with the primary epigraphic literature.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A defender might argue: this is a minor nomenclature error. "EA" vs. "TM" is a technical bibliographic detail; Huff's substantive point (that the Ebla text says something relevant to creation ex nihilo) stands or falls on the translation, not the call number. The content of the quote is what matters.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **It's not minor.** Confusing the Amarna archive (14th-c. BCE diplomatic letters from Egypt) with the Ebla archive (3rd-millennium BCE Sumerian texts from Syria) is a category error spanning a millennium, two different languages, two different genres, and two different archaeological sites. It's equivalent to citing a Medieval papal bull as a Dead Sea Scroll.
2. **It's diagnostic of method.** Someone who has done primary research on Ebla would know the TM designation. Using "EA 250" indicates Huff is copying a citation from a secondary source (likely Geisler's apologetics work) without checking the original.
3. **It undermines credibility on ANE evidence.** If Huff can't correctly identify which archaeological corpus he's citing, his claims about what that corpus "explicitly affirms" carry no weight.
