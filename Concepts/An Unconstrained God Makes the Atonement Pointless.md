# An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
This is the *theological* edge of the omnipotence problem (the metaphysical edge is [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). The narrator turns O'Connor's "unconstrained God" challenge back on creedal Christianity: "The entire biblical story makes no sense if God could have simply saved and exalted all mankind with the snap of his fingers" [^1]. If God truly has no constraints, several pillars of Christianity collapse into incoherence.

## The Rebuttal
1. **The cross becomes gratuitous.** "If God can do anything at all and has no constraints, why is Jesus dying on the cross? Why couldn't God make a different plan that just made sure none were lost and no sacrifice was needed at all?" [^1]. Rating: **strong internal critique** — the Atonement presupposes some necessity the sacrifice satisfies; an omnipotent-without-constraint God could waive that necessity by fiat, making Gethsemane pointless.
2. **Hell becomes self-inflicted by God.** "In the creedal worldview, God created hell. If God is not bound by anything, why not just forego making the need for hell in the first place by only creating beings that won't go there?" [^2]. Rating: **a real bite against unconstrained sovereignty** (it parallels the standard "transworld depravity / why not actualize only the saved" debate); the LDS answer presupposes libertarian free will plus eternal law that even God honors.
3. **Free will cannot be overridden.** "Could God save a person against their will? If I hated God and chose to reject him with the complete knowledge of him... can God just give me a pass and let me into his kingdom?" [^3]. The implied LDS answer is no — salvation is relational and consensual, so God's power is bounded by the agent's will. Rating: **coherent** and explains why God "needs" a plan rather than a decree.
4. **Scriptural engine — justice and mercy in Alma 42.** Alma frames the whole plan as God navigating an *eternal* tension between justice and mercy that He cannot simply dissolve: "mercy could not take effect except it should destroy the works of justice. Now, the work of justice could not be destroyed. If so, God would cease to be God" ([[Alma 42.22-25|Alma 42:22-25]]); "What? Do you suppose that mercy can rob justice? I say unto you, nay, not one whit" [^4]. The Atonement exists precisely *because* God is constrained: it is how mercy is extended without robbing justice. Rating: **the load-bearing text**; it converts the abstract "constrained God" into a soteriology.
5. **Steelman the creedal reply.** Classical theism answers that the necessity of the cross is rooted in God's *own nature* (His holiness/justice), not an external law — God must punish sin to be true to Himself, so the Atonement is necessary without God being constrained by anything outside Him; and hell is the just consequence of creaturely rejection, freely permitted, not a design flaw. The narrator's critique lands hardest against *voluntarist* "God can do absolutely anything" theology and softer against *intellectualist* classical theism where God's nature itself supplies the constraints. Honest tension: both camps end up saying God "cannot violate His nature" — the dispute is whether that nature is self-grounded (classical) or accountable to co-eternal law (LDS).
