## The Point
As congregations grew beyond small clusters and the liturgy became more regulated (formalized baptismal initiation, cyclical scripture readings, authoritative clergy instruction, a Eucharist separated from the fellowship meal), the physical spaces had to be adjusted. By the early-to-mid third century, larger domestic structures were renovated into dedicated worship buildings called *domus ecclesiae* ("assembly houses"): walls were removed to create larger meeting halls, modest liturgical furnishings (baptismal fonts, speaking platforms) were installed, and walls were adorned with biblical or Christ-centered frescoes. Unlike house churches, several *domus ecclesiae* are archaeologically attested.

## Evidence
1. The shift accompanied a developing leadership structure — from a patron(ess)/deacon(ess) presiding over domestic gatherings to a hierarchical organization of regional bishops and localized elders (see [[Rise of the Monarchical Bishop]]).
2. The Eucharist of consecrated bread and wine became the standardized remnant after the fellowship/agape meals had gradually disappeared.
3. The oldest and most impressive example is the *domus ecclesiae* at [[The Dura-Europos House Church and Baptistery|Dura Europos]] in eastern Syria, renovated in the 240s AD.
4. Renovations highlighted growing distinctions among congregation members: catechumens (unbaptized), baptized laity, and ordained clergy each had designated access to certain parts of the building.
5. The period also saw the increased prominence of institutional leadership, the accompanying decline in ecclesiastical roles for women, and the formalization of ritual routine (catechistic, baptismal, and Eucharistic practices).
6. Other possible surviving examples: a renovated prayer hall at Megiddo, the "house of Peter" at Capernaum, altered structures at Il-Medjdel and Umm idj-Djimal in Syria, the villa at Lullingstone in Britain, and the *tituli* churches of Rome.

## Best Quotes
> "These homes that were renovated primarily to house Christian ritual gatherings were called domus ecclesiae ('assembly houses')." [^1]

> "The renovations that occurred within domestic church spaces highlighted the growing distinctions among congregation members (with catechumens [the unbaptized], baptized laity, and ordained clergy each having designated access to certain parts of the building)." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Grey lists the *domus ecclesiae* as a parallel to the way the Restoration moved from repurposed private structures toward "more formal and dedicated buildings," reflecting unfolding ecclesiastical needs and rituals over time. [^3]
