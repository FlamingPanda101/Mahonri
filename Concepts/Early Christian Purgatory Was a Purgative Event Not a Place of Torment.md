# Early Christian Purgatory Was a Purgative Event Not a Place of Torment

## The Point
[[D. Jill Kirby]] argues that the **earliest Christian idea of purgatory differed sharply from the medieval one**. Early Christians envisioned purgation as a *purifying event* — grounded in [[1 Corinthians 3.13-15|1 Corinthians 3:13–15]] ("saved; yet so as by fire") — rather than "a protracted experience in a place of torment" with quantified penalties. The non-negotiable condition was that a person's life "must be founded in Christ": only committed disciples could benefit. [[Augustine]] held, as "most Christians," that the status of *certain* sinners could improve after death if their lives had been "correctly oriented toward God."

## Evidence
1. **Two key passages.** Paul's fire is both *probative* ([[1 Corinthians 3.13-15|1 Cor 3:13–14]]) and *purifying* (3:15); and [[Matthew 12.32|Matthew 12:32]] ("neither in this world, neither in the world to come") implied some sins could be forgiven after death [^1].
2. **Event, not place.** "The earliest Christian ideas about purgatory were built on this passage, often envisioning it as a purgative *event* rather than a protracted experience in a place of torment" [^2].
3. **The Christ-founded condition.** "In order to benefit from a purgatorial experience, a person's life must be founded in Christ … one must have been committed to being a disciple of Christ while living" [^2].
4. **Sanctification takes time.** "Although a person might be justified, the process of sanctification takes time, during which death may intervene" — so postmortem improvement was possible for the rightly-oriented [^3].
5. **Augustine's middle category.** A life "neither good enough to dispense with the need for [intercession] … nor bad enough to preclude their being of advantage after death." His example: the man who loves his wife "in the world's fashion" — sin to be expiated, but not damnable because in lawful marriage [^4].
6. **Means of intercession.** Prayers, alms, and the Eucharistic sacrifice — "traditional forms of importuning God for his mercy," appropriate for living and dead alike (cross-ref to ch. 11 work for the dead) [^5].

## LDS Connection
Kirby's "Between Death and Resurrection" box explicitly maps the early purgative model onto LDS doctrine: spiritual labors and progression continue after death "until we are finally prepared to meet God" (D&C 138:25–34, 50–52), conditioned on mortal orientation ([[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:32]]). The early-Christian *purgative event* — cleansing for the Christ-founded soul — resembles the LDS intermediate spirit world far more than the medieval debtor's-prison purgatory; see [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]].
