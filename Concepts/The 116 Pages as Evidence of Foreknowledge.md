# The 116 Pages as Evidence of Foreknowledge

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues against open theism (and for divine foreknowledge of the actual future) using the **lost 116 pages** of the Book of Mormon. God knew the first translated portion (the Book of Lehi, dictated to [[Martin Harris]]) would be lost, so He prepared the **small plates of Nephi** centuries in advance as a "backup." Carroll's question: was the small-plates provision "a backup plan, or was it plan A because he knew?"[^1] [[Cardon Ellis]] supplies the analogy of a careful car designer who installs a seatbelt — not designing the car to fail, but foreknowing a real possibility of a crash and preparing for it. Conclusion: God "is a good car designer and still installed a seatbelt," i.e., He foreknew and provisioned accordingly.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The provision implies foreknowledge of a *contingent* event — rate: suggestive, not decisive.** If God commanded Nephi to make duplicate plates specifically because He knew the Lehi portion would later be lost, that is knowledge of a future free act (Harris's loss of the manuscript), which open theism denies God can have.[^1] See [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]], [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]. **Steelman of the open theist:** Ellis's own seatbelt analogy concedes the weaker reading — a wise agent provisions against a *foreseen possibility* ("99% chance they won't, but in the 1%...") without certain foreknowledge. [[1 Nephi 14.10|1 Nephi 9]] has Nephi say he makes the small plates "for a wise purpose... which purpose I know not," i.e., the *human* prophet did not know — consistent with God acting on high-probability anticipation rather than exhaustive foreknowledge. So the episode underdetermines exhaustive foreknowledge vs. probabilistic providence.
2. **The "plan A vs backup" dichotomy is the crux — rate: a sharp framing.** Carroll presses that if it was "plan A because he knew," that is strong foreknowledge; if merely a backup, it is compatible with open theism. The text's "wise purpose" language tilts toward foreknowledge in the LDS reading but is genuinely ambiguous.[^1]
3. **It functions as Carroll's reason to reject open theism, not as a creation-dilemma argument — rate: contextual.** This concept matters because it explains *why Carroll won't take the open-theism exit* even though he grants it would solve the dilemma — pushing his solution onto denying creation *ex nihilo* instead (see [[God Found Spirits He Did Not Create - Book of Abraham Solve]]). Ellis offers a parallel scripture-based argument: God commanding sinners to "sin no more" presupposes a real, unforced possibility of repentance He may not have predetermined — which actually leans *toward* open theism, showing the panel is not fully united on foreknowledge.[^3]

[^1]

> "I'm going to install a seatbelt. So, to me, the small plates were a seatbelt. So, God is a good car designer and still installed a seatbelt in his car." [^2]

> "Why would he command somebody to go out and sin no more if there were not an opportunity that the person would stop sinning... and he didn't know whether or not that person would take that opportunity?" [^3]
