# The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ

## The Claim
The Binding of Isaac ([[Genesis 22|Genesis 22]]) is a deliberate type pointing beyond itself to the Father's sacrifice of His Only Begotten Son. [[Don Bradley]] argues the [[Book of Abraham]] backstory sharpens this typology: because Abraham himself was nearly sacrificed and God intervened, his obedience with Isaac becomes an act of *trust* in a God who saves, illuminating the symbolic, Christ-pointing nature of the event.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The text flags its own typology (rated: strong, scriptural).** In [[Genesis 22|Genesis 22]] God calls Isaac Abraham's "only son" though Abraham has Ishmael — "it's portending the Only Begotten. It's portending Christ and the sacrifice of Jesus Christ."[^2] The deliberate "only son" language signals the antitype. The LDS reading (Jacob 4 / the "similitude of the Only Begotten") is explicit that the event is a similitude.

2. **Backstory transforms obedience into trust (rated: moderate, interpretive).** Against Abraham 1, the image is "not just a guy who's willing to" do anything commanded; rather "he knows that he can trust God. ... God has come through for him. God is going to come through for Isaac."[^3] Abraham's prior rescue from human sacrifice grounds his confidence that God can intervene again. See [[Abraham as a Backstory to Genesis]]. *Steelman:* a historical-critical reader would object that Genesis 22 reads most naturally as a *test of unconditional obedience* (the angel says "now I know that thou fearest God"), and importing prior "trust that God will intervene" softens the text's stark demand and is driven by the extra-biblical Book of Abraham, not by Genesis itself. The typological/Christ-pointing layer is a later Christian (and LDS) overlay, not the plain sense of the Hebrew narrative.

3. **The contrast of two fathers (rated: moderate).** Abraham's earthly father sought a *destructive* sacrifice of his son (cooperating with a pagan priest); the Heavenly Father intends a *redemptive* sacrifice. "There is a wild parallel there" — the destructive pagan sacrifice inverted into the redemptive atonement.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "In the story of Isaac, God actually calls Isaac Abraham's only son. He's not his only son. He has Ishmael. But it refers to him as his only son. ... It's portending Christ and the sacrifice of Jesus Christ."[^2]

> "It's pointing to the Father's sacrifice of His Beloved Son."[^2]

> "What Abraham's father is trying to do in sacrificing his son... it's a destructive sacrifice. Whereas what God the Father wants to happen is a redemptive sacrifice."[^4]
