# Translation Density Shows the Book of Mormon Text Fits

## The Claim

Using the correct method — counting *characters* and modeling "translation
density" against real ancient scripts — the entire Book of Mormon, including the
116 lost pages, fits comfortably on a few hundred plates of the size and
character density the witnesses describe.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Count characters, not words

[[Josh Coats]] counts total English *characters* (not words, which vary in
length) and compares English-to-source-character ratios across attested
translations: English-to-Hebrew ≈ **2.4:1**; English-to-Demotic and -hieratic
Egyptian ≈ **2.6 and 3.1:1**; English-to-Chinese ≈ **3.9:1** as an upper bound.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (empirical ratios derived from actual parallel texts, e.g. Genesis in Hebrew vs English).

### 2. Reformed Egyptian should be at least as dense as Hebrew

Moroni states the writing was *more* compact than Hebrew, so the density should
exceed 2.4. Coats adopts a range of roughly **3–4 (up to 5) English characters per
reformed-Egyptian character**, comparable to Demotic.[^3]

### 3. The combinatorial model: it fits

Coats's mathematical model evaluates millions of possible plate/character
configurations and finds many — within the 3–4 density band — that fit the entire
text (with the 116 pages) on roughly the ~200 plates he estimates.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a quantitative model rather than an anecdote; published in *Interpreter* and presented at FAIR).

### 4. Rappleye's independent 24-artifact survey

[[Neal Rappleye]]'s chapter surveys 24 real ancient metal inscriptions (Semitic,
Egyptian, Near Eastern) computing words-per-square-inch in translation. Examples:
the Ketef Hinnom silver scrolls (Jerusalem, ~600 BC) ≈ 17.5 words/sq-in → ~199
plates; a denser case ≈ 61 words/sq-in → ~57 plates. The 24-sample **average ≈ 40
words/sq-in → ~86 plates** for the whole text.[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a broad, representative artifact sample, the methodological opposite of the critics' one-or-two cherry-picks).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can note Rappleye admits he *selected* densely written inscriptions to
match the witnesses' "covered with small characters" description, which biases the
sample toward dense writing. Fair — but that selection is principled, since the
witnesses specifically describe compact engraving, and even so the *average* (86
plates) sits well under the hundreds available. See
[[Reformed Egyptian Characters Were Small]] for the character-size basis.

## Best Quotes

> "There are many [configurations] that are within the translation density of three to four characters... where you could fit the entire text including the last 116 pages on the roughly 200 plates."[^4]

> "Across the 24 examples that I found, the average would be about 40 words per square inch and would require about 86 plates for the entire Book of Mormon text." — [[Neal Rappleye]][^6]

> "You cannot take one or two examples and make a sweeping generalization about what is actually possible... there are thousands of examples of it."[^7]
