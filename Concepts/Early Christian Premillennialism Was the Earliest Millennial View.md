# Early Christian Premillennialism (Chiliasm) Was the Earliest Millennial View

## The Point

The earliest identifiable Christian position on the Millennium was **premillennialism** (ancient *chiliasm*): the belief that Jesus would physically return *before* a literal thousand-year reign on earth. [[Nicholas J. Frederick]] traces a chain of literalist defenders — [[Papias]], [[Justin Martyr]], [[Irenaeus of Lyons]], and [[Tertullian]] — who "would continue to discuss ideas such as Jesus's Second Coming and the Millennium as real events, foregrounding them as important Christian beliefs." [^1]

## Evidence

1. **Papias was among the first to promote it.** According to [[Eusebius of Caesarea]], Papias (c. AD 60-130), bishop of Hierapolis, "was among the first to seriously promote the premillennial idea that Jesus would reign for a thousand years on earth." Eusebius, who rejected this view, "was quite critical of Papias's literal interpretation... and blamed him for Irenaeus's similar premillennial leanings." [^2]
2. **Justin held it but conceded it was not universal.** Justin affirmed "a resurrection of the dead and a thousand years in Jerusalem, which will then be built, adorned, and enlarged," yet admitted "many who belong to the pure and pious faith and are true Christians think otherwise" — a remarkable acknowledgment that one could be a Christian without believing in a literal millennial kingdom. [^3]
3. **Irenaeus devoted Book 5 of *Against Heresies* to it.** A self-described premillennialist living "in the last times," Irenaeus taught a literal new heaven and new earth where "the righteous shall reign in the earth... and shall enjoy in the kingdom conversation and communion with the holy angels." He explicitly rejected those who would "allegorize prophecies of this kind." [^4] Irenaeus quoted Papias directly on the millennial state of the just.
4. **Tertullian aligned with the literalists.** Though he "rarely... mentions the actual return of Jesus," Tertullian "clearly viewed the events surrounding Jesus's return and the Millennium as actual, future events." Jonathan Menn notes this "early millennialism... had a physical, sense-oriented quality in which the blessings of the Millennium were amplified versions of contemporary, earthly life." [^5]
5. **It became the minority position.** By the end of the second century, premillennial literalism "was becoming more and more the minority position" — Jesus had not returned and Rome stood firm. Diarmaid MacCulloch notes Irenaeus's vivid end-times views "caused such embarrassment to the next generations of Christians" that their original Greek expression "has entirely disappeared" and even Latin copies were censored. [^6]

## Best Quotes

> "Like Justin, Irenaeus was a premillennialist and felt that he and others were living 'in the last times.'" [^7]

> "The righteous shall reign in the earth, waxing stronger by the sight of the Lord... and shall enjoy in the kingdom conversation and communion with the holy angels, and union with spiritual beings." — Irenaeus, quoted by Frederick [^8]

## LDS Connection

Frederick identifies premillennialism as the view "held by many Christians in the United States, including members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints," placing the modern LDS position in continuity with Papias, Justin, and Irenaeus rather than with the later Augustinian mainstream. [^9]
