#type/claim

# Dying for a Belief Proves Sincerity Not Truth

## The Claim
The popular apologetic — "nobody lays down their life for a lie they made up, so the apostles' deaths prove the Resurrection" — is a **straw man** that confuses two different things: (1) whether the witness *consciously fabricated* a claim, and (2) whether the claim is *true*. [[Dan McClellan]] argues the martyrdom-witness inference fails because **dying for a belief establishes only the witness's sincerity, never the truth of the object believed.** Critics of the Resurrection do not say the disciples lied; they say the disciples "probably sincerely believed that Jesus was resurrected" — and "there is an enormous difference between sincerely believing something that's not true and making up a lie." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Nobody alleges fabrication, so the rebuttal misses its target.** The apologetic asserts that no one would die for a lie *they themselves invented* — but, McClellan notes, "I don't know anybody who is suggesting that Jesus's 12 apostles made up the lie of his resurrection." Serious critical scholars challenge the *historicity* of the Resurrection while granting the disciples' sincerity. Refuting a position no one holds is the straw-man fallacy. [^1] *Rating: strong; it correctly identifies that "they died for a lie they made up" attacks a claim the relevant skeptics do not advance.*

2. **People demonstrably die for false — and even fabricated — beliefs.** Across history and "down to today," people give their lives "for things that they believed that were not true, and sometimes even for things that they imagine to be true or fabricated as true," because they value the claim and its benefits more than their own safety. So even *if* a belief were false, willingness to die for it is fully explicable. [^2] *Rating: strong; the premise "no one dies for a falsehood" is simply untrue.*

3. **Self-sacrifice is ordinary human nature, not an anomaly requiring a miracle.** To the objection "that's not human nature," McClellan replies "it's exactly human nature": people routinely sacrifice their lives for "family or a loved one or community or their identity, their nation, their ethnicity," even just their "legacy" or "memory within a community." The martyr's death needs no supernatural explanation. [^3] *Rating: strong; broad, uncontroversial anthropological point.*

4. **The witness-to-truth inference is rhetoric, not logic.** Countless people have died for Islam, Mormonism, Buddhism, and secular nationalism; if dying for a claim proved it true, all these incompatible claims would be true. "The notion that the giving of one's life indicates that they must have known something to be incontrovertibly true and therefore their death constitutes evidence of the truthfulness of those claims is just nonsense. That's not logic. That's just rhetoric." [^4] *Rating: strong; the parade of mutually-exclusive martyrdoms is a clean reductio.*

## LDS Relevance
This argument cuts **straight across the LDS "witnesses" apologetic**, which runs on the very inference McClellan rejects. The vault's [[Apostle Analogy - Sincere Belief unto Death]] note has a Ward Radio host arguing the Book of Mormon witnesses are "as bedrock as the original 12 apostles" because both "altered their lives... in a way that could only be explained by sincere belief" — and that note's own steelman already concedes the decisive gap: "**sincere belief doesn't mean that the object of belief is true.**" McClellan presses exactly that gap. **The honest LDS response is not to deny it but to narrow the claim**: martyrdom (or lifelong refusal to recant) rules out *conscious fraud*, which dispatches the [[Witnesses Conspiracy Theory Rebutted|conspiracy]] and grifter theories — but it does not by itself adjudicate between a veridical experience and a sincere delusion, which is why the real battleground is the [[Mass Hallucination Hypnosis Theory Rebutted|hallucination/visionary debate]] and the physical, multiply-attested character of what the witnesses described, not the bare fact that they suffered for it. Notably, McClellan does **not** dispute that people make costly sacrifices for their convictions (cf. the LDS appeal to [[Naturalistic Explanations Cannot Account for Collective Sacrifice|collective sacrifice]]); he disputes only that such sacrifice can carry the evidential weight of *truth*.
