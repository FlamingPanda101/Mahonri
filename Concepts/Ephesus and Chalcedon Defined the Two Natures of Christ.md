# Ephesus and Chalcedon Defined the Two Natures of Christ

## The Point
Nicaea (325) created a new problem by insisting Jesus is fully God: *how is he then also human?* It took two more councils — Ephesus (431) and Chalcedon (451) — and over a century of dispute to answer. The councils navigated between [[Nestorius]] (two natures functioning separately, almost two persons) and [[Eutyches]] (one nature after the Incarnation, swallowing the human into the divine = monophysitism). The Chalcedonian Definition settled on the *hypostatic union*: two natures (divine and human), unmixed and undivided, in one person — using the unscriptural term *hypostasis*. The drama was driven as much by imperial politics (Empress [[Pulcheria]]) as theology.

## Evidence
1. After Nicaea: "if Jesus IS God, how is he also human? This wasn't resolved until the Council of Chalcedon (451)."[^1]
2. By 451 the political stage had shifted: Theodosius I made Christianity the *official* religion in 380; Rome was sacked by Visigoths in 410; in the east, the boy-emperor [[Theodosius II]] reigned with his sister [[Pulcheria]] as "the power behind the throne."[^2]
3. **Ephesus (431):** condemned [[Nestorius]], bishop of Constantinople, who held Jesus's "humanity and divinity operated separately" and that Mary could not be called *Theotokos* (Mother of God). The council "rejected the idea that Jesus's humanity accomplished some things without his divine nature."[^3]
4. **Eutyches:** head of a Constantinople monastery; held "from two natures before the union; but after the union I acknowledge one nature" — that one nature being the *divine*. He affirmed Christ *homoousios* with the Father but denied Christ *homoousios* with humanity. This is *monophysitism*, declared heresy.[^4]
5. **Leo's Tome:** [[Leo the Great]], bishop of Rome, wrote against Eutyches: it is dangerous "to believe the Lord Jesus Christ to be merely God"; Christ must retain both natures so that "lowliness is assumed by majesty, weakness by power, mortality by eternity."[^5]
6. **Politics:** [[Theodosius II]] died in a 450 riding accident with no heir; [[Pulcheria]] married the senator [[Marcian]], becoming empress, and had him convene Chalcedon.[^6]
7. **Chalcedon (451):** 300–520 bishops, 16 sessions. They accepted [[Cyril of Alexandria]]'s Second Letter to Nestorius and Leo's Tome, then produced the Chalcedonian Definition: Christ "consubstantial [*homoousios*] with the Father as regards his divinity, and the same consubstantial with us as regards his humanity … acknowledged in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation … into one person and one subsistent-being [*hypostasis*]."[^7]
8. The term "hypostatic union" "is unscriptural," but represents the council's effort to summarize scripture.[^8]
9. **Aftermath:** the councils meant to unite but produced new schisms — *miaphysite* churches split after Chalcedon; the Second (553) and Third (680–81) Councils of Constantinople followed, the Third concluding Christ had *two wills* (Luke 22:42).[^9]

## Best Quotes
> Eutyches: "I acknowledge that our Lord came into being from two natures before the union; but after the union I acknowledge one nature." [^4]

> Chalcedon: Christ is "acknowledged in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation … coming together into one person and one subsistent-being [hypostasis]." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints "have not developed a Christological creed" defining Christ's natures like Chalcedon; they would differ "primarily in our doctrine of the innate divine potential of human nature" — that we are spirit children of Heavenly Parents (D&C 93:29–40; Abraham 3:22–23). The century-long, politically driven, multi-council struggle to define the unscriptural *hypostatic union* exemplifies the LDS thesis that creedal Christology was constructed, not revealed.
