# Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai (John's Preferred Subtext)

## The Claim
The author of John may be reading [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]] through an **early rabbinic tradition** in which Psalm 82 refers to **Israel at Sinai**: upon receiving the word of God (the Law), the Israelites were freed from death and rendered immortal/divine, then consigned back to mortality after the golden calf. On this reading, "the word of God can make people divine," which fits John's logos christology — since Jesus *is* that word made flesh.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The reading is earlier than the "honorific humans" reading (moderate)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** offers this as "a much better reading which also appeals to rabbinic exegesis but an interpretation that occurs earlier in rabbinic literature." [^1] He summarizes: "Upon the reception of the law the word of God the Israelites were freed from the power of the angel of death and they were rendered immortal, effectively made gods. But then by sinning with the golden calf, they are then consigned back to mortality. And that is the trajectory of Psalm 82." [^1]

### 2. It strengthens rather than weakens John's christology (strong, internal coherence)
Unlike the "lesser-to-greater" reading (see [[Lesser-to-Greater Reading Weakens Johannine Christology]]), the Sinai reading raises the stakes: "the point here is the word of God can make people divine... it also makes it more significant for Jesus to be the word of God because the word of God is what turned humans divine. And Jesus is that very word itself, come in the flesh." [^2] "And that word is standing before you." [^3]

### 3. It fits John's soteriology (strong)
The Sinai reading dovetails with John's divinization soteriology (see [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology]]): "The word of God is what renders things divine." [^4] Believers become children of God by union with the divinizing Logos — the same pattern Sinai models.

### 4. Steelman / limitations
McClellan is appropriately tentative: he says *if* the author "has this in mind," not that it is certain, and grants the honorific-humans reading is also available. The Sinai-divinization tradition's date relative to John is debated, and a critic could argue John shows no explicit Sinai allusion in chapter 10. McClellan's case is one of *best fit* with John's logos and divinization themes, not a claim of direct citation — which is why he frames it as "a more powerful assertion of John's christology" rather than a proof.
