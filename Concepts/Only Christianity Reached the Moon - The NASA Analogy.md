# Only Christianity Reached the Moon — The NASA Analogy

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]]'s analogy for Christian abolition: NASA failed to reach the moon far more often than it succeeded (1958-1972), "yet only NASA astronauts have walked on the moon." Likewise, "Christians have been on the wrong side of this question quite a lot," but "only Christian ideals have proved themselves remarkably abolitionist in ways others haven't."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Failure record doesn't negate the unique achievement.** The point: counting Christianity's failures (pro-slavery Christians) no more refutes its abolitionist achievement than NASA's failed launches refute the moon landing. The *unique successful instance* is what matters.[^1] *(Rhetorically effective; isolates the unique-achievement claim from the failure-rate objection — see [[Own the Anti-Abolitionist Christianity Too]].)*

2. **Frames abolition as a singular Christian "first."** Pairs with [[Gregory of Nyssa First to Condemn Slavery Wholesale]] — the first wholesale condemnation — to argue abolition is a distinctively Christian summit.[^1] *(Consistent with the "unthinkable without" thesis.)*

3. **Steelman of O'Connor — the analogy breaks where the church *discouraged* the goal.** O'Connor: "Imagine if there was a period where NASA was *proactively discouraging* people from going to the moon" — telling China not to go because "the essence of our organization tells us..." That is the disanalogy: the church didn't merely fail at abolition, it often actively opposed it. O'Connor's concession nonetheless: "you would still ask NASA how to get to the moon — they'd still be the people you'd look to."[^2] *(Sharp — the analogy assumes NASA always *wanted* the moon; it fails where the institution opposed the very goal it later claims. But O'Connor grants Christianity remained the locus of the successful abolition.)*

4. **The deeper irony O'Connor presses.** The whole argument is that the Bible is *not sufficiently* abolitionist — and Scrivener's reply ("the whole Bible culminating in Christ crucified") points to "a really bad place to turn if you're trying to justify abolition," since the cross is itself a slave's death. Scrivener turns this around: the cross is precisely the "slave's death" that liberates, "proving the thesis."[^3] *(A genuine clash of framings rather than a resolved point.)*

## Best Quotes

> "No one has walked on the moon except the NASA astronaut... it is these Christian ideals that have proved themselves remarkably abolitionist in ways others haven't."[^1]

> "Imagine if there was a period where NASA was proactively discouraging people from going to the moon." — O'Connor[^2]
