# Joseph Smith Cited View of the Hebrews as Evidence

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
**Objection:** Joseph Smith secretly used *View of the Hebrews* as the hidden source for the [[Book of Mormon]] and would have concealed it.[^1]

**Rebuttal:** Joseph Smith *publicly cited* *View of the Hebrews* — as **supporting evidence for** the Book of Mormon — in the 1842 *Times and Seasons*, of which he was editor. "It's not what you'd expect in the behavior of a plagiarist." Worse for the theory, he didn't even cite it directly: he quoted it secondhand through [[Josiah Priest]], suggesting he had no intimate firsthand familiarity with the book.[^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **A plagiarist does not advertise his source (rated: strong, documented).** In the 1842 *Times and Seasons*, "Joseph Smith is citing View of the Hebrews as evidence for the Book of Mormon. ... If you were plagiarizing from this book, you wouldn't be like showing your source."[^2]
2. **His access was secondhand (rated: strong, documented).** Joseph "is getting it through Josiah Priest, which is also not what you'd expect from someone who was intimately familiar with the book itself. ... His exposure to the book actually seems to come through secondary [sources]."[^3] An author who supposedly built a 500-page scripture on a book had not even read it directly.
3. **The positive-citation tradition long predates the plagiarism charge (rated: strong, documented).** Joseph "starts the trend" of citing it favorably; Smoot tracked the same positive use through the *Millennial Star*, the *Deseret News*, and 19th-century LDS scholarship — always as evidence "for ancient Hebrews in the Americas," never as a confession of borrowing.[^4] Latter-day Saints read it and none recoiled, "oh crap, this looks like the Book of Mormon actually came from this." *Steelman:* a critic can argue that openly citing the source is exactly the misdirection a confident fraudster would use ("hide in plain sight"), and that secondhand citation in 1842 doesn't prove Joseph never saw the book earlier. Smoot's reply: this multiplies unfalsifiable assumptions, and the simplest reading — a man citing a book as corroboration he plainly hadn't read closely — is incompatible with secret dependence (see [[No Contemporary Accusation of Plagiarism]]).

## Best Quotes

> "Joseph Smith is citing View of the Hebrews as evidence for the Book of Mormon ... It's not what you'd expect in the behavior of a plagiarist."[^2]

> "He's getting it through Josiah Priest, which is also not what you'd expect from someone who was intimately familiar with the book itself."[^3]

> "A lot of Latter-day Saints were pointing to View of the Hebrews as evidence for the Book of Mormon ... none of them were like, oh crap, this looks like the Book of Mormon actually came from this."[^4]
