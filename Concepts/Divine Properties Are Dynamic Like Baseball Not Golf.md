# Divine Properties Are Dynamic Like Baseball Not Golf

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues the divine attributes (knowledge, power, love, presence) are not static, capped, immutable maxima but **dynamic, always in growth**. To be divine is to be in dynamic growth in loving relationship, "where new vistas are entered" — not to possess a fixed upper-limit perfection. The contrast: golf has a best-possible score (18 on an 18-hole course); baseball has *no* maximum number of runs. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The baseball/golf analogy (rating: strong; conceptual illustration)
"What's the greatest possible golf score? 18 on an 18-hole golf course. What's the greatest number of points that could be scored in a baseball game? There is no such number." Divine properties "are like a baseball game, not like a golf game. They're always dynamic, always in growth." [^1]

> "the properties of divinity are like a baseball game, not like a golf game. They're always dynamic, always in growth. To be divine means to be in a dynamic growth, in a loving relationship where new vistas are entered." [^1]

### 2. Properties are not static Platonic forms (rating: moderate; metaphysical)
Ostler rejects "the Platonic notion of properties" — "static, immutable, timeless, out-of-our-physical-reality existence." To be a human "is to be a person who's in growth and process or in degeneration toward death." Properties exist *in potentiality* and unfold, for humans and for God alike. [^2]

### 3. Fullness of divinity = full co-inherence (rating: moderate; systematic)
"To be fully divine means to be in a full relationship, to co-inhere, to dwell in God and have him dwell in us fully — that actuates the divine properties in us so that we possess the full knowledge, the full power, the full presence, the full love." See [[Co-inherence as Mixing Light to Form the Godhead]]. [^3]

### 4. Targets classical immutability/aseity (rating: strong; polemical)
The analogy is aimed at the "Platonic, neoplatonic, middle-Platonic" God who "is immutable... an upper-limit absolute," who would not be God "if he depended on anything for his existence" — "that's the Thomistic view." Ostler counters that love itself is a dependence: "I can't love you unless you exist." See [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Classical theists argue that *infinite* perfections are not "capped scores" but unbounded actuality (*actus purus*) — God is not improving toward a limit because he already possesses being without potency; "growth" implies prior lack, which is incompatible with perfection. On this view Ostler's dynamic God is a *finite, improving* deity, which classical theism regards as an idol, not God. Ostler's reply is that "perfection-as-static-completeness" is a Greek import; a relational, ever-creative God who enters "new vistas" of love is *more* perfect, not less, and matches the biblical, embodied, responsive deity. See [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]].

## Best Quotes

> "if I ask the question, what's the greatest number of points that could be scored in a baseball game? Well, there is no such number." [^1]

> "the Platonic notion of properties is mistaken because properties are not like Plato's ideas. They're not the static immutable timeless existence." [^2]

> "love is a dependence. I can't love you unless you exist." [^4]
