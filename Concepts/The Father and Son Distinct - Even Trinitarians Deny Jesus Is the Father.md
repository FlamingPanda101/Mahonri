# The Father and Son Distinct - Even Trinitarians Deny Jesus Is the Father

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common apologetic syllogism: (1) Yahweh is the Father; (2) Jesus claims to be Yahweh (e.g., "before Abraham was, I am"); therefore (3) Jesus is the Father — taken as "evidence of the Trinity." [[Blake Ostler]] argues this self-defeats. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The conclusion is a heresy the Trinitarian rejects (rating: strong; logical)
If "Jesus is Yahweh" and "Yahweh is the Father" yield "Jesus is the Father," that is **modalism** — which no orthodox Trinitarian accepts. "Not even a traditional Christian could accept that." The argument therefore proves too much. [^1]

> "this is kind of an illicit move... 'Oh well, Yahweh is the father. Jesus is Yahweh. Therefore, Jesus is the father.' But not even a traditional Christian could accept that... They're distinct. They're not identical." [^1]

### 2. The persons must be distinct, so the proof-text backfires (rating: strong; internal critique)
Since Trinitarians insist "there has to be some distinction" between Father and Son, the very texts they cite to prove "Jesus is Yahweh" cannot also prove "Jesus is the Father." The apologist is "using this scripture in an illicit way outside of its context and culture to try to make a point that doesn't even support your view." [^2]

### 3. Modalist-sounding texts cut both ways (rating: moderate; exegetical)
John's "he who has seen me has seen the Father" ([[John 14.9|John 14:9]]) could be marshaled for modalism too — but the same pericope has the Father *sending* the Son, so they are distinct. The resolution: seeing Jesus = seeing the *actions/will* of the Father, because Jesus does the Father's will as his agent. See [[Book of Mormon Father-and-Son Texts Resolve in Context]]. [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian's actual claim is not the crude syllogism but a *predication* one: "Jesus is Yahweh" means Jesus *shares the one divine being* that the Father also is, not that the person of the Son = the person of the Father. On this reading "Yahweh" names the *being* (the *what*), and Father/Son name *persons* (the *who*), so no modalism follows. Ostler's reply is that this only relocates the problem: it forces "Yahweh" to mean the *what* (the Godhead) in some texts and a single divine *person* in others — equivocation he addresses via the who/what test. See [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]] and [[Substance Is Equivocal in the Trinity]].
