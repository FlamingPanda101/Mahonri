# Original Sin Was a Late Western Development (Augustine)

## The Point
The doctrine of "original sin" — that Adam's *guilt* and corrupted nature are inherited by all his posterity — is not found in the Bible and was not the view of the earliest Christians. "Prior to the fifth century AD, there was no notion that all human nature had been corrupted as a result of Adam and Eve's disobedience."[^1] It became dominant in the Western Church only through [[Augustine]] (354–430), built on a *mistranslation* of Romans 5:12 and on precursors like [[Ambrosiaster]] and [[Tertullian]]. The Greek Fathers ([[Irenaeus of Lyons]], [[Origen]]) held that humanity shares in the *effects* of the Fall but not Adam's collective *guilt*.

## Evidence
1. The word "Fall" is not in Genesis 3 or anywhere in the Bible to describe Adam and Eve's act. [[Methodius of Olympus]] (260–311) "may have been the first to use the term Fall."[^2]
2. Earliest Jewish commentary portrayed Adam as patriarch, hero, and wise man (Sirach 17:1–12; Wisdom of Solomon 10:1–2), not as the corrupter of human nature.[^2]
3. Early Christians blamed the *serpent* for sin and *Cain* for death, not Adam — noting that God cursed Cain, the serpent, and the ground, but never cursed Adam and Eve (Genesis 3:14, 17; 4:1–11).[^3]
4. [[Augustine]] made Adam fully culpable ("sinned knowingly and deliberately") and, crucially, rejected Adam-as-mere-type: "By the evil will of that one man all sinned in him, since all were that one man, from whom, therefore, they individually derived original sin."[^4]
5. **The mistranslation:** Romans 5:12's Greek *eph hō* ("because" all have sinned) was rendered in Latin as *in quo* ("in whom"). [[Ambrosiaster]] then glossed it: "In whom that is, in Adam all sinned … all sinned in Adam as a lump (quasi in massa)." This "provides the scriptural proof-text for Augustine's theology."[^5]
6. Augustine echoes the "lump/mass" language: "we have all become one mass of clay … a mass of sin."[^6]
7. Augustine's view was confirmed by councils — the Second Council of Orange (529) made it Western doctrine — while the opposing view of [[Pelagius]] was condemned at the First Council of Ephesus (431).[^7]
8. Augustine's deeper aim was not condemnation but to drive home humanity's "vital need for divine grace"; even a sinless human would still need Christ.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Prior to the fifth century AD, there was no notion that all human nature had been corrupted as a result of Adam and Eve's disobedience." [^1]

> "By the evil will of that one man all sinned in him, since all were that one man, from whom, therefore, they individually derived original sin." [^4]

> "In whom that is, in Adam all sinned … It is clear, consequently, that all sinned in Adam as a lump (quasi in massa)." — [[Ambrosiaster]] [^5]

## LDS Connection
The Latter-day Saint reading aligns far more with the *pre-Augustinian* Greek Fathers: the 2nd Article of Faith holds that "men will be punished for their own sins, and not for Adam's transgression." Irenaeus's view that humanity shares the *effects* but not the *guilt* of the Fall mirrors LDS teaching; Augustine's inherited-guilt doctrine, resting on a Latin mistranslation, is precisely what the Restoration rejects. Augustine's emphasis on dependence on Christ's grace, however, resonates (compare Mosiah 3:19; 2 Nephi 25:23).
