# The Nephite Interpreters Were Shaped Like the Masonic Compass and Square

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that the two interpreters set over the gold plates were physically shaped as two triangles — an **equilateral triangle and a right triangle** — corresponding to the Masonic **compass and square**, and that on the top plate of the record they sat above engraved compass-and-square marks. The point is double-edged: critics treat any Masonic resemblance as proof Joseph Smith fabricated the plates out of his later Freemasonry; Bradley turns this around, arguing the descriptions come from witnesses ([[Joseph Smith Sr]] via the [[Fayette Lapham]] interview, and [[Lucy Mack Smith]]) reporting the shape *before* Joseph's 1842 Masonic initiation — so the resemblance, if anything, is an ancient feature, not a back-projection. He notes the two triangles can be overlaid to form a **Star of David** (a sealable hexagram), a temple/seal motif. See [[The Jaredite and Nephite Interpreters Carry the Endowment Ascent Pattern]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The witness descriptions predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry
[[Joseph Smith Sr]] (in the non-Mormon [[Fayette Lapham]] interview) and [[Lucy Mack Smith]] describe the top plate bearing compass-and-square-style engravings with the two triangular interpreters set over them. If these recollections reflect descriptions given before Joseph's 1842 initiation into Freemasonry, the Masonic-shaped interpreters cannot be a post-1842 invention — which is Bradley's central evidential move.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the descriptions exist, but both are later recollections (Lapham second-hand; Lucy's memoir retrospective), so dating the *report* to before 1842 is the load-bearing and contestable step.

### 2. The geometry is internally specific
Bradley reconstructs the pair as an equilateral plus a right triangle, which is not a vague "looks Masonic" gesture but a definite geometric claim that maps onto the compass (equilateral spread) and square (right angle), and that combines into a hexagram/Star of David — a recognizable temple-seal shape.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the reconstruction is concrete; whether the sources actually specify these two triangle types, versus Bradley supplying the geometry, is the question.

### 3. It reframes, rather than denies, the Masonic parallel
The claim coheres with the vault's methodological point that a Masonic *resemblance* is not itself an argument for fabrication unless the critic can locate specific borrowed *content* on a workable timeline. See [[The Masonry Objection Must Locate the Specific Content Not the General Pattern]] and [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — coherence with the broader thesis; not independent confirmation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) compass-and-square symbolism was **ubiquitous** in early-19th-century New York (Masonic lodges, gravestones, tools), so the Smith family could describe such shapes without anything ancient being involved — the resemblance is unremarkable for the time and place; (b) the sources are late recollections that may themselves have been colored by the later Masonic association, so using them to prove the shape *predates* that association is potentially circular; (c) "equilateral plus right triangle = compass and square = Star of David" stacks several interpretive choices Bradley makes, none of them stated outright in the witnesses; and (d) at most this shows two stones in a bow-frame, which the canonical text already says ([[Mosiah 28.11-16|Mosiah 28:13]]) — the Masonic geometry is added. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument is defensive, not offensive — it does not need the shape to *prove* anything, only to neutralize the inverse charge that the Masonic look betrays fabrication; if the descriptions trace to before 1842 (the historical question Bradley researches), then the resemblance is at worst neutral and at best a genuine prolepsis, and the ubiquity of the symbol cuts both ways.
