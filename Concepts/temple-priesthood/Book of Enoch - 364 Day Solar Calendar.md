# Book of Enoch — 364-Day Solar Calendar

## The Claim

The Book of Luminaries (1 Enoch 72–82) describes a 364-day solar calendar — twelve months of exactly thirty days each, with four intercalary days at the equinoxes and solstices. This calendar competed with the lunisolar calendar that became normative in rabbinic Judaism, and the calendar dispute may have been a factor in the Qumran community's separation from the Jerusalem Temple establishment.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Calendar Described
> "We got a 364-day year that is laid out here — that's the solar year. And then we also have a 354-day lunar year. So we've got two different years." [^1]

The 364-day year divides into:
- 12 months × 30 days = 360 days
- Plus 4 intercalary days (one at each season marker: 3rd, 6th, 9th, and 12th months)
- Total: 364 days

This calendar has the property that every date falls on the same day of the week every year (364 ÷ 7 = 52 exactly) — meaning festivals always occur on the same weekday. This was theologically significant for communities that wanted a fixed liturgical calendar.

The text also acknowledges a 354-day lunar year (12 lunar months) but privileges the solar reckoning.

### 2. The Cosmological Framework
McClellan describes the cosmology behind the calendar with characteristic humor:
> "I mentioned that in Enoch you got this part where he's like 'in the firmament on the east there are three holes and the sun comes out of the hole and follows the track and then goes in the west — it leaves the firmament through another hole.' This was the cuckoo clock part of the firmament theology." [^2]

The sun moves through six gates in the east, traverses the firmament, and exits through six gates in the west. The cosmology is mechanical and orderly — the celestial bodies follow fixed tracks through openings in the solid firmament.

Beecher adds: "Just like in the ride Pirates of the Caribbean where you see the people who are on a track come out... or the Glockenspiel if you want to get Teutonic about it." [^3]

### 3. Angel Uriel as Revealer
> "Enoch is with another angel — Angel Uriel — who reveals details of the calendar and the four ruling luminaries." [^4]

Uriel (אוריאל, "Light of God") functions as the revealer of astronomical and calendrical secrets — a role associated with this angel throughout Jewish apocalyptic literature (cf. 2 Esdras, 2 Enoch).

### 4. Chapter 80 — The Calendar Breaks Down
> "There's a part where things seem to malfunction... Some scholars think this is like a later interpolation, because the whole point of this book is everything works perfectly, nothing breaks down. But that's also the only eschatological part of that book." [^5]

The cosmic order breaking down signals the eschaton — when the sun, moon, and stars deviate from their appointed paths, the end is near. This apocalyptic motif (cosmic disorder as eschatological sign) appears in the Synoptic Apocalypse (Mark 13:24–25) and Revelation.

### 5. The Qumran Calendar Connection
The 364-day solar calendar was adopted by the Qumran community (the Essenes, per the dominant scholarly identification) and may have been a point of contention with the Jerusalem priesthood, which used a lunisolar calendar. The calendar dispute was not merely technical — it determined when festivals were celebrated, meaning the Qumran community observed Yom Kippur, Passover, etc. on DIFFERENT DAYS than the Jerusalem Temple.

This calendar dispute may explain why 1 Enoch was so highly valued at Qumran (11 manuscripts) — it provided scriptural authority for their calendrical practice.
