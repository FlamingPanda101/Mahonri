# Christ's Blood on the Wedding Cloth Covers the Impure Bride

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the ancient custom of proving a bride's virginity with blood on a white cloth is fulfilled christologically: since none of us is a pure bride, **Christ's own blood** is what appears on the wedding cloth, so the bridegroom can present his bride to the father as if spotless. This, she contends, is the deep logic behind [[Revelation 19.11-16|Revelation 19]], where the saints come out of the wedding chamber clothed in **white** while Christ is robed in **red**: the bride is clean because the bridegroom wears the blood.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The blood-on-the-cloth custom

At the consummation the bride brought "a piece of embroidered... white cloth"; blood on it proved her virginity, and its absence carried a death penalty (stoning). This is the very jeopardy [[Matthew 1.16|Joseph faced with Mary]]: she was visibly pregnant and, having been kept apart during the betrothal year, he knew the child was not his, and it fell to him whether she would be exposed.[^3]

### 2. The impure bride's penalty is death — so she needs a redeemer

"If we are the bride and none of us are innocent, what's our penalty? Death." The wedding therefore demands a redeemer who can pay that price — connecting directly to the bridegroom drinking the cup of wrath (see [[The Bridegroom Drank the Bitter Fourth Cup So the Bride Would Not]]).[^4]

### 3. Whose blood is on the cloth — and the red robe of Revelation

When the bridegroom emerges with his bride, "the scriptures talk about she doesn't get stoned... she's celebrated," even though "none of us were virgins going in there." "Whose blood's on the cloth? Christ's." Woodmansee ties this to [[Revelation 19.11-16|Revelation 19]]: at Christ's return the saints are "clothed in white linen" and "he is wearing red" — "our robes are white because he's willing to wear the red robe." The bridegroom "comes wearing a red robe covered in our blood."[^5][^6]

> **Steelman:** Scripture independently has Christ tread the winepress alone and come with garments "dyed red" (Isaiah 63:1-3) while the redeemed "wash their robes... white in the blood of the Lamb" (Revelation 7:14); Revelation 19 explicitly pairs the blood-dipped robe of the rider with the bride "arrayed in fine linen, clean and white." The white-bride / red-bridegroom contrast is textual, and reading it through the marriage-cloth custom gives a vivid, if novel, atonement image.

## Counter-arguments / Related

- This is among the most speculative readings in the episode; Woodmansee and the hosts repeatedly call it "strange." Nothing in Revelation 19 mentions a virginity-proof cloth, and importing that custom into the atonement is the author's typology, not the text's statement.
- The historical practice of public virginity-cloth proof (cf. Deuteronomy 22) was a legal safeguard, not a redemptive rite; the leap from forensic token to substitutionary atonement is interpretive.
- See [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] for the general caution against over-reading ritual parallels.
