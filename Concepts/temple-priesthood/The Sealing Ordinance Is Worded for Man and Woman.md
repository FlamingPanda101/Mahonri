#type/claim

# The Sealing Ordinance Is Worded for Man and Woman

## The Claim
The [[Salty Saints - Why Gay Temple Marriage Will Never Happen|Salty Saints]] panel argues that the temple sealing is not generic "love is love" plus a venue, but a rite whose **liturgy is specifically worded for a man and a woman** and whose purpose is eternal procreative family. [[Tikla Fe]]: "there is not a single sentence in that entire ordinance that would not have to be changed to accommodate a gay union." On this view the impossibility of same-sex sealing is internal to the ordinance, not mere policy that a future leader could relax.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Line-by-line specificity (moderate, testimonial).** The panel (drawing on temple-attending experience, without quoting the wording) asserts the sealing's language is so man-and-woman specific that "every sentence" would require alteration for a same-sex union — i.e., it is not a unisex template.[^1]
2. **Vertical and horizontal sealing (interpretive).** The sealing knits a vertical chain (children to parents to ancestors) and a horizontal man-woman bond; both are framed around the procreative family that the [[Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother|"making a mother"]] reading highlights.[^1]
3. **Authorization, not just children (moderate).** Echoing the infertility answer, the sealing authorizes a couple to "engage in the action that… has the potential" for eternal increase, so its meaning is intact for childless heterosexual couples but absent for a same-sex pairing.[^2]
4. **Protecting women, even against their wishes (interpretive, contested).** Tikla frames the sealing as the Father "protecting women" — even those who "beg not to be protected" — by reserving an ordinance "for you, women," which she ties to a critique of feminizing trends in some wards.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The strongest affirming reply is twofold. First, *liturgy can change*: Latter-day Saint temple wording has been revised repeatedly (most recently 1990 and 2019, including changes to women's covenants and veil language), so "the ordinance is worded this way" is not a proof of doctrinal impossibility — what is worded can be reworded, and the Church itself teaches continuing revelation. The panel's "no future prophet will change it" therefore rests on *doctrinal* conviction (eternal gender, the Proclamation), not on the mere current text. Second, the "love is love is too thin" critique cuts both ways: defenders also owe an account of *why* eternal increase requires sexual complementarity rather than, say, adoption-style sealing, and the panel asserts more than it argues here. The "protecting women who beg not to be protected" framing is also rhetorically risky — it can read as overriding women's own moral agency — and an affirming critic will seize on it. The defensible core is narrower and firmer: *given* the doctrine of eternal gender and procreative exaltation, the present sealing is coherently man-and-woman, and changing it would not be a tweak but a redefinition of the ordinance's theology.

## LDS Relevance
The concept answers a specific in-house anxiety (voiced about figures like Jacob Hansen): the worry that "the older brethren will pass and the next ones will just let it in." The panel's reply is that same-sex sealing is not a loose policy but is woven into the ordinance's wording and purpose, so it is not the kind of thing quietly relaxed. The disciplined statement grounds this in doctrine (eternal gender, the Family Proclamation, the procreative theology of exaltation) and treats the current wording as *expressing* that doctrine — while honestly acknowledging that temple liturgy has changed before, so the argument's real weight is doctrinal, not merely textual. See [[Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother]] and [[The Same-Day Sealing Squeeze Was a Post-Sixties Overcorrection Not Doctrine]] for how the vault distinguishes doctrine from changeable practice.
