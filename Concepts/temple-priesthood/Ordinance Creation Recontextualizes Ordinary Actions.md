# Ordinance Creation Recontextualizes Ordinary Actions

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] and [[Mike Day]] answer the "Joseph cribbed the endowment from Freemasonry" charge with a general principle: **creating an ordinance takes ordinary actions — which carry other meanings or none in another context — and gives them a new meaning.** On this view, even if Joseph repurposed Masonic *forms*, that no more makes Masonry the *source* of the temple than going under water makes swimming the source of baptism. The content and context, not the gestures, are what matter.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The principle stated (rated: the argument).** "The creation of any ordinance takes activities or actions that could have other meanings or no meaning at all in some other context and gives it a new meaning."[^1]
2. **Baptism and the Eucharist as parallels (rated: illustration).** "We've all been underwater before, but baptism gives a special meaning to going underwater in a certain context. We've all eaten bread and drunk wine before… the Eucharist gives a new meaning to behavior that already existed."[^1]
3. **A "dictionary" of forms (rated: the model).** The hosts describe a stock of complex actions used "almost like a dictionary to… take some things and give them a new meaning in the endowment" — so borrowing a vocabulary is not borrowing a doctrine.[^2]
4. **The content is absent from Masonry (rated: contrast).** Elsewhere in the video the hosts stress that Freemasonry's lodge is *not* about deification, eternal life, the atonement, the resurrection, or becoming an heir of God — "the meaning or the ideology is totally different." So the shared element is form, not the load-bearing content. See [[The Masonry Objection Must Locate the Specific Content Not the General Pattern]].

**Steelman of the critic.** A critic responds that this proves too much: any ritual resemblance can be waved away as "just a repurposed form," which makes the borrowing thesis unfalsifiable from the apologist's side. If the *sequence* and *specific symbols* (handclasps, aprons, compass and square, penalties) match Masonry closely and appeared right after Joseph's 1842 initiation, "recontextualization" may understate genuine dependence. The principle is sound in the abstract (rituals do re-signify ordinary acts); the question is how much specific *content*, not just form, was actually shared — which is where the vault's [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]] (chronology) does the heavier lifting.

## Best Quotes
> "The creation of any ordinance takes activities or actions that could have other meanings or no meaning at all in some other context and gives it a new meaning."[^1]

> "We've all eaten bread and drunk wine before… the Eucharist gives a new meaning to behavior that already existed."[^1]

## LDS Connection
This is the conceptual core of the Latter-day Saint reply to the Masonry charge: it reframes the debate from "did the gestures resemble Masonry?" (often, yes) to "where did the *meaning* come from?" — and the meaning (ascent, covenant, deification, atonement) is argued to be ancient and absent from the lodge. It pairs with [[Temple Origins Run Deeper Than Freemasonry]], [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]], and the principle that ritual forms legitimately change over time, [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]].
