# The Initiatory Is a Daily Order of Operations for Drawing Down Grace

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] argues that the temple **initiatory** — the washing-and-anointing blessing that pronounces blessings on the parts of the body in sequence — is not merely a one-time rite but a repeatable **order of operations** for an ordinary day: a fixed sequence by which a person initiates God's power "way faster than we think," the way hitting the right buttons in order starts a jet engine that would not move if you simply shoved the throttle "midstream." Because a priesthood is "an order, a pattern," the ceremony is meant to be *embodied and reflected* daily, not treated as a finished trophy — confusing the ordinance with the reality it patterns turns it into a "false idol." See [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]] and [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]] [^1][^2].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The body-blessing read as a morning sequence — rate: moderate (devotional structure)
McLauchlin walks the initiatory's order onto a daily practice [^3][^4]:
- **Mind** first — the seat of agency, "the portals between heaven and earth"; the first place one can act, and "you are never alone in your thoughts," so deliberately orient the mind to God (prayer as "what is right? what should I do?").
- **Ears** — now "prepared to hear the word of the Lord": take the first impression given, write it down, and act on it immediately, since "an action in obedience to a prompting will always produce far more light than it took to act on it."
- **Eyes** — "single to my glory" ([[D&C 88|D&C 88:67-68]]): draw energy *only* from the light of Christ, not from social approval, lust, or status-seeking.
- **Nose** — breathe in the Spirit.
- **Mouth** — speak the word as it is given (the "true order of prayer"), praying in faith because the words are Heaven's, not one's own.

*Strength: the sequence is a real feature of the ordinance; mapping it to a discipleship routine is a coherent devotional application, not a claim about the rite's original meaning.*

### 2. Grace flows from initiating God by free will — rate: moderate
The engine of the sequence is that **acting first**, by one's own agency, draws down power: "when you initiate the Lord by your own free will and choice, grace flows into you" (he cites D&C 121:45-46, "let virtue garnish thy thoughts unceasingly; then shall thy confidence wax strong... and the Holy Ghost shall be thy constant companion"). The initiatory's *order* matters because the blessings build — like the jet's generator before its throttle — so attempting the higher blessings ("midstream") without the foundation produces burnout [^5]. This is paired with [[Worship and Praise Initiates Gods Power Before Feeling Follows]].

### 3. It coheres with the vault's temple-as-lived-pattern cluster — rate: moderate
The reading is the initiatory counterpart of [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms]] and [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]]: the temple presents a *pattern* meant to "become alive within us" fractally — drawing the self, then family, then others "into one" — rather than a transaction completed at the veil [^2].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic (and many thoughtful Latter-day Saints) will note that the initiatory's sequence was not *given* as a daily self-help order of operations, so reading it that way is a devotional overlay that could be mapped onto almost any ordered list — the "jet engine" and "order of operations (PEMDAS)" analogies are vivid but illustrative, and McLauchlin presents much of this as a personal, real-time discovery. An evangelical critic adds that framing grace as something one's *own* initiating act "draws down" risks sounding synergistic — as if human effort triggers grace — which cuts against sola gratia (the reply, which McLauchlin himself voices, is that one has *no* power to overcome sin alone; the act of worship merely *orients* the person so Christ's power, the only power there is, can flow "without compulsory means"). And the speed claim ("transformation can happen way faster than we think") is experiential testimony, not a generalizable result. A measured Latter-day Saint reply: the practice is offered as application, not as the ordinance's official meaning; its components (orienting the mind, acting on promptings, eyes single to God's glory, true-order prayer) are each independently scriptural; and the "free-will initiation" is about *receptivity*, not merit — the grace and the power remain Christ's.
