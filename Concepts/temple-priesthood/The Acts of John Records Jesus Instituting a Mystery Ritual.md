# The Acts of John Records Jesus Instituting a Mystery Ritual

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the **second** ancient suppression of the temple mystery is visible in a specific document: the [[Acts of John 94-96|Acts of John]], a 2nd-century text in which, before his arrest, Jesus gathers his apostles into a **circle** with himself at the center, leads them in a **responsive ritual** ("they answer 'Amen' after each line"), instructs them to **imitate his gestures**, and tells them to **maintain silence about the mystery**.[^1] [[Dave Butler]] frames this within the broader argument that Jesus ran a *mystery* in the full ancient technical sense — [[Matthew 13.10-11|Matthew 13:11]]'s *mystērion* (initiated knowledge) and Paul's wisdom spoken "among the perfect" (*teleioi*, [[1 Corinthians 2.6-7|1 Cor 2:6-7]]) are initiation language, not generic "things that are hard to understand."[^2] The councils condemned the *Acts of John* and ordered it burned; its first scholarly English translation appeared in **1924** ([[Montague Rhodes James]]), **82 years after** the endowment was introduced in 1842.[^1] Arnold notes the same circle-ritual content recurs *independently* in the Gospel of Bartholomew, the Questions of Bartholomew, and the **Qasr el-Wizz** codex (recovered in the 1960s UNESCO Nubian excavations) — "multiple independent witnesses to the same tradition, none of it accessible to Joseph, and none of it in Freemasonry."[^3] He is explicit that the *sacred specifics* are kept private; the argument rests on **structural parallels** that scholars of the text and endowed members have independently identified, not on his describing the ceremony.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The text exists and says what is claimed.** *Acts of John* 94–96 (the "Round Dance" / "Hymn of Jesus") is a real, datable 2nd-century apocryphon with a circle, antiphonal "Amen," imitative gestures, and a command to silence — anyone can read M. R. James's 1924 translation. *Rating: the primary datum is solid and verifiable.* [^1]
2. **The chronology is load-bearing and checkable.** The endowment dates to 1842; the first English translation to 1924. The parallel content was not available in English to Joseph Smith. *Rating: a genuine evidential asymmetry, not a vague "parallel."* [^1]
3. **Independent attestation reduces coincidence.** The recurrence of the same circle/silence tradition across the Gospel of Bartholomew and the later-recovered Qasr el-Wizz means the *Acts of John* is not a lone outlier — it witnesses a wider suppressed tradition. *Rating: strengthens the case, though the Bartholomew/Qasr el-Wizz parallels are themselves debated in the patristics literature.* [^3]
4. **The "mystery" vocabulary is in the canonical Gospels too.** [[Matthew 13.10-11|Matthew 13:11]] and [[1 Corinthians 2.6-7|1 Cor 2:6-7]] use *mystērion*/*teleioi*, so the claim that Jesus operated an initiatory mystery is not built solely on apocrypha. *Rating: defensible lexically; the technical-initiation sense is contested by mainstream exegetes (see steelman).* [^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic raises several points. (a) **Genre and orthodoxy:** the *Acts of John* is a Gnostic-leaning apocryphon the early church rejected partly for *docetic* Christology; treating its ritual as a window onto authentic apostolic practice (rather than 2nd-century Gnostic invention) begs the question. (b) **The parallel is generic:** circle dances, antiphonal Amens, and commands to secrecy appear across Hellenistic mystery cults; that the endowment also has "circle + silence" does not show *this text* is its ancestor — both could independently draw on common ritual grammar. (c) **Exegesis:** mainstream scholars read *mystērion* in Matthew 13 / 1 Cor 2 as "a previously hidden plan of God now revealed to all believers," and *teleioi* as "the mature," with no esoteric rite implied — so the canonical hook is weaker than Arnold suggests. (d) The **silence about specifics** cuts both ways: if the load-bearing parallels are precisely the parts Arnold won't describe, an outsider cannot independently audit the strongest form of the claim. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument is *not* that the *Acts of John* is canonical or doctrinally reliable, but that it is documentary evidence that a *circle-ritual-with-commanded-silence tradition* was attached to Jesus early, was suppressed, and surfaced in English only after 1842 — so its structural overlap with the endowment is a fact in need of explanation, and the *independent* recurrence (Bartholomew, Qasr el-Wizz) blunts the "generic borrowing" reply.
