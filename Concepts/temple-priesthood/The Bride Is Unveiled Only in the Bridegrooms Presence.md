# The Bride Is Unveiled Only in the Bridegrooms Presence

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the wedding custom of veiling the bride answers "why veil a woman?" — a bride is veiled **until she is presented to the bridegroom**, then **unveiled in his presence** when the covenant is made. She reads [[Genesis 24|Genesis 24:63-67]] (Rebekah) as the paradigm: Rebekah travels *unveiled* with the servant, and **veils herself only on meeting Isaac**, the bridegroom; both "lift up their eyes" to each other; she "lighted off" (Hebrew *naphal*, "fell/jumped") the camel in eager haste. Woodmansee then offers — explicitly as "a tantalizing possibility and nothing more than a possibility" — that recent removals of the temple veil for women (alongside dropping the angel Moroni from newer temple spires) might signal that the Church is *near the Bridegroom's return*: the bride is unveiled "right before the wedding." This is a worked case of [[The Veil Marks Sanctity Not Shame and Is Meant to Be Traversed]] and [[Ancient Hebrew Weddings Are a Three-Phase Type of the Temple]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Rebekah veils only on meeting Isaac.** [[Genesis 24|Genesis 24:64-65]]: Rebekah, having traveled with the servant unveiled, "took a veil and covered herself" the moment she learned the approaching man was her bridegroom. Woodmansee: "she only veils herself when she encounters the bridegroom." *Rating: the text does have Rebekah veil at the meeting; the reading is well-anchored here.*[^1]
2. **Mutual "lifting up of the eyes" and eager descent.** Isaac "lifted up his eyes," Rebekah "lifted up her eyes" — read as the mutual gaze of marriage — and *naphal* ("she lighted off the camel") connotes falling/jumping, "she got off this camel like she meant it," modeling the eagerness with which one should anticipate "our heavenly bridegroom." *Rating: *naphal* does mean "fall"; the "eager descent" reading is plausible and devotional.*[^3]
3. **The father lifts the veil; unveiling = entering the presence.** In the modern traditional ceremony "my father unveiled me and presented me to my husband"; the bride is veiled "up until the point she meets her husband and is presented... then the veil is removed when she is finally in his presence." So unveiling marks the covenant being made / entering the presence. Coheres with the temple veil. *Rating: maps a familiar wedding custom onto the temple; the alignment is the argument.*[^1]
4. **The speculative "near the wedding" reading of temple changes.** Woodmansee ties recent temple changes (veil removed for women; angel-Moroni-less newer temples — "his friends run it up and say he's coming") to nearness of the Second Coming — the bride unveiled "right before the wedding" — while repeatedly bracketing it: "a tantalizing possibility and nothing more," "I can't say I was in that meeting." She also gives the *non*-cynical frame for changes generally: the ceremony *should* change because it teaches "something about the time and place that we're in now." See [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]] and [[Ten Abandoned Temple Practices Show Continuity Through Change]]. *Rating: explicitly flagged as speculation by the speaker; included as such.*[^2][^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Reading sacred meaning into *recent administrative changes* to the temple ceremony is exactly the kind of move that invites the "unfalsifiable / read-the-tea-leaves" objection: a change can be narrativized as eschatological signal *after the fact*, and the cynical alternative Woodmansee herself names (the Church accommodating modern sensibilities about veiling) fits the same data without any Second-Coming inference. Genesis 24 establishes only that *one* bride veiled at *one* meeting; generalizing it into a fixed "unveiled only in his presence" rule, then onto temple policy, is a double extrapolation. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the speaker frames the eschatological reading as bare possibility, not claim, and offers the *principled* defense of ceremonial change (it teaches the present relationship to Christ) as the primary point; the Rebekah datum is genuine, and the value is devotional resonance, held loosely — consistent with the vault's posture that the temple is adaptive, not a frozen one-to-one copy.
