# Shem Was Melchizedek

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Cardon Ellis]] and guests (drawing on **David J. Ridges'** *Old Testament Made Easier* and a **November 1973 *Ensign*** article) argue that **[[Shem]]**, the son of [[Noah]], **is** [[Melchizedek]], the priest-king of Salem in [[Genesis 14.18|Genesis 14]]. The case is cumulative: Melchizedek enters Genesis abruptly "without genealogy" while Shem's later life goes unrecorded — so "Shem's life starts and Melchizedek's life ends," and the two silences fit together. Seven enumerated reasons: (1) Shem inherited the land of **Salem**, which Melchizedek rules; (2) the name **Melchizedek** = "king of righteousness," and Shem reigned in righteousness; (3) Abraham, who stands next to Shem in the patriarchal order, paid tithes to Melchizedek; (4) **Jewish tradition** equates them; (5) [[D&C 84.14|D&C 84:14]] runs the priesthood line from Melchizedek straight back "even till Noah," making Shem the natural next holder; (6) [[Joseph F. Smith]]'s vision (D&C 138) names **Shem** among the great patriarchs but **omits Melchizedek**; (7) *Times and Seasons* (1844) speaks of "Shem, who was Melchizedek."[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Jewish tradition really does identify them.** Targum Pseudo-Jonathan on Genesis 14:18, and Talmudic discussion (b. Nedarim 32b), identify Melchizedek with Shem — so reason (4) is a genuine, ancient interpretive datum, not an LDS invention. *Rating: solid and checkable in the rabbinic corpus.*[^2]
2. **The *Times and Seasons* statement exists.** The 1844 Nauvoo periodical does contain the equation "Shem, who was Melchizedek," giving the identification a 19th-century Latter-day Saint pedigree. *Rating: a real primary-source citation, though Times and Seasons is editorial, not canonized revelation.*[^2]
3. **The priesthood-lineage verse fits.** [[D&C 84.14|D&C 84:14]] traces the priesthood from Melchizedek "through the lineage of his fathers, even till Noah" — with Shem the immediate descendant of Noah, the identification removes an otherwise-puzzling gap between Noah and Melchizedek. *Rating: consistent with the text; the verse permits but does not state the identity.*[^3]
4. **The silence/abruptness is genuinely in the text.** [[Hebrews 7.1-3|Hebrews 7:1-3]] famously stresses Melchizedek's lack of recorded genealogy ("without father, without mother, without descent"); Genesis says nothing of Shem's ministry. The complementary silences are a real feature the claim exploits. *Rating: the textual silences are accurate; reading them as one person is the inference.*[^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Hebrews 7:3 is the strongest objection *against* the identification: it makes Melchizedek's significance precisely that he is **"without genealogy"** — a type of Christ's eternal, non-inherited priesthood — whereas Shem has one of the **best-documented genealogies** in Genesis (the whole point of the Genesis 10-11 tables). Equating them arguably collapses the typology Hebrews is building. Mainstream scholarship treats Melchizedek as a separate Jebusite/Canaanite priest-king of (pre-Israelite) Jerusalem; the rabbinic Shem-identification is a *midrashic* harmonization (motivated partly to keep priesthood within the Noachic line and away from a Canaanite), not a historical datum. And the LDS internal evidence is uneven: D&C 84:14 only requires Melchizedek to *descend from* the Noah line, not to *be* Shem, and Joseph F. Smith's omission of Melchizedek in D&C 138 is an argument from silence. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the identification is offered as a plausible, tradition-backed harmonization — not a doctrine to lose faith over (the hosts stress no one does) — and Hebrews 7:3's "without genealogy" is standardly read as a literary/ritual statement (the record is silent for typological purposes) rather than a metaphysical claim that Melchizedek literally had no parents; on that reading a *named* patriarch can still be presented "without genealogy" in the Melchizedek role.
