# The Bridegroom Drank the Bitter Fourth Cup So the Bride Would Not

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the four cups of wine of the Hebrew wedding feast supply the background for Christ's "bitter cup." A traditional Jewish wedding includes **four cups of wine** with names; the fourth is the **cup of wrath / bitterness / judgment**. In an ordinary wedding no one actually drinks the bitter cup — but [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]], "the bridegroom of us all," drank it "so his bride would not have to." The Book of Mormon makes the move explicit: covenanters are spared "the cup of the wrath of God" precisely because the bridegroom drank it.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Four named cups, one of them bitter

By the time a couple is married "they've had three cups of wine," and "there's a fourth cup that's called the cup of wrath, or the cup of bitterness, or the cup of judgment." In a typical wedding the groom "would not drink a bitter cup — it was just that there was supposed to be a bitter cup," left undrunk.[^3]

### 2. Christ alone drinks it

"What cup did Jesus say he drank? The bitter cup." Gethsemane ("Father, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless... I'll drink it") and the Doctrine and Covenants ("I... drank the bitter cup") identify Christ as the one who, as bridegroom, drinks the cup of wrath in the bride's place.[^4]

### 3. The Book of Mormon spells out the substitution

- [[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5]]: the covenanters enter the covenant "that we may not drink out of the cup of the wrath of God" — i.e. the cup the bridegroom drank for them.[^5]
- [[3 Nephi 11.10-11|3 Nephi 11]]: the risen Christ announces himself as having "drunk out of that bitter cup which the Father hath given me," coming as the light in the dark to his Nephite bride — "all Hebrew wedding."[^6]

> **Steelman:** The "cup of wrath/judgment" is a deep biblical image (Psalm 75:8; Isaiah 51:17; Jeremiah 25:15), and Jesus's Gethsemane "cup" plus the Book of Mormon's "cup of the wrath of God" form a tight, text-internal substitution pattern: the Mediator drinks what the covenant people are spared. Whether or not a literal fourth "bitter cup" was standard at weddings, the cup-of-wrath-borne-by-the-bridegroom theology is scripturally anchored.

## Best Quotes

> "It's even built into this ancient tradition that the bridegroom would drink the bitter cup so his bride would not have to." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^2]

> "The only one that we know of is the bridegroom of us all, who drank the bitter cup." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^3]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The claim that a named "cup of wrath" is one of four standard wedding cups is asserted from popular accounts; the four cups are firmly attested for **Passover** (the Seder), and mapping a "bitter/wrath" fourth cup specifically onto the *wedding* is the contested step.
- A critic can accept the biblical cup-of-wrath theology while rejecting the wedding-custom framing as the source of it.
