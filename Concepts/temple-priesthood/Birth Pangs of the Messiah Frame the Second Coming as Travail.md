# Birth Pangs of the Messiah Frame the Second Coming as Travail

#type/claim

## The Claim
Presenter **Tia** argues that scripture deliberately figures the **Second Coming and the tribulation of the last days as a woman's labor** — not as the sports metaphors she says dominate modern discourse. The key terms: the Hebrew **"chevlei mashiach,"** literally "the birth pangs of the Messiah," names the eschatological tribulation as labor pains; and [[1 Thessalonians 5.3|1 Thessalonians 5:3]] explicitly says the day of the Lord comes "as travail upon a woman with child... and they shall not escape." The panel reads the whole experiential structure of labor onto the last days: the **"transition" phase** of labor (a real obstetric term for the most intense stage) maps the transition into the Millennium; the unpredictability of a due date maps "no man knows the hour"; the inability to stop labor once begun maps the inevitability of the Coming; and the laboring mother's terror-mixed-with-longing maps the "great and terrible" day. Christ's Gethsemane surrender is read as a mother "laying down her life" to deliver.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Chevlei mashiach" is a real rabbinic category.** The "birth pangs of the Messiah" (*chevlei [shel] mashiach*) is a genuine concept in Jewish eschatology for the tribulations preceding the messianic age — so the panel's central term is authentic, not coined. *Rating: the term and its eschatological meaning are well attested in Jewish tradition.*[^1]
2. **[[1 Thessalonians 5.3|1 Thessalonians 5:3]] is explicitly there.** Paul's own words pair "thief in the night" (v. 2) with "as travail upon a woman with child... they shall not escape" (v. 3) — the labor metaphor for the Coming is in the canonical text, and the panel's observation that v. 3 is "the one we don't hear as much" is fair. *Rating: the scriptural datum is exact and load-bearing.*[^2]
3. **The New Testament uses birth-travail eschatologically elsewhere.** Jesus calls the signs "the beginning of sorrows" (Greek *ōdines*, birth-pangs; Matthew 24:8), Romans 8:22 has creation "groaning and travailing," and John 16:21 uses a woman in labor for the disciples' sorrow-to-joy — a real, repeated biblical pattern the panel is drawing on (even if it cites mainly 1 Thessalonians). *Rating: strong canonical footing for travail-as-eschatology.*
4. **"Transition" is a genuine stage of labor.** The panel's pivot on the obstetric term "transition" (the intense late stage before pushing) is medically real, lending the "transition to the Millennium" reading a true point of contact. *Rating: the term is real; the eschatological mapping is the interpretive move.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic grants the metaphor is biblical but resists the panel's maximalism: Paul's simile makes a **single** point (the Coming is *sudden and inescapable*, like the onset of labor), not a sustained allegory in which every clinical stage of childbirth (transition, the due date, the relax-or-stall dynamic) secretly encodes eschatology. Reading "transition" and unstoppability as deliberate Coded eschatology over-reads a comparison into an allegory. The panel also pivots through personal childbirth memory and folk obstetrics, and even-handedly notes some labors are traumatic — underscoring that the resonance is devotional and experiential, not exegetical. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the core is undeniably scriptural (the *chevlei mashiach* category; [[1 Thessalonians 5.3|1 Thess 5:3]]'s explicit travail; the wider *ōdines*/groaning pattern), so figuring the last days as labor is *the Bible's own metaphor*, not the panel's invention; the extended mapping is offered as meditation that makes a fearful doctrine humane ("the world feels so pregnant and ready for him"), and is presented as such rather than as proof.
