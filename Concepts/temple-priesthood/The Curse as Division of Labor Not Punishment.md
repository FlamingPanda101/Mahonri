# The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection: "Pharaoh was cursed as pertaining to the priesthood" ([[Abraham 1.21-27|Abraham 1]]) means the descendants of [[Ham]] are under a punitive curse barring them from the priesthood — the standard 19th-century "[[curse of Canaan]] / curse of Ham" used to justify slavery and, later, the LDS [[priesthood ban]].[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The pro-slavery reading was the cultural default, but not Joseph Smith's (rated: strong, historical).** [[Don Bradley]] grants that in Joseph Smith's time the [[Genesis 10|Genesis]] curse of Ham (whose descendant Mizraim/Egypt links Pharaoh to Ham) was "probably the most commonly-used text from the Bible to support slavery."[^1] The question is whether Joseph adopted that meaning — and Bradley says no.

2. **The decisive primary source: the Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language (rated: strong, primary document).** Produced alongside the Book of Abraham translation in Kirtland (partly in Joseph Smith's handwriting, on the Joseph Smith Papers), it describes Pharaoh as "a prince of the royal blood, a true descendant from Ham, the son of Noah, an inheritor of the kingly blessings from under the hand of Noah, but not according to the priestly blessing, which fell upon Shem from under the hand of Noah."[^2] Bradley says this "has never been used before in discussions about the Book of Abraham and the curse." It defines the "curse" as *lacking the priestly line but holding the kingly line* — a stewardship distribution, not a penalty. See [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]]. *Steelman:* the relationship of the Kirtland Egyptian Papers to the canonical Book of Abraham is genuinely disputed among scholars — some see them as a post-hoc reverse-engineering project, not Joseph's interpretive key — so leaning on them to fix Abraham 1's meaning is contestable. Bradley brackets that controversy ("tangential to what we're talking about"), which is a real limitation of the argument.

3. **Ancient priesthood was a lineage office, like kingship (rated: strong, biblical).** Anciently "priesthood meant literally priesthood... being a priest," a vocation alongside farmer, soldier, prophet, king. Kings and priests were *different* lineal offices: David (king, line of Judah) could not be high priest; Aaron's line held the priesthood; "Abiathar was the high priest," not the king.[^3] So a line "not according to the priestly blessing" simply has a *different* inherited role (kingship), exactly as the document says.

4. **It is stewardship, not deprivation (rated: moderate, interpretive).** "It's framing it in terms of division of labor or different stewardships. Not punishment. ... You are the priests here and you are the kings here." Bradley likens it to the Indian caste system or tribal divisions in the [[House of Israel]] (Ephraim and Manasseh) — different lines, different responsibilities — a frame natural to the ancient world but alien to modern social mobility.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "An inheritor of the kingly blessings from under the hand of Noah, but not according to the priestly blessing, which fell upon Shem from under the hand of Noah."[^2]

> "It's framing it in terms of division of labor or different stewardships. Not punishment. ... Here is your role and here is your role. You are the priests here and you are the kings here."[^4]

> "Anciently, priesthood meant literally priesthood. It meant being a priest. ... Prophets and priests were not the same back then."[^3]
