# Water Is a Better Symbol of Christ Than Wine

## The Claim
[[David Sword of Laben]] makes the "hot take" that **water is a superior symbol of Christ to wine** — not merely an acceptable substitute forced by Word-of-Wisdom policy. Water carries a far larger "Venn diagram" of scriptural meaning that overlaps the sacrament, whereas Old Testament wine is mostly the *cup of wrath*. So Latter-day Saint sacramental water is "better this way," not a compromise.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Water = Jehovah and salvation (strong, scriptural).** Living water offered to the Samaritan woman ([[John 4.10-14|John 4]]) and "rivers of living water" ([[John 7.37-38|John 7]]); water from the rock with Jehovah standing on it ([[Exodus 17.6|Exodus 17:6]]) which "was Christ" ([[1 Corinthians 10.4|1 Corinthians 10:4]]); the "fountain of living waters" (Jeremiah 2; 17). Water repeatedly *is* Jehovah/salvation.[^1]
2. **Multivalence is a feature (interpretive).** As a basic element, water means chaos (the sea/deep), life (agrarian survival), and purification (Levitical washings) — "you would expect of such a basic thing… multifaceted meaning," giving it a bigger overlap with the sacrament's meaning than wine's narrow range.[^2]
3. **Wilderness-salvation typology (moderate).** Water (from thirst), manna/quail (from hunger), and the brass serpent (from sickness) are read as a salvation triad pointing to Christ; the sacramental water keys into the same Exodus pattern, and the Red Sea crossing is read as passing through a veil (Jehovah).[^3]
4. **Wine's OT valence cuts the other way (strong, scriptural).** "Almost always [wine in the OT] is… a cup of wrath, of indignation, of shame… drink the dregs of a bitter cup." That is "not really the sorts of things we think about when we take of the sacramental wine," so the wine-blood connection is "somewhat broken."[^4]

## Apologetic Engagement
A critic can press several points. First, the *New Testament* institution is explicit: Christ blessed the cup and said "this is my blood" (Matthew 26; Mark 14), and the wine→blood symbolism (the "fruit of the vine," the trodden winepress of Isaiah 63 / Revelation 19) is a deliberate, rich typology — so wine is hardly a *weak* symbol of the atonement. Second, the panel's method (counting symbolic "overlap") is impressionistic; one could equally amass wine/blood/atonement passages. Third, the argument risks proving too much: if water out-symbolizes wine, why did Christ institute the cup with wine at all? The honest position (which the hosts mostly hold) is that water is a *fully adequate and richly meaningful* emblem, not necessarily a *superior* one — and "it mattereth not" ([[D&C 27.5|D&C 27:5]]) already secures the legitimacy without needing the stronger comparative claim.

## LDS Relevance
The concept reframes a perceived liability (LDS use of water) as a devotional strength: the sacramental water connects to living-water Christology, Exodus salvation, and purification, deepening reflection at the sacrament table. It pairs naturally with the episode's capstone — that the emblems *sustain* the new life of baptism (see [[The Sacrament Sustains the New Life of Baptism]]) — and with the revelation that the emblem's substance "mattereth not" so long as it is taken "with an eye single to my glory."
