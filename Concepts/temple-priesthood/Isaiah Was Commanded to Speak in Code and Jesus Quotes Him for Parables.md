# Isaiah Was Commanded to Speak in Code and Jesus Quotes Him for Parables
#type/claim

## The Claim
Isaiah's prophetic commission (Isaiah 6:9-10) explicitly directs him to speak so that the people "hear... but understand not" and "see... but perceive not" — that is, to deliver his message in deliberate code, intelligible to the temple-literate initiate but opaque to the outsider. Centuries later, when Jesus is asked why he teaches the crowds in parables, he quotes exactly this Isaiah passage (Matthew 13:10-17), explaining that "it is given" to the disciples "to know the mysteries of the kingdom" but to others it is not. This commission-to-encode grounds the whole "written in code" reading of Isaiah and the Book of Mormon: prophets intentionally veil temple content for those with eyes to see.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The commission to obscure.** Per [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] and [[Mike Day]], Isaiah 6:9-10 commissions the prophet to make the people hear without understanding — a mandate to speak in veiled, layered language rather than plain prose. This is presented as authorial license for prophets to encode.
- **Jesus invokes it for parables.** In Matthew 13:10-17 Jesus quotes this very Isaiah text to explain the parable method: the initiated disciples are given the "mysteries of the kingdom," while the crowd receives the veiled form. See [[Matthew 13.10-17]]. The New Testament thus treats Isaiah's code-commission as a live hermeneutical principle, not a one-off.
- **It licenses the temple-code readings.** This grounds the broader Stick of Joseph project of reading Isaiah 3-6 and Lehi/Nephi's vision as temple "code" written by initiates for temple-literate readers (see [[Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah]] and [[Lehi's Dream Encodes the Three Temple Rooms by Hebrew Name-Puns]]). If prophets were commanded to encode, then dense punning and layered allusion are expected features, not over-readings.
- **Steelman / caveats.** Mainstream exegesis reads Isaiah 6:9-10 not as a how-to-write-in-cipher instruction but as a statement of *judicial hardening* — the message will be rejected, so its preaching effectively dulls the hearers — a theme about the people's obstinacy, not a literary technique. Likewise Jesus's citation is usually read as explaining the *effect* of parables on a hardened audience, not as endorsing esoteric encoding. So the passage can fund the general point that prophetic speech operates on two levels (revealing to some, concealing from others) without licensing the more aggressive Hebrew-pun "temple code" decodings, which remain the contested step. The defensible core is that scripture itself frames prophetic communication as deliberately stratified between initiate and outsider.
