# The Keys of Peter Bridge the Living and the Dead

## The Claim

[[Luke Hansen]] reads [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18]]–19 not merely *defensively* (the church survives death) but **positively** (the church is given power *over* the boundary of death). His exegetical chain:

- "Gates of hell" is *gates of Hades* — and Hades just means "the realm of the dead, the place where spirits reside that aren't living on earth." Gates are **defensive structures**, so the picture is "the barrier separating the realm of the living from the realm of the dead."[^1]
- Immediately after, Jesus hands Peter **the keys of the kingdom** with power to *bind on earth and in heaven, loose on earth and in heaven*. Hansen connects the keys to the gates: the keys are the authority to "bridge and prevail against the separation between the realm of the living and the dead" (a link he says is reinforced in Revelation).[^2]
- Therefore the verse is "**the power that the church has**… not the persistence of the church on earth throughout all time." And that power is *concretely exercised* in Latter-day Saint practice: [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead|baptism for the dead]] (a living proxy receives the ordinance for the deceased) and **eternal marriage / sealing** that "does not end at death." His pointed observation: Catholics and Orthodox *don't* teach this — "you can't get baptized after you're dead. Marriage does end" (a sacrament not efficacious after death).[^3]

This pairs with his Great Apostasy account: even when the *fullness* of authority left the earth, the apostles retained the keys as "judges of Israel" and continued "doing work on the other side of the veil," so the church "still existed in other senses."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The gates→keys juxtaposition is exegetically real — rate: moderate
Matthew genuinely places "gates of Hades" (v. 18) immediately beside the bind/loose keys (v. 19). Reading the two as a single unit — death's barrier, then the keys that act upon it — is a defensible literary observation, and it gives the *bind/loose* language a definite referent (the living-dead boundary) rather than leaving it as generic disciplinary authority.

### 2. It is the *positive* counterpart to an already-attested *defensive* reading — rate: strong as a complement
The vault's [[The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church]] establishes the "death will not imprison the church" reading (gates lock in; resurrection bursts them). Hansen's move extends the *same* lexical base (Hades = death's realm) in the opposite direction: not only does death fail to hold the church, the church holds keys *over* death. The two readings reinforce rather than compete.

### 3. It does real apologetic work — rate: strong (internally)
Tying baptism for the dead and eternal sealing to *Peter's keys* reframes them from "strange LDS add-ons" into the **direct cash-value** of a verse all sides revere, and turns the absence of such ordinances in Catholic/Orthodox practice into evidence that those traditions lost what the keys were *for*. It connects to the universally-attested descent tradition in [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested]] and the dispensational key-transfer model in [[Priesthood Keys Frame the Seven Dispensations]].

### Steelman of the creedal/Catholic reply — rate: this is the strongest counter
"Bind and loose" in its Second-Temple Jewish setting standardly means **rabbinic/disciplinary authority to forbid and permit (and to retain or remit sins)**, not necromantic access to the dead; Matthew 18:18 gives the *same* bind/loose formula to the whole assembly in an unambiguously *disciplinary* context, which tells against the living-dead-bridge gloss. The Catholic answer to "you don't reach the dead" is that the Church *does* — through prayer for the dead, indulgences, and the communion of saints — without needing *proxy baptism*, and that [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead|1 Corinthians 15:29]] is too obscure to bear a sacrament. Hansen's own framing concedes the bridging reading is "not like the official church's understanding," so it is offered as a *plausible* construal, not a settled one.

## Best Quotes

> "Gates separating realm of the living from the dead. And then what does Jesus do right after that?… He's given the keys of the kingdom that include a-binding on earth and in heaven, e-loosing on earth and in heaven. So Jesus is connecting the keys that Peter has to bridging and prevailing against the separation between the realm of the living and the dead." [^2]

> "It's not the persistence of the church on earth throughout all time that Jesus is talking about. It's the power that the church has to bridge the realm of the living and the dead." [^3]

> "Catholics and Orthodox don't teach this. Like you can't get baptized after you're dead. Marriage does end." [^3]
