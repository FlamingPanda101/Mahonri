# Eating the Sacrifice Establishes Kinship With God

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler|David Butler]] argues, drawing on **W. Robertson Smith's *Lectures on the Religion of the Semites***, that in the ancient Semitic world a family's animals were not mere property but *kin*, so to slaughter and eat one was a "solemn, serious, sacrificial act" — and that "one of our kinsfolk is our God." Therefore eating the covenant meal (especially eating the sacrificed animal of the family, not just bread) was a "very strong statement of *this is the same family — we are the same people now*." This illuminates the betrothal meal of the Hebrew wedding (two families becoming one through a shared meal) and, ultimately, **eating the flesh and blood of the Lord**, the great *goel* (kinsman-redeemer) who is simultaneously the sacrificed animal, the sacrifice, and the bridegroom.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Family animals are kin, not commodities (Robertson Smith).** Butler relays Robertson Smith's anthropology of Semitic (incl. Arab) religion: the family animal "is not just a goat like you would go and buy a slab of goat meat down at the Kroger — it is a member of our family," so eating it "is a real sacrifice in every sense... a holy act." *Rating: a faithful summary of a real (if dated, 1889) classic in the anthropology of sacrifice.*[^1]
2. **Deuteronomy's innovation: separating eating from sacrifice.** Butler notes "one of the innovations of Deuteronomy is it separates eating from sacrifice, maybe for the first time" — implying that *before* that reform, ordinary meat-eating *was* sacrificial/kinship-forming. *Rating: tracks the scholarly view that Deuteronomy 12 desacralizes profane slaughter; a real datum.*[^1]
3. **God is in the kinship group.** The crux: "who else, by the way, Robertson Smith would say is one of our kinsfolk? Our God... he is also in the same kinship group." So the shared sacrificial meal asserts kinship *with God*, not merely between two human families — the theological engine behind covenant meals. *Rating: Butler's extension; Robertson Smith does discuss the deity as a member of the kin-group in totemic sacrifice, so the move has warrant.*[^2]
4. **Christ as the kinsman-sacrifice.** This frames the Eucharistic eating of Christ's "flesh and blood": he is "our great *goel*, our great kinsman," and "not just the animal sacrifice — he is the sacrifice, and he is also the bridegroom." The kinship-meal logic thus unifies sacrament, atonement, and wedding. See [[Two Covenant Meals - The Betrothal Meal and the Wedding Feast]] and [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]]. *Rating: a typological synthesis; coherent but interpretive.*[^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Robertson Smith's totemic theory of sacrifice — that sacrifice originates in eating a kindred animal to commune with the deity — is a *contested, century-old* anthropological hypothesis, criticized since Évans-Pritchard and largely superseded; building a reading of the Eucharist on it inherits that fragility. The Gospel of John's "eat my flesh" discourse and the Synoptic Last Supper have their own covenant-blood backdrop (Exodus 24; Jeremiah 31) that needs no totemic kinship theory, and "God is in our kin-group" is precisely the animistic premise modern scholars resist reading into Israelite religion. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the point does not require Robertson Smith's *origin* theory to be correct, only that *covenant meals signified incorporation into a shared family*, which the biblical covenant-blood material independently supports (see [[Ancient Covenant Pattern and the Blood of the Covenant]]); the kinsman-redeemer (*goel*) frame for Christ is biblical (Ruth; Isaiah), so the synthesis stands on canonical legs even if the anthropology is held loosely.
