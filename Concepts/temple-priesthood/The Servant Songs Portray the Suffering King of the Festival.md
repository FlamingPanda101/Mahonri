# The Servant Songs Portray the Suffering King of the Festival

## The Claim
Following Ivan Engnell, [[John Eaton]] reads the Servant Songs of Isaiah 40–55 (Isaiah 42, 49, 50, and 52:13–53:12) as portraying the **king of the festival** — the Davidic monarch who, in the festal drama, suffers, is humiliated, descends, and is redeemed, whose redemption brings about the redemption of Israel — rather than a single named historical individual.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The king, not a specific king (rated: Eaton/Engnell; cult-functional).** Engnell argued in the 1940s that "the servant songs in Isaiah are about the king, the Davidic king… set in the fall festival." Eaton (who said Engnell "made terrible arguments for what I think is a right idea") affirms it: the servant is "the king of the feast" — the same figure who "goes out, defeats the forces of chaos, comes back," then suffers and is redeemed.[^1][^2]
2. **Psalm 22 as the liturgical backdrop (rated: textual parallel).** The drama's suffering-king scene ("my enemies are surrounding me… they've parted my garments") matches Psalm 22, "literally the backdrop to what Catholics call the passion narrative."[^3]
3. **Rival readings acknowledged (rated: fair survey).** The hosts note the common Jewish reading (the servant = Israel — "a little odd because the servant is said to *save* Israel"), Claus Westermann's "the servant is Moses," and the autobiographical "Isaiah himself" reading — and present Eaton's royal reading as "a fairly common view at this point."[^4]
4. **Historicized in the exile (rated: Eaton).** Eaton suggests the "dramatic mysteries of royal suffering in the old rites" seemed to "come real" to the prophets when "the cutting off of the dynasty and the long imprisonment of the virtually innocent Jehoiachin" occurred — the last Davidic king enslaved in Babylon.[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** The royal/cultic interpretation of the Servant is one option among several long-debated readings (corporate Israel, a prophetic figure, Moses, an eschatological individual); it is not consensus, and the underlying festal-drama framework is itself the contested Mowinckel-Engnell program. Christians traditionally read Isaiah 53 messianically of Christ, and many critical scholars read the Servant as collective Israel. The data (the Servant suffers and is exalted) are agreed; the "king of the festival" identification is Eaton's school.

## Best Quotes
> "Engnell has this hypothesis… the servant songs in Isaiah are about the king, the Davidic king, and they are set in the fall festival."[^1]

> "His role is to be the one who is defeated, suffers, and then is redeemed, and his redemption brings about the redemption of Israel — and that's what the servant songs are about."[^2]

## LDS Connection
