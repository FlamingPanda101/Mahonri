## The Point
The early Christian baptismal liturgy included a washing and an anointing with oil — both before and after immersion — along with symbolic white robes. These washing-and-anointing ceremonies, together with the formalized Eucharistic liturgy, are the elements most often noticed by Latter-day Saints as resembling aspects of modern temple worship. Both Grey and Ellison treat the resemblance as real but methodologically delicate.

## Evidence
1. In the fourth-century rite the candidate "received an anointing with oil" before entering the font, and after immersion received "the laying on of hands with another anointing of oil" and was clothed in a white robe.
2. In Syria, Christians emphasized the prebaptismal anointing; female deacons assisted in the anointing and baptism of women (since baptism involved disrobing).
3. Third-century Syrian baptismal rites of anointing and washing are documented in the Didascalia, the Gospel of Philip, and the Odes of Solomon.
4. At Dura Europos the baptistery had a niche for anointing oil and a veiled font, supporting reconstruction of anointing-then-immersion sequences.
5. Grey lists "a washing and anointing at baptism, prayer with uplifted hands, and intercessory rites on behalf of the deceased" as ceremonies that "resemble aspects of modern Latter-day Saint temple worship."

## Best Quotes
> "It is true that early Christians did have ceremonies that resemble aspects of modern Latter-day Saint temple worship (such as a washing and anointing at baptism, prayer with uplifted hands, and intercessory rites on behalf of the deceased), and a comparison between these ritual practices can be mutually informing." [^1]

> "There are intriguing resemblances between Latter-day Saint practices and elements of ancient Christian rituals — especially in the ancient baptismal liturgy (its washing and anointing ceremonies and symbolic white robes)." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is one of the clearest acknowledged points of contact between ancient Christian practice and Latter-day Saint temple ordinances; the authors urge that the comparison "can be mutually informing" if handled with care (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). [^1]
