# Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother

## The Claim
[[Tikla Fe]] argues that the word **"matrimony" derives from *mater* (mother)** and therefore names a "holy order of making a mother." On this reading the temple sealing — the one ordinance the Church calls "the holy order of matrimony" — is *intrinsically* ordered toward motherhood: the entire male covenant path (priesthood from age 12 onward) exists to make a man "worthy to be authorized" to "unlock a woman's potential for motherhood." A man-and-man union, she concludes, cannot be "matrimony" because it cannot make a mother.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The etymology (partly sound).** "Matrimony" does descend from Latin *matrimonium*, rooted in *mater* (mother) — Tikla's core lexical claim is correct. She contrasts it with masculine-facing words (mankind, actors, kingdom) that encompass both sexes, noting matrimony is the rare feminine-facing word for an institution of men and women.[^1]
2. **The covenant path as mother-making (interpretive).** She reads the priesthood/temple sequence as a "pretty extensive training process to become a good father" culminating in "the pinnacle ordinance," which is named for a mother — "the path for how to be worthy to be initiated into the holy order of making a mother."[^2]
3. **Motherhood as orientation, not only outcome (moderate).** Anticipating the infertile-couple objection, she argues a woman is initiated into "the order of a mother" when she begins the monthly "blood sacrifice" toward that potential, so the ordinance is about *authorization toward* family, not a guarantee of children — which is why childless heterosexual sealings still fit.[^3]
4. **Coheres with LDS theology of gender (moderate).** The reading dovetails with the Restoration's eternal-gender and Heavenly-Mother theology (see [[Anthropomorphic God Grounds Heavenly Mother and Eternal Families]]), where exaltation is becoming like Heavenly Parents — a Father and a Mother.

## Apologetic Engagement
The etymology is **half right and the inference is the weak link**. *Matrimonium* is indeed from *mater*, but the standard explanation of the form is "the legal state/condition of *motherhood*" — i.e., the institution that made a woman a lawful mother and secured the legitimacy/inheritance of children — *not* literally "the act of *making* a mother" by a male agent; the suffix *-monium* denotes a state or action, not "making by a man." (Compare *patrimony*, *mater*-less, from *pater* = an inheritance from a father; the *-mony* there clearly means "estate/condition," not "making a father.") So the word does encode a deep link between marriage and motherhood — a genuinely striking point — but it will not, by itself, *prove* that a same-sex sealing is conceptually impossible; that conclusion rests on LDS *doctrine* (eternal gender, the procreative purpose of sealing in the Family Proclamation, the wording of the ordinance), for which the etymology is illustrative rather than probative. An affirming interlocutor would also note that civil "matrimony" has long included infertile and elderly couples without anyone calling it a contradiction, so "can it make a mother?" is not how the word's boundaries have actually been drawn. The strongest version of Tikla's case therefore leads with the doctrine and uses the etymology as a memorable illustration, not as the load-bearing premise.

## LDS Relevance
For Latter-day Saints the concept offers a vivid, teachable link between the sealing and the theology of eternal family/Heavenly Parents — the temple's "matrimony" really does point at motherhood, and at exaltation as becoming like a Father *and* a Mother. The disciplined apologetic move is to (a) state the doctrinal basis first (the Proclamation's man-woman, procreative definition; the gendered sealing liturgy — see [[The Sealing Ordinance Is Worded for Man and Woman]]), (b) present the *mater* etymology as illustration, and (c) avoid overclaiming that a word-origin *settles* the question, since that overreach is easily punctured. Paired with [[The Patriarchal Order Is the Order of Fatherhood Not Male Rule]], it frames the whole temple covenant path as oriented toward becoming a good father and a mother.
