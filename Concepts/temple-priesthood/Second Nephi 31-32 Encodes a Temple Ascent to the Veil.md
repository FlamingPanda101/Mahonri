# Second Nephi 31-32 Encodes a Temple Ascent to the Veil

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler]] argues that [[Nephi]]'s discourse on the "doctrine of Christ" in [[2 Nephi 31]]-32 is a **veiled temple-ascent text**: read with temple eyes, its sequence maps onto entering the temple, passing through ritual stages, and arriving at the veil to meet Christ. The path: enter by **the gate** (2 Nephi 31:9, 17 — baptism), follow/repent/be baptized, then **speak with the tongue of angels** (31:13-14), press forward with faith, hope, and charity (31:20), **knock** and enter (32:4 — "ye receive not because ye ask not... neither do ye knock"), receive the **Holy Ghost** which "will show you all things what ye should do" (32:5), until **Christ manifests himself in the flesh** (32:6) — at which point Nephi says the Spirit stops his utterance and he can say no more (32:7).[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The gate-and-path language is in the text.** Nephi explicitly frames salvation as entering "in by the way" through a "strait and narrow path" after baptism ([[2 Nephi 31]]:17-20). Butler reads the gate/path as temple architecture (the entry and the ascent), which fits Nephi's own spatial metaphor. *Rating: the gate/path imagery is Nephi's own; the temple gloss is interpretive but textually anchored.*[^1]
2. **"Speaking with the tongue of angels" is ritual, not merely missionary.** Butler reads 31:13-14's "tongue of angels" as the initiate acting the part of an angel — repeating what the Lord says at a specific place — which coheres with the broader vault thesis that putting on priestly garments means "doing angel things" (see [[The Garment Is the Veil of the Temple and the Body of the Lord]]). *Rating: a defensible reading; "tongue of angels" is genuinely unusual phrasing inviting explanation.*[^1]
3. **Knock-and-enter and the light behind the veil.** In 32:4 Nephi says people "are not brought into the light" because they "ask not, neither do they knock." Butler: "the light is behind the veil," so knocking → entering → light is an ascent to the veil. Then [[2 Nephi 32.5-6]]: the initiate receives the Holy Ghost and Christ manifests himself "in the flesh." *Rating: the knock/light/veil sequence is a coherent typological reading; the "behind the veil" identification is Butler's.*[^2]
4. **The deliberate cutoff matches temple secrecy.** At the climax Nephi says "the Spirit stoppeth mine utterance, and I am left to... I cannot say more" (32:7) — precisely where, on Butler's reading, the temple content would begin. He notes this parallels how the Book of Mormon "says the right things, but veiled," so that "you read it again... and you see the ritual of the temple is almost on every page." *Rating: the abrupt stop at the moment of divine encounter is real and suggestive.*[^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The natural reading of 2 Nephi 31-32 is a straightforward summary of the gospel — faith, repentance, baptism, the gift of the Holy Ghost, enduring to the end — with no temple ceremony in view; "the doctrine of Christ" is explicitly named as such (31:21). Critics will say the temple ascent is *read into* the chapter by a reader already holding the 1842 endowment, the very pattern-projection [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] warns against; and that the most economical account of any resemblance is the standard secular one — Joseph Smith later "invented a ceremony" and devotees retroject it ("so convenient... that it lines up with our current," as the hosts themselves joke). A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument does not claim the *full* endowment is spelled out, only that Nephi's *sequence* (gate, angelic speech, knocking, light, encounter, deliberate silence) is an unusually temple-shaped way to describe conversion — and that the silence falls exactly at the threshold of the divine presence, which is hard to explain if no esoteric content is being guarded. The parallelomania caution argues for restraint, not refutation. See [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]] for the broader case.
