# Reading Abraham 1 Without the Priesthood-Ban Lens (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection: [[Abraham 1.21-27|Abraham 1]]'s phrase "cursed as pertaining to the priesthood" is a racial priesthood restriction — it is the scriptural charter for the LDS [[priesthood ban]] on men of African descent. Therefore the Book of Abraham is a racist text and Joseph Smith built the ban into scripture.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Anachronism: there was no priesthood ban when Abraham was translated (rated: strong, historical).** [[Don Bradley]]'s central move is methodological. The Book of Abraham was translated in 1835; the priesthood ban does not appear until the [[Brigham Young]] era (1852) "and for years afterward, there is no priesthood ban. And so when we're looking at Abraham 1 through the lens of the priesthood ban, we're looking at it through a lens that Joseph Smith didn't have."[^1] To read 1835 through 1852–1978 is to commit the historian's cardinal error. See [[Chronological Prejudice Against the Past]].

2. **Distinguish *use* from *meaning* (rated: strong).** Bradley concedes the phrase was "certainly used as a scriptural rationale for the priesthood ban on men of African descent."[^2] But "what did this phrase mean to Joseph Smith? And what did it not mean? We can tell what it did and didn't mean to Joseph Smith because we have his actions and we have other things that he said."[^3] Later weaponization of a text does not fix its original sense. *Steelman:* a critic ([[Paul Reeve]]'s historiography is relevant here) can grant the anachronism yet still hold that the *text itself* — independent of Joseph's intent — supplied the racialized raw material later generations needed, so the apologetic does not absolve the text of downstream harm. Bradley's reply is the document evidence (see [[The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment]]) plus Joseph's contrary actions (see [[Joseph Smith Ignored Race-Based Scriptural Distinctions]]).

3. **The text has built-in reversals (rated: moderate).** Abraham 1 is "radical" and contains "a number of reversals": God seeks the seeker (not arbitrary election), Abraham is the one almost sacrificed (not just Isaac), and — Bradley argues — the curse is a kingly blessing, not slavery.[^4] Reading it flatly as restriction misses its inversive structure.

## Best Quotes

> "We're looking at it through a lens that Joseph Smith didn't have. ... That's not how he was looking at it."[^1]

> "It was certainly used as a scriptural rationale for the priesthood ban on men of African descent. For sure. ... But there is something that puts this in a completely different framework."[^2]

> "What did this phrase mean to Joseph Smith? And what did it not mean? We can tell... because we have his actions."[^3]
