# We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that the temple garment's purpose is **not modesty** ("that was not the purpose at all... it's not the purpose now") but **name-bearing**: the wearer literally takes upon themselves the name of Jesus Christ. [^1] The hinge is the Hebrew **Tetragrammaton, Y-H-W-H** — the name of Christ as the God of the Old Testament (rendered "Lord" / "Jehovah"), four consonants of which two repeat. [^2] The Israelite high priest wore a golden plate engraved **"Holiness to the Lord," bearing the Name**, on his miter ([[Exodus 28.30|Exodus 28]]); since LDS temple clothing is patterned on the biblical priestly robes, "he literally wore the name of Jehovah... he wore the name of Christ on his temple clothing — well, I'm suggesting we do the same." [^3] This decodes the [[D&C 109]] dedicatory phrase that the Lord will **"put his name upon"** his servants (v. 22, 79): a 2024 First Presidency letter teaches that the garment "reminds us of the veil in the temple," and "that veil is symbolic of Jesus Christ," with the garment carrying its own **sacred markings** — so putting on the garment is putting on a symbol of Christ's name. [^4] Bradley pairs the Lord's *putting* with our *taking*: "they're two sides of the same thing — if the Lord is putting his name upon us, we have to receive it," which is the covenant renewed every week in the sacrament ([[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5]], to "take upon us the name of Christ"). [^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The high-priestly name-plate is straight biblical data.** [[Exodus 28.30|Exodus 28:36-38]] commands an engraved gold plate reading *qodesh la-YHWH* ("Holiness to the LORD") on the high priest's turban — the Tetragrammaton worn on the body in the temple is a fact of the text, and that LDS temple clothing is consciously modeled on those robes is openly stated. So "wearing the Name" is an attested ancient practice, not a novel gloss. *Rating: solid.*

2. **It supplies the missing referent for "put his name upon them."** [[D&C 109]] twice asks God to put his name upon his servants and his Church, language otherwise abstract. Reading it through the high-priestly Name-plate gives it a concrete, ritual sense and ties the dedicatory prayer to the garment. *Rating: a genuinely illuminating connection.*

3. **It corrects an internal LDS misframing, which strengthens credibility.** Bradley explicitly rejects the popular *modesty* rationale as a trivialization ("making it about how much of our skin is covered... that trivializes it"). Critiquing the in-group's own folk-explanation is a costly, non-defensive move that focuses the garment on Christ. *Rating: rhetorically strong, theologically substantive.*

4. **It is distinct from, and complementary to, the covering argument.** The vault's [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]] argues the garment from *kaphar* ("to cover") and the Genesis "coats of skins"; this claim argues from the *Name* (Tetragrammaton, the high-priestly plate, "put his name upon them"). Same object, two independent biblical warrants — name-bearing and covering — which reinforce rather than repeat each other. *Rating: additive, non-duplicative.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The objection is **over-reading the markings and the timeline**. (a) The Church does not publicly teach that the garment's marks *are* the Tetragrammaton; Bradley says only that the garment "has sacred markings" and that he is *suggesting* we wear the Name "in different languages and sacred scripts" with different symbols — so the identification of the marks with YHWH is his inference, hedged, not a disclosed doctrine. (b) The high priest wore the Name on a *visible external plate*, not on an undergarment, so the parallel is thematic, not formal. (c) The recognizable garment form arrived with [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry|Joseph Smith's 1842 ritual]], so even if the symbolism is biblical, the artifact's continuity with the Aaronic priesthood is asserted, not demonstrated. A Latter-day Saint reply: the load-bearing claim is only that the garment *means* name-bearing and is *patterned* on priestly clothing that demonstrably bore the Name — a reading anchored in [[Exodus 28.30|Exodus 28]] and in [[D&C 109]]'s own "put his name upon them," and confirmed devotionally by the First Presidency's veil-as-Christ teaching — so whether the specific stitches encode YHWH is secondary to the point that the garment is *about Christ's name*, not modesty.

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus Christ has a name in scripture as the God of the Old Testament... in Hebrew, Y-H-W-H, four consonants — two of those symbols are exactly identical. So when we're told that he's putting his name upon us... we're taking it upon us, literally." [^2]

> "There was a name written, it said 'Holiness to the Lord,' it was Y-H-W-H — so he literally wore the name of Jehovah. He wore the name of Christ on his temple clothing. Well, I'm suggesting we do the same." [^3]

> "It's not about how much of our skin is covered. That's not the purpose. If this is a symbol of Jesus Christ, then making it about how much of our skin... trivializes it." [^1]
