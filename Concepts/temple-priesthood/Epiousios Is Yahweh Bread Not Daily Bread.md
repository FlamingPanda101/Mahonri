# Epiousios Is Yahweh Bread Not Daily Bread

#type/claim

## The Claim
Within the reading that [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the Sermon on the Mount is a temple ordinance]], [[Mike Day]] argues that the petition "give us this day our **daily** bread" ([[Matthew 6.11|Matt 6:11]]) is mistranslated. The Greek word behind "daily" is **ἐπιούσιος** (*epiousios*), which Day stresses is a **hapax legomenon** — a word that "only appears once" in the New Testament, so the usual method of fixing meaning by comparing other occurrences is unavailable. [^1] He breaks it into *epi* ("upon" — as in *epidermis*, *epiglottis*) plus *ousios*, the participle of the verb "to be" ("existent / that-ish"), giving "upon the existent one." [^2] The King James translators, facing an opaque word, guessed "the bread that is *for the existing day*" → "daily." But — and this is the payload — the name **Yahweh / Jehovah is itself the verb "to be"** in Hebrew (Exodus 3's *ehyeh asher ehyeh*, "I AM THAT I AM," and the John "I am" sayings, [[John 8.58|John 8:58]]). So *epiousios* bread is not the bread *for the day* but the **"Yahweh bread" — "give me the bread that Yahweh is upon."** [^3] Day ties this "right back into the Eucharist," and David Butler notes the payoff arrives within the same chapter when the Lord "shows up" to feed the worshippers ([[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast|the temple feast]]). [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The hapax problem is real and is the standard crux.** *Epiousios* genuinely occurs only in the Lord's Prayer (Matt 6:11; Luke 11:3) in all of extant pre-Christian Greek; Origen already remarked it seemed coined by the evangelists. Every translation of "daily" is therefore a reconstruction, which legitimately opens the door to a non-mundane reading. *Rating: the philological vacuum is accurately described; "daily" is itself a guess, not a given.*

2. **An *einai*-participle derivation is one recognized option.** Deriving *epiousios* from *epi* + a participle of *einai* ("being") is a real, long-standing proposal (alongside the rival derivation from *epi-ienai*, "the coming day"). The "supersubstantial" rendering of the Vulgate (*panem supersubstantialem*) shows the early church itself read it as more-than-ordinary bread. *Rating: defensible; the participial parse has pedigree, even if "the bread Yahweh is upon" is a further devotional step.*

3. **The Yahweh = "to be" link is uncontested.** That the Tetragrammaton is bound to the Hebrew verb *hayah* ("to be") is mainstream (Exodus 3:14), and the Johannine *egō eimi* sayings deliberately echo it. So *if* *ousios* carries "the Existent One," the bridge to Yahweh is not invented. See [[The Divine Name Yahweh Was Given to Jesus]] and the [[Exodus 3.14|I AM]] datum. *Rating: each link is individually sound; the question is whether Matthew intended the chain.*

4. **It coheres with the feast that follows.** The reading does not float free: the very next movement of the chapter stages the Lord descending to feed the assembly the bread of his own flesh, so "the Yahweh bread" is cashed out narratively rather than left as a bare etymology. See [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The decisive objection is that the **majority scholarly derivation runs the other way**: most lexicographers take *epiousios* from *hē epiousa (hēmera)*, "the coming/following day," yielding "give us today tomorrow's bread" (eschatological provision) or simply "our bread for the day" — exactly the homely sense the KJV captured. The *epi + einai* parse is a minority option, and even granting it, "upon-being" does not obviously mean "the bread *that Yahweh is upon*"; that final move imports the Tetragrammaton by assonance, not grammar. A skeptic notes this is the same retrofitting pattern flagged in the parent ordinance note: a believer who already reads the chapter as a Yahweh-feast finds Yahweh in the one untranslatable word. A Latter-day Saint reply concedes the gloss is interpretive but observes that *some* exalted reading is forced by the church's own "supersubstantial" tradition and by the Eucharistic framing — the bread was never merely caloric — so reading it sacramentally is conservative, and the Yahweh-link simply names *whose* substance the bread is.

## Best Quotes
> "This is what is called a hapax legomenon, which means a word that only appears once... so ordinarily you go look at other places where it's used — but this doesn't have that." [^1]

> "*Usos* is the verb 'to be' and it's a participle, so it means existent, or the existent one... but the name Yahweh, Jehovah, is the verb 'to be' in Hebrew." [^2]
