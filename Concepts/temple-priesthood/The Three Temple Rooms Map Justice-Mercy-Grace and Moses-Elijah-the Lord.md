# The Three Temple Rooms Map Justice-Mercy-Grace and Moses-Elijah-the Lord

#type/claim

## The Claim
Extending the reading that [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the Sermon on the Mount is a temple ordinance]], [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that each of the temple's three rooms carries a **patterned triad**: a *priest* ([[Moses]] → [[Elijah]] → the Lord), a *legal/judicial idea* (**Justice → Mercy → Grace**), and a *spiritual gift* (**Faith → Hope → Charity**). [^1] The structure is signalled at the "hinges" between rooms. Matthew 6 opens with a teaching on almsgiving that is actually two Greek words — *dikaiosynē* ("justice," which appears once) followed by *eleēmosynē* ("mercy") — placed exactly at the doorway from room one into room two; the same Justice-teaching recurs at the hinge into room three (Matthew 7's "judge not"). [^2] Room one is the **room of Justice** (Moses; "Justice is I get what I deserve" — break the law, get punishment; the gift is **Faith**, defined four times in 1 Nephi as "God gives no commandment without making a way"). Room two is the **room of Mercy** (Elijah; the Lord descends for the feast; the gift is **Hope**). Room three is read as **Grace** — "abundance living," "the rest of God," where the initiate has moved beyond both deserving-punishment and needing-mercy and "can just abundantly give." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The Justice/Mercy word-pair at the hinge is a real lexical datum.** Matthew 6:1-4 does sit on two distinct Greek words the KJV flattens into "alms": *dikaiosynē* (righteousness/justice) in v.1 in the critical text, then *eleēmosynē* (almsgiving/mercy) in vv.2-4. That the chapter pivots from a justice-word to a mercy-word at its very opening, and that "judge not" (a justice theme) reopens chapter 7, is observable in the text and fits a room-by-room escalation. *Rating: the word-pair and its placement are accurate; "this marks a hinge between temple rooms" is the interpretive overlay.*

2. **Faith–Hope–Charity is a canonical triad already keyed to ascent.** Pairing the three rooms with the Pauline/Book-of-Mormon triad (1 Corinthians 13; Ether 12; Moroni 7-8) is not invented — these three virtues travel together in scripture, and Ether 12 is explicitly a discourse on faith that climbs to charity. Mapping them onto a graded approach to God is a natural fit. *Rating: the triad is scriptural; assigning one virtue per room is a devotional schematization.*

3. **The priest-sequence has independent anchors.** The Moses–Elijah–Lord order matches the Transfiguration cast (already noted in [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Part I]]), and Butler supplies room-two reasons for Elijah specifically — Elijah is fed by ravens, passes a mantle (clothing), and raises the widow's son (themes that surface in Matthew 6's feeding/clothing). He also notes Gabriel announces the Elijah-role to Zechariah "to the right of the incense altar" — i.e. *in the middle room* (Luke 1). See [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]]. *Rating: the Elijah associations are textually real; whether they were *designed* into Matthew is the open question.*

4. **It coheres with the ordinance frame.** A graded rite needs graded content; assigning escalating legal principles (you owe → you're forgiven → you freely give) tracks a recognizable spiritual progression and dovetails with [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]]. The third-room "rest of God" language reuses the *shalem/shalom* "enter into the rest" motif from Moroni 7 already developed in Part I.

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This is the parent note's **parallelomania** objection at its sharpest: the triads are *imposed* grids, and a symbol-dense ordinance plus three canonical triads (Justice/Mercy/Grace, Faith/Hope/Charity, Moses/Elijah/Lord) can be lined up after the fact with almost any three-part structure. Butler himself moves "a little bit out of order" and admits the Grace assignment is tentative ("maybe it's Justice, mercy, and then Grace... I don't know what you call this"). Critical exegesis reads Matthew 6-7 as redacted sayings material with no room-architecture in view; *dikaiosynē* in 6:1 means "piety/righteous acts," not a courtroom "Justice" keyed to a temple chamber. Granting the word-pair does not show Matthew intended a Justice-room. A Latter-day Saint reply: the schema is offered as the *best unifying explanation* of why the chapters escalate the way they do, and the convergence of three independent triads on the *same* three-room sequence is harder to dismiss than any one mapping — though the honest move is to hold it as a *functional* pattern (per [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]), not a demonstrated authorial design.

## Best Quotes
> "Each room is associated with a priest — Moses, Elijah, the Lord — and a kind of a legal idea. The first room is the room of Justice... Justice is I get what I deserve." [^1]

> "We're getting this instruction right at the hinge — we're going from one room into the next... Justice at these two moments, at the hinges." [^2]

> "I've gotten beyond where I've got to do what I'm told or I get punished, and I've gotten beyond even where I sin and I need mercy, and I can just abundantly give... I'm living in a state of grace, the rest of God." [^3]
