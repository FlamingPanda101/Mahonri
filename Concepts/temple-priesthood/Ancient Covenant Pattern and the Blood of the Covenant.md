# Ancient Covenant Pattern and the Blood of the Covenant

## The Point
[[T. Benjamin Spackman]] traces an Old Testament covenant template inherited from the ancient Near East — parties, stipulations, blessings, and *cursings*, sealed by a ratification ceremony. Crucially, formal covenants were *cut*: the slaughtered animal was a "simile curse" enacting what should befall a covenant-breaker. The blood Moses sprinkled on Israel is "the blood of the covenant" — the exact phrase Jesus reuses at the Last Supper. The point disarms the charge that the temple's former symbolic penalties were uniquely "culty," since the biblical and ancient covenant world was far more graphic, and shows ancient ritual quietly embedded in the weekly sacrament.

## Evidence
1. **The ANE covenant pattern.** Covenants (God–people, king–people, king–vassal — the Assyrian vassal treaties) typically establish the parties, the stipulations ("the commandments"), the blessings for keeping them, and "the opposite," called *cursings*. Compare [[Hammurabi]]'s code and Assyrian treaties as the cultural backdrop (see [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]]).
2. **"Cut a covenant" and the simile curse.** Ratifying a formal covenant "typically involved the death of an animal," and so the Hebrew idiom is "not to make a covenant but to cut a covenant." The animal's death "was a simile curse... enacting physically what would happen to that person or people if they violated the covenant."
3. **Exodus 24.** Moses reads the commandments; the people answer "Amen. All that the Lord has said, we will do"; animals are sacrificed and "half of the blood is splashed on the people," which scholars read "as if their own throats had been cut." Israelites are not struck down wholesale because the covenant "presumes that you will be crummy at keeping it" yet also that you are trying — so when you breach it "you will do your best to repair that breach" through repentance and sacrifice.
4. **The blood of the covenant in the sacrament.** That Exodus phrase "shows up somewhere very very important": at the Last Supper Jesus, adapting the Passover, says "this is my blood of the new covenant." "Jesus is the sacrificial animal that enacts the new covenant," a meaning "built into the sacrament liquid" that Christians "touch on every week without knowing where that phrase goes back to." Modern temples lack animal sacrifice, but Spackman argues it has been "replaced with Jesus."
5. **Penalties in proportion.** The Masonic penalties once brought into the temple are, for Spackman, a mild echo, "not a perfect analogy, but... definitely saying how serious things were" — far less graphic than the ancient covenant's slaughtered animals and splattered blood that critics overlook while calling the temple "culty."

## Best Quotes
> "In Hebrew the idiom is not to make a covenant but to cut a covenant... the death of that animal... was a simile curse." [^1]

> "Half of the blood is splashed on the people. And that is representative or analogical of the cutting of the throat of the animals... as if their own throats had been cut." [^2]

> "The wine is the blood of the covenant, but it's the new covenant. Jesus is the sacrificial animal that enacts the new covenant that we make with him." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The covenant pattern gives the temple's covenant-and-atonement structure a deep, demonstrably ancient pedigree that Joseph Smith is unlikely to have engineered — a companion to [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]] and to the Eden/atonement logic of [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]]. It also reframes the discontinued symbolic penalties: Spackman insists "the Bible has nothing to do with blood oaths" is a claim that collapses on a careful reading of [[Moses]] in Exodus 24.
