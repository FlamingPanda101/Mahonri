# Afterlife - Competing Frameworks in Second Temple Judaism
#type/claim

## The Claim
Second Temple Judaism did not hold a single, settled doctrine of the afterlife. Within one influential text alone — 1 Enoch — at least three incompatible frameworks for postmortem fate coexist: annihilation (the wicked destroyed as if they never existed), purgative punishment (suffering followed by destruction or eventual salvation), and eternal conscious torment. These competing views were taken up unsystematized in the New Testament and were only harmonized by later theological tradition, which means "the biblical doctrine of hell" is not the clear, monolithic teaching it is often assumed to be.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Three frameworks in one book.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], 1 Enoch contains "a few different perspectives" — destruction "as if they never existed," punishment then destruction-or-salvation, and "the idea of eternal conscious torment." Because many authors contributed over centuries, the text never reconciles them. Developed in [[Book of Enoch - Postmortem Punishment and Reward Varieties]].
- **The same diversity in the New Testament.** The competing Enochic views mirror unresolved tensions in the NT itself — "eternal punishment" (Matthew 25:46), "eternal destruction" (2 Thessalonians 1:9), and universal reconciliation (Colossians 1:20). Both corpora carry plural views that later systematic theology (infernalism, annihilationism/conditionalism, universalism) tried to force into one.
- **Gradations and waiting-places.** The Book of Watchers shows Enoch differentiated holding-places for the dead (righteous awaiting judgment; sinners judged in life; sinners unjudged), a proto-purgatorial picture of stages and waiting rather than a single immediate fate.
- **Steelman / caveats.** A traditional reader may argue the apparent diversity resolves once genre and audience are accounted for (apocalyptic imagery vs. didactic statement), and that the church's later harmonization is a legitimate Spirit-guided clarification rather than the imposition of order on chaos. A conservative could also reject 1 Enoch's relevance to Christian doctrine outright. The claim's force is descriptive: the Second Temple sources, including one quoted by Jude, demonstrably held a *range* of afterlife conceptions, so appeals to a self-evident single biblical hell overstate the evidence. The plurality also bears on which texts carried authority (see [[Pseudepigrapha and Authority]]).
