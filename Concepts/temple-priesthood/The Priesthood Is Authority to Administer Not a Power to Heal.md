# The Priesthood Is Authority to Administer Not a Power to Heal

## The Claim
[[Bruce Porter]] argues that a "false tradition" has made Latter-day Saints speak of "the healing power of the priesthood," reverencing priesthood "more sometimes than we revere the gospel." Correctly understood, priesthood is **not a power** but an **authority to administer the gospel and its ordinances within a stewardship, and to have those ordinances recorded on the records of the church.** Healing, miracles, and spiritual gifts come by **faith**, not by priesthood, and are available to members and non-members alike — so the Church's only genuine "monopoly" is priesthood *authority* and the ordinances tied to it, not spiritual power.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **No monopoly on spiritual power (rated: strong, doctrinally careful).** "We don't have a monopoly on righteousness ... spiritual experiences ... even miracles, blessings and healings ... The only thing we have a monopoly on would be priesthood authority and the ordinances that are connected to that priesthood authority."[^1] The Lord "is not going to restrict somebody else's spiritual blessings because they're not a member of the church."

2. **Scripture never invokes priesthood in a miracle (rated: moderate-strong; an argument from silence, but a pointed one).** Porter says he searched and found "nowhere in the scriptures" that Christ or the apostles "invoked the priesthood" in performing a miracle or healing; in scripture the priesthood is invoked "so it can be recorded on the records of the church."[^2] *Rating:* an argument from silence, but it correctly isolates the recording/ordinance function from raw thaumaturgy.

3. **The gifts of the Spirit are open to all (rated: strong; textual).** [[Doctrine and Covenants 46.11-14|D&C 46:11-14]] (echoing 1 Corinthians 12 and Moroni 10) gives "to some ... the faith to heal" and "to some ... the faith to be healed" — gifts that "can be to member and non-member both" and "doesn't have to have priesthood."[^2] Faith, not priesthood, is the operative cause.

4. **Faith — not priesthood — moves mountains (rated: strong; Christ's own words).** "Even Christ says it's the faith that moves the mountain, not the priesthood." Confusing the two means "we sometimes get carried away and think more of the priesthood than we do of the gospel of Jesus Christ."[^3] Cf. the corrective First Presidency counsel Porter recalls — that ordinations should invoke "the authority of the priesthood," not "the power of the priesthood."[^4]

5. **Coheres with the "order, not power" reading (rated: integrating).** Porter's authority/recording emphasis dovetails with [[Todd McLauchlin]]'s claim that priesthood is "an order or a pattern," not "a power you can give somebody" — power *follows* the pattern (D&C 121:36) rather than being the thing held. See [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]].

**Steelman of the counter-view.** A traditional Latter-day Saint can answer that scripture and modern revelation do tie priesthood to power: D&C 121:36 says "the rights of the priesthood are inseparably connected with the **powers** of heaven," healings are administered "by the authority of the Melchizedek Priesthood" in the official ordinance, and Mark 16:17–18 attaches signs to those who minister in Christ's name. Porter's sharp severing of authority from power may overcorrect; the more defensible synthesis is that priesthood is the *authorized channel* through which God's power operates in ordinances, while the *gift* of faith-healing is indeed broader than the priesthood and available to all. Porter himself concedes the point is about avoiding a *false emphasis* ("we revere the priesthood more than the gospel"), not denying that God works power through priesthood ordinances.

## Best Quotes
> "We don't have a monopoly on faith ... charity ... these. The only thing we have a monopoly on would be priesthood authority and the ordinances that are connected to that priesthood authority."[^1]

> "There's nowhere in the scriptures that a miracle or a healing took place where the apostles or Christ ever invoked the priesthood."[^2]

> "Even Christ says it's the faith that moves the mountain, not the priesthood."[^3]
