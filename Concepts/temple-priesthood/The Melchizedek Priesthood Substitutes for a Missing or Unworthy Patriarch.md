# The Melchizedek Priesthood Substitutes for a Missing or Unworthy Patriarch

## The Claim
[[Bruce Porter]] offers a functional account of *why* the Melchizedek priesthood exists and *why* women do not hold it. The Melchizedek (the "holy priesthood after the order of the Son of God") is **Christ's** authority for "the temporal and spiritual welfare of all his father's creation." Its technical purpose is to **step in where a patriarch is missing or unworthy** — to "take care of the fatherless, the widows, and the orphans." Because women sealed in the temple already hold the **patriarchal** priesthood within their own family, they have no need of the Melchizedek office, whose defining job is to supply the patriarch a family lacks.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Patriarchal vs. Melchizedek jurisdiction (rated: moderate; a clean conceptual map).** A sealed parent can bless his own children "by the authority of the patriarchal priesthood," but "the second I go to my neighbor or somebody that I minister to, I can't use the patriarchal authority — I have to use the authority of Christ," the Melchizedek, "to administer the gospel outside of your family line."[^2] The patriarchal is *intra-familial*; the Melchizedek is *extra-familial*.

2. **The fatherless/widow/orphan key (rated: the central exegetical move).** Porter ties the Melchizedek's purpose to the recurring Old Testament triad: "the common element between the fatherless, the widows and the orphans ... is a missing patriarch — a missing or unworthy patriarch. So the technical purpose of the Melchizedek authority is to step in and make sure the temporal and spiritual needs are met for those who do not have a patriarch."[^1] *Rating:* an elegant unifying reading, though it is Porter's inference rather than an explicit scriptural definition.

3. **Why women do not hold it (rated: the apologetic payoff).** "This is why women will not hold the Melchizedek priesthood. They already hold a patriarchal priesthood within their own family ... the Melchizedek authority is to take the place of a missing or unworthy patriarch."[^3] The claim recasts non-ordination not as exclusion but as redundancy — a woman is not a "missing patriarch."

4. **Grand patriarchs needed both (rated: illustrative).** Porter applies the model to the "grand patriarchs" (Adam, Seth, Enos ... Abraham): Seth, after Abel's murder, needed Melchizedek authority "to assist his father in all of his father's posterity," not merely patriarchal authority over his own house.[^4] See [[Joseph Smith Taught Three Priesthoods Including the Patriarchal]].

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The substitution thesis is a *post hoc* rationale that the standard works do not state in these terms; the scriptural reasons given for the all-male priesthood office are generally framed as revelation and pattern (the Proclamation; D&C 107) rather than "men fill in for absent fathers." Critics — both feminist and traditional — can press that the "missing patriarch" reading proves too much: single fathers, widowers, and unworthy *men* would by the same logic need a *matriarchal* substitute, yet none is posited; and ward Melchizedek-priesthood service (home teaching, blessings, ordinations) extends far beyond ministering to the literally fatherless. The most defensible core is narrower and pastoral: priesthood authority is oriented to *service for others' families*, especially the vulnerable, and is patterned on Christ's self-giving (see [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]]) — Porter's "missing patriarch" gloss being a memorable teaching device, presented as his own synthesis, not magisterial doctrine.
