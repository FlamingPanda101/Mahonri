# The Christian Prayer Circle Descends From an Ancient Temple Dance

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]], drawing on Nicoletta Isar's *Choros: The Dance of Adam* (a study of Byzantine choreography), argue that early Christians practiced **prayer circles** — holding one another by the wrist in a ring, singing or praying — descended from an ancient temple dance (*choros*) that itself runs back through Greek and Near Eastern tradition.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Anastasis icon and the wrist-grasp (rated: art-historical).** Isar shows the Resurrection (Anastasis) icon in which the Son of God raises Adam (right hand) and Eve (left) "by the wrist" — a sign of resurrection that is "also part of the dance." She documents vase paintings of people "holding each other by the wrist in a dance… a circle," and calls the early-Christian versions "Christian prayer circles."[^1]
2. **Etymology of the circle (rated: linguistic).** *Choros* (whence "choir") "means to make space… to be in a circle"; the pilgrim *hag* (to Jerusalem three times a year) "probably comes from *hagga*, to dance in a circle," and "the Hajj to Mecca is the same word" — pilgrims "come to the altar in a circle, a procession in song or dance or both."[^2]
3. **The right-hand grasp ties to the enthronement Psalms (rated: cross-link).** The host connects the dance's wrist-grasp to [[John Eaton]]'s observation that in Psalm 110 "the Lord sustaining the king by grasping him by the right hand" — "the dance is the Lord grasping Adam's right hand."[^3]
4. **A fragmented tradition surviving in many heirs (rated: framing).** The hosts read the circle dance as surviving in Orthodox liturgy, the Mecca *tawaf*, and synagogue practice — "a shattered, fragmented tradition" of which Christianity, Judaism, and Islam each carry pieces.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Isar's *choros* scholarship is real art history, but the leap from Byzantine iconography and Greek dance to a *continuous* temple-dance tradition reaching first-temple Israel is the hosts' synthesis, not Isar's thesis; the etymological chain (*hag* → *hagga* → circle dance) is suggestive but not decisive, and the Anastasis icon is a late (post-Constantinian) Christian image. The data (ancient ring dances; the wrist-grasp motif; the *choros* etymology) are genuine; the unbroken-temple-origin claim is interpretive.

## Best Quotes
> "She shows images of the Son of God grabbing Adam and Eve by their wrists… the early Christians had what she calls Christian prayer circles… and she talks about how this came out of the ancient tradition."[^1]

> "*Choros*, which means to make space, to have a place to dance, to be in a circle… these are all etymologically related terms."[^2]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint temple practice includes prayer-circle elements (a ring of participants), and the hosts present the *choros* material as non-LDS scholarship corroborating that such circles are ancient, not a 19th-century invention.
