# Ordinance Was a Protestant Loanword for Rites That Function as Sacraments

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonathan Stapley]] argues that the Latter-day Saint word **"ordinance"** is a *Protestant loanword* the early church inherited, but that LDS rites actually function more like **sacraments** in the Catholic/Orthodox sense — ceremonies believed to do real metaphysical work and convey grace. Protestants coined "ordinance" precisely to *deny* that baptism accomplishes anything (since for them salvation is by faith alone); the Restoration took the word but not the deflationary theology, which is why Joseph Smith could call faith and repentance "ordinances" — for him an ordinance was a *law*, and faith and repentance are laws no less than baptism.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Sacrament vs. ordinance: a deliberate Protestant distinction (strong)
Stapley: broader Christianity speaks of *sacraments* — ceremonies that "convey the grace of God" and do "real metaphysical work." Many Protestants, especially in the Calvinist vein, reject sacramentalism (you are saved by faith alone; nothing you *do* influences it), so baptism cannot save or do material work. They needed another term for a commanded rite that nonetheless does nothing salvific — hence "ordinance" (as in a *municipal* ordinance, a law/code).[^1]

### 2. LDS rites "do a thing" (strong)
The Restoration believes its ceremonies actually accomplish something — they convey grace and effect real change — so they resemble *sacraments* more than the deflated Protestant "ordinance." Stapley notes LDS theology in this respect "resembles Catholicism and orthodoxy more."[^2]

### 3. Joseph Smith used "ordinance" to mean *law* (strong)
The Articles of Faith today read "first principles and ordinances of the gospel," but Joseph Smith wrote that the "first *ordinances*" are faith, repentance, baptism, and the gift of the Holy Ghost — because, in his usage, "having faith and repentance is just as much of a *law* as baptism is." The word carried its legal sense, not a liturgical one.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic counters that this is a semantic sleight: Latter-day Saints standardly call their *rituals* "ordinances" (baptism, endowment, sealing), so insisting the word "really" means "law" looks like a convenient redefinition to dodge the charge that LDS practice is works-based sacramentalism dressed in Protestant vocabulary. And if the rites *do* convey grace and effect salvation, that revives exactly the works-righteousness Protestants reject — the terminology debate doesn't resolve the theological one.

## Counter-Objection
The point is historical, not evasive: it explains *why* a single English word now does double duty in LDS discourse (Joseph's legal sense + the later liturgical sense) and resolves an internal puzzle — how rites can be both "unchangeable ordinances" and a ceremony that visibly changes over time. The answer is that what is unchangeable is the *law/ordinance* (faith, repentance, baptism, the Holy Ghost — the plan of salvation), while the *liturgy* enacting it develops (see [[Ordinance Meant Law Not Liturgy When Joseph Smith Said Ordinances Cannot Change]] and [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]]). On works: the Restoration sides with the sacramental traditions that rites genuinely mediate grace, but frames the temple as "learning heaven, not earning heaven" — grace received and covenanted, not wages earned.
