# Sacred Secrecy Is Pearls Before Swine Not Shame

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Dave Butler]] reframe the "fight club" criticism of temple secrecy (both from outsiders who call it cultish and from members embarrassed by it). Their case has several moves:

1. **Christ himself kept sacred things hidden.** "Christ all over the New Testament is saying, 'Don't tell anybody'... if you have ears to hear, he's winking all the time" — speaking openly to crowds but privately to disciples ([[Matthew 13.10-11|Matthew 13:10-11]], "unto you it is given to know the mysteries... but to them it is not given"). The command "**cast not your pearls before swine**" ([[Matthew 7.7-8|Matthew 7:6]]) means sacred things are "pearls... not ours to throw" — withholding them is **obedience, not shame**: "we don't have the authority to" profane them. [^1]
2. **The reticence is a feature, not a defect.** Echoing the [[The Sacred Garment Is a Credibility-Enhancing Display|sociological point]], being "a little ashamed" to discuss the temple signals "something worthwhile"; "you're darn right we have secret things in our religion — absolutely we do, because they're pearls." [^2]
3. **Every serious tradition has sacred space and vestments.** A religion with *no* sacred space, holy vestment, or guarded mystery "should be really suspicious" — "your religion can fit on a fortune cookie and you're proud of this?" The "magic underwear" jeer is **ethnocentric**: it is never aimed at a Hasidic Jew's *tzitzit* or a Sikh's turban; "you can tell they haven't traveled much." It only looks weird to modern America, not to "all of human history." [^3]

The constructive caveat (shared with [[Dave Butler]]'s "great apostasy" thesis): the *modern internet* now forces disclosure ("if you're not going to teach your kids about it, Google will"), so the answer is to **teach the meaning better in advance**, not to hide — give "a ready defense... for the faith that is in you" ([[1 Peter 3.15|1 Peter 3:15]]). [^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "messianic secret" and esoteric teaching are real Gospel features.** [[Matthew 13.10-11|Matthew 13:10-11]] / Mark 4:11 explicitly distinguish public parables from private explanation "of the mysteries," and the Synoptics repeatedly have Jesus enjoin silence — so "Jesus kept some things in-group" is textually solid, not apologetic spin. *Rating: strong scriptural basis.* [^1]
2. **"Pearls before swine" is a real dominical saying about guarding the holy.** [[Matthew 7.7-8|Matthew 7:6]] ("neither cast ye your pearls before swine") is precisely about *not* exposing what is *holy* to those who will trample it — an apt warrant for reverent reticence. *Rating: on-point proof-text.* [^1]
3. **The "all traditions have sacred investment" point is empirically true.** *Tzitzit*, the turban (*dastar*), priestly vestments, the *ihram*, monastic habits — sacred clothing is cross-culturally normal, so the mockery *is* selectively applied. This is the same de-parochializing move as [[Ritual Has Been Normal for Four Thousand Years]]. *Rating: well-grounded.* [^3]
4. **The "teach better, the internet forces it" diagnosis is sound.** It coheres with [[The Mysteries Were Converted From Lived Experience Into Doctrine]] and the wider recognition that the Church has shifted toward more openness (published covenants) precisely because of information access. *Rating: a fair read of the current situation.* [^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Two objections. First, **"sacred, not secret" can be used to deflect legitimate scrutiny**: appealing to *pearls before swine* risks special pleading — *any* group can label its undisclosed practices "too holy to examine," and a critic will say secrecy *itself* is what fuels suspicion and prevents informed consent before temple participation (a real pastoral problem the speakers concede with the "why didn't anyone tell me?" worry). Second, the **"all religions have vestments, so the mockery is bigoted" tu quoque** rebuts the *mockery* but not any *substantive* concern (e.g., about specific covenants or historical origins); showing a jeer is ethnocentric does not validate the practice, only disarms the cheap shot. A measured reply: the claim is **calibrated** — it does *not* say "never explain the temple," but the opposite: *teach the meaning openly and in advance* (the internet leaves no choice), while keeping the few specifically-covenanted points reverently undisclosed, exactly as Jesus reserved "the mysteries" for the prepared. So it answers the *mockery* and the *embarrassment* without claiming immunity from honest inquiry.

## Best Quotes
> "Christ all over the New Testament is saying, 'Don't tell anybody.'... if you have ears to hear, he's winking all the time... Don't cast these pearls before swine. There are secret things all over the place... they're sacred things, and they came from God. They're not ours to monkey around with." [^1]

> "If you're in a religion that doesn't have sacred holy vestments or a sacred space, you should be really suspicious. That's weird... your religion can fit on a fortune cookie and you're proud of this." [^3]

> "The temple — if you're not going to teach your kids about it, well guess what? Google will... We have to talk about what this stuff means, because we have to." [^4]
