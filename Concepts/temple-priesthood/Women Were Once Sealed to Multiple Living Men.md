#type/claim

## The Claim

The sealing asymmetry critics point to today — a man can be sealed to a living second wife while still sealed to his first, but a woman can only be sealed to one *living* man — is not a permanent doctrine but a *changed policy*. In early Utah the same arrangement applied to women: a woman could be sealed to one man, civilly divorce, and be sealed to a second living man, remaining sealed to both until a cancellation. Sealing cancellations only normalized around the 1890s, and the one-living-man rule for women became policy only in the 1910s–early 1920s.[^1] The implication critics often assume — that current asymmetry is fixed dogma — is historically false, and many Latter-day Saints expect it to equalize again.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

The argument's force is that it converts an apparent doctrinal embarrassment (gender-asymmetric sealing) into a *datable administrative practice* that has already moved once — so it can move again without anyone alleging apostasy, exactly the pattern in [[Apostasia Means Deliberate Rebellion Not Mere Change]] and [[The Same-Day Sealing Squeeze Was a Post-Sixties Overcorrection Not Doctrine]].

1. **A named, documented case, not a generality.** Brigham Young's daughter [[Susa Young Gates]] was sealed to Alma Dunford in the Salt Lake Endowment House in 1872; after a civil divorce she married Jacob Gates in the St. George Temple in 1880, and was *technically sealed to both living men* until [[Wilford Woodruff]] approved cancellation of the Dunford sealing in 1890.[^3] A specific person with dates resists the charge of apologetic hand-waving.

2. **It was normal, not an aberration.** Keystone states this "kind of scenario was pretty normal in early Utah before sealing cancellations became more normal around the 1890s," and the one-living-man-at-a-time rule for women only hardened into policy in the 1910s–1920s.[^1] A widely-practiced arrangement that later changed is a policy, by definition.

3. **The asymmetry is therefore contingent, which answers the fairness critique on its own terms.** If the present rule is the *product* of an early-20th-century tightening rather than a revealed constant, then the egalitarian intuition critics press — that it should apply equally to both sexes — is consistent with the Church's own history, and "many Latter-day Saints are of the opinion that those policies will equalize across genders at some point in the future."[^2] The critic and the believer can share the same expectation.

4. **It distinguishes the covenant from the bookkeeping.** Per [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]], a sealing is an eternal *relationship* ordinance, not a marriage license; whether one is administratively sealed to one or two living persons is a policy about *cancellations and records*, not about the nature of the covenant. The change touches the bookkeeping layer, like the culture/doctrine cut in [[Conflating Anecdote and Culture With Doctrine Misrepresents the Church]].

**Steelman of the critic.** The strongest counter grants the Susa Young Gates case and still presses three points. First, *"it used to be symmetric" is a double-edged concession*: it concedes that for decades the Church really did seal women to two living men and then *removed* that from women specifically — so the present asymmetry was an affirmative, gendered restriction, not a neutral inheritance, and "it could change back" is a hope, not a policy. Second, *contingency does not equal justice*: showing the rule is changeable does nothing to show the *current* arrangement is fair to women now, and pointing to a 1920s tightening arguably makes it look worse (the asymmetry was *introduced*, not merely never-corrected). Third, *the future-equalization claim is unsourced sentiment*: "many Latter-day Saints are of the opinion" is a read on member attitudes, not a revelation or a stated trajectory, and a defender should not let a hoped-for change carry the weight of an answer. The honest reply is narrow and holds: the *specific* claim — that female sealing to one living man is an unchangeable, anciently-fixed dogma — is false, because the practice demonstrably differed within living institutional memory ([[Mistakes Versus Leading Astray]]); whether today's policy *ought* to change is a separate normative question that this history reframes but does not settle.

## Best Quotes

> "This kind of scenario was pretty normal in early Utah before sealing cancellations became more normal around the 1890s. And it wasn't until the 1910s or early 1920s that it became policy for women to only be sealed to one living man at a time."[^1]

> "Many Latter-day Saints are of the opinion that those policies will equalize across genders at some point in the future. But what many don't know is that the current policy for men actually did apply to women in the church up into the early 1900s."[^2]

> "She was technically sealed to both Alma and Jacob until 1890 when Wilford Woodruff approved a cancellation of her sealing to Alma Dunford."[^3]
