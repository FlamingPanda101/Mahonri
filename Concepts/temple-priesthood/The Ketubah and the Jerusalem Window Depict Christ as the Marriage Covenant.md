# The Ketubah and the Jerusalem Window Depict Christ as the Marriage Covenant

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the **ketubah** — the ancient Hebrew written marriage contract — is a type of Christ, and that the 2020 Latter-day Saint symbol (the resurrected Christ standing beneath an arch on a blue ground) is, formally, a ketubah. Anciently the contract's terms were inscribed inside an arched **"Jerusalem window"** on a blue background, and every term was a promise of what the **groom** would do for the bride. Placing Christ inside that window says: Christ *is* the covenant, the contract itself — and also the bridegroom who fulfills it.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The ketubah recorded the groom's obligations, not the bride's

In the betrothal (*erusin*) the couple signed a *ketubah* "in triplicate" — one copy for the couple, one for the bride's family, one filed with the city magistrate — and the contract listed the groom's duties: "I will provide for you... food... clothing... and if you're ever captured and taken into slavery I will redeem you." The bride's "only job was to be loyal and true." This is the bridegroom-does-everything structure echoed in [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]] and in the Sermon on the Mount's "consider the lilies."[^3]

### 2. The Jerusalem window and the blue ground

The terms were written inside an arch — a "Jerusalem window" — and "anciently... almost always on a blue background." Real ketubot, still produced by Jews today, are decorated documents in exactly this form.[^4]

### 3. Christ in the window = Christ as the covenant

When President Nelson unveiled the 2020 Church symbol in General Conference — Christ under an arch on blue — Woodmansee "saw a ketubah": since the window's contents are the groom's promises, putting Christ there identifies him as the contract and the covenant, "he is the bridegroom." She adds that the temple recommend carries this same image of Christ in a Jerusalem window on one side and **two witness signatures** on the other — mirroring the two non-family witnesses who signed an ancient ketubah.[^5][^6]

> **Steelman:** Christological readings of the marriage covenant are scripturally rooted — Christ is repeatedly the bridegroom (John 3:29; Matthew 9:15; Revelation 19) and the *mediator/guarantor* of a new covenant (Hebrews 8-9). Reading the contract-figure as Christ-the-covenant coheres with "he is our righteousness"/"the covenant" language in the New Testament, even granting the visual identification with the modern logo is the author's own typology.

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The 2020 symbol was designed as a representation of the living, resurrected Christ reaching toward humanity; the Church's own explanation does not invoke a ketubah, so the identification is an interpretive overlay, not the designers' stated intent.
- Standardized ketubot and their decorative "Jerusalem window" forms are largely medieval and later; projecting the exact visual convention back onto biblical-era betrothal is anachronistic, even if a written marriage contract is ancient.
