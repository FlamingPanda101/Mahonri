# The 1877 Lecture at the Veil Required Adam-God

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] calls the **1877 Lecture at the Veil** the *strongest* evidence that Adam-God was binding: it was part of the temple **endowment**, and (he assumes) you could not be inducted without affirming it. [[Luke Hansen]] rebuts that it was a **lecture, not a covenant**, given in only one temple, and tied to *exaltation* rather than church membership.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Lecture is unambiguous Adam-God (concession — primary text)
Hansen does not dispute the text: "Adam was an immortal being when he came on this earth... had received the priesthood... and had begotten all the spirits that were to come to this earth... Father Adam's oldest son, Jesus the Savior" [^1]. Heschmeyer: this is "not just a private theological opinion... it's actually required."

### 2. It was a lecture, not a sworn covenant (strong — distinction)
Hansen: "there was no covenant that 'yes, I swear that I affirm this thing' before you can complete it. It was just a lecture that was taught to people in that setting." So one "could disagree with the stuff being taught in the temple endowment and still get endowed" [^2].

### 3. Only the St. George Temple; the endowment was always "evolving" (moderate — limited scope)
"In the St. George Temple... we actually don't really have records of it being taught in the other temples" [^3]. Hansen adds Joseph Smith told Young the Nauvoo endowment "isn't quite right... it needs some polishing" — so the ceremony was explicitly conceived as evolving, "never set in stone" [^4].

### 4. The endowment is for *exaltation*, not membership (strong — corrects Heschmeyer's premise)
Hansen corrects a key misunderstanding: the endowment is "for people who are already members." Baptism is "for the remission of sins"; the temple covenants "revolve more around exaltation" — full theosis, entering God's presence [^5]. Heschmeyer concedes ("Oh, right. Okay.") and reframes: "somebody who doesn't believe in Adam-God, they just can't be exalted?" [^6].

### Steelman (Heschmeyer's side)
Even granting it's "only" about exaltation, Heschmeyer's point survives: in 1877 the highest LDS blessing was gated behind a doctrine the church now calls false — "you're not talking about the true God then... you're talking about Adam, who is not the true God." That it was a lecture rather than a sworn oath doesn't change that the church was *teaching* it as truth at its most sacred moment [^1].

## Best Quotes
> "Adam was an immortal being when he came on this earth... and had begotten all the spirits that were to come to this earth." — Lecture at the Veil (1877), quoted [^1]

> "There was no covenant that 'yes, I swear that I affirm this thing'... It was just a lecture." — Luke Hansen [^2]

> "The temple endowment ceremony is for people who are already members of the church." — Luke Hansen [^5]
