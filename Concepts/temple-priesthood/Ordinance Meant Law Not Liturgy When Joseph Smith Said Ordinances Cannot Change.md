# "Ordinance" Meant Law, Not Liturgy, When Joseph Smith Said Ordinances Cannot Change

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Antagonists quote [[Joseph Smith]] — "ordinances instituted in the heavens before the foundation of the world... are not to be altered or changed" — and then show that temple ordinances *have* changed, presenting this as an "existential gotcha" that exposes the restoration as incoherent.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Joseph used "ordinance" to mean *law*, not ritual
Historian [[Jonathan Stapley]]: Joseph "used words that we used but had different understanding of their meanings." In his usage — shared by much of his Protestant context — an *ordinance* was a law one is commanded to obey. [[John Wesley]], father of Methodism, called "the ordinances of the church... fasting and Bible reading"; the everyday "municipal ordinance" is simply a city law.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the lexical claim is documentable from period usage; Wesley and "municipal ordinance" are concrete anchors).

### 2. Joseph's own list proves it
Joseph wrote that "the first ordinances of the gospel are faith, repentance, baptism." Faith is not a liturgy; listing *faith* as an ordinance shows the word meant a saving law/principle, not a ceremony.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (his own enumeration is the decisive internal evidence).

### 3. So the unchanging thing is the plan of salvation
"It wasn't this sort of cosmic fundamentalism that there's this ideal baptism in the mind of God that if you deviate from, then it doesn't count." What cannot change is "the principles of salvation, the plan of salvation, the things that you need to do to be redeemed" — not the wording, clothing, or gestures.[^4] This is the conceptual sibling of [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]], which makes the same point from the angle of context-bound meaning.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (follows from 1–2; the line between "saving principle" and "form" is drawn by the apologist).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies that this is a convenient semantic escape hatch: if "ordinance" can be redefined to exclude exactly the things that demonstrably changed (penalties, wording, garments), then the prophet's strong-sounding guarantee has been quietly emptied of content, and *no* change could ever falsify it. The honest reply concedes the redefinition must be grounded in Joseph's *actual* nineteenth-century usage rather than invented post hoc — which is why the Wesley parallel and Joseph's "faith... is an ordinance" list are load-bearing: they show the broad sense predates the controversy and is not a rescue device.

## Best Quotes
> "So faith is an ordinance for Joseph Smith because it's the law... He's saying the principles of salvation, the plan of salvation, the things that you need to do to be redeemed, was established in the beginning, and those don't change."[^4]

> "The ordinances of the church are fasting and Bible reading." — [[John Wesley]], cited by Stapley[^2]
