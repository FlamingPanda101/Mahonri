# Gnostic Apocrypha Preserve Temple Ordinance Patterns

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that several Gnostic and apocryphal works — chiefly **Pistis Sophia** and the **Books of Jeu** (the Bruce Codex), the **Gospel of Philip**, and the **Acts of John** — preserve, in scattered and partly garbled form, the elements of an early-Christian temple ordinance: sacred **garments** and robe-changes, passage through concentric **veils** guarded by **secret names / seals / ciphers**, a **prayer circle** (round dance), and a fixed **ordinance sequence**. On this reading the Gnostics did not invent these rites; they leaked and corrupted a genuine, restricted temple mystery (the lost forty-day teaching) during the apostasy, so the recurrence of ordinance-shaped material across independent apocrypha is evidence the ordinance was real before it was lost. This is the broad pattern that the Secret Mark and forty-day arguments feed into. See [[The Lost Forty-Day Teaching Points to a Suppressed Temple Endowment]] and [[Clement of Alexandria and Secret Mark Attest a Suppressed Temple Gospel]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Gospel of Philip lists an ordinance sequence
Philip names a graded series — "baptism, chrism, eucharist, redemption, and the **bridal chamber**" — i.e. a staged progression of ordinances culminating in a sealing-like rite, which maps onto the temple's tiered ascent rather than a single conversion event.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — Philip genuinely lists these five; that the list is a *temple ordinance sequence* rather than Valentinian sacramental theology is the interpretive step.

### 2. Pistis Sophia and the Books of Jeu describe veils, names, and seals
These Bruce-Codex texts describe the soul ascending through ranks/veils, presenting **secret names and seals** to guardians at each threshold — a guardians-of-sacred-space-with-passwords motif that parallels the temple ascent. See [[The Christian Prayer Circle Descends From an Ancient Temple Dance]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the name/seal/threshold material is really in these texts; reading it as the LDS endowment specifically is the inference.

### 3. The Acts of John records a round dance and commanded silence
The Acts of John has the risen Jesus lead the apostles in a circular hymn/dance and enjoin secrecy — already treated in the vault as a mystery-ritual institution. See [[The Acts of John Records Jesus Instituting a Mystery Ritual]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the round-dance text exists and is striking; its ritual-institution reading is developed in its own note.

### 4. Convergence across independent witnesses
The load-bearing point is not any single text but that garments, veils, secret names, prayer circles, and ordinance-order recur across *multiple, independently transmitted* apocrypha — a pattern Barnes argues is best explained by a common lost original. See [[The Mysteries Were Converted From Lived Experience Into Doctrine]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — convergence is a real argumentative strength, but shared Hellenistic mystery-cult vocabulary is an alternative common cause.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) the Gnostic corpus is precisely the wrong place to find *primitive* Christian practice — these texts are 2nd–4th-century, theologically idiosyncratic, and saturated with **Hellenistic mystery-cult** imagery (robes, passwords, ascents through the spheres) that was the common religious idiom of late antiquity, so the resemblances are explained by shared cultural vocabulary, not a suppressed apostolic ordinance; (b) "bridal chamber," "seal," and "ascent" in Valentinian/Sethian usage have well-studied *Gnostic* meanings (the reunion of the soul with its heavenly counterpart, baptismal sealing) that do not require an LDS-style endowment; (c) selecting the ordinance-shaped fragments from sprawling, contradictory texts and assembling them into a single "pattern" is a composite the sources themselves never present; and (d) inferring a lost original from late, divergent copies is an argument from absence. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is not that any one Gnostic text *is* the endowment, but that the *recurrence* of garment/veil/name/circle/sequence elements across independent streams calls for an explanation, and a common (later corrupted) ritual is at least as economical as independent borrowing of the same mystery-cult furniture; the shared-vocabulary objection explains the idiom but not why the *cluster* keeps reappearing together.
