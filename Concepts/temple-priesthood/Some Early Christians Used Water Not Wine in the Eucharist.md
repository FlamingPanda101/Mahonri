# Some Early Christians Used Water Not Wine in the Eucharist

## The Claim
[[David Sword of Laben]] argues, against the assumption that the Eucharist was "always wine," that **several early Christian groups celebrated with water** (or watered wine), and that a water-and-bread Eucharist "may stretch back to the very beginning of Christianity rather than being a later deviation." The point defuses the perception that the Latter-day Saint use of sacramental water is a strange modern innovation (or a mere reaction to an 1830s wine-poisoning scare).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Patristic witnesses, mostly hostile (moderate, second-hand).** The panel cites: **Irenaeus** (c. 180) on the Ebionites rejecting "the commixture of the heavenly wine" and using "water of the world only"; **Epiphanius** (375) on Marcionite/encratite sects using water in their mysteries; **Clement of Alexandria** on those who "celebrate the Eucharist with mere water"; and **Cyprian** (c. 250, *Epistle* 63) rebuking water-only practice and insisting on mixture — evidence that water-Eucharist existed widely enough to require rebuttal.[^1]
2. **A New Testament hint (weak, conceded gray area).** Romans 14:21 ("It is not good to eat meat or drink wine… [that] makes your brother stumble") is floated as a *possible* allusion to some groups omitting wine out of consideration, not heresy — though the panel concedes "we're not really sure this is what this means."[^2]
3. **Three practices coexisted (moderate).** David frames the early situation as three options — pure wine, wine mixed with water, and water only — with no reliable data on their proportions; water "might have been majority… for a large chunk of the early church. We just don't know."[^3]
4. **Scholarly backing (moderate, second-hand).** Paul Bradshaw's *Eucharistic Origins* is cited for the view that water-and-bread Eucharist may be original rather than a deviation — "reasonably well understood in the scholarly literature."[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The honest weakness — which the panel half-acknowledges — is that **most of the named water-using groups were the losing, heterodox sects** (Ebionites, Marcionites, encratites), and the orthodox witnesses (Irenaeus, Cyprian) cite them precisely to *condemn* the practice and defend the wine (or mixed) cup. So the evidence shows water-Eucharist *existed* and was *contested*, not that it was the apostolic norm; David's stronger "may go back to the very beginning / may have been the majority" claims rest on Bradshaw's reconstruction and the admitted absence of good data ("we just don't know"), not on a documented orthodox water-Eucharist. A critic could fairly say the encratites avoided wine on *ascetic/dualist* grounds (rejecting the material creation), which is the opposite of an LDS rationale.

## LDS Relevance
For Latter-day Saints the apologetic payoff is modest but real: it rebuts the rhetorical jab that sacramental *water* is self-evidently "wacky" by showing water-Eucharist was a live, attested option in the early centuries — and the LDS warrant is explicitly *not* the encratites' creation-denial but a revelation that "it mattereth not… what ye shall eat or… drink" ([[D&C 27.5|D&C 27:5]]) plus the symbolic case in [[Water Is a Better Symbol of Christ Than Wine]]. The historical claim is best stated cautiously (water-Eucharist existed and was debated), with the heavier interpretive weight carried by the symbol and sustenance arguments.

## Best Quotes
> "There's several early Christian groups that we know drank water, and we learn about these mostly from their enemies."[^1]

> "Water and bread Eucharist may stretch back to the very beginning of Christianity rather than being a later deviation from a recognized norm." (Bradshaw, as quoted)[^3]
