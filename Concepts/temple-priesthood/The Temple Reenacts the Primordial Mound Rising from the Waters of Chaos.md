# The Temple Reenacts the Primordial Mound Rising from the Waters of Chaos

#type/claim

## The Claim
Keystone argues that the temple does not merely symbolize *a* mountain but hearkens back to *the first* mountain — the **primordial mound**, the original dry land that rose out of the waters of chaos at creation. This extends the more familiar [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary|Eden-as-sanctuary]] reading one stage *earlier*, into cosmogony itself: before Eden there is the cosmic ordering of chaos, and the temple re-stages that primal act of order-from-chaos. The specific move — temple = primordial mound — is what is new here; the mountain-as-meeting-place-of-heaven-and-earth and the east-west Fall-reversal are treated in the sibling note [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]].

## Evidence
1. **Ancient creation begins with chaotic water, not a void.** Keystone contrasts the modern intuition (an empty nothingness God fills) with the ancient Near Eastern picture: the pre-creation state was "cosmic chaotic water," and God's first act was to create a *space* within it — a "firmament or a solid dome" dividing the waters above from the waters below, with land then emerging at the floor of that space [00:00]–[02:00]. This is the same cosmology catalogued in [[Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology Not Modern Science]] and [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]], and pictured in [[Psalm 104.5-9|Psalm 104:5–9]].
2. **The primordial mound is the first land out of chaos.** Citing LDS scholar [[Donald W. Parry]], Keystone states that "according to one Hebrew tradition, the primordial mound was the first land that emerged from the waters of chaos during the creative period," representing "order and definition amidst the unruly chaotic waters" [02:00].
3. **Therefore the temple hearkens back to that first mound.** "Temples not only represent a mountain, they hearken back to *the* mountain, the first mountain, the first sign of safety and organization to be called forth from chaos" [02:30]. The mound is then identified with the holy space of Eden (the garden "on a hill or mountain," Ezekiel 28), tying the cosmogonic claim into the Eden-temple typology [03:01].
4. **Cosmology, not borrowing.** As with the rest of the cluster, the link is typological and symbolic — the temple recapitulates a *pattern* of order overcoming chaos, not a literal geological claim. This keeps it inside the [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy|adaptation/accommodation]] frame the sibling source insists on.

## Best Quotes
> "That empty void of nothingness was believed to actually be filled with just cosmic chaotic water until... God came along and he created a space within that water. He created a firmament or a solid dome which divided the waters which were under the firmament from the waters which were above the firmament." [^1]

> "The primordial mound was the first land that emerged from the waters of chaos during the creative period. The primordial mound represented order and definition amidst the unruly chaotic waters." [^2]

> "Temples not only represent a mountain, they hearken back to the mountain, the first mountain, the first sign of safety and organization to be called forth from chaos." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This pushes the temple's symbolic "deep history" back behind Eden to the creation event itself, reinforcing the Restoration reading of creation as *ordering* pre-existing material rather than [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation ex nihilo]] — the same point made textually in [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]. The temple becomes a re-enactment of the whole arc of sacred space: chaos → ordered cosmos (the mound) → Eden → tabernacle → modern temple. It sits directly upstream of [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]] and shares [[Donald W. Parry]] as its scholarly source.
