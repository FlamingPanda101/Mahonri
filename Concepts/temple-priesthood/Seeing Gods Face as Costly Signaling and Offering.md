# Seeing God's Face as Costly Signaling and Offering

## The Claim

The command to "see the face of God" three times a year was not a free contemplative privilege but a mechanism fusing the **beatific vision** with **mandatory offerings**. The same verse forbids appearing "empty-handed," tying the pilgrimage to recurring material contributions and functioning as **costly signaling** that obligated males to physically present themselves (and their gifts) at the central sanctuary. [[Dan McClellan]] reads it as, in effect, a "quarterly offering" requirement.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **"Not empty-handed" is built into the command.** [[Deuteronomy 16.16|Deuteronomy 16:16]] ends "they shall not appear before the LORD empty-handed." McClellan: "it even says at the end you shall not see God's face empty-handed... it's being worked into this demand for offerings that are supposed to be three times a year."[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — the offering clause is explicit in the text.)*

2. **Costly signaling and compelled attendance.** Requiring males to appear bodily, with gifts, at fixed festivals "was part of costly signaling" that bound participants to the temple economy and community.[^3] *(Evidence rating: a standard cognitive-science-of-religion framing; analytically useful though interpretive.)*

3. **Festival timing enforced simultaneity.** Because attendance was pegged to the three pilgrimage festivals, "everyone has to do it at the same time" — producing a queue, a quick offering, and departure rather than leisurely devotion (see [[Beatific Vision in the Israelite Temple]]).[^4] *(Evidence rating: inference from festival logistics.)*

4. **The vision and the tithe are inseparable in the text.** The reframing of the command as merely "appearing before" obscures that the original "see God's face" act was the occasion for the offering — vision and economic obligation are one institution.[^5] *(Evidence rating: coherent, ties to the mistranslation thesis.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A traditional reader agrees the offering requirement is real but maintains it attaches to "appearing before" a transcendent God, not to literally seeing an image — the "empty-handed" clause works equally well under the standard translation, so it does not by itself establish the active "see" reading. The offering point is therefore corroborative of the institution's character, not decisive for the grammar (which rests on [[Infinitive Construct in Isaiah Proves Active Reading]]).

## Best Quotes

> "It's incorporated into this commandment in Deuteronomy 16:16 as a way to require that the males actually present themselves at the temple. This was part of costly signaling, but also there were almost certainly offerings."[^1]

> "It even says at the end you shall not see God's face empty-handed... it's being worked into this demand for offerings that are supposed to be three times a year. So it's time for your quarterly offering."[^2]
