# Divine Translatability and the Baal Cycle Mirror the Plan of Salvation

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] argues that the Canaanite **Baal cycle** preserves, in "translated" form, the structure of the LDS plan of salvation — and that recognizing this is licensed by what [[Mark Smith]] calls **"divine translatability"**: the ancient Near East routinely treated the gods of neighboring peoples as the *same* gods under different names. Read "from the Canaanites' own argument" (rather than through their biblical opponents' polemic), the Ugaritic pantheon maps onto the family of heaven: **El**, the ancient high "Father," with his consort **Asherah/Athirat**, the mother-goddess personified as a tree, and their son **Baal** ("husband/lord"), who descends to earth, conquers death and the underworld, is killed, is resurrected, and builds his house atop **Mount Zaphon**. The LDS reading substitutes the same offices — Father, [[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship|Heavenly Mother]], and **Yahweh** the Son, who likewise descends, conquers death and hell, dies, rises, and builds his house on **Mount Zion** ("almost Mount Zaphon"). Day's point is *not* syncretistic worship of Baal but that the recurring pattern reflects a truth "encoded in our DNA," surfacing also in Greece and in the [[Osiris Is a Type Not a Person - The Blessed Dead Become Osiris|Osiris]] cycle. This complements [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]] (tree = goddess) by adding the *full triadic structure* of the divine family, not just the mother-as-tree datum.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Divine translatability" is a recognized category, not LDS invention.** Day credits [[Mark Smith]] for the term: across the ancient Near East — and into Greece — there are "similar overtones," gods identified across cultures, so comparing systems is methodologically standard rather than special pleading. Smith's broader work documents Israel's emergence from a [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon|divine-council pantheon]] (see also [[The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh]]). [^1]
2. **The Baal-cycle plot is a death-and-resurrection narrative.** "You have a god named El — he's the high God, the ancient father... he has a wife named Asherah, personified as a tree, a mother goddess, and they have a son named Baal, whose name means husband. Baal comes down to earth, conquers death and hell, is killed, and then is resurrected, and has his house on top of Mount Zaphon." The structural parallel to a descending, dying, rising Yahweh is the whole point. [^2]
3. **Yahweh as the Son who repeats the pattern.** Day reads the LDS substitution explicitly: El ≈ the Father, Asherah ≈ Heavenly Mother, and their son ≈ Yahweh, who "comes down to earth, conquers death and hell, dies in the process, is resurrected, and builds his house not on Mount Zaphon but Mount Zion" — the near-identity of the mountain names underscoring the mapping. [^3]
4. **The recurrence is read as a witness, not a debunking.** Like the way "Harry Potter, Star Wars... blow up" because they re-tell the plan of salvation "wrapped up in different names," Day treats the cross-cultural pattern as evidence that the gospel story is primordial and widely (if imperfectly) preserved — the Canaanites may have lacked authority, but "it's encoded in our DNA where we came from, why we're here, and where we're going." This is the same interpretive logic the vault applies to [[Osiris Is a Type Not a Person - The Blessed Dead Become Osiris|Osiris]] and (with caution) to [[The Facsimiles Encode Abraham's Endowment Through the Osiris Myth|the facsimiles]]. [^4]

**Steelman of the skeptic.** Day flags this himself — "this is going to sound like crazy talk," "I don't have an altar to Baal in my house." A critic will press: (a) "divine translatability" describes ancient *equating* of deities; it does not establish that any cycle is *true* or that Yahweh "is" Baal; (b) the dying-and-rising-god category is contested — [[Trygve Mettinger]]'s *The Riddle of Resurrection* argues some cases are real but the comparison has historically been overstated (Frazer), and the Baal "death" is seasonal/agricultural, not soteriological; (c) Mount Zaphon and Mount Zion are different mountains, and the resemblance of names is not identity; (d) reading the cycle "from the Canaanites' side" still relies on fragmentary Ugaritic texts whose theology is debated. **But** the modest core holds: the El–Asherah–Baal *family structure* (high father, tree-goddess consort, royal son) is straightforwardly attested at Ugarit, and it is the same structure the [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms|reform]] is said to have stripped from Israel — so a pre-exilic Israelite who knew this pattern reading it into the divine family is historically plausible, whatever one concludes about Baal's resurrection.

## Best Quotes
> "You have a god named El — he's the high God, the ancient father. He has a wife named Asherah, and she's personified as a tree, a mother goddess. And they have a son named Baal... Baal comes down to earth, conquers death and hell, is killed, and then is resurrected, and has his house on the top of Mount Zaphon. Does that sound like anything you've heard of before?" [^2]

> "There's a divine translatability — that's what Mark Smith calls it — when you start to study the religions of the ancient Near East, and not just there but even in Greece, there's similar overtones." [^1]

> "It's encoded in our DNA where we came from, why we're here, and where we're going — and we'll figure out weird ways to clothe it all up, but in the end it's the story." [^4]
