# Hearing and Doing the Sermon Is a Creative Act

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler|David Butler]] makes two Greek-lexical observations on the close of the Sermon on the Mount. First, in [[Matthew 7.7-8|Matthew 7:8]] "everyone who asks *receiveth*" uses *lambanō*, whose basic sense is "to take into the hands" — so "grasp" is a live translation: the petitioner actively *takes hold*. Second, in [[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:24]] "whosoever heareth these sayings of mine, and *doeth* them" uses *poieō* — not *prassō* ("do/perform") but *poieō*, "to create/make" (the root of *poet*), the verb of creation in the Genesis 1 Septuagint ("God *made* the heaven and the earth"). So the wise man who hears and "does" the Sermon is performing a *creative* act, and the worshipper "has to become a creator" — echoing the Eden creation story behind the temple ordinance.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. *lambanō* **as "grasp."** Butler: "the verb 'receiveth' is *lambanō*... the basic meaning is to take into your hands, so 'anyone who asks, grasps' would be another translation." This reframes the asking/seeking/knocking as *active reception*, not passive waiting — fitting the ordinance arc of petition → admission. *Rating: lexically accurate; *lambanō* does carry "take/grasp," though "receive" is the standard rendering.*[^1]
2. *poieō* **as "create."** Butler: "the verb to do here is *poieō*... not *prassō*. *Poieō* is to create — it's the word that *poet* comes from... in the Greek Old Testament, [God] *made* the heaven and the earth." So "doeth these sayings" carries a creation overtone. *Rating: *poieō* genuinely spans "do/make/create" and is the LXX Genesis 1 verb; the *creative* nuance is a real semantic possibility, though *poieō* is also the ordinary Greek verb for "do."*[^2]
3. **The worshipper as creator, echoing Eden.** Butler ties this to the temple ordinance enacting the creation/Eden story: "the person, the worshipper, has to become a creator... you have to hear these things and then you have to also create them." This dovetails with the [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Sermon-as-ordinance]] reading and [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]]. He notes the wise man "builds his house upon a rock" — wisdom + house (each temple room is a "house" in Kings) + the foundation stone — so the "doing" *is* the building/creating of the kingdom. *Rating: an attractive synthesis with the temple cluster; the creator-worshipper move is interpretive.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** *Poieō* is simply the default Greek verb for "do/make" and appears thousands of times with no creational freight; choosing the "create" sense in Matthew 7:24 over the plain "do/practice" is selecting the connotation the argument wants. Matthew's point is the homely one of the parable: the wise builder *acts on* Jesus's words (vs. the foolish who only hears), and "house on a rock" is about a storm-proof foundation, not the *debir* boulder. Likewise *lambanō* as "receive" needs no "grasp" gloss to make sense of answered prayer. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the readings are offered as *resonances* layered on the plain sense, not replacements for it, and they gain plausibility from the surrounding ordinance frame (mountain ascent, *téleios*, the wise/foolish builders) where creation-of-the-self is thematically apt; the discipline is to hold them as suggestive echoes, consonant with the becoming-like-Christ theme of [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works]].
