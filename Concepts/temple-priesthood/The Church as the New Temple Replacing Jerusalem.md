## The Point
After the Romans destroyed the Jerusalem temple in AD 70, Christians compensated for its diminishing role by reinterpreting the temple in three ways: Jesus's death fulfilled and replaced the temple's sacrificial system; the Christian community itself became the new "spiritualized temple" that housed the divine presence on earth; and attention turned to the heavenly temple where Christ serves as the Great High Priest. This metaphor, loosely applied to believers since the first century, eventually found physical expression in monumental churches.

## Evidence
1. Jesus's death is presented as the ultimate fulfillment of the temple's sacrificial system (John 1:29–34; 19:14–37; Romans 3:23–26; 5:8–11; Hebrews 8–10; Revelation 1:6; 5:6).
2. The community is the new spiritualized temple housing the divine presence (1 Corinthians 3:16–17; 2 Corinthians 6:16–18; [[Ephesians 2.19-22|Ephesians 2:19–22]]; 1 Peter 2:4–10).
3. Second- and third-century writers symbolically called the prayers and Eucharistic meals of house-church gatherings the new spiritual sacrifices replacing temple rituals (Didache 14; Irenaeus, fragment 37; Tertullian, Against Marcion 3.7; Apostolic Tradition 4.1–13).
4. Some third-century Syrian communities began calling their *domus ecclesiae* a "temple [hayklā] of the church" (Syriac Edessene Chronicle for AD 201; Porphyry, Against the Christians fragment 76) or "houses of the Lord" (Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History 9.10.10–11).
5. This gradual adoption of temple-holiness — "templization" — would culminate in the monumental basilicas of the fourth century and beyond.

## Best Quotes
> "Christians began to compensate for its diminishing role in their religious life by seeing Jesus's death as the ultimate fulfillment of the temple's sacrificial system ... by seeing the Christian community itself as the new spiritualized temple that housed the divine presence on earth ... and by turning their attention to the heavenly temple where Jesus provided the ultimate intercession for humanity as the Great High Priest." [^1]

> "These renovated church spaces seem to have gradually adopted aspects of temple-holiness that added an elevated level of sanctity to Christian liturgy — a process of 'templization' that would culminate with the monumental church buildings of the fourth century and beyond." [^2]

## LDS Connection
While Grey notes these spaces did not function as temples "in the ancient or modern (Latter-day Saint) sense," the early Christian conviction that the worshipping community housed God's presence offers conceptual vocabulary for Latter-day Saint temple theology. [^2]
