# The Patriarchal Order Is the Order of Fatherhood Not Male Rule

## The Claim
[[Tikla Fe]] reframes "patriarchal order" away from "men ruling": *patri* = fatherhood, *archy* = its relation to leading/beginning, so the patriarchal order is **the order of fatherhood**. The priesthood, being within that order, is therefore "an order of fatherhood" that forms men into good father-figures — and "the best thing you can do for a woman and children is to have good fathers." The point is to recast male priesthood as relational service, not dominion.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The etymological reframe (moderate).** "Patriarchal actually means fatherhood (patri) and… its relationship to leadership or beginning (archy)" — so "it's not men leading, it's fatherhood and the order of fatherhood as it relates to leading."[^1]
2. **Priesthood as fatherhood-formation (interpretive).** "Whether you're a dad or not, you're initiated into this order that is the order of fatherhood," and initiates "behave like good fathers and good father figures" — a relational identity that exists only "in relation to women, in relation to children."[^2]
3. **Oriented to the good of women and children (interpretive).** "Is there anything that could be better for women and children than good fathers? No." The order is justified by its fruits for the family, not by male prerogative.[^2]
4. **Asymmetry with a "matriarchal order" (moderate).** Tikla argues motherhood/feminine power is "organic… circular… sisterhood" needing no rigid hierarchy, so there is no parallel regimented "matriarchal order" — except the one feminine-named order, matrimony (see [[Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother]]).[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
A feminist or affirming critic will press hard. First, the etymology is selective: "patriarchy" in ordinary and scholarly usage *does* denote male rule, and re-glossing it as "fatherhood-as-service" can look like a way to retain male-only authority while renaming it — the lived reality (men hold the priesthood and preside) is unchanged by the word-study. Second, the claim that women's power is "organic/circular" and needs "no hierarchy" can be read as a benevolent essentialism that conveniently explains why women are *not* ordained. Third, "good fathers are the best thing for women and children" is true but does not entail that *only* a male-defined priesthood order can secure it. The strongest honest version concedes that the *structure* is indeed male (priesthood is held by men) while arguing the *theology* of that structure is service/fatherhood patterned on Christ (cf. [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]]) — and rests the case for the structure on revelation and the Proclamation, not on an etymology that critics can contest.

## LDS Relevance
The concept is pastorally useful: it reframes priesthood for Latter-day Saint men as fatherly stewardship that exists *for* women and children, countering both external "patriarchy = oppression" readings and internal abuses of priesthood as personal power (D&C 121). Its limits should be marked: the word-study illustrates a theology of service but does not by itself answer the ordination question, which the Church grounds in revelation. Paired with [[Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother]], it sets up the episode's claim that the temple covenant path forms a man toward being a good father and authorizes the making of a mother.
