# The Oath of Vengeance Was a Prayer for Justice, Later Removed

## The Claim
The so-called "oath of vengeance" — a temple element antagonists describe as a sworn commitment to personally avenge the blood of the prophets against the United States — originated, historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] argues, as a **prayer for divine justice** modeled on [[Revelation 6.9-11|Revelation 6:9-10]], occasioned by the 1844 murders of [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Hyrum Smith]]. It was a petition that *God* avenge their blood, not a personal vendetta, and it was removed by the 1920s.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The documented origin is a martyr-prayer.** On the first anniversary of Joseph Smith's death (1845), church leaders prayed a temple prayer "that sort of invokes the book of Revelation" — [[Revelation 6.9-11|Revelation 6:9-10]], where the slain "cried with a loud voice... How long, O Lord... dost thou not judge and avenge our blood?" They "want God to avenge the blood of the prophets," thinking of Joseph and Hyrum.[^2] *Rating: strong — a datable origin tied to a specific scriptural template.*

2. **Antagonists recast it in litigation.** "Antagonistic folks would cast it in terms of people were... required to personally avenge the blood of the prophets... against the United States." This surfaced in late-19th-century court cases over whether a Latter-day Saint could become a U.S. citizen, where ex-members testified it was an oath against the nation and members "equivocated," denying any promise to personally avenge against the United States.[^3] *Rating: moderate–strong — the citizenship-hearing context is historical; the exact wording was contested even then, which the source openly notes.*

3. **It was removed as its generation faded.** The injunction "endures until the 1920s," when "the people that lived through it and remembered it" — for whom it "was personal" — were passing, and it had become "incongruous with lived experience."[^4] This fits the broader pattern that early-church temple elements "made sense for early church members" but were "baffling and alienating to modern" ones — see [[Ten Abandoned Temple Practices Show Continuity Through Change]]. *Rating: moderate — the dating and rationale are Stapley's documented reconstruction.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can stress that, whatever its scriptural pedigree, surviving accounts (largely from disaffected members, Stapley concedes) describe participants *personally* covenanting to pray for or seek vengeance, and that "pray for God to avenge" still encoded real political grievance against the United States in the Utah-era context — so calling it merely "a prayer for justice" softens a genuinely martial element. The fair reading grants that the sources are hostile and the wording uncertain, while maintaining the core documented fact: the element grew out of a Revelation-6 martyr-prayer after a double murder, and the Church removed it — a change, not a cover-up.

## Best Quotes
> "They have a temple prayer in which they pray for those same things. They use that same language. They want God to avenge the blood of the prophets. And clearly they're thinking about Joseph and Hyrum."[^2]

> "That generation was fading... it became incongruous with lived experience."[^4]
