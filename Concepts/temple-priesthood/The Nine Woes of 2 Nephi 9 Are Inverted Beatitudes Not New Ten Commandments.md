# The Nine Woes of 2 Nephi 9 Are Inverted Beatitudes Not New Ten Commandments

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that Jacob's list of "woes" in **2 Nephi 9:30-38** is not, as a 1990s FARMS article proposed, a "new Ten Commandments" for the Nephites — it is a set of **inverted Beatitudes**, and therefore a Book-of-Mormon witness that the [[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance|Beatitude ordinance]] is older than Jesus. [^1] Two problems sink the "Ten Commandments" reading: there are only **nine** woes (the article's author conceded "but it's close"), and they do not *sound* like commandments ("Thou shalt not kill") — they are structured **"woe unto X, *for they shall* Y"**, which is the **mirror image of a Beatitude** ("blessed are X, *for they shall* Y"). [^2] The number matches Matthew's **nine** Beatitude-statements, and — a detail the hosts catch on air — the punishments reduce to **seven repeated** sanctions ("shall perish," "thrust down to hell"), matching the seven distinct Beatitude *blessings* (the 9-vs-7 seam flagged in [[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance|Part I]]). [^3] Jacob is "ripping them a hole" using language his audience recognizes as **authoritative temple language** — "language that is their own temple language... that they heard when they were making covenants of the sort that they're breaking now." [^4] Butler stacks corroborating Book-of-Mormon reuse: 2 Nephi 9:41-42 ("the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel... whoso knocketh, to him will he open"; "the rock of your salvation" behind the gate) recycles the [[Matthew 7.13-14|Matthew 7]] ask/knock/narrow-gate ordinance; 2 Nephi 4 (the Psalm of Nephi) and 2 Nephi 1 (Lehi urging Laman and Lemuel to "put on... the armor of righteousness" and "come forth out of darkness") reuse the same vocabulary — because [[Nephi]], Lehi, and even Laman/Lemuel were initiated and expected the reader to be. [^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The structural inversion is exact and checkable.** A Beatitude is "blessed are X, for they shall [reward]"; Jacob's woes are "woe unto X, for they shall [punishment]." That is a clean grammatical inversion, not a loose theme — and it is a far better fit than "Ten Commandments," which are apodictic imperatives ("thou shalt not"), a different literary form entirely. *Rating: strong on form-critical grounds — the woe-formula genuinely mirrors the macarism, and the Decalogue reading is the weaker rival on its own terms.*

2. **The count corroborates rather than coincides.** Nine woes against Matthew's nine *blessed-are* statements, collapsing to seven repeated punishments against seven distinct blessings, reuses the *same* 9→7 asymmetry independently observed in the Beatitudes. A motivated reader could tune one number; matching *both* the surface count and the reduced count is harder to engineer. *Rating: the counts are accurate; their alignment is the load-bearing evidence, stronger than the bare menorah-seven of Part I.*

3. **The surrounding chapter is saturated with ordinance language.** 2 Nephi 9 independently carries Eden/adversary material, the "robe of righteousness" (vv.7, 14), a judgment-seat appearance (v.15), and the narrow-gate/keeper-of-the-gate/knock sequence (vv.41-42) — so the woes sit inside a passage already doing temple work, which makes the inverted-Beatitude reading contextually motivated rather than imposed. See [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]]. *Rating: the context genuinely supports a liturgical reading of the chapter as a whole.*

4. **It is the episode's apologetic payload, and it cuts at authorship.** If Jacob (a 6th-century-BC figure in the narrative) is reworking the Beatitude *form* with the right number and structure, the implication Butler draws is that "the Beatitudes didn't find their origin in Christ on that mountain... Jesus is an inheritor and a passer-on of a tradition that's before him" — and that a hat-and-stone dictation is an implausible way to manufacture such a buried form-critical match. *Rating: the inference is the strongest version of the channel's "written by initiates for initiates" thesis.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic notes the woe-form is a *common prophetic genre* (Isaiah 5's "woe unto them"; Habakkuk; Luke 6's woes paralleling its Beatitudes) — so Jacob's woes need not derive from *Matthew's* Beatitudes at all; a 19th-century author steeped in the KJV would naturally produce woe-oracles, and the "for they shall" tail is simply how such oracles read. The reduction to "seven repeated punishments" again requires choosing which to merge, the same tunability flagged in [[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance]]. And the "Ten Commandments" article being wrong does not make "inverted Beatitudes" right — it could just be a prophetic woe-list. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim does not rest on the woe-genre alone but on the *convergence* of (a) the exact macarism-inversion, (b) the matching 9→7 counts, and (c) the chapter's independent narrow-gate/robe/judgment ordinance vocabulary — and unlike Luke's woes (which Luke pairs *with* explicit Beatitudes), Jacob supplies only the inverted half, presupposing an audience that already holds the Beatitude original as covenant language.

## Best Quotes
> "There are two basic problems with thinking that this is a new Ten Commandments. There's only nine of them... and the commandments don't sound like this. These are Beatitudes — they're inverted Beatitudes. Instead of 'theirs is the kingdom of God,' it's 'thrust down to hell.'" [^2]

> "The number of them is the number of Beatitudes in Matthew 5, and the structure of them is that they are inverted Beatitudes... and what's crazy is that it matches up with seven repeated punishments." [^3]

> "In the sermon he's ripping them a hole in language that they recognize as authoritative — in language that is their own temple language, language that they heard when they were making covenants of the sort that they're breaking now." [^4]
