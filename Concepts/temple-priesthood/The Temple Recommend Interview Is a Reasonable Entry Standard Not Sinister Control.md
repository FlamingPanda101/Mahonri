# The Temple Recommend Interview Is a Reasonable Entry Standard, Not Sinister Control

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Johnny Harris]] frames the temple-recommend interview — sitting "alone with this man... your authority" answering 15 questions, including tithing — as coerced affirmation: "it is also about affirming your loyalty to the doctrine, to the organization, to the people in charge."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Standards for sacred space are normal, not sinister
[[Keystone]]: "I don't think it's weird for there to be a standard that people are expected to meet in order to enter the temple." Gatekeeping a sacred space by readiness is unremarkable.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a framing point — entry requirements for sacred or restricted spaces are commonplace across religions and institutions.

### 2. The ancient temple had entry requirements too
"Potential patrons of the ancient Israelite temple were expected to be righteous, speak the truth, do right by their neighbor, and to keep their oaths" — and inscriptions around the sanctuary warned that unauthorized entrants "could be killed." Modern recommend questions are mild by comparison.[^3] See [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]] and [[Guardians of Sacred Space and the Password Motif]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the Israelite temple "entrance liturgy" and the Soreg warning inscription are historically attested.

### 3. Coherence, not loyalty, is the point
"If you're not striving to follow the law of chastity, you probably shouldn't be going to the temple where you promise to follow the law of chastity. If you reject the law of 10% tithing, you probably shouldn't be going to the temple where you promise to be willing to live the law of 100% consecration."[^4] The interview checks that one is not covenanting to a law one rejects — an integrity condition, not a loyalty oath.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — internally coherent; entering a covenant one disbelieves would be the genuinely odd act.

### 4. The questions are not frozen
Keystone grants the recommend questions "have changed somewhat over time, and I'm sure they'll be refined more in the future" — consistent with the institutional development of [[Movements Must Institutionalize to Survive Their Charismatic Founder]].[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a candid concession that strengthens the "reasonable standard" framing.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Harris's stronger point survives: whatever the precedent, the interview *does* condition access to a rite the Church teaches is necessary for the highest blessings (and for attending family weddings) on assent to specific beliefs, leaders, and a 10% payment — and that combination of social, familial, and financial pressure can feel coercive regardless of ancient parallels. "Ancient temples did it too" justifies *having* a standard but not the *content* of any particular question. The fair conclusion: gatekeeping sacred space by readiness and covenant-integrity is reasonable and well-precedented, while the specific stakes attached to the recommend (family exclusion, tithing) are a legitimate target for critique that the ancient-precedent move does not fully answer.
