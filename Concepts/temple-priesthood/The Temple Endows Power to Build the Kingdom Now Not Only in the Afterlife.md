# The Temple Endows Power to Build the Kingdom Now, Not Only in the Afterlife

## The Claim
Historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] argues that the temple **endowment** is misunderstood when it is framed merely as a credential for the next life ("magical box you have to check to get the thing"). Read through its scriptural source, the endowment is a bestowal of *power to act in the present* — to build up the kingdom of God now — and exaltation itself is something disciples *construct*, not a reward they passively receive.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The word comes from a commission to act.** From [[Luke 24.49|Luke 24:49]], the risen Christ tells the disciples to tarry "until ye be endued with power from on high" — power for a stated task: "preach the gospel throughout the entire world and build up the kingdom of God." The Doctrine and Covenants "follows that pattern exactly," substituting Kirtland for Jerusalem.[^2] *Rating: strong — the purpose clause ("power to do what?") is explicit in the source text.*

2. **"Power to do what?"** Stapley resists "decontextualizing the temple every time": the endowment is not free-floating spiritual energy but power *for* a mission, so it "immediately makes the temple applicable to right now... get your butt in gear, build the kingdom with power today" rather than only "so that one day I can be like God in the eternities."[^3] *Rating: strong — a direct, repeated emphasis of the interview, framed as the host's "light-bulb moment."*

3. **Exaltation is constructed, not awarded.** Joseph Smith "rejected" the idea that heaven is "a reward for the good" or "a gift for the elect"; instead "heaven is something that you construct. We build it on the altars of the temple" through sealing relationships — "we are the priesthood of heaven." Checking the ordinance "box" is incidental to the real work of *building*.[^4] See [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]]. *Rating: moderate — a theological reading of Joseph Smith that coheres with the [[Revelation 7.9-14|Revelation 7]] vision of kings and priests Stapley cites as the endowment's pattern.*

4. **It reframes work for the dead.** Asked how this applies to vicarious temple work, Stapley extends the logic: the same endowment of power equips the dead to labor in the spirit world — the present-tense, kingdom-building reading scales beyond mortality without collapsing back into a mere afterlife-ticket.[^5] *Rating: moderate — a consistent extension rather than a separately argued point.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic — or a traditional believer — can object that the "power to act now" reading downplays the temple's explicit afterlife claims (exaltation, eternal marriage, sealing for eternity), which the Church's own teaching foregrounds, and that "you construct heaven" risks a works-righteousness that strains against grace. Stapley's framing is best taken as a corrective rather than a denial: the endowment really does promise eternal blessings, but he insists those blessings are *relational and built*, not transactional, so the present-tense empowerment and the eternal outcome are two ends of one process rather than rivals.

## Best Quotes
> "He said, you guys need to preach the gospel throughout the entire world... therefore wait in Jerusalem until you're endowed with power from on high. And the Doctrine and Covenants follow that pattern exactly."[^2]

> "Heaven is something that you construct. We build it on the altars of the temple... Then it's less a thing — of course you check the box along the way, because that's how you construct heaven."[^4]
