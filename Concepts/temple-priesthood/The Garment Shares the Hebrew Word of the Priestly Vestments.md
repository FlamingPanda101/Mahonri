# The Garment Shares the Hebrew Word of the Priestly Vestments

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]], following [[John Walton]], argues that the "magic underwear" caricature of the temple garment collapses on a lexical fact: the Hebrew word for the garments God makes for Adam and Eve after the fall — **kothoneth** ([[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3:21]], "coats of skins") — is **the same word** used in [[Exodus 28|Exodus 28]] for the **priestly vestments** of the Aaronic priesthood.[^1] Walton's reading is that Genesis 3 has been building Adam and Eve as *priestly figures* throughout — guardians and tenders of a garden-temple — so the garments at the chapter's end are the **conclusion of a priestly commissioning, not a punishment**: "the mortal condition is a priestly assignment, and the garment marks who you are in relation to God during that assignment."[^1] Arnold then turns the critic's own standard around: the high priest of ancient Israel wore specific white linen garments *against his body, under his robes,* when he entered God's presence, and "the Hebrew word for those garments and the root of the word for the endowment garment are the same."[^2] So "if wearing consecrated clothing against the body is superstitious or primitive, then that critique applies equally to the Levitical High Priesthood" — and to many religions across time. "Make the critique consistently, or just don't make it."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The lexical link is verifiable.** That *kethoneth/kothoneth* (כְּתֹנֶת) denotes both the Eden garments (Gen 3:21) and the priestly tunic (Exod 28:4, 39–40; Lev 8:7) is a checkable fact of the Hebrew text, independent of any LDS source. *Rating: solid, load-bearing datum.* [^1]
2. **Walton's priestly-Eden reading is mainstream.** [[John Walton]] is an evangelical Wheaton professor who draws *no* LDS conclusions; his garden-as-temple, Adam-as-priest thesis is widely cited ANE scholarship, so the foundation is not LDS special pleading. *Rating: strong; the LDS application is downstream of independent work.* [^1]
3. **The tu-quoque is fair.** The "primitive/superstitious" charge against consecrated under-clothing does apply equally to the Levitical priesthood (and to vestments across traditions), so the critic must either retract the charge or extend it to the biblical priesthood. *Rating: a clean consistency argument.* [^2]
4. **It connects to the wider garment theology in the vault.** The garment as priestly investiture and "covering of Christ" is developed in [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]] and [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]], and the priestly-guardian motif in [[Guardians of Sacred Space and the Password Motif]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic replies: (a) **shared vocabulary is not shared meaning** — *kethoneth* is simply the ordinary Hebrew word for "tunic" (Joseph's "coat of many colors" is a *kethoneth* too, Gen 37:3), so its appearance in both Genesis 3 and Exodus 28 may reflect a common garment-word, not a deliberate priestly typology; (b) Walton's *Adam-as-priest* reading, while respectable, is **one interpretation** and is contested by scholars who read Genesis 2–3 without a temple frame; (c) even granting an Eden-temple typology in the *biblical* text, that does not establish that the *modern* LDS garment descends from it rather than being a 19th-century institution given an ancient rationale after the fact. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim here is modest and defensive — it does **not** need to prove lineal descent, only to defeat the "magic underwear" *mockery* by showing the practice of consecrated clothing against the body is biblical and priestly, so the ridicule indicts the Levitical high priest too; and the *kethoneth*-of-Joseph point actually reinforces the garment-as-investiture theme (Joseph's robe marks status and is stripped in his "death," a recognized type) rather than undercutting it.
