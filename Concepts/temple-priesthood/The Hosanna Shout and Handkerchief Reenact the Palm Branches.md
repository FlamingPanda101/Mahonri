# The Hosanna Shout and Handkerchief Reenact the Palm Branches

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] and [[Stephen Jones]] answer the common complaint that the **hosanna shout and the waving of a white handkerchief** at a temple dedication are weird, distinctively "Mormon" inventions. Their argument: these acts are a direct reenactment of the crowd at **Christ's triumphal entry**, who shouted "Hosanna" and waved **palm branches** to welcome the Messiah into Jerusalem ([[Zechariah 9.9|Zechariah 9:9]]; [[Matthew 21.18-22|Matthew 21]]). The handkerchief stands "in similitude of the palm branches," and *hosanna* itself means **"save us"** — so the congregation is shouting an ancient Hebrew plea and waving the ancient royal welcome to invite Christ into the building the Saints have built and consecrated to him. [^1] Because Jesus, immediately after the palm-branch welcome, entered the Jerusalem temple to **cleanse it**, the dedication's hosanna liturgy is the act by which Latter-day Saints invite the Lord to cleanse and take possession of *his* house — and the same act, the prayer says, is what the **seraphs around God's throne** are doing in heaven ([[D&C 109]]:79), so the dedication is "on earth as it is in heaven." [^2] The dedicatory prayer even anticipates the worshippers themselves standing "with palms in our hands" in eternity, an image Bradley ties to the palm-bearing multitude of [[Revelation 7.9-14|Revelation 7]]. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The biblical anchor for the gesture is explicit, not inferred.** All four Gospels record palm/branch-waving and hosanna-shouting at the entry; *hosanna* is a transliterated Hebrew/Aramaic cry (Ps. 118:25, "save now"), so "save us" is its actual meaning, not a gloss. The LDS rite reproduces a documented liturgical action rather than improvising one. *Rating: strong scriptural footing.*

2. **The follow-on logic is textually tight.** In the Gospels the welcome leads directly into the **temple cleansing**, and Latter-day Saint dedication is precisely the moment a building *becomes* a temple ("before that, it's just a pretty building"). Mapping welcome → cleansing → consecration onto the Jerusalem sequence is therefore a structural match, not a free association. This is the gesture-level counterpart to [[The Kirtland Dedication Recapitulates Christ's Final Week|the broader chronology argument]]. *Rating: coherent and specific.*

3. **It reframes the "weird ritual" objection on its own terms.** The hosts grant the gesture *looks* foreign to week-by-week worship, then show it is more scriptural than the sacrament's "ordinary" eating and drinking. This is the same move as [[Ritual Has Been Normal for Four Thousand Years]]: unfamiliarity is cultural, not evidence of fabrication. *Rating: rhetorically effective, defensible.*

4. **The "on earth as in heaven" frame is in the canonized prayer itself.** [[D&C 109]] explicitly joins the worshippers' hosannas to the angels' praise around the throne and pictures palms in their hands — so the heavenly-liturgy reading is the text's own self-description, not a later imposition. *Rating: grounded in the primary document.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic concedes the palm-branch parallel and still objects that it proves *derivation*, not *restoration*: anyone who reads the Gospels can stage a hosanna-and-branches ceremony, so the resemblance shows only that Joseph Smith borrowed a vivid New-Testament image, not that the rite descends from an ancient temple practice. The handkerchief is a 19th-century substitution (palms being unavailable in Ohio), which is candidly a *re-enactment prop*, not a preserved artifact — undercutting any claim to unbroken continuity. And reading the dedication as the angels' heavenly liturgy is a devotional interpretation of [[D&C 109]], not a neutral one. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim here is modest and defensive — it does **not** assert the handkerchief is ancient, only that the gesture it enacts is the *scriptural* welcome of Christ, so the "weird Mormon custom" charge fails; and that the act is *about Christ* (welcoming, cleansing, consecrating) is the whole point, whether one classifies it as restoration or as deliberate scriptural patterning.

## Best Quotes
> "Shouting hosanna, waving the handkerchief in similitude of the palm branches, they were welcoming him into the Kirtland Temple to cleanse it. That's what we do as well. That's the dedication. That's the consecration. That's what turns it into a temple." [^1]

> "Hosanna means 'save us.' Come in to this place we prepared for you... they thought that Jesus was going to save them from the Romans, when he was really coming to save them from everything — save the whole world from sin and death." [^4]

> "The angels in heaven are singing hosanna to God and the Lamb, and we're joining our voices to the voices of the angels in heaven, waving the palm branches... on earth as it is in heaven." [^2]
