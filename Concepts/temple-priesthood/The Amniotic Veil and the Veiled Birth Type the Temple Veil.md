# The Amniotic Veil and the Veiled Birth Type the Temple Veil

#type/claim

## The Claim
Tia argues that the temple veil has its bodily archetype in **birth**. The amniotic sac is a membrane — a "veil"; when "the water breaks," that veil is *torn* and the child is delivered. Rarely, a baby is born inside the intact sac, which obstetrics calls an **"en caul" or "veiled birth"** — "this is not a Latter-day Saint thing, this is just standard midwifery." [^1] The burst membrane on the newborn's face "looks literally like a wedding veil." The typological link: when Christ delivered humanity at the crucifixion, **the temple veil was rent** ([[Matthew 27.46|Matthew 27:50–51]]); so "when your mother gave birth to you, delivered you, her veil was rent." [^2] The conclusion reframes the women's temple veil: since a woman literally carries a veil within her body, "who [is] more fitted to wear the symbol of the veil than the woman" — a veil marks a sacred, Godly presence behind it, not erasure. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Veiled birth" / en caul is a genuine obstetric term.** Birth with the amniotic sac intact over the head is medically real and historically remarked (the dried caul was a sailor's good-luck "caul-bearer" charm) — so the linguistic bridge from *amniotic membrane* to *veil* is external to LDS usage and checkable. *Rating: solid factual anchor.* [^1]
2. **The rent temple veil at the crucifixion is canonical.** The Synoptics report the veil of the temple torn at Christ's death ([[Matthew 27.46|Matthew 27:51]]); the vault already reads that tearing as an *opening of access* in [[The Veil Is Christ Reversing the Day of Atonement]] — a birth-as-deliverance gloss layers onto that without contradicting it. *Rating: the scriptural datum is firm; the birth overlay is devotional.* [^2]
3. **It supplies a positive answer to a real pastoral wound.** The episode plays a clip of a woman who felt "erased," "faceless," a "white blob" while veiled; the argument's payoff is to recast the veil as a marker of sacred presence (cf. [[The Veil Marks Sanctity Not Shame and Is Meant to Be Traversed]], and the priesthood-veil symbolism in [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]]). *Rating: a constructive reframe, not a proof.* [^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The whole inference rides on the **English word "veil"** doing double duty for an amniotic membrane and a ritual curtain — a coincidence of translation, not a demonstrable ancient connection; Hebrew/Greek do not pun "amniotic sac" with the temple *paroketh/katapetasma*. A critic also notes the reframe does not actually answer the woman's complaint (that *only women* were veiled while men remained individuated). A measured reply: the host concedes the women's veil practice was later changed and that some discomfort is unrelated to symbolism; the claim is offered as devotional *meaning-making* — a way to see the veil as sacred rather than demeaning — anchored by the genuinely external fact that a "veiled birth" is real midwifery, with the crucifixion's rent veil supplying the canonical hinge.

## Best Quotes
> "When the baby is born in the amniotic sac, it's called a veiled birth... and it looks literally like a wedding veil on this baby's face. So we had to go through a torn veil to come into this world." [^1]

> "What happened in the temple when Jesus Christ delivered us? The veil was rent. So when your mother gave birth to you, delivered you, her veil was rent." [^2]

The note joins the vault's veil cluster ([[The Veil Marks Sanctity Not Shame and Is Meant to Be Traversed]], [[The Veil Is Christ Reversing the Day of Atonement]]) and the divine-feminine recovery, arguing that the very symbol critics call demeaning to women is one for which a woman's own body is the prototype — a marker of the sacred presence behind it (the womb before mortality, the Holy of Holies, the bride before the bridegroom).
