# The Narrow Gate to Life Is Birth Through the Wall of Thorns and Thistles

#type/claim

## The Claim
At the entrance to the holy of holies in the [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Sermon-as-ordinance]], [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] reads "enter ye in at the **strait gate**... narrow is the way, which leadeth unto **life**" (Matt 7:13-14) as a **reversal of the Eden expulsion staged as a birth**. The decisive datum, from [[Mike Day]]: the Greek words for "thorns and thistles" in the warning about false prophets (Matt 7:16, "do men gather grapes of *thorns*, or figs of *thistles*?") are **ἄκανθα** (*akantha*) and **τρίβολος** (*tribolos*) — the **exact words** the Septuagint uses when Adam and Eve are cursed and the ground brings forth "thorns and thistles" ([[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3]]). [^1] So the thistles and thorns are not random — they are **Eden's curse functioning as a wall**, a barrier ringing the garden: "you're passing through the wall of *akantha* and *tribolos*, the narrow gate, to get to life." [^2] And "life" is loaded: Eve's name *ḥavah* ("mother of all living") was rendered in the LXX as **Zōē** ("life"), tying the gate's destination to **Eve and the tree of life** — "every good tree bringeth forth good fruit" (7:17) puts the tree back on the far side. [^3] Mike layers a birth-reading: a child literally "passes into life through a narrow and strait gate," seeing its mother (the tree) on emerging; and **mitzraim** (Egypt) means a "narrow/tight space" (place of confinement/bondage), which "one rabbi" reads as **the womb** — Israel reborn coming "out of mitzraim, through the waters." [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The LXX lexical match is exact and checkable.** *Akantha* and *tribolos* are precisely the nouns the Septuagint of Genesis 3:18 uses for the cursed ground's "thorns and thistles," and they reappear in Matthew 7:16 (and the *tribolos* in Hebrews 6:8's curse imagery). A word-for-word recurrence of the Eden-curse vocabulary at the narrow-gate moment is a genuine intertextual signal, not a vibe. *Rating: strong — this is the load-bearing and verifiable core of the claim.*

2. **The Eve = Zōē / "life" wordplay is in the text the LXX preserved.** Genesis 3:20 has Adam name Eve "because she was the mother of all living"; the Septuagint renders the name as *Zōē*, preserving the *living*-pun over the Hebrew form — so the link between Eve, "life," and the tree is embedded in the Greek Old Testament the evangelists used. *Rating: accurate; the pun is real and ancient.*

3. **The Eden-reversal frame is the vault's settled temple grammar.** Reading the temple as a staged return *into* Eden (thorns-and-thistles world → through the wall → to the tree) is exactly the pattern of [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]] and [[Lehi's Dream Recapitulates the Eden-Temple Return]], where the expelled Adam/Lehi passes back through guarded boundaries to the tree of life. The narrow gate as the *last* boundary before the tree fits that arc precisely. *Rating: coheres tightly with established concepts.*

4. **The *mitzraim* "narrow place" etymology is real.** *Mitzrayim* is widely connected to *tzar/metzar* ("narrow, distress, strait"), and the Exodus-as-rebirth (through the waters) reading is a known rabbinic and Christian typology — so the womb/birth overlay, while the most poetic move, is not invented out of nothing. *Rating: the etymology is sound; the womb-application is a recognized but homiletical extension.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The lexical match is the one move a critic must take seriously, but its *significance* is overstated: *akantha* and *tribolos* are simply the ordinary Greek words for thorny weeds, so their appearance in a proverb about bad plants ("grapes of thorns, figs of thistles") need not allude to Genesis at all — any Greek text about thorns would use them. Mainstream exegesis reads Matthew 7:13-20 as two independent sayings (the two ways; the tree-and-fruit test of false prophets) with no Eden, no wall, and no birth — the "narrow gate" is moral difficulty, not a birth canal, and the thorns/thistles belong to the *false-prophet* pericope, not the gate. The womb/*mitzraim* layer is frankly midrashic. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim does not rest on the word *akantha* in isolation but on its landing *at the threshold of the room that contains the tree* (7:17's "good tree, good fruit") inside a chapter already read as the holy of holies — and the convergence of curse-vocabulary, the Eve/*Zōē* pun, and the tree on the far side is the kind of *patterned* echo that the bare-proverb reading leaves unexplained. The honest caution (per [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]) is to keep the parallel functional and not to lean on the womb overlay.

## Best Quotes
> "Thistles and thorns are *akantha* and *tribolos* in Greek... and when in Genesis Adam and Eve are cast out of the garden and the ground gets the thistles and thorns, it gets *akantha* and *tribolos*. So here we have it coming up as we're passing through a strait and narrow gate to life." [^1]

> "Maybe yes, they are the curse of the earth after the fall — but they're also like a wall, a barrier around Eden. You're passing through the wall of *akantha* and *tribolos*, the narrow gate, to get to life." [^2]

> "To pass into life you passed through a narrow and strait gate. Anyone who watches a child be born — they immediately come out into mortality filled with the thorns and thistles... and then what is the first thing they see? Their mother. The tree." [^4]
