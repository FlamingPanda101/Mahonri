# Cannabis Incense and the Mystical Temple Experience

## The Claim

The temple encounter that constituted "seeing the face of God" was likely a **mystical, sensorially altered experience**, shaped by heavy incense smoke that both **obscured** the divine image and may have been **psychoactive**. Residue analysis at the [[Arad Temple Standing Stone|Arad]] sanctuary found that incense altars beside the standing stone burned cannabis ~2,700 years ago. [[Dan McClellan]] suggests this physical setting helped produce the sense of communion with God's presence.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Cannabis residue at Arad.** Two incense altars found a few feet from the Arad standing stone were tested; the substances burned ~2,700 years ago included three kinds of cannabis.[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — the 2020 Arad residue study (Arie, Rosen, Namdar) identifying cannabis and frankincense is published and widely reported.)*

2. **Smoke obscured the image.** McClellan ties this to broader biblical descriptions of incense filling the sanctuary; the high priest entering the Holy of Holies amid smoke ("trying not to run into anything") indicates the divine image/presence was visually hazy.[^3] *(Evidence rating: coherent with priestly ritual texts, e.g. Lev 16:13.)*

3. **Haziness + psychoactivity heightened the mystical character.** A worshipper seeing only "a hazy outline of something in the mist" while inhaling psychoactive smoke could plausibly emerge reporting "I saw God."[^4] *(Evidence rating: reasonable but speculative as to subjective effect; McClellan frames it tentatively/humorously.)*

4. **Reinforces the experiential reading of the vision.** This supports treating the pilgrimage "vision" as an embodied, atmospheric temple experience rather than a metaphor for abstract worship (see [[Beatific Vision in the Israelite Temple]]).[^5] *(Evidence rating: contextual support.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A skeptic of the cannabis-experience link can note that residue at one Judahite fort-sanctuary (Arad) does not establish that psychoactive incense was standard in the Jerusalem temple or central to the pilgrimage festivals, and that the "I saw God" reports may be entirely cultic-formulaic rather than drug-induced. McClellan himself keeps this point light and tentative, so it functions as colorful corroboration of the *experiential* reading, not load-bearing proof.

## Best Quotes

> "We also have two incense altars that were found right just a few feet away from that standing stone. And they tested the substances that had been burned on those incense altars 2,700 years ago and there were three different kinds of cannabis that were being burned."[^2]

> "If you just see a hazy outline of something in the mist while you're getting stoned off your gourd... maybe you do come out of there going, 'I saw God.'"[^4]
