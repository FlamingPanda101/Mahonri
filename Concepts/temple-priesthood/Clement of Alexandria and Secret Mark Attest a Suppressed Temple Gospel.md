# Clement of Alexandria and Secret Mark Attest a Suppressed Temple Gospel

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that a letter attributed to [[Clement of Alexandria]] (the *Letter to Theodore*, describing "Secret Mark") is documentary evidence that a *second, more sacred* version of the Gospel of Mark existed in Alexandria, reserved for "those being perfected," and that it carried temple-mystery teachings withheld even from that inner gospel. On this reading the forty-day mysteries (see [[The Lost Forty-Day Teaching Points to a Suppressed Temple Endowment]]) were written down by Mark, kept secret in Alexandria, and partly leaked to the Gnostics during the apostasy — corroborating the LDS claim that an early-Christian temple ordinance was real, restricted, and then lost.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The letter describes two Gospels of Mark — basic and "perfected"
Clement writes that Mark, during Peter's Roman stay, wrote an account "not... declaring all of [the Lord's doings], nor yet hinting at the secret ones," then after Peter's death brought his and Peter's notes to Alexandria and "composed a more spiritual gospel for the use of those who were being perfected." Two tiers — milk for the instructed, a fuller gospel for the perfected — map onto the temple's initiate structure.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the letter genuinely says this; whether the letter is authentic is the central dispute (see steelman).

### 2. Even the secret gospel withheld the "hierophantic" teachings
Clement adds Mark "did not divulge the things not to be uttered, nor did he write down the hierophantic teaching of the Lord." Barnes stresses *hierophant* is the Greek word for a **temple worker**, so "hierophantic teachings" means secret temple teachings — an explicit ancient label for a restricted ordinance.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the term *hierophantēs* does denote a mystery-cult officiant; the inference that this equals the LDS endowment is the interpretive step.

### 3. It fits the wider pattern of restricted teaching
The two-tier gospel coheres with Jesus' withholding of "mysteries" from the crowd ([[Matthew 13.10-11|Matt 13]]) and the forty-day gap in Acts, and with the LDS principle that sacred things are restricted, not shameful. See [[Sacred Secrecy Is Pearls Before Swine Not Shame]].[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — internal coherence with the broader thesis; not independent confirmation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The decisive critical point is **provenance**. (a) The *Letter to Theodore* survives only in a 1958 transcription by Morton Smith from an 18th-century hand copied into a book at Mar Saba; the manuscript subsequently went missing, was never subjected to modern forensic testing, and a substantial body of scholars regards it as a **modern forgery** (possibly Smith's own) — so citing it as bedrock evidence for a suppressed temple gospel rests on a text many specialists do not trust. (b) Even granting authenticity, "more spiritual gospel for the perfected" in Clement's own usage means deeper *catechesis* for mature believers (Clement constantly distinguishes milk from solid food), not a secret *ritual*; *hierophantic* is Clement's borrowed mystery-cult vocabulary for advanced doctrine, which he elsewhere uses metaphorically. (c) The Secret Mark fragments Clement quotes are a Lazarus-like raising and a rebuke, with no ordinance content — so the leap from "Clement mentions a secret Mark" to "the endowment was in it" is unsupported by what the letter actually contains. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument does not stand on Secret Mark alone — it is one strand alongside the Acts forty-day gap and the independently attested Gnostic ordinance-texts (Pistis Sophia, Acts of John, Books of Jeu) — and the recurrence of a *restricted-teaching* tradition across multiple early sources is the pattern in need of explanation, whatever the verdict on this one letter.
