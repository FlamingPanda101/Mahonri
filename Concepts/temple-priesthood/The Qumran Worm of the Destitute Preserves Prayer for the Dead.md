# The Qumran Worm of the Destitute Preserves Prayer for the Dead

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that a textual variant in [[Psalm 102]] preserves an erased reference to **work for the dead**. Where the Masoretic/KJV tradition reads that God "will regard the **prayer** of the destitute, and not despise their prayer" (Psalm 102:17), the Dead Sea Scrolls reading has God regard the "**worm** of the destitute." Barnes links *worm* — via Mark 9:48 ("where their worm dieth not") and Isaiah 66:24 — to the **disembodied spirit / the dead**, and concludes the original verse had God hearing the prayers *of the dead*. He argues a later scribe, uncomfortable with that vicarious/resurrection theology, obscured it by **doubling "prayer"** (so the line reads "the prayer of the destitute... their prayer"), erasing the *worm*. The variant is offered as a Qumran "receipt" that the canonical Psalms were edited to remove temple/resurrection material — part of the same suppression argued in [[The Day of Atonement Was Edited Out of Deuteronomy and the Psalms]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. There is a real textual difference at the spot
The claim is anchored to an actual variant tradition at Psalm 102:17 — a consonantal difference between a word read as "prayer/cry" and one read as "worm." A genuine textual divergence (not an invented one) is the precondition of the argument, and Barnes presents it as a checkable manuscript reading.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — there is real variation in how the consonants here can be vocalized/read; whether the Qumran witness actually reads "worm" as Barnes claims (versus this being his preferred reconstruction) is the point to verify, and popular summaries can overstate it.

### 2. "Worm" carries death/grave associations in Hebrew idiom
*Worm* (Hebrew *rimmah*/*tola'ath*) is bound up with death, the grave, and corruption across the Hebrew Bible (Job 17:14; 25:6; Isaiah 14:11; the undying worm of Isaiah 66:24 / Mark 9:48), so connecting "worm" to the realm of the dead is not arbitrary — it draws on an established semantic field.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the death-association of *worm* is solid; "worm = a praying disembodied spirit whose prayers God hears" is a much more specific and contestable extension.

### 3. The doubled "prayer" looks like a smoothing
The Masoretic line's slightly redundant "regard the prayer... not despise their prayer" is the kind of repetition that *can* result from a scribe replacing a hard reading with an easier one — consistent (though not uniquely) with Barnes's editing hypothesis. It coheres with the vault's broader Deuteronomist-corruption thesis. See [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]].
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate — the redundancy is real, but Hebrew poetry routinely uses parallel repetition, so this is at most suggestive and has an innocent explanation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) the entire reading hinges on a **single, fragile lexical variant**, and the inference chain is long — variant reading → "worm" → "disembodied spirit" → "prayers of the dead" → "vicarious work for the dead" → "a scribe deliberately erased it" — with each link weaker than the last; (b) even if "worm" is the older reading, "worm of the destitute" most plausibly means the *afflicted person likened to a worm* (cf. "I am a worm, and no man," Psalm 22:6), i.e. an image of human lowliness, **not** the dead — so the resurrection/vicarious gloss is imported; (c) Hebrew **parallelism** routinely repeats a key word, so the doubled "prayer" is ordinary poetic form, not evidence of tampering; and (d) attributing the variant to a *theologically motivated* scribe (rather than ordinary copying noise) is a conspiratorial reading of mundane textual transmission. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim does not rest on this verse alone but joins a cluster of Qumran "receipts" (the Day-of-Atonement omission, the altar-liturgy coda) pointing the same direction, so the cumulative case is the argument, not the single variant; and the "I am a worm" parallel actually *reinforces* a humiliation-unto-death reading that is at least compatible with the realm of the dead. Still, the honest version concedes (b) and (c) are strong and that "worm = the dead" here is a minority reconstruction.

## Best Quotes

> Where the Masoretic Psalm 102 reads "prayer of the destitute," Qumran reads "the **worm** of the destitute" — *worm* (Mark 9:48) being the *disembodied spirit*, so God hears the prayers "of the dead." (Barnes) [^1]
