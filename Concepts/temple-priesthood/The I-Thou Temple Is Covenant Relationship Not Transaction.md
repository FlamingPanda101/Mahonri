# The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction

#type/claim

## The Claim
Michael Myrrono reads the temple through [[Martin Buber]]'s distinction between the **I-It** and **I-Thou** relations. Buber, he notes, "gives the bird to the I-It relationship" — the transactional, object-using stance — and insists the God of the Abrahamic faith is "a personal God, a covenantal God, a relational God," properly met as an **I-Thou** of reverence and covenant. [^1] Applied to the temple: covenant-making with God should be **relational and covenantal**, an encounter that reveals "my brokenness but also my belovedness" and "my eternal worth." [^2] It becomes **blasphemy** when made transactional — "I'll obey God because he'll smite me if I don't," "I'll do this if God blesses me," prosperity-gospel bargaining — because that reduces a *Thou* to an *It*, using God as a means to an end. [^3] He draws a deliberate analogy: just as sacred marital union is profaned when a partner is used "as a means to an end" (pornography, hookup culture), so the temple's "intimate acts with God" are profaned if treated transactionally. Both should be **I-Thou**: covenantal, not instrumental. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **It reframes the "earning" criticism at the level of *posture*, not mechanics.** Where [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]] answers the works-charge by appeal to *enactment*, the Buber move answers it by appeal to *relational intent*: the same outward covenant is meritorious bargaining if held as I-It and grace-formed relationship if held as I-Thou. This pre-empts the caricature of temple covenants as a contract for blessings. *Rating: a genuine philosophical reframe.*

2. **Buber is a serious, mainstream source.** *I and Thou* (1923) is a canonical text in 20th-century philosophy of religion; invoking it is not fringe proof-texting. The I-It/I-Thou grammar maps cleanly onto the biblical covenant idea (*hesed*, covenant loyalty), which the vault already treats in [[Covenant Relationship Over Theology Quiz]] and [[Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge]]. *Rating: strong sourcing.*

3. **The marriage analogy has biblical precedent.** Scripture itself figures the God-Israel and Christ-Church bond as marriage (Hosea; Eph 5), and profaning that bond as adultery/harlotry. Myrrono's move — sacred union profaned by instrumentalizing the partner — is the prophets' own logic transposed. *Rating: well-grounded analogy.*

4. **It connects worthiness to relationship, not score-keeping.** If the temple is an I-Thou encounter, the worthiness interview reads as fidelity to a relationship rather than points earned — consonant with the vault's [[Worship Is Devotion to What Can Save Not Ritual Appeasement]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic can accept Buber wholesale and still press two points. First, *appropriation*: Buber's I-Thou was a Jewish dialogical philosophy aimed at unmediated encounter; bolting it onto a scripted, ordinance-gated temple rite arguably re-introduces exactly the mediated, ritual "It" Buber was reacting against. Second, *unfalsifiability*: "do the same covenant with the right inner posture and it's not transactional" risks making the distinction unobservable — any works-based structure can claim its works are really relationship. The honest reply is that the I-Thou/I-It line is a claim about the *meaning* a tradition teaches its members to give the rite, and that the temple's own framing (covenant, belovedness, being *seen* through the veil — see [[Hebrews 10.19-22|Hebrews 10:19-20]]) is relational rather than mercantile; whether any given participant lives it as Thou or It is a pastoral question, not a refutation of the design.

## Best Quotes
> "Buber basically gives the bird to the I-It relationship and says screw that, and instead says that it should be an I-Thou relationship of reverence, but more importantly of relation and of covenant." [^1]

> "When it becomes I-Thou instead of I-It... it's not transactional. In the past maybe if I saw God as an It, it's kind of using God as a means to an end... 'I'll obey God because he'll smite me if I don't.' Very transactional." [^3]

> "Similarly to the temple, or similarly to the other intimate acts with... God where we're becoming relational and covenantal with God — it's blasphemy if you view it in a transactional way. It should be I-Thou. It should be relational and covenantal. Just like union between a man and a woman." [^4]
