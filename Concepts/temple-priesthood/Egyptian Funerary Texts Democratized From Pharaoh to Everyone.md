# Egyptian Funerary Texts Democratized From Pharaoh to Everyone

#type/claim

## The Claim
Egyptian "instructions on exaltation" underwent a centuries-long **democratization**, and that trajectory typologically anticipates Joseph Smith's opening of the temple endowment to all. The hosts trace the sequence: **Pyramid Texts** (oldest, carved only in the king's pyramid — e.g. Unas at Saqqara — "only for the Pharaoh," who "kind of represented the people") → **Coffin Texts** (still wealthy; truncated "spells" on stone sarcophagi) → **Book of the Dead** (a less-wealthy funerary scroll) → **Book of Breathings** ("a pamphlet version… for the poor people," tucked into the wrap of an un-embalmed burial). [^1][^2] The same texts double as living-religion liturgy — "instructions on how to… get to exaltation," focused on resurrection and exaltation, "what we do in the temple." [^3] The payoff: "this is what we do in the temple, and we are all… it is now democratized through Joseph Smith fully to everyone to become kings and queens or pharaohs and pharaohesses, and priests and priestesses." [^4] (The same democratizing instinct appears within the restored canon at [[Benjamin Son of the Right Hand - Democratized Enthronement]], where a king's enthronement name is given to the whole covenant people.)

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The four-stage sequence is mainstream Egyptology.** Pyramid Texts → Coffin Texts → Book of the Dead is a standard periodization, and the "democratization of the afterlife" (royal funerary privilege gradually extended down the social scale) is a recognized scholarly description, not an LDS coinage — co-host [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] supplies the technical detail (spells, sarcophagi, wealth tiers). The Book of Breathings as a late, cheaper, abbreviated form is likewise accurate. *Rating: the historical scaffolding is solid.*
2. **It reframes the "funerary text" objection.** Critics dismiss the Joseph Smith papyri as mere burial paperwork; the democratization frame answers that the *same corpus* encodes a living exaltation liturgy (see [[The Facsimiles Encode Abraham's Endowment Through the Osiris Myth]]) — burial clothes that are also temple clothes, in the hosts' analogy. *Rating: a fair rhetorical counter.*
3. **The pattern, not the texts, is what's claimed.** The argument is typological — Egypt's partial, gradually-broadening access *anticipates* a fuller restoration — which is a softer, more defensible claim than direct dependence. *Rating: appropriately modest.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A historian grants the four-stage democratization as Egyptology while denying it implies anything about Joseph Smith. (a) **Parallel ≠ lineage**: nearly every expanding religious culture broadens once-elite rites to the masses; that LDS practice also democratizes a temple liturgy is unremarkable and needs no Egyptian ancestry. (b) **Equivocation on "exaltation"**: Egyptian afterlife texts aim at a safe passage through the Duat and assimilation to Osiris, *not* the LDS theology of eternal increase and deification into "kings and queens"; calling both "exaltation" smuggles in the conclusion. (c) **Selective endpoint**: the tidy "ends at the Book of Breathings = Joseph Smith's papyri" framing is built to land on the facsimiles, and skips that the Book of Breathings is a *narrowing/abbreviation* of content, not a theological climax. The Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is explicitly *typological* ("a form of the truth," not derivation), and the structural fit — a broadening of sacred access culminating in a fully democratized endowment — is offered as resonance, with the [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple|parallelomania caution]] acknowledged.
