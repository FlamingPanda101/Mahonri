# Psalm 110 and Psalm 2 Are Enthronement Texts of the Festal Drama

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] argues that the two most-quoted Psalms in the New Testament — [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110]] and [[Psalm 2.7|Psalm 2]] — are **royal/enthronement Psalms** of the festal drama, depicting the king washed, anointed, enthroned at Yahweh's right hand, declared God's son, and made "a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek." In Jesus of Nazareth, Day argues, the royal Psalms (what the king does) and the enthronement Psalms (what Yahweh does) converge: he is both the royal king and Yahweh incarnate.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The right-hand seat (rated: textual + cult-functional reading).** Psalm 110:1, "the LORD said unto my Lord, sit thou at my right hand," is, in the drama, a priest "speaking as if he were God," presenting the enthroned king beside his wife as Yahweh's "right-hand man."[^1] [[John Eaton]] notes the image of Yahweh sustaining the king by grasping his right hand.
2. **Begotten as God's son (rated: textual).** Psalm 2:7, "thou art my son; this day have I begotten thee," reflects the ANE convention that "the king would be the son of the god"; the warrior imagery of Psalm 2:5–7 fits a king who must defend a vulnerable people.[^2]
3. **Priest after the order of Melchizedek (rated: textual).** Psalm 110:4 makes the enthroned king "a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek" — a datum the hosts flag as theologically loaded for Latter-day Saints. See [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]].
4. **New Testament citation frequency (rated: reception history).** Day notes Psalm 110 is quoted or echoed "like 40 times in the New Testament," Psalm 110:1 in five different books — evidence the early church read these as enthronement texts fulfilled in Christ.[^3]

**Steelman of the critic.** That Psalm 110 and Psalm 2 are *royal* Psalms tied to a coronation setting is widely accepted; the stronger claim — that they scripted a specific annual *festal drama* with a ritually suffering king — is the contested Mowinckel-Eaton reconstruction (see [[The Festal Drama Reconstructs a Pre-Exilic Enthronement Ritual]]). The convergence "royal king + Yahweh incarnate" is an explicitly LDS/Christian theological synthesis the host attributes to himself ("Eaton doesn't say this; I put the two together"), not a critical-scholarship conclusion.

## Best Quotes
> "When the king was enthroned and anointed in the temple ceremony, the idea is that he was Yahweh's right-hand man… a priest who acted in the place of God… turned to the audience with the king enthroned next to his wife and said, 'This is my right-hand man.'"[^1]

> "In Jesus, Jesus of Nazareth is both: he is the royal king but he is Yahweh incarnate, and that is the argument of the New Testament writers."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The enthronement Psalms supply a temple-and-kingship matrix for Christology (the Son enthroned at the right hand, begotten, a Melchizedek priest) that the vault already touches in [[Psalm 110.1]] and [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]]. Read with the festal drama, they frame the endowment's washing-anointing-enthronement pattern and the Book of Mormon's royal-priestly themes.
