# Everyone Worships Something So Choose What to Be Conformed To

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] argues that worship is universal and unavoidable: "every human being on this earth... is in a form of worship," because worship is "the acknowledgement and the identification of a hierarchy of good... that you're going to sacrifice to." Whatever you most desire — money, status, social media — you worship, and "you will take upon yourself the image of that thing." The discipline of worshiping God, then, is the discipline of moving from a state where *God is a reflection of you* to one where *you are a reflection of God*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A diagnostic: what you do with free time.** McLauchlin says the surest indicator of what you worship is "what do I do when I have free time?" — for him, reflexively scrolling social media, where "the things I love are being given to me in a loop" that he then "starts to reflect" (politics, memes, peer reinforcement). "You become what you're exposed to the most."[^1] *(Moderate — a sharp, testable self-examination; the "you become the image of what you worship" principle is its devotional core.)*
2. **The projected God.** A second argument explains why ordinary faith stays weak: most people pray to "the God you've imagined up into yourself" — a projection of "my own self perfected," "my own egoic state in its perfected way." McLauchlin draws the corollary that "this is why everyone believes God agrees with their politics... your vision of God is just your projected state," and "politics is just a reflection of your faith." True worship requires letting God *reveal himself* rather than praying to a mirror.[^2] *(Moderate-to-strong — the "everyone's God agrees with their politics" observation is a pointed and widely recognized critique of projected religion.)*
3. **Conformation by exposure.** "Worshiping is to become informed — literally... you're in the form of the thing you're worshiping." Sustained, consistent exposure to God's presence is how one "obtain[s] the image of Christ"; "if you sit in hell long enough, you're going to look like hell."[^3] *(Moderate — an "imitation by attention" claim; intuitive but asserted, not demonstrated.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The "everyone worships" move is rhetorically strong but can overreach. (a) Stretching "worship" to cover any strong desire risks equivocation — caring about money or scrolling a feed is not obviously *worship* in the religious sense, and the broadened definition can make the claim unfalsifiable ("whatever you value, that's your god"). A secular critic would say plenty of people order their lives around goods they do not treat as ultimate or sacrificial. (b) The "projected God" critique cuts against the speaker too: if everyone's God is a self-projection, the believer must explain why *his* revealed God is not also one — McLauchlin's answer (let God reveal himself, test by the fruit/"does it taste good," [[Worship Is a State of Being That Draws Heaven Down|Alma 32]]) is itself experiential and so vulnerable to the same charge. (c) The diagnostic "what you do with free time" can shame ordinary rest or leisure as idolatry. The fair, narrower point survives: human beings are *formed by what they habitually attend to*, so the objects of one's attention and sacrifice are not neutral — which is a defensible claim about the moral psychology of habit, independent of the strong metaphysics.

## Best Quotes
> "Everyone worships — you can't escape worship... worship is the acknowledgement and the identification of a hierarchy of good or something desirable that you're going to sacrifice to."[^1]

> "This is why everyone believes God agrees with their politics... your vision of God, your image of God, is just your projected state. And what is politics but your religious faith views extrapolated and put into a social structure."[^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the diagnostic half of McLauchlin's worship teaching (the constructive half is [[Worship Is a State of Being That Draws Heaven Down]]): because everyone is already being conformed to *something*, the question is not whether to worship but what to be conformed to. It also supplies a devotional frame for the channel's [[Authenticity Is What the Millennial Generation Wants From the Church|authenticity]] concern — a religion of polished performance risks worshiping the performance rather than God.
