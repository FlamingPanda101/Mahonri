# The Secret Gospel of Mark Implies a Hidden Temple Gospel

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] makes the Secret Gospel of Mark his **#1** entry in the apocrypha countdown, arguing that the disputed Mar Saba letter (attributed to [[Clement of Alexandria]] and reported by [[Morton Smith]] in 1958/1960) points to a fuller, esoteric Gospel of Mark that contained **temple-style ordinances** later edited out of canonical Mark. Two textual hooks anchor the reading: (1) Secret Mark describes a young man who comes to Jesus by night "**wearing a linen cloth over his naked body**" to be taught "the mystery of the kingdom of God" — which Barnes ties to the otherwise-unexplained naked young man in a linen cloth (*sindōn*) at Jesus' arrest in [[Mark 14.51-52|Mark 14:51-52]]; and (2) the LDS scripture [[D&C 124.41|D&C 124:40-41]] promises temple "things which have been kept hid from before the foundation of the world," supplying the doctrinal frame for *why* such a gospel would be suppressed and later restored. The fuller case (Clement's two-tier "perfected" gospel and the *hierophantic* = temple-worker vocabulary) is developed in [[Clement of Alexandria and Secret Mark Attest a Suppressed Temple Gospel]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Canonical Mark has an unexplained linen-cloth young man
[[Mark 14.51-52|Mark 14:51-52]] reports a young man who, seized at Jesus' arrest, abandons his linen cloth and flees naked — a famously abrupt detail with no narrative payoff. Secret Mark's young man "in a linen cloth over his naked body" coming for night instruction supplies a context, suggesting (on Barnes's reading) that canonical Mark preserves the *scar* of an excised initiatory scene.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the canonical detail is genuinely odd and unexplained; linking it to Secret Mark depends entirely on Secret Mark's authenticity.

### 2. The linen-cloth-by-night motif reads as initiation
A candidate coming at night, clad only in a single linen garment, to receive "the mystery of the kingdom of God," resembles an initiation/washing-and-clothing scene more than ordinary teaching — which is the basis for the temple-ordinance gloss.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the imagery is suggestive; that it is specifically the LDS endowment is the interpretive leap.

### 3. D&C 124 explains the suppression-and-restoration frame
[[D&C 124.41|D&C 124:40-41]] casts the temple ordinances as "kept hid from before the foundation of the world," now revealed in the dispensation of the fulness of times. A live caller raises the tension (if it was hidden, how do apocrypha show it anciently?); the hosts answer by distinguishing the uniquely restored **endowment of power / covenants** from the **narrative and patterns** (creation, fall, garments, tokens) that survive as "vestiges" in the apocrypha. See [[The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha]].
> **Evidence rating:** Weak as *evidence* (it is the LDS premise, not independent confirmation); useful as the explanatory model.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The decisive objection is **provenance**: the *Letter to Theodore* survives only in Morton Smith's 1958 transcription of an 18th-century hand copied into a book at Mar Saba; the manuscript then disappeared, was never forensically tested, and a substantial body of scholars regards it as a **modern forgery**, quite possibly Smith's own — so building a "hidden temple gospel" on it stacks a contested inference on a distrusted text. Further: (a) the naked young man of [[Mark 14.51-52|Mark 14:51-52]] has ordinary explanations (an eyewitness signature, a discipleship/abandonment motif, an allusion to Amos 2:16) that need no secret rite; (b) the Secret Mark fragment Clement quotes is a **Lazarus-like raising and a rebuke** with *no ordinance content* — the leap from "a young man in a linen cloth" to "the endowment" is supplied by the reader; and (c) "mystery of the kingdom of God" is standard Synoptic language for the revealed plan of salvation, not a technical rite. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument does not rest on Secret Mark alone but on its convergence with the Acts forty-day gap and the independently attested Gnostic ordinance-texts (see [[Gnostic Apocrypha Preserve Temple Ordinance Patterns]]); the recurring *restricted-teaching* tradition is the pattern needing explanation, whatever the verdict on this one letter — and the canonical linen-cloth oddity remains genuinely unexplained on the standard readings.
