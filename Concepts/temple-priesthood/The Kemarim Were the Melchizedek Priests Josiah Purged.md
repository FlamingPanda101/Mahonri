# The Kemarim Were the Melchizedek Priests Josiah Purged

## Overview

An argument advanced by [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] (following [[Margaret Barker]]) on the *Mormonism with the Murph* interview: the *kemarim* — translated "idolatrous priests" — whom [[Josiah]] puts down in [[2 Kings 23.5|2 Kings 23:5]] were not pagans at all, but the **Melchizedek priesthood** that the Deuteronomist reformers were suppressing.

## The Claim

Barker's case is that the *kemarim* were the older, royal/Melchizedek priestly order, recast as "idolatrous" by the reforming editors who centralized worship and elevated the Aaronic/Levitical line (the [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology|Deuteronomistic reform]]). Supporting evidence offered:

- The **Peshitta** (Syriac OT) calls Melchizedek a *kumra* — the same root — in its rendering of Genesis 14, linking the term directly to Melchizedek rather than to idolatry.
- The purge in 2 Kings 23 is read as a *political* suppression of a rival priesthood, consistent with the reform's broader pattern of editing out first-temple theology (atoning Messiah, the divine feminine, the Day of Atonement liturgy).

A speculative onomastic extension is proposed: **Cumorah** as "place of the *kemarim*," tying the Nephite stone box / ark to the Melchizedek-priest motif. This last move is presented as wordplay, not demonstration.

The argument fits the vault's larger thesis that the [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology|Book of Mormon preserves pre-reform first-temple theology]] the reformers removed, and it complements the temple's two-priesthood structure described in [[The Aaronic-Melchizedek Dualism Maps the Temple's Kings and Priests]].
