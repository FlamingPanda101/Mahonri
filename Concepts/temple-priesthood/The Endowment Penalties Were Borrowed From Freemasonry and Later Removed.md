# The Endowment Penalties Were Borrowed From Freemasonry and Later Removed

## The Claim
The "penalties" once attached to the endowment — gestures invoking violent consequences (death, dismemberment) for breaking the covenant of secrecy — were, historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] concedes, taken from the **Freemasonic** initiation that [[Joseph Smith]] adapted in Nauvoo. Joseph "dumped out" the Masonic *content* but kept its *method* of communication, including the secrecy-penalties; "super alienating and scary" to moderns, they were removed in the late 20th century.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Joseph Christianized the Masonic form.** "It's very, very clear that Joseph Smith used Freemasonry... to communicate the endowment." The Masonic ceremony narrated the murder of Hiram, chief mason of Solomon's temple — "a tragic tale of fidelity" — as an oral teaching tool "useful for semi-literate audiences." Joseph kept this pedagogical method, "the content of it is dumped out," and "he Christianizes it... adds Christian symbology."[^2] This is the *form-not-content* borrowing also defended in [[The Masonry Objection Must Locate the Specific Content Not the General Pattern]] and complemented by [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]] (which argues the *substance* predates 1842). *Rating: strong — Stapley states the Masonic connection plainly; it is the scholarly consensus on the Nauvoo ceremony's vehicle.*

2. **The penalties were among the retained Masonic features.** "Punctuating the Masonic drama are promises to keep everything... secret," and "when you promise to do that, you also accept a violent penalty in the case that you break that secrecy." Joseph "keeps... these promises of secrecy and the associated penalties."[^3] *Rating: strong — directly attested; the parallel to Masonic penalties is well documented.*

3. **They were removed because the work they did was no longer needed.** The penalties "performed a certain work... reinforcing this idea that there is a sort of privacy or secrecy to certain elements." Church leaders, believing themselves "authorized... to remove them," dropped them in the late 20th century "in a way that made sense to modern participants."[^4] *Rating: moderate — the rationale is Stapley's framing; the removal (1990) is a documented fact.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic argues this concession is more damaging than it sounds: if the most solemn, fear-laden element of the endowment was a borrowed Masonic oath that the Church later judged dispensable, that undercuts the claim that the ceremony restores an *ancient* divine pattern — it looks instead like a nineteenth-century construction edited for modern comfort. The believer's reply distinguishes vehicle from revelation: borrowing a recognizable ritual *form* to carry restored content is exactly what one expects of a prophet "meeting people in their context," and the freedom to remove a non-essential form (with claimed authorization) is itself the thesis that temple forms flex around a fixed covenant — not evidence the covenant was invented.

## Best Quotes
> "He uses this way of communicating to teach the plan of salvation and the redemption of Christ... He Christianizes it. He adds Christian symbology into it. But one of the things he keeps are these promises of secrecy and the associated penalties."[^3]

> "Violent penalties invoking death and dismemberment... super alienating and scary... In the late 20th century those elements were completely removed."[^4]
