# Magnifying a Calling Means Coming Clean From the Blood and Sins of This Generation

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] reads "magnify their calling" in [[D&C 84.33-44|D&C 84:33]] not as a bishop doing his administrative duties well, but as **coming clean from the blood and sins of this generation** — the *same* calling for everyone, "from President Nelson to your father." [^1] The "calling" is to repent of both one's own sins *and* the inherited "traditions of the fathers" ([[D&C 93.39|D&C 93:39]], the evil one "taketh away light and truth… through the traditions of our fathers"), which can drain light as devastatingly as personal sin. [^2] One *magnifies* that calling through **sacrifice** that softens others' hearts so they will hearken — the controlling proof-texts being [[Jacob 1.19|Jacob 1:19]] ("we did magnify our office… answering the sins of the people upon our own heads… that their blood might not come upon our garments") and [[Jacob 2|Jacob 2:2]] ("that I might rid my garments of your sins"). [^3] McLauchlin illustrates with a father sitting in a parking lot outside his jailed son's cell to *be near him* — a sacrifice that may soften the son's heart — and with [[King Benjamin]], whose sacrifices "rid his garments" and won his people a collective born-again experience ([[Mosiah 5.7|Mosiah 5:7]]), and with Lehi and Nephi pulling down the mass conversion of the Lamanites ([[3 Nephi 9|3 Nephi 9:20]], "baptized… by fire… and they knew it not"; [[Ether 12|Ether 12]]). [^4] Renewing/sanctifying "your bodies" is "fractal" — spirit, flesh, resurrection, spouse, family, ward, church. [^5]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Jacob texts genuinely define "magnify" by blood-guilt.** [[Jacob 1.19|Jacob 1:19]] and [[Jacob 2|Jacob 2:2]] explicitly tie "magnify mine office" to taking the people's sins "upon our own heads" and ridding the prophet's garments of their blood — so McLauchlin's gloss is the *text's own* sense of magnification, not an imported one. Ezekiel's watchman (Ezek 3:18-21) and Jacob's "garments" share this blood-accountability grammar. *Rating: strongly text-anchored — arguably the note's best evidence.*

2. **"Blood and sins of this generation" is temple-endowment language.** The phrase comes from the washing/anointing wording about being "clean from the blood and sins of this generation," so reading the priesthood calling through it is internally consistent with McLauchlin's larger thesis that the oath and covenant *is* the endowment. See [[Calling and Election Is the Oath of the Father Sealed in the Endowment]]. *Rating: coherent within the framework.*

3. **The inherited-traditions point has a real prooftext.** [[D&C 93.39|D&C 93:39]] does name "the tradition of their fathers" alongside disobedience as how light and truth are lost, supporting the claim that coming clean includes repenting of *inherited* patterns, not only personal acts — and that one "brings the generations with you" into Christ (a tie to genealogical/temple work for the dead). *Rating: defensible.*

4. **The King Benjamin model fits the narrative.** [[Mosiah 5.7|Mosiah 5:7]]'s covenant born-again experience follows Benjamin's self-emptying service ("I have not… commanded you… that ye should fear me," Mosiah 2), illustrating redemption "magnified into" a people by a leader's sacrifice. The Lehi/Nephi mass conversion ([[3 Nephi 9|3 Nephi 9:20]]; [[Ether 12|Ether 12:14]]) likewise pairs faith with collective sanctification. *Rating: a fair reading of the Book of Mormon pattern.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic objects that McLauchlin has *flattened* a specific scriptural promise into a single universal calling by chaining together verses from different contexts: [[D&C 84.33-44|D&C 84:33]]'s "magnify your calling" need not mean the temple's "blood and sins" formula, [[Jacob 1.19|Jacob 1:19]] is about *prophets'* teaching responsibility, and welding them produces a totalizing demand ("take responsibility for all suffering," à la Jordan Peterson) that the individual texts do not jointly assert. He also concedes he is being "dogmatic" and reasoning by deduction, especially when he extends the principle to women ("I just don't know enough"). And the "two-edged sword" corollary — that calamity falls when people don't hearken a sacrificing leader — risks a troubling theodicy in which natural disasters are read as failed magnifications. A measured reply: the chain is not arbitrary, because [[D&C 84.33-44|D&C 84:33]]'s very next clause ("sanctified… unto the renewing of their bodies") is the same washing/anointing vocabulary, and Jacob is the canonical *definition* of magnification by garments — so the reading follows the Book of Mormon's own usage rather than overriding it. The universality ("same calling for everyone") is also a humbling *leveling* of the hierarchy, not a power grab — compare [[Unrighteous Dominion Condemns the Holder Not the Ordinance]] and the two-way circuit in [[Hierarchy as a Two-Way Circuit of Power]].

## Best Quotes
> "The calling I believe is the same calling for everyone. We all have the same calling, and the call is to come clean from the blood and sins of this generation." [^1]

> "By laboring with our own might, their blood might not come upon our garments… we did magnify our office unto the Lord, taking upon us the responsibility, answering the sins of the people upon our own heads." [^3]

> "That kid might say, 'Dad's such a jerk… but man, he's sitting out there.' Because of that sacrifice that father's doing, he might soften his heart… that's what's happening when Jacob says come up to the temple." [^4]

> "The traditions we inherit can have just as devastating effect as our own sinful iniquity in terms of extracting light out of us. And so part of coming to Christ is bringing the generations with you." [^2]
