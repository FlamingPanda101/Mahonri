# Heaven Is a Constructed Relational Network and Hell Is Disconnection

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonathan Stapley]] argues that in the Nauvoo cosmology Joseph Smith revealed, **heaven is not a place you are sent or a reward you earn — it is a relational structure human beings *construct*** through the sealing ordinances of the temple. Heaven *is* "the durable relations formed through expanded temple ceremonies"; where those relationships do not exist, "there's simply no heaven." Correspondingly, **hell is disconnection** — being "separate and alone." This reframes salvation from a verdict about individuals into the building of a connected kinship network.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Heaven as material kinship, not a destination (strong)
Stapley: heaven shifts from "a place that you go when you're good" (or, in Calvinism, because God chose you) to "something that we construct... through the relationships that are formed in the temple. And where those relationships don't exist, there's simply no heaven. It becomes a material structure." Sealing builds the network that *is* the celestial order.[^1]

### 2. Hell as isolation (strong)
Joseph Smith conceptualized damnation as being "separate and alone." In the King Follett sermon (April 7, 1844) he went so far as to deny a literal burning hell ("I don't believe in hellfire... it don't exist"), recasting hell as *disconnection* from the relational web of heaven.[^2]

### 3. The early adoption sealings as network-building (medium)
The practice confirms the theology: after Joseph Smith's death the apostles sealed converts to church leaders ("adoption") to build a connected kinship network *in the present*, before multigenerational family chains could form — heaven literally under construction. See [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]].[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A Protestant critic objects that this makes salvation a human *construction project* — works-righteousness at cosmic scale — and risks reducing heaven to family sentiment rather than communion with God; if "no relationships, no heaven," then heaven's existence seems contingent on human ritual performance rather than on God's grace and presence. It also appears to instrumentalize people as nodes in a network.

## Counter-Objection
The relational model is grounded *in God*, not apart from him: the network's hub is the throne of God (John of Patmos's vision of robed kings/queens and priests/priestesses surrounding it), and the relationships are *covenantal* I-Thou bonds, not mere affection (see [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]]). Far from denying grace, it specifies grace's *shape* — the temple is "learning heaven, not earning heaven" ([[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]]) — and it answers the perennial problem of an *impersonal* heaven by making heaven the very thing humans most want: enduring loving relation with God and one another. It is the opposite of instrumentalizing persons, because persons-in-relation *are* the end.

## Best Quotes
> "Heaven is something that we construct... and it's constructed through the relationships that are formed in the temple. And where those relationships don't exist, there's simply no heaven."[^1]

> "Hell was a disconnection, being separate and alone."[^2]
