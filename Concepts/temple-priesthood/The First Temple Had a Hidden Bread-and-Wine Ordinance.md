# The First Temple Had a Hidden Bread-and-Wine Ordinance

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler]], reading [[Menahem Haran]]'s *Temples and Temple-Service in Ancient Israel* (pp. 216-217), argues that the **First Temple contained a forgotten ordinance of bread and wine** performed by priests in the inner sanctuary — and that this is the ancient root of both [[Melchizedek]]'s bread and wine and the Christian sacrament. The hinge is an oddity Haran spotted: the vessels described as standing in the temple's middle room include **vessels for holding wine**, yet there is *no* description anywhere of wine being poured out or drunk inside the temple. Haran's solution: the wine was *consumed* by the priests, together with the showbread, and changed each Sabbath — a rite the surviving text otherwise edited out, leaving only the wine vessels as a clue.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The wine vessels are a real textual datum.** Haran observes that the inner furnishings include wine vessels but that no libation or pouring rite is described inside (libations were poured at the outer altar, not within). The presence of the vessels with no described use is a genuine puzzle in the priestly texts, not an LDS invention. *Rating: the observation is Haran's, grounded in the text; strong as a reading of the data.*[^1]
2. **The showbread was eaten by the priests.** [[Leviticus 24]]:5-9 has [[Aaron]] and his sons eat the twelve loaves "in the holy place" as "most holy." Haran's inference is that the wine accompanied this weekly bread-meal — "it must have originally been the custom to change this wine every Sabbath together with the loaves of the showbread." *Rating: a reasonable extension; the bread-eating is explicit, the paired wine is inferred from the vessels.*[^1]
3. **The terminology is memorial/invocatory.** The showbread is associated with *zikaron* (Hebrew) / *anamnesis* (Greek) — "a remembering" or invocation/summoning of a divine presence. That is the very word Jesus uses at the Last Supper: "this do in *remembrance* (anamnesis) of me" ([[Luke 22.42|Luke 22]]:19), over **bread and wine**. *Rating: the lexical link (anamnesis) is real and striking.*[^2]
4. **It connects to Melchizedek and the sacrament.** [[Melchizedek]] "brought forth bread and wine" ([[Genesis 14.18]]) as priest of God Most High; Psalm 110 makes the messianic king a priest "after the order of Melchizedek." A bread-and-wine rite in the inner temple would unify Melchizedek's offering, the showbread, and the sacrament into one ancient priestly pattern. See [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]] and [[Two Covenant Meals - The Betrothal Meal and the Wedding Feast]]. *Rating: a typological synthesis; coherent and suggestive.*[^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Several cautions. (a) **Argument from silence.** The case rests on the *absence* of a described use for the wine vessels; Haran fills that silence with a conjectured rite, but vessels can have mundane explanations (storage, libation wine kept inside but poured outside, ceremonial display). (b) **Haran's pre-exilic P is a minority dating** (see [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]]); the more this depends on his reconstruction of inner-temple practice, the more it inherits that contestedness. (c) **The anamnesis link may be coincidence of vocabulary.** *Zikaron* is a common cultic term; its reuse at the Last Supper need not imply a literal continuity of a wine-and-bread temple ordinance. (d) **Even Latter-day Saint scholars warn against parallelomania** (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). A measured Latter-day Saint reply: the claim does not need certainty about the *mechanics* of the rite — it needs the convergence (wine vessels + eaten showbread + anamnesis + Melchizedek's bread and wine + the sacrament) to be more economically read as a preserved ancient pattern than as accident; and Haran, a credentialed non-LDS scholar, reached the bread-and-wine inference on his own textual grounds, which blunts the pattern-seeking charge. As Butler concedes, "none of us know because we weren't there."[^1]

## Best Quotes
> "This wine was not actually poured out on any altar... It was apparently consumed by male priests in a holy place just as were the loaves of the showbread. It must have originally been the custom to change this wine every Sabbath together with the loaves of the showbread." (Haran, read aloud) [^1]

> "It's described as a zikaron in Hebrew or an anamnesis in Greek, which means a remembering or an invocation... And there is also a role for wine. 'This wine is my blood. Drink it in remembrance.' And in that room is where that tradition was carried on." — [[Dave Butler]] [^2]
