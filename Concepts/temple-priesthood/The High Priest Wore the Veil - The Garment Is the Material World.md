# The High Priest Wore the Veil - The Garment Is the Material World

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues the ancient high priest's robe was made of the **same fabric and colors as the temple veil**, so the priest "in essence is wearing the veil." Citing **Philo of Alexandria**, she says the ancient veil bore "a panoramic history of the earth" embroidered on it, woven of blue/purple/crimson representing the material elements (air, water, fire, earth) — and the high priest wore that same elements-fabric. From this she draws two further claims: (1) Joseph's **"coat of many colors"** ([[Genesis 37|Genesis 37]]) was not mere parental favoritism but a **priesthood/birthright garment** (the high priest's many-colored coat, marking the birthright son who may traverse the veil); and (2) the modern temple garment, worn against the body, is the wearer's personal "veil" — which is why it stays covered (not a vanity display). This grounds [[The Veil Is Christ Reversing the Day of Atonement]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The veil's panoramic embroidery (Philo).** Woodmansee: "I think it was Philo of Alexandria who wrote that this ancient temple had a panoramic history of the earth embroidered on it." The veils were huge (she cites "300 yards of fabric"), wool-and-linen, in multiples so one could be washed while another hung. *Rating: Philo (and Josephus) do describe the veil as a cosmic tapestry of the elements; the "panoramic history of the earth" is a loose paraphrase but in the right spirit.* See [[Philo of Alexandria]].[^1]
2. **The priest wears the same fabric/colors as the veil.** "What did the high priest of the temple wear? He wore the same fabric, the same colors... a coat of many colors... in essence he's wearing the veil." The priest thus *embodies* the boundary he crosses. *Rating: the high-priestly vestments (Exodus 28) share the veil's blue/purple/scarlet and fine linen — a genuine textual overlap.*[^2]
3. **Joseph's coat as a priesthood garment.** Because the high priest's coat was "a coat of many colors," Woodmansee reads Joseph's coat ([[Genesis 37|Genesis 37]]) as "a priesthood thing" — Joseph the birthright son inheriting the high priest's coat "so that he can traverse the veil," not just a favored son's finery. *Rating: a midrashic reading with some rabbinic precedent (the *ketonet passim*), but the specific "high-priestly coat" identification is interpretive.*[^2]
4. **The modern garment as personal veil.** Connecting to [[The Garment Shares the Hebrew Word of the Priestly Vestments]] and [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]], Woodmansee frames the temple garment as the wearer's own veil "staying with you," kept covered because it is "not a vanity thing... not a status symbol." Coheres with [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]]. *Rating: consistent with the vault's garment cluster; the priest-wears-the-veil logic is the connective tissue.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Josephus and Philo allegorize the veil's colors as the four elements, but that is a *Hellenistic-Jewish allegory*, not necessarily the original priestly intent, and it does not by itself yield "the veil is mortality" or "the priest wears the veil as the material world." Joseph's *ketonet passim* (Genesis 37) is more plausibly "a long-sleeved / ornamented tunic" of status than a high-priestly vestment — the high priesthood did not yet exist in the patriarchal narrative — so reading it as the priest's many-colored coat is anachronistic. And the modern temple garment's relation to ancient vestments is itself the contested question, not a given. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim leans on a *real* ancient interpretive tradition (the cosmic veil) and a *real* fabric overlap between veil and vestments (Exodus 28), and the Joseph-coat reading is offered as typology within that pattern, not as historical-critical exegesis; the vault elsewhere argues the garment/vestment link on independent lexical grounds ([[The Garment Shares the Hebrew Word of the Priestly Vestments]]).
