# Patriarchal Blessings Reveal Tribal Lineage and Cannot Be Faked

## The Claim

In the LDS Church, a stake **patriarch** pronounces a one-time blessing that, among other things, declares the recipient's tribe of Israel. [[Jonah Barnes]] argues, from a firsthand mission account, that this is genuine revelation rather than rote formula: a traveling patriarch in Russia began declaring *unusual* tribes (Gad, Reuben, Naphtali, Simeon) to converts; a second patriarch, sent to vet him *without being told why*, independently produced the same distribution; the corroboration prompted the Quorum of the Twelve to investigate and [[Russell M. Nelson]] to visit Siberia in 2004. The supporting frame, voiced by [[Oak Norton]] and the hosts, is that "the one calling in the church you can't fake is a patriarch" — an administrative office can be performed, but spirit-channeled blessing cannot.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Independent corroboration of an unexpected pattern
Two patriarchs — Elder Schütze and, sent to check him, BYU Russianist [[Gary Browning]] (himself a patriarch, not told the purpose) — independently assigned the same surprising tribes to Russian converts. Barnes frames this as the structure of a verification test: the second observer, blind to the first's results, reproduces them.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate as evidence — the story is a secondhand, unverifiable personal account; *if* accurately reported, blind concordance would be striking, but there is no documentation, control, or base-rate.

### 2. The blessing is physically costly to the patriarch
Barnes, translating at a blessing, reports the patriarch could give only ~four a day because each one visibly drained him; he compares it to Sidney Rigdon collapsing after the vision of [[D&C 76]] ("Sidney is not as used to it as I am"). The argument: the toll signals a real spiritual transaction, not recitation.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Weak — fatigue is consistent with intense ritual focus and has obvious naturalistic explanations; it does not establish the *content's* accuracy.

### 3. "The calling you can't fake"
Oak Norton relays his father's predecessor's line — "the one calling in the church that you can't fake is a patriarch" — because most callings are administrative (read the manual, teach the lesson) but a blessing's specific declarations cannot be performed on demand. Both Norton and Barnes report oddly specific predictions in their own and ancestors' blessings that later "came to pass."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Weak — selective, anecdotal confirmation (the recipients recall the hits); vulnerable to confirmation bias and the Barnum effect (which the episode itself raises as the "boilerplate" criticism).

### 4. Tribal lineage is read as covenant assignment, not genetics
The tribes named (mostly Ephraim in the modern dispensation; the rare tribes in Russia) are treated as lineage *within the covenant*, consistent with the vault's distinction that declared Israelite lineage is genealogical/adoptive, not a DNA claim. See [[Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent]] and [[Latter-day Saints Are the Ephraimite Gentiles in the Book of Mormon]].[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate as a *framing* point — it correctly insulates the claim from a population-genetics rebuttal, but does not make the tribal declarations independently testable.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic raises several decisive points. (a) **Unfalsifiable and undocumented:** the entire case is one apologist's mission memory; there is no record of the blessings, no blind protocol actually established, and no way to check whether Browning was truly uninformed — so the "independent corroboration" is asserted, not demonstrated. (b) **Barnum/expectation effects:** patriarchs share a common theological framework and may have known the church was emphasizing the gathering; assigning "rare" tribes in a new mission field could reflect shared expectation, not independent revelation, and recipients remember the specific "hits" while forgetting misses — the very "boilerplate" charge the hosts concede many feel. (c) **The fatigue and "can't fake" points are about sincerity, not accuracy:** a deeply sincere, physically drained patriarch can still be producing humanly-generated content. (d) The most a neutral observer can grant is that the practice is meaningful to participants — which is a claim about religious experience, not verifiable lineage. A Latter-day Saint reply (in the episode): the claimed *blind concordance between two patriarchs* is the part that resists the Barnum explanation, and the value of the patriarchal blessing is pastoral and devotional — Barnes notes even "boilerplate"-seeming blessings yielded specific guidance on study and reflection — so the argument functions as a faith-promoting testimony, not a proof.
