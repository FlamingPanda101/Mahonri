# Joseph Smith's Sealing Text Was More Egalitarian Than Brigham Young's

## The Claim
Historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] argues, by comparing two published sealing texts, that [[Joseph Smith]]'s 1842 sealing revelation framed husband and wife in **more egalitarian** terms than the Nauvoo-temple sealing text (which Stapley attributes largely to [[Brigham Young]]). Joseph has man and woman "take up similar obligations" and "relate to each other equally"; Brigham's "subordinates women" — men become kings and priests *to God*, women queens and priestesses *to their husbands*. The asymmetry "endures for well over a hundred years," and recent revisions have reduced but not erased it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two comparable texts survive.** Joseph Smith "dictated a revelation in 1842... a sealing text" (uncanonized, readable "on the Joseph Smith Papers"), and in 1852 [[Orson Pratt]] published the Nauvoo-temple sealing ceremony in his eastern newspaper. Both are "available on church-published websites," so they "can compare them."[^2] *Rating: strong — the documentary basis is public and named.*

2. **The crucial caveat: no egalitarian marriage existed in the 1800s.** Stapley front-loads the honest qualifier — "there's no such thing as egalitarian marriage... in the 19th century. Full stop. So whether it's monogamous or polygamous." The claim is *comparative*, not that Joseph's text was egalitarian by modern standards.[^3] *Rating: strong — the qualification disciplines the claim and forestalls anachronism.*

3. **The substantive difference.** Joseph's text: man and woman "take upon similar obligations... relate to each other equally." Brigham's: "men become kings and priests to God and women become... queens and priestesses to their husbands," which "subordinates women... in the ceremonies."[^4] *Rating: moderate–strong — Stapley's reading of the two texts; he flags it as an argument he finds "compelling," i.e. a scholarly interpretation.*

4. **It changed — recently and incompletely.** Stapley recounts counseling a young woman distressed by the "gender asymmetry," telling her his "money is on, in your lifetime, those asymmetries being removed" — a bet that "since changed," though "there are still some asymmetries that remain."[^5] This pairs with [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]] as datable evidence that sealing forms have shifted. *Rating: moderate — the recent revisions are real; "not entirely egalitarian" is Stapley's assessment.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A traditionalist may object that reading Joseph's text as a charter of equality projects modern values onto a document whose own author lived and taught patriarchal order, and that Stapley's attribution of the subordinating language specifically to Brigham Young (rather than to Joseph or to shared revelation) is a contestable historical judgment that conveniently locates the uncomfortable element in a later, more criticized figure. A critic from the other side notes that conceding the liturgy "subordinated women... for well over a hundred years" is a significant admission against an unchanging-ordinance claim. The measured conclusion: the comparison is grounded in two real texts, the difference Stapley identifies is plausible, and the subsequent revisions are documented — but the *causal* attribution to Brigham and the *egalitarian* gloss on Joseph are interpretive, not settled.

## Best Quotes
> "Joseph Smith's sealing text is more egalitarian. The man and the woman take upon similar obligations. They relate to each other equally. And the Nauvoo Temple sealing text, which I argue is largely Brigham Young's, it subordinates women."[^4]

> "There's no such thing as egalitarian marriage in the 19th century. Full stop."[^3]
