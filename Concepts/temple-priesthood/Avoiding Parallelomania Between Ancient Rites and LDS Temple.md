## The Point
Both Matthew Grey and Mark Ellison caution Latter-day Saints against an "overly enthusiastic parallelomania" — attempting to find one-to-one correlations between ancient Christian rituals and modern temple ceremonies where they may not exist. While early Christians did have ceremonies resembling aspects of Latter-day Saint temple worship (washing and anointing at baptism, prayer with uplifted hands, intercessory rites for the deceased), responsible comparison requires good historical method: start with the evidence, then interpret — not start with a theory and seek confirming evidence.

## Evidence
1. Grey's three methodological cautions: avoid parallelomania; be informed about both similarities and differences of the rituals (performances and meanings); be respectful of the different socio-historical contexts of each community.
2. The Sant'Apollinare Nuovo mosaic (Ravenna) of saints processing to offer crowns to Jesus has been "misinterpreted as reflecting our modern temple practices and clothing" by some Latter-day Saints.
3. Ellison notes that many seeming parallels "do not appear so convincing when examined closely in context," and that some Latter-day Saint scholars have been critiqued for selectively citing parallels while ignoring counter-evidence and ancient contexts.
4. Doctrine and Covenants passages (D&C 121:26–27; 124:39–42; 128:16–18) imply that Latter-day Saint temple ordinances were among revelations reserved for "the dispensation of the fullness of times" and not previously known.
5. The constructive alternative: appreciate the "shared conceptual vocabulary" and let ancient rituals provide perspective on similar practices in modern ceremonies.

## Best Quotes
> "Efforts to understand the connections between the two must be approached in ways that are methodologically responsible. This includes avoiding an overly enthusiastic 'parallelomania' (attempting to find correlations where they may not exist)." [^1]

> "It is better historical practice to start with the evidence and proceed to interpretation rather than to start with a theory and seek evidence to prove it." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is itself a direct address to Latter-day Saint readers: the authors argue that taking the aspiration "to turn our hearts to our ancestors" seriously means understanding ancient Christians "for who they were rather than ... exactly as we are," which both honors them and helps Saints "see more clearly the Lord's 'line upon line' incremental revelation" ([[2 Nephi 28.30-32|2 Nephi 28:30]]).
