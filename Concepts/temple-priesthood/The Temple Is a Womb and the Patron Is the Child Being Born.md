# The Temple Is a Womb and the Patron Is the Child Being Born

#type/claim

## The Claim
Tia (Ward Radio Women) proposes that the **LDS temple is an extended allegory of woman, the womb, and birth** — and that the patron passing through it is always positioned as the *child* being gestated and born, never as the mother. The move begins with "born again": when Nicodemus hears Jesus and asks whether he must "enter the second time into his mother's womb," Jesus does *not* correct the womb image but doubles down — "except a man be born of water and of the Spirit" ([[John 3.5|John 3:5]]). [^1] Baptism compresses death-and-rebirth into one ordinance; the temple "pulls it out into all of its glory," and every temple parallel (the water one is "born from," the veil, the cozy enclosure) casts the worshipper as the baby inside a divine womb. [^2] The pastoral payoff: in the temple "we don't go through a mother role... we go through a child of a Divine Mother and Father role," so a woman's deepest covenant identity is *child*, which reframes worries about childlessness. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Jesus affirms rather than rejects the womb metaphor.** [[John 3.5|John 3:5]] really does answer Nicodemus's literalized "womb" question with "born of water and of the Spirit," so reading new birth as a *gestational* image is grounded in the text's own logic, not imposed on it. *Rating: the textual hook is real; the temple application is devotional.* [^1]
2. **Water-as-feminine is a defensible biblical association.** The waters one is "born from" in baptism, and the recurrent "mother earth / mother country" personification, give the womb reading cultural and scriptural texture (cf. the Eden-as-sanctuary and divine-mother material in [[Eve Was Recreated in Eden as a Heavenly Mother Figure]]). *Rating: associative but well-attested in the tradition.* [^2]
3. **It coheres with the vault's temple-as-return thesis.** The endowment as a staged return to the divine presence ([[The Veil Marks Sanctity Not Shame and Is Meant to Be Traversed]]) maps naturally onto a birth narrative; the "child" framing complements, rather than competes with, the bride/bridegroom and Eden-return readings already in the vault.

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This is **typology layered on a rite, not the rite's stated meaning** — the endowment nowhere says "the temple is a womb," and the host concedes the whole thing is "extra credit," "not where my testimony lies." A critic will note the womb-allegory is *generated* by the interpreter: any enclosed sacred space with an entrance and an exit can be read as a birth canal, so the resemblance is cheap (the parallelomania risk the vault flags in [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). And building it on [[John 3.5|John 3:5]] over-reads a single dialogue. A measured reply: the claim is explicitly offered as *multivalent devotional symbolism* meant to help women "see more of the woman's story" in the gospel, not as the ordinance's lone intended meaning; its load-bearing facts (Jesus's non-correction of Nicodemus; the obstetric details in the companion notes) are checkable, and the synthesis is presented as a lens, not a doctrine.

## Best Quotes
> "I am convinced personally that when we say born again we're not just talking about baptism — we're talking about the temple... the temple is a birth story." [^2]

> "We don't go through a mother role in the temple — we go through a child of a Divine Mother and Father role. Our most important identity is child." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The reading reframes a recurring pastoral problem — women feeling "erased" or inadequate in temple worship — by relocating the patron's identity from *mother* to *child of Heavenly Parents*, and by recovering a feminine register for ordinances usually taught in male/priesthood terms. It sits alongside the vault's divine-feminine material ([[Eve Was Recreated in Eden as a Heavenly Mother Figure]], [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]]) as a devotional, not doctrinal, recovery of "the woman's story" in the temple.
