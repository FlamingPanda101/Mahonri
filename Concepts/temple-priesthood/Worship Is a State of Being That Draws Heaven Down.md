# Worship Is a State of Being That Draws Heaven Down

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] reframes worship as far more than praise: it is an active *practice* that puts a person into a *state of being* — actively humbling oneself before God — so that "the full spectrum of things that he has to give you... come to you as quickly as it's possible." Worship cultivates an interior and exterior "atmosphere" in which the Spirit, angels, and "even the Lord himself" can reside; it "always culminates in prayer" but is *communion*, not the petition-and-negotiation McLauchlin says his prayers used to be.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Worship as accelerated sanctification.** McLauchlin reports an experience in early 2024 in which trying to "worship God" (apart from his usual prayer) "developed the momentum of the Spirit" and brought a broken heart and contrite spirit faster than anything else. He reads [[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]] as describing "the mechanics of building a tree of life" within — the seed swelling until the fruit becomes "delicious" — so that worship "brings into unity your mind, your heart, your spirit and your body." Worship "is active sanctification."[^1] *(Evidence rating: experiential testimony, explicitly first-person and flagged as new; functions devotionally, not as proof.)*
2. **Communion, not negotiation.** His prior prayers were short (~10 minutes) because he was "praying to extract a blessing" — climbing a mountain to get something. The shift came when "the Lord" asked, "what do you seek at the top of that mountain?... what if you just sought me for me?" Worship is therefore seeking God "for his sake," after which the needed blessings can be "magnified" beyond what ordinary petition yields.[^2] *(Moderate as a devotional distinction; the contrast with transactional prayer is its substance.)*
3. **Spiritual warfare against fear.** McLauchlin calls worship "a form of spiritual warfare": because "fear is the first fruit of the fall" (as in the Eden/endowment pattern, where Adam and Eve first fear and hide), and most sin is a fear-response, an encounter with God "displaces fear" and so uproots sin. He cites Nephi worshiping amid the storm ([[1 Nephi 17.1|1 Nephi 17–18]]) and "the sacrifice of praise" ([[Romans 10.17|Romans]]) as shifting the spiritual environment, including around one's family.[^3] *(Moderate — a coherent devotional theology of fear/sin; the causal claims about "shifting the environment" are testimonial.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Several cautions are fair. (a) The framework is almost entirely one person's recent private experience, presented — by the speaker's own admission — as "new" and "a little out of the ordinary"; it is not drawn from authoritative teaching and could as easily reflect temperament or auto-suggestion as revelation. (b) Critics of "manifestation"-style spirituality note that promising that worship will "restructure your world in a week" and "produce results faster than anything" courts a prosperity-gospel cadence the host elsewhere distances himself from; unmet expectations can wound faith. (c) An outside observer might note the experiences are unfalsifiable and self-confirming ("it's never ceased to produce something that surprises me"). McLauchlin partly anticipates this by insisting worship is *obedience and surrender* ("the greatest worship... is obedience"), not a technique for extracting blessings — which reframes the practice as self-giving rather than self-help, and grounds it in [[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]] and [[3 Nephi 9|3 Nephi 9:20]] rather than in results.

## Best Quotes
> "Worship is a practice that you can do that will put you into a state of being... so that the full spectrum of things that he has to give you and endow you and bless you with come to you as quickly as it's possible."[^1]

> "He's like, 'I know you believe that I'm there with you in that thing, but what if you just sought me for me and sought me for my sake?' And that was the top of the mountain."[^2]

## LDS Connection
McLauchlin presents worship as the practical "bridge" across the gap between what Latter-day Saints *profess* about God and the rich spiritual experiences described in scripture — the "delta" he has spent a decade trying to close. It connects to his temple-centered teaching that the [[Calling and Election Is the Oath of the Father Sealed in the Endowment|endowment is an endowment of priesthood]] and that [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power|priesthood is an order, not a power]], and pairs with [[Everyone Worships Something So Choose What to Be Conformed To]].
