# The Covenants and Blessings Are Identical in Monogamous and Plural Sealings

## The Claim
Against the fear that exaltation required polygamy — or that the eternal blessings of a plural sealing exceed a monogamous one — historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] cites Elder [[Marcus Nash]]'s 2015 article: the **covenants and blessings of the sealing ordinance are identical** whether the marriage is monogamous or plural. So "eternal exaltation does not ride on your monogamous or polygamous marriage status."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The documentary basis is [[Orson Pratt]]'s recorded ceremony.** "Gratefully, Orson Pratt recorded very carefully the exact words of the sealing ceremony during a plural marriage and everything that happened during that ceremony," so "we have a complete record of the covenants that were made and the blessings that were promised."[^2] *Rating: strong — a real primary-source record enables the comparison.*

2. **The general-authority confirmation.** "Elder Marcus B. Nash... published a great article in 2015 where he confirmed that the covenants and blessings are identical between both polygamous and monogamous marriages. So from a ritualistic perspective, the covenants and blessings are exactly the same."[^3] *Rating: moderate–strong — an official, citable conclusion; it is church commentary rather than independent scholarship, hence [[Marcus Nash]] is tier C.*

3. **The reassurance it provides.** Many members fear that "in the afterlife they will need to share their spouse, and that's scary and uncomfortable." The identical-covenants finding is offered to "reassure people" that ritual status does not stratify exaltation, with the modern posture being "we'll sort it out in the next life."[^4] This connects to [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]] and the broader sealing-flexibility discussed in [[Belief in Polygamy Was Required for the Temple Only in 1856]]. *Rating: moderate — the pastoral application; it addresses anxiety more than it resolves the underlying theology of [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]].*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic observes that "identical *ritual* covenants" does not settle the *theological* asymmetry: [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] is the revelation that introduced eternal plural marriage and still stands, men can be sealed to multiple living women while women cannot, and the "we'll sort it out later" stance leaves the lived fear of "sharing a spouse" unanswered in substance. The fair conclusion: the claim is true and useful as far as it goes — the *wording and promises* of the ordinance do not differ — but it brackets, rather than resolves, the harder doctrinal questions about how plural sealings function in the eternities, which the source openly treats as unresolved.
