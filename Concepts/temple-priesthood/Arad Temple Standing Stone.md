# Arad Temple Standing Stone

## The Claim

The Judahite temple excavated at **Arad** (in the Negev) contained, in its inner sanctuary (Holy of Holies), a **standing stone (massebah)** flanked by two **incense altars**. [[Dan McClellan]] uses this find as the primary material evidence that Israelite/Judahite temples housed cult stones that served as the visible divine image — the thing one "saw" as the face of God — and that the cult involved heavy (cannabis-bearing) incense.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A massebah in the Holy of Holies.** McClellan: "we have a standing stone found in a Holy of Holies in a Judahite temple" at Arad — direct archaeological confirmation that such stones stood in Israelite/Judahite sanctuaries.[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — the Arad sanctuary with its massebah is a well-documented excavation.)*

2. **Two incense altars beside it.** "Two incense altars... were found right just a few feet away from that standing stone."[^3] *(Evidence rating: strong, published.)*

3. **Cannabis residue, ~2,700 years old.** Residue testing of the burned substances identified three kinds of cannabis (see [[Cannabis Incense and the Mystical Temple Experience]]).[^4] *(Evidence rating: strong — corresponds to the 2020 Tel Arad residue study.)*

4. **Function as material medium of presence.** The stone is interpreted not as God himself but as the medium transmitting the divine presence — to see it was to "see the face of God" (see [[Standing Stone Massebah as Material Medium of Divine Presence]]).[^5] *(Evidence rating: interpretive synthesis grounded in the archaeology.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A conservative reader can argue the Arad sanctuary represents *unauthorized* or syncretistic worship at a peripheral fort, condemned by the Deuteronomic reformers (who dismantled high places), and therefore does not reveal normative Yahwism centered on the imageless Jerusalem temple. McClellan's reply is that the broader pattern of cult imagery (Arad, Kuntillet Ajrud) plus the lateness of the aniconic polemic (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]) indicates such installations were once mainstream, not deviant.

## Best Quotes

> "We've actually found a standing stone in an Israelite Judahite temple in Arad. So we know that they had standing stones in their temples. But to see the standing stone was to see God."[^1]
