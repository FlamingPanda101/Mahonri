# Temple Origins Run Deeper Than Freemasonry

## The Point
Critics charge that [[Joseph Smith]] simply plagiarized the Latter-day Saint temple endowment from [[Freemasonry]], which he was initiated into at Nauvoo in 1842. Fife answers that while some surface parallels exist (ritual handclasps, sacred embraces), the *differences* are more informative: the endowment is built around creation, fall, redemption, and enthronement — a near-universal pattern of *ancient Near Eastern* temple rites that Freemasonry lacks and that Joseph Smith could not have known in 1842. The "borrowed from Masonry" thesis is, in Fife's words, "too simple."

## Evidence
1. **Ancient Egyptian rites.** Fife lists six recurring topics in Egyptian temple ritual that track the endowment but not Masonry: a primordial written document, purification (anointing, lustration, clothing), creation, garden, travel, and ascension/coronation/admission to the heavenly company. Ritual handclasps and sacred embraces also pervade Egyptian iconography (citing Stephen Ricks's *Interpreter* article).[^1]
2. **Cross-cultural temple patterns.** Chinese tradition depicts the first humans Fuxi and Nüwa holding a square and a compass; Fuxi wears animal skins, offers the first open-air sacrifice on a mountain, and institutes marriage — motifs Fife argues were implausibly available to Joseph in 1842.[^2]
3. **Early Christian and apocryphal parallels.** Texts unavailable before 1842 describe baptism for the dead, washing/anointing/investiture, new names, handclasps, secret words, prayer circles, passing the angels, and admittance into God's presence. Fife notes the argument that "mystery" in such texts may originally have meant "ordinance," with Christ initiating disciples into the "mysteries of the kingdom."[^3]
4. **The anti-Mason/pro-Mason whipsaw.** Fife observes that critics alternately accuse Joseph of being an anti-Mason (the Gadianton robbers as Masons) and a Mason-plagiarist — an inconsistency that undercuts the charge.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "While some parallels exist between freemasonry and the Latter-day Saint temple endowment, the differences are the most informative." [^1]

> "Crediting Joseph's ability to restore ancient traditions solely on freemasonry is too simple. Freemasonry does not remotely explain this connection." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Fife frames the endowment as a *restoration* of an ancient pattern rather than a borrowing, pointing to a "shared conceptual vocabulary" across cultures and Joseph's claim to have "instituted the ancient order of things" (History of the Church 5:1–2). Responsible Latter-day Saint scholarship cautions that such parallels must be argued from the evidence rather than asserted — see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] — but Fife's point stands that the temple's roots reach far beyond a single 1842 Masonic lodge.
