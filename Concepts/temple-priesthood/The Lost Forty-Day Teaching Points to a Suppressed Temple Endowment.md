# The Lost Forty-Day Teaching Points to a Suppressed Temple Endowment

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the opening of Acts preserves the trace of a *lost* body of secret teaching that the resurrected Jesus gave during his forty days with the apostles — teaching the canonical Gospels do not contain — and that this missing instruction is the early-Christian temple **endowment**. The argument has two moves: (1) [[Acts 1.1-5|Acts 1:1-5]] says the author's "former treatise" recorded "all that Jesus began both to do and teach until the day he was taken up," and that the risen Christ spent forty days "speaking of the things pertaining to the kingdom of God" — yet no such forty-day curriculum survives; and (2) the Greek behind "the promise of the Father" they are told to await is linked to *endo-* ("to clothe upon"), the root of *endowment*, so the apostles are told to wait in Jerusalem for an endowment.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Acts itself flags a teaching record the Gospels do not contain
The author says he already wrote of *all* Jesus "began both to do and teach" up to the Ascension; but the canonical Gospels give almost nothing of the forty days of post-resurrection instruction "pertaining to the kingdom of God." Barnes: "this resurrected Jesus Christ is revealing the secrets of the universe and we don't have any of it; it's just gone."[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the forty-day gap is a real feature of the text; that the missing content was specifically a *temple ordinance* is the interpretive leap.

### 2. The "mystery" vocabulary is initiation language, not "hard to understand"
Jesus tells the disciples it is given to them to know "the mysteries of the kingdom" while the crowd gets parables ([[Matthew 13.10-11|Matt 13:11]]); Barnes argues *mystērion* in Greek denotes a temple ordinance, and the secret/perfected tiers run "all through the New Testament," so an esoteric inner teaching is native to the Gospels. See [[The Mysteries Were Converted From Lived Experience Into Doctrine]].[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the layered audience is textual; the technical "temple ordinance" gloss of *mystērion* is contested by mainstream exegetes.

### 3. The endo→endowment etymology
"The word promise in Greek is *endo*... which is where we get the word endowment" — so Acts 1:4 ("wait for the promise of the Father") is read as "wait for the endowment." This makes the Pentecost-adjacent "promise" an endowment of secret teaching, not only the gift of the Spirit.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Weak — the actual Greek of Acts 1:4 is *epangelia* ("promise/announcement"), a different root from *endyō* ("to clothe"); the link is a homophonic gloss, not a real cognate.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers as follows. (a) **The etymology is simply wrong:** "the promise of the Father" in Acts 1:4 is *epangelia*, and the text identifies the promise two chapters later as the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2); *endyō*/"endued with power" appears at Luke 24:49, a *different* word in a different verse, so building "endowment" out of Acts 1:4 is a folk etymology. (b) **The forty-day silence is an argument from silence:** Luke summarizing rather than transcribing the resurrection teaching does not imply the content was a suppressed ordinance — it more plausibly means Luke had no detailed source or no need to record it. (c) **Mainstream reading of *mystērion*:** in Matthew 13 / 1 Cor 2 it means God's previously hidden *plan of salvation now revealed to all believers*, with no rite implied. (d) The whole inference runs: gap in the record → therefore a temple endowment → which conveniently matches the modern LDS ceremony, which is the conclusion in search of evidence. A Latter-day Saint reply: even setting the etymology aside (Luke 24:49's "endued/clothed with power from on high" *does* use clothing language for the same promised event), the load-bearing point is the documented forty-day teaching gap plus the converging apocryphal witnesses (Secret Mark, Pistis Sophia, Acts of John) that a *secret ordinance* tradition was attached to the risen Christ — see [[Clement of Alexandria and Secret Mark Attest a Suppressed Temple Gospel]] and [[Gnostic Apocrypha Preserve Temple Ordinance Patterns]].
