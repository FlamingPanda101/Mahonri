# Profane Literally Means Outside the Temple

#type/claim

## The Claim
Against the charge that gating temple entry behind worthiness is a uniquely Mormon cruelty, Ward Radio argues from etymology that restricting the uninitiated from sacred space is the *ordinary grammar of religion*. The word **profane** "literally means outside the temple" — from Latin *pro fano*, "in front of / outside the temple" — and "in ancient times it referred to things or people not initiated into religious mysteries or practices… not permitted to enter into sacred spaces like temples."[^1] The panel adds that the Greek term **mysteries** did not mean "puzzles" but **temple rituals**: "the mysteries of God were actually like the temple initiation practices of God… euphemized in the Greek as mysteries, but that actually meant temple ceremonies."[^2] So the structure the critic attacks — an inner rite open only to the initiated, with everyone else "profane" in the literal sense of *standing outside* — is "all over… ancient religion" and presupposes "at least at some level of being good in order to get in these holy places."[^3] The exclusion is not contempt for outsiders; it is the definition of a temple.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The etymology is genuinely correct (rated: strong).** *Profane* does derive from Latin *profanus / pro fano*, "before (outside) the temple," originally marking what lay outside consecrated ground. The "secular / irreverent" meaning is the *derived* sense; the spatial-temple sense is primary.[^1] This is a real datum, not a folk etymology, and it reframes "profane" from an insult hurled at outsiders into a neutral topographical term — you are *profane* simply by being *outside the fane*.

2. **Graded sacred space is cross-cultural and ancient, not a Mormon invention (rated: strong; coherence).** The vault already documents that the Israelite temple was structured by [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple|gradations of holiness]] with [[Guardians of Sacred Space and the Password Motif|guardians and entrance requirements]], and that early Christians withheld the meaning of the sacraments from the uninitiated under [[Mystagogy - Teaching Sacraments Only After Baptism|mystagogy]]. A threshold of preparation guarding an inner rite is therefore the historical norm across Israel, the mystery cults, and early Christianity — exactly the company the panel claims.

3. **"Mysteries = temple rituals" has real ancient warrant (rated: moderate).** In the Greco-Roman *mystēria* (Eleusis, etc.) the term did denote rites of initiation closed to outsiders, and early Christian writers used *mystērion* for the sacraments. The panel's gloss is broadly sound as a claim about initiatory rites, even if "the mysteries of God" in scripture carries a wider semantic range than "temple ceremony" alone.

4. **It dissolves the "shaming" charge by reframing the boundary (rated: moderate-strong).** If a worthiness threshold is what *makes* a space a temple rather than a public hall, then declining to admit the unprepared is not a verdict on their human worth but the ordinary logic of consecration — consonant with reading the temple as covenant relationship rather than a club that ranks people (see [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]]).

**Steelman of the critic.** The strongest counter concedes the etymology and still resists the inference. *An "is" is not an "ought":* showing that the mystery cults and ancient temples excluded the uninitiated establishes that exclusion is *common*, not that it is *good* — plenty of normal ancient practices are now rejected, so "everyone did it" cannot by itself justify keeping a believing parent out of a child's sealing. *The analogy can over-prove:* the secrecy of the pagan mysteries is not obviously a flattering precedent for a church that elsewhere presents itself as the open restoration of *primitive* Christianity. *And the live grievance is relational, not topographical:* the critic's complaint is not "temples have boundaries" in the abstract but "this particular boundary runs between a parent and their child at a wedding," which an appeal to Latin word-roots does not soothe. The honest reply keeps the etymology in its lane: it refutes the narrower premise that worthiness-gated sacred space is a *peculiar Mormon innovation* designed to shame, by showing it is the default structure of temples across the ancient world; it does not, by itself, settle whether *this* threshold should be drawn where it is — that is the covenant-access question argued separately.
