# The First Temple Contained the Endowment Pattern Barker Reconstructed Independently

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the Latter-day Saint **endowment** preserves the ritual content of the pre-reform **First Temple** — and that the key witness is [[Margaret Barker]], who reconstructed that content from the Dead Sea Scrolls and ancient Near Eastern texts *without starting from LDS practice or any interest in LDS conclusions*. On Barker's reading the First Temple contained: **divine-council cosmology** (God presiding over an assembly of divine beings — [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82]], [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8]]'s "sons of God"); **deification theology** (the high priest underwent a genuine transformation entering the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur); **sacred garments** with theological symbolism; **anointing rituals** conferring divine status; **veil ceremonies** marking the human/divine boundary; and **direct access to God** for ordinary Israelites — the element [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category|Josiah's reform]] eliminated most completely.[^1] The endowment matches each element: an enacted creation–fall–mortality–return drama in first person, sacred clothing, new names, signs tied to covenants, and a veil ceremony — structured as *dromena, legomena, deiktumena* ("things done, said, shown").[^2] [[John Walton]] supplies the narrative anchor: Adam and Eve are temple priests, and their post-fall garments (Hebrew *kothoneth*, [[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3]]) use the same word as the Aaronic vestments of [[Exodus 28|Exodus 28]] — making Eden and the endowment "the same story in two different forms."[^3] Because Ezekiel 9–10 and Daniel 10 describe that same white linen as the clothing of *divine beings*, the garment, in Barker's reading, meant "taking on the identity of a heavenly being."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Independent reconstruction breaks the circularity charge.** Barker (a non-LDS Methodist, former president of the Society for Old Testament Study) built her First-Temple model from textual variants, comparative ANE literature, and internal biblical evidence — not from the temple. If an outsider's reconstruction of the ancient form matches the endowment element-by-element, the match cannot be dismissed as LDS pattern-seeking. *Rating: strong if Barker's reconstruction holds; she is credentialed but her thesis is a contested minority position (see the steelman).* [^1]
2. **The textual data is real and convergent.** The [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8]] "sons of God" reading (4QDeut, LXX, vs. Masoretic "sons of Israel") and [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82]]'s divine assembly are concrete, documented variants pointing to a suppressed divine-council theology — corroborated independently by [[Michael Heiser]]. *Rating: the manuscript evidence is solid; the inference to a full ritual system is Barker's synthesis.* [^1]
3. **The *kothoneth* lexical link is checkable.** That the same Hebrew word covers Eden's garments and the priestly vestments of Exodus 28 is a verifiable lexical fact, and [[John Walton|Walton]]'s priestly reading of Adam/Eden is mainstream evangelical scholarship, not an LDS invention. *Rating: a genuine, load-bearing datum — see [[The Garment Shares the Hebrew Word of the Priestly Vestments]].* [^3]
4. **It coheres with the broader First-Temple case.** The vault argues independently that the Book of Mormon encodes pre-exilic temple theology ([[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]]) and that the endowment is already in the 1830 text ([[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]]), so this is not an isolated parallel.

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Three objections deserve their due. (a) **Barker is a minority.** Her "Temple Theology" is influential but *not* the consensus of Old Testament studies; mainstream scholars contest how much of her reconstructed pre-exilic religion is real versus inferred, so an argument resting on it inherits that uncertainty. (b) **Parallelomania.** The endowment is symbol-rich, and ancient temples broadly share garments, anointing, sacred space, and ascent imagery; a motivated reader can map almost any initiatory rite onto another. Responsible LDS scholars themselves warn against exactly this — see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]. (c) **Lay access is the weakest link.** Arnold *concedes* that whether ordinary pre-exilic Israelites (not just the high priest) had this access "remains debated," and his evidence for it (garment-transformation imagery in prophetic visions; democratized ANE parallels) is suggestive, not decisive. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument does not need *every* element certain — it needs the *cluster* (divine council, deification, garments, veil, new name, first-person drama) to be more economically explained by a preserved ancient pattern than by a 19th-century farmer inventing it; and the lexical and manuscript data (Deut 32:8, *kothoneth*) are not Barker-dependent. The parallelomania caution is a reason to argue carefully, not a refutation.
