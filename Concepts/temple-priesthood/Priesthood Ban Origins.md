# Priesthood Ban Origins

From roughly **1852 until 1978**, The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints withheld priesthood ordination and temple ordinances from members of Black African descent. The restriction was first articulated publicly by **[[Brigham Young]]** (announced before the Utah territorial legislature in 1852) and was maintained by his successors, justified over time with folk explanations drawn from the wider 19th-century American Christian milieu — the "curse of Cain/Ham" tradition and a less-valiant-in-the-pre-existence theory — none of which was ever issued as a documented revelation. The ban was lifted by the **1978 revelation** received by President **[[Spencer W. Kimball]]** and the Quorum of the Twelve, canonized as **Official Declaration 2 (OD-2)**. In 2013 the Church published the Gospel Topics essay "Race and the Priesthood," which *disavows* the past theories ("a curse... mark of a curse... reflection of unrighteous actions in a premortal life") and notes the restriction's origins are not fully clear.

## Relevance
The ban is the standard hard case thrown at LDS truth claims and at the vault's epistemology, so it appears as a stress test rather than a strength. Its central use is in [[The Diachronic Collective Witness - Read the Prophets Across All Time]]: a critic ([[Steven Pynakker]]) argues a 1940s Latter-day Saint would have invoked "the collective witness" to *defend* the ban, so the model proves too much. The apologetic reply ([[Jacob Hansen]]) is that the collective witness is read *across all time* and flags **contradictions, not novelty** — the ban is treated as an *aberration* (like Adam-God) because no affirmative revelation ever established it, and OD-2 corrected it. The note candidly steelmans the critic: contemporaries *did* mistake the ban for doctrine, which exposes the risk of sorting "aberration" from "witness" only in hindsight. It also connects to "a man of his time" framings of Brigham Young.
