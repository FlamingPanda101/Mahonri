# The Fag el-Gamous Coptic Burial Garments Mark an Ancient Temple Connection

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that burial garments excavated from the **Fag el-Gamous** cemetery in Egypt (burials roughly 200 BC–AD 800) materially link the first-century Christian temple to the modern LDS endowment garment — and that, because the site was excavated in 1981 (a BYU project), Joseph Smith could not possibly have known of it, making the resemblance a **prolepsis** rather than a borrowing. The reported features: shirts bearing **rosette knots over each breast and over the right leg**, and a **slit across the abdomen**, on bodies buried **facing east** for the resurrection. Barnes reads these marked garments as a temple-clothing pattern surviving in Coptic Christian burial practice. The garment-as-covering theology behind the claim is developed in [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The site and date are real and post-date Joseph Smith
Fag el-Gamous (near the Seila pyramid in the Fayum) is a genuine, large Greco-Roman/Coptic-era cemetery excavated by BYU teams beginning in 1981. Whatever the interpretation of the garments, the *inaccessibility* of the finds to Joseph Smith is straightforward — which is the core of the prolepsis argument. See [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong for chronology/inaccessibility; the interpretive weight is elsewhere.

### 2. East-facing burial is well-attested Christian practice
Orientation of the dead toward the east "for the resurrection" is a documented early-Christian convention, so that detail is solidly grounded and consistent with the bodies being Christian.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong for the fact; neutral as to the temple thesis (east-facing burial is broadly Christian, not distinctively temple).

### 3. Marked garments on the body suggest ritual clothing
The reported rosette marks over the breasts and right leg and the abdominal slit are specific, repeated placements rather than random decoration, which is what licenses reading them as a *patterned* (ritual) garment rather than ordinary clothing.
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate — the placements as described are suggestive, but the published archaeology of the textiles is limited and popular summaries may overstate the regularity and the marks' meaning.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) the load-bearing claim is **archaeological**, and the description circulating in LDS apologetics outruns the cautious published record — rosettes, knots, and slits in Greco-Roman Egyptian textiles have ordinary functions (decoration, manufacture, dressing the corpse) and were widespread across non-Christian burials, so reading them as temple markings is selection plus interpretation; (b) a cemetery spanning a millennium (200 BC–AD 800) mixes pagan, Jewish, and Christian dead, so attributing a uniform "Christian temple garment" to it is anachronistic; (c) east-facing burial proves Christianity, not a temple endowment; and (d) mapping ancient marks onto the *specific* placements of the modern garment is exactly the parallelomania the vault elsewhere warns against (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). A Latter-day Saint reply: the prolepsis point is modest and secure — Joseph Smith demonstrably could not have known of a 1981 excavation, so even a *partial* resemblance is striking rather than suspicious; and the marked garment is offered as one material data point alongside the textual apocrypha (see [[Gnostic Apocrypha Preserve Temple Ordinance Patterns]]), not as standalone proof. The honest version of the claim flags that the textile interpretation is contestable and rests on the as-yet-thin published analysis.
