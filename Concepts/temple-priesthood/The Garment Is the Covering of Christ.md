# The Garment Is the Covering of Christ

#type/claim

## The Claim
The Latter-day Saint temple garment is not occult underwear but a wearable enactment of **being clothed in Christ**, rooted in the Bible's own clothing language. The hosts build the case from etymology and Hebrew: the New Testament word for *garment* and the word behind *endow* (Latin *induere*) both mean "to clothe upon," so to be endowed is to be **clothed upon** — and what one is clothed in is the name and atoning grace of Jesus Christ. [^1] In [[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3:21]] the first thing God does for fallen Adam and Eve is **make coats of skins and clothe them**; this God-given covering (replacing their self-made fig leaves) is "symbolic of the covering grace of Jesus Christ." [^2] They reinforce it with the Hebrew *kaphar* — the basic word for **atonement** literally means **to cover** — so a 2024 general-conference teaching can say the garment "reminds us that the Savior and the blessings of his atonement cover us throughout our lives." Wearing the garment daily is therefore "wearing Jesus every day." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The clothing language is genuinely biblical, not improvised.** Paul writes "put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ" (Rom 13:14) and "as many of you as have been baptized into Christ have put on Christ" (Gal 3:27); Isaiah's "garments of salvation / robe of righteousness" (Isa 61:10) and Revelation's white robes (see [[Revelation 7.9-14|Rev 7:9-14]]) all treat salvation as a *clothing*. The temple garment makes that metaphor a daily, tangible practice rather than a one-time figure of speech. *Rating: strong on the biblical motif.*

2. **The Genesis 3:21 typology is old and widespread.** Reading the divinely-made "coats of skins" as a type of Christ's atoning covering — bought at the cost of an animal's death, prefiguring the Lamb — appears across Jewish and patristic interpretation, not only in LDS sources. The fig-leaves-to-skins progression (inadequate self-covering superseded by a God-given one) is a natural reading of the text. *Rating: well-attested typology.*

3. **The *kaphar* = "cover" point is linguistically sound.** The Hebrew root *k-p-r* underlying *kippur* (as in Yom Kippur) does carry the sense of covering/wiping; tying the garment that *covers* the body to the atonement that *covers* sin is a coherent Hebrew wordplay, the same kind of move Hebrews 7 makes on "Melchizedek." *Rating: defensible wordplay.*

4. **It reframes a standard criticism.** Critics treat the garment as embarrassing or magical. Recasting it as "we wear Christ every day" answers the criticism on devotional rather than defensive grounds and connects it to the ancient baptismal **washing, anointing, and re-clothing** of early Christianity (see [[Baptismal Washing and Anointing in Early Christianity]]).

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** An outsider can grant every etymology and still object that the *explanation* is post hoc: the symbolic gloss ("garment = Christ's covering grace") is layered onto a practice whose recognizable form arrived with [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry|Joseph Smith's 1842 Freemasonry]], and the Greek/Latin "clothe upon" point proves only what the words mean, not that the modern garment is what those texts had in mind. The Genesis 3:21 reading is also a *typology*, i.e. a devotional pattern read back into the text, not the plain authorial intent. A Latter-day Saint reply is that the convergence — the clothing motif saturates the canon, the *kaphar* cover-language is built into the atonement vocabulary, and the Restoration claims to *recover* the rite rather than invent the symbol — is itself the argument, and that whether the garment is "weird" is a question of cultural unfamiliarity (see [[Ritual Has Been Normal for Four Thousand Years]]), not of theological incoherence.

## Best Quotes
> "Garment in Greek, the original word means to clothe upon. In *induere*, the Latin word that we get *endow* from, also means to clothe upon. So we're taking the name of Christ upon us, we're receiving our garments... we are clothed upon with truth." [^1]

> "In Genesis 3:21, it talks about how the Lord God made a coat of skins and clothed Adam and Eve. And this covering is symbolic of the covering grace of Jesus Christ." [^2]

> "*Kaphar* is the basic Hebrew word for atonement, and one of its meanings is *to cover*. Our temple garment reminds us that the Savior and the blessings of his atonement cover us throughout our lives... So we wear Jesus every day." [^3]
