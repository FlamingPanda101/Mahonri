# Calling Upon the Name of the Lord Is the True Order of Prayer

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] argues that the recurring biblical/Book-of-Mormon phrase **"to call upon the name of the Lord"** is a scriptural cipher for the [[The True Order of Prayer Is the Heartbeat of the Endowment|true order of prayer]] — and that endowed Latter-day Saints "know what that means." [^1] He builds it from a chain of temple-flavored passages where the phrase appears at moments of ascent or chastisement:
- **[[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:16]]** — immediately after the expulsion, "Adam and Eve... ceased not to call upon God"; McLauchlin says those who have the endowment recognize "the pattern" of what Adam is doing. [^1]
- **[[Ether 2|Ether 2:14]]** — the Lord chastens the brother of Jared "three hours" *because he remembered not to call upon the name of the Lord*; McLauchlin's gloss: "I don't think he wasn't praying — I think he wasn't praying in this way, in the true order." [^2]
- **[[Ether 3.14|Ether 3]]** and **[[3 Nephi 19|3 Nephi 19]]** — the brother of Jared's veil prayer, and the Nephite disciples being "given... what they should pray," are the same pattern of receiving the word and giving it voice. [^3]

The underlying logic is that **prayer = obtaining the word of God**, and the word is priesthood: at Babel the "confounding of languages" was really a confounding of *priesthood* (Adamic being "the power to declare in the heavens and on earth"), so the brother of Jared's plea "do not confound our language" is a plea to retain the power to call upon and obtain from God. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The phrase really does cluster at theophany/covenant moments.** "Call upon the name of the LORD" attends Enos's wrestle, Abraham's altars, Elijah's contest ([[1 Kings 18.24|1 Kings 18:24]]), and Adam's post-Eden worship — so treating it as a marked, weighty idiom (rather than a generic word for "pray") is textually reasonable. *Rating: the idiom is genuinely loaded; the specific "= true order" identification is the interpretive add.* [^1]
2. **[[Ether 2|Ether 2:14]] supports the "wrong *kind* of prayer" reading.** The brother of Jared is faulted for not *calling upon the name*, not for prayerlessness in general (he is otherwise a man of staggering faith), which fits McLauchlin's claim that a *particular mode* of approach is in view. *Rating: a fair inference from the chastisement.* [^2]
3. **"Word = priesthood/power to declare" has a scriptural backbone.** Helaman 10's sealing promise to Nephi ("whatsoever ye shall... I will... because he obtained the word") and [[Alma 32|Alma 32]]'s "the word" growing in you do tie the *obtaining of the word* to divine power, supporting the prayer-as-obtaining-the-word frame. *Rating: coheres with the cited texts.* [^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** "Call upon the name of the LORD" most plainly means **invoke / worship / pray to** Yahweh — it is used of ordinary public worship (Gen 4:26; Joel 2:32; "whosoever shall call on the name of the Lord shall be saved," Acts 2:21) with no implication of an esoteric rite. Reading it as a *technical* term for a specific veil-ritual is an endowment-shaped meaning imported into the phrase; McLauchlin even leans on "if you've had your endowment, you know what that means," which is unfalsifiable to outsiders and inaccessible as evidence. The Babel-as-confounded-*priesthood* move likewise reinterprets "language" against the plain sense. A measured reply: the claim is offered as devotional pattern-recognition, not lexicography; and it does latch onto a real feature — the idiom's concentration at covenant/ascent scenes and the [[Ether 2|Ether 2:14]] chastisement for *not* calling on the name — so even a reader who rejects the technical identification can grant that the phrase marks a heightened, covenantal mode of approaching God.

## Best Quotes
> "They ceased not to call upon the name of the Lord. Now if you've obtained your endowment, you've seen that pattern; we know what that means, what Adam is doing specifically — he's engaging in the true order of prayer. Another term for that is calling upon the name of the Lord." [^1]

> "He's saying to him... I actually don't think he wasn't praying. I think he wasn't praying in this way, in the true order." [^2]

> "When it says they confound their languages, what he's doing is he's confounding their priesthoods — their knowledge of how to call upon God and how to obtain from him." [^4]
