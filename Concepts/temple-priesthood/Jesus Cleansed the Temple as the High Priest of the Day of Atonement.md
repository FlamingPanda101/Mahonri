# Jesus Cleansed the Temple as the High Priest of the Day of Atonement

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] argues that Jesus did not reject the temple — he came to *restore* the true (first) temple. In John's account, his cleansing of the temple "with a whip of cords" is a deliberate symbolic act: he is performing the high priest's Day-of-Atonement action of cleansing the sanctuary "with the action of a whip," thereby declaring himself high priest and repossessing the polluted second temple. Protestant claims that Jesus dismissed the temple (the rent veil "proves" it passed away) misread the text.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A whip of cords is symbolic, not practical (rated: Barker's reading of John 2).** "A whip of cords is a few bits of string; you wouldn't drive an ox out of the temple with one of those." So "what's he doing? He's doing something symbolic." John has "no spare lines" — "why is that line there? Jesus is cleansing that temple."[^1]
2. **It mirrors the Yom Kippur sprinkling (rated: Barker; Mishnaic).** "The action of the high priest on the day of atonement was to sprinkle the cleansing blood with the action of a whip — it actually says [he sprinkles] *with the action of a whip*." The detailed pattern of where he sprinkles (in the Mishnah, "not in Leviticus… how it was done in the second temple period") matches what Jesus enacts, so the temple authorities "would have known immediately what he was doing… and they did not like it."[^2]
3. **The earliest Christians saw themselves as the new temple (rated: textual).** 1 Peter 2:4–5 calls the community "living stones… built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood"; "if Jesus had rejected the temple… his earliest followers would not have seen themselves as a temple."[^3] See [[The Church as the New Temple Replacing Jerusalem]].
4. **The torn veil opens, not abolishes (rated: Barker's symbolism).** The veil (woven of the four colors = the four elements = matter) divides the material world from the holy of holies; its tearing means "that the veil… is now opened to all," not that the temple is finished — the high priest, dressed in the veil's materials, *was* the veil, so when Jesus the veil dies, the veil splits.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Mainstream exegesis usually reads the temple cleansing as a prophetic protest against commercial/cultic corruption (and a sign of judgment fulfilled in 70 AD), and reads the rent veil (Mark 15:38) as signifying *access* through Christ that supersedes the temple cult — not Jesus claiming the high-priestly office in a Yom Kippur reenactment. Barker's whip-equals-aspersion identification depends on a Mishnaic detail and her larger temple-theology framework, which is a contested minority program. The Gospel data are agreed; the high-priestly-reenactment reading is Barker's.

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus cleanses the temple with a whip of cords. Now a whip of cords is a few bits of string… what's he doing? He's doing something symbolic. Now we know that the action of the high priest on the day of atonement was to sprinkle the cleansing blood with the action of a whip."[^2]

> "Jesus came to restore the temple, the true temple."[^1]

## LDS Connection
This supplies a first-temple, atonement-centered reading of Jesus that undergirds the Restoration's temple theology: Christ as the great high priest restoring (not retiring) temple worship, the community as a living temple, and the veil as opened access rather than an abolished cult. It complements the vault's [[Cleansing of the Temple]] and [[The Day of Atonement]], and the Book-of-Mormon typology in [[Barabbas and Jesus Are the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]].
