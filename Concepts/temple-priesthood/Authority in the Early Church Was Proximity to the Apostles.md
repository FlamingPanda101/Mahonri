# Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles

## The Claim
In the apostolic-father era (late 1st–2nd century) authority was understood primarily as **proximity to the source** — "the closer you are to the source, the more authoritative you are." Bishops like [[Polycarp]], [[Ignatius of Antioch]], and [[Clement of Rome]] commanded respect because they *knew* the apostles, not because they held a formally-transmitted priesthood office in the later Catholic or LDS sense.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The apostolic fathers' own argument (rating: strong; patristic)
Ignatius urges "listen to the bishops; they'll keep the doctrine pure," and his warrant is partly "because I knew the apostles." Polycarp (d. 156, age 86) is authoritative because tradition says he "sat at John's feet." Authority is genealogical-by-acquaintance. [^1]

### 2. It mirrors how historians weigh sources (rating: moderate; analogy)
Day's analogy: a thousand years from now, of two books about what someone said, the one by an eyewitness wins — and a primary journal beats both. The apostolic fathers were the eyewitness-adjacent generation, so their voice carried. [^2]

### 3. Bishops (*episkopos*) appear only thinly in the NT (rating: moderate; lexical/critical)
*Episkopos* ("overseer," *epi*- "over" + *skopos* "watcher") appears in the Pastorals (Timothy, Titus), but Day concedes the scholarly view that the Pastorals may be a 2nd-century development and possibly not by Paul. "You don't see Jesus going out appointing people to be bishops in the text." The office solidifies as Christianity emerges from synagogue structures. See [[Rise of the Monarchical Bishop]]. [^3]

### 4. Early bishops were elected, not ordained-down a chain (rating: moderate; the Didache)
Didache ch. 15 has congregations *elect* their ecclesiastical leaders — closer to a vote than to LDS line-of-authority ordination. See [[The Didache as Earliest Church Manual]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Catholic claim (which Day reports seeing charted "all the way from Peter" in a church) is that authority *was* a formally transmitted thing — apostolic succession by ordination, with [[Clement of Rome]] (96 AD) and later writers asserting the apostles appointed bishops who appointed successors. On this view proximity and transmission coincide: the early bishops were both eyewitnesses *and* ordained successors. Day grants this "is a good argument" but notes the explicit succession-and-primacy claims are later (see [[Roman Primacy Was a Later Claim Not First-Century]]).

## Best Quotes

> "The closer you are to the source, the more authoritative you are. And history says this too." [^2]

> "Ignatius is saying, 'Listen to the bishops, they're going to keep the doctrine pure.' And why is he saying it? Partially because 'I knew the apostles.'" [^1]

> "You don't see Jesus going out appointing people to be bishops in the text. Now maybe he did. It's just not in the Bible." [^3]
