# Two Covenant Meals - The Betrothal Meal and the Wedding Feast

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues there are **two covenantal meals** in the Hebrew wedding, not one, and that this resolves a puzzle in [[Luke 13.6-9|Luke 13:26]], where the rejected protest "we have eaten and drunk in thy presence" yet are told "I know you not." The first meal is the **betrothal meal** (at the *erusin*): the bridegroom breaks bread to establish kinship and pours a cup of wine which, if the bride drinks it, seals the match — they are then "as good as married" though the wedding feast is still a year off. The second is the **wedding feast** itself. So "eating and drinking in thy presence" can be the *betrothal* meal — read as the sacrament / baptismal covenant — which is binding but **not sufficient** to pass into the wedding feast; "just showing up to church on Sunday, taking the sacrament as rote practice, might not be enough."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Luke 13:26 names a meal, then a rejection.** The rejected say "we have eaten and drunk in thy presence, and thou hast taught in our streets," and are still told "I know you not." A *two-meal* model explains how one can have shared a covenant meal yet not be admitted: the betrothal meal is not the wedding. *Rating: the text does pair a shared meal with non-recognition; the two-meal reading is the inference.*[^1]
2. **The betrothal meal is a real, distinct rite.** In the *erusin*, breaking bread "established kinship" (Woodmansee cites Abraham feeding the strangers, Genesis 18), and the bridegroom's poured cup, drunk by the bride, sealed a binding match — "at that point they're considered married... as good as married," though they then separate to prepare. See [[Ancient Hebrew Weddings Are a Three-Phase Type of the Temple]]. *Rating: leans on reconstructions of Jewish wedding custom, which are debated, but the betrothal-then-feast structure is well attested.*[^2]
3. **It maps onto the temple ascent.** [[Dave Butler|David Butler]] ties this to the Sermon's three-room ascent: "we ate and drank in thy presence" is the *second-room* covenant feast (cf. Exodus 24's meal before God), which is "emblematic" but does not by itself secure entry to the third room — "more is required." See [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms]] and [[The Three Temple Rooms Map Justice-Mercy-Grace and Moses-Elijah-the Lord]]. *Rating: coheres with the vault's three-room cluster; the second-meal/second-room alignment is the synthesis.*[^3]
4. **The sacrament as betrothal meal.** Woodmansee reads the sacrament (bread + wine + baptismal covenants) as the betrothal: "you are now part of that covenant... but there's more." The pastoral payoff is that covenant membership is the *beginning* of a marriage relationship, not its completion — dovetailing with [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works]]. *Rating: a devotional application; the identification of the sacrament with the betrothal meal is typological.*[^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Luke 13:26 reads naturally without any wedding apparatus: the rejected simply claim *acquaintance* ("you taught in our streets; we ate with you"), and Jesus denies a *saving* relationship — table-fellowship with the historical Jesus is not discipleship. The "two covenant meals" come from reconstructed Jewish wedding custom (betrothal cup + later feast) read back onto a Gospel saying that never mentions a wedding, and the further identification of the betrothal meal with the LDS sacrament is a second typological leap. The same [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple|parallelomania]] caution applies. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the wedding frame is not imposed on Luke 13 in isolation but drawn from the pervasive bridegroom/feast imagery Jesus himself uses; on that frame, "ate and drank in thy presence" *yet* "I know you not" is exactly what a betrothal-without-fidelity would produce, and the reading's value is pastoral coherence, held provisionally rather than as proof.
