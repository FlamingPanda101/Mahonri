# The Veil Marks Sanctity Not Shame and Is Meant to Be Traversed

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues against the modern intuition that to *veil* something is to demean or hide it. In an ancient covenant mindset the logic is the reverse: "something that is sacred and pure and good should be covered... because it's not there just for anyone to participate in or consume." A veil is a **mark of sanctity**, not subjugation — and a veil over a woman (or bride) signals holiness, not subservience. Critically, a veil is **not a wall**: "a veil is meant to be traversed," indicating "there's something sacred and precious on the other side, and if you'll do a little bit of preparation you can go there." This both explains the temple veil and answers a feminist/authenticity culture that equates virtue with total exposure. It presupposes that [[Profane Literally Means Outside the Temple|*profane* means "outside the temple"]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Concealment as sanctity, not shame.** Woodmansee: "veiling was considered to be a mark of sanctity." A culture that "equates authenticity with revealing everything" reads concealment as nefarious or lesser; the ancient mind held the opposite — the sacred is veiled *because* it is holy. *Rating: aligns with cross-cultural sacred-space practice; the cultural-critique framing is the value-added.*[^1]
2. **The veil divides sacred from [[Profane Literally Means Outside the Temple|profane]].** The hosts add the etymology: *profane* comes from Latin *pro fano*, "outside the temple," so a profane society is one where "everything is out in the open" and keeping anything sacred is viewed "suspiciously" — "calling good evil and evil good." The veil's purpose is "to create a division between the sacred and the profane." *Rating: the etymology is correct (see the parent note); a real datum.*[^2]
3. **A veil is permeable, unlike a wall.** "It's not a wall, it's a veil, and so a veil is meant to be traversed... we're not talking about putting a veil at the southern border, because a veil is not a permanent structure." It signals that with "certain commitments" / preparation one may enter the presence. Coheres with [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]] and [[Guardians of Sacred Space and the Password Motif]]. *Rating: a genuine and useful distinction — the temple veil is designed to be passed, unlike a barrier.*[^3]
4. **God dwells behind a traversable curtain.** [[Isaiah 40.17|Isaiah 40:22]] ("stretcheth out the heavens as a curtain... as a tent to dwell in") pictures God enthroned behind a curtain/tent — and "it's hard to lock a tent," so the boundary invites, with preparation, an approach. Coheres with [[Chancel Screens and Veils Demarcating Sacred Space]]. *Rating: the tent/curtain image is in the text; the "permeable, you can enter" reading is the application.*[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The historical record on veiling is mixed: in many ancient (and some biblical) contexts veiling a woman *did* mark control, status, or marital ownership, not only sanctity — so "veil = sanctity, never subservience" over-corrects. And while the temple veil was indeed crossable, it was crossable by *one* person, *once* a year, under threat of death — "meant to be traversed" understates how forbidding the boundary was. A critic also notes that recovering the *sacred* sense of concealment does not settle any present dispute about who may cross a given threshold. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim's target is the specific modern misreading that *all* concealment is repressive; against that, the ancient sacred-veil logic is a real and sufficient counterexample, and the "traversable with preparation" point is exactly what distinguishes a veil from a wall — the restrictions on *who* and *when* are the separate covenant-access question, addressed elsewhere ([[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]]).
