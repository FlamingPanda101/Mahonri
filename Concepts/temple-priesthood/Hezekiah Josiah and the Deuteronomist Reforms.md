# Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms

## The Point
Building his temple case, Fife enlists the work of non-Latter-day Saint biblical scholar [[Margaret Barker]]: the radical religious reforms of King Hezekiah (~750 BC) and King Josiah (~620 BC) stripped older, "pre-Josiah" elements out of Israelite temple worship — a divine Father and Mother, the Tree of Life, an atoning Messiah. Fife argues that the Book of Mormon's [[Lehi]], a contemporary of Josiah, was preserving exactly these purged "plain and precious" truths — and that Joseph Smith's recovery of them is inexplicable on a naturalistic account.

## Evidence
1. **The Deuteronomist purge.** Barker holds that Josiah's reform (2 Kings 23) removed sacred trees, pillars, and outlying altars — the religion "of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob" — so that the "Moses religion" with the Aaronic priesthood displaced "the Abrahamic faith and the Melchizedek priesthood" only just before the first temple fell.[^1]
2. **A "de-Christianized" Israel.** On this theory the reforms removed teaching of an atoning Messiah; the scope of the changes to the temple ceremony is unknown.[^2]
3. **Lehi against the reformers.** Fife places Lehi "acutely in conflict with the Deuteronomists": the Jews sought to kill him for prophesying of a Messiah (1 Nephi 1:19-20), while Laman and Lemuel deemed Jerusalem righteous for keeping the law of Moses (1 Nephi 17:22) — aligning them with the pro-Josiah party. Lehi's emphasis on the Tree of Life and the atoning Messiah (1 Nephi 10:11,14) is then a deliberate preservation of purged truths.[^3]
4. **Probable pre-Josiah parallels to modern temples.** Fife lists a divine Father and Mother, the Tree of Life, and teachings on creation, fall, atonement, and a suffering Messiah who conquers death.[^4]
5. **Haran ties the priestly source to *Hezekiah's* reform (not only Josiah's).** On The Stick of Joseph, [[Mike Day]] and [[Dave Butler]] introduce [[Menahem Haran]], who dates the priestly source (P) *before* the exile and argues that "in the same way that D is the constitution for Josiah's reforms... P was the constitution for Hezekiah's reforms" — priestly, eighth-century, and "the way priests wanted to run things." On this reading P is *older* than D (though younger than J and E). Haran further treats the tabernacle as described as a "priestly utopia," a retrojection of Hezekiah's *temple* backward into the wilderness as a tent. This supplies an independent, non-LDS scholarly basis for the reform-era framing Fife borrows from Barker, and it reinforces that the contest over the temple long predates Lehi.[^5][^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Almost everything that Josiah swept away can be matched in the religion of the patriarchs Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob." (Margaret Barker) [^1]

> "It makes sense that Lehi would emphasize the atoning Messiah and the Tree of Life because those elements were believed to have been removed from temple worship during the reforms. He was, in short, preserving the plain and precious truths." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is a striking convergence between mainstream "Deuteronomistic History" scholarship and the Book of Mormon's internal setting: if Barker is right that pre-exilic Israel knew a Heavenly Mother, a Tree of Life, and a coming Messiah, then a 19th-century farm boy reproduced the *content* of a religion scholars only reconstructed in the late 20th century. Fife uses this to reinforce that the temple's roots predate Freemasonry by millennia — see [[Temple Origins Run Deeper Than Freemasonry]].
