# Temple Work for the Dead Fulfills the Gates of Hell Promise

## The Claim
Having reread "the gates of hell" as the gates of *hades/sheol* (the realm of the dead — see [[The Gates of Hell Objection to Apostasy Conflates Survival With Soundness]]), Marvelous Wonder argues that the LDS Church is the *only* body actually fulfilling Christ's [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18]] promise. The problem the promise must solve is that "billions of our brothers and sisters were born at a time or place that kept them from ever hearing Jesus's name, much less being baptized." If nothing answered that, "the gates of hell certainly would prevail against the church." The Restoration's temple ordinances — proxy baptism and sealing for the dead — are precisely the key that "unlocks the gates of Sheol or the spirit prison," extending salvation to the dead and so keeping the gates of death from prevailing.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The promise is keyed to the dead, not institutional survival (rating: moderate; exegetical-internal).** Because Matthew 16:18 is set amid the giving of "the keys of the kingdom" and the power to "bind and loose... on earth and in heaven," the channel ties it to ordinances that bind across the veil — the sealing keys restored by Elijah. The verse's victory is over *death's* claim on the unevangelized.[^1]
2. **Baptism for the dead has NT precedent ([[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]]) (rating: moderate; scriptural).** Paul invokes "baptized for the dead" as an argument for resurrection, which the channel reads as evidence the practice is apostolic, not invented. See the fuller treatment in [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]].[^1]
3. **Elijah's keys turn the hearts of fathers and children ([[Malachi 4.5-6|Malachi 4:5-6]]) (rating: moderate; restoration-history).** The 3 April 1836 Kirtland Temple visitation (Christ, then Moses, Elias, and Elijah committing priesthood keys) is presented as the structural parallel to the Mount of Transfiguration, restoring the sealing power by which the living are bound to their dead — "more than a generic mending of family relationships."[^2]
4. **D&C 138 supplies the mechanism (rating: moderate; restoration-scripture).** Joseph F. Smith's 1918 vision of Christ organizing missionary work among the spirits (the spirit world) grounds the claim that the dead can still accept the gospel and have proxy ordinances performed for them — see [[D&C 138 Restores the Harrowing of Hell Recorded in the Gospel of Nicodemus]].[^2]

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)
A traditional Christian will object on two fronts. (a) **Exegetically**, Matthew 16:18 says nothing about ordinances for the dead; reading temple work into "the keys of the kingdom" is eisegesis, and the binding/loosing grant is naturally about teaching and disciplinary authority (the reading documented in [[Matthew 16.18]]). (b) **Soteriologically**, Hebrews 9:27 ("it is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment") is taken to foreclose post-mortem conversion, and 1 Corinthians 15:29 is notoriously obscure — Paul reports the practice in the *third* person ("they") without endorsing it, so it cannot bear the weight of a restored ordinance. On this view the "gates of hell" promise is adequately fulfilled by the church's perpetual existence and the gospel's continual availability to the living.

## Counter-Objection
The "once to die, then judgment" objection assumes judgment immediately follows death; the LDS intermediate-state model (the spirit world as a probationary realm before a future judgment — [[Hell Is Not in the Bible It Is the Spirit World Awaiting Judgment]]) blocks that inference, and the early-Christian descent tradition (Christ preaching to "the spirits in prison," 1 Peter 3:19-20) shows the dead *awaiting*, not yet finally sentenced. As for 1 Corinthians 15:29, the third-person framing is weak counter-evidence: Paul builds a *positive* argument for resurrection *on* the practice, which presupposes its legitimacy among his readers. The exegetical gap (Matthew 16 not naming temple work) is real, but the claim is an inference to best *fulfillment*: of the available readings, only one explains how a loving, omnipotent God keeps death from prevailing over the billions who died unevangelized.

## Best Quotes
> "With the restoration of temples and the institution of ordinances performed there, including baptism and confirmation for people who have passed on, the Lord has provided a way to extend his salvation to all the works of his hands, even those who were born at the wrong time and wrong place."[^1]

> "The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is the only church on the earth today that is actually fulfilling this promise from Jesus that the gates of hell would not prevail against his church."[^2]
