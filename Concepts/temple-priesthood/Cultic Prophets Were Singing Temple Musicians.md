# Cultic Prophets Were Singing Temple Musicians

## The Claim
[[Aubrey Johnson]] argues that prophecy in ancient Israel was not always anti-institutional: there were **cultic prophets** — prophets attached to the temple, organized in guilds, who prophesied *through music*. [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] note that David appoints these singing prophets, that they are the human counterpart to the singing angels of the temple vision, and that they may be the very temple personnel Josiah purged.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **David appoints prophesying musicians (rated: textual).** [[1 Chronicles 25.1|1 Chronicles 25:1–3]] has David set apart the sons of Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun "who should prophesy with harps" — the Hebrew verb is *naba*, "to prophesy." These are "prophets with salaries and with symbols," three musical guilds.[^1]
2. **The data is in Chronicles, not Kings (rated: source-critical).** The detail appears in Chronicles (Greek *Paralipomena*, "the things left out") but not in Kings, a Deuteronomistic book — fitting the pattern that the reform-edited histories omit the music, the prophecy, "the golden chariot of the cherubim, and all these things."[^2]
3. **Singers in the vision are angels (rated: Johnson via the hosts).** In Isaiah 6 the singers in the temple are angels; in the lived liturgy, the human singers "doing the singing… is probably these guys." So when "Josiah destroys the houses of the holy ones [*qedeshim*]… next to the temple," those may be "musical prophets who participate in temple worship as angels" whose form of worship the reform rejected.[^3] See [[The Kemarim Were the Melchizedek Priests Josiah Purged]].
4. **They became mere musicians after failed prophecy (rated: Johnson's reconstruction).** Johnson suggests the guilds "lost a lot of credit when they prophesied peace" before Babylon ("you say peace but there is no peace," Jeremiah); after the city fell "no one saw them as prophets anymore" and they survived as musicians only.[^4]
5. **Prophetesses too (rated: Johnson).** Johnson treats Miriam, Deborah, Hannah, and Huldah as cultic prophetesses whose songs (Exodus 15; Judges 5; 1 Samuel 2) fit the same pattern — "these seem to have been men and also women."[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** "Cultic prophet" is a recognized category in the cult-functional school, and temple singers prophesying is plainly in Chronicles — but identifying the *qedeshim* Josiah removed (2 Kings 23:7, traditionally rendered "male cult prostitutes") with these *singing prophets* is a speculative, minority move (the hosts grant it is "a big maybe"). Many scholars read the Chronicler's portrait as a post-exilic retrojection of the Second-Temple musical establishment back onto David, which would weaken the "old religion" claim. The singers are textual; the angel-priest identification is conjecture.

## Best Quotes
> "David appoints prophetic musicians into the temple… who should prophesy with harps — and that is in fact the verb *naba*. They are called *neviim*, they're prophets, prophets with salaries."[^1]

> "When Josiah destroys the houses of the holy ones, which are next to the temple… maybe those are musical prophets who participate in temple worship as angels."[^3]

## LDS Connection
Singing, prophesying temple personnel — later reduced to "just musicians" by the reform — fit the recurring vault thesis that the first temple held offices and practices the Deuteronomists stripped out (cf. [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]], [[The Kemarim Were the Melchizedek Priests Josiah Purged]]). The hosts also use it to normalize liturgical music (the Catholic/Orthodox cantor) as an ancient temple inheritance rather than an oddity.
