# Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy

## The Point
[[T. Benjamin Spackman]] argues that Latter-day Saints "get this wrong" when they defend the temple by hunting for a coherent ancient ceremony to match the modern one one-to-one. The temple *is* a restoration — but one saturated with *adaptation*, *accommodation*, and *progressive revelation*. Looking for a single rite to "pick up from the year 30 and dump into 1830" misunderstands how God works: He meets people in their culture and language, so the right search is for corresponding *parts*, not a transplanted whole.

## Evidence
1. **The brethren built in flexibility.** Standardizing the temple after Joseph Smith, 1800s leaders noted the endowment "has both Aaronic and Melchizedek portions" that "could split in half," with "obviously some flexibility in how this can be done." So one should look for "parts that we find together," "analogues" that "correspond well in context."
2. **Adaptation (transforming what is already there).** God takes existing cultural material and gives it new meaning: circumcision (Egyptian/Babylonian, made the sign of the covenant); Psalm 29 (adapted from Canaanite material to the God of Israel); Deuteronomy (structured like an Assyrian vassal treaty); the law of Moses (overlapping Hammurabi and Assyrian treaties); the tabernacle's Egyptian loan-words and an ark "a lot like the Egyptian solar boat." Spackman's image: at Cana "Jesus did not make wine out of nothing. He transformed water into wine."
3. **Accommodation (meeting us where we are).** Quoting Church materials: "God spoke to the ancient Israelites according to their ancient near eastern understanding... He comes down and meets us where we are. Which means sometimes even direct revelation will not be eternally ideal." In Genesis 1 God "deals with what knowledge they already have" rather than correcting their cosmology.
4. **Freemasonry as catalyst, not source.** Per the Church's own statement, "the endowment did not simply imitate the rituals of Freemasonry. Joseph's encounter with masonry evidently served as a catalyst for revelation." Even granting heavy borrowing, the temple "is still quite a different thing," and the strong ancient/modern dichotomy ignores elements "modified over time."

## Best Quotes
> "If you're looking to match a pattern, you have to make sure that the pattern you're trying to match is actually there... I think sometimes we've gone looking for the wrong thing." [^1]

> "The temple is a restoration, but it's a restoration that has a lot of adaptation and accommodation and progressive revelation and flexibility in it." [^2]

> "That wouldn't be very useful simply because the cultural meaning in 30 of an element is not the same as it would be understood in 1830. So accommodation... is a sign of God's mercy." [^3]
