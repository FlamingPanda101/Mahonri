# The Psalms Were Temple Altar-Liturgy Not Mere Poetry

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues from the **Psalm Scroll (11Q5)** — the great Psalms manuscript from Qumran Cave 11 — that the biblical Psalms were not a devotional poetry collection but **temple altar-liturgy**: prophetic, prayer-circle texts performed before the altar in connection with the daily burnt offering and the festival calendar. The key witness is 11Q5's prose coda ("David's Compositions"), which says David, given "a spirit of understanding and light," composed 3,600 psalms plus songs "to sing before the altar over the whole burnt offering for every day... and for the **Day of Atonement**." Barnes treats this as the Qumran community's own description of the Psalms' *function* — liturgical and sacrificial — and reads features like *Selah* as call-and-response performance cues, and Christ's cry from the cross ([[Matthew 27.46|Matthew 27:46]], quoting [[Psalm 22]]) as a high-priestly altar-prayer recited with raised hands. This is the framing claim under which the Day-of-Atonement and "worm of the destitute" arguments sit. See [[The Day of Atonement Was Edited Out of Deuteronomy and the Psalms]] and [[The Qumran Worm of the Destitute Preserves Prayer for the Dead]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. 11Q5's "David's Compositions" calls the psalms liturgical and prophetic
The prose coda of the Psalm Scroll explicitly frames David's songs as sung "before the altar over the whole burnt offering" on the daily and festival schedule, and as composed "through prophecy." This is an ancient source describing the Psalms' cultic, calendrical use, not a modern imposition.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong that 11Q5 says this; the leap from "sung at the altar" to a specifically *temple-endowment* liturgy is the interpretive layer.

### 2. The Psalms' own headings are cultic
Many canonical psalm titles carry liturgical/musical rubrics ("to the chief Musician," instrument and tune directions, "A Song of degrees"), independently supporting the view that the Psalter was performed temple material. This connects to the vault's note that the temple singers were prophetic figures. See [[Cultic Prophets Were Singing Temple Musicians]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong for cultic use generally (this is mainstream); the distinctively LDS gloss is the extension.

### 3. The crucifixion cry reads as an altar-prayer
The Gospels give Jesus' "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani" in Aramaic *and then translate it* — Barnes argues this signals a sacred, near-unutterable prayer rather than a casual quotation, and that quoting [[Psalm 22]] from the cross enacts the psalm as Day-of-Atonement altar-liturgy (cf. the sacred prayer too holy to record in [[3 Nephi 19|3 Nephi 17]]).
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the Aramaic-plus-translation feature is real and curious; the high-priestly-altar-prayer reading is plausible but is Barnes's construction.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) that the Psalms had **liturgical use** is uncontroversial mainstream scholarship and needs no LDS framing — temple performance does not imply an endowment, a prayer circle, or suppressed esoterica; (b) 11Q5 is a **sectarian** arrangement (with non-canonical compositions and an idiosyncratic order) reflecting the Qumran community's *theology of David*, so "David's Compositions" is evidence for how one late group conceived the Psalter, not proof of original temple practice; (c) *Selah* is of uncertain meaning and the call-and-response/prayer-circle reading is a guess dressed as data; and (d) reading [[Psalm 22]] on Jesus' lips as a staged high-priestly rite over-reads a cry of dereliction that the Gospel frames as genuine anguish fulfilling prophecy. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim does not require the endowment to be *in* 11Q5 — only that the Psalms were performative altar-liturgy, which 11Q5 and the psalm headings jointly support; the more specific readings (Selah, the cross-prayer) are offered as suggestive corroboration, and the Aramaic-with-translation oddity is a genuine textual datum the standard "anguished quotation" reading leaves unexplained.

## Best Quotes

> David "wrote 3,600 psalms; and songs to sing before the altar over the whole burnt offering every day, for all the days of the year... and for the Day of Atonement." (11Q5, "David's Compositions") [^1]

> The Psalms are "group prayers led before the altar" — liturgical, prophetic temple texts, "not a hymnbook." (Barnes) [^1]
