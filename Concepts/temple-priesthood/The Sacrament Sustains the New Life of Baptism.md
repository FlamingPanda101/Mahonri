# The Sacrament Sustains the New Life of Baptism

## The Claim
[[David Sword of Laben]] resolves a puzzle — why baptism (immersion in water) and the sacrament (eating bread, drinking a little water) are *such different symbols* if one is meant to recall the other. His answer: **baptism is a symbol of death and resurrection into a new life, and the sacrament sustains that new life.** Baptism is birth; the sacrament is the ongoing food and drink that keeps the reborn person spiritually alive, like manna and water in the wilderness.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Baptism = death and rebirth, not washing (strong, scriptural).** Romans 6:3-4 ("buried with him by baptism into death… walk in newness of life"), Colossians 2:12, and Mosiah 18 (which never mentions washing sins, only following Christ and bearing burdens) frame baptism as following Christ through death into new life — see the expanded [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]].[^1]
2. **The sacrament as sustenance (interpretive).** Just as Israel had "water come out of the rock and… manna and quail from heaven" to sustain them, "eating and drinking refers back to the death and resurrection because we resurrected into this new person and now we are spiritually sustaining that person." That is why the sacrament "looks so different" from baptism — it is not a mini-baptism but the *feeding* of the new life.[^1]
3. **Tree-of-life and partaking imagery (moderate).** The panel ties it to Lehi's tree of life: to "partake" (part + take) is to claim one's share of the fruit that nourishes; the sacrament table can be "envisioned as the tree of life," the bread of life of [[John 6.35|John 6:35]] that means the partaker "shall never hunger… never thirst."[^2]
4. **A pastoral reframe — the aid station, not the principal's office (moderate).** Because sacrament is sustenance, not a weekly verdict on sin, missing it is not "losing forgiveness for the week"; it is "a little aid station at a race… designed to help you get to the end," shifting the emblem's meaning from band-aid to fuel.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
A traditional reading (LDS and broader Christian) could object that the sacrament's *primary* stated meaning is *remembrance of Christ's body and blood* and *renewal of covenants* (Moroni 4-5; D&C 20:77-79), not "sustenance of the baptismal life" — David's framing is an attractive theological synthesis, but it is one valid layer among several, and he says so ("things can represent multiple things"). A critic could also note the death-resurrection reading leans on Romans/Colossians while down-weighting the explicit institution language ("this is my body… in remembrance of me"). The claim is best held as an enriching, multivalent reading, not a displacement of the covenant-renewal meaning.

## LDS Relevance
The framing gives Latter-day Saint families a positive, scripturally grounded way to teach both ordinances together: baptism as following Christ into newness of life, the sacrament as the food that sustains it — which the panel explicitly recommends as a home-centered lesson (read Romans 6, Colossians, Titus, Mosiah 18 in turn). It also pastorally counters the scrupulosity that treats a missed sacrament as lost forgiveness, reframing the emblem as nourishment for endurance "to the end of the race." See [[The Sacrament Sustains the New Life of Baptism|this note]]'s pairing with [[Baptism at 8 Is a Gift Not a Burden]].

## Best Quotes
> "Baptism is a symbol of death, following Christ through death and resurrection into a new life. And sacrament is the sustaining of that new life."[^1]

> "It's a little aid station at a race… it just gives you that little oomph to keep going… so that you can make it to the end of the race."[^3]
