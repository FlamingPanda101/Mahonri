# Free Will Is the Choice to Focus the Mind — Ayn Rands Account

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]] enlists [[Ayn Rand]] — "though I would
not agree with her on many things" — for a *positive* account of where free will
lives, answering the determinist's "where else could our choices come from if
they are not the result of prior causes?"[^1] Rand's answer is that free will is
not the (impossible) power to choose our knowledge or circumstances, but the
power to **direct the mind's focus**: "man's free choices come from focusing his
mind — whether to focus it or not, how much to, and what to focus on. Man can
keep his mind in full focus, drift away without focus, or avoid focusing and
refuse to think."[^2]

On this view, advocates of free will "do not argue there are no reasons which
motivate our actions," only that "we are free and in control of what facts and
ideas of the mind will focus on, which allows us to select what we think is
truth."[^3] Freedom is upstream of belief and action, located in the regulation
of attention and thought. Jones reinforces the point with Rand's social
corollary: "a social environment can neither force a man to think nor prevent
him from thinking. But a social environment can offer incentives or
impediments... it can encourage thinking and penalize evasion, or vice
versa."[^4] Circumstances raise or lower the cost of thinking; they do not make
the choice for us.

Jones endorses the *structure* of Rand's argument while rejecting her
metaphysics: "though Ayn Rand can make a good case for free will, her belief that
we can have free will in the realist objective world is false"[^5] — which is why
he turns next to quantum indeterminism (see
[[The Conway-Kochen Free Will Theorem - Indeterminism Preserves Freedom]]).

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Free will is relocated, not inflated.** Rand concedes everything the
   determinist says about *unchosen inputs* — genes, motives, available knowledge
   — and locates freedom in a narrower, defensible place: the act of focusing.
   "We obviously cannot make free choices on some knowledge we acquire, but our
   freedom is controlling what ideas and arguments to focus on."[^3] **Rating:
   strong as a dialectical move** — it dovetails with
   [[Maximal Autonomy Is Not Free Will - Plantingas Bait and Switch]] by refusing
   the maximal-autonomy bar and offering a minimal, lived locus of freedom
   instead.

2. **Reasons motivate without compelling.** Rand grants that reasons *incline*
   action yet denies they *necessitate* it, paralleling the agent-causal reply
   that "reasons incline without necessitating."[^3] **Rating: coherent** — it is
   the standard libertarian distinction, stated in the vocabulary of attention.

3. **The social-environment test.** Incentives and penalties shift the
   *difficulty* of thinking but cannot cross the line into *forcing* or
   *forbidding* it.[^4] **Rating: a useful diagnostic** — it predicts the lived
   datum that we hold people responsible for *failing to think* (evasion) even
   under pressure, which a pure determinism struggles to motivate.

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)

A determinist will say Rand has merely **pushed the problem back a step**. "Choosing
to focus" is itself a mental event; on a physicalist account it has prior neural
causes just like any other, so the act of focusing is no more free than the
beliefs it produces. Libet-style data, the determinist adds, suggest even the
initiation of attention has unconscious antecedents. Calling focus "the locus of
freedom" renames the explanandum without exempting it from the causal order: if
*whether you focus* is fixed by prior brain states, Rand's freedom is illusory at
exactly the point she places it. And Rand's own commitment to a **realist,
deterministic-friendly** physics gives her no resources to block this regress —
a weakness Jones himself flags.

## Counter-Objection

The steelman correctly exposes Rand's vulnerability *within her own naturalism* —
which is precisely why Jones treats her case as incomplete and supplements it.
Two replies follow. (1) The regress only bites if the focusing self is itself a
caused physical mechanism; if the agent is a non-reducible source of action
(agent causation), then "choosing to focus" is the primitive exercise of agency,
not a further event needing a prior cause — the same structure defended in
[[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]. (2) The Libet rejoinder
is itself contested: readiness-potential data are consistent with the agent
acting *through* the brain with a transmission lag, as argued in
[[The Libet Experiments Are What Dualism Predicts Not Evidence Against Free Will]]. The Latter-day-Saint frame supplies what Rand's realism cannot: the
focusing agent is an uncreated intelligence that "act[s] for itself" (D&C
93:29–30), so the regulation of attention is a real exercise of a fundamental
capacity rather than one more domino. Rand gets the *phenomenology* right
(freedom = directing the mind); the Restoration supplies the *metaphysics* that
keeps it from collapsing.
