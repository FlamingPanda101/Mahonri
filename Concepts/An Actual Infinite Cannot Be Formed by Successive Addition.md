# An Actual Infinite Cannot Be Formed by Successive Addition

## The Claim
This is the second of [[Al-Ghazali]]'s two philosophical arguments for the Kalam's premise that *the universe began to exist*, as presented by [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]. It is logically independent of the first ([[Hilbert's Hotel - Why an Actual Infinite Cannot Exist]]): even granting, for argument's sake, that an actual infinite *could* exist, it still could never be **formed by adding one member after another**. The series of past events is formed exactly that way — "an infinite series of past events has been formed by adding one event after another." But such a process can never *complete* an actual infinite. Therefore the past series of events cannot be infinite; the universe began.[^1]

Craig gives the argument two illustrations: a thought experiment about planetary orbits (which exposes an absurdity in a beginningless past) and the falling dominoes (which exposes the impossibility of *traversing* an infinite to reach the present).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The planetary-orbits absurdity (Rating: A — al-Ghazali's own illustration).** Suppose that for every one orbit Saturn completes, Jupiter completes two, and suppose both have been orbiting "from eternity past." Which has completed more orbits? "Strangely enough, the number of the orbits is exactly the same. Infinity." Yet this "seems absurd, for the longer they orbit, the greater the difference becomes."[^2] A beginningless past forces the conclusion that two series with a constantly *widening* gap have nonetheless completed an *equal* number — a result that discredits the assumption of an infinite past.
2. **The falling-dominoes impossibility (Rating: A — the core of the argument).** "If the universe has always existed, then an infinite series of past events has been formed by adding one event after another. It's like a sequence of dominoes falling one after another, until the last domino, today, is reached. The problem is that the final domino could never fall if an infinite number of dominoes had to fall first. So today could never be reached, but obviously, here we are. It's today."[^3] The present moment *is* reached; an infinite cannot be traversed by successive steps to reach a present; therefore the number of prior steps is finite.
3. **The conclusion about the past (Rating: A).** "So the number of events leading to today could not possibly be infinite."[^4] Joined to premise (1) of the Kalam — whatever begins to exist has a cause — this entails a cause of the universe (developed in [[The Cause of the Universe Must Be Personal, Timeless, and Spaceless]]).
4. **Hilbert's summary verdict (Rating: B — supporting authority).** The same line Craig uses to close the actual-infinite case applies here: "The infinite is nowhere to be found in reality... The role that remains for the infinite to play is solely that of an idea."[^5] The infinite is a coherent *concept*, but it is never *built* in the concrete world by successive addition.
5. **Convergence with the scientific case (Rating: B).** This philosophical result reaches the same destination as the physics in [[Scientific Evidence That the Universe Began (Thermodynamics and the Big Bang)]] and the [[Borde-Guth-Vilenkin Theorem - Any Expanding Universe Had a Beginning]] — two independent roads (a priori metaphysics and a posteriori cosmology) to a finite past.

## Best Quotes

> "The final domino could never fall if an infinite number of dominoes had to fall first. So today could never be reached, but obviously, here we are. It's today. So the number of events leading to today could not possibly be infinite." [^3]

## Steelman the Critic
The strongest reply is the **no-starting-point rejoinder** associated with critics of the Kalam: the domino picture allegedly smuggles in a *beginning* from which the traversal must "get all the way" to today, but a *beginningless* past has no first domino and no point from which a "completion" is attempted — for *any* past event, only finitely many events separate it from now, and that is all the present's arrival requires. On this view "you can't traverse an infinite" equivocates between *completing an infinite from a starting point* (impossible, granted) and *an infinite having already elapsed with no start* (allegedly unproblematic). A defender of the argument answers that this merely relocates the difficulty: at *every* past moment the same infinite already had to have elapsed, so the puzzle of "how was an endless series gotten through?" recurs at each stage and is never discharged; and the planetary-orbits result (Evidence 1) shows the beginningless picture yields *positively absurd* equalities, not merely counterintuitive ones, independent of any "starting point." A second, related objection notes that a *potential* infinite (the past growing without an actual-infinite *total*) is unobjectionable — but Craig's claim is precisely that a *real, completed* past, if beginningless, would be an *actual* infinite formed by addition, which is the thing alleged to be impossible. Honest verdict: the successive-addition argument is widely regarded as the more contested of al-Ghazali's two arguments (the "started vs. beginningless" disanalogy has serious defenders), which is exactly why Craig runs it *alongside* the actual-infinite argument rather than relying on either alone.

## LDS Connection
Like its companion, this argument is an *ally against atheism* and carries no commitment to creation *ex nihilo*: what it establishes is a **finite past series of cosmic events** — a beginning of *this* organized order, congenial to Restoration cosmology and the Big Bang. The interpretive care-point is the same. LDS scripture teaches that intelligences and the elements are *eternal* and uncreated (D&C 93:29, 33), which is not the thesis that an *actual infinite of discrete past events has been successively completed*; it is the thesis that the *substrate* God organizes has no beginning of *being*. *Eternal* in the Restoration sense is closer to Hilbert's "idea" of endlessness as the *mode of existence* of intelligence/matter than to a tallied, traversed infinite of events (see [[Infinite and Eternal Are Not the Same - The Regress Objection Rebutted]] and [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). A Latter-day Saint can therefore press the domino argument against a beginningless *event-history* cosmos while affirming that God organizes eternally existing materials rather than originating being from non-being ([[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]).
