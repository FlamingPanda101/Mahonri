# Aaronic Priesthood Restoration

## The Claim

The Aaronic (lesser) priesthood was restored to Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery by the ministration of **John the Baptist**, conferred by the laying on of hands. In the apologetic use of this event (e.g., the George Q. Cannon discourse discussed on The Stick of Joseph), the restoration is offered as evidence of Joseph Smith's *sincerity and restraint*: despite earlier manifestations (the First Vision, Moroni), Joseph did not presume to baptize or officiate until priesthood authority was *explicitly conferred* by an authorized messenger — the Aaronic order by John the Baptist, and later the Melchizedek order by Peter, James, and John.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A theophany does not by itself confer authority to act.** Cannon's point, amplified by the hosts: visions and angelic visitations may *call* or *instruct*, but the power to administer saving ordinances must be *given* by one who holds it. Joseph's waiting for an explicit conferral is read as principled and "temperate, sober-minded" rather than opportunistic. See [[A Vision Does Not Confer Priesthood Authority]].

2. **It fits the dispensational pattern of keys.** The Aaronic restoration is one link in the chain by which priesthood keys are "the skeleton of all seven dispensations," running from Adam down to Joseph — each dispensation's head receiving keys from authorized predecessors. See [[Priesthood Keys Frame the Seven Dispensations]].

3. **The Aaronic/Melchizedek dualism maps the temple.** Aaron as *priest* and Melchizedek as *king* (Hebrew *melek-tsedeq*, "king of righteousness") correspond to the temple's language of priests/priestesses and kings/queens; the lesser priesthood restored by John the Baptist is the entry tier of that structure. See [[The Aaronic-Melchizedek Dualism Maps the Temple's Kings and Priests]].

## Steelman / Counter

The critic's reply: that Joseph waited for a conferral does not establish that the conferral was real — sincerity is not authenticity, and a restorationist *narrative* of angelic ordination cannot be independently corroborated. A defender concedes the event is not externally verifiable but argues the *logic* (authority must be conferred, not assumed) is itself a coherent and scripturally grounded principle, distinguishing Joseph's claim from mere self-appointment.
