# The Nicene Creed Was Left Ambiguous Unlike the Athanasian Creed

## The Claim
The guest argues for a sharp gradient of dogmatic rigidity among the creeds: the **Nicene Creed** was *deliberately* left ambiguous to accommodate diverse 4th-century opinion, whereas the **Athanasian Creed** and the **Westminster Confession** are rigidly dogmatic "litmus tests" that LDS theology rejects. This gradient is what makes it possible to affirm Nicaea while rejecting the others. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "left ambiguous" claim (the core)
"The Nicene Creed was specifically left ambiguous because there was so much diversity in thought of the fathers that were at that council. So — Nicene Creed not very dogmatic, as opposed to the Athanasian Creed, as opposed to the Westminster Confession." [^1] **Rating: partly defensible, partly overstated.** It is true the post-Nicene decades (325–381) saw enormous diversity and conflict (Arian, semi-Arian, Nicene parties), and the 325 wording left some terms underdetermined. But Nicaea was *not* irenically vague — it appended explicit anathemas against Arius, so "left ambiguous" understates its polemical precision.

### 2. Defining "dogma" (clarifying the stakes)
The guest defines a *dogma de fide*: "something you must profess[;] if you don't profess it, you're out of the faith." [^2] He contrasts Catholic/Protestant creedal dogmatism with LDS practice, where "the only dogmas we really have are the temple recommend and the baptismal interview questions" — pass those and "you can hold whatever beliefs you want outside ... and be in full communion with Salt Lake City." [^3] **Rating: an accurate description of the LDS orthopraxy-over-creed structure** (the temple recommend interview is the functional dogmatic gate), though it idealizes how much doctrinal latitude members actually have.

### 3. The "metaphysical dogmatics should not be a litmus test" thesis
"The metaphysical dogmatics, specifically of the Athanasian Creed ... should not be a litmus test to see if [you're a Christian]." [^4] This separates *propositional assent to metaphysics* from *Christian identity* — a move that lets the guest say LDS disagree with no Nicene content while rejecting creedalism as a salvation test. **Rating: a coherent ecclesiological position** shared by some non-creedal Protestants (e.g. the Anabaptist Bercot tradition he cites).

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A defender of the creeds would argue the Nicene/Athanasian distinction is real but does not help the LDS case: the Athanasian Creed and Westminster Confession largely *spell out* what Nicaea already implied (co-equality, one being, divine simplicity), so affirming Nicaea-325 while rejecting its later explications is to accept a premise and deny its conclusion. The "ambiguity" the guest exploits is the gap the later creeds were written to close *in the Nicene direction*, not away from it. Furthermore, treating creeds as non-binding "metaphysics" assumes a sharp doctrine/identity split the early church did not share — for them, confessing the right God *was* the boundary of the faith.
