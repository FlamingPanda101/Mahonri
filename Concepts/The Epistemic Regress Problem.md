# The Epistemic Regress Problem

## The Point

For any belief you take to be justified, you can be asked *why* you hold it; whatever reason you give, you can be asked for a reason for *that* reason, and so on. This regressive demand for "reasons for your reasons" is the **epistemic regress problem**. [[Kevin McCain]] presents it as a very old problem — traceable to Aristotle and used by the ancient skeptic [[Sextus Empiricus]] to argue for skepticism — but argues its most important role is *diagnostic*: it forces us to get clear about the **structure of epistemic justification**, i.e. how reasons must be related to one another and to our beliefs for those beliefs to count as justified.[^1][^2]

## Argument

1. **Take any justified belief and demand its reason (steelman of the skeptic's setup).** McCain illustrates with a child's endless "why?" to a parent, then transposes it onto belief-justification: you believe the president is at the White House; *why?* You saw it on the news; *why trust the news?* The news is usually accurate; *why think that?* — the same regressive questioning, now aimed at your reasons.[^3]

2. **The regress threatens to generalize to all beliefs.** Because the very same questioning can be applied to *any* belief whatsoever, the skeptic (e.g. Sextus Empiricus) infers that if the regress is a problem for one belief, it is a problem for all of them — yielding global skepticism.[^4]

3. **Only three structural options exist for a chain of reasons.** When you give a reason, then a reason for that reason, and so on, the chain can only (a) **end** at some stopping point; (b) **continue infinitely**, with a further reason for every reason; or (c) **circle back** on itself (your reason for P is Q, for Q is R, for R is P).[^5]

4. **The skeptic says none of the three can yield justification — so nothing is justified.** This is the skeptical conclusion. McCain notes that *most* epistemologists reject it, treating the trilemma instead as a map of the available theories of justification.[^6]

5. **The payoff is a taxonomy, not despair.** The three options correspond to the three main positive theories — [[Foundationalism Ends the Regress in Basic Beliefs|foundationalism]] (the chain ends), [[Infinitism Accepts an Endless Chain of Reasons|infinitism]] (it continues forever), and [[Coherentism Replaces the Chain with a Web of Beliefs|coherentism]] (the picture is reconceived as a web) — with skepticism as the fourth, negative option that denies any beliefs are justified.[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "The most important role of the regress problem is its function in helping us get clear about the structure of epistemic justification... how our reasons must be related to one another and to the things we believe in order for those beliefs to be justified."[^2]

> "This sort of regressive questioning where you give a reason and your interlocutor asks why do you have that reason or what's your reason for your reasons is the regress problem and this is something that we can apply to any belief that you have."[^4]

> "It seems like we have three options: one, this chain of reasons comes to an end... two, it continues infinitely... or three, this chain of reasons circles back on itself."[^5]
