# Regenerate Science That Does Not Explain Away

## The Point
Lewis does not end in despair, nor is he an enemy of science. His proposed remedy is a **"regenerate science"** — a renewed natural philosophy that studies and uses nature without "explaining it away," one that "would not do even to minerals and vegetables what modern science threatens to do to man himself."[^1] The disease he diagnoses is not science but **scientism**: the habit, useful as a temporary method, of suspending value-judgements and treating things "in terms of quantity," hardened into "a general attitude toward the world."[^2] A regenerate science would always remember that "the 'natural object' produced by analysis and abstraction is not reality but only a view," and would keep "correcting the abstraction."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward highlights Lewis's unusual diffidence here — "I hardly know what I am asking for" — a rare moment of hesitancy from an author of the "rhetoric of certitude."[^3] The cure, strikingly, is to come "from Science herself."[^4] Ward draws out the analytic-reduction problem: "when we understand a thing analytically and then dominate and use it for our own convenience, we... suspend our judgements of value about it, ignore its final cause (if any), and treat it in terms of quantity."[^2] This works wonderfully for some studies but, generalized, breeds "the illusion... that people are therefore free to make of the world anything that fits their purpose" (Wesley Kort, quoted by Ward).[^2] Lewis insists the reductive analyst forgets that "the object, stripped of its qualitative properties and reduced to mere quantity," is not "wholly real."[^5] Ward connects the remedy to Martin Buber's **I–Thou**: a regenerate science studying the "It" "would not lose what Martin Buber calls the Thou-situation."[^6] He notes the term "regenerate" carries an unmistakably theological, "born again" connotation (John 3:3-8), even though Lewis's argument is officially philosophical.[^7] John West, quoted by Ward, observes the remedy is finally too important to be left to scientists alone — "it requires the cooperation of both scientists and non-scientists" to defeat scientism.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "The regenerate science which I have in mind would not do even to minerals and vegetables what modern science threatens to do to man himself." (Lewis) [^1]

> "Is it, then, possible to imagine a new Natural Philosophy, continually conscious that the 'natural object' produced by analysis and abstraction is not reality but only a view, and always correcting the abstraction? I hardly know what I am asking for." (Lewis) [^3]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's call for a science that perceives the "Thou" in creation, never reducing the world to "mere quantity," harmonizes with the Latter-day Saint conviction that "all things... are created and made to bear record of me" (Moses 6:63) — that the natural order is sacramental, not inert raw material. The Restoration explicitly refuses any war between truth discovered by study and truth received by faith: we are to seek learning "by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118), and "the glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36). A "regenerate" knowing — knowing that does not "explain away" the soul — fits the scriptural promise that the Spirit leads into *all* truth, material and moral alike. Against [[Science Can Determine Human Values]], which would let empirical method alone settle questions of value, Lewis's regenerate science keeps the moral and the measurable distinct yet both real — much as LDS thought distinguishes eternal law from the things it governs without collapsing one into the other.
