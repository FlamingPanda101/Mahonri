# Canon Began as a Rule of Faith Not a List of Books

## The Point
In the second and third centuries the idea of a *canon* was first a **rule of faith** (Latin *regula fidei*) — a body of authoritative **teaching** about God, Christ, and the Spirit — not a fixed list of books. As [[Thomas A. Wayment]] puts it, "the idea of canonical teachings developed before an actual list of books was compiled." Belief derived from authoritative teaching was more important than a list of authoritative books; the list-making came later (4th c.) and was a *consequence* of defending the rule of faith.

## Evidence

### 1. The word "canon" originally meant a measured boundary
Paul uses Greek *kanōn* (κανών) in 2 Corinthians 10:13–16 to mean a measured, bordered space — the limits of his own missionary territory ("we will observe the limits God has appointed"). The earliest canonical idea is thus a **boundary of authority**, physical and spiritual, not a table of contents. [^1]

### 2. Clement's "famous and revered canon" was handed-down teaching, not a book-list
*1 Clement* (c. AD 95–96) counsels the Corinthians to "walk in the famous and revered canon that has been handed down to us." Wayment draws two guidelines from this: (a) the canon was *famous* — recognized by the widest body of believers; (b) it had been *handed down*, establishing a genealogy of authority back to earlier reputable Christians. It functioned to call people to repentance, exactly as the prophets had. [^2]

### 3. Tertullian's threefold hierarchy: scripture, nature, discipline
Tertullian (early 3rd c.) argued the defense of Christian opinion rests on "scripture, nature, and discipline … Scripture is of God, nature is of God, and discipline is of God." Teachings (scripture) lead to action (discipline), and the rule of faith "the church handed down from the apostles, the apostles from Christ, and Christ from God." This moves governance away from spiritual manifestations toward consistent application of scriptural principle. [^3]

### 4. Irenaeus: the Church preserves one doctrine everywhere
Irenaeus (c. AD 130–202) made the Church the vehicle for preserving and handing down true teaching: "While scattered throughout the whole world, the Church … as if living in only one house carefully preserves it. She believes these points of doctrine as if she had only one soul." The institutional church had both the authority and the *means* (copying scriptures) to maintain global consistency. [^4]

### 5. The rule of faith is creed-shaped before there were creeds
Both Irenaeus (*Demonstration of the Apostolic Preaching*) and Tertullian (*Prescription against Heretics*) summarize the faith in three articles — Father (Creator), Son (the Word, made flesh), Holy Spirit (who inspired the prophets). Wayment notes one can "draw direct lines from it to the fourth-century Apostles' Creed." Its power was twofold: to bound those who would stand with the church and to argue for excluding those who could not accept it. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "In the second and third centuries the idea of canonical teachings developed before an actual list of books was compiled." [^4]

> "belief derived from authoritative teaching was more important than a list of authoritative books." [^2]

> "everyone of us walks in this rule, which the church handed down from the apostles, the apostles from Christ, and Christ from God" — Tertullian, *Prescription against Heretics* 37.1 [^3]

## LDS Connection
Wayment frames this for [[Latter-day Saints]], who hold a larger and *open* canon and "stand outside" the shared closed-canon tradition. Understanding that canon was first authoritative teaching, not a book-list, reframes [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon|how authority precedes the list]] — an open canon keeps the locus of authority in living prophets rather than a closed deposit.
