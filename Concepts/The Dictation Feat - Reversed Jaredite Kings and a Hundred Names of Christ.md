# The Dictation Feat - Reversed Jaredite Kings and a Hundred Names of Christ

## The Point
Multiple witnesses (including Emma Smith) report that Joseph Smith dictated the Book of Mormon with no manuscript, notes, or source text in front of him, picking up after meals and interruptions exactly where he left off. Fife argues that, granting those conditions, several internal features of the text are extraordinarily hard to explain as off-the-cuff invention: a list of thirty-odd Jaredite kings given in one order and then unwound in *reverse* order across several later chapters; over a hundred distinct names and titles for Christ woven throughout; and a non-biblical prophet (Zenos) whose lost olive-tree allegory turns out to have a striking ancient analogue.

## Evidence
- **No source text during dictation.** Emma Smith: "After meals, or after interruptions, [Joseph] would at once begin where he had left off, without either seeing the manuscript or having any portion of it read to him. This was a usual thing for him to do." [^1]
- **The reversed king-list.** The Book of Ether names roughly thirty Jaredite kings in genealogical order (Ether 1) and then narrates each of them again in *reverse* sequence across Ether 6-11 — a bookkeeping feat that would be remarkable for someone improvising aloud [^1].
- **~100 names for Christ.** Fife reproduces Susan Black's 1978 *Ensign* tabulation of at least 100 distinct names and titles for Jesus Christ in the Book of Mormon (e.g., "Holy One of Israel" 40×, "Lamb of God" 35×, "Lord of the Vineyard" 33×, down to single-instance titles like "True Vine" and "Wonderful"), woven naturally into the narrative [^2].
- **Zenos and the olive-tree allegory.** Jacob 5 quotes a long, horticulturally detailed olive-tree allegory attributed to a non-biblical prophet, Zenos — for which early critics mocked Joseph Smith. In 1887 scholars recovered the *Apocalypse of Peter*, in which the Lord interprets a fig/fruit-tree parable as the house of Israel, with a gardener pleading to dig, weed, and water the tree before it is uprooted — a close structural echo of Jacob 5 [^3]. (Compare the temple-metaphor reading of the same fig-tree imagery in [[Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor for Temple]].)

Fife frames all of this under his recurring rhetorical question — "How did Joseph Smith know?" — and concedes no single item proves divine origin, but argues the cumulative weight is what critics avoid (see also [[The Ethan and Ether Parallel Roberts Rejected]]).

## Best Quotes
> "How did Joseph Smith remember a list of 30 Jaredite Kings/names in the Book of Ether (chapter 1) and then remember to discuss each king/name in reverse order in Ether 6-11?" [^1]

> "No other author in the Bible references the olive tree allegory or the prophet Zenos. Interestingly though, in 1887, scholars discovered an early Christian apocryphal writing called the 'Apocalypse of Peter.'" [^3]
