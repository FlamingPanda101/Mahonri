# Heiser Species-Uniqueness Is an Anachronistic Metaphysical Distinction

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Michael Heiser]]'s influential claim, deployed by Trinitarians: yes, there are multiple *elohim* (beings of the spiritual realm — angels, demons), and even humans can be loosely called *elohim*, but among them **Yahweh is "species unique"** — ontologically unlike anything else; "nothing in creation is like him." So the divine council does not put other beings on Yahweh's level. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "species-unique" distinction is Heiser's contribution, never made in scripture (rating: strong; textual)
[[Blake Ostler]] argues the metaphysical reading "simply won't hold up." Heiser converts a distinction of *greatness and honor* into a *metaphysical* distinction the text never makes. "The claim is never made in scripture and in fact is clearly and powerfully counter-indicated." [^2]

### 2. *Elohim* is used of plain humans (rating: strong; lexical)
The term applies to David, Moses, and the judges of Israel — all called *elohim* — so Heiser's claim that *elohim* marks "where a being is" (the spiritual realm) is, per Ostler, "bogus... not accurate and doesn't reflect the reality of the text." See [[Deceased Humans Called Elohim]], [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]] (for the contrasting view). [^3]

### 3. *Bene Elohim* = genetic likeness (rating: strong; lexical/ANE)
The OT term for council members is *Ben Elohim*, "sons of God." Ostler: "If calling the gods sons of gods isn't enough to show a genetic likeness, what would possibly do the trick?" In the ANE, sonship language denotes a *genetic resemblance*, the opposite of species-uniqueness. [^4]

> "The term used for the Elohim in the Old Testament is Ben Elohim. That means sons of God. If calling the gods sons of gods isn't enough to show a genetic likeness, what would possibly do the trick?" [^4]

### 4. It smuggles in metaphysical monotheism (rating: moderate; methodological)
Ostler's deeper charge: Heiser (an evangelical scholar) wants "to drag into this the assumptions of metaphysical monotheism — that God is ontologically unique" — even though Genesis 1's "let us create" addresses a council that creates with him. The move "begs the question" by assuming the very ontological uniqueness it claims to find. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Heiser's strongest case is that the Hebrew Bible does mark Yahweh off from the *bene elohim* in striking ways: he alone is uncreated and worshiped, the council derives existence from him, and "sonship" in royal-adoption idiom need not entail *ontological* co-equality (Israel's king is "son" without being divine in kind). Ostler grants honor-language but insists that without an explicit ontological claim in the text, "species unique" is eisegesis; he treats the burden as resting on the one positing a metaphysical category the writers lacked. Note Ostler speaks respectfully of Heiser ("a good scholar... may he rest in peace"). [^2]

## Best Quotes

> "Except for the distinction that Heiser makes as a metaphysical distinction, calling him species unique, not the distinction that is actually being made of greatness and honor. This functions in a very different thought world than Heiser wanted to place it." [^1]

> "this saying that Yahweh is species unique is his contribution. The claim is never made in scripture and in fact is clearly and powerfully counter-indicated by the use of the term Ben Elohim." [^2]

> "what he's doing is simply ignoring the thought world and the entire Near-eastern background of the way that the term actually was used and functions in the scripture." [^4]
