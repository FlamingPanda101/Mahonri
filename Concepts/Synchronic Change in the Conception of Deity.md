# Synchronic Change in the Conception of Deity

## The Claim
The conceptualization of deity (the meaning and use of *elohim*, *eloah*, *el*) **changes substantially across the ~1,000-year span of the Hebrew Bible**. Treating all uses as one stable concept ("univocality") flattens this development and lets late, post-exilic ideologies be read back into the earliest layers. This is the methodological fault line between McClellan (and the majority of Hebrew Bible scholars) and [[Michael Heiser]]. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Majority of Hebrew Bible scholars hold the developmental view (rating: strong; consensus claim)
McClellan: Heiser holds that the conception of deity "doesn't really change throughout the Hebrew Bible," but "the majority of Hebrew Bible scholars myself included are of the opinion that the conceptualization of deity changes quite a bit." [^1]

> "Mike is of the position that the conceptualization of deity doesn't really change throughout the Hebrew Bible and the majority of Hebrew Bible scholars myself included are of the opinion that the conceptualization of deity changes quite a bit" [^1]

### 2. Univocality smuggles in late ideology (rating: strong; methodological)
Reducing *elohim* to "idols-that-aren't-real or angels/demons" presupposes univocality and "flattens the synchronic change," importing frameworks McClellan dates to **post-exilic Persian-period and Greco-Roman-period ideological innovation**. [^2]

> "the notion that uses of the Hebrew term Elohim are one of these two things I would suggest presupposes univocality and kind of flattens the synchronic change that we see evid throughout the Hebrew Bible" [^2]

### 3. Backed by McClellan's own cognitive-linguistic scholarship (rating: strong; primary, open-access)
He grounds the developmental thesis in three works on his Linktree: the Trinity Western M.A. thesis *You Will Be Like the Gods* (cognitive linguistics on *elohim/eloah/el*); the University of Exeter doctoral dissertation *Deity and Divine Agency in the Hebrew Bible in Cognitive Perspective* (cognitive science of religion); and the monograph *YHWH's Divine Images: A Cognitive Approach*. [^3]

### 4. *Elohim* has multiple referents beyond gods and idols (rating: strong; lexical)
The word covers gods, the deceased (see [[Deceased Humans Called Elohim]] — Samuel called *elohim* by the witch of Endor), and even divine attributes — itself evidence the concept is not a single fixed category. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Heiser's position (which McClellan represents fairly) is that the biblical conception of deity is essentially *stable* — Yahweh always uniquely uncreated and supreme — and that the developmental account over-reads ordinary rhetorical variation as conceptual change, projecting an evolutionary scheme onto the text. McClellan answers that the developmental reading is what the cognitive-linguistic data actually support and that the stability thesis can only be maintained by presupposing the very univocality at issue (the dogma is "read into the text" not "detected in the text"). [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "here we're getting to the part of the argument where we're flattening the synchronic change that occurs throughout the Hebrew Bible over this course of like a thousand years" [^6]

> "is presupposing univocality is presupposing a lot of dogmas that can only be read into the text and cannot be critically identified or detected in the text" [^5]
