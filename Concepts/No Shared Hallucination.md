# No Shared Hallucination

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The witnesses were sincere but hallucinating — their minds manufactured the angel, the plates, or both.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. There is no documented case of a *shared* hallucination
Peterson borrows an argument from an evangelical physician writing about the resurrection appearances of Christ: across the medical literature, there is no case of multiple people hallucinating the *same* content at the same time.[^2] Hallucinations are individual — products of each person's distinct psyche and memory.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a clinical generalization, used cross-traditionally; the same move defends the resurrection appearances).

### 2. Shared *content* is psychologically implausible
Even if a shared trigger existed (a gas in the room, common food), what each mind produces "out of the reserves of their minds" would differ from person to person — "it's just not going to be the same for this man, the lawyer, as for that illiterate maid."[^3] Peterson likens it to asking an image generator for the same picture repeatedly: it diverges every time.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the individuality of hallucination is the crux).

### 3. The descriptions are too specific to be coincidental hallucination
Witnesses converge on the same fine detail: three rings, the feel of thick book-like pages along the edge, the metallic sound of leaves scraping together. "That's awfully specific for a hallucination."[^5] See [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]] for the parallel checklist argument.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (specificity + convergence resists the hallucination model).

### 4. The eight's experience has nothing to hallucinate
A daylight hefting of an ordinary metal object in a clearing offers no fantastical content for a hallucination to generate — "if I took eight friends out into the woods and handed around some lead pot, would anybody afterward say they were hallucinating?"[^6] See [[Three vs Eight Witnesses Different Kinds of Experience]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (deflates hallucination specifically for the eight).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The most serious counter: "shared hallucination" is a straw target — a sophisticated skeptic invokes *shared expectation plus memory conformity*, where a religiously charged group setting, suggestion, and decades of retelling converge accounts onto a common script (the rings, the weight) without any single simultaneous hallucination. The plate descriptions could be later-standardized through repeated communal narration. Peterson's reply leans on the *eight's* mundane, tactile, contemporaneous experience (hard to standardize away) and on the absence of any divergence one would expect from independent fabrications. The believer should concede that "no shared hallucination" rebuts a narrow version of the objection, not the broader social-memory version.

## Best Quotes

> "In all the literature, I cannot find a case of a shared hallucination — people hallucinating at the same time."[^2]

> "It should be kind of like telling ChatGPT to make an image of something — it's going to be drastically different every time."[^4]

> "The descriptions of the plates are pretty specific. They have the three rings and they feel along the edge like the thick pages of a book and they scrape together with a metallic sound. That's awfully specific for a hallucination."[^5]
