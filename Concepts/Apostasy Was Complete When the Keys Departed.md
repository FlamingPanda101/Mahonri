# Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed

## The Claim
[[Bruce R. McConkie]]'s argument: the Great Apostasy was complete not at Nicaea (325) but much earlier — when the **priesthood keys** departed. Once the keys went, there was no one left on earth authorized to confer the priesthood, so any subsequent ordinations were invalid. Authority requires *both* priesthood and keys.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The McConkie quotation (rating: strong; the source claim)
Day reads McConkie (from Tad Callister's book): the apostasy "was completed by the time the apostles quit ministering among men, coupled with the period... while there were still some legal administrators... It was complete when the keys went, because once the keys went then there was nobody on earth left to authorize somebody else to confer the priesthood. And it always takes two things — it takes priesthood and it takes keys." [^1]

### 2. The keys/priesthood distinction (rating: moderate; LDS systematics)
The argument rests on the LDS distinction between *priesthood* (the power) and *keys* (the authorization to direct its use). Even surviving ordained men could not validly ordain others once the directing keys were gone. This is the "early apostasy" view: off-track long before the creeds. [^2]

### 3. Day partially disagrees (rating: the source's internal tension)
Day "likes the quote" for stressing that keys and keyholders matter, but does not fully agree: if the keys were gone "it doesn't mean somebody still doesn't have authority and is following God" — [[Gregor McCarti]]'s gradualist counter. And what about [[John 21.24|John]], who (per LDS belief) never left the earth? "The turtles all the way down." See [[Donatism and the LDS Model of Priesthood Validity]]. [^3]

### 4. It dates the apostasy early (rating: moderate; periodization)
McConkie's real point, per Day: "the apostasy happened early... a few people after the apostles had authority, and then nothing" — well before 325 or the 381 reaffirmation of Nicaea. This complements the gradual-drift framing while pinpointing the authority-loss as the decisive moment. See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view (within LDS thought)
[[Gregor McCarti]] argues the opposite from inside the tradition: ordinations demonstrably continued, John never died, and the faith of post-apostolic Christians was not instantly "null and void" — so authority may have faded gradually rather than vanishing at a clean "keys departed" moment. Day finds both compelling and refuses to adjudicate ("doesn't matter what I say"), holding the strict-keys view and the gradualist view in tension.
