# Psalm 137 Babylonian Babies as Allegory for Impure Thoughts

## The Claim

The imprecatory close of [[Psalm 137.9|Psalm 137:9]] — "blessed are the ones who take your babies and dash them against the rocks" — is, on its plain sense, a post-exilic author "fantasizing... about killing Babylonian children." [^1] [[Origen]] recognized this as morally problematic and therefore refused the literal reading. Instead he allegorized it: "The babies of Babylon are your impure thoughts. And so you've got to take those thoughts and you got to dash them against the rock really, really hard." [^2] This is the show's flagship example of how the early church did *not* read scripture literally where the surface sense was immoral.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The plain sense is genuinely violent.** The text is "not poetry" in the sense of being harmless ornament — it expresses real exilic rage at Babylon (the imagery reflects ancient siege atrocity). McClellan reads it as the author "just so looking forward to it so much." [^1] Origen did not dispute the surface meaning; he judged it unworthy of God and so sought a deeper sense. **(Strong — the verse's brutality is the standard exegetical crux.)**
2. **Origen's move is method, not improvisation.** This exemplifies his ranking of senses: the surface sense is "the least important," and "what God is trying to communicate through this is something more moral... something more spiritual." [^3] The "Rock" is read as Christ, the standard patristic identification. See [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]]. **(Well-attested.)**
3. **It demonstrates the historical thesis.** If the most influential 3rd-century exegete openly allegorized a canonical psalm to escape its plain meaning, then "literal-sense-only" reading cannot be the historic Christian default. See [[Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention]]. **(Strong.)**
4. **Steelman.** A literalist could note that Psalms is poetry/lament and that *no* careful reader treats an imprecatory psalm as a divine command to commit infanticide — so this is a strawman of literalism. McClellan's reply (elsewhere in the episode) is that this is precisely the point: *everyone* concedes some texts can't be read flatly, which proves the real work is drawing the figurative/literal line — see [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]]. **(Fair caveat; the example functions historically rather than as a gotcha.)**
