# John Gave Christianity Its High Christology and Pre-existence

## The Claim
Without the Gospel of John, Christianity would likely not view Jesus as so fully divine. John is the text that "blows the whole cosmic cover off Jesus," giving Christians the tools to articulate Jesus' **pre-existence**, **divinity**, and a **proto-Trinitarian** father/son/spirit framework — the book the Trinitarian councils read most intensely.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The councils read John intensely.** When the councils defined the Trinity they were "reading John intensely and closely"; [[Athanasius]] and the Cappadocian Fathers, architects of Trinitarian doctrine, were "intense readers of John." *(Strong; reception-historical fact.)* [^1]
2. **John states the divinity the Synoptics only imply.** In Mark/Matthew/Luke Jesus looks like a special, even supernatural human (e.g. the Transfiguration); only John "straightforwardly" calls Jesus divine. *(Strong; comparative reading of the four gospels.)* [^2]
3. **John supplies pre-existence.** Echoing Genesis, John places Jesus "at the very beginning" ([[John 1.1|John 1:1]]) — ideas of pre-existence and divinity that, while glimpsed elsewhere (e.g. Philippians), John "brings all together." *(Strong; the prologue is the clearest NT pre-existence text.)* [^3]
4. **Proto-Trinitarian vocabulary flows from John.** John gives Christianity "a very proto-Trinitarian sense of a father, a son, and a spirit" related such that the Spirit is sent from the Son from the Father — "at the very least, the terminology we use flows out of the gospel." Note: Méndez holds John's own Christology is **subordinationist and pre-Nicene** — see [[John 1-1c Anarthrous Theos and Subordinate Divinity]]. *(Strong, with the careful caveat that "proto-" ≠ Nicene.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Defenders of an early high Christology (e.g. Bauckham, Hurtado) argue Jesus' divinity is not a Johannine *innovation* but already present in Paul (Philippians 2, 1 Cor 8:6) and early devotional practice decades before John — John makes explicit what the earliest church already confessed. On a traditional reading John 1:1 and 20:28 ("my Lord and my God") teach full deity, not a subordinate divinity, so John testifies to rather than invents Christ's divinity.

## Best Quotes
> "If you didn't have the Gospel of John, you would possibly not have a Christianity that views Jesus as much divine as Christianity will eventually do it." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^2]

> "John is the book that blows the whole cosmic cover off Jesus... The church fathers like Athanasius who are developing trinitarian doctrine, the Cappadocian fathers, they're intense readers of John." [^1]

> "It's only the Gospel of John that will tell you straightforwardly: Jesus is — and the best translation is — divine... that associates him frankly with a pre-existence." [^2]

> "John is going to give, I think, Christianity a very proto-trinitarian... sense of a father, a son, and a spirit... the terminology we use flows out of the gospel." [^4]
