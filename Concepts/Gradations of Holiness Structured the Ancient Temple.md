# Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple

## The Point
[[T. Benjamin Spackman]] explains that the ancient temple operated by "concentric gradations of holiness": the nearer one came to the Holy of Holies, the holier one had to be and the more access was restricted. Guardian figures and physical boundaries enforced these gradations on pain of death. Spackman draws a *functional* parallel — not a literal identity — to the modern temple recommend interview and the bishop as a "first line" guardian figure, reframing a practice critics call "culty" as the ancient logic of keeping unholy things out of holy places.

## Evidence
1. **Herod's dividing wall.** Spackman notes the New Testament uses two words rendered "temple": one for the whole complex (with the courts where Gentiles and animal-sellers could be) and one for the holy temple proper. A plaque in Greek at the inner wall warned that "any Gentile that crosses this line shall be responsible for his own death" — a Gentile could come close, "but you haven't made covenants let alone purity stuff."
2. **Sinai as a temple mountain.** The mountain is "symbolic of the temple... the meeting place between heaven and earth where humans ascend and gods descend." Moses warns the people to make a line and not cross it "or they will die," "because priests can cross that line, but regular Israelites can't" — the same gradation principle.
3. **Guardian figures across cultures.** Spackman would animate cherubim, Israelite doorkeepers, the Egyptian Sphinx, and Assyrian/Babylonian guardian figures, then "fade out to your bishop is the first line as a guardian figure." At the door, the actual guardians check "the tokens of your worthiness."
4. **Functional equivalence, not identity.** "I'm not claiming that the old people checking your recommend are like cherubim... but no flame. There's an analogy here. There's a functional equivalence. This is a holy place. You have to be sure to not let in unholy things into holy places."

## Best Quotes
> "Temple typology like concentric gradations of holiness where the closer you come to the holy of holies, the holier you have to be and the more restricted it is and the more requirements there are." [^1]

> "The closer you come to God's presence, the holier you have to be or you simply can't handle it." [^2]

> "You don't have to be perfect. You don't have to be sinless. But is your holiness and commitment sufficient to get you in the door?" [^3]

## LDS Connection
This recasts the recommend interview as "a legitimate time for self-reflection," "vulnerability," and "stifling of pride" rather than arbitrary gatekeeping — the modern instance of an ancient, cross-cultural pattern. It is the access logic that makes sense of [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]] (passing the guardian figures back into God's presence) and connects to entering the divine presence in [[Beatific Vision in the Israelite Temple]]; [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]] keeps the parallel functional rather than one-to-one.
