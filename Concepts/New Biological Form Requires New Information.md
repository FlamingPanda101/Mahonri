# New Biological Form Requires New Information

## The Point
The chain of reasoning is: a new form of life requires a new protein, a new protein requires a new gene, and a new gene means new **code** — digital information inscribed along the DNA molecule.[^1] This is the payoff of the molecular-biological revolution: just as giving a computer a new function requires writing new code, "you want to invent a new form of life, you've got to have code in the form of the information inscribed along the spine of the DNA molecule."[^2] Crucially, the 1957 *sequence hypothesis* showed the nucleotide bases function "like alphabetic characters in a written text or like the zeros and ones in a section of computer code" — their function comes not from chemistry but from their **specific arrangement** under an independent symbol convention (the genetic code).[^3] And in all uniform experience, information traces back to a mind.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The software analogy.** "If you want to give a computer a new function, write a new program... you've got to give it new code. And the big discovery of 20th-century biology... is the same thing is true in life."[^2]
2. **The sequence hypothesis (1957).** The information in DNA functions "like alphabetic characters in a written text or like the zeros and ones in a section of computer code... it wasn't their chemical properties that gave them their function but rather their specific arrangement in accord with an independent symbol convention."[^3] (Crick, who proposed it, "was a code breaker in World War II."[^5])
3. **Darwin's outdated cell.** In Darwin's day a cell could be imagined as "a little brick of jell-o" — undifferentiated and simple.[^6] In fact "the cell is an unbelievably complex bit of machinery, unfathomably complex," and every new look reveals "an additional layer of revolting complexity," so the explanatory goal is *receding*, not approaching.[^7]
4. **The inference to a mind.** "Whenever we find information and we trace it back to its ultimate source, we always come to a mind, not a material process."[^4] Since undirected mechanisms fail to supply the information (the search space is too vast), what we see in life "is evidence of the activity of a directing mind."[^8] See [[Information from a Mind - The DNA Code in Debate]] and [[DNA as Digital Code - The Information Enigma]].

## LDS Connection
The information argument is a congenial natural-theology resource for Latter-day Saints, who affirm a designed and ordered cosmos and a Creator who *organizes*. The fit is broad rather than doctrine-specific: the inference lands on a generic "directing mind," not on creation *ex nihilo* (which LDS theology rejects in favor of God organizing eternal, uncreated matter and intelligences). An LDS reader can also affirm the provisional, evidence-based posture Meyer takes — holding the inference open to a genuine naturalistic account — as consonant with a faith that prizes both study and revelation (D&C 88:118). The point is the inadequacy of *unguided* mechanisms to generate new information, not any claim about the age of the earth. See [[Information Only Comes from Minds - Turek]].
