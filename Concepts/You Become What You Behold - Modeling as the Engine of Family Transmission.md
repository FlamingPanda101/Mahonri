# You Become What You Behold - Modeling as the Engine of Family Transmission

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] argues that family patterns transmit across generations chiefly by **modeling**: "you become what you behold… whatever it is you're looking at, you're going to become like that."[^1] Children absorb the relational template they witness, so the way out of a destructive pattern is not willpower but *beholding a different model* — and, ideally, being discipled by people who live the life you want.

The claim has a descriptive and a prescriptive half. **Descriptively**, the patterns you watch become your default: children raised by single mothers are "much more likely to also become a single mother"; "you're 45% more likely to get divorced yourself when you grow up" if you are a child of divorce. This is not fate — "any child is [not] doomed" — but "you're going to have additional challenges on the road ahead."[^1] **Prescriptively**, escaping the pattern requires a substitute model: "you have to behold something else… watch somebody else live the way you want to live and pattern yourself after them."[^2] Faust illustrates with her own marriage: she and her husband are both children of divorce (his parents' was high-conflict), and they broke the pattern because "every church that we went to, there was a couple or two with healthy marriages… they let us behold them… they coached us" — "we got discipled in a whole new way of doing marriage and family," now 27 years in.[^2]

[[John Anderson (media)|John Anderson]] supplies the corollary for *absent* models, especially for boys: a son with no father (or, in Anderson's case, no mother) cannot watch how a man should "respect and honor… the number one woman in his life." "Boys especially… look to models. They copy. So they copy a good dad or they copy a bad dad… If there's no male to copy… they'll join a gang. You find somebody to follow. Everybody's going to follow somebody."[^3] Faust closes the loop: "that actually is one of the reasons why God ensured that children always had a male and female parent" — and, absent that, why public models matter (she and Anderson discuss [[Jordan Peterson]] as a responsible "uncle figure" set against Andrew Tate).[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The intergenerational-transmission findings are well-supported (rate: strong).** Social-learning theory (Bandura) and a large family-sociology literature do find that divorce, single-parenthood, and relationship styles show measurable intergenerational correlation; a child of divorce is statistically more likely to divorce. Faust states this with appropriate hedging ("additional challenges," "not doomed"), which matches the data better than a deterministic claim.[^1]
2. **The "behold a substitute" prescription is constructive and falsifiable (rate: moderate, plausible).** The idea that mentoring and exposure to healthy-marriage models can buffer intergenerational risk is supported by research on mentorship and on marriage-education programs, and Faust's own case is a vivid (if anecdotal) instance. It also gives the argument a *hopeful, agentive* edge rather than fatalism.[^2]
3. **The absent-male-model point connects to real findings on fatherlessness (rate: moderate).** Anderson's "they'll join a gang" is rhetorically sharp, but the underlying correlation between father-absence and adverse outcomes for boys (delinquency, etc.) is documented, even if causation is entangled with poverty and other factors.[^3]

### Steelman of the progressive view

A fair response: (a) **Correlation is not destiny, and the framing risks stigma.** Telling children of single mothers they carry a 45%-style deficit can become a self-fulfilling stereotype and stigmatizes families that are doing fine; many children of divorce or single parents form stable, loving marriages, and resilience research stresses protective factors beyond two-biological-parent structure. (b) **The transmitted variable may be conflict/instability/poverty, not structure.** "You become what you behold" could just as well mean children replicate *conflict* or *economic stress* — which can exist inside intact marriages and be absent from amicable single-parent or same-sex homes — so the lesson may argue for *low conflict and stability*, not specifically for the mother-father form. (c) **Models need not be biological parents.** Faust's own remedy — being discipled by *other* couples — concedes that the crucial modeling can come from non-parents, which undercuts the claim that the *child's own* mother and father are irreplaceable as models; mentors, step-parents, grandparents, teachers, and same-sex parents can all model healthy relationship. (d) **"God ensured a male and female parent" is a theological premise, not a finding.** The social-learning data show children benefit from good models; they do not by themselves establish that the models must be one man and one woman. Faust's reply is that the *statistical* pull of beholding is real and that intact, low-conflict, two-biological-parent homes are where stable models most reliably appear — while granting that substitute models can and do rescue children who lack them.

## LDS Relevance

"You become what you behold" closely tracks the LDS emphasis on the **home as the primary place of formation** and on parents' duty to model and teach. *The Family: A Proclamation to the World* assigns parents the obligation "to rear their children in love and righteousness" and to be "responsible… to teach them to love and serve one another." The mechanism Faust describes — that children default to what they witness — is the same logic that underwrites [[Childhood Formation as a Universal Gift Not Mormon-Specific]] (every culture forms its young; the question is *toward what*) and complements [[The Law of Chastity as a Family-Stability Engine]] (covenant-keeping models reproduce stability downstream). The "behold a substitute / be discipled" remedy also resonates with the LDS ward as an extended family that supplies models and mentoring to those whose homes lacked them (cf. [[The Church as the New Family and Household of God]]). For a Latter-day Saint, the discipleship-by-imitation theme echoes Paul's "be ye followers of me, even as I also am of Christ" — formation by beholding Christ and those who follow Him.
