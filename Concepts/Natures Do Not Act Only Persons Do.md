# Natures Do Not Act Only Persons Do

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The two-natures doctrine (hypostatic union) resolves the contradictions in Christ's deity: Christ does X "in his human nature" but not "in his divine nature." So Jesus can be limited and unlimited, created and uncreated, ignorant and omniscient — at once, in one person.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. A nature is an abstract list of properties; it does not *do* anything
A nature is "merely a list of properties you have to have to be a [kind]." It is an abstraction; it cannot act until **instantiated in a person**. Persons act; natures don't. So "in his human nature he did X but in his divine nature he didn't" misassigns agency. *(Strong: tracks a real point in philosophy of action — natures are not agents.)* [^1]

### 2. The "Bob" reductio
Bob has a "brother nature" and a "son nature." If Bob eats pizza, it is incoherent to say "in his brotherly nature he's eating pizza but in his fatherly nature he's not." The *person* Bob eats; the natures don't partition his actions. By parity, "Jesus suffers in his human nature but not his divine nature" is equally incoherent. *(Strong as a reductio of the nature-as-agent move.)* [^2]

### 3. The two natures are mutually exclusive in one person
Classical theism makes divine and human natures contradictory: all-knowing vs limited in knowledge, uncreated vs created, cannot suffer vs suffers — and you cannot hold contradictory properties in the same person at the same time. Saying "you have a frog nature and a human nature simultaneously" is nonsense; your nature is *what you are*, not something you *possess* like a phone. Scripture itself ([[Hebrews 2.17|Hebrews 2:17]]) says Christ was made like his brethren "in all things" — biblically, not just philosophically, pressing for where the exempt "divine nature" is taught. *(Moderate-strong; the Chalcedonian reply is that the natures are united "without confusion," which is exactly what's contested — see steelman.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Avery's "smooth move" (credited on the stream): Christ does not *possess* a square and a circle; the right model is that the *person* of the Word, who is divine, *assumes* a complete human nature — so the one person genuinely lives a human life (suffers, grows in knowledge) through the human nature while remaining divine. Chalcedon affirms two natures "without confusion, change, division, or separation" in one *hypostasis*, and "communication of idioms" lets properties of each nature be predicated of the one person. Defenders (e.g. analytic Christologists like Thomas Morris's "two-minds" model) argue this is coherent: the *person* acts, with two ranges of capacities. The LDS rejoinder is that this still requires the one person to *be* both omniscient and ignorant, which no qualification dissolves — and that the move ultimately rests on "it's a mystery" (see [[Hypostatic Union]]).

## Best Quotes
> "A nature doesn't do anything. A person does, and those natures exist within a person." [^2]

> "Your nature is what you are. It's a description of that... no, you can either are a human or you are a frog. You can't be a human and a frog at the same time." [^1]

> "You can't have all knowledge and be limited in knowledge at the same time in the same person. You can't be uncreated and created at the same time in the same person." [^3]
