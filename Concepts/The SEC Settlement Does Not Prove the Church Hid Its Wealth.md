# The SEC Settlement Does Not Prove the Church Hid Its Wealth

## The Point
A common modern charge runs: "The SEC slapped a massive fine on the Church for breaking the law... Church leaders are trying to deceive its members about its wealth so that members continue to pay tithing." [[Austin Fife]] answers that the settlement is real but the sinister motive is invented. The method the Church and its investment arm Ensign Peak used — filing SEC Form 13F through shell entities to keep holdings private — **was legal in the 1990s**; the law later changed, the Church received "erroneous legal counsel" that it still complied, and when the SEC disagreed the matter was resolved in February 2023 with the appropriate fine paid. Critically, "The SEC order said the Church admitted no wrongdoing in agreeing to a settlement."[^1]

## Evidence
1. **A filing dispute is not proof of a cover-up of wealth.** Fife distinguishes the *fact* (a fine for how investments were reported) from the *narrative* (that leaders concealed wealth to keep tithing flowing). The first is conceded; the second is asserted without evidence — "their motivations beyond that are unknown."[^1] This is the same evidence-over-narrative discipline he applies throughout the letter: "Anything can sound evil when the least flattering versions of events are shared with ominous-sounding music playing in the background."[^2]

2. **The "greedy corporation" charge fails the follow-the-money test.** To the broader myth that "The Church is a greedy corporation," Fife's reply is a question: "What individuals would be enriched by the Church's money if this was true?"[^3] No leader personally pockets tithing — the same structural point made in [[Grift Does Not Track With Disbelief Rebuttal]], that financial self-enrichment does not track with the actual flow of money.

3. **The lay-ministry objection is answered numerically.** A related claim — "The Church claims to have a lay ministry, but the general authorities get paid" — gets Fife's concrete rebuttal: "99% of church leaders are unpaid. 100 or so church leaders out of our 17-million-member church receive a modest salary. If the Church received more or fewer tithing dollars, no church leader would directly benefit or lose out."[^4]

4. **Members still give generously beyond the Church.** Against "Church members only give money to the Church; they don't care about other causes," Fife notes that "after tithing and church callings, church members still donate as much, if not more, time and money to outside causes."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Their method of filing SEC Form 13F was legal in the 1990s. Laws changed, and the Church received erroneous legal counsel that they still complied with the law... The SEC order said the Church admitted no wrongdoing in agreeing to a settlement." [^1]

> "[The Church is a greedy corporation.] Not true. What individuals would be enriched by the Church's money if this was true?" [^3]

> "99% of church leaders are unpaid... If the Church received more or fewer tithing dollars, no church leader would directly benefit or lose out." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Tithing in the restored Church is administered by an unpaid lay ministry (Doctrine and Covenants 119–120 places its disbursement under a council, not an individual), and consecration aims at building the kingdom rather than enriching officers. Fife's reply reframes the SEC episode as a regulatory misstep corrected without admission of fraud, consistent with the broader pattern of holding leaders to reliability rather than [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught|an infallibility standard]] the Church never claimed.
