# Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics

## The Claim

Biblical inerrancy apologetics systematically commits the logical fallacy of **begging the question** (*petitio principii*): the conclusion that the Bible does not contradict itself is BUILT INTO the premise that supports the argument. When an apologist confronts two passages that appear to contradict, they do not ask "do these passages contradict?" — they ask "how can I reconcile these two passages, which cannot contradict?" The assumption of non-contradiction is never tested; it is presupposed. This makes the apologetic enterprise circular from the outset.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Argument Structure Makes It Explicit

McClellan identifies the circular structure clearly:

> "This is built on a foundation of the presupposition of univocality. In other words, it's begging the question because the conclusion is built into the premise that supports the argument. And the conclusion is that the Bible doesn't contradict itself." [^1]

The apologist's reasoning takes this form:
1. **Premise (unstated):** The Bible is univocal (cannot contradict itself)
2. **Observation:** Passage A and Passage B appear to conflict
3. **Conclusion:** Therefore, we have misunderstood them and need a harmonizing framework

But step 3 is only valid if step 1 is true — and step 1 is precisely what is in question. The apologist never asks "is step 1 correct?" — they assume it and proceed directly to harmonization.

### 2. Without the Presupposition, the Enterprise Collapses

> "In the absence of that presupposition of univocality, there is no need to create the separate third thing. We can allow the text to operate on their own terms. And when we do that, they contradict." [^2]

McClellan's point is methodological: if you drop the assumption of inerrancy, the texts are free to speak for themselves. When Genesis 6 says "two of every bird" and Genesis 7 says "seven pairs of every bird," the simplest reading is that these are two different traditions with different numbers. It is only the PRIOR COMMITMENT to non-contradiction that forces the invention of a harmonizing framework.

### 3. Apologists May Not Be Cognizant of the Circularity

McClellan allows that this may not be deliberate: "whether you are cognizant of it or not, intuitively, subconsciously, your mind is making you accept laughable arguments." [^3] The circular reasoning operates at the level of cognitive bias, not necessarily conscious deception. The apologist genuinely BELIEVES the Bible does not contradict itself, so any apparent contradiction MUST be resolvable — and any resolution, no matter how weak, will be accepted because the alternative (admitting contradiction) is unthinkable.

### 4. The Consequence: Accepting "Laughable Arguments"

> "You are being compelled to accept logic that is fallacious." [^3]

When the conclusion is pre-determined, the quality of the argument becomes irrelevant. The apologist does not evaluate harmonizations by whether they are good arguments — they evaluate them by whether they successfully remove the contradiction. A rationalization that "just doesn't fit" the text (the bird problem in Noah's ark) is accepted because the ALTERNATIVE — admitting contradiction — is psychologically impossible under the inerrancy presupposition.

### 5. Same Pattern in Both Examples

Both harmonizations in this video exhibit the same circular structure:
- **God's visibility:** "They saw limited essence / pre-incarnate Christ" — assumes John 1:18 and Genesis 32:30 must be describing compatible realities
- **Noah's ark:** "Two is baseline, seven pairs are extra" — assumes Genesis 6 and Genesis 7 must be consistent instructions

In both cases, dropping the assumption of univocality reveals the simpler explanation: the texts come from different theological stages or different literary sources.

## The Underlying Epistemology

McClellan's critique has an epistemological dimension. The apologetic method reverses the direction of inquiry:
- **Evidence-first approach:** Examine the texts → discover contradictions → conclude they reflect different sources/theologies
- **Apologetic approach:** Assume no contradictions → encounter apparent conflict → invent rationalization to preserve assumption

The apologetic approach is not "wrong" because the rationalizations happen to fail — it is METHODOLOGICALLY circular regardless of whether any given rationalization succeeds.
