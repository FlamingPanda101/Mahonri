# Rain Means Covenant Is a Localizing Eisegesis

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

After conceding (only in passing) that McClellan was right about the Hebrew of Genesis 2:5 — that *siach* and *eseb* together describe both wild and cultivated vegetation — The Word Room pivots to a new argument: that "rain" in the Hebrew Bible "always means covenant," that rain is "covenantally and agriculturally local" rather than a "global meteorological abstraction." Because the rain is covenantal and tied to Eden/Adam and Eve, the whole passage is a *leitmotif* cueing the reader that only Eden-relevant rain (and thus only Eden-relevant vegetation) is in view.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **It is a bait-and-switch from the original argument (rhetorical, strong).** McClellan documents that the apologist's *original Twitter claim* localized the passage via "the phrase **of the field**," not via rain. Having had the lexical claim refuted, they quietly drop it and substitute the rain-covenant argument without conceding the error: "they're going to acknowledge that the Hebrew is on my side here, but only in passing... and then just pivots right into this other argument."[^2]

2. **The causal clauses are not necessarily local (exegetical).** The apologist treats the two "because" clauses of 2:5 (no rain; no human to till) as locally scoped. But McClellan argues the text "does not require that"; nothing forces the rain/tilling explanations to be restricted rather than global mechanisms for the absence of *all* vegetation.[^3]

3. **The merism points global, not local (decisive).** Genesis 2:5 is "somewhat of a merism" — wild vegetation (gotten by rain) plus cultivated vegetation (gotten by human tilling), i.e., *all* vegetation. "It says there was neither of either type on the entire earth... widely agreed by the overwhelming majority of scholars to indicate there was no vegetation of any kind until after the human had been created."[^4] Reducing this to "only cultivated crops in Eden" both gets the Hebrew wrong and ignores half the verse.

4. **Rate the evidence: the rebuttal is strong; the "rain = covenant" claim is weak.** "Rain always means covenant" is an overgeneralization (rain in the Hebrew Bible carries many senses — judgment, blessing, ordinary meteorology); a lexeme having covenantal associations *somewhere* does not make it covenantal *here*. This is the same lexical-range-as-determination error McClellan flags elsewhere. See [[Eretz Defaults to Earth Without Contextual Markers]].

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Rain in Deuteronomy (e.g., 11:13–17, 28:12, 28:24) is genuinely covenant-conditioned — obedience brings rain, disobedience drought — so a defender argues Genesis 2:5's mention of "no rain from Yahweh" evokes that agricultural-covenantal frame and signals a bounded, cultivable land rather than the cosmos. McClellan's reply: even granting rich covenantal resonance, it cannot override the merism (which explicitly covers wild *and* cultivated vegetation) nor the global default of *eretz*, and it certainly cannot localize *shamayim* in 2:19.

## Best Quotes

> "They're going to acknowledge that the Hebrew is on my side here, but only in passing... and then just pivots right into this other argument where they say that when it talks about rain, that always means covenant in the Hebrew Bible."[^2]

> "Rain in the Hebrew Bible is not a global meteorological abstraction. It is covenantally and agriculturally local." (The Word Room, quoted by McClellan)[^1]

> "Genesis 2 is actually somewhat of a merorism. It's describing vegetation that grows wild and vegetation that requires cultivation. And it says there was neither of either type on the entire earth."[^4]
