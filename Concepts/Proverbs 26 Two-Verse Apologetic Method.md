# Proverbs 26 Two-Verse Apologetic Method

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] makes [[Proverbs 26.4-5|Proverbs 26:4-5]] the master text of the course ("if you don't remember anything else tonight, remember this scripture"). The famous paradox — v.4 "Answer not a fool according to his folly, lest you be like him" vs. v.5 "Answer a fool according to his folly, lest he be wise in his own eyes" — he reads as a **two-step apologetic directive**: **verse 4** = do *not grant* a critic's false presuppositions; **verse 5** = *do answer* by exposing the incoherence of those presuppositions and showing where they logically lead.[^1] (Note: Carroll repeatedly mislabels the reference as Proverbs 24:4-5 on the board and orally, but quotes and applies Proverbs 26:4-5.)[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The two-step reading fits the verses' surface logic — rate: moderate, homiletical
Carroll's gloss is a *legitimate application*, not the consensus exegesis. Verse 4 warns against adopting the fool's flawed frame (don't argue *on his terms*); verse 5 warns against leaving folly unchallenged (do *refute* him so he isn't confirmed in his error). Mapping v.4 → "don't accept the presupposition" and v.5 → "expose and refute it" is a coherent and rhetorically tidy use. **Scholarly context:** the rabbinic and standard reading treats the pair as *situational wisdom* — sometimes silence is best, sometimes refutation — with the canonical juxtaposition deliberately teaching discernment about *which* applies. The Talmud (b. Shabbat 30b) famously cites this pair as an example of seemingly contradictory verses reconciled by context (folly *about Torah* vs. *worldly* folly). Carroll's reading is narrower and more procedural than the text demands, but not contrary to it.

### 2. He gives concrete worked applications — rate: strong (as teaching)
Carroll applies it to the Trinity charge ("you deny the Trinity, therefore you're not a Christian"): v.4 = refuse the premise that Trinitarianism defines Christianity; v.5 = show why that premise is unjustified.[^3] He also extends it to everyday disputes (a spouse's accusation) to make the structure memorable.[^4] This concretizes the abstract presuppositional method into a scriptural mnemonic, which is the pedagogical payoff. See [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]].

### 3. Provenance of the reading — rate: noted
Carroll credits the application to "reformed Baptist Calvinist [[Daniel Constantino]]" (whom he says later left Christianity) and notes [[Jeff Durbin]] preaches the same passage against atheists.[^5] So the *hermeneutic* is borrowed from evangelical presuppositionalists and repurposed against them — consistent with the course's overall "turn the method back on them" strategy. **Steelman of the critic:** an evangelical could fairly object that proof-texting a Proverbs couplet as a debate manual is itself the kind of decontextualized reading Carroll elsewhere condemns; the verses are wisdom aphorisms, not an epistemology. Carroll partly pre-empts this by flagging "we could debate on what that means, but I'm going to give you the application."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Verse four is, do not grant false assumptions or presuppositions. Do not answer a fool according to his folly."[^7]

> "Verse five is don't let bad premises go unchallenged. Show why their assumptions or presuppositions are wrong... and show where they logically lead."[^8]

> "If you don't remember anything else tonight, remember this scripture."[^9]
