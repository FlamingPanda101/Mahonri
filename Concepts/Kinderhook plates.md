# Kinderhook plates

A set of six small, bell-shaped brass plates bound by a ring and covered with fake "inscriptions," forged in 1843 by [[Wilbur Fugate]], Robert Wiley, and the blacksmith Bridge Whitten near Kinderhook, Illinois (about 60–70 miles south of Nauvoo). The characters were *etched* with acid and beeswax (not engraved), the plates aged to look ancient, planted in a burial mound, and "discovered" before witnesses in May 1843 — a forgery designed to tempt [[Joseph Smith]] into translating them and producing a sequel to the Book of Mormon.[^1]

This note is the topic anchor for the Kinderhook material; the substantive arguments live in the linked concepts.

## What is actually in dispute
- **Was it an entrapment or a prank?** Fugate's later confession traces it to "a joke" inspired by [[Parley P. Pratt]]'s *Voice of Warning*; [[Don Bradley]] and [[Mark Asher McGee]] argue the long-assumed anti-Mormon malice is overstated.[^1]
- **Did Joseph "fall for it"?** The core apologetic: he used no revelatory instrument, "translated" at most a single character via secular tools, and produced no revelation. See [[Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation]].
- **What the one matched character was.** Bradley/McGee tie [[William Clayton]]'s secondhand "descendant of Ham through the loins of Pharaoh" line to a single glyph in the [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]].
- **The sincerity inferences.** That a forger would have spotted a rival forgery ([[A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax]]) and that Joseph sought outside authentication ([[Joseph Smith Sought Secular Authentication of Artifacts He Believed Ancient]]).

## Aftermath
The plates left Nauvoo and were never translated or acquired. Latter-day Saints (down to B. H. Roberts) cited them as authentic for decades, until Stanley B. Kimball's 1981 *Ensign* article — one of the first publications anywhere to call them a forgery — and later metallurgical testing of the lone surviving plate (19th-century brass) confirmed the fraud.[^2]
