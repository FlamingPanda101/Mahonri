# Goff - The Father Is Not a Mind, God Is the Only Non-Conscious Thing

#type/claim

## The Claim
Pressed by [[Jacob Hansen]] on whether God is a disembodied mind, [[Philip Goff]] gives a striking and self-described novel answer: the ultimate God — the Father, the unknowable source of existence — is **not a mind and not conscious at all**. "I guess I don't think ultimately God is a mind. God the Father, the ultimate source of existence, I think is not a mind, is beyond comprehension. Yes, Jesus is a mind, because Jesus was the coming together of that unknowable reality and the physical universe." He then draws the consequence aloud, noticing it for the first time: "Maybe God is the only thing that's not consciousness, because I think the whole of physical reality is pure consciousness as a panpsychist... I haven't really thought of that. No, God is the only thing I think is *not* consciousness."[^1] The inversion is total: for a panpsychist, *everything physical* is conscious, so the one reality that transcends the physical — God's ultimate essence — is the lone exception, a reality "beyond understanding" rather than a great cosmic mind.

## Supporting Evidence
- **It flows from Goff's mysticism, not his panpsychism.** Goff explicitly grounds the move in the **Eastern-Orthodox essence/energies** picture and the wider mystical traditions (Advaita Vedanta, Abrahamic mysticism): the *essence* of God is "totally beyond understanding," while the *energies* — God's manifestation, supremely in Jesus — are knowable and participable.[^2] So "God is not a mind" is not Goff conceding impersonalism by accident; it is his considered preference for an *apophatic* ultimate over the "big mind" of classical theism. He says the personal "big mind" idea is "a bit not very spiritual... I agree with the new atheists at this point — if we've just got this big mind, you've got the question, why does that big mind exist?"[^3]
- **It separates Goff's God sharply from the LDS Father.** This is where the kinship Hansen has been building **breaks**. Hansen's whole project is that God the Father is a *person* — "I can't follow, worship, or have a relationship with a metaphysical abstraction, but I can have a relationship with a being" — known concretely through the risen Christ ("my starting point... is that Jesus walked out of that tomb"). Goff's apophatic, non-conscious ultimate is exactly the "metaphysical abstraction" Hansen rejects. The disagreement is the same seam the vault tracks in [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]]: does "God" name the *impersonal terminus of being* (Goff) or the *supreme personal agent* (Hansen)?
- **It refines, rather than repeats, the Craig-vs-Goff panpsychism material.** In the [[Reasonable Faith - Does God Have Limited Power Craig vs Goff|Craig debate]] Goff defended a *cosmic conscious subject* and then retreated toward panentheism (see [[A Person Cannot Be a Part of Another Person (Craig vs Cosmic Panpsychism)]]). Here he goes further and clarifies the *theology* of that retreat: the cosmos is conscious, but **God-the-source is not** — Jesus alone is the mind, as the union of the non-conscious unknowable with the all-conscious physical. This is a genuinely new datum about Goff's positive view, not covered by the cosmic-panpsychism notes.
- **Steelman both sides.** *For Goff:* an apophatic ultimate dodges the regress objection he turns on classical theism ("why does the big mind exist?") and fits the cross-cultural testimony of mystical experience to *ineffability* better than a tidy personal mind does; calling the source "not consciousness" may simply mean it transcends the consciousness/non-consciousness distinction rather than falling below it. *For Hansen:* a non-conscious, non-personal ultimate cannot be the **Father** Jesus prays to, cannot **love**, and cannot stand in the covenant relationship the gospel is about — and Goff's own Christianity smuggles personality back in through Jesus while denying it to the source, raising the question of how a non-conscious essence "manifests" as a conscious person at all (cf. [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]] and the one-mind problem in [[One Mind Dilemma Against Two Natures]]).

## LDS Relevance
This note marks the **outer boundary of the Goff–LDS convergence**: ally on a *limited* God, ally on a *mind-suffused cosmos* (intelligences), but flat opponents on whether the ultimate God is a **person**. The Restoration's most non-negotiable claim about God's nature is that the Father is an embodied, conscious, personal being — the same *kind* as humanity (see [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]] and [[A Body from the Physical Creation Joined to a Premortal Spirit]]) — which is the precise inverse of Goff's "the Father is the *only* non-conscious thing." It also clarifies why the LDS reader can affirm panpsychism's instinct (consciousness is fundamental and uncreated — [[Consciousness Is Fundamental and Irreducible]]) while rejecting Goff's apophatic terminus: in LDS cosmology the most fundamental conscious realities are **intelligences**, and the supreme one *is* a personal God, not an unknowable non-mind behind him. Hansen's reply supplies the apologetic counter to every "ground of being" theology at once: a God who is not a conscious person cannot be the Father of Jesus Christ, so the figure who "walked out of that tomb" is the disconfirming datum against an impersonal absolute (see [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]] and [[The Source and Sustainer - Who the God of the Bible Actually Is]]).
