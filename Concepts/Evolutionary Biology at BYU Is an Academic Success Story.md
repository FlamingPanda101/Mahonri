# Evolutionary Biology at BYU Is an Academic Success Story

## The Point

A common assumption — even among Latter-day Saints — is that BYU either does not teach
evolution or teaches it half-heartedly, "tongue-in-cheek," merely for accreditation.
Michael F. Whiting, an entomologist, NSF program officer, and director of BYU's Bean
Life Science Museum, marshals the data to refute this: BYU not only teaches evolution,
it teaches it *better than most universities in the country* and has built a
nationally recognized, prolifically funded research program in the field — all done
"in the light of the gospel."[^1] The legitimacy of evolutionary science at a faithful
university is therefore not aspirational but a documented, decades-long achievement.

## Evidence

- **Over a century of continuous instruction.** Evolution "in some form" has been
  taught at BYU for well over a hundred years — back to the 1907–1911 hires of the
  Chamberlin and Peterson brothers (dismissed in 1911 for teaching evolution), through
  Vasco M. Tanner's 33-year chairmanship, to [[Duane Jeffrey]]'s formal evolution course
  approved by the Board of Trustees circa 1970. Since then BYU has *always* offered at
  least one formal undergraduate evolution course plus graduate courses.[^2]

- **Documented teaching excellence (1997–2020).** On BYU's standardized ETS Major Field
  Test in Biology, the BYU student average exceeds the national average by about one
  standard deviation, and roughly **92%** of BYU students score above the national
  average in any given year — consistently.[^3] Some measured outputs over those years:
  7,553 undergraduates through the 400-level evolution course, 1,040 undergraduates
  mentored in evolution research, 43 MS and 54 PhD degrees, 53 postdocs, and 934
  peer-reviewed publications (~4.7 per faculty per year, in journals up to *Nature*,
  *Science*, and *PNAS*).[^4]

- **Research funding with "no parallel" on campus.** From 1997–2020 BYU evolutionary
  biologists won 112 NSF grants (~$20.5M direct to BYU), including four prestigious
  *Assembling the Tree of Life* grants — more than Whiting is aware of at any other
  university. NSF program officers joked that a BYU proposal always arrived with a
  "giant whooshing sound" as the budget headed to Provo.[^5] The same molecular-
  systematics infrastructure (the BYU DNA Sequencing Center, biologist–computer-
  scientist collaborations) seeded BYU's nationally ranked **bioinformatics** major.[^6]

- **A real-world payoff.** The tools of evolutionary biology — phylogenetics, molecular
  systematics — were exactly what equipped science to track and respond to the
  SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19) pandemic, demonstrating the discipline's practical power.[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "Not only do we teach evolution at BYU, but... we do an excellent job of teaching evolution, that our students perform very well, that we have developed a research program that is on par with the best programs in evolutionary biology across the country." [^1]

> "You would be hard-pressed to find another research group at BYU that has had the same level of consistent, scholarly productivity over the past twenty-five years as BYU evolutionary biologists." [^1]

> "Evolution is a fundamental truth, and BYU is an institution that seeks all truth." [^8]

## LDS Connection

Whiting's chapter is a case study in faithful scholarship operating at the highest
academic standard. Far from compromising belief, BYU evolutionary biologists' scientific
credentials have let them *defend* the faith — Whiting specifically notes their central
role in addressing criticisms such as "DNA and the Book of Mormon."[^9] The unspoken
premise — that a university devoted to God can pursue the best secular science without
fear — is the lived version of the conviction that there is no conflict between true
science and the gospel (see [[Theism Makes Science Possible]]) and that the right posture
is to [[Teach the Best Science in the Light of the Gospel]].
