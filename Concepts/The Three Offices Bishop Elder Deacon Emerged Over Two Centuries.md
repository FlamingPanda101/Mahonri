# The Three Offices Bishop Elder Deacon Emerged Over Two Centuries

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] traces how **bishop, elder, and deacon** became "the three central leadership offices in the Church" only "over the course of the second and third centuries." The hierarchy was not delivered ready-made; it crystallized as the church shifted from a missionary movement to a managed institution — and writers progressively connected these offices to a "priesthood" (Latin *sacerdotium*).

## Evidence

### 1. 1 Timothy first lays out office qualifications
1 Timothy (late 1st / early 2nd c.) gives "an extensive explanation of the qualifications for church offices": bishops must be good household managers, of good reputation, married once, with submissive children (1 Tim 3:2–5); deacons serious, well-mannered, faithful, married once, proven (3:8–10); ruling elders honored for preaching and teaching (5:17). This is when "early offices of the clergy were becoming more visible and well-defined." [^1]

### 2. Clement and Ignatius elevate the offices
*1 Clement* (attributed to the bishop of Rome, AD 88–97) is "the earliest to record that bishops had been appointed by the Apostles to be their successors." [[Ignatius of Antioch]] (d. c. AD 110) tied bishops, deacons, and elders to a "divine, mystical relationship between the Father, Son, Apostles" and was "the first Christian author in known history to assert the presiding authority of the bishop over rituals such as baptism and the Eucharist." [^2]

### 3. The rise of the "priesthood" (sacerdotium)
In the 3rd century, writers like [[Tertullian]] understood bishops, elders, and deacons as holders of "priesthood" offices by connecting them to the **Hebrew/Levitical** priesthood. Tertullian argued that, like the Levites, Christian "priests" must be men married only once (*Exhortation to Chastity* 7.1–2; *On Monogamy* 12.2). [^3]

### 4. The Eastern church order maps offices onto Israel's priesthood
The *Teachings of the Apostles* (Didascalia Apostolorum, early 3rd c., Syria) likens the bishop to the high priest, the elder to the Israelite priest, and sees Levites in "the deacons, lectors, cantors, porters, female deacons, widows, virgins, and orphans, 'but the high priest is above all of these.'" Male deacons stand to the bishop "as the Son stands next to the Father." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Over the course of the second and third centuries, bishop, elder, and deacon emerged as the three central leadership offices in the Church." [^1]

> Christian office holders "must also be men who have only been married one time" — Tertullian's Levitical analogy [^3]

## LDS Connection
Laughton's candid conclusion is significant for [[Latter-day Saints]]: the restored church's offices "have far more in common with the church hierarchy of bishops, elders, and deacons established in the third century and beyond" than with the fluid, home-based "primitive Church" of Article of Faith 6. She reframes this not as embarrassment but as evidence that church institutions are "constantly being born, dying, and evolving … both in response to prophetic revelation and the cultural and social needs of the community." [^5]
