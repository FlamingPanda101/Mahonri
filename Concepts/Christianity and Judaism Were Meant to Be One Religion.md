# Christianity and Judaism Were Meant to Be One Religion

## The Claim
Butler's closing restorationist frame: "Christianity" is "a bad word for what it is." There was one ancient tradition revealed to mankind from the beginning; Christ came to *remind* everyone of it and to fulfill its central sacrifice, not to found a new religion. Therefore "the fact that we call Christianity and Judaism separate religions is in itself... an evidence of the apostasy," because "it was always supposed to be one unified religion from the beginning to the end." The Restoration, the Book of Mormon, and the temple recover that single continuous tradition.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The split is symptomatic, not original (rating: moderate; theological framing)
Butler treats the existence of two separate religions as a *symptom* of the apostasy rather than a neutral historical fact: a unified ancient tradition fractured, and one fracture line is the Judaism/Christianity divide itself. This extends the episode's earlier point that the first churches were never one unified thing (see [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]] and [[Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree]]). [^1]

### 2. Continuity, not replacement (rating: moderate; restorationist)
Christ "didn't destroy whatever Judaism was — it was only the natural progression of this ancient tradition." Joseph Smith's restoration of the temple and the Book of Mormon are cast as "the most complete amazing text that shows what that ancient tradition is" — a recovery of the original, not a third religion. See [[LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity]]. [^2]

### 3. Rejecting the Messiah is itself a form of apostasy (rating: weak-moderate; polemical, conceded as contested)
Butler concedes "a lot of Jews would disagree," but argues the symmetry cuts both ways: "the guy you've been looking for since the beginning shows up, you reject him and kill him — that is a form of apostasy"; and later, those who called themselves Christians apostatized too. The framework charges *both* communities with departing from the one tradition. [^3]

### 4. The grounding text is James 1 (rating: moderate; LDS devotional)
The episode ties the whole conversation back to [[James 2.14-26|the Epistle of James]] (esp. James 1, "if any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God") as the very seed of the Restoration — the passage that sent Joseph Smith to the grove — and Butler reads James 1 as "the temple." James, the Torah-keeping brother of Jesus, becomes the hinge between the old tradition and the restored one. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Both Jews and traditional Christians would reject the premise from opposite directions. Most Jews hold that Judaism is the continuing covenant and that Christianity's claims about Jesus are precisely the departure. Most Christians hold that the church is the fulfillment of Israel (or its replacement/expansion) and that the two are genuinely distinct religions by divine design after the cross — "neither Jew nor Greek," a new covenant superseding the old. Calling the distinction an "evidence of apostasy" assumes the LDS restorationist meta-narrative that is itself the thing in dispute; it is a theological judgment, not a historical datum.
