# The Infant-Damnation Reductio Against Inherited Guilt Plus Required Profession

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] runs a live Socratic **reductio** against a soteriology that holds *both* (a) every person is born guilty in sin (inherited guilt / total depravity) *and* (b) salvation requires a conscious act — "accepting Jesus Christ" — that an infant cannot perform. Conjoin the two and the conclusion is forced: **every infant who dies is damned.** The interlocutor must therefore deny one premise. Carroll states the fork explicitly:

> "You have to either deny that we're all born sinners or deny that we have to accept Jesus Christ. Are you willing to deny either of those?" [^1]

The apologetic payoff is twofold. First, it is a clean *internal* critique — it uses only the preacher's own commitments. Second, the preacher's instinctive escape hatch (an "age of accountability") is **not found in the Bible he insists is the sole authority**, so taking the exit costs him his [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture|sola-scriptura]] principle. The doctrine that resolves the dilemma cleanly is the Latter-day Saint one Carroll names from the Doctrine and Covenants: children are not under condemnation until accountability.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Both premises extracted on tape.** The preacher affirms inherited guilt — "we're all born sinners… through Adam came sin… we inherit it" — and affirms required profession — you must "accept Jesus Christ as your savior."[^2] Carroll only has to lay them side by side.
2. **The conclusion is conceded, not dodged.** Pressed on whether dead infants are therefore in hell, the preacher retreats to "It could be a possibility. I mean, we don't know," and agrees a baby is "condemned from birth."[^1] That concession *is* the reductio landing: a soteriology whose honest output is "maybe all dead babies are in hell" has indicted itself, much as [[Salvation by Theology Damns Almost Everyone|salvation-by-correct-theology damns almost everyone]].
3. **The escape hatch is extra-biblical.** The preacher invokes "the age of accountability… at age eight."[^3] Carroll immediately notes the irony: that doctrine "in the Doctrine and Covenants… It's not in the Bible, right?" — and the preacher who demands every doctrine come from the Bible has just smuggled in one that does not.[^4] When the preacher reaches for a proof-text he names Psalm 51 ("Behold, I was shapen in iniquity") — which supports *his* original-guilt premise, not the exception he needs.[^5]
4. **It connects to a real historical fault line.** This is not a debater's gotcha but the very tension that split [[Pelagius]] and [[Augustine]] — whether unbaptized infants are innocent by youth or "carry the remnants of original sin." See [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]] and [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]].

## Steelman (the preacher's side)
The Reformed/evangelical position has principled answers Carroll does not fully engage, and honesty requires naming them:
- **Election covers covenant infants.** Many Calvinists hold that elect infants dying in infancy are regenerated *immediately by the Spirit without conscious profession* — the Westminster Confession (10.3) says exactly this. On that view premise (b) is not a universal rule but the ordinary means for those capable of it; God is free to save the incapable directly. This dissolves the dilemma without denying either premise as stated — a move the street preacher, untrained, never reaches for.
- **"We don't know" can be reverent agnosticism.** Deferring to God's mercy on a hard case ("we don't know all things") is not necessarily evasion; Scripture itself leaves some judgments to God.
- **Psalm 51 and Romans 5 are real data.** The preacher's inherited-sin premise is not invented; it is a serious reading of Paul and the Psalter, and the LDS rejection of inherited guilt is itself a contestable theological choice, not a neutral default.

Where the steelman runs out: the *street preacher in this exchange* does not invoke election-of-infants; he affirms required profession as a flat universal and then cannot follow it to its conclusion. Against *his* stated position the reductio holds. And the Westminster escape, while coherent, sits awkwardly beside the same preacher's repeated insistence that one must actively "accept" Christ — it rescues infants only by granting that God saves some *without* the conscious step the preacher elsewhere makes mandatory.
