# Joseph Smith Could Not Have Used a Nehem Map

#type/claim

## The Claim

The skeptical theory that [[Joseph Smith]] lifted "Nahom" from an 18th-century European map of Arabia (which labeled the region "Nehhm" / "Nehem") is, [[Neal Rappleye]] argues, extremely improbable and — even if granted — explains almost nothing. The nearest known copy of such a map was ~300 miles away, it spelled the name differently, and it carried none of the geographic data (trails, an eastward turn, a fertile coast) that actually makes [[Lehi]]'s account fit Arabia. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Which map, and where?** The relevant maps are Jean-Baptiste d'Anville's (1755, "Nehem") and Carsten Niebuhr's (1789, "Nehhm"), plus later cartographers who simply copied those names without ever visiting Arabia. The Smith family was destitute, the Palmyra library held no such map, and Joseph was translating in even-more-remote Harmony. The closest copy Rappleye could ever locate was at Allegheny College, ~300 miles from Harmony by modern roads. [^2]

2. **The spelling doesn't match.** If Joseph copied a map, why does "Nahom" differ from the map's "Nehem"? And how would he have known to vary the vowels — i.e., that ancient South Arabian was written without vowels so the consonantal NHM could be vocalized differently? The mismatch tells against copying, not for it. [^2]

3. **Bayesian comparison favors coincidence over map-access.** Kyler Rasmussen's "Estimating the Evidence" series (Interpreter) ran a Bayesian analysis and concluded it is *more* likely that the Nahom correlation is pure coincidence than that Joseph traveled to Allegheny College and saw such a map. Either way the map theory is a poor explanation. [^3]

4. **The maps lack the load-bearing data.** A map gives a name and an approximate location — but no travel routes, no indication the trail turns *eastward*, and no fertile area to the east. Worse, the period sources on "Arabia Felix" placed the lush region in the *southwest* corner (right where Nahom already is), so a map-using Joseph would have sent Lehi *south/southwest*, not east, to reach fertility. On the d'Anville map, going east from Nehem literally crosses into a zone labeled "Barren Desert." The map would have *misled* a forger, not guided him. [^4]

5. **Early Saints' own "19th degree" guess shows no map use.** If some early Latter-day Saints (per the disputed Frederick G. Williams statement) thought Lehi turned at the 19th parallel, that is the *wrong* latitude for the maps' Nehem (~17th on the old maps, ~16.5 in reality) — evidence they were *not* matching the Book of Mormon to a map's "Nehem." [^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The strongest skeptical reply, which Rappleye acknowledges, is the parity argument: "It is also very improbable that Joseph Smith saw an angel." If one is already committed to naturalism, then *any* naturalistic route — however unlikely — outranks a miraculous one, and a 300-mile map is at least a physical object that could in principle be traced. Rappleye answers that (a) this is a *presupposition*, not an argument from the evidence; (b) on purely historical grounds we have abundant first- and second-hand testimony that Joseph *claimed* an angel and *zero* historical evidence he ever saw such a map; and (c) the map theory, even if conceded, leaves the convergence (routes, eastward turn, Bountiful) unexplained — so it is a costly hypothesis that buys very little.

## Best Quotes

> "It's very, very improbable to me that Joseph Smith ever saw one of these maps." [^2]

> "If we do say he saw one of these maps, I don't really feel like that does very much for you. It gives you a name... but it doesn't have travel routes on there... there is no indication of trails turning eastward... and there is no indication of a fertile area." [^4]

> "We have way more historical evidence that Joseph Smith saw an angel than he saw one of these maps... and we have no historical evidence to say he saw one of these maps. Zero. None." [^1]
