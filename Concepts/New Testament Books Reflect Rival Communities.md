# New Testament Books Reflect Rival Communities

## The Claim
The New Testament is not a single unified book dictated from heaven; it is a collection of independent letters and gospels written by different authors for different communities with different theological emphases — and only assembled into one volume in the late 4th century. Reading them as one seamless, inerrant book misreads what they are.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Four gospels, four christologies (rating: strong; standard NT scholarship)
The episode summarizes the mainstream view: **Matthew** presents a royal/Davidic Jesus packaged as a second Moses (born amid slaughtered babies, 40-day ministry mirroring 40 years, Sermon on the Mount as new Sinai); **Mark** a more human Jesus; **Luke** a universal Jesus descended from Adam; **John** a cosmic, high-christological Jesus (the *Logos*). Each community's pastoral concerns show in the differences. [^1]

### 2. The books were written as occasional documents (rating: strong; documentary)
"There were letters... written by specific people with a specific stance in Christianity." It was "never the plan... to have it all together." The canon is "not one book until late 300s, late 4th century." So unity is an editorial achievement, not an original feature. [^2]

### 3. Raymond Brown's community-reading of John (rating: moderate; cited scholar)
Following [[Raymond Brown]], John is read as addressing the post-70-AD separation from Judaism — signaling to Rome "we have no beef with you" (Pilate's hand-washing; "my kingdom is not of this world"), which explains the gospel's apparent anti-Judaism as community self-definition. See the [[Ward Radio - The Great Apostasy Deep Dive with Mike Day and Dave Butler|source]]. [^3]

### 4. Matthew 5 as a window on a Torah-keeping community (rating: moderate; Butler)
Butler reads [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5]] ("I am come to fulfill the law") as evidence that Matthew's readers still considered themselves Jews under the law, receiving *additional* (harder) obligations — not absolution from Torah. A different community than Paul's. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Inerrantist Christians argue the differences are complementary, not contradictory — four portraits of one Christ, divinely superintended so that the whole canon coheres (cf. the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy, which the episode names). On this view diversity of emphasis is a feature of multiple inspired witnesses, not evidence of rival, mutually-correcting factions. Day's counter is that the books also *disagree* (the Paul/James conflict, differing christologies), which complementarity strains to absorb.

## Best Quotes

> "For Matthew, Jesus is royal. For Luke, he's descended from Adam, so he's universal. And for John, he's a god... he's cosmic." [^1]

> "It's not one book until late 300s, late 4th century... different perspectives, different theology." [^2]

> "It's not like the Bible was a fax machine... What we have is a canonized fight." [^1]
