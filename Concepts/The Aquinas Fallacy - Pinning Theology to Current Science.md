# The Aquinas Fallacy - Pinning Theology to Current Science

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hollis R. Johnson]] coins the **Aquinas fallacy** as the name for the mistake of "assuming that current science, including both fact and speculation, provides final answers" and then building permanent theology on it.[^1] He names it after Thomas Aquinas, who wove the Aristotelian science of his day into a systematic theology (scholasticism) that the Roman church taught for centuries and later had to revise — though Johnson notes the fallacy "could equally well have been named after any number of other figures — Jewish, Christian, and Muslim — who shared his presuppositions."[^1] The charge is leveled directly at Paul Copan and [[William Lane Craig]]: they "take the standard hot big bang model as a final scientific description of the origin of the universe and use it to establish a doctrine of 'creation from nothing,'" when "science... is an ongoing, self-correcting process" in which "many wrong ideas are suggested and discarded before a corrected understanding eventually emerges."[^1] Crucially this is the *inverse* of the usual God-of-the-gaps worry: the error is not invoking God where science is silent, but welding doctrine to a scientific model that science itself later overturns — so that "as our knowledge of the universe changes, religious ideas tied to previous scientific knowledge become inadequate."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
- **The model they relied on is obsolete.** Copan and Craig employ "the standard hot big bang cosmology that was current in the late 1970s, including a singularity at the origin of the universe, without understanding that quantum mechanics prevents a true singularity — a fact known much earlier."[^3] (See [[Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow]].)
- **They omit the 1998 discovery.** They "do not mention the surprise discovery in late 1998 of the current acceleration of the expansion of the universe," whose background (dark) energy "alone may destroy the argument of creation from nothing." Johnson calls the omission "an excellent example of the Aquinas fallacy."[^2]
- **A historical precedent for the danger.** When science could not yet explain the big bang's origin, "certain theologians jumped into the fray and declared that God initiated the big bang. In 1951, for instance, Pope Pius XII pointed to the big bang as the biblically described creation event" — a claim Johnson says "is not a scientific explanation."[^4]
- **Two incomplete canons.** "Both the scientific and the religious canons of knowledge are incomplete, and it would be wrong to assume that either gives definitive answers about the other"; "one should be cautious in employing scientific results to support dogmas about God."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The Aquinas fallacy consists of assuming that current science, including both fact and speculation, provides final answers... Science, however, is an ongoing, self-correcting process leading to increased knowledge and understanding, and many wrong ideas are suggested and discarded before a corrected understanding eventually emerges." [^1]

> "As our knowledge of the universe changes, religious ideas tied to previous scientific knowledge become inadequate." [^2]

For a Latter-day Saint the fallacy cuts toward intellectual humility on both sides: it warns LDS thinkers off the same trap as it does evangelical apologists, and it pairs with the observation that [[Modern Physics Is Consistent With Eternal Matter Not Ex Nihilo|modern physics is consistent with eternal matter]] rather than with absolute creation from nothing — a *consistency*, Johnson would stress, not a proof. It also dovetails with the broader point that [[Biblical Interpretation Has Always Renegotiated Under New Cosmologies|scripture has always been re-read under new cosmologies]] and that [[Concordism Assumes Scripture and Science Describe the Same Thing|concordism wrongly assumes scripture and science describe the same event]].
