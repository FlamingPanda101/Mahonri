# Moral Foundations Theory

## The Point

[[Jonathan Haidt]]'s account of where morality comes from: across cultures, the moral sense is built on a small set of innate, intuitive "taste buds" — **care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity**, and (a sixth) **liberty**. Different cultures and political tribes emphasize different combinations, yielding three "moral cuisines": progressive, social-conservative, and libertarian. Morality is therefore plural and partly intuitive, not a single rationally-derived code — a claim with direct epistemic consequences. [^1]

## Argument

1. **Morality varies but around shared themes.** Reading anthropology and ethnographies and doing fieldwork in India and Brazil, Haidt found "morality varies a lot around the world," yet certain issues "just tweak our minds." [^2]
2. **The foundations are like taste buds.** Seeing a child harmed "activates a taste bud that says no, this is wrong"; so too do shirking, disloyalty, disobedience, and degrading/disgusting behavior. He names five: **care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity** — and notes some listeners feel the last two "aren't really morality." [^3]
3. **The sixth foundation: liberty.** From "what I think is the largest study of libertarian psychology," Haidt adds **liberty** as a sixth foundation; libertarians have "the strongest sense of personal liberty" and "the weakest sense of group loyalty, respect for authority, or ... sanctity." [^1]
4. **Three moral cuisines.** The taste buds combine into "the three major families of moral cuisines ... what you might call progressive, social conservative, and libertarian." Different tribes are not irrational; they are seasoned by different foundations. [^4]
5. **Libertarian cognitive profile.** On the personality dimensions of empathizing and systemizing, libertarians are "the best of any group at thinking systemically" and "statistically significantly lower" on empathizing — "the most masculine cognitive style" (even female libertarians), which Haidt says libertarians themselves confirm. He cites his free paper "Understanding Libertarian Morality." [^5][^6]
6. **Epistemic upshot.** If your own morality is one "cuisine" among several, your moral intuitions are partial — grounding [[Listening to Those Who Disagree Reveals Your Blind Spots]] and [[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think]]. (Note: the masculine-cognitive-style and empathizing/systemizing claims draw on contested sex-difference literature; Haidt presents them as statistical tendencies, not absolutes.)

## Best Quotes

> "I just named five foundations: care, fairness, loyalty, authority, and sanctity." [^3]

> "Libertarians have the strongest sense of personal liberty ... and the weakest sense of group loyalty, respect for authority, or a sense of personal sanctity or purity." [^1]

> "The three major families of moral cuisines ... progressive, social conservative, and libertarian." [^4]
