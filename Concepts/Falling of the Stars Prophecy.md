# Falling of the Stars Prophecy

## The Claim
On 6 October 1833, [[Joseph Smith]] is said to have prophesied (per Brian Stutzman's book) that "40 days shall not pass and the stars shall fall from heaven." On the 39th day, the night of 12–13 November 1833, the great Leonid meteor storm lit the sky over North America — "more than a billion shooting stars" in nearly four hours. The account comes from [[Philo Dibble]]'s diary, relating what [[Joseph Hancock]] told him: Hancock, lost while hunting, lodged with a skeptic who mockingly produced a written copy of the prophecy on the 39th day; that very night the stars fell, and the skeptic "turned pale as death and spoke not a word."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The astronomical event is undisputed and spectacular.** The 1833 Leonid storm is one of history's most documented meteor displays; if the prophecy's 40-day window is genuine, the hit is striking. The host: "if Joseph Smith did prophesy this within 40 days and if this story really did happen... that's miraculous." (Evidence rating: high *if* the prophecy and timing are authentic — two large ifs.)[^2]
2. **The diary is six decades late.** The host checked the source: Dibble's diary "comes from his diary which was written in 1892," many decades after 1833. (Evidence rating: strongly weakening; see [[The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims]].)[^3]
3. **Chained, secondhand testimony.** Dibble "is recording what was told to him by brother Hancock" — not a firsthand account, and the original written note allegedly held by the skeptic does not survive. (Evidence rating: weak chain of custody.)[^4]
4. **The telling silence.** The host's sharpest objection: "Why do we have no one in Joseph Smith's time who is talking about the fulfillment of this prophecy? Surely this would have gotten more attention." A fulfilled prophecy this dramatic should have left abundant contemporary traces. See [[Absence of Contemporaneous Sources Undermines a Prophecy]]. (Evidence rating: strong objection.)[^4]
5. **Steelman of the believing view.** The 40-day window is unusually specific for prophecy, and the narrative detail (the skeptic's own written copy condemning him on the final night) is vivid and coherent. If even the kernel is authentic — that Joseph named a short window for a star-fall shortly before the Leonids — it would be a notable hit. The host remains "open to it being a fulfilled prophecy" while withholding strong confidence.

## Best Quotes
> "40 days shall not pass and the stars shall fall from heaven."[^1]

> "He turned pale as death and spoke not a word."[^1]
