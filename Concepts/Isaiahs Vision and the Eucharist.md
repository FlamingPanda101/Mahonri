## The Point
In Eastern Christianity (Byzantine and Syriac), details and imagery from Isaiah's vision of God in the heavenly temple (Isaiah 6) were gradually brought into the Eucharistic liturgy. The seraphic hymn "Holy, holy, holy" suggested that the worshipping community on earth joined its voice with the heavenly hosts, and the Eucharistic bread was compared to the live coal that purged Isaiah's "unclean lips" — encouraging worshippers to see themselves as Isaiah approaching God's throne, receiving forgiveness, and being sent forth transformed.

## Evidence
1. The seraphic hymn "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts" ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6:3]]) was an element of Jewish and Christian worship from very early (cf. Revelation 4:6–8), implying that earthly singers joined the heavenly hosts (cf. D&C 109:79).
2. In the fourth century, some Christians compared the Eucharistic bread to the coal the seraph touched to Isaiah's lips: "This has touched your lips; your iniquity is taken away, and your sin purged" (Isaiah 6:7).
3. By the sixth century, liturgies made frequent reference to Isaiah 6, and liturgical vessels and spaces were decorated with the seraphim and cherubim surrounding God's throne.
4. These elements "encouraged worshipers to imagine themselves in the role of Isaiah, approaching the throne of God as they approached the church altar, receiving forgiveness and purification anew through the Eucharist, and being transformed into people who could, like Isaiah, go forth with newfound confidence."

## Best Quotes
> "These ritual elements encouraged worshipers to imagine themselves in the role of Isaiah, approaching the throne of God as they approached the church altar, receiving forgiveness and purification anew through the Eucharist, and being transformed into people who could, like Isaiah, go forth with newfound confidence to bear testimony of God's word to the world." [^1]

> "By celebrating it regularly, the solemnity renews it for loving hearts." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison shares a personal reflection: as he takes the small piece of sacrament bread in his "relatively simple Latter-day Saint sacrament meeting," he recalls the angelic words to Isaiah and feels renewed to say "Here am I, Lord; send me" (Isaiah 6:8) — an explicit bridge between ancient Eucharistic theology and modern Latter-day Saint sacrament devotion.
