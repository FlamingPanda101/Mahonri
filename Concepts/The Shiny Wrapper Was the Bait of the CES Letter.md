# The Shiny Wrapper Was the Bait of the CES Letter

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Michael Peterson]] argues that the persuasive force of the [[CES Letter]] came not from its contents but from its *packaging* — a sympathetic origin narrative he and co-author Jacob Hess call the "shiny wrapper." The wrapper is the set of storylines surrounding the document: that the author was merely "a disaffected and doubting member... seeking answers to difficult questions," that his purpose was to *restore* his faith, that it was aimed solely at one CES director, and that the Church refused to answer him. Peterson's contention is that this framing was a deliberate marketing device: the body of the letter is largely recycled from [[Sandra Tanner|Gerald and Sandra Tanner]], MormonThink, and [[Grant Palmer]]'s *Insider's View of Mormon Origins* — material a faithful, temple-attending member would normally "thumb through and toss" — so the "honest seeker" introduction was the bait that got people to open the box and read the recycled content anyway. As Peterson puts it, the wrapper "makes you want to open the box."[^1]

A corollary the panel stresses: [[Jeremy Runnells]] effectively cast *himself* as his own target audience. By presenting as a frustrated, sincere member, he invited every frustrated member to think "I am living your story" — maximizing identification and emotional leverage. The hosts liken the technique to a Trojan horse: a single relatable figure smuggling in "every anti-Mormon argument since the 1830s" condensed into one professional-looking PDF with "the thin veneer of academia."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **Reactions tracked the wrapper, not the content.** Peterson says that across hundreds of early (2013–2015) public reactions on Facebook and Reddit, "two out of three, three out of four" responded to the surrounding story more than to the arguments. He cites a representative commenter ("Miss Scrivinsky"): "the letter wasn't intended to be this big exposure of the church... Jeremy had legitimate questions... why couldn't anyone just honestly answer his questions?"[^3]
- **Even debunkers embraced the wrapper.** People who *rebutted* the letter's content nonetheless "wrapped their arms around Runnells," believing he was a sincere seeker. BYU's Stephen Harper is quoted as a recent convert away from that view: "I took for granted that he was who he said he was... I wanted to wrap my arms around Jeremy Runnells and say man that sucks... and that's just not what happened, that's not the truth."[^4]
- **The recycled body.** By the author's own "stand on the shoulders of giants" admission, the material is compiled from prior critics (Tanners, Palmer's *Insider's View*, MormonThink); one early Reddit responder called the draft "MormonThink in PDF."[^5]
- **The introduction collides with the text.** Peterson notes the irenic opening ("you may have new information... I'm genuinely interested in your thoughts") is immediately followed by a "no-holds-barred" attack — a tonal mismatch he argues betrays the wrapper's artificiality.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "We call that the shiny wrapper. It includes his background story... 'I was just a disaffected doubting member and the church was not responding to me.' ... and who wouldn't be taken with such a story? ... this was the hook to get people to actually go into the content of the letter and read it." [^1]

> "If you handed a returned missionary... *Insider's View of Mormon Origins* or one of the Tanner volumes, nine times out of ten they would thumb through it and toss it. ... So why didn't people do that? That's where the wrapper comes in." [^5]
