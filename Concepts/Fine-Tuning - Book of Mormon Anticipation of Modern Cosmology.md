# Fine-Tuning - Book of Mormon Anticipation of Modern Cosmology

## The Claim

The Book of Mormon contains passages that appear to anticipate modern
cosmological fine-tuning arguments — arguments that were not scientifically
developed until the late 20th century. [[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]] references the
earth's "motion" and planets moving in "regular form" as evidence of a Supreme
Creator. [[D&C 130.20-21]] describes "a law, irrevocably decreed in heaven
before the foundations of this world" — language that maps onto the concept of
eternal physical constants. If [[Joseph Smith]] fabricated the Book of Mormon
in 1829, he could not have known about fine-tuning or physical constants. This
suggests either genuine ancient provenance or prophetic insight — either of
which supports LDS truth claims against naturalistic critiques.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Alma 30:44 Anticipates the Structure of Fine-Tuning Arguments (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

The full argument is developed in [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument
from Alma 30]]. The key textual features:

- **"Its motion":** The earth is described as being in motion — not stationary.
  Ancient and medieval cosmologies typically placed the earth at the center,
  unmoving. The Book of Mormon text reflects a cosmos in dynamic motion.[^1]
- **"The planets which move in their regular form":** The planets follow
  law-like, mathematically regular orbits — precisely what fine-tuning
  arguments emphasize: the stability of planetary orbits depends on exquisitely
  balanced gravitational forces. If gravity were slightly different, orbits
  would decay or planets would fly apart.
- **"All things denote there is a God":** This is a design inference from
  cosmic order — the same logical structure as the modern fine-tuning
  argument.

[[David Butler]] notes that Alma is "the original teleological arguer way
before St. Aquinas or any of the other early church fathers who used it."[^2]

### 2. Joseph Smith's 19th-Century Context Lacked Fine-Tuning Science (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Historical)

[[Hayden Carroll]]:

> "First of all, Joseph Smith, if he wrote the Book of Mormon, he didn't know
> about the fine-tuning argument. The fine-tuning argument was not — as far as
> the initial conditions and the constants — didn't exist, right? Because we
> didn't understand what was out there the way that we do."[^3]

The sciences relevant to fine-tuning emerged long after 1829:
- The Big Bang theory: 1920s (Lemaitre, Hubble)
- Cosmic microwave background: 1965 (Penzias, Wilson)
- Precise measurement of physical constants: 20th century
- Fine-tuning as a scientific problem: 1970s-1980s (Carter, Barrow, Tipler)

Joseph Smith could not have known that the universe had a beginning (steady-state
theory dominated until the mid-20th century), that physical constants had
precise, life-permitting values, or that planetary orbits depend on gravitational
fine-tuning. Yet [[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]] speaks of cosmic order as evidence
of God in ways that align with modern discoveries.

### 3. Pre-Joseph Smith Design Arguments Were Biological, Not Cosmological (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Historical)

Carroll acknowledges that "there were pre-Joseph Smith theologians who were
using the teleological argument" — but these were biological design arguments
(William Paley's watchmaker, 1802; Thomas Aquinas's Fifth Way, 13th century).
They argued from the complexity of living organisms, not from cosmic
fine-tuning.

> "The physical constants, the scientific ones, the theologians didn't know.
> They've actually been verified or confirmed or validated in time."[^3]

The distinction matters: Paley's biological design argument could be countered
by Darwin (1859), who provided a naturalistic mechanism for biological
complexity. But cosmological fine-tuning has no comparable naturalistic
explanation — the multiverse remains speculative and untestable. If Joseph
Smith were fabricating, he'd likely have produced a biological design argument
like Paley's, not a cosmological one that would not be scientifically
appreciated for over a century.

### 4. D&C 130:20-21 and Eternal Physical Law (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

[[D&C 130.20-21]] (revealed 1843): "There is a law, irrevocably decreed in
heaven before the foundations of this world, upon which all blessings are
predicated."

Butler connects this to physical constants:

> "It's almost as if there is a law irrevocably decreed in heaven before the
> foundations of this world upon which all blessings are predicated. And when
> we obtain any blessing from God, it is by obedience to that law upon which it
> is predicated."[^4]

The concept of ETERNAL law — laws that exist independently of God and that God
Himself follows — is distinctive to LDS theology. In most Christian theologies,
God creates the laws of nature and could change them at will (voluntarism). LDS
theology posits that physical and moral law are co-eternal with God. This maps
remarkably well onto the scientific concept of universal constants that don't
change — and the phrase "irrevocably decreed... before the foundations of this
world" anticipates a cosmological structure where the laws of physics preceded
and govern the universe's formation.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Alma 30:44 Is a Generic Design Argument
**Steelman:** Alma 30:44 is a generic design-from-nature argument of the kind
found in many religious traditions. Psalm 19:1 ("The heavens declare the glory
of God"), Romans 1:20, and Wisdom of Solomon 13:1-9 all make similar arguments
from cosmic order. Joseph Smith was steeped in the Bible and could easily have
adapted biblical design language. There's nothing specifically "fine-tuning"
about "the planets move in regular form" — that's observable by anyone who
looks at the night sky over time.

**Response:** See the discussion in [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument
from Alma 30]]. The claim is not that Alma 30:44 contains technical fine-tuning
parameters, but that (a) its cosmic-scale framing (planets, earth's motion,
regular form) is more aligned with modern cosmological design arguments than
with Paley-style biological arguments, and (b) Joseph Smith, if fabricating,
managed to avoid the kind of biological design argument that was popular in his
day and produce a cosmic design argument whose full force would only be
appreciated later. The Book of Mormon's design argument "aged well" in a way
Joseph Smith couldn't have predicted.

### Objection 2: Design Arguments Were Common in Joseph Smith's Era
**Steelman:** Natural theology — arguing for God from nature — was enormously
popular in the 18th and 19th centuries. Joseph Smith didn't need to anticipate
modern science to include a design argument; he just needed to reflect the
intellectual milieu of his time. The specific reference to planets and their
motions reflects Newtonian astronomy, which was well-known by 1829.

**Response:** Newtonian astronomy described HOW planets move (elliptical orbits
governed by gravity), but it didn't ask the fine-tuning question — WHY is
gravity precisely the strength that permits stable orbits? That question only
arose with 20th-century cosmology, when physicists realized how narrow the
life-permitting ranges are. Alma 30:44 is compatible with Newtonian astronomy,
but it's ALSO compatible with modern fine-tuning in a way that purely
biological design arguments (Paley) are not.

### Objection 3: D&C 130:20-21 Is About Moral Law, Not Physics
**Steelman:** The context of D&C 130:20-21 is blessings and obedience — moral
and spiritual law, not physical constants. Butler's connection to fine-tuning
is an interesting homiletic move but not a faithful reading of the text. The
"law irrevocably decreed" refers to the law of the harvest, tithing, or other
commandments with attached blessings — not the gravitational constant.

**Response:** Butler's reading is expansive rather than restrictive. The text
says "a law" and "all blessings" — it presents a general principle that law
precedes blessing, which can be applied at multiple levels (moral, spiritual,
physical). And the temporal marker — "before the foundations of this world"
— explicitly places this law prior to creation, which is precisely where
fine-tuning locates the physical constants. Even if the primary referent is
moral law, the principle generalizes.
