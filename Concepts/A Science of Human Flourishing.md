# A Science of Human Flourishing

## The Point
In his closing chapter ("The Future of Happiness"), [[Sam Harris]] gathers the book's thesis into a hopeful program: because values reduce to "facts about the well-being of conscious creatures," and because brains and primary human emotions "clearly transcend culture," there *must* be truths about human flourishing to be discovered — even if a finished "science of morality" never arrives.[^1] Moral progress is therefore real and possible (he points to the collapse of public lynching and open newspaper racism within a century as evidence), and the chief obstacle to it is the "moral relativism of science," which has ceded to "faith-based religion — that great engine of ignorance and bigotry — a nearly uncontested claim to being the only universal framework for moral wisdom."[^2]

## Evidence
Harris argues the thesis is empirical-in-principle and even falsifiable: it would fail if "human well-being were completely haphazard and unrelated to states of the brain," or if antithetical states of the world produced identical well-being — in which case "the entire field of neuroscience would constitute an elaborate and very costly method of misunderstanding the world."[^3] He marshals positive psychology to show our intuitions about happiness are systematically wrong: too many choices reduce satisfaction; "affective forecasting" research (Daniel Gilbert) shows we badly overestimate how much events will affect us.[^4] He addresses Kahneman's "experiencing self" vs. "remembering self," arguing the split dissolves once we treat all of life as ~2.5 billion seconds of conscious experience, so "consciousness and its ever-changing contents remain the only subjective reality."[^5] He grants morality may never become a tidy science — "is economics a true science yet?" — but insists that, as with economics, "there are better and worse ways" and it would be absurd to call criticizing another society's response to a banking crisis "bigotry."[^6] The book closes: there "must be something to know" about morality "in principle," and admitting this "will transform the way we think about human happiness and the public good."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "If our well-being depends upon the interaction between events in our brains and events in the world, and there are better and worse ways to secure it, then some cultures will tend to produce lives that are more worth living than others." [^2]

> "For nearly a century, the moral relativism of science has given faith-based religion — that great engine of ignorance and bigotry — a nearly uncontested claim to being the only universal framework for moral wisdom." [^2]

> "Whether or not we ever understand meaning, morality, and values in practice, I have attempted to show that there must be something to know about them in principle." [^2]

## Apologetic Engagement
This conclusion is, ironically, the place where Harris and the theist *agree most* — and exactly where his project is most exposed. (1) **The shared ground is large.** Moral realism, real moral progress, the rejection of "all cultures are equally valid" relativism, the conviction that there are truths about human flourishing — Latter-day Saint and classical theism affirm every one; the vault's [[Two Catastrophes - Dogmatic Absolutism and Moral Relativism]] frames precisely this middle path. Harris is right that thoroughgoing relativism is corrosive. (2) **But the central inference is invalid as stated.** "Well-being depends on brain states, therefore science can tell us what we *ought* to pursue" silently inserts the premise that *promoting* well-being is obligatory — a value, not a fact, and one no scan can supply. This is the standing objection the vault records in [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]] and in [[The Moral Landscape - Grounding Value in Conscious Well-Being]]: Harris helps himself to the very "ought" he claims to have derived. (3) **His own escape hatch is question-begging.** Asked what could falsify the thesis, Harris answers that a value "having nothing to do with the well-being of conscious creatures… could be of no possible interest to anyone, by definition." But that defines the rival (e.g., duties grounded in God, or intrinsic goods beyond felt experience) out of existence rather than refuting it — and it sits awkwardly with his earlier admission of equivalent but irreconcilable "peaks," and with the worry he himself raises that evil might prove as reliable a path to happiness as goodness (see [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]]). If saints and sinners can occupy "equivalent peaks," his landscape is no longer *moral*. (4) **The progress he celebrates leans on borrowed capital.** The abolition of slavery and lynching that Harris reads as secular moral progress was driven in large part by religiously motivated reformers appealing to the imago Dei — the equal worth of persons that naturalism struggles to ground (see [[Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls]]). Harris narrates the harvest while disputing the seed. The fair verdict: he has powerfully shown that moral questions *have answers* and that relativism fails — a conclusion theism welcomes — but he has not shown that *naturalism* can supply the binding, obligatory worth of conscious creatures his whole program presupposes. For that ground, see [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] and [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]].
