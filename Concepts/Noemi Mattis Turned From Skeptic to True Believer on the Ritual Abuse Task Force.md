# Noemi Mattis Turned From Skeptic to True Believer on the Ritual Abuse Task Force

## The Claim
[[Noemi Mattis]], chosen as the Utah ritual-abuse task force's voice of rational skepticism, instead became a defender of the unsubstantiated claims — the prime case study in how Utah's skeptics joined the panickers rather than checking them.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Selected precisely as a skeptic (strong)
The governor chose Mattis as co-chair seeking balance. She had described herself to the *Deseret News* (September 1991) as "a New York humanist Jew and a quintessential skeptic, part of a group of people who didn't even believe in Satan," and had been "dismissive of abuse allegations," comparing the hysteria to what "drove her parents out of Nazi Germany" [^1].

### 2. She reversed course (strong — the turn)
"What an excellent choice for a voice of rationalism, right? Wrong. Mattis would turn out to be a horrendous choice." On the committee she defended the claims rather than scrutinizing them [^2].

### 3. A proposed mechanism: recovered-memory Holocaust theory (moderate — explanatory)
The episode links her shift to emerging theories that very young Holocaust-era children "could still bear the scars of the trauma" without conscious memory — a recovered-memory framework newly appealing to survivors like Mattis, who escaped the Nazis as a young child. This made the recovered-memory paradigm personally resonant [^3].

### 4. She explained away the missing evidence (strong)
Confronted with no bodies or death certificates, "Dr. Mattis dismissed the skepticism. She claimed doctors and morticians were involved in Utah's satanic cults," accounting for bodies that "were never found," and held that ritual abuse is known through "the cumulative experience of many therapists across the world" rather than physical proof. See [[The Breeder Myth Was Invented to Explain Missing Bodies]] [^4][^5].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender could argue Mattis was an experienced professional persuaded by the volume of consistent clinical testimony she encountered — that a sincere skeptic *should* update toward belief if the evidence warrants, and labeling her conversion a failure assumes the conclusion. The rebuttal: what she updated *on* was not physical evidence but "the cumulative experience of many therapists" — precisely the recovered-memory testimony the broader episode shows to be contaminated — and she patched the decisive missing-evidence gap with an unfalsifiable conspiracy (complicit morticians). Updating toward belief is rational only when the new evidence is sound; here it was the very artifact under suspicion.
