# Act and Potency - Aristotle's Analysis of Change

## The Point
Aristotle's reply to Parmenides is the metaphysical engine of the whole Aristotelian proof: change is the **actualization of a potential**. [^1] Parmenides argued change is impossible because it would require something to come from nothing — the cold coffee's coldness, not existing while the coffee is hot, would have to come into being from non-being, and "something can't come from nothing." [^2] Aristotle's solution is to deny the dichotomy of pure being vs. pure nothing. Between the actually-hot coffee and sheer nothingness lies a third category: the coffee's real **potential** to be cold. [^3] That potential "is not exactly nothing, even if it is not yet actual either." [^3]

## Evidence
- The coffee can become cold, can stain the floor, can make you alert — but it cannot fuel a gasoline engine or "morph into a live chicken and begin squawking." That it has *some* potentials and lacks others shows potentiality is a real feature of things, not mere nothingness. [^3]
- Four kinds of change all reduce to actualized potential: qualitative (coffee cooling), local (a leaf falling), quantitative (a puddle growing), and substantial (a living thing becoming dead matter). [^4]
- Even radical skepticism cannot escape change: entertaining one premise after another and reaching a conclusion is itself "a kind of change — change with respect to your thoughts and experiences." "The very act of casting doubt on whether change occurs presupposes that it occurs." [^5]
- This is not "something coming from nothing — which, Aristotle agrees, is impossible — because, again, a potential is not nothing." [^1]

## Best Quotes
> "What change involves, then, is for Aristotle the actualization of a potential. The coffee has the potential to become cold, and after sitting out for a while that potential is made actual." [^1]

> "That it has the potential to become cold while lacking certain other potentials shows that the coldness is not exactly nothing, even if it is not yet actual either." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The act/potency distinction is, at this level, **metaphysically neutral** — it is simply a careful account of what change is, and Latter-day Saints have no quarrel with it. Indeed the distinction is a powerful weapon against the materialist slogan that "something can come from nothing" (compare [[Nothing Comes from Nothing - The Krauss Equivocation]]). The tension with the Restoration appears only later, when Feser pushes act/potency to its terminus and concludes the first cause must be **purely actual** — utterly devoid of potentiality, and therefore immutable and timeless (see [[The Purely Actual Actualizer - Aristotle's Unmoved Mover]]). LDS theology holds that God the Father is dynamic and embodied, possessing the capacity to act and relate in time; the critique [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] argues the "pure actuality" conclusion reshapes the living God of scripture into Aristotle's static principle. So: embrace act and potency as a tool; contest the inference that the ultimate cause can have *no* potentiality whatsoever.
