# King Benjamins Speech Strengthened Martin Harriss Testimony More Than Seeing the Plates

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] resolves a puzzle in [[Martin Harris]]'s biography. Just before the 1828 lawsuit (brought by Martin's wife Lucy), Martin insisted he must *see the plates* to testify; the Lord denied him that sight ([[D&C 5]]) and instructed more translation. Yet Martin then testified boldly in court without ever seeing the plates. What changed him? Bradley's answer: **the text they translated together**. By the manuscript evidence, translation resumed after the lost 116 pages at the start of [[Mosiah 18|Mosiah]] — King Benjamin's sermon — and "it was not seeing the plates, it was the actual text of the Book of Mormon… that pushed Martin's testimony over the top."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Martin came demanding a *witness of the plates*.** Facing Lucy Harris's lawsuit (and her own "three witnesses" against the project), Martin went to Harmony: "I got to see the plates… because my wife's got these witnesses saying, You don't have the plates." The revelation now [[D&C 5]] promised, for the first time, Three Witnesses of the plates — but Martin "does not get a witness at that time."[^1]
2. **He testified anyway, and powerfully.** At the trial Lucy Mack Smith records Martin testifying "with boldness, decision, and energy": "I can swear that Joseph Smith has never got one dollar from me by persuasion… And as to the plates… if you do not believe it but continue to resist the truth, it will one day be the means of damning your souls." The judge dismissed the case.[^2]
3. **The change agent was the words, not the artifact.** "I think it was not seeing the plates. It was the actual text… What he was translating, the words he's translating is what's changing him." Scholars place the post-lost-pages resumption at the beginning of our Book of Mosiah — King Benjamin's speech.[^3]
4. **The content is maximally converting — and self-referentially apt.** Benjamin announces an angel revealed the Messiah's name ("his name shall be Jesus Christ," [[Mosiah 5.8|Mosiah 5:8]]) and that the Messiah will come "in a tabernacle of clay" — and the narrative's own setting is the **Feast of Tabernacles** (the people pitch tents facing the temple), the very feast on which they were *translating*. [[John Tvedtnes]] and John W. Welch have laid out the implicit Tabernacles setting. Martin had long sought "a Church that could bear Christ's name legitimately"; Benjamin's name-of-Christ covenant answered that yearning.[^4]
5. **It ties to a recurring Oaks theme.** "Taking upon you the name of Christ" became a thread in Oaks's ministry (his 1985 talk "Taking Upon Us the Name of Christ") — extending the Harris heritage of faith into the present.[^5]
6. **Steelman of the skeptic.** A critic notes the resumption point (Mosiah) is a scholarly *reconstruction* from internal/manuscript clues, that we cannot prove *which* passage Martin scribed on a given day, and that "the text converted him" is an inference about an inner state. Bradley grants the reconstruction is probabilistic ("as I've tried to piece together a timeline") but argues it is the consensus landing spot after the lost pages, and that the behavioral datum is hard: Martin demanded ocular proof, was refused, kept scribing the Benjamin material, and emerged willing to stake his soul in court. The inference to the text as catalyst is the best explanation of that reversal — and it doubles as evidence Joseph was *receiving* rich content, not improvising (cf. [[The Seer Stone Swap and Jerusalem Walls Show Joseph Was Receiving Not Composing]]).

## Best Quotes

> "I think it was not seeing the plates. It was the actual text of the Book of Mormon that they were translating that pushed Martin's testimony over the top."[^3]

> "King Benjamin predicts that the Messiah will come down in a tabernacle of clay, his exact words. What's the feast of tabernacles? He's anticipating that the Messiah is going to come down in a tabernacle."[^4]

> "Even though he hasn't yet seen the plates… his testimony gets strengthened tremendously by hearing the word about Jesus Christ."[^3]
