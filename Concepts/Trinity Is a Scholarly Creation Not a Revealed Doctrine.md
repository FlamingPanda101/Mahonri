# Trinity Is a Scholarly Creation Not a Revealed Doctrine

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Trinity is presented by classical Christians as the true doctrine of God. [[Blake Ostler]] argues it is neither *revealed* (no text states it) nor *reasoned* coherently — it is "a scholarly creation," the product of councils "cogitating," and even classical theologians admit it cannot be reached by reason. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. No text says the three persons are one substance (rating: strong; textual)
"There is no text that says that the three divine persons are one substance. That's a scholarly creation. It's not based on revelation." If it were revealed it would be *stated*; it is not. [^1]

> "The trinity is never expressed in a fuller revelation. There is no text that says that the three divine persons are one substance. That's a scholarly creation. It's not based on revelation." [^1]

### 2. Classical theologians admit it is not reasoned, either (rating: strong; uses their authorities)
Citing [[Edward Feser]] ("human reason cannot fully grasp the Trinity even after it has been revealed") and [[Thomas Aquinas]], Ostler notes "just about every Christian author" distinguishes revealed truths from those reachable by reason — "and they admit the Trinity is not in scripture." "The Trinity is a reasoned doctrine, not a revealed doctrine — and you can't arrive at the Trinity through reason." [^2]

### 3. It is arguably contrary to reason (rating: moderate; philosophical)
"There's a certain sense in which the Trinity is contrary to reason. Nobody just by reasoning is going to coherently come up with one being that is three beings." See [[The Trinity Cannot Count to Three]]. [^3]

### 4. Ostler would accept genuine revelation (rating: strong; concessive)
He is explicit: "If you have some claimed revelation that gives you this further knowledge... I'm willing to listen, because it's a revelation from God." The objection is specifically to deriving the Trinity by *cogitation* ("imposing back onto these texts our neoplatonistic view of God"). "That's how Christianity actually came up with its various ideas about who God is" — councils, not revelation. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian denies the dichotomy: the doctrine is *revealed implicitly* in the pattern of NT data (Father, Son, Spirit each called God; one God) and *formulated* by the Church under the Spirit's guidance to guard that revelation against heresy — the councils articulated, not invented, the deposit of faith. They add that "no single verse states it" is a Restorationist/biblicist standard the LDS canon itself fails (no verse states the LDS Godhead in those terms either). Ostler's reply is that the conciliar *content* (one substance) is drawn from middle/neo-Platonism rather than from any revealed statement, so it is philosophy dressed as dogma, whereas the LDS view claims explicit modern revelation (e.g., the First Vision, D&C 130). See [[Council of Nicaea]] and [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].

## Best Quotes

> "The trinity is a reasoned doctrine, not a revealed doctrine. You can't arrive at the trinity through reason." [^2]

> "It was a fuller revelation, not a fuller council of scholars getting together to cogitate about it." [^5]

> "if what you want to say is, 'Oh, we figured it out by imposing back onto these texts our neoplatonistic view of God,' I'm going to say, no, what you're doing is reading back into the text something that simply isn't there." [^4]
