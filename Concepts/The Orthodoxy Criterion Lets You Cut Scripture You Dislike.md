# The Orthodoxy Criterion Lets You Cut Scripture You Dislike

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A standard proposed criterion for canonicity is **orthodoxy** — a book is canonical if it teaches sound doctrine. [[Joe Heschmeyer]] argues this criterion is uniquely dangerous: it lets the interpreter's prior theology sit in judgment over Scripture, so any book (or passage) one finds doctrinally inconvenient can be excised [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Luther did exactly this to James — rate: strong (historical)
In his original preface to James in his German New Testament, [[Martin Luther]] argued James could not have been written by an apostle because it contradicts [his reading of Paul on] justification by faith. Heschmeyer: "I'm happy to agree with Luther that the Apostle James contradicts [Luther's theology]... The problem is that he threw out the part of the New Testament that doesn't agree with him" — the "epistle of straw." See [[Luther and the Epistle of Straw]] and [[James 2.24|James 2:24]] [^1]. **Steelman:** Luther never removed James from his printed Bible and his judgment was contested by other Reformers; the orthodoxy criterion as he used it was idiosyncratic. But that it was *available to him at all* is the structural problem.

### 2. The criterion is infinitely abusable — rate: strong
"What stops someone from doing that — to just say, 'Well, I don't find this orthodox because... it's against my view of how we should approach homosexuality, or I don't like what it has to say about the trinity'?" Once orthodoxy is the test of canonicity, "you blow the authority of scripture to smithereens. If it's only as binding as you find it orthodox... you determine orthodoxy and then judge scripture" [^2].

### 3. It inverts the proper order — rate: strong
Scripture is supposed to *determine* orthodoxy; the criterion makes the reader's prior orthodoxy *determine* Scripture — a circular inversion. And "it's not just whole books — why not just say, 'Okay, I accept this book except this passage'? There's no end to it" [^3]. See [[Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy in Canon Criteria]] and [[The Fallible Canon Is Hostage to Critical Scholarship]].
