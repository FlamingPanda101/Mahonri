# The Two Serpents of Eden and the Divine Name Switch

#type/claim

## The Claim

The serpent of [[Genesis 3.4-5|Genesis 3]] is best read as a **counterfeit** of the *true* divine serpent — Satan impersonating the God of the garden. [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that Eden hides a "two serpents" / "thunder twins" drama: there are **two** seraphim/Nehushtanim in the Holy of Holies ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]] — "above him stood the seraphim," at least two), and Genesis 3 shows one of them gone corrupt. The tell is a **switch in the divine name**: the garden narrative calls God "Yahweh Elohim," but inside the serpent's dialogue with Eve only the bare word "Elohim" is used — because the impostor is *posing* as Yahweh Elohim and therefore cannot quote him by name. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **The name-switch is textual, not invented.** Genesis 2:4–3:24 uses "Yahweh Elohim" (the LORD God) throughout — Yahweh plants the garden, forms man, walks in the garden ([[Genesis 3.14-19|Gen 3:8]]). But from the second half of [[Genesis 3.4-5|Genesis 3:1]] through v. 5, while the serpent speaks, the name is just "Elohim" ("Hath *God* said…?" / "*God* doth know…"). At v. 8 it reverts to "Yahweh Elohim." Butler reads the gap as the counterfeit masking himself as the gardener-God. [^1][^3]
- **"Beguilement" *is* the transformation.** [[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9:9]] places back-to-back the being "who beguiled our first parents" and "who transformeth himself nigh unto an angel of light." Butler asks: what if these are the *same* event — the beguilement simply *is* Satan transforming into a being of light, i.e. appearing as the shining serpent? In [[Moses 4|Moses 4]], Satan "put it into the heart of the serpent" and elsewhere comes claiming "I am a son of God," exactly the counterfeit-of-the-true-Son pattern. [^4][^5]
- **The curse rips off *wings*, not legs.** The standard gloss on "upon thy belly shalt thou go" ([[Genesis 3.14-19|Genesis 3:14]]) is that God removes the serpent's legs. But seraphim are *winged* serpents ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]]; see [[Two Serpents - Mehen and Apophis Parallel the Brazen Serpent]]). Butler proposes the cherubim — "the tough boys of Eden, the gatekeepers" — pin the impostor and tear off his wings one at a time, hurling him "down into the dust as a mere snake." Losing wings = losing the ability to ascend. [^6][^7]
- **The early-Christian identification of the serpent with Satan is "for reasons."** Genesis 3 never literally names Lucifer, but the New Testament writers made the identification early; Butler argues "we should not discount the Christian witness… they thought it for reasons," and the LDS [[Moses 4|Moses account]] supplies the mechanism (Satan possessing/impersonating the serpent). [^8]
- **It completes the "two of everything" temple pattern.** The two serpents join a recurring pair-motif the hosts trace: the two seraphim singing to each other, the two goats of the Day of Atonement, the two sons of the tree of life — the mythic "thunder twins," where a good son and his near-identical counterfeit drive the action. [^9]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **The divine-name variation is the ordinary source-critical datum:** mainstream scholarship explains the Elohim/Yahweh-Elohim alternation by *documentary sources* (the very E/J distinction the hosts elsewhere endorse), not by an in-story disguise; reading it as the serpent's "mask" is a harmonizing move the text does not demand. The hosts partly concede the source layer but add a *narrative* reading on top. (b) **"Seraphim are winged, so the curse removes wings" assumes the identification it needs:** that the Eden serpent is one of the *winged* throne-seraphim is exactly the disputed premise; on the plain reading the Genesis 3 *nachash* is a beast of the field, and "on your belly" is an etiology for why snakes are legless. (c) **"Two Nehushtanim / thunder twins" is admitted midrash:** Butler repeatedly flags this as a first-time, on-air speculation ("I'm hearing this for the first time," says Mike Day), not an exegetical result. A Latter-day Saint reply: the reading is offered as devotional "handmade midrash" in [[James Charlesworth]]'s sense — a *proposal* that knits Genesis 3, [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]], [[Moses 4|Moses 4]], and [[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9]] into one coherent picture — and its strength is explanatory unification, not proof.

## Best Quotes

> "When he transformeth himself nigh unto an angel of light is the moment when he beguiled Eve… What does he look like in Genesis in the moment when he beguiles Eve? He looks like a snake." [^4]

> "The bad son has shown up and he's disguising himself as the good son… as Yahweh Elohim. Therefore he can't say, 'Well, didn't Yahweh Elohim say this?' — because he's pretending to be Yahweh Elohim." [^2]

> "They're not pulling off his legs to turn him into a snake. They're ripping off his wings one at a time and then hurling him down into the dust as a mere snake." [^7]
