# Cleansing of the Temple
#type/claim

## The Claim
The "cleansing of the temple" — Jesus entering the Jerusalem temple, overturning the money-changers' tables, and calling it "a den of thieves" (Mark 11:15-19) — functions in Mark not as an isolated act of zeal but as the theological center of a deliberate literary structure. Mark sandwiches it inside the two halves of the cursed fig tree, so that the unfruitful tree withered to its roots interprets the temple action: the temple and its leadership have failed to "bear fruit" and stand under judgment, foreshadowing the temple's destruction.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The Markan sandwich.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], Mark uses an A-B-A' intercalation: the fig tree is cursed (A, Mark 11:12-14), the temple is cleansed (B, 11:15-19), and the tree is found withered (A', 11:20-25). The frame makes the temple the referent of the tree — the cleansing is "boom, roasted — no more fruit ever," representing "the destruction of the temple, the scattering of the leadership." See [[Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor for Temple]] and [[Markan Sandwich Literary Device]].
- **"Den of thieves" and bearing fruit.** Jesus's charge invokes the prophetic "bearing fruit" motif (covenant faithfulness producing good works); the temple establishment is depicted as failing it, which is why the chief priests then seek to kill him.
- **Written after 70 CE.** Mark composed at or just after the Roman destruction of the temple, so the metaphor matches the lived reality of his audience: "the tree is cursed and has withered, the temple is gone, the leadership is gone." Jesus's pivot to "have faith in God" reassures followers they no longer need the condemned temple institutions.
- **Steelman / caveats.** Traditional readers take the cleansing as a substantially historical prophetic act (attested, with variation, in all four Gospels, and placed early by John), expressing Jesus's authority over his Father's house rather than functioning merely as Markan literary symbolism. One can hold both: a historical core can still be arranged by Mark into the fig-tree frame to make its meaning explicit. The contested move is reducing the event to pure metaphor; the well-grounded point is that Mark's *placement* of it inside the fig-tree sandwich is intentional and interpretive.
