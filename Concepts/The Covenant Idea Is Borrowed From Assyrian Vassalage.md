# The Covenant Idea Is Borrowed From Assyrian Vassalage

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the very concept of **covenant** in the Bible is a borrowing of the Assyrian institution of **vassalage** — the suzerain-vassal treaty (*adê*) by which an empire bound a conquered nation. "When people talk about covenants in the Bible, they're talking about the borrowing of the idea of vassalage and vassal treaties from the empire of Assyria. And that then becomes the metaphor for your relationship with God."[^1] God is recast in the role of the imperial overlord who "comes in and says, 'This sure is a lovely establishment… I would hate for something bad to happen to it.'"[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Covenant inherits the treaty's "love" language.** The vassal's required "love" of the suzerain ([[Esarhaddon]]'s treaty §24, "love Ashurbanipal… like yourselves") becomes the covenant-love of [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deut 6:4]]'s Shema — loyalty owed to a sovereign, not affection (compare the vault's [[Shema Echad Means Covenant Loyalty Not Ontology]]).[^3]
2. **The structure is the treaty's structure.** Parties, exclusive-loyalty stipulations, divine witnesses, and blessing/curse sanctions are the treaty form transposed onto God and Israel — see the detailed parallels in [[Deuteronomy Was Patterned After the Vassal Treaty of Esarhaddon]].[^4]
3. **The "stick" outweighs the "carrot."** As in the treaty, the biblical covenant is mostly threat: Deuteronomy's blessings are a "token carrot" against dominating curses (Deut 28). McClellan reads this lopsidedness as the imprint of an extortion-style instrument — "the real point is the stick."[^5]
4. **The Jesus-at-the-door meme as the same logic.** McClellan likens it to "Jesus knocking on the door… 'let me in' — why? so that I can protect you against what I'm going to do to you if you don't let me in" — the protection-racket grammar of vassalage carried into Christian piety.[^6]

## LDS Relevance
The existing temple-apologetics note [[Ancient Covenant Pattern and the Blood of the Covenant]] already grants that the biblical covenant template was inherited from the ancient Near East, naming "the Assyrian vassal treaties" explicitly and using their *simile-curse* logic to defend (not undercut) the temple's former symbolic penalties. McClellan's framing pushes harder: if covenant is *borrowed imperial machinery*, then covenant theology is culturally derived rather than uniquely revealed. LDS engagement can run two ways. (a) Continuity-friendly: an authentic ANE pedigree is exactly what a genuinely ancient covenant religion (and a genuinely ancient Book of Mormon) should display — the form being borrowed says nothing about whether God adopted it. (b) Pressure point: the "God as protection-racket suzerain" reading sits uneasily with LDS emphasis on covenant as a relationship of mutual love and exaltation rather than coerced tribute, so the apologist must distinguish *form* (borrowed) from *theological content* (transformed) — the same move made for the covenant simile-curse and for [[Shema Echad Means Covenant Loyalty Not Ontology]].
