# Shedim Are Foreign Deities Not Demons

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that [[Deuteronomy 32.17|Deuteronomy 32:17]] ("they sacrificed to demons that were no gods") shows the biblical "other gods" are really demons — created malevolent spirits behind pagan worship — and that Paul confirms this ([[1 Corinthians 10.20|1 Cor 10:20]]), so the gods of the nations are demons and do not threaten monotheism.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. *Shedim* does not mean "demons" (rating: strong; lexical)
The Hebrew *shedim* (Deut 32:17) is "a specific class of deity," only *traditionally* translated "demons" because that is how the Septuagint translators (c. mid-3rd century BCE) rendered it with the Greek vocabulary available to them. Earlier Aramaic evidence — the **Deir Alla inscription** and related texts — indicates *shedim* are classes of **foreign deity**, not demons. [^1]

> "I would point out that shedim doesn't really mean demons it's a specific class of deity and it is only traditionally translated as demons because that's how... the translators of the septu agents around the middle of the 3D Century BCE are trying to interpret this text" [^1]

### 2. The "demon" category is a Greco-Roman-period innovation (rating: strong; historical)
There is no good case that the Hebrew Bible has a concept of "demons" as later Christianity understands them; the demonology is a theological innovation of the Hellenistic/Greco-Roman period (cf. the development traced via the [[Book of Enoch - Origin of the Satan Figure|Book of Enoch]]). [^2]

> "there's not a really good case to make that the Hebrew Bible has any idea about these things called demons that's a theological innovation of the GRE Roman period" [^2]

### 3. The translation Ortlund displayed mispunctuates the verse (rating: moderate; Heiser cited *for* McClellan)
Ortlund's on-screen translation reads "demons that were no gods," making the author seem to deny the *shedim* their divinity. McClellan argues the clause should read "to *shedim* and **not** *Eloah*" (i.e., to demons/foreign deities rather than to Yahweh) — and cites a paper by [[Michael Heiser]] as "a very good argument for why that should be the case." [^3]

> "it should actually say to shedim and not capital G God in other words to Demons rather than to Adonai the god of Israel" [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's position is bolstered by Paul, who in [[1 Corinthians 10.20|1 Cor 10:20]] explicitly says pagan sacrifice is "to demons" — and Ortlund argues even a mistaken worshiper like Jezebel may have "a real entity at work" behind her cult. [^4] McClellan accepts that this concedes the *target is a real entity, not the idol* (which actually helps his idol-polemic point), but insists the argument illegitimately retrojects the 3rd-century-BCE-and-later "god = demon" interpretation back over every earlier reference to other gods, "and once again that is anachronistic." [^5] See [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]].

## Best Quotes

> "according to earlier Aramaic texts like the dear Allah in scription and others suggest that they are classes of foreign deity rather than something that we would now identify as a demon" [^1]

> "this argument has to try to take that Greco Roman period interpretation of the shedim and these Gods as demons and retroject it over all of the references to the other gods so that any reference to Gods Must Be either Angels or demons and once again that is anachronistic" [^5]
