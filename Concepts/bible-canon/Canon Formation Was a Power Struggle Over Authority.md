# Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority

## The Point
[[Thomas A. Wayment]] argues the closing of the canon in the 4th century was the outcome of a **power struggle** between two camps: charismatic teachers (who put spiritual manifestation, allegory, and personal vision first) and bishops (who wanted strong, liturgically-controlled church governance). The bishops — Athanasius and his anti-Arian allies — won, and were thereby "able to control the canon and thereby limit the availability and authority of some Christian texts used by their antagonists." The canon "reflects a message of stability, consistency, and authority." Wayment nonetheless **problematizes** the popular notion that canonization was simply an attempt to squash an "authentic" early Christianity.

## Evidence

### 1. Two competing models of authority
Charismatic/Alexandrian teachers (Clement of Rome, Valentinus, Pantaenus, Ptolemy, Origen, Arius) ran school environments favoring new thinking, allegorical interpretation, and a broad canon that even included pagan philosophers' books. The anti-Arian bishops favored a **liturgically oriented** approach: by controlling how services were conducted and what was taught, "they were able to control what the masses heard in church." [^1]

### 2. The turning point: Arius "transgressed the limits"
The conflict crystallized "when Arius's teachings appeared to Alexander [Athanasius's predecessor] to transgress the limits of acceptable diversity" (quoting [[David Brakke]]). The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) denounced Arius and bolstered the church-minded party. [^2]

### 3. Athanasius: read the apostles, not living charismatics
In his second festal letter, Athanasius wrote that disciples "should of right be our teachers" are the New Testament authors — "eye-witnesses and ministers of the Word" — not charismatic teachers of one's own time. This positions the word of God and its approved interpretation as the unique authority while branding rivals as outsiders to "the true Nicene faith." [^3]

### 4. Condemning Origen's allegory
Epiphanius (d. AD 403) condemned [[Origen]]'s analytical and allegorical methods as unreliable and as yielding interpretations "outside the rule of faith." Origen was posthumously excommunicated at the Second Council of Constantinople (AD 553). The broad canon of the liberal Alexandrians is visible in the eclectic Nag Hammadi tractates (which even include copies of Plato). [^4]

### 5. Wayment's caveat — not a cover-up of "true" Christianity
Wayment explicitly resists the conspiratorial reading: the early commentators "recognized the need for something greater than a localized approach to canon," and the canon debate functioned to maintain "the purity and truth of the Church's teachings," not merely to suppress a hidden authentic faith. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Those who came out victorious, Athanasius and his allies, were able to control the canon and thereby limit the availability and authority of some Christian texts used by their antagonists." [^1]

> "the need for harmony and unity of message prevailed over the charismatic interests of the members, and the canon reflects a message of stability, consistency, and authority." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Wayment turns this toward [[Latter-day Saints]]: "In reopening the canon, Latter-day Saints have shifted authority away from those who support the traditional canon and relocated that authority in modern Latter-day Saint apostles and prophets." Both the open and closed canon are "part of larger structures of authority in the Church." See [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority]].
