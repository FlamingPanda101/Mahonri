# The Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament Are Not the Same Book

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues (per the session summary) that the Jewish **Tanakh** and the Christian **Old Testament** are not the same text, even though they share most of the same books. They differ in two structural ways and one translational way:

1. **Order of books.** The Christian Old Testament places the prophetic books last and ends with [[Malachi 4|Malachi]], whose closing prophecy of the returning Elijah Christians read as a bridge into the New Testament (Elijah → John the Baptist / Jesus). The whole collection is thereby shaped to point *forward* to Christ.
2. **The Tanakh's shape and endpoint.** The Tanakh has three divisions — Torah (Law), Nevi'im (Prophets), Ketuvim (Writings) — and ends not with Malachi but with [[2 Chronicles 36|2 Chronicles]], on the Persian king [[Cyrus the Great|Cyrus]] allowing the exiles to return and rebuild the temple. It closes on **restoration and historical hope**, not a messianic cliffhanger.
3. **Translation bias.** Christian translations frequently nudge the Hebrew toward Christian theology — the flagship case being [[Isaiah 7.14 Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated|Isaiah 7:14]] rendered "a virgin will conceive" where the Hebrew *almah* means "young woman."

So "the Old Testament" is already a Christianized object — the same books, re-sequenced and re-translated to read as prologue to the Gospel.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The order is demonstrably different and demonstrably consequential.** That the Tanakh ends on 2 Chronicles and the Christian OT on Malachi is not disputed; it is a fact of the two canons' structure. The *interpretive payoff* of each ending (forward-to-Christ vs. return-from-exile) follows directly from where the editors chose to stop. McClellan presents this as a clear illustration that canon *order* is itself a reading.
2. **The translation point is independently documented in the vault.** The Isaiah 7:14 case is worked out in full at [[Isaiah 7.14 Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated]]; the general phenomenon of theology steering renderings is documented at [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text]]. This source adds the framing that such manipulation is *systematic*, not incidental — the Christian OT is shaped to support Christian theology.
3. **It dovetails with the post-biblical-canon thesis.** That two traditions can carve the same materials into differently-ordered, differently-bounded canons presupposes that **the canon is a later editorial product**, not a divinely fixed list — see [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]] and the manuscript-choice point in [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]].

**Steelman of the traditional Christian view.** A Christian can grant the different ordering and still hold that the Christian sequence is *legitimate*, not deceptive: the Septuagint-influenced ordering is ancient (not a modern invention), and Christians read the Old Testament *as* promise-and-fulfillment on theological, not merely editorial, grounds. The Elijah-at-the-end framing is a real feature of Malachi, not smuggled in. McClellan's reply (per the summary) is not that the Christian reading is impossible but that it is a *reading* — the text does not arrange or translate itself, and presenting the Christianized shape as simply "what the Old Testament says" hides the interpretive hand.

## Apologetic Engagement
For traditional Christian apologetics this cuts against the habit of treating the Old Testament as a self-evidently Christ-shaped book. The Elijah-bookend and the *parthenos* of Isaiah 7:14 are not neutral data points that "happen" to point to Christ; they are the product of editorial sequencing and a particular translation choice. An apologist who appeals to "the Old Testament's own ending" as forward-pointing must reckon that the Jewish canon of the *same books* ends somewhere else entirely. The honest apologetic move is to argue for the Christian reading on its merits rather than to present it as the bare meaning of the text.

## LDS Relevance
The point lands differently for Latter-day Saints. (a) It reinforces the Restoration claim that scripture has been editorially shaped by communities with agendas — the same logic the vault applies to the Deuteronomistic reform (see [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]]) — so the idea that important meaning was reordered or translated *out* is historically ordinary, not special pleading. (b) The Tanakh's restoration-shaped ending (return from exile, rebuild the temple) resonates with the LDS emphasis on gathering and temple, an ending Latter-day Saints can read as theologically congenial even though McClellan draws no confessional conclusion. (c) It supports the LDS instinct that no single translation is sacrosanct — the recent LDS multi-translation policy already concedes the KJV's Christianizing renderings are choices among others.

## Best Quotes

> The Christian rendering of *almah* as "virgin" at Isaiah 7:14 is offered as the primary example of how "Christian translations frequently manipulate the Hebrew to support Christian theology" (per the session summary).
