# The Assumption of Moses Was the Fifth Book of Moses Replaced by Deuteronomy

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] (in his book *Key to the Keystone*, presented on [[Ward Radio]] with [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Dave Butler]]) argues that the "five books of Moses" on Lehi's [[brass plates]] were **not** the canonical Pentateuch. The fifth book was not **Deuteronomy** but the now-lost **Assumption (Testament) of Moses** — Moses's dying testament delivered before Israel entered Canaan. On this reconstruction the [[Josiah]]/Deuteronomist reform later overwrote Moses's last words with Deuteronomy, a bloated, scribe-serving law-book that recasts God "in the image of the scribe." Barnes frames the whole thesis with a self-deprecating "please don't excommunicate me" disclaimer: it is his own provocative, explicitly speculative reading, not settled history.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "five books of Moses" are never enumerated — rate: moderate (textual argument)
[[1 Nephi 5.11|1 Nephi 5:11]] says the brass plates contained "the five books of Moses" but does **not** list them. Barnes proposes Genesis–Exodus–Leviticus–Numbers plus the **Assumption of Moses** in the fifth slot, arguing that a late vassal-treaty law-book (Deuteronomy) is too late and too redundant to be one of Moses's own five books carried by Lehi out of Jerusalem.[^1]

### 2. Deuteronomy has a suspicious "discovery" story — rate: moderate (source-critical)
[[2 Kings 22|2 Kings 22]] has the scribal establishment "find" a self-exalting book of the law in the temple and press it on the boy-king [[Josiah]] — a book conveniently discovered, authenticated, and benefiting the scribes who produced it. Barnes reads this as the historical moment the Deuteronomist reform replaced the older fifth book of Moses. (This dovetails with the existing Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament cluster.)[^2]

### 3. Nephi's sermon tracks the Assumption of Moses, not Deuteronomy — rate: moderate (parallel)
Critics cite the "a prophet like unto me" line in [[1 Nephi 22.20-21|1 Nephi 22:20-21]] (paralleling [[Deuteronomy 18.15|Deuteronomy 18:15]]) as proof Deuteronomy was on the brass plates. Barnes counters that the *preceding* verses of Nephi's sermon — the scattering of Israel, the Gentiles, Satan bound, blood/fire/vapor of smoke — track the **Assumption of Moses** almost in order, and that the "prophet like me" prophecy likely sat in the *torn-off* ending of the Assumption of Moses rather than originating in Deuteronomy.[^2]

### 4. Moses was translated, not buried — and the Book of Mormon agrees — rate: strong (internal contradiction)
[[Deuteronomy 34.5-6|Deuteronomy 34:5-6]] says Moses "died... and he buried him." But [[Alma 45.18-19|Alma 45:18-19]] takes for granted that "the Lord took Moses unto himself" (translation), and notes "the saying went abroad in the church" — i.e. it was *common* Nephite knowledge, implying it came from their brass-plates source. This matches the New Testament (Moses at the Transfiguration; [[Jude]]'s dispute over the body of Moses) and the Assumption of Moses tradition. So Deuteronomy, not the Book of Mormon, is the outlier on Moses's end.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The thesis is **avowedly speculative** and Barnes flags it as such. (a) The mainstream identification of Josiah's "found book" with Deuteronomy is a *literary–historical* judgment that says nothing about a suppressed Assumption of Moses on Israelite plates. (b) The surviving Assumption/Testament of Moses is a first-century-CE Jewish pseudepigraphon (discovered by Antonio Ceriani, published 1861) — so positing a much earlier form on Lehi's plates is an inference beyond the manuscript evidence. (c) The "prophet like me" line is more naturally read as Deuteronomic; relocating it to a lost ending is unfalsifiable. What survives is the narrower, better-grounded point — the Book of Mormon and New Testament independently assume Moses was *translated*, putting Deuteronomy 34's burial notice in genuine tension with them — even if the full "fifth book swap" remains Barnes's provocative reconstruction.

## Best Quote
> The fifth book on the brass plates was not Deuteronomy but the lost Assumption of Moses — Moses's dying testament — and the Deuteronomist overwrote it. — [[Jonah Barnes]] *(thesis paraphrase)* [^1]
