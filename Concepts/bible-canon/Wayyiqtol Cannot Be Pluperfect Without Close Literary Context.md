# Wayyiqtol Cannot Be Pluperfect Without Close Literary Context

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

To dissolve the contradiction that Genesis 2:19 creates the birds *after* the human (versus day five in Genesis 1), apologists and translations (ESV, LSB, NIV) read the opening *wayyiqtol* verb of 2:19 as an English pluperfect: "Now the Lord God *had formed* every animal of the field." This relocates the animal/bird creation *before* the events narrated in Genesis 2, removing the contradiction.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The *wayyiqtol* is not the pluperfect construction (strong, grammatical).** McClellan: "the wiktol is not how you form a pl perfect... It only happens when close literary context demands it. And in Genesis 21:19, there is no such close literary context."[^2] The *wayyiqtol* is the default Hebrew narrative-sequence form (consecutive past/preterite); a pluperfect sense is the marked exception, licensed only when the surrounding clauses force it.

2. **The academic pluperfect cases are circular (decisive).** "The academic cases that have been made for reading it as a ploo perfect require you understand Genesis 1 to be the close literary context. In other words, it's pretty circular reasoning. You already have to imagine that Genesis 1 was written by the same authors as Genesis 2 in order for the argument to work."[^3] The proposed solution presupposes the univocality it is meant to prove.

3. **Rate the evidence: strong but contestable.** The grammatical default and the charge of circularity are both sound. The weak point is that some grammarians (e.g., on Genesis 2:8 "had planted") do allow a pluperfect *wayyiqtol* on discourse grounds; McClellan's case rests on there being *no independent* (non-question-begging) literary trigger in 2:19.

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Standard reference grammars (e.g., Joüon–Muraoka, Waltke–O'Connor) recognize that *wayyiqtol* can be used "out of sequence" for flashback/recapitulation when the discourse logic requires it, and Genesis 2:8/2:19 are stock examples. A defender argues the recapitulation reading is therefore not an ad hoc rescue but an attested usage. McClellan's reply is that the *only* discourse trigger available is Genesis 1 itself — and invoking it to harmonize Genesis 2 with Genesis 1 begs the question.
