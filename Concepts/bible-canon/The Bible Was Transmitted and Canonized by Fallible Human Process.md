# The Bible Was Transmitted and Canonized by Fallible Human Process

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the Bible reached us "by hand across 15 centuries before the printing press," with "no original manuscripts" surviving — "copies of copies" across multiple languages, made by people "under varying conditions of training, time, and theological motivation," some changes "intentional."[^1] [[Bruce Metzger]] (Princeton, "a committed Christian") catalogued the result and "used the word *corruption*... in the title of his foundational work."[^1] There are "over 400,000 variant readings across New Testament manuscripts alone... larger than the New Testament itself"; ~99% are trivial, but "approximately 4,000 significant variants," several affecting "major doctrines."[^2] His examples:
- **[[1 John 5.7|1 John 5:7]]** (the Johannine Comma), "the most explicitly Trinitarian verse," which "does not appear in any Greek manuscript before the 15th century" — [[Erasmus]] excluded it until pressured by a manuscript "now known to be a 16th century forgery." [^2]
- The **woman caught in adultery** (John 8) and **Mark's long ending** (16:9-20), absent from the oldest complete manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus). [^2]

On the **canon**: "it wasn't decided all at once or cleanly or by any divine declaration," but "over centuries through councils, disputes, and judgment calls" — Catholics 73 books, Protestants 66 ([[Martin Luther]] "removed those seven in the 16th century"), Eastern Orthodox 78–81, Ethiopian 81+. "These are literally all different Bibles," and "the Bible itself does not contain a list of which books should be in the Bible" — a foundational problem for *sola scriptura*.[^3] Translation compounds it: [[Isaiah 7.14|Isaiah 7:14]]'s *alma* ("young woman") became Greek *parthenos* ("virgin"), "a translation choice... driven by theological application rather than the Hebrew original."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The manuscript facts are mainstream.** No autographs; ~400,000 variants; the trivial-vs-significant breakdown; and [[Bart Ehrman|Ehrman]]'s and [[Bruce Metzger|Metzger]]'s documentation are standard textual criticism, and the [[1 John 5-7 - Johannine Comma Interpolation|Comma Johanneum]], pericope adulterae, and longer Markan ending are textbook examples. *Rating: solid, non-controversial as data.* [^1][^2]
2. **The canon-diversity facts are checkable.** The differing book-counts across Catholic/Protestant/Orthodox/Ethiopian Bibles are simply true and countable. *Rating: accurate.* [^3]
3. **The Isaiah 7:14 *alma*/*parthenos* point is well documented.** The Hebrew/Greek shift and Matthew's use of the LXX are standard, and the vault treats it in [[Isaiah 7.14 Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated]]. *Rating: accurate (the *theological consequence* is debated — see steelman).* [^4]
4. **The *sola scriptura* tension is genuine.** "You cannot use the canon to establish the canon" is a real and much-discussed problem; the vault develops it in its canon cluster ([[Canon - Development and Closure]]) and feeds it into [[The Restoration Answers an Authority Problem Scholarship Cannot Solve]]. *Rating: a strong structural point.* [^3]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A conservative/evangelical scholar replies: (a) **variants ≠ corruption of doctrine** — the very abundance of manuscripts lets critics *reconstruct* the text with high confidence; the ~4,000 "significant" variants are mostly *known* (the Comma, the pericope, Mark's ending are *flagged in every modern Bible*), and no *cardinal* doctrine rests on a disputed reading, so "corruption" (Metzger's technical term for any scribal change) is rhetorically inflated when applied to *meaning*; (b) **canon recognition vs. creation** — many argue the church *recognized* (did not *invent*) books by apostolicity and catholicity, and the *core* (four Gospels, Paul) was stable by the 2nd century, so "all different Bibles" overstates a disagreement confined largely to the Deutero-/apocryphal margins; (c) **Isaiah 7:14** — *alma* denotes a young woman who in that culture was *presumptively* a virgin, the LXX *parthenos* predates Christianity (so it is not a Christian fudge), and Matthew may invoke a *sensus plenior*, so "mistranslation driven by theology" is contestable; (d) the *sola scriptura* canon problem has Protestant answers (self-authentication, providential preservation). A Latter-day Saint reply: granting that the *core message* survives, the cumulative force is that the text is demonstrably a *human-mediated, edited, and selected* artifact — exactly what the Restoration's "plain and precious things... taken away" (1 Nephi 13) predicts — so the *category* of loss-and-recovery is historically warranted; and the canon problem is not dissolved by "recognition" language, since *which* church, by *what* authority, recognized *which* list is precisely the authority question — see [[The Restoration Answers an Authority Problem Scholarship Cannot Solve]].
