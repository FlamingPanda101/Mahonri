# Luke 2-49 May Read Among My Fathers People Not About My Fathers Business

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] argue that the famous boy-Jesus line in Luke 2:49 — KJV "I must be about my Father's business" — is a translator's guess, because the Greek (*en tois tou patros mou*) leaves the noun unexpressed. They propose the most natural supply is "among my Father's **people**," which would mean Jesus was found among temple teachers who, as carriers of an older tradition, recognized him.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Greek has no noun (rated: grammatical).** *En tois tou patros mou* means "in the … of my Father"; the article *tois* is dative plural, masculine or neuter, and "you have to guess what the missing noun is."[^1]
2. **"House" is a poor fit (rated: lexical).** "My Father's house" requires a neuter plural for the temple, but the New Testament refers to the temple with a *singular* (*to hieron* / *ton naon*), "so why would it in this one passage talk about the temple as a plural?"[^2]
3. **"People" matches verse 44 (rated: contextual).** Reading *tois* as masculine yields "among my Father's people." When Joseph and Mary cannot find Jesus they look "among their kinsfolk" (v. 44); they then find him "among his Father's *real* father's people," the temple teachers (*didaskaloi*, v. 46) who were "astonished at his understanding."[^3]
4. **An old-tradition recognition scene (rated: the hosts' reading).** The hosts tie this to [[Aubrey Johnson]]'s cultic prophets and to the temple figures who recognize the infant Jesus (Simeon, and *Anna* — "which is just Hannah"): perhaps "there were people in the time of Jesus associated with the temple who were still holding on to these old traditions," and that is why they are amazed.[^4]
5. **A noted-but-rejected option (rated: scholarly acknowledgment).** Raymond Brown observed that "his Father's people" was a translation option but rejected it, judging Jesus could not refer to the temple establishment with approval — a judgment the hosts dispute by positing old-tradition insiders rather than the establishment.[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** The standard scholarly rendering is "in my Father's house," which is grammatically defensible (the idiom *en tois + genitive* can mean "in the [house/affairs] of") and fits the temple setting; Raymond Brown and most translators reject "among my Father's people." The "people" reading is a minority option, and the further claim that those people were old-tradition temple insiders who recognized Jesus is the hosts' speculation layered on top. The ambiguity of the Greek is real; the preferred resolution is contested, and the "old-tradition insiders" gloss is extra-textual.

## Best Quotes
> "The Greek says *en tois tou patros mou*, which literally means 'in the … of my Father,' and you have to guess what the missing noun is."[^1]

> "The passage might say, 'didn't you know I would be among my Father's people?' … when they can't find him, where do they look? Among their kinsfolk."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is a translation-ambiguity argument in the family of the vault's [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]]: the received "business"/"house" rendering is a choice, not the text. Read with the cultic-prophet thesis, it supports the picture of a surviving first-temple remnant in Jesus' day who recognized him — connecting to [[Cultic Prophets Were Singing Temple Musicians]] and [[Margaret Barker]]'s "somebody remembers the first-temple religion."
