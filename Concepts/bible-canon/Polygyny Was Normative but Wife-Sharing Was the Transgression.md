# Polygyny Was Normative but Wife-Sharing Was the Transgression

## The Claim
In the patriarchal narratives, a single man taking **multiple wives is treated as unremarkable** ("polygamy is normative in this time period"); what generates scandal and divine punishment is not plural marriage but **two men laying claim to the same woman**.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Polygyny passes without comment (rating: strong; narrative-internal)
The Genesis narrator never flags a man's having several wives or concubines as a problem — Abraham with Sarah and Hagar, Jacob with Leah and Rachel and their handmaids. [[Dan McClellan]] states the baseline plainly while reading the Egypt episode: "polygamy is normative in this time period."[^1]

### 2. The real problem is overlapping claims (rating: strong; narrative-internal)
What McClellan singles out as "problematic" is "for two different men to be sharing the same wife." When Pharaoh ([[Genesis 12]]) and Abimelech ([[Genesis 20]]) move to take a patriarch's wife, the deity intervenes with plagues and a death-threat dream — not because a man has many wives, but because *this* woman is already covenanted to another man.[^1][^2]

> "polygamy is normative in this time period, but... for two different men to be sharing the same wife is problematic." [^1]

### 3. The "married woman" framing of the offense (rating: moderate; philological)
In [[Genesis 20]] God's indictment of Abimelech is precise: "You are about to die because of the woman you have taken, for she is a **married woman**." The transgression is defined by her prior marital bond, and God claims credit for *preventing* Abimelech from touching her — the violation would have been the wife-sharing, not the plurality.[^2]

### 4. Coheres with Hebrew Bible portrayals of plural marriage elsewhere (rating: moderate; comparative)
This matches the matter-of-fact plural-marriage world reflected in texts like [[Song of Songs 6.8-9|Song of Songs 6:8–9]] (sixty queens, eighty concubines) and the patriarchal/royal polygyny later invoked in [[Doctrine and Covenants 132]] ("Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, as also Moses, David and Solomon... their having many wives and concubines"). The Bible's own moral attention falls on adultery and contested claims, not on the number of a man's wives.

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
One could argue the episodes do not endorse polygyny so much as ignore it, and that later biblical and prophetic critique (e.g., the dangers of Solomon's many wives) shows an arc *away* from polygyny. That is fair as a trajectory across the canon; the narrower claim here is only about the patriarchal narratives' own assumptions, where the text registers no objection to plural wives as such.

## LDS Relevance
This distinction is directly useful to Latter-day Saint engagement with plural marriage. Critics often treat any plurality of wives as self-evidently the offense; the Genesis material instead locates the wrongdoing in **wife-sharing and adultery**, framing plural marriage itself as a culturally normal institution. That is the same patriarchal precedent [[Doctrine and Covenants 132]] explicitly appeals to in justifying [[Joseph Smith]]'s practice. Compare the LDS-historical arguments that plural marriage was covenantal/sealing rather than libertine ([[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]], [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem]]).
