# Standing Stone (Massebah) as Material Medium of Divine Presence

## The Claim

In early Israelite and Judahite religion, "seeing the face of God" in the temple meant gazing upon a **divine image** — most plausibly an upright cult stone (a *massebah*, "standing stone"). The stone was not God but the **material medium** that transmitted the divine presence; to see the standing stone was to see the face of God. [[Dan McClellan]] grounds this in the excavated standing stone from the Judahite temple at [[Arad Temple Standing Stone|Arad]].[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Archaeology: a standing stone in a Holy of Holies.** A massebah was found in the inner sanctuary of the Judahite temple at Arad, demonstrating that Israelite/Judahite temples did house cult stones.[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — the Arad sanctuary and its standing stone are well-published archaeological data.)*

2. **God conceived as corporeal but located elsewhere.** McClellan: "they certainly understood God to be corporeal in this time period but to be located elsewhere, and the standing stone was really the material... medium that transmitted the divine presence." To be in the Holy of Holies was to be in God's presence, sensed more than gazed upon, with the stone as the material manifestation.[^3] *(Evidence rating: this is the mainstream view of an embodied, locatable deity in early Israelite religion — see [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]].)*

3. **It connects vision-language to a concrete cult object.** Isaiah 1:12's "to see my face" is set "about coming into my temple" / "trample my courts no more," i.e., the vision is a temple act directed at the cult installation, not an abstract apparition.[^4] *(Evidence rating: contextual, coherent.)*

4. **No anthropomorphic statuary recovered.** McClellan notes we have *not* recovered anthropomorphic divine statues from ancient Israel/Judah; the standing stone is non-representational, though anthropomorphic features (e.g., painted eyes/face) could in principle have been added — none have survived.[^5] *(Evidence rating: an argument from current evidence; see [[De Facto Aniconism Mettinger]] for the explanatory framework.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A confessional reader could object that the biblical writers themselves condemn cult stones and images (Deut 16:21–22; the aniconic polemic), so reading the temple "face of God" as a literal stone imposes a banned practice onto orthodox worship. McClellan's reply (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]) is that the anti-image polemic is a *later* redactional layer, and the earlier strata plus the archaeology indicate cult images were in fact used.
