# The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the second judgment is which **manuscripts** you use "to cobble together your canon," and like the others it has no objectively correct answer.[^1] Even with a fixed 66-book Protestant canon, you must choose: for the Hebrew Bible, the **Masoretic** text (e.g., the Leningrad Codex) or readings from the **Dead Sea Scrolls** or the **Septuagint**? For the New Testament, a **critical text** (UBS 4/5, NA 28/29) or the **Textus Receptus** — and which edition?[^2] Different choices yield materially different Bibles. See [[Three Judgments Stand Between You and a Bible]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The choices are real and consequential — rate: strong
McClellan notes the Jewish Study Bible / JPS Tanakh sticks "dogmatically to the Leningrad Codex" and resists altering readings toward the Septuagint or Dead Sea Scrolls, whereas Greek Orthodox tradition uses the **Septuagint** as its Old Testament — so "their Old Testament is going to be very different from the Old Testament of an English-speaking Protestant."[^3] This is uncontroversial in textual criticism: the LXX differs from the MT in order, length, and content of whole books. **Steelman of the believer:** a confessional reader can argue the differences are minor relative to the message, and that text-critical method (e.g., reasoned eclecticism) approximates an *original* text, recovering a real object. McClellan's point is narrower — there is no agreed base, so the resulting Bible is still a *choice*, not a given.

### 2. Least-known of the three judgments — rate: moderate
McClellan observes the manuscript question is "the one that the people are least aware of, least familiar with."[^4] Most evangelicals have a say in translation, less in canon, and "are not even aware of and have little say in the manuscript question."[^4] KJV-onlyists are an exception because the critical-text-vs-Textus-Receptus dispute is built into their dogma; see [[KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text]].

### 3. A Greek translation of a Greek book — rate: moderate
McClellan owns a diglot with the Septuagint on one page and a *modern Greek translation* on the facing page; even native Greek speakers in the Orthodox tradition consume the LXX in translation.[^3] [[Dan Beecher]] highlights the irony of "a Greek translation of a Greek book," underscoring that the manuscript and translation questions interlock — there is no unmediated text. See [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

## Best Quotes

> "Are you going to go from for the Hebrew Bible, the Masoretic text? What New Testament critical text are you going to use?... Are you going to go back to the Textus Receptus? Which edition of the Textus Receptus?"[^2]
