# Son of Man - Development of the Title

## Overview

This is a hub note for the **development of the title "Son of Man"** from a collective image into a personalized heavenly mediator — and its significance for early Christology. The trajectory runs: Daniel 7:13–14 presents "one like a son of man" coming on the clouds to the Ancient of Days and receiving everlasting dominion, where the figure *represents* the righteous remnant of Israel. The Book of Parables (1 Enoch 37–71) then **personalizes** that figure into a specific, named, pre-existent individual — identified as Enoch himself (1 Enoch 71:14) — who is concealed until the eschaton, sits on the throne of glory, receives worship, and judges the wicked. The New Testament inherits this developed conceptual pool when Jesus adopts "the Son of Man" as his preferred self-designation.

The flagship source is [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]], reinforced by [[Christology - Enochic Background]]: Jesus' title carries Danielic/Enochic resonances of authority, hiddenness, pre-existence, and eschatological judgment, while also retaining Ezekiel's plainer sense ("human one"). A one-letter Old Greek variant in Daniel 7 (πρός / ὡς) even blurs the Son of Man *into* the Ancient of Days — an ambiguity 1 Enoch exploits and early Christians later applied to Jesus.

## The Steelman

A defender argues Jesus draws *directly* from Daniel without Enochic mediation, and notes that the Parables (1 Enoch 37–71) are precisely the section *absent* from the Qumran Enoch fragments — so a late-1st-century date could make them contemporaneous with, not prior to, the Gospels. The cautious form of the claim (granted across the cluster) is that 1 Enoch evidences a *milieu* in which a heavenly, pre-existent Son of Man was already conceivable; the strong claim of direct literary borrowing is more contested.
