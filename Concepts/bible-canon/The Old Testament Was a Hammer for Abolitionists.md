# The Old Testament Was a Hammer for Abolitionists

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (O'Connor): the Exodus is Israelite-specific and the black church "misread" it as universal abolition; the OT endorses slavery — see [[The Exodus Is Israelite-Specific Not Universal Abolition]].

## The Rebuttal

[[Glenn Scrivener]]: the enslaved themselves found in the Old Testament a profound resource for liberation — it was "a hammer in the hands of the abolitionists," not a Stockholm-syndrome misreading.

1. **[[Frederick Douglass]] and the image of God.** Douglass said "the image of God on page one of the Bible was a hammer in the hands of the abolitionists." [[Olaudah Equiano]]: "if God did not make man slave, how dare man make man slave" — drawn from [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1's]] making humanity to "have dominion rather than to be slaves."[^1] *(Strong — the imago Dei argument was central to actual abolitionist rhetoric.)*

2. **Exodus-shaped black spirituality.** The Negro spirituals were "very profoundly Old Testament focused"; [[Harriet Tubman]] was "the great Moses" of the Underground Railroad. Scrivener: "I don't think it's Stockholm syndrome — it's a profound sense of feeling understood as an oppressed people in a furnace of affliction."[^2] *(Strong — the historical reception of Exodus by the enslaved is well-documented and overwhelmingly liberationist.)*

3. **The Jubilee trajectory.** [[Leviticus 25.10|Leviticus 25]]'s Jubilee (debt cancellation, release) and the OT's priority for "the poor, the widow, and the sojourner" point beyond Israel-specific release toward Christ as universal Jubilee.[^3] *(Coherent within the canonical reading; see [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]].)*

4. **Steelman of O'Connor.** O'Connor maintains a "thorough reading of the Old Testament" shows the Exodus is covenantal and Israel-specific (freed Hebrews take foreign slaves per [[Leviticus 25.44-46|Lev 25:44-46]]); the enslaved who universalized it were reading *against* the plain sense — even if their reading was morally admirable and existentially powerful.[^4] Scrivener flatly disagrees: "The black church got it right." *(This is the live exegetical clash: whose reading is the *correct* one — the historical-critical Israelite-specific sense, or the canonical-Christian universalized sense. Both grant the abolitionists were Christians using the Bible; they dispute whether the Bible "really" says it.)*

## Best Quotes
