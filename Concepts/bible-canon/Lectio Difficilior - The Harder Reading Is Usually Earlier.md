# Lectio Difficilior - The Harder Reading Is Usually Earlier

## The Claim

A core principle of textual criticism — *lectio difficilior potior*, "the more difficult reading is the stronger" — holds that when two manuscript readings compete, the **harder** one (the more awkward, theologically uncomfortable, or contradiction-producing reading) is usually the **earlier** one. The reason is the direction of scribal change: copyists tend to alter a text toward what is *easier to accept and more theologically comfortable*, not the reverse. McClellan stresses this is "not a hard and fast rule" but a probabilistic principle that helps reconstruct the earlier text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Scribes smooth, they don't roughen
When a text is changed, the change generally runs from a more difficult reading to a less difficult one — making the passage easier to swallow and doctrinally tidier. A reading that *creates* a problem is unlikely to have been introduced on purpose; a reading that *resolves* a problem is exactly what a harmonizing scribe would produce.[^1]

### 2. Worked example — Goliath's slayer
Between [[2 Samuel 21.19|2 Samuel 21:19]] (Elhanan killed **Goliath**) and [[1 Chronicles 20.5|1 Chronicles 20:5]] (Elhanan killed **Lahmi the brother of Goliath**), the Samuel reading is the difficult one: it flatly contradicts the famous David-and-Goliath story of 1 Samuel 17. The Chronicles reading is the easy one: it dissolves the contradiction. By *lectio difficilior*, the contradiction-bearing Samuel text is the likely earlier reading — which converges with the other lines of evidence (the post-exilic square-script confusion, the impossible "Lahmi" name) to mark Chronicles as the secondary, harmonized text. See [[Elhanan Killed Goliath - Chronicles Harmonized the Contradiction]].

### 3. It is one tool among several, not a trump card
McClellan presents the principle as *converging* with — not replacing — the script-history argument and the onomastic (naming) argument. "All of the methodologies we can bring to bear on the question strongly suggest" the Chronicles text is corrupt.[^2] The principle's value is that it supplies an independent reason to prefer the harder reading, so that multiple methods point the same way.

### Steelman of the opposing view
*Lectio difficilior* can be abused. Scribes also introduce errors that *accidentally* make a text harder (slips, dittography, misread letters), so a "harder" reading can be a late corruption rather than an early original — which is why text critics call it a guideline, not a law, and weigh it against external (manuscript) evidence and the specific kind of change at issue. A defender of inerrancy could note that the principle alone cannot decide a case. The critical reply is precisely that no one is resting the Goliath verdict on this principle alone: it is the *combination* of script history, name plausibility, and the harder reading that makes the judgment "almost certain," and *lectio difficilior* simply rules out the easy assumption that the smoother Chronicles text is the original.

## LDS Relevance

The principle is a concrete demonstration of why Article of Faith 8's qualifier — the Bible is the word of God "as far as it is translated correctly" — is methodologically sound rather than a dodge. Transmission demonstrably moves the text away from its earlier form in identifiable, rule-governed ways, and critical tools can often recover the earlier reading. For a Latter-day Saint this is congenial: it gives a principled, non-arbitrary way to recognize where the received text has drifted, and it undercuts the assumption — central to strict inerrancy — that the canonical wording must always be the original wording. It also exposes the harmonizing impulse as the very mechanism that *generates* the smoother readings (see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]).

## Best Quotes

> "What textual critics call *lectio difficilior*, the more difficult reading... usually when texts change they change from a more difficult reading to a less difficult reading. In other words, when people are going to change the text they're going to change it to make it easier to accept and more theologically comfortable." [^1]

> "The more difficult reading and therefore the likely earlier reading — not definitely earlier but likely earlier reading, based on this text-critical principle — would be 2 Samuel 21:19." [^1]
