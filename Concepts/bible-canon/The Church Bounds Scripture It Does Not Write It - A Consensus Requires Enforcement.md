# The Church Bounds Scripture It Does Not Write It — A Consensus Requires Enforcement

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] grants the apologist's slogan that "the church didn't *write* scripture" but denies the inference Wes Huff draws from it — that the church therefore only *recognized* a canon it did not shape. McClellan's thesis: a canon **is** a consensus, and a consensus over disputed boundaries cannot exist without a social-institutional framework to **facilitate** it, **authorize** it, and **enforce** it. Therefore the church played a *determinative* role — not in authoring the texts, but in drawing and policing the boundary around what was "allowed to be called scripture." "Without the church, you don't have a New Testament canon — or at least you don't have widespread agreement about a New Testament canon."[^1] This directly inverts Huff's "the Bible made the church, the church didn't make the Bible."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Canon is by definition an agreement, not a discovery — rate: strong
McClellan's core move is conceptual: "the notion of a canon is the notion of a consensus, an agreement about what is going to be recognized as inspired and authoritative. And that cannot happen without some kind of social institutional framework."[^3] The texts pre-existed the debate, but "the discussion and the debate that resulted in the consensus view, that was then authorized and enforced by that institution, absolutely requires the church."[^1] This is the descriptive-historical consensus and it neutralizes the gravity/postal-service analogies (Newton recognized a mind-independent force; canon is not mind-independent). The *strong* form of the claim — that institutional authority is "a necessary ingredient in the recipe" — is hard to dispute once "canon" is defined as a settled, enforced boundary rather than the mere existence of revered texts.

### 2. Enforcement was coercive, not merely declarative — rate: distinctive
McClellan goes further than the Catholic "the church *declares* the canon" model (cf. [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]], [[Thermometer vs Thermostat - The Church Declares the Canon]]). He stresses the *coercive* machinery: "you would not have had the vilification and the criminalization of other texts resulting in their burial and their abandonment. You would not have a centralized authority that could say this is the agreement — you will sign on the dotted line or you will no longer be welcome here."[^4] This sharpens [[Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority|Wayment's power-struggle account]] from the academic-biblical-studies side: the canon's closure is partly a story of which texts were *suppressed*, not only which were chosen. **Steelman of Huff/Packer:** the recognition model says inspired books bear intrinsic marks the community *discovers*, so enforcement is downstream pastoral housekeeping, not the thing that makes a book scripture — McClellan grants books pre-exist but answers that *recognition without an authorized, enforced boundary never yields a closed list*, which is what "canon" denotes.

### 3. The church "wrote it / bounded it" distinction is the real fault line — rate: strong
McClellan concedes the half Huff is right about and isolates the half he is wrong about: "It's fine to say the church didn't write scripture. It is entirely false to say the church did not play a determinative role in facilitating, authorizing, and enforcing the declaration of what is and is not allowed to be considered scripture."[^5] Granting authorship to the inspired writers while assigning *boundary-setting* to the institution dissolves the equivocation in "the church is the servant of scripture, not its origin" — origin of the *texts*, yes; origin of the *canon*, no.

## LDS Relevance
This is a direct asset for the LDS open-canon position. If the boundary of scripture was set by an authorized, enforcing institution rather than self-evident from the texts, then canon-closure is an exercise of *ecclesiastical authority*, not a brute fact — which is exactly the LDS claim. A church that can *close* a canon by authority can, in principle, *reopen* it by authority; the locus of authority is living and institutional, not a sealed deposit. This is the descriptive premise that [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority|Wayment turns toward the Restoration]]: both the closed and the open canon are "part of larger structures of authority." The double edge: McClellan's coercion emphasis ("sign on the dotted line") is the same critique reopening a canon must answer about *its own* authority.

## Best Quotes
> "It is an agreement about what texts are going to be recognized as inspired and authoritative. It is an agreement that took centuries to arrive at and required a centralized ecclesiastical authority in order to facilitate, authorize, and enforce that agreement."[^6]

> "The church did not write scripture, but the church did determine the boundaries around what was allowed to be called scripture."[^5]

> "You will sign on the dotted line or you will no longer be welcome here. We would not have all of that without the church."[^4]
