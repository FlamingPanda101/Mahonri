# Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship

## The Claim
Apologetics and critical biblical scholarship operate from fundamentally opposite methodologies. Apologists *begin with the conclusion* and work backward to construct arguments that make that conclusion at least *not impossible*. Critical scholars *allow the data to lead where it leads*, even when that direction challenges traditional beliefs.[^1]

## The Two Methods Contrasted

### Apologetics: Conclusion-First

> "Apologists begin with the conclusion in mind and then their goal is just to try to find a way around the data to generate an argument that sounds at least possible — to say the data do not make it impossible for me to arrive at the predetermined goal."[^1]

The bar is set at **mere possibility** ("it's not impossible"), which McClellan considers trivially easy to meet for almost any claim — invoking Russell's teapot: "There's an awful lot of stuff that's not impossible, and if that's the bar, it is underground."[^2]

### Critical Scholarship: Data-First

> "What critical scholars do is they will allow the data to operate on their own terms and allow the data to lead them where the data are going."[^1]

## Rhetorical Prophylaxis

McClellan coins the term **"rhetorical prophylaxis"** for a common apologetic tactic: claiming that critical scholars are "trying to destroy the Bible" or "undermine belief in God's inspired word." This functions as a preemptive barrier (*prophylaxis* = protection against harmful effects) that shields believers from engaging with scholarship:[^3]

> "Rhetorical prophylaxis is my term for when people use positions or arguments that only exist to deflect away criticism. ... It is a way for someone to dismiss critical scholarship so that it does not hurt their faith. It's not a legitimate claim or concern — it's a fallacy. But if you can convince enough people that these people are out to harm your faith, then they will immediately put up a barrier."

He notes he is accused daily of trying to "destroy the Bible or destroy Christianity or destroy Mormonism or destroy Judaism" — but destruction as an *effect* of scholarship is not the same as destruction being the *motive*.[^4]

## The gotquestions.org Example

McClellan references a common counter-claim from apologetics sites: that "the theory of multiple Isaiahs is one example of skepticism from those who want to call into question the Bible as God's inspired word." He responds that this is "making some pretty irresponsible assumptions about where these theories are coming from" — the scholars who developed these theories were not motivated by anti-Bible sentiment but by honest engagement with textual data. Many were (and are) believers themselves who wanted to understand the text better.[^5]

## Best Quote

> "Apologists begin with the conclusion in mind and then their goal is just to try to find a way around the data to generate an argument that sounds at least possible. ... What critical scholars do is they will allow the data to operate on their own terms and allow the data to lead them where the data are going. Those are two very different approaches — one is 'let's go see where this takes us,' the other is 'this is going to take us here and we just need to see how many train transfers and walking routes we have to take to get there.'"[^1]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-arguments
- Apologists would argue that critical scholarship is equally biased by its own methodological naturalism, and that "allowing the data to lead" is impossible without presuppositions.
- See [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship]] for the critical response.
