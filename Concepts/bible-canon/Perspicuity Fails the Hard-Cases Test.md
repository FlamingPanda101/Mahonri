# Perspicuity Fails the Hard-Cases Test

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] attacks the Westminster claim that things "necessary to be known, believed and observed for salvation" are so clear that "the unlearned, in a due use of the ordinary means, may attain a sufficient understanding." He grants some doctrines are clear (Jesus is Messiah; salvation through Christ) but argues Scripture is *not* clear enough on many salvation-relevant questions where sincere Protestants deeply divide — so perspicuity is false.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The catalogue of hard cases — rate: strong
Horn lists: can salvation be lost (perseverance)? which acts are gravely sinful? is infant baptism the ordinary means of an infant's salvation? conditional vs unconditional election? the Eucharist (memorial vs sacramental union)? surrogacy/IVF? female pastors and same-sex weddings? the free-grace heresy?[^1] These are not academic trivia; some bear on salvation. **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers replies the existence of disagreement does not undermine the *objective* clarity of the text — "the fact that somebody else fails to understand something doesn't prove that thing itself is not clear." Cataracts don't dim the sun. See [[Noetic Effects of Sin Explain Interpretive Disagreement]].

### 2. The infant-baptism live-audience stunt — rate: rhetorically strong, logically partial
Horn asks the room to raise hands if they believe in sola scriptura, then to keep them up if they agree with Westminster that it is "a great sin" not to baptize a baby — "the vast majority of hands went down."[^2] A vivid demonstration that committed sola-scriptura believers cannot deduce a doctrine their own confession calls a great sin. **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers (a paedobaptist) says Scripture *is* clear (household baptisms; the covenant sign applied to households including infants) and that dissenters err via the noetic effects of sin or upbringing — though he resists calling them damnably "blind."

### 3. The 0.1% argument — rate: strong, the closing thrust
Horn: "99% of scripture can be clear, but to kill a soul you only need 0.1% of scripture to be unclear to go on to a heresy to your own damnation," citing [[2 Peter 3.15-16]] (the unlearned twist Paul "to their own destruction").[^3] He gives real examples: free-grace antinomianism, progressive readings of slavery texts repurposed for same-sex marriage, and 19th-century pro-slavery exegesis (Josiah Priest, *Bible Defense of Slavery*).[^4] **Steelman of Rogers:** "*some* things" hard ≠ *most* things; the obscure are interpreted by the clear; and Scripture itself demarcates essentials ([[1 Corinthians 15.1-4]] "of first importance"; [[John 8.24]] "if you do not believe that I am, you will die in your sins"). See [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]] for Horn's "guard rail" conclusion — only an authority *beyond* Scripture can stop the slide.

## Best Quotes
> "It doesn't take much. If even only 0.1% of scripture is unclear on these important matters, it can lead millions to spiritual destruction. And I've seen it."[^3]

> "How do you explain the fact that almost everybody here can't avoid the sin of not baptizing their baby?" — "The noetic effects of sin." — "So sin made them all stupid."[^5]

> [Rogers] "The fact that somebody else fails to accurately understand something doesn't prove that thing itself is not clear."[^6]

> "There are things where even just a handful of passages is all it takes for somebody to fall down the rabbit hole of heresy. Unless you have an authority beyond scripture ... sola scriptura doesn't provide that."[^3]

**Parallel from the Latter-day Saint side.** In a separate debate, [[Shiloh Lofgren]] presses the same point empirically — sola-scriptura Protestants cannot agree on what baptism does, and a Ligonier *State of Theology* survey found ~43% of Bible-supreme evangelicals denying the deity of Christ (2020) or the personhood of the Holy Spirit (2025). See [[Protestant Disunity Is Evidence Against Perspicuity]].
