# The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument

#type/claim

## The Claim

Latter-day Saint apologetics usually frames biblical corruption as *subtraction*: the Book of Mormon (1 Nephi 13) says "plain and precious truths" were "taken away" from the Bible. The [[1 John 5-7 - Johannine Comma Interpolation|Comma Johanneum]], the panel argues, is the mirror image — a case where something was **added in** rather than removed. "This is what Joseph Smith meant when he said the plain and precious truths having been removed from the stick of Judah. Actually, they were *added* in — and this one was added in." [^1] The hosts note Joseph Smith allowed for multiple mechanisms of corruption: "sometimes it's translation errors, sometimes it's just innocent mistakes... sometimes it's straight up corruption." [^1]

The sharper edge is a *tu quoque* on the canon-closing proof text: trinitarians who cite [[Revelation 22.18-19|Revelation 22:18–19]] ("if any man shall add unto these things, God shall add unto him the plagues") to condemn Latter-day Saint scripture have themselves built their flagship Trinity verse by *adding* to the text. "These are the people who look us straight in the eye and they say, 'Revelation says you're all going to burn... because you're adding things to the Bible.' Your whole Trinitarian Christianity is based off a verse that *you* inserted." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

**Why the inversion is rhetorically powerful.** It neutralizes the most common evangelical opener against the Restoration in a single move. The standard charge ("you added the Book of Mormon to a closed canon") presupposes that the received Protestant Bible is the fixed, uncorrupted baseline. The Comma is a concrete, scholarly-conceded counterexample showing the *Protestant* text was itself edited after the fact — so the baseline is not fixed, and the "adding to scripture" prohibition, read as a blanket rule, would condemn the King James translators before it reaches Joseph Smith. This dovetails with the vault's standing rebuttal at [[Revelation 22 - Add or Take Away Rebutted]] (the warning in Revelation 22 applied to the book of Revelation, not a canon that did not yet exist) and the broader [[Manuscripts Were Altered by Scribes Over Centuries]].

**The steelman of the traditional reply.** A careful Protestant would distinguish two senses of "adding." (1) *Textual corruption within a book* — an uninspired scribe inserting a clause — is exactly what textual criticism exists to detect and excise; the modern critical text *removing* the Comma is the tradition policing itself, not endorsing the error. (2) *Adding new books of scripture* claiming independent revelatory authority is a categorically different act. On this reading the Comma's exposure actually vindicates the Protestant method (sola scriptura plus textual criticism recovering the original) rather than embarrassing it. The honest LDS rejoinder is not that the two are identical but that the Comma proves the *principle*: the transmitted text was demonstrably altered to serve a doctrinal agenda, so appeals to an inerrant, closed, self-evidently complete Bible — the kind used to foreclose continuing revelation — claim more than the manuscript record can bear. See [[KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text]], [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]].

**Caveat on the Book of Enoch aside.** In passing the host claims the v. 8 "spirit, water, and blood" language "is from the book of Enoch that Joseph Smith revealed in 1830." [^3] This is loose: 1 John 5:8's triad is not a quotation of 1 Enoch, and the "Book of Enoch" in LDS scripture (the Enoch material in the Book of Moses / Pearl of Great Price) is distinct from the ancient 1 Enoch. The note flags this as an over-claim that the core argument does not need.
