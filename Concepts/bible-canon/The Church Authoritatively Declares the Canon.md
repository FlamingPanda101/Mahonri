# The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] articulates the Catholic position on canon formation against the "Euthyphro-style" dilemma (does the church recognize inherent qualities, or arbitrarily decree?). His answer: the church neither *fallibly discovers* nor *legislates* the canon — **God determines it by inspiring each author**, and the **church authoritatively declares** it under the Holy Spirit's guidance, exercising the binding/loosing authority of [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18-19]]. This declaration proceeds through escalating levels of authority: regional councils (Hippo, Carthage, 4th c.), then infallibly at Florence and Trent.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Determine vs discover vs declare — rate: strong as a distinction
Horn threads the needle: God *determines* (by inspiration), the church *declares* (authoritatively). The criteria considered — apostolic authorship, reception by the faithful (*sensus fidelium*) — are real but "never reduced just to external markers to reverse engineer."[^2] This blocks the dilemma's first horn (if purely inherent markers, the church is superfluous) and second horn (if pure decree, the church could have chosen otherwise). **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers presses exactly this dilemma — if inherent qualities, others can recognize them without Rome; if Rome determines, it could have determined otherwise. Rogers says Horn's answer doesn't dissolve it. See [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]].

### 2. Binding and loosing (Matthew 16) — rate: contested
Horn grounds declarative authority in Jesus' grant to Peter and the apostles to bind/loose.[^3] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers says there is "no papal office in scripture ... all that stuff is being read into texts like Matthew 16," and that councils were "always conducted from Scripture," giving them *ministerial*, not *magisterial*, authority. The Matthew 16 reading is the deep Catholic-Protestant fault line, not a neutral premise.

### 3. The need for a final authority to "bring discussion to an end" — rate: moderate
Horn's functional argument: "there has to be one final authority to bring the discussion to an end ... infallible teachings are definitive."[^4] This is the practical motor of the whole Catholic case (cf. [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]). **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers retorts that Rome's alternative *also* fails to end disagreement — Horn himself wrote *Confusion in the Kingdom* about progressive Catholicism — so the "disagreement disproves sola scriptura" sword is double-edged. Horn concedes Catholic teaching isn't as *perspicuous* as Westminster claims Scripture is, but says a *living* authority can always re-clarify ("you didn't get it, here it is again").

## Best Quotes
> "The church does not determine the canon of scripture. God determines the canon of scripture when he inspires each sacred author to write. But the church also did not fallibly discover the canon either. The church has the power to authoritatively declare the canon."[^1]

> "There are external factors that are considered, but there's no rigorous set of external factors that says oh it's just these and these alone. The Holy Spirit also guides the church."[^2]

> "There has to be one final authority to bring the discussion to an end."[^4]

> [Rogers' dilemma] "If the church is determining it based on some inherent qualities, then the church isn't needed ... On the other hand, if the church is actually determining it, then the church could have determined something else to be scripture."[^5]
