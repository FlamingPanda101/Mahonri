# The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] coins **"the Great Forsaken"** for a *pre-Christian* apostasy — distinct from, and "as important or perhaps even more important" than, the post-apostolic Great Apostasy. Around 800–600 BC, culminating after the Babylonian exile, "the scribes of Judah, the keepers of the Torah, removed the truth, they redacted out the truths, they destroyed the plain and precious things that [[Nephi]] talks about." The name derives from [[Jubilees|Jubilees]] 1:14, where God foresees a day when "they have forsaken my ordinances ... my sabbaths and my holy place" (Hebrew *shik / shakach*, "forsaken").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Apocryphal texts narrate a self-aware suppression (rate: moderate, textual).** Jubilees, the *Testament of the Twelve Patriarchs*, and the Ethiopic *Book of Enoch* repeatedly warn that "someday the righteous are going to forsake or forget the truths." Many apocryphal books open by saying, in effect, "don't listen to what everyone else has told you; the scribes are lying to you, they've corrupted the truths" — a counter-narrative implying they fell out of favor with some canonizers. That such competing traditions *exist outside the canon at all* is itself the evidence: "why are there competing narratives ... out there at all?"[^2]

2. **A scribe held the only extant text (rate: moderate, mechanism).** Barnes' thought experiment: an aspiring scribe finds a scroll saying "the priest looking over your shoulder is wicked ... the Son of God is coming." His options are to be executed/burn it, or to **redact it** — "or just don't include it." With "control of the only extant text," a scribe "could write anything he wanted." Today corruption happens "by reinterpreting scripture," but then "a scribe had control."[^3]

3. **The Bible records its own factional rewriting (rate: strong, internal).** The Bible itself shows competing factions seizing the narrative: the bronze serpent (Nehushtan) Moses made was venerated and later destroyed as an idol by a reforming king; Jeremiah's true prophecy is preserved alongside a false prophet (Pashhur/Hananiah) who contradicted him "in the name of Jehovah"; Micah and Isaiah complain "prophesy unto us smooth things." See [[Deuteronomists Rewrote the Histories of Israel]].[^4]

4. **The recurring restoration pattern (rate: moderate, biblical-theological).** Scripture shows a repeated cycle of apostasy and restoration — Adam→Enoch, Noah, Abraham's idolatrous fathers, Israel in Egypt, the wilderness, the sons of Eli before Samuel — so a pre-exilic forsaking fits the Bible's own rhythm.[^5]

5. **Excised content: the suffering Messiah, the divine mother, the Gentile mission (rate: moderate, thematic).** Barnes argues the scribes centered an ethnocentric, political-Messiah, blood-of-Abraham theology and cut what offended it — the suffering/atoning Messiah, the Tree/Asherah/Heavenly Mother, the virgin birth, and the allegory of the olive tree (Gentiles grafted in), which survives in [[Jacob 5]] (Zenos) and apocrypha. See [[The Day of Atonement Was Edited Out of Deuteronomy and the Psalms]].[^6]

## Steelman of the Critic
The thesis is a maximal reconstruction stitched from disputed texts. (a) **Jubilees and the Testaments are late** (Second Temple, some post-dating the very "forsaking" they describe), so reading them as memories of a 7th-century redaction is circular — they may be sectarian polemics inventing a golden age, not witnesses to one. (b) Mainstream source criticism *does* find Deuteronomistic editing, but it does not license the claim that specific Christian doctrines (atoning Messiah, Heavenly Mother) were *deleted* — that is an LDS theological overlay, not a critical result; see the careful version in [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]]. (c) "The scribes had total control of the only text" overstates the messiness of transmission (multiple text families, the LXX/MT/Samaritan divergence) which actually *preserves* variants rather than enabling clean erasure. (d) A skeptic notes the argument is unfalsifiable: any absent doctrine can be attributed to "the forsaking." The defensible core is the well-evidenced Deuteronomistic reform and the existence of suppressed-tradition apocrypha; the speculative shell is the specific list of excised Christian content.

## Restated on Ward Radio — Isaiah's Lost Prophecies
[[Jonah Barnes]] reprises "the Great Forsaken" on Ward Radio with [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[David John Butler]], expanding the etymology to Jubilees 1:3 (the people will "forsake," Hebrew *shakach/shik*, the law, sabbaths, festivals, and ordinances) and contrasting it with the post-apostolic "Great Apostasy" (Greek *apostasis*, driven by hellenization) — the pre-Christian forsaking being driven instead by the Deuteronomistic scribes and Assyrian, not Greek, influence. He ties it to [[Nephi and Isaiah Shared the Same Ascension Vision]] and the brass-plates Isaiah.[^7]
