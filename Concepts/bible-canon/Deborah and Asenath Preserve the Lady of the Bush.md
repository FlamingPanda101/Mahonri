# Deborah and Asenath Preserve the Lady of the Bush

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] argues that several biblical women are **linguistic survivals of the suppressed mother-figure**, "the great lady," hidden in names and unpointed Hebrew:

- **The lady of the bush.** [[Deuteronomy 33.16|Deuteronomy 33:16]] (Joseph's blessing) reads "the good will of *him* that dwelt in the bush," but the unpointed text plus an **archaic feminine ending** (the *Sarah*/*Sarai* pattern) makes "the favor of *the woman* who dwelt in the bush" the better reading. The "bush" (*s-n-h*, a rare word) is the burning bush / the tree — and in later Orthodox iconography the burning bush *is* an image of Mary. [^1]
- **Asenath.** *S-N-H* (bush) + the definite article + the archaic feminine ending yields ***Asenath*** — Joseph's wife in Genesis, but in the later *Joseph and Aseneth* legend a figure who "lives high up in a temple," visited by the **dawn angel** at her east-facing window amid **bees and honeycomb**. [^2]
- **Deborah.** In [[Judges 4|Judges 4:4-5]], Deborah's name means **bee** (cf. Asenath's bees); via the old-Hebrew interchange of *d* and *g*, "Deborah" can be read as ***gevirah***, the standard Hebrew for the **great lady / queen-mother**. "Wife of *Lapidoth*" is better "lady of the **torches/lamps**" (*lapidot* is not a personal name) — associating her with the menorah lights of a holy-of-holies. So "Deborah the prophetess" is "the great lady, the lady of the torches," judging Israel under the **palm tree** (see [[The Palm Branch Signified the Secret Knowledge of the Holy of Holies]]). [^3]

The cumulative move: bee → honeycomb → torches → great lady → bush → tree → Mary are all threads of one erased maternal divine figure, surfacing (tellingly) in **Joseph's** blessing and lineage. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The unpointed-text ambiguity is genuine.** The Masoretic vowel points are a later addition, so consonantal forms in [[Deuteronomy 33.16|Deuteronomy 33:16]] can in principle support more than one reading; appealing to an archaic feminine ending is a recognized philological move (cf. the real *Sarai*/*Sarah* doublet). *Rating: the method is legitimate; this specific re-vocalization is Barker's, and contestable.* [^1]
2. **"Lapidoth" really is an odd "husband's name."** *Lappidot* means "torches/flames" and the "wife of Lappidoth" gloss has long puzzled commentators (some already read it "woman of torches/of spirit"), so Barker's lamp-lady reading is not unprecedented. *Rating: a real interpretive crux she exploits plausibly.* [^3]
3. **The *gevirah* office is a genuine biblical institution.** The queen-mother (*gevirah*) is an attested court role (1 Kings 15:13; Jer 13:18; 29:2), so linking a "great lady" title to royal/cultic standing is grounded — though equating *Deborah* with *gevirah* via the d/g script-swap is the speculative step. *Rating: the office is real; the name-equation is the leap.* [^3]
4. **Coheres with the wider divine-feminine dossier.** The figurine evidence ([[The Judean Pillar Figurines Were the Smashed Mother in the First Temple]]) and *El Shaddai* ([[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]]) make the maternal-figure hypothesis a converging case, not a single etymology. *Rating: strong cumulative fit.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** These are **paronomastic readings, not established etymologies**. Standard Hebrew lexicons take *Deborah* simply as "bee" and read "wife of Lappidoth" as a personal name; the *d*/*g* swap and the *Deborah → gevirah* equation are **not** mainstream and require accepting a chain of "could-be-read-as" moves. *Asenath* is generally derived from Egyptian (e.g. "belonging to [the goddess] Neith"), and the *Joseph and Aseneth* romance is a **Hellenistic-era novella**, far too late to witness first-temple goddess theology. Re-vocalizing [[Deuteronomy 33.16|Deuteronomy 33:16]] to feminine is possible but unsupported by the versions, which read masculine. So the "lady of the bush" network is an **elegant pattern imposed on the consonants**, exactly the kind of sound-play an enthusiast can multiply at will. A measured reply: Barker presents this *as* a detective's cumulative case, not a string of independent proofs — each thread (feminine ending, torches, bee/honeycomb, bush/tree/Mary) is individually modest but jointly suggestive, and it is anchored to *non*-linguistic evidence (the smashed figurines, the breasts-and-womb blessing); the reader can reject any single equation and still find the *direction* — a maternal figure repeatedly half-surfacing in the text — worth taking seriously.

## Best Quotes
> "The Hebrew there is more likely to be translated as the favor of the woman who dwelt in the bush, because it's an unpointed text... and this looks like an archaic feminine ending." [^1]

> "In the very old Hebrew script a d and a g are the same letter, so you could read Deborah as gevirah — gevirah is the regular Hebrew for the great lady... the queen mother. And the wife of Lapidoth — lapidot means torches or lamps — so she is the Lady of the Torches." [^3]

> "Asenath... is presented as a figure who lives high up in a temple, and the angel of the dawn appears to her one morning when she's looking through her window towards the east." [^2]
