# Jeremiah's Secret-Death Vision Parallels Nephi's Tree of Life

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that an apocryphal account of [[Jeremiah]]'s death — the **Paraleipomena of Jeremiah (4 Baruch)**, alongside the *Apocalypse of Baruch* — describes Jeremiah receiving a vision near the end of his life that closely mirrors [[Nephi]]'s tree-of-life vision: he sees God and a separate, distinct **Son of God** (two personages with physical bodies), plus a tree bearing fruit "white as snow," a Messiah, twelve disciples of the Lamb, and people rising against him. Because Joseph Smith demonstrably could not have accessed these texts (the relevant manuscripts surfaced/were published in the 1880s), the parallel is offered as evidence the Book of Mormon preserves authentic pre-exilic material. The Book of Mormon already places Jeremiah in Lehi's world: Lehi's contemporaries possess "the words of Jeremiah" on the brass plates, and [[Helaman 8.7|Helaman 8]] cites Jeremiah prophesying Jerusalem's destruction.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Joseph's lack of access is real (rated: moderate-to-strong).** 4 Baruch / Paraleipomena of Jeremiah and the relevant Baruch apocalypses were not available in English in 1829–30; Barnes dates the manuscripts he cites to 1886/1889 (Syriac Orthodox), so straightforward textual borrowing is excluded. (Caveat: the *traditions* about Jeremiah's death by stoning circulated more broadly than any single late manuscript.)[^2]
2. **The biblical Jeremiah was genuinely controversial and persecuted (rated: strong, on the canonical core).** Jeremiah was imprisoned and put in stocks; false prophets (Hananiah) opposed him; King Jehoiakim **burned his scroll** ([[Jeremiah 36.31|Jeremiah 36:31]]). So the Book of Mormon's portrait of a contemporary, persecuted Jeremiah whose words a household risked recording is consistent with the canonical record.[^3]
3. **The two-personage / tree-of-life details are striking parallels (rated: moderate).** Barnes says when he lines the apocryphal vision up against Nephi's "it's uncanny." A distinct Father and Son, a luminous-fruit tree, twelve disciples, and a slain seer match the Lehite vision pattern — the kind of multiple-attestation case the vault catalogs ([[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]]).[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Co-host [[Brad Whitbeck]] supplies much of the caution, and it is decisive here. (a) These are **late, fluid pseudepigrapha** (4 Baruch in its surviving Christian form is usually dated 1st–2nd century AD with Christian interpolations; the manuscripts Barnes cites are far later) — Christian copyists could have *added* the Son-of-God and tree imagery, so the "parallel" may reflect later Christianization, not a pre-exilic source. (b) **Parallels are cheap without textual analysis** — a thematic resemblance (visions, trees, a Messiah) is the weakest tier of evidence; see the vault's standing caution [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]]. (c) Whitbeck explicitly warns against the conspiratorial "the deuteronomists deleted the Messiah" frame, urging that Josiah's priests may have been "doing their best to follow God" and that rigid us-vs-them readings are exactly what the Book of Mormon warns against. The honest form: the parallel is *suggestive* and the no-access point is real, but the late, Christian-edited character of the sources means it cannot bear proof-weight — it belongs in a cumulative case, not as a standalone "Joseph couldn't have known."

## Best Quotes
> "We dig this thing up and, oh my gosh, it says exactly what the Book of Mormon says. I compare them in the book and it's uncanny ... you line them up next to each other and you're like, this is Nephi's vision."[^4]

> "If somebody in Laman's house is writing down the words of Jeremiah, they could be risking their life ... it's like having BBC Radio in German-occupied France."[^3]

> [Whitbeck] "Anybody who thinks that all the deuteronomists were evil — that's not what I'm saying. Of course they weren't ... I think there's a lot of room for these guys to have been doing their best to follow God."[^5]
