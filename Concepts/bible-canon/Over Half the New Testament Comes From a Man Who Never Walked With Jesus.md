# Over Half the New Testament Comes From a Man Who Never Walked With Jesus

## The Claim
Two facts about New Testament authorship complicate the picture of a seamless, apostolic, eyewitness Bible: (1) more than half of it is attributed to [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]], "a guy that by all accounts didn't walk with Jesus" while he was alive; and (2) of the ~13–14 letters bearing Paul's name, only about seven are widely regarded as genuinely his, the rest being **deutero-Pauline** (written in Paul's name or assembled from Pauline material after his death). Day offers this not to "trash" scripture but to argue Latter-day Saints must know the arguments.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The genuine vs. disputed split (rating: strong; mainstream NT scholarship)
Day relays the standard division: the undisputed letters include **Romans, 1 Corinthians, Galatians**, and **Philemon** (early and genuine); the later letters "don't sound like Paul" and don't deal with Paul's concerns. **Hebrews** "has long been thought not to be by [Paul]" — "that's why it's out of order" in the canon — and is, Day adds, theologically "Jamesian." [^1]

### 2. Ephesians as a posthumous, circulatory compilation (rating: moderate; Day's "middle of the road" take)
Day did an hour-long podcast on Ephesians and lands on the view that, whether or not Paul wrote it, "it probably came later" — a general circular letter assembled from Pauline "bits and discourses." One clue: some manuscripts lack "to the Ephesians," reading more like an open letter. It is also notably "temple-esque." [^2]

### 3. The *Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith* analogy (rating: moderate; LDS parallel)
Day's home-tradition analogy: just as *Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith* is a posthumous compilation Joseph "didn't write" — recorded by secretaries, stitched with ellipses, sometimes not reflecting "his actual thinking" — Ephesians can be a real-but-secondhand collection of Paul's teaching. This reframes pseudonymity as ancient editorial practice, not fraud. [^3]

### 4. The point is apologetic preparedness, not deconstruction (rating: framing)
Day insists he is "not going to lay all this out... teaching gospel doctrine in my ward," but "we better know those arguments because people are going to have questions... and there's evidence for this — we're not just speaking out of our hat." The honest acknowledgment is itself the apologetic posture. See [[New Testament Books Reflect Rival Communities]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Conservative scholars defend the traditional attributions: differences in style and vocabulary across the Pauline corpus are explained by different secretaries (amanuenses), audiences, and subject matter, not by forgery; ancient pseudonymity in sacred letters was condemned, not accepted, by the early church; and [[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter 3:15-16]] already treats Paul's letters as a known, authoritative collection "scripture." On this view "deutero-Pauline" is a critical hypothesis, not a fact, and Paul's non-eyewitness status is irrelevant because he claims a direct revelatory commission from the risen Christ.

## Best Quotes

> "Over half the New Testament is written by a guy that by all accounts didn't walk with Jesus." [^4]

> "Of the Pauline epistles... I think seven of them are considered legit, and several of them are called deutero-Pauline, meaning Paul didn't necessarily write it." [^1]

> "What we have in Ephesians is, in my opinion, a lot of Pauline bits and discourses that were later put together after Paul's long gone." [^2]
