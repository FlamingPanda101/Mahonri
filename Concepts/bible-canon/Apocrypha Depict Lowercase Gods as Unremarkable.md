# Apocrypha Depict Lowercase Gods as Unremarkable

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that Old Testament apocrypha and pseudepigrapha — predating the church fathers — routinely treat **lowercase-g gods as ordinary**: a populated divine world where Lucifer can claim "I am one of the gods" and no one finds it strange. This, he says, is the native theological background against which the fathers' apotheosis language (and the LDS divine-council picture) makes sense; the "different species" assumption only enters later with the Deuteronomists and the creeds.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Satan's god-claim raises no eyebrows (rated: moderate, primary texts)
In the Armenian and Egyptian *Books of Adam and Eve*, the serpent tells Eve she will "become as the gods" — the very way the Father "became a God ... and rose in wisdom." When Lucifer appears claiming to be "the God of this world," Adam and Eve "are prone to believe him," even ask God in prayer whether "the other gods" are present too. "No one ever says ... you can't be God because no one can see God ... no one ever assumes that God is a different species."[^1]

### 2. A new name in the heavenly ascent (rated: moderate)
Across the *Books of Enoch*, the *Apocalypse of Elijah*, and the *Ascension of Isaiah*, ascending seers pass princes, angels, and **gods**, and receive a **new name** — the source, Barnes argues, of [[Revelation 2.17|Revelation 2:17]]'s white stone with "a new name ... which no man knoweth." John "did not come up with this; this is from older lost texts." The motif carries into the Book of Mormon (2 and 3 Nephi) and the temple.[^1]

### 3. "Lesser Yah" / taking the divine name (rated: speculative)
In the Hebrew *3 Enoch*, ascending figures are called the "lesser YHWH" / "mini-Jehovah." Barnes ties this to the sacrament's "take upon them the name of thy Son" — taking upon oneself the name of God as a deification motif.[^1] *(He flags this as his reading of the texts, not consensus scholarship.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) these apocrypha are heterogeneous and often late, and their "gods" frequently mean angels or members of a subordinate heavenly host, not deities in the LDS sense — so "lowercase gods everywhere" overreads the genre; (b) the *canonical* trajectory deliberately *negates* the divine plurality (the Deuteronomistic and Second-Isaiah "there is no god besides me"), and a tradition's later self-correction is not obviously a "corruption"; and (c) the new-name and "lesser YHWH" links are suggestive but contested, and Barnes himself presents them in a loose, brainstorming register. The mainstream view reads Revelation's new name as eschatological identity in Christ, not deification. The LDS counter is the vault's divine-council case (see [[Other Gods Are Real Divine Beings Not Merely Idols]], [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]]) — that the negation rhetoric is monolatry, not strict ontological monotheism.
