# Sola Scriptura "Muzzles God"

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Behind the [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]] attack lies sola scriptura: "gospel" = the closed Bible, so no new revelation is possible. [[Ward Radio]] argues this position effectively "muzzles God" — forbidding even God from speaking again.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. A closed canon forbids God's own prophesied speech — strong
If "the canon is closed" and "God has already spoken and cannot speak anymore," then end-time prophecies become incoherent: when Christ returns to the Mount of Olives, he is prophesied to *speak* to his people, and [[Revelation 14.6|Revelation 14:6]] foresees an angel bringing "the everlasting gospel." Closing revelation "preempts" God. The hosts: "you're putting yourself above God practically."[^2] Rating: **strong** as a reductio.

### 2. Interpreting "gospel" as "the Bible" creates self-contradiction — strong
If "another gospel" means "anything outside the Bible," then God himself "couldn't come down and give you anything different" — which contradicts the Bible's own promises that Christ returns and speaks, and that an angel preaches the everlasting gospel. So the critic's own definition makes Scripture contradict itself.[^3] See [[Revelation 14-6 Angel With the Everlasting Gospel]]. Rating: **strong** (internal coherence argument).

### Steelman of the opposing view
The historic Protestant does not claim God *cannot* speak, but that God *has chosen* to speak finally and sufficiently in Christ and the apostolic deposit (cf. Hebrews 1:1-2, "in these last days spoken unto us by his Son"); the canon is closed not by muzzling God but by recognizing the once-for-all completeness of special revelation. Christ's audible words at the Second Coming are eschatological, not new *doctrine* added to the gospel, so they pose no problem for sola scriptura. **Response within the source:** the hosts contend that *any* genuine future divine speech — angelic or in person — falsifies the strong "canon is permanently closed, no more Bible" formulation they hear from critics; the sufficiency-vs-cessation distinction is precisely the move they are pressing the critic to make, which already softens the Galatians 1:8 absolutism. See also [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]].
