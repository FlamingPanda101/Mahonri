# Doublets Were Preserved to Unify Two Kingdoms

## The Claim
[[Richard Elliott Friedman]]'s answer to "why are there doublets?" — stories told twice in the Pentateuch — is that an editor in the court of Hezekiah (or his successors) deliberately **kept both versions** to make a single Bible acceptable to both the northern (E) and southern (J) populations after the fall of the northern kingdom. Erasing one version would alienate half the people; preserving both says "we're all us."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Two tribal epics, J and E (rated: documentary-hypothesis framework).** J (Yahwist, Judah) and E (Elohist, Ephraim/north) are parallel national epics covering much of the same ground; the hosts compare it to a North/South split where "if the South won the war, our history books would read differently" (and in some Southern U.S. classrooms, "the War of Northern Aggression," they still do).[^1]
2. **The northern refugees triggered the merger (rated: Friedman + archaeology).** After Assyria destroyed the northern kingdom in 721 BC, refugees flooded south; Israel Finkelstein documents a population explosion in Jerusalem c. 720 BC (Hezekiah's wall). They brought their records (E), so "somebody in the court of Hezekiah said we need to create a single written document… everyone will hear their remembered story."[^2][^3]
3. **Why not just delete one? (rated: the explanatory move).** "If we preserve it, we're keeping everyone happy: national unity." Friedman lists ~18 doublets in Genesis alone (two creation accounts, two genealogies of Adam, the threefold wife-sister story, the two flood strands, the two sale-of-Joseph traders — Ishmaelites in J vs. Midianites in E).[^4]
4. **A scribe-redactor with a theology (rated: Friedman's candidates).** Friedman proposes the redactor may be Baruch the scribe (or, for the Deuteronomistic history, Jeremiah, then revised); the editor has sources *and* a theological aim — "biased, but also doing history."[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** The "doublets preserved for political unification" thesis assumes the documentary-source division it is explaining; conservative scholars (Cassuto, Kitchen) read the "doublets" as intentional literary repetition (parallel panels, escalation, type-scenes) by a single author, not spliced rival epics, and dispute the Hezekian-redaction scenario as conjectural reconstruction. Finkelstein's population spike is real but its interpretation (and the low chronology generally) is itself debated. The doublets are textually present; the unify-two-kingdoms *motive* is Friedman's reconstruction.

## Best Quotes
> "The doublets are preserved because we have to make a Bible that is for everyone — a Bible for the E folks, a Bible for the J folks… if we preserve it, we're keeping everyone happy. National unity."[^4]

> "They're all us. The refugees are us too… so we're all us, and it's a document to unify everybody."[^3]
