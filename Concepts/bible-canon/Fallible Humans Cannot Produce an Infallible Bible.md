# Fallible Humans Cannot Produce an Infallible Bible
#type/claim

## The Claim
The doctrine of an inerrant, infallible Bible founders on a chain of fallible human links. Fallible people wrote the texts, fallible copyists transmitted them, fallible councils selected which books counted, and fallible translators rendered and interpret them. An unbroken chain of fallible processes cannot, on its own, yield an infallible product. The Latter-day Saint conclusion is not that scripture is worthless but that the *terminal* authority cannot be the book itself — it must be the living God who reveals (see [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]).

## Supporting Evidence
- **Revelation precedes and exceeds the text.** Per Hayden Carroll, people received truth from God *before* any Bible existed — "God gave it to them, through His Spirit." The authors knew what to write only because God revealed it, so the Spirit is logically prior to and more ultimate than the book; the text is a *product* of revelation, not its ceiling.
- **Even Reformed confessions locate assurance in the Spirit.** The Westminster Confession (1.5) grounds "full persuasion and assurance" of scripture's truth in "the inward work of the Holy Spirit," not in the text certifying itself — conceding that something beyond the book secures it (cf. [[James White Appeals to the Spirit Not the Bible for Assurance]]).
- **The transmission/canon problem.** Inerrancy claims typically retreat to the lost "original autographs," but what believers actually possess are copies, translations, and a canon list — each fixed by fallible human hands. This is the canon-and-transmission strand of [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon]].
- **Language itself is a limit.** A further argument holds that even a perfectly transmitted text could not be strictly infallible because human language is inherently ambiguous and interpreted by fallible minds (see [[A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible]]).
- **Steelman.** The classic inerrantist reply distinguishes the *source* from the *channel*: God can speak an infallible message *through* fallible humans by superintending the process (inspiration, providential preservation), just as a perfect signal can pass through imperfect wires. On this view the autographs were God-breathed and providence has preserved the text's substance, so fallible links do not entail a fallible product. The LDS rejoinder is that this simply *relocates* authority to God's superintending act of revelation — which is precisely the point: the ultimate authority is the revealing God, and a living God who still speaks is more secure than a closed, humanly mediated text.
