# The Canon Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the first judgment behind any Bible — *which books* — admits no objectively correct answer. We can find collections that function as a Bible that disagree with each other across Christian groups and within Jewish groups; "nobody can authoritatively say this one's the real one and the other one is not."[^1] The canon is decided per-group; "your answer cannot be authoritative universally... cannot be objectively correct. It's just what you prefer, or what your group prefers, or a rule that someone enforces."[^2] See [[Three Judgments Stand Between You and a Bible]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The canons demonstrably differ in size — rate: strong
McClellan walks the spectrum: the **Samaritan** canon is just the five books of Moses (which McClellan links to the Sadducees' rejection of scriptures after the Pentateuch);[^3] the **Protestant** canon has 66 books; **Catholic** adds the Deuterocanon; **Eastern Orthodox** adds more; **Armenian** and especially the **Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo** canon adds "orders of magnitude" more.[^4] Each of these is "a Bible," and "none of us can say... that's the wrong Bible" authoritatively.[^3] This is straightforwardly accurate; canon scholarship (e.g., [[Lee Martin McDonald]]) documents this diversity.

### 2. Historical fluidity within traditions — rate: strong
The Deuterocanon was formalized only at the Counter-Reformation, producing a "graded canon" within Catholicism; [[Martin Luther]] tried to relegate Hebrews, James, and Revelation alongside the Apocrypha before later Protestants pulled the NT books back.[^5][^6] See [[Canon Criteria Were Post Hoc Rationalizations]] and [[Reformers Disagree With One Another on the Canon]]. **Steelman of the believer:** a Protestant or Catholic can argue that *providence* or the Spirit-guided church settled the true canon, so the diversity reflects human error around a real divine list. McClellan's reply: any such claim only "speaks for your group" — there is no neutral standpoint from which to certify one tradition's list as *the* canon (see [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]).

### 3. The reader rarely chooses — rate: moderate
For most traditions canon is "already done and dusted"; you cannot stand at a pulpit and freely alternate between canons "and be long for that pulpit."[^7] Beecher's quip about reading **Bel and the Dragon** in an Anglican church captures that canon is institutionally enforced, not individually self-evident.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "We can find collections of texts that constitute what we would call a Bible that don't agree with what Christians today think of as the canon... nobody can authoritatively say this one's the real one and the other one is not."[^1]

> "Your answer cannot be authoritative universally. Your answer cannot be objectively correct. It's just what you prefer, or what your group prefers, or a rule that someone enforces."[^2]

> "The probably the smallest biblical canon is that of the Samaritans, which is just the five books of Moses."[^3]
