# The Deuteronomistic History Books Are Absent From the Brass Plates

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]], on [[Ward Radio]], observes that the **Deuteronomistic History** package — Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings — does **not** appear among the contents of Lehi's [[brass plates]], whereas Genesis through Numbers, plus prophets like Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Micah, and Chronicles-type material, do. He reads this absence as corroboration that the Deuteronomist layer was a late "infiltration" into the canon: Deuteronomy and its attached histories were a corrupting interpolation that recast God "in the image of the scribe," rather than part of the original Mosaic record Lehi carried out of Jerusalem.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The brass-plates contents skip the Deuteronomistic books — rate: moderate (textual argument)
[[1 Nephi 5.11|1 Nephi 5:11]] and the surrounding Book of Mormon descriptions enumerate the five books of Moses, the prophets down to Jeremiah, and a genealogy — but never Joshua/Judges/Samuel/Kings as such. Barnes treats this silence as meaningful: if the Deuteronomistic History were already canonical and authoritative in Lehi's day (~600 BC), one would expect it on the plates of brass, yet it is conspicuously missing.[^1]

### 2. The absence fits an "infiltration" timeline — rate: moderate (source-critical synthesis)
On the Deuteronomist reconstruction, the school of scribes that produced Deuteronomy also **back-edited** Joshua–Kings to rate every king by law-keeping (praising [[Josiah]] as Moses-like). If that editorial program postdates Lehi's departure, the brass plates would naturally preserve a *pre-reform* library without it — which is exactly the pattern Barnes points to, and which reinforces the existing Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament thesis.[^1]

### 3. Deuteronomy as the corrupting interpolation — rate: speculative (Barnes's framing)
Barnes frames Deuteronomy itself as the wedge by which a "God in the image of the scribe" entered the canon, displacing the older fifth book of Moses (the Assumption of Moses). The missing histories are, on his reading, the *downstream* literature of that same reforming school. See [[The Assumption of Moses Was the Fifth Book of Moses Replaced by Deuteronomy]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The argument leans on **silence**. (a) The Book of Mormon's brass-plates inventory is summary, not a complete table of contents — it need not list every book present, so absence from the narration is not proof of absence from the plates. (b) Mainstream source criticism dates a *Deuteronomistic History* edition to the exile/post-exile, but the underlying narratives (conquest, judges, monarchy) plausibly existed in some form by Lehi's day; "the books were not yet on the plates" and "the events were unknown" are different claims. (c) Casting the histories as "corruption" imports a value judgment the academic model does not make. What survives is the modest, defensible observation: the Book of Mormon's own account of the brass plates foregrounds Moses and the prophets and is silent on the Deuteronomistic corpus — consistent with, though not proof of, a pre-reform library.
