# The Fallible Canon Is Hostage to Critical Scholarship

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Some Protestants ground canonicity in **apostolicity** (a book is Scripture if written by an apostle, e.g. Peter vouching for Paul in [[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter 3:15-16]]). [[R.C. Sproul]] concedes the Bible is "a fallible collection of infallible books." [[Trent Horn]] and [[Joe Heschmeyer]] argue this makes the canon perpetually vulnerable to being overturned by historical-critical scholarship [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Apostolic authorship is exactly what critical scholarship disputes — rate: strong
"What do you do when you have modern scholarship that calls into question whether Paul really wrote, especially things like First and Second Timothy?" Most critical scholars deny Pauline authorship of the Pastorals. If inspiration is *defined by* Pauline authorship, "then you kind of become a slave to what modern scholars say." See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]] [^1].

### 2. The Catholic model is insulated; the Protestant model is not — rate: strong
"The Catholic position would still survive even if one of Paul's disciples wrote one of those letters and attributed it to him, because the church doesn't infallibly declare all the historical circumstances of each writing — it just declares that it is inspired, that it belongs to the canon." But the Protestant "who's hitched their wagon to 'it's inspired *if* it was written by Paul, and if it wasn't, then it's not' becomes a slave to scholarship" [^2]. See [[Thermometer vs Thermostat - The Church Declares the Canon]]. **Steelman:** a Protestant can broaden "apostolicity" to "apostolic *era* / apostolic *circle*" rather than strict authorship, dodging the authorship objection — but this loosened criterion no longer reverse-engineers the exact canon (see [[Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy in Canon Criteria]]).

### 3. The safe example: Hebrews — rate: strong
"You thought [[Hebrews 1.1-2|Hebrews]] was written by Paul for a long time, and now you don't think that. Okay, so what happens to it?" If apostolicity is the criterion and Hebrews' Pauline authorship is abandoned, its canonical status is suddenly in doubt [^3].

### 4. No closed canon is possible on this view — rate: strong
"You can't really have a closed canon on the Protestant view if you have this fallible canon" — tomorrow a scholar "could come and blow apart your view," or "here's some other writing that wasn't included before." The canon stays permanently open to revision [^3].
