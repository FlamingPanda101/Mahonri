# LDS Biblical Literacy Declined as the Church Domesticated

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] and [[Benjamin Park]] argue that Latter-day Saint biblical literacy and
hermeneutical creativity *declined* over the 20th century, as the church "got domesticated" and
"hitched our wagon to the evangelicals" to gain access to power and resources — turning into "the
stepbrother of American evangelicalism, constantly tagging along trying to pick up scraps."[^1]
McClellan laments not living in the 1860s, when Latter-day Saints "were such radical readers of the
Bible."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. From 1860s radicals to mid-century domestication — rate: moderate (historical thesis)
"Why couldn't I have been a member of the church in the 1860s? We were such radical readers of the
Bible in the early history of the church… then in the early 20th century I think we got domesticated,
and we decided in the middle of the 20th century we were going to hitch our wagon to the
evangelicals."[^1] The cost: "biblical literacy, hermeneutic creativity, power, all those different
things." This is the diachronic counterpoint to the (still-true) synchronic claim that
[[LDS Excel in Religious Literacy and Practice|LDS members out-read most Christians today]].

### 2. The "I Have a Question" decline — rate: strong (anecdotal evidence)
As a young branch president in Uruguay, McClellan read old 1970s *Liahona/Ensign* magazines whose
"I Have a Question" section tackled hard issues ("how come the Joseph Smith papyri don't match the
Book of Abraham translation?"), answered by scholars. By the present the same section asks "why do
I need a clean room to have the spirit?" — "When did we go from hard-hitting questions to this fluff
and buff stuff?"[^2] He links this to "the manual of instruction… progressively gotten thinner and
thinner."[^3]

### 3. Topical curriculum erodes textual literacy — rate: strong (Park)
[[Benjamin Park]]: "It used to be that Latter-day Saints were much more biblically literate… because
they would go through seminary and actually read through the scriptures." A curriculum that is "much
more topical… you're learning about topics rather than the scriptural text itself" risks "not being
as biblically literate." Park judges it "a top-down approach" driven by the bureaucratic difficulty
of "global instruction" and reluctance to take stands on interpretive issues.[^4] This is the
mechanism behind [[Baby Steps - LDS Scripture Reform Is Deliberately Incremental]]. **Steelman:** a
defender can argue that "squishy, feelings-based moral" lessons serve a vast, doctrinally diverse
global church better than technical exegesis, and that devotion is not the same as literacy; Park
and McClellan grant the bureaucratic logic but insist "the consequences are real."

## Best Quotes
> "We've just turned into the stepbrother of American evangelicalism… constantly tagging along trying to pick up scraps."[^1]

> "When did we go from hard-hitting questions to this fluff and buff stuff?"[^2]

> "I worry that with this instruction that is much more topical… [it] is going to have these consequences of just not being as biblically literate." — [[Benjamin Park]][^4]
