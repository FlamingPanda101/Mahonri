# The Creation Story Is a Model for How to Live

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]], citing the historian of religion [[Mircea Eliade]] (whom he first read at BYU), argues that **a culture's creation/origin story functions as a paradigm for how its people are to live**. He applies this to the Book of Moses: since the Father creates "by the word of my power, which is mine Only Begotten Son" (Moses 1:32; cf. [[John 1.1|John 1:1-3]], "the Word... made all things"), and since humans are made "in the similitude of the Only Begotten," the way God creates becomes the way *we* are to act — we must "do all that [we] do in the name of the Son." The cosmogony is thus a behavioral template: Christ is both the *model* for our creation and the *means* of God's creation, so our own creating and living are to be done through Christ. Bradley names the upshot **consecration** — Christ-centered action.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Cross-cultural cases: the origin story patterns the practice
Bradley generalizes from Eliade with examples: Jews rest on the seventh day because God rested on the seventh ([[Genesis 1.5|Genesis]]); the Jewish day begins at sunset because Genesis 1 moves "evening... morning" — darkness to light; Judaism centers on living by God's *word* because God creates by speaking ("Let there be..."); Daoism's *wu wei* (actionless action) mirrors a universe that "spontaneously happened" rather than being made. In each case the cosmogony scripts the ethic.[^3] *Rating: a fair summary of Eliade's paradigmatic-cosmogony thesis; the specific glosses (e.g., why the Jewish day starts at evening) are illustrative, with simpler liturgical explanations also available.*

### 2. The LDS application: create "in the name of the Son"
The Book of Moses says the Father created everything through the Son (Moses 2-3); after the Fall the angel tells Adam his sacrifice is "in similitude of the sacrifice of the Only Begotten... wherefore thou shalt do all that thou doest in the name of the Son" — language Bradley notes is "familiar from the temple." [[2 Nephi 32.9|2 Nephi 32:9]] echoes it: do nothing "unto the Lord" without first praying "to the Father in the name of Christ." So if the Father creates the world *through* Christ, our acts of creation within it are likewise to be done through Christ — concretely, "Christ-centered lives" and consecration.[^2][^4] *Rating: a coherent intratextual synthesis; the move from "God creates through the Son" to "therefore consecrate your actions" is homiletic application, not entailment.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
[[Mircea Eliade]]'s "cosmogony as paradigm" is a useful but **contested** generalization in religious studies — critics (J.Z. Smith and others) charge Eliade with imposing a universal "archaic" pattern that flattens local differences, so the premise itself is not settled scholarship. Even granting it descriptively, the inference here is **normative-from-descriptive**: that origin stories *function* as behavioral models (a fact about how cultures work) does not establish that one *ought* to consecrate one's actions to Christ (a theological obligation) — that conclusion is carried by the LDS scriptural reading, not by Eliade. The supporting glosses are also debatable: the Jewish day's sunset start and the Sabbath rest have straightforward halakhic/liturgical rationales that need not derive from a "live as the creation lived" principle, and reading [[Genesis 1.5|Genesis 1]] as a how-to-live template rather than an ANE ordering account is itself the move questioned in [[Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology Not Modern Science]]. The fair statement: the framework is a rich *devotional* lens (creation-through-the-Son grounding consecration) and is internally consistent, but it leans on a disputed comparative thesis and supplies inspiration rather than proof.
