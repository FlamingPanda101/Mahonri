# The Desert Island Bible Test Smuggles In Human Agency

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A fundamentalist standard quoted by [[Trent Horn]]: read the Bible "as if we were on a desert island and it had washed ashore," as free as possible from human interpretation — the text alone, encountered directly, will authenticate itself [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Bible that "washes ashore" was already assembled by humans — rate: strong
"Someone is putting together the books that are in the Bible that washes on your particular shore." The washed-up volume could be a Catholic Bible (73 books), a Protestant one (66), an Orthodox one, the Joseph Smith Translation used by some, a Jehovah's Witness translation, or *The Message* paraphrase. Which canon and which translation reaches you is itself "this incredible role of human agency" — the very thing the test claims to eliminate [^2]. See [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]].

### 2. The only "human-free" text is unreadable — rate: strong
"Unless you're just getting ancient Greek and Hebrew manuscripts that wash ashore — in which case, as a non-Greek or Hebrew speaker, you're probably not struck by the profundity of them. You're probably at a loss to know what they mean" [^2]. To be read at all the text must be selected, translated, and arranged — all human acts. **Steelman:** the desert-island image is a heuristic for *minimizing* tradition-bias in interpretation, not a literal epistemology — but as a basis for self-authentication it collapses, because the canon must be settled *before* the island scenario can even begin.

### 3. It is "a terrible standard" even on its own terms — rate: moderate
Horn calls it "a terrible standard": reading free of all interpretive community discards the linguistic, historical, and canonical context required to understand the text at all — the opposite of how [[2 Timothy 3.14-17|Scripture]] envisions teaching being handed on [^1].

## Best Quotes
> "We should read the Bible as if we were on a desert island and it had washed ashore. So we'd read it as free as possible from any human interpretation." [^1]

> "Someone is putting together the books that are in the Bible that washes on your particular shore. It could be a Catholic Bible, it could be a Protestant one... It could be the message where it's the paraphrase of the Bible." [^2]

> "Unless you're just getting ancient Greek and Hebrew manuscripts that wash ashore, in which case as a non-Greek or Hebrew speaker, you're probably at a loss to know what they mean." [^2]
