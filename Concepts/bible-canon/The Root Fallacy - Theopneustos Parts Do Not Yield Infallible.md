# The Root Fallacy — Theopneustos Parts Do Not Yield Infallible

## The Claim
The single Greek word Protestants lean on hardest for biblical infallibility is **θεόπνευστος** (*theopneustos*) in [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]] — "All scripture is God-breathed." [[Hayden Carroll]], citing [[Trent Horn]], argues that the standard Protestant inference fails on two **linguistic** grounds, both drawn from the Protestant scholar **D.A. Carson**: (1) you cannot fix a word's meaning by adding up its component parts (the *root fallacy*), so "*theos* + *pneuma*" does not license "God-breathed-and-therefore-inerrant"; and (2) *theopneustos* is a *hapax legomenon* (it appears only once in the Bible), so there is no second usage to constrain its sense.[^1] The conclusion: "infallible rule of faith" is an inference *added* to the word, not a meaning *contained in* it.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
- **The root fallacy named by a Protestant.** Carroll appeals to D.A. Carson's *Exegetical Fallacies*, where Carson labels deriving a word's meaning purely from its etymological roots "the root fallacy." Carroll's examples follow Carson: *hippopotamus* literally means "river-horse," but "a hippo is not a horse"; "lady-killer" means a female *charmer*, not a female *murderer*.[^2] The point is structurally sound and is conceded by the Protestant scholar Carson himself, which gives the argument unusual force against a *sola scriptura* defender.
- **Carson's own apostolos example.** Carroll presses further with Carson's case of ἀπόστολος (*apostolos*, "apostle"): though cognate with the verb ἀποστέλλω ("I send"), the noun's *usage* in the New Testament does not reduce to "one who is sent" — it means a *messenger* or *representative*. Etymology and attested usage diverge, so usage, not roots, governs meaning.[^3]
- **The hapax problem.** Because *theopneustos* occurs only once, "we don't have anything else in the Bible to make a comparison in order to show what the word means," and "Paul never extrapolates what he means by that word."[^4] Carroll frames this as a Greek scholar's term ("hapax legomenon… a one-of-a-kind") and uses it to argue the passive gloss "God-breathed" is not the only live option; an *active* sense ("God-breathing," i.e. spiritually life-giving) is grammatically available — paralleling God breathing life into Adam in Genesis 2:7.[^5] See [[Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible]] for the life-giving reading itself; this note isolates the distinct *root-fallacy / hapax* argument about why no reading can be forced from the parts.
- **Honest assessment / steelman of the Protestant.** Carson raises the root fallacy precisely to *discipline* word studies, not to dissolve all lexical meaning — usage still fixes that *theopneustos* means "produced by God's breath / divinely sourced." The strongest Protestant reply (cf. B.B. Warfield) is that inerrancy is not claimed to be *lexically encoded* in the word at all; it is a *theological* inference — what God breathes out cannot err — so refuting the etymology does not by itself refute the doctrine. Carroll's argument lands cleanly against the *crude* form ("the parts just mean God-breathed, so it must be infallible") that he documents Protestant apologists actually using, but it does not touch the more careful theological version of the case.

## Best Quotes
> "A lot of Protestants just say the word must have this meaning because that's what theos and pneuma mean. But that is what the Protestant scholar D.A. Carson called the root fallacy. You can't determine a word's meaning only from the meaning of its root or constituent parts."[^2]

> "Hippopotamus means river-horse, but a hippo is not a horse. Lady-killer means female charmer, not female murderer."[^2]

> "It only appears once in the Bible. Greek scholars use the fancy term hapax… so we don't have anything else in the Bible to make a comparison in order to show what the word means."[^4]
