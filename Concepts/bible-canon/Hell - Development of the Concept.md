# Hell - Development of the Concept

## Overview

This is a hub note for the **historical development of the concept of hell** — the claim that "hell" is not a single, fixed, biblical doctrine handed down whole, but a composite idea that *developed* over time from diverse and sometimes competing strands. The recurring point across the cluster is that what English readers experience as one place ("Hell") is the flattening of several distinct concepts (Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, Tartarus) and that the New Testament's fiery, dark, judgment imagery was significantly shaped by Second Temple texts — above all the Enochic tradition — rather than appearing fully formed in the Hebrew Bible.

The flagship source is [[Book of Enoch - Origin of Hell and Judgment Imagery]]: the bound angels, jagged rocks, cursed valleys, "smooth places," and differentiated holding-realms of 1 Enoch supply imagery picked up in 2 Peter 2:4, Jude 6, and Revelation. Because 1 Enoch is a composite text, it already contains *competing* afterlife frameworks — annihilation, temporary punishment unto salvation, and eternal conscious torment — that the New Testament inherits unsystematized.

## The Steelman

A defender argues that diversity of *imagery* does not entail diversity of *doctrine*: Scripture progressively reveals a coherent reality of final judgment, and later texts clarify rather than contradict earlier ones. Jesus' own teaching on Gehenna and eternal punishment (Matthew 25:46) is taken as authoritative regardless of imagery's provenance. The cluster's reply: the genuinely *competing* outcomes (annihilation vs. eternal torment vs. remedial punishment) are not mere imagery — they are incompatible doctrines, and which one "won" is a fact of historical development.
