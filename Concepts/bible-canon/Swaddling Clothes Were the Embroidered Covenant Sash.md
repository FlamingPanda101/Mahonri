# Swaddling Clothes Were the Embroidered Covenant Sash

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the **swaddling clothes** of the infant [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] were not poverty-rags but the **embroidered covenant sash** of the wedding — the long, two-sided embroidered band the bride made, wrapped around the couple's joined hands at the wedding ("tying the knot"), and afterward used to swaddle their newborn as a sign of birth *into the covenant*. So Jesus's swaddling marks him as **born-in-the-covenant** and legitimate within Joseph's family, not merely as a poor child laid in a manger.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The bride embroidered a covenant sash

While the groom built the bridal home during the year of separation, the bride prepared her dress and embroidered a long strip of fabric (four or five inches wide) with "family symbols, the family genealogy... anything meaningful," worked **on both sides so there was no wrong side**. At the wedding it was wrapped around the bride and groom's joined hands "to symbolize that binding covenant" — "that's where we get the term tying the knot."[^3]

### 2. The same sash swaddled the newborn

"When a child was born to the couple they would use that to swaddle the child as a sign that this child is born into our covenant." Middle Eastern tradition also anointed the newborn with oil and rubbed it with salt — both "symbolic of covenant making" — before wrapping it.[^4]

### 3. Therefore Jesus's swaddling = covenant birth, not rags

"When you see Jesus being wrapped in swaddling clothes... it was not rags. This was a sign that even though Joseph wasn't the father, he was his father — he was part of that family and part of that covenant, and he was not illegitimate." This connects to the broader LDS phrase "born in the covenant," and underwrites Jesus's legitimate covenant standing despite the virgin conception.[^5]

> **Steelman:** [[Luke 2.2-3|Luke 2]] presents the swaddling matter-of-factly, and salt-and-oil treatment of newborns is attested in the ancient Near East (cf. Ezekiel 16:4, which mentions salting and swaddling). Reading the swaddling as a covenant act draws on real cultural data even where the specific "wedding-sash reused as swaddle" custom is the interpretive bridge.

## Best Quotes

> "When you see Jesus being wrapped in swaddling clothes, sometimes we think that means rags — it was not rags. This was a sign that... he was not illegitimate, he belonged to them." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^5]

> "It would be wrapped around the bride and groom... to symbolize that binding covenant — and that's where we get the term tying the knot." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^3]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The "wedding sash reused as swaddling cloth" custom is asserted from popular tradition rather than documented sources; Luke draws no covenant inference from the swaddling, and the manger context is plausibly read as humility/poverty.
- The folk-etymology "tying the knot" → wedding-sash is a charming but unverified derivation.
- See [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] for the general caution.
