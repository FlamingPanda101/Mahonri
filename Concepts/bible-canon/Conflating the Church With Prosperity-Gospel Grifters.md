# Conflating the Church With Prosperity-Gospel Grifters

## The Point
The hosts diagnose the *root* of Nathan Apffel's "profits from war" segment as a category error driven by his prior worldview. Apffel's stock-in-trade is auditing prosperity-gospel mega-churches — the "Kenneth Copelands of the world" with mansions and private jets — and his theology holds that "any organized institution of religion... [is] corrupt."[^1][^2] He then views the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints "through the same eyes," importing the grifter template onto an institution whose finances and leadership do not fit it. The hosts name the move directly: "It is conflation. It is conflation."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The framing imputes malice, not just facts.** The hosts identify the rhetorical structure: the segment is "attributing malice to the church... Church is bad, institution bad. Look how much money they have. Look at what they're using it for and therefore bad intentions to amass wealth and be greedy and prideful and selfish. That... is the frame that it's being put in."[^4] The data (a small, screened defense holding) is made to carry a conclusion about motive it cannot support.

2. **The worldview is anti-institutional by premise.** Apffel believes "everyone should... worship Jesus in a decentralized way," "every man his own priest, every man his own pope," and that "all institutions, they're all corrupt. Every single institution is corrupt."[^5][^6] Given that premise, *any* organized church with reserves is guilty before the evidence is examined — which is why the hosts say, "if we're just tearing down every institution on the planet, then just come out and say it."[^7]

3. **Leaders do not fit the prosperity-preacher pattern.** Against the Copeland comparison, the hosts stress the contrast: full-time Church leaders receive a modest stipend "very reasonable compared to the amount of work," fly commercial, and "eat at Burger King." The running "find Dallin's yacht" bit (offering a satirical "million dollars" to anyone who locates [[Dallin H. Oaks]]'s nonexistent yacht) dramatizes that the wealth-and-luxury image simply does not attach to the brethren — "to have Tucker Carlson criticize the brethren... like they're all living high on the hog is deliciously ironic."[^8][^9]

4. **The grift label fails the follow-the-money test.** A grift requires individuals enriched by the institution's money. With an unpaid lay ministry and ~100 modestly-salaried leaders out of a 17-million-member church, no one personally pockets the reserves — the same structural disproof developed in [[Grift Does Not Track With Disbelief Rebuttal]] and in [[The SEC Settlement Does Not Prove the Church Hid Its Wealth]].

5. **Stated mission is the opposite of the imputed one.** The Church's declared purpose is "to administer the ordinances of salvation" — to teach the restored gospel, bring people to covenants, build temples — and the hosts contrast that with the sarcastic "ordinances of war" / "ordinances of destruction" framing critics project onto it.[^10] Reading a salvation-oriented institution as a war-profiteering one is the conflation in miniature.

## Best Quotes
> "It is conflation. It is conflation." [^3]

> "If we're just tearing down every institution on the planet, then just come out and say it. There should be no institutions on the planet." [^7]

> "To have Tucker Carlson criticize the brethren of the church like they're all living high on the hog is deliciously ironic." [^8]

## LDS Connection
The Church explicitly distinguishes its lay, stipended leadership and consecration model from prosperity-gospel monetization; tithing is disbursed by council (cf. Doctrine and Covenants 119–120), not by an enriched individual. The conflation error is a species of guilt-by-resemblance — assuming that because two organizations both hold money, they share motives. Naming the critic's anti-institutional premise exposes why no amount of context satisfies him: the verdict precedes the evidence.
