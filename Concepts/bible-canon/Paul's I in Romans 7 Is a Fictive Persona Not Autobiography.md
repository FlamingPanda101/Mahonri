# Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the famous first-person "I" of **Romans 7** ("I had not known sin, but by the law... the good that I would I do not") is **not Paul's autobiography** but a recognized Greek rhetorical device, **prosōpopoeia** — a "fictive 'I'" in which the writer assumes a *character* (Greek *prosōpon*, "face/mask," whence *persona*) to drive a point. In Romans 7 Paul adopts the persona of a **Roman adopting the Mosaic law and failing** — "I as a Roman, I'm trying to adopt the Mosaic law, and I'm finding I'm failing over and over." The reader is *meant to inhabit* the "I," not read Paul's biography. Thomassen notes Paul does this elsewhere (assuming the role of a Jew, or a Gentile), and that **Origen** later faulted Celsus precisely for botching the *prosōpon* of a Jew. Reading every "I" in Romans as a fictive persona, he says, transforms the letter — and this is the mechanism behind [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Prosōpopoeia is a standard ancient device.** Thomassen: "this is a Greek literary device that is *prosōpopoeia*, which is a fictive 'I' — the writer of a rhetorical piece assumes the character of someone to drive home a point." It was part of the *paideia* of composition and interpretation. *Rating: prosōpopoeia (speech-in-character) is a genuine, well-documented rhetorical technique; modern Pauline scholars (e.g. Stowers) do argue Romans 7 uses it — a respectable reading.*[^1]
2. **The persona is a Roman failing under the law.** On [[Romans 7.7-9|Romans 7:7-9]] ("I was alive without the law once; but when the commandment came, sin revived, and I died"), Thomassen reads a *Roman* who, before encountering Judaism, "life seemed kind of good... but then once I allowed the law to take force upon me, I've got the concept of sin... and what happens? You fail." *Rating: coheres with the *akrasia*/alien-law thesis; the *Roman* identity of the persona is Thomassen's specific construal.*[^3]
3. **Paul switches personas elsewhere.** "Paul does this all over the place... where he assumes the role of being a Jew... or being a Gentile, all to drive home this point." The "I" is mobile, not fixed to Paul. *Rating: Romans does shift voice (e.g. the diatribe interlocutor of Romans 2-3); the multiple-persona claim has textual support.*[^2]
4. **The Origen/Celsus control.** Thomassen cites Origen's *Contra Celsum*: Origen tells Celsus "your *prosōpon* is all wrong, because that's not how a Jew would actually talk" — showing ancient readers *expected* and *critiqued* speech-in-character, evidence the device was live and recognized. *Rating: a real and apt patristic example of *prosōpopoeia* being evaluated as such.*[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The *prosōpopoeia* reading of Romans 7 is *one* respected option, but a long interpretive tradition (Augustine's mature view; Luther; many today) reads the "I" as voicing the *real* experience of the divided self under sin — whether pre-Christian Israel, Adam, or the believer — and the passage's anguished immediacy ("O wretched man that I am," [[Romans 7.24|Romans 7:24]]) reads naturally as more than a staged mask. Even granting prosōpopoeia, identifying the persona specifically as *a Roman adopting an alien law* is a *further* and more contestable step than the general "speech-in-character" point; most who accept the device cast the speaker as Israel-under-Torah or Everyman-under-sin, not a Gentile dabbler. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** recognizing the "I" as a *persona the reader inhabits* (rather than Paul confessing ongoing personal defeat) is exegetically liberating and well-precedented, and it rescues the passage from the despairing "even the apostle can't stop sinning" reading; whether the mask is precisely "a Roman" or more broadly "anyone under an externally-imposed law," the structural point — that Romans 7 dramatizes the law's *insufficiency* to produce mastery, answered by union with Christ in 7:4 — holds, and is offered as a recovered rhetorical reading rather than a dogmatic claim.

## Best Quotes
> "What Paul is doing here is a Greek literary device that is *prosōpopoeia*, which is a fictive 'I' — the writer assumes the character of someone to drive home a point... he's assuming the position as a Roman." — [[Brian Thomassen]][^1]

> "So *prosōpon* is a face... he's setting up a persona. The 'I' does not mean I, Paul — 'I' is the persona that the listener is supposed to inhabit." — host, with [[Brian Thomassen]][^2]
