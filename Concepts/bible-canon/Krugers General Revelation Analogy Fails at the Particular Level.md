# Kruger's General Revelation Analogy Fails at the Particular Level

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Michael Krueger]] argues by a "how much more" analogy: "If the created world (general revelation) is able to speak clearly that it is from God, then how much more so would the canon of scripture (special revelation) speak clearly that it is from God?" Just as nature points to a Creator, Scripture should self-evidently point to its divine Author [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The analogy collapses from the general to the particular — rate: strong
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] grants it is "a clever rhetorical move" but says "it falls apart once you actually think about it carefully." We can speak *broadly* of nature testifying to a Creator, "but if you were to look at a particular thing in the world, you wouldn't necessarily know from looking at it whether it was of human or divine authorship." A watch on a beach does not announce whether God or a watchmaker made it. Likewise, a general sense that "the Bible seems to speak of God" does not tell you *at the book level* whether the Epistle of [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]] belongs in — "without Jude, you might still get the same warm fuzzy" [^2]. The model must work at the *particular* level (which exact books), and there it fails.

### 2. The empirical record refutes "speaks clearly that it is from God" — rate: strong
[[Trent Horn]] notes that the church fathers *did* unanimously agree, from general revelation, that "God created the world" — yet they did *not* all agree on the limits of the canon [^3]. So special revelation manifestly did *not* "speak more clearly" than general revelation; the canon was disputed precisely where creation was not. "But that's just patently false." See [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]]. **Steelman of Kruger:** he could restrict the claim to the *collective* recognition of the covenant community over time rather than instant individual recognition — but that retreat reintroduces the church as the recognizer (see [[Self-Authentication via Church Consensus Backs Into the Catholic Model]]).

### 3. Human-authored things are also ultimately God's — blurring the test — rate: moderate
Heschmeyer adds that "even stuff of human authorship ultimately is of divine authorship," so "is this from God?" is too coarse a filter to sort the 66/73 inspired books from everything else [^2].

## Best Quotes
> "If the created world general revelation is able to speak clearly that it is from God, then how much more so would the canon of scripture special revelation speak clearly that it is from God?" (Kruger, quoted) [^1]

> "If you were to look at a particular thing in the world, you wouldn't necessarily know from looking at it whether it was of human or divine authorship... without Jude, you might still get the same warm fuzzy." [^2]

> "All of the church fathers agreed that God created the world... even though they might've had disagreements about the nature of the canon... but that's just patently false." [^3]
