# Teleios Means Mature and Whole Not Flawless

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] argues that the Greek *téleios* in [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] ("be ye therefore perfect") **does not mean morally flawless**. Across Greco-Roman, Jewish, and New Testament usage it carries a range — *mature, complete, whole, mighty, efficacious, fully-invested* — and the Theological Dictionary of the New Testament concludes the NT never uses it for "the gradual advance to moral perfection." The English "perfect" is a misleading proof-text rendering.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The lexical range is wide (rated: strong, standard lexicography).** In an *educational* context *teleios* describes the student who has advanced to *teacher* ("most mature," not error-free); in a *medical* context, a physician who has "reached the limit of professional study" and is ready to treat; it can mean "mighty / efficacious"; in a *biological* context, a "fully grown adult." None of these means flawless — they mean *brought to the intended end or capacity*.[^2]

2. **The Hebrew equivalent confirms it (rated: strong).** The underlying Hebrew *tamim* ranges from "without blemish" (in the rare sacrificial context) to integrity/innocence/devotion — and is used of **Abraham and Job**, who are "obviously not perfect." So one can translate Matthew 5:48 "be therefore mature / mighty / wholly invested... even as your Father is."[^3]

3. **TDNT's verdict (rated: strong, scholarly).** The Theological Dictionary of the New Testament's tracking of the word concludes: "one does not find in the New Testament any understanding that this word means the gradual Christian advance to moral perfection." That sense is a Latter-day Saint overlay driven by interest in exaltation, read back into the verse.[^4] See [[Toxic Perfectionism Lower Among Religious Than Non-Religious]] for the pastoral fallout of the mistranslation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A defender of the traditional rendering can note that *teleios* in some moral contexts (e.g. James 1:4, "that ye may be perfect and entire, wanting nothing") does shade toward completeness-of-character, and that "mature/whole" can itself imply a high ethical bar — so "perfect" is not simply wrong, just easily misheard. The Latter-day Saint reading also has a real textual hook: the Book of Mormon's Sermon (3 Nephi 12:48) adds "as I... am perfect," extending the standard to the resurrected Christ. Hatch's point is best taken as *de-isolating* the verse (see [[The Therefore in Matthew 5.48 Ties Perfection to Loving Enemies]]), not as denying that discipleship aims high.

## Counter-Objection
"If it just means 'mature,' you've lowered the bar Jesus set." Hatch's reply: the bar is not lowered but *redirected* — from an inward checklist of flawlessness to a wholehearted, God-like completeness *in loving even one's enemies*, which is in fact harder (see [[Be Perfect Defined Elsewhere as Caring for the Poor]]).
