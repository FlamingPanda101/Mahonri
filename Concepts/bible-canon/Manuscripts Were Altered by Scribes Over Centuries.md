# Manuscripts Were Altered by Scribes Over Centuries

## The Claim
[[Dan Beecher]] and [[Dan McClellan]] argue that before any translation, the manuscripts themselves were copied by hand for thousands of years, with scribes "inserting their own stuff and taking out their own stuff," so "those manuscripts don't match up with each other throughout the centuries."[^1] The result is a layered artifact: "you got a translation of a manuscript that was a rewriting of another manuscript that somebody got a wild hair and decided to add something."[^2] "What the Bible *is* changes over time... depending on time, place, people, circumstance" — "a moving target."[^3] See [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Scribal transmission was not perfect copying — rate: strong
This restates the consensus of New Testament textual criticism: hundreds of thousands of variants exist across manuscripts, ranging from spelling to whole-passage additions. Beecher's framing — additions and deletions accumulating across centuries — is accurate at the level of phenomenon. **Steelman of the believer:** advocates of "providential preservation" or of the substantial stability of the text argue that the vast majority of variants are trivial and that no central doctrine hangs on a disputed reading, so the "moving target" overstates the instability. McClellan's reply: even if many variants are minor, *some* are doctrinally loaded (the Trinitarian [[1 John 5.7|Johannine Comma]], the longer ending of Mark, the woman caught in adultery), and the existence of *any* such layer means there is no single fixed text to call "the Bible."

### 2. The woman caught in adultery as the showcase variant — rate: strong
Beecher cites the *pericope adulterae* — "something about a lady caught in adultery" — as a concrete scribal addition.[^2] [[John 7.53-8.11|John 7:53-8:11]] is absent from the earliest and best manuscripts and is the standard classroom example of a beloved passage that entered the text late. See [[Pericope Adulterae Is a Later Interpolation]].

### 3. No miraculous transmission — rate: moderate
Beecher's theological corollary: "it's not like God didn't reach down his hand and scribble all of these words in a way that would easily just be then transmitted perfectly to everybody else."[^4] McClellan generalizes: the Bible is "a moving target," its identity shifting with time, place, and community.[^3] This connects the manuscript layer to the episode's thesis and to the library framing (see [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]]). Rate moderate because the "no miraculous transmission" point is an inference from the variant data rather than independent evidence.
