# The Heresy of Authority Alone

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] names "authority alone" as a false epistemology shared by Calvinist *sola scriptura* and by some Latter-day Saints: treating an authority figure — dead or living prophets, scripture, scientific experts — as the **trump card** whose word is true "regardless of what the other Witnesses of Truth say." He calls the religious version "blind obedience" and insists it "is not our Doctrine."[^1][^2]

## Argument
1. **The *sola scriptura* exemplar.** In a debate with Calvinist friends at Apologia Church, his interlocutors argued the Bible "is literally in every word infallible because every word was placed there by God himself," making it "the trump card over all other Witnesses" — "it doesn't even matter what your eyes tell you or what reason tells you... if the Bible says it it's true."[^1] (Their illustrative claim: creation was "perfectly clear," "six days," because "that's what it says.")[^1]
2. **The LDS qualification on scripture.** Latter-day Saints hold the Bible to be "an important and extremely reliable source of Truth" but "do not claim its infallibility or that it's the ultimate trump card."[^2] Cf. [[Sola Scriptura Muzzles God]], [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]].
3. **The parallel LDS error.** Some Latter-day Saints "think that they are required to ignore all other Witnesses of truth if the living prophets and apostles speak on the matter." Hansen labels this "the heresy of authority alone," an epistemology "shared by both Calvinists and some Latter-day Saints," in which living or dead prophets/apostles are the trump card.[^2][^3]
4. **The Cannon warning against blind self-security.** Hansen quotes [[George Q. Cannon]]: he is "afraid that this people have so much confidence in their leaders that they will not inquire for themselves of God whether they are led by him" — settling into "blind self-security, trusting their Eternal Destiny in the hands of their Leaders with Reckless confidence," which "would thwart the purposes of God in their salvation."[^3]
5. **It generalizes to the secular.** The same attitude appears when "the scientific establishment or some other class of experts are to be trusted even when reason, outcomes and direct experience say otherwise."[^4]

### Steelman (the authority-alone side)
The position is not arbitrary: it secures certainty and unity by removing private judgment as a defeater, and it answers the regress problem ("by what standard do *you* judge the authority?"). Hansen does not deny authority real weight — he ranks it heavily in institutional religious matters (see [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]]); he denies only its *exclusivity* and *infallibility*.

## Best Quotes
> "We might call this false idea the heresy of authority alone. It's an epistemology shared by both Calvinists and some Latter-day Saints — this is where some authority figure... [is] viewed as the trump card: what they say is true regardless of what the other Witnesses of Truth say." [^3]

> "It doesn't even matter what your eyes tell you or what reason tells you or anything else; if the Bible says it it's true." — characterizing the *sola scriptura* worldview [^1]

> "I am more afraid that this people have so much confidence in their leaders that they will not inquire for themselves of God whether they are led by him." — [[George Q. Cannon]], quoted [^3]
