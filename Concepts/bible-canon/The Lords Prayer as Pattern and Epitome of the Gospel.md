## The Point
The Lord's Prayer ([[Matthew 6.9-13|Matthew 6:9–13]]; Luke 11:2–4) served early Christians as both a pattern and a formula for prayer. By the late first century some were urging its recitation in thrice-daily prayers, and it became a key part of the catechetical teaching given before and after baptism. Early Christians recognized that each line evokes moments from Jesus's own life and teachings, so reciting it thoughtfully drew them into reflecting on and emulating Christ.

## Evidence
1. Tertullian (ca. AD 200) wrote that the Lord's Prayer embraces "almost every discourse of the Lord, every record of his discipline; so that, in fact, in the prayer is comprised an epitome of the whole gospel."
2. The plural pronouns ("Our Father," "give us," "forgive us") urged believers from the start of prayer to be mindful of others and to see themselves as one member of God's larger family.
3. Cyprian (ca. AD 252) wrote: "Remember, that when we call God Father, we ought to act as God's children," and that "our prayer is public and common ... we pray not for one, but for the whole people."
4. Patristic readings of each petition tie it to Jesus's life: "Thy will be done" recalls Gethsemane (Luke 22:42); "give us this day our daily bread" recalls Jesus as the bread of life (John 6:35) and the feeding miracles; "forgive us our debts" recalls Jesus's parable of the unforgiving servant.
5. Gregory of Nyssa saw forgiveness as a way humans become like God: "If ... a man imitates in his own life the characteristics of the Divine Nature, he becomes somehow that which he visibly imitates."

## Best Quotes
> "Tertullian (active circa AD 200), for instance, wrote that the Lord's Prayer embraces 'almost every discourse of the Lord, every record of his discipline; so that, in fact, in the prayer is comprised an epitome of the whole gospel.'" [^1]

> "From start to finish, the prayer brought individuals into proper orientation with both God and neighbor — yet another way it drew believers into following Jesus, who taught that love of God and neighbor forms the heart of true religious life." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The early Christian use of the Lord's Prayer as a pattern that orients the believer toward both God and neighbor aligns with the two great commandments (Matthew 22:34–40), central to Latter-day Saint discipleship.
