# Minimal Facts Fails - Grief Experiences Explain the Resurrection Appearances

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the **minimal-facts argument** for the resurrection — popularized by Gary
Habermas — fails. The argument grants a set of facts most critical scholars accept (Jesus was
crucified; former skeptics became believers; followers reported seeing him alive afterward) and
claims the only rational explanation is a literal resurrection. McClellan: that inference "only
betrays everyone's abject ignorance of how the human mind works and how traditions about post-mortem
appearances work." There is "a far better explanation… they came to believe that they had seen the
risen Jesus."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Post-mortem appearance experiences are ordinary — rate: strong
"There are hundreds and hundreds of years of examples of people having experiences with deceased
loved ones."[^1] Grief phenomenology — bereaved people seeing, hearing, and sensing the recently
dead — is a well-documented, cross-cultural feature of human psychology. Given "an adequate grasp of
human psychology and how grief works and how religions work," the reported appearances need no
miracle. This is the same evidential terrain as [[No Shared Hallucination]], argued from the
skeptical side. Contrast the *application* of minimal-facts reasoning in
[[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]].

### 2. The resurrection is not made "one inch less impossible" — rate: strong
McClellan concedes the agreed facts but denies they move the probability: "the resurrection still
does not even become one inch less impossible."[^2] An explanation's availability (resurrection) does
not make it the *best* explanation when a mundane, well-attested alternative (grief experiences +
belief formation) covers the same data.

### 3. The burden is plausibility, not bare possibility — rate: strong
"I allow for it. Demonstrate it's plausible. Demonstrate it's anything other than something that is
not impossible. Once you can do that, then we can have a conversation."[^3] McClellan reframes the
debate: the apologist must show the resurrection is "even close to being as likely as all the other
explanations that already exist" — not merely logically possible. **Steelman of Habermas:** a
defender responds that *group* appearances, an *empty tomb*, and the rise of resurrection belief in a
Second-Temple context where it was not expected for an individual exceed what individual-grief
bereavement visions explain. McClellan's reply (consistent with [[No Shared Hallucination]]) is that
"shared experience" claims are exactly the sort of tradition-shaped reports the data lead us to
expect, and that none of this lifts the resurrection above the naturalistic baseline.
