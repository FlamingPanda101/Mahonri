# The LDS Tradition Emptied the Canon Rather Than Expanded It

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Aaron Shafovaloff]] inverts the usual framing of the LDS open canon. Latter-day Saints describe themselves as having *expanded* the canon (adding the Book of Mormon, Doctrine and Covenants, Pearl of Great Price). Shafovaloff argues the opposite has happened: because the LDS apologetic framework (he claims) holds *no* book to be infallible and inerrant — the "very speech of God" with "finally binding authority for all of faith and practice" — it has not added infallible books but *removed the property of infallibility from the category altogether*. "If I could be bold... they have emptied the canon, they've deconstructed the canon. It's an empty canon of any infallible books."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The definitional pivot (rated: strong on its own terms).** Everything turns on what "canon" *means*. For Protestants, Shafovaloff says, a canonical book is "the infallible and inerrant binding word of God... which have a finally binding authority for all of faith and practice." On that definition, to "expand the canon" is to "add a book that has the infallible speech of God without error."[^1] If the LDS tradition has authoritative-but-fallible books, then by the Protestant definition it has zero canon, regardless of how many volumes sit on the shelf. See [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]] for the three-tier distinction this collapses.

2. **"Infallible revelation never started" (rated: strong rhetorical inversion).** Against Hansen's complaint that Protestants wrongly hold "revelation stopped," Shafovaloff reframes: "in the Latter-day Saint framework, as I understand it, infallible revelation never *started*." So the live disagreement is not *when* revelation ceased but *whether God ever gave an inerrant text at all.* "If we're going to add to the canon, we need to add infallible books... there are no infallible books. So again, they've emptied the canon."[^2]

3. **Connects to Article 8's escape clause (rated: moderate).** Shafovaloff notes the LDS Article of Faith 8 ("the Bible... so far as it is translated correctly") and argues the modern LDS apologetic goes further, appropriating higher criticism to treat parts of scripture as uninspired on principle, not merely mistranslated — which (he says) demonstrates the absence of any text functioning as inerrant final authority.[^3] Hansen indeed affirms exactly this: "I would say that not every word in scripture is even given by God... much of the Old Testament records were legal codes that weren't inspired at all."[^4] See [[Rejecting Biblical Inerrancy Solves the Moral Contradiction Problem]].

4. **Steelman of the LDS reply (rated: strong — the definition is the whole fight).** [[Jacob Hansen]] would say Shafovaloff has *assumed* the very thing in dispute — that "canon" must mean "inerrant." The LDS counter-question is prior: *nothing inside the canon lists or defines the canon*, so the Protestant criterion of infallibility is itself an extra-scriptural tradition. If no book can self-certify which books are infallible, then "infallible canon" is an incoherent demand and a *reliable but fallible* canon under living prophetic authority is the more honest model. On this view the LDS have not emptied the canon; they have *correctly described every canon's actual epistemic status* and supplied the missing authority (revelation) to adjudicate it. See [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority]], [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]], [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]], and [[A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible]].

5. **The standoff (rated: genuine impasse).** Each accuses the other of begging the question. Shafovaloff: the LDS lack any infallible word, so they have no canon in the meaningful sense. Hansen: the demand for an infallible word is an unscriptural Protestant tradition, and Protestants cannot justify *their* canon list from scripture either. The dispute is therefore not about *contents* but about whether infallibility is the right category for revelation at all. See [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon]] and [[Scripture Is Self-Authenticating Because God Is Self-Contained]].
