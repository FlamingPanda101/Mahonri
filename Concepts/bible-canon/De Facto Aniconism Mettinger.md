# De Facto Aniconism (Mettinger)

## The Claim

The non-representational character of early Israelite/Judahite cult imagery resulted not from an explicit doctrinal ban but from **material and market conditions**. [[Dan McClellan]], citing scholar [[Trygve Mettinger]], frames this as a *de facto aniconism*: the divine imagery was symbolic/non-anthropomorphic because small territorial states like Israel and Judah lacked the resources, materials, craftsmen, and demand for elaborate anthropomorphic statuary — not because there was institutional opposition to it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Mettinger's de facto aniconism thesis.** McClellan: "there's a scholar Trygve Mettinger who argues that in ancient Israel and Judah there was a de facto aniconism in the sense that the divine imagery seemed to be non-representational, not because there was actual... institutional opposition to anthropomorphic statuary, but just because the market wasn't there."[^2] *(Evidence rating: accurately represents Mettinger's published "No Graven Image?" thesis; it is one influential position in an ongoing scholarly debate.)*

2. **No anthropomorphic statuary recovered from Israel/Judah.** By contrast, anthropomorphic divine statuary is abundant from Egypt and Mesopotamia. The asymmetry, McClellan argues, reflects the comparative poverty and smaller scale of Israelite/Judahite states, not a unique theological aniconism.[^3] *(Evidence rating: the absence is real; the *explanation* is contested.)*

3. **Symbolic rather than figural representation.** The representations that do exist are "more symbolic" — e.g., the standing stone, or the [[Yahweh and His Asherah Inscriptions|Yahweh and his Asherah]] drawing with [[Bes]]-style bovine imagery argued by some to depict God's face.[^4] *(Evidence rating: the Kuntillet Ajrud iconography is genuine but its identification as Yahweh is itself debated.)*

4. **It remains an open debate.** McClellan explicitly flags that whether there was anthropomorphic representation of the God of Israel is "an ongoing debate" — "we don't know for sure."[^5] *(Evidence rating: appropriately hedged; this is honest about scholarly uncertainty.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Against Mettinger, scholars such as those favoring "programmatic aniconism" (and confessional readers) argue the aniconism was *theologically principled* from early on — Yahweh's incomparability and the second commandment — and that resource-scarcity cannot fully explain it, since even poor cultures produce figural cult objects. The fact that even cheap standing stones were eventually condemned suggests a principled rather than merely economic aniconism. McClellan presents Mettinger's view as one (compelling) option, not settled fact.

## Best Quotes

> "There's a scholar Trygve Mettinger who argues that in ancient Israel and Judah there was a de facto aniconism... not because there was institutional opposition to anthropomorphic statuary, but just because the market wasn't there."[^2]

> "They didn't have near the complexity and the resources and the market that you had in Babylon or in Egypt... but that's an ongoing debate as well. Was there anthropomorphic representation of the God of Israel? We don't know for sure."[^5]
