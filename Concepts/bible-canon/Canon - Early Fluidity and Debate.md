# Canon - Early Fluidity and Debate

## Overview

This is a hub note for the **early, pre-closure state of the canon** — the period in which the boundary of "scripture" was fluid, porous, and actively debated rather than fixed. The cluster's recurring point is that the assumption of a single, obvious, closed canon is an *anachronism* read back onto communities for whom "scripture" was a broader and contested category. Treating the eventual boundary as self-evident to first-century Christians is circular: the very persistence of canonical debate for centuries demonstrates the boundary was not self-evident.

The flagship illustration is [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude]]: an apostolic-era author quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 with the prophetic verb *proephēteusen* ("prophesied"), and the book is among the most-copied at Qumran — yet it later fell outside the boundary. That a New Testament author treated as authoritative a text the later church rejected is direct evidence of early fluidity.

## The Steelman

A defender grants a degree of early diversity but insists a *core* (the four Gospels, Paul's main letters) was effectively settled very early, with only the fringes (Hebrews, Revelation, 2 Peter, the Antilegomena) genuinely disputed — so "fluidity" overstates the case for the books that matter. The cluster's reply: even a stable core does not yield a *closed* canon, and the disputed margins (including books quoted as scripture by NT authors) are exactly where the doctrine of a fixed, inspired list breaks down.
