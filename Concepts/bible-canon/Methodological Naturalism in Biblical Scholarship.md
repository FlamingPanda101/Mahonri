# Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship

## The Claim
Critical biblical scholarship does not reject real prophecy out of anti-supernatural bias or hostility to faith. Rather, it is a **methodological necessity**: if scholarship must "allow for the possibility of real prophecy," there is no consistent way to interrogate *any* claim to prophecy, because the normal critical tools (linguistic analysis, historical context, comparative literature) must be selectively suspended for whichever text one claims is genuinely prophetic.[^1]

## The Argument

### The Double-Standard Problem

McClellan poses the dilemma directly:[^1]

> "When you say we have to allow for real prophecy, you're saying the normal critical scholarly methodologies and frameworks and lenses — we're going to exempt this story right here. They do not apply. But to say *anything* is *not* real prophecy requires the imposition of those very frameworks and lenses and methodologies."

The result is methodological cherry-picking: you apply historical-critical methods to [[Joseph Smith]]'s prophecies, the Quran, the Bhagavad Gita, or [[Harold Camping]]'s predictions — but exempt the biblical text from the same scrutiny. There is no principled way to draw the line.[^2]

### The Failed Prophecy Problem

Even within traditions that claim real prophecy, apologists make excuses for failed prophecies. McClellan gives the example of groups who predicted "that comet over there — we're going to be riding that thing next week" and then rationalized the failure. Without methodological naturalism, "you don't have a way to consistently apply the same approach and wind up at 'that's real prophecy, that's fake prophecy.'"[^3]

### Not Anti-Faith

McClellan is explicit that this is a **scholarly method**, not a metaphysical claim:[^4]

> "That doesn't mean that you can't have a faith that does include prophecy. ... Anybody can — you get the meaning and the significance and the utility out of it that's going to make you a better person, it's going to help make you a more contributing member of society."

The critical scholar's goal is not "look how awesome the Bible is" but "try to use the data to find out what we can about where this thing came from, how it came together, how it has been used in the past, what it means to us now."[^4]

## Best Quote

> "Out of methodological necessity, critical scholarship cannot accept real prophecy — at least in my opinion — which means that no, if the text says there's this dude named Cyrus, that text was written after that dude named Cyrus was on the scene."[^5]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-arguments
- Believers may argue that methodological naturalism is itself a philosophical commitment that arbitrarily excludes divine action a priori.
- See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]] for the broader contrast in methods.
