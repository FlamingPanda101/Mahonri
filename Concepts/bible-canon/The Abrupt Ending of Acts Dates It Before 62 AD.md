# The Abrupt Ending of Acts Dates It Before 62 AD

## The Claim
[[Michael Jones]] argues (following an observation he traces to the skeptic [[Adolf von Harnack]]) that the **enigmatic, abrupt ending of Acts** — Paul left alive under house arrest awaiting his trial before Caesar — is best explained by Acts being written *before* that trial occurred, ~62-63 AD. Because Luke used Mark as a source and wrote his Gospel before Acts, this pulls **Mark, Luke, and Acts** all before ~63 AD. This is an argument from *presence* (unpaid foreshadowing), not from silence. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Acts builds elaborate foreshadowing to a trial it never narrates.** Paul says he will go to Rome; Jesus says he will; an angel on the ship tells him "you're going to stand before Caesar" — "epic foreshadowing." Then Acts simply ends: "he lived there a full two years at his own expense and people came and visited him." Dramatically, "this would have been a horrible way to end any work." *Rating: strong; the narrative set-up genuinely demands a payoff.* [^1]
2. **It's argument from presence, not silence.** Jones is explicit: "it's not absence of evidence... we're arguing from presence. There's all this foreshadowing of this trial before Caesar... [that] never happens. That is horrible fiction if that's what you're writing." A post-trial author would have included the climactic courtroom scene (and the gospel preached to the emperor). *Rating: moderate-strong; distinguishes this from the weaker "Acts doesn't mention X" arguments.* [^2]
3. **The chain pulls the synoptics early.** If Acts is ~62-63, then Luke's Gospel is earlier still, and (on Markan priority) Mark earlier again — so "Mark, Luke, and Acts all prior to like 63 AD." *Rating: moderate-strong; the inference is only as strong as Markan priority and Lukan authorship of Acts, both of which Jones grants.* [^3]
4. **Matthew's temple instructions assume a standing temple.** Matthew preserves teaching on "how to give proper gifts before the altar at the temple" (Matt 5:23-24) — odd to include "if the temple had been destroyed," suggesting Matthew too wrote pre-70. *Rating: moderate; suggestive rather than decisive.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Critics offer non-early explanations for the ending: (a) Luke ends where his *theological* goal is met — the gospel reaching Rome "unhindered" — not where the chronology stops; (b) Luke may have *known* Paul's death but chose not to narrate a martyrdom that would end on a downer or implicate Rome; (c) a planned third volume; (d) the majority of scholars still date Mark ~65-75 and Luke-Acts ~80-90 on other grounds (Luke's apparent knowledge of Mark's "abomination of desolation" sharpened toward the siege). On these readings the abrupt ending is a literary or theological choice, not a dating clock.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: the theological-goal explanation does not account for the *specific* unpaid foreshadowing of the *trial before Caesar* (not merely "arrival in Rome") — an author writing after the verdict had a ready-made climax and a chance to show Paul preaching to the emperor, and omitting it is far stranger than simply stopping at 62. The "knew but didn't narrate the death" option still concedes an early *composition* if the trial itself had not yet happened. Pre-70 dating for the synoptics also coheres with their relatively *pro-Roman* portrayal (see [[Post-70 Dating Presupposes Prophecy Is Impossible]]).

## Best Quotes
> "He finally gets his trial... an angel appears to him on a ship and says, 'You're going to stand before Caesar.' Epic foreshadowing. Then he gets to Rome and then Luke writes, 'And he lived there a full two years at his own expense'... the end. It's an enigmatic ending... It's not absence of evidence... we're arguing from presence." — [[Michael Jones]] [^1][^2]

> "You have Mark, Luke, and Acts all prior to like 63 AD, all right in there. So that's a pretty good case that at least three of our New Testament books were written very early on." — Jones [^3]
