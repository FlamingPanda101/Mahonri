# Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category

## The Claim
The word "monotheism" is not a neutral, descriptive term for "belief in one God" that can simply be read back into the Bible. It is a 17th-century theological coinage that has *always* functioned as an identity marker and value judgment — sorting "true" religion from "false" — and contemporary scholars revise its definition precisely so they can keep finding it in the Bible. Asking whether the Bible is "monotheistic" therefore smuggles in a modern, ideologically-loaded category. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The term was coined in the 17th century by Henry More (rating: strong; historically verifiable)
The word was created by the Cambridge Platonist and philosopher Henry More in the 17th century. McClellan stresses it did not arise from "critical interrogation of the religious traditions of the world" but as an in-group/out-group label. [^1]

> "the word was first created in the 17th century it was coined by a Cambridge platinus a philosopher named Henry Moore... it was used not as the product of some kind of critical interrogation of the religious traditions of the world but as an identity marker and a value judgment as a way to distinguish good religion from Bad Religion" [^1]

### 2. Its earliest uses policed the *nature* of the deity, not the *number* (rating: strong)
More denied Muslims were true monotheists ("a hypocritical pretension to monotheism") and denied that materialists who posited one material deity counted at all — proving the term tracked what *kind* of deity qualified, not arithmetic. The deists then claimed only Unitarians, not Trinitarians, were real monotheists. [^2]

> "so it wasn't really about the number of deities it was about the nature of the deity did it qualify as actual religious belief an actual deity according to the beliefs of Christianity so it was about distinguishing us from them" [^2]

### 3. "Belief that there is only one God" is an etymological fallacy (rating: moderate; a defensible methodological claim)
McClellan calls the dictionary definition (e.g., Webster's "belief that there is only one God") an etymological fallacy, "because that's never how the term was actually used." [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's position — and that of [[Larry Hurtado]] and [[Benjamin Sommer]] — is that "monotheism" is a useful descriptor for the *functional* exaltation of one deity as absolutely supreme creator over all others, and that quibbling over the word's modern origin does not change the underlying biblical reality. McClellan concedes the question is partly "a matter of terminology," but insists the revision is *ad hoc*: it is performed only because communities descended from the Bible need ideological continuity, "so however we need to understand monotheism to find it in the Bible we will understand it in that way." [^4] He frames this as renegotiating the definition to serve a rhetorical goal rather than describing the texts on their own terms.

### 5. McClellan's published treatments (rating: cite-forward)
He notes a paper presented at a Brown University conference (forthcoming edited volume) and an entry in the *Oxford Research Encyclopedia of Religion* developing the historiography of the term. [^2]

### 6. Restated to Alex O'Connor (rating: corroborating)
In the [[Alex OConnor]] interview McClellan makes the same case: "monotheism first pops up in the 17th century," has "always been used primarily as an identity marker and a value judgment" (monotheists = good people), and the modern attempt to keep it in the Bible — e.g. redefining Yahweh as "species unique" (a [[Michael Heiser]] term) — is driven by communities needing ideological continuity: "we get our beliefs from the Bible, monotheism is one of those beliefs, so obviously it must be in the Bible." He concludes that once everything is "situated historically, we don't see what people understand today to be monotheism in the Bible," so it "is not a helpful analytical framework to impose upon" it.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "this just raises that observation without engaging the concerns that have been expressed... for the way the concept of monotheism has been revised and redefined for no other reason than that that is necessary in order to find it in the Bible" [^5]

> "it has always been more about the nature of the one God being worshiped than the number of the Gods" [^3]

> "we have to have that ideological continuity so however we need to understand monotheism to find it in the Bible we will understand it in that way as long as that means we can find monotheism in the Bible" [^4]
