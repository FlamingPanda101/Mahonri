# Moses 1 Supplies the Missing Temptation in the Moses-Christ Parallel

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] reports an argument he first met at BYU through his JST professor [[Robert Millet]] (relaying an unnamed biblical scholar): the life of [[Moses]] runs closely parallel to the life of [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] — but with **one conspicuous gap**. Both were targeted for death as infants while other babies were killed, both were taken into/associated with Egypt for protection (see [[Matthew 2.13-15|Matthew 2:13-15]]), both fed God's people miraculously (manna / the 5,000), both fasted forty days in the wilderness. The single missing episode is the **temptation in the wilderness**: the Gospels narrate Christ's, but the Bible records no parallel temptation of Moses. Bradley's payoff: **Moses 1 *is* that missing temptation narrative** (Satan confronts Moses, "worship me," and is repulsed), completing the typology — "everything else parallels, and it's not there, but then it is here."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The parallel set is dense and the gap is specific
Bradley lists the correspondences (infancy slaughter survived, Egypt, miraculous feeding, forty-day fast) and isolates the lone exception: "the one big missing experience from the life of Christ... that's missing from the life of Moses is the temptation in the wilderness."[^1] *Rating: the listed parallels are conventional and real in the texts; the rhetorical force depends on treating the set as a deliberate pattern with one slot to fill.*

### 2. Moses 1 fills exactly that slot
"Moses 1 is the story of the temptation of Moses in the wilderness, just like the Gospels tell us the story of the temptation of Jesus in the wilderness. It's that story." The structural fit — a wilderness temptation by Satan, resisted — is presented as completing a pattern the canonical Bible leaves open.[^2] This dovetails with [[Discernment Comes From Prior Experience of God's Glory]] (how Moses resists) and with [[Moses Is a Son of God in the Likeness of Jesus Not Jesus a Prophet Like Moses]] (why Moses mirrors Christ at all). *Rating: internally elegant; load-bearing only if Moses 1 is accepted as recovering ancient Moses-tradition.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is a textbook target for the parallelomania critique the vault applies elsewhere (see [[The Quetzalcoatl Parallel Names Moses Not Christ]] and [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]]). First, the parallels are **selected and elastic**: many heroic-birth narratives feature threatened infants (Sargon, Cyrus, Krishna), forty is a stock biblical round number, and "associated with Egypt" means different things for Moses (raised there) and Jesus (briefly sheltered there). Second, the argument is **circular as evidence for Moses 1's antiquity**: it assumes the very pattern (Moses' life was *designed* to prefigure Christ's, gap included) that only holds if Restoration scripture is already granted; a critic says the Gospels deliberately styled Jesus as a new Moses (a known redactional motif), which explains the overlaps without any lost Moses-temptation. Third, "the pattern has a hole, therefore the hole was filled by a text that conveniently supplies it" is **affirming the consequent** — a 19th-century author who knew the Gospels' temptation could simply have written one for Moses. The fair conclusion: as *typology* the completed parallel is homiletically striking, but it carries no independent weight toward Moses 1 being ancient, and it cuts the other way under the vault's own standard for parallels.
