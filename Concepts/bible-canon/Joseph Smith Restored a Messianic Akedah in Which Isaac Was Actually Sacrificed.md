# Joseph Smith Restored a Messianic Akedah in Which Isaac Was Actually Sacrificed

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] (author of *The Lost Gems of Genesis*), with [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Britney Chapman Nash]], argues that the binding of [[Isaac]] ([[Genesis 22|Genesis 22]]) has been editorially "covered up." On his reconstruction the *original* story was a **messianic resurrection** account: Abraham, having been shown the resurrection and the day of the Messiah, actually **slaughtered and burned Isaac**, who was then revived — Isaac himself consenting and even urging it, confident God could raise him. The [[Josiah|Deuteronomist]] editors, who disliked child sacrifice, resurrection, and messianism, rewrote the chapter's second half (inserting the angel who stays Abraham's hand, swapping the promised *lamb* for a *ram*, and switching the divine name). Joseph Smith, via the JST (especially JST Genesis 15), restored the "key": Abraham knew Christ and the resurrection, so the Akedah is about *faith in God's power over death*, not blind obedience.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Internal seams in Genesis 22 — rate: real textual data, contested reading
The chapter shows genuine source-critical features the panel reads as editorial fingerprints: the speaker shifts from **Elohim** (first half) to **Yahweh/Jehovah** (second half); Isaac is called Abraham's "only son" though [[Ishmael (son of Abraham)|Ishmael]] exists; the four who ascend (Abraham, Isaac, two servants) are reduced to Abraham alone returning ([[Genesis 22|22:19]] — "where's Isaac?"); and the substitute animal shifts from a *lamb* (promised, 22:8) to a *ram* (offered, 22:13).[^2] *The seams are real and noted in critical scholarship; reading them specifically as "Isaac died, then was edited to survive" is the speculative leap.*

### 2. A Jewish tradition that Isaac actually died and rose — rate: genuinely attested, minority strand
Drawing on [[Shalom Spiegel]]'s *The Last Trial*, plus the Book of Jasher, the Slavonic Palea, and Pseudo-Philo, Jonah documents rabbinic/piyyut traditions in which Isaac's soul departs and returns, his ashes lie on the altar, and "the blood of Isaac" purchases Israel's chosenness — with Isaac requesting his own binding (the "Aqedah") so he won't flinch.[^3] *Spiegel's collection is a real scholarly work; these are authentic (if non-mainstream and largely post-biblical) Jewish traditions, not invented.*

### 3. New Testament texts that "know" a death-and-resurrection Akedah — rate: suggestive proof-texts
[[Hebrews 11.17|Hebrews 11:17-19]] says Abraham offered Isaac "accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead; from whence also he received him in a figure"; [[James 2.20-23|James 2:21]] says Abraham was "justified by works, when he had offered Isaac"; and the Isaac references in [[Acts 3.13-15|Acts 3]] sit inside resurrection preaching that provokes the resurrection-denying Sadducees.[^2] Jonah argues the NT writers presuppose a resurrection-Akedah. *Suggestive; Hebrews 11:19's "in a figure/parable" is the natural canonical basis and does not require an actual death.*

### 4. The LDS "key": Abraham knew the Messiah — rate: distinctive restoration claim
JST Genesis 15 shows Abraham the days of the Son of Man and the resurrection ("though thou wast dead, yet am I not able to give it thee?… he must first be quickened"), which reconciles [[John 8.56|John 8:56]] ("Abraham rejoiced to see my day") and matches [[Jacob 4.4-5|Jacob 4:5]]'s "similitude of God and his Only Begotten." On this reading the Akedah is faith in Christ's power over death, not legalistic obedience. See [[Abraham as a Backstory to Genesis]] and [[The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The strongest reply: (a) **the plain text says Isaac lived** — the angel stops Abraham, the ram is substituted, and the NT (Hebrews 11:19) explicitly says Abraham received Isaac back "in a figure/parable," i.e., *as if* from the dead, which is the whole point; positing an actual death requires treating the canonical ending as a forgery on the strength of a *later* minority midrash. (b) **The Spiegel traditions are post-biblical and polemical** — Spiegel himself frames them partly against the Christian charge of borrowing; they are medieval aggadic expansions, not evidence of an original Genesis 22 in which Isaac died, so they cannot establish what the *Urtext* said. (c) **The seams have a standard explanation** — Elohim/Yahweh variation and "only son" hyperbole are explicable within ordinary source/redaction theory and Hebrew idiom (Isaac is the son *of the promise*) without a deletion of resurrection-messianism; and the lamb/ram detail trades on an English near-homophone, not the Hebrew (*seh* vs. *ayil*), where Abraham's "God will provide *himself* a lamb" is naturally read as fulfilled christologically in the ram or ultimately in Christ. A reasonable LDS reply (made in the episode): the case is cumulative and the JST is doing the decisive work — the restored Genesis 15 independently supplies the messianic-resurrection frame, the rabbinic "blood of Isaac" strand shows the theme is ancient enough to be *remembered* even if suppressed, and the [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|Deuteronomist reform]] gives a documented motive and mechanism for muting atonement/resurrection themes (cf. [[The Day of Atonement Was Edited Out of Deuteronomy and the Psalms]]). The narrow, defensible point: Genesis 22 is *more* than blind-obedience theater, and the Christ-pointing/resurrection layer is textually invited (Hebrews, Jacob 4), even if "Isaac literally died" outruns the evidence.

This is the maximal version of the vault's binding-of-Isaac material: where [[The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ]] reads the Akedah as Christ-pointing *similitude* (the defensible core), this note pushes to a literal death-and-resurrection that the Deuteronomists allegedly erased — fitting the broader thesis of [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]] and [[Surviving Traces of Human Sacrifice in the Hebrew Bible]].
