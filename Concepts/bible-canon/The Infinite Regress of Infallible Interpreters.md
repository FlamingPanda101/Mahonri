# The Infinite Regress of Infallible Interpreters

#type/claim

## The Claim
This is the **Protestant counter** to the standard LDS/Catholic demand for a living, infallible interpreter (see [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]). [[Brother Oz (Austin)]] argues that the demand is self-defeating: if an infallible *text* is worthless without an infallible *interpreter*, then that interpreter is in turn worthless without an infallible interpreter *of the interpreter*, and so on — "an infallible interpreter of infallible interpreters of infallible interpreters of infallible books."[^1] Since an infinite chain of infallible causes is impossible, **no** system terminates the regress, so the absence of an institutional final interpreter is not a defect peculiar to *sola scriptura*. The only place the regress can stop, Oz says, is in the **Logos** himself: "that yearning for epistemological certainty can only be met in the Logos who is Christ. There can be no other means by which perfect certainty can be established."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Evangelical (Brother Oz)
- **Symmetry / tu quoque.** Carroll presses: "What good is the infallibility of the text if you have no infallible interpreter?"[^3] Oz's reply reframes the burden — the LDS prophet must *also* be interpreted, and a disputed prophetic statement needs an authoritative reading just as a disputed verse does (the debate's own opening, where Carroll concedes Latter-day Saints struggle to say which prophetic statements are authoritative, illustrates the point).[^4] So pointing at a living magisterium relocates the regress; it does not end it.
- **Certainty is eschatological, not institutional.** Oz cheerfully concedes you "can't be perfectly certain of anything" — "you can't be certain that you're not in the matrix right now" — invoking the Cartesian/Kantian point that no finite knower escapes radical doubt.[^5] This aligns the argument with the vault's epistemic-humility cluster: [[100 Percent Rational Certainty Is Essentially Impossible]], [[Perfect Certainty of Experience Not of Interpretation]], [[The Epistemic Regress Problem]]. What grounds knowledge is not a human court but the Logos as "the God who exists as knower, as knowledge, and as known" — tying this rebuttal to [[Relationality Requires Three - The Trinitarian Grounding of Meaning]].
- **A genuinely shared concession.** Strikingly, Carroll **agrees**: "We don't believe in an infallible [interpreter]... that's actually my criticism of Catholicism," and Oz calls it "a really good one."[^6] So the Latter-day Saint and the Protestant *jointly* reject the Catholic infallible-magisterium model; the live disagreement narrows to whether *living prophetic guidance* (LDS) meaningfully reduces interpretive chaos better than *Scripture-plus-Spirit* (Protestant), even if neither is infallible.

### Steelman of the Latter-day Saint (Hayden Carroll)
- **The regress is a red herring against the LDS model.** Carroll's actual claim (see [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]) is not that the interpreter must be *infallible* but that there must be a *living, final adjudicator* — someone with authority to settle disputes for the covenant community, as Peter does in [[Acts 15]]. An authoritative ruling can be binding without being infallible; the LDS Quorum functions like a supreme court whose judgment is final-for-now even if revisable, which is exactly what *sola scriptura* lacks.[^7] So Oz's "infinite regress" attacks a maximalist (Catholic) demand the LDS need not make.
- **Practical fruit, not theoretical regress.** The LDS point is empirical: a tradition with living apostolic authority produces fewer church-splitting schisms over essentials than a tradition where "each reader is his own final court." Oz's regress argument, even if it wins philosophically, does not answer *who decides today* when two sincere readers disagree — it only shows that nobody decides *with certainty*, which is precisely the LDS complaint about Protestantism.
- **Certainty in Christ still needs a channel.** Granting that ultimate certainty rests in the Logos, the Latter-day Saint asks *how* the Logos communicates a binding reading now: continuing revelation through called servants, versus the Protestant's appeal to private illumination. Locating certainty "in Christ" is common ground; the question is the *mechanism*, which returns to the authority question rather than dissolving it.

**Honest assessment.** Oz's regress argument is a clean and correct rebuttal to the *strong* form of the objection ("an infallible text is useless without an infallible interpreter") — that form really does prove too much, indicting Catholicism and LDS prophets alike. But it under-engages the *weak* form Carroll actually presses: the need for a living, *authoritative* (not infallible) adjudicator. Both men ultimately agree no infallible interpreter exists and that certainty rests in Christ; the unresolved residue is institutional, not epistemic — *who has authority to settle disputes for the body*, where the LDS appeal to living prophets and the Protestant to Scripture-and-Spirit genuinely diverge.
