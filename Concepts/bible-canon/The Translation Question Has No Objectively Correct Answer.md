# The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the third judgment is which **translation** you will read, and that the overwhelming majority of people — even those who speak the original languages — consume the Bible *in translation*.[^1] "There are tons and tons of different translations and... people getting phenomenally dogmatic about which English translation is the right one or the best one."[^2] McClellan's barb: "the most dogmatic ones are the ones who are convinced that the worst translation is the right one."[^2] See [[Three Judgments Stand Between You and a Bible]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Even original-language readers translate — rate: strong
The number reading devotionally in ancient Hebrew/Greek is "vanishingly small."[^1] Greek Orthodox believers read the Septuagint in modern Greek (Katharevousa or Demotic), choosing among translations like the Vamvas; "even for someone who speaks the language in which it was originally written, the majority... are going to be consuming it in translation."[^3] So translation is not an optional layer but the *normal* mode of access. **Steelman:** an advocate of a "best" translation can argue that translation quality is *not* purely subjective — some renderings are demonstrably more accurate (lexically, grammatically) than others, so there is a real better/worse axis even without a single perfect text. McClellan agrees quality is real ("some of them better than others") but stresses no rendering is *the* objectively correct Bible, and lay readers usually lack the qualification to assess quality.

### 2. Multi-translation "negotiation" without qualification — rate: moderate
McClellan notes evangelical influencers recommend *multiple* translations to let readers "negotiate" which reading they like best "even if you have zero qualification to assess the quality."[^4] He mocks the criterion as "which one sets off the tuning fork in your loins."[^4] The point: in practice the translation choice is driven by taste and tribe, not philology. See [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text]].

### 3. The KJV-only joke — rate: moderate
The old joke "if the King James version was good enough for Paul, it's good enough for me" exposes the absurdity of treating a 1611 English translation as the original; "most people who are dogmatically holding on to the King James wouldn't understand" the joke.[^5] McClellan reports the Data Over Dogma policy is to use the **NRSVue** "unless until a better translation comes out."[^6] See [[KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text]].

## Best Quotes
