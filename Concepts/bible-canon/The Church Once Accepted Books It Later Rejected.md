# The Church Once Accepted Books It Later Rejected

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Kelly holds that the Spirit guided the church to recognize the right canon. Hayden's rebuttal: the recognized canon **changed over time** — Christians once accepted books (Tobit, Wisdom) now rejected by Protestants, and there was **no fixed Jewish OT canon** in Jesus' day. If the "Spirit-led recognition" process erred before, it cannot guarantee an *infallible* result now.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. No settled OT canon in the first century — rate: strong
"In Jesus's day, there is not a set Old Testament... Did every Jew agree on an Old Testament canon? The answer is no. That came about two or three or four hundred years later when the councils wanted to establish orthodoxy."[^2] **Scholarly context:** broadly correct — the Pharisaic/Masoretic boundary hardened later (Jamnia discussions, etc.), Sadducees prized the Torah, and the Septuagint circulated wider OT contents. Kelly's reply that the apostles cite the OT presupposes *which* OT — the contested point.

### 2. The "Spirit-led recognition" trap — rate: strong dilemma
Hayden's pointed cross-examination: "Did the church ever wrongly identify books of scripture? ... If you want to posit an infallible standard, you can't be sure which books should go in your canon, because the church at one time accepted books that you now reject — and they would say the same thing: 'We were led by the Spirit to recognize them.'"[^3] **Assessment:** a genuine dilemma for a fallible-recognition-of-infallible-books model. **Steelman of Kelly:** Protestants distinguish books the *whole* church universally received (homologoumena) from disputed books (antilegomena); the "core" was stable even while edges were debated, so growth in clarity ≠ prior error.

### 3. Tobit / Wisdom and the Spirit-witness reliability problem — rate: strong tu quoque against the spirit test
Kelly concedes some early Christians accepted Tobit as scripture and "would say they were Spirit-led," then turns it: "Is being Spirit-led a reliable source on your own standard, because they thought they were being Spirit-led?"[^4] **Assessment:** this is Kelly's best counter-punch — it impales Hayden's own appeal (testing prophets by personal spiritual witness, see [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]]). Hayden's answer leans on the later councils settling on 39 OT / 27 NT while "tradition of man" later added books — but that reintroduces *tradition* as the canon-identifier, conceding [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]].

### 4. Catholic 73, Orthodox 81–88 — rate: contextual
Kelly notes Catholics have 73, Orthodox ~81, Orientals up to ~88, while LDS and evangelicals share the same 66 (with the Song of Solomon caveat).[^5] **Assessment:** the spread of canons across communions is itself evidence that no *public, unambiguous* divine designation settled the list — supporting Hayden's authorization thesis even as Kelly cites it to stress evangelical-LDS agreement.

## Best Quotes
> "Did the church ever wrongly identify books of scripture? Did they ever think some books were scripture, but they actually weren't? ... the church at one time accepted books that you now reject. And they would say the same thing: we were led by the Spirit to recognize them, and now you reject them."[^3]

> "In Jesus's day, there is not a set Old Testament. Just do a simple Google search... that came about two or 300 or 400 years later when the councils wanted to establish orthodoxy."[^2]

> "Is being Spirit-led a reliable source on your own standard, because they thought they were being Spirit-led?" (Kelly's counter)[^4]
