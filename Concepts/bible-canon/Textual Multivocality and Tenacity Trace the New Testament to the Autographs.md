# Textual Multivocality and Tenacity Trace the New Testament to the Autographs

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Wes Huff]] argues that two properties of the New Testament's manuscript tradition — **multivocality** (many independent lines of transmission across places and copyists) and **tenacity** (early readings survive and resurface) — let scholars recover the original wording with high confidence, *because of* the abundance of variants, not in spite of it. He reframes the popular "telephone game" objection: the telephone game is a *single* line of transmission designed to corrupt; the NT is the opposite — written by multiple authors, in multiple places, copied by multiple scribes, to multiple audiences. The right analogy, he says, is a **100,000-piece jigsaw puzzle with extra pieces**: once you assemble it, the duplicates and intruders are obvious.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The numbers.** ~6,000 Greek manuscripts (2nd-17th c.), ~10,000 Latin, and ~24,000 total counting Coptic, Syriac, Georgian, Old Church Slavonic, Ethiopic, etc. With ~140,000 words in the Greek NT and 400,000-600,000 variants, "anything handwritten has mistakes" — but the variants are mostly trivial and locatable. *Rating: these figures track standard apologetic/text-critical citation (Ehrman gives similar variant counts); strong as orders of magnitude.*[^1]
2. **Most variants are mechanical and obvious.** Huff's worked examples: the **movable nu** (Greek *n* added/dropped before vowels, like English a/an), which scribes copying-not-speaking misplaced; and **homoeoteleuton** ("similar endings") in [[1 John 3.1-3|1 John 3:1]], where a scribe's eye skipped from one *-men* ending to the next, dropping *kai esmen* ("and we are") — recoverable by comparing earlier manuscripts (Codex Sinaiticus). *Rating: these are textbook, verifiable examples of scribal variant types.*[^2]
3. **The "Wicked Bible" illustrates why few copies are dangerous.** The 1631 King James printing dropped "not" from "thou shalt not commit adultery" ([[Exodus 20.3|Exodus 20:14]]). If that were one of only two copies, recovering the original would be hard; with thousands of witnesses, such an error is trivially corrected — more data is *better*, not worse. *Rating: a real printing; a clean illustration of the comparative-analysis principle.*[^3]
4. **Early, well-preserved witnesses anchor the text.** Huff cites P66 (late 2nd / early 3rd c., Gospel of John) as reading "word for word, verbatim" with a modern critical edition at John 1:1 and 1:14 — early manuscripts that let scholars "see the spider web and trace all the lines back to the original." *Rating: P66 is genuine and early; the verbatim claim is broadly fair for the cited verses.*[^2]

## Apologetic Engagement
Huff grants the honest core of the critical case — "anything handwritten has mistakes," the autographs are lost, and some variants are "significant" and some manuscripts "very fragmentary." His claim is bounded: no variant "affects any essential Christian doctrine," and "it's never the case that we just don't know what it is" — the choice is always between *this* reading and *that* reading, one of which is original. The **strongest critical reply** (Bart Ehrman's) is that "no essential doctrine is affected" smuggles in a value judgment — variants like the long ending of Mark, the *pericope adulterae* ([[Pericope Adulterae Is a Later Interpolation|John 7:53-8:11]]), and the [[1 John 5.7|Johannine Comma]] *do* matter theologically and historically even if the gospel survives them — and that "reliable" (we can reconstruct the 2nd-century text) is not the same as "original" (what the author first wrote) or "true." Huff's framework largely concedes the first distinction (he is a recoverability argument, not an inerrancy argument) and does not claim the third; so the engagement is less a clash than a difference of emphasis between Huff and Ehrman over how much the genuine variants matter.

## LDS Relevance
This argument cuts both ways for Latter-day Saints. It strongly supports the LDS commitment to a **generally reliable Bible** and undercuts the caricature that the NT is hopelessly corrupt. But the LDS "plain and precious things removed" thesis ([[1 Nephi 13.40|1 Nephi 13]]) sits in tension with Huff's conclusion that the NT *text* is recoverable and that no major content was lost in *copying*. The reconciliation most LDS scholars take: the losses were primarily in the Old Testament's pre-canonical *formation* and redaction (the Deuteronomist reforms — see [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]]), and in doctrine/covenant rather than in the wording of extant NT books. A Latter-day Saint can thus affirm Huff's text-critical optimism about the NT while locating "plain and precious" loss upstream of the manuscript tradition he is describing. See also [[The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument]].

## Best Quotes
> "Unlike something like the telephone game, I would argue that the New Testament is much more like a 100,000-piece puzzle... once you start to put the pieces together you can see where there are pieces that don't belong." — [[Wes Huff]] [^1]

> "It's never the case that we just don't know what it is. It's always the difference between this word or that word, this phrase or that phrase — one of them is the original, and we know this because of the tenacity of the text." — [[Wes Huff]] [^1]
