# The High-Greek Objection Ignores Scribes and Amanuenses

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Bart Ehrman]]: "The gospels are written in high-level, very educated literary Greek. How did [illiterate Galilean fishermen] learn highly literate Greek?... It took years of training... none of the disciples had that." So the disciples could not have written the Gospels. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Michael Jones]] gives several replies that, taken together, defeat the objection for three of the four Gospels and neutralize it for the fourth. [^2]

1. **The Greek is pop-level, not elite.** "These were not highly sophisticated biographies... not as sophisticated as Plutarch or Josephus or Suetonius — they were pop-level biographies." And Mark's Greek is notoriously *rough* ("written really rough and hard... always in present tense"), which is why Matthew and Luke clean it up. So the premise ("high literary Greek") is overstated. *Rating: strong on Mark; broadly fair on the synoptics.* [^2]
2. **Ancient authors used trained scribes (amanuenses).** "Most people in the ancient world didn't write themselves like we do. They hired scribes, trained professionals... [who] would help craft a work." Even the highly-educated Cicero "talks about his scribe constantly helping him write and draft letters." So literary polish reflects the *scribe*, not the named author. *Rating: strong; amanuensis use is well-documented — even Paul used Tertius (Romans 16:22).* [^3]
3. **Matthew was literate by trade.** "Matthew was a tax collector" — a profession requiring literacy and numeracy — so the fisherman stereotype does not apply to him. *Rating: strong; tax collectors were literate.* [^4]
4. **Only John is genuinely contestable — and he dictated.** Granting John "was just a fisherman," the Muratorian Fragment reports John was urged to write while "with Andrew and a bunch of other brethren," after fasting — i.e., he *dictated* to scribes in a collaborative effort, "not this idea where he just sits in a corner." So even the hardest case is covered by ordinary ancient composition practice. *Rating: moderate-strong; the dictation model is plausible and attested.* [^5]
5. **Ehrman concedes the principle elsewhere.** Jones notes Ehrman once told a Jesus-mythicist that "every ancient author wrote via a scribe" — so the demand that the Gospels be exceptions is special pleading. *Rating: strong if the concession is accurately reported; it is consistent with Ehrman's general view.* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A skeptic replies that the amanuensis move can prove too much: if a scribe can supply *any* level of Greek for *any* named author, then literary style can never count against *or for* an attribution — and the same scribal latitude that rescues "fisherman John" also means the polished text tells us nothing about who stood behind it, weakening the broader case for apostolic authorship. Moreover, some argue the Gospels' Greek (especially Luke-Acts) reflects an author thoroughly at home in Greek literary culture, not a Galilean using a secretary.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: the amanuensis point is *defensive* — it removes Ehrman's stylistic objection — while the *positive* case for authorship rests on other grounds (unanimous attestation, the improbable Mark/Luke attributions, onomastic congruence). Style not settling authorship is fine, because no one is arguing *from* style *to* authorship; they are answering an argument *from* style *against* it. And Luke-Acts being polished is fully consistent with Luke (an educated Gentile companion of Paul), not a problem for the traditional view. See [[Onomastic Congruence Shows the Gospels Used Eyewitness Sources]].

## Best Quotes
> "Most people in the ancient world didn't write themselves like we do. They hired scribes, trained professionals... Cicero talks about his scribe constantly helping him write and draft letters. And Cicero was highly trained and educated, but why is he still using a scribe? That's just how ancient people wrote." — [[Michael Jones]] [^3]

> "Matthew was a tax collector, and then Luke and Mark probably were the scribes... So it fits exactly with what we'd expect on those three. The only one that you have a question mark on is John." — [[Jacob Hansen]] / Jones [^4]
