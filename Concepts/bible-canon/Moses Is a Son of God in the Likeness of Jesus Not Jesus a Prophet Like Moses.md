# Moses Is a Son of God in the Likeness of Jesus Not Jesus a Prophet Like Moses

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that Moses 1 corrects an inversion that arises from reading the Bible in bare chronological order. Because [[Moses 1.6|Moses 1:6]] tells [[Moses]] he is "in the similitude of mine Only Begotten," the proper relation is that **Moses is a son of God modeled on [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]]** — not that Christ is merely "a prophet like unto Moses." The familiar phrase "a prophet like unto Moses" comes from [[Deuteronomy 18.15|Deuteronomy 18:15]], where Moses predicts the Lord will raise up a prophet like himself; reading forward, one meets Moses first, then Jesus "living up to the standard of Moses." But that gets the priority backwards: "Moses comes first chronologically in the flow of human history... but Christ comes first in priority, and He's first in the eternal history of God's plan, before the Creation." The preface restores the order the timeline obscures.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The similitude runs Moses → Christ, not Christ → Moses
"I have a work for thee, Moses, my son, and thou art in the similitude of mine Only Begotten" ([[Moses 1.6|Moses 1:6]]). The likeness is predicated of Moses with Christ as the standard; Bradley reinforces it from [[Ether 3.14|the Book of Ether]], where Christ tells the brother of Jared man was created "in his image" — so humanity (Moses included) is in the likeness of God's Son, the Son is not in the likeness of Moses.[^3] *Rating: a coherent reading of the LDS text; the direction of the simile is explicit in Moses 1:6.*

### 2. The "odd phrasing" diagnoses the distortion
Bradley presses the strangeness of calling "God the Son, the Lord, the Savior of humankind" a prophet *like Moses*: "who's the greater here and who's the lesser?" Taken flatly, Deuteronomy 18:15 could imply Moses is the benchmark and Jesus the imitator. The point is that the phrase encodes a *prediction* (Moses foretold a coming prophet), not a ranking, and only the eternal-priority frame keeps it from misleading.[^4] *Rating: a real interpretive tension; the resolution depends on the LDS premortal-Christ premise.*

### 3. Chronology distorts the bigger picture
"Because the Old Testament follows through in chronological order... that chronological order of human history in some ways distorts the bigger picture." Reading sequentially, Jesus "comes after Moses," which can suggest he is less than Moses — "that's what caught people up" when Jesus arrived. The preface supplies the non-chronological, eternal ordering: there is God the Son the Creator, and *then* Moses, a son of God in his likeness.[^5] *Rating: an explanatory claim about why the Messiah was misrecognized; plausible as theology, not historically demonstrable.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A biblical scholar reads [[Deuteronomy 18.15|Deuteronomy 18:15]] in its plain Deuteronomic sense — a promise of an ongoing prophetic office (a *succession* of prophets like Moses to replace divination), not a cryptic statement of Christ's eternal priority; its later messianic application (Acts 3:22) is a New Testament appropriation. The whole inversion-correction depends on [[Moses 1.6|Moses 1:6]], a Restoration text unattested in any Genesis-Exodus manuscript, and on the LDS doctrine of a premortal Christ who pre-exists creation — premises a critic need not grant. On a traditional Christian reading there is no real tension to fix: Jesus is "like Moses" in *prophetic role* while remaining ontologically greater as the incarnate Word (Hebrews 3:3, "counted worthy of more glory than Moses"), so the supposed problem ("who's greater?") is already resolved in the NT without Moses 1. The fair statement: the inversion is a genuine *teaching device* inside the LDS frame — useful for ordering Christ before Moses theologically — but it solves a problem mainstream exegesis does not have and rests on a verse outsiders regard as 19th-century.

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus is sometimes later identified in the Bible... as a prophet like unto Moses. But think how odd that phrasing is, that God the Son, the Lord, the Savior of humankind, should be described as being a prophet like Moses."[^4]

> "Moses comes first chronologically in the flow of human history... But Christ comes first in priority, and He's first in the eternal history of God's plan, before the Creation."[^2]

> "So Jesus isn't a prophet like Moses. Moses is a son of God in the likeness of Jesus."[^6]
