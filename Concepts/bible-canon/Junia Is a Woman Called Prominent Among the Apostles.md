# Junia Is a Woman Called Prominent Among the Apostles

## The Point
In Romans 16:7 Paul greets "Andronicus and Junia … prominent among the apostles." [[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] presents Junia as a **woman** to whom Paul applies the title *apostolos* — a striking datum, since later Christians restricted the title to the Twelve. Translators across the centuries literally **changed her name to a man's** ("Junias") on the unargued assumption that a woman could not be called an apostle.

## Evidence

### 1. Paul calls Junia "prominent among the apostles"
Paul uses the Greek *episēmos* ("prominent") — an adjective that, per Robert Jewett, "lifts up a person … as distinguished or marked in comparison with other representatives of the same class, in this instance with the other apostles." Junia is being ranked *among* the apostles, not merely known *to* them. [^1]

### 2. She and Andronicus manifested apostolic "signs and wonders"
Paul elsewhere cites "signs and wonders and mighty works" (2 Cor 12:12) as markers of his apostolic office. Calling Junia "prominent among the apostles" "may indicate that they too had manifest" these signs — that Junia "along with her spouse Andronicus, were commissioned agents of Jesus Christ who held a special calling of leadership and authority among the disciples." They were converts before Paul and had been imprisoned with him. [^2]

### 3. The name was changed to erase the woman
"In many translations of the New Testament, Junia has been mistakenly translated as Junius, simply on the assumption that she could not have been a woman if she was called 'apostle' by Paul." Laughton cites Hope Stephenson's review of the ancient and modern evidence ("Junia, Woman and Apostle"). [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "When he called Junia and Andronicus 'prominent among the apostles,' it may indicate that they too had manifest the 'signs, wonders, and mighty works' Paul considered the markers of his own apostolic office." [^2]

> "Junia has been mistakenly translated as Junius, simply on the assumption that she could not have been a woman if she was called 'apostle' by Paul." [^3]

## LDS Connection
For [[Latter-day Saints]], Junia exemplifies the chapter's thesis that women held leadership the later church narrowed — and supports Elder [[Dallin H. Oaks]]'s framing of women exercising real authority. Laughton explicitly lists "apostles (in Paul's broad sense)" among the offices ancient Christian women held. [^2]
