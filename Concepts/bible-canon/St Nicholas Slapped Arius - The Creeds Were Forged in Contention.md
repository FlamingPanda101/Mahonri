# St Nicholas Slapped Arius - The Creeds Were Forged in Contention

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and Quku use a famous bit of church-history lore to deflate the idea that the historic creeds emerged from serene, Spirit-led consensus. At the **Council of Nicaea (AD 325)**, as **[[Arius]]** argued his view of the nature of God, **Bishop Nicholas of Myra** (the historical [[St. Nicholas|Saint Nicholas]] — "Santa Claus") grew so enraged that he **crossed the room and struck Arius across the face**; the bishops were scandalized, Constantine had him stripped of his episcopal garments and jailed. [^1] The polemical point: "these are your creeds, people" — the foundational creedal definitions were hammered out in a "knockdown, drag-out... Game of Thrones" of human politics and tempers, "and we're told contention is of the devil." [^2] The same skeptical lens is turned on the rest of the conciliar process: only two of the participating fathers can most people even name, and the council's definitions came by the "voting patterns of these old fuddy-duddies." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The slap is a genuine (if late-attested) tradition.** The story that Nicholas struck Arius at Nicaea is a well-known piece of Christian lore, documented and circulated (e.g., the St. Nicholas Center collects the tradition); the council itself, Constantine's convening of it, and Arius's condemnation are firm history. *Rating: the council and Arius's condemnation are solid history; the slap is a venerable tradition, not contemporaneously documented.* [^1]
2. **Nicaea really was contentious and politically charged.** The Arian controversy was a fierce, protracted, empire-spanning dispute with exiles, reversals, and imperial intervention — so "the creed came out of contention" is an accurate description of the historical process, whatever one thinks of the result. *Rating: strong as a description of the conflict.* [^2]
3. **It coheres with the vault's canon-as-power-struggle line.** The point parallels [[Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority]] — that foundational fourth-century definitions (creed and canon) were settled by contested human councils, not delivered ready-made. *Rating: consistent with existing vault content.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Contention attending a council **does not refute the council's conclusion** — true doctrines can be defended heatedly, and the genetic move ("they argued, therefore the creed is suspect") is a fallacy; the vault elsewhere grants the [[The Nicene Creed Is Not an Abomination|Nicene Creed is not an abomination]] in intent. The slap is also *legendary*, not in the contemporary acts of the council, so leaning on it as a debunking is itself uncritical use of a late tradition. And "only two fathers are nameable today" says nothing about whether the bishops reasoned well. A measured reply: the argument's real target is narrower — the *romanticized* picture of creedal formation as placid, unanimous, Spirit-dictated consensus; against that, even the friendly tradition concedes raw human politics and tempers. For the Latter-day Saint the value is corrective, not dispositive: it shows the creeds bear the fingerprints of contingent human councils, supporting a Restoration narrative without claiming that contention alone proves the creeds false.

## Best Quotes
> "Straight up slapped the guy that was making your creed because it was so offensive even back then... it was a knockdown, drag-out — Game of Thrones with your precious doctrine — and we're told contention is of the devil." [^2]

> "Well thank goodness we don't base our entire religion off of the voting patterns of these old fuddy-duddies, only two of which we can even name, and who are getting into slap fights." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The note serves the Restoration's critique of creedal authority: the historic creeds were products of contested, politically fraught councils (Nicaea's Arian fight, with even Saint Nicholas losing his temper), not serene revelation — paralleling [[Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority]] and the broader claim that fourth-century definitions cannot bear the weight of "the faith once delivered." It should be used to *relativize* creedal certainty, not as a standalone disproof (cf. [[The Nicene Creed Is Not an Abomination]]).
