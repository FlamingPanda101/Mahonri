# Sola Scriptura Is Not Word-for-Word Literalism

#type/claim

## The Claim
Discussing Burge's three-way breakdown of clergy views on the Bible — (1) literal, word-for-word; (2) inspired and inerrant but partly symbolic; (3) inspired yet not all historically accurate — the panel debates what genuine *sola scriptura* commits one to. The skeptical host argues that thoughtful sola-scriptura advocates land on **option 2 (inspired/inerrant, not necessarily literal)**, not option 1 (literal word-for-word); inerrancy does not require wooden literalism.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The data (strong contrast).** Catholic clergy: ~99% reject the "literal word-for-word" view (option 1) — the panel jokes, "Do not act like you're sola scriptura." Evangelicals: ~17% literal, ~70% inspired-without-errors-but-symbolic (option 2), ~13% not-historically-accurate. Mainline: almost none literal, ~70% in option 3.[^2] *Rating: strong primary data; clarifies who holds what.*

2. **Inerrancy ≠ literalism (the core argument).** "The sola scriptura people don't believe in number one... it doesn't have to be word-for-word accurate for it to be infallible."[^3] The Bible can be inspired and without error while containing genre, metaphor, and figurative language. *Rating: theologically sound — this matches mainstream evangelical inerrancy (e.g., the Chicago Statement, which affirms inerrancy yet allows figurative genres).*

3. **Most evangelicals self-locate in option 2 (corroboration).** The host who initially leaned toward literalism concedes that those who have "deeply thought... about the implications of sola scriptura" would land on option 2 — infallibly God's word, but "not literal in every sense."[^4] The survey's 70% evangelical figure for option 2 corroborates this.

4. **A real subset *does* hold option 1 (steelman concession).** The panel grants that zealous lay believers — "these are the guys that say... there's only one book that I really need on my shelf, the Bible" — may genuinely interpret their faith as option 1 literalism, and misuse proof-texts like [[John 4.24|John 4:24]] ("*pneuma ho theos*") without understanding the grammar.[^5] So the literalist category is not empty; it is just a minority.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** One host resists over-defining others' beliefs: "We should be very careful about reading into what they believe." The methodologically humble move is to *ask* sola-scriptura adherents directly rather than infer — which is exactly what the survey does, and most chose option 2.[^6] This both supports the claim and cautions against the panel's own tendency to characterize outsiders.

**Linguistic/scholarly context.** [[John 4.24|John 4:24]]'s zero-copula construction (*pneuma ho theos*, "spirit [is] God") is the panel's worked example of how flat literalism can over-read grammar: treating a predicate-nominative description as a metaphysical exclusion of any divine body. Proper exegesis (option 2's instinct) attends to genre and syntax rather than wooden word-matching.

## Best Quotes

> "We should just ask them, right? ... And most of them said number two. So there you go." [^6]
