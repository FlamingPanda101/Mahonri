# The New Testament Does Not Teach Abolition

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: "At the very best you get a condemnation of the kind of slavery practiced in the transatlantic period — the brutality of it. But you are not getting abolitionism." The NT regulates and softens slavery; it never abolishes it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Paul softens but does not abolish.** Masters should be "good to their slaves"; [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] sends the runaway slave Onesimus back to his master. "You're not getting abolitionism."[^2] *(Strong — Paul's letters regulate the master-slave relationship rather than condemning the institution.)*

2. **[[1 Peter 2.18|1 Peter 2:18]] commands obedience to cruel masters.** "Slaves should be obedient to their masters, not just the good ones but also the cruel and unjust ones."[^3] *(Strong — explicitly tells the enslaved to submit to unjust masters; hard to square with an abolitionist ethic.)*

3. **The "kidnapping is condemned" rebuttal fails.** Scrivener's book cites man-stealing being condemned in OT and NT. O'Connor: "stealing" means *unauthorized* taking; if you're authorized to take slaves from the nations ([[Leviticus 25.44-46|Lev 25]]), that isn't stealing — just as the killing of Amalekite infants isn't "murder" because it was commanded. See [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]].[^4] *(Sharp — the man-stealing prohibition does not reach the sanctioned slave trade, so it cannot bear the abolitionist weight placed on it.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener — "yeast working through dough."** Manumitting one's whole slave population was *literally illegal* in the early empire (laws ~2 BC and 6 AD); social upheaval of abolition was immense (Britain paid off abolition debt until 2015). Paul did "what you'd expect a missionary to do to a brutal culture — work within the structures and sow the seed." And [[Gregory of Nyssa]] (c. 379) was the first in *human history* to condemn slavery wholesale. See [[Gregory of Nyssa First to Condemn Slavery Wholesale]] and [[Yeast Working Through Dough - Christianity's Slow Abolition]].[^5] *(Strong — abolitionism was genuinely unthinkable in antiquity, and the legal constraints were real.)* O'Connor's rejoinder: Christianity was *also* illegal yet practiced openly because believers "thought it was the right thing to do" — so illegality didn't stop them defying Rome on idolatry; why would it on slavery, if abolition were truly a Christian conviction?[^6] *(Effective tu quoque — the early church *did* break Roman law for conviction's sake.)*

## Best Quotes

> "At the very best, what you get is the idea that the way slavery was being done in the transatlantic period was wrong. But abolitionism would be seen as a massive overstep."[^1]

> "Peter writes that slaves should be obedient... not just the good ones but also the cruel and unjust ones."[^3]

> "You know what else was illegal for a time in the early Roman Empire? Christianity. But they still practiced that because they thought it was the right thing to do." — O'Connor[^6]
