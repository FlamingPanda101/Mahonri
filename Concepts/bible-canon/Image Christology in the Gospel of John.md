# Image Christology in the Gospel of John

## The Claim
John's portrait of Jesus is best understood as **image christology**: Jesus is "kind of like a divine image" who *bears, manifests, and mediates* the divine presence, agency, and authority — so that seeing Jesus is seeing the Father, and Thomas can say "my Lord and my God" — *not* because Jesus *is* the Father/Yahweh, but because a divine image makes the deity present.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Whoever has seen me has seen the Father" (strong)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** roots this in [[John 14.9|John 14:9]]: Jesus reveals the Father "because the father dwells in him." [^1] "This is image christology. This is the notion that Jesus is kind of like a divine image because Jesus bears the divine presence, divine agency, divine authority and manifests it. So that anyone who sees Jesus... is seeing God. Not because Jesus is God but because Jesus bears God's presence." [^2]

### 2. Thomas's confession fits the image model (strong)
"This is why Thomas at the end can say my Lord and my God ([[John 20.28|John 20:28]]). Thomas finally gets it. Jesus is not the father, but Jesus bears the father's presence just like a divine image does." [^3] The confession marks recognition of mediated divine presence, not an equation of Jesus with the Father. See [[John 20-28 - My Lord and My God (Divine Image)]].

### 3. It integrates the whole passage (strong, synthetic)
Image christology ties together the strands McClellan develops elsewhere: the mutual indwelling of [[I and the Father Are One Means Mutual Indwelling]], the divinizing Logos of [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]], and his broader "divine images" model in [[Divine Images Explain Jesus Relationship to God]]. The result is "an entirely different picture... Jesus identifying as the one mutually indwelling the father... identifying as the son of god as divine." [^4]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A Trinitarian / Nicene reader holds that "whoever has seen me has seen the Father" and "my Lord and my God" express *consubstantial* deity, not merely mediated presence, and that the image-bearing language (e.g. Col 1:15, Heb 1:3) is itself a statement of full deity. McClellan grants Jesus is "the son of God in the fullest sense," "set apart and sent into the world to reveal the father" [^5] — his disagreement is over *mechanism* (mediated divine presence vs. shared essence), which he argues better fits John operating "on its own terms" before later Trinitarian formulation.

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus bears the divine presence, divine agency, divine authority and manifests it. So that anyone who sees Jesus... is seeing God. Not because Jesus is God but because Jesus bears God's presence." [^2]

> "Jesus is not the father, but Jesus bears the father's presence just like a divine image does." [^3]

> "We see Jesus identifying as the one mutually indwelling the father who has the father indwelling him and manifests the father's presence to those who see him, identifying as the son of god, as divine." [^4]
