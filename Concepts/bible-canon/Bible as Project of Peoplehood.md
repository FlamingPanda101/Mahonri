# Bible as Project of Peoplehood

## The Claim
The Bible is best understood not as the scripture of a religion but as a "project of peoplehood" — a collaborative literary effort by a defeated, divided people to form an enduring identity (a "people") that could survive without a kingdom, temple, or territory. The biblical authors asked a question no ancient people had asked before: "What does it mean to be a people?"[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Distinction Between State and People:** Wright distinguishes "state" (physical institutions, borders, armies — conquerable) from "peoplehood" (a "state of mind" — shared narratives, memories, future aspirations — unconquerable by armies). The Bible creates the latter to survive the destruction of the former.[^2]

2. **No Hebrew Word for "Religion":** The biblical authors had no concept of "religion" as a discrete sociocultural dimension — that category developed between the Renaissance, Reformation, and Enlightenment. What they were organizing their identity around was peoplehood, not religion.[^3] The Hebrew word *amim* (peoples) and *goyim* (nations) refer to ethnic/political identity, not religious affiliation.[^4]

3. **Defeat as Impetus, Not Religion:** Wellhausen argued the Bible emerged as "scripture for a religion" after the nation was destroyed. Wright argues what emerged was properly *nation* in the ethnic sense — peoplehood — and Wellhausen conflated "kingdom" (the state) with "nation" (the people).[^5]

4. **Coherence Across Diverse Texts:** What makes the entire corpus coherent, Wright argues, is that "it's in a certain sense very real sense all responses to what makes the people, what brings us together as a people, how do we survive…"[^6] — from law to wisdom to love poetry to narrative, all genres serve the project of people formation.

## Best Quote

> "What makes this whole corpus so coherent is — it's in a certain sense, very real sense, all responses to what makes the people, what brings us together as a people, how do we survive… what role does a kingdom play in all — how is that not everything? What do we do when that kingdom is defeated and conquered?"[^6]
> — [[Jacob Wright]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-Arguments
- [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins]] — The traditional view that the Bible *is* scripture of a religion replacing a fallen nation.
- Traditional theological view: The Bible is divinely inspired scripture for the community of faith — the "peoplehood" framing may reduce its theological claims to sociological function.
