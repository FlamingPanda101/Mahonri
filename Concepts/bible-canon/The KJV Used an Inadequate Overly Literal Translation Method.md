# The KJV Used an Inadequate Overly Literal Translation Method

## The Claim

The King James Version was "overly literal," frequently reproducing the *form* of the Greek/Hebrew source rather than rendering its *sense* — producing English that is, in places, indecipherable. Modern translation theory and a better grasp of the ancient languages yield clearer renderings. [[Dan McClellan]] gives this as the third of three reasons the KJV obscures the biblical authors. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Form-over-sense yields "indecipherable English" — rate: strong

McClellan: the KJV "frequently punted to just reproducing the form of the source language in the target language, which led to indecipherable English." Today we have "a much better understanding of ancient Greek, ancient Hebrew, ancient Aramaic, and much better translation philosophies and methodologies." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the formal-equivalence limitations of early modern translation are well recognized in translation studies.

### 2. The worked example: 2 Corinthians 2:5 — rate: strong

The KJV of [[2 Corinthians 2.5|2 Corinthians 2:5]] — "But if any have caused grief, he hath not grieved me but in part that I may not overcharge you all" — is "utterly incomprehensible." The reason: it "follow[s] slavishly the word order of the Greek, which doesn't help syntactically because we don't have cases like they do in Greek." The updated NRSV renders the sense: "But if anyone has caused grief, he has caused it not to me, but to some extent — not to exaggerate it — to all of you." [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a side-by-side that makes the methodological point concrete.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Advocates of formal-equivalence (word-for-word) translation argue that preserving the source's form lets the reader see the underlying structure and guards against the translator smuggling in interpretation — a real risk in "dynamic"/sense-for-sense translation. On this view the KJV's literalism is a *virtue* of transparency, not a defect. McClellan's reply is that reproducing Greek word order in a caseless language like English does not preserve meaning, it *destroys* it — the reader cannot recover the syntax the cases originally carried. This connects to the vault's point that [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer|translation is always interpretive choice]]; the issue is which method better serves the reader's actual goal of understanding.

## Best Quotes

> "The King James version was overly literal and frequently punted to just reproducing the form of the source language in the target language, which led to indecipherable English." [^1]

> "This is utterly incomprehensible. You can try as long as you want to figure out what exactly is going on here. You will be wrong, because what they've done here is follow slavishly the word order of the Greek." [^2]
