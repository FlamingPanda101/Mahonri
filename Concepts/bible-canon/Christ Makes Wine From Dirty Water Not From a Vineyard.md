# Christ Makes Wine From Dirty Water Not From a Vineyard

#type/claim

## The Claim
The Cana miracle ([[John 2.1-11|John 2:1–11]]) is a type of personal transformation: [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]] "does not need a vineyard to make wine" — only dirty water brought to him. He will not make something out of nothing, but he is "fully capable of transforming something that isn't into something that is." Applied to the sinner, this means Christ can not only cleanse sin but turn the *repented sin itself into nourishment*: "the sting of our sins may be that which leads us to him," and the faith won in forsaking old sins "may nourish us in the very kingdom we forsook them to enter." In a phrase, "Christ can turn faith-destroying sin into faith-promoting experience" — he "does not need a good man to make a saint. Give him dirty water."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
- **The "wanting to want" problem.** The transformation answers a bind the author names from his own life: he knew what was right but lacked the *desire* — "I not only needed help doing, but something even more difficult, wanting to do." Surrendering to Christ produced not just changed behavior but changed *desire*: dreaded duties (prayer, scripture, seminary) "became delicious to me," and "I was being given desires and attributes that were not mine."[^3][^4]
- **God transforms something, not nothing.** "No man on earth can turn water to wine because water is not wine. But to the Son of God it is no impossible task." The point is not creation from nothing but *re-creation*: the raw material (a flawed, sinful self / dirty water) must be *brought* to Christ — "he won't make something out of nothing, but is fully capable of transforming something that isn't into something that is."[^1]
- **A repeatable, incremental process.** The change is "little by little, day by day," "from grace to grace," and ongoing: "every day that I fill that pot with my carnal desires and bring it to the Lord, he changes me. Day by day, pot by pot." This frames sanctification as a continual returning rather than a single event, consonant with [[Theosis as Likeness and Union So Far as Possible|the LDS arc toward becoming like God]].[^2][^5]
- **Sin recycled into nourishment.** Just as the wine came from the purification water, "once repented of, [sins] may become something that nourishes us." The very experiences that nearly destroyed faith become, after repentance, faith-promoting fuel within the kingdom — a transformation of the moral order, not merely a pardon.[^6]
- **Christ overturns the natural order.** "Jesus has power to manipulate what we regard as the natural order of things." Water is not wine; a sinner is not a saint — yet Christ converts each, requiring only the sinner's surrender: "you have to be willing to surrender everything that you are."[^7]
- **Logos by association.** The mechanism of change is proximity to Christ as the *logos* of [[John 1.1|John 1:1]]: "as I surrounded myself with Jesus, the embodiment of the logos, I was slowly adopting that logos." See [[Logos Hymn Belongs to the Original Gospel]].[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus does not need a vineyard to make wine. Dirty water will do, but you have to bring it to him. He won't make something out of nothing, but is fully capable of transforming something that isn't into something that is."[^1]

> "Christ can turn faith-destroying sin into faith-promoting experience… He does not need a vineyard to make wine, and he does not need a good man to make a saint. Give him dirty water."[^6][^2]
