# Critical Scholarship

**Critical scholarship** (also "academic biblical studies") is the data-first, methodologically naturalistic study of the biblical texts in their historical, linguistic, and cultural context, in which the analysis is meant to *follow the evidence where it leads* even when that challenges traditional belief. In the vault it is repeatedly set against **apologetics**, which is characterized as *conclusion-first* — beginning with the desired outcome and constructing arguments to show that the data do not make that conclusion impossible. The bar for apologetics is "mere possibility"; the bar for critical scholarship is the best-supported reading of the evidence.

## Relevance

This term names the methodological stance from which figures like [[Dan McClellan]] argue — e.g. that the Trinity is "a post-biblical philosophical framework imposed on the texts" rather than a doctrine the biblical authors held, so that calling it "unavoidably biblical" begs the question. The contrast is foundational to the vault's treatment of how doctrine is read out of (or into) scripture, and recurs whenever an apologetic claim is tested against the historical-critical consensus. See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]] and [[Apologists Engage Pre-Digested Caricatures of Critical Scholarship]].
