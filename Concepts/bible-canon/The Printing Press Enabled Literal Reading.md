# The Printing Press Enabled Literal Reading

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the printing press materially changed *how* ordinary people encounter scripture, and that this shift made literal, cross-referencing reading possible for the first time. Before print, "normally they would hear it read... from the pulpit," lacking the literacy and the money to own handwritten manuscripts. [^1] Big, expensive Bibles "were usually chained to the pulpit... intended for the clergy to be read out loud." [^2] So readers "weren't reading it back to front" and "couldn't just flip through" to catch a discrepancy. [^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Hearing vs. reading produces different relationships to the text.** When scripture is heard liturgically rather than read privately, listeners cannot pause to compare "this passage over here" with "this other different thing." [^3] McClellan ties this to the Michael Peppard discussion of how Catholics encounter the Bible — "closer to how Christians have experienced the scriptures throughout history." [^5] **(Strong — reflects standard book-history scholarship.)**
2. **No access means no fact-checking.** "Even if you did recall two things and you were like, 'I'd love to check that out,' you had no means of checking it out... because you didn't own the book." [^4] The capacity to detect contradiction is itself a product of cheap, portable, owned texts. **(Strong.)**
3. **Print + Reformation = a snowball toward literalism.** The press, combined with the Reformation's transfer of interpretive authority away from the institution, "created this snowball effect where people were gaining more access to the text... and were reading it a little bit more literally." [^6] This is a *necessary condition* for, not the cause of, later hard literalism — which still required the modern reaction against science. See [[Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention]]. **(Moderate-to-strong as a causal chain.)**
4. **Steelman.** One could object that early printed Bibles remained costly and that mass private Bible-reading is later still; the press alone did not democratize scripture overnight. McClellan acknowledges this ("Bibles were incredibly expensive... very big... chained to the pulpit"). [^2] His claim is directional — the press *began the ball rolling* — not that literacy was instant. **(Fair; the claim is appropriately hedged.)**

## Best Quotes

> "Normally they would hear it read from the pulpit... they would not have the opportunity to actually hold and read the texts unless they learned to read." [^1]

> "You couldn't just flip... because you didn't own the book." [^4]

> "That kind of created this snowball effect where people were gaining more access to the text... and were reading it a little bit more literally." [^6]
