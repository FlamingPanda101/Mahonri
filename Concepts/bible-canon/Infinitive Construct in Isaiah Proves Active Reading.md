# Infinitive Construct in Isaiah Proves Active Reading

## The Claim

[[Isaiah 1.12|Isaiah 1:12]] provides the decisive proof that the pilgrimage idiom means "to **see** the face of God." Unlike the finite verbs in [[Deuteronomy 16.16|Deuteronomy 16:16]] and [[Exodus 34.24|Exodus 34:24]] (where active and passive can share consonants), Isaiah uses an **infinitive construct**, a form whose active and passive have **different consonants**. The consonants present in Isaiah 1:12 can only be the active "to see my face" — yet the [[Masoretes|Masoretes]] vocalized them as the passive anyway, overriding what the consonants permit.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Active and passive infinitive constructs differ in consonants.** McClellan: "in the finite form, the active and the passive can have the same consonants. In the infinitive construct, they do not have the same consonants." The active infinitive is **לִרְאוֹת (lir'ot)**; the passive (Niphal) would be **לְהֵרָאוֹת (lehera'ot)** — note the extra *he* (ה).[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — the morphological difference between Qal and Niphal infinitive constructs of *ra'ah* is a hard grammatical fact.)*

2. **The text lacks the consonant required for the passive.** The Masoretic consonants spell *lir'ot panai* ("to see my face"), without the *he* that the Niphal infinitive construct requires. The Masoretes added the *vowels* of the passive (*lehera'ot*) onto consonants that cannot support it.[^3] *(Evidence rating: strong — this is the crux; the vowel pointing here is in tension with the consonantal skeleton itself, not merely choosing among ambiguous options.)*

3. **The NRSVUE footnote concedes the point.** At Isaiah 1:12 the NRSVUE marks "When you come to appear before me" with a footnote reading "or see my face," acknowledging the active reading — but only in a footnote at one verse, while the main text and the parallel passages keep the passive.[^4] *(Evidence rating: corroborating — a mainstream translation committee admits the active is in play.)*

4. **Why the parallels lack the footnote.** McClellan explains the absence of a comparable footnote in Deuteronomy 16:16 / Exodus 34:24 by their consonantal ambiguity: "the consonants could go either way in the verb, so we're just going to take the passive reading. But with Isaiah you can't."[^5] *(Evidence rating: coherent account of the translators' inconsistent practice.)*

**Steelman of the traditional reading:** A defender could argue the Masoretes had access to a vocalization tradition that legitimately read a passive nuance even into the infinitive, and that scribal-aural transmission can preserve readings the bare consonants underdetermine. But this is weak precisely here, because the Niphal infinitive construct of *ra'ah* requires a consonant (the *he*) that the text does not contain; the active is not merely preferable but is what the letters spell.

## Best Quotes

> "In the finite form, the active and the passive can have the same consonants. In the infinitive construct, they do not have the same consonants. And the consonants that we have in Isaiah 1:12... the consonants are lir'ot, which would be when you come to see my face. Lir'ot panai."[^2]

> "The Masoretes have added the vowels of the passive form. So it's lehera'ot... but it should be... the consonants very definitely are intended to be active."[^3]

> "With Isaiah you can't. The consonants very clearly of the infinitive construct have to be active. So they're like, just put it in the footnotes there."[^5]
