# The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Kelly defends the authority of the 66-book canon. Hayden's structural rebuttal: **before** you can say what the canon *teaches* or *how authoritative* it is, you must first know **which books are in it** — and the act of identifying the list necessarily comes from *outside* the list. "Whatever identifies the canon necessarily stands logically prior to the canon it identifies."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The canon's table of contents is not in the canon — rate: strong
This is the well-known "canon paradox": no inspired book contains a divinely-revealed index of the inspired books. So whatever fixes the boundary (church councils, historical consensus, scholarly reasoning, *argumentum ad populum*) is a non-canonical authority that the canon depends on.[^2] **Scholarly context:** R.C. Sproul's famous Protestant concession — the canon is "a fallible collection of infallible books" — actually grants Hayden's premise; F.F. Bruce and Bruce Metzger document that recognition was a centuries-long process. **Steelman of Kelly:** logical priority of *recognition* need not mean priority of *authority* — a witness who identifies the king is not greater than the king. The church *recognizes* (not *confers*) canonicity. Hayden's reply: recognition by a fallible body cannot yield certainty that the result is *infallible*.

### 2. "By what authority was this canon identified?" — rate: strong, unanswered in debate
Hayden poses this as the simple, decisive question. Kelly's answers across the debate — apostolic citation, early-father usage, providential preservation, prayerful councils — all locate the identifying authority in *human historical processes guided by the Spirit*, which Hayden classifies as **tradition**.[^3] Kelly never produces a divine act *naming the list*, which Hayden treats as conceding the point.

### 3. The affirmative's own three-part burden — rate: framing
Hayden formalizes Kelly's task into three demonstrations: (1) **God** (not humans) identified the 66 books; (2) God revealed this canon is **incapable of error**; (3) God established it as the **ultimate authority** correcting all others.[^4] Item (1) is the canon-identification problem; this concept supplies it. **Steelman:** debate resolutions can be drafted to be near-unprovable; demanding an explicit divine proof-text for the *meta*-question of canon may set a bar no historic Christian tradition (including LDS, for its own canon) can clear.

## Best Quotes
> "The act of identifying which books belong in the canon is logically prior to any claim about what the canon teaches or how authoritative it is. So the question becomes very simple: by what authority was this canon identified?"[^2]

> "Whatever identifies the canon necessarily stands logically prior to the canon it identifies."[^1]

> "First, that God, not unauthorized human agents, has identified the canon of the 66 books he is defending. Second, that God has told us that this modern Protestant canon is incapable of error. And third, that God has identified the modern Protestant canon as the ultimate authority for Christian doctrine."[^4]
