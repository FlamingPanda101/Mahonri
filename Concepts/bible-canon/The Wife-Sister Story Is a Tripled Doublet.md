# The Wife-Sister Story Is a Tripled Doublet

## The Claim
The "my wife is my sister" deception is narrated **three times** in Genesis — twice of [[Abraham]] and Sarai/Sarah ([[Genesis 12]], [[Genesis 20]]) and once of [[Isaac]] and Rebekah ([[Genesis 26]]). [[Dan McClellan]] presents this as a textbook case of the same tradition surviving in multiple independent versions that were later stitched together rather than harmonized away by an editor.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Three tellings of one story-type (rating: strong; the consensus reading)
The structure repeats: famine or sojourn → fear of being killed for a beautiful wife → "say you are my sister" → a foreign king (Pharaoh, then Abimelech, then Abimelech again) is endangered → the patriarch leaves enriched. McClellan: "it's clear that this is something that was in circulation with... different tellings of very similar stories. And rather than... be like, 'You can't tell the same story three times,' they... were like, 'We're keeping it all.'"[^2]

> "it is the consensus view that the stories of Abraham, the stories of Isaac, the stories of Jacob all circulated independently of each other before being stitched together into the single lineage." [^1]

### 2. Arbitrary name-changes as editorial seams (rating: strong; source-critical)
Characters' names "keep getting changed kind of arbitrarily" (Abram → Abraham, Sarai → Sarah), which many scholars read as the join between variant traditions in which the character bore a different name; a reason for the change ("God changed his name") is then supplied to smooth the seam.[^1]

### 3. Accretion of explanatory detail (rating: moderate; internal-developmental)
The tellings grow in the way folk traditions tend to grow. [[Genesis 12]] "moves in fits and starts... you don't have all the detail and the explanation," and is plausibly the earliest; [[Genesis 20]] adds the warning dream, the **half-sister** excuse ("she is indeed my sister, the daughter of my father but not... my mother"), and the closed-wombs subplot; [[Genesis 26]] adds the anachronistic "Philistine" setting and drops the abduction entirely.[^3][^4]

### 4. The divine-name shift as a later hand (rating: moderate; philological)
Throughout [[Genesis 20]] the deity is *Elohim* — until the final verse (20:18), the only verse in the chapter using the Tetragrammaton, which McClellan reads as "a secondary addition to the text from a later author who's writing in a period when now suddenly we know who Adonai is." He notes no personal name in all of Genesis contains the *yod-he* theophoric element, suggesting these traditions predate widespread knowledge of the name Yahweh.[^5]

### 5. Steelman of the harmonizing view
A traditional reader can object that the patriarchs simply repeated a working ruse on three separate occasions (different famines, different generations), and that the narrator's insistence that Genesis 26 is a "totally different famine" supports treating them as distinct historical events. McClellan's reply: the narrator "is working very hard to convince you that this is not the same story," which itself betrays awareness that the tellings are suspiciously identical.[^6]

## LDS Relevance
The same source-critical lens — multiple traditions preserved side-by-side rather than flattened — is one Latter-day Saints can hold without threat, since the LDS view of scripture already allows for fallible human transmission and editorial layering (cf. [[Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch]]). The episode also sets up the patriarchal-wealth-by-deception pattern that frames how D&C 132 invokes "Abraham, Isaac, and... Jacob" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 132]]) as exemplars — a canonized appeal to figures whose Genesis portraits are themselves composite and morally tangled.

## Best Quotes

> "we might have a story where Abram and Sarai go to Egypt and... another story where Abraham and Sarah go to Gerrar and they're like, 'Keep them both. We'll just make it work.'" [^2]

> "the narrator is working very hard to convince you that this is not the same story... it's like different famine, different famine. And they didn't go to Egypt." [^6]
