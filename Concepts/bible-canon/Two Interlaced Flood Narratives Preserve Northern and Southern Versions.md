# Two Interlaced Flood Narratives Preserve Northern and Southern Versions

## The Claim
Following [[Richard Elliott Friedman]]'s *Who Wrote the Bible?*, [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] demonstrate — by reading Genesis 6–9 aloud twice — that the flood story is **two complete, originally separate narratives** (a J/Yahwist account and a P/Priestly account) spliced together verse-by-verse, not one continuous story. Each strand reads as a whole; together they contradict; and the splicing was done to preserve both a northern and a southern version.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Two coherent strands, read separately (rated: source-critical; near-consensus).** When the hosts read only the J verses, they get a complete flood story (Yahweh; 40 days and nights; a dove; "seven pairs of all clean animals"); when they read only the P verses, they get another complete story (Elohim/God; 150 days; a raven; "two of every sort"). "You two read zero overlapping words… but they're stuck together in the text."[^1][^2]
2. **The contradictions are systematic (rated: textual).** The two strands differ on the divine name (Yahweh vs. Elohim), the duration (40 vs. 150 days), the bird sent (dove vs. raven), and the animal count (seven pairs vs. two) — and at one seam the J text still has water on the earth while the P text says it was dry. The vault's [[Bible Contradiction - Noahs Ark Animal Count]] catalogs the same divergences from a different source.[^3]
3. **P is the temple-flavored, detail-obsessed source (rated: Friedman).** The P flood adds the ark's three decks, the *tsohar* (a shining stone/window), pitch (*kaphar*, "to cover"), and the rainbow covenant — Temple-oriented imagery; the hosts even note the parallel to the Jaredites' shining stones. P withholds the divine name until Exodus 6:3. See [[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses]].
4. **The color-coded demonstration (rated: pedagogy).** The hosts use Friedman's *The Bible with Sources Revealed* color-coding (J green, P blue) so listeners can see the interlacing themselves — "double double double double, pulling them out, oh it's the same story told twice that's been smooshed together."[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Conservative scholars (Cassuto, Kitchen) argue the flood account is a single, deliberately structured (often chiastic) narrative whose "doublets" are repetition-with-variation typical of ancient Near Eastern storytelling, and that the divine-name alternation reflects theology (Elohim the creator, Yahweh the covenant God), not separate documents. They note no separate J or P scroll has ever been found. The two-source reading is, however, "one of the most long-standing, widely agreed upon, best-supported source-critical arguments" in the Hebrew Bible (per the vault's McClellan note); the dispute is real but lopsided in the academy.

## Best Quotes
> "The flood story is not one flood story, it is two… he went through and jammed two complete accounts of the flood together into one, and we don't notice it because we tend to just turn our brain off and mechanically read."[^1]

> "You two read zero overlapping words… but they're stuck together in the text like this, beginning, middle, and end."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The hosts use the interlaced flood as the showpiece of [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration]]: a Bible visibly stitched from sources is exactly what the Book of Mormon (an openly edited anthology) predicts, and the "mistakes of men" frame embraces the contradiction rather than denying it. It also feeds [[Doublets Were Preserved to Unify Two Kingdoms]] and the temple reading of the Priestly source.
