# Teraphim Were Normative Divine Images Never Condemned

## The Claim
The **teraphim** — household divine images — appear repeatedly in early Hebrew Bible narratives as a **normal, even desirable** feature of Israelite religion, and are **never condemned** by God or the narrator, including when they sit in the homes of ostensibly pious figures.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. What a teraphim was (rating: moderate; philological)
[[Dan McClellan]] describes teraphim as anthropomorphic divine images ("statuettes that looked kind of human"), usually translated "idol," "household idol," or "image." The etymology is uncertain; some derive it from the root *rapha* ("to heal"), making them implements for channeling divine healing power — comparable to a Catholic saint candle today.[^2]

### 2. Found in the homes of the pious, "the idol" not "an idol" (rating: strong; narrative-internal)
In [[1 Samuel 19]] Michal uses a teraphim to fake David asleep in bed. The text says she took *the* idol, not *an* idol — "it makes it very much seem like this is just the normal thing that you have about the house." McClellan presses the point: why does "the ostensibly pious and righteous king of Israel, David, whose heart was after the Lord," simply have an idol lying around the bedroom?[^3]

> "Everybody's got a teraphim or two. You'd be dumb not to." [^3]

### 3. Oath-and-oracle function: "bring them to *ha-Elohim*" (rating: moderate; comparative-legal)
McClellan connects teraphim to the ritual law of [[Exodus 22.29|Exodus 22]] (and the debt-slave ear-boring rite of Exodus 21), where a disputed-property defendant is brought "to *ha-Elohim*, the gods" to swear an oath, and "whomever the gods convict is the guilty party." Rather than the apologetic gloss that "the gods" means *judges*, he argues these were accessible **divine images** in a house or city gate used for oaths, ordeals, and divination.[^4]

### 4. Wildly varying size across stories (rating: moderate; narrative-internal)
The physical teraphim differs by story: in [[1 Samuel 19]] it is "close to life size" (it impersonates a sleeping adult man with a wig of goat hair — "a Ferris Bueller's Day Off situation"), whereas in [[Genesis 31.19|Genesis 31:19]] Rachel hides multiple small ones in a camel saddle and sits on them. "This is the Costco version... Rachel is the dollar-store version."[^5]

### 5. Never condemned, even in Micah's homemade cult (rating: strong; the core observation)
In [[Judges 17]]–18 Micah the Ephraimite installs a cast idol, an ephod, teraphim, a private shrine, and his own son (then a wandering Levite) as priest — folk religion with no authorized oversight — and the Danites covet and steal the whole apparatus. Across all of it the teraphim "are never condemned by God or by the Bible or by anything"; there is no later editorial gloss explaining the practice away.[^1]

> "for the patriarchs and others in the early history of Israel, there was really not a problem with having these divine images." [^1]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional reader notes that the Decalogue forbids graven images (the Second Commandment, Exodus 20:4–5) and that Rachel's theft and Micah's shrine sit inside narratives ([[Judges 17]]–18) the book itself frames as a lawless period ("everyone did what was right in their own eyes"). McClellan grants the Genesis cases are pre-Sinai and that Judges is "not exactly examples of how the Mosaic law is a success" — but his point stands that the **narrator never editorializes against the teraphim themselves**, treating them as unremarkable rather than condemning them.[^6]

## LDS Relevance
The episode is a useful corrective to flat "the Bible always opposed idols/material religion" assumptions, and it dovetails with Latter-day Saint comfort toward **material and embodied religion** — sacred objects, anthropomorphic deity (cf. [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]]), and an open, non-creedal canon. It also illustrates that "biblical religion" was historically diverse and developmental, undercutting the apologetic move that treats one late, normative layer as the timeless original — a parallel to LDS arguments about doctrinal development and restoration.
