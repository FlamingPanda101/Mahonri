# Deutero-Isaiah Theory

## The Claim
The book of Isaiah is a multi-stage composition produced over centuries by at least two (and likely three) distinct authors. Isaiah 40–55 ("Deutero-Isaiah" or "Second Isaiah") was composed during or after the Babylonian exile (~520 BCE), roughly **200 years after** the 8th-century BCE prophet Isaiah of Jerusalem, who is responsible for the bulk of Isaiah 1–39.[^1]

## The Three-Author Model

The classical scholarly division, crystallized by [[Bernhard Duhm]] (1892):[^2]

| Section | Chapters | Period | Context |
|---------|----------|--------|---------|
| First Isaiah (Proto-Isaiah) | 1–39 | 8th–7th c. BCE | Assyrian crisis; Isaiah son of Amoz, court prophet under Hezekiah |
| Second Isaiah (Deutero-Isaiah) | 40–55 | ~520 BCE | Post-exilic; return from Babylon; rebuilding the temple |
| Third Isaiah (Trito-Isaiah) | 56–66 | Persian period | Later post-exilic restoration |

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Isaiah's name vanishes after chapter 39:** The name "Isaiah" appears over a dozen times in chapters 1–39 ("the word of the Lord which came to Isaiah son of Amoz," "the Lord said to Isaiah," "Isaiah said to Hezekiah") — occurring four times in ch. 37, three times in ch. 38, three times in ch. 39. **His name is never mentioned again in chapters 40–66.**[^3]

2. **Cyrus the Great named by name (Isaiah 45:1):** [[Isaiah 45.1-7|Isaiah 45:1]] explicitly names Cyrus, the Persian emperor who conquered Babylon in 539 BCE — roughly **200 years after the historical Isaiah**. The text calls Cyrus "my anointed one" (*mashiach*, translated *christos* in the Greek). This is not presented as prophecy — it speaks of Cyrus's actions as accomplished facts: "he did this thing for me."[^4] McClellan argues this is the single strongest clue, because biblical prophecy is otherwise characteristically vague (statues with gold heads, beasts with horns) — it does not name specific historical figures centuries in advance.[^5]

3. **Temporal shift from future to past:** Isaiah 1–39 prophesies the *coming* destruction by Babylon ("this is coming, this is coming"). At chapter 40, the tone shifts dramatically — Babylon's fall is spoken of as *already happened*, the exile as past, and restoration as underway. "None of it is speaking prophetically; none of it is attributing anything to Isaiah; it is all talking about 'here's how we are picking up the pieces.'"[^6]

4. **Aramaic loanwords:** The Judahites picked up Aramaic (including the Aramaic script) while exiled in Babylon. A higher concentration of Aramaic loanwords in Isaiah 40–55 indicates composition in closer proximity to Aramaic-speaking environments — i.e., the exilic or post-exilic period.[^7]

5. **Thematic and stylistic shifts:** Language, topics, and theological concerns shift markedly from chapters 1–39 to 40–55. The earlier chapters focus on judgment and the Assyrian crisis under Hezekiah; the later chapters focus on comfort, restoration, and monotheistic declarations (e.g., "I am the Lord, and there is no other" — [[Isaiah 43.10]]).[^3]

## History of the Theory

The theory did not begin from anti-supernatural bias. It developed gradually through honest engagement with textual data:[^8]

- Late 18th century: [[Johann Benjamin Koppe]], [[Johann Christoph Döderlein]], and [[Johann Gottfried Eichhorn]] begin identifying passages that appear to come from a later period.
- 1892: [[Bernhard Duhm]] publishes a commentary arguing for three distinct authors (First, Second, Third Isaiah).
- Late 20th century to present: Scholars like [[Marvin Sweeney]] argue the compositional history is even more complex — some passages in Isaiah 1–4 may be from the same or even later layers as Deutero-Isaiah, meaning redactors wove later material into earlier sections. The "majority of Isaiah 1–39 is probably the work of an 8th-century prophet named Isaiah," but with later additions.[^9]

## Best Quote

> "When you gather up all the data ... all of this combines to make it far more likely that Isaiah 40–55 at least was probably composed after the Babylonian exile. Most scholars would say somewhere around 520 BCE — so after the return, right around the time that the second temple is being rebuilt is probably when you have the first layers of Deutero-Isaiah being produced."[^10]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-arguments
- Traditional/conservative scholars attribute the entire book to the 8th-century prophet Isaiah, arguing that naming Cyrus in advance is genuine predictive prophecy.
