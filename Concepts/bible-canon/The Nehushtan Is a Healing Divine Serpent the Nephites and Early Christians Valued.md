# The Nehushtan Is a Healing Divine Serpent the Nephites and Early Christians Valued

#type/claim

## The Claim

Ancient Israel knew a **good serpent** — a divine, fiery being that heals from death and purifies from sin — and the Nephites and earliest Christians belonged to the strand of Israelite religion that *valued* it and identified it with the coming Messiah. [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argue that the serpent-on-a-staff (later nicknamed "[[2 Kings 18.4|Nehushtan]]") appears across both Testaments and especially the Book of Mormon as a positive Christ-type, and that its history records a genuine *religious conflict* inside ancient Israel over whether the serpent was holy or idolatrous. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **The same serpent solves both death and sin.** In [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] the problem is *death* (snakebite) and the cure is a serpent on a pole; in [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]] the problem is *sin* ("I am a man of unclean lips") and the cure is again a seraph — a burning serpent — who touches Isaiah's lips with a live coal. Same word (*seraph* / *saraph*), same saving function. [^1]
- **"Nehushtan" is a slur, which explains a subtle data point.** When Hezekiah destroys the serpent ([[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]]) he *names* it "Nehushtan" — probably an insult ("sneaky thing, brassy thing"). So the word is conspicuously **absent** from [[John 3.16|John 3:14]] *and* from the Book of Mormon: the communities that revered the serpent talked about "the serpent who was raised up," "the serpent that Moses made," never the mocking nickname. The hosts call this "an extremely subtle little bull's-eye." [^3][^4]
- **Hezekiah vs. Isaiah = multiple religions in the First Temple.** Hezekiah destroys the serpent because Israel "burned incense" to it; yet his contemporary Isaiah sees the seraphim amid clouds of temple incense ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]]). J.J.M. Roberts (Hermeneia) reads Isaiah's seraphim as the Nehushtanim physically present in the temple ("above him stood the seraphim"). Citing Ziony Zevit's work on "the religions of ancient Israel," the hosts argue this is a direct doctrinal clash between two parties. [^5][^6]
- **The Book of Mormon preserves the suppressed strand — five times.** The Book of Mormon has roughly **five** serpent-on-a-staff passages ([[1 Nephi 17.1|1 Nephi 17:41]], 2 Nephi 25:20, Alma 33:19-22, Alma 37:43-47, [[Helaman 8.7|Helaman 8:13-18]]), all *positive* and all *explicitly tied to Christ* ("look and live") — as many as the entire Old and New Testaments combined. The hosts tie this to [[Richard Friedman]]'s source criticism: [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] is an E (northern) text, and the Book of Mormon is a northern Josephite record that kept the pro-serpent reading the southern editors and later rabbinic Judaism dropped. [^7][^8]
- **The "look and live" tell distinguishes it from the Liahona.** In Alma 37 the counsel "see that ye look to God and live" is, Butler notes, *not* the obvious lesson of the Liahona (a compass that shows the *way*) — "look and live" is the *serpent's* logic, so Alma is reading the Liahona through the Nehushtan story. [^9]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **The mainstream reading is "apostasy corrected," not "suppressed true religion":** [[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]] presents Hezekiah's act approvingly, and most historians read the bronze serpent as a tolerated relic that *became* an idol — exactly the [[Golden Calf and Bronze Serpent as Idolatry Tests|idolatry-boundary]] reading. The "good serpent the editors censored" thesis is a confessional inference, not the consensus. (b) **The Book of Mormon count proves intra-LDS-text consistency, not antiquity:** that the Book of Mormon repeats and develops the [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] type is unsurprising on *any* account of its composition, so it cannot by itself adjudicate the historical question. (c) **Isaiah's seraphim need not be the Nehushtan:** identifying the throne-seraphim of [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]] with physical bronze serpents is one scholarly proposal (Roberts), not a settled result; the seraphim may be purely visionary throne-attendants. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is comparative and cumulative — the texts are *more* intelligible if a healing serpent-type was a live (if contested) part of First-Temple religion, and the Book of Mormon's density of positive Christ-tied references is at least a striking convergence with the early-Christian reading of [[John 3.16|John 3:14]].

## Best Quotes

> "Nehushtan is probably the name applied to the serpent by people who disparage it… it's a mocking nickname. So the Nephites, like the first Christians, seem to have valued the serpent." [^3]

> "Numbers 21, the problem is death; the solution is the special serpent, the burning serpent. Isaiah 6, the problem is sin… What's the solution? Again the serpent. It's the same serpent." [^1]

> "Pound for pound it has a lot more references. It has as many as the Old and New Testament put together." [^7]
