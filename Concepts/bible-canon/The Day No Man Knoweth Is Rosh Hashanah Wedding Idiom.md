# The Day No Man Knoweth Is Rosh Hashanah Wedding Idiom

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that Jesus's saying that no one knows "the day nor the hour" of his return ([[Mark 13.32|Mark 13:32]]) is a double idiom: it is **Hebrew wedding speech** (only the father of the groom sets the wedding) *and* a specific reference to **Rosh Hashanah** (the Feast of Trumpets), which Jewish tradition itself calls "the day no man knoweth" because it begins on the new moon that cannot be seen. The trumpet-blowing wedding procession and the Feast of Trumpets are the same imagery — and [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]], depicted with a trumpet, came to [[Joseph Smith]] on Rosh Hashanah.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Rosh Hashanah is literally "the day no man knoweth"

Unlike the other feasts, whose dates could be read from the moon's phase, Rosh Hashanah falls on the **first of the month — a new moon**, which "you can't see." Anciently two witnesses had to spot the new moon at dusk and run to tell the temple priests before the trumpets sounded; hence it is "referred to as... the day that no man knoweth." So when Christ says no man knows the day or hour, he is also naming a feast.[^3]

### 2. The wedding trumpet is the Feast of Trumpets

In the wedding, the groom's friend (best man) ran ahead **blowing a trumpet** to warn the bride. Rosh Hashanah (*Yom Teruah*) is "the day of blowing trumpets." The two images coincide: the bridegroom's coming and the trumpet-feast are one motif, and the bride must "watch" and "listen" for the trumpet — exactly Christ's repeated "watch."[^4]

### 3. Moroni and Joseph Smith on Rosh Hashanah

The friend-of-the-groom who runs ahead blowing the trumpet to announce the bridegroom maps onto [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] — "how do we now depict Moroni? With a trumpet" — who delivered the warning Book of Mormon to [[Joseph Smith]]. Woodmansee notes Moroni's visit (September 1823) fell on Rosh Hashanah for that year: "how did Joseph Smith know the Hebrew holidays?"[^5]

### 4. Joel and the procession

[[Joel 2.30-31|Joel 2:16]] gathers the people, "let the bridegroom go forth of his chamber, and the bride out of her closet," immediately after "blow the trumpet in Zion" (Joel 2:15) — wedding-procession language built on the trumpet. The "thief in the night" likewise fits: the temple high priest was idiomatically the thief in the night who came to check if the guards were awake, so Christ "coming as a thief" means coming as the high priest to see if we are watching.[^6]

> **Steelman:** *Yom Teruah* (Rosh Hashanah) is genuinely the "feast of trumpets" tied to a new-moon sighting, and 1 Thessalonians 4:16 / 1 Corinthians 15:52 connect Christ's return to a trumpet. Hearing "no man knows the day or hour" with a Rosh-Hashanah resonance is a recognized line of Jewish-Christian typology, not unique to this author.

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The neat alignment of Moroni's visit with Rosh Hashanah depends on a particular conversion of the 1823 Hebrew calendar date; such date-matching is easy to over-sell.
- Mainstream exegesis reads "no man knows the day or hour" as a straightforward statement of eschatological ignorance and a call to readiness, without requiring the Rosh Hashanah idiom; the feast connection is a devotional enrichment (cf. [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]).
