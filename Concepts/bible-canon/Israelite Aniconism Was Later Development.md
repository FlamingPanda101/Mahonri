# Israelite Aniconism Was a Later Development

## The Claim

The biblical prohibition of divine images (aniconism) was **not original** to Israelite religion but a **later development**. Earlier strata and the archaeological record show divine images in use in Israel and Judah; the anti-idol polemic — "you heard a voice but you saw no image, so don't make divine images" — emerges later, especially in Deuteronomic literature. [[Dan McClellan]] argues this undercuts the assumption that image-free worship was the pristine, primitive Israelite norm.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The prohibition clusters in later texts.** McClellan: the anti-image command appears "really later on, in particularly Deuteronomy, where they're like, you heard a voice but you didn't see any image, so don't go making divine images" — whereas earlier (parts of Genesis) you have images.[^2] *(Evidence rating: aligns with the documentary/redactional dating of the Deuteronomic image-prohibition as a later theological program.)*

2. **Both stones and statues were "unapproved" together.** The later polemic did not approve standing stones while banning anthropomorphic statues; "they were all unapproved." This shows the prohibition is a blanket later development, not a refinement of an existing stone-vs-statue distinction.[^3] *(Evidence rating: strong internal-textual point.)*

3. **Archaeology shows earlier divine images.** "We clearly have divine images from earlier periods in Israel and Judah." The [[Arad Temple Standing Stone|Arad]] standing stone and the "[[Yahweh and His Asherah Inscriptions|Yahweh and his Asherah]]" drawing (with [[Bes]]-style, bovine-appearing imagery) are cited as material evidence of pre-aniconic practice.[^4] *(Evidence rating: strong — the Kuntillet Ajrud material and Arad sanctuary are central data points in this debate.)*

4. **The polemic's lateness explains the translation pressure.** If aniconism is secondary, the discomfort with "see the face of God" (driving the Masoretic passive vocalization) is itself a product of the later theology, not the original meaning of the pilgrimage commandment. See [[Masoretic Vowels Reversed the Active Verb to Passive]].[^5] *(Evidence rating: coherent linkage to the episode's main thesis.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A traditional/conservative position holds that aniconism is original — rooted in the Sinai revelation and the second commandment — and that the archaeological images represent *apostate* or folk practice that the prophets and Deuteronomic reformers rightly condemned, not the authentic norm. The presence of condemnation in the text, on this view, presupposes an original standard being violated. McClellan's counter is the redactional lateness of the prohibition layers plus the absence of any early stone-vs-statue distinction.

## Best Quotes

> "It's really later on, in particularly Deuteronomy, where they're like, you heard a voice, but you didn't see any image. So don't go making divine images."[^2]

> "It's not like the standing stone was the approved version and then anthropomorphic statuary was the non-approved version. They were all unapproved, but... that just shows that the anti-idol polemic is a later developed thing. Because we clearly have divine images from earlier periods in Israel and Judah."[^3]
