# The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that many early Christians operated with a **two-tier canon**: *tier one* (canonical scripture, authoritative for dogma) and *tier two* (edifying books that may be read and even "cited as scripture," but do not establish doctrine). The deuterocanonical/apocryphal books were frequently placed in tier two. This distinction, Ortlund says, dissolves the single most common move in popular canon debates — pointing to a church father quoting Tobit or Sirach "as scripture" and declaring the question settled. The citation proves nothing until you see *how that father classified the book in his canon list*. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Catholic/Orthodox use of patristic citations
The strongest form of the pro-deuterocanon argument is cumulative: the fathers don't merely *mention* these books, they introduce quotations with "it is written," "the scripture says," and weave them into doctrinal and liturgical arguments exactly as they do Isaiah or the Psalms. If the lived practice of the ancient church was to read Wisdom and Sirach *as* scripture in worship and to argue from them, then a later theoretical "canon list" excluding them looks like an antiquarian's Hebrew-purism overridden by the church's actual reception. On this reading, *usage* is the better index of canonicity than a scholar's enumeration.

### Ortlund's reply
- **The distinction is explicit in the fathers themselves.** Rufinus separates "canonical" from "ecclesiastical" books; [[Athanasius]] distinguishes canonical books from those "merely to be read for the sake of edification" (and both from the "secret"/apocryphal writings). The fathers tell us, in their own words, that not every readable book is canonical. [^2]
- **Tier-two books could still be called "scripture."** Ortlund's key concession-and-counter: "many times the tier-two books can still be cited... and even cited as scripture even while they are excluded from tier-one canon that establishes dogma." So the bare fact of a "scripture" citation does not settle tier. [^3]
- **The decisive test is the canon list plus the father's own statement.** With [[Jerome]], people quote his citation of Sirak as scripture — but his preface to the books of Solomon classifies Sirach and Wisdom as read "for the strengthening of the people but not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical dogmas." The explicit classification overrides the loose citation. [^4]
- **Trent removed the distinction.** Ortlund's historical punchline: for centuries the church *had* a two-tier mechanism; "the Council of Trent comes along and takes that distinction off the table," flattening tier-two books into full canonical status — which is itself a change from the medieval situation. [^5]

## LDS Relevance
The two-tier model is a Protestant *defensive* tool — it lets Ortlund affirm a closed shorter canon while explaining away centuries of apocryphal citation. For the LDS open-canon view it is double-edged. On one hand it concedes Ortlund's opponents' best point — that the early church really did read and cite these books authoritatively — which resonates with the LDS claim that valuable scripture extends beyond the Protestant 66 (compare [[Jesus Referenced the Apocrypha]]). On the other hand, the LDS tradition does not finally want a permanent *second* tier of merely-edifying texts; living-prophet revelation can *promote* material into binding scripture (and demote it), so the fixed tier-one/tier-two wall is as foreign to the restored canon as Trent's single flat list. The shared insight both traditions exploit against the others: "canonicity" in the ancient church was graded and contested, not binary.

## Best Quotes
> "Many times the tier-two books can still be cited, can still be read, can still be used, and even cited as scripture, even while they are excluded from tier-one canon that establishes dogma." [^3]

> "Don't allow people to say, 'oh look, this particular church father cited a book as scripture, therefore that settles the question,' when they don't even tell you in what sense they cited it... or how they classified their canon list." [^1]

> "Many of the church fathers have a distinction between first tier and second tier... and the Council of Trent comes along and takes that distinction off the table." [^5]
