# Philemon Reads Coherently Through Stoic Indifference to Status

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] uses the epistle to [[Philemon]] as a test case for Stoic embeddedness: the puzzle of why Paul sends the runaway slave Onesimus *back* to his master (rather than insisting on his freedom) dissolves on a Stoic reading. For the Stoics, outward status — slave or free — is *adiaphoron*, "indifferent"; what matters is the inner life. So Onesimus returning to servitude "doesn't ultimately matter," and the letter could enter the canon "without any question."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The moral puzzle (rated: the setup).** "How do we justify that? Why is Paul sending this guy who is living the good life as an escaped slave back into servitude? That's a bugger."[^1]
2. **The *adiaphoron* resolves it (rated: Stoic doctrine).** Stoicism is "open to everyone" (consul or tradesman), and "the *adiaphoron* means that which is indifferent — if you're a slave or not, it doesn't matter. So if he goes back into servitude, it doesn't ultimately matter; what matters is what's happening [in the heart]."[^2]
3. **Epictetus the enslaved Stoic (rated: corroboration).** The objection "he has to stay free" fails on Stoic terms: [[Epictetus]] "himself was a slave and yet was one of the great philosophers of the Stoic tradition."[^3]
4. **A "massive" canonical implication (rated: the thesis).** "From a Stoic perspective the book of Philemon is a non-issue… because whether he's a slave or isn't a slave, whether he's under his master or not, is irrelevant. What is relevant is the heart." Thomassen offers this as evidence of how deeply Stoic assumptions are "embedded" in the canon.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Standard exegesis reads Philemon without any Stoic premise: Paul works *within* Roman legal reality (a harbored runaway exposed both slave and harborer to penalty) while subverting it from inside — calling Onesimus "my son," "no longer as a slave but… a beloved brother," and hinting Philemon should free him ("knowing that thou wilt do more than I say"). On this reading Paul is not treating freedom as "indifferent" but laying the relational groundwork that undermines slavery. Critics also note that reading Pauline ethics as Stoic *indifference* to slavery sits uneasily beside the gospel's elevation of the lowly. The Stoic *adiaphoron* parallel is real; whether Paul *shares* it (vs. merely operating in the same legal world) is the contested point.
