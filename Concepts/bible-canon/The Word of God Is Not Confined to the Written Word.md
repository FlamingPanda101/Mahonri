# The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] argues the central equivocation in [[Anthony Rogers]]' case is the phrase **"the word of God."** Both sides agree the word of God is the highest authority; the *real* question is whether it is **confined to the written word alone**. Horn contends Scripture *always* uses "word of God" for an *orally proclaimed* message from a prophet, apostle, or God Himself — never for a written document. A scroll may *record* the word of God, but no verse says "this writing is the word of God." So the word of God remains both written and unwritten, and sola scriptura is false.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Word of God" denotes oral proclamation — rate: strong (general), overstated (absolute)
Horn's distributional claim is largely borne out: [[1 Thessalonians 2.13]] ("the word of God which ye *heard* of us"), the prophetic formula "the word of the LORD *came to*" Ezekiel/Hosea/etc., [[2 Thessalonians 2.15]] ("by word or by letter").[^1] **Steelman of Rogers (lands a hit):** Rogers cites [[Matthew 15.6]] — Jesus calls the *written* command "honor your father and mother" (recorded only in written Scripture, not on the tablets) "the word of God" — and [[John 10.33-35]] ("the word of God came ... and the scripture cannot be broken"). Horn parries that Matt 15 quotes God's *spoken* command at Sinai (note the textual variant "law/commandment of God"), and John 10 refers back to God *speaking* in Psalm 82, not to the scroll. **Assessment:** Horn's "*always*" is too strong if Matt 15:4–6 is granted; but his weaker thesis (the word of God is *not exclusively* written) survives easily, since [[1 Thessalonians 2.13]] alone shows spoken proclamation called the word of God.

### 2. The textual-variant move — rate: contested
Horn notes [[Matthew 15.6]] variants read "law of God"/"commandment of God" rather than "word of God."[^2] Rogers calls the variant non-preferred and a red herring, and stresses v.4's "he who speaks evil of father or mother is to be put to death" comes from *written* Scripture, not the Decalogue tablets.[^3] **Assessment:** Rogers has the stronger textual point here on Matt 15 specifically.

### 3. The stakes — rate: framing
If Horn is right that "word of God" ⊃ unwritten proclamation, then Rogers' avalanche of "word of God" proof-texts proves only the authority of God's *speech*, not the *sufficiency of the written word* — exactly Horn's reframing. See [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]].

## Best Quotes
> "The Bible never uses the term word of God to refer to a written human document. It is always used to refer to an orally proclaimed message about God from a prophet, an apostle or from God himself."[^1]

> "A written document might record what God or Moses spoke ... but it does not say this scroll or this writing is the word of God."[^2]

> [Rogers] "He calls the scriptures the word of God right there explicitly [John 10]. This is found all over the place. You could only possibly miss it if you've got the pope's glasses on."[^4]

> [Rogers, on Matt 15:4] "That wasn't put on the tablets. That is part of written scripture alone ... And it is this that Jesus calls the word of God."[^3]
