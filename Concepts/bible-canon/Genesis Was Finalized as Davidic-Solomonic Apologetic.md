# Genesis Was Finalized as Davidic-Solomonic Apologetic

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] argues that the book of Genesis received its **final, edited form during the Davidic–Solomonic monarchy or shortly after** (tail end of David, more likely tail end of Solomon, possibly up to two centuries later), and that its purpose was *apologetic*: to make the Davidic dynasty and Jerusalem look inevitable, divinely-chosen, and ancient. The pre-existing traditions (Eden, the flood, the patriarchal cycles) are older, but the *redactor* who stitched and arranged them "embedded into that narrative anticipations of the present" — they "tell the story about the past but reflect upon the present." On this reading the unknown editor of Genesis is a court scribe writing a national charter for a contested royal house, exactly as [[Mormon Is an Ethnocentric Redactor Focused on the Witness of Christ|Mormon]] is a visible redactor of the Book of Mormon.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Geography matches the United Monarchy (rated: moderate; the load-bearing external check).** The boundaries of Canaan promised to Abraham in [[Genesis 15.6|Genesis 15]] coincide with the heyday of David and Solomon. Mount Moriah, where Abraham nearly offers Isaac ([[Genesis 22]]), is the later Temple Mount — so a tradition of Abraham sacrificing "a son at a place" gets *fixed* at the future temple site. Hatch grants it could be perfect history, but the recurrence of such gap-filling points to retrojection. He likens it to "discovering" a 1620 Mayflower dream that already describes a nation from the Atlantic to the Pacific including Alaska and Hawaii — obviously written later.[^3][^4]

2. **Jerusalem is legitimized on three fronts.** The Gihon spring (David's water source) flows out of Eden in Genesis 2; [[Melchizedek]], "king of righteousness," is stationed at *Salem* (Jerusalem) and receives Abraham's tithe; and the sacrifice happens on the future temple mount. Priesthood + sacrifice + Gihon together "legitimize Jerusalem as the current administrative and sacrificial capital" of the kingdom.[^5]

3. **Genesis polemicizes David's real-world rivals.** The nations David subjugated — Ammon, Moab, Edom — get hostile origin stories: Ammon and Moab are born of Lot's incest; Edom is Esau, the rejected twin. This deploys the ancient polemical convention of branding an enemy nation sexually perverse. Isaac's blessing that Jacob be "lord over your brothers" reads as a direct jab at Esau (Rebekah's only other son). The [[Mesha Stele and Biblical Defeat Theology|Moabite Stone]] shows the rival side running the same rhetoric in reverse ("we exterminated the tribe of Gad").[^6][^7]

4. **The legitimacy-defense fits the post-David situation.** Even generations after David, scribes "are writing apologies for the Davidic line" because critics kept "casting doubt on the Davidic line" amid the dynasty's dysfunction. Everything in the canon "reverberates" around David and points to the [[2 Samuel|2 Samuel 7]] everlasting-dynasty promise — structurally parallel to how the Book of Mormon counts toward and away from the visit of Christ in 3 Nephi.[^8][^9]

5. **It converges with the source-critical dating.** On the [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins|Documentary Hypothesis]], the J source is dated to the 10th century partly *on this same boundary evidence*; even early datings (Friedman, Rendsburg) put Genesis's *final form* after David and Solomon. Hatch's thesis thus rides on, rather than against, mainstream scholarship.[^10]

**Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) Hatch repeatedly concedes the data are *cumulative and circumstantial* — "standing alone, not great evidence" for any single point — and that perfect history is possible; the case is a gestalt, not a proof. (b) There is a **circularity risk** he himself raises: reconstructing *when* a text was "really" written depends on a picture of the target period that is itself mostly built from texts that were edited; he answers that external (archaeological) confirmations and the self-validating fit of the younger-brother motif mitigate it. (c) The thesis partly *competes* with the vault's [[People's History vs Palace History in the Bible|Jacob Wright reading]], where the editors *bracket David away* to imagine peoplehood without a monarchy — the opposite redactional motive; both cannot be the whole story, so the *direction* of Genesis's politics is genuinely contested. (d) Much of the evidence is literary "close reading," which can over-pattern.

## Best Quotes

> "The scribes wrote a national epic incorporating all of the earlier traditions back to Abraham and embedded into that narrative anticipations of the present… they tell the story about the past but reflect upon the present. This was the major accomplishment of the anonymous authors in Jerusalem who created the book of Genesis." — [[Gary Rendsburg]], quoted by Hatch[^2]

> "It's as if… someone produced a document saying this is a dream by one of the 1620s passengers on the Mayflower, and you read the dream and it's a dream of a mighty nation that goes from the Atlantic to the Pacific and it includes Alaska and Hawaii. You would say this seems difficult to imagine that actually it was produced in 1620."[^4]

> "Who would want to do this? … It feels to me like you're somebody who's maybe a generation or two after David, but your legitimacy depends on David… you're trying to create a narrative that creates this sort of sense of inevitability."[^8]
