# Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Michael Heiser]] (and through him, [[Gavin Ortlund]]) argues that the biblical exaltation of Yahweh — sole creator, supreme over all other *elohim*, incomparable — manifests "species uniqueness": Yahweh differs from other gods *in kind*, not merely *in degree*. This qualitative difference, the argument goes, is what makes the Bible effectively monotheistic.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The exact same rhetoric is attested 1,000+ years before Hebrew existed (rating: strong; primary text)
The "rhetoric of incomparability" — one deity made all that exists, created the other gods, is exalted above them, receives their worship — is "standard rhetoric in ancient Southwest Asian literature." McClellan's centerpiece is the **Great Cairo Hymn of Praise to Amun-Ra** (translation from *The Context of Scripture*; best-preserved version from the reign of Amenhotep II, c. 1400 BCE). [^1]

> "you are the sole one who made all that exists one alone who made that which is from whose two eyes mankind came forth on Whose mouth the gods came into being" [^2]

> "the gods bowing to your majesty exalting the might of him who created them rejoicing at the approach of him who begot them" [^3]

### 2. Nobody calls the Amun-Ra cult monotheism (rating: strong; consensus)
Egyptian religion of the Second Intermediate Period / 14th century BCE is uncontroversially polytheistic, yet it deploys *more* emphatic incomparability rhetoric than the Bible. The rhetoric therefore cannot, by itself, indicate species uniqueness. [^4]

> "all of the exact same stuff is said about the other gods in relation to Amun re who were said to be created by Amun Ray and who are said to give praise to and to worship Amun Ray but if we go back to the second intermediate period... we're not going to find something that we would identify as monotheism" [^4]

### 3. The burden of proof falls on the apologist (rating: strong; methodological)
To make the species-uniqueness case, one must demonstrate that the Bible's incomparability rhetoric is **qualitatively distinct** from the identical pre-biblical rhetoric — not merely point to its existence. McClellan argues no such distinguishing feature has been produced. [^5]

> "if you want to make that argument you've got to find some way to demonstrate that the rhetoric as found in the Hebrew Bible is qualitatively distinct from the exact same rhetoric that we find in literature that long predated the Bible" [^5]

### 4. The Broncos/Raiders analogy (rating: illustrative)
McClellan repeatedly contrasts incomparability ("the Raiders are nothing compared to the Broncos" — a difference of degree) with creation ("one team did not create the other" — a difference of kind). Yahweh's relation to other *elohim* in the Bible, he argues, is the *creator* relation — but so is Amun-Ra's, which is why creation rhetoric still does not yield monotheism. [^6]

### 5. Steelman of Heiser/Ortlund
Heiser's strongest move (which McClellan quotes approvingly on a *different* point) is the formula: "Yahweh was an Elohim but no other Elohim was Yahweh and never was nor could be." [^7] The claim is that *pre-existence* and *being uncreated* set Yahweh categorically apart. McClellan answers that the Amun-Ra material asserts the same primacy and uncreated creator status for Amun, so even those predicates fail to isolate a uniquely biblical category. [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "the Hebrew Bible has nothing on the rhetoric of incomparability that we find in other places like this hymn" [^1]

> "this is the rhetoric of incomparability which is identical to the idea that the Raiders cannot compare to the Broncos" [^6]

> "we already had even more emphatic and even more vehement rhetoric of incomparability being directed toward the God who the authors wanted to exalt over all the other gods even though they were all equally Divine" [^8]
