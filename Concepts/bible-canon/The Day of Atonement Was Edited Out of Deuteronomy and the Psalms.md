# The Day of Atonement Was Edited Out of Deuteronomy and the Psalms

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the **Day of Atonement** (Yom Kippur) — the supreme temple festival in the priestly system — is conspicuously *missing* from the places one would expect it, and that its absence is the fingerprint of an anti-temple **Deuteronomist** editing program rather than an accident. Two data points: (1) the Psalm Scroll (11Q5) singles out the Day of Atonement as the high point of David's altar-liturgy, yet the festival is **absent from Deuteronomy's calendar** (Deut 16 lists Passover, Weeks, and Tabernacles but not Yom Kippur) and is not foregrounded in the canonical Psalm-collection; and (2) this fits the broader pattern of a scribal tradition (the "lawyers," hostile to the temple) suppressing the corporeal-Messiah/covering theology that the Day of Atonement encodes. The claim is the canon-level counterpart to the altar-liturgy and "worm of the destitute" arguments. See [[The Psalms Were Temple Altar-Liturgy Not Mere Poetry]] and [[The Qumran Worm of the Destitute Preserves Prayer for the Dead]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Deuteronomy 16 really omits the Day of Atonement
Deuteronomy's festival calendar (Deut 16:1-17) enumerates the three pilgrimage feasts — Passover/Unleavened Bread, Weeks, Tabernacles — but does **not** mention the Day of Atonement, which is otherwise central in Leviticus 16 and 23 and Numbers 29. The omission in Deuteronomy is a genuine, observable feature, not an invention.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong that Deuteronomy 16 omits it; the *significance* (deliberate suppression vs. Deuteronomy's different focus on pilgrimage feasts) is the contested step.

### 2. 11Q5 makes the Day of Atonement the climax of the Psalms' use
The "David's Compositions" coda in the Psalm Scroll names the Day of Atonement specifically as an occasion for the altar-songs — so an ancient witness ties the festival tightly to the Psalter's liturgical function, sharpening the contrast with its low profile in the received Psalm-collection.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — 11Q5 does highlight the Day of Atonement; that the canonical Psalms were *edited* to demote it (rather than simply not centering it) is the inference.

### 3. It coheres with the Deuteronomist-corruption thesis
A scribal stratum that downplayed the temple cult, the divine council, and a corporeal Messiah would have reason to soft-pedal the Day of Atonement's covering/blood theology — which is the vault's wider argument about Deuteronomistic editing. See [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — internal coherence with the broader thesis; not independent confirmation, and it inherits that thesis's contestedness.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers: (a) Deuteronomy 16 lists the **pilgrimage** feasts (the ones requiring appearance "before the LORD" at the central sanctuary) — the Day of Atonement was not a pilgrimage feast, so its absence there is **expected and innocent**, and it is fully present in Leviticus and Numbers within the same Torah, which undercuts any "edited out of the Bible" claim; (b) the Psalms are a prayer/praise collection, not a festival calendar, so the lack of a Yom-Kippur-specific psalm proves nothing about suppression; (c) 11Q5 is a late sectarian arrangement reflecting Qumran's priestly preoccupations, not a neutral baseline against which the Masoretic text can be judged "censored"; and (d) the whole argument presupposes the contested Deuteronomist-as-anti-temple-conspiracy frame, then reads an ordinary genre difference as evidence for it. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is cumulative, not a single smoking gun — the Day-of-Atonement profile is one of several Qumran "receipts" (alongside the altar-liturgy coda and the "worm of the destitute" variant) that jointly suggest the canonical text's temple/atonement emphasis was flattened; and even granting that Deuteronomy 16 lists only pilgrimage feasts, the *pattern* of de-emphasis across Deuteronomy and the received Psalter is the thing in need of explanation. The honest version concedes (a) is a strong, ordinary explanation that the suppression reading must outweigh, not ignore.

## Best Quotes

> 11Q5 "highlights the Day of Atonement as supreme; it is absent from Deuteronomy and from the canonical Psalm-list" — read as the Deuteronomist scribes suppressing the covering theology of Yom Kippur. (Barnes) [^1]
