# Pleroo Means to Complete a Law Not to Abolish It

#type/claim

## The Claim
The whole supersessionist reading of [[Matthew 5.17-20|Matthew 5:17]] hinges on the Greek verb *plēroō* (πληρόω), translated "fulfil." [[Dan McClellan]] consults **BDAG** (the standard New Testament Greek lexicon) and argues that the operative senses are "to bring to completion that which was already begun" and "to bring to a designed end / to fulfil" — of a prophecy, obligation, promise, or **law**. Critically, *plēroō* is **antithetical to** the verb Jesus contrasts it with, *kataluō* ("to destroy / abolish"). So Jesus is saying he came to bring the law to its *intended completion*, **not** to render it null, obsolete, or non-binding. "Fulfil" cannot be stretched to mean "and therefore you no longer have to obey it."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The lexicon's senses point to completion, not cancellation (rating: strong; lexical)
McClellan reads BDAG's entries aloud, ruling out the irrelevant senses ("to fill" a vessel, "to complete a period of time") and landing on "to bring to completion that which was already begun" and "to bring to a designed end... a prophecy, an obligation, a promise, a law." The sense is *arrival at an appointed goal/terminus*, i.e., "this is how things are going to be until this moment." [^1]

### 2. The text itself opposes "fulfil" to "destroy" (rating: strong; grammatical)
[[Matthew 5.17-20|Verse 17]] sets *plēroō* in explicit antithesis to *kataluō* ("destroy/abolish"): "I am **not** come to destroy, but to fulfil." Whatever "fulfil" means, the verse defines it as the *opposite* of abolition — so reading "fulfil" as a polite synonym for "abolish/supersede" inverts the sentence. [^1]

### 3. "Fulfil" is governed by the eschatological time-frame (rating: moderate; contextual)
Because verse 18 ties completion to "till heaven and earth pass" (see [[Dual Chronological Markers Keep the Law in Force Until the Eschaton]]), the "designed end" toward which the law is being brought is the **end of the age**, not the first coming. The law is *completed* (reaches its purpose and terminus) when there is "no more context for the law" — when the present world wraps up. [^2]

### 4. "You can't fulfill a negative" limits the sense further (rating: moderate; conceptual)
Beecher notes that prohibitive commandments cannot be "completed" the way a positive promise or prediction is; this constrains *plēroō* away from any sense in which a "do not" command gets used up and discharged, reinforcing that "fulfil" here means *uphold to the appointed end*, not *exhaust and retire*. [^3]

### 5. Steelman of the traditional "fulfilment = telos in Christ" reading
Defenders of supersessionism agree that *plēroō* means "bring to completion," but argue the *completion* is **christological**: Christ fulfills the law by perfectly keeping it, by being the substance to which its types/shadows pointed (sacrifice, priesthood, temple — cf. Hebrews), and by transposing its commands into their deepest intent (the antitheses). On this view the moral law abides as God's character while the ceremonial/civil functions reach their telos *in him* — so "not abolish but fulfil" is precisely how the New Covenant supersedes the administration of Moses without lawlessness (Rom 8:3-4; 10:4, "Christ is the *telos* of the law"). The traditional reading thus claims the same lexeme. McClellan's counter is contextual rather than lexical: BDAG's "designed end" plus Matthew's *dual time-markers* and verse 19 locate that completion at the eschaton and keep the commandments binding in the interim.

## LDS Relevance
LDS scripture uses fulfilment language emphatically: the resurrected Christ in 3 Nephi 15:4-5 says "I am he that gave the law... the law in me is fulfilled, for I came to fulfil the law; therefore it hath an end." This sides with the *christological-completion* sense of *plēroō* (the law reaches its telos in Christ) rather than McClellan's eschatological-deferral sense. The distinctive LDS nuance is that fulfilment of the *Mosaic* law does not end *law as such*: a "higher law" (the law of the gospel) replaces it, and the open canon allows further commandments to be revealed. So the lexical debate over *plēroō* matters less for LDS theology than the question of *which divine law is currently in force* — which the LDS tradition answers through living revelation rather than through a fixed reading of one Greek verb.
