# Internal Anonymity Was the Convention of Ancient Biography

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Bart Ehrman]]: "The gospels don't claim to be written by eyewitnesses. They are all anonymous... [the titles] were added by later editors" — so the Gospels' internal anonymity counts against their traditional authorship. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Michael Jones]] (citing [[Simon Gathercole]]) argues internal anonymity is **how ancient biography was written**, not a mark of fabrication. "Just about every biography from the ancient world was internally anonymous." Authors were identified by a *title*, a tag on the scroll, or a fly-leaf — not by saying "I, Matthew, am writing this." So the absence of an in-body self-identification is exactly what an authentic ancient *bios* looks like. [^2]

1. **Plutarch never names himself either.** "Plutarch never identifies himself as the author [in] any of Plutarch's lives" — yet no one doubts Plutarch wrote them. The convention is universal enough that the few internally-named examples Gathercole found include a *fictional* biography (the life of Aelius in the *Historia Augusta*), so internal naming is not even a reliable mark of historicity. *Rating: strong; the genre point is well-attested.* [^2]
2. **An in-body "I, Matthew" would be jarring.** Jones: ancient readers expected the anonymous form; a first-person authorial intrusion "would be too jarring for the audience. It wouldn't sound like an actual biography of the ancient world." Hansen's analogy: a Wikipedia article that began "I, Joe, am writing this" would read as a *forgery*, not as more authentic. *Rating: moderate-strong; a fair cultural-form argument.* [^3]
3. **Two Gospels do claim eyewitness connection internally.** Ehrman's blanket "they don't claim" is false for **John** (21:24; 19:35 — "he who saw it has borne witness") and **Luke** (1:1-4 — explicitly *not* an eyewitness but gathering from "those who from the beginning were eyewitnesses"). *Rating: strong as a factual correction of Ehrman's overstatement.* [^1]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The skeptical reply (cf. [[Gospel of John Is Pseudepigraphal Not Eyewitness]]): genre anonymity cuts both ways — if the form is anonymous, then the *later* titles carry the entire weight of attribution, and those titles are external, second-century additions whose accuracy is the very thing in question. That Plutarch is known to be Plutarch by independent attestation does not transfer that certainty to four anonymous Gospels whose authorship rests on tradition. And a pseudepigrapher could deliberately adopt the anonymous form precisely to sound authentic.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply continues in the linked notes: the *content* of the attribution argues for authenticity (no one would invent **Mark** and **Luke** as authors — see [[Attributing Gospels to Mark and Luke Argues for Authenticity]]), and the attribution is **unanimous and uncontested** until the 4th century (see [[Unanimous Patristic Attestation of the Gospel Authors]]). The same critics who treat anonymity as decisive against the Gospels rarely apply it to anonymous OT books — the [[The Anonymous-Authorship Double Standard on Genesis|double standard]].

## Best Quotes
> "Just about every biography from the ancient world was internally anonymous. That's how you wrote a biography... The overwhelming majority never identify an author internally... like Plutarch never identifies himself as the author [in] any of Plutarch's lives." — [[Michael Jones]] [^2]
