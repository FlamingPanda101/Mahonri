# Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The most common prooftext for biblical infallibility is [[2 Timothy 3.14-17|2 Timothy 3:16]] — "All scripture is **θεόπνευστος** (*theopneustos*), 'God-breathed.'" [[Hayden Carroll]] makes two replies: **(1)** "all scripture" at the time could not mean the later Protestant canon (the author had no closed OT or NT canon), and **(2)** *theopneustos* does not mean "infallible" — it more plausibly carries a **life-giving** sense (as God "breathed" life into Adam), since the concept of textual infallibility did not exist when the verse was written.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "all scripture" scope point is strong — rate: strong
Whatever *theopneustos* means, "all scripture" (πᾶσα γραφή) in a first-century letter cannot designate the 66-book Protestant Bible, which did not yet exist; at most it points to the Jewish writings then in use (which for many included books Protestants later excluded). So the verse cannot do the work of *identifying* the canon. See [[Canon Cannot Self-Reference - Compilation Postdates the Books]]. This is uncontroversial among scholars.

### 2. The *theopneustos* = "life-giving" gloss is contestable — rate: moderate, partly overstated
*Theopneustos* is a NT *hapax legomenon* (used only here). The morphology θεός + πνέω ("God-breathed") most naturally renders "breathed out by God," and the dominant scholarly translation is *expired/breathed-out by God* (so B.B. Warfield's classic study, which argued the word is *active* "produced by God's breath" rather than *passive* "breathed into"). Carroll's "life-giving" reading is a *minority* sense and is closer to a passive/animating gloss. He credits [[Trent Horn]] and contributors on [[Jacob Hansen]]'s channel for the argument.[^2] **Steelman of the Protestant:** even granting "God-breathed" does not *lexically* entail "inerrant," the inference (what God produces by his breath cannot err) is theological, not merely linguistic — so the Protestant case does not rest on the bare word. **Honest assessment:** Carroll is right that the *word itself* does not encode "infallible" (that is a later dogmatic addition), but his positive "life-giving" translation is weaker than he implies; the safer form of his argument is the negative one — *theopneustos* says scripture is divinely sourced, not that it is errorless or that it identifies its own table of contents.

### 3. Anachronism of "infallibility" — rate: moderate
Carroll argues "the concept of infallibility of man did not exist at the time this was written," paralleling the Catholic doctrine of papal infallibility (which he dates loosely and incorrectly to "the 11th century" — it was actually defined in 1870 at Vatican I, though developed earlier).[^3] The general point — that *infallibility* is a later technical category being read back into a first-century text — has merit as a caution against anachronism, but the specific historical dating is shaky and should not be relied on. **Caution:** the date error illustrates the risk of confident historical claims made from memory in a lecture setting.

## Corroboration from Keystone (Poirier)
A separate [[Keystone]] explainer gives this "life-giving" gloss a much stronger scholarly footing than the bare assertion above: it cites the published monograph of New Testament scholar [[John C. Poirier]], who found that in the era 2 Timothy was written *theopneustos* was used for **sandals, burial ointments, and streams of water** — i.e. *life-giving / vivifying* things, none of which are "inerrant." Keystone also reports independent agreement from non-LDS scholars (Rev. Dr. [[Angela Parker]]; Dr. Anna Cwikla) and from [[Dan McClellan]] that the inerrantist "inspired" reading is a third-century development traceable to [[Origen]]. This is the dedicated, well-sourced treatment — see [[Theopneustos Was Used for Sandals Ointments and Streams Not Inerrancy]].[^6]
