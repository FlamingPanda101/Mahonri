# Attributing Gospels to Mark and Luke Argues for Authenticity

## The Claim
[[Michael Jones]] argues that the early church's attribution of two Gospels to **Mark** and **Luke** — men who never met Jesus and play tiny roles in the New Testament — is strong evidence the attributions are *genuine*, not invented. A forger seeking authority would have picked an apostle (Peter, Andrew, Thomas), exactly as the later Gnostic gospels did. Choosing minor non-eyewitnesses only makes sense if that is simply who actually wrote them. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Forgers maximize authority; these picks minimize it.** "Why are you going to attribute a gospel to Mark, someone who never met Jesus according to Papias? Why not just attribute it to Peter if you really want to put authenticity on this?" Mark and Luke "play extremely small roles throughout the New Testament." *Rating: strong; a clean application of the criterion of embarrassment/dissimilarity.* [^1]
2. **The Gnostic contrast.** "We see the later Gnostic gospels attributing their gospels to people like Thomas and Judas and Mary" — i.e., big names — which is what fabricated attributions look like. The canonical pattern is the opposite. *Rating: strong; the comparison is apt.* [^2]
3. **Ehrman inadvertently makes the argument.** When Ehrman explains that Luke "is identified as Luke to connect it with Paul, just like Mark is... a companion of Peter" — choosing "two that were connected with the two most important apostles" — Jones notes this *concedes* that the names chosen are *not* the apostles themselves: "if these were making up titles, why did they attribute it to Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John?" rather than directly to Peter and Paul. *Rating: strong dialectically; Ehrman's own account presupposes the second-tier names.* [^3]
4. **The Hebrews counter-case.** "If they were just making up names for books, why didn't they make up a name for the book of Hebrews?" The early church openly admitted it didn't know who wrote Hebrews — showing they did *not* freely invent authors — "but you know what they did know? The authors were Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John." *Rating: moderate-strong; absence of an invented Hebrews-author is telling.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The skeptic replies that the attributions could still be *educated guesses* by the second-century church rather than fabrications-for-prestige: faced with anonymous texts, they reasoned from internal clues (a Pauline-style travel companion → Luke; Peter's interpreter per Papias → Mark) and attached the best-fitting names. On that model the names are neither cynical forgeries nor reliable memory, but inference — which would explain second-tier choices without establishing authentic authorship. Ehrman's actual position is closer to this than to "they picked random famous names."

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: educated-guess attribution should have left *traces of debate* (competing candidates, hesitation) the way the church openly debated Hebrews — yet for the Gospels the attribution is **unanimous and uncontested** across regions until the 4th century (see [[Unanimous Patristic Attestation of the Gospel Authors]]). A guess that everyone, everywhere, independently lands on with no dissent is better explained as preserved knowledge than as parallel inference.

## Best Quotes
> "Why are you going to attribute a gospel to Mark, someone who never met Jesus according to Papias? Why not just attribute it to Peter if you really want to put authenticity on this? Attributing two of the gospels, Mark and Luke, to two men who never met Jesus would be completely unlikely." — [[Michael Jones]] [^1]

> "If they were just making up names for books, why didn't they make up a name for the book of Hebrews? They debated about that. They didn't know. But you know what they did know? The authors were Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John." — Jones [^4]
