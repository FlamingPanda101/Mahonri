# "Scripture Is the Word of God" Is Itself a Doctrine of Sacred Tradition

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]]'s thesis, stated positively: the proposition "Scripture is the word of God" is not found *in* Scripture and was recognized only gradually by the Church; therefore it is itself a **deliverance of sacred tradition** — exactly parallel to the canon. The apostolic teaching office, continued in their successors, is what "clearly reveals to us that scripture is the word of God."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The canon parallel makes the move symmetrical — rate: strong (internal logic)
We know *which* books are Scripture not from any inspired table of contents but from the tradition of the canon (an "infallible rule of faith beyond scripture"); by the same logic we know *that* Scripture is the word of God through tradition.[^1] This symmetry is the heart of the Catholic "canon problem" against sola scriptura — see [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]] and [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]]. The two unknowns (which books, and their status as God's word) have the same solution: an authority outside the text.

### 2. The magisterium serves, not supersedes, the word — rate: framing
Horn pre-empts the charge that this elevates the Church over Scripture by quoting [[Dei Verbum]]: the teaching office "is not above the word of God, but serves it… listening to it devoutly, guarding it scrupulously, and explaining it faithfully."[^2] The claim is not that the Church *creates* Scripture's authority but that it *recognizes and transmits* it — which is precisely what a fallible, text-only system cannot do infallibly. **Steelman of the Protestant:** this still concentrates interpretive finality in an institution, reintroducing the very problem (who guards the guardians?) that sola scriptura tried to avoid by anchoring to a fixed text; and "tradition recognized it" does not prove "tradition is infallible." Horn's reply runs through [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]]: only an infallible recognizer can ground an infallibly-known rule.

### 3. The conclusion against sola scriptura — rate: strong (if premises granted)
"The teaching authority of the apostles has continued in their successors and it clearly reveals to us that scripture is the word of God."[^1] If the premise "Scripture is the word of God" is itself handed on by tradition, then Scripture cannot be the *sole* rule — tradition is logically prior to one's confidence in Scripture. See [[The Word of God Was Never Confined to the Written Word]].

### 4. Cross-tradition resonance — rate: framing
The same structural critique appears from a Latter-day Saint angle (living authorized prophets/apostles rather than a self-interpreting text) at [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]]. Catholic and LDS apologists converge in locating the missing ingredient in *authorized, living teaching authority* — while disagreeing about where that authority now resides.

## Best Quotes
> "Just as we know which human writings are scripture through the sacred tradition of the canon of scripture which represents an infallible rule of faith beyond scripture, we know that scripture is the word of God through sacred tradition as well."[^1]

> "The teaching authority of the apostles has continued in their successors and it clearly reveals to us that scripture is the word of God."[^1]

> "This teaching office or magisterium is not above the word of God, but serves it… guarding it scrupulously, and explaining it faithfully." — Dei Verbum, as quoted[^2]
