# A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] presses a linguistic-epistemic objection to biblical *infallibility*: a text is made of words, words do not carry "set meanings," and meaning shifts with reader and language — so **infallibility cannot attach to any text**, least of all a translated and transmitted one. "Epistemologically, no... when I say *God*, I view God very differently than you do. There's not an objective understanding of what God is amongst all people."[^1] The default condition of humans is fallibility, so positing an infallible *document* is, for Carroll, a category error.[^2] [[Brother Oz (Austin)]] resists hard ("That's not true... epistemologically, in order for anything to mean anything, there must be some objective identity, some Logos") — see [[Relationality Requires Three - The Trinitarian Grounding of Meaning]] — but then **concedes** the practical point: "any text that is translated is not infallible in the way that you and I would tend to mean infallible," and "the transmission is not infallible."[^3] The two converge on a nuanced settlement: Scripture is "true but not precise," "true in what it accomplishes."

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Latter-day Saint (Hayden Carroll)
- **Abstract theological terms have no fixed referent.** Carroll's wedge example: "*God is love* — that phrase, if it's an infallible phrase, who gets to decide what the meaning of that is?" Concrete words ("microphone," "cup") can be pinned down by ostension, but high-level theological abstractions ("God is love," "God is simple") are exactly where readers, and even popes, diverge.[^4] So even granting an inerrant autograph, *infallibility of meaning* never transfers to the reader.
- **Translation and transmission are confessedly fallible.** Carroll secures Oz's agreement that the English/Spanish text the believer actually reads is not "incapable of error," because it depends on a translator. He generalizes to the LDS standard — **reliability/sufficiency, not infallibility**: "maybe it's sufficient... to give the message, to communicate the message that was intended."[^5] This is the vault's [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]], [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]], and the eighth Article of Faith ("as far as it is translated correctly"). It also connects to [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].
- **A Restoration-friendly conclusion.** If transmission and translation cannot be infallible, then the believer needs a *living* corrective — continuing revelation and prophetic interpretation — to recover meaning the text alone cannot guarantee. The objection thus feeds directly into [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]].

### Steelman of the Evangelical (Brother Oz)
- **The Logos secures objective meaning.** Oz's principled rebuttal: if there is no objective identity, "everything is completely subjective and you have an entire collapse of epistemology." Because Christ is the Logos through whom all things were made ([[John 1.1|John 1:1]]), "the category of human is not merely socially constructed" — meaning is "baked into the nature of reality itself."[^6] He rejects the postmodern slide where identity is "the result of power games." So Carroll's premise, taken to its end, would dissolve *all* communication (including the Book of Mormon and prophetic statements), not just biblical infallibility.
- **"True but not precise."** Oz's nuance is the heart of the convergence: he affirms Scripture is infallible "in the sense that it's true, a reflection of reality," while denying it is *precise* "in the way that we would mean precise." He cheerfully accepts radically inculturated translations — "Jesus broke the banana and the wine" for Madagascar, "the seal of God" for the Inuit — because "it is true in what it accomplishes."[^7] Inerrancy, properly understood, is about *truth of what the inspired authors affirm*, not spreadsheet-like exactness ("if the Bible was a spreadsheet, would all the numbers light up — that's just not how they would see it").[^8]
- **Original autographs vs. copies.** When Carroll asks whether the *Greek/Hebrew autographs* are infallible, Oz declines a flat yes, saying "infallible means too many things to too many people." Classic evangelical inerrancy locates infallibility in the *autographs* and reliable *preservation* of copies — a distinction Oz gestures at but does not fully deploy, which Carroll exploits to collapse "infallible" into "incapable of error" and then deny the English text meets it.[^9]

**Honest assessment.** This exchange ends closer to *agreement* than either man advertises. Carroll's strong claim — that *no* text can mean anything objectively — is self-undermining (it would void his own scriptures and the very sentence asserting it), and Oz rightly flags the epistemic collapse. But Carroll's *weak* claim — that **infallibility does not survive translation and transmission**, so "reliable/sufficient" is the honest standard — is one Oz essentially grants. The real residue is the contested middle: whether the *autographs* were infallible (evangelical inerrancy) and whether that matters if the believer can never access them without a fallible chain. On that, the LDS appeal to living revelation and the evangelical appeal to providential preservation part ways, but both reject the caricatured "my English Bible is incapable of error" that Carroll says he usually meets.

## Best Quotes
> "Infallibility doesn't work when language is involved, because language is fallible and because we can view language differently based on the person."[^2]

> "Epistemologically, in order for anything to mean anything, there must be some objective identity... otherwise everything is completely subjective and you have an entire collapse of epistemology." — Brother Oz[^6]

> "Yes, scripture is infallible in the sense that it's true, in the sense that it's a reflection of reality. But... it's a little bit western for us to try to zoom all the way out... and ask if the Bible was a spreadsheet, would all the numbers light up."[^8]

> "The transmission is not infallible." / "But maybe it's sufficient just to give the message, to communicate the message that was intended." — Oz, then Carroll[^5]
