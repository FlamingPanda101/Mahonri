# The KJV Was Already Out of Date in 1611

## The Claim

The King James Version's English was archaic **at the moment of its publication** in 1611, and 400-plus years have only widened the gap. It is therefore a poor instrument for understanding what the biblical authors meant — its value is as a literary artifact, not a translation. [[Dan McClellan]] presents this as the first of three reasons the KJV "is not for you" if you want the authors' meaning. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. It is a revision of a revision of a revision — rate: strong

McClellan: the KJV is "a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision going back almost a century" to the New Testament of [[William Tyndale]] and the work of Tyndale and [[Miles Coverdale]]. Because it deliberately conserved older renderings, "when it was published, it was already being criticized for using out-of-date language." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the KJV's textual genealogy through Tyndale/Coverdale/Bishops' Bible is well documented.

### 2. "Making a difference" is a worked example of semantic drift — rate: strong

[[Jude 1.22|Jude 22]] reads "of some have compassion, making a difference." Modern readers hear "making a difference" as "having a positive impact" — "a wonderful message that has absolutely nothing whatsoever to do with what the King James translators were trying to say." That sense only attached to the phrase around 1900; in 1611 it meant **making a distinction**: "Have compassion, but be discerning on whom you're going to have compassion." [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a clean, checkable case of an idiom whose meaning inverted, producing a sincere misreading.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A KJV defender can answer that the cadence and influence of the KJV on English literature and liturgy is itself a reason to keep reading it, and that any competent reader uses study aids to recover archaic senses — so "making a difference" is a teaching opportunity, not a defeater. McClellan grants the *literary-artifact* value explicitly; his narrower claim is only that for the specific goal of *understanding the biblical authors*, an archaic translation "frequently gets in the way." The point connects to the vault's broader thesis that [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer|every translation is a choice]].

## Best Quotes

> "It is a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision going back almost a century to the New Testament of William Tyndale." [^1]

> "That phrase 'making a difference' didn't become connected to the notion of having a positive impact... until around the year 1900. In 1611, it meant making a distinction." [^2]
