# The Holy Spirit Is Not Individuated Until John 14-17

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that the Holy Ghost is **never presented as a distinct, individuated person in the Old Testament**, and only emerges as an individuated divine person late — in John 14–17. The underlying Hebrew (*ruach*) and Greek (*pneuma*) terms mean "wind / breath / spiritual influence," which are not individuated entities. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Ruach* is breath/wind, not a person (rating: strong; lexical)
"The term they use is *ruach* and it simply means a spiritual influence and is not individuated. *Ruach* also means wind or breath... and wind and breath are not individuated." [^1]

> "I do not think that the Holy Ghost is ever presented as a third person or an individuated person in the Old Testament. The term they use is ruach and it simply means a spiritual influence and is not individuated." [^1]

### 2. *Pneuma* has the same semantic range (rating: strong; lexical)
"*Pneuma* is the Greek term and it has the very same semantic range. It means breath and wind and spirit." So the Greek does not, by itself, supply an individuated third person either. Cf. [[John 4.24|John 4:24]] ("God is spirit"). [^2]

### 3. Individuation appears in John 14–17 (rating: moderate; textual)
"It's not until we get to the New Testament, and in particular really until we get to John 14 through 17, that we have an individuated divine person who is spirit." The term *ekeinos* ("he," referring to an individual person) is "used throughout John 14–17." [^3]

### 4. The Spirit is never called Yahweh (rating: moderate; observation)
Ostler notes the Holy Ghost "is never called Yahweh," unlike the Son who bears the name as the Father's agent — a further sign the Spirit's individuation is distinct and late. See [[The Son Bears Yah but Never El Elyon]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Trinitarians argue the Spirit's *personhood* is genuinely present earlier — the Spirit speaks, can be grieved, distributes gifts, and is the agent of creation and prophecy — and that progressive *clarity* (culminating in John's Paraclete) does not mean the Spirit was *impersonal* before; the same canon that "develops" the Son's identity develops the Spirit's. They also note *ruach*/*pneuma* having a "wind/breath" range no more reduces the Spirit to an impersonal force than *logos* reduces the Son to a mere "word." Ostler's reply: lexical individuation (the personal pronoun *ekeinos*) and explicit person-language *first appear* in John, so reading a fully individuated third person back into the Hebrew Bible is anachronistic—consistent with his broader anti-isogesis method. See [[Isogesis - Reading Metaphysical Monotheism Back Into the Text]].
