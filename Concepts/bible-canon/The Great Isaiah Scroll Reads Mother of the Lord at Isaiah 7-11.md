# The Great Isaiah Scroll Reads Mother of the Lord at Isaiah 7-11

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]]'s signature textual argument: in the photographs of the Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ), Isaiah 7:11 reads "ask a sign of the **mother** of the Lord your God," where the Masoretic Text (and so the KJV) reads "ask a sign of the **Lord** your God." The difference is a single Hebrew letter — *aleph* (giving "mother of," *em*) versus *ayin* — and Barker argues a scribe changed the *aleph* to an *ayin* to remove a reference to a Heavenly Mother that "really ought to disappear."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The one-letter difference (rated: Barker's paleographic claim).** "All you have to do to the Hebrew to change that is to change an *aleph*, which would give you 'mother of the Lord,' to an *ayin*." Barker says she has "demonstrated this many, many times": look "not at critical editions, look at a photograph of the original scroll as it came out the cave," and the letter "is clearly an *aleph* because you can see a few lines down how that scribe wrote an [*ayin*] and it's nowhere like the letter."[^1][^2]
2. **It sits in the Christmas-prophecy verse (rated: contextual).** This is the immediate run-up to Isaiah 7:14 ("behold, the virgin shall conceive"), so a "mother of the Lord" reading would, Barker says, both reframe the virgin prophecy and imply a divine Mother distinct from the Father and Son — "it gets rid of the idea of the Trinity altogether… the Trinity then is the Father, the mother, and the Son."[^3]
3. **The scroll is the older, less-edited witness (rated: Barker's framing).** Barker notes that before the Dead Sea Scrolls the earliest complete Hebrew Bible was post-1000 AD; the scroll shows "our Isaiah text is in fact a revision" (an *aleph* here, a different word there), so the photograph predates "the editors."[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** This reading is a **minority paleographic claim**, not the scholarly consensus on 1QIsaᵃ; standard transcriptions (e.g. the official DJD edition and Donald Parry's text) read the line with the Masoretic sense, and most epigraphers would dispute that the disputed character is an *aleph*. The vault's own [[1QIsaiah-a Aligns With the Masoretic Text Under 92.5 Percent]] (from Dan McClellan) shows the scroll's variants are real and meaning-bearing but counted from the critical edition, not from a single contested letter — a caution against over-reading any one variant. A conservative also notes that "mother of the Lord" is theologically loaded enough that the burden of proof is high.

## Best Quotes
> "That doesn't say 'ask a sign of the Lord your God.' It says 'ask a sign of the mother of the Lord your God. Behold the virgin you'll conceive.'… somebody realized that was a rather major prophecy and really ought to disappear."[^1]

> "Don't look at critical editions; look at a photograph of the original scroll as it came out the cave… it is clearly an *aleph*."[^2]

## LDS Connection
If a Heavenly Mother stood in the oldest Isaiah text and was edited out, it corroborates the reform's removal of the divine feminine that the vault documents in [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]] and connects to the broader "Mother in heaven / Wisdom" material. It parallels the vault's other Masoretic-pointing argument, [[Masoretic Vowels Reversed the Active Verb to Passive]], as a case where later editorial choices are said to mask an earlier reading — while remaining, here, a contested paleographic claim rather than settled text.
