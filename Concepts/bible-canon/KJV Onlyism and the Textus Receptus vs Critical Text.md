# KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection rebutted is KJV-onlyism: the claim that the King James Version, based on the **Textus Receptus** / Byzantine tradition, is the true, preserved Bible, and that the **critical text** (Alexandrian) is corrupt. [[Dan McClellan]] notes KJV-onlyists are the one popular group for whom the manuscript question "sits a little closer to the surface," because their dogma requires defending the Textus Receptus against the critical text.[^1] He paraphrases the rhetoric: "The critical text is Alexandrian. Dirty, dirty Alexandrians. We've got to get back to the Byzantine tradition."[^2] See [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. KJV-onlyism inadvertently concedes the manuscript question is real — rate: strong
By making the Textus-Receptus-vs-critical-text dispute a doctrinal hill, KJV-onlyists demonstrate McClellan's larger point: the manuscript base is a *choice* with no neutral arbiter. Most evangelicals are unaware of this question, but KJV-onlyists prove it is live and consequential.[^1] **Steelman of the KJV-onlyist:** the "providential preservation" argument holds that God preserved the text in the majority (Byzantine) tradition that the church actually used, so the abundance of Byzantine manuscripts is evidence of divine guardianship over the received text. McClellan's reply: this is a *theological* preference dressed as a text-critical conclusion — it cannot be established on neutral evidentiary grounds, and the Alexandrian witnesses are older. The dispute is exactly the kind of undecidable group-preference his thesis predicts.

### 2. The "dirty Alexandrians" framing is polemic, not philology — rate: strong
McClellan's caricature captures a real rhetorical pattern in KJV-only circles: textual families are moralized ("dirty") rather than evaluated by date, geography, and copying habits. Geographic origin does not determine reliability; this is genus-level name-calling. See the vault's [[Historical Method Over Name Calling]].

### 3. Scribal additions are the concrete stakes — rate: strong
The episode ties the manuscript question to actual scribal interpolations — "somebody got a wild hair and decided to add something about a lady caught in adultery" ([[John 7.53-8.11|John 7:53-8:11]], the *pericope adulterae*), present in the Textus Receptus/KJV but absent from the earliest manuscripts.[^3] This is the textbook example where choosing the Textus Receptus literally adds a story to your Bible. See [[Manuscripts Were Altered by Scribes Over Centuries]] and the existing [[Pericope Adulterae Is a Later Interpolation]].
