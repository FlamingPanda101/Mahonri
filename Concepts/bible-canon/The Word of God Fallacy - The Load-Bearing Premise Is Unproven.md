# The Word of God Fallacy — The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Modern Protestant apologists have shifted from defending *sola scriptura* with proof-texts to defending it with a **logical/ontological argument**. As [[Wes Huff]] frames it: "Scripture is ontologically unique. It is the direct speech of God. Nothing we possess as a rule is akin to it."[^1] [[Gavin Ortlund]] argues the parallel: Scripture is "divine speech or the words of God… This explains why scripture is infallible… because God is infallible and scripture is God's speech."[^2] The syllogism: Scripture is the direct word of God; nothing else has this ontological status; therefore Scripture is the only infallible rule of faith.[^3]

[[Trent Horn]] grants the *conclusion's* premises about God's infallibility but argues the **first premise is the fallacy**: that Scripture *is* the direct speech of God "is either assumed to be true or it's defended with irrelevant examples."[^4]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The argument's validity depends entirely on an unjustified premise — rate: strong
Horn isolates the move: even if "God is infallible" and "God's speech is infallible" are granted, the conclusion only follows if *this specific collection of human writings* is in fact God's speech. That identification is the contested premise, and it is smuggled in rather than demonstrated.[^4] **Steelman of Huff/Ortlund:** the premise is not arbitrary — Scripture's own self-description ("God-breathed," [[2 Timothy 3.14-17|2 Tim 3:16]]) and Jesus' treatment of the OT as God speaking arguably establish it from within. Horn's reply (developed in [[No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God]]) is that θεόπνευστος does not say *which* writings qualify, and that no verse calls a human writing "the word of God."

### 2. "Ontologically unique" is doing rhetorical, not logical, work — rate: moderate
Calling Scripture "ontologically unique" asserts the conclusion as a property rather than arguing for it. If the unique ontological status (being God's direct speech) is precisely what must be shown, then asserting it as a premise is **begging the question**.[^4] Compare the structurally similar critique in [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]]. **Steelman:** ontological uniqueness can be argued inductively from the phenomenon of fulfilled prophecy, internal coherence, etc. — but Horn notes that such arguments establish reliability, not the specific status "word of God," and certainly not the canon's boundaries.

### 3. Horn concedes the orthodox conclusion while denying the Protestant route to it — rate: framing
Crucially, Horn affirms that Scripture *is* part of the word of God, citing Vatican II's [[Dei Verbum]]: Scripture together with sacred tradition forms the word of God.[^4] His objection is not to the *content* of the premise but to the *epistemology*: a Catholic can hold the premise because he grounds it in tradition; a sola-scriptura Protestant cannot, because grounding it outside Scripture concedes a second infallible authority. See [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]].
