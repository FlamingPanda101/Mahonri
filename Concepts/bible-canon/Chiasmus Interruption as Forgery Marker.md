# Chiasmus Interruption as Forgery Marker

## The Claim
[[Cardon Ellis]] argues that the literary integrity of chiasmus is a recognized tool in textual criticism: when scholars do **historical manuscript analysis (HMD)** and a long chiasm is suddenly broken by a verse or two that "don't look like the same author" and "interrupt a kayazm," scholars often flag those as **scribal insertions / interpolations**. He cites "Bartman" (likely a garbled reference to a critical scholar) as someone who "cites the interruption of a kayasmus as a reason why he thinks verses could be forged." The point: since the field *uses* chiasmus to detect later tampering, a well-formed chiasm conversely supports authentic, single-author composition [^1][^2].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Real critical practice.** It is genuinely true that text critics and source critics use disruptions in poetic/parallel structure as one signal (among many) of interpolation or redaction — Cardon's general description of the method is broadly accurate [^2]. *Rating: the methodological premise is sound; the application to vindicate the Book of Mormon is where it strains.*
2. **Symmetry as design.** If interruption signals a later hand, then intact, dense chiasm (as Jonah claims for [[Alma 24.12-13|Alma 24]]) plausibly signals an undisturbed original [^1].
3. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) The argument is **selectively deployed**: scholars use chiasmus-interruption alongside vocabulary, theology, and manuscript evidence — never as a standalone proof — and they apply it to documents with manuscript traditions, which the Book of Mormon's gold plates lack. (b) Detecting an interpolation in an *otherwise* well-attested chiasm is not the same as proving a freshly composed English text is ancient. (c) Cardon's source ("Bartman") is unverified and possibly a misremembering, weakening the citation. (d) Even granting the tool, it says nothing about whether the underlying [[Concentric Chiasmus in Alma 24|Alma 24 chiasms]] are statistically real rather than imposed. (e) Cardon delivers this amid the episode's self-mocking "PhDs as airbags" riff, signaling the hosts hold credentialed methods at arm's length even as they borrow one [^3].

## Best Quotes
> "Bartman, for example, oftentimes cites the interruption of a kayasmus as a reason why he thinks verses could be forged." [^1]

> "If they notice there's like a 17-verse kayazm and then all of a sudden there's these two verses that don't look like they're the same author and they interrupt a kayazm... they'll think there's a good chance that this is a scribal insertion." [^2]
