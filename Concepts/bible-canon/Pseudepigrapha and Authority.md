# Pseudepigrapha and Authority
#type/claim

## The Claim
Pseudepigraphy — attributing a writing to a "towering figure" (Enoch, Moses, Peter) who very clearly did not write it — does not by itself settle a text's authority or disqualify it. Pseudonymous attribution was a recognized literary convention in antiquity, and several books inside the biblical canon are themselves regarded by critical scholars as pseudepigraphal. So the fact that 1 Enoch is a "false writing" cannot be the lone reason to reject it, just as a book's pseudonymity is not the lone reason to accept it: authority is decided by reception and use, not merely by authorship label.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Definition and irony.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], pseudepigrapha "means false writing... something attributed to some towering figure from Jewish or Christian history that was very clearly not written by that person," and he immediately notes "plenty of Bible books that are part of the canon are pseudepigrapha as well" (e.g., disputed Pauline letters, 2 Peter on critical readings). Developed in [[Book of Enoch - Pseudepigrapha and Late Composition]].
- **Authority tracks reception, not the byline.** 1 Enoch was quoted as prophecy by Jude ([[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude]]) and used heavily at Qumran, yet was later excluded from the Western canon — showing that communities granted or withdrew authority based on use and theological fit rather than on whether the named author truly wrote it (see [[Book of Enoch - Exclusion from the Canon]]).
- **Why this matters apologetically.** The point cuts both ways: a critic cannot dismiss 1 Enoch *solely* because it is pseudepigraphal without applying the same standard to canonical books; and a defender cannot canonize it *merely* because Jude cited it. The argument relocates the canon question from authorship to the community's reception history.
- **Steelman.** A confessional reader will resist the parallel, arguing that the canonical books' traditional attributions are defensible and that the church's recognition of inspiration is exactly the relevant authority-conferring act — so pseudepigraphy in 1 Enoch and (alleged) pseudepigraphy in, say, the Pastorals are not symmetrical cases. The mediating claim stands: pseudonymity alone is not a decisive verdict on authority in either direction; it must be weighed alongside reception, content, and canonical recognition.
