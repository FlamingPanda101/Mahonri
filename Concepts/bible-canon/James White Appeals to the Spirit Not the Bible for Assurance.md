# James White Appeals to the Spirit Not the Bible for Assurance

## The Point
Carroll's sharpest illustration that even committed defenders of Sola Scriptura treat the Spirit as more ultimate than the Bible: in a 2013 debate with Michael Brown, the Reformed apologist [[James White]], asked how sure he was of his own election and salvation, appealed **not to the Bible but to the testimony of the Holy Spirit to his heart**. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **White's answer.** Asked if he could be certain of his salvation: "in the sense that the Holy Spirit testifies to me of my sonship, as certain as a person CAN be at that point given our human limitations, yes." Pressed again, he said his certainty is "as far as the Holy Spirit testifies to my heart." [^1]
2. **The observation.** "Did you notice James did not cite to the Bible when asked about the truth of his election and salvation. He cited the Spirit testifying to His heart." On the most personal and important question — *am I saved?* — the Bible cannot answer for the individual; the Spirit does. [^2]
3. **The implication.** If the Spirit's witness is what ultimately assures even a Sola Scriptura champion, then the Spirit is "more essential and authoritative than the Bible itself" — which is itself a contradiction of Sola Scriptura, and exactly the Latter-day Saint position ([[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]). [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "I would take infallible knowledge as a divine category. So, in the sense that the Holy Spirit testifies to me of my sonship... yes." — James White (quoted by Carroll) [^1]

## LDS Connection
A turnabout argument: rather than attacking the Spirit's witness as subjective, Carroll shows the Evangelical's own assurance rests on it. This grants the Latter-day Saint reliance on personal revelation a respectability the critic implicitly shares, and supports the broader thesis that God — by His Spirit — not the canon, is the ultimate authority.
