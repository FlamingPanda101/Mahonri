# The P and E Sources Say Yahweh's Name Was Unknown Before Moses

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] presents the **divine-name criterion** — one of the oldest and strongest source-critical observations. In two strands of the Pentateuch, God reveals the name **Yahweh** to [[Moses]] as something *no one had known before*: [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]] (E) and especially [[Exodus 6.3|Exodus 6:3]] (P), where God says he appeared to the patriarchs as *El Shaddai* "but by my name YHWH was I not known to them." Yet in the J/Yahwist material the name is used freely from [[Genesis 2.4-7|Genesis 2]] onward — including on the lips of characters long before Moses. The same text cannot consistently hold both that the name was unknown before Moses and that it was in common use in Genesis; the tension is a fingerprint of distinct sources.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The two "name revelation" texts (rated: strong; textual).** Day: "we don't get the divine name till Exodus 6" in the Priestly account, and Exodus 3 introduces it in the E material — "Nobody knew my name by Yahweh. Like that's literally what Exodus 3 says... 'No one has known my name. And now, Moses, I'm telling you.'"[^1]

2. **J contradicts this from Genesis 2 (rated: strong).** "The problem with that is if you just pick up Genesis, his name's all through the place, all over. And so scholars say that that's got to be the Yahwist." In J, *Yahweh* (rendered "the LORD" / "Lord God," *Yahweh Elohim*) is doing creation work from [[Genesis 2.4-7|Genesis 2:4b]], and the name is even used in narration and dialogue across Genesis (e.g., Genesis 4:26 has people begin to "call upon the name of the LORD").[^2][^3]

3. **It coheres with the broader name-distribution pattern (rated: mainstream within the field).** The divine-name alternation (Elohim vs. YHWH) was the seed observation of the whole hypothesis from Astruc and Eichhorn onward, dovetailing with [[Two Independent Creation Accounts in Genesis|the Genesis 1 / Genesis 2 split]] (Elohim in P's Genesis 1; *Yahweh Elohim* in J's Genesis 2) that the vault already documents. Day folds this into [[Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch|the cluster of internal "tells"]].

4. **LDS resonance.** Day notes the E portrait of God — remote, communicating "via dreams and angels" — and the J portrait — anthropomorphic, walking and smelling sacrifices — both find echoes in the Book of Mormon (Lehi's and Nephi's dream-and-angel revelations; an embodied, personal God), without his claiming this resolves the source question.[^4]

**Steelman of the harmonizer.** The classic conservative reading (e.g., U. Cassuto, and many evangelical commentators) takes Exodus 6:3 not as "the *name* was unknown" but as "I was not *known* (experientially, in the character the name signifies) to the patriarchs" — i.e., the patriarchs knew the syllables but did not yet experience the covenant-keeping reality the name denotes. On this reading, Genesis' use of YHWH is the narrator's retrospective convention, not a claim the patriarchs pronounced it, so there is no contradiction. Day's reply is that this is precisely the kind of after-the-fact smoothing the redactor's seams invite, and that the *simplest* account of why one strand withholds the name until Exodus while another uses it from Genesis is multiple sources — but he characteristically declines to force the verdict ("these are some of the things that scholars kind of grapple with").[^5] [[Umberto Cassuto]] is the named representative of the "let the doublets sit / single-author" resistance in this lecture.
