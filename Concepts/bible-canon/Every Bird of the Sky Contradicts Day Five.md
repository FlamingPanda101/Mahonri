#type/claim

# Every Bird of the Sky Contradicts Day Five

## The Claim

Genesis 2:19 states that Yahweh God formed "every animal of the field, and every bird of the sky" (*kol of ha-shamayim*) *after* creating the human, planting the garden, and placing the human in it. But Genesis 1 has the birds created on **day five** (Genesis 1:20–23), *before* the human (day six). Therefore the order of creation in Genesis 2 contradicts Genesis 1, and the "zoom-in" harmonization fails.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The *shamayim* argument defeats the localization move (decisive).** Even if one grants the apologist's restricted reading of *eretz* ("land of Eden"), McClellan argues "*shamayim* clearly doesn't mean that." "Every bird of the sky... is not limited to a specific territory or subsection of the land."[^2] The sky cannot be zoomed in on Eden, so the birds of Genesis 2:19 are the same global category as the birds of day five.

2. **Sequence, not just inventory, is the problem.** "According to Genesis 2:19, every bird of the sky was not created until after the creation of the human, the planting of the Garden of Eden, and the placing of the human in the Garden of Eden."[^3] This directly inverts the day-five-before-day-six order of Genesis 1.

3. **Translations alter the verb to hide it (strong).** ESV, LSB, and NIV render the *wayyiqtol* of 2:19 as a pluperfect — "the Lord God *had formed*" — to place the animal creation before the events of Genesis 2. McClellan calls this an "arbitrary" change, because the *wayyiqtol* is not how Biblical Hebrew forms a pluperfect; it only reads that way "when close literary context demands it," and 2:19 has no such context. See [[Wayyiqtol Cannot Be Pluperfect Without Close Literary Context]].[^4]

4. **Rate the evidence: very strong.** The argument combines a semantic point (*shamayim* is non-localizable), a narrative-order point, and a syntactic point (the pluperfect rescue is grammatically unjustified). Each is independently defensible; together they make the contradiction hard to dissolve.

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Defenders argue Hebrew *wayyiqtol* genuinely can be epexegetical/pluperfect (citing cases like Genesis 2:8 "had planted"), so 2:19 simply recapitulates earlier creation, not a new act. McClellan's counter is that every academic case for the pluperfect reading here *presupposes* Genesis 1 as the "close literary context" — which is circular, since that is the very univocality being disputed. See [[Wayyiqtol Cannot Be Pluperfect Without Close Literary Context]].
