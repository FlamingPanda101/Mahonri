# The KJV Rests on the Inferior Textus Receptus

## The Claim

The King James Version was translated from an inferior Greek New Testament — the **Textus Receptus**, first assembled by [[Erasmus]] in the early 16th century — and the KJV used Erasmus' *third edition* to revise the Bishops' Bible. Modern textual criticism, drawing on far more, far earlier, and far more reliable manuscripts, reconstructs a more accurate source text. [[Dan McClellan]] gives this as the second of three reasons the KJV obscures the authors' meaning. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Textus Receptus was great 16th-century work but is now superseded — rate: strong

McClellan: the Textus Receptus, "while a phenomenal work of scholarship in the 16th century, is out of date. We have many more manuscripts, far more reliable manuscripts, far earlier manuscripts, and a far more refined art of textual criticism that has allowed us to cobble together a much more reliable source text." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — mainstream text-critical consensus; the Textus Receptus rested on a handful of late medieval manuscripts.

### 2. ~16 KJV passages are absent from the earliest manuscripts — rate: strong

"There are about 16 or so passages that were in the King James Version that were not in the earliest manuscripts to which we have access." Many modern translations omit them — "which freaks a lot of people out, but is actually a more accurate, more reliable translation." The textbook case is the *pericope adulterae*, [[John 7.53-8.11|John 7:53-8:11]] (cross-listed at [[Pericope Adulterae Is a Later Interpolation]]). [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the longer ending of Mark, the Comma Johanneum, and the pericope adulterae are standard examples.

### 3. The manuscript base changes the meaning, not just the wording — rate: strong

In [[Jude 1.22|Jude 22]] the critical text shows the participle the KJV translates is **accusative**, not nominative. So the verse is not telling you *how* to have compassion but *on whom* — "on those who are trying to make a distinction, on those who are doubting." The choice of text yields a different sense, not merely different spelling. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a concrete case where text-critical choice is exegetically load-bearing.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The KJV-only "providential preservation" argument holds that God preserved his word in the majority (Byzantine) tradition the church actually used, so the abundance of Byzantine manuscripts behind the Textus Receptus is evidence of divine guardianship, and the older Alexandrian witnesses are "corrupt." McClellan's reply (developed at [[KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text]]): this is a *theological* preference dressed as a text-critical conclusion — it cannot be established on neutral evidentiary grounds, the Alexandrian witnesses are demonstrably older, and moralizing manuscript families ("dirty Alexandrians") is polemic, not philology. KJV-onlyists nevertheless prove his larger point: the manuscript base is a *choice* with no neutral arbiter (see [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]]).

## Best Quotes

> "It is based on a traditional Greek New Testament cobbled together beginning with Desiderius Erasmus at the beginning of the 16th century. This tradition has become known as the Textus Receptus." [^1]

> "We now know that there are about 16 or so passages that were in the King James Version that were not in the earliest manuscripts to which we have access." [^2]

> "The participle that is being translated 'making a difference' is not in the nominative. It should be in the accusative. So it's not just telling you how you're supposed to have compassion. It's telling you on whom." [^3]
