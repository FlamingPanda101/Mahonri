# Scripture as a Spectrum Not a Binary

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] offers a **three-category taxonomy of biblical inspiration** that he poses to his BYU students, arguing that "scripture is a spectrum… a range," not an all-or-nothing binary. A text can be Scripture even if it is not a verbatim, capital-R Revelation — and even, in the extreme, if its human author never intended it to be canonized. This reframing is what lets a believer accept that [[Genesis Was Finalized as Davidic-Solomonic Apologetic|Genesis was humanly edited as Davidic apologetic]] without concluding it is "not scripture."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The three options.** Biblical texts may be: **(1)** *capital-R revelations* — words "completely revealed by God," like many Doctrine & Covenants sections (entirely divine); **(2)** *inspired explanations of revelation* — human retellings of what someone (Moses, David) experienced (part divine, part human); or **(3)** *unrevealed cultural explanations* — a people's own human account of the world's beginnings and of God's dealings (entirely human). Hatch argues any of the three "could be considered scripture," because God can say "you keep records in your human way and then I'll deal with it in the aftermath — I'll inspire you when you read it."[^1][^3]

2. **The canon is already this messy.** Hatch points to texts inside the LDS canon that are plainly *low-R*: D&C 135 (the eulogy of Joseph Smith, attributed to John Taylor but uncertain) sits beside D&C 76 and 93; the Articles of Faith began as 1838 propaganda in the Wentworth-type letter and were canonized only in 1880; the Pearl of Great Price gathers miscellaneous texts the brethren later judged useful. So "spectrum" is a description of the existing canon, not a concession.[^4][^5]

3. **Authorial intent does not gate scripture.** The hosts press the radical edge: Paul writing a letter to solve a local conflict (and venting "personal anger," e.g. Galatians 5) almost certainly did not think it would sit "next to Isaiah" as equal authority; the author of Matthew may not have thought he was writing on par with Zechariah. Hatch's framework absorbs this — "can something be scripture if the person it came through never intended it to be scripture?" Yes, if it functions as scripture for the community.[^6][^7]

4. **The functional definition.** Hatch's "closest approximation": *Scripture is writing that can be meditated on to bring a person into relationship with God so they can have a personal experience with the divine.* On this view *Jesus the Christ* is "soft-canonized" (approved missionary reading, full of dated scholarship yet spiritually potent), and even a hypothetical "inspired fiction" Book of Mormon could still be scripture **except** that the Book of Mormon also makes historical claims about its *own coming forth* that cannot be divorced from it — so for Hatch its case is narrower than Genesis's. This connects to the vault's principle that [[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin|editing is compatible with divine origin]].[^8][^9]

5. **The temple supplies the answer.** Hatch and Dave Butler ground it liturgically: "the word of God is met at the veil," and the Davidic-kingship motif is worth preserving "even if it was read back into history… because we participate in a Davidic kingship ritual." Scripture's purpose is to give "a map and symbols" for encountering the Lord — echoing the Catholic answer that scripture exists "to preserve the liturgy."[^10][^11]

**Steelman of the skeptic / traditionalist.** (a) An inerrantist will object that a functional, spectrum definition **dissolves the category of canon** — "the idea of canon kind of disappears," as a host cheerfully admits — leaving no principled line between Isaiah and *The Alchemist* (which Hatch calls "personal scripture"). The vault's canon cluster presses exactly this worry from multiple angles (e.g. [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]], [[The Word of God Fallacy - The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven]]). (b) Reducing scripture to "whatever brings you closer to God" risks collapsing revelation into reader-response. (c) Hatch repeatedly stresses he only *poses* the taxonomy to students "to sit with," not as settled doctrine — it is a pedagogical heuristic, not a defended epistemology. (d) The "inspired fiction" concession is precisely what many LDS apologists resist for the Book of Mormon, so Hatch's own carve-out (historical self-claims) is doing heavy and contestable work.

## Best Quotes

> "I think any [of those] three could be considered scripture… because God could say, 'you guys write it down, you keep records in your human way, and then I'll deal with it in the aftermath. I'll inspire you when you read it.'"[^3]

> "We don't have to be so rigid and tight and binary in our thinking — black and white. Scripture is a spectrum. It's a range."[^2]

> "Scripture is writings that can be meditated on to help you come into relationship with God so that you can have an experience with the divine personally. That's the closest approximation."[^8]
