# The 2019 Pew Biblical Literacy Plummet Is a Survey Artifact

#type/claim

## The Claim

When Latter-day Saints note that surveys rank them at or near the **top** of U.S. religious groups in
biblical/religious-knowledge tests, critics reply that a later survey shows their score "plummeted"
~25 points. [[Jonah Barnes]] argues this apparent collapse is a **methodological artifact** of changes
between the 2010 and 2019 Pew religious-knowledge surveys, not a real decline — and that on the
*biblical* questions specifically, Latter-day Saints still place second (within the margin of error of
Evangelicals), ahead of Catholics.[^1] This is the *synchronic snapshot* counterpart to the vault's
[[LDS Biblical Literacy Declined as the Church Domesticated|diachronic decline thesis]]; the two are
compatible (members may out-rank peers *today* even if 20th-century catechesis thinned over time).

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "plummet" mixes two different buckets — rate: strong (reading the cross-tabs)
The headline drop conflates the **Bible-knowledge** subscore with the broader **world-religions**
subscore. The 2010 number critics cite was the Bible bucket (LDS #1); the 2019 "drop" lumps in all 32
questions including world-religions items. On the *biblical* questions alone in 2019, LDS score ~5.1/7
— **second**, behind Evangelicals (5.2) and within the margin of error — not a collapse.[^2]

### 2. The questions skew toward non-LDS vocabulary — rate: moderate (item bias)
Several knowledge items turn on terms central to *other* traditions' catechesis but rare in LDS
discourse: **Trinity (homoousios)**, **Purgatory**, **sola fide**, **Prosperity Gospel**. A faithful
Latter-day Saint may simply never encounter "Prosperity Gospel" from the pulpit, so missing it
measures unfamiliarity with Evangelical jargon, not biblical illiteracy.[^3]

### 3. The sampling method changed between waves — rate: moderate (methodology)
- **Mode change:** 2010 was a *random phone survey* (the half-century standard); 2019 used the
  opt-in **Ipsos KnowledgePanel** — a different population of people who *choose* to be surveyed.[^4]
- **Oversampling + self-selection:** Because random sampling could not yield enough Latter-day Saints,
  2019 oversampled by actively seeking self-identified "Mormons," inviting self-selection bias
  (including non-members or critics self-identifying).[^5]
- **The "Mormon" label shift:** Between 2010 and 2019 the Church publicly de-emphasized the term
  "Mormon," so a 2019 instrument hunting for "Mormons" captures a skewed subset of the membership.[^6]
- **Largest margin of error:** Barnes reports the LDS subsample carried the **highest margin of error**
  of any group (he cites ~9-point figures) — "most studies are less than two" — making the LDS number
  the least stable in the set.[^7]
- **Birthday-method age skew:** The panel selected the household member with the *next birthday*; large
  LDS families skew that person younger, and younger respondents tend to score lower.[^8]

### 4. One outlier item dragged the score — rate: moderate (item analysis)
The lowest-scoring question asked which woman "escaped with her Jewish family from destruction"
(answer: Esther). LDS respondents, primed by their larger canon, plausibly mis-answered "Sarah"
(escaping Jerusalem) — a *side effect of knowing more scriptural narratives*, not of biblical
ignorance.[^9]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Pew is a serious pollster**, and survey-mode changes over a decade are defensible, not a conspiracy;
  a critic can fairly say Barnes is *motivated* to explain away an unflattering number.
- **A high margin of error cuts both ways** — it means the true LDS score is *uncertain*, not
  necessarily *higher*; "we're actually still #2" also lives inside that uncertainty.
- **Item bias is partly the point:** if Latter-day Saints don't know what the Trinity or Purgatory
  *is*, that is itself a (contestable) form of religious illiteracy, even if not *biblical* illiteracy.
- **None of this is peer-reviewed**; it is a podcaster reading cross-tabs on air.

**What survives:** the strong, checkable core — that the "25-point plummet" compares a **Bible-only**
figure to an **all-topics** figure, and that the 2019 wave changed *mode, sampling, and the target
label* — means the headline "Mormons forgot the Bible" is not supported. On the biblical items alone,
LDS remained near the top. The item-bias and age-skew points are plausible but softer.
