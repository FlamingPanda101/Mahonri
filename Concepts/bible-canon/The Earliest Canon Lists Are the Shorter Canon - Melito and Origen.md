# The Earliest Canon Lists Are the Shorter Canon - Melito and Origen

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]]'s "most important" section: the *earliest* surviving Christian Old Testament canon lists tilt toward the **shorter, Hebrew-aligned canon**, not the wider deuterocanonical one. Eusebius preserves the 2nd-century list of [[Melito of Sardis]] — "perhaps the earliest" canon list we possess — which excludes most of the deuterocanon; Eusebius also reports [[Origen]]'s 22-book canon; and the 2nd-century **Bryennios list** (discovered in the 19th century) agrees. Ortlund's thesis is that what ultimately has authority "is what goes back to Jesus and the apostles," and the data point toward the shorter list being "the earliest, closest to the apostles." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the reply
The early lists are fragile and ambiguous. (a) **Melito's list is uncertain at the edges**: Esther is *missing* (possibly an accidental omission), and his entry "Wisdom" is debated — an older view (cited from Archibald Alexander) reads it as just another name for *Proverbs*, but this is "disputed today and... unclear." (b) Origen's enumeration has its own wrinkles, and some argue he *included* Maccabees. (c) Critics say Melito was merely **reporting the Jewish canon**, not advocating a Christian one. (d) A handful of 2nd-century lists can't bear the weight of "what the apostles held" when the broader church plainly used the Septuagint, which carried the wider books. So the earliest evidence is thin and cuts both ways.

### Ortlund's reply
- **Melito framed it as Christian.** He introduces the list as "concerning our entire faith" and calls them "the books of the **Old Testament**" — a term Jews would never use of their own scriptures. So this is a *Christian* Old Testament canon, not a bare report of the synagogue's. [^2]
- **Melito's pedigree is apostolic-adjacent.** If Eusebius is right, Melito is reckoned among the Apostolic Fathers, thought to have known Polycarp and Ignatius (disciples of John), and he *traveled to Palestine specifically to research the Jewish canon* (per Philip Schaff). [[Jerome]] himself cites Melito. This is a careful, early, well-placed witness. [^3]
- **Origen's rationale is tradition.** Eusebius identifies Origen with the 22-book Hebrew canon, and the stated rationale is *tradition* — "another very interesting early appeal to tradition," not merely a report of Jewish practice. [^4]
- **The Bryennios list corroborates.** Many scholars date the Bryennios list to the 2nd century; it too "corresponds very closely with the Jewish and Protestant canon," and Ortlund commends the study of it by Ed Gallagher and John Meade. Multiple independent early witnesses converge. [^5]
- **The argument is cumulative, not about heroes.** Ortlund stresses Protestants are not motivated by "individual figures... in isolation" but by what they reveal about "the earliest canon list that goes back to Jesus and the apostles." The shorter canon is the *best inference to the apostolic original.* [^1]

## LDS Relevance
This is the sharpest point of *contrast* with the LDS open-canon view. Ortlund's entire payoff is an **apostolic-origins** argument: the canon that is *earliest* and *closest to the apostles* is normative, and that canon is the closed shorter list. The LDS position inverts the logic — authority flows not from recovering a fixed first-century list but from *living* apostolic/prophetic authority that can keep adding scripture. Ortlund's appeal to Melito and Origen also leans on the same "proximity to the apostles" criterion the restored church invokes for the early period (see [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles]]), but he uses it to *close* the canon at the apostolic deposit, whereas the LDS use of early-church plurality aims to show the deposit was never sealed. Against Catholic/Orthodox *and* LDS claims of a settled wider canon, Ortlund's earliest-evidence argument is a genuine ally; as a positive case it terminates in a closed Bible the open-canon view rejects.

## Best Quotes
> "Melito introduces this canon list as concerning our entire faith, and he calls them the books of the Old Testament, which Jews would never call their scriptures. This is a Christian Old Testament." [^2]

> "What Protestants are motivated by... is not merely the testimony of individual figures like Jerome or Athanasius or Cyril or even Melito in isolation, but what that reveals about... the earliest canon list that goes back to Jesus and the apostles. That is what ultimately has authority for us." [^1]
