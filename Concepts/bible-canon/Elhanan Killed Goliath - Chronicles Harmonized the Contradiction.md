# Elhanan Killed Goliath - Chronicles Harmonized the Contradiction

## The Claim

In the earliest preserved tradition it is [[Elhanan]] the Bethlehemite, not [[David]], who kills Goliath. [[2 Samuel 21.19|2 Samuel 21:19]] states plainly that "Elhanan the son of Jaare-oregim, the Bethlehemite, slew Goliath the Gittite, the shaft of whose spear was like a weaver's beam." The famous David-and-Goliath narrative of [[1 Samuel]] 17 is a later retelling that transferred Elhanan's victory onto David. Centuries later the Chronicler — or the source text he relied on — altered the parallel verse at [[1 Chronicles 20.5|1 Chronicles 20:5]] so that Elhanan killed "Lahmi the **brother** of Goliath," a scribal **harmonization** that conceals the contradiction. The KJV preserves this "Lahmi the brother of" reading; it is not original but a later fix. This is, per McClellan, "the overwhelming academic consensus."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "weaver's beam" tag proves the same Goliath is meant
2 Samuel 21:19 reuses the exact description of Goliath's spear from 1 Samuel 17:7 ("like a weaver's beam"). It is therefore not a different giant who happens to share the name — the text is unmistakably pointing to the very Goliath David is famed for killing.[^2]

### 2. The change only works in the post-exilic Aramaic square script
The Hebrew of the two verses is nearly identical. In 2 Samuel, *bêt-hallaḥmî* ("the Bethlehemite") is followed by the direct-object marker *ʾet* (marking who is killed: Goliath). In Chronicles, the *bêt* is reread, the object marker is shifted to the front as a new proper name *Laḥmi*, and the final *ʾet* becomes the visually similar *ʾaḥî* ("brother of"). Crucially, these letters resemble each other **only in the Aramaic square script** adopted by Judahites during the Babylonian exile — not in the older paleo-Hebrew script. The confusion therefore cannot predate the exile, which dates it to the editing of Chronicles, not to 2 Samuel.[^3]

### 3. "Lahmi" is not a credible Philistine name
The invented victim "Lahmi" would mean something like "bread" — a naming convention unattested anywhere in that time and place. Worse, it would be a **Semitic** name, but a brother of Goliath would be a Philistine and, in this early period of Philistine history, would almost certainly bear a Philistine name. The name betrays its origin as a textual artifact, not a real person.[^4]

### 4. The harmonizing motive is visible to the commentators themselves
McClellan marshals a long line of scholarship, from 1899 to the present, agreeing that Chronicles is the corrupted text and 2 Samuel the more original:[^5]
- **Henry Preserved Smith** (1899, ICC): "the harmonistic purpose of the chronicler in making the victim the brother of Goliath is evident."
- **Hugh Williamson** and **Roddy Braun** (1980s, Chronicles commentaries): the Chronicler "deliberately altered his Vorlage in order to effect a harmonization," though Williamson notes the Samuel text is itself textually rough (*oregim* / "weavers" dittographed into the father's name).
- **Sara Japhet** (Chronicles commentary): the alteration "severs the bonds between the stories" — David killed Goliath, Elhanan killed Lahmi Goliath's brother — and may even have looked like an accidental corruption; it need not be the Chronicler's own hand and could have entered the *Vorlage*.
- **Ralph Klein** (Hermeneia), **Graeme Auld**, and **A. A. Anderson** (2 Samuel, WBC) concur that a victory over a Philistine giant was "reattributed to David as his legend developed."
- **Marvin Sweeney** ([[Marvin Sweeney]]): of the two solutions — that David's birth name was Elhanan, or that David took credit for a warrior's victory — "the second is the one that is the overwhelming academic consensus."
- **Joel Baden**, *The Historical David*: "the Goliath of 1 Samuel 17 is a secondhand creation and the entire narrative is a literary exercise in Davidic glorification."

### 5. The text-critical principle confirms the direction of change
By *lectio difficilior potior* (the harder reading is usually earlier), 2 Samuel's flat contradiction with the David story is the difficult reading scribes would tend to smooth, making it the likely earlier text. See [[Lectio Difficilior - The Harder Reading Is Usually Earlier]].

### Steelman of the opposing view
Two traditional harmonizations exist: (a) **"Elhanan" was David's birth name** (so the same man is meant) — the dominant rabbinic/Targumic move, which treats David and Elhanan as two appellatives for one person; and (b) **David really did kill *a* Goliath while Elhanan killed the brother**, with Chronicles simply preserving accurate detail. Japhet and Sweeney both note option (a) is the more common traditional reading. The critical reply is that (a) is ad hoc — there is no independent evidence David was ever called Elhanan, and it exists only to dissolve the contradiction — while (b) depends on the Chronicles reading whose secondary, scribal character the consonantal and onomastic evidence (points 2–3) exposes. The harmonization is thus the *effect* of a perceived contradiction, which is itself evidence that the contradiction is real.

## LDS Relevance

Article of Faith 8 — "we believe the Bible to be the word of God **as far as it is translated correctly**" — anticipates exactly this kind of finding. The Goliath case is a textbook instance of a transmission error (here, a deliberate scribal harmonization) entering the received text, so a Latter-day Saint can affirm the verse without being committed to its inerrancy. More pointedly, the episode is a clean illustration of the danger the LDS tradition critiques in creedal inerrancy: when a community treats **harmonization as a dogmatic obligation** rather than a conclusion, it will alter the text to protect a prior commitment (see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]). The Restoration's openness to an imperfectly transmitted Bible — and to the [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal|Bible as a library of differing voices]] — handles a contradiction like this without the strain that strict inerrancy requires.
