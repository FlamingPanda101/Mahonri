# McConkie's Unpublished Pearl of Great Price

#type/claim

## The Claim
In the 1970s, apostle [[Bruce R. McConkie]] — serving on the scripture committee that produced today's LDS standard works (alongside Boyd K. Packer, Thomas S. Monson, and others) — compiled an actual physical *mock-up* of a radically expanded Pearl of Great Price and presented it to the First Presidency and the Twelve. It proposed canonizing a large body of material that had either been decanonized or never canonized. The proposal was **rejected**. [[Stephen Smoot|Steven Smoot]] owns a privately printed reproduction (one of ~20 copies a vanity publisher made) and says he verified its provenance with the Church History and Scripture Departments — calling it "Bruce R. McConkie's fantasy football roster of what he wants in the Pearl of Great Price." It is the episode's concrete proof that even an apostle's proposed scripture can be turned down.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A fully realized mock-up, not a memo.** McConkie produced "an actual physical mock-up for presentation... versification, chapters, pagination, all of that standardized." It was never published by the Church; Smoot obtained a privately reproduced copy by accident at an estate sale [^1][^2].

2. **A massively expanded Book of Moses.** McConkie wanted the Book of Moses extended from 8 to **13 chapters**, adding JST-Genesis material: Melchizedek's blessing of Abraham (JST Genesis 14, 15, 17), Jacob's blessings to his sons (JST Genesis 48, 50) prophesying the seed of Joseph — i.e., JST-Genesis "all the way to the end of the patriarchs" [^3][^4].

3. **New books and a flood of JST Gospels.** He proposed JST Psalms (11, 12, 14, 24) packaged as a "**Book of David**" (David 1–4), plus a large block of JST Gospels (Matthew 2, 3, 5, 7, 21, 23; Luke 3, 12, 17; John 1). Smoot: "it almost sounds like he wanted to canonize the JST" — much of which was instead "soft canonized" in the modern LDS Bible's JST appendix and footnotes [^5][^6].

4. **The whole Wentworth letter — and a second First Vision account.** McConkie wanted the *entire* Wentworth letter canonized (not just the Articles of Faith extracted from it), which "would have meant two canonical First Vision accounts." Smoot raises the striking hypothetical: would the modern "faith crisis" over differing First Vision accounts have happened at all if members had grown up with two of them in their scriptures? He notes [[BH Roberts]] had likewise prized the Wentworth letter decades earlier [^7][^8].

5. **Re-canonizing the Lectures on Faith.** McConkie sought to restore the **Lectures on Faith** (mostly Sidney Rigdon, Kirtland-era; canonized in the 1835 D&C, *decanonized* in 1920 because they were theological lectures rather than revelatory pronouncements) "back in through the back door with the Pearl of Great Price." McConkie cited them constantly in *Mormon Doctrine* and his commentaries [^9][^10].

6. **Two new Articles of Faith he wrote himself.** McConkie authored proposed **Articles of Faith 14 and 15** — #14 on the restored fullness of the gospel being preached to all the world before the end, and #15 essentially the plan of salvation (premortal life, eternal marriage, salvation for the dead, resurrection, eternal judgment, kingdoms of glory). Smoot poses to his students whether these fit the Articles' original purpose (a brief outsider summary for newspaper editor John Wentworth) or are "a little insider baseball" [^11][^12].

## Best Quotes
> "This is a Pearl of Great Price that was never published, but it was compiled by a fellow named Bruce R. McConkie." [^1]

> "Only Bruce R. McConkie could get away with wanting to add two Articles of Faith to Joseph Smith's Articles of Faith." [^12]

> "In some parallel universe out there where this did get canonized, it'd be interesting to go there and see what that looks like." [^8]

## LDS Connection
The artifact is Smoot's centerpiece illustration that canonization is genuinely deliberative and can reject even apostolic proposals — feeding directly into [[Canonization Is a Community Process Not a Unilateral Prophetic Act]] and [[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug]]. It parallels McConkie's separately documented (and likewise unrealized) wish to canonize the King Follett Discourse; see [[King Follett Need Not Be Canonized]].
