# Conscience Is the Protestants True Ultimate Authority

## The Claim
[[Robert Boland]] argues that under *sola scriptura* the *functional* ultimate authority is not the Bible at all but the **individual believer's private conscience**. The reasoning: the Protestant is obligated to assent to a doctrine *if and only if* he judges it to be "biblical." To say a faculty operates "in an ultimate sense" just is to say one is *bound to submit to its judgments without further appeal*. Since the Protestant submits, in the last instance, to *his own conscience's verdict* on what scripture means, **the conscience — not the text — plays the ultimate normative role.** "There is no such thing as an appeal to a text; you always appeal to an *interpretation* of a text" (Keith Mathison). So *sola scriptura* collapses into *solo* conscience, and "private interpretation" is a constitutive element of the doctrine that, if it falls, takes the doctrine with it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Boland / LDS negative):**
- Boland grounds this in **Charles Hodge**, who states that Protestants deny "that Christ has appointed any officer or class of officers... to whose interpretation of scripture the people are bound to submit as of final authority," and affirm instead that God "has made it obligatory upon every man to search the scriptures for himself and to determine on his own discretion what they require him to believe and to do."[^2] On Boland's read, that is an explicit relocation of final authority to the individual.
- The corruption-by-the-fall caveat does not help: even *after* regeneration, Protestants privilege the regenerate conscience's reading — so the noetic effects of sin do not displace the conscience from the judgment seat.
- In cross-examination this surfaces concretely: when Boland asks who decides which *implicit* doctrines bind the conscience, Powers can only answer "each of us... read the scriptures for ourselves and make a god-given conscious decision" — which is precisely Boland's thesis.[^3]
- It connects to his [[The Canon-as-Article-of-Faith Dilemma]] (the believer's private "Berean test" can even discard a canonical book) and is the negative-side mirror of [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]. The LDS positive alternative is [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]].

**Steelman (Protestant affirmative):**
- Hodge distinguishes *ministerial* from *magisterial* authority: the conscience is the *instrument* of apprehension, but the *object* it is bound to is the God-breathed text, which exists and means what it means independent of the reader. The reader being the one who *grasps* the rule does not make the reader the *rule* — just as using one's eyes to read a map does not make the eyes the territory.
- Every position faces the same regress: the Catholic must use *private judgment* to decide the Magisterium is the authority, and the Latter-day Saint to decide the prophet is — so "you finally trust your own assessment" is not a defeater unique to Protestantism but a universal feature of finite knowers. (The vault's [[Debate - Sola Scriptura and the Canon]] notes this symmetric burden.)
- Powers' framing is that scripture is a *fixed external standard* precisely so the conscience is *not* sovereign — it can be corrected *by* the text; the Berean ideal ([[Acts 17.10-11|Acts 17]]) is submission of judgment to scripture, not exaltation of judgment over it.

## Best Quotes
> "His conscience plays the ultimate normative role... to say that some faculty functions in an ultimate sense is to say that one is bound or obligated to assent to the judgments of that faculty — here one's conscience — without any [higher] acceptance."[^1]

> [Hodge, quoted by Boland] "He has made it obligatory upon every man to search the scriptures for himself and to determine on his own discretion what they require him to believe and to do."[^2]

> [Mathison, paraphrased by Boland] "There's no such thing as an appeal to a text; you always appeal to an interpretation of a text."[^4]
