# The Deuterocanon Was Disputed Until Trent Not Removed

#type/claim

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection (from Catholic apologist Alex / Voice of Reason, against [[Wes Huff]]): the 66-book Protestant canon "cannot be found anywhere in the first 1500 years of church history," so Protestants *removed* books that the church had always received. [[Gavin Ortlund]]'s reply: the **status of the deuterocanon was genuinely disputed right up to the Council of Trent (1546)** — so "you can't remove books from a place they don't yet occupy." The framing of a settled wider canon being later "pruned" is historically false. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the objection
The Catholic argument has real force. (a) The dominant lived Bible of the Greek-speaking church and the Latin West was the **Septuagint / Vulgate**, both of which *contained* the deuterocanon, so in practice these books were bound, read, and preached as scripture for over a millennium. (b) Regional councils — **Hippo (393), Carthage (397), Florence (1442)** — listed the wider canon, and [[Augustine]], the most influential Western theologian, defended it. (c) Protestants *did* physically relocate these books to an "Apocrypha" appendix and eventually drop them, which from the pew looks exactly like removal. The objection captures something true about the *majority practice* of the church.

### Ortlund's reply
- **Catholic scholarship concedes the dispute.** Ortlund quotes the *Catholic Encyclopedia*: "in the Latin church all through the Middle Ages we find evidence of hesitation about the character of the deuterocanonical... a current friendly to them, another distinctly unfavorable to their authority and sacredness." Even [[Thomas Aquinas]] is among those who do not unequivocally affirm their canonicity. [^2]
- **No definitive list existed before Trent.** He cites Yves Congar: "an official, definitive list of inspired writings did not exist in the Catholic church until the Council of Trent." A canon you only *close* in 1546 was not a fixed deposit the Reformers vandalized. [^3]
- **Dissent ran to the eve of Trent — inside the Catholic hierarchy.** Cardinal **Cajetan** (a leading opponent of Luther) and Cardinal **Ximenes** excluded the deuterocanon and appealed to [[Jerome]], "even after the Council of Florence." The shorter canon was a *live Catholic option* in the 16th century, not a Protestant invention. [^4]
- **The logical point.** "You can't remove books from a place that they don't yet occupy, and the status of the deuterocanon was disputed all the way up into the 1500s." The books had no settled, universally-binding canonical status to be removed *from.* [^1]

## LDS Relevance
This rebuttal is squarely an *intra-Christian* fight (Protestant vs. Catholic) over Old Testament boundaries, and the LDS open-canon view sits outside it — but it supplies useful ammunition. The demonstrated fact that the "biblical canon" was *contested for 1500 years and only closed by a 16th-century council vote* (see [[The Council of Trent Canon Vote Was a Tiny Minority]]) undercuts any apologist who treats the present Bible as a seamless, divinely-sealed list handed down whole. That serves the restoration claim that canon formation was a human, historically-situated, and reversible process. The asymmetry to keep in view: Ortlund concludes the dispute is *settled by going back to the apostolic shorter canon*, a **closed** Bible; the LDS conclusion is that an open process implies an **open** canon. Both, however, agree against the "always-fixed Bible" narrative.
