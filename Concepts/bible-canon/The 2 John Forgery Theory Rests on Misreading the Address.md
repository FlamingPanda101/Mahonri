#type/claim

# The 2 John Forgery Theory Rests on Misreading the Address

## The Claim
A vein of modern scholarship held that 2 John could not share an author with [[3 John]]: 3 John is a normal personal letter, while 2 John's bizarre opening to a metaphorical "elect lady" / personified universal church looked like a later imitator copying 3 John's style — making 2 John effectively a pseudonymous (forged) composition by a second author. [[Lincoln H. Blumell]] argues this entire theory is an artifact of a two-letter scribal error: once *eklektē kyria* is correctly read as "to **the** lady *Eklekte*" (see [[Eklekte Is a Named Woman Not the Elect Lady of 2 John]]), 2 John's opening is a perfectly ordinary Roman-period personal letter with the *same* sender-then-named-recipient formula as 3 John, and the case for two authors evaporates. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The whole oddity was the address.** What flagged 2 John as anomalous — and fed both the "catholic letter to all churches" reading and the second-author theory — was that, unlike 3 John's "to Gaius," 2 John seemed addressed to no real person. Restore the dropped definite article and 2 John reads exactly like 3 John: elder → named addressee → article → title. The supposed stylistic gulf collapses. [^2]
2. **A misreading breeding a "wild theory."** Blumell frames the forgery hypothesis as a case study in how one corrupted reading metastasizes into elaborate scholarship: a real grammatical problem (created by haplography) gets "solved" by inventing a second author and a literary forger, when the actual fix is restoring two letters of Greek. He clarifies the claim was not that scholars wanted to *delete* 2 John, but that they denied common authorship with 3 John. [^3]
3. **The content fits a single author writing to a patroness.** Read as a letter to *Eklekte*, the house-church proprietor, 2 John's warning against receiving false teachers ("antichrist") "into the house" reads naturally and parallels 3 John's concern with hospitality and Diotrephes — two genuine letters from the same elder, not an original and a forged copy. [^4]

## Broader Lesson
Blumell generalizes: "accepted theory" in biblical scholarship is not the same as *true*, and a single new papyrus or a re-examined reading can force a paradigm shift — so even longstanding consensus deserves healthy skepticism grounded in the primary sources (cf. [[Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point]]). It is a mirror image of the [[Vocabulary Differences Signal Two Authors|vocabulary-based partition arguments]] used elsewhere: here the partition rests not on style but on a textual corruption. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "It shows for me in some ways how a misreading of a text can lead to... a wild theory... these two letters and say they're not written by the same person. And it all comes down largely to how you read the opening address." [^3]

> "People didn't actually want to get rid of it, but the argument was it could not have been written by the same author as 3 John... when it's just actually a simple Greek error of two letters that actually accounts for this." [^3]
