# High Christology Is Already Present in Mark Not a Late Development

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Bart Ehrman]] (and figures like Alex O'Connor) argue for an *evolving* Christology: Jesus is portrayed as merely a human messenger in the early Gospel (Mark) and only becomes fully divine by the latest Gospel (John, with its "I am" statements). The high divinity of Jesus is thus a late *development*, not original. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Michael Jones]] argues there is **no developing Christology** — a high Christology is already explicit in Mark; John is simply *more explicit* about the same claim, for a different (Gentile) audience. "This is the same thing Mark was saying... John is just going to be explicit about it as much as I possibly can." [^2]

1. **"Lord of the Sabbath" (Mark 2:28).** "The Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath" — but the Lord of the Sabbath is the one who *gave* it, namely Yahweh; to claim it "is to claim to be on par with the Father." *Rating: moderate-strong; a recognized high-Christology reading of Mark.* [^3]
2. **Mark opens by applying Yahweh's herald to Jesus.** Mark 1 cites the prophecy that one will "prepare the way for Yahweh" — and John the Baptist prepares the way for *Jesus*, identifying Jesus with the coming LORD. *Rating: strong; the Malachi/Isaiah citation is applied to Jesus.* [^3]
3. **The Son of Man on the clouds (Daniel 7).** At his trial Jesus claims to be the [[Daniel 7.13|Daniel 7]] Son of Man "coming riding on the clouds of heaven" — and in the OT the cloud-rider is Yahweh. *Rating: strong; this is the climactic divine claim in Mark 14:62.* [^4]
4. **"Lord Lord" in Luke/Matthew echoes *Adonai Yahweh*.** Jones (citing Jason Staples, Michael Patrick Barber, et al.): Jesus calls himself "Lord, Lord" ([[Luke 6.46|Luke 6:46]]; Matt 7:21-22), and "no one did that... in any Greek text from that time or prior" except as the Septuagint's rendering of *Adonai Yahweh* (e.g., Ezekiel's *kyrie kyrie*). So Jesus is "explicitly claiming to be God." *Rating: moderate; a developing scholarly argument, not yet consensus, but a serious case.* [^5]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The developmental view remains mainstream: the *explicitness* and *frequency* of divine claims demonstrably increase from Mark to John (the "I am" absolute statements, the pre-existence Logos, "before Abraham was, I am" appear only in John), and many scholars read Mark's Son-of-Man and Sabbath sayings as *messianic/agential* rather than straightforwardly *identifying Jesus as Yahweh*. Ehrman's point is not that Mark has *no* exalted Christology but that the *equation of Jesus with God* is clearest latest — which the texts arguably show.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: "more explicit" is not the same as "higher" — if the *substance* (Jesus as the divine Lord, cloud-rider, Sabbath-giver) is already in Mark, then John's clarity is a difference of presentation for a Gentile audience, not a new doctrine. He grants the synoptics are "subtle... to the untrained eye" but insists the divine claims are really there. Note the **LDS framing**: Latter-day Saints affirm Jesus's full divinity throughout the Gospels while *denying* the Nicene metaphysics later read onto these texts — so for Hansen the early-and-explicit divinity of Christ supports the Restoration's Christ without entailing the creedal Trinity. See [[The Enochic Son of Man Matches LDS Christology Not Nicene Homoousios]].

## Best Quotes
> "I don't know if it's a developed christology. I just think by the time you get to John, he's just like, 'No, let me tell you it as plainly as possible that he is God.' This is the same thing Mark was saying... In Mark you have Jesus saying things like the Son of Man is the Lord of the Sabbath." — [[Michael Jones]] [^2]

> "Jesus claiming to be the Son of Man from Daniel 7 who comes riding on the clouds of heaven. The only person who does that in the Old Testament again is Yahweh. So I think Mark's Christology is extremely high." — Jones [^4]
