# Babylon I Am and There Is No Other Shows Rhetoric Means Relevance

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
McClellan had argued that the same "I am, and there is no other" formula is placed in the mouth of the city of **Babylon** (Isaiah 47), proving the phrase is conventional self-exaltation rhetoric, not a unique ontological claim. [[Gavin Ortlund]] replies: yes, but Babylon is immediately judged and doomed — she "is just wrong," her assertion is false, and "the fact that an evil entity can ape this language doesn't deprive it of its monotheistic implications."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Babylon's accuracy is irrelevant to McClellan's point (rating: strong; logical)
McClellan's argument was never that Babylon's claim is *true*. It is about what the *rhetoric means*. If "I am and there is no other" meant "no other cities exist," it would make no sense for a city to use it — there obviously are other cities. It only makes sense if it means "I am the only one that matters within my domain." [^1]

> "whether or not the city is accurate is really irrelevant to my argument because my point is not that the city is right my point is that it doesn't really make sense for the city to be appropriating that rhetoric if that rhetoric is intended to be understood to mean that no other cities exist" [^1]

### 2. The form fixes the meaning regardless of speaker (rating: strong)
Put in the mouth of a city (Babylon, Nineveh) or a nation (Moab), the phrase can only mean "I'm the only one that matters to my constituents, within my purview, within the rhetorical scope" — whether the speaker is right or wrong. The same conventional meaning then governs the phrase when Yahweh uses it. [^2]

> "it really only makes sense for the city to be appropriating that rhetoric if the rhetoric is intended to mean I'm the only one that matters to my constituents within my domain my perview within the rhetorical scope no other cities matter whether the city is right or wrong" [^2]

### 3. Coheres with the relevance/incomparability reading (rating: strong)
This is the same conclusion reached lexically (the "nothing/less than nothing" words applied to existing nations) and grammatically (the Shema's *echad*): the negation formulas denote exclusive relevance within a domain, not sole existence. See [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]] and [[The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence]]. [^2]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's point has force: in Isaiah 47 Babylon's boast is explicitly set up as *hubris that invites judgment* — the narrative frames the claim as a usurpation of a status only Yahweh can rightly hold, which arguably implies the claim is uniquely *true* of Yahweh (hence monotheistic). McClellan answers that this conflates two questions — *who may rightly make the boast* (a value claim) versus *what the boast asserts* (a meaning claim). The literary judgment on Babylon's arrogance does not change the conventional semantics of the formula, which everywhere denotes domain-relevance, not ontology. [^3]
