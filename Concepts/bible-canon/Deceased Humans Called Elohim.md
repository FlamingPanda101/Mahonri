# Deceased Humans Called Elohim

## The Claim
The Hebrew Bible uses *elohim* for **the deceased**, not only for gods, idols, or angels. The ghost of Samuel, summoned by the medium of Endor for Saul, is called *elohim*. This reflects a significant conceptual overlap between deity and the dead in ancient Israel and Judah, and is further evidence that *elohim* is not a single, fixed category. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The witch of Endor calls the dead Samuel *elohim* (rating: strong; the text, 1 Samuel 28)
When the medium sees the spirit she has raised, she describes it as *elohim* coming up from the earth. McClellan notes Ortlund's framing "inadvertently omits" that this is specifically **the ghost of Samuel** — a deceased human called a god. [^1]

> "Samuel is called Elohim by the witch of Endor when speaking to Saul so what seems to be inadvertently omitted from this reference is the fact that this is the deceased Samuel this is the ghost of Samuel" [^1]

### 2. Deity and the deceased conceptually overlapped (rating: strong; McClellan's monograph)
"The deceased were frequently identified precisely as *elohim* as gods because the conceptualization of deity and the conceptualization of the deceased significantly overlapped" — the engine behind ancestor worship. [^2]

> "the deceased were frequently identified precisely as Elohim as Gods because the conceptualization of deity and the conceptualization of the deceased significantly overlapped and this is what led to things like ancestor worship" [^2]

### 3. Temple worship overlaps with denigrated ancestor worship (rating: moderate; archaeological)
McClellan notes that the temple worship described in the Hebrew Bible has significant overlap with the ancestor worship the same Bible denigrates — a practice nonetheless "preserved in the mortuary remains that we find in ancient Israel and ancient Judah." He recommends [[Kerry Sonia]]'s *Caring for the Dead in Ancient Israel*. [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional reading takes the witch-of-Endor *elohim* as either a loose/honorific usage ("a divine/supernatural being") or as a deceiving spirit, not evidence that Israelites genuinely deified the dead; and treats biblical denunciations of necromancy and ancestor cult as proof Israel *rejected* such overlap. McClellan's reply is that the very need to denounce these practices, plus the mortuary archaeology, shows they were live realities, and that the lexical fact (a dead human called *elohim*) stands regardless of the narrator's stance. [^2]

## Best Quotes

> "this is what led to things like ancestor worship and this is also why the temple worship that we see described in the Hebrew Bible has significant overlap with the ancestor worship that we see denigrated in the Hebrew Bible" [^3]
