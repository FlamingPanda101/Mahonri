# Bible Permits Beating Slaves Short of Immediate Death

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Joshua HS argued that the Bible protected enslaved people from abuse: if you strike your servant and he dies you are "punished" (which Joshua claimed meant the death penalty), and if you destroy his eye or tooth you must free him ([[Exodus 21.26-27|Exodus 21:26-27]]) — "right there, that is nothing like the transatlantic slave trade."[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **"Punished" is not the death penalty (rated: strong; linguistic).** The verb in [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20]] is **naqam** in the passive — "to be avenged." It "absolutely indisputably is not" the death penalty; most scholars hold the punishment is deliberately unspecified because it would differ for an Israelite vs. a foreign victim.[^2]

2. **Verse 21 negates the protection (rated: strong; primary text).** If the beaten slave "continue[s] a day or two," there is **no punishment**, "for he is his money." The death is treated as the accidental result of a permissible disciplinary beating; the master merely loses property.[^3]

3. **Net effect: licensed abuse (rated: strong; argued).** "You're allowed to beat your slave as much as you want as long as you don't kill them" outright. "The Bible explicitly allows you to abuse an enslaved person."[^4]

4. **Joshua's self-undermining admission (rated: moderate).** Joshua sensed the implication, backtracked ("it doesn't say there's no punishment, but I don't remember what punishment it actually does say") — the text "explicitly says there is no punishment."[^4] See [[Apologist Misremembers and Hallucinates Bible Passages]].

5. **Steelman.** The eye/tooth manumission of [[Exodus 21.26-27|Exodus 21:26-27]] is a genuine, real protection — a maimed slave goes free. McClellan grants it but notes it presupposes a regime where routine beating is lawful; it caps *permanent disfigurement*, not violence as such.[^1]

## Best Quotes

> "You could beat your slave and they could be at death's door, but survive for a day or two and then pass away and there would be no punishment. According to the Bible, yes, that is abuse. The Bible explicitly allows you to abuse an enslaved person."[^4]

> "The verbal root is *naqam*. It occurs in the passive. It means to be avenged... most scholars agree it is unclear precisely what the punishment is because it would be different depending on whether the slave was a native Israelite or a foreigner."[^2]
