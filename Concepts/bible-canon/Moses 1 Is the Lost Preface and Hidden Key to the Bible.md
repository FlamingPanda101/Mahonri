# Moses 1 Is the Lost Preface and Hidden Key to the Bible

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] and [[Stephen Jones]] argue that **Moses Chapter 1 of the [[Book of Moses]] is the divinely intended preface to the Bible** — a "revelatory preface," a "key," a "lens" through which the rest of Scripture (beginning with [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis]]) is meant to be read. Most books open with an author's introduction telling the reader "here's what this book is about"; jump into the middle without it and "you might really miss a lot of the meaning." Moses 1 is that front matter for the Bible, and it answers two questions Genesis never poses: *who am I?* (answered at the chapter's start — "thou art my son") and *what story am I living in?* (answered at its end — "this is my work and my glory"). Without it, the Bible "can look like just legalistic," a checklist from a "task-oriented God."[^1][^2]

The stronger form of the claim is that this preface was not merely missing but **deliberately hidden**: the text says these words were "taken away from the Bible" because of "the wickedness of men," and even when restored the Saints were at first told to "keep this under wraps" — so it is God's *hidden* key. "You've got a lock. Somebody steals and throws away the key... How are you supposed to unlock it?"[^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Chronology: the revelation precedes the translation
Moses 1 was given to [[Joseph Smith]] in June 1830 — the Book of Mormon published three months earlier, the Church organized two-plus months earlier — *immediately before* Joseph began the inspired revision of the Bible (the JST). Bradley suspects the very idea of the JST "comes from this revelation." So the preface is positioned, historically, exactly where a preface belongs: "Here's what you need to know about the Bible ahead of time."[^5] *Rating: defensible as LDS source-chronology; the causal claim ("the JST idea comes from Moses 1") is Bradley's inference, flagged by him as needing more historical work.*

### 2. Genre: Genesis lacks the relational frame a preface supplies
Read cold, Genesis introduces no relationship between God and Adam/Eve — "it's just what God made them, and then He tells them what to do." Commandments appear "right away in the Garden of Eden" with no prior account of *who* these characters are to each other, which is what makes the surface read look like "performance... religion. If you do this, then you get that." Moses 1 inserts the missing premise — God is a father, the human is his child — "before I tell you what to do, I'm going to tell you who you are."[^6][^2] *Rating: a literary/theological observation, persuasive within the LDS frame; see Steelman for the source-critical objection.*

### 3. The text claims its own suppression
Moses 1 is internally self-describing as lost: verse 23 says "these words were taken away from the Bible," verse 41 frames the loss as caused by human wickedness, and verse 42 instructs that it be kept back at first. The narrative thus *predicts* the very gap it fills, which Bradley reads as evidence it is the displaced key rather than a late addition.[^3][^7] *Rating: internally coherent but circular for an outsider — the document attesting the loss is the document in question.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A biblical scholar will reject the framing at its root. Moses 1 is not an ancient prologue that "fell out" of Genesis; it is a 19th-century revelation produced by Joseph Smith, and there is no manuscript, versional, or patristic evidence that Genesis ever carried such a preface. Calling it "the lost key" assumes the conclusion (Restoration scripture is recovering pre-existing text) that a critic denies — see [[The Pearl of Great Price Is the Most Unstable Volume of LDS Scripture]] on the volume's textual instability. The "legalistic surface of Genesis" charge also overstates: Genesis 1 frames creation as gift and blessing ("be fruitful"), and the covenantal-relational reading of the Pentateuch is available *within* the Hebrew Bible without an added prologue. The honest LDS position is the one Bradley half-concedes — this is a claim of *revealed* framing, valuable as a theology of reading rather than a text-critical recovery; its payoff is coherence (it explains why Genesis can read as cold and how to read it warmly), not a demonstration that the canon was physically edited. Compare [[Moses Received Genesis by Revelation as Joseph Smith Received the Book of Moses]], which makes the parallel-revelation move explicit.
