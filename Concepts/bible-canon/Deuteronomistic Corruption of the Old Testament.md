# Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament

## The Claim
The dominant religion of Jesus's day was **Deuteronomism** (= Phariseeism) — the prophet-killing school that "found" Deuteronomy in the defiled temple and rewrote the canon to suppress messianic prophecy. This is a recurring LDS framing of Old Testament textual corruption (cf. the Book of Mormon's "plain and precious things" removed).

## Supporting Evidence
- "The dominant religion of the day when Christ was there was... Deuteronomism. That's Phariseeism" — the same school that killed Isaiah, Zechariah, Jeremiah, Uriah, and drove off Lehi *(speaker's thesis)* [^1].
- Deuteronomy was "found" (2 Kings 22, under Josiah) in the blood-soaked temple of Solomon, after which Jerusalem was promptly destroyed by [[Nebuchadnezzar]] *(speaker's argument)* [^2].
- The hand-washing laws the Pharisees press on Jesus derive from this Deuteronomistic legal layer — so his rebuke targets the corrupt source, not just the critics *(argument)* [^3].

## The Weinfeld Account (Mike & Dave)
[[Mike Day]] and [[Dave Butler]] give the same claim a scholarly spine by walking through [[Moshe Weinfeld]]'s *Deuteronomy and the Deuteronomic School* (1972). On Weinfeld's thesis the Deuteronomist is not one author (against [[Martin Noth]]) but a *school* of scribes and lawyers, active before [[Josiah]] (~640 BC) and into the exile, whose distinctive ideology can be itemized:[^4]
- **Yahweh-alone and Jerusalem-alone.** The [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Shema]] ("the Lord our God is one Lord") is the program; the school delegitimizes the rival sanctuaries at Dan, Bethel, Beersheba, and Elephantine, leaving only the Jerusalem temple. The "other gods" targeted were not Odin or Amaterasu but deities "understood to be Yahweh's father, or Yahweh's mother, or Yahweh's bride." *(Moderate-to-strong — mainstream source-critical scholarship; the LDS gloss on the divine family is the interpretive add-on.)*
- **"Name theology."** God does not *dwell* in the temple; only his *name* dwells there (Deuteronomy says "the place where Yahweh shall choose to put his name"). The ark is demoted from throne/chariot of God to a mere box holding the law. *(Strong — a recognized feature of Deuteronomic theology.)*
- **Invented Levites and secular slaughter.** Once only Jerusalem priests may sacrifice, the dispossessed local priests become "Levites"; and because most Israelites live too far to sacrifice every animal, Deuteronomy *invents* non-sacrificial ("secular") slaughter. *(Moderate — a standard reading of Deuteronomy's centralization.)*
- **Back-edited histories.** The school edited Joshua–Kings to rate every king by law-keeping, praising [[Josiah]] as Moses-like for doing "all the things Deuteronomy says." [[Deuteronomy 1.1|Deuteronomy 1:1]] betrays a later author writing from "the other side of the Jordan."
- **Bloody enforcement.** [[2 Kings 23.5|2 Kings 23]] has Josiah burning the rival *kemarim* on their own altars; Day and Butler argue the "Canaanites" Deuteronomy says to exterminate are really fellow Judahites of the wrong religion, and (speculatively) that Josiah may have been killed by his own archers ([[2 Chronicles 35.22-24|2 Chronicles 35]]). On this reading [[Laman]] and Lemuel are *pious* Deuteronomists and [[Lehi]] one of the hunted [[The Kemarim Were the Melchizedek Priests Josiah Purged|kemarim]] — see [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The Documentary-Hypothesis / Deuteronomist framework is mainstream, but the apologetic use of it is contestable. (a) Weinfeld describes a *literary and theological* development; he says nothing about a suppressed "atoning Messiah" or "divine Mother" — that overlay comes from [[Margaret Barker]]'s contested "temple theology," not from Weinfeld, so citing Weinfeld lends consensus-scholarship credibility to a non-consensus LDS thesis. (b) "Corruption" is a value-judgment the academic model does not make: source critics see the reform as one stage in Israel's religious evolution, not as the loss of an original pure religion. (c) Casting Josiah as a villain inverts the biblical text's own verdict and rests partly on argument-from-silence (the unnamed archers) the speakers themselves flag as "not doctrine." Day's fair, measured move — which distinguishes this from the cruder version of the claim — is that he *does not* throw Deuteronomy out: he keeps "the treaty/covenant pattern" and the book's humane concern for the poor while rejecting its "kill the heterodox" ideology (see [[The Vassal-Treaty Covenant Pattern Links Deuteronomy King Benjamin and the Endowment]]).
