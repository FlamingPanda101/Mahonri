# Conflicting Traditions on Seeing God's Face

## The Claim

The Hebrew Bible contains **conflicting traditions** about whether a human can see God's face and live. One tradition ([[Exodus 33.20|Exodus 33:20]]) insists no one can see God's face and survive; multiple other texts narrate people doing exactly that. [[Dan McClellan]] argues these texts are "a little conflicted" — evidence of competing ancient conceptions of divine visibility rather than a single harmonizable doctrine.[^1]

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The standard apologetic harmonization holds that the "no one can see God and live" texts refer to God's full glory/essence, while the texts where people "see God" describe limited or mediated manifestations — so there is no real contradiction. McClellan's data, drawn from the texts' own wording, rebuts the assumption that they were ever meant to fit together.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The prohibition tradition.** In [[Exodus 33.20|Exodus 33:20]]–23, God hides Moses in the rock, covers him with his hand, and shows only his back, "for no one can see me and live" (LXX: "no one can see my face and live").[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong, explicit.)*

2. **Counter-texts where God is seen.** McClellan lists: the elders "saw the God of Israel" with sapphire-like pavement under his feet ([[Exodus 24.10|Exodus 24:10]]); Moses spoke to God "face to face, like a man speaks with his friend" and beheld "the form of the LORD" ([[Numbers 12.8|Numbers 12:8]]); [[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]] Jacob saw God "face to face" and his life was preserved.[^3] *(Evidence rating: strong — these are direct narrations of seeing God.)*

3. **The authors are openly "conflicted."** McClellan: "they're very clearly... a little conflicted on how much they know." The juxtaposition is best explained by distinct source traditions, not one coherent theology.[^4] *(Evidence rating: matches source-critical consensus on the Pentateuch.)*

4. **This conflict drove the translation pressure.** The "cannot see God" tradition is the very discomfort McClellan says motivated the [[Masoretes|Masoretic]] passive vocalization of the pilgrimage verb (see [[Masoretic Vowels Reversed the Active Verb to Passive]]).[^5] *(Evidence rating: coherent linkage.)*

**Steelman of the harmonizing view:** The conservative reader notes that the Hebrew uses different verbs/idioms and that "face" can denote full immediate presence vs. a mediated theophany; thus Exod 33:20 (full unveiled glory = death) and Exod 24:10 / Gen 32:30 (accommodated appearance = survivable) need not contradict. This is internally consistent *if* one presupposes a single intending author and inerrancy. McClellan's point is that this presupposition, not the texts, generates the harmony — see the broader pattern in [[Rationalization C - The Third Thing Harmonization]].
