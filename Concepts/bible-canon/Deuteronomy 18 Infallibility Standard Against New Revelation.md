# Deuteronomy 18 Infallibility Standard Against New Revelation

## The Claim
In the debate, Reverend Dr. [[Timothy J. Whitehead]] makes [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18]] the affirmative standard for canon and the bar that any claimed new revelation must clear. The standard, he says, is **infallibility when speaking for God**: a prophet need not be sinless or never mistaken, but "whenever he is claiming to be speaking from God, he is speaking the infallible truth of God's revelation" — and a prediction that fails marks a word the Lord "has not spoken." On this test the Protestant canon qualifies (sent by God, validated by signs, prophecies proved true), the deuterocanon and self-professed later prophets (including [[Joseph Smith]]) do not, and — crucially — Latter-day Saints, to keep their prophets level with Scripture, have had to **lower the standard of Scripture** by calling the word of God itself fallible.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The standard is infallibility-when-speaking-for-God (rated: the core claim).** "When a prophet speaks in the Lord's name and the message does not come true ... that is a message the Lord has not spoken." Whitehead grants the prophet is "a fallible human" who can err — "Paul had to confront Peter" — "but whenever Peter was in the wrong, he was not giving revelation."[^1] So Deut 18 does not require sinlessness, only infallibility *under inspiration*. Compare the Latter-day Saint reading that the verse targets the *presumptuous* prophet, not the merely mistaken one: [[Deuteronomy 18 Condemns the Presumptuous Prophet Not the Mistaken One]].

2. **The canon self-selects against this bar (rated: strong, internal to his system).** Old and New Testament authors were "sent by God ... validated by signs and wonders ... their prophecies proved true," and the texts attest each other as Scripture (Paul citing Luke in 1 Tim 5:18; Peter citing Paul in 2 Pet 3:15-16). New revelation "must prove itself by meeting the biblical standard of infallibility" and "add to a story that's complete ... without contradicting the ancient." See [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]] and [[Every Worldview Has a Self-Authenticating Ultimate Standard]].[^2]

3. **The "LDS lowered the standard of scripture" charge (rated: the sharpest thrust).** Whitehead argues early Latter-day Saints shared the Christian assumption — Joseph Smith said the Book of Mormon was "the most correct of any book," and that there is "no error" in his revelations; Brigham Young called his sermons scripture. Held to Deut 18, their failed predictions and contradictions then forced a retreat: to keep prophets on Scripture's level, the LDS "lowered the standard of scripture" by teaching that "the word of God ... is in fact fallible" ("God works through fallible men"). The redefinition of inspiration (against [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:20-21]]) makes Scripture "depend on fallible human interpretation."[^3]

**Steelman of the Latter-day Saint counter ([[Shiloh Lofgren]]).** Three replies. (1) **Jonah / conditionality**: Jonah prophesied Nineveh's overthrow in forty days and it did not occur; by the mechanical reading he is a false prophet, so the test cannot be "any failed prediction" without a double standard — many oracles are conditional (see [[Deuteronomy 18 Condemns the Presumptuous Prophet Not the Mistaken One]] and [[Prophetic Failed-Prophecy Test Is Self-Refuting for Cessationists]]). (2) **All scripture is not prophecy**: Whitehead slides from "no *prophecy* came by the prophet's own interpretation" ([[2 Peter 1.4|2 Pet 1:20]]) to "no *scripture* is fallible," but "is all prophecy scripture? No — and not all scripture is prophecy," so the verse does not establish a uniformly inerrant text. (3) **The recognition test already failed**: "my sheep know my voice" did not stop the church from receiving the Johannine Comma, the long ending of Mark, or the 73-book canon as Scripture for centuries — so an infallible bar enforced by fallible recognition is not self-executing (see [[An Infallible Autograph We No Longer Possess Cannot Be the Rule of Faith]]). The LDS position is not that revelation is unreliable but that *living prophets remain fallible men whose office survives error*, while still speaking God's word when they speak for Him.

## Best Quotes
> "Notice the standard. The standard is infallibility. When the prophet is claiming to be speaking from God ... he is speaking the infallible truth of God's revelation."[^1]

> "They've had to lower the standard of scripture by arguing that the word of God — contrary to its own standard — is in fact fallible."[^3]

> [Lofgren] "Is all prophecy scripture? No. Not all scripture is prophecy. So that's a conflation of what that verse is saying."[^4]
