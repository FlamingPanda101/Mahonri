# The Canon-as-Article-of-Faith Dilemma

## The Claim
[[Robert Boland]]'s signature move against *sola scriptura*: a **dilemma over whether the canon (the *tota scriptura*) is an article of faith**. Either horn breaks the doctrine.[^1]

- **Horn 1 — the canon is *not* an article of faith.** Then the *fact of inspiration* of any individual book (say [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]]) is not binding. So a Protestant has **no consistent basis to condemn as a heretic** a fellow Protestant who, after much study and prayer (the "Berean test"), concludes that **Luther was right** to doubt James and Jude and jettisons them. The believer's private judgment is sovereign and the canon is negotiable.
- **Horn 2 — the canon *is* an article of faith.** Then a doctrine binds the conscience (the inspiration of Jude) that is **nowhere explicated in the biblical text** — no verse tells you Jude is canonical. That is a conscience-binding truth *outside* scripture, which directly **violates** *sola scriptura* (per which all binding sources are subordinate to the text).

Either way, the Protestant cannot get an inspired *table of contents* from inside the books, so the rule of faith is incomplete on its own terms.

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Boland / LDS negative) — the two reinforcing examples:**
- **Jude and 1 Enoch.** Jude 1:14-15 quotes **1 Enoch** and introduces it with the standard prophetic verb "*prophesied*," ascribing divine inspiration to it. Boland presses: if "prophesied / thus saith the Lord" is inspiration-language (which Powers concedes), then Jude treats a non-canonical book as inspired — and (Lewis Donaldson) "the basic theology of 1 Enoch *is* the basic theology of Jude." See [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude]]. Powers' own rejoinder concedes the bind cuts both ways but cannot resolve it.[^2]
- **The missing first Corinthian epistle.** 1 Corinthians 5:9 ("I wrote unto you in an epistle...") references a *prior, now-lost* Pauline letter. Boland quotes **Powers himself** (and Reformed scholars Robert Bowman and Calvin) granting it was a genuine, inspired apostolic letter that is simply gone. So an inspired, God-breathed text once existed *outside* the 66 books — defeating the claim that the canon *exhausts* inspired scripture (*tota scriptura*). Powers concedes the missing letters (also Laodiceans) but holds God preserved "what we were supposed to have."[^3]
- The dilemma dovetails with [[Profitable Is a Fractional Word Not a Claim of Sufficiency]] (the text never claims to be the *totality*) and is the LDS-debate sibling of the Catholic [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]].

**Steelman (Protestant affirmative):**
- Powers' reply: one need not hold a formal "article of faith" listing the books; the **same recognition** that authenticated the books early (apostolicity, the church's reception, the Spirit's witness) grounds rejecting a later attempt to discard Jude as likely *insincere* or heretical — judged by *why* the person does it, not by a prooftext.[^4]
- That an inspired Pauline letter is lost does not make the canon "imperfect" (Calvin): God preserved what the church needs; missing ≠ proof that the surviving canon is insufficient. A "fallible collection of infallible books" is the classic Reformed self-understanding and absorbs both examples.
- Jude's *use* of 1 Enoch need not entail that Jude regarded the *whole book* as canonical scripture — citation for a true datum (cf. Paul quoting pagan poets) is not canonization.

## Best Quotes
> "If the canon — the *tota scriptura* — is not an article of faith... their teachings cannot be bound upon professing believers. Therefore, on what consistent basis would my opponent... condemn as a heretic a professing Protestant who argues that... Luther was right in his initial skepticism concerning works such as James and Jude?"[^1]

> [Donaldson, quoted by Boland] "Jude does not simply cite 1 Enoch as authoritative. Jude sees the world through the theology of 1 Enoch."[^2]

> [Powers, conceding the missing epistle] "What we call first Corinthians today would actually be technically second Corinthians; he [Paul] actually wrote a letter to them that we don't have."[^3]
