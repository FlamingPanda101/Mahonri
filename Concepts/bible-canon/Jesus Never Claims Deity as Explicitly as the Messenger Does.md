# Jesus Never Claims Deity as Explicitly as the Messenger Does

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common apologetic move treats Jesus' sayings as self-claims to be God. [[Dan McClellan]] rebuts this by contrast: the Hebrew **messenger** is *more* explicit than Jesus ever is, yet even the messenger is best explained by divine-image logic rather than ontological deity. Jesus, by comparison, "never claims to be the king" and "uses a lot of ambiguous language," so the Johannine "high" statements are about **manifesting** the Father's presence via the divine name, not asserting identity with the Father. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Jesus' language is consistently indirect.** He never says "I am the God of your father" or "I am the God of Bethel." The New Testament also uses ambiguous terms like *kyrios*, which can substitute for the divine name *or* function as a "secular term of respect." *Rating: a strong philological caution against over-reading the Gospel data.* [^1]

2. **The Father indwells Jesus via the divine name (John).** McClellan reads [[Scriptures/John 14.9|John 14:9]] ("to see me is to see the Father") and the Father/Son **mutual indwelling** as a name-bearing manifestation, framed by the "hybrid Johannine Christology that incorporates the Logos theology of Philo and Middle Platonism." *Rating: a defensible reading that locates John's Christology in its Hellenistic-Jewish context; competes with the Trinitarian reading.* [^2] *Steelman of the orthodox side:* the Logos-is-God prologue ([[Scriptures/John 1.1|John 1:1]]), Jesus exegeting the Father in [[Scriptures/John 1.18|John 1:18]], and Thomas's "my Lord and my God" form an inclusio that many read as a deliberate confession of Jesus' deity, not mere manifestation.

3. **Thomas's confession fits manifestation, not identity.** In [[Scriptures/John 20.28|John 20:28]] Thomas realizes "where Philip got it wrong" and says "My Lord and my God" because "Jesus as a walking, talking divine image manifests the divine presence." *Rating: coherent within the framework; this verse is the hardest case and the reading is contested.* [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus never claims to be the king. Jesus uses a lot of ambiguous language... So the messenger is far more explicit than Jesus ever is." [^1]

> "Jesus is not the Father, but by virtue of possessing the divine name, Jesus bears the presence of the Father and manifests it. That's why in John 1:18, Jesus exegetes the Father to the world." [^2]

> "Thomas in John 20:28 can say... 'My Lord and my God.' Because Jesus as a walking, talking divine image manifests the divine presence. To see him is to see the Father as well." [^3]
