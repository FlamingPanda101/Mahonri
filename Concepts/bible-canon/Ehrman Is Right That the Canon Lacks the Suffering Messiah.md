# Ehrman Is Right That the Canon Lacks the Suffering Messiah

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the critical New Testament scholar [[Bart Ehrman]] is **correct** when he says that no pre-Christian Jew read the Old Testament as predicting a suffering, executed Messiah — and that this concession, far from refuting the Book of Mormon, is exactly what the Book of Mormon predicts: the surviving canon was *stripped* of its plain messianism by the scribes, so of course it now reads as Ehrman says. The Latter-day Saint, uniquely, can *agree* with Ehrman's data and turn it into evidence.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Ehrman's stated claim — rate: strong (direct quotation of an A-tier scholar)
Barnes quotes [[Bart Ehrman]]: "No one prior to Christianity took these passages [Isaiah 53, etc.] to refer to a future Messiah. There was not a Jew on the planet who thought the Messiah was going to be crushed by his enemies, humiliated, tortured, and executed… To call Jesus the Messiah made no sense — i.e., it was nonsense virtually by definition." Barnes affirms this is right *as a reading of the canon*: "if I read the Old Testament like Bart Ehrman… not a lot of Messiah in here."[^1] (Ehrman is tier A — UNC Chapel Hill — and is not endorsing the LDS conclusion; his scholarship is being repurposed.)

### 2. The canon whispers where it should shout — rate: moderate (interpretive)
Barnes' framing: the Messiah is "the most important thing in the history of things," yet in the Old Testament "we can barely whisper it… always in a poem, in a metaphor, shrouded in allegory." The thinness of explicit messianism is the *symptom* of redaction, not its absence from the original religion. This is the canon-side of [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes]] and [[The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah]].[^2]

### 3. Even a hostile critic concedes the Book of Mormon is consistent here — rate: moderate (anecdote)
Eber D. Howe (*Mormonism Unveiled*) is quoted *attacking* the Book of Mormon for putting Christianity 600 years before Christ ("we must infer there has been a great deficiency in the record") — which, Barnes notes, is precisely the Book of Mormon's own claim: the record *is* deficient because it was corrupted. The critic accidentally states the thesis.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Granting Ehrman's premise does real work *for* the critic, not only the apologist. (a) **The simplest explanation of "no suffering Messiah in the OT" is that there wasn't one** — i.e., Christians (and, on this view, the Book of Mormon) read it in *after* the fact, which is the mainstream critical conclusion, not evidence of deletion. Positing a *removed* original requires independent evidence of the removal; "it's missing, therefore it was taken out" is unfalsifiable (any absence confirms the theory). (b) **Ehrman would reject the use being made of him.** His point is about the *development* of messianic interpretation, and he does not hold that a pre-exilic suffering-Messiah doctrine existed and was scrubbed; citing him as a friendly witness inverts his actual position. (c) **The Book of Mormon cannot be the lever.** Barnes's move — "we *know* there was Christ before Christ because the Book of Mormon says so, therefore dismiss the circular-dating objection" — presupposes the very thing in dispute (the Book of Mormon's antiquity), so to a non-LDS interlocutor it begs the question. The disciplined version of the argument is narrower: Ehrman shows the *canon* lacks a clear suffering Messiah; the Book of Mormon and a body of (contested-date) apocrypha *claim* it was once present; whether that claim is true turns on independent dating evidence (e.g. the [[Testament of Naphtali Was Vindicated by the Dead Sea Scrolls|Naphtali]] case), not on Ehrman's concession by itself.
