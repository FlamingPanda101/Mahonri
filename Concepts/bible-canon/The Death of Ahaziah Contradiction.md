# The Death of Ahaziah Contradiction

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] presents the death of King Ahaziah of Judah as a Hebrew Bible contradiction: [[2 Kings 9.27|2 Kings 9:27]] and [[2 Chronicles 22.8-9|2 Chronicles 22:8–9]] give incompatible locations, manner of death, and who buries him. As with the Judas case (see [[The Death of Judas Contradiction]]), harmonizing requires inventing unattested events and overruling the plain text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Where and how he dies differs (rated: strong).** 2 Kings: shot in his chariot at the ascent of Gur near Ibleam, he "fled to Megiddo, and died there." 2 Chronicles: "captured while hiding in Samaria," brought to Jehu, and "put to death." Different city (Megiddo vs Samaria), different manner (dies of a chariot wound while fleeing vs captured and executed).[^1]

2. **Who buries him differs (rated: strong).** 2 Kings: "*His officers* carried him in a chariot to Jerusalem, and buried him." 2 Chronicles: "*They* [Jehu's men] buried him, for they said, He is the grandson of Jehoshaphat." The burying party is different, and the text of Chronicles plainly attributes it to Jehu's side.[^2]

3. **Characteristic Chronicler divergence (rated: moderate-strong, source-critical).** This is "a Kings-vs-Chronicles divergence of the kind the Chronicler routinely introduces" — the same phenomenon as the Chronicler's "fix" of who killed Goliath (see [[Elhanan Killed Goliath - Chronicles Harmonized the Contradiction]]). The Deuteronomistic History and the Chronicler are distinct compositions with distinct agendas.[^2]

**Steelman of the harmonizing view.** The standard reconciliation: Jehu's men *captured* Ahaziah in Samaria, brought him toward the Jezreel Valley, put him in a chariot, shot at him near Ibleam, he fled wounded to Megiddo and died there — so Chronicles narrates the capture and Kings the death, telescoping the same event. The burial discrepancy is smoothed by saying Jehu's men *permitted* Ahaziah's officers to bury him, so both "Jehu's men buried him" and "his officers buried him" are loosely true. McClellan grants this is not strictly impossible, but it requires stitching together events neither text records and reading "they buried him" against what Chronicles actually says — an unevidenced harmonization that multiplies improbabilities (see [[Stacked Improbable Harmonizations Multiply Down to a Sliver]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
For inerrancy this is harder than a mere gap, because the two texts make *positive* incompatible assertions (died at Megiddo while fleeing vs executed after capture in Samaria; buried by his officers vs by Jehu's men). To save inerrancy one must overrule the plain subject of "they buried him" in Chronicles. McClellan's framing: that is treating the text as "raw data that we get to manipulate" to serve a dogma — the dogma, not the text, is the real authority.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan draws no Latter-day Saint conclusion. The case sits comfortably with a Restoration view that the Bible is inspired "as far as it is translated correctly" (Article of Faith 8) and need not be inerrant. Latter-day Saints can treat the Kings/Chronicles divergence as evidence of composite, community-shaped historiography — the same critical lens the vault applies to scripture generally — while applying the same standard evenhandedly to harmonizations of Restoration texts (cf. [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]]).
