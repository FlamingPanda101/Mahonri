# The Church Recognizes Scripture It Does Not Make It

## The Claim

[[Jacob Hansen]] poses the Catholic-flavored canon trap to [[Aaron Shafovaloff]]: *if the Bible cannot list its own books, didn't **tradition** give us the Bible — making tradition, not Scripture, the ultimate authority?*[^1] Shafovaloff's Reformed-Baptist answer is the **recognition (not determination)** model stated positively: **"The church did not determine what is Scripture. Rather, it recognized what is Scripture. She, the bride of Christ, received Scripture; she did not make Scripture."** No "inspired table of contents" is required, because each book can be "received for what it is in itself."[^2]

His supports:[^2]
- **Sheep hear the Shepherd's voice** (John 10:27): "We do not need an infallible council of sheep to tell us that we've heard the voice of the Shepherd."
- **The burning-bush analogy** (via Gavin Ortlund): when Moses heard God at the bush, "he did not need a second voice whispering in his ear that this was indeed God." This is what Protestants mean by Scripture being **self-authenticating**.
- **Radical receivers** (1 Thessalonians 2:13; London Baptist Confession 1.4–5): Paul thanks the Thessalonians for receiving the word "not as the word of men but as what it really is, the word of God." The authority of Scripture "does not depend on the testimony of any person or church, but on God the author." So "God's people are not infallible, but they can receive and recognize the infallible word of God." The bride "does not preside over the word of the groom... she does not confer authority upon the word."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It dodges the infallible-regress on the Protestant side (rate: strong as a move).** By denying that recognition must be *infallible*, Shafovaloff blocks the demand for an infallible canon-declaring body: a fallible church can truly recognize an inherently authoritative book, just as a fallible Moses truly recognized God. This is the same distinction the Catholic uses (recognize vs. legislate) but deployed *without* conceding a second infallible magisterium — see [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]] and [[Thermometer vs Thermostat - The Church Declares the Canon]].
2. **It has experiential and textual anchors (rate: real, contested).** The "sheep hear my voice" / 1 Thess 2:13 reception language gives the position scriptural footing, and the phenomenology Shafovaloff describes — a reader "in bed with the Gospel of John open" encountering Christ "not thinking about any councils" — is a genuine datum about how Scripture has functioned. See [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]].
3. **The "circulation precedes councils" historical claim (rate: partly true, overstated by both sides).** Pressed by Hansen that it was *councils* (not the diffuse body of believers) who fixed the canon, Shafovaloff (citing Ortlund's *What It Means to Be Protestant*, ch. 5) replies the Gospel of John "was already in wide circulation, received as Scripture, before the councils ever deliberated over canonicity," so the councils *ratified* a prior reception rather than *creating* it.[^3] This is historically defensible for the core/"homologoumena" (the four Gospels, Paul's major epistles) but weaker for the disputed "antilegomena" (2 Peter, Jude, James, Revelation, Hebrews), where reception genuinely *was* contested into the fourth century.

### Steelman of the LDS / Catholic rebuttal ([[Jacob Hansen]])

Hansen's historical counter is sharp and substantially correct on the hard cases:
- **It was a particular *authoritative institution*, not "the sheep," that decided.** "It simply is the case that the church that actually selected which books would be canon was not the general body of believers but a particular set of councils that had a particular claim to authority." There *was* real "discussion about which books should be canonized and which shouldn't" — so the smooth "everyone just recognized it" story understates documented disputes (Marcion's rival canon, the Muratorian fragment's hesitations, Athanasius' Festal Letter finally fixing 27 books in 367).[^4] See [[Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority]] and [[The New Testament Canon Is a Reaction to Marcion]].
- **"The Bible means a library — who decided which books go in the library?"** This reframes the question as inescapably *historical and institutional*: an authority with "a particular claim to authority" did the selecting, which is exactly the authority-question the Restoration presses.[^5]
- **The deeper LDS point:** if a fallible-but-authoritative process gave the canon, then the Protestant has *already* admitted authority resides in more than the bare text — which is the wedge for both the Catholic magisterium and the LDS living priesthood. See [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]] and [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority]].

### Where the Catholic ([[Joe Heschmeyer]]) stands

Heschmeyer sides with Hansen *on the history* against Shafovaloff ("you both said burning in the heart; so did the men on the road to Emmaus — and that did not produce a Bible"), previewing the next night's authority debate. The Catholic agrees the canon required an authoritative declaring church; the disagreement with the LDS is *which* church.[^6]
