# Jesus Quotes Deuteronomy Against the Deuteronomists

## The Claim
A standard objection to the "Great Forsaken" / Deuteronomistic-corruption thesis is: *Jesus quoted Deuteronomy, so he held it authoritative and inerrant.* [[Jonah Barnes]] replies that "every single time" Christ quotes Deuteronomy, he quotes it **to the Deuteronomists** — using their own book ironically against the scribes and Pharisees who compiled it. So Jesus's use of Deuteronomy is a rhetorical weapon turned on his opponents, not an endorsement of biblical inerrancy.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Jesus warred against the scribes, the Torah's custodians (rate: strong, framing).** The word *scribe* "means the guys who write down the law ... who keep the Torah ... who write the Bible." Jesus's recorded conflict is overwhelmingly *with* the scribes and Pharisees over their legalism, not a complaint that Israel was insufficiently Mosaic. "Did Christ come and talk about how people weren't being Mosaic enough? No — it was 'you guys are misunderstanding the law.'"[^2]

2. **The quotations are ironic, aimed at the deuteronomists (rate: moderate, exegetical).** Barnes says he footnotes every instance in his book: "render unto Caesar that which is Caesar's" and similar uses deploy Deuteronomy *against* "these scheming Pharisees" — "he's using their false book against them ... a characteristic irony Jesus Christ does this all the time."[^3]

3. **The Pharisees followed the *written* word, hence "scribalism" (rate: moderate).** What the Pharisees followed was the written text; Jesus "pushed back on that overemphasis on the law meaning the written text," insisting on "the spirit behind this law." This is the behavioral signature of the scribal centralization the Deuteronomists produced. See [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]].[^4]

4. **It fits the restoration-of-suppressed-text pattern (rate: moderate, coheres).** The same Christ who quotes Deuteronomy ironically also *restores* excised verses (Micah) when teaching the Nephites — see [[Christ Restores Suppressed Verses of Micah in Third Nephi]] — showing his stance toward the scribal canon was corrective, not deferential.[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
The "always ironic" reading is strong but overstated. (a) In the wilderness temptation Jesus answers Satan with Deuteronomy ("man shall not live by bread alone," Deut 8:3) **not** addressed to deuteronomists at all — he uses it as straightforwardly authoritative Scripture, which cuts against "every single time ... to the deuteronomists." (b) Jesus affirms the Shema (Deut 6:4–5) as the greatest commandment, again positively. (c) Reading "render unto Caesar" as an anti-Deuteronomy jab is idiosyncratic; most exegetes see it as a trap-evasion, not a critique of Deuteronomy's reliability. (d) Even granting irony in places, *using* a text rhetorically does not entail believing it corrupt — and an LDS view that the Bible is "reliable but not infallible" does not actually require Jesus to have disowned Deuteronomy. The defensible point is narrower: Jesus's primary conflict was with scribal legalism, so his citations cannot be flatly converted into a proof of inerrancy.

## Best Quotes

> "Every single time the Christ quotes Deuteronomy ... Christ quotes Deuteronomy to the deuteronomists ... he's using their false book against them. It's a characteristic irony."[^3]

> "The word *scribe* — what do you think it means? It's the guys who write down the law, who keep the Torah, who write the Bible. Jesus Christ was angry at the guys who were compiling ... the Old Testament."[^2]
