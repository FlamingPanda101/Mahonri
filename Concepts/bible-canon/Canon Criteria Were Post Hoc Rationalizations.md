# Canon Criteria Were Post Hoc Rationalizations

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Apologists commonly claim a book had to meet objective **criteria** (apostolicity, antiquity, orthodoxy, catholicity) to enter the canon — implying the canon is a principled, rule-governed list. [[Dan McClellan]] rebuts this: "if you look at the history of this, it's not what happened. What happened is they said, 'These are our books.' And then people came in later and said, 'What criteria can we use to rationalize why these are the ones we landed on?'"[^1] The criteria are after-the-fact justifications, and "there are exceptions to all the criteria that they come up with."[^1] See [[The Canon Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The decision preceded the criteria — rate: strong
McClellan describes the canon's development as "a very slow process that was done rather arbitrarily based on a lot of factors, some... ad hoc."[^2] The historical order was: groups affirmed "these are our books," *then* generated rationalizing criteria. This is the vault's [[Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy in Canon Criteria]] applied to history — draw the target around the existing shots. **Steelman of the apologist:** even if criteria were articulated late, they may *describe* a real (Spirit-guided) recognition process that operated implicitly before being named — the church "received" rather than "decided" the canon. McClellan's reply is the existence of *exceptions to every criterion*: if the criteria were truly operative, there would be no anomalies (and no historical disputes; see [[The Church Once Accepted Books It Later Rejected]]).

### 2. Luther's exclusions were preference, not principle — rate: strong
McClellan and [[Dan Beecher]] note [[Martin Luther]]'s criterion was often bare distaste — "a lot of his was just like, I'm not a fan... That one doesn't cut the mustard for me, so it's out."[^3] Luther tried to relegate not only the Apocrypha but Hebrews, James, and Revelation; later Protestants overruled him ("Shut up, Martin. We're taking these back").[^4] This shows canon boundaries tracked the preferences of powerful individuals and communities, not neutral tests. See [[Luther and the Epistle of Straw]] and [[Reformers Disagree With One Another on the Canon]].

### 3. Formalization came late and reactively — rate: moderate
The Catholic Deuterocanon and a formalized canon emerged only at the **Counter-Reformation**, producing a "graded canon."[^5] A list canonized in the 16th century in *reaction* to Protestant subtraction is hard to portray as the timeless output of fixed criteria.
