# The Self-Authenticating Canon Refuted

## Overview

This is a hub note for the LDS/critical rebuttal to the Protestant doctrine that **Scripture is self-authenticating** — the claim (advanced by Aaron Shafovaloff, citing Gavin Ortlund and Michael Kruger, in the Maybe God debate) that the church does not *confer* authority on the canonical books but merely *receives* and *recognizes* books that authenticate themselves: "my sheep hear my voice," Moses needed no "second voice" at the burning bush, "Christianity is a religion of radical receiving."

The cluster's refutation runs on several fronts: (1) the canon question is *logically prior* — nothing inside the canon lists the canon, so whoever decided which books "feel" authoritative is the real ultimate authority (a [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority|prior-question]] problem); (2) self-authentication is *circular* — it uses the conclusion (these books are God's voice) as the premise; (3) the move *backs into the Catholic model*, since in practice it is *church consensus* doing the recognizing; and (4) it is structurally identical to Moroni's promise — a subjective inner witness — which Protestants reject when a Latter-day Saint uses it (the [[Moroni-Calvin Parallel - The Self-Authentication Gambit|Moroni-Calvin parallel]]).

## The Steelman

Kruger's strongest form: self-authentication is not naive subjectivism but a tri-perspectival model — the books bear divine *attributes*, the church *corporately* receives them, and the Spirit *internally* testifies; circularity is unavoidable for *any* ultimate authority (the Catholic appeal to the church is equally self-referential). The cluster's reply: granting that every ultimate authority is in some sense self-attesting only concedes the LDS point — the Protestant has no *non-circular* advantage over either Rome or Moroni, so "self-authentication" cannot do the anti-LDS work it is enlisted for.
