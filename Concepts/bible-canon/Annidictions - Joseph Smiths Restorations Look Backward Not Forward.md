# Annidictions - Joseph Smith's Restorations Look Backward Not Forward

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] coins a word — *annidiction* — to name what the three 1830 [[Book of Moses]] revelations actually are, and to distinguish them from the rest of the Pearl of Great Price. A **prediction** foretells the future; a prophet says "here are the things that are going to happen." An **annidiction** (Barnes' invented term) looks *backward*: a seer says "here are things that *did* happen." On this taxonomy the Book of Moses material (the revelation to Joseph, on Moses, and concerning Adam) is not prophecy about Adam or Enoch yet to come — it is claimed *restoration of ancient events that already occurred*. This sets the Book of Moses apart from the future-facing portions of the volume and frames it as a recovery of lost text rather than a forecast.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Prediction vs. annidiction, illustrated by Nephi at the judgment seat
Barnes' worked example is Nephi son of Helaman. Nephi *predicts* the people will be destroyed for wickedness and is mocked. He then makes an **annidiction**: go to the judgment seat and you will find the chief judge already slain. The crowd runs and finds the judge dead — a claim about something that *already happened*, "demonstrated immediately." Some conclude Nephi must be a prophet *because his annidiction checked out*, then credit his future predictions.[^1][^2] Barnes maps this onto Joseph Smith: "this is not a prediction he's making… He's saying these things did happen in the past. So I call them annidictions."[^2]

### 2. The annidictions are dense, unexplained, and arrive right after the Book of Mormon
Barnes stresses that these "three very thick annidictions come out of the sky right after the Book of Mormon is published with no explanation," and "unlike the other parts of the Pearl of the Great Price, they are not predicting the future… they're supposed to be **restorations of ancient texts**."[^3] This is the hinge that lets him argue the Book of Moses is recovered Genesis content — see [[The Book of Moses May Reconstruct the Lost 116 Pages]].

### 3. The other PoGP contents are a different genre
By contrast, Barnes treats Joseph Smith—History and the Articles of Faith as "contemporaneous records" — "he's just writing it about what happened," not restoring an ancient text — which is why he says the volume "almost doesn't quite belong together" ("Skittles and M&M's"). The annidiction category isolates the genuinely restorative core of the book.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "There's predictions which are trying to foretell what is going to happen… And then there's annidictions which tell that something *did* happen. They look back." [^1]

> "This, from Joseph Smith — this is not a prediction he's making. He's not prophesying that someday Adam will do this or someday Enoch will do that. He's saying these things *did* happen in the past. So I call them annidictions." [^2]

## LDS Connection
"Annidiction" is a coinage, not an established LDS term, so it carries no authority of its own; its value is rhetorical/heuristic. It reframes the Book of Moses from "Joseph Smith Translation" (a forward-corrective relabeling Barnes rejects in [[Book of Moses Is a Posthumous Label Joseph Smith Never Gave]]) to a backward-looking restoration, which is the conceptual setup for the [[The Book of Moses May Reconstruct the Lost 116 Pages|116-pages theory]]. It also dovetails with [[Moses Received Genesis by Revelation as Joseph Smith Received the Book of Moses]]: both treat Joseph's Genesis material as revealed access to the deep past rather than commentary on the present.
