# Markan Sandwich Literary Device

## The Claim
The Gospel of Mark frequently employs an **A-B-A' intercalation** (commonly called a "Markan sandwich"), where a story begins, is interrupted by a different story, and then resumes — with the outer story (A) functioning as a metaphorical commentary on the inner story (B). The fig tree cursing + temple cleansing + fig tree withering ([[Mark 11.12-25|Mark 11:12-25]]) is a prime example.[^1]

## The Fig Tree as a Markan Sandwich

| Layer | Verses | Content |
|-------|--------|---------|
| A (outer) | Mark 11:12-14 | Jesus curses the fig tree |
| B (inner) | Mark 11:15-19 | Jesus cleanses the temple |
| A' (outer) | Mark 11:20-25 | The fig tree is found withered |

The fig tree (A/A') represents the temple and its leadership (B). Jesus seeks fruit from the tree and finds none; he enters the temple and finds it a "den of thieves." The tree is cursed; the temple is condemned. The tree withers; the temple is destroyed (~70 CE).[^2]

## Best Quote
> "What we've got here is one of Mark's sandwich stories, where we have the introduction and the conclusion and in between we have a separate story. But usually these stories are bookending whatever is in between and they relate to each other — and so the fig tree is a metaphor, surprise surprise."[^1]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast
