# Theopneustos Was Used for Sandals Ointments and Streams Not Inerrancy

## The Claim
Inerrantists read [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]] — "all scripture is *theopneustos* (God-breathed)" — as proof that scripture is perfect and without error. [[Keystone]], summarizing the published work of New Testament scholar [[John C. Poirier]], argues this is a mistranslation: in the era 2 Timothy was written, *theopneustos* most plausibly meant "**life-giving / vivifying**," not "inerrant." The verse is a sincere compliment to scripture — it brings spiritual life — but it does not assert that scripture is errorless.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The contemporaneous usage describes life-giving things, not error-free things.** This is the load-bearing datum. Poirier's monograph (Library of New Testament Studies) on what *theopneustos* meant when 2 Timothy was written found the word used to describe **sandals**, **burial perfumes and ointments**, and **streams of water**. None of these are "inerrant"; what they share is that they are *life-giving / life-extending* — streams give life, burial ointments made deceased bodies "look more lifelike," sandals are helpful in that sense.[^2] *Rating: strong within the source — a published lexical study of period usage, which is exactly the right method for a rare word.*

2. **"Inspired" is anachronistically loaded.** Poirier notes the asymmetry: "if I say I'm inspired, no one thinks I'm claiming to be inerrant. On the other hand, if scripture claims to be inspired, then the claim to inerrancy is supposedly loud and clear. The rules are clearly flexible." The word "inspired" has "served as the means for smuggling a whole system of ideas into the Bible."[^3] *Rating: moderate — a sound caution against reading a later dogmatic category into a first-century word.*

3. **The inerrant reading is a third-century innovation.** Per Poirier, "the inspirationist understanding did not arise until the time of [[Origen]] in the 3rd century." [[Dan McClellan]] adds this was no accident: Origen "makes the most ardent and sustained case in early Christianity for the unified, univocal, and verbally inspired nature of the scriptures," and had rhetorical incentive to read *theopneustos* as marking a divine source for all scripture. That reading has dominated ever since.[^4] *Rating: moderate — a plausible reception-history claim that locates the inerrantist gloss after the apostolic era.*

4. **Independent non-LDS corroboration.** Keystone reports that an anonymous published non-LDS scholar agreed "inspired by God" in 2 Timothy 3:16 was "a bad translation aimed at reinforcing biblical inerrancy when it's just not there"; Rev. Dr. [[Angela Parker]] (New Testament and Greek) agreed it "is definitely not a proof text for biblical inerrancy"; and Dr. Anna Cwikla said Poirier's translation "seems fine," liking "God vivifies" if the idea traces to God breathing into the human's nostrils in Genesis 2:7.[^5] *Rating: moderate — the corroboration is real but partly informal (one source anonymous, others brief endorsements rather than independent studies).*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The strongest inerrantist reply is that inerrancy was never claimed to be *lexically encoded* in *theopneustos*. Even granting the word means "produced by God's breath / divinely sourced," the inference "what God breathes out cannot err" is *theological*, not merely linguistic (cf. B.B. Warfield, who argued the word is the *active* "breathed-out by God"). On this view, refuting the etymology or the "life-giving" gloss does not by itself refute the doctrine. A defender might also note that "life-giving" and "divinely sourced" are not mutually exclusive — a text can vivify *because* it is from God.

## Counter-objection
The rebuttal's force is cumulative and does not depend on the positive "life-giving" gloss being the *only* possible sense. Even granting Warfield's "breathed-out by God," that establishes divine *sourcing*, not *inerrancy*; and the burden then falls on the inerrantist to supply the missing premise that everything God sources in human language is errorless — a premise this verse does not state and which "all scripture" (a first-century category, not the later canon) cannot bear. See [[Canon Cannot Self-Reference - Compilation Postdates the Books]]. Keystone's safer claim is the negative one: the verse endorses scripture as good and life-giving without asserting it is inerrant.

## Best Quotes
> "During that time period, theopneustos was used to describe sandals. It was used to describe burial perfumes and ointments. It was used to describe streams of water. Does it make sense for us to consider these sandals or ointments or streams to be inspired in the sense that they are inerrant? Obviously not."[^2]
