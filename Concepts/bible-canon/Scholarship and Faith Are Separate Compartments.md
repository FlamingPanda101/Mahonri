# Scholarship and Faith Are Separate Compartments

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] and [[Benjamin Park]] articulate a "separate compartments" model of the
scholarship/faith relationship: critical scholarship operates "at a table… that has rules that govern
our evidence," and those rules cannot adjudicate supernatural faith claims. McClellan therefore
"never criticizes faith" — only "behavior that is atrocious," "bad scholarship," and "arguments that
are dumb."[^1] The believer who tries to "beat scholarship at its own game" by supplying naturalistic
proofs for miracles is making a category error.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Two languages, different terms — rate: strong (Park's framing)
[[Benjamin Park]]: "there's a language of scholarship and a language of faith… we are operating at a
table, in a space that has rules that govern our evidence. And we're not saying you can't have those
discussions of the resurrection [or] restoration, but that's just taking place on different
terms."[^2] Scholarship's silence on the supernatural is a feature of its method
(methodological naturalism), not a metaphysical verdict. This is the epistemic frame behind
[[Minimal Facts Fails - Grief Experiences Explain the Resurrection Appearances]].

### 2. Naturalizing miracles is self-defeating — rate: strong
Since the 19th century "a lot of believers have felt compelled to provide rational naturalist
explanations" — e.g. the Red Sea split "when there was this wind from the south." The motive is to
"be taken seriously by critics, scholars, experts," so they "try to beat scholarship at its own game,
but you can't." McClellan's advice: "why not just be in your compartment and allow them to be in
their other compartment?"[^3] A naturalized miracle is no longer a miracle and still loses the
evidential contest.

### 3. McClellan critiques arguments, not belief — rate: strong (method statement)
"I don't criticize faith ever on my channel. I criticize behavior that is atrocious and arguments and
bad scholarship and arguments that are dumb… if somebody comes out and says, 'Oh, I believe this,' I
wouldn't make a video responding to them. I'm going to respond to people who say, 'Look at this, this
is evidence,' or 'You have to believe this.'"[^1] The trigger for critique is an *evidential* or
*coercive* claim, not a confession. This is why his method pairs cleanly with the pastoral
[[Start From the Critical Reading Then Negotiate|read-critically-then-negotiate]] approach: the
historical-critical floor is for *claims about the text*; what a community then *does* devotionally
lives in the other compartment. **Steelman of an apologist (and a critique of the model):** a
Christian who holds that God acts *in history* (a bodily resurrection leaving an empty tomb) will
deny the compartments are clean — the resurrection is a historical claim, so methodological
naturalism is not neutral but smuggles in an anti-supernatural bias. McClellan's answer is the
plausibility standard of the companion note: history can decline to *infer* a miracle as best
explanation without ruling it metaphysically impossible.
