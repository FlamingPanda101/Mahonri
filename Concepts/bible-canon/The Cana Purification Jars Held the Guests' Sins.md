# The Cana Purification Jars Held the Guests' Sins

#type/claim

## The Claim
At the wedding at Cana ([[John 2.1-11|John 2:1–11]]), the six stone water-pots that Jesus had filled were "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews" — vessels tied to ritual hand-washing. The claim is that these pots were **not empty** but already partly held the *used, dirty* washing-water of the wedding guests, so that when [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]] turned that water into the best wine of the feast, he was symbolically transforming the *accumulated impurities — the sins — of everyone present*. The unclean state of the water is therefore not incidental; John foregrounds the purification function precisely so the reader sees that Christ made his finest wine out of filth.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
- **How the ritual reused water.** In Jewish (and Orthodox Jewish) practice, ritual hand-washing was constant — after sleep, before and after meals, before prayer. Water was kept in large basins; the worshipper poured water over each hand three times with a cup, and the dirtied water **drained back into the basin to be reused**. After dozens of guests had rinsed their impure hands before a feast, that reservoir would be foul.[^2]
- **The text implies pre-filled pots.** From the language of John 2:6–7 — pots "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews" that the servants "filled… up to the brim" — the author infers the vessels were already partly full of returned washing-water, so Jesus merely had them *top them off*: "and they filled them up to the brim." The "fill" command tops up rather than starts from empty.[^1]
- **John's emphasis on purification is deliberate.** Because John specifies the pots' purification function, "it seems fair to assume that the state of the water was important to John's story." John "wanted us to know that not only was it water in those pots, but that it was dirty water, unclean, unfit, dirty water."[^1]
- **The symbolism survives either way.** Even granting the pots were refilled with *fresh* water, the reading holds: as purification vessels they "symbolically held the sins of all those in attendance that night," so Christ's act still figures the cleansing and transformation of the guests' impurity.[^3]
- **The servants' faith.** Drawing out what looked like dirty water and bearing it to the most important guest "was no small faith," yet the servants obeyed Mary's charge ([[Mary (mother of Jesus)]]) — "whatsoever he saith unto you, do it" — and the governor pronounced it the best wine of the feast.[^4]
