# The Alabaster Box Was the Bride's Dowry Oil to Anoint the Bridegroom

## The Claim

[[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the woman who anoints [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] from an alabaster box ([[Luke 7.36-50|Luke 7]]; cf. Matthew 26 and Mark 14) is not a random admirer making a spontaneous gesture, but a bride performing a **wedding rite**: the alabaster box of ointment was part of a bride's **dowry**, kept to anoint her husband's body at his death. By breaking it over Christ she recognizes him as the **bridegroom** — the redeemer who will pay her bride-price and free her from captivity to sin. Read this way, the anointing is the bride anointing the bridegroom, and Christ's defense of her ("she is come aforehand to anoint my body to the burying") confirms the ritual.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The alabaster box was dowry equipment

Among the gifts of the dowry, "one of the pieces... would include an alabaster box of ointment that the bride would have kept... to anoint her husband's body upon his death." So the woman of Luke 7 acts not from impulse but with an heirloom oil already designated for exactly this purpose.[^3]

### 2. The Gospel parallels point the same way

In Matthew 26 and Mark 14 the anointing is **explicit**: she pours it "on his head," and Jesus says, "she did it for my burial" / "she is come aforehand to anoint my body to the burying." The bystanders' anger may be recognition that he is about to be killed; their indignation, and the woman's foreknowledge of his death, suggest "this wasn't just some random woman" but someone close to him — plausibly [[Mary Magdalene]], introduced immediately after the Luke account.[^4][^5]

### 3. The bridegroom redeems his captive bride

The ketubah obligated the groom to redeem a bride taken into slavery (see [[The Ketubah and the Jerusalem Window Depict Christ as the Marriage Covenant]]). The woman, "taken captive by sin," recognizes Christ as the bridegroom who would pay the redemption price — and the price to free "a human soul... from the eternal cost of sin" is "the life of a god." The oil she pours, pressed from olives of the tree, links *wisdom*, the tree of life, and the anointing of the bridegroom — the same temple-anointing logic as [[The Gifts of the Magi Were Temple Items for Anointing the High Priest]].[^6]

> **Steelman:** The Gospels themselves frame the anointing as preparation for burial, and "Messiah/Christ" means *anointed one*; a woman anointing Jesus toward his death is textually tied to his identity as the Anointed. That the act carries nuptial weight is a coherent extension of the pervasive bridegroom imagery, even if the specific "dowry alabaster box" custom is the interpretive addition.

## Best Quotes

> "This was probably something from her dowry that she had been given, and she's recognizing Christ as the bridegroom... who would fulfill the terms of his contract to redeem her, because she had been taken captive by sin." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^2]

> "In the more archetypal terms... this is the woman coming to anoint the bridegroom." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^7]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The four Gospel anointing accounts differ in setting, host, and the woman's identity; harmonizing them into one nuptial rite by one bride (and identifying her as Mary Magdalene) goes beyond what any single text states.
- There is no firm evidence that brides routinely received a dedicated "alabaster box for the husband's burial" as standard dowry; the custom is asserted rather than documented. Mainstream reading takes the anointing as devotion plus prophetic burial-preparation, without a wedding rite.
