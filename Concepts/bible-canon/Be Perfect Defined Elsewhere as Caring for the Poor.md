# "Be Perfect" Defined Elsewhere as Caring for the Poor

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] corroborates the love-reading of [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] by showing that, elsewhere, the same author and tradition **define *teleios* by outward care for others** — selling goods for the poor, loving one's brother — and that the whole pattern is rooted in the Torah's *imitatio Dei*. The verse is not an isolated demand for self-perfection but one node in a scriptural web about loving the least.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Matthew's own gloss (rated: strong).** In [[Matthew 19.21|Matthew 19:21]] Jesus tells the rich young ruler: "If thou wilt be *perfect* (*teleios*), go and sell that thou hast, and give to the poor." Matthew himself defines being *teleios* as wholehearted self-giving toward the poor — "doing something for others, helping the least among you" — not flawlessness.[^1]

2. **The Johannine echo (rated: strong).** [[1 John 4.18|1 John 4:18–21]] binds the word to neighbor-love: "perfect love casteth out fear," and "he that loveth not his brother... is not made *perfect* in love." Jesus's followers carried the saying forward as a command about loving the brother one can see.[^2]

3. **Torah roots — imitatio Dei (rated: strong, intertextual).** Jesus draws the ethic from the Hebrew scriptures: [[Leviticus 11.44|Leviticus 11:44]] and Leviticus 19 ("be holy, for I am holy" — the same chapter as "love thy neighbour"), expanded by [[Deuteronomy 10.18-19|Deuteronomy 10:18–19]] ("[God] loveth the stranger... love ye *therefore* the stranger"). The "God does X, *therefore* you do X" structure — note the same "therefore" — is the template behind Matthew 5:48. The Hebrew prophets' complaint was almost always idolatry *or* failure to care for "the least among you" (Amos et al.), and Jesus continues that line.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
One could argue Hatch is cherry-picking the social-justice uses of *teleios* while underweighting passages where perfection-language is individual and cultic (e.g. the "without blemish" sacrificial sense of *tamim*, or James's "perfect and entire, wanting nothing"). And the Latter-day Saint tradition's exaltation reading is not baseless: becoming like God plausibly includes more than almsgiving. A fair synthesis is that outward love is the *primary and defining* content Jesus gives the word — not its only possible resonance.

## Counter-Objection
"Caring for the poor is one command among many — why let it define 'perfect'?" Hatch's reply: because Jesus and his followers *explicitly* attach the word to it (Matt 19:21; 1 John 4), and because the Torah/prophetic tradition Jesus quotes makes love of the vulnerable the test of likeness to God — so the burden is on the inward-checklist reading to show a single text where *teleios* means flawless self-management. See [[The Therefore in Matthew 5.48 Ties Perfection to Loving Enemies]].
