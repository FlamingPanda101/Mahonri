# Luke 6:36 and Sinaiticus Show Matthew 5:48 Belongs to the Love Pericope

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] gives three converging lines of evidence that [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] is the capstone of the *mini love-sermon* (vv. 43–48), **not** the climax of the whole Sermon on the Mount: its mid-sermon placement, the Lukan parallel, and the paragraph demarcation in Codex Sinaiticus. All three locate "be perfect" inside the enemy-love unit (see [[The Therefore in Matthew 5.48 Ties Perfection to Loving Enemies]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Placement (rated: strong, structural).** If Jesus meant 5:48 to crown the entire Christian worldview, "he would have put it at the end of chapter 7, not right in the middle." He is "not done yet" — the Sermon runs through Matthew 7. A capstone does not sit in the middle of the speech.[^1]

2. **The Lukan parallel (rated: strong, source-critical).** [[Luke 6.36|Luke 6:36]] — "Be ye therefore **merciful**, as your Father also is merciful" — is the parallel to Matthew 5:48, and Luke keeps the saying *inside* a love-context, "sandwiched" between the love-your-enemies material and the Golden Rule, rather than building a longer sermon around it. Matthew's *teleios* and Luke's "merciful" render the same logion, so "perfect" ≈ "merciful / wholly loving."[^2] (Hatch notes [[Luke 6.40|Luke 6:40]], "everyone that is perfect shall be as his master," reads just like Matthew 5:48 when isolated — illustrating how easily such verses get pulled out of context.)

3. **Codex Sinaiticus (rated: strong, manuscript).** Hatch asked a textual/scribal scholar to pull up Codex Sinaiticus (the earliest complete New Testament, 4th c.). The punctuation and **paragraph demarcation** group vv. 43 and 48 "together in one block... one thought" — an ancient reader's structural judgment that the verses form a single unit.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A harmonizing reader could push back: Matthew and Luke arrange Jesus's sayings differently throughout (the "Sermon on the Mount" vs the "Sermon on the Plain"), so Luke's placement does not dictate Matthew's intent; Matthew may well have *promoted* the saying to a thematic hinge between the antitheses (ch. 5) and the piety instructions (ch. 6). Paragraph divisions in Sinaiticus reflect a 4th-century scribe's reading, not necessarily the evangelist's. So the evidence is suggestive rather than decisive. Still, the convergence of three independent lines (placement, parallel, manuscript) makes the love-pericope reading the more economical one.

## Counter-Objection
"Sinaiticus is centuries later than Matthew — why trust its paragraphing?" Hatch's reply: it is not the *proof* but one corroborating witness; the decisive evidence is internal (the "therefore" and the Lukan "merciful"), with the manuscript demarcation showing the earliest readers already grouped the verses as one thought.

## Best Quotes
> "If Jesus wanted this verse to... be the culmination of his entire sermon then he would have put it at the end of chapter 7, not right in the middle."[^1]

> "Luke 6:36 is the parallel to Matthew 5:48... it says be therefore merciful."[^2]

> "The punctuation and the paragraph demarcation... shows that verses 43 and 48 are together in one block... it's one thought."[^3]
