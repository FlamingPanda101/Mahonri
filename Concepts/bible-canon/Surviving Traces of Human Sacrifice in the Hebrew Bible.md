# Surviving Traces of Human Sacrifice in the Hebrew Bible

## The Claim

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] argues the Hebrew Bible preserves traces of an older practice of human/child sacrifice that was later "renegotiated." The clearest is [[Exodus 22.29|Exodus 22:29]]: "the firstborn of your sons you shall give to me... on the eighth day you shall give it to me" — placing the firstborn son in parallel with sacrificial oxen and sheep.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The grammatical parallel with firstling animals.** The firstborn son is treated like a firstling animal ("seven days it shall be with his dam; on the eighth day thou shalt give it me"), and only *elsewhere* is the son ransomed/redeemed.[^1] *(Strong — this is a recognized critical reading; O'Connor notes "I'm not the first person to think that verse is a command for human sacrifice that survives into the biblical tradition that is then renegotiated.")*

2. **The Binding of Isaac as encoded transition.** O'Connor: "many scholars see [[Genesis 22|Genesis 22]] as a mythological retelling of how human sacrifice came to an end — replaced by animal sacrifice."[^2] *(Moderate — a real scholarly reading (e.g. the substitution motif), though Scrivener notes it is not the world the Bible *describes*, where Yahweh says child sacrifice "never entered my mind" (Jer 7:31).)*

3. **The symmetry trap.** O'Connor notes one could lazily run the reverse: Christianity is "predicated on a human sacrifice" (Jesus), and Rome condemned Gaulish human sacrifice (per [[Julius Caesar]]) — so one could "credit Rome" with ending human sacrifice. He calls this "too simple" to make the symmetrical point against Scrivener's method.[^3] *(Effective reductio — see [[Both the Jewish and Roman Worlds Were Brutal]].)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener.** The firstborn was *ransomed* (Ex 13:13; 34:20; Num 18:15-16) — "they were ransomed... the cost of that life was given towards the temple as money." Read narratively, Genesis 22 *abolishes* child sacrifice (the ram substitutes), pointing to Christ. See [[The Narrative Reading of Scripture vs Proof-Texting]].[^4] *(Strong — redemption-of-the-firstborn is explicit in the Torah; the dispute is whether Ex 22:29 is a *survival* of an earlier stratum or always implied the ransom.)* Scrivener closed the episode promising to "think very very much harder about Exodus 22:29," conceding "it probably was [a sacrifice command], but it's easy enough to say it's not."[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "In Exodus 22:29 there's this very strange injunction to sacrifice your children to God, which is immediately renegotiated."[^1]

> "Many scholars see this as a mythological retelling of the story by which human sacrifice came to an end."[^2]

> "I'm going to go back and think very very much harder about... Exodus 22:29. I think it probably was, but it's easy enough to say it's not."[^5]
