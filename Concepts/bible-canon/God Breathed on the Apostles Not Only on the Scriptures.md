# God Breathed on the Apostles Not Only on the Scriptures

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Protestants press [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Tim 3:16]] — "all scripture is **God-breathed** (*theopneustos*)" — as the charter of sola scriptura. [[Shiloh Lofgren]] turns the metaphor: God also literally **breathed on people**. In [[John 20.17|John 20:21-23]] the risen Christ "breathed on" his apostles, said "Receive the Holy Spirit," and gave them authority to forgive or retain sins. If "God-breathed" confers infallible authority on a text, the same divine in-breathing — on persons — confers living authority on the apostolic church, so the breath of God is not confined to the page.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The wordplay is exegetically pointed — rate: moderate-strong
"Protestants love to say that scripture is God-breathed, which of course it is. But you know what else? God breathed on people — his apostles ... 'Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven; if you withhold forgiveness, it is withheld.'"[^1] The same Johannine verb (*enephysēsen*, evoking Gen 2:7 and the new creation) puts the divine breath on *commissioned persons*, not only on Scripture. **Steelman of [[Timothy J. Whitehead]]:** the in-breathing on the apostles was *inspiration/commission* belonging to the foundational era, later inscripturated; it does not establish a perpetual, post-apostolic office. *Reply:* that limits the breath to the apostolic moment by assumption; the text attaches ongoing binding/loosing authority (cf. [[Matthew 18.15-17|Matthew 18:18]]) to the church.

### 2. Believers are subject to the church for the forgiveness of sins — rate: strong (the uncomfortable datum)
Lofgren: Protestants "get extremely fidgety when verses start coming up about believers being subject to the church for the forgiveness of their sins." All forgiveness is through Christ's grace, "however Jesus can mediate the giving of that grace however he chooses" — and he chose to authorize servants: "If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven." "That does not sound like believers are bound only to answer to the Bible. That sounds like they answer to the church."[^2] **Steelman of Whitehead:** the keys/forgiveness language is declarative (announcing the gospel's terms) rather than a priestly power, and is exercised *by* the gospel Scripture contains. *Reply:* the grant is to persons ("if **you** forgive"), which at minimum shows authority vested in living representatives, not in a text alone.

### 3. It dovetails with the "no commissioned writing" point — rate: moderate
The in-breathing scene is a *commissioning* ("as the Father has sent me, even so I send you"), not a command to write — reinforcing that Christ founded an authorized people, the church, whose writings followed. See [[The Church Is the Pillar of Truth and Scripture Is Its Byproduct]]. **Steelman of Whitehead:** commissioning to preach the infallible gospel and inscripturating that gospel are the same deposit; the apostles' *written* word is where their breath-borne authority is now found — which is the heart of [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]].

**Net assessment.** The argument does not by itself prove an *ongoing* office (Whitehead's cessation case must be met on covenant grounds), but it effectively blocks the inference "God-breathed text ⟹ text-only authority": the same idiom of divine breath lands on apostolic *persons* with real, sin-remitting authority. It pairs with the broader rebuttal that weaponizing [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Tim 3:16]] proves too little — "profitable for" is not "sufficient as the sole rule." See [[Keystone - This Is Why Weaponizing 2 Timothy 3-16 Is a Problem]].
