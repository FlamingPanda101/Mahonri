# The Truth-Knowledge Distinction Protects an Infallible Standard

## The Claim
Reverend Dr. [[Timothy J. Whitehead]] opens his affirmative case for sola scriptura with a presuppositional distinction: **truth** is infallible and comes from God directly (it flows from the *logos*, Christ who is "the truth," [[John 14.6|John 14:6]]); **knowledge** is our fallible apprehension of that truth. Truth "is not error and therefore it cannot err"; but our senses and reasoning, "affected by sin, are not infallible." Sola scriptura, he argues, exists precisely to *protect this distinction* — to keep one infallible standard (God's word) above all fallible interpretations, so that competing readings can be normed rather than relativized.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Truth ≠ knowledge (rated: a coherent presuppositional frame).** "Truth is what comes from God directly. Knowledge is our apprehension of it." There is natural truth (interpreted fallibly by the sciences), moral truth (by the courts), and special revelation (by the church) — but in every case the *interpreter* is fallible while the *truth* is not.[^1] *Rating:* internally consistent; it relocates the whole debate from "is the Bible reliable?" to "which standard is ultimate?"

2. **Without the distinction, truth gets relativized (rated: the load-bearing move).** "When one confuses the two, we either insist an interpreter is infallible — in which case we have competing sources of truth — or we make truth fallible, in which case we lose the necessary infallible standard ... and all competing interpretations become equally valid."[^2] Sola scriptura keeps a fixed, infallible target so disagreement can be adjudicated *against* it. See [[Every Worldview Has a Self-Authenticating Ultimate Standard]].

3. **Inspiration keeps revelation out of the fallible-interpretation category (rated: the canon link).** Whitehead reads [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:20-21]] ("no prophecy of scripture comes from the prophet's own interpretation ... men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit") to mean inspiration is *not* a two-step "God gives ideas, prophet interprets" process; God superintends so that "what came out of the prophet's mouth was exactly what God intended."[^3] This is why he can place Scripture on the "truth" side and every human teaching tradition on the "knowledge" side.

4. **It frames his answer on Christian disagreement (rated: consistent application).** When disagreement among Protestants is raised, Whitehead replies that it does not prove Scripture insufficient — "unless scripture is true and infallible, you do not have a target to aim at to bring about unity." Disagreement indicts fallible *knowledge*, not the infallible *truth*.[^4]

**Steelman of the Latter-day Saint counter ([[Shiloh Lofgren]]).** The distinction proves too little for sola scriptura: granting that *truth* is infallible, the **Bible we actually possess and read is on the "knowledge" side** — it is a transmitted, translated, interpreted text, not the unmediated *logos*. Lofgren's four-guarantees argument (language, transmission, translation, interpretation must each be infallible) targets exactly this gap; the Johannine Comma shows fallible "knowledge" masquerading as infallible "truth" for centuries (see [[An Infallible Autograph We No Longer Possess Cannot Be the Rule of Faith]]). Moreover, if every interpreter is fallible and there is no living authority to adjudicate, then in practice each reader's *interpretation* becomes the operative rule — the very relativization Whitehead fears (cf. [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]). Whitehead's reply is that a living "infallible interpreter" merely multiplies competing infallible sources; the distinction is meant to be held by faith in Scripture's self-witness (see [[Every Worldview Has a Self-Authenticating Ultimate Standard]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Truth is not error, and therefore it cannot err ... but these [senses and minds], affected by sin, are not infallible. So we must maintain this basic epistemological distinction between truth and knowledge."[^1]

> "When you confuse the two ... we either insist an interpreter is infallible ... or we make truth fallible, in which case we lose the necessary infallible standard."[^2]
