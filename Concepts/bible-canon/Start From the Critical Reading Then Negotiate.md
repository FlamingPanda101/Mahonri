# Start From the Critical Reading Then Negotiate

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] prescribes an *order of operations* for hard biblical texts: **begin from the
historical-critical reading**, honestly acknowledging what the text meant (including that it
advocates genocide, slavery, or beating children), and *then* use that as a springboard for a
productive, inspiring reading. The opposite order — starting with "it doesn't actually mean any of
that" — is dangerous, because when believers later discover the real meaning they conclude "I've
been lied to… and I don't have the tools to be able to deal with that."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Pre-softening backfires — rate: strong
On Proverbs' "rod": if you start with "no, it just means a shepherd's crook," a person who is glad
the passage isn't harmful will eventually "find out the reality is that that is not an accurate
reading. Oh, I've been lied to. Oh, it actually is harmful and I don't have the tools to deal with
that."[^1] The harm is downstream and pastoral, not merely academic. This is the constructive
complement to [[We Negotiate With the Text - It Does Not Speak for Itself]] — negotiation is fine,
*concealment* is not.

### 2. The genocidal passages — rate: strong
"The best thing is to acknowledge, hey, this is advocating for genocide in its original reading" —
because it was written by "imperfect humans… under the thumb of larger empires… fantasizing about
what if we were the ones beating up on the empire." Then "use that as a springboard for an actual
productive reading."[^2] This presupposes
[[Rejecting Biblical Inerrancy Solves the Moral Contradiction Problem|rejecting inerrancy]]: "there's
nothing wrong with acknowledging that they were just wrong."[^3] Compare the LDS-prophet version,
[[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].

### 3. McClellan helped build this into a Come Follow Me manual — rate: strong (first-hand)
Teaching Old Testament gospel doctrine four years prior, he "helped put together the Come Follow Me
manual," getting in "a couple of pages on the historical parts of the Old Testament… these are texts
written by imperfect humans and not all of it's accurate" — ethnocentric, misogynist in places.[^4]
He reports mission presidents telling him this approach "allows me to engage with the Old Testament
in an entirely new and productive way." **Steelman of a devotional reader:** one might fear that
foregrounding "this advocates genocide" damages testimony more than a gentle reading; McClellan's
counter is empirical — the gentle reading is a time-bomb, whereas honesty "gives people a slot" for
the uncomfortable material and lets them keep engaging rather than "shut it all off."

## Best Quotes
> "I think it can be problematic when we start with, oh, it doesn't actually mean any of that… and then they go find out the reality is that that is not an accurate reading. Oh, I've been lied to."[^1]

> "The best thing is to acknowledge, hey, this is advocating for genocide in its original reading… and then use that as a springboard for an actual productive reading."[^2]

> "There's nothing wrong with acknowledging that they were just wrong."[^3]
