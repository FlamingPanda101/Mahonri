#type/claim

# Christ Restores Suppressed Verses of Micah in Third Nephi

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] offers a Book of Mormon datum as evidence for the "Great Forsaken": when the resurrected Christ teaches the Nephites ([[3 Nephi]] 20–21), he recites the prophecies of [[Micah 5.2|Micah]], and partway through inserts **two verses that are not in the received Book of Micah** before resuming the text — verses about the **sonship of the Messiah** and the **destiny of the Gentiles**, precisely the themes Barnes says the scribes who compiled Micah disliked and excised.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A verifiable interleaving (rate: moderate, textual claim).** "Open up Micah chapter 5 and open up 3 Nephi 20 and 21 ... you can read it and see where they've taken individual lines out of the text." Christ quotes Micah verse by verse, then "recites two verses that aren't in Micah, and then he picks back up again and carries on like nothing," reciting them "as if that's what Micah should have been like."[^2]

2. **The restored content matches the alleged suppression (rate: moderate, thematic fit).** "The verses that he reinserts into Micah are about the sonship of the Messiah and the destiny of the Gentiles — two things hated by the dominant reformers and scribes who compiled Micah. They didn't like those verses so they took them out, and here the Savior is restoring them for the Nephites."[^3]

3. **It coheres with the broader case (rate: moderate, coheres).** This dovetails with [[Jesus Quotes Deuteronomy Against the Deuteronomists]] (Christ's corrective stance toward the scribal canon) and the olive-tree/Gentile-grafting theme of [[Jacob 5]] that Barnes says the scribes cut. Micah and Isaiah, per apocryphal tradition, were "murdered for preaching messianically."[^4]

## Steelman of the Critic
The argument assumes its conclusion at the key step. (a) A critic reads the same data the opposite way: 3 Nephi is a 19th-century text that *expands* Micah, so the "extra verses" are evidence of Joseph Smith **adding** to Micah, not of scribes having **removed** them — the direction of dependence is exactly what is disputed. (b) There is no manuscript or textual-critical evidence (no LXX, Qumran, or variant tradition) that Micah ever contained these lines, so "they took them out" is an inference from the Book of Mormon's truth, not independent corroboration — it is circular as an argument *for* the Book of Mormon. (c) Christ freely interweaving exposition with quotation is ordinary midrashic/homiletic practice and need not imply the surrounding text was corrupted. The honest status: this is a *coherent internal* reading that *presupposes* the Restoration, not a neutral proof of scribal excision; its evidential value depends on prior credence in the Book of Mormon.
