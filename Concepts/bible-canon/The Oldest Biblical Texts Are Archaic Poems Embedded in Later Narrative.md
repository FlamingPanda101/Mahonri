# The Oldest Biblical Texts Are Archaic Poems Embedded in Later Narrative

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] explains (per the session summary) that the notion of an ancient, fixed, God-dictated text is mistaken, and lays out the actual textual timeline:

- **Language floor.** Hebrew did not emerge as a distinct language until roughly **1100 BCE**, so *no* biblical text can be older than that.
- **Oldest material.** The oldest surviving parts of the Bible are not whole books but **fragmented poems embedded in later narratives**: the [[Judges 5|Song of Deborah]] (Judges 5), the [[Exodus 15|Song of the Sea]] (Exodus 15), and the [[Deuteronomy 32|Song of Moses]] (Deuteronomy 32).
- **Earliest prose.** The earliest narrative *historical prose* was written in the **Northern Kingdom of Israel in the 9th–8th century BCE** — centuries after the events many texts purport to describe.
- **Manuscripts.** The base text for modern translations is the Leningrad Codex (~1008 CE), supplemented by the Dead Sea Scrolls (~250 BCE–60 CE) and the Greek Septuagint (~250 BCE) — there is no autograph and nothing remotely contemporary with Moses (see [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]]).

The upshot: the Bible's oldest layer is poetry preserved inside prose that was composed much later, and the "closed canon dictated by God" picture is false.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Archaic poetry is a mainstream category.** That these three poems (with a handful of others) are the oldest stratum of biblical Hebrew is a long-standing position in the field, argued on **linguistic and metrical grounds** — the poems' grammar and vocabulary are demonstrably older than the prose that frames them. McClellan is reporting consensus, not innovating.
2. **The poems-inside-prose pattern signals composite formation.** If the oldest material is poetry quoted within later narrative, then the narratives are *later compositions that preserved older songs* — the same compositeness the vault documents for the Psalms (see [[The Psalms Predate and Were Not Rewritten by the Deuteronomists]]) and for the histories. The text is an anthology assembled over centuries, not a single dictated whole.
3. **The Hebrew-language floor bounds every "ancient" claim.** Because Hebrew as such postdates ~1100 BCE, any apologetic appeal to a text older than that is ruled out at the level of historical linguistics — which connects to McClellan's broader burden-of-proof point that **change, not stasis, is the default** for societies and their texts (see [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]).

**Steelman of the traditional view.** A conservative can grant that the *written* form of these poems is old while holding that they preserve genuinely *ancient oral tradition* reaching back to the events — so early dating of the poetry can actually support antiquity of the underlying memory, not undercut it. And precise dating of archaic poetry is contested at the edges (some scholars compress or stretch the range). McClellan's reply (per the summary) is methodological: the question is what the *evidence* supports as the date of composition, and the data place even the oldest material well after a Hebrew language that did not exist before ~1100 BCE — so the dictated-by-God-from-the-beginning picture cannot stand whatever one thinks about oral prehistory.

## Apologetic Engagement
This pressures any apologetic that leans on the antiquity or autograph-purity of the Old Testament text. There is no Mosaic autograph; the oldest layer is embedded poetry, the earliest prose is centuries post-event northern writing, and the base manuscripts are medieval. An inerrancy case that needs an early, stable, singular text has to be argued against this timeline rather than assumed over it. (It also reframes "the Bible is the oldest book" rhetoric: the *material* is old in places, but as a bounded book it is late.)

## LDS Relevance
The composite, layered picture is congenial to the Restoration's account of scripture as humanly transmitted and editable. If the oldest stratum is older poetry preserved inside later prose — and the histories around it are northern compositions later appropriated and edited in Judah — then the claim that **earlier, "plainer" material was reframed by later editors** is exactly the mechanism LDS apologists invoke for the Deuteronomistic reform and the Book of Mormon's pre-exilic theology. McClellan draws no LDS conclusion (he is non-confessional), but his timeline removes the assumption of a pristine, fixed Old Testament against which the Book of Mormon's differences would look anomalous.
