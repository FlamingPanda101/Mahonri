# The Judean Pillar Figurines Were the Smashed Mother in the First Temple

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] argues that the single most common class of first-temple-period archaeological find around Jerusalem and Judah is itself the buried evidence of a suppressed **mother-goddess**, "the great lady." These are the **Judean pillar figurines** — thousands of them — depicting a **nursing mother with big eyes and large breasts**, the lower body tapering to a cone (made to stand on something), *not* an explicit fertility idol. [^1] Barker's reading of their treatment is the crux: they "have been a bit of an embarrassment" to Bible-based archaeology, first thrown in the trash as "nothing to do with the Bible," then explained away as "dolls" or evidence of a pagan "mass falling away" — until it "began to dawn that this lady had a major place in the first temple." Many were found **systematically smashed**, "which suggested that there had been a concerted effort to destroy her." [^2] The question that launches her whole project: "who was she, whom did she offend, that she ended up written out of the way we read the Bible?" — and Barker ties this physical Lady to the philological one, the **breasted God** *El Shaddai* (see [[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]]), of whom "we found hundreds." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The figurines are real and abundant.** Judean (Judahite) pillar figurines are a well-documented eighth–sixth-century BCE phenomenon; hundreds-to-thousands have been excavated in and around Jerusalem and Judah, overwhelmingly female with emphasized breasts. The *quantity and findspots* near the temple are genuine data, not Barker's invention. *Rating: solid archaeological fact.* [^1]
2. **Many show deliberate breakage.** Intentional decapitation/smashing of cultic figurines is attested in the record and is plausibly tied to a reform program; reading systematic breakage as a *purge* of a female cult object is a defensible (if interpretive) inference. *Rating: the breakage is real; "concerted campaign" is a reasonable reading.* [^2]
3. **It dovetails with the broader Asherah dossier.** The vault already documents the suppressed mother in [[Yahweh and His Asherah Inscriptions]], [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight]], and [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]] — so Barker's figurine argument is one strand of a converging, partly non-LDS case. *Rating: strong cumulative fit.*
4. **It supplies a material anchor for the linguistic claim.** Pairing the *physical* breasted figurine with the *lexical* "breasted God" ([[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]]) makes the divine-feminine reading harder to dismiss as mere wordplay. *Rating: mutually reinforcing.* [^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The dominant scholarly interpretation of the pillar figurines is **contested and cautious**: many archaeologists read them as **domestic/folk fertility or lactation amulets** (household magic for nursing mothers), *not* as cult statues of a temple goddess — their overwhelmingly *domestic* findspots arguably cut against a "major place in the first temple." The identification with **Asherah specifically** is debated; some scholars decline to name the figure at all. "Systematically smashed... concerted effort" can be over-read: figurines break, get discarded, and end up in fill for many reasons, so deliberate iconoclasm is one explanation among several. And the leap from "a female figurine cult existed in Judah" to "the *temple* enshrined a Mother who was *edited out of the Bible*" is Barker's larger reconstruction, a minority thesis. A measured reply: Barker's case does not rest on the figurines alone but on their *convergence* with the breasts-and-womb blessing of [[Genesis 49.25|Genesis 49:25]], the *El Shaddai* etymology, and the bush-lady survivals (see [[Deborah and Asenath Preserve the Lady of the Bush]]); even granting a domestic function, the sheer volume near Jerusalem and the evidence of deliberate destruction fit a reform that targeted female imagery, which is exactly what the Asherah-purge texts describe.
