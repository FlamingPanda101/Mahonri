# Monasteries Preserved and Transmitted Scripture

## The Point

[[Miranda Wilcox]] credits medieval monasteries with preserving and transmitting scripture by hand — one of the four achievements for which she says medieval Christians should be revered. [^1] Because performing the scripture-based liturgy "required literacy," religious communities "became centers of education, preserving and transmitting scriptural and cultural traditions." [^2] The exemplar is [[Bede]]'s monastery at Wearmouth-Jarrow.

## Evidence

1. **Wearmouth-Jarrow produced the oldest complete Vulgate.** Bede's monastery "produced the oldest surviving copy of the complete Vulgate, Jerome's fifth-century Latin translation of the Bible" — the **Codex Amiatinus**, which included illustrations such as Christ in Majesty "at the junction between the Old and New Testaments." [^3]
2. **A staggering physical artifact.** Bede's abbot "intended to take the seventy-five-pound Bible, now known as the Codex Amiatinus, to the shrine of Peter the Apostle in Rome but died on the long journey." [^3]
3. **Monastic life centered on the Bible.** The "primary focus of Bede's life and that of his fellow monks... was 'the study of the Bible and the integration of its lessons to Christian life.'" [^3]
4. **Lay piety funded the sacred environment.** By the late Middle Ages laity took "responsibility to fund, make, donate, and maintain the sacred environment and objects" — "churchkeeping" became "a mark of lay piety." [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "They preserved and transmitted scripture." [^1]

> "Their faith motivated them to transmit sacred scriptures by hand and to preserve Christian worship, education, and hospice amid the dynastic political turmoil of their kingdoms." [^5]
