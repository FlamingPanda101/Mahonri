# The "We" of John 21:24 — A Single-Author Explanation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[John 21.24|John 21:24]] — "This is the disciple... who wrote these things, **and we know that his testimony is true**" — shows that the Gospel was completed or vouched for by a **group** (a "we," perhaps the Johannine community or an editorial circle), not a single author. The sudden shift from "he" to "we" betrays multiple hands.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The verse is stitched from earlier verses of John
[[Hugo Mendez]] argues (at length in his book, chapter 4) that 21:24 is "very clearly modeled on a few other verses in the Gospel of John," and that the author has "smooshed together language from different parts of [the] gospel" — some where he refers to himself in the third person, some where he uses the first-person plural. *(Strong; an internal-evidence, source-of-the-phrase argument.)* [^1]

### 2. The model verse is John 1:14's "we have seen his glory"
One of the verses 21:24 answers to is [[John 1.14|John 1:14]], where the author already uses "we" ("we have seen his glory") while being closely identified with the Beloved Disciple. The "we" is thus a known Johannine self-reference, not a new editorial voice. *(Strong; ties the "we" to the author's own established idiom.)* [^2]

### 3. Ancient narrators routinely move between he / we / I
The very next line, 21:25, shifts again to first-person *singular* ("I suppose"). Ancient writers — Méndez cites [[Polybius]] and [[Josephus]] — regularly slide between "he," "we," and "I" when speaking of themselves as narrators, sometimes within very tight spaces. *(Strong; comparative evidence from primary historians.)* [^3]

### 4. Therefore the traditional single-author reading is "close to the truth"
Given the modeling, the Johannine "we" idiom, and the comparative narratorial practice, Méndez concludes the long-standing single-author interpretation — "we language be damned" — is essentially correct. *(Conclusion; note he still treats chapter 21 itself as a later addition — the single author is the author of that material too, not a committee.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Many capable scholars read the "we" as a genuine editorial/community endorsement: it is the most natural sense of "*we* know his testimony is true" appended to a description of "*the* disciple," and it coheres with the independent evidence that chapter 21 is a later addition. The he→we→I sequence can be read as an editor speaking about the author (he), on behalf of a circle (we), and then in his own voice (I).

## Best Quotes
> "That verse... is very clearly modeled on a few other verses in the Gospel of John, and one of them is 1:14 where the author... has also used we language: 'we have seen his glory.'" — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^2]

> "We see this in Polybius. We see this in Josephus. We see writers who kind of moved between he, we, I language... when speaking just about themselves as narrators, sometimes in really tight spaces." [^3]

> "The traditional interpretation that suggested there's just one author for this entire book — we language be damned — I think is actually close to the truth." [^4]
