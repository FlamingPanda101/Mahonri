#type/claim

# Scribalism Is the Idolatry of an Infallible Book

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] names the deep error behind the canon wars **"scribalism"**: the craving for a single, infallible, self-sufficient book that one can read and obey without prophets, priesthood, or living revelation — "an idol made of ink and paper." On this view the real enemy is not "which book is better" (Bible vs Book of Mormon) but *book-worship itself*; even a perfectly transmitted canon, "if they got it all right," would still be an idol if it displaces a living relationship with God.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The scribes wanted a manual that replaces God (rate: moderate, framing).** After the exile the scribes produced the Deuteronomistic History whose theme is "listen to the scribes, obey the law ... all you need is us." Its appeal is a book "you can just read and never question — you don't have to pray, you don't need a relationship with the Messiah, you don't need to listen to any priesthood authorities." That is "scribalism." See [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes]].[^2]

2. **An idol of ink is no better than one of wood (rate: moderate, theological).** "Abraham chopped up idols made of wood and stone; building one of paper and ink is no better — it's still an idol. Books can't save you; they never could." Even granting the Catholics "canonized everything perfectly ... it doesn't matter; it's ink." The thing needed "is a relationship with Jesus Christ." See [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]].[^3]

3. **Christ left no book and named no canon (rate: moderate, observation).** "When Jesus was on the earth, Jesus didn't write a book ... nor did he list the canon." What he did was criticize the *scribes* — the custodians of the written law — which signals where ultimate authority does *not* lie. The "rock" of Matthew 16 is "living revelation," not ink.[^4]

4. **Joseph Smith deliberately did not canonize an infallible Bible (rate: moderate, historical).** Joseph made only one substantive change to Deuteronomy in his translation (Moses translated, not buried) and "basically never touched it"; tellingly, the church does *not* canonize the Joseph Smith Translation as a trustworthy-100% text. That restraint "is very wise" — it refuses the scribal demand for a worship-object book. See [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
"Scribalism" risks becoming a slur that dodges a real epistemic need. (a) Canons were created precisely to give a **public standard** by which to test competing truth-claims and false prophets; calling that "idolatry" leaves the believer with no shared check against "I feel God wants me to..." subjectivism — Hansen himself raises this worry in the dialogue (answered by [[No Infallible Source Only Reliable Guides]]). (b) Most defenders of biblical inerrancy do **not** literally worship the book or deny relationship with Christ; the charge attacks a caricature (bibliolatry) rather than the actual doctrine of Scripture's authority. (c) Replacing "the book is infallible" with "the living prophet is the rock" can simply relocate the idol — Barnes concedes "don't replace it with an idol of a prophet ... Russell Nelson is not God." (d) Sola scriptura proponents argue a fixed text *constrains* leaders, preventing exactly the unbounded ecclesiastical authority an open/living model risks. The defensible core is the theological point that no text is self-interpreting or self-authenticating and that authority ultimately rests in God; the overreach is treating every high view of Scripture as idol-worship.
