# John's Pervasive Symbolism

## The Claim
The Gospel of John takes the occasional symbolic technique found in the Synoptics and **runs with it across the entire gospel**: nearly every story — miraculous and non-miraculous, memorable and "transitional" — is turned into a symbol embodying a key idea of Jesus' teaching. Understanding John's symbolism requires understanding all of John's theology.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The technique is inherited from Mark, then amplified.** Mark occasionally lets an action symbolize teaching (Jesus flipping the temple tables ~ the temple's coming destruction). John's author "took this literary device and decided to run with it across the gospel." *(Strong; identifies the source and the escalation.)* [^1]
2. **Key words bleed from teaching into narrative.** Jesus' signature terms — believe, life, dwell/abide — recur inside the *stories*, so the miracles function "as sort of parables," with Jesus' gestures representing ideas in his teaching. *(Strong; a concrete linguistic mechanism.)* [^2]
3. **"They didn't understand" is a tell.** When Jesus acts (e.g. washing the disciples' feet, John 13) the text says the disciples "don't get it" — precisely because the gesture is encoding the key words of his teaching, signaling a symbolic layer to the reader. *(Moderate-strong; an internal narrative marker of symbolism.)* [^3]
4. **The book's chapter 3 works through it story by story.** Méndez's favorite chapter goes story by story, including ones "scholars haven't even considered yet," showing John's theology expressed "through the gestures of Jesus" — extending recognized symbolism into new places. *(Context; the scholarly contribution claimed.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A maximalist "everything is symbolic" reading risks over-reading: critics may hold that John mixes genuinely symbolic "signs" (sēmeia) with straightforward narrative, and that pressing every transitional passage into symbolism imposes a pattern rather than finding one. Méndez himself frames this as going "beyond some current scholarship," i.e. a contested extension, not a settled result.

## Best Quotes
> "That author took this literary device and decided to run with it across the gospel... nearly every story in the Gospel of John, the miraculous ones and the non-miraculous ones... have been turned into symbols." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^1]

> "These stories of Jesus' miracles weave in these key words in ways that turn the miracles themselves into, if you will, sort of parables." [^2]

> "I go story by story in the Gospel of John... and I show how everything we... know about the theology of John is actually expressed through the gestures of Jesus." [^4]
