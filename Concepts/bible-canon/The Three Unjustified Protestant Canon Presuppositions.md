# The Three Unjustified Protestant Canon Presuppositions

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Protestant objection to the Church is grounded in the assumption that the **modern 66-book canon is infallible, sufficient, and the final authority** for faith, practice, and morals — and that the Church therefore fails this standard.[^1] [[Hayden Carroll]] argues all three properties are *unjustified presuppositions* smuggled into critiques: God never publicly designated this canon (authorization), never said it is all we need (sufficiency / closed canon), and never declared it the final authority (ultimacy).[^2] He adds a fourth downstream problem — **interpretation** — even a perfect text needs a final interpreter.[^3]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Three distinct claims, three distinct burdens — rate: strong (as taxonomy)
Carroll's analytic separation is genuinely clarifying: *infallibility* (the text cannot err), *sufficiency* (a closed canon containing everything needed), and *ultimacy / final authority* (the apex standard over all rival authorities) are logically independent. A book can be reliable without being infallible, infallible without being sufficient, and sufficient without being ultimate. By splitting them, Carroll forces the Protestant to justify each separately. This overlaps the existing [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]] and [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]]. **Steelman of the Protestant:** the Reformed reply (Westminster Confession 1.5) is that the Bible's authority rests not on church or proof but on the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit — God *self-authenticates* the canon to the believer, so no external public designation is required. Carroll treats this (via [[Daniel Constantino]]'s "God makes us believe") as admitted circularity.[^4] See [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture]].

### 2. The diagnostic questions — rate: strong rhetorically, contestable theologically
Carroll's three set-piece questions: "Where and when did God say your canon is **infallible** / is **all we need** / is the **final authority**?"[^5] He claims these are "impossible for Protestants to answer" while "Catholics will answer it" (by appeal to Church authority — hence they get treated differently).[^6] **Scholarly context:** this is the well-known *canon problem* — the list of canonical books is not itself in any book (cf. the Catholic apologetic of Trent Horn, Jimmy Akin; the Protestant replies of Michael Kruger's *Canon Revisited* arguing for self-authentication and providential recognition). Carroll's framing is fair to the structure of the debate but presents the Protestant side at its weakest (bare assertion) rather than Kruger's more developed recognition model.

### 3. The interpretation backstop — rate: strong
Even granting infallibility/sufficiency/ultimacy, Carroll presses: *who is the final interpreter* when sincere readers disagree? He recounts cornering Apologia Church members who said "scripture interprets scripture," until a Calvinist named Brad admitted "[[Jeff Durbin]] gets the final say — it's his church."[^7] This exposes a *de facto* human magisterium behind the "scripture alone" claim. See [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]. This is among the strongest moves in the course because it is empirical, not merely logical.

### 4. The common-ground hook — rate: moderate
Carroll notes the questions work *because* they share Protestant premises: both sides agree "we only have to believe what God has said." So if God didn't say the canon is infallible/sufficient/ultimate, the Protestant's *own* principle undercuts the claim.[^8] **Caution:** the LDS positive alternative (living prophets) faces the symmetric demand — *where did God publicly designate the modern First Presidency as the final authority?* — which Carroll answers elsewhere by appeal to the Book of Mormon and the historical pattern of prophets, not by conceding the parallel. See [[The Three Unjustified Protestant Canon Presuppositions]] companion [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]].

## Best Quotes
> "Their modern 66-book canon Bible is infallible, sufficient, and the final authority for Christian faith, practice, and morals. They presuppose that. The question is, do they justify it?"[^1]

> "Where and when did God say that your canon is infallible? This is an impossible question for Protestants to answer."[^5]

> "They will admit that neither God nor any of his prophets have ever taught what they are presupposing. This is foolproof."[^9]

> "The final hurdle is... who is the final arbiter or adjudicator of the correct interpretation of the text?"[^3]
