# The Death of Judas Contradiction

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] presents the death of Judas as a clear biblical contradiction: [[Matthew 27.3-5|Matthew 27:3–5]] and [[Acts 1.18-19|Acts 1:18–19]] give "entirely different" stories — different manner of death, different purchaser of the field, different timing, and a different reason the plot is called the "Field of Blood." Harmonizing them requires inventing scenarios "not in evidence" and overruling what each text plainly says.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Manner of death differs (rated: strong).** Matthew: Judas "departed, and... went and hanged himself." Acts: "falling headlong, he burst open in the middle and all his bowels gushed out." Nothing in Acts mentions hanging; nothing in Matthew mentions a fall or bursting.[^1]

2. **Who bought the field, and when, differs (rated: strong).** In Acts, *Judas* "acquired a field with the reward of his wickedness" (before his death). In Matthew, Judas throws the silver into the temple and the *chief priests* later buy the potter's field (after his death) "to bury foreigners." Different buyer, different sequence.[^2]

3. **Why it is "Field of Blood" differs (rated: strong).** Acts: named for *Judas's* blood/gore on the field. Matthew: named because it was bought with *blood money* — Jesus's blood. The two etiologies are incompatible.[^2]

4. **Matthew's motive explains the divergence (rated: moderate-strong, redactional).** McClellan notes Matthew "liked to tell the stories... in ways that connected them with things in the Hebrew Bible so he could say they were fulfilling prophecy" (Zechariah 11 / Jeremiah). The shaping is editorial, which is why the details diverge from Acts.[^3]

**Steelman of the harmonizing view.** The classic harmonization: Judas hanged himself, and then the rope or branch broke (or the decomposing body was cut down), so he *also* fell and burst open — and "acquired a field" can be read (Daniel Wallace's middle-voice/causative reading of *ktaomai*) as "the field was acquired *with his money*," i.e., by the priests on his behalf, reconciling the buyer. On this view both accounts are partial-but-true. McClellan grants none of this is strictly *impossible* — but each move is independently implausible and unattested, and stacking them collapses the probability (see [[Stacked Improbable Harmonizations Multiply Down to a Sliver]]). The composite-authorship explanation (two communities telling the story differently) is simpler than a single inerrant account requiring multiple rescues.

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a load-bearing test case for biblical inerrancy. The inerrantist must (a) supply a fall *after* the hanging that neither text mentions, (b) reinterpret "acquired" against its plain sense, and (c) reconcile two different reasons for the field's name — and all three must be true at once. McClellan's point is methodological: if "not impossible" is the bar, nothing is ever a contradiction (see [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]]); the honest reading is that two authors told the story differently.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan is non-confessional and draws no Latter-day Saint conclusion, but the case is congenial to a Restoration epistemology that already **rejects biblical inerrancy** ("as far as it is translated correctly," Article of Faith 8). Latter-day Saints can grant the contradiction without theological cost — the Bible's authority does not rest on flawless harmonization. The caution (cf. [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]]) is even-handedness: the same refusal to "jin up scenarios not in evidence" should apply to harmonizing apparent tensions in Restoration scripture, not only the Bible.

## Best Quotes
> "Entirely different story. The order of events is different. The nature of the events is different. Judas's death is just described with this simple, He went and hanged himself."[^1]

> "The field being called Hakeldama, field of blood, is different from one story to the other. Who purchased the field is different from one story to the other."[^2]

> "The author of Matthew liked to tell the stories he was telling in ways that connected them with things in the Hebrew Bible so that he could say they were fulfilling prophecy."[^3]
