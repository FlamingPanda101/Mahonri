# The Early Church Did Not Use Self-Authentication

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Self-authentication claims the canon is recognizable to Spirit-led believers without church authority (see [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]]). The historical rebuttal: **no one in church history actually determined the canon this way** — the fathers reasoned from church consensus and apostolic authority, never from interior feeling [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The earliest post-apostolic writers barely cite the NT as Scripture — rate: strong
By the era of [[Ignatius of Antioch]] (early 2nd century), after public revelation had closed, authors "barely ever cite the New Testament as scripture" and far more often quote the Old Testament as Scripture [^1]. If the NT canon were self-evidently divine, the earliest readers would already treat these books as Scripture; they did not. **Steelman:** absence of *citation as Scripture* is partly a function of genre and the ongoing living memory of apostolic teaching — but it still cuts against the claim of immediate, obvious recognizability.

### 2. McDonald: the canon was open into the 4th–5th centuries — rate: strong (Protestant scholar)
Baptist canon scholar [[Lee Martin McDonald]]: "The notion of a closed New Testament canon was not a second century development... and there were still considerable differences of opinion about what should comprise that canon even in the fourth and fifth centuries" [^2]. A Protestant authority concedes the disagreement the self-authentication thesis cannot accommodate.

### 3. Augustine decided by church consensus, not feeling — rate: strong
[[Augustine]], in *On Christian Doctrine*, gives explicit rules: follow the books accepted by the *greater number* of churches and by churches of *greater authority* (those founded by apostles). "He doesn't say, just search your feelings and you'll know it to be true... He's clearly acknowledging that there are discrepancies" and resolving them by ecclesial authority [^3].

### 4. Even Jerome, the skeptic of the deuterocanon, did not appeal to feelings — rate: strong
[[Jerome]] is "the one exceptional case" who doubted the deuterocanon, yet he reasoned from the *Jewish authorities* in his region, not from self-evidence or an interior witness. So even the father Protestants most want to cite "is not pro self-authentication" [^4]. See [[The Church Once Accepted Books It Later Rejected]].

## Best Quotes
> "Here you have authors that barely ever cite the New Testament as scripture. They far more likely cite the Old Testament as scripture." [^1]

> "[Augustine] doesn't say, just search your feelings and you'll know it to be true... He's clearly acknowledging that there are discrepancies in which books are and aren't in different Christian canons." [^3]

> "Even Jerome doesn't claim that his view is self-evident from his feelings... He's going off of Jewish authorities in the area where he's at." [^4]

> "Even when you find people who would agree with the 66 books, they don't get there by the same avenue that you're claiming is the real way to get there. Nobody gets there that way." [^5]
