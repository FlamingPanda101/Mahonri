# Genesis 2-19 Pluperfect Is a Test for a Dogmatic Translation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection being rebutted is that mainstream evangelical translations (NIV, ESV, LSB) are faithful renderings. [[Dan McClellan]] offers a single-verse diagnostic: "there's one verse you can look at to tell you if you have a *bad* translation" — [[Genesis 2.18-19|Genesis 2:19]].[^1] The Hebrew says God *formed* the animals from the ground after creating the human, contradicting [[Genesis 1.24-26|Genesis 1]], where animals precede humanity. Dogmatic translations render the verb as a **pluperfect** ("the LORD God *had formed*") to manufacture agreement.[^2] "If your translation uses the pluperfect at the beginning of Genesis 2:19... there's your problem. You got a dogmatic translation."[^3]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The pluperfect is theologically motivated, not grammatically required — rate: strong
The Hebrew *wayyiqtol* (וַיִּצֶר, *wayyitser*) is the standard sequential narrative form; rendering it as a past-perfect ("had formed") is a marked, non-default choice deployed precisely to make Genesis 2 "catch you up on what happened previously" so the animals are already created.[^2] McClellan: "they translate this verb as a pluperfect or a past perfect... and that way they can say, oh, they had already been created."[^2] The NIV, ESV, and **Legacy Standard Bible** all do this.[^3] See [[Sequence of Creation Differs Between Genesis 1 and 2]] and the existing rebuttal [[Genesis 2 Is a Zoomed-In Sixth Day - Rebutted]]. **Steelman of the translators:** Hebrew narrative *can* on occasion bear a pluperfect sense (some grammarians allow it where context demands), so the rendering is not *impossible* — defenders argue context (the prior creation of animals in Gen 1) licenses it. McClellan's rebuttal: that defense *presupposes univocality* — it imports Genesis 1 to override Genesis 2's own sequence, which is begging the question (see [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]]).

### 2. The test is asymmetric and modest — rate: strong
McClellan is careful: the verse "can't tell you you have a *good* translation," only that you have a *bad* one. "If it doesn't say that, it doesn't mean you have a good translation. But if it does say that, you got a bad translation."[^3] This is an honest, falsifiable diagnostic rather than a blanket condemnation.

### 3. The LSB made the NASB worse — rate: strong
The clinching evidence: the **NASB does not** use the pluperfect, but [[John MacArthur]]'s revision, the **Legacy Standard Bible**, *added* it.[^4] "When they revised this, they were like, we're going to add the pluperfect here. We're going to make this translation worse."[^4] A revision that deliberately introduces a harmonizing distortion is transparent proof of dogma overriding philology. See [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text]].
