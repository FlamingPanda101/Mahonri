# The Wise Men Were Bearers of Preserved First-Temple Covenant

## The Claim

The "wise men from the east" of [[Matthew 2.1-12|Matthew 2]] were not Persian astrologers or wealthy Zoroastrians paying a courtesy call on a newborn king. They were **keepers of preserved first-temple covenant knowledge** — a priestly line, descended from those carried to Babylon in the 600 BC captivity, who guarded the original temple "rites, understandings, and prophecies" that were lost to mainstream Judaism. Read through a Restoration lens, they are the eastern counterpart to [[Lehi]]: covenant-bearers who knew the ancient temple, watched for the Messiah who would restore it, and travelled to receive him — not merely to worship, but to perform a sacred ordinance ([[The Gifts of the Magi Were Temple Items for Anointing the High Priest|anointing him as the great High Priest]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Magi" means keepers of secrets, not sorcerers

The Greek *magoi* (KJV "wise men") meant sorcerer/magician/"oriental scientist." Stripped of modern connotation, a *magos* is "somebody who knows the secrets," not a worker of tricks. [[Philo of Alexandria]] described the magi as a priestly class "initiated in divine virtues" — language pointing to something *secret and divine*, i.e. temple initiation, rather than astrology or magic.[^2]

> **Steelman:** The Greek term genuinely carries the sense of an initiated priestly/learned class (cf. its Old Persian background in the Median priestly caste), and Philo is a first-century witness who did associate the magi with a priesthood "initiated" into divine things. The move from "initiated priestly class" to "temple-covenant keepers" is interpretive, but the lexical starting point is real.

### 2. The Babylonian captivity deported temple priests

When Babylon conquered Jerusalem (~600 BC) it carried off the upper classes and the *temple priests* — the men who knew the **first temple** (Solomon's) before it was destroyed and replaced by the Second Temple. These exiles knew the "original temple theology, the original rites and understandings and prophecies." Some of them, on this reading, were "Lehi's" — co-witnesses to the same pre-reform covenant Lehi carried out of Jerusalem. See [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]] and [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]].[^3]

> **Steelman:** It is historically secure that the Babylonian deportations targeted elites and that priestly families (Ezekiel was a priest in exile) were among them, and that exilic/post-exilic Judaism was transformed. That a remnant preserved older temple traditions is exactly the thesis [[Margaret Barker]]'s temple theology defends.

### 3. A concrete diaspora priestly line: the Reish Galuta

A Babylonian Jewish priestly class — the *Reish Galuta* ("Resh") — remained in Babylon after the exile and traced its lineage to King David, claiming to retain the "wisdom of Solomon." Woodmansee uses them as a real-world example of an eastern, Davidic, temple-claiming Jewish community persisting east of Jerusalem into the era of the magi — making "wise men from the east" a plausible designation for such covenant-keepers rather than gentile stargazers.[^4]

### 4. "From the east" can mean "from ancient times"

The Hebrew behind "from the east" (*qedem*) doubles as "from ancient times," so "magi from the east" reads as "keepers of secrets from ancient times." Reinforcing the motif: Eden — the temple prototype (cf. [[Margaret Barker]]) — lay "eastward," and the Messiah is prophesied to enter Jerusalem from the east. Magi, Messiah, and Eden all share the eastern/temple vector.[^5]

### 5. "Wisdom" is temple wisdom, not cleverness

It is not "wise" to ride a camel a thousand miles to see a baby; the magi's "wisdom" is not good judgment or smarts. As "the wisdom of Solomon" was *temple* wisdom (Solomon the temple-builder, not Solomon the clever judge), the magi's wisdom is the same preserved temple knowledge and covenant — the very thing [[Lehi]] said the reformers were losing.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "These were men who had been initiated into the ancient temple rights and the ancient temple covenants that they had preserved through the Babylonian captivity. They had been watching and waiting for a Messiah who would come to restore what they had lost." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^7]

> "You have wise men from the east, wise men from the temple, bringing that to the prophesied Messiah who would restore those ancient covenants." — [[Andrea Woodmansee]][^8]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- [[Bethlehem Birth Is Likely an Invention to Fulfill Prophecy]] — the critical-academic position holds the nativity (and thus the magi narrative) is a post-hoc, prophecy-driven construction; on that view there is no historical magi tradition to decode. This claim is its devotional inverse: it treats Matthew 2 as encoding a real temple-priesthood event.
- The thesis is avowedly speculative — Woodmansee concedes it is a theory "some Bible scholars would probably discount." Mainstream scholarship more often situates the magi as Parthian/Persian (Zoroastrian) figures; the "preserved first-temple covenant" identification is an LDS-inflected reconstruction layered on [[Margaret Barker]]'s contested minority thesis.
