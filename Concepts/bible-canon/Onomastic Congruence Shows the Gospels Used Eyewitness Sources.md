# Onomastic Congruence Shows the Gospels Used Eyewitness Sources

## The Claim
[[Michael Jones]] argues (drawing on [[Luke VanderLaan]] and [[Richard Bauckham]]) that the **distribution of personal names** in the Gospels matches the actual name-frequencies of first-century Palestinian Jews — the most popular real names (Simon, Joseph) are most frequent in the Gospels, mid-tier names mid-frequent, rare names rare. Fiction and later legend cannot reproduce this "onomastic congruence," so the Gospels (Luke especially) drew on genuine eyewitness sources. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Gospels' name-frequencies match the epigraphic/documentary record.** "The most popular names of the time were names like Simon and Joseph, and those are the most popular names in the Gospels too... they have the right distribution of names." VanderLaan's running statistical tests keep confirming the match. *Rating: strong; Bauckham's *Jesus and the Eyewitnesses* established the data, and it is hard to fake.* [^1]
2. **Fiction can't do it; some real histories can.** "Fictional accounts couldn't do that... Plutarch could get onomastic congruence; modern novels can't, ancient novels just couldn't." The point is not that congruence *proves* eyewitness authorship, but that its *presence* tracks authors with real access to the milieu. *Rating: moderate-strong; congruence is necessary-but-not-sufficient evidence of good sourcing.* [^2]
3. **Luke explicitly claims eyewitness sources.** Per VanderLaan's *Living Footnotes on the Gospel of Luke*, "Luke says he interviewed eyewitnesses" ([[Luke 1.1-4|Luke 1:1-4]]), and the Greek shows it: vivid *eyewitness-style* detail ("femitism" / immersive narration) clusters where Jesus interacts with named people, whereas the "we passages" in Acts (where Luke claims firsthand presence) read differently — Luke writes about *received* eyewitness material in the Gospel and *own-experience* material in Acts, in distinguishable hands. *Rating: moderate-strong; a stylistic-internal corroboration.* [^3]
4. **The Amish analogy.** Hansen: a story full of "Jebediah" and "Zedekiah" sounds fake until you learn it is set among the Amish, who use exactly those names — context fixes the distribution. Faking the *right* distribution (correct proportions of common-to-rare names) "would be very hard." *Rating: moderate; illustrative.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Skeptics (cf. [[Gospel of John Is Pseudepigraphal Not Eyewitness]]) reply that onomastic congruence shows the *tradition* originated in first-century Palestine — which almost no one disputes — but not that the *evangelists* were eyewitnesses or even used them directly. A skilled author working from earlier Palestinian sources, or from the same oral milieu, would inherit correct names without personal eyewitness contact. Bauckham's thesis is also contested in degree: some argue the named-vs-unnamed patterns are over-interpreted, and that "congruence" tolerates a range of source models, including non-eyewitness ones.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: the argument is cumulative, not standalone — congruence rules out *late legendary fabrication* (the Ehrman-adjacent picture of Gospels drifting far from the events) and, joined to Luke's explicit eyewitness claim and the stylistic split between Luke and Acts, supports proximity to eyewitness testimony rather than mere milieu. Even granting "tradition is first-century Palestinian," that already defeats the strong skeptical claim that the Gospels are unreliable, audience-driven inventions.

## Best Quotes
> "The most popular names are showing up more frequently in the gospels and the least popular names are showing up less frequently... they have the right naming distribution. You can't get that kind of stuff if you're writing fiction." — [[Michael Jones]] [^1]

> "Luke says he interviewed eyewitnesses... it's not like Luke is just telling us his traveling notes. He's talking to eyewitnesses." — Jones, on VanderLaan [^3]
