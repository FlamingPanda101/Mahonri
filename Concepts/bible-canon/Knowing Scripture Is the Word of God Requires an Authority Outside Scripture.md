# Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The sola-scriptura advocate holds that Scripture alone is the infallible rule of faith. [[Trent Horn]] presses the epistemological question this leaves unanswered: **how do we know Scripture has the quality of being the word of God?** He builds a dilemma whose either horn is fatal to sola scriptura.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Horn's dilemma: external knowledge implies a second infallible authority — rate: strong
"If we know this apart from scripture, such as through sacred tradition or the teaching office of Christ's church, then that means another infallible authority exists apart from scripture, since this authority provides the foundation for believing in an infallible rule like scripture in the first place. But the existence of another infallible rule of faith nullifies the doctrine of sola scriptura."[^1] The logic is clean: the proposition "Scripture is the infallible word of God" must itself be known *infallibly* to ground an infallible rule; if that knowledge comes from tradition/Church, the Protableant has conceded a co-equal infallible authority. This is the same structural argument made about the canon in [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]] and [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]].

### 2. The circular horn: "Scripture says so" — rate: strong, with a twist
A Protestant might answer "We know scripture is the word of God because scripture tells us it is the word of God."[^2] Horn notes this is **circular** (using Scripture's authority to establish Scripture's authority) — but his more lethal point is empirical, not just formal: there is *no* passage that says any human writing is "the word of God" at all.[^2] So the circular escape route does not even have a verse to stand on. See [[No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God]]. **Steelman of the Protestant:** self-attestation is not viciously circular if Scripture is properly basic / self-authenticating (cf. Calvin, Kruger). Horn's prior series rebuts that move in [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]]; here he simply notes the textual data isn't present.

### 3. The Catholic position is internally consistent where the Protestant's is not — rate: framing
Horn's own position embraces the "external authority" horn without embarrassment: "I believe books like Exodus, Matthew, and the other canonical scriptures are the written word of God, but I don't know this truth through scripture alone, which is fine for me as a Catholic, but fatal to a sola scriptura Protestant."[^3] The asymmetry is the whole argument: needing tradition to know Scripture's status is a *feature* for Catholicism and a *contradiction* for sola scriptura. See [[Scripture as Word of God Is Itself a Doctrine of Sacred Tradition]]. **Steelman:** a Protestant can distinguish the *ground* of Scripture's authority (God) from the *means* of recognizing it (a fallible human/historical process) and deny that the means must be infallible. Horn's counter (paralleling Hayden Carroll's debate point) is that a fallible recognition cannot yield an infallibly-known rule.

## Best Quotes
> "If we know this apart from scripture, such as through sacred tradition or the teaching office of Christ's church, then that means another infallible authority exists apart from scripture… But the existence of another infallible rule of faith nullifies the doctrine of sola scriptura."[^1]

> "We know scripture is the word of God because scripture tells us it is the word of God. But aside from being a circular argument, this explanation doesn't work because there is no passage of scripture that says any human writings are the word of God."[^2]

> "I believe books like Exodus, Matthew, and the other canonical scriptures are the written word of God, but I don't know this truth through scripture alone, which is fine for me as a Catholic, but fatal to a sola scriptura Protestant."[^3]
