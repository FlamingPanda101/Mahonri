# Marcion Was Right That a God of Pure Love Cannot Be Reconciled With a Literal Old Testament

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] offers a partial, qualified rehabilitation of [[Marcion]] — "the first great dissenter from Christian orthodoxy," from Pontus in the second century. Marcion held "a lot of screwy ideas" (Givens lists three: he was anti-Semitic; he rejected most of the New Testament; he denied that God or Christ had a physical body). **But** "what I think he was right about" is that "Jesus represents a new revelation of God's love" that "can't be reconciled with the Old Testament... if we take the Old Testament literally." Marcion's solution (throw out the OT) was wrong; his *perception* — that an unqualifiedly loving God sits uneasily with a literally-read OT of "retribution... vengeance... judgment" — Givens treats as a genuine insight that the Marcionite church's two-century survival confirms, "because the idea is so innately appealing."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The appeal was real and durable — rate: accurate (church history).** "The Marcionite Church continues for a couple of centuries because the idea is so innately appealing — that we can reside our faith in a God who is only love." The longevity is Givens's evidence that Marcion tapped something deep, not merely eccentric. (The orthodox reaction also shaped the canon — see [[The New Testament Canon Is a Reaction to Marcion]].)[^2]
2. **Tertullian's rebuttal is the wrong cure — rate: pointed.** Givens recounts [[Tertullian]]'s answer ("the bulldog of the early church"): "your God, if he's just characterized by love, his... unqualified love is unreasonable, and God is a God of reason, so he has to love within reasonable limits." Givens rejects this: "I think that's kind of the definition of love. It's unreasonable, uncalculating. That's the love of a parent... There's nothing reasonable about a parent's unqualified love."[^3]
3. **The LDS escape route Marcion lacked — rate: the key move.** Marcion's dilemma (literal brutal OT vs. loving Jesus) only forces you to discard the OT *if you must read it literally*. The Restoration's alternative is to deny the literalism: the harsh portrait is a product of textual transmission/redaction, not the character of God — the vault's [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]] thesis. So Latter-day Saints can keep both Testaments *and* a God who is "only love," dissolving Marcion's either/or. This connects directly to [[Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide]].[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing (proto-orthodox) side:** The church was right to reject Marcion wholesale, and not merely for his anti-Judaism. Severing the loving Father of Jesus from the Creator of Israel destroys the unity of salvation history, makes the Incarnation a rescue *from* creation rather than its redemption, and discards the very Scriptures Jesus and the apostles treated as the Word of God. [[Tertullian]]'s "reasonable limits" need not mean stingy love; it can mean love ordered by justice and truth, so that forgiveness and judgment are not contradictory. And the OT's "vengeance," read in covenant context, is frequently the protection of the oppressed, not arbitrary cruelty — so the literal/loving tension is overstated.

**Counter-objection (Givens's rejoinder):** Granting the unity of salvation history, Givens's point stands one level up: the church preserved unity by *also* keeping the brutal texts as literal divine acts, and then had to defend them — which drove it to "God's goodness is not our goodness" (see [[Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide]]). Marcion at least refused to call cruelty love. The better path keeps unity *and* refuses that move, by locating the cruelty in fallen transmission rather than in God — an option unavailable to Marcion and to the creedal tradition, but native to the Restoration.
