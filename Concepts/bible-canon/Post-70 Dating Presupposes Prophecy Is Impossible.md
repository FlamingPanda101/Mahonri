# Post-70 Dating Presupposes Prophecy Is Impossible

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The standard reason for dating Mark (and the synoptics) after 70 AD: Mark records Jesus *predicting* the destruction of the Jerusalem temple, which fell in 70 AD; therefore the Gospel must have been written *after* 70, when the event was known. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Michael Jones]] argues this dating rests entirely on the anti-supernatural **presupposition that genuine prophecy is impossible** — strip that assumption and the post-70 argument collapses, while independent evidence (people *did* predict the temple's fall before 70; the abrupt ending of Acts) points earlier. [^2]

1. **People predicted the temple's fall before 70.** "Most scholars recognize that people were predicting the destruction of the temple before 70 AD" — Josephus reports another "Jesus" (son of Ananias) doing so in the 60s, and the book of Daniel already speaks of the temple's destruction. So a pre-70 prediction needs no hindsight. *Rating: strong; the precedent is historically attested.* [^2]
2. **The only real premise is "prophecy can't happen."** Hansen and Jones agree that every time you trace the post-70 argument, "it comes back to this same thing... this presupposition that well, they couldn't have actually predicted that this would have happened" — i.e., "presuming the prophecy [is impossible]... and therefore it had to have been written after." That is a *metaphysical* assumption, not a historical datum (cf. [[Methodological Materialism Smuggled Into Metaphysical Materialism]]). *Rating: strong as a diagnosis of the argument's load-bearing premise.* [^3]
3. **Pro-Roman tone fits a pre-70 setting.** The Gospels are "very nice to the Romans" — the centurion at the cross confesses Jesus is the Son of God; Pilate gets "a pass" while the Jewish leadership is blamed. After 70 (and Nero's persecution) Christian writing turns *hostile* to Rome (e.g., Revelation's Babylon). The synoptics' Roman-friendliness suggests they predate that shift. *Rating: moderate-strong; the contrast with later anti-Roman literature is real, though tone arguments are not decisive.* [^4]
4. **Independent early anchor.** This dovetails with [[The Abrupt Ending of Acts Dates It Before 62 AD]], giving a converging (not merely negative) case for early dates. *Rating: moderate-strong as corroboration.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The mainstream reply does *not* reduce to "miracles can't happen." Scholars argue the *specificity* and *framing* of Mark 13 (and especially Luke 21's siege-language) read as **post-eventu** description colored by the actual 66-70 war, that the synoptic "little apocalypse" addresses a community already living through or just after the crisis, and that the consensus rests on a web of considerations (Markan priority, Luke's redaction, the Gospels' Sitz im Leben) rather than on a bare anti-supernatural axiom. A naturalist can grant Jesus made a *general* prediction and still date the *written* form after 70 on literary grounds.

### Counter-objection
Jones's reply: granting Jesus made a general prediction is already enough to remove the *necessity* of a post-70 date, and the appeal to "specificity" often smuggles the anti-prophecy premise back in (only an impossible-prophecy assumption makes vagueness the test of authenticity). Where specificity is debated (Luke), the early case still stands on *positive* evidence — Acts' ending and the pro-Roman tone — which the literary arguments do not neutralize. The honest core remains: absent the assumption that detailed prediction can't occur, the strongest stated reason for post-70 dating evaporates.

## Best Quotes
> "Most scholars recognize that people were predicting the destruction of the temple before 70 AD. Josephus mentions another alleged prophet named Jesus who in the 60s was doing it. Then you have the book of Daniel... So most scholars seem to recognize that's a bad argument, but they're still stuck on the 70 AD date for some reason." — [[Michael Jones]] [^2]

> "It's just this presupposition that, well, they couldn't have actually predicted that this would have happened... presuming there's no such thing as prophecy and therefore there's no way they predicted it and therefore it had to have been written after." — [[Jacob Hansen]] [^3]
