# No Final Interpreter — The Protestant Adjudication Problem

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Even granting an ultimate *text*, Hayden argues there is a second authority question Protestantism cannot answer: **who has the final say on what the text means?** When two sincere believers read the same verse differently, *sola scriptura* supplies no living, final interpreter — so the system's fruit is endless schism.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Authority of a text presupposes authority of interpretation — rate: strong
Hayden separates two prior questions: "Who has the final interpretation of what the text means? And who determines which texts are authoritative?"[^2] An ultimate authority must "have the final say when conflicts arise"; a text that cannot adjudicate disputes about its own meaning does not function as a true ultimate authority — it needs a living interpreter.[^3] **Scholarly context:** this is the standard Catholic/Orthodox critique of *sola scriptura* (cf. Newman, and the "hermeneutical" objection); the LDS form substitutes living prophets/apostles for the magisterium.

### 2. "Protestants created this problem in the 16th century" — rate: strong, slightly overdrawn
Hayden: the Reformation's founding premise ("we don't need a living authority to tell us what the Bible says") *guarantees* unresolvable disagreement, because admitting a final interpreter would undermine the premise.[^4] **Steelman of Kelly:** denominational diversity is not unique to Protestantism — LDS believers also disagree (Kelly cites disputes over Joseph Smith's First Vision accounts and the King Follett discourse).[^5] Kelly's tu quoque has force: a *living* authority does not in practice eliminate interpretive disagreement among the faithful either.

### 3. Kelly's answer concedes the point — rate: decisive in the exchange
Asked who adjudicates between two believers reading the same verse, Kelly says: pray, go to scripture openly, and "at times we have to make a decision for ourselves."[^6] Hayden: "So there's no final adjudicator on interpretation... and if your interpretation differs from somebody else, you just go with your own?"[^7] Kelly: "The final authority for me is what scripture says." This reduces to *each reader is his own final court* — exactly the privatized authority Hayden is targeting. **Assessment:** rhetorically Hayden wins the exchange; philosophically the deeper question of whether *any* system escapes the regress (someone must finally interpret the prophet too) is left unresolved.

## Best Quotes
> "When two Bible believers disagree about what the text says, there is no final interpreter, no final judge. Protestants created this problem for themselves in the 16th century and have still not posited a solution."[^4]

> "Schism and doctrinal disagreement is the ultimate outcome of their foundation, which is: the Bible is the ultimate authority, but no one has dibs on the ultimate interpretation."[^8]

> "When two sincere Protestants disagree about what your canon means... who has the final say?" — "The final authority for me is what scripture says." (Hayden, then Kelly)[^9]
