# Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]]'s second argument: the **absence of sola scriptura in Scripture**. Using [[James White]]'s own concession that sola scriptura "is not a valid concept during times of revelation," Horn argues the doctrine was *false* in the OT (prophets kept adding revelation) and *false* during the NT era (apostles kept adding Scripture). So to be true, sola scriptura must have *become* true at some later point — but no biblical passage says public revelation would one day cease and all doctrine be confined to the written word alone. Therefore sola scriptura is unproven and false.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "becoming true" dilemma — rate: strong
Horn's logic via the Westminster phrase ("unto which nothing at any time is to be added whether by new revelations ... or traditions of men"): if nothing may be added, then prophets and apostles could *never* have added — contradicting how revelation actually worked. So Rogers must "cite a scripture passage which teaches that at some point in the future ... all doctrine would be confined to the written word alone."[^2] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers replies sola scriptura is "sufficient at every stage at which God gives it" — the law was sufficient for Moses' generation though more revelation came; and the rule applies once the canon is complete. But Rogers offers no *verse* announcing the cutoff, conceding the cessation point rests on "logical inference."

### 2. The "don't go beyond what is written" verse backfires — rate: strong
Horn turns [[1 Corinthians 4.6]] against the Protestant: taken literally, the Corinthians could not receive 2 Corinthians without "going beyond what is written."[^3] This neatly illustrates the apostolic-age problem. **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers calls this an oral-phase point he already anticipated — the rule binds the *completed* deposit, not the in-progress one.

### 3. Paul's unwritten teaching about "the restrainer" — rate: strong (Horn's best evidence here)
In [[2 Thessalonians 2.15|2 Thess 2]], Paul reminds them "do you not remember what I told you about who is restraining the man of lawlessness?" — content Paul taught *orally* but never wrote, which scholars cannot reconstruct (Roman Empire? angelic power?).[^4] This concretely refutes "the apostles only preached what was in scripture." **Steelman of Rogers:** not every apostolic utterance was revelation binding on later generations; what was *meant* to be the perpetual rule was inscripturated. But Rogers cannot show *which* oral teachings were so designated, which is exactly Horn's point.

### 4. The same argument from the Latter-day Saint side — rate: parallel corroboration
In a separate debate, LDS apologist [[Shiloh Lofgren]] runs the identical structure against Reformed pastor [[Timothy J. Whitehead]], quoting James White verbatim ("Protestants do not assert that sola scriptura is a valid concept during times of revelation") and concluding that "no verse in the New Testament can be teaching sola scriptura ... because it would be teaching a principle that was false at the very time the verse was being written."[^5] He likewise turns [[1 Corinthians 4.6|1 Cor 4:6]] ("not to go beyond what is written") — "Paul could have and literally did go beyond what was written" — and adds [[Ephesians 4.11|Eph 4:11-13]]: the offices continue "until we all attain unto the unity of the faith," a goal unmet, so there is no scriptural cutoff for living revelation. See [[The Church Is the Pillar of Truth and Scripture Is Its Byproduct]]. **Steelman of Whitehead (his rebuttal):** revelation is covenantal; the new covenant is "new and everlasting" (Hebrews 13), so no further covenant — and thus no further canon — is anticipated, even though, he concedes, the cessation point is "an assumption," not a verse.

## Best Quotes
> "Solos scriptura could not have been true in the old testament because then prophets could never have added to god's revelation. Sola scriptura could not have been true during the time of the new testament authors because then the apostles could have never added to scripture."[^2]

> [Lofgren] "No verse in the New Testament can be teaching sola scriptura ... it would be teaching a principle that was false at the very time the verse was being written."[^5]

> "If the Corinthians took 1 Corinthians 4:6 literally — don't go beyond what is written — well, they'd have to throw away 2 Corinthians."[^3]

> "He told the Thessalonians that, but he didn't write it down for the rest of us ... So it is not true that the apostles only preached what was in scripture."[^4]

> [James White, quoted by Horn] "Protestants do not assert that sola scriptura is a valid concept during times of revelation. How could it be since the rule of faith to which it points was at that very time coming into being?"[^1]
