# The Fourfold Sense of Scripture

## The Point
[[John Cassian]] (c. AD 360–435) articulates the classic fourfold sense of scripture: a single passage can be read literally/historically, allegorically, anagogically, and tropologically. This scheme systematized the multilayered patristic reading of scripture in which the plain narrative is only the first of several legitimate meanings.

## Evidence
The four senses, as Cassian describes them (*Conferences* 14.8.4): [^1]
1. **Literal/historical** — scripture gives "knowledge of past and visible things."
2. **Allegorical** — scripture is "a metaphor, or a type that is prefiguring a mystery."
3. **Anagogical** — scripture reads as prophecy about "what lies in the future" (i.e., the afterlife).
4. **Tropological** — scripture offers "moral explanation pertaining to correction of life and practical instruction."

**Cassian's worked example — Jerusalem:** [^1]
- According to history: "the city of the Jews."
- According to allegory: "the Church of Christ."
- According to anagogy: "the heavenly city of God 'which is the mother of us all'" (Galatians 4:26).
- According to tropology: "the soul of the human being."

### The medieval hardening of the scheme
By the medieval period this patristic multiple-senses approach (see [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]]) hardened into the classic fourfold sense — **literal, allegorical, moral (tropological), anagogical** — as [[Dan McClellan]] describes it. The anagogical ("leading up") sense, "a method of mystical or spiritual interpretation… that looks beyond literal, allegorical, or moral meanings to discover a text's hidden reference to eternal life, heaven, or the afterlife," ranked **highest**; the literal ranked **lowest**. [^2][^3]
1. **The christological sense overruled all others.** "If there were problematic parts of it you just had to find a way to read Christ into it… the christological sense overrules any other sense." [^4] (McClellan flags the modern echo in the LDS "where's Waldo" habit of finding Christ everywhere.)
2. **Erotic poetry shows the system at work.** The [[Song of Songs 4.5|Song of Songs]] ("I love your breasts, church… they look like some deer") is read not as the erotic poetry it plainly is but christologically as Christ and the church: "the last thing they were going to do is champion the plain sense of this erotic poetry." [^5] (The allegorical reading of the Song is the dominant pre-modern interpretation.)
3. **Four open layers located authority in the church, not the text.** Because multiple legitimate senses meant *someone* had to adjudicate the right reading, "the church is" the ultimate authority — "which endows the institution with an awful lot of authority." This sets up the Reformation backlash; see [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]] and [[Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention]]. [^3]
4. **Steelman.** A defender of allegory could argue the fourfold sense is hermeneutically richer and more pastorally fruitful than wooden literalism, and that the medievals still affirmed the literal as the foundation on which the others rest (Aquinas: the spiritual senses presuppose the literal). The comparative-historical point stands either way: pre-modern Christianity granted *enormous* interpretive latitude — the opposite of modern hard literalism.

## Best Quotes
> "Jerusalem can be understood in a fourfold manner. According to history it is the city of the Jews. According to allegory it is the Church of Christ. According to anagogy it is the heavenly city of God … According to tropology it is the soul of the human being." (John Cassian, *Conferences* 14.8.4) [^1]

> "If there were problematic parts of it you just had to find a way to read Christ into it… the christological sense overrules any other sense." [^4]

> "The last thing they were going to do is champion the plain sense of this erotic poetry." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The fourfold sense supplies the interpretive grammar behind early Christian preaching (e.g., Origen's good-Samaritan allegory). It demonstrates that reading scripture for multiple layered meanings — historical, symbolic, prophetic, and moral — is an ancient, mainstream practice, relevant to Latter-day Saint typological and "likening" readings of scripture.
