# The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence

## The Claim
The Shema — *Adonai eloheinu Adonai echad*, "Yahweh our God, Yahweh is one" ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deut 6:4]]) — is an assertion of an **exclusive covenant relationship**, not a numerical claim that only one god exists. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Our God" presupposes other gods (rating: strong; grammatical)
*Eloheinu* takes the generic noun "god" (*elohim*) and marks it as "the one that pertains to **us**." Specifying "our God" only makes sense against a backdrop of gods that are not ours. [^1]

> "elohenu takes a generic noun God and identifies it specifically as the one that pertains to us our God so this phrase already presupposes the existence of many other gods" [^1]

### 2. *Echad* ("one") parallels Song of Songs 6:9 (rating: strong; closest grammatical analog)
In [[Song of Songs 6.8-9|Song of Songs 6:8–9]], amid sixty queens, eighty concubines, and maidens beyond number, the singer says of the beloved *achat hi* — "she is **one**." This is grammatically analogous to *echad* in the Shema (masculine form of "one"). It plainly does not mean "she is the only woman who exists"; it means "the only one *for me*" — an exclusive relationship. [^2]

> "she is one this is grammatically analogous to Adonai which is the masculine form of one and what does this mean it does not mean she's the only one that exists we've just acknowledged the existence of numerous other women she's the only one for me because it's about an exclusive relationship" [^2]

McClellan gives [[Alex OConnor]] the same Song of Songs parallel and attributes the exclusive-relationship reading of the "there is no other" rhetoric to [[Nathan McDonald]]'s *Deuteronomy and the Meaning of Monotheism*: the phrase is scope-limited and means "I'm the only one *for you*," directly analogous to the lover's "my dove is one" among the concubines and maidens.[^5]

### 3. Coheres with the rest of Deuteronomy and Israel's inheritance theology (rating: strong)
The Shema's exclusivity flows from Israel being Yahweh's allotted purview (see [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8–9]], [[Deuteronomy Acknowledges Other Gods While Using Negation Rhetoric]]): the people in Yahweh's domain "have nothing to do with any other gods but Adonai because Adonai is *ha-Elohim* in that purview." [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A monotheist can object that *echad* in Deut 6:4 may mean "Yahweh is a *unity* / Yahweh alone is God," and that the Song of Songs parallel is a love poem, a different genre that need not govern a creedal confession. McClellan's reply is that the grammar is the same construction and that "we take it seriously on its own terms rather than read into these texts what we want to be there" — the exclusive-relationship reading fits both the immediate syntax and the surrounding acknowledgement of other gods. [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "what precisely does that mean well the closest analog would probably be Song of Songs 6 veres 8 and 9 where the singer talks about how there are all these other women around all these other concubines all these other maidens but then they say my Dove my perfect one aat he she is one" [^2]

> "so that's the best case that we can make for what this rhetoric means when we consider it within its context... rather than read into these texts what we want to be there what we demand the texts be to say and not allowed to say" [^4]
