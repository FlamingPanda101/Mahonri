# James Read Amos 9 Through the Septuagint at the Jerusalem Council

## The Claim
[[Robert Boland]] argues that the **Council of Jerusalem ([[Acts 15|Acts 15]])** is a live demonstration that the early church did *not* operate by scripture alone, on two fronts:

1. **A faulty translation did the work.** James clinches the decision to admit Gentiles without circumcision by quoting **Amos 9:11-12** — but in the **Septuagint** form, which differs from the Masoretic Text. The Hebrew speaks of Israel possessing "the remnant of **Edom**"; the LXX reads that "the **remnant of men / the rest of mankind** may seek the Lord." James's universalist application *depends on the Greek mistranslation* and is absent from the Hebrew. (Boland notes the verse is also missing from Qumran's Damascus Document and 4QFlorilegium, which read it of Israel's takeover of Edom.)[^1]
2. **Modern revelation was the hermeneutical lens, not scripture.** What actually drove James's reading was not the text alone but **Peter's then-recent visionary experience** of God accepting the Gentiles (Acts 15:7-11) — i.e. *contemporary revelation* supplied the interpretive key, with scripture invoked as *confirming* evidence afterward.[^2]

The conclusion: the council **immediately bound the consciences** of all believers by its own God-given authority (Acts 15 issues binding decrees), acting as a *rule of faith* alongside and prior to a settled text — exactly what *sola scriptura* denies. This is a NT companion to his OT case at [[Hezekiah and Josiah Bound a Word of God Never in the Bible]].

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Boland / LDS negative):**
- Boland stacks **Protestant and Lutheran scholars conceding the LXX divergence**: Dean Wenthe ("James' interpretation... rests squarely on the Septuagint translation [which]... changes the sense of the Masoretic text"); David Maine (James uses Amos "contrary to its intended meaning... based on a faulty translation"); Mikael Selbach (the use "is clearly contingent on the universalist tendency in verse 12 as found in the Septuagint").[^1]
- The decree binds *immediately* — believers who never witnessed Peter's vision are nonetheless obligated — showing an authority that does not wait for, and is not derived from, the bare written word. Cf. [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]].
- The "Berean" objection (test everything by scripture) does not rescue the affirmative: the Bereans were *pre-conversion non-Christians* examining a claim, not members of the believing community receiving binding *didactic* teaching from the church's officers (Acts 15 "the apostles and elders").[^3]

**Steelman (Protestant affirmative):**
- James's argument *is* an appeal to scripture: he treats Amos as the authoritative warrant, which is precisely the sola-scriptura instinct — the Gentile influx is accepted *because* it is confirmed by the prophetic word.[^4]
- The NT writers' freedom to use the LXX (a translation they regarded as carrying the sense of the Word) is a fact about *which textual form* counted as scripture, not evidence against scripture's authority. (Compare the vault's [[The Septuagint of Isaiah 9-6 Is Expansive Not Truncated]] and [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]].)
- Acts 15 falls within the **apostolic age of public revelation**, so it cannot by itself show what the rule is *after* revelation ceased — the standard affirmative reply (see [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]]).

## Best Quotes
> "The hermeneutical lens... that helped James' reading was not scripture alone but Peter's experiences and perception of then-modern revelation as recounted in verses 7 to 11."[^2]

> [Wenthe, quoted by Boland] "James' interpretation and application of this passage rests squarely on the Septuagint translation... [which] changes the sense of the Masoretic text."[^1]

> "Acts 15 shows the church can immediately bind the conscience of believers due to its God-given authority and so act as a rule of faith."[^3]
