# The Old Testament Is Less Reliable Than the New Testament

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] and [[Jonah Barnes]] argue that Christian apologetics has it backwards: defenders spend their effort on the **New Testament's** textual reliability (manuscript dating, low rate of variants), where the case is genuinely strong, while almost never confronting the **Old Testament**, where the harder problem lies. Their thesis: the Old Testament is "more corrupt than the New Testament" — not mainly at the level of copying, but at the level of *composition*, because of how and by whom it was assembled (the "Great Forsaken").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The presenters grant the New Testament's reliability (rate: strong, concession).** Hansen says he has "gone and listened to a lot of the Christian scholars on the reliability of the text of the New Testament and I think they make some very compelling arguments" — manuscripts going back to the first/second century, "very little change to the actual words." See [[The New Testament Is Historically Reliable - The Seven E's]]. The concession sharpens the contrast.[^2]

2. **Apologists avoid the Old Testament (rate: moderate, observation).** "They only ever talk about the New Testament ... what's funny is most people don't have big problems with the New Testament; most people have trouble with the Old Testament." In the evangelical mind "the Old Testament doesn't matter anymore," which the presenters find an "amazing statement."[^3]

3. **The problem is composition, not just copying (rate: moderate, framing).** The Old Testament "is the product of arguably an even bigger, longer, and worse forsaken" — its core books (the Deuteronomistic History) were compiled after the Babylonian exile by interested scribes, so its issues are editorial/authorial, upstream of manuscript transmission. See [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes]] and [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]].[^4]

4. **"Reliable but not infallible" (rate: framing).** The conclusion is not rejection: "the Bible is reliable but it is not infallible." It is "something you can use to understand the mind and will of God," but inerrancy claims fail "on the historical level when you start to realize the way the Bible was put together." See [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
The reliability comparison conflates two different things and is double-edged. (a) "Reliability" in the apologetic literature usually means **textual/transmissional** fidelity, and on *that* axis the Hebrew Bible is impressively stable — the Great Isaiah Scroll matches the medieval Masoretic text closely across a millennium, undercutting "more corrupt." The presenters are really making a **compositional/source-critical** claim, which is a different debate; equivocating between the two inflates the case. (b) An inerrantist will say source-critical reconstruction (the Documentary Hypothesis, Deuteronomistic History) is itself contested and methodologically naturalistic, not a settled "corruption." (c) The move "the OT is unreliable, and that's good news (it makes room for the Restoration)" is convenient for the LDS conclusion and should be flagged as motivated. The defensible residue: the Old Testament's *formation* is demonstrably more layered and editorially complex than the New Testament's, so flat appeals to its inerrancy are weaker than the corresponding case for NT textual stability — which is a claim about *formation*, not a license to call the text "corrupt" in transmission.

## Best Quotes

> "I would just hypothesize that the Old Testament is more corrupt than the New Testament, and that's exactly what we find when we dig into it."[^4]

> "They always like to go and defend the New Testament, and fine, I think you can make a good argument ... however the Old Testament you cannot make that argument if you go and actually understand the way that this whole book came together."[^1]
