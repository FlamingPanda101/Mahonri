# Early Christian Writers Were Literate Networks, Not Faith Communities

## The Claim
The go-to assumption that early Christian literature emerged from worshipping churches is overstated. The New Testament's authors were **highly literate individuals** embedded in **literary networks** — reading, sharing, and reworking one another's drafts and the works of famous past and contemporary writers — rather than scribes of a communal oral tradition. (This is the general model behind [[Johannine Community Did Not Exist]].)

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A scholarly shift away from "the church" as the formative site.** [[Hugo Mendez]] credits [[Stanley Stowers]] and his student [[Robin Faith Walsh]] (*The Origins of Early Christian Literature*) with reframing where the texts come from — toward "literary communities rather than communities of faith." *(Strong; names the specific scholarship.)* [^1]
2. **NT writers had above-average literacy and training.** They were "people who had been trained to be literate," who had "gulped down large amounts of ancient literature" as part of their training in writing — not typical congregants. *(Strong; a claim about the social profile of authors.)* [^2]
3. **They formed interacting networks.** Whether or not they met personally, they shared writings and drafts, improved one another's work, and at minimum read famous past and contemporary writers — "important and powerful influences" on the biblical texts. *(Strong; supplies the alternative social mechanism.)* [^3]
4. **Oral/communal tradition isn't denied — just de-centered.** Méndez doesn't deny that oral and written traditions about Jesus "did flow through these communities"; he relocates the *formative* act to literate authorship. *(Context; a careful, bounded claim.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Form criticism's communal model is not baseless: the Jesus tradition demonstrably circulated orally in worship, catechesis, and preaching before being written, and texts like the letters address real congregations with real problems. Critics worry the "literary network" model imports an elite, individualistic authorship anachronistically and underrates the church as the matrix that preserved and shaped the tradition.

## Best Quotes
> "Robin Faith Walsh has written... [that] we should be talking more about literary communities rather than communities of faith." — [[Dan Beecher]], summarizing [^1]

> "The writers that we see working in the New Testament are individuals who had higher than the Christian average... levels of literacy. They had read, really kind of gulped down large amounts of ancient literature." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^2]

> "They formed networks of people who interacted... sharing writings, sharing drafts, improving one another's work... reading the works of contemporary writers." [^3]
