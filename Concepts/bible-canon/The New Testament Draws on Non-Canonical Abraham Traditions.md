# The New Testament Draws on Non-Canonical Abraham Traditions

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the New Testament's retelling of the Binding of Isaac shows its authors drawing on an **expanded Abraham tradition** not present in canonical Genesis. [[Hebrews 11.17|Hebrews 11:17-19]] says Abraham offered Isaac "accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead" — a resurrection motive that **does not appear in Genesis 22** but does appear in apocryphal Akedah accounts (e.g. the Slavonic Palaea, where Isaac asks to be bound, trusting God will resurrect him). So the author of Hebrews "is reading an apocryphal version," and the canonical Genesis is not, by itself, the whole story the NT presupposes.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Hebrews supplies a detail Genesis lacks (rated: moderate, textual)
"Paul is reading an apocryphal version ... that doesn't mean Paul agrees with the whole thing, but ... Genesis is incomplete ... our version of the story cannot be wholly reliant upon Genesis because it wasn't for Paul." The resurrection rationale in Hebrews 11:19 has no source in Genesis 22.[^1] Barnes had earlier read the same Isaac-asks-to-be-bound / resurrection detail from the Old Slavonic Palaea.[^1]

### 2. Part of a broader NT pattern (rated: moderate)
The hosts generalize: "there's a hundred references in the New Testament to verses or scriptures or stories that don't exist [in our canon], even words of Christ himself referencing things in like Enoch." The Akedah case is one instance of the NT treating a wider body of scripture as authoritative.[^1] (See [[Jesus Referenced the Apocrypha]] and [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude]].)

### 3. The apologetic turn against inerrancy (rated: moderate)
Because evangelical critics often wield biblical "completeness/inerrancy" against the Book of Mormon and the JST, the hosts ask how they reconcile Hebrews 11:19 drawing on non-canonical material — pressing that a closed, self-sufficient canon is itself an anachronism (cf. [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]]).[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) Hebrews 11:19 need not depend on any *text* — the resurrection inference is a natural theological reading of Genesis 22 itself (Abraham believed God's promise through Isaac, so trusted God could restore him), so "he's quoting an apocryphal version" overreads a plausible exegesis; (b) NT allusions to Enoch (Jude) or other traditions show the authors *knew* that literature, not that they treated it as canonical scripture on par with Torah; and (c) "Genesis is incomplete" is a loaded inference — that the NT elaborates a story is normal Second-Temple midrashic practice, not evidence the canonical text was corrupted or that the Book of Mormon/JST model follows. The defensible core is narrow and well-supported: NT authors demonstrably engaged extra-canonical Jewish traditions, which complicates a naive closed-canon inerrancy.
