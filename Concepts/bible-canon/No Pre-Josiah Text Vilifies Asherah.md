# No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]]'s central, recurring exegetical point — the one his critic "entirely ignores" — is a claim about **dating**, not theology: "There are no biblical texts that scholars can confidently date prior to the reign of King [[Josiah]] toward the end of the 7th century BCE that demonize or vilify or condemn Asherah or Asherah worship."[^1] Recognition of Asherah as the consort or wife of Yahweh "was 100% normative prior to" Josiah's cult-centralization campaign and the exile.[^2] All the literature condemning Asherah begins "around the reign of Josiah," and there is a historical explanation for why opposition appears *suddenly* with Josiah.[^3]

The apologetic rebuttal — "of course there was Asherah worship, because there was apostasy" — only works by **retrojecting** that later opposition into earlier periods for which no such opposition is attested. The texts that *narrate* pre-Josiah time periods (patriarchs, Moses, the early monarchy) were, on the critical view, "actually written in later time periods," so they cannot be used as pre-Josiah evidence of an anti-Asherah norm.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The condemnation is textually localized to Josiah-or-later.** "The only places where we find condemnation or vilification or demonization of Asherah or Asherah worship are texts that date to around the time of the reign of Josiah or after."[^5] The narrative *setting* of a text (e.g. the patriarchal era) is not its date of *composition*.
2. **The "apostasy" reading is a presupposition, not a datum.** "You have no data that support the conclusion that worshiping Asherah prior to the reign of Josiah actually represents any kind of apostasy. It's a presupposition." It assumes the text "is all historical, that it was all written by the people we traditionally assign authorship, and that it is inerrant and univocal."[^6]
3. **The critic demands work already done.** When the critic says critical scholars "would have to show that the Torah and prophets were all invented after King Josiah's reign," McClellan answers (a) it is not *all* of them, "it's just the parts that talk about Asherah," and (b) "critical scholarship has already done that" — the arguments are in the literature; "this is not where the scholarship is done. This is where I'm just sharing the findings of the scholarship."[^7] He treats Asherah at length in the relevant chapter of *The Bible Says So*.[^8]
4. **Archaeology fits the developmental, not the apostasy, model.** The critic concedes the archaeological discovery of Asherah worship in Israel is "exactly what you'd expect"; McClellan agrees with the data but denies the framing — the data show Asherah worship was *normative and uncondemned* until the reform, which is the opposite of the "idolatrous descent" narrative. (On the material evidence — Kuntillet Ajrud, Khirbet el-Qom — see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]].)

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A confessional reader can grant the dating distribution yet argue that the *absence* of early condemnation is an argument from silence: a small, mostly-lost pre-exilic corpus could have condemned Asherah in texts that simply did not survive, and the Sinai covenant's exclusivity (even if redacted later) may preserve a genuinely early norm. **But** McClellan's point is comparative and evidential: the apologist must *positively* establish a pre-Josiah anti-Asherah norm to call earlier worship "apostasy," and there is no datable text that supplies it — whereas the sudden, explicable clustering of the polemic at Josiah is a real, datable pattern. The burden sits with the claim of an unattested early norm (see [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]).

## LDS Relevance
This is the same Josianic/Deuteronomistic reform that Latter-day Saint apologists invoke for the Book of Mormon's pre-exilic, "First Temple" theology — see [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]] and [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]]. McClellan's narrower claim sharpens the LDS reading of the **divine feminine**: if Asherah-as-consort was *normative and uncondemned* until Josiah, and the anti-Asherah polemic is a late editorial program rather than the original norm, then the LDS recovery of a Heavenly Mother is recovering something the reformers suppressed, not importing a foreign "apostate" goddess — precisely the move made in [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]]. (McClellan, a non-confessional scholar, draws no LDS conclusion; the relevance is that his dating argument removes the "it was always apostasy" objection that LDS critics raise against the Heavenly Mother reading.)
