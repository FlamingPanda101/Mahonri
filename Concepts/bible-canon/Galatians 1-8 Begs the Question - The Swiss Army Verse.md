# Galatians 1:8 Begs the Question — The "Swiss Army Verse"

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics deploy [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]] ("though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel... let him be accursed") as a one-line refutation of Latter-day Saints. [[Keystone]] argues this is not the "slam dunk" it appears to be: the move commits the **begging-the-question** fallacy, because it presupposes the very thing in dispute — that the accuser already possesses "the fully correct gospel."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "Swiss Army verse" pattern.** Keystone names a recurring tactic: a verse "often used to quickly dismiss opposing views without really engaging with those views." Commenting "Galatians 1:8 little bro" and assuming the work is done substitutes a citation for an argument.[^2] *Rating: moderate — a fair description of how the verse is frequently used rhetorically.*

2. **The circular structure.** "The conclusion that I have a false gospel is based on the premise that he has the fully correct gospel. But that is a premise that has not been proved." This is a textbook case of begging the question (circular reasoning): a conclusion resting on an unproven premise. So "throwing Galatians 1:8 at people is really not persuasive. It's just judgmental. It begs the question, 'Why should I believe you?'"[^3] *Rating: strong — the logical point is valid; the verse only "bites" if one assumes the accuser's gospel is the standard.*

3. **The fallacy can only be escaped by defining the gospel.** Because the dispute is over *which* gospel is true, the verse cannot settle it without first answering "what is the gospel?" — and on Paul's own definition ([[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Corinthians 15:1-4]]: Christ died, was buried, rose the third day) Latter-day Saints affirm it. See [[Galatians 1-8 Latter-day Saints Preach Pauls Gospel]]. The argument thus collapses back into the substantive doctrinal question it was meant to bypass.[^4] *Rating: strong — relocates the debate from a proof-text to the actual content of the gospel.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A careful critic could grant the logical point while insisting the premise is *easily* discharged: the "true gospel" is the historic apostolic deposit preserved in the New Testament, which (the critic claims) is publicly accessible and excludes distinctive LDS doctrines, so the charge is not viciously circular but a shorthand for an argument that could be spelled out. On this view the burden is just deferred, not unmet.

## Counter-objection
Even granting that the premise *could* be argued, the bare citation of Galatians 1:8 does not argue it — and once it is argued, the dispute is about the *content and boundaries of the gospel* (Christology, soteriology, the nature of God), which is precisely the contested ground. Paul himself reduces "the gospel I preached" to the death-burial-resurrection kernel and names "two separate beings" in his epistle greetings, so the proof-text does not obviously favor the trinitarian accuser. The verse is therefore the wrong tool: it presupposes the verdict instead of supplying it.
