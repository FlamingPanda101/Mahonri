# Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor for Temple

## The Claim
Jesus's cursing of the fig tree in [[Mark 11.12-25|Mark 11:12-25]] is not a historical miracle-narrative about a tree, but a literary metaphor — specifically a "Markan sandwich" — in which the fig tree represents the Jerusalem temple and its leadership, which have failed to "bear fruit" and are therefore condemned to destruction.[^1]

## The Markan Sandwich Structure

Mark employs an A-B-A' intercalation ("sandwich") technique:[^1]

1. **A — The Fig Tree Cursed (Mark 11:12-14):** Jesus sees a fig tree in leaf, approaches it, finds no fruit, and says "May no one ever eat fruit from you again." The narrator notes "it was not the season for figs" — which McClellan argues is Mark's concession that he's fitting the metaphor in wherever he can, since the Passover (when Jesus dies) is March/April, too early for figs.[^2]

2. **B — The Temple Cleansing (Mark 11:15-19):** Jesus enters the temple, overturns the tables of the money changers, and declares it "a den of thieves." The chief priests and scribes begin seeking to kill him.[^3]

3. **A' — The Fig Tree Withered (Mark 11:20-25):** The next day, the disciples find the tree "withered away to its roots." Jesus responds not by explaining the tree but by teaching about faith that can "move mountains" — a reassurance to his followers that they do not need the temple/leadership structures that have been condemned.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "bearing fruit" motif:** Throughout the NT and Hebrew Bible, "bearing fruit" is a metaphor for producing good works and fulfilling one's covenantal obligations. The temple leadership is depicted as failing this test.[^3]

2. **Mark's historical context (~70 CE):** Mark was written at or shortly after the destruction of the Jerusalem temple by the Romans. From Mark's perspective, "the tree is cursed and has withered, the temple is gone, the leadership is gone" — the metaphor matches the historical reality the author and his audience just experienced.[^5]

3. **Jesus's response confirms the metaphor:** Rather than explain the tree, Jesus pivots to "have faith in God" and promises that through faith his followers "can accomplish all these great things" — they don't need the temple institutions that were "not bearing fruit."[^4] This would be incoherent if the tree were merely a tree.

4. **Matthew compresses it, losing some metaphor:** In [[Matthew 21.18-22|Matthew 21:18-22]], the story happens all at once (no intervening day, no sandwich structure). McClellan argues Matthew "missed some of the metaphorical message" that Mark embedded in the sandwich structure, or at least understood it differently, resulting in a story that is "even more peculiar" — the tree withers immediately, and the disciples don't ask why Jesus killed it.[^6]

5. **Luke removes the destructive act entirely:** In [[Luke 13.6-9|Luke 13:6-9]], the fig tree story becomes an explicit parable (labeled as such) where a landowner wants to cut down an unfruitful tree but the gardener asks for one more year. McClellan suggests Luke "probably wanted to distance Jesus from a destructive act," since by Luke's time the Jesus tradition had developed to emphasize compassion more, and Luke champions the vulnerable and marginalized.[^7]

6. **The infancy Gospel of Thomas** (mid-late 2nd century CE) reuses the withering metaphor: "behold now you also shall be withered like a tree, and shall not bear leaves nor root nor fruit" — showing the metaphor was circulating broadly.[^8]

## Best Quote

> "This is a metaphor for Jerusalem, for the temple, for the Jewish leadership. He shows up as the Messiah ... there are leaves everywhere and he wants fruit, and they have none to offer him. ... The cursing of the fig tree is: Jesus says, 'Hey, I showed up looking for fruit, I didn't see any. Guess what? Boom, roasted — no more fruit ever.' And this represents the destruction of the temple, the scattering of the leadership of early Judaism."[^5]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-arguments
- Traditional readings treat this as a historical miracle story demonstrating Jesus's power, not a metaphor.
- See also [[Luke 13.6-9|Luke 13:6-9]] for the Lukan adaptation.
