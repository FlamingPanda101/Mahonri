# The Communities That Preserved Older Texts Recognized Jesus First

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues the decisive evidence is a *pattern*: "the communities that were the most receptive to Jesus were disproportionately communities that had maintained older scriptural traditions outside or alongside the mainline Pharisaic canon."[^1] His examples:
- **The Essenes at Qumran** "preserved 1 Enoch, Jubilees, the Book of Giants, and other apocalyptic texts that the Jerusalem establishment was marginalizing."
- **The Galileans** were "geographically and culturally distant from the temple institution and its standardized canon."
- **The Ethiopian Jewish community**, "who converted to Christianity early and thoroughly, had never removed Enoch from their scriptures" ([[Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church]]).
- **The Samaritans** "maintained older versions of the Torah that diverged from the version that the Jerusalem priesthood standardized."[^1]

"So the communities closest to the centralized temple controlled institutionally added canon were the communities most resistant to Jesus's claims. The communities that preserved older and broader apocalyptic traditions were the communities that were the most receptive. That is a pattern with real explanatory power."[^2] It suggests the question of Jewish non-recognition "has a more specific and more interesting answer than just spiritual failure alone": those reading texts that preserved the [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We|divine council]] and the [[The Parables of Enoch Preserve a Preexistent Divine Messiah|pre-existent Son of Man]] "were looking for exactly the figure that Jesus claimed to be."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The text-preservation facts are real.** Qumran did preserve Enoch/Jubilees/Book of Giants; the Samaritans do keep a divergent Torah; the [[Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church]] does canonize Enoch — all verifiable. *Rating: the underlying data points are solid.* [^1]
2. **The correlation is suggestive.** Galilee's distance from Jerusalem and the early Christian movement's traction there is historically attested; a correlation between "older/broader canon" and "receptivity" is a real, testable shape. *Rating: a genuinely interesting pattern, if the categories are drawn carefully (see steelman).* [^2]
3. **It connects mechanism to outcome.** Paired with [[The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah]], the pattern supplies the *predicted* social distribution of recognition — those with the un-narrowed conceptual toolkit recognized the heavenly-mediator claim. *Rating: coherent and explanatorily ambitious.* [^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A historian objects: (a) **the categories are leaky** — the Essenes preserved Enoch but were *intensely* sectarian and there is no evidence they converted to Christianity *en masse*; "Galileans" were not canon-deviants but ordinary Jews who used the same scriptures, so geography, class, and Jesus' own Galilean ministry explain their receptivity better than canon; (b) **counter-examples abound** — Paul, the apostles, and thousands at Pentecost came *out of* mainstream Judaism with the standardized canon, while the Samaritans accepted Jesus yet rejected the Prophets *and* Enoch (they keep only the Pentateuch), which cuts against "broader canon ⇒ receptivity"; (c) **the Ethiopian case is later and complicated** — the deep Enochic canon there postdates and reflects Christianization as much as it explains it; (d) the pattern risks **post hoc selection** — picking groups that fit and under-weighting those that don't. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is a *tendency*, not a law, and the Samaritan case actually fits the deeper point (they preserved an *older, non-Jerusalem-standardized* Torah and were receptive), while the apostles'/Paul's recognition is consistent with the *fuller* tradition still being partially present in the canon and fully present to those the Spirit opened; the argument's force is cumulative with the textual evidence (Deut 32:8; the Parables), not a stand-alone sociology — its modest, defensible form is that *proximity to the centralized, standardized canon correlated with resistance*, which is at least a more specific account than "they were just hard-hearted."

## Best Quotes
> "The communities closest to the centralized temple controlled institutionally added canon were the communities most resistant to Jesus's claims. The communities that preserved older and broader apocalyptic traditions were the communities that were the most receptive." [^2]
