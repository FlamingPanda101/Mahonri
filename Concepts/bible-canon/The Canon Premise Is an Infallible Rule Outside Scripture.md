# The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]]'s first and (he says) most fatal argument against sola scriptura is a self-refutation: the proposition **"the 66 books of the Protestant Bible are the sole infallible rule of faith"** is *itself* an infallible rule of faith — yet it is *not stated in Scripture*. Therefore an infallible rule of faith exists *outside* Scripture, and sola scriptura is false.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The structure: an undeniable infallible premise not in the text — rate: strong
Horn's syllogism is tight: (P1) the canon proposition is infallibly true [[Anthony Rogers]] agrees]; (P2) it is not in Scripture; (C) so an infallible rule exists outside Scripture.[^1] The premise is genuinely load-bearing — every appeal to "Scripture says" presupposes knowing *which writings* count. **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers denies P2 — he claims the canon *can* in principle be shown internally (Hebrew connectives, the "seams of Scripture," Deuteronomy 18's forward-pointing prophet who ratifies the OT and authorizes the apostles). See [[The Internal-Seams Argument for the Canon]]. Horn's reply: even granting markers, no verse *enumerates* the list, and Rogers admitted he had no time to actually demonstrate it ("the qualities were not revealed tonight").

### 2. The self-authentication answer fails the historical test — rate: strong
Horn anticipates the "Scripture self-authenticates which books are Scripture" reply and gives a *positive defeater*: if books self-authenticated, the early church would not have had serious, sustained doubts about 2 Peter, James, 2–3 John, and Revelation (absent from the canon lists of Gregory of Nazianzus and Cyril of Jerusalem; [[Martin Luther]] threatened to throw James "into the fire").[^2] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers (via Nemesius of Emesa) argues every inspired book *is* self-authenticating because inspired — but concedes books like Philemon or 3 John don't "scream" their inspiration. Horn calls this "one giant circular argument." See [[The Self-Authentication Circularity Charge]].

### 3. We received the canon by trusting prior Christians — rate: strong
Horn: "if he's honest, he'll admit the reason he believes in the 27 books ... is because he and you and I trust Christians who came before us" — which shows infallible rules can be transmitted by *unwritten* means, disproving sola scriptura.[^3] This mirrors the classic "the canon is a list of authoritative books, but the list is not in any book" argument. **Steelman of Rogers:** trusting prior Christians is *ministerial* recognition of a self-evidencing reality (the sun analogy), not *magisterial* conferral; recognition ≠ authorization. See [[Thermometer vs Thermostat - The Church Declares the Canon]].

## Best Quotes
> "The proposition the 66 books in the Protestant Bible are the sole infallible rule of faith for the church — that itself is an infallible rule of faith. This proposition is not in scripture. Therefore, an infallible rule of faith exists outside of scripture. Therefore, sola scriptura is false."[^1]

> "The early church fathers did not agree on what books belonged in the New Testament. They had serious doubts about second Peter, James, second and third John and Revelation ... So much for being self-authenticating."[^2]

> "If he's honest, he'll admit the reason he believes in the 27 books in the New Testament ... is because he and you and I trust Christians who came before us who said these books were scripture."[^3]

> "Solos scriptura absolutely cannot get off the ground if you have an infallible rule of faith that defines what the canon is."[^4]
