# Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] argues that the Pentateuch contains **internal "tells"** — anachronisms and third-person framing — that betray composition by hands later than (and other than) [[Moses]]. These are not imposed by skeptical scholars but sit in the text itself, and were already noticed by early Jewish rabbis. Day affirms a historical Moses with an oral tradition, but holds that "what we have is like echoes of echoes" — edited material, not a single Mosaic autograph.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Moses praising his own humility ([[Numbers 12.3]]) (rated: classic; suggestive).** Numbers 12:3 — "the man Moses was very meek, above all the men which were upon the face of the earth." Day's point is the oddity of the world's *most humble* man writing that he is the most humble man: "Would the most humble guy write that he's the most humble guy?"[^3]

2. **The narration of Moses's own death ([[Deuteronomy 34]]) (rated: strong; the textbook case).** Deuteronomy 34 recounts Moses's death and burial, that "no man knoweth of his sepulchre unto this day," and that "there arose not a prophet since in Israel like unto Moses." A narrator writing "to this day" and "since" stands well after the events — a point conceded even by many traditional readers. Day notes the Book of Mormon's [[Alma 36.1|Alma]] gives a *different* take on Moses's end (translation/no burial), adding an LDS wrinkle.[^4]

3. **The "Canaanite was then in the land" gloss ([[Genesis 12.6]]) (rated: strong).** Genesis 12:6 says "the Canaanite was then in the land" — phrasing that presupposes the writer lives *after* the Canaanites are gone, i.e., long after Abraham. "It's way after Abraham... and it does this in a couple places."[^5]

4. **The geography of Deuteronomy 1:1 (rated: strong; lost in translation).** The Hebrew of Deuteronomy 1:1 says Moses spoke these words "on the other side of Jordan" (*be-ʿever ha-Yarden*) — a phrase that locates the *writer* on the **west** side of the Jordan, inside the land. But the Pentateuch insists Moses never crossed into the west. Day: early rabbis read this and concluded "this must have been written later... it's right there." The KJV obscures it because the translators rendered it in a way that hides the contradiction.[^6]

5. **The divine-name "tell" ([[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses|Exodus 6:3]]) (rated: strong; cross-listed).** A further internal seam — God revealing the name *Yahweh* as new to Moses while Genesis already uses it throughout — is treated in its own note, [[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses]].[^7]

**Steelman of the traditional view.** Classic Jewish and Christian readers long granted that *some* verses (Deuteronomy 34 especially) were added by a later hand — by tradition, Joshua or Ezra completing the scroll — without abandoning substantial Mosaic authorship of the rest; the Talmud (Bava Batra 14b–15a) already discusses this. So "Moses didn't write *these verses*" need not entail the full JEDP thesis. On Genesis 12:6, a defender can argue the clause is a later editorial gloss inserted into a Mosaic core, or even that Moses (knowing the Canaanites would be dispossessed) wrote prospectively. Day's framing is consistent with this modest reading: he is establishing that the text *was edited*, the premise the Documentary Hypothesis then systematizes — not proving JEDP from these verses alone. As he repeatedly says, "I'm just going to present some evidence, and you decide for yourself."[^2][^8]

## Best Quotes

> "Deuteronomy chapter 1 in the Hebrew literally says, 'These are the things that Moses said when he was on the other side of Jordan.' Which tells us that the person writing it is on the west side of Jordan. But the Pentateuch says Moses never went into the west side... Can you see the contradiction?"[^6]

> "Moses was the most humble guy ever. That's in Numbers 12:3. So if Moses wrote it, what's the problem? Would the most humble guy write that he's the most humble guy?"[^3]

> "There was a historical Moses, and he probably had an oral tradition. There may have been some stuff written, but what we have is like echoes of echoes."[^1]
