# Authorization — Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The affirmative (Kelly) claims the 66-book Protestant canon is **infallible and the ultimate authority** for Christian doctrine. Hayden's central rebuttal is that such a claim requires a distinct, missing ingredient he calls **authorization**: an explicit, public act of God designating *this specific collection* as incapable of error and supreme. Absent that act, the claim is merely a human conclusion — however sincere or widespread.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Infallible + ultimate" is a categorically higher claim than "inspired" — rate: strong
Hayden's framing is logically clean: he grants the Bible may be "inspired, trustworthy, and even authoritative" while denying it is *infallible* (incapable of error) or *ultimate* (the apex authority correcting all others).[^2] By isolating the resolution's two adjectives, he shifts the burden squarely onto the affirmative to justify the *additional* claims, not merely the Bible's general value. See [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]]. **Steelman of Kelly:** a Protestant can reply that "God-breathed" (θεόπνευστος) at [[2 Timothy 3.14-17|2 Tim 3:16]] entails inerrancy — what God breathes cannot err — so inspiration and infallibility are not separable. Hayden pre-empts this by arguing θεόπνευστος concerns God *giving life* through his word, not a guarantee of inerrancy, and in any case does not *identify which books* are God-breathed.[^3]

### 2. Authority must be conferred, not self-asserted — rate: strong (philosophically), contested (theologically)
"An unauthorized person or group of people, no matter how large, saying that Kelly's canon is the ultimate authority does not magically make it so."[^4] This is a sound point about the *source* of binding authority: declaration ≠ designation. **Scholarly context:** this mirrors the classic Catholic/Orthodox "canon problem" argument (the canon is a list of authoritative books, but the list itself is not in any book), and resembles Roger Beckwith vs. Lee McDonald debates on canon formation. **Steelman of Kelly:** God can authorize *providentially* (by preserving and causing the church to recognize the right books) without a dramatic public proclamation — recognition, not legislation. Hayden's reply: providential recognition is still a *fallible human process*, so it cannot ground an *infallible* result. See [[The Church Once Accepted Books It Later Rejected]].

### 3. The demand for a "public, unambiguous" revelation — rate: moderate, double-edged
Hayden repeatedly asks: "Has God publicly and unambiguously identified your 66 books as scripture?" and notes that if Kelly succeeds "he'd literally be the first person in history to do so."[^5] This is rhetorically powerful and historically defensible. **But it is double-edged:** the same demand can be turned on the LDS canon (where is the *public, unambiguous* divine designation of the Book of Mormon as scripture, beyond a claimed private vision?). Hayden's defense is that the resolution only obligates *Kelly* (the affirmative); the negative carries no burden to authorize an alternative.[^6] This is formally correct as debate procedure but leaves the LDS positive case untouched rather than vindicated.

### 4. Authorization underlies the LDS worldview itself — rate: framing
The deep motive: Latter-day Saints locate doctrinal authority in *living, divinely-authorized prophets and apostles*, not in a self-interpreting text. Hayden's "authorization" requirement is the wedge that makes room for continuing revelation and priesthood authority — if no static canon can be self-authorizing, an ongoing authorized voice is needed. See [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]] for the affirmative's counter-mechanism.

## Best Quotes
> "For any authority to bind belief, especially an infallible and an ultimate one, it must be authorized by God. Otherwise, it remains a human conclusion, however sincere or widespread."[^1]

> "The question before us is not whether Kelly's canon is reliable or even authoritative, but whether God has identified this specific collection of books as infallible and the ultimate authority over doctrine."[^7]

> "An unauthorized person or group of people, no matter how large, saying that Kelly's canon is the ultimate authority does not magically make it so."[^4]

> "If the Protestant specific canon and its infallible and ultimate authority nature are so important, why has no one ever produced a revelation or a record of God or of Jesus Christ or of any of his apostles teaching such?"[^8]
