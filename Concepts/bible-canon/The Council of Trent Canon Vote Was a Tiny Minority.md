# The Council of Trent Canon Vote Was a Tiny Minority

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Responding to [[Trent Horn]]'s claim that the church *infallibly declared* the canon (see [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]]), [[Anthony Rogers]] argues the historical reality undercuts the grandeur of that claim: the Council of Trent — per the *Catholic Encyclopedia*, the *first* ecumenical council to define the canon — settled it with a "piddling" handful of bishops. Of ~800 bishops then in the Catholic Church, only 55 attended the session, and only about 25 voted for Rome's canon (not even a majority of those present; many abstained). And these were the heirs of men like Hosius who said Scripture has "as much force as the fables of Aesop" apart from the church.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The numbers argument — rate: moderate, rhetorically effective
Rogers' figures (800 bishops, 55 present, ~25 in favor) dramatize how few hands settled "the entire Christian church throughout all of human history."[^1] It punctures any image of an overwhelming conciliar consensus. **Steelman of Horn:** on the Catholic view, conciliar infallibility does not depend on *vote tallies* or attendance but on the Spirit's guarantee through the church's teaching office (ratified by the pope); a small validly-assembled session can still bind. Horn would also note Trent *re*affirmed a canon already declared at Hippo/Carthage (4th c.) and Florence — Trent was not inventing it ex nihilo.

### 2. The "guilt by association" with Hosius — rate: weak as argument, strong as polemic
Rogers links the canon-definers to Cardinal Stanislaus Hosius' "fables of Aesop" line to discredit the spirit of the council.[^2] **Steelman of Horn:** Hosius' rhetorical hyperbole about the church's role in authenticating Scripture is not the same as the council's dogmatic decree; this is a *poisoning-the-well* move rather than a defeater of conciliar authority.

### 3. Genetic vs normative — rate: framing
The deeper exchange: Rogers uses the messy *genesis* of the conciliar canon to argue it cannot be the *infallible* ground of Scripture; Horn would distinguish the human messiness of the process from the Spirit-guaranteed *result*. This mirrors the broader Reformation argument that a fallible historical process cannot yield an infallible canon — see [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]] and Horn's counter that recognition is Spirit-guided declaration, not mere human tallying.

## Best Quotes
> "It's the council of Trent ... the first ecumenical council to decide this matter. Out of the 800 bishops that existed ... only 55 were present for the session that determined the canon ... only about 25 people voted for Rome's canon out of the 55 present. That's not even a majority."[^1]

> "For the entire Christian church throughout all of human history, you had a piddling number of bishops ... determining the canon."[^1]

> "Remember the kind of people who were determining the canon — those who said it has about as much force and authority as fables apart from us. You're going to trust that?"[^2]
