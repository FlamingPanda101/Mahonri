# 1, 2, and 3 John Are Pseudepigrapha

## The Claim
The letters 1, 2, and 3 John are **pseudepigrapha**: their authors knew and loved the Gospel of John and deliberately wrote in its style to present their works as supplemental writings by the Gospel's original author, while addressing new challenges. They are not the communal byproduct of a "Johannine community."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The letters smack of the Gospel on first reading.** Opening 1 John beside John, the shared keywords (beginning, word, life) and the shared self-presentation as those who have "seen" the Word made flesh "smacks you in the face." This is the overlap the community hypothesis was built to explain. *(Strong as description; the overlap is uncontested.)* [^1]
2. **Pseudepigraphy is the documented ancient mechanism.** Imitating an earlier author's style to appear to be that author is a known phenomenon; Méndez's colleague (Bart Ehrman) prefers the blunt term "forgery," though the field avoids it as too pejorative. *(Strong; the practice is well attested across Christian and classical literature.)* [^2]
3. **The letters do new work the Gospel did not.** The authors "saw what the Gospel of John didn't do when it was first published" and wrote letters addressing "new sorts of challenges, exploring new sorts of directions" — i.e. supplementation, the typical motive of pseudepigraphal continuation. *(Moderate; explains the differences as well as the similarities.)* [^3]
4. **The Pauline precedent.** Letters were written in Paul's name (some canonical, some not), establishing that early Christians produced pseudonymous letters tied to a revered author. *(Strong; comparative.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A traditional reader can hold that one author (or close associates) wrote both Gospel and letters, which would equally explain the verbal overlap without any deception; and the "community" model explains it as honest tradition rather than forgery. The strongest counter is that "pseudepigraphy" is an inference about authorial intent that the texts themselves never confess.

## Best Quotes
> "I think the authors of 1, 2, and 3 John knew the gospel. I think they loved the gospel... they wrote letters that present themselves as if they're... supplemental works by that original author." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^3]

> "Pseudepigraphy — you know, my colleague Bart... prefers the vocabulary of forgery... something where an author is using another person's name for some reason." [^2]

> "Paul wrote letters and we know that other people wrote letters in the name of Paul. Some letters that probably made it into the Bible and some that definitely did not." [^4]
