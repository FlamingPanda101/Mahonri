# The "Therefore" in Matthew 5:48 Ties Perfection to Loving Enemies

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]]'s central exegetical move: the connective **"therefore"** in [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] ("Be ye *therefore* perfect") points backward to the immediately preceding *love-your-enemies* unit ([[Matthew 5.43-45|Matthew 5:43–45]]). So "be perfect, even as your Father is perfect" means *be perfect in loving even your enemies* — as God "maketh his sun to rise on the evil and the good." Whatever "perfect" (*teleios*, see [[Teleios Means Mature and Whole Not Flawless]]) means, the verse specifies the *domain*: enemy-love.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **"Therefore" demands an antecedent (rated: strong, grammatical).** Hatch: "whenever you see the word *therefore* in scripture, ask yourself what's the *therefore* there for?" Like the Book of Mormon's "thus we see," it draws an inference from what came just before — here vv. 43–47 on loving enemies, blessing persecutors, and not merely greeting one's brethren.[^1]

2. **The logic is "be like God by loving enemies" (rated: strong).** vv. 44–45: love your enemies "*that ye may be children of your Father which is in heaven*" — because He sends rain on just and unjust. v. 48 caps it: *therefore* be perfect (in this) as He is. "Anybody can love someone who loves them — even gangsters do that"; the God-like, hard part is loving those who hate you.[^2]

3. **Amy-Jill Levine's grammar (rated: strong, scholarly).** The Jewish NT scholar [[Amy-Jill Levine]] notes the verb is **not an imperative** but a **future indicative** — an *if/then*: "if you love your enemies as God loves his enemies, *then* you will be perfect." v. 45 already says as much three verses earlier.[^3] This dovetails with the Luke parallel and manuscript evidence in [[Luke 6.36 and Sinaiticus Show Matthew 5.48 Belongs to the Love Pericope]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Other able readers (Hatch names Taylor Halverson and Tyler Griffin) treat 5:48 as the *culmination of the whole Sermon* in a covenant/temple key — a maximal call to total Christlike living, not just enemy-love. On that view the "therefore" can gather the entire preceding sermon, and the verse's placement as a hinge to chapter 6 is deliberate. Hatch concedes this reading is "valuable"; his counter is structural (the verse sits mid-sermon, and Luke isolates the saying inside a love-context). The two readings are not strictly exclusive — enemy-love can be the *lead instance* of a broader maturity — but the immediate antecedent of "therefore" is the love unit.

## Counter-Objection
"'Be perfect as God is perfect' is too cosmic to be only about enemies." Hatch's reply: that is precisely the point — Matthew grounds the *highest* God-likeness in the *hardest* concrete act (loving enemies), the same ethic that runs from the Hebrew prophets through Jesus; cf. [[Matthew 19.21|Matthew 19:21]] and [[1 John 4.18|1 John 4:18-21]] where *teleios* is again defined by love and self-giving (see [[Be Perfect Defined Elsewhere as Caring for the Poor]]).
