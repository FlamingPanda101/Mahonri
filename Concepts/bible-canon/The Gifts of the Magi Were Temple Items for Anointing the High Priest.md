# The Gifts of the Magi Were Temple Items for Anointing the High Priest

## The Claim

Gold, frankincense, and myrrh were not random luxury baby-gifts or a Marco-Polo-style "test" of the child's nature. They are **three first-temple materials**, and the magi — as [[The Wise Men Were Bearers of Preserved First-Temple Covenant|bearers of preserved temple covenant]] — brought them to the infant Jesus to **perform an ordinance**: to anoint him as the great High Priest, the Anointed One (Messiah/Christ). The magi came not merely to *worship* but to *officiate*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. All three are temple substances

- **Gold** — the temple interior was gold-plated throughout: the candlestick (menorah), the table of showbread, the mercy seat. Gold is the temple's material.
- **Frankincense** — compounded into the sacred incense burned in the temple, whose smoke "invoked the presence of the Lord" and carried prayers to heaven; a crucial element of temple worship.
- **Myrrh** — the base of the holy anointing oil, said to "impart holiness," which **could not be used outside the temple**. It was reserved for temple anointing alone.[^2]

> **Steelman:** The three gifts genuinely map onto temple/cultic categories — gold (sanctuary fittings, e.g. Exodus 25), frankincense (the incense of Exodus 30:34 and the showbread), and myrrh (a principal ingredient of the holy anointing oil of Exodus 30:23). The cultic association of all three is textually grounded, even if Matthew does not interpret the gifts this way.

### 2. The anointing oil "disappeared" around 600 BC

Tradition holds the holy anointing oil vanished from the Holy of Holies around 600 BC — the same horizon as the Babylonian captivity and Lehi's departure — so its reappearance in the magi's hands marks a *restoration* of a lost temple instrument by those who preserved it.[^3]

### 3. Adam brought the three from Eden (Cave of Treasures)

The apocryphal *Cave of Treasures* says Adam carried gold, frankincense, and myrrh out of the Garden of Eden, and at his death they were sealed in a cave. Read with [[Margaret Barker]]'s Eden-as-first-temple thesis, the three gifts are temple objects from the prototype sanctuary — "so precious and sacred" they were hidden away, paralleling the recurring motif of sacred things (e.g. the brass plates) removed before a temple falls.[^4]

### 4. The recipient is the High Priest, so the act is an anointing

If the bearers knew what gold, frankincense, and myrrh were *for*, and the child is "the great High Priest," the natural inference is that they came to **anoint** him into the temple rites — an ordinance, not a gift exchange. Woodmansee notes this may be why the text shifts from "Jesus" to "the Christ" (the Anointed) at this point; the magi's act is what marks him *as* Messiah, the role later echoed when Mary anoints him for burial. See [[The Heavenly Temple and Christ as Great High Priest]].[^5]

> **Steelman:** "Messiah"/"Christ" literally means *anointed one*, and high priests were inducted by anointing (Exodus 29:7). Framing the magi's temple-substance gifts as a priestly anointing is a coherent typological reading that takes the title "Christ" at its etymological face value.

## Counter-arguments / Related

- The mainstream symbolic reading (gold = kingship, frankincense = divinity, myrrh = death/burial) already gives the three gifts theological weight without a literal anointing ordinance; the "anointing" thesis is an additional, speculative layer.
- The reading depends on the prior thesis ([[The Wise Men Were Bearers of Preserved First-Temple Covenant]]) and on the contested first-temple reconstruction of [[Margaret Barker]] and [[Hugh Nibley]]. As with the parent claim, [[Bethlehem Birth Is Likely an Invention to Fulfill Prophecy]] represents the critical-academic view that the magi pericope is not historical reportage to begin with.
