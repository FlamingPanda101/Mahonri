# Love Makes a Family Means Child Loss Makes That Family

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] inverts the slogan at the heart of the marriage debate. If a child has a *natural right* to be known and loved by his or her own biological mother and father, then "love makes a family" cannot be the whole story — because for many of the arrangements the slogan is used to validate, **a child must first lose a biological parent to be placed in that family**. So "from a children's-rights perspective… the idea that love makes a family functionally means *child loss* makes that family."[^1] Specifically for same-sex households: "If love makes a family for same-sex couples, that means we are going to require this child lose their mother or father to be in this family."[^1]

Faust's strongest evidence is autobiographical and offered as a reductio. The premise behind redefining marriage and family, she argues, is that "a child's own mother or father is optional to them" — that "two men could have replaced my mom… and it would have zero impact on my identity formation, my development… the safety and investment and connection that I had in my family." Against her own life that is "not just statistically a lie," it is "demonstrably" false: "I needed my father and I needed my mother… before the divorce… after the divorce."[^2] She presses the point: she defends the mother-father norm not in spite of loving her mother (who is in a long-term same-sex partnership) but *because* she loves her — the redefinition project implies her mother "was optional," which she knows to be false.[^3]

She extends the same structure to surrogacy and donor conception (a child "intentionally created to be motherless or fatherless," triificating a woman into "genetic mother, birth mother, and… social mother") and to no-fault divorce, treating all three as mechanisms that "incentivize" the very losses that "create… a primal wound in the life of a child."[^4] The companion empirical claim — that these losses are real and that one *kind* of loss wounds worse than another — is developed in [[Loss by Intentionality Wounds Differently Than Loss by Tragedy]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The logical move is valid given its premise (rate: strong as analysis).** If one *grants* a child's right to his biological parents, then any arrangement that is constituted by severing that tie does entail a loss to the child — the slogan "love makes a family" hides the subtraction. The argument's whole weight rests on the disputed premise (the child's right), not on a logical slip.[^1]
2. **The autobiographical reductio is rhetorically potent (rate: real but anecdotal).** Faust's "two men could have replaced my mom" framing makes vivid that the no-difference thesis implies the *interchangeability* of a specific parent — a conclusion most people resist on reflection. As evidence it is a single case and self-selected, but it functions to expose what the opposing premise commits one to, which is legitimate.[^2]
3. **It reframes the debate around a third party.** The argument's force is that marriage/parenting debates usually weigh two sets of *adult* interests, while the child — who cannot vote or consent — is the party whose stake is largest and most permanent. This connects to [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]] (worth grounded in being, not capacity).[^4]

### Steelman of the progressive view

The progressive case is substantial and should not be caricatured. (a) **"Family" is not reducible to genetics.** A vast literature on adoption, step-families, and kinship care shows children thrive when they have stable, committed, loving caregivers; the bond that matters is social and relational, and treating the biological tie as a trumping *right* devalues the millions of flourishing non-biological families — including Faust's own household, which adopted a child. (b) **The "loss" is often not a loss the child ever had.** A donor-conceived or adopted child placed at birth never possessed a relationship with the absent biological parent to lose; framing their existence as constituted by "child loss" can be experienced by such children as a denial of their family's legitimacy. (c) **The counterfactual is frequently non-existence, not a two-biological-parent home.** For many children of surrogacy, donor conception, or same-sex couples, the alternative was never "raised by mom and dad" but "never born at all," which complicates calling their situation a deprivation. (d) **Comparative welfare, not the slogan, should decide.** The honest question is whether children in same-sex or donor-conceived families *actually* do worse on measured outcomes — an empirical question (joined in [[The No-Difference Same-Sex Parenting Studies Are Methodologically Weak]]), not one settled by re-wording a slogan. (e) **Rights can conflict and be weighed.** Even granting a child's *interest* in biological parents, adults also hold real interests in marriage and family formation; a liberal framework balances competing interests rather than declaring one categorically "bows down" (the move critiqued by progressives in [[Child Rights Confront Sexual Autonomy as the Highest Value]]). Faust's reply is that a genuine *right*, unlike a mere interest, does take priority, and that the comparative-welfare data (on her reading) confirm the deprivation rather than dissolve it.

## LDS Relevance

The claim maps closely onto *The Family: A Proclamation to the World*, which declares that "children are entitled to birth within the bonds of matrimony, and to be reared by a father and a mother who honor marital vows with complete fidelity," and that gender is "an essential characteristic of individual premortal, mortal, and eternal identity." Faust's child-centric framing supplies a *natural-law* articulation of what the Proclamation states as revelation: the mother-father structure is not an arbitrary preference but is owed to the child. This parallels the way [[Evolution Points to Family as the Locus of Meaning]] reads nature as "designed for that Proclamation," and it coheres with the LDS conviction that exalted family relationships are the substance of eternal life — so the family is precisely *not* an adult convenience to be reconfigured at will. An LDS reader can affirm the children's-rights argument as a public-square translation of the Proclamation while leaving its theological grounding (premortal identity, sealing) in reserve for a different audience.
