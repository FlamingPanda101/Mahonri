# Is It Moral to Be Correct - Bridging Objective Truth to Objective Morality

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Shad Brooks]] offers a single question that, he says, "has stumped every single one" of the atheists he has debated, as the bridge from objective truth to objective morality. After first securing that **objective truth exists** — "1 + 1 = 2... irrespective of someone's perspective," and a subjective denial "just means you're wrong, not that you have your own truth to live by"[^1] — he asks the interlocutor: **"Is it moral to be correct?"**[^2] The trap: an atheist who denies God has no ground for morality, so being correct would be "unnecessary... if not in the ends of just furthering your own purpose, which is then based off of internal desires and ego."[^3] Yet, Shad argues, everyone inescapably *intuits* that it *is* moral to be correct, because "that would be the nature of the universe": "you tried to fight and deny being correct, that will lead to disaster and destruction. If you go out of your way to be incorrect on everything, you will die."[^4] Granting that being correct is moral concedes objective morality.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The two-step structure.** Step 1: objective truth is "a stronger and a more easy kind of thing to argue" (math, non-contradiction); step 2: most people "fight tooth and nail" against objective *morality*. The question "is it moral to be correct?" is engineered to run step 2 off the back of step 1 — if truth is objective and being correct is good, then good is objective.[^5] Rate: **the hinge of the argument** — it converts an admission about epistemics into an admission about ethics.
- **The survival illustration.** Rejecting truth is self-destructive: "DON'T EAT THAT. — I reject that truth, and I choose to be incorrect and I will eat it anyway."[^6] Being correct is not a neutral preference but tracks flourishing vs. death, which Shad takes as evidence that correctness is woven into "the nature of the universe." Rate: persuasive as intuition-pump; weaker as proof (see steelman).
- **Continuity with the truth=morality thesis.** This argument is the lemma that lets Shad later assert "truth is morality" outright via [[D&C 88]]'s truth=light=law chain — see [[Truth Is Law Is Morality - The D&C 88 Equivalence Chain]]. Cardon reformulates it as "truth leads to morality... truth, law, commandments, morality," and Shad pushes further: "truth *is* morality."[^7]
- **Steelman the critic.** A naturalist can answer "yes, being correct is *instrumentally* good" (it aids survival/goals) without conceding *objective moral* value — collapsing the gap Shad needs. This is the classic is/ought and grounding-vs-knowing distinction the vault treats in [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]], [[Being Moral vs Grounding Morality - Hitchens Conflation]], and [[Harris Describes Morality But Cannot Ground Concern for Others]]. The survival appeal also risks the very evolutionary grounding Shad would reject ([[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]]). The question is rhetorically powerful but the inference "being correct is good ⇒ objective morality" needs the further premise that this good is *moral* rather than merely *prudential*.

## Best Quotes
> "1 + 1 = 2. That is an objective truth irrespective of someone's perspective... If you have a subjective interpretation that 1 plus 1 doesn't equal two, that just means you're wrong, not that you have your own truth to live by." [^1]

> "I then ask them: is it moral to be correct? Because if you don't believe there is a God, then there is no morality. Therefore being correct is unnecessary." [^2]

> "There is something inside us that we inherently know that it is moral to be correct. In fact, that would have to be the case, because that would be the nature of the universe." [^4]
