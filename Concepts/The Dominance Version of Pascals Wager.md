# The Dominance Version of Pascals Wager

#type/argument

## The Point
There is a stronger, simpler form of [[Blaise Pascal]]'s wager — argued by [[Susanna Rinard]] to be closer to Pascal's own intent — that sidesteps the standard objections. Its key move: you are better off as a believer *even if God does not exist*. If belief wins in every state of the world, then by a basic principle of rational choice you should believe.[^1]

## Argument
1. **Premise 1 (unchanged).** If God exists, you are better off as a believer than as a non-believer.[^2]
2. **Premise 2 (the new claim).** Even if God does *not* exist, you are still better off as a believer than as a non-believer.[^2]
3. **Dominance principle.** "If, no matter what the world is like, the outcome of choosing option A will be better than the outcome of choosing option B, then you should choose option A."[^3] Rinard calls this "very plausible" and "hard to argue with."
4. **Conclusion.** Since believing wins whether or not God exists, you should believe in God.[^2]
5. **The argument's center of gravity.** Because Premise 1 and the dominance principle are nearly unassailable, "the crucial question for this version is whether the second premise is right."[^4] The whole dispute reduces to an empirical question — is the believer actually better off in this life? — which the video answers from the sociological literature (see [[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view:** The dominance form only works if Premise 2 is true *for the individual unbeliever in question*, and that is contestable. (a) Aggregate sociological correlations (religious populations do better on average) do not establish that *this* skeptic would be better off adopting a belief they regard as false; "healthy-user," selection, and reverse-causation confounds dog the religion-and-wellbeing literature. (b) Many would count *believing what is false* as itself a cost — a loss of intellectual integrity not captured in the payoff table. (c) If Premise 2 makes the wager turn on this-worldly benefit, the "infinite payoff" that gave the original its force has quietly dropped out; the argument becomes a claim about flourishing, not about eternity, and a sufficiently fulfilled atheist could simply deny Premise 2.

## Best Quotes

> "The second premise of this argument is different. It claims that even if God doesn't exist, you're still better off as a believer than as a non-believer. If so, then you're better off as a believer whether or not God exists, and so you should believe in God."[^2]

> "If no matter what the world is like the outcome of choosing option A will be better than the outcome of choosing option B, then you should choose option A. It's hard to argue with that."[^3]

> "The crucial question for this version of Pascal's wager, though, is whether the second premise is right."[^4]
