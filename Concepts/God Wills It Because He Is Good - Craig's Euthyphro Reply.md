# God Wills It Because He Is Good - Craig's Euthyphro Reply

## The Point
Grounding morality in God invites the Euthyphro dilemma: "Is something good just because God wills it, or does God will something because it is good?"[^1] The first horn makes morality arbitrary (God could have willed cruelty); the second makes the Good independent of God (so God is dispensable). Craig answers with a third option that splits the dilemma: "The answer is: neither one. Rather, God wills something because he is good. God is the standard of moral values."[^2] God neither invents the Good by fiat nor consults a standard above himself — God's own perfect nature *is* the standard, so his willing tracks his being.

## Evidence
Craig makes the "God is the standard" claim concrete with a fidelity analogy: "just as a live musical performance is the standard for a high-fidelity recording. The more a recording sounds like the original, the better it is. Likewise, the more closely a moral action conforms to God's nature, the better it is."[^3] Goodness is thus a measure of *conformity to a fixed original*, not conformity to a changeable command or to an abstract Form floating free of God.

The reply also protects the rest of the argument. If morality were grounded in a standard *above* God, that standard — not God — would be the real moral foundation, and the atheist could access it directly; if it were *mere* command, the "arbitrary tyrant" objection would bite. The third option preserves both objectivity (the standard is fixed in God's unchanging nature) and theism's necessity (the standard is God himself), which is what premise one of the moral argument requires.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Is something good just because God wills it, or does God will something because it is good? The answer is: neither one. Rather, God wills something because he is good." [^2]

> "God is the standard of moral values, just as a live musical performance is the standard for a high-fidelity recording." [^3]

> "The more closely a moral action conforms to God's nature, the better it is." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Craig's "neither/nor — God wills it because he *is* good" is the same third-horn strategy Turek frames as rejecting the false "A and B" dilemma ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]), and it is the affirmative counterpart to the critique that *voluntarist* divine-command theory collapses under Euthyphro ([[The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory]]). The Restoration sharpens the reply: where Craig grounds the standard in God's *nature*, LDS theology adds that this moral order is *eternal law* — co-eternal with God, which he perfectly embodies, honors, and administers rather than authoring from nothing ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]). This blocks the arbitrariness horn even more decisively than the bare "nature" reply, since God's goodness is not a brute fact about his will but his perfect alignment with eternal law. The recording analogy maps cleanly onto theosis: becoming good is conforming ever more closely to the divine original (see also [[Moral Values From God's Nature, Moral Duties From God's Commands]]).
