# The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The most common reply to [[The Creation Dilemma]] is the **free-will defense**: people *freely* choose hell, and God respects their freedom. [[Hayden Carroll]] rebuts that this "quietly assumes the first morally decisive moment is human rebellion — and it is not. The first morally decisive moment is creation itself. Before anyone chooses anything, God chooses whether that person will exist at all. Freedom operates within existence, and creation comes first."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Temporal/logical ordering** — rate: strong. Human free choices are *downstream* of God's prior choice to create the chooser. "You cannot justify creating someone under known catastrophic conditions by appealing to choices they will later make." *(logical-priority)* [^1]
2. **Dangerous-tool parallel** — "If I knowingly give a dangerous tool to someone I know will misuse it, I share responsibility for what happens. Freedom of the individual does not erase the initial moral causation of the creation of that person." *(moral-parallel)* [^2]
3. **Therefore free will is a red herring** — Hayden's striking concession: he has *no problem* with libertarian free will or with someone freely choosing hell. The argument survives even granting maximal free will, because it targets the creation decision, not the damnation decision. See [[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]]. *(argumentative-jiujitsu)* [^3]

**Steelman of the free-will defender:** (a) *Existence is a baseline good* — by creating, God confers the gift of being and the genuine possibility of beatitude; the misuse is the creature's, not the giver's, so responsibility does *not* transfer (the dangerous-tool analogy fails because a person is not a mere instrument and retains authorship of their acts). (b) *Molinism* — perhaps every feasible world containing this person's free goods also contains their free damnation, so "refrain from creating" forecloses real goods. Hayden's reply to (a): knowledge + power + freedom-to-refrain still yields shared causation; reply to (b) is undeveloped in the debate (he does not engage middle knowledge by name).
