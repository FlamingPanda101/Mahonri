# God Is Perfect Goodness Itself

## The Point
Feser argues that goodness is objective and convertible with *actuality*: a thing is good to the degree it realizes the nature or essence of its kind, and bad to the degree it fails to. [^1] Badness is therefore a **privation** — the absence of a perfection a thing ought to have, not a positive reality. [^2] Since God is pure actuality with no unrealized potential, "we have to attribute to God pure goodness and the utter absence in him of any sort of badness or evil." [^3] Equivalently, since whatever good is in the world derives from God, he is the source of all possible goodness and so "all good." [^4]

## Evidence
1. **Goodness is objective, not subjective preference.** A carefully drawn triangle is a *better* triangle than a wobbly one scrawled on a moving bus; a tree with deep roots is a *good* tree. These judgments "simply follow from the objective facts about the nature" of the thing, not personal taste. [^1] Moral good and bad are special cases: free creatures choosing to facilitate or frustrate the flourishing their nature requires. [^5]
2. **Goodness = actuality, badness = privation.** "Goodness is a kind of actuality, and badness a kind of unrealized potentiality." [^2] Lacking a fourth side is no defect in a triangle (a perfect triangle has three), but a crooked side is — just as blindness is the absence of sight, not a positive feature. The privation account survives objections about pain, sadism, and the reality of evil. [^6] (See [[Evil Is a Privation Not a Thing]].)
3. **Pure act entails pure goodness.** Because actuality corresponds to goodness and God is purely actual, he is wholly good. And by proportionate causality, whatever good *could* exist must in some way be in him. [^3]
4. **Why evil need not be in God.** Proportionate causality says positive *features* of an effect must be in its cause — but evil is not a positive feature. Making a world with evil is "only a matter of making good things, but also refraining from making some of the good things that could have been there," like a lecturer deliberately drawing an incomplete triangle to make a point. [^7] God's permitting localized badness is consistent with perfect goodness when the whole is better for it — e.g., "courage could not exist unless people faced real danger of suffering harm." [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Goodness is a kind of actuality, and badness a kind of unrealized potentiality. To be bad in some respect is, ultimately, to lack something rather than to have something, just as to be blind is simply to lack sight." [^2]

> "If actuality corresponds to goodness and badness to unrealized potentiality, then we have to attribute to God pure goodness and the utter absence in him of any sort of badness or evil." [^3]

> "Evil is not some thing that God has put into the world alongside all the good things he has put there. Rather, evil is the absence of certain good things he refrained from putting there." [^7]

## LDS Connection
This is among Feser's most ecumenically usable arguments. Latter-day Saints wholeheartedly affirm that God is perfectly good and never the author of evil, and the privation analysis is congenial: it protects God from the charge of "creating evil" without denying evil's reality ([[Evil Is a Privation Not a Thing]]). The first divergence is metaphysical. Feser's "God *is* Goodness Itself" rests on divine simplicity (God's goodness = his existence = pure act); the Restoration's embodied, progressing Father is good by perfectly *conforming to* and embodying eternal moral reality rather than by being identical to an abstract Goodness ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). This bears directly on Euthyphro: Feser takes God's own nature as the standard of goodness ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]), whereas LDS thought is more comfortable saying goodness answers to principles God himself honors. Second, on the problem of evil, Feser's "God refrains from making certain goods" theodicy assumes ex nihilo sovereignty over every detail; the Restoration grounds much evil in the genuinely free acts of co-eternal agents in a world of real opposition ([[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]). In fairness, Feser's courage-requires-danger point and his airtight refutation of the privation-theory objections are strong, and Latter-day Saints can borrow the insight that goodness tracks the fulfillment of a thing's nature.
