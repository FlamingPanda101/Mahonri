# Protestantism Inherited a Divided Church It Did Not Break Unity

## The Claim
The common objection — Protestantism shattered the one unified church Christ founded — rests on a myth. There was no unified church to shatter. Protestantism "doesn't divide a church that's united; it inherits a divided church," and it inherits a *long, savagely persecuted tradition of protest* that predates Luther by centuries. The pre-Reformation church was riven by the East–West schism, the Western (papal) schism, and the papalist-versus-conciliarist wars over the institution itself; proto-reformers like the Hussites, Lollards, and Waldensians were crusaded against and burned. The Reformation therefore introduced "further institutional separations" but did not originate division — and it reinterpreted what such separation *means*, rejecting the premise that the church is "one institution with one visible hierarchy" outside which everyone is "off Noah's ark."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The medieval "unity" was mutual anathema.** "The Eastern Orthodox and the Roman Catholics in the medieval era looked across the way at each other and saw the other side as outside of the one true church and off of Noah's ark and cut off from salvation." Even within the West there were "a series of different schisms... regarded as absolutely severe by those involved." [^1]
2. **Four popes excommunicating each other.** During the Western Schism, "for an entire century from 1305 to 1377, there were at one point four popes excommunicating each other." Hans Hillerbrand is quoted: "there would have been a division in the church if Luther had died in his cradle." [^2]
3. **Pope Benedict's own description.** Ortlund quotes [[Pope Benedict XVI]]: "For nearly half a century the church was split into two or three obediences that excommunicated one another, so that every Catholic lived under excommunication by one pope or another... The church no longer offered certainty of salvation... The true church, the true pledge of salvation, had to be sought outside the institution." [[Erasmus]] is paired with this: the church had never been "more corrupt from head to toe, from the pope all the way down" — plus the papalist-vs-conciliarist "major wars... within the church." [^3]
4. **A persecuted tradition of protest predates Luther.** Ortlund's "personal hero" Jan Hus, "Bohemian reformer, great preacher," was burned at an ecumenical council in 1415 for protesting simony, "the purchasing of church offices," and "papal overreach," and for wanting "the Bible to be translated into the vernacular." "The Roman Catholic hierarchy waged five distinct crusades against the Hussites alone." The Lollards and followers of John Wycliffe were likewise persecuted. The ordinary Christian of the 1300s–1500s "would be somewhat starved of the word of God in [his] vernacular language, starved of frequent participation in the Eucharist," amid indulgence-abuse and "superstition." [^4]
5. **Unity is reconceived, not abandoned.** Protestantism "introduces a different way of thinking about these institutional splits... not having such a hardline on institutional unity, which is only one piece of the pie for unity" — "one of the gifts of Protestantism is this ability to think more expansively about the nature of unity." [^5]

## LDS Relevance
A Latter-day Saint can grant the historical point enthusiastically — the medieval church was neither pure nor unified — but draws the opposite *conclusion*. For the panel, inherited division licenses a Protestant rethinking of unity as something looser than one visible hierarchy. For the LDS view, the same wreckage (mutual anathemas, popes excommunicating popes, no institution that "offered certainty of salvation") is evidence the keys of authority had already departed, requiring a [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed|restoration]] rather than a redefinition of unity downward. Where Protestantism answers a divided Christendom with "many true churches, invisible unity," the Restoration answers it with the re-establishment of a single divinely authorized church and living apostles — recovering the visible-unity ideal the panel sets aside (cf. [[Name the True Church or Concede the Apostasy - the Negative's Burden]]). The Hussite/Lollard "remnant" the panel celebrates is, for the LDS reading, light kept partially alive in the dark — genuine but lacking the priesthood keys (see [[Medieval Christians Kept the Light of Christ Alive Not Apostates]]).
