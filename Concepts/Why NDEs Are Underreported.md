# Why NDEs Are Underreported

## The Point
To the objection "if near-death experiences are so common, why isn't this more generally known?" Moody gives three reasons [^1]. First, the "temper of our times" is hostile to talk of survival of death, so experiencers stay silent for fear of being called "crazy" [^2]. Second, a psychology of *attention*: people skip over what they have no category for, just as one fails to notice a word until learning its meaning [^3]. Third, physicians — the group most likely to encounter NDEs — are specifically trained to discount the patient's subjective reports [^4].

## Evidence
1. **A hidden mass of testimony.** At every public lecture someone afterward volunteers an experience, including people who did not come for the topic; Moody is "convinced... an enormous mass of material lies hidden in the minds of persons" who, fearing the label "crazy" or "over-imaginative," have told only one or two confidants [^2].
2. **The attention phenomenon.** "A lot of what we hear and see every day goes unregistered." Like learning a new word and then "seeing the word in everything," the experiences were present all along but skipped over for lack of a concept [^3].
3. **The doctor who hadn't heard — until he asked.** When a physician objected that he'd never heard of such cases, Moody turned the question to the audience and the doctor's *own wife* raised her hand with a close friend's story [^3].
4. **Medical training discounts symptoms.** M.D.s are "constantly" taught to trust objective "signs" and "take the subjective reports ('symptoms') of the patient with a grain of salt"; since few ask resuscitated patients about their perceptions, doctors are "in fact not much more likely to hear of near-death experiences than are other persons" [^4].

## Best Quotes
> "an enormous mass of material lies hidden in the minds of persons who have had such experiences but who, for fear of being labeled 'crazy' or 'over-imaginative,' have never related them to more than one or two close friends or relatives." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The cultural reflex to ridicule reports of the supernatural as belonging to a "superstitious past" is the same secular-materialist temper that dismisses Joseph Smith's First Vision and modern revelation a priori. The counsel to share sacred experience only with those prepared to receive it (D&C 63:64) helps explain the same reticence Moody documents — sacred things are guarded, not broadcast, which naturally suppresses the public record.
