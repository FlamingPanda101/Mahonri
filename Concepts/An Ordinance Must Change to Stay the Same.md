# An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same

## The Claim

Responding to media attention on revisions to the temple endowment and garments, Luke Hansen (on Ether's Elephant) argues that changes to temple ordinances are not evidence of corruption or instability, because **the ordinance is the covenant, not its form**. The deeper, paradoxical point: even if the words and clothing *never* changed, shifting historical context would silently change their *meaning* — so to preserve the same significance, the outward form must sometimes change. "If it's going to stay the same, it has to change."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Form vs. covenant.** "The ordinance is not the form. The ordinance is the covenant." The mutable elements (gestures, garments, wording) are the vehicle; the covenant relationship they enact is the constant. Revising the vehicle need not alter the covenant.

2. **Meaning is context-bound.** Hansen's worked example: Nauvoo-era washings were a *literal* bath because pioneers had not bathed in a week — a practical significance no modern rite could reproduce. Keeping the identical practice today would *change* its meaning (from a real cleansing to mere symbolism), so faithful continuity can require adaptation.

3. **A general hermeneutical principle.** The argument generalizes the truism that a fixed text or ritual lands differently as its world changes; preserving intended meaning across centuries is an active, not a static, task. This pairs with the broader epistemic posture that the surviving historical record is a poor instrument for adjudicating such developments — see [[History Is Bad Data - The Wrong Instrument to Falsify Faith]].

## Steelman / Counter

The critic's reply: "it has to change to stay the same" is unfalsifiable — any revision, in either direction, can be retro-fitted as preserving the covenant, so the principle explains away all evidence and predicts nothing. If garments and wording can change freely while "the covenant" is declared constant, the claim of an unchanging restored ordinance becomes unanchored. The apologetic rejoinder distinguishes the *covenantal core* (claimed invariant) from negotiable *presentation*, and argues that critics conflate the two precisely to manufacture a contradiction.
