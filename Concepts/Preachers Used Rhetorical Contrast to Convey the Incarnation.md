# Preachers Used Rhetorical Contrast to Convey the Incarnation

## The Point
Early Christian preachers deployed vivid imagination and the rhetorical figure of *contrast* to convey the marvel of the Incarnation — God's condescension into flesh. [[Isaac the Teacher]] paints the silent unawareness of Judea when the Magi arrive; [[Augustine]] piles up paradoxes of the infinite God become a helpless infant. These flourishes "reeducated the Christian mind to see the world around them in sacramental and symbolic terms."

## Evidence
1. **Imagination as the preacher's art.** "Part of the preacher's art was 'appealing to the imagination of his hearers in descriptive pictures.'" The fifth-century Isaac the Teacher, preaching on the Magi (Matthew 2:1–12), imagines their arrival. [^1]
2. **Isaac's contrast of festival vs. stillness.** The Magi find Judea "silent — a people not in a state of rejoicing! … 'Within Persia there is a great deal of excitement, but here, a great stillness. … God is in this land, but its inhabitants are still sleeping! The Lord of All is among the earthly inhabitants, but the Hebrews are unaware of Him!'" Heal: the believer can "walk into this country with the Magi and be amazed that no one knows what has happened." [^2]
3. **Augustine's Christmas paradoxes.** In his Christmas sermon (AD 411/412): "The maker of man, he was made man, so that the director of the stars might be a babe at the breast; that bread might be hungry, and the fountain thirsty; that the light might sleep, and the way be weary from a journey; … that the cluster of grapes might be crowned with thorns, … that strength might grow weak, eternal health be wounded, life die." [^3]
4. **The effect of such flourishes.** Augustine here "rises from the simple exposition to a rapturous artistry which transcends the boundary between poetry and prose." Such rhetoric "reeducated the Christian mind to see the world around them in sacramental and symbolic terms … reinforcing the reality of God's action in the world by reimagining it and restaging it Sunday after Sunday." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "The maker of man, he was made man, so that the director of the stars might be a babe at the breast; that bread might be hungry, and the fountain thirsty; that the light might sleep … that strength might grow weak, eternal health be wounded, life die." (Augustine, Sermon 191) [^3]

> "God is in this land, but its inhabitants are still sleeping!" (Isaac the Teacher) [^2]

## LDS Connection
The preachers' rhetoric of divine condescension expresses the same wonder Nephi names in 1 Nephi 11:16–26 — "the condescension of God" — the central marvel that the infinite God should descend to mortal flesh, central to Latter-day Saint Christology.
