# Probability of God Must Be Weighed Against All the Evidence

## The Point
Craig's second reply to the evidential problem of evil is a point about how probability works: **probabilities are always relative to background information.** When the atheist says "God's existence is improbable," the correct response is a question — "improbable relative to *what* background information?"[^1] If we restrict the evidence to the suffering in the world alone, theism may indeed look improbable; but that is an artificially narrowed evidence base. Weighed against "the full scope of background information" — including "the powerful arguments for God's existence" — "we may come to a very different conclusion."[^2] The probabilistic argument smuggles in a selective evidence set, and once the full set is admitted, its conclusion no longer follows.

## Evidence
Craig illustrates with the **sumo wrestler**. Considering only how much a certain man weighs, "we would say it's highly improbable that he's a world-class athlete." But add the information that "he's a professional sumo wrestler and the world champion," and "we quickly revise our view."[^3] The weight datum was real but partial; a probability judgment built on it alone was badly misleading. By analogy, the suffering in the world is real evidence, but a verdict of "God is improbable" reached on that evidence *alone* commits the same error — it answers "improbable relative to a deliberately impoverished slice of the evidence." The repair is to fold in the rest: the cosmological, moral, teleological, and other arguments treated across this vault all belong in the background set the atheist must weigh. This dovetails with reply one — the limited-vantage point (see [[We Cannot Judge That God Lacks Reasons for Suffering]]) — and with Feser's observation that *demonstrated* theism reverses the burden, since "we already have independent reason to think that God exists" (see [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]]). The methodological lesson is general: an evidential argument is only as good as the completeness of the evidence it lets in.

## Best Quotes
> "Probabilities are always relative to background information ... when the atheist claims that God's existence is improbable, we should ask, improbable relative to what background information?" [^1]

> "If we consider only how much this man weighs, we would say it's highly improbable that he's a world-class athlete. But when we're willing to consider new information, that he's a professional sumo wrestler and the world champion, we quickly revise our view." [^3]

> "If we're willing to look at the full scope of background information ... to take into account the powerful arguments for God's existence, we may come to a very different conclusion." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This reply is fully shared ground for Latter-day Saints, and the Restoration *widens* the relevant background set in two directions. First, the LDS case for God does not rest on philosophical theism alone; it adds the **witness of the Spirit** and concrete restorational evidences (the Book of Mormon, the testimony of witnesses, Joseph Smith's first vision) as further background information that bears on the probability of God's existence — Moroni's promise that one "may know the truth of all things" by the Holy Ghost (Moroni 10:4–5) is, in Craig's terms, an invitation to enlarge the evidence base rather than judge on suffering alone. Second, Restoration theology furnishes additional *theodicy*-relevant background that lowers the surprise of evil (the pre-mortal estate, eternal agency, opposition in all things) — see [[Christian Doctrines Raise the Probability of Evil]] for how doctrine itself functions as background that raises the conditional probability of suffering. A fair caution: this point establishes only that "improbable on suffering alone" does not entail "improbable, full stop"; it does not by itself prove theism. Its force is to dismantle a selective inference and hand the burden back — the atheist must engage the whole evidential picture, exactly the posture the Restoration encourages a seeker to take.
