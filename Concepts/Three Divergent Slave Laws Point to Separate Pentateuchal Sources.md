# Three Divergent Slave Laws Point to Separate Pentateuchal Sources

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] argues that the Pentateuch contains **three different, mutually divergent slave laws** — in Exodus (the "Covenant Code," which he assigns to E), Deuteronomy (D), and Leviticus (P) — that disagree on "major monumental issues," most clearly on whether a freed slave leaves with or without compensation. The divergence is hard to explain if one author (Moses) wrote all three close together, and is naturally explained by the Documentary Hypothesis as separate legal traditions "reacting to each other." Day insists the observation stands *even if* one rejects the DH: "you don't even have to believe in the Documentary Hypothesis... but we have to acknowledge the text."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The three laws actually diverge (rated: strong; textual).**
   - **Exodus / Covenant Code ([[Exodus 21.6|Exodus 21:2-6]], = E):** the male Hebrew slave serves six years and "leaves without payment"; if he wishes to stay, his ear is pierced (the "really cool piercing").[^3]
   - **Deuteronomy ([[Deuteronomy 15.12-15|Deuteronomy 15:12-15]], = D):** the slave leaves *with* a payment — the master must "furnish him liberally" from flock, threshing floor, and winepress, grounded in the refrain "remember that you were a slave in Egypt." Day reads D as the "softest"/kindest toward the slave.[^3][^4]
   - **Leviticus ([[Leviticus 25.39-43|Leviticus 25:39-43]], = P):** no mention of payment upon release; the Israelite is treated as a hired servant freed at the Jubilee.[^3]

2. **The Covenant Code is treated as the earliest stratum (rated: mainstream within the field).** Day notes most DH scholars read **D as reacting to** the Covenant Code, which they call the oldest legal text — and cites [[David Wright]], who argues the Covenant Code itself adapts still-older Near Eastern sources (Wright's thesis: dependence on the Laws of Hammurabi). He concedes the *relative* order of P and D is unsettled ("Did P come after or before Deuteronomy? I can't settle that for you").[^5]

3. **The argument is independent of full JEDP (rated: strong).** Like the vault's [[Source Criticism Is Not Reducible to the Documentary Hypothesis|distinction between source criticism and the DH]], Day's point needs only that these are *distinct legal traditions*, not the entire Wellhausen reconstruction. Acknowledging the divergence costs the reader nothing doctrinally; harmonizing it requires special pleading.[^2]

4. **LDS framing — "sit in the mess."** Day reports being emailed by Latter-day Saints distressed that the Old Testament regulates slavery while [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C]] 101:79 says "it is not right that any man should be in bondage one to another." His answer is deliberately non-harmonizing: "Sit in the mess... I'm not here to fix it. I can't fix it. I just acknowledge it." The Restoration, not a forced reconciliation of the legal codes, carries the doctrinal weight.[^6][^7]

**Steelman of the harmonizer.** A defender can argue the three laws address *different situations* rather than contradicting: Exodus and Deuteronomy treat the six-year Hebrew debt-slave while Leviticus 25 treats the impoverished Israelite released at Jubilee, and Deuteronomy's "furnish liberally" could be read as a humane *development* of, not a contradiction to, Exodus — a single legislator refining policy. Day's reply is that the divergences are on "monumental issues" and too systematic to dissolve, and (crucially) that even granting the harmonization, the laws still read as distinct strata with distinct theological tempers. Note also that this differs in scope from the vault's [[Covenant Code Lexical Precision on Social Status|Covenant Code lexical note]], which concerns *who* the Exodus terms protect, not the *three-way divergence across sources*.

## Best Quotes

> "There's basically three different slave laws in the Pentateuch and they're all different... these three laws are divergent on major monumental issues."[^1]

> "In Exodus... the slave serves for six years, leaves without payment... In Deuteronomy, you leave with a payment... And then Leviticus, there's no mention of payment upon release... my point is these are not the same. And we just — it just is."[^3]

> "You don't even have to believe in the Documentary Hypothesis... but we have to acknowledge the text."[^2]
