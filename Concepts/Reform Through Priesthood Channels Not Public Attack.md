#type/claim

# Reform Through Priesthood Channels Not Public Attack

## The Claim
When [[Steven Pynakker]] asks whether a member may criticize the church *in order to help and reform it*, [[Jacob Hansen]] answers: yes — but only **within the priesthood structure**, never by public attack. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is "a top-down structure, not a bottom-up structure"; an ordinary member "is not the arbiter of what is good for the church." Legitimate avenues are (1) bringing concerns to local priesthood leaders, and (2) the right to *sustain or not sustain* leaders. Going *outside* that structure — publicly and repeatedly — is what triggers discipline, "which is what happened, for example, with [[John Dehlin]]." Good intentions do not license the public route. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Intent to help is not enough; the venue is the point.** "It isn't just that you have an intention to want to make the church better because you're not the arbiter... An ordinary member of the church is not the arbiter of what is good for the church... we are a top-down structure, not a bottom-up structure." [^1] Within priesthood channels, reform-minded members "can do that"; outside them, "you will be excommunicated for such." [^2] *Rating: strong as ecclesiology;* it cleanly separates the *content* of a concern (which may be valid) from the *forum* (which is governed), and it is the mechanism that makes [[A Member Can Be an Anti-Mormon|a publicly-campaigning member]] count as anti while a privately-dissenting one does not.

2. **The sustaining vote is the member's real lever — and should be used honestly.** Hansen affirms public criticism "is something that you're not supposed to do as a member," but adds that members "who do not sustain leaders should be frank about that fact... And when leaders do things that are wrong, they should stop sustaining them." [^3] He tells Kolby Reddish that if he felt leaders failed to stop abuse, "you should... let your leaders know that you no longer support that." [^3] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it answers the "so members are powerless?" objection by locating real agency in the sustaining vote rather than in a public platform.

3. **The institutional analogies: military and basketball coach.** "If I was in the military, I'm not allowed to go out and publicly attack the president. Does that mean I can't talk... within the right channels about decisions I think are wrong? Sure." [^4] And: if you disagree with the coach mid-game, "do you get into an argument with your coach" or work the play and raise it "after the game or in private"? Ultimately, "if I think the coach is just a terrible coach... fine, leave the church." [^5] *Rating: moderate;* the analogies illuminate the "hierarchical, not democratic" claim and supply a clean exit (leave) as the honest alternative to public insurgency — but they presuppose the church's divine top-down authority, which is exactly what a critic denies, so they persuade insiders more than outsiders.

**Steelman of the critic.** A reformer can answer that some institutional failures — especially the mishandling of abuse that Kolby Reddish raises — are precisely the cases where internal channels have *already failed* and where publicity is the only remaining corrective; "take it to your bishop" is cold comfort when the bishop is the problem. The military analogy also imports a chain-of-command whose legitimacy is contested. Hansen's reply is that publicity that *erodes the fundamental claims* is different from publicity aimed at a specific abuse, and that the honest endpoint of irreconcilable disagreement is to leave rather than to remake the institution from outside (see [[Reforming a Thing's Essence Is Destroying It Not Reforming It]]). The unresolved tension is whether there is any legitimate public whistle-blowing lane for a member who believes the hierarchy itself is failing.
