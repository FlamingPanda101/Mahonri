# Cross-Cultural Convergence on a Divine Council Is the Oldest Picture of God

## The Claim

Strict philosophical monotheism is **not** the original or default human conception of God. When you trace religious belief to the origin point of every literate ancient tradition, the same complex architecture keeps appearing independently: a supreme God, a tiered council of divine beings beneath him, and a populated heaven that deliberates, governs, and relates to humanity. [[Alex Arnold]] argues that ~17 unconnected civilizations across every inhabited continent converged on this same *structurally specific* framework — and that this convergence is a serious problem for the claim that [[Joseph Smith]] invented divine-council cosmology in 1830.

This is the comparative-religion complement to the Hebrew-Bible-internal case in [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]]: the same picture is in the oldest layer of Israel's scripture **and** everywhere else.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The roster of independent traditions (the breadth is the point).** Sumer (the **Anunnaki** under Anu); the Akkadians/Babylonians (**Igigi** and Anunnaki as two tiers, with the *Enuma Elish* divine assembly saying "let us create mankind" — the same plural deliberation as [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We|Genesis 1:26]]); Canaan (**El** over 70 *Bene El*, with Baal subordinate — known from the [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon|Ugaritic tablets]]); Egypt (the **Ennead** and Ogdoad); the Hittites (the **Pankus** assembly); Assyria (Ashur at the head); the Greeks (Zeus over the Olympians) and Romans (Jupiter); the Norse (Odin over the Aesir); Vedic India (Indra over 33 Devas); the Aztecs (Ometecuhtli with Quetzalcoatl, Tezcatlipoca, Tlaloc); the Maya (the *Popol Vuh* council deliberating creation in the plural); China (the Jade Emperor's celestial bureaucracy); Shinto kami; and even Aboriginal Australian Dreamtime — isolated for tens of thousands of years — with multiple creator beings who deliberate together. [^1] [^2] [^3]
2. **It is structural precision, not vague similarity.** The convergence is "a tiered hierarchy, a deliberative function, and a supreme being in a defined relationship with subordinate divine persons who hold named roles across domains of creation." Random coincidence does not produce that degree of overlap across 17 independent traditions. [^4]
3. **The trilemma.** Arnold reduces the explanations to three. (a) *Independent coincidence* — fails on structural specificity. (b) *A universal psychological impulse* — fails because psychological universals produce **more** variation in the details, not less, and "if anything should be universal, it's the simplest possible conception of God," which is not what the ancient world produced. (c) *Fragments of one original revelation* preserved in pieces across civilizations — which Arnold notes is "the entire framework that the LDS central thesis operates on," while stressing the specific LDS application "is not required for the logic to work." [^5] [^6]
4. **The narrow, defensible conclusion.** The argument does **not** claim to find full LDS cosmology (the nature of the Father, Christ's role, the plan of salvation, priesthood, temple ordinances are "distinctly their own") in the ancient record. It establishes only that the *underlying cosmological architecture* matching what Joseph Smith described "isn't a 19th-century invention" — "the oldest picture of the divine that human civilization has consistently produced, including in the Hebrew Bible itself." [^7] [^8]

## Best Quotes

> "Every one of these traditions, independently and across every inhabited continent with no meaningful contact between most of them, arrived at the same basic cosmological structure. A supreme being with a council of divine persons beneath him and a populated heaven full of divine beings with actual agency and relationships." [^2]

> "Pointing out that Sumer had a divine council doesn't make LDS theology Sumerian any more than pointing out that Sumer also had flood narratives and therefore makes Genesis Sumerian." [^7]

## Steelman the Critic

(1) **Diffusion, not independence** — Many of these cultures (the entire ANE chain Sumer→Babylon→Canaan; Greece→Rome) were in contact, so "17 independent traditions" overstates the independence; the genuinely isolated cases (Aboriginal Australia, Mesoamerica) are fewer. (2) **Polytheism is the cheap explanation** — A populated divine realm may simply be the natural pre-scientific way to model an unpredictable world (personified forces), which is closer to Arnold's rejected "psychological universal" than he allows. (3) **Cherry-picked structure** — "Council of gods" is a loose enough template that almost any pantheon can be made to fit; the disanalogies (no Father-Son plan of salvation, no exaltation of humans in most systems) are precisely the LDS-distinctive parts, which the convergence does **not** supply (a point Arnold concedes). (4) The trilemma may be a false trilemma — common cognitive architecture + cultural diffusion together can cover the data without "one original revelation."
