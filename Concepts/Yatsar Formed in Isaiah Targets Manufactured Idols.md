# Yatsar Formed in Isaiah Targets Manufactured Idols

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]] — "before me no god was **formed**, nor shall there be any after me" — asserts Yahweh's eternality and pre-existence over against all other gods, a claim no mere football-rivalry analogy could make, and therefore implies the other gods do not really exist.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Formed" is *yatsar* — the verb for sculpting an idol (rating: strong; lexical)
The Hebrew root *yatsar* means to form, mold, or sculpt, "as one would do with pottery or with an idol." It is **not** the language used in the ancient Near East for the origin of gods, which is normally **procreation**. [^1]

> "the word that is rendered formed in this translation is the Hebrew root yatsar which means to form or to mold or to sculped as one would do with Pottery or with an idol this is not the language that is commonly used in ancient West Asia to refer to the creation of the Gods it's usually procreation that results in the gods" [^1]

### 2. So the verse denies divinity to *manufactured* things (rating: strong)
Read with its verb, the text says: things that are *formed by human hands* (idols) were not there before Yahweh and will not be after — they are not actually divine. It is the same idol polemic found elsewhere in Deutero-Isaiah, not a denial that other (non-manufactured) gods exist. [^2] See [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]].

> "what this text seems to be saying is those things that are formed with human hands are not gods they were not there before me they're not going to be there after me it's not saying no other gods exist it's saying that the things that are formed are not actually Divine" [^2]

### 3. Coheres with the "nothing/less-than-nothing" lexical argument (rating: strong)
This pairs with [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]]: the same chapters call existing nations "nothing," and call *idols* "nothing," using the manufacture vocabulary throughout. The target is consistently the made object.

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's strongest point is that Isaiah 43–48 stacks *yatsar* alongside genuinely temporal/eternal claims — "I am the first and the last," "declaring the end from the beginning," "no savior besides me" — which read most naturally as predicates of a uniquely eternal deity, not just a polemic against statues. [^3] McClellan answers that (a) the governing verb is still the idol-manufacture term, and (b) sole-creator / pre-existence rhetoric is paralleled in the Amun-Ra hymn without producing monotheism (see [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]). Note the LDS-apologetic version of this same lexical point is logged separately at [[Isaiah 43-10 - No God Formed Before Me Rebutted]].
