# The "Amen, Brother" Apologetic Strategy

## The Claim
[[Ward Radio]] proposes a "counter sound bite" to the [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]] attack: instead of a long rebuttal, *agree* enthusiastically — anyone preaching against Christ's resurrection is accursed — then note Latter-day Saints emphatically affirm the resurrection. The agreement disarms the accuser and exposes how shallow the proof-text is.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Sound bites need sound bites — sound reasoning
The hosts observe a "classic problem in discussion": it takes far more words to debunk a slur than to throw one, and "you can't hand a master's thesis" to an accuser or third-party observers feel no answer was given. So the rebuttal must be compressed into a comparably short reply.[^2] Rating: **sound** rhetorical principle.

### 2. Affirming the resurrection inverts the trap — strong on its own terms
By saying "Absolutely — anyone denying Christ rose is accursed; we *know* he rose, and we even have what he did afterward (3 Nephi)," the Latter-day Saint accepts the verse's logic while denying its application. This leverages [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|Paul's gospel]] and [[Galatians 1-8 Latter-day Saints Preach Pauls Gospel|the shared-gospel rebuttal]].[^3] Rating: **strong** as disarming rhetoric; depends on the shared-gospel argument for substance.

### 3. Relational disarming over debate-winning — anecdotal
The hosts argue the goal is to "jar them," break the script their pastor gave them, and bond ("Amen, brother. High five. Here's a strudel."), opening real conversation. Falter recounts this working on his mission to defuse rehearsed challenges, though he candidly admits it has not always "worked."[^4] Rating: **anecdotal** (self-reported, mixed results).

### Steelman of the opposing view
A critic could call this a rhetorical sleight-of-hand: cheerfully agreeing with the verse's *form* while quietly redefining "gospel" sidesteps the real charge (that LDS doctrine of God, exaltation, and additional scripture *is* materially "another gospel"). Warm agreement can substitute affect for argument and leave the substantive disagreement untouched. **Response within the source:** the hosts present "Amen, brother" as an *opener* that earns the chance to make the actual case (Paul's defined gospel, the "we," Revelation 14:6), not as the whole argument — its job is to stop reflexive mud-throwing so a genuine exchange can begin.

## Best Quotes
> "I think the answer is you're right. Well, Galatians 1:8 says an angel did it, and you go absolutely, you say... Anybody says Jesus didn't rise from the grave, Amen, brother. He absolutely rose from the grave after 3 days." [^1]

> "There's a classic problem in discussion that it takes a lot more words and sentences to debunk a nasty slur... You can't hand a master's thesis to somebody who accuses us of that." [^2]

> "It's such a shallow mud throwing that you have to stop and freak them out a little bit and be like, 'Yeah.' And jar them. And only then can you maybe break through to having an actual conversation." [^4]

> "Sorry, brother. You're totally wrong. We're with you. Woohoo! Give them a high five. Give them a strudel. Send them on their way." [^3]
