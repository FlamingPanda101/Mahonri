# Why the Necessary Being Must Be God Not the Universe

## The Point
Granting that there must be a necessary being, the critic asks: why call it *God* rather than just the universe itself? [^1] Feser answers that the necessary being's attributes can be derived from its necessity alone, and that these attributes are exactly the divine ones. A necessary being must be purely actual, absolutely simple, and subsistent existence itself; and (from earlier chapters) there can be only one such thing, which is therefore the unique uncaused cause of everything else, and which must also be immutable, eternal, immaterial, incorporeal, perfect, omnipotent, fully good, intelligent, and omniscient. [^2] "But for there to be such a thing is for God to exist." [^3] The universe cannot be that being, because it changes (so has actualized potentials and is not purely actual) and has parts (so is composite) — hence it is contingent, not necessary. [^4]

## Evidence
1. **Necessity entails pure actuality, simplicity, subsistent existence.** Any potentiality would make the being depend on something to actualize it; any composition would make it depend on something to combine its parts; any essence distinct from existence would require a cause to impart existence. In each case it "wouldn't really be necessary after all." [^5]
2. **Uniqueness.** There cannot in principle be more than one thing that is purely actual / absolutely simple / subsistent existence itself, so there is exactly one necessary being, and it is the cause of all contingent things — "the cause of everything other than itself." [^6]
3. **The universe fails the test.** "The universe undergoes change, which entails that it has potentials which are actualized and thus is not purely actual; and it has diverse parts, which entails that it is not simple or noncomposite. Hence, it cannot be a necessary being." [^4]
4. **"Who caused God?" misfires here.** The rationalist proof never claims everything has a *cause* (only that everything has an explanation), and it makes no exception for God. God's existence does not lack an explanation: the explanation lies in his own nature as that which is purely actual, simple, and subsistent existence itself. The universe, by contrast, cannot be self-explanatory. "The difference between God and the world then is not that one has an explanation and the other lacks it, but rather that one is self-explanatory while the other is not." [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "What exists in a necessary way must be purely actual, simple or noncomposite, and so on. But the universe undergoes change... and it has diverse parts... Hence, it cannot be a necessary being." [^4]

> "God's existence does not lack an explanation. The explanation lies in his own nature as that which is purely actual, simple or noncomposite, and subsistent existence itself." [^7]

> "The difference between God and the world then is not that one has an explanation and the other lacks it, but rather that one is self-explanatory while the other is not." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this step is valuable: it refutes the popular "the universe itself is the necessary thing" gambit by showing the cosmos is changing and composite, hence contingent — it cannot be the self-existent ground. Latter-day Saints agree the physical cosmos is not the ultimate, self-existent reality. The honest divergence is sharp here, because the *content* of Feser's God is the absolutely simple, immutable, immaterial *actus purus* of classical theism — precisely the conception the Restoration regards as a Hellenized overlay on the embodied, dynamic God of scripture (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]] and [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]]). The LDS Father is embodied and acts within eternal law rather than being unconditioned simplicity (see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). The fair point to grant Feser: *if* one accepts divine simplicity and the act/potency framework, the inference from "necessary being" to "the classical God" is tight. The LDS reply targets those premises, not the validity of the move from them — affirming a self-existent divine ground while denying it must be metaphysically simple and impassible.
