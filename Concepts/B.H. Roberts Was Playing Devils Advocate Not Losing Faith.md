# B.H. Roberts Was Playing Devil's Advocate Not Losing Faith

#type/claim

## The Claim
Keystone calls this "probably the most egregious error in [Johnny Harris's] entire video." To support the assertion that "even some of the church leaders and official historians" studied the Book of Mormon and "sorrowfully conclude[d] that the evidence points to Joseph Smith as having created all this himself," Harris supplies a single quotation from [[B.H. Roberts|B.H. Roberts]]. But Roberts never lost his faith in the Book of Mormon: in his *Book of Mormon studies* he was deliberately playing **devil's advocate**, marshalling the strongest case that *could* be made against the book precisely so the Church could answer it — not stating his own conclusions.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Roberts's own framing letter (decisive primary source).** Mid-study, Roberts wrote to President Heber J. Grant and the Quorum of the Twelve explaining the work. He says he wrote "from the viewpoint of an open mind, investigating the facts," and — "once for all so as to avoid... repeated explanation" — that "what is herein set forth does not represent any conclusions of mine," but is "a study of Book of Mormon origins for the information of those who ought to know everything about it."[^2] *Rating: strong — Roberts disclaims the conclusions in his own words, in writing, at the time.*

2. **He explicitly affirms unshakable faith.** In the same letter: "I am taking the position that our faith is not only unshaken but unshakable in the Book of Mormon, and therefore we can look without fear upon all that can be said against it."[^3] *Rating: strong — directly contradicts the "sorrowfully concluded it was fraud" reading.*

3. **The lifelong public record.** Keystone notes the "public record is absolutely unambiguous that B.H. Roberts affirmed his faith in the Book of Mormon and Joseph Smith until he die[d]," a point treated in more depth in a prior [[Stephen Smoot]] interview.[^4] *Rating: strong — biographical consensus.*

4. **The general pattern behind "juicy" Roberts quotes.** When a critic produces a "damning" Roberts quotation, what Roberts is doing is "laying out the argument that can or could be made" and saying "we need to address this" — it is the adversary's brief, not Roberts's verdict. This is why the quotation needs its surrounding context, which Harris omits.[^5] See [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]] and [[Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point]]. *Rating: strong — explains the recurring misuse.*

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman of Harris)
The Roberts material is genuinely double-edged. Roberts did privately judge some problems (e.g., the *View of the Hebrews* parallels, linguistic and population issues) to be serious and unresolved, and his tone in places is more troubled than a mere neutral exercise. A fair critic can argue that "devil's advocate" understates how unsettled Roberts personally was, and that a scholar can both retain belief and quietly concede the apologetic case is weak on specifics. The honest LDS reply is narrower than a blanket dismissal: the *specific* quotation Harris uses is voiced as the adversary's argument, and Roberts's contemporaneous letter forecloses the claim that he "concluded" Joseph fabricated the book — whatever residual worries he carried, he affirmed his faith to the end.

## Best Quotes

> "What is herein set forth does not represent any conclusions of mine... namely a study of Book of Mormon origins for the information of those who ought to know everything about it." — B.H. Roberts, quoted by Keystone[^2]

> "I am taking the position that our faith is not only unshaken but unshakable in the Book of Mormon, and therefore we can look without fear upon all that can be said against it." — B.H. Roberts, quoted by Keystone[^3]

> "What he's doing there is he's laying out the argument that can or could be made and he's saying we need to address this. It's not actually reflecting what he himself actually believed."[^5]
