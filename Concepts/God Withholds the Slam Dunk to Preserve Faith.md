# God Withholds the Slam Dunk to Preserve Faith

## The Point

Griffiths offers a **theological reason** the decisive artifacts are absent: the missing plates are not an evidentiary failure but a feature of how God deals with humanity. "I don't think right now God intends for us to have the slam dunk because the question of faith still comes up."[^1] On this framing, possessing the gold plates (or any coercive proof) would collapse the space in which trust operates — so the artifact question is downstream of a deeper point about the nature of faith. This reframes the apologetic posture: the goal is not to manufacture certainty but to make belief reasonable while leaving room for trust.

## Evidence

- **Proof would end the test.** Griffiths: if he "could prove 100% that Jesus had been resurrected it'd be an easy go," but God evidently does not intend the slam dunk because "if we're going to trust and accept in things that are not seen," faith must remain operative.[^2] This echoes the biblical link between faith and the unseen (Hebrews 11:1).
- **Even firsthand witnesses don't transfer certainty.** He notes he knows people who say they have seen Christ and that Christ testified to them — yet that does not convert the question into a public, coercive proof for everyone else; the structure of faith is preserved.[^3]
- **He still wishes for the plates — and that's the point.** "I wish we had the plates... if they were here, that would make things a lot easier" — Griffiths does not pretend the absence is costless; he frames it as an intentional withholding rather than a defeat.[^4]
- **Consistent with the evidentiary model.** Because proof is deliberately withheld, the case is built on converging, non-coercive evidence rather than a single decisive relic ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]) and on a spiritual witness that invites rather than compels ([[Burning in the Bosom vs Self-Evidencing Light]]).

## Best Quotes

> "I don't think right now God intends for us to have the slam dunk because the question of faith still comes up."[^1]

> "If I could prove 100% that Jesus had been resurrected it'd be an easy go... but if we're going to trust and accept in things that are not seen."[^2]

> "I wish we had the plates. If we had them, could we be able to translate them? That's a whole other issue, but if they were here, that would make things a lot easier."[^4]

## LDS Connection

This is the pastoral capstone of Griffiths's answer on artifacts: having separated the withdrawn sacred objects ([[The Sacred Book of Mormon Artifacts Were Withdrawn Not Lost]]) from the surviving documentary ones ([[The Surviving Book of Mormon Artifacts Are Documentary Not Sacred]]), he locates the absence of decisive proof within an LDS theology of faith and agency — the same logic by which the Book of Mormon promises a *spiritual* witness to the sincere seeker (Moroni 10:3–5) rather than a museum exhibit. Belief is meant to be reasonable yet free.
