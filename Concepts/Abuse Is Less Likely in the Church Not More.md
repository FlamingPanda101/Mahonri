# Abuse Is Less Likely in the Church Not More

## The Point
Critics charge that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is *systematically* abusive — that something in its doctrine, ordinances, or structure inspires harm. Fife answers with comparative data: the best available dataset shows abuse rates *far lower* in Latter-day Saint settings than in homes, schools, or other churches, and the Church's distinctive practices function as safeguards. Whatever abuse does occur happens *in spite of* the Church's teachings, not *because of* them.

## Evidence
1. **The Boy Scout "Perversion Files."** Fife relays therapist and convert Jennifer Roach (FAIR address): historically ~30% of Boy Scout troops were Church-sponsored, yet Latter-day Saint troops accounted for only **5.16%** of abuse cases in the files — about **75% less** abuse than their participation rate would predict. Roach's line: "I mean, I know where I would put my kid."[^1]
2. **Baseline rates elsewhere are dire.** Fife cites ~7% of 8th–11th graders reporting physical sexual contact from an adult (often a teacher or coach), student-on-student assault seven times higher than adult-on-child, and federal data that 16.5% of children experienced physical abuse and 9.4% sexual abuse in 2020 — with most abuse perpetrated by a parent (77% of substantiated cases).[^2]
3. **Seven structural safeguards.** Roach identifies practices unique or unusually strong in the Church: the calling system (no self-selection into youth roles); sustaining votes (public objection possible); geographic ward boundaries (a predator can't church-hop); the membership-number system; gender-specific leaders for young women; withdrawal of membership for abusers (with First Presidency approval to return); and the abuse Helpline staffed by trained experts.[^3]
4. **Handbook policy.** Fife quotes General Handbook 38.6.2: abuse "cannot be tolerated in any form," leaders must report to civil authorities, take reports seriously, prioritize protecting the abused, and never counsel a victim to remain in an unsafe home.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "While the Church sponsored around 30% of the Boy Scout troops, the number of abuse cases involving LDS troops was only 5.16% of the cases. That's 75% less abuse among Latter-day Saint troops." [^1]

> "Like many other condemnations, critics are taking a strength of the Church and pretending it is a weakness." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Fife's argument is comparative and falsifiable: rather than denying that abuse ever occurs, he asks whether membership *raises or lowers* a child's risk relative to the alternatives — and answers that the Church's structure lowers it. This parallels his broader thesis (see [[Naturalistic Explanations Cannot Account for Collective Sacrifice]]) that critics consistently invert the Church's measurable strengths into alleged weaknesses. He grounds the moral stakes in Matthew 18:6 ("better for him that a millstone were hanged about his neck").[^4]
