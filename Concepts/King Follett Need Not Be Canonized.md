# King Follett Need Not Be Canonized

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Critics (and some members) ask why, if the [[King Follett Discourse]] is so important, it has never been canonized — implying the church is quietly disowning it. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] answers that its non-canonical status reflects redundancy, not repudiation: the sermon's ideas are *already* in canon.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The substance is already scripture.** "If King Follett offends you, go read [[D&C 93|section 93]]... If the idea of a plurality of gods offends you, go read [[Abraham 4.1|Abraham]] — Joseph Smith said directly that's where he got the idea of a council of gods."[^2] Canonizing the sermon would largely duplicate existing revelation (cf. [[King Follett Is Arrangement Not New Substance]]).
2. **Canonization sometimes circles back to old revelations.** Griffiths corrects the assumption that new canon requires new revelation: D&C 137 was received in 1836 and canonized in 1979; D&C 138 was received in 1918 and canonized in 1979. Sometimes the church "circles back to one of these ideas that we've allowed to marinate" and then canonizes it — so King Follett *could* be canonized later, though "it's not super necessary."[^3]
3. **Personal enthusiasm, institutional caution.** "If I was king for a day I would totally canonize it" — Griffiths affirms its value while accepting the reasons it remains out (redundancy plus its esoteric edges). He would also canonize the full Wentworth Letter and parts of the Lectures on Faith.[^4]
4. **Steelman of the critic.** The critic can argue redundancy cuts the other way: if the doctrines are already canonical (93, Abraham), the church cannot claim King Follett's teachings are merely "esoteric" or peripheral — they are scripture. And if the once-mortal-God idea is *not* clearly in canon, then King Follett *does* add something, and its exclusion looks like deliberate distancing (see [[Modern LDS Tensions on King Follett Discourse]]). Either way the "redundant, so unnecessary" defense is in tension with the "esoteric, so we don't emphasize it" defense.
5. **Evidence rating.** The canon-history examples (D&C 137/138, 1979) are factually solid and effectively dismantle the naive "uncanonized = rejected" inference. The deeper apologetic tension the critic raises is real and is handled in the companion note on modern LDS ambivalence.

## Best Quotes

> "If King Follett offends you, go read section 93 and think about it for a few minutes. If the idea of a plurality of gods offends you, go read Abraham — it's right there."[^5]

> "Section 137 was received in 1836, it was canonized in 1979. Section 138 was received in 1918, it was canonized in 1979... sometimes we circle back to one of these ideas that we've allowed to marinate for a long time and then we decide to canonize it."[^6]
