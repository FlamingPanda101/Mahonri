# Irreducible Complexity - Behe's Molecular Machines

## The Point
[[Michael Behe]] argues that the cell is run by genuine **molecular machines** — propellers, trucks, electrical apparatus — and that many are *irreducibly complex*: like a mechanical mousetrap, they need all their parts in place at once to perform any function.[^1] Behe coined the term because such a system "is complex, can't reduce it"; take away one piece and it does not work.[^2] This is fatal to a Darwinian origin, because Darwin insisted his theory had to work by "numerous, successive, slight modifications," each conferring an advantage — but an intermediate missing a part confers no function for natural selection to favor, so there is "nothing for natural selection to select on the way from the simple to the complex."[^3] Darwin himself set the test: "If it could be demonstrated that any complex organ existed which could not possibly have been formed by numerous, successive, slight modifications, my theory would absolutely break down."[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The cell is a city of machines.** Modern science (the past ~70 years) shows the cell is not the "blob of jello" Darwin's era imagined but contains "electrical apparatus… vehicles… information" — sophisticated machines made of molecules.[^1]
2. **The mousetrap analogy.** A mousetrap needs a spring, base, hammer, and other parts together; you cannot build it gradually with each stage functioning. So too a flagellum: "it needs all of the parts to work."[^5]
3. **The bacterial flagellum.** Meyer describes it as a "30-part rotary engine" whose protein parts must "fit together in an integrated fashion," each protein requiring "a long stretch of genetic information."[^6]
4. **Borrowed-parts objections fail.** You cannot just grab a spring from a grandfather clock and a crowbar for a hammer — "the pieces aren't adjusted to each other." Natural selection hones parts "for what they were doing right now," not for some future assembly.[^7]
5. **Darwin's "sneaky" burden-shift.** Behe notes Darwin demanded opponents prove a *negative* ("could not possibly"), "which science cannot do and never has"; meanwhile there is "absolutely no evidence that natural selection acting on random mutation could build much of anything."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "I invented the phrase irreducible complexity, because it's complex, can't reduce it." [^2]

> "Intermediate stages confer no functional advantage. Therefore there's nothing for natural selection to select on the way from the simple to the complex." (Meyer, summarizing Behe) [^3]

## LDS Connection
Irreducible complexity is a natural-theology argument congenial to a faith that affirms a designed, ordered cosmos and a divine Creator. The fit is broad, not doctrinally specific: the inference yields only "a designing intelligence," not the embodied, *organizing* God of the Restoration. Latter-day Saint theology holds that God organized eternal, uncreated matter rather than creating ex nihilo, so honesty requires noting that Behe argues for a generic designer, and the bridge from "designed machinery" to the God of the Restoration is a further step (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]). Compare the quantitative companion argument, [[The Edge of Evolution - The Quantitative Limits of Darwinism]].
