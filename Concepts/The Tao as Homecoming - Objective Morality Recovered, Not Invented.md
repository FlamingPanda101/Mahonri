# The Tao as Homecoming - Objective Morality Recovered, Not Invented

## The Point
*Abolition* can be read, in part, as the philosophical distillation of Lewis's own autobiography. As a young man he fell into a "vicious subjectivism," yet found he "never completely lost his tether to the Tao." [^1] When he met a group of non-Christian friends "who knew, and tried to obey, the moral law," their decency challenged and re-formed him — evidence that awareness of objective morality "is available as much to non-Christians as to Christians." [^2] To accept traditional morality, Lewis concluded, is not to adopt a new code but "to wake up to the implications of the code within which one already lives": a homecoming. [^3]

## Evidence
Ward draws three lessons from Lewis's *Surprised by Joy* relevant to *Abolition*. First, Lewis described his moral defects in dehumanising terms — "a zoo of lusts, a bedlam of ambitions… My name was legion" — the same logic by which the man who abandons the Tao becomes a "trousered ape." [^4] Second, his good pagan friends "were good by decent Pagan standards, and that was quite enough to challenge his own scrofulous behaviour," showing "a moral ecology known to a great number of different traditions." [^2] Even as a teenage atheist, Lewis insisted ethics is owed "to our own manhood & dignity and not to any imagined god or gods," tying morality to anthropology. [^5] Third, Lewis retained "a residual respect for the Tao, even as he disobeyed it," knowing "from personal experience, that the Tao was a real thing, an immovable norm or standard." [^6] His recovery came through Samuel Alexander's distinction between "Contemplation" and "Enjoyment": Lewis learned to "look along" his desire to its object rather than "look at" his own mind, discovering that Joy "proclaims itself sheerly objective" — a "road right out of the self." [^7] In "On Ethics" he wrote, "I send you back to your nurse and your father, to all the poets and sages and law givers, because, in a sense, I hold that you are already there whether you recognize it or not." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "I send you back to your nurse and your father, to all the poets and sages and law givers, because, in a sense, I hold that you are already there whether you recognize it or not." [^3]

> These are "things we owe to our own manhood & dignity and not to any imagined god or gods." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's "homecoming" — waking to a moral law already written within — resonates with the doctrine that every person comes into the world with the Light of Christ and a memory, however faint, of moral truth (Moroni 7:16; cf. Romans 2:14–15). His insistence that virtue is owed "to our own manhood & dignity" anticipates the Restoration's high anthropology: the human person bears a divine nature and an innate sense of right that conditioning can dull but not erase. Repentance, in this light, is less the importing of a foreign rule than a return — a relaxation "into one's earlier or original self," the self God made.
