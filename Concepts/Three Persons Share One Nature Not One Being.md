# Three Persons Share One Nature Not One Being

## The Point
The single point [[Jacob Hansen]] names as "the primary distinction" between Latter-day Saints and creedal Christians is *consubstantiality*. He affirms three fully divine persons who "share one nature... just like we're two people but we both share the fullness of human nature," but denies they are "one being": "what makes you your own person is to have your own being."[^1] He reads "I and the Father are one" through John 17 — the same indwelling oneness Christ wants His disciples to share — and John 1:1 as "the Word as to His nature [is] deity" (crediting James White).[^2] [[Allie Beth Stuckey]] defends the Nicene formula of "three persons, one being / one God," pressing John 1:1, Hebrews 13:8, and Old Testament monotheism, and noting the Trinity is "a bit mysterious" because analogies collapse into tritheism.[^3]

## Evidence
- Hansen's "persons share a nature like two humans share humanity" is precisely the move that yields, on the creedal reading, three Gods — which is why Stuckey insists on numerically *one being* (see [[Consubstantiality]] and [[Divine Unity - There Can Be Only One God]]).
- His John 17 reading is the standard LDS rebuttal catalogued in [[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality]]: oneness is relational indwelling, since Christ prays His disciples be one "as we are one."
- Stuckey treats the *content* of "God" — one being vs. one nature shared by three beings — as what makes the LDS Jesus [[Different Jesus Both Uncharitable and Precisely True|a different Jesus]], not merely a different vocabulary.

## Best Quotes
> "We believe that they are three persons who share one nature... just like we're two people but we both share the fullness of human nature." [^1]

> "When people begin to say that you have two persons... but they share the same being, I have a hard time with that, because it seems to me that what makes you your own person is to have your own being." [^1]

## LDS Connection
This is the metaphysical core of the "social trinity / Godhead" view: unity of *nature and purpose* among distinct beings, not unity of *being*. It drives the rest of the debate — the embodied Father, divine kinship of humanity, and henotheism all follow from refusing the one-being premise (see [[Divine Unity - There Can Be Only One God]]).
