# The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council, Not the Trinity or Royal We

#type/claim

## The Claim

The plural cohortative of [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26]] — "Let *us* make humans in *our* image, according to *our* likeness" — is best explained as God addressing his **divine council** (his heavenly court), not as a royal "we," a Trinity, or mere self-deliberation. [[Stephen Smoot]] argues this is "well established in the mainstream of biblical scholarship," not an edgy Latter-day Saint reading, and that it tells against *strict ontological monotheism* — the philosophical claim that no divine being whatsoever exists besides the one capital-G God. The same council appears in the [[King Follett Discourse]] ("the head of the gods called a council of gods") and the [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]] ("the gods took counsel among themselves"). While the vault already has [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]], this concept's distinctive contribution is the **head-to-head taxonomy** of the four competing explanations of the Genesis plural.

## Supporting Evidence

Smoot evaluates four readings of the plural and argues only the fourth survives:

1. **Royal "we" (*pluralis majestatis*) — fails.** Monarchs sometimes speak of themselves in the plural to signal majesty (e.g. God in the Quran; European royalty). But this is **unattested for God anywhere else in the Hebrew Bible** — Gen 1:26 would be the lone exception, which is special pleading. The one biblical *pluralis majestatis* (a Persian king in Aramaic Ezra) is neither God nor Hebrew. [^1] *(Strong: the absence of any other Hebrew-Bible instance of God self-addressing in the plural is a real problem for this reading.)*

2. **The Trinity — fails as eisegesis.** Reading Father/Son/Spirit into Gen 1:26 imports a Christian doctrine that "only evolved much later" onto an Israelite text written (Smoot dates) before ~600 BC — "isogesis [eisegesis] in its purest form." Smoot notes that even many Christian apologists and theologians are now moving away from arguing the Trinity from Gen 1:26-27. [^2] *(Strong on the historical point; see Steelman below for the canonical/retrospective defense.)*

3. **Self-deliberation / address to the earth or to wisdom — too weak.** (Treated implicitly.) These ad hoc readings lack the textual support and ANE parallels that the council reading enjoys. [^3]

4. **Address to the divine council — succeeds, "no problems."** This is "a well-established concept across numerous passages": God says "Come, *let us* go down and confound their language" at Babel ([[Genesis 10|Genesis 11:7]]); the motif recurs in 1 Kings 22, Job 1-2, [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82]], and Isaiah 6. Richard J. Clifford counts no fewer than **seven** instances in the primeval history (Genesis 1-11) implying God's "divine entourage." The plural is God consulting his heavenly court as he executes creation. [^4] *(Strong: the council motif is mainstream and textually pervasive; cf. [[Michael Heiser]] and [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]].)*

5. **Garr fuses council + image.** Per W. Randall Garr, the council reading explains the *imago Dei*: "angelic gods look like God and they look like men… the gods' shape is intermediate between the two worlds they connect," so *tselem* and *demut* "indicate that human beings resemble God because they resemble angelic godlike beings that resemble God." This binds Gen 1:26-27's two puzzles together. (See [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son]].) [^5]

6. **LDS convergence.** Joseph Smith: in the King Follett discourse "the head one called the gods together in grand council… and concocted a scheme to create this world"; the [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]] has the gods organizing and counseling together. Smoot reads this as the *same* divine plurality preserved in Genesis 1, concluding that "strict ontological monotheism… is not found in the text of Genesis." (See [[Bene Elohim Are Sons Not a Different Species]].) [^6]

## Steelman of the Traditional / Creedal Reading

The Trinitarian and royal-"we" readings are not baseless, and the honest case for them includes:

- **Canonical / retrospective reading.** On a Christian canonical hermeneutic, a text can carry a fuller sense (*sensus plenior*) than its first author grasped; the plural can be read *with* later revelation as an intra-divine deliberation without claiming the Israelite author had Nicene categories. Smoot's "eisegesis" charge bites only against the claim that Gen 1:26 *historically meant* the Trinity, not against a retrospective Christological reading.
- **Grammatical cohortative of self-encouragement.** Hebraists note the cohortative can express self-deliberation ("let me/us…") without implying additional addressees; a strict monotheist can read 1:26 as God resolving within himself, then v. 27's *singular* verbs ("So God created… in *his* image") as the decisive clarification that one God acts alone.
- **The singular of v. 27 as a deliberate corrective.** That the very next verse drops to the singular is the traditional reading's strongest card: whatever the plural of v. 26 signals, the narrator immediately frames the act monotheistically. A council defender must explain why creation is then executed by God *alone* — which Smoot grants ("in the priestly account it's God himself who executes the creation, his divine council is still consulted and still present").
- **Council ≠ polytheism.** Even granting a council, a monotheist can insist its members are *created* angels, not co-eternal gods — so the verse need not overturn monotheism in the *ontological-uniqueness-of-the-Creator* sense, only in a narrow "no other *elohim* exist at all" sense. The live dispute is thus over the *status* of the council members, which this verse alone does not settle.

The sharpest, fairest formulation: the divine-council reading is the best *historical-grammatical* account of the plural, but it underdetermines the later metaphysical question of whether those beings are creatures or co-eternal divinities.
