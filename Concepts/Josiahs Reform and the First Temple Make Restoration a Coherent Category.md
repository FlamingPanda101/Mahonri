# Josiah's Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category

## The Point
[[Margaret Barker]] reconstructs a First Temple tradition — deification, divine council, sacred garments, ascent into the divine presence — that was stripped out by [[Josiah]]'s 7th-century-BC reform. If original doctrines were removed by institutional reform, then *restoration* becomes "a coherent historical category rather than a 19th-century invention," and the Book of Mormon's mismatch with the post-reform Bible reads as evidence of authenticity, not fraud.

## Evidence
- **Barker's First Temple inventory.** "She spent decades reconstructing what the First Temple tradition actually contained before Josiah's reforms reshaped it. Deification theology? Present. Divine council structure? Present. Sacred garments associated with transformation? Present. Ascent rituals... Present." Documented across the Dead Sea Scrolls, Merkabah texts, Israelite inscriptions, and the priestly descriptions in Exodus and Ezekiel — "all of it removed in the institutional religious reform of the 7th century BC." [^1]
- **Barker's reaction to the LDS temple.** In a 2003 paper delivered at BYU she said, "When I first read about the Mormon temple ceremony, I was astonished. Here, I said to myself, is someone who knows about the First Temple." [^2]
- **The reform itself.** Per the documentary hypothesis (consensus for a century, now debated), the Deuteronomistic history was composed or heavily edited under Josiah ~621 BC; the "found" book of the law in 2 Kings 22 "just happens to authorize exactly the reforms that Josiah was already inclined to make" — high places destroyed, Asherah worship eliminated "despite the archaeology showing that it was widespread," alternative priestly traditions suppressed. [^3]
- **Why it matters for the Book of Mormon.** Lehi left Jerusalem ~600 BC, "approximately 20 years after Josiah's reform, carrying the brass plates," a pre-reform tradition. "A fraudulent text written in 1830 would most likely mirror the Bible of 1830, the King James version... But a genuine pre-reform record would look like what the Book of Mormon actually looks like. The mismatch that critics cite as evidence of fraud is, on closer inspection, actually evidence of authenticity." [^4]
- **The cost of following it.** Barker's work "means admitting original doctrines were removed by institutional religious reform, which makes restoration a coherent historical category rather than a 19th century invention." [^5]
