# Four Ancient Atonement Models

## The Point
Cecilia M. Peek presents the four classic scholarly classifications of how ancient Christians described the saving work of Christ (*sōtēria*). These are not rival creeds but overlapping **paradigms** that early authors freely combined: **Christ the Illuminator** (educational model), **Christ the Restorer** (physical model), **Christ the Victor** (classic model), and **Christ the Victim** (sacrifice model). [^1] Each "grew out of the careful reading of scripture," and "early Christian authors often assert and bridge more than one paradigm." [^1] For Latter-day Saints this multiplicity models the LDS conviction that the Atonement's full import is "incomprehensible to us" (McConkie) and no single human theory exhausts it.

## Evidence
1. **Christ the Illuminator (educational).** Christ is the perfect exemplar and the divine *logos* who frees humanity from ignorance and false religion; salvation is gained "through a knowledge of God" (John 17:3). The Didache and Epistle of Barnabas describe "two ways"; Irenaeus says we learn salvation "by seeing our Teacher." [[Clement of Alexandria]] makes pedagogy central — God "ministers eternal salvation to those who co-operate." [^2]
2. **Christ the Restorer (physical).** Knowledge alone is insufficient since "the wages of sin is death" (Romans 6:23); salvation requires deliverance from mortality. Immortality is made possible through the **Incarnation** — Christ "commenced afresh the long line of human beings." This is the home of Irenaeus's **recapitulation** (see [[Recapitulation - Christ Reverses Adam in Irenaeus]]). [^3]
3. **Christ the Victor (classic / *Christus Victor*).** Christ's life, death, and Resurrection are the "culminating acts in the cosmic struggle" against Satan, sin, and death, narrated in warfare imagery. Includes the **ransom-by-deception** trope (Gregory of Nyssa's fishhook; Augustine's mousetrap). See [[Christus Victor and the Ransom by Deception]]. [^4]
4. **Christ the Victim (sacrifice).** Drawing on Romans and Hebrews, Christ's death is an effective sacrifice; the word *hilastērion* ("propitiation," Romans 3:25) refers to a sin offering and to the **mercy seat** on the ark. Athanasius works out a "divine dilemma" logic in *On the Incarnation*. [^5]
5. **Models interlock.** Athanasius's own statement of Christ "giving over to death in the stead of all" (Victim) is immediately followed by a Restorer claim that Christ "might turn them again toward incorruption." [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Early Christian authors often assert and bridge more than one paradigm in their attempts to articulate and explicate the processes and results of the salvific work of Jesus Christ." [^1]

> "We do not know, we cannot tell, no mortal mind can conceive the full import of what Christ did in Gethsemane." — Bruce R. McConkie [^7]

## LDS Connection
Peek pairs the four ancient models with modern LDS apostolic statements affirming the Atonement's incomprehensibility: [[Bruce R. McConkie]] ("in some way, incomprehensible to us, his suffering satisfied the demands of justice"), [[James E. Faust]], and [[Dallin H. Oaks]] ("magnificent and incomprehensible effect... based on God's love"). [^7] The plurality of models legitimizes the LDS posture that the Atonement is experienced and partly understood, but never fully theorized.
