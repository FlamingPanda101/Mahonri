# Joseph Smith 1826 Trial Conviction Question

#type/claim

## The Claim
Whether [[Joseph Smith]] was actually convicted at the 1826 Bainbridge proceeding (on a disorderly-person charge tied to seer-stone treasure seeking) is **historically inconclusive**. The surviving evidence is "kind of a tossup" — there is no contemporaneous conviction record, the later sources conflict, and the legally required documentary and custodial traces of a conviction are absent.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Only two contemporaneous records exist (foundational).** The justice of the peace Albert Neely's fee bill and the constable's fee bill are the only contemporary documents; everything else postdates the event by about half a century.[^2] *Rating: high — establishes the evidentiary baseline.*

2. **Five later, conflicting sources.** (a) The published **Neely notes** end with a "convicted" line; (b) [[William Purple]] (1877) says "there wasn't enough evidence to convict him and he was discharged"; (c) [[Oliver Cowdery]] says "honorably acquitted"; (d) non-LDS witnesses Abram Benton and another report Joseph was "condemned but allowed to escape" — ambiguous and internally odd.[^3] *Rating: moderate — direct contradiction prevents a confident verdict.*

3. **The corroboration cuts both ways (key).** The Neely notes are not a fabrication: the itemized costs at the end *match* Neely's actual fee bill, lending the notes credibility.[^4] But that same itemization is decisive against conviction — under the 1825 NY Justice of the Peace Manual a conviction required Neely to **file a conviction record** (a billable act) and the defendant to serve 1–60 days in the bridewell. The notes itemize every charge yet include **no charge for a conviction record**, immediately after the "convicted" line, and there is no incarceration evidence.[^5] *Rating: strong — the absence of the billable conviction record is the most telling single datum.*

4. **The "convicted" line may be interpolated.** Some LDS apologists suspect that line was added; it can't be tested because the originals are lost.[^6] This is speculative on its own but gains weight from Purple's independent "discharged" account. *Rating: weak-to-moderate — unfalsifiable but not baseless.*

5. **Steelman of the conviction view.** The Neely notes are the closest thing to a contemporaneous court record (Neely's own docket, transcribed), so they "hold more historical gravity" than partisan recollections; a fair historian must weight them heavily.[^7] Historians who have weighed in include Wesley Walters, Gordon Madsen, and [[Dan Vogel]]; Mark recommends the Joseph Smith Papers legal series (David Grua) for the photographic facsimiles and even-handed analysis.[^8]

## Best Quotes

> "Maybe he was convicted, maybe he wasn't ... it's completely inconclusive."[^1]

> "He doesn't charge for a conviction record ... it's one right after the other ... 'therefore he was convicted,' and then here's the itemized cost with no charge for the conviction record."[^5]

> "There's very few data points, but it's just like waterfalls and branches into all these possibilities."[^9]
