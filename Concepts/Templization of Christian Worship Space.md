## The Point
From the fourth century onward, Christian writers celebrated how their monumental churches evoked, replicated, replaced, or superseded the Jerusalem temple. The tripartite Jewish temple (outer court, inner court, Holy of Holies) was mapped onto church architecture: the atrium as the new outer court for all, the nave as the inner court for the initiated, the chancel screen as the veil covering God's presence, and the apse as the new Holy of Holies accessible only by the priesthood. Builders were hailed as a "new Solomon" or "new Zerubbabel."

## Evidence
1. Eusebius may have been the first to draw these associations: in his dedicatory sermon for the church at Tyre (ca. AD 315–317) he called the nave the *naos*, the apse the Holy of Holies, and the presiding bishop a new Solomon and Zerubbabel (Ecclesiastical History 10.4).
2. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem was considered a new temple supplanting the biblical sanctuary, with Golgotha as the new altar of sacrifice and Christ's tomb as the new Holy of Holies.
3. Justinian's sixth-century Nea Church displayed treasures from Herod's temple; the Church of Saint Polyeuktos in Constantinople physically imitated the layout, proportions, and décor of Solomon's temple.
4. Churches replicated temple functions through cosmic symbolism, ritual performances (priests burning incense, uttering prayers, pronouncing blessings, offering a bloodless Eucharistic sacrifice), and construction rhetoric.
5. The Chapel of the Theotokos on Mount Nebo depicts the Jerusalem temple, its altar, and sacrifices at the chancel-screen access point — illustrating the Eucharist as the new bloodless sacrifice replacing animal sacrifice.

## Best Quotes
> "Christian writers celebrated the ways in which their new buildings and liturgy evoked, replicated, replaced, or superseded the features and function of the Jerusalem temple from biblical times." [^1]

> "Monumental churches now fulfilled each of these functions through designating and dedicating them as 'temples,' as well as through their architectural layout, with the atrium of the church being seen as the new outer court for all, the nave as the new inner court for the initiated, the chancel screen as the new veil covering the presence of God, and the apse as the new Holy of Holies only accessed by the priesthood." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This explicit Christian self-understanding as temple-successors — complete with veils, gradations of sacred access, and a mediating priesthood — provides Latter-day Saints a vocabulary of templization, even as Grey insists early Christians built no temples for vicarious ordinances separate from their churches. [^3]
