# Initial vs Ongoing Justification

## The Claim
The Catholic view distinguishes **initial** justification (by grace *alone*, apart from faith or works, at baptism) from **ongoing** justification (increased by works done in grace). James 2:24 speaks of the *ongoing* kind — which is why no contradiction with Paul arises.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Initial**: "No one can merit the initial grace of justification" (Catechism); we simply receive God's gift in baptism — no work, not even feeding the poor, takes us from dead-in-sin to alive-in-Christ. The Council of Trent: justification's grace is not "by works" (quoting Romans 11:6); cf. [[Ephesians 2.8-9|Ephesians 2:8-9]] *(magisterial sources)* [^1].
- **Ongoing**: justification is a *process*; "faith cooperating with good works increase in that justice which they have received... and are still further justified" (Trent). Trent cites James 2:24 **only** for this increase, never for initial salvation *(magisterial source)* [^2].
- [[Ben Witherington]] (Protestant): if James 2:24 is about God's final verdict on one's life, "even Paul can be said to have agreed" *(scholarly concession)* [^3].
- [[Jimmy Akin]]'s **lamp analogy**: baptism gives *pure* light (righteousness); good works increase its *brightness* (intensity), without earning it like a wage *(analogy)* [^4].

## Best Quote
> "Faith cooperating with good works increase in that justice which they have received through the grace of Christ and are still further justified." — Council of Trent [^2]
