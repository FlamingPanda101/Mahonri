# Faith Is Blind vs Faith Is Evidence-Based

## The Point
The first thesis of *The God Delusion* debate is the claim that "faith is blind, science is evidence-based." [[Richard Dawkins]] argues that "one of the truly bad effects of religion is that it teaches us that it is a virtue to be satisfied with not understanding," and that the very word *faith* presupposes the absence of evidence: "if there were evidence for it, why would you need to call it faith? You'd say just evidence."[^1] [[John Lennox]] grants half the thesis and denies the other half: science *is* evidence-based, but not all faith is blind. Faith carries "the ideas of belief, trust, commitment and is therefore only as robust as the evidence for it." Faith in the flying spaghetti monster is blind because there is no evidence; faith in relativity theory is not, because there is.[^2] Christian faith, he insists, "is rational and evidence-based" — part of the evidence objective (science, history), part experiential.[^3]

## Evidence
Lennox's decisive counterexample is interpersonal knowledge. Dawkins concedes he has "plenty of evidence" that his wife loves him; Lennox presses the generalization: we know someone loves us "because of all sorts of little signs, little catches in the voice, little looks in the eye" — and *that just is* evidence-grounded faith.[^4] Dawkins resists, calling it "pure semantics" and accusing Lennox of being "influenced too much by Kant."[^5] Lennox's deeper move is to deny the equation of science with rationality: "what is beyond science is not necessarily irrational." Science can tell you strychnine in your grandmother's tea will kill her, "but science cannot tell you whether it's morally right to do so," nor whether a poem is beautiful; he cites Nobel laureate Sir Peter Medawar that science cannot answer a child's elementary questions — "Who am I? What is the purpose of my existence?"[^6] He also distinguishes proof (available only in mathematics) from being "convinced beyond reasonable doubt," the standard in science and in faith alike.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "But not all faith is blind faith because faith itself carries with it the ideas of belief, trust, commitment and is therefore only as robust as the evidence for it." [^2]

> "There seems to me a creeping danger of equating science with rationality. But what is beyond science is not necessarily irrational." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration's account of faith squarely rejects the "blind faith" caricature. Alma teaches that faith is "not to have a perfect knowledge of things; therefore if ye have faith ye hope for things which are not seen, which are true" ([[Alma 32]]:21) — true, and confirmable: he frames belief as an *experiment* on the word, where one acts and then observes whether the seed "beginneth to enlarge my soul" and "to be delicious to me" (Alma 32:28). This is precisely Lennox's evidence-responsive faith rather than credulity, and it harmonizes with the LDS conviction that one can "know" by personal verification (Moroni 10:4–5). Faith and reason are thus collaborators, not rivals — matching Lennox's refusal to surrender rationality to science alone.
