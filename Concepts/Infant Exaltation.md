# Infant Exaltation

In the [[King Follett Discourse]], [[Joseph Smith]] taught that infants and children
who die will be resurrected and exalted *in their infant state* — remaining the same
size, "not one cubit added to their stature" — reigning on thrones of glory as
children for eternity. This doctrine later became a source of discomfort and internal
LDS debate.

## The Claim

Children who die before the age of accountability are saved and exalted through
Christ's atonement, but they remain in their infant/child form for eternity — they
do not grow to adulthood in the resurrection.[^1]

## Best Quotes

From the *Times and Seasons* amalgamated account (combining Clayton and Bullock):

> A child dies, so it shall rise from the dead and be forever living in the year
> learning of God. It shall be the child the same as it was before it died out of
> your arms. Children dwell and exercise power in the same form as they laid them down.[^2]

From [[Wilford Woodruff]]'s diary account:

> Eternity is full of thrones upon which dwell thousands of children reigning on
> the thrones of glory, not one cubit added to their stature.[^3]

## Later Revision and Discomfort

1. **Wilford Woodruff's later claim:** Woodruff would later assert that Joseph Smith
   *developed* this theology *after* the sermon, saying "no, they'll eventually grow"
   — a key point of debate among future interpreters.[^4]

2. **Joseph F. Smith's objection:** [[Joseph F. Smith]] had lost 13 children of his
   own and "could not fathom not being able to watch them grow in the eternities."
   This was one of his primary objections to the King Follett Discourse as prepared
   by [[BH Roberts]], contributing to the 1912 suppression.[^5]

3. **Joseph Fielding Smith's editing:** When [[Joseph Fielding Smith]] included the
   discourse in his influential *Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith* volume,
   "the portions like the infant exaltation were removed."[^6]

4. **Bruce R. McConkie's critique:** [[Bruce R. McConkie]], while a champion of the
   discourse, was "upfront of his critiques of BH Roberts's footnotes" because they
   disagreed on infant exaltation.[^7]

## Significance

This doctrine, while now largely de-emphasized in LDS teaching, was a major flashpoint
that drove internal conflicts over which parts of the King Follett Discourse to
preserve, edit, or suppress — directly contributing to the [[King Follett Discourse Publication History|publication drama]] of the early 20th century.
