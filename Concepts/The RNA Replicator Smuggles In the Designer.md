# The RNA Replicator Smuggles In the Designer

## The Point
The cartoon's climax is the first self-replicating molecule — the "replicator" that, once formed, copies itself and fills the void.[^1] Meyer answers that the leading real-world candidate, an RNA molecule (the "RNA world"), badly underperforms the story. In the lab, scientists have only ever produced an RNA that copies "about 10%" of itself, "maybe as much as 20" — a small section, never the whole.[^2] Worse, even that partial self-replication depends on nucleotide bases that are "very precisely arranged" — and **the experimenter does the arranging.** So what such an experiment actually demonstrates is "the need for intelligence to generate information" to get even limited self-replication. The simulation of a chance origin secretly imports a designer.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The cartoon's replicator.** The animation introduces a shape whose blobs "just happen to attract similar blobs," producing spontaneous replication — "the birth of the first replicator," which then mutates and diversifies.[^1]
2. **RNA copies only a sliver of itself.** "Scientists have been unable to produce an RNA that will copy more than about 10% of itself, maybe as much as 20"; only "a very small section between 10 and 20 max."[^2]
3. **Even that depends on arranged bases.** "Even that limited degree of self-replication is dependent upon very precisely arranged bases... they have to be arranged very specifically" to produce the primitive partial replicators made in the lab.[^4]
4. **The experimenter supplies the specificity.** "The experimenter has to do the arranging. They have to specify the sequence. So what's being simulated... [is] the need for intelligence to generate information to generate even limited self-replication capability."[^3] (On why arrangement — not chemical attraction — is the crux, see [[Complementarity Is Not Specificity - The Salt-Crystal Confusion]].)
5. **No bridge to translation.** Even granting an RNA replicator, "you're only going to get an RNA molecule"; there is "no explanation for how you get to the translation system" — digital information directing protein production, "like a 3D printer" where code builds mechanical parts. That jump "is rarely even addressed in these RNA replicator scenarios."[^5] See [[DNA as Digital Code - The Information Enigma]].

## Best Quotes
> "The experimenter has to do the arranging. They have to specify the sequence. So what's being simulated in an experiment like that? The need for intelligence to generate information." [^3]

> "Scientists have been unable to produce an RNA that will copy more than about 10% of itself, maybe as much as 20." [^2]

> "You don't get information without mind. That's the problem that is overlooked here." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The point is that "simulating" a chance origin of life actually requires an intelligent agent to arrange the building blocks — an honest reading of the experiments that fits the Latter-day Saint picture of a God who *organizes* eternal, uncreated matter into functional, specified forms rather than creating *ex nihilo*. The needed specificity does not come from chemistry; in our uniform experience it comes from a mind. The inference lands on a generic designing intelligence, which an LDS reader can affirm while leaving the biological mechanism open. See [[The Blobs Cartoon and Why Life Will Not Polymerize in Water]].
