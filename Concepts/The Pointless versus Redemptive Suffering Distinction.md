# The Pointless versus Redemptive Suffering Distinction

## The Claim
Replying to the feminist critic Deidre Green — who charges that, on the [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Compassion Theory]], a repentant sinner "sadistically" foists pain on Christ (and that women, socialized to self-sacrifice, are especially harmed by such a model) — [[Blake Ostler]] draws a distinction he says "vitiates her critique":[^1]

- **Pointless suffering:** "Causing another to suffer psychological and/or physical pain for the sake of experiencing the pain or for no reason at all."[^2]
- **Redemptive suffering:** "Giving occasion for another to *freely choose* to suffer... pain so that suffering further pain can be avoided or for some benefit that outweighs the dis-value of the pain."[^2]

Christ's pain in atonement is redemptive, never pointless or "sadistic": he "willingly chooses to enter into relationships that entail momentary but excruciating pain in order to realize the joy of healing and indwelling union."[^3] The point parallels the vault's [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]] — that freely-borne, outcome-justified suffering is categorically unlike gratuitous pain.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The two analogies of redemptive love.** (a) An 1850 couple choosing to conceive a child, knowing childbirth will bring the wife "great though momentary pain" and possibly death — yet the choice is loving and justified, *with the wife cast as the willing savior figure*, which Ostler offers as a deliberately non-patriarchal reading "even a feminist could see." (b) An adulterer who confesses to his wife, *causing* her pain, because authentic relationship requires honoring her freedom to know him as he is — "the most loving thing that he can do."[^4] *(Strong: both show one may justly *occasion* another's freely-chosen pain for the sake of genuine relationship and new life.)*
2. **The central motivating point Green misses.** "To avoid creating pain for Christ in atonement one must avoid sin." Sin *itself* "consists in precisely the evil of pain committed when interpersonal relationships are injured"; the only way to stop the pain once we have sinned is to trust Christ and repent.[^5] *(Strong: the theory is *more* motivating to repent, not less — the opposite of Green's worry.)*
3. **Re-reading the unpunctuated D&C 19:16-17.** Green's "standard reading" of [[D&C 19|D&C 19]] has Christ already suffer for *all* people regardless of repentance, which Ostler says generates four problems: **backward causation**, **denial of free will**, **double punishment**, and **violation of the innocence principle**.[^6] Because the original revelation manuscript "didn't have any punctuation," he reads "all" as "all *those who repent*": "Behold I God have suffered these things for all that they might not suffer if they would repent but if they would not repent they must suffer even as I."[^7] *(Strong: a genuinely textual argument — the manuscript's lack of punctuation is documented in the Joseph Smith Papers — that dissolves the backward-causation/free-will problems at once.)*
4. **Green's own counter-proposal is self-defeating.** Green suggests Christ suffered "the maximal amount" of all possible human sin in Gethsemane (even sins never committed), and that this is "not unjust, since his suffering is volitional." Ostler turns this back: that view makes Christ "suffer the maximum amount of *pointless* suffering possible" — punished "for nothing at all" — and requires incoherent **middle-knowledge** of an "upper-bound" of sin. "What could be more sadistic and calculating than this view suggested by Green?"[^8] *(Strong: a clean *tu quoque* — the critic's alternative violates the innocence principle "three times over.")*
5. **Steelman of Green's deeper worry.** Green (following Ivone Gebara) argues that exalting the crucifixion as "the ultimate act of love" "incites women to seek unnecessary sacrifice." Ostler answers that neither provides "a shred of evidence" for the empirical claim, and that "victimology" which refuses to see love in Christ's suffering "devalues and denigrates Christ's gift" and itself "trivializes" the real failure to value women's voices. He grants "there is nothing good *per se* in suffering itself" — the cross is "a reminder of... the depth of human depravity in sin" — but insists it is also "the ultimate expression of God's loving choice to be with us sinners."[^9] *(Honest: Ostler concedes the historical neglect of women's voices is real while denying the empirical bridge from atonement-theology to incited self-harm.)*

## Best Quotes
> "Redemptive Suffering: Giving occasion for another to freely choose to suffer psychological and/or physical pain so that suffering further pain can be avoided or for some benefit that outweighs the dis-value of the pain."[^2]

> "Green thus misses the central motivating point of the Compassion Theory: To avoid creating pain for Christ in atonement one must avoid sin."[^5]

> "Christ's suffering is thus maximally redemptive on the Compassion Theory of Atonement and maximally pointless on Green's reading. That is ironic, to say the least, given her critique."[^8]
