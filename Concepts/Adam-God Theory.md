# Adam-God Theory

## Overview

This is a hub note for the **Adam-God theory** — the teaching, most associated with Brigham Young's April 9, 1852 sermon, that "our Father and our God… is Adam," and that Jesus was begotten in the flesh by the same personage who was in the Garden of Eden. It is repeatedly invoked across the vault both as a critic's case against Brigham Young's prophetic reliability (a prophet teaching what the modern church does not affirm) and as a test case for how Latter-day Saints distinguish a prophet's speculation from binding doctrine.

The cluster's recurring observations: (1) Young *did* teach it — the folk-apologetic that he never said it is abandoned by informed defenders; (2) Young's own formulation was unstable and "muddled," changing between 1852 and 1854; (3) even in Young's scheme Adam was *not* the apex — Elohim stands above "Jehovah-Michael-Adam," so Young relocated *who fathered Jesus* rather than denying a higher Father; and (4) moderns frequently misunderstand what was actually meant. The doctrine was later set aside through codification pressure, though apologists dispute whether it was ever "official binding teaching."

## The Steelman

The critic's strongest form (Heschmeyer): the very *instability* — "it's not even defined the same way every single time" — is itself the problem for a man claiming to speak God's mind, and the 1852 "begotten in the flesh by the same character that was in the Garden of Eden" line is "unambiguously Adam-God." The apologetic reply: prophets are fallible and speculate; the church's mechanism for sifting (continuing revelation, correlation) eventually corrected it without impugning the man's sincerity.
