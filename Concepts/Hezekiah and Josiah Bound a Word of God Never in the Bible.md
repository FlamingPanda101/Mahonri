# Hezekiah and Josiah Bound a Word of God Never in the Bible

## The Claim
[[Robert Boland]] offers the **liturgical reforms of Kings Hezekiah and Josiah** as a concrete Old Testament counterexample to *sola scriptura*: an example of a binding, authoritative *word of God* that was **never part of the Bible**, yet was treated as inspired and obligatory.

- [[2 Chronicles 29.25|2 Chronicles 29:25]] (Hezekiah): he set the Levites in the temple "according to the commandment of David, and of Gad the king's seer, and Nathan the prophet: **for so was the commandment of the LORD by his prophets**."
- [[2 Chronicles 35.4|2 Chronicles 35:4]] (Josiah): he ordered the priests "according to the writing of David... and according to the writing of Solomon."

At this point David, Gad, and Nathan had been **dead about 250 years**, yet the kings enforced a *commandment of the LORD by his prophets* prescribing how temple worship must be conducted — and **that prescription is found nowhere else in the Bible**. Since the regulation of worship is a matter God treats with deadly seriousness (Nadab and Abihu, Leviticus 10), this is an inspired, conscience-binding word of God that exists *outside* the inscripturated text. Therefore "word of God" is not coextensive with "the Bible," and an authoritative rule of faith operated that *tota scriptura* cannot account for.[^1]

This distinct from the vault's [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]] note (a documentary-hypothesis / first-temple *theology* argument from Austin Fife): Boland's point is narrowly an *authority* argument about non-inscripturated binding revelation, not about purged temple doctrines.

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Boland / LDS negative):**
- The text itself attributes the command to *the LORD by his prophets*, not merely to royal policy or human tradition — so it cannot be dismissed as a non-revelatory custom.[^1]
- Boland cites Catholic apologist Gary Krebel: "these words from God not preserved in scriptures were consulted and applied authoritatively by the reformers." And he distinguishes (with W. Gary Crampton, *By Scripture Alone*, endorsed by Reformed theologian Robert L. Reymond) the *eternal word of God* from the *written Bible* — the word of God can exist in forms the canon never captured (cf. Jeremiah 36, the rewritten scroll).[^2]
- It functions as an OT analogue to his New Testament cases (Acts 15; 1 Thess 2:13) that more than one authoritative source of revelation operated alongside the text. See [[James Read Amos 9 Through the Septuagint at the Jerusalem Council]] and [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]].

**Steelman (Protestant affirmative):**
- A *temporary, ceremonial* directive for First-Temple worship is not a *doctrinal* rule of faith for the post-canonical church; *sola scriptura* concerns the standard for **doctrine**, and (as Powers frames the whole debate) the live question is the rule for *Christians today*, after public revelation has ceased.[^3]
- The Protestant grants that during the era of active prophecy there were inspired oral/situational commands; this is the standard "tota scriptura first" reply — revelation was still being given, so the absence of a sola-scriptura paradigm *then* says nothing about *now*. (This is exactly the dynamic catalogued in [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]].)
- That a particular command is not *quoted* elsewhere does not prove it was never written; arguments from the silence of the surviving canon are weak.

## Best Quotes
> "David, Gad and Nathan were dead for about 250 years at this point... they passed on a command from the Lord which was prescribed by God's prophets [for] how worship is to be conducted in the temple... Such a prescription and commandment is found nowhere else in the Bible."[^1]

> [Krebel, quoted by Boland] "These words from God not preserved in scriptures were consulted and applied authoritatively by the reformers spoken of in 2 Chronicles."[^2]
