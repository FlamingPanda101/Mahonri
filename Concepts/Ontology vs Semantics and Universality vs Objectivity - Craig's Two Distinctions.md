# Ontology vs Semantics and Universality vs Objectivity - Craig's Two Distinctions

## The Point
Much of Craig's defensive work in the debate consists of two clarifying distinctions that, he argues, dissolve Harris's objections by showing they miss the target. **(1) Moral ontology vs. moral semantics (and epistemology).** "Moral ontology asks what is the foundation of objective moral values and duties. Moral semantics asks what is the meaning of moral terms."[^1] Craig insists he is making *no* semantic claim that "good means commanded by God"; his claim is purely ontological — about the *ground* of value. He illustrates with light: people "knew how to use the word light long before they discovered its physical nature," and "in exactly the same way we can know the meaning of moral terms… without being aware that the good is grounded in God ontologically."[^2] This is the live-debate version of [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]. **(2) Universality vs. objectivity.** When Harris says "you don't need religion in order to have universal morality," Craig answers: "that's a confusion… The issue isn't universality. The issue is objectivity." A morality can be *universally held* yet not *objectively binding* — "the Nazis… could have won World War II and established a universal morality."[^3] Objectivity means "valid and binding independent of human opinion," and that is what atheism cannot supply.[^4]

## Evidence
Craig uses the ontology/epistemology distinction to absorb the Q&A challenges about *moral disagreement and change*: that there is moral growth, development, or even "moral degeneration of a society" only shows our *apprehension* of the good shifts, not that there is no objective good — "affirming that there are objective moral values and duties doesn't mean that we always know them infallibly."[^5] The same move handles the "consensus" objection: lack of consensus is "an epistemological question, not an ontological question."[^6]

He then adds a distinct **ought-implies-can** problem that attacks the very *possibility* of moral duty on Harris's view: "a person is not morally responsible for an action which he is unable to avoid," but Harris denies free will in both libertarian *and* compatibilist senses, and even concedes in his endnotes that "moral responsibility… is a social construct, not an objective reality."[^7] So "if there is no free will, then no one is morally responsible for anything… puppets do not have moral responsibilities."[^8] This is the debate form of the vault's [[Denying Free Will Dissolves the Moral Responsibility a Critic Needs]], engaging Harris's own [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]. (Harris does not take up the free-will thread in this debate.)

## Best Quotes
> "That is to confuse moral ontology with moral semantics… my concern is moral ontology. What is the ground or the foundation of moral values and duties?" [^1]

> "The issue isn't universality. The issue is objectivity. And I'm maintaining that in the absence of God, there isn't any reason… for the existence of objective moral values." [^3]

> "If there is no free will, then no one is morally responsible for anything… puppets do not have moral responsibilities. Machines have no moral obligations." [^8]

## LDS Connection
Both distinctions are clean tools for the Latter-day Saint apologist. The ontology/epistemology split maps directly onto the Restoration's two-track moral theology: the **Light of Christ** gives every person the *knowledge* of good and evil ("given to every man, that he may know good from evil," Moroni 7:16), while the *ground* of the good is **eternal law** that God honors and reveals ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]; [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]) — so universal moral *knowledge* (universality) and the objective *foundation* (objectivity) come from different places, exactly Craig's point. The ought-implies-can argument is where the LDS view is *stronger* than Craig's classical theism and diametrically opposed to Harris's: the Restoration affirms **libertarian agency** rooted in the co-eternal, uncreated intelligence of each person ([[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]), securing the real moral responsibility that Harris's hard determinism dissolves and that any objective duty presupposes.
