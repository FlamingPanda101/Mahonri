# Qetzef Gadol Is Always Divine Fury in Hebrew Narrative

#type/claim

## The Claim
The "great fury against Israel" in [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3:27]] is named by the Hebrew word *qetzef* (in the phrase *qetzef gadol*, "great fury"). McClellan's philological point: in historical-narrative prose, *qetzef* is **only ever** used to denote **divine** fury — never merely human dread, dismay, or anger. This forecloses the popular harmonization that the "great fury" was simply the Israelites' own horror at witnessing the child sacrifice. The fury must be a god's. The only question left is *which* god's — and the text gives no reason for it to be Yahweh's. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The lexical restriction (rating: strong; the load-bearing claim)
> "the word there in Hebrew is ketchf. Kzf Goddole. And ketch is only ever used in historical narrative pros, which is what we have going on here to refer to divine fury." [^1]

Because the genre here *is* historical narrative prose, the term's attested usage in that genre constrains the reading: the agent of the fury is divine, not a crowd's emotion.

### 2. Yahweh-as-agent is excluded on plausibility grounds (rating: strong; internal logic)
In every *other* narrative occurrence, *qetzef* is Adonai's fury against Israel — so a reader primed by concordance might default to "Yahweh was furious with Israel." McClellan argues this is *not* a rational reading here: there is "absolutely no reason for Adonai to suddenly be furious with Israel as a result of the king of Moab offering his son as a human sacrifice upon the city wall." Nothing Israel did triggers it; the sacrifice is Moab's act. [^2]

### 3. The remaining candidate is Chemosh (rating: strong; consensus inference)
With a human-emotion reading blocked by the lexical restriction and a Yahweh reading blocked by the narrative logic, the "great fury" is most defensibly **Chemosh's** fury — catalyzed by the sacrifice and discharged against the invaders in Moab's own territory. This is the linchpin that turns a grammatically vague clause into the [[Chemosh Defeats Yahweh Outside His Purview|Chemosh-intervention reading]]. [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A harmonizer could (a) read the fury as Yahweh's, positing an *unstated* Israelite sin that provoked it, or (b) dispute that the narrative *qetzef* restriction is exceptionless, or (c) argue the burst of "fury" is a human battlefield panic the narrator describes in theologically charged language. McClellan's reply is that (a) imports content "not in evidence" purely to protect a dogma, while (b)/(c) run against the word's attested usage in the very genre at hand; the divine-fury reading follows the text as it stands.

## LDS Relevance
The argument is a small philological brick in the larger divine-council edifice that LDS apologetics draws on: it helps establish that the Hebrew Bible's authors treated a rival patron deity (Chemosh) as *real and effective in his own land* (cf. [[Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods]], [[Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament]]). But McClellan is not making an LDS point. For him the upshot is a **failed prophecy** and an embarrassed redactor — data he presents "over dogma," resisting both creedal-monotheist harmonization *and* any metaphysical or restorationist gloss. The brick is usable by LDS readers; the builder disclaims the building.
