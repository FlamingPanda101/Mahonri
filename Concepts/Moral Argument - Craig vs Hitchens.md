# Moral Argument - Craig vs Hitchens

## The Point
Craig's third argument: (1) if God does not exist, objective moral values do not exist; (2) objective moral values *do* exist; (3) therefore God exists. By "objective" he means "valid and binding whether we believe in them or not." He notes that many atheists agree with premise (1) — quoting Michael Ruse ("ethics... considered as a rationally justifiable set of claims about an objective something... is illusory") — and claims Hitchens agrees too, treating moral values as evolutionary "predispositions." On atheism, Craig says, rape is merely socially disadvantageous, "but that does absolutely nothing to prove that rape is really morally wrong"; yet "deep down we all know" some things really are wrong.[^1]

The crux of the exchange is a recurring talking-past. Hitchens reframes the question as whether *believers behave better* and issues two challenges: name "an ethical action or statement... made by a believer that I couldn't undertake," versus the easy task of naming "a wicked act undertaken... in the name of God." He grounds morality in "essential emotional solidarity," human "patrimony," and the fact that "there's never been a society found where rape and murder and perjury are not condemned" — moral discoveries that "long predate" monotheism.[^2] Craig repeatedly answers that this misunderstands the argument: it is "not a matter of whether or not we can *know* what is right and wrong... it's rather that we need to have some sort of an objective *foundation*." Under cross-examination Hitchens concedes the point that morality might be "purely evolutionary and functional" — "that could be true, yes."[^3]

## Evidence
**Craig (affirmative):**
- Without God, morality is "the sociobiological spinoffs of the evolutionary process" and so not objective; he stacks quotations from Ruse, Massimo Pigliucci ("on atheism there is no such thing as objective morality"), and Darwin.[^4]
- The real question is *grounding*, not *knowing*: "who are you to judge that the Nazi ethic was wrong... the ethic of ancient Hinduism... apartheid?" without a transcendent standard.[^5]
- He even answers Hitchens' challenge directly: tithing, and "you shall love the Lord your God," are moral acts only a believer can perform — though he adds this is "beside the point."[^6]

**Hitchens (negative):**
- Religion confers no moral advantage: name a good act a believer can do that an unbeliever cannot (none offered that survives), versus countless evil acts done in God's name (genital mutilation, suicide bombing, biblical genocide and slavery).[^2]
- Morality is human "patrimony" and "emotional solidarity"; the Golden Rule predates Christianity (Hillel, Confucius) and is even flawed (what to "do unto" Charles Manson).[^7]
- Deferring morality "upward to a supreme dictator" is "servile"; the wish for an "immovable celestial authority" is "a kind of heavenly North Korea."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "On the atheistic view there's nothing really wrong with raping someone. But the problem is that objective values do exist, and deep down we all know it." [^1]

> "You have to name for me an ethical action or an ethical statement... made or undertaken by a believer that I couldn't undertake or say... I haven't yet had an example pointed out to me." (Hitchens) [^2]

> "It's not a matter of whether or not we can know what is right and wrong... it's rather that we need to have some sort of an objective foundation for right and wrong." [^9]

## LDS Connection
The moral argument tells *against atheism* and is fully usable by Latter-day Saints: objective moral values and duties require a transcendent ground, which a personal God supplies. The note of caution is the classical "divine command" framing in which morality is grounded in God's bare will (the "supreme dictator" Hitchens caricatures). Restoration theology fits more comfortably with the third horn of the Euthyphro dilemma — that the good is grounded in God's *nature*, and that moral law is *eternal* and not arbitrarily invented (compare [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]). LDS thought also affirms genuine moral agency rooted in uncreated intelligences, which answers Hitchens' "servility" complaint: obedience to God is the free response of an eternal agent, not the determinism of a "heavenly North Korea." Hitchens' "believers behave no better" challenge is, as Craig says, simply orthogonal — the argument is about the *ontological foundation* of value, not the *behavior* of believers (compare [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]).
