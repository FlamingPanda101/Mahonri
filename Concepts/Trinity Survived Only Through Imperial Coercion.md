# Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the doctrine of the Trinity persisted not because it was coherent or self-evidently true, but because the institutional church — empowered by the Roman Empire — could **threaten, exile, and coerce** dissenters into conformity. On his telling, the Trinity is a doctrine whose survival is owed to "might and coercion and force," and absent the empire it "would have gone the way of the dodo long, long ago."[^1] The formula, he says, "makes enough sense that we can threaten people if they don't agree with it."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Nicene settlement was imposed, not persuaded — rate: strong on the politics, contested on causation
[[Constantine]] convened the [[Council of Nicaea|Council of Nicaea (325 CE)]] explicitly to end intra-Christian conflict, and signature on the creed was enforced under threat of exile.[^3] McClellan frames Constantine as a frustrated patriarch calling a family meeting: "I don't care what you figure out. Figure it out."[^4] Historically, the imperial role in convening and enforcing the council is well attested; [[Arius]] and his allies were in fact exiled after refusing the *homoousios* formula.[^5] *Rating: the political coercion is historically real.* **Steelman:** a Trinitarian historian would respond that imperial enforcement explains uniformity of *profession*, not the *truth* or even the *origin* of the doctrine — the church had already been moving toward high Christology and triadic worship for two centuries (see [[Divine Mediation Predates the New Testament]]). Coercion is a genetic claim about transmission, not a refutation of content (the genetic fallacy).

### 2. The "dodo" counterfactual is a historical conjecture — rate: moderate
The claim that the Trinity would have died out without imperial backing is a counterfactual that cannot be tested. Pre-Nicene Christianity *did* sustain a range of Christologies (subordinationist, modalist, adoptionist, proto-Trinitarian) without an empire; the empire is what eliminated the *rivals*, not necessarily what created proto-Trinitarian devotion. *Rating: rhetorically powerful, evidentially a plausibility argument rather than a demonstrated fact.*

### 3. Enforcement language is documented — rate: strong
"Anybody you see saying anything else, you report them to us. We're going to exile them and we're going to use the empire to enforce this new philosophical framework."[^6] The post-Nicene apparatus of heresy-hunting, anathema, and imperial exile is a matter of record (the later careers of Athanasius, the banishments under successive emperors, etc.). *Rating: strong as a description of how orthodoxy was maintained.*
