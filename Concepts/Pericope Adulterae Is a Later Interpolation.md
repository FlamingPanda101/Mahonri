# The Pericope Adulterae (John 7:53–8:11) Is a Later Interpolation

## The Claim
The story of the woman caught in adultery ([[John 7.53-8.11|John 7:53–8:11]]) was **not part of the original Gospel of John**; it is a later addition inserted into the text by a scribe.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Absent from every manuscript before the 5th century.** We possess many manuscripts of John datable earlier than the fifth century, and the story appears in none of them — strong text-critical evidence it was added later. *(Strong; this is the decisive datum and reflects scholarly consensus.)* [^1]
2. **An early witness places the story in a different gospel.** One early reference to (probably) this episode locates it in another gospel altogether — the *Gospel of the Hebrews* — suggesting it was a floating tradition not native to John. *(Moderate; the attribution is uncertain but suggestive.)* [^2]
3. **Its form is Synoptic, not Johannine.** The scene — Jesus put on the spot, escaping with a brilliant one-liner — "reminds you a lot of the gospels of Mark, Matthew, and Luke," unlike typical Johannine discourse. *(Moderate; literary/form-critical, corroborating the text-critical case.)* [^3]
4. **It is one of only two additions Méndez grants.** Affirming single authorship for the rest of John, he isolates this passage and chapter 21 as the genuine later expansions — a carefully bounded claim, not a wholesale redaction theory. *(Context for scope.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Some defend the passage's authenticity (or at least apostolic-era antiquity) on the grounds that it is widely attested from the later tradition onward, fits Jesus' character, and may be a genuine oral tradition that simply entered the written stream late. Even most who grant it is non-original consider the underlying story plausibly authentic to Jesus — a separate question from whether John wrote it.

## Best Quotes
> "The problem with this passage is that we don't find it in any manuscript before the fifth century... It seems to be a later edition that someone added to the text." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^1]

> "It doesn't help that one early reference to probably this story places it in a different gospel altogether — the Gospel of the Hebrews supposedly." [^2]

> "It's a kind of test that reminds you a lot of the gospels of Mark, Matthew, and Luke where Jesus is put on the spot and then he comes up with a brilliant one-liner that gets him out of the situation." [^3]
