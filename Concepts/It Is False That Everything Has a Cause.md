# It Is False That Everything Has a Cause

## The Point
**Objection:** It is simply false that everything has a cause or explanation — Stenger attributes some events to "chance," Dennett and Rosenberg appeal to quantum mechanics, and Grayling repeats Hume's claim that "we can conceive of effects independently of causes."[^1] **Reply:** None of these touches the causal principles the arguments actually use. Chance does not abolish causality but *presupposes* it; quantum mechanics at most removes *deterministic* causation, which the principle of causality and the principle of sufficient reason never required; and Hume's conceivability argument is fallacious and underdescribed.[^2]

## Evidence
- **Chance presupposes non-chance causality.** A farmer's plow striking buried treasure is a "chance" event only because two non-chance causal lines converge (his decision to plow there; someone's decision to bury treasure there). Likewise a boulder shattered after an earthquake is "chance" only given regularities like plate tectonics and gravity.[^3]
- **Quantum physics misses or begs the question.** It shows at most that some events lack a *deterministic* cause, but neither causality nor PSR demands determinism specifically. And quantum events occur "only given the laws of quantum mechanics, which are contingent and by themselves merely potential until a universe that follows them is actualized" — so quantum theory is itself part of what needs explaining.[^4]
- **Three problems with the Humean move** (it is no "definitive" refutation, *pace* Grayling):[^5]
  1. **Special pleading.** No working physicist, chemist, biologist, or neuroscientist would, mid-investigation, accept "maybe there just is no cause here." Per Lloyd Gerson, since the cosmological argument has *itself supplied* an explanation, "Maybe there's no explanation" won't do — the critic owes a positive reason to reject the one given.[^6]
  2. **A fallacy of conception.** From "we can conceive of effects independently of causes" it does not follow that an event could exist without a cause — just as we can conceive a triangle without conceiving it as trilateral, though no triangle could fail to be trilateral, or conceive a man without his height, though no man lacks some height.[^7]
  3. **Anscombe's point.** Hume imagines an object "suddenly appearing" where nothing was. But what makes that a case of coming-to-be *without a cause*, rather than coming-to-be with an *unseen* cause, or being *transported* (teleported)? The scenario is underdescribed, and anything added to make it Hume's case smuggles causation back in — e.g., distinguishing "coming into being" from "being transported" requires a *generating* rather than a *transporting* cause.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "There is simply nothing about chance that rules out causality. On the contrary, chance presupposes nonchance causal regularities." [^3]

> "The fact that the cosmological argument is itself a proposed explanation suffices to show that it is no good to say 'Maybe there's no explanation in this case.' The argument has given one." (Feser, on Lloyd Gerson) [^6]

## LDS Connection
This defense of the intelligibility of the world — that reality is causally ordered and not punctuated by genuinely uncaused, brute events — is common ground for any theist against the atheist who pleads "uncaused quantum events" to dodge a first cause. Latter-day Saint thought is robustly anti-chaos here: the cosmos is law-governed by "the light of truth... which is the law by which all things are governed" (D&C 88:13), so an LDS reader endorses Feser's rejection of literally causeless events and the self-undermining appeal to quantum indeterminism. A modest divergence concerns the *status* of the causal principle. Feser presses it as a metaphysical truth about act and potency within a classical frame; the Restoration tends to treat such law as co-eternal with God rather than imposed on a creation made from nothing — see [[Causality Applies Beyond Space and Time]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]. On the decisive point against atheism — that "it just happened, uncaused" is special pleading, not science — Feser and the Restoration stand together. The structurally similar use of Hume's conceivability maxim against LDS miracle claims is treated in [[Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates]].
