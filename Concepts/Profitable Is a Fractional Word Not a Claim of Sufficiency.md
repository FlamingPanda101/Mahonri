# Profitable Is a Fractional Word Not a Claim of Sufficiency

## The Claim
The single most-cited prooftext for *sola scriptura* is [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16-17]] — "All scripture is God-breathed and is **profitable** for doctrine." [[Robert Boland]] argues that the word translated "profitable," Greek **ὠφέλιμος (*ōphelimos*)**, is a *weak, "fractional," quality word* meaning merely *useful, beneficial, advantageous*. It denotes **utility, not formal sufficiency**. If Paul had wished to portray scripture as *formally sufficient* — the complete and only rule of faith — Greek offered far stronger words he could have used and did not (e.g. terms in the *autarkēs* / *holoklēros* family used elsewhere in the Pastorals). Moreover, the *sufficiency* in the passage is predicated in v.17 of the **man of God** (ἄρτιος, "complete/equipped"), not of scripture. Therefore 2 Timothy 3:16 cannot bear the weight of formal sufficiency.[^1]

This is distinct from the vault's [[Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible]] note, which targets the *other* word in the verse (θεόπνευστος) and the scope of "all scripture." Boland's argument is specifically about *ōphelimos* and the lexical gap between "profitable" and "sufficient."

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Boland / LDS negative):**
- *Ōphelimos* is rated a marginal/fractional term by the major lexicons (BDAG, and the *Exegetical Dictionary of the New Testament* vol. 3, which Boland quotes glossing its full semantic domain as "useful, advantageous, of value").[^2]
- The Pastorals themselves use *sufficiency*-language for other things — [[1 Timothy 6.6-8|1 Tim 6:6-8]] ("having food and raiment, let us be therewith content/sufficed"); [[2 Timothy 2.2|2 Tim 2:2]] commits the deposit to "faithful men who shall be able to teach others also," i.e. an *ongoing oral* transmission, not the bare text.[^3]
- Grammatically, *artios* ("complete," v.17) modifies *the man of God*, so the verse says scripture makes the *minister* equipped, not that scripture is the *whole* rule.[^1]

**Steelman (Protestant affirmative):**
- "Profitable for X" plus "that the man of God may be *complete, equipped for every good work*" is naturally read as functional sufficiency for the task in view (teaching, reproof, correction). [[Kelly (Brain Perspective)|Kelly Powers]] explicitly grants he has *never* claimed the verse proves *all-sufficiency* in Boland's strong sense — his case is that scripture is the *standard/blueprint* for testing doctrine, a more modest claim the word *ōphelimos* does support.[^4]
- A word need not lexically encode "sufficient" for the *sentence* to entail sufficiency; the Protestant inference is from the whole clause, not the bare adjective.

## Best Quotes
> "The Greek term translates as profitable is *ōphelimos*, which is actually a quality weak word. It does not denote formal sufficiency but something that is useful or beneficial... it's a marginal or fractional word."[^1]

> [Powers] "You brought up 2 Timothy chapter 3... the word profitable, that it's not all sufficient. I have never claimed that."[^4]

> [Powers] "Profitable *ōphelimos* in second Timothy just means useful. It's a fractional word." *(Powers conceding Boland's gloss while still pressing sufficiency-as-standard.)*[^5]
