# Definitely Believe Wording Skews Survey Results

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A specific sub-objection within the broader data debate: the survey asked who "**definitely** believe[s] in the following," and the word *definitely* is doing too much work. A thoughtful respondent may believe a doctrine sincerely yet decline to say they believe it "definitely" — because nobody has *certain rational* belief, or because they read "definitely" as demanding absolute epistemic certainty rather than ordinary conviction.[^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The wording effect is real but bounded (moderate).** The panel concedes people "get stuck on the word definitely"[^1] and that mood, recent bereavement, or temperament can flip an answer.[^3] But the bounding host caps the plausible distortion at ~5–10 points, not the 15–25+ points seen in the worst categories.[^4] *Rating: moderate — a genuine effect, insufficient to explain the largest gaps.*

2. **It should depress all groups equally (strong).** If "definitely" is uniformly intimidating, it would lower Evangelical scores too — yet Evangelicals reach ~99% on bodily resurrection.[^5] A wording effect that hits only mainline clergy is not a wording effect; it is a belief difference.

3. **Some "definitely no" answers are theologically motivated (concession).** The hosts note a possibly *sophisticated* reason to hesitate: a Trinitarian worried that affirming a *bodily* risen Christ co-substantial with a bodiless Father commits a contradiction (attributed to [[Jacob Hansen]]) might withhold "definitely" not from disbelief but from doctrinal scruple.[^6] See [[Embodied Risen Christ Strains the Trinity]]. This actually concedes the objection's logic for one item while limiting its scope.

**Steelman of the objection.** Psychometrically, "definitely believe" is an unusually strong stem. For contingent or rare phenomena (miraculous healing, astrology), even firm believers may rationally answer "I believe it can happen, but not *definitely* in every claimed case" — so the binary collapses two different things (existence of the phenomenon vs. certainty about it). Ryland's instinct that the instrument is blunt is, on this item, well-grounded.

**Linguistic/scholarly context.** Survey methodologists distinguish *content* (do you believe X?) from *intensity/certainty* (how sure are you?). The NSRL stem fuses them. This is exactly why the panel's strongest evidential moves rely on **between-group variance** (where the fused effect is constant) rather than on absolute "definitely" levels (where it is confounded).
