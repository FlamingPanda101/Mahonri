# The Tahrif Debate Among Classical Muslim Scholars

#type/claim

## The Claim
*Tahrif* — the doctrine that the Jewish and Christian scriptures were "corrupted" — is the load-bearing escape from horn 1 of [[The Islamic Dilemma]]. [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]]'s key historical point is that classical Muslim scholarship was *itself divided* over what *tahrif* even means, and that the strongest classical voices held the prior text was **textually preserved** (only its *meaning* mishandled) — using the very arguments Christian apologists deploy today.

> "Even in the early sources you'll see these different opinions and views pop up in regards to the preservation of the previous scriptures... Ibn Qayyim talks about how there were three split opinions."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Ibn Qayyim's report of three positions.** Avery cites Ibn al-Qayyim (a student, with Ibn Taymiyya, of the same teacher) as distinguishing parties on scripture-preservation: one holding *textual* corruption (*tahrif al-nass*), and an opposing party holding the text was "perfectly preserved textually but only misinterpreted and mishandled with the meaning, not the text" (*tahrif al-ma'na*).[^1] *(This maps onto a real and well-known distinction in Islamic theology between corruption-of-text and corruption-of-interpretation; the precise tripartite taxonomy and attributions are Avery's on-air summary and should be checked against Ibn al-Qayyim directly.)*
2. **The "preservationist" party used today's Christian proof-texts (steelman of the Christian historical claim).** Avery names Ibn Abbas, al-Bukhari, and (he says) al-Razi as holding textual preservation, and reports their evidences: **Q 6:115** ("none can change the words of Allah") and the hadith **Sunan Abu Dawud 4449**, in which Muhammad places the Torah on the judgment seat and says, "I believe in you and in the One who revealed you" — "Muhammad would never have said this if the Torah was corrupted."[^2] The rhetorical payoff: "a lot of the Muslim scholars, the prominent ones... were using the same arguments that we're using today."
3. **The textual-corruption party "gave no evidence" (Avery's charge).** Avery claims the *tahrif al-nass* party "didn't give any evidence... just had an accusation, like 'oh, there's different translations' — how does that show textual corruption?"[^3] *(This is a polemical generalization. A fuller account would note that classical and modern proponents of textual corruption did cite specific passages — e.g. perceived predictions of Muhammad allegedly excised, or contradictions between the Gospels — so the claim that they offered "none" is a debate flourish, not a neutral history.)*
4. **Why this matters for the dilemma.** If the authoritative classical reading is *tahrif al-ma'na* (meaning, not text), then the Muslim cannot cleanly take horn 2 ("the Bible's text is corrupt") without breaking with major Sunni authorities and the proof-texts above — which is precisely the squeeze the dilemma applies. **Muslim steelman:** *tahrif al-nass* is also a thoroughly attested classical position (and dominant in much later polemic), and Q 2:79 / 3:78 / 5:13 are read by many as asserting textual tampering, so the appeal to "the classical consensus" cuts less cleanly than Avery suggests. The honest summary is that Islam contains *both* traditions, which is exactly why the *da'wah* community fractures over the dilemma (see [[The Islamic Dilemma]]).
