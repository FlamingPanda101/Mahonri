# Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health

#type/claim

## The Claim
"There is a mountain of evidence that being religious, being involved in religion and spirituality is related to better mental health, physical health, family relationships" — across "thousands of analyses with multiple data sets," religiosity tends to correlate positively with virtually any life-outcome measured.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Breadth of the researcher's own work (Evidence: strong, first-person).** "I've conducted thousands of analyses with multiple data sets and anytime you say, 'Okay, how is religion related to this?' it's typically related to that in positive good ways, whatever the life outcome might be."[^1] The consistency across outcomes and datasets is the central evidential weight.

2. **Convergent literature (Evidence: well-established).** The general religion-and-health correlation (lower depression/anxiety, longevity, social support, lower substance abuse) is one of the more replicated findings in the social-scientific study of religion, lending external corroboration to Dyer's in-house results.

3. **Mechanistic plausibility (Evidence: theoretical).** Demand → service → benefit (see [[High-Demand Religion Retains More Members]]) and community support supply candidate causal pathways, making the correlation more than coincidental.

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Correlation is not causation, and the religion-health literature is dogged by confounders — religious populations differ in marriage rates, substance use, social class, and *selection* (healthier, more stable people may be both able to attend and inclined to stay). "Healthy-user" and reverse-causation effects are real. Some studies find the benefit attaches to social *attendance* and community, not belief per se — meaning a bowling league or activist group might deliver comparable gains, which complicates the "religion specifically" claim (a point relevant to [[Secularism Does Not Fill the Soul Like Religion]]). Publication and researcher allegiance bias should also temper a faith-affiliated scholar's "mountain of evidence" framing.

## Best Quotes

> "There is a mountain of evidence that being religious ... is related to better mental health, physical health, family relationships."[^1]

> "I've conducted thousands of analyses ... anytime you say, 'How is religion related to this?' it's typically related to that in positive good ways."[^1]

> "When you have a religion that asks more of its members ... they're going to derive more of the benefits of that religion."[^2]
