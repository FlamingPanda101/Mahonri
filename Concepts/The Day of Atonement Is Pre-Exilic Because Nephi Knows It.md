# The Day of Atonement Is Pre-Exilic Because Nephi Knows It

#type/claim

## The Claim
Co-host [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues that if [[Nephi]] demonstrably structured 1 Nephi 4 around the **Day of Atonement** (see [[Laban and Nephi Enact the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]]), then the Book of Mormon bears on a live debate in **Old Testament source criticism**. Following Julius Wellhausen, some scholars date the **Priestly source (P)** — and therefore the Day of Atonement legislation in Leviticus 16 — *late*, claiming it was learned by the Jews only during the **Babylonian exile**. But Nephi left Jerusalem ~600 BC, *before* the exile; if he already knows and deploys the Day of Atonement as a structuring liturgy, that ritual must have been part of the **Kingdom of Judah's religious life before the Exile** — an argument against the late-P dating. Butler frames it half-jokingly: the thesis "bears on the composition of the Old Testament," even if "Julius Wellhausen is not going to listen to us if we Ouija-board him." [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Wellhausen late-dating of P is a real scholarly position.** It is a standard documentary-hypothesis claim that the Priestly material, including the developed Day of Atonement, is post-exilic — which is why "Leviticus has to be late." Nephi's apparent pre-exilic knowledge of that very rite is therefore a relevant counter-datum. *Rating: accurately represents a real (though much-debated) critical position; the Book of Mormon's evidentiary weight here is contingent on its own date and on the goats-reading holding.* [^1]
2. **Nephi's vantage is genuinely pre-exilic.** Nephi departs Jerusalem at the commencement of Zedekiah's reign, decades before 587 BC — so any festal liturgy he uses is, by the narrative's own chronology, pre-exilic Judahite practice. *Rating: internally valid given Book of Mormon historicity; a critic who rejects that historicity will not grant the premise.* [^2]
3. **Converges with the vault's pre-reform first-temple thesis.** The claim that Nephi preserves first-temple-era religion already runs through [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]]; an early, pre-exilic Day of Atonement is one more instance of the Book of Mormon reflecting older Israelite practice than critical reconstructions allow. *Rating: coherent with an established line in the vault.* [^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The objection is straightforward and Butler half-concedes it: the argument is **circular for an outside critic** — it presupposes the Book of Mormon is an authentic ~600 BC document, which is the very thing in dispute; a secular scholar dating P late will simply treat 1 Nephi as a 19th-century text whose Day-of-Atonement imagery was drawn from the *received* (and on their view late) Leviticus, carrying zero weight on P's date. Further, (a) the goats-reading of 1 Nephi 4 is itself contested, so the premise "Nephi knows the Day of Atonement" is not secure; and (b) even granting an early rite, the documentary debate turns on the *literary* dating of the P *source*, which can post-date the *practice* — knowing the ritual early does not by itself prove Leviticus 16's *text* is early. The claim is therefore best stated as an *internal* consideration for those who already accept Book of Mormon antiquity, not a knockdown against Wellhausen.
