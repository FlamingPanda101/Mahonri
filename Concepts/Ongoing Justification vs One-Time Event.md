# Ongoing Justification vs One-Time Event

## The Core Distinction
Catholic theology distinguishes between:
- **Initial justification**: the transition from spiritual death to life —
  by grace alone, received in baptism (no work can earn this).
- **Ongoing justification**: the increase of righteousness after baptism —
  through good works performed in cooperation with God's grace.

## Protestant Conflation
Most Protestants treat justification as a one-time forensic declaration:
God declares the sinner righteous based on Christ's imputed righteousness.
Sanctification (growth in holiness) is a separate category that doesn't
affect one's standing before God.

## Catholic Integration
Justification IS sanctification — the process of being made actually
righteous, not merely declared so. God doesn't engage in "legal fiction."
He transforms the soul. Good works increase the righteousness He has given.

## Key Scriptural Evidence

### Romans 6:7
> "For he who has died is freed from sin."
The Greek for "freed" is *dedikaiōtai* — literally "has been justified."
Paul uses justification language for a post-baptismal state, not a
one-time declaration.

### Romans 2:6-13
> "God will render to every man according to his works... the doers of the
> law will be justified."
Future-tense justification based on works — incompatible with a purely
past one-time model.

### Philippians 2:12-13
> "Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling. For God is at work
> in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure."
Salvation as an ongoing cooperative process.

## Jimmy Akin's Lamp Analogy
| Quality | Meaning | When |
|---------|---------|------|
| Purity (whiteness) | Freedom from sin | Received at baptism |
| Intensity (brightness) | Degree of righteousness | Increased by good works |

## Council of Trent Usage
The Council of Trent cites James 2:24 ONLY when discussing the **increase**
of justification — never for initial justification. The Council explicitly
affirms that initial justification is unearned:
> "We are justified freely by faith because none of those things which
> precede justification, whether faith or works, merit the grace itself of
> justification."

## Contrast with Extreme Protestant Views
Norman Geisler: "You are not only justified by faith alone, but you are also
being sanctified by faith alone." This leads to antinomianism — the idea that
works have no relation to standing before God. A Christian in grave sin still
goes to heaven.

## Practical Difference: Assurance
- **Protestant**: Must deduce from works whether faith was ever "genuine" →
  constant doubt (Alan Parr: "they may even believe wholeheartedly that they
  have faith only to one day sadly realize that they really never had saving
  faith at all").
- **Catholic**: Examine conscience honestly. Not in grave sin? In a state of
  grace. No need to retroactively question conversion.
