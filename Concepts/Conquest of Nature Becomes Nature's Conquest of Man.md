# Conquest of Nature Becomes Nature's Conquest of Man

## The Point
This is the paradox that gives the book its title. When the Conditioners, having abandoned every objective value, finally remake humanity, they have nothing left to direct their choices but their own irrational impulses — that is, "mere Nature." So the supposed triumph of Man over Nature turns, at the very instant of its completion, into Nature's triumph over Man.[^1] As Ward summarizes Lewis: "if man chooses to treat himself as raw material, raw material he will be: not raw material to be manipulated... by himself, but by mere appetite, that is, mere Nature, in the person of his de-humanized Conditioners."[^1] "Man's final conquest has proved to be the abolition of Man."[^2]

## Evidence
Ward unpacks the logic of self-defeat. Once the Conditioners step outside the Tao, every value they might impose is exposed as a mere "natural product" — an impulse, an animal "spring of human action."[^3] Lewis calls it "the magician's bargain: give up our soul, get power in return."[^4] Ward links this to the Faust legend (Marlowe, Goethe) and to Lewis's diagnosis that magic and applied science share a single aim: "how to subdue reality to the wishes of men," in contrast to the older wisdom that asked "how to conform the soul to reality."[^5] The decisive move is the **reductio**: this is "one of the many instances where to carry a principle to what seems its logical conclusion produces absurdity," like the Irishman who concluded that two fuel-saving stoves would heat his house for free.[^6] Ward underscores Lewis's most chilling formulation of why the conquest is hollow — "this time the being who stood to gain and the being who has been sacrificed are one and the same."[^7] He also notes Lewis's striking refusal to exempt the democracies: "The process which, if not checked, will abolish Man goes on apace among Communists and Democrats no less than among Fascists," because all share "a common starting point in the rejection of the natural moral law."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Man's conquest of Nature turns out, in the moment of its consummation, to be Nature's conquest of Man." (Lewis, the book's central thesis) [^1]

> "It is the magician's bargain: give up our soul, get power in return." (Lewis) [^4]

> "A wholly transparent world is an invisible world. To 'see through' all things is the same as not to see." (Lewis) [^9]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's paradox — that to "treat himself as raw material" is to surrender man to "mere appetite" — is the natural-law form of King Benjamin's warning that the "natural man is an enemy to God" and remains so "forever, unless he yields to the enticings of the Holy Spirit" (Mosiah 3:19). The conquest that becomes a self-conquest mirrors 2 Nephi 2:27: agency exercised against eternal law delivers a person not into freedom but into captivity. The Restoration's answer to "the magician's bargain" is the opposite exchange — losing one's life to find it (Matthew 16:25), conforming the soul to reality (the Tao / the will of God) rather than subduing reality to appetite. And Lewis's image of a society that "sees through everything and therefore sees nothing" parallels the scriptural motif of those who, rejecting the **Light of Christ**, are left in darkness "at noon-day" (D&C 95:6; cf. 2 Nephi 9:28-29 on worldly wisdom that perishes). The foil [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] hopes science can ground value after value is "explained away"; Lewis answers that the last reductive step undoes all the others.
