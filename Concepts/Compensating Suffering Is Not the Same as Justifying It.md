# Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It

## The Point
This is the crux of the back-and-forth between [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] over whether a *future reward* can redeem *present* suffering — especially for animals who never consented to mortality. O'Connor's principle: "you don't just get to inflict suffering and then compensate people and think that that's okay."[^1] Hansen's competing principle: suffering "is only a problem when it is not justified by a greater good," and the suffering in question is the kind that *could not be avoided* if the good is to be had at all.[^2] The two are arguing about the structure of justification: O'Connor treats God (on creedal terms) as someone who *creates the need* and then pays it off; Hansen replies that the constrained LDS God does *not* create the conditions of injustice but works within them, so the cases are disanalogous.

## Evidence
- **O'Connor's Burlington Club analogy.** Posh Oxford students trash a restaurant, then over-pay the owner so "they could get away with it... if anything they've done those people a favor." O'Connor's verdict: still "terrible, awful behavior," because compensation after the fact does not erase the wrong of having caused the harm — and "it still hasn't been explained why that suffering exists."[^3] Rate: **a sharp intuition pump** against pure compensation theodicy; over-payment plainly does not license vandalism.
- **Hansen's two replies.** (1) *Reframe the analogy*: it is not "I'll create cancer so we can have the good of chemotherapy" (which would be insane), but "son, the only way you can become the way I am is to go out into that world" — the suffering is intrinsic to the goal, not manufactured to be cured.[^4] (2) *Stakes-scaling*: "What if the only way to achieve world peace for the next thousand years was to allow that group of boys to smash up a few restaurants," with full compensation — "would that be worth it?"[^5] Rate: **the reframe is the stronger move**; it shifts from compensation to *necessity*, which is Hansen's whole theodicy (see [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]]). The world-peace version partly concedes O'Connor's point by needing an enormous offsetting good.
- **The animal-consent problem.** O'Connor's cleanest objection: humans (on the LDS view) consent premortally, "if animals don't do that then I don't think their suffering is justified on the same ground."[^6] Hansen first stumbles ("I don't know — this was a bad response," he admits in narration), then offers that in LDS thought animals "have souls that existed before this life and perhaps some degree of agency," so it is "not outside our theology to say animals chose to come."[^7] Rate: **O'Connor wins the local exchange**; Hansen concedes his on-the-spot answer was weak, and the premortal-consent solve (strong for humans — see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]) is speculative for animals.
- **O'Connor's pain-experience hedge.** O'Connor distinguishes *feeling* pain from *experiencing* it as trauma: a fly likely does not grieve a crushed sibling as a human would, so "there is a real question as to how they experience that pain."[^8] Notably this *softens* his own animal-suffering objection — he grants the moral weight may be lower than human suffering, though he still says he would stop someone torturing a dog.
- **Hansen's turnabout — the "all fall, no atonement" challenge.** Hansen's best counter-thrust: if O'Connor is right that animal (and human) life is "irredeemably unjust," then "why are we assuming it's good for life to continue?" A world of "birth, suffering and death" with no redemption gives no reason to keep the project going; the Christian claim is precisely that "any and all suffering... can be and will be redeemed through Christ."[^9] Rate: **a real *tu quoque* pressure** on the pessimistic-atheist picture — it does not prove redemption is real, but it shows O'Connor's framing has its own cost (anti-natalist implications). O'Connor brackets it as "a separate question."

## Best Quotes
> "You don't just get to inflict suffering and then compensate people and think that that's okay." [^1]

> "If I said I was going to create cancer so that we could have the good of chemotherapy... you'd look at me like I'd lost my mind. It's like saying to my son: the only way you can become the way I am is to go out into that world." [^4]

> "If animals don't [consent] then I don't think their suffering is justified on the same ground." [^6]

> "If life is mostly suffering even for the animals, then isn't the logical conclusion to end this project altogether?" [^9]

## LDS Connection
The dispute is general philosophy of religion, but Hansen's resolution is specifically Latter-day Saint: the difference between *compensating* and *justifying* suffering collapses only if God *created the need* — and the restored gospel's constrained God does not. The premortal-consent doctrine ([[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]) is what lets Hansen say the sufferers *accepted* the terms, converting "inflicted and paid off" into "freely undertaken for a necessary end." The animal case exposes the limit of that solve, since animal premortal agency is far more speculative than human agency — a point Hansen concedes he handled poorly.
