# The Improbability of New Functional Proteins - Combinatorial Inflation

## The Point
A protein is a chain of amino acids, with 20 possible choices at each position — like stringing a necklace where each bead can be one of 20 gems.[^1] Because the number of possible sequences grows exponentially with length, even a short protein has an astronomical number of possible arrangements; Berlinski calls this **combinatorial inflation**.[^2] The decisive fact, unknown until the early 2000s, is how *rare* the functional sequences are within that space: for a short protein of 150 amino acids, the ratio of sequences that fold into a working structure to "gibberish" sequences is about **1 in 10^77**.[^3] Random mutation searching such a space is overwhelmingly likely to fail. The math itself is simple ("a high school student can compute" it) — what stumped Darwin was the biology he could not have known.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The necklace model.** Each position in the protein "has to be occupied by one of 20 amino acids," and "the number of ways you can arrange that is huge, grows exponentially as the string gets longer."[^2] Even "a cheap necklace for your very first girlfriend" yields "an astronomical number of choices."[^5]
2. **The rarity was measured only recently.** A 1960s conference of MIT scientists ("Mathematical Challenges to Neo-Darwinism") could compute the *number* of arrangements but "didn't know at the time how many of the arrangements would result in functional proteins."[^6] By the early 2000s, experimental measures supplied the ratio: "one protein that will fold into a functional structure compared to 10 to the 77th gibberish sequences."[^3]
3. **The search budget is far too small.** Estimating ~10^40 organisms in Earth's history, each replication a chance for a mutation, gives ~10^40 trials against a space of 10^77 — meaning life could search only "one ten-trillion-trillion-trillionth of the possible combinations."[^7] You are therefore "overwhelmingly more likely to fail"; the chance hypothesis is "overwhelmingly more likely to be false than true. It just didn't happen."[^8] See [[Unlimited Chances Destroy Probability - The Royal Flush Objection]].
4. **Unlikely vs. impossible.** Pressed on whether Darwinian origin is *unlikely* or *impossible*, Berlinski answers, "there's hardly a difference... there's no practical difference."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "For a short protein 150 amino acids long the ratio is one... compared to 10 to the 77th gibberish sequences." (Meyer) [^3]

> "It's not the mathematics that stumped him, it's the biology. The mathematics is simple — a high school student can compute how many choices there are." (Berlinski) [^4]

> "From whatever angle you come at it the answer is no — there has not been enough time. The number of throws we've had is too puny even to talk about." (Gelernter) [^8]

## LDS Connection
The argument targets the adequacy of an *unguided* mechanism, not the age of the earth or the fact of common descent, and Latter-day Saint thought has no fixed position on the mechanism of evolution. What the probabilistic case supplies is a natural-theology resource compatible with the LDS picture of a God who *organizes* matter through ordered processes: if functional biological order is too rare to be plausibly reached by chance, that points toward an organizing intelligence rather than toward creation *ex nihilo* specifically. The inference lands on a generic designing mind, which an LDS reader can affirm while leaving the biological details open.
