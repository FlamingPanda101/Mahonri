# Deuteronomy Acknowledges Other Gods While Using Negation Rhetoric

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] appeals to Deuteronomy 4 ("there is no other besides him") as a defense for reading Deutero-Isaiah's negation rhetoric as denying the existence of other gods.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The same book that says "no other" assumes other gods exist (rating: strong; internal contradiction for the apologist)
Within Deuteronomy 4 itself, the Israelites are told not to worship the sun, moon, stars, "the whole host of heaven" — a standard West Asian way of referring to the gods — *because those were allotted to the other nations to worship*. [[Deuteronomy 4.19|Deut 4:19]] presupposes the very beings the "no other" rhetoric supposedly denies. [^1]

> "in Deuteronomy 4 we just got done telling the Israelites not to worship the Sun the moon the stars the whole host of Heaven which is a very traditional West Asian way to refer to the gods because they were given to the other nations to worship" [^1]

### 2. It rests on the allotment theology of Deut 32:8–9 (rating: strong)
Deut 4:19 is "a later riffing on" [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8–9]], where the Most High divides the nations "according to the number of the sons of God," Yahweh receiving Israel as his inheritance. The land of Israel is repeatedly "the inheritance of Yahweh." [^1]

### 3. Deut 32:39's "no god besides me" is bracketed by other gods (rating: strong)
[[Deuteronomy 32.39|Deut 32:39]] says "I am, and there is no god besides me," yet just four verses later (32:43, reconstructed from the Dead Sea Scrolls and Septuagint) the *other gods are summoned to worship Yahweh* — and this is quoted in Psalm 97:7. The "no other" rhetoric sits beside an explicit address to existing gods. [^2]

> "in verse 43 as we can reconstruct from the Dead Sea Scrolls from the septu aent the other gods are called on directly to worship Adonai and this is what is quoted in Psalm 977" [^2]

### 4. Why scholars treat Deutero-Isaiah but not Deuteronomy as "the threshold of monotheism" (rating: strong; consensus observation)
Because Deuteronomy so explicitly acknowledges other gods, most critical scholars decline to call it monotheistic — which, McClellan argues, suggests the identical negation rhetoric in Deutero-Isaiah is *also* not denying existence. [^3]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund can argue that Deut 4:35/39 ("the LORD is God; there is no other") is the interpretive key that *overrides* the host-of-heaven language, reading 4:19 as describing what the nations *wrongly* worship rather than real beings. McClellan answers by deferring to [[Nathan McDonald]]'s detailed treatment (ch. 2 §3) showing that "no other" denotes presence/proximity within a domain, not ontological exclusivity — and by noting the consensus reluctance to find monotheism in Deuteronomy at all. [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "the other gods are there throughout Deuteronomy and this is why most critical Scholars who identify deutero Isaiah as the threshold of monotheism based on that rhetoric are not so eager to do the same for Deuteronomy because it explicitly acknowledges the existence of the other gods" [^3]

> "I would highly recommend Nathan McDonald's book Deuteronomy and the meaning of monotheism where he demonstrates that that kind of rhetoric is used in many different places not to refer to any kind of ontological exclusivity... but to refer to proximity or the presence of something within someone's domain" [^4]
