# Atonement as Indwelling Union That Transforms Darkness Into Light

## The Claim
The mechanism at the heart of [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Ostler's Compassion Theory]] is **coinherence / indwelling union**: in atonement Christ and the repentant person mutually take up abode in each other and *commingle the light of their lives*, so that "the darkness of our lives that we share with him is transformed by the light of his love to a greater brightness."[^1] [[Blake Ostler]] grounds this in the same Johannine mutual-indwelling that the vault reads in [[John 17.20-23|John 17]] — the oneness Christ has with the Father, extended to believers (see [[I and the Father Are One Means Mutual Indwelling]], [[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality]]). To be "at-one" *is* to be in that divine union; atonement is therefore not a forensic verdict applied from outside but an ontological *sharing of life's energy*.[^1]

This indwelling is *why* our sin causes Christ pain (Compassion Theory feature 5): "when the darkness of our sins is mingled with the perfect light of Christ we are enlightened, but the darkness that is in us causes him to experience momentary but excruciating pain."[^2] Crucially, "Christ is **not punished** for our sins, nor does he bear our shameful guilt or moral culpability; rather, what he experiences is the pain and subsequent joy of entering into relationship of shared life and light with imperfect humans."[^2] This distinguishes the model sharply from penal substitution.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The sacramental ordinances *are* the union, not mere reminders.** Baptism symbolizes dying and rising with Christ; the sacrament symbolizes "taking his life into ours, and indeed making him the energy of our lives and becoming embodied in our own bodies, by symbolically eating his flesh and drinking his blood." This answers desideratum 5 (how the ordinances signify atonement) and dovetails with the vault's [[The Eucharist as Communion with Christ|eucharist-as-communion]] reading.[^3] *(Strong: the ordinances become participatory, not declaratory.)*
2. **2 Cor. 5:21 read as transformation, not imputation.** Ostler glosses [[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor. 5:21]] as: "he takes the effects of our sin into him to heal it and thus the sin in us is transformed into life and light in Christ. We become the righteousness of God in Christ by accepting the light of Christ into our hearts."[^4] He explicitly contrasts this with the New Perspective's denial that Paul taught "imputation of Christ's righteousness to us so that we are deemed righteous when in fact we are not."[^5] *(Strong: an *infusion/transformation* reading rather than a legal-fiction one — compare the vault's [[Justification - Infusion vs Legal Fiction]].)*
3. **Continuous with deification/theosis.** The endpoint of the commingled-light process is sanctification "toward deification... the fulness of glorification in union with the divine persons in the Godhead."[^6] This situates atonement within the LDS theosis cluster (see [[Human Deification in Mormonism]], [[Theosis as Likeness and Union So Far as Possible]]). *(Fair: union-soteriology and deification are the same trajectory on this model.)*
4. **Steelman of the objection.** One may object that "commingling light" is metaphor doing metaphysical work — what *is* the "energy" or "light" transferred, and how does another's darkness cause a divine person literal pain without making God a victim of our sin? Ostler's reply is that love by its nature entails vulnerability to the beloved's free choices, so a relational God who shares life genuinely *can* be pained by what the beloved brings into the union; the pain is freely accepted and "turned to joy through repentance and healing relationship," which he argues is redemptive, not pointless (see [[The Pointless versus Redemptive Suffering Distinction]]).[^2] *(Honest: the load-bearing premise is a relational, passible God — itself a distinctively LDS commitment that a classical-theist critic will contest.)*

## Best Quotes
> "He takes up abode in us and we take up abode in him. We take his name upon us and his image is renewed in us. In this sense, we are at-one with Christ."[^1]

> "When the darkness of our sins is mingled with the perfect light of Christ we are enlightened, but the darkness that is in us causes him to experience momentary but excruciating pain... Christ is not punished for our sins, nor does he bear our shameful guilt."[^2]

> "He takes the effects of our sin into him to heal it and thus the sin in us is transformed into life and light in Christ."[^4]
