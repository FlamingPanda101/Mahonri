# Hiawatha Belt Parallel — Buried Weapons Under a Tree

## The Claim
[[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah]] floats a speculative parallel between the **Anti-Nephi-Lehi "bury the weapons" covenant** ([[Alma 24.22|Alma 24]]) and the **Iroquois Hiawatha wampum belt** tradition: five nations come together in peace and bury their weapons under a **Tree of Peace** (the origin, he says, of "bury the hatchet"). He notes the belt depicts **five** nations and an evergreen tree, and links the evergreen to George Washington's "Appeal to Heaven" tree flag. He suggests the buried-weapons-under-a-tree peace legend could have "persisted in the New World zeitgeist" from Book-of-Mormon times — a **Heartland-model-friendly** reading [^1][^2][^3].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Surface motif match.** Both stories feature multiple nations renouncing war by burying weapons and forming a covenant peace; the "five nations" of the Iroquois Confederacy echo Jonah's [[The Five Motif in Alma 24|"five" motif]] in Alma 24 [^1][^3]. *Rating: a thematic resemblance, not a derivation.*
2. **"Origin story" persistence argument.** Jonah argues the speech is a tribe-defining "creation story" (Lamanites becoming Anti-Nephi-Lehies), so the kind of foundational narrative that *would* be transmitted for centuries [^4].
3. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) **Burying weapons to make peace is a near-universal symbol** — it needs no shared origin; independent invention is the parsimonious explanation. (b) The Hiawatha/Great Law of Peace tradition has its own well-documented Iroquoian origin and date (commonly placed centuries before contact, debated 12th-15th c.) with no textual link to a Nephite source. (c) Jonah misattributes/garbles details on air (he confuses the Hiawatha belt with the George Washington Covenant belt, gets the tree "upside down," and admits "I'm no expert in symbology... just as a layman"). (d) He explicitly says it is "pure speculation" and frames the Mesoamerican-vs-Heartland angle as a *joke* to "make academics mad." Co-host [[Cardon Ellis]] presses: this is a "long way to go from Mesoamerica up to George Washington territory," and asks if it's been peer-reviewed [^5]. (e) The "appeal to heaven" evergreen-flag link to a Native belt is a free association with no evidentiary chain.
4. **Bury-the-hatchet etymology is uncertain.** Jonah concedes "we don't know exactly where it comes from," undercutting the load-bearing etymological claim [^6].

## Best Quotes
> "The legend is that they buried their weapons underneath this tree... five great nations coming together saying we're at peace... Five of them under a tree of peace where we buried our weapons — is actually where the phrase 'bury the hatchet' comes from." [^3]

> "I'm no expert in symbology, but just as a layman, I noticed... armchair historian, I noticed." [^2]

> "Why not? ...It was a ceremonial speech that the king gave that changed the identity of his tribe forever... That's like a creation story for a tribe. I imagine it would persist quite a bit." [^4]
