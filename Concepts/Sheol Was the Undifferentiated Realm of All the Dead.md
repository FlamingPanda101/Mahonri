# Sheol Was the Undifferentiated Realm of All the Dead

## The Claim

In ancient Israelite religion there was no place of postmortem reward or punishment. The Hebrew word **Sheol** — the grave literally, or the realm of the dead figuratively — named a single, common destination for everyone, righteous and wicked alike. There was no sorting of the good from the evil; how you lived "really only affected your lot in life, because once you were dead, everybody's lot was the same." [^1] Many English Bibles flatten Sheol to "hell," "grave," or "pit," importing a moralized afterlife the Hebrew did not carry.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The dead kept existing — but in a dreary, neutral state
Contrary to the popular idea that ancient Israel had *no* afterlife (death as total annihilation), the archaeological record of tombs and grave goods shows the Israelites believed the dead "kept on living in one sense or another," some agency (spirit, soul, ghost) tethered to the corpse and its location. [^2] Yet the nature of this existence was "dreary, murky, ambiguous," fraught with dangers but with no distinction of bliss for the good or torment for the wicked. [^3]

### 2. Care, feeding, and ancestor worship for the dead
Because the dead persisted, the living provided food, light, and protection for them, and visited at prescribed intervals to feast, petition for aid, or seek strategic information. McClellan notes that **necromancy was "phenomenally common" in ancient Israel**, and that the dead could even be designated *elohim* (a deity) — overlapping the categories of ancestor and god. [^4] (See [[Deceased Humans Called Elohim]].)

### 3. The necromancer of Endor
The narrative of [[1 Samuel 28|1 Samuel 28]] confirms the picture: Saul, having outlawed the practice, secretly visits the necromancer of Endor, who calls up the deceased Samuel. The woman reports seeing "Elohim... rising up from the underworld," and Samuel — irritated at being disturbed — pronounces Saul's coming death. The dead Samuel still *exists*, in a non-fleshly sense, and can be summoned and petitioned. [^5]

### 4. Sheol is "negative" only in being murky, not in being punitive
When pressed, McClellan clarifies Sheol carries no moral valence: it is "negative in the sense that it's mysterious, it's murky" and "it's going to suck to be there," but there is no "do good and you'll get on... to the better part of Sheol." [^6] The moralized two-tier afterlife is a *later* innovation (see [[Divine Punishment in the Afterlife Emerged as Theodicy Under Imperial Oppression]]).

## LDS Relevance

The Israelite picture of a single, conscious-but-shadowy realm of the dead — where the departed persist and can be communed with — is far closer to the LDS **spirit world** (D&C 138) than to the binary heaven-or-hell of creedal Christianity. Restored doctrine likewise holds that the spirit survives death retaining "that same spirit which doth possess your bodies" (Alma 34:34) and is gathered to "a state of happiness... or a state of misery" pending resurrection (Alma 40:11–14) rather than immediately consigned to eternal bliss or eternal fire. That Israel's earliest afterlife had *no* eternal-conscious-torment hell undercuts the claim that such a hell is the bedrock, original biblical teaching — a key plank in the LDS rejection of infernalism (compare [[Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell - DC 76 and DC 18]], [[LDS Afterlife - Degrees of Glory]]).
