# Contradictory Truth Claims Cannot All Be True - The Resurrection Test

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]]'s decisive move against the pluralist thesis that "all religions are true" is to force the conversation onto a single **binary historical question**: *Was Jesus Christ resurrected?* "The answer is not yes and no." [^1] Pluralism can accommodate moral and experiential overlap, but it cannot dissolve a yes/no fact into [[The Noise Theory of Religious Difference|"noise"]]. And the answer has hard exclusionary consequences: "if he was [resurrected], that has great implications. That means Islam is untrue. Judaism is untrue. The other major religions are untrue." [^1] Crucially, Carroll pairs this with the *inclusivist* concession that other religions still contain real truth and good (see [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]]) — what they cannot do is *all be true in their exclusive claims*. The argument is a clean wedge: truth-content is shareable; mutually contradictory truth-*claims* are not.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman of the argument)
1. **The Resurrection is a yes/no event, not a value.** Carroll insists the question is historical, not a matter of preference: either the tomb was empty and Jesus appeared bodily, or not. When Alexa offers "the answer is I don't know," Carroll grants that as honest agnosticism but notes it is still not "yes and no" — *uncertainty about which disjunct is true is not the truth of both.* [^2] *Rating: strong; the law of non-contradiction does not bend for religious topics, a point Alexa herself conceded against atheism earlier in the conversation.*
2. **The contradictions are doctrinally specific, not vague.** Carroll spells out *why* the Resurrection excludes its rivals rather than merely asserting it: "Judaism says that Jesus Christ was an apostate Jew... they would say he is not the Messiah. They're still waiting." [^3] Islam likewise denies the divine sonship and the crucifixion. These are not stylistic differences ("noise") but flat negations of the Christian claim — both cannot be true. *Rating: strong; the religions' own self-understanding supplies the contradiction.*
3. **Contradiction has practical stakes.** Against Alexa's "it doesn't matter," Carroll: "It does matter, because it has implications of how we live our lives." If Islam is true you face Sharia; if Judaism is true, "613 laws"; "you can't have all of it at the same time," and which is true determines how you must live. [^4] *Rating: strong; the traditions issue incompatible practical demands, so indifference between them is not neutral.*
4. **Other-faith truth is conceded, so this is not crude exclusivism.** The argument's force depends on Carroll *granting* that "Islam can have truth and good things in it. Same with Judaism... pick any religion... you can draw good from it." [^5] He thereby honors the pluralist's real datum (good people, real wisdom everywhere) while denying only the inference that contradictory *claims* are all true. *Rating: strong as a dialectical posture — it removes the "you think everyone else is just wrong" caricature.*

### Limits and counter-pressure
- **It leans on the Resurrection actually being well-evidenced.** Carroll appeals to the apostles' testimony and their willingness to die ("they gave their lives for it... they weren't lying with deceit"). [^6] But dying for a belief establishes sincerity, not truth — see [[Dying for a Belief Proves Sincerity Not Truth]]. The wedge only excludes the rivals *if* the Resurrection claim is independently credible; Alexa's "it was so long ago... I don't know" keeps that premise open. [^2]
- **A pluralist may bite the bullet at the symbolic level.** A sophisticated pluralist (Hick) could say the Resurrection is "true" mythologically for Christians without being a literal historical fact — relocating the claim out of the yes/no register Carroll demands. Carroll's reply is essentially [[The Noise Theory of Religious Difference|that this evades rather than answers]] the historical question.
- **Inclusivism, not exclusivism, is the LDS home.** The note pairs naturally with [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]]: the LDS frame can hold both that other traditions carry genuine light *and* that the exclusive truth-claims cannot all stand — the Resurrection (and the Restoration that presupposes it) being the non-negotiable fact.
