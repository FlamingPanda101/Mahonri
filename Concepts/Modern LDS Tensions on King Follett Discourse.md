# Modern LDS Tensions on King Follett Discourse

Since the late 20th century, LDS leaders and scholars have oscillated between
embracing the [[King Follett Discourse]]'s radical distinctiveness and downplaying
it to emphasize common ground with mainstream Christianity — creating a persistent
"awkwardness" around the sermon's most provocative claims.

## The Pull Toward Ecumenism

During the 1980s, 1990s, and early 21st century, "a number of prominent LDS
intellectuals and academics and BYU professors" engaged in dialogues with Christians,
especially evangelicals. "To be able to show those commonalities, there's been a
decline of emphasizing the King Follett Discourse."[^1]

## Gordon B. Hinckley's Ambivalence

[[Gordon B. Hinckley]] exemplified the tension in a 1997 national interview:

> [Regarding the King Follett Discourse] that gets into some pretty deep theology
> that we don't know very much about.[^2]

When asked about the [[Lorenzo Snow]] couplet ("As man is, God once was. As God is,
man may become"), Hinckley responded: "I'm not sure we really teach that" — though
"in other interviews he does try to double down, which shows just this constant
awkwardness."[^3]

## The David Paulsen Anecdote

At a gathering between BYU professors and evangelical professors in Nauvoo, [[David
Paulsen]] was "alarmed" at colleagues like [[Steven Robinson]] and [[Robert Millet]]
who were downplaying the discourse:

> "'Oh, that might be just some fringe doctrines. I don't know if it's fully
> canonized.' And when it came around to David Paulsen, he said, 'I belong to the
> church of King Follett. We are not moving away from it. I will always be a
> professor at the University of King Follett.'"[^4]

[[Benjamin Park]] notes this reveals "a divided sense — some who exalt in the radical unique
doctrines... the exceptionalism of Mormon theology, and others who wish that it
weren't as central because they want to emphasize the connections of mainstream
Christianity."[^5]

## The Current Settlement: Theosis Framing

The modern LDS approach, per [[Benjamin Park]], is represented by the Church's Gospel Topics
essay "Becoming Like God" (likely authored by [[Terrell Givens]]):

> "This new train of thinking — well, maybe we can embrace the radical doctrines
> of the King Follett Discourse and connect it to things like Eastern Orthodoxy
> and these other notions of Christian theosis. So emphasizing both the
> exceptionalism of Mormonism as well as its connections to the broader Christian
> world — which is where I think the Latter-day Saints currently are today."[^6]

The key pivot: frame Mormon exaltation as *part of* the broader Christian tradition
of theosis, not as a uniquely radical departure — while still retaining the
distinctive materialist framework.

## Apologetic Significance

For Christian critics: the sermon remains Exhibit A that Mormonism teaches a
"different God" — an exalted, once-human being fundamentally unlike the uncreated,
immaterial God of classical theism.

For LDS apologists: the modern strategy is to find common ground in theosis while
acknowledging (or downplaying) the radical materialism. The discourse's non-canonical
status provides rhetorical cover for those who want to soften its authority.
