# The Cult Label — History and Academic Critique

## The Term's Trajectory

### Phase 1: Neutral Scholarly Usage (pre-20th c.)
"Cult" originally described ritual-focused devotional practice within a religion
(e.g., "the cult of the Virgin Mary" in Catholicism). It was a descriptive
category, not a pejorative.

### Phase 2: Sociological Category (early 20th c.)
Weber and Troeltsch developed the **church/sect/cult** typology:
- **Church**: Large, inclusive, low-tension with society, professional clergy
- **Sect**: High-tension, high-demand, voluntary membership, lay leadership
- **Cult**: Residual category — small, loosely organized, mystical, personal.
  Troeltsch had in mind what we'd now call New Age or metaphysical movements.

This was fuzzy and underdeveloped as a category — not negative, just vague.

### Phase 3: Evangelical Hijacking (mid-20th c.)
The turning point. American evangelical theologians wrote books critiquing
Latter-day Saints, Jehovah's Witnesses, Christian Scientists, and Seventh-day
Adventists as **"cults"** — meaning heretical, non-Christian, a threat to
mainstream Protestantism. These groups were growing and prospering.

Dawson: "They applied the term 'cult' in a highly pejorative sense as a way
of basically saying they're not Christian, they're not proper churches."

### Phase 4: The Great American Cult Scare (1970s–80s)
- Hare Krishna, Scientology, Unification Church recruited university students.
- Media panic. Parents organized. Over 100 major legal cases.
- Self-appointed "cult experts" charged money for testimony about brainwashing.
- This controversy **kicked off serious academic scholarship** by sociologists,
  psychologists, and religious studies scholars who recognized the distortion.

### Phase 5: Academic Reclamation (1980s–2000s)
- Scholars adopted the neutral term **"New Religious Movement" (NRM)** to
  replace the contaminated "cult."
- Dawson's book is titled *Understanding Cults* only because that's the
  "lightning term" that makes someone pick it up — the subtitle is *The
  Sociology of New Religious Movements*.
- Courts began rejecting self-appointed cult experts. US v. Fishman (1990) and
  subsequent precedents established that brainwashing theories are not
  scientifically admissible.

### Phase 6: Relapse (social media era)
Despite the legal and scholarly consensus, "cult" has returned as a pejorative
in popular culture, YouTube, and social media. Dawson: "In a sense, the
scholarship's been forgotten."

## The Core Problem with "Cult" as Pejorative

1. **It hides more than it reveals** — it's a conversation-stopper, not an
   analytical tool.
2. **It's applied selectively** — early Christianity was an NRM by any
   definition. Jesus + 12 followers, miracle claims, small, strange to
   outsiders. "From the perspective of a Roman citizen... it would fit all
   the negative pejorative concepts of cult."
3. **It conflates theology with sociology** — the evangelical usage is a
   doctrinal dispute disguised as a sociological claim.
4. **Abuse is not unique to NRMs** — abuse happens in the Catholic Church,
   the LDS Church, and every religious organization. "You need to work with
   individual cases, individual instances, and not draw stereotypical
   negative condemnations."

## Apologetic Application
When someone calls Mormonism a "cult," the informed response is:
- "Cult" in what sense? Sociological? Theological? Pop-culture?
- The sociological category describes organizational structure, not truth.
- The pejorative sense originated as an evangelical polemic against LDS in
  the mid-20th century — it's a doctrinal insult, not a social-scientific
  finding.
- Every world religion began as an NRM.
