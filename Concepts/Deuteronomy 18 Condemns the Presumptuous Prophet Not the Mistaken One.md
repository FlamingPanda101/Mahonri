# Deuteronomy 18 Condemns the Presumptuous Prophet Not the Mistaken One

#type/claim

## The Claim
Critics deploy [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:22]] as a mechanical "false-prophet test": if any prediction a prophet utters "follow not, nor come to pass," he is a false prophet "not to be followed" — therefore [[Joseph Smith]]'s allegedly unfulfilled prophecies disqualify him. [[Hayden Carroll]] answers that this misreads the verse. The text does not condemn a prophet for a single prediction that fails to materialize; it condemns the prophet who speaks **presumptuously** — who claims YHWH's name for words YHWH "hath not commanded." The diagnostic ("if the thing follow not") identifies words spoken presumptuously; it is not itself a bright-line capital test of every utterance. And the relevant remedy is not execution but "thou shalt not be afraid of him."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman)
1. **The text's own wording.** Deuteronomy 18:20 targets the prophet "which shall **presume** to speak a word in my name, which I have not commanded him." Verse 22 closes "the prophet hath spoken it **presumptuously**: thou shalt not be afraid of him." The operative category is *presumption* (Hebrew *bezadon*, "with insolence"), not honest error — see [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:20-22]]. The penalty clause attaches to the presumptuous prophet of v. 20; v. 22's practical instruction is merely to not fear the speaker.[^1]
2. **Jonah is the disproof by counterexample.** [[Jonah 3.4|Jonah]] proclaimed in YHWH's name that Nineveh would be overthrown in forty days; Nineveh repented and God relented, so the prediction plainly "came not to pass." If the critic's mechanical reading were correct, Jonah is a false prophet — a conclusion no Bible-believing Christian accepts. The critic cannot apply to Joseph Smith a rule he refuses to apply to a canonical Hebrew prophet without a **double standard**.[^2] (This sharpens the conditionality point of [[All Prophecies Are Conditional]] into a hermeneutical move about Deut 18 specifically, rather than open-theism.)
3. **Carroll's prior triage of the prophecies.** Before invoking Deut 18, Carroll classifies every contested Joseph Smith prophecy into one of three buckets: **(a) conditional** (like Old Testament oracles contingent on human response), **(b) already fulfilled** but unacknowledged by critics, or **(c) awaiting future fulfillment**. On this account none is a genuine presumptuous-and-failed prophecy of the kind Deut 18 targets.[^3]
4. **The cessationist self-refutation lurks behind it.** Wielding Deut 18 to retire prophecy entirely proves too much, since the same test would indict biblical prophets — the structural problem catalogued in [[Prophetic Failed-Prophecy Test Is Self-Refuting for Cessationists]]. Latter-day Saints hold that [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine|prophets remain fallible men]] who can err without forfeiting their calling.

**Counter (the critic's reply).** A defender of the mechanical reading argues that verse 22 *is* the operational test precisely because presumption is invisible — fulfillment is the God-given external sign that distinguishes the presumptuous prophet from the true one, so a failed prediction just *is* the evidence of presumption. Jonah is then handled as an *implicit conditional* warning-oracle ("forty days unless you repent"), not a flat prediction — which, if granted, lets the critic keep Jonah while still demanding that Joseph Smith's failures be shown conditional one by one rather than waved off. The burden returns to case-by-case analysis of the specific prophecies.

## Best Quotes
> "This verse does not say that this is a false prophet. The verse says that if the prophet speaks presumptuously, we should not be afraid of him." [^1]

> "Does Mark reject Jonah in the Old Testament as a prophet because he had a false prophecy? He told the people of Nineveh that they were going to be destroyed. And then God said, 'Actually, no, I'm not going to destroy them.'" [^2]

> "All of Joseph Smith's prophecies can fit into one of at least three categories: conditional prophecy... that they did happen and he just doesn't recognize it... or that we're still waiting for [the] prophecy to be fulfilled." [^3]
