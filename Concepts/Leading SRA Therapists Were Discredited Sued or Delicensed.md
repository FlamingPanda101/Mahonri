# Leading SRA Therapists Were Discredited Sued or Delicensed

## The Claim
The therapists who drove the satanic-ritual-abuse panic were, one by one, sued for implanting false memories, stripped of their licenses, or forced to recant — including the very psychiatrist who coined the term. Their professional collapse is the clearest verdict on the movement they led.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Bennett Brown — multimillion-dollar settlements and expulsion (strong)
[[Bennett Brown]], "the Dr. Lawrence Pazder of the East Coast," was sued in 1993 for "falsely convincing [a patient] that she had engaged in satanic rituals, cannibalism, and infanticide"; he settled for $10.6 million, with another case settled for $7.5 million. "Brown's medical license was suspended, and he was expelled from the Illinois Psychiatric Society and the American Psychiatric Association in 2000" [^1].

### 2. Barbara Snow — charged with implanting false memories (strong)
In 2007 the Utah Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing "charged that Dr. Snow had implanted false memories of sexual abuse into her patients." Investigators tested this by feeding her false information that "immediately began appearing in the children's accounts"; she accepted probation and license suspension. The Utah State Court later cited "the suggestive and coercive nature of Dr. Snow's techniques" in reversing a conviction, and the 10th Circuit called her conduct "disturbing and irresponsible." See [[Barbara Snows Interview Methods Manufactured Abuse Allegations]] [^2][^3].

### 3. Lawrence Pazder — the coiner recants (decisive — hostile-to-claim admission)
"Even [[Lawrence Pazder]] … the very man who coined the term satanic ritual abuse … was forced to admit in 1994 that perhaps satanic abuse memories are more an expression of the deep level of violation caused by abusive family members than actual accounts of satanic ritual abuse." When the movement's founder concedes the memories are not literal, the core claim is abandoned from within [^4].

### 4. The professions tightened the rules in response (moderate — institutional)
"The American Medical Association issued a set of new recommendations to therapists … Foremost among those principles, recording interviews" — the precise safeguard Snow "loathed to do." The remedial response presupposes the prior practice was producing false results. See [[False Memory Reports Caused Documented Wrongful Convictions]] [^5].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender could argue that lawsuits and licensing actions reflect litigation risk, insurance economics, and shifting professional fashion rather than proof that every recovered memory was false — settlements are not admissions, and a pioneer's late hedging may be overcautious capitulation to a skeptical backlash. The rebuttal: the pattern is not one ambiguous case but a convergent collapse — independent suits in different states, a controlled test in which planted falsehoods surfaced verbatim in a therapist's case notes, appellate courts naming the techniques as coercive, and the term's own originator disclaiming its literal referent. Any one item might be explained away; together they are the signature of a discredited method, not a fashion swing.
