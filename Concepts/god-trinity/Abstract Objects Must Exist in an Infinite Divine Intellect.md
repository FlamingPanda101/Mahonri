# Abstract Objects Must Exist in an Infinite Divine Intellect

## The Point
This is the core of the **Augustinian proof** (the "argument from eternal truths"). Once realism is granted, abstract objects must exist either in some intellect or in a Platonic "third realm." Feser argues the third realm is incoherent, so they must exist *in an intellect*.[^1] But they cannot ultimately depend on any *contingent* intellect — human, extraterrestrial, or even angelic — because they would be true even if no such mind existed.[^2] Therefore they must exist in an intellect that exists of **absolute necessity**: an infinite, eternal, divine intellect. This is **Scholastic realism**, the view "famously associated with Saint Augustine" — equivalent to what Greg Welty calls **"Theistic Conceptual Realism."**[^3] In short: God exists.[^4]

## Evidence
Feser surveys three forms of realism and eliminates two:
- **Platonic realism** posits a "third realm" of Forms outside all minds. Problems: the Forms would be causally inert (so we could never know them, nor could they explain why things fit their patterns); a Form is incoherently both universal and a particular individual (the "Third Man" regress); and animality-as-Form cannot be either rational or nonrational, yet every individual must be one or the other — so a universal cannot exist as an individual Form.[^5]
- **Aristotelian realism** says universals exist only in the concrete things that instantiate them, or in minds that abstract them — "nothing abstract exists without abstraction."[^6] This rightly avoids the third realm, but cannot ground the abstracta that have *no* instances and depend on *no* actual mind: pure possibles (unicorns, a world with different physics), necessary truths that would hold if no material world or finite mind existed, and propositions about a world in which nothing material or mental exists.[^7]
- **Scholastic realism** keeps the Aristotelian thesis (universals exist in things or in intellects, never in a free-floating third realm) but adds, with the Platonist, that there must be a realm beyond the material world and all finite minds — namely an **infinite, eternal, divine intellect** in which universals, propositions, mathematical and logical truths, and all necessities and possibilities exist.[^3]

The argument to a *single, divine* mind:
1. Abstracta do not depend on the material world (it is contingent and particular; many truths are necessary and universal).[^8]
2. They cannot depend on contingent intellects — human, alien, or angelic — since they would be true even if none of those existed; even an angel exists only if God chooses to create it.[^2]
3. So they must exist in an intellect that "could not possibly have not existed."[^9]
4. The abstracta form a single **interlocking system** (grasping one universal requires a network of others; every proposition is logically related to every other), so their ground cannot be split among several minds — it must be *one* mind grasping the whole infinite system: a "conceptually omniscient being."[^10]
5. A necessarily existing intellect must be **purely actual** (any unactualized potential would make its existence contingent). By the results of Feser's Aristotelian proof, what is purely actual must be unique, omnipotent, perfectly good, immutable, immaterial, incorporeal, and eternal — and must know all truths, including contingent ones, on pain of unrealized potential.[^11]
6. "But for there to be such a thing is just what it is for God to exist. So, God exists."[^4]

Feser then rebuts objections: that ideas in a mind aren't "really abstract" (begs the question — for the Scholastic, an abstract object *just is* what the intellect forms by abstraction);[^12] Leftow's "deity theory" worry that God's nature would depend on facts about water (parallel to the harmless fact that 2+2=4 entails truths about rocks);[^13] and Grim's Cantorian argument that there is no set of all truths (it would prove too much, and divine simplicity means God's knowledge is not a set of discrete ideas).[^14]

## Best Quotes
> "Endorsing a thesis famously associated with Saint Augustine—it holds that universals, propositions, mathematical and logical truths, and necessities and possibilities exist in an infinite, eternal, divine intellect." [^3]

> "Since Scholastic realism entails that there is an infinite divine intellect, then there really must be such an intellect. In other words, God exists." [^4]

> "The ultimate ground of the existence of the abstract objects we've been discussing would have to be a conceptually omniscient necessarily existing intellect, rather than a collection of intellects." [^15]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this argument is a formidable ally: it takes realities the naturalist already relies on (numbers, logic, modal truth) and argues they can only be grounded in a necessary divine Mind — converging with the Restoration's conviction that intelligence and truth are divine and pervade reality (D&C 88:6–13; D&C 93:36; compare [[The Laws of Logic Are Immaterial and Point to a Mind]]). There is even a striking *partial convergence*: both Feser and Joseph Smith locate the ultimate explanation of reality in mind/intelligence rather than blind matter. The honest, load-bearing **divergence** — and Feser deserves to be met at full strength here — lies in two classical commitments this note depends on: (1) **divine simplicity**, that God's intellect, will, power, and nature are strictly identical, and (2) **pure actuality / aseity**, that God is the unconditioned ground of *all* else, authoring even the eternal truths as ideas in his mind. The Restoration instead holds that certain laws and truths are *co-eternal* with God and honored by him rather than authored by him ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]), and that God is embodied rather than absolutely simple and immaterial. So a Latter-day Saint can affirm the conclusion (necessary truth is grounded in a divine Mind) while declining Feser's specific metaphysics of how — a real fork within theism, not a defeat of the theistic conclusion atheism must still answer.
