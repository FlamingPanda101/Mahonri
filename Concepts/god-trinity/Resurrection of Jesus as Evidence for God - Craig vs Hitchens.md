# Resurrection of Jesus as Evidence for God - Craig vs Hitchens

## The Point
Craig's fourth argument: the historical Jesus came with "an unprecedented sense of divine authority," and the supreme confirmation of his claim was his resurrection. Craig rests the case on "three established facts recognized by the majority of New Testament historians": (1) the empty tomb, discovered by women followers; (2) the post-mortem appearances to individuals and groups, including skeptics; (3) the disciples' sudden belief in a risen Messiah *despite* having "every predisposition to the contrary" in Judaism. The best — and only adequate — explanation, he argues, is that "God raised Jesus from the dead," which entails God exists.[^1]

Hitchens' reply is twofold. First, he attacks the *inference* via N.T. Wright: Wright says no explanation of Christianity's success works except its being true, but Hitchens calls that "a very unsafe assumption" that "must surely apply to Islam and to Mormonism" as well — fast-growing religions with ancestors "absolutely determined of the truth of it."[^2] Second, in cross-examination he probes whether miracle-working proves anything: even if "the laws of nature can be suspended and great miracles can be performed, it doesn't prove the truth of the doctrine of the person who's performing them" — Pharaoh's magicians did wonders too, and "the people who got the closest look at him, the Jewish Sanhedrin... thought he was bogus."[^3] He also notes that Matthew's crucifixion narrative has *other* tombs opening and saints walking Jerusalem, making resurrection "rather a commonplace."[^4]

Craig answers that Wright's argument is *not* "success proves truth" (which would indeed apply to Islam/Mormonism) but that the *origin of the disciples' belief* in a dying-and-rising Messiah is so uncharacteristic of Judaism that only the empty tomb and appearances explain it.[^5]

## Evidence
**Craig (affirmative):**
- The three facts enjoy wide scholarly support: he cites Jakob Kremer (empty tomb), Gerd Lüdemann (appearances "historically certain"), and N.T. Wright on the disciples' transformed belief.[^1]
- Naturalistic alternatives (stolen body, apparent death) are "universally rejected by contemporary scholarship."[^6]
- Wright: the empty tomb and appearances have "a historical probability so high as to be virtually certain, like the death of Augustus in AD 14 or the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70."[^7]

**Hitchens (negative):**
- "Success implies truth" is unsafe and would equally validate Islam and Mormonism.[^2]
- Miracles, even granted, don't certify a doctrine; exorcists and Pharaoh's magicians worked wonders, and the eyewitness Sanhedrin "thought he was bogus."[^3]
- The Gospels are not "literate, written down, properly attested" witnesses; "it's depressingly easy to get a religious rumor started" among "credulous... populations."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "There is no plausible naturalistic explanation of these facts, and therefore... the Christian is amply justified in believing that Jesus rose from the dead and was who he claimed to be." [^1]

> "If you're going to grant this for one religion... you may indeed be compelled to make this concession for all of them." (Hitchens, on Wright's argument) [^2]

> "Even if the laws of nature can be suspended and great miracles can be performed, it doesn't prove the truth of the doctrine of the person who's performing them." (Hitchens) [^3]

## LDS Connection
The bodily resurrection of Jesus is the foundation of the Christian hope and is affirmed wholeheartedly by Latter-day Saints; Craig's historical case (empty tomb, appearances, the inexplicable rise of resurrection belief) is shared apologetic ground against atheism. Hitchens' jab that Wright's argument "must apply to Mormonism" is meant as a *reductio*, but a Latter-day Saint can simply accept that the willingness of witnesses to suffer for what they claimed to have seen is genuine evidence — the question in each case is the quality of the testimony, and the Restoration likewise rests on named, plural witnesses (e.g., the Three and Eight Witnesses of the Book of Mormon). Craig's clarification is decisive against Hitchens here: the argument is not "many believers, therefore true," but that a *specific historical effect* (Jewish disciples suddenly proclaiming a crucified-and-risen Messiah) needs a sufficient cause. On miracles failing to certify doctrine, both can agree the resurrection's force comes not from raw wonder but from its vindication of Jesus' unique claim to divine authority (compare [[Miracles Are Suspensions Not Violations of Natural Law]]).
