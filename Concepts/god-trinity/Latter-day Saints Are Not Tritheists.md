# Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The LDS view is **tritheism**: three fully-divine persons = three gods. [[Go With Jordan]]: "The LDS view cannot demonstrate that there are three gods... The Father is fully divine, the Son is fully divine, the Holy Spirit is fully divine. Three. Can you count?"

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The same diagram indicts the Trinity
Any standard Trinity diagram says the Father is God, the Son is God, the Spirit is God — *and that they are not each other*. The LDS affirm everything except the paradoxical add-on that the three are numerically one being. So if "three who are each fully God" is tritheism, the diagram is too; the only difference is the contradiction the Trinitarian inserts. *(Strong as a tu quoque: it shows the count of "three fully divine persons" is shared, not distinctive to LDS.)* [^1]

### 2. "One God" = one Godhead, not three loose gods
The three persons are "united in one purpose" as one Godhead/entity ([[Three Whos and One What Framework]], [[The Court Analogy for the Godhead]]). Scripture affirming "one God" ([[James 2.19|James 2:19]]) is affirmed — referring to the one *what*. So LDS hold three persons / one Godhead, not three independent deities. *(Moderate-strong; depends on the "one what = real entity" claim, which Trinitarians dispute as a mere collective.)* [^2]

### 3. The opponent kept disclaiming polytheism himself
Tellingly, [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]] "four or five times" insisted "we're not polytheists" while spending his opening establishing multiple divine persons — showing the *Trinitarian* feels the same pressure of accounting for three divine persons within monotheism. The "tritheism" charge is therefore a straw man that boomerangs. *(Moderate; an observation about debate dynamics rather than a metaphysical proof.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The critic's serious point survives the tu quoque: classical Trinitarianism avoids tritheism by *numerical identity of essence* (one *being*), so the three are not three gods but one God in three persons — whereas the LDS "one Godhead of three separate beings" looks, to the critic, like three gods plus a *social* unity that monotheism's grammar ("there is one God," "no god beside me") will not bear. Calling a collective "the Godhead" (the court analogy) does not, they argue, make it *one God* in the biblical sense any more than "the pantheon" makes many gods one. The LDS reply is that biblical "monotheism" is better described as monolatry / exclusive devotion (see [[Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category]], [[The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence]]), so "one God" need not mean one being — leaving the dispute exactly where the whole debate sits: the nature of the one "what."
