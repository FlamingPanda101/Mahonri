# An Agent Cannot Claim to Be the King in Ancient West Asian Literature

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
This is the objection [[Avery (God Logic)]] presses against [[Jacob Hansen]]: a royal agent "can speak as though they were the king... come in the name of the king... make it clear, 'I'm a representative of the king' — but can they claim to be the king himself? No, he cannot." [^1] The point targets divine investiture: if no human agent could declare "I *am* the king," then [[Scriptures/Genesis 31.13|the messenger]] saying "I am God" cannot be explained by investiture and must (Avery argues) point to deity proper. [[Dan McClellan]] **concedes the empirical premise** but rejects the Trinitarian conclusion drawn from it.

## The Rebuttal

1. **The premise is factually correct.** McClellan agrees: "nowhere in any of the other broader ancient West Asian literature is a messenger allowed to say I am the king in light of the divine investiture of authority... So Jacob loses the battle in that regard." He also affirms Avery's parallel that an agent of Yahweh who claimed *to be* Yahweh would "drop dead." *Rating: strong — McClellan explicitly endorses the opposing debater's factual point.* [^2] [^3]

2. **But the correct conclusion is divine images, not the Trinity.** Conceding the investiture problem does not vindicate Trinitarianism, which is "most likely going to... presuppose some kind of trinitarianism or at least binitarianism... and no such frameworks actually existed in that time period." The category that *did* allow an agent to be identified as the deity was the **divine image** — a consecrated medium that "could be explicitly identified as the deity that the image indexed." *Rating: this is McClellan threading the needle — the king/agent analogy fails, but the image/deity relation succeeds.* [^4] *Steelman of Avery's side:* if the only ancient category that lets the messenger say "I am God" is a *cult-image* relation, a Trinitarian could argue that is precisely an admission that ordinary agency is insufficient and something ontologically stronger than delegation is in view.

3. **Jesus' indirectness reinforces the point.** Because no agent could licitly claim to be the king/deity, McClellan notes Jesus correspondingly "never claims to be the king" and "uses a lot of ambiguous language" (e.g., *kyrios*, which can be a mere term of respect). *Rating: consistent with his comparison that the Hebrew messenger is more explicit than Jesus.* [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Can the agent of the king claim to be the king? No, he cannot... The same way Yahweh can send agents, but the agents cannot claim to be Yahweh, or else they're going to drop dead. So God Logic is correct here." [^2]

> "Nowhere in any of the other broader ancient West Asian literature is a messenger allowed to say I am the king... So Jacob loses the battle in that regard. However, God Logic's position... is even less supported by the data." [^4]

> "Jesus never claims to be the king. Jesus uses a lot of ambiguous language... the substitution kurios for the divine name, but which can also function as just a secular term of respect." [^5]
