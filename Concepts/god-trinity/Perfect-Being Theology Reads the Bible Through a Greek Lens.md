# Perfect-Being Theology Reads the Bible Through a Greek Lens

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that creedal "perfect-being theology" runs the order of operations *backwards*: instead of starting with the God revealed in scripture, it starts with the Greek axiom that God is "that than which no greater can be conceived," reasons philosophically to an immaterial, timeless, spaceless, impassible being, and *then* forces the Bible to fit that pre-set definition. The Latter-day Saint move is to reverse it — "go to Jesus and say, you rose from the dead, you tell me who God is" — and read the embodied, passionate God the text actually presents.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The method begins from a Greek definition — rate: strong (intellectual history)
Hansen: "You have to ask people, what do you mean by God? And they say, 'that of which no greater can be conceived.' That's Greek." The "great-making properties" of philosophy (à la William Lane Craig's perfect-being theology) lead deductively to immateriality, timelessness, and spacelessness — "that is where the logic of great-making properties leads you. They have defined God as this, and then they go to the Bible and say, how do we make this fit?"[^1] This is the methodological engine behind [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and [[The Creedal God Is the Platonic One Not the God of the Bible]].

### 2. Augustine said he was doing it — rate: strong (primary admission)
Hansen claims the Hellenizing was sometimes *explicit*: "Augustine [said] 'this is what I'm doing — I'm trying to make Christianity palatable to Greeks, I'm trying to infuse it with enough Hellenism that it will feel more comfortable to us Greeks.'" Once God is *defined* as the philosophers' absolute, "you're not open to anything else; God must fit within these parameters of philosophy."[^2]

### 3. The reverse method yields the biblical God — rate: moderate (LDS-internal)
Reading the Bible without the Greek lens, Hansen argues, gives "not the abstract monotheistic creation of the Greek philosophers" but an embodied God ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]) with passions — the Risen Lord with a body. The apostasy thesis is that the lens, not the text, produced the creedal God.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The classical theist denies the lens is alien. (a) **Perfect-being reasoning is itself biblical.** Anselm and Aquinas argued the "greatest conceivable being" is a *conclusion from* revelation (God as "I AM," Exodus 3:14; "God is spirit," John 4:24; "with whom is no variableness," James 1:17), not an import from Athens; the anthropomorphic "passions" are read as accommodative language. On this view scripture and reason *converge*, and Aquinas adopted Aristotle's mover because he judged it *consonant with* revelation, not because he was smuggling Greece in. (b) **The Hellenization thesis is contested.** Harnack's claim that doctrine was Greek corruption is itself debated scholarship (the vault records the rebuttal in [[Hellenization Was Not Corruption of an Original Pure Church]]); many historians argue the Fathers *critiqued and transformed* Greek categories rather than capitulating to them. (c) **The LDS alternative has its own metaphysics.** "Just read the Bible plainly" is not metaphysically innocent — an embodied, in-time God raises its own philosophical problems (how an embodied being is omnipresent, etc.), so the dispute is between two metaphysical pictures, not between "philosophy" and "the pure text." The disciplined version: it is *fair* to say creedal theology was articulated in Greek philosophical vocabulary and that perfect-being theology starts from an abstract definition; whether that constitutes *corruption* or *legitimate clarification* is exactly the contested question, and the "Augustine admitted it" point proves engagement with Greek audiences, not necessarily distortion of the gospel.
