# Two Self-Existent Realities Cannot Coexist

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Christian Mario]] argues that the Latter-day Saint position — God plus eternal, uncreated matter — posits **two self-existent realities**, which is metaphysically impossible. "If matter is eternal, then it shares God's defining attribute, self-existence. So two self-existent realities can't coexist. Each would limit the other." The dilemma he forces: "if God depends on matter to act, he is not omnipotent. And if matter depends on God, then God is no longer eternal — and all you have is eternal matter." Because each of two purportedly independent ultimates would bound the other, neither can be the unconditioned ground; the very notion of a second co-eternal reality alongside God reduces, Mario says, to **dualism** (God placed "inside something" becomes "a creature within a larger reality").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Orthodox / classical-theist side).** (1) *Mutual limitation.* Two genuinely independent, self-existent realities would each have to lack whatever the other possesses (otherwise they would be identical), so each conditions the other — and a conditioned thing is, by definition, not self-existent. The conclusion that there can be at most one absolutely independent ground is the classical *uniqueness* result (only one thing can be subsistent existence itself; cf. [[Why the Necessary Being Must Be God Not the Universe]] and [[The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One]]). (2) *The omnipotence horn.* If God must work *with* a matter he did not produce and cannot un-produce, his power is intrinsically capped by that matter's nature — "he is not omnipotent" in the unqualified sense the creeds require. (3) *The collapse horn.* If, to save God's ultimacy, one says matter depends on God after all, then matter is no longer co-eternal and uncreated — the Latter-day Saint has abandoned the distinctive and conceded *creatio ex nihilo*. Either way the LDS middle position is unstable. Mario ties this directly to the realm-objection: putting God "inside" any independent reality "turned him into a creature" (see [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]]).

**Steelman (LDS reply — Hansen).** Hansen resists the "shared attribute → mutual limitation" inference and the omnipotence premise. (1) *Reject unqualified omnipotence.* "I reject that God is omnipotent in a radical sense." On the LDS view God is supremely powerful *within* the eternal order, not unbounded over it — so "matter caps God's power" is not a *reductio* but simply the doctrine (cf. [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]]). (2) *Co-existence is not mutual dependence.* Hansen denies that God and eternal matter *depend* on each other in the strong sense Mario needs; matter's existing eternally does not make God *derive* from it, nor vice versa — they simply both are. The "each would limit the other" step assumes a competition for a single throne of being that LDS metaphysics does not grant. (3) *The substrate is not "matter" as ordinarily conceived.* Hansen specifies an idealist reading — a fundamental "spirit matter," akin to "mind is fundamental" — so the picture is not two rival lumps of stuff but a minded reality within which the supreme Mind organizes (see [[A Cosmic Mind Grounds the Universe (Theistic Idealism)]] and [[The Uncreated Element Is Only the Most Fundamental Substrate]]). (4) *Plurality is permitted.* The deeper LDS rejoinder is that the contingency/uniqueness proof smuggles in divine simplicity; deny simplicity and there is no a priori bar to more than one self-existent reality (cf. [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]]). The dispute thus turns on whether "self-existence" is the kind of attribute that can be instanced more than once — Mario says no on pain of mutual limitation; Hansen says the impossibility is asserted, not demonstrated.
