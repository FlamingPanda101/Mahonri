# Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The biblical text teaches the consubstantial Trinity — one *being*, one *substance/essence* (*ousia*), shared by three persons.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Ontology and consubstantiality are Greek thought-categories, absent from Hebrew
"Being the same species," "ontological oneness," "consubstantiality" — these are **Greek** philosophical categories. Hebrew thought worked in terms of **unity** and **covenant loyalty**, not shared metaphysical essence. Trinitarians read the Bible "using Greek glasses," imposing categories that "literally did not exist in that world." *(Strong: aligns with much biblical-studies scholarship that the NT authors did not think in Nicene metaphysical terms.)* [^1]

### 2. It is not that the terms are missing — the *framework* is missing
[[Jacob Hansen]] grants you need not have the *words* in the Bible. The deeper problem is that the conceptual **framework** is absent: "Nobody in the Bible had a notion of consubstantiality... that they were the same being." Reading 4th-century conclusions back into 1st-century texts inverts exegesis — starting from the creedal answer instead of asking what the original author meant. *(Strong.)* [^2]

### 3. There was no Neoplatonism in Second Temple Judaism
The metaphysical apparatus the creeds rely on (Neoplatonic essence-talk) simply was not present in the 1st century or in Second Temple Judaism. A first-century reader would have "slapped you on the wrist" for the consubstantial reading of, say, the Shema or [[Genesis 1.26|Genesis 1:26]] ("let us make man in our image" → divine council, plurality-in-unity). *(Moderate-strong; the broad point is sound, though "zero Neoplatonism" is a rhetorical overstatement — Hellenistic-Jewish thinkers like Philo show real Greek influence.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A serious Trinitarian (or a scholar like Larry Hurtado) responds that (a) Second Temple Judaism was already **deeply Hellenized** — Philo of Alexandria fuses Torah with Middle-Platonism a generation before Christ — so "Greek categories were alien" is overstated; and (b) doctrine need not be *phrased* in the source culture's categories to be *true to* the data: later precision (homoousios) can legitimately articulate what the texts entail, the way "Trinity" formalizes the worship of Father, Son, and Spirit found across the NT. Chandler partly concedes this elsewhere on the stream by granting the Fathers' effort was *good-faith* (see [[Trinity Was a Good-Faith Effort Not Dishonesty]]); the live dispute is whether the Greek articulation *preserves* or *distorts* the Hebrew data.

## Best Quotes
> "Ontological oneness and being the same species, that is all Greek thought category... that is not Hebrew thought category in any way, shape, or form. Unity is. Covenant loyalty is." [^1]

> "It isn't just that the terms aren't in the Bible... The problem is you don't have the thought categories at all. The framework." [^2]

> "There's no Neoplatonism in the 1st century. Zero. None in the Second Temple Judaism." [^3]
