# The Blasphemy Charge Makes the Divine Jesus the Most Crucifiable Jesus

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brant Pitre]] argues that the *only* cause of Jesus's death on which all four Gospels agree is the charge of **blasphemy**, and that blasphemy makes best historical sense if Jesus claimed to be a *divine* Messiah, not merely the Davidic heir. At the Sanhedrin (Mark 14) Jesus answers Caiaphas by fusing [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7]] (the Son of Man coming on the clouds) with [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110]] (enthroned at God's right hand) — a heavenly, co-enthroned Messiah. Hence "the divine Jesus is actually the most crucifiable Jesus": his divine self-claim is the most historically vulnerable, best-attested explanation for the execution.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Claiming to be Messiah is not a capital crime (strong)
If merely claiming Davidic messiahship were blasphemy, no one could ever identify the legitimate Davidic heir. So the question is not only why *Rome* executed Jesus (the *titulus*: "King of the Jews") but why the *Jewish authorities* handed him over. Anthony Harvey's point: the single cause all four Gospels share is **blasphemy** (Mark 14; Matthew 26; John 19's "by our law he ought to die").[^1]

### 2. The trial answer is a fused divine claim (strong)
Asked "Are you the Messiah, the Son of the Blessed?" Jesus says "I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming with the clouds of heaven" — combining [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7]] (a heavenly being who receives dominion and worship) with [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110]] (sitting on a divine throne, which in an ancient Near Eastern frame implies equality with the divine king). Caiaphas tears his robe — the scripted reaction to blasphemy — and the council condemns him.[^2] See the existing [[The Sanhedrin Blasphemy Charge Presupposes the Enochic Son of Man]].

### 3. Executions of early Jewish Christians were stonings (medium)
Pitre notes a neglected pattern: Stephen, James the brother of Jesus (in Josephus), and the early Jerusalem Christians executed by the Sanhedrin were all *stoned* — the legally prescribed penalty for blasphemy against the deity ([[Leviticus 4|Leviticus]] 24). Making divine claims about a human is exactly the capital offense the pattern fits, and it explains the "parting of the ways" between Judaism and Christianity.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
O'Connor mounts several counters. (1) The **Caiaphas problem**: for the high priest to hear "Son of Man... at the right hand of Power" as a claim to *be God*, he must already have read Daniel 7 / Psalm 110 as describing a divine figure — which seems out of line with mainstream Jewish teaching. (2) The **two-powers timing problem**: the "two powers in heaven" idea may post-date Christianity (the Talmudic text a questioner cited turned out to be later), so it cannot be assumed live in Caiaphas's day. (3) The **Stephen counter** ([[Acts 7.55|Acts 7]]): people were charged with blasphemy for far less — speaking against the temple, against Moses, "changing the customs" — so Jesus needn't have claimed deity to draw the charge; the authorities were "trigger-happy" about blasphemy. (4) John 19 has them say he claimed to be "the *Son of God*," not "Yahweh."

## Counter-Objection
Pitre replies: (1) Second-temple texts — *4 Ezra*, the *Similitudes of Enoch* (1 Enoch 37–71), and Qumran's 11QMelchizedek on Psalm 110 — *already* interpret Daniel 7 and Psalm 110 in superhuman, pre-existent categories, so a *plausible* (not universal) divine reading was "in the air"; it need not be orthodox for *every* Jew, only available to Caiaphas, who was looking for a reason. (2) He concedes the two-powers-as-*heresy* is a later rabbinic debate and explicitly drops it, resting only on the first-century sources. (3) On Stephen ([[Acts 7.55|Acts 7]]) Pitre reads the data *the same way but to the opposite conclusion*: what actually gets Stephen stoned is his *declaration about the Son of Man at God's right hand* — the same two texts — applied to the exalted Jesus; this is *confirmatory* coherence, not a counterexample. The other charges (temple, Moses) are bad but not what triggers the stoning.

## Apologetic Engagement
The Restoration gains on two fronts. First, this is among the strongest non-LDS scholarly cases that Jesus claimed divinity in *Enochic Son-of-Man* terms — the very Christology the Restoration prefers to *homoousios* (see [[The Enochic Son of Man Matches LDS Christology Not Nicene Homoousios]]). Second, it vindicates the LDS reading of blasphemy: Jesus was condemned for claiming *divine status and enthronement at the Father's right hand*, i.e. a *distinct* exalted divine being beside God ([[Acts 7.55|Stephen sees two]] — Jesus standing at the right hand of God), not for claiming to *be* the one he sits beside. See [[Blasphemy Did Not Require Claiming to Be Yahweh]].

## LDS Relevance
Stephen's vision ([[Acts 7.55|Acts 7:55]]) — "the glory of God, *and* Jesus standing at the right hand of God" — is a primary LDS proof-text for two distinct divine persons; Pitre's argument independently routes the trial Christology through Daniel 7 + Psalm 110, the same enthronement texts the Restoration uses ([[Psalm 110 and Psalm 2 Are Enthronement Texts of the Festal Drama]]).
