# Hypostatic Union

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The doctrine of the hypostatic union — that Jesus is one person with two natures (fully God and fully human) — is the clear teaching of the New Testament.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The hypostatic union is a 5th-century solution to a problem the NT doesn't solve
After Nicaea (325 CE) established that Jesus is consubstantial with the Father, a new problem emerged: "Jesus is God, but Jesus was also a human — how's that work? Is it 50/50?" The Council of Chalcedon (451 CE) answered with the hypostatic union: Jesus is "100% God but Jesus is 100% human."[^1]

### 2. When asked "how does that work?" — the answer is "shut up"
McClellan's irreverent but pointed summary: "How does that work? Shut up. It's a mystery. We're not... it just is the way it is."[^2] This is not a doctrine derived from exegesis; it's a philosophical patch to reconcile contradictory commitments.

### 3. The pattern: make it "plausible enough"
"What you see in these arguments is basically: we just need to make it work. And so we just need to come up with an argument that is plausible enough that everybody can be like, 'Yeah, I can get on board with that.' Okay, then get on board with it. Sign right here... anybody you see saying anything else, you report them to us, we're going to exile them, and we're going to use the Empire to enforce this new philosophical framework."[^3]

### 4. It doesn't actually "make a ton of sense"
McClellan's assessment: "Doesn't make a ton of sense — it makes enough sense that we can threaten people if they don't agree with it."[^4] The doctrine is the product of institutional enforcement, not logical clarity.

### 5. The NT authors weren't trying to solve this problem
The Gospels present Jesus in functional/relational terms — what Jesus does, who Jesus represents, how Jesus relates to the Father — not in the ontological categories of "natures" and "persons." The hypostatic union is a solution to a question the NT authors weren't asking.[^5]

## Best Quote
> "It doesn't make a ton of sense. It makes enough sense that we can threaten people if they don't agree with it." [^4]
