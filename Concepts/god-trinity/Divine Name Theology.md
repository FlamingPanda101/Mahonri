# Divine Name Theology

## The Claim
In the ancient Near Eastern and biblical worldview, the **divine name** was a communicable vehicle of divine agency. An authorized bearer of the divine name could manifest God's presence, speak as God, and even be addressed as God — without being ontologically identical with God. This framework, not later Trinitarian categories, is the proper context for understanding John's high Christology.

## The Evidence

### 1. The Divine Name as Transferable Agency
The divine name could be written on a standing stone or cult image, consecrating it as a vehicle of divine presence. "When you impose the divine name, that charged the material media with the divine presence, which allowed the material media to presence the deity, manifest the presence, the power" — provided the materials and authority were right.[^1]

### 2. Biblical Precedent: The Angel of the Lord
In [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]], the angel of the Lord appears to Moses in the burning bush and then speaks AS God: "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, the God of Jacob." An authorized agent bearing the divine name speaks in the first person as the deity.[^2]

### 3. The Septuagint's Fusion of Divine Self-Identifications
The Greek *ego eimi* ("I am") in the Septuagint renders two different Hebrew phrases:[^3]
- *Ehyeh asher ehyeh* ("I will be / I am what I am") from Exodus 3:14
- *Ani hu* ("I am he") from Deutero-Isaiah and elsewhere

This fusion makes *ego eimi* a loaded phrase — a coded reference to divine self-identification — without either phrase being the Tetragrammaton itself.

### 4. Jesus as the Name-Bearing Agent in John
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that Jesus's "I am" statements in John ([[John 8.58|John 8:58]], the seven "I am" declarations) are claims to possess the divine name — "this is a claim to being this exalted human or this name-bearing angel."[^4] This is a claim to a *type* of deity (divine agency), not to being the God of Israel.

### 5. The Divine Name in John 17
In [[John 17.20-23|John 17:11]], Jesus says "Holy Father, protect them in your name that you have given me" — explicitly claiming to have been given the divine name. This is the mechanism by which Jesus manifests God: not by being God, but by bearing God's name.[^5]

## Implications for Christology
This framework resolves the tension in John's Gospel: Jesus says and does things that seem to identify him with God, but never says "I am God," and distinguishes himself from the Father (e.g., [[John 17.20-23|John 17:3]]: "that they may know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent"). Divine name theology explains the high Christology without requiring the anachronistic imposition of post-Nicene Trinitarian categories.
