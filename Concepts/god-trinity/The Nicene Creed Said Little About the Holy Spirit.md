# The Nicene Creed Said Little About the Holy Spirit

## The Point
The original Nicene Creed of 325 was almost silent about the third member of the Godhead — it ended simply "And in the Holy Spirit," with no description. Because Nicaea was convened to answer [[Arius]]'s view of the *Father and Son*, the doctrine of the Holy Spirit was effectively unfinished. The pneumatology had to be supplied by the *First Council of Constantinople* (381), which produced the expanded Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed. This is concrete evidence that "the Trinity" was assembled piecemeal across multiple councils over the 4th–5th centuries, not delivered whole.

## Evidence
1. "Since the Council of Nicaea was assembled primarily in response to Arius's view of God the Father and Jesus Christ, the Creed included little about the Holy Spirit."[^1]
2. The 325 creed's entire statement on the Spirit was: "And in the Holy Spirit." — full stop.[^2]
3. The First Council of Constantinople (381) "remedied" this, creating the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed, which expanded the clause to: "And in the Holy Spirit, the Lord and the life-giver, who, going out from the Father, being worshipped and glorified with the Father and the Son, spoke through the prophets."[^3]
4. Even this fuller statement became the seed of later division: the *filioque* question — whether the Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or "from both the Father and the Son" — helped split Greek Orthodox and Roman Catholic Christians by AD 1054.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Since the Council of Nicaea was assembled primarily in response to Arius's view of God the Father and Jesus Christ, the Creed included little about the Holy Spirit." [^1]

> 325 Creed: "And in the Holy Spirit." (the entire clause) [^2]

## LDS Connection
That the Holy Spirit's status required a *second* ecumenical council 56 years after Nicaea undercuts the claim that the fully-formed Trinity was the apostolic faith. The Godhead's third member was a doctrinal afterthought at 325 and a cause of schism by 1054 — supporting the LDS view that creedal Trinitarianism is a gradually constructed, contested development.
