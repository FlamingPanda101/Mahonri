# Canonization Forced the Harmonization That Produced the Trinity

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the Trinity is a downstream effect of **canonization**. As long as the texts now in the New Testament circulated as independent documents read by independent communities, each could "let it work whatever way resonated with them" — there was no pressure to reconcile Mark's humanlike Jesus, Paul's letters, Matthew's Judaizing, and John's high Christology.[^1] Only when these texts were bound into "one book" governed by "a social institution that has authority over all the people associated with this one book" did harmonization become mandatory, and Greek philosophy was recruited as "the most efficient and effective means" of making all the texts "point in the same direction."[^2] The Trinity, therefore, "is the result of that systematization."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The NT genuinely contains diverse Christologies — rate: strong
McClellan's premise — Markan, Matthean, Lukan, Pauline, and Johannine communities held distinguishable views — is mainstream NT scholarship. The Synoptic vs. Johannine difference in Christology, the "messianic secret" in Mark, Matthew's Torah-positive emphasis, and John's pre-existent Logos are standard textbook distinctions.[^4] *Rating: strong.* See [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]], [[John Gave Christianity Its High Christology and Pre-existence]].

### 2. Harmonization is the predictable demand of a single authoritative corpus — rate: persuasive
McClellan's mechanism is sociological: once an institution treats a plural collection as one normative book, internal tension becomes a problem to be solved rather than a feature to be tolerated. "Suddenly they're not going to allow them to operate in their own little corners... We need unity."[^5] *Rating: persuasive; coheres with the documented apologetic and creedal project of the 2nd-4th centuries.* **Steelman:** an orthodox respondent would say the unity was *discovered*, not *imposed* — the church recognized as canonical those texts it judged apostolic and mutually coherent, and the creeds articulate a unity that was really there. On this view harmonization is legitimate development (cf. Newman) and the diversity is complementary, not contradictory. McClellan's reply (elsewhere) is that harmonization "defends a dogma, not a conclusion" (see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]).

### 3. The counterfactual is internally consistent — rate: moderate
"Prior to the unifying framework, when the texts were written, there was no Trinity because no one was forced to reduce it to a single conceptual framework."[^3] This is a claim about *origins*: the doctrine could not have existed in the minds of the authors because the harmonizing pressure that generates it did not yet exist. *Rating: moderate-to-strong; it is the strongest form of the "John had no Trinity" argument because it supplies a causal story for why, not merely an assertion that, the doctrine is absent in the sources.* See [[Trinity Did Not Exist When John Was Written]].

## Best Quotes
> "Prior to the unifying framework, when the texts were written, there was no Trinity because no one was forced to try to reduce it to a single conceptual framework. Everyone was allowed to let it work whatever way resonated with them."[^3]

> "When you bring them all together and you say this is now one book... there's a social institution that has authority over all the people who are associated with this one book. Suddenly they're not going to allow them to function in their own little corners. We need unity."[^5]

> "Greek philosophical ideas are going to be the most efficient and effective means of doing this. Let's come up with Greek philosophical ideas that are going to allow us to say all the texts fit together... and don't ask how it works because we're just going to exile you."[^2]
