# Genesis 3, Isaiah 14, and Ezekiel 28 Are Not About Satan

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the three texts most often read as describing Satan or Satan's fall — the Eden serpent ([[Genesis 3.14-19|Genesis 3]]), the "morning star" of [[Isaiah 14.12-15|Isaiah 14]], and the cherub of [[Ezekiel 28]] — are "not really talking about divine figures at all." None would be identified with Satan until the turn of the era at the earliest; in McClellan's view, that identification is an early *Christian* innovation post-dating the New Testament.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Genesis 3 is a snake etiology (rated: strong).** "The Garden of Eden is just talking about a snake. It's kind of like Aesop's Fables. This is just a snake as representative of the species." The story explains why snakes have no legs and why people fear them — "This is not Satan." The serpent is identified with Satan only in much later texts (Wisdom 2:24; Revelation 12:9).[^1]

2. **Isaiah 14 taunts the king of Babylon (rated: strong).** It is "sarcastically referring to him as if he were this divine figure... because of his hubris" — the "morning star / Day Star" (Latin *Lucifer*) is a mocking comparison applied to a *human* king, not a name for a fallen angel.[^1]

3. **Ezekiel 28 is about the king of Tyre (rated: strong).** It uses "a very different version of a story of the Garden of Eden to represent that human king of Tyre as the first human, the prototypical human." Again a human ruler, not Satan.[^2] (Cf. the scriptural notes [[Isaiah 14.12-15|Isaiah 14:12-15]] and [[Ezekiel 28]].)

4. **The figure develops later (rated: strong, scholarly).** "The character was not created until Greco-Roman period Judaism," primarily in the Enochic literature and "firmed up in the New Testament." None of Genesis 3, Isaiah 14, or Ezekiel 28 would be identified with Satan "until the end of the first century BCE and the beginning of the first century CE at the very earliest." See [[Satan - Development of the Figure]] and [[Book of Enoch - Origin of the Satan Figure]].[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The conservative reply points to later canonical readings as inspired interpretation: Revelation 12:9 explicitly calls the Eden serpent "that ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan," and Jesus' "I saw Satan fall like lightning" (Luke 10:18) and "your father the devil... a murderer from the beginning" (John 8:44, often linked to Eden) suggest the New Testament already reads these texts christologically. On this view the OT *foreshadows* a figure later revelation discloses. McClellan's counter (developed in [[Satan - Development of the Figure]]): those identifications are *later* and themselves draw on the Second Temple development; the Hebrew texts on their own terms are about a snake and two human kings.

## Apologetic Engagement
This dismantles the standard prooftext chain (Eden serpent → Isaiah's Lucifer → Ezekiel's cherub) used to narrate "Satan's fall" from the Old Testament. If each passage is about a snake or a human king, the fall-of-Satan narrative cannot be built from them; it must be sourced from Second Temple literature (1 Enoch) and the New Testament. Apologists who cite Isaiah 14 as "the fall of Lucifer/Satan" are reading a later figure into a Babylonian taunt.

## LDS Relevance
The stakes are real for Latter-day Saints, since the [[Pearl of Great Price]] reads a premortal Satanic rebellion into the Eden/Isaiah material (Moses 4; cf. D&C 76; 2 Nephi 2 cites Isaiah's "Lucifer"). (a) Latter-day Saints can accept McClellan's *historical-critical* point — that the Hebrew texts, on their own, are not about Satan — while (b) holding that Restoration revelation *discloses* the premortal reality those texts only gesture at, i.e., reading them as more-than-they-say by inspiration rather than by exegesis. McClellan, non-confessional, treats the developed Satan as invention; the Restoration treats the development as the unveiling of a truth lost from the record (see [[The Old Testament Barely Mentions Satan]]).
