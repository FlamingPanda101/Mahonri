# Autotheos Collides With Eternal Generation

## The Point
In the debate [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]] said that the Son "in his essence is not generated by anything outside of his own essence" — i.e., the Son is ***autotheos***, "of himself God," depending on no other person for his existence. The episode argues this quietly contradicts the very Nicene grammar Avery had just praised. In first-millennium Trinitarian theology the **Father generates the Son** (eternal generation), so the Son's *substance/nature depends upon the Father*; the Nicene "we believe in one God the Father" reserves *ho theos* for the Father alone (the **monarchy of the Father**). Make the Son *autotheos* and you no longer have one source-God with a begotten Son — you have "two gods separately existing," which is a heresy by Eastern Orthodox lights. So pulling toward *autotheos* to avoid sounding like **subordinationism** simply lands on a different error: a second self-existent deity.

## Evidence
1. **Avery's claim:** "Jesus in his essence is not generated by anything outside of his own essence. So that is how he is self-existent" — glossed by the guest as "the Son is autotheos... he is of himself God."[^1]
2. **The contradiction:** "the father in good first-thousand-years Trinitarian theology... generates the son. And so the son actually depends — his substance, his nature, depends upon the father."[^2]
3. **The monarchy of the Father:** "when the Nicene Creed says 'we believe in one God the Father,' ho theos there is referring to the father alone... so you can't say that the son is autotheos, because then you have two gods separately existing."[^3]
4. **It is contested even in the West:** the speakers note the term traces to [[Augustine]] and that "Western Christians do see the son as autotheos as well," so it is "a heresy according to Eastern Orthodoxy" but tolerated by some Latin theology — illustrating that the traditions cannot even agree on whether *autotheos* is orthodox.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "What he's saying is that the Son is autotheos, which is a fancy term to say that he is of himself God."[^1]

> "The son actually depends — his substance, his nature, depends upon the father... you can't say that the son is autotheos, because then you have two gods separately existing."[^3]

## LDS Connection
The Latter-day Saint reads this tangle as a feature of trying to have it both ways: the Father must be the sole fount of deity (monarchy) yet the Son must be in no way *less* (anti-subordinationism), so the Son ends up both eternally generated by the Father *and* self-existent — pick either horn and a heresy waits. The LDS Godhead dissolves the dilemma by accepting what the Nicene phrasing actually encodes — the **monarchy of the Father** — and pairing it with *distinct persons*: the Son is genuinely begotten of and subordinate to the Father in order, without that subordination diminishing his divinity (see [[Begotten Implies Source and Origin]], [[Born of the Father Before All Ages Affirms Christs Preexistence and Subordination]], [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]], [[I Believe in One God the Father Almighty Encodes the Monarchy of the Father]]).
