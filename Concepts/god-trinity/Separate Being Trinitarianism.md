# Separate Being Trinitarianism

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Grant Underwood]] coins **"separate being trinitarianism"** as an irenic self-description for the LDS Godhead. The strategy is two-sided: *keep* the word "trinitarianism" to "signal camaraderie" with creedal Christians, and *qualify* it with "separate being" to mark the one genuine distinctive. Most historic creeds describe the Trinity, on the *being* side, as "three persons in one being"; Underwood affirms the three persons and their oneness "in mercy and love and justice, in knowledge and everything" while relocating the difference to ontology — those three "incredible beings of love, justice, mercy, knowledge, all the omni characteristics… are all there in these three separate glorified beings." [^1]

The coinage is built directly on a [[Jeffrey R. Holland]] passage that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost "are one in every conceivable way except physically united," which Underwood says "inspired me… to kind of go further." [^2] His pastoral aim: defuse a reflexive LDS "anti-trinitarianism" rooted in *not understanding* trinitarianism, so the term becomes "to be understood," not "disliked." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **It is a labeling/rhetorical move, not a new metaphysic.** Underwood is explicit that he is choosing vocabulary to maximize shared ground: "I'm signaling camaraderie by continuing to use the word trinitarianism, and yet acknowledging distinctiveness by saying… we believe in separate being trinitarianism." [^1] This is the same concede-the-label-keep-the-substance manoeuvre catalogued in [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]] — here applied to "trinitarian" rather than "Christian."
2. **It rides on an apostolic source, not just philosophy.** Grounding the phrase in [[Jeffrey R. Holland]]'s "one in every way except physically united" gives it confessional weight: the *only* subtraction from creedal oneness is physical/bodily union, which dovetails with the embodiment distinctive (D&C 130) and [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]].
3. **It is narrower and more conciliatory than the vault's other "three beings" arguments.** Where [[Three Persons Sharing a Nature Are Still Three Beings]] and [[Most Christians Describe Three Beings Not the Trinity]] press "three beings" *against* the Trinity as a defeater, Underwood keeps the trinitarian label and treats the Godhead as a *species* of trinitarianism — closest to the "separate centers of consciousness… separate beings" social-trinitarian self-identification in [[Social and Subordinate Trinitarianism Is the Early Church View]] and the relational-oneness reading in [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]].

## Best Quotes
> "I'm trying to say, my friends, in reality we don't have to despise or be put off by trinitarianism, because so much of what they're saying about the Father, Son, and the Holy Ghost, we absolutely embrace and share. It's just we have a different kind of sense of their being." [^1]

> "Elder Holland… says they are one in every conceivable way except physically united. And that inspired me… to then kind of use a phrase: separate being trinitarianism." [^2]

> "Over the centuries most formulations of trinitarianism have described it… in terms of the being part of it as three persons in one being. And so I'm saying, okay, all that you say about the three persons, most of it we're right on board with — but we believe in separate beings." [^1]

## Steelman the Critic
A creedal trinitarian would object that "separate being trinitarianism" is a contradiction in terms, not a variety of the doctrine. The Trinity is *defined* by numerical unity of being (one *ousia* in three *hypostases*); to posit three *beings* is precisely tritheism, the position every ecumenical creed was written to exclude. On this view Underwood has the Godhead and is merely borrowing a prestige label — the same charge the co-host levels at social trinitarians in [[Social and Subordinate Trinitarianism Is the Early Church View]] ("you don't want to be kicked out of the Trinitarian club"). The reply that the persons are "one in every way except physically united" understates the gap: classical Trinitarianism denies *three* omniscient/omnipotent centers of being, so the disagreement is not merely about bodies but about how many divine beings exist. A hardline Latter-day Saint might press the opposite worry — that wanting the trinitarian label at all concedes too much, since the Restoration's claim is that the Godhead is *not* the creedal Trinity.
