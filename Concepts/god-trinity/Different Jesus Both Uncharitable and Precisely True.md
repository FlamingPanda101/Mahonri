# Different Jesus Both Uncharitable and Precisely True

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] frames the "do Mormons worship a different Jesus?" question as a paradox: the claim that Latter-day Saints "worship a completely different Jesus other than traditional Christianity is both rationally uncharitable and yet precisely true at the same time." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Why it's "uncharitable" — the shared data.** Beshears lists attributes both traditions affirm: Jesus "born of the Virgin Mary, had disciples, performed miracles, suffered under Pontius Pilate," then adds the kicker: "I got all of that information from the Book of Mormon." [^2] *Rating: a strong charity-of-reading point* — the bare label "different Jesus" ignores massive overlap and the Book of Mormon's own high Christology.

2. **Why it's "precisely true" — the particulars diverge.** "The particulars of christology in Mormonism [are] very different... the materiality of him, was he ungenerated and always the Son of God, did he become God's Son somehow?" [^3] *Rating: identifies the real fault line* — ontology (generated vs. eternal; material vs. immaterial), not biography.

3. **Mormonism "both is and is not."** Beshears generalizes a frustration of the outsider studying Mormonism: "very often Mormonism both is and is not the thing that you're looking at." [^4] *Rating: a genuinely useful interpretive caution;* surface vocabulary matches while underlying metaphysics differs (cf. [[Worshiping the Right Jesus - Created vs Uncreated]]).

4. **These threads are "critical to our faiths."** The christological questions "pull at theological threads that are critical to our faiths." [^3] *Rating: locates the disagreement at the foundational, not peripheral, level,* consistent with [[Are Mormons Christian (Beshears)]].

**Steelman of the LDS view.** A Latter-day Saint would stress the "uncharitable" half: the same Jesus of Nazareth is worshipped — virgin-born, atoning, resurrected, the only path to the Father — and that disputing the *metaphysics* of his generation is not the same as worshipping a different person. Beshears grants exactly this with the "uncharitable" verdict, while insisting the ontological particulars are too foundational to wave away. The paradox is deliberately left standing.

## Best Quotes

> "The materiality of him, was he ungenerated and always the Son of God, did he become God's Son somehow? Those are very very important conversations to have because they pull at theological threads that are critical to our faiths." [^3]
