# Modalism, Monarchianism, and Sabellianism

## The Point
Modalism is the early view that God is *one person* who appears in different "modes" — sometimes as the Father, sometimes the Son, sometimes the Spirit — rather than three distinct persons. It went by three names ([[Praxeas]]'s modalism, monarchianism, and [[Sabellius]]'s Sabellianism) and was rejected as heresy. It matters because (a) it shows the threeness/oneness of God was genuinely contested c. 200, and (b) modalism is the heresy that *Trinitarians themselves* most often slide into when they over-stress God's oneness — including, the author notes, even after Nicaea.

## Evidence
1. **Modalism (Praxeas, c. 200):** God the Father, Jesus, and the Spirit "are one person who is manifest at different times in different modes." Other names follow.[^1]
2. **Monarchianism:** so-called because some modalists held God's "monarchy" — God as sole ruler — "depended on the existence of only one person called God."[^2]
3. **Sabellianism ([[Sabellius]], c. 220):** argued "against any distinction between the persons of God the Father and his Son," even calling God the "Son-Father."[^3]
4. [[Tertullian]] refuted Praxeas by coining the Trinity and mocking modalism: scripture never says "I am my own Son, today have I begotten myself."[^4]
5. **It persisted even after Nicaea:** a 4th-century mosaic in the Catacomb of Domitilla bears the inscription "You who are called the Son are found to be also the Father," which "appears to represent modalistic ideas … still in circulation even after the Council of Nicaea."[^5]
6. After Nicaea, Marcellus of Ancyra "interpreted the *homoousios* language of Nicaea in the direction of modalism" — showing the new creedal term could itself be read modalistically.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> Praxeas "believed that God the Father, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost are one person who is manifest at different times in different modes: in the mode of the Father or of the Son or of the Spirit." [^1]

> An inscription bordering a post-Nicene mosaic reads, "You who are called the Son are found to be also the Father." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints reject modalism outright — the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three distinct beings/persons (Article of Faith 1; cf. the First Vision). Apologetically, this concept is double-edged: critics sometimes mistake LDS belief for modalism's opposite (tritheism), while many ordinary Trinitarians actually *describe* a modalist or three-beings God when pressed — see [[Most Christians Describe Three Beings Not the Trinity]] and [[Trinity Metaphors Are All Heresies]].
