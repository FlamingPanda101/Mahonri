# Psalm 82 Is About the Divine Council, Not Human Judges

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
In Psalm 82 ([[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]) "God addresses Israel's judges... human rulers who were supposed to represent God's authority," and "calls them gods... because they'd been entrusted with his word and his authority." The corrupt human judges ruled unjustly, so God warns they "would still die like men."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The *elohim* of Psalm 82 are the gods of the nations (strong; cites McClellan's own JBL article)
**[[Dan McClellan]]**: "That's absolutely not what's going on in Psalm 82. Psalm 82 is a reference to the other gods of the Israelite pantheon who are functioning as patron deities over the other nations of the earth." [^1] He grounds this in his peer-reviewed article, "The Gods' Complaint: Psalm 82 as a Psalm of Complaint," *Journal of Biblical Literature* (2018) — "freely available through my link tree." [^2] (This raises the evidence tier toward A for this specific claim, since it rests on a peer-reviewed primary publication.)

### 2. The plot is a divine-council deposition, not a warning to judges (strong)
McClellan reconstructs the scene: "The God of Israel is standing up in the divine council and accusing them of failing to uphold their duties as patron deities to uphold cosmic and social justice and so condemns them to mortality. In effect deposing them from their positions on the divine council." [^3] The crime is "showing preferential treatment and... allowing the Babylonian exile to occur" — i.e. cosmic/national injustice by patron deities, not municipal corruption by human magistrates. [^4]

### 3. The climax demands the divine-council reading (strong)
The psalm's last verse only makes sense if the deposed parties are the nations' gods: with their council seats vacated, "the psalmist stands up in the last verse and tells the God of Israel to rule the whole world for you will inherit all nations." [^5] This connects directly to [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8-9]], where the nations were apportioned to divine sons while "Israel is the inheritance of Adonai." Now that "the other nations have no patron deities, Adonai can inherit all nations" — a post-exilic move that extends Yahweh's worship across the diaspora. [^6]

### 4. Steelman of the human-judges view
The "human judges" reading (held by, e.g., many older commentators and some Jewish tradition) draws on the fact that Hebrew *elohim* can take human referents in rendering like [[Exodus 21.6|Exodus 21:6]], and on the moral language ("judge unjustly," "die like men") that sounds apt for officials. McClellan's reply: that reading is a *post-biblical renegotiation* (see [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]]), and "die like men" is precisely the point if the addressees are *gods* being stripped of immortality — it is a *demotion*, not a banal reminder that humans are mortal.
