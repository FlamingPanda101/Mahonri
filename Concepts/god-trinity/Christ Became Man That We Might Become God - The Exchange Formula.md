# Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula

## The Point
Daniel Becerra documents that **deification** (Greek *theosis*, *theopoiesis*, *apotheosis*) was a real and developing teaching across the first Christian centuries, rooted in the **Incarnation**. Its compact slogan is the "exchange formula": Irenaeus's "the Son of God became what we are in order to make us like himself," restated by Athanasius as the Word "was incarnate that we might be made god." [^1] Justin Martyr (d. before AD 167) was "the first time such language appears so explicitly," teaching from Psalm 82:6 that "all humans are capable of progressing toward godhood." [^2] This is the patristic spine of the LDS appeal to ancient deification.

## Evidence
1. **Justin Martyr — humans can "become a god."** Building on Psalm 82:6 ("Ye are gods; and all of you are children of the most High"), Justin held godhood "is only fully realized in the resurrection," made possible because humans possess the *logos spermatikos*, "the seed of divinity present in human beings." [^2]
2. **Irenaeus — the exchange formula.** The Son "became what we are in order to make us like himself." Deifying union occurs as persons are "adopted" by Christ (Romans 8), "initiated at baptism and maintained by receiving the Holy Spirit, participating in the sacraments... and living a moral life." [^3]
3. **Athanasius — full divinity required to deify.** The Word "was incarnate that we might be made god" (Greek *theopoieō*, "to be made divine/a god"). Against Arius, Athanasius reasoned "if the Son is not fully divine, then he cannot fully save humans; full divinity is necessary to enable others to become divine." [^4]
4. **Gregory of Nazianzus — full humanity required too.** "What has not been assumed has not been healed": Christ had to assume a full human nature and body to save them. He was "the first Christian author to use the term *theosis*." The Council of Chalcedon (AD 451) later declared Christ "perfect in divinity and humanity." [^5]
5. **Scriptural seedbed.** Becerra lists Genesis 1:26-27; 3:5; Psalm 82:6; John 10:34-36; Matthew 5:48; Romans 8:14-17; 2 Corinthians 3:18; 1 John 3:1-2; and 2 Peter 1:4 ("participants of the divine nature") as the verses on which deification was built. [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The Son of God became what we are in order to make us like himself." — Irenaeus [^1]

> "The Word of God was incarnate [that is, made flesh] that we might be made god." — Athanasius [^1]

> "What has not been assumed has not been healed." — Gregory of Nazianzus [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the central patristic evidence Latter-day Saints cite for the antiquity of deification. The "exchange formula" parallels [[The Pleroma Exchange Formula]] (Ostler's NT version) and undergirds [[Human Deification in Mormonism]]. But Becerra is careful: see [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]] — the fathers held God and humans to be different *kinds*, so patristic theosis is participation, not the LDS materialist same-species ontology. The honest apologetic concedes the difference while claiming the broad theosis tradition. Compare the *foil* on this point: [[Dan McClellan]] argues the 4th–5th-century version of the formula is *diluted* from the more radical 1st-century one — so the fathers should not be used as a Mormon proof text (see [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]]).
