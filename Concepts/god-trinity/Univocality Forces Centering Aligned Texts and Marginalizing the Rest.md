#type/claim

# Univocality Forces Centering Aligned Texts and Marginalizing the Rest

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] identifies the concrete **two-step editorial procedure** that the assumption of biblical univocality forces on a reader. It is not merely that harmonizing "begs the question" in the abstract (that is [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]]); it is that, *once a doctrine such as the Trinity has been chosen*, the interpreter must operationally (1) **center and give priority** to the passages that already align with the dogma, and (2) **reinterpret, marginalize, or ignore** every passage that does not. McClellan presents this as the actual mechanism by which the Trinity "becomes unavoidable" — unavoidability is manufactured by this procedure, not found in the text. He names it repeatedly as the through-line of the video, applying it to Colossians 1:15's "firstborn of all creation," John 17's oneness, and the subordinationist "neither I nor the angels… but only the Father" sayings.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It is stated as a *requirement of univocality*, not a tendency.** "This is a requirement of univocality: once you have made a decision about a doctrine, you then have to go through all the passages that disagree with that decision… and you have to reinterpret them, and you have to marginalize them, or you have to ignore them."[^1] *Rating: strong* — McClellan locates the move at the level of method, making it falsifiable by anyone who can show the priority-ranking is driven by the data rather than by the prior dogma.

2. **The two steps are named explicitly and together.** "This is the unifying framework that one imposes on the text, that centers and gives priority to the passages that already align with the new dogma, and then… you have to reinterpret, marginalize, or ignore everything that does not align with that dogma."[^2] *Rating: strong* — the "center the aligned / marginalize the disaligned" pairing is the distinctive content; it converts the generic charge of question-begging into a specific, observable editorial operation.

3. **Worked case — Colossians 1:15.** "Firstborn of all creation" plainly implies Jesus *was created*; "but once we had the dogma of the Trinity develop, and Jesus had to be co-eternal and co-equal with God, you had to reinterpret that stuff away."[^3] *Rating: strong illustration* — the reinterpretation is admitted to be *downstream of* the dogma's requirements, not of the clause's grammar.

4. **Worked case — John 17 vs John 10:30.** Trinitarians "take the passage that agrees with you and center it and give it priority, and then ignore or reinterpret the passages that disagree." When the *same author* uses "oneness" for believers in the intercessory prayer, defenders say it "obviously" means a different oneness — which "is just you using that interpretive lens of the Trinity and being forced to reinterpret something that very clearly is intended to mean the exact same thing."[^4] *Rating: strong* — a single-author control case where the marginalizing move is visible. (The believers-folded-into-substance reductio itself is catalogued at [[John 10-30 - I and the Father Are One Rebutted]].)

5. **Worked case — subordination texts.** The passages "where Jesus explicitly says that he's less than the Father, that he's not equal to the Father… those have to be reinterpreted because we have chosen to impose this interpretive lens of the Trinity which was developed after the Bible had been completed."[^5] *Rating: strong* — ties the procedure to the explicit subordinationism of the NT (cf. [[Subordination Within the New Testament]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A confessional reader will answer that *all* synthesis prioritizes some texts as clearer and reads obscurer ones in their light — the "analogy of faith" / *scriptura sui ipsius interpres* is a mainstream hermeneutic, not a Trinitarian trick, and historians routinely read ambiguous passages against clearer ones in the same corpus. The reply is that the principle is only legitimate when the priority-ranking is itself *earned from the evidence* rather than fixed in advance by a 4th-century dogma; McClellan's charge is precisely that here the ranking is set by the conclusion, so contrary data can never count against it — which is what makes it non-negotiable harmonization rather than interpretation (cf. [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]], [[Putting Dogma Over Data]]).

## LDS Relevance

The same procedure underwrites the standard creedal reading of the "problem" passages Latter-day Saints cite for a distinct, subordinate Son — [[John 14.28|John 14:28]] ("the Father is greater than I"), [[Philippians 2.5-8|Philippians 2]], the "only the Father knows the hour" saying. On McClellan's analysis these are not genuinely *reconciled* with the Trinity; they are *marginalized* by it. That gives the Latter-day Saint a methodological rather than merely proof-textual rejoinder: the Godhead reading lets the diverse and subordinationist texts mean what they say, whereas the Trinity must center a handful of late, ambiguous texts (Titus 2:13; 2 Peter 1:1; John 1:1) and demote the rest. See [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]] and [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]].
