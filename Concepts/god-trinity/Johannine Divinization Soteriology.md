# Johannine Divinization Soteriology

## The Claim
The Gospel of John has a **divinization (theosis) soteriology**: the followers of Jesus are made children of God and are drawn into the same mutual indwelling that unites Jesus and the Father — they are *divinized*, not absorbed into the divine essence as new persons of the Trinity.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The disciples are folded into the indwelling (strong)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** reads [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] as extending the Father–Son mutual indwelling to believers: "may they also be in us... I in them and you in me, that they may become completely one." His summary: "They're all going to be divinized. They're not going to become another co-eternal, co-equal, consubstantial person of the single essence or substance of the God of Israel. They're going to participate in this mutual indwelling and be divinized." [^1]

### 2. It coheres with John's "children of God" language (strong)
"This also makes much better sense of John's soteriology where the followers of Jesus are made children of God. The followers of Jesus will indwell Jesus and God and have them indwell them just like Jesus indwells God and God indwells Jesus rendering them one." [^2] The believer's status mirrors Jesus' own relationship to the Father by participation, not by ontological identity.

### 3. It is reinforced by the Psalm 82 reading (moderate)
McClellan links this soteriology to his preferred reading of [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]] as Israel divinized at Sinai by receiving the word of God (see [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]]): "The word of God is what renders things divine. And that word is standing before you." [^3] Believers are divinized by union with the Logos who divinizes — the same pattern as Sinai.

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Trinitarian and Reformed readers would say John's "oneness" of believers is *covenantal union* and *adoption*, carefully distinguished from the unique consubstantial unity of Father and Son, and that "divinization" overreads metaphors of indwelling. McClellan's reply is that the *same* indwelling vocabulary is used for both relationships, so the text positively invites the participatory/divinization reading rather than excluding it. This overlaps with LDS theosis claims — see [[Human Deification in Mormonism]] — though McClellan argues from the historical-critical method, not a confessional stance.
