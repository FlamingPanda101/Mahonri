# Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible

## The Claim

The earliest strata of the Hebrew Bible present God as an **anthropomorphic, corporeal, male-presenting, and visible** entity — a God with a body that can be seen, touched, and physically interacted with. This is the overwhelming scholarly consensus, supported by textual, archaeological, comparative ancient Near Eastern, and cognitive-scientific evidence. The conception of God as an invisible, immaterial, "without body, parts, or passions" essence is a LATER development, arising under the influence of Greek philosophy in the Hellenistic period and solidified in early Christian and rabbinic theology.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. God "Walks Around" in Physical Form

McClellan describes the trajectory: "You go from God is a human-shaped male-presenting corporeal entity who walks around and enjoys a succulent Chinese meal with Abraham and with others." [^1] Genesis 18 depicts Yahweh eating with Abraham in embodied form. Genesis 3:8 has God "walking in the garden in the cool of the day." These are not metaphorical — in their ancient Israelite context, they reflect a genuine belief in a physically present deity.

### 2. God Has a Face, Back, Hands, and Feet

[[Exodus 33.20-23|Exodus 33:20-23]] is the key passage. Moses asks to see God's *kavod* (glory, but literally "body" or "heaviness"). God covers Moses with His hand, passes by, and allows Moses to see His "back parts" (*achor*) but not His face. This passage ONLY makes sense if God has a physical form with spatial orientation — a face in front, a back behind, and a hand capable of covering a human face. "The thing that you cannot see is God's face because that is where all the glory comes from." [^2]

### 3. The "Face to Face" Encounters

Multiple figures claim to have seen God's face directly:
- **Jacob** in [[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]]: "I have seen God face to face, and my life is preserved"
- **Moses** in Exodus 33:11: "the LORD spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend"

These are not visions of a disembodied light or an abstract essence. They are encounters with a being who has a FACE — the physical locus of presence and glory.

### 4. Later Editors Inserted Angels to Soften the Encounters

McClellan notes a tell-tale editorial pattern: "You have a number of other stories where later editors have come through and written *mal'ak* to make it seem like they were actually just talking with a messenger of Adonai or a messenger of God." [^3] The scribal tradition itself recognized the theological problem of a corporeal, visible God and tried to distance the encounters by inserting angelic intermediaries. The editorial layer proves the earlier, more direct conception.

### 5. The Scholarly Consensus Is Overwhelming

"The scholars who study this and write about this are in widespread agreement that God had a body in the Hebrew Bible that was visible and corporeal and could be touched. And then that changed because the authoritative knowledge changed." [^4]

Key scholarly works (all cited by McClellan):[^5]
- **Benjamin Sommer**, *The Bodies of God in the World of Ancient Israel* (2008) — "phenomenally influential"
- **Brittany Wilson**, *The Embodied God: Seeing the Divine in Luke-Acts and the Early Church* — "a bunch of wonderful work on the visibility of God in the New Testament"
- **Christoph Markschies**, *God's Body: Jewish, Christian, and Pagan Images of God* — "a very philosophical, very erudite discussion"
- **Esther Hamori**, *When Gods Were Men: The Embodied God in Biblical and Near Eastern Literature*
- **Andreas Wagner**, *God's Body: The Anthropomorphic God in the Old Testament* (English translation of German original)
- **Luke Irwin**, *Jesus and the Visibility of God: Sight and Belief in the Fourth Gospel*
- **Dan McClellan**, *Adonized Divine Images* (open-access academic monograph) and chapter in *The Bible Says* (trade book)

### 6. Ancient Near Eastern Context

The anthropomorphic conception of deity was not an Israelite anomaly. Ancient Near Eastern cultures across Mesopotamia, Canaan, and Egypt understood their gods as having bodies, being gendered, eating, drinking, walking, and physically interacting with the world. Israel's early theology participated in this broader cultural framework. The LATER movement toward an incorporeal, invisible God was the innovation — not the baseline.
