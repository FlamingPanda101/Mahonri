# Jesus Claimed Divinity in a Jewish Way Through Riddle and Allusion

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brant Pitre]] argues that the apparent **paradox** of New Testament scholarship — a "low" historical-Jesus Christology paired with an explosively early "high" Christology in the first (all-Jewish) Christians — is best solved by a *historical connection*: Jesus himself made divine claims during his lifetime, but **in a Jewish idiom**. He did not say "I am the second person of the Trinity" (an anachronism); he revealed his identity the way an apocalyptic Jewish teacher would — through riddles, *meshalim* (parables), and allusions to loaded scriptures (the *Shema*, the Decalogue, Exodus 3, Isaiah 43, Daniel 7, Psalm 110). The "smoke" of early divine Christology rises from "the fire" of Jesus's own divine messianism.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The paradox is real (strong)
Since the 18th–19th centuries (Reimarus, Strauss, Schweitzer), historical-Jesus research has been *defined* by the axiom that the historical Jesus made no divine self-claims — the 4,000-page *Handbook for the Study of the Historical Jesus* has no article on the question. Yet the "early high Christology club" (Hurtado, Bauckham, Fredriksen) shows the earliest Christians treated Jesus as divine almost immediately: [[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Corinthians 8:6]] folds "one Lord Jesus Christ, through whom all things exist" into the *Shema* itself.[^1] E.P. Sanders (after Joseph Klausner): *ex nihilo nihil fit* — nothing comes from nothing.

### 2. The identity is deliberately a mystery (strong)
Even John's Gospel has Jesus's contemporaries beg, "If you are the Messiah, tell us plainly." William Wrede's "messianic secret," the riddling questions, and Jesus's "flesh and blood has not revealed this to you... but my Father in heaven" ([[Matthew 16.16|Matthew 16:17]], *apokalypto*) all show a self-disclosure that is **apocalyptic** — an unveiling of a heavenly mystery — not a flat, univocal "I am God with a subject and predicate."[^2]

### 3. It is a *cumulative* case decided by context (medium)
Pitre stresses that no single passage settles it; "divine in what sense?" varies passage to passage, and the meaning of any allusion (e.g. *ego eimi*) is fixed by its scriptural backdrop and the audience's reaction. The argument is impressionistic and converging, not a single proof.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
O'Connor's charitable counter (which Pitre grants is "plausible" and held by "serious scholars"): a reader can walk away from the Synoptics *without* concluding Jesus claimed deity, precisely because the claims are oblique. Many "divine" acts are elsewhere *delegated* to disciples (forgiving sins, judging, even walking on water — Peter), suggesting a theosis-shaped *low* Christology in which Jesus is the firstborn exemplar, not a unique divine being. On this reading the riddles are genuinely ambiguous, and "Jewish-way" divinity is unfalsifiable — any non-claim can be re-described as a coded claim.

## Counter-Objection
Pitre's reply: the cumulative pattern resists the deflationary reading because *some* acts are conspicuously **not** delegated — Jesus takes the divine name in a theophanic setting (Mark 6) and demands a super-parental love (Matthew 10) that Second Temple Jews reserved for God alone. And the method is not special pleading: it is the ordinary historical task of reading first-century allusions in their first-century context, the same way one reads Caligula's bridge-crossing or Antiochus's self-divinization.

## Apologetic Engagement
For the Latter-day Saint this "Jewish-way, allusion-driven" method is a powerful ally *against creedal over-reading* and *for* a Restoration Christology. It concedes the central Restoration point that the Nicene formula is anachronistic to Jesus's own lips, and it relocates the evidence for Jesus's divinity in **Second Temple categories** (a heavenly, enthroned, name-bearing Messiah) rather than in *homoousios*. The danger to watch: Pitre's Catholic framing ultimately routes these allusions toward Chalcedon ("a both/and... divine and human"), whereas the same data fit a distinct, exalted divine Son who bears the Father's name and shares his throne without being numerically identical to him.

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration already reads Jesus's divinity through the [[Son of Man - Development of the Title|Enochic Son of Man]] and divine-investiture lens: Jesus is divine as the bearer of the Father's name and authority (see [[John 8-58 - Before Abraham Was I Am (Divine Name Agency)]], [[Divine Name Theology]]), enthroned at the right hand ([[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110:1]]; [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7]]). Pitre's "divine messianism" — heavenly, pre-existent, name-bearing, but distinct from the Father — maps onto [[The Enochic Son of Man Matches LDS Christology Not Nicene Homoousios]] far more naturally than onto consubstantiality.
