# Babylon Taunt Formula Shows Negation Is a Boast of Superiority

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Trinitarian/unitarian objection: Isaiah's negation texts ("I am Yahweh and besides me there is no savior"; "I am the first and the last, besides me there is no God"; "I am God and there is none else") prove the Bible teaches that exactly one God exists. Latter-day Saints, by affirming "a multiplicity of gods that are separate beings," therefore directly contradict the Bible. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The exact formula was a stock ANE royal taunt (rating: strong; comparative-ANE)
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that "every single one of those statements could be taken directly and were taken directly out of the mouth of Xeroxes [Xerxes], the Persian king," who made the same boasts about being incomparable "even though he knew there were other kings." The formula is "really a taunt of superiority and... a challenge," not a metaphysical inventory of existing beings. [^2] See [[Cyrus the Great]] for the named-king context.

> "every single one of those statements could be taken directly and were taken directly out of the mouth of Xeroxes the Persian king who made all of those kinds of claims about other gods in the ancient near east even though he knew there were other kings it's really a taunt of superiority" [^2]

### 2. Reading them as Trinity-support is a category error twice over (rating: strong; logical)
Ostler notes the texts could not support the Trinity *even if* they taught one God: the speaker is **Yahweh, a single divine person**, and these are therefore *unitarian* statements; "the notion that Yahweh alone is God could not possibly be taken to support the notion that there are three divine persons in one substance." [^3]

### 3. The OT openly assumes a divine council (rating: strong; textual)
Deuteronomy 32, Psalm 82, and Genesis 1 ("Let us create — who is he talking to?") presuppose a council of gods, and the council members are called *bene elohim*, "sons of God." See [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8-9]], [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82:1]], [[Genesis 1.24-26|Genesis 1:24-26]]. [^4]

### 4. The term "monotheism" is anachronistic (rating: moderate; methodological)
Ostler says he "rarely uses the term monotheism because monotheism assumes a conceptual metaphysical framework that clearly was not on the horizon" for biblical writers; it derives from Platonism/middle-Platonism that later defined God in the creeds. See [[Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category]]. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The strongest Trinitarian reply (cf. [[Michael Heiser]], [[Larry Hurtado]]) is that whatever the rhetorical genre, Isaiah's accumulation of *creator-of-all* + *first-and-last* + *none-like-me* language functionally exalts Yahweh as ontologically supreme, not merely politically preeminent — and that "taunt" framing under-reads the cosmic scope of Deutero-Isaiah. Ostler concedes a diversity of biblical voices but insists that importing a single, *metaphysical* monotheism does "violence to the text" because the genre is incomparability-rhetoric, not ontology. See [[Heiser Species-Uniqueness Is an Anachronistic Metaphysical Distinction]].

## Best Quotes

> "First, the Bible doesn't teach monotheism. That's a modern category. It doesn't teach that there's only one God. In fact, it teaches there's a council of gods. And even if it taught one god, that would be bad news for the trinity." [^1]

> "this kind of a claim reading these scriptures in this way as if it supported the trinity is simply a bad faith reading of these scriptures" [^3]

> "I'm claiming I'm incomparable and if you contest that, you're committing treason. So that's actually how it functions... not a statement of ontological incomparability." [^6]
