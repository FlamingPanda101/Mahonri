# The Hypostatic Union Is a Greek Solution to a Greek Problem

## The Claim
The doctrine of the [[Hypostatic Union|hypostatic union]] — that the perfectly transcendent God the Son became incarnate in a human body — is not a *revealed mystery* but a **contradiction** generated by trying to force the embodied, risen Jesus into the Greek philosophical definition of God. The very thought-category of a *hypostasis* is Greek; "ancient Hebrews just did not have this concept." So the hypostatic union is "the Greek solution invented to try and fit Jesus into a philosophical box," and it stands or falls with the [[Consubstantiality|homoousios]] Trinity. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The conceptual vocabulary is Greek, not Hebrew.** *Hypostasis* and *homoousios* ("consubstantial / made of the same essence") are technical terms from Greek metaphysics; the Hebrew Bible has no such categories. *Rating: strong, uncontroversial — even orthodox historians grant the apparatus is Hellenistic.* [^1]
2. **The "mystery" is really a set of flat contradictions.** "You can't be embodied and immaterial. You can't be without passions but at the same time able to feel passions. You can't be without parts and at the same time be embodied." When pressed, the answer is "well, it's a mystery" — but these are not *biblical* mysteries; they "only arise due to the post-biblical mixing of philosophical and Christian principles." *Rating: this is the load-bearing philosophical claim; strong if the creedal attributes are taken in their strict classical sense, which the steelman disputes.* [^2]
3. **A revealed mystery vs. a manufactured one.** The narrator distinguishes mysteries we *find in scripture* (genuinely above reason) from contradictions *produced by a framework imposed on scripture* (against reason). The hypostatic union is the latter. *Rating: moderate-strong; a fair conceptual distinction.* [^2]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Classical theists respond that the union is **paradox, not contradiction**: the divine and human natures are united in one person *without confusion*, so the predicates ("impassible," "embodied") attach to different natures, not to the same nature in the same respect — dissolving the alleged contradiction. Chalcedon's "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation" is precisely a rule for *avoiding* contradiction. "Mystery" here means a truth exceeding comprehension, not a logical impossibility. See [[Chalcedon Hypostatic Union as Mystery Defense]].

### Counter-objection
The narrator (and LDS theology generally) replies that splitting the predicates across natures only relocates the problem: the *person* who is the subject of both is still asserted to be both simple/immaterial and embodied, and the classical attributes (simplicity, impassibility, timelessness) were themselves imported from Platonism rather than revealed — so the paradox is an artifact of the Greek premises, removable by rejecting them. Latter-day Saints instead define Godhood as a *fullness of divinity* in an embodied, dynamic person, dissolving the puzzle at its root. See [[The Creedal God Is the Platonic One Not the God of the Bible]].

## Best Quotes
> "The very thought category of a hypostasis is Greek. Ancient Hebrews just did not have this concept in their thought. So the hypostatic union is the Greek solution invented to try and fit Jesus into a philosophical box, and it goes hand in hand with the doctrine of the Trinity." [^1]

> "The trouble is it's not a mystery, it's a contradiction. You can't be embodied and immaterial. You can't be without passions but at the same time able to feel passions... And when you point out these contradictions, the answer is usually, well, it's a mystery. But the thing is, these aren't revealed mysteries that we find in the Bible." [^2]
