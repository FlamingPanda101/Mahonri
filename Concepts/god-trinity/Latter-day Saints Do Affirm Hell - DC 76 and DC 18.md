# Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell — D&C 76 and D&C 18

#type/claim

## The Claim
A common evangelical misconception is that Latter-day Saints **deny hell** — that LDS soteriology is so universalist (three degrees of glory for nearly everyone) that no one really suffers for unrepented sin. In this conversation [[Brother Oz (Austin)]] voices exactly that surprise: "I always misunderstood the Mormon conception... Mormons have said the opposite of what you just said about hell."[^1] [[Hayden Carroll]] corrects him directly from the **Doctrine and Covenants**: those who knowingly sin and refuse to repent or accept Christ **will go to hell** and must suffer for their own sins, since "there's only two people who can suffer for your sin — Jesus Christ... or self" (the framing of D&C 19). He cites **[[D&C 76]]** ("we have a doctrine that says you will go to hell if you don't accept Jesus Christ") and **D&C 18** ("if you do not repent, you must [suffer even as I]"), and pins the popular misimpression on under-trained "18-year-old missionaries who don't know," whose job "is to hand you a Book of Mormon... theology is not their strong suit."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Latter-day Saint (Hayden Carroll)
- **The texts are explicit.** D&C 19:15–18 has Christ command repentance "lest... your sufferings be sore," describing his own Gethsemane agony as the alternative the unrepentant must bear themselves. D&C 76 maps the perdition of the sons of perdition and the suffering of the telestial who must "suffer the wrath of God on earth" and in hell until the last resurrection. So the *standard works* plainly teach a real hell; the universalist caricature mistakes the **graded-glory** structure for the *absence* of punishment. See [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]].
- **Two senses of "hell."** LDS usage distinguishes (a) the temporary **spirit prison** where the unrepentant suffer until they pay or accept Christ's payment, from (b) **outer darkness**, the permanent state of the sons of perdition. The "Mormons don't believe in hell" line trades on collapsing these — Latter-day Saints *do* reject the single, eternal, fiery-torment-for-all model of popular Protestantism, but they affirm genuine, severe, sin-consequent suffering. This nuance is what Oz had absorbed only in its negative half.
- **The missionary-vs-doctrine point is fair and important.** Carroll's deflection ("you're talking about 18-year-old missionaries") is a legitimate methodological correction: door-step paraphrases by teenagers are not the LDS doctrinal standard. The canonical texts, not the simplified pitch, define the teaching — a principle that cuts both ways in interfaith dialogue (one should cite the other tradition's confessions, not its weakest lay representatives).

### Steelman of the Evangelical (Brother Oz)
- **The confusion is understandable, not invented.** Oz's surprise is honest and his evidence real: Latter-day Saints often *foreground* the near-universal reach of the three kingdoms (almost everyone inherits *some* glory), and lay members and missionaries frequently soften or omit the hell/spirit-prison material. So the evangelical impression that "Mormons don't really believe in hell" tracks a genuine difference in *emphasis*, even if it misreads the doctrine.
- **The substantive disagreement remains.** Granting that Latter-day Saints affirm a hell, the evangelical still presses two points: (1) LDS hell is largely **temporary and remedial** (spirit prison empties as people accept the gospel; only the rare sons of perdition are permanently lost), which is much nearer to purgatory/universalism than to the Protestant doctrine of eternal conscious torment for all the unredeemed — see [[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]]; and (2) the *escape route* (post-mortem repentance and vicarious ordinances) is precisely what Protestants reject. Earlier in the same exchange Oz had pushed a *harsher* (Calvinist) hell against Carroll, so "Mormons affirm hell" does not erase the real distance on its **duration, finality, and conditions**.

**Honest assessment.** Carroll is straightforwardly right on the narrow factual point: the LDS standard works teach a real hell, and citing missionaries as evidence that "Mormons deny hell" is a category error. But the episode also shows why the misconception persists — LDS soteriology *is* dramatically less retributive than Protestant eternal-torment doctrine, so an outsider hears the universalist melody and misses the minor key. The accurate summary is not "Mormons do/don't believe in hell" but "Mormons affirm a hell that is mostly **temporary, remedial, and escapable**, with a small permanent category" — a position closer to Eastern-Orthodox and purgatorial models than to either flat universalism or Calvinist double-predestination. The clarification is useful precisely because it forces the real debate (duration and finality of punishment) instead of a strawman.
