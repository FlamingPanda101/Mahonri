# Satan - Development of the Figure

## Overview

This is a hub note for the **historical development of the figure of Satan** — the claim that Satan as a cosmic rebel (a fallen angel who leads other angels against God) is not present in the Hebrew Bible but develops through Second Temple literature, especially the Enochic tradition. In the Hebrew Bible, *ha-satan* ("the adversary/accuser") is a *title* for a member of YHWH's divine council — a prosecuting attorney who requires divine permission (Job 1–2) and is rebuked by YHWH (Zechariah 3), not a name for a cosmic enemy. The narrative of a leader, a covenant of rebellion, corrupting forbidden knowledge, and judgment/imprisonment is supplied by the Watcher myth.

The flagship source is [[Book of Enoch - Origin of the Satan Figure]]: Asael/Shemihazah/Azazel — "a few different names" for one lead malevolent angel — provides the template that Revelation 12 repurposes ("the Great Dragon fell from Heaven like a star… swept a third of the stars with them"). The Watchers' dead spirits also become the demons of the Greco-Roman world, giving 1 Enoch an origin story for demonic forces generally.

### The Hebrew-Bible baseline (from [[Dan McClellan - Satan isnt in the Old Testament]])
The "before" state against which the development is measured — four atomic notes establishing that the Old Testament has *no* named Satan:
- [[Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary]] — bare *satan* = "adversary," used of humans and even the angel of Yahweh (Numbers 22:22).
- [[Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name]] — *ha-satan* in [[Job 1]] and [[Zechariah 3]] is a council prosecutor, not a name.
- [[Genesis 3 Isaiah 14 and Ezekiel 28 Are Not About Satan]] — the classic prooftexts are a snake and two human kings.
- [[1 Chronicles 21 Is the Only Possible Old Testament Satan]] — the single contested candidate (McClellan: generic; [[Esther Hamori]]: a personal name).

## The Steelman

The conservative reply points to earlier strata: the Genesis 3 serpent, the "morning star" of Isaiah 14, and Jesus' "I saw Satan fall like lightning" (Luke 10:18) as evidence the concept predates or is independent of Enoch. The cluster's rebuttals: Genesis 3 never names the serpent as Satan (that identification is later — Wisdom 2:24, Revelation 12:9); Isaiah 14 is explicitly a taunt against the *king of Babylon* using Canaanite myth; and Luke 10:18 postdates 1 Enoch and may itself draw on Watcher tradition. The development is real, even on a steelmanned reading.
