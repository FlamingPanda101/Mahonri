# Islam Denies the Deity and Divine Sonship of Christ

## The Claim
The substantive divide between Islam and the LDS (and broader Christian) message is christological. Islam affirms much about Jesus — the virgin birth, his miracles — but flatly denies that God has a son "of any kind," and therefore denies the deity of Christ.[^1] For Peterson this, not the existence of Muhammad as a prophet, is where the messages genuinely conflict.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Islam accepts Jesus' virgin birth and miracles but denies divine sonship.** "Islam believes that Christ was born of a virgin, that he performed miracles, and so on, but does not believe that God has a son of any kind — anybody, not us, not anyone."[^1] *(Strong — accurately states Quranic Christology; the Quran honors Jesus as prophet/Messiah while rejecting *ibn Allah*.)*
2. **The Edison-and-lightbulb analogy.** Peterson frames the Islamic God–creation relationship as maker to made, not father to child: "The relationship between God and humanity, or God and Jesus, is like the relationship between Edison and a light bulb. He created it. He's not the father of it."[^2] *(Strong as an illustration of strict Islamic creator-creature ontology.)*
3. **Muhammad may have rejected a garbled Trinity, not the real one.** Peterson is uncertain how much Muhammad even knew of orthodox Christianity in 7th-century Arabia; the Quran seems to attack a "trinity" of Father, Son, and **Mary** — possibly a local heterodox group's teaching — which Muhammad then rejected.[^3] *(Moderate — the Quran (e.g. 5:116) does appear to address a Father–Son–Mary triad; whether this reflects a real sect is debated among scholars.)* This complicates any claim that Muhammad consciously contradicted Nicene Christianity.
4. **Steelman.** A Trinitarian Christian will note that the LDS critique of Islam's Christology is double-edged: mainstream Christianity also says humans are not God's children by *nature* but by adoption — "we're a different species altogether." Peterson concedes this, which is why he pivots to the genuinely distinctive LDS claim (see [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]]): Islam, mainstream Christianity, and Judaism *all* deny that humans share God's species, so on this axis Islam is no more distant from LDS thought than Nicene Christianity is.
