# The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination

## The Claim
[[Luke Hansen]] reduces the predestination problem to a three-premise syllogism he uses in live conversations: God (1) **knows all of time**, (2) is **completely sovereign / all-powerful** (can do anything possible), and (3) **creates the entire universe out of nothing**. If God possesses all three, then every person is predestined for heaven or hell. You can only escape by denying one of the three. Hansen reports holding up three fingers in a debate and saying, "You're not going to be able to accept all three of these and reject predestination."[^1] The LDS move is to deny #3 — God did not create out of nothing but, per the Book of Abraham, "finds himself in the midst of spirits" — which removes His moral responsibility for who fails.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The syllogism's escape map is internally rigorous — rate: strong.** Hansen walks each exit: deny sovereignty/power (#2) and God can't guarantee the outcome; deny foreknowledge (#1) and He creates but doesn't know what follows (open theism); deny *ex nihilo* (#3) and He works with pre-existing, uncreated material. Each denial genuinely defuses predestination, which shows the three premises are jointly, not individually, sufficient.[^3] See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]].
2. **The standard creedal reply is "foreknowledge ≠ causation" — rate: this is the crux.** Hansen anticipates it: when a Christian says "just because God knows it doesn't mean He caused it," Hansen replies that this is actually a covert *denial of premise #2* (God's power to set the initial conditions however He wants). If God freely chose to instantiate exactly this world from nothing, knowing the outcome, the outcome is sourced in His creative choice even without proximate causation.[^4] **Steelman:** the Arminian/Molinist holds that God can know counterfactuals of *libertarian* freedom that He does not determine; on that view #1 and #2 are both true yet the agent, not God, is the ultimate source of the choice. The disagreement reduces to whether libertarian agency is coherent under *ex nihilo* creation — see [[Foreknowledge Is Not Causation Objection]] and [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]].
3. **The LDS exit is doctrinally specific, not merely philosophical — rate: distinctive.** Denying #3 is grounded in the Book of Abraham's pre-existent intelligences and D&C 93's "intelligence was not created or made" — so there is a part of you "not generated by God," making you fully morally responsible for your own outcome.[^2] See [[God Found Spirits He Did Not Create - Book of Abraham Solve]], [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]. **Steelman of the creedal critic:** denying *ex nihilo* trades the predestination problem for a finite, dependent God who shares the stage with co-eternal matter — a cost classical theists regard as fatal to monotheism (cf. [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]] for the historical-development counter to that worry).

## Best Quotes
> "There's three facts about God that Christians affirm. Number one, God knows everything. He knows all of time. Number two, he's completely sovereign, he's all powerful... And number three, he creates the entire universe out of nothing. The problem is if God possesses these three characteristics you are predestined for heaven or hell." [^1]

> "They said, well no, just because God knows what's going to happen doesn't mean he caused it to happen. And I said you just made an excellent defense for how God can possess characteristic number two and you're not predestined. It's a whole package." [^4]

> "If God merely as the Book of Abraham says, finds himself in the midst of spirits and then decides to try to invite those spirits into the kind of life that he has, then he is not morally responsible for people failing to do so." [^2]
