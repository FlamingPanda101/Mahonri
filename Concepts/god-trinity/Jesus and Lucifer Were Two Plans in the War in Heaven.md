# Jesus and Lucifer Were Two Plans in the War in Heaven

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection (voiced by the campus apologist "Cliff" in the clip) is that the LDS teaching "Jesus and Lucifer are brothers" is blasphemous: Jesus is the eternal God, while Lucifer is a created, fallen angel — so to call them brothers is to drag Christ down to the level of a creature [^1]. [[Colton Miller]] and [[Josh (Latter-day Christian)|Josh]] grant the kinship language and reframe it through the **premortal council**: all of God's children, Christ and Lucifer included, are His spirit offspring, and the "brotherhood" names a shared *Father*, not a shared createdness. What distinguished the two was not species but the **two plans** they presented — and Lucifer's loss of that contest, not his "creation," is his fall [^2].

This is the premortal-narrative complement to [[Jesus and Satan Are Brothers Only If Nothing Is Created]] (which answers the ontology) — here the focus is the *war in heaven* storyline and the angels-are-humans premise.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Two competing plans, decided by agency — rate: moderate (LDS-internal narrative)
The hosts narrate the council: Lucifer proposed to save everyone by *compelling* them — removing agency — and demanded the glory for himself; Jesus proposed to preserve agency, be the Savior, atone for sin, and give "all glory to the Father." Lucifer's **pride** at losing that vote is the thing that caused him to fall — "two competing political parties in the premortal war in heaven... Lucifer wanted all the glory" [^2]. Christ's status as Savior was therefore *earned by self-giving*, the opposite of Lucifer's grasping — so bracketing them as "brothers" does not equate their roles.

### 2. The biblical "first estate" and "third of the host" — rate: moderate (proof-text)
[[Jude]] 1:6 speaks of "the angels which kept not their first estate, but left their own habitation," and [[Revelation 12.4|Revelation 12]] of the dragon drawing "the third part of the stars of heaven." The hosts read these together as the premortal rebellion: a *first estate* (premortal life) that some kept and some forfeited, and a third of God's children who followed Lucifer. Those who followed Christ came to a *second estate* — mortality — to choose again [^3]. *Strength: the LDS reading gives "first estate" a concrete referent the text leaves undefined; it is a plausible but not a compelled gloss.*

### 3. Angels are humans in another phase, not a separate species — rate: moderate
The mainstream objection assumes angels are a distinct created order (cherubim/seraphim of Isaiah 6, the non-human winged beings of iconography). The hosts answer that in the restored gospel **an angel is just a human being** not currently in mortality — a premortal spirit, a disembodied spirit after death, or a resurrected being — and "angel" (Greek *angelos*) simply means *messenger*. Moroni was "a man who lived here on earth" and returned as a messenger. So Christ being the brother of an "angel" is not a category scandal once angels are understood as kin, not a separate creation [^4]. See [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]].

### 4. The brotherhood subtracts nothing from Christ — rate: moderate (rhetorical)
The repeated refrain: "Where exactly does Lucifer and Jesus being spirit brothers take anything away from what Christ is and what he accomplished?" Christ remains the uncreated God, the Creator (John 1; Hebrews 1:2), the sinless atoning Savior; the spirit-sibling relation is a premortal-genealogy fact, not a demotion [^5].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The creedal Christian answers that the objection was never *only* about createdness. On the Nicene view the eternal Son is *God of God*, *homoousios* with the Father — categorically other than every creature, including the highest angel. To file Christ as the senior member of a family that also contains Lucifer still places him *within* the created/begotten order of "spirit children" and so denies his unique, uncreated deity, however much the LDS affirm he is "eternal." The "two plans" narrative, moreover, is extra-biblical: Jude 1:6 and Revelation 12 do not describe a vote between rival salvation proposals, and reading one in is precisely the kind of move the critic distrusts. And Isaiah 14 / Ezekiel 28, the classic "fall of Lucifer" texts, present pride and self-exaltation against God — not the loss of a democratic contest — as the cause. The LDS reply is that the objection only bites if one already assumes *creatio ex nihilo* and a species-gap between God and man (the very points in dispute, per [[Jesus and Satan Are Brothers Only If Nothing Is Created]]), and that the council-in-heaven is supplied by restored scripture (Abraham 3; Moses 4; D&C 29) the critic rejects but the Latter-day Saint accepts — so the disagreement is at the level of *sources and ontology*, not internal coherence.

## Best Quotes
