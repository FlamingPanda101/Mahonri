# Tertullian Coined "Trinitas" and Held Subordinationism

## The Point
[[Tertullian]] of Carthage (c. 200) was "the first to use the Latin *trinitas* (Trinity) for the Godhead" — the very word "Trinity" is a coinage of one man around AD 200, not a term from scripture. He invented it polemically, to argue against the [[Modalism Monarchianism and Sabellianism|modalism]] of [[Praxeas]]. Yet Tertullian's *own* Trinity was **subordinationist** — he taught the Son is subordinate to the Father (citing John 14:28) — a position the later Church declared heresy. So the founder of Trinitarian vocabulary held a view that later Trinitarianism condemned.

## Evidence
1. Tertullian was "the first important Christian theologian to write in Latin," producing 45+ treatises; *Against Praxeas* is the key text on God and Christ.[^1]
2. His opponent [[Praxeas]] held [[Modalism Monarchianism and Sabellianism|modalism]] — "the Father [is] identical with the Son in the sense that both are one person" — manifesting in different "modes." Tertullian mocked this: scripture nowhere says "The Lord said unto himself, I am my own Son, today have I begotten myself."[^2]
3. Tertullian's solution: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are *three distinct persons* but one in "essence (*substantia*), condition, and power," three in "position, form, and appearance." He summarized this as a "tri-unity," the Trinity.[^3]
4. **The coinage:** "Tertullian is the first to use the Latin *trinitas* (Trinity) for the Godhead. And he does so in order to argue against the modalist view."[^4]
5. **The subordinationism:** Tertullian held God is three in "position" (*gradus*) — "Jesus does not stand in a position entirely equal with God the Father but is subordinate to him," quoting John 14:28, "my Father is greater than I." "This subordinationist thinking was later declared heresy."[^5]
6. The author notes: "one could say that subordinationism is precisely the question at stake in the Council of Nicaea."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Tertullian is the first to use the Latin *trinitas* (Trinity) for the Godhead. And he does so in order to argue against the modalist view that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are all one person." [^4]

> "As [Jesus] himself professes, Because my Father is greater than I" (quoting John 14:28). This subordinationist thinking was later declared heresy. [^5]

## LDS Connection
The term "Trinity" is post-biblical, coined c. 200 by a man who himself believed the Son was subordinate to the Father — a hierarchy closer to the LDS [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead]] than to later Nicene co-equality. That the inventor of the word was retroactively branded a heretic on the equality question shows the doctrine was still being constructed, not received intact from the apostles.
