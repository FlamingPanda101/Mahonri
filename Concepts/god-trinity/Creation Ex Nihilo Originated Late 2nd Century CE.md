# Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE

#type/claim

## The Claim

The doctrine of creation out of nothing (*creatio ex nihilo*) was not articulated until the second half of the 2nd century CE. Its first explicit proponents were Tatian (c. 160 CE), Theophilus of Antioch (c. 169 CE), and Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180 CE). Prior to this, Jewish and Christian thinkers — including the authors of 2 Maccabees, the Shepherd of Hermas, Philo of Alexandria, and the New Testament — operated within the conventional Greco-Roman philosophical framework that held creation involved ordering pre-existent material substrate, not generating matter from absolute nothingness. The doctrine of creation ex nihilo emerged as a response to Gnostic and philosophical debates about the resurrection, the morality of matter, and the sovereignty of God — and its originators then retroactively reinterpreted earlier texts to conform to the new doctrine.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Tatian of Assyria (c. 160 CE): The First Articulation

> "Tatian, an Assyrian Christian convert writing around AD 160, stated that matter is begotten and not produced by any other being, but brought into existence by the framer of all things alone."[^2]

Tatian's *Oration to the Greeks* marks the first explicit affirmation that matter itself is created — not eternal, not co-principle with God. This was a radical philosophical departure from the prevailing Platonic and Stoic consensus that matter was eternal.

### 2. Theophilus of Antioch (c. 169 CE): Explicit Formulation

> "Theophilus of Antioch around AD 169 wrote that God made all things out of things that were not into things that are."[^2]

Theophilus, in *Ad Autolycum*, uses language that explicitly reverses the standard philosophical claim: where Greeks said "nothing comes from nothing," Theophilus says God DOES make things from "things that are not" (ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων). This is the precise formulation that distinguishes creation ex nihilo from the earlier "not out of existing things" language of 2 Maccabees.

### 3. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180 CE): Doctrinal Consolidation

Irenaeus, in *Against Heresies*, consolidated and expanded the doctrine, making it a cornerstone of anti-Gnostic polemic. For Irenaeus, creation ex nihilo was essential to defending:
- **God's absolute sovereignty:** Gnostics posited a demiurge who shaped pre-existent matter; Irenaeus insisted the one true God created matter itself.
- **The goodness of creation:** Gnostics denigrated matter as evil; Irenaeus argued matter is good because God created it from nothing.
- **The resurrection of the body:** If God created matter from nothing, God can certainly reconstitute bodies at the resurrection.

### 4. The Philosophical Context: Why Then?

McClellan explains why the doctrine emerged when it did:

> "It is because of their philosophical debates with Gnostics and with other Greco-Roman philosophers who are arguing that nothing can come from nothing. And they're arguing about the resurrection. They're arguing about the morality of unformed pre-existent matter. And they come up with creation out of nothing. They are the originators of this doctrine."[^3]

Retelling this to [[Alex OConnor]], McClellan dates the first recognizable articulation to "around 170 CE" and fills in the concrete resurrection debate that drove it: Greek philosophers asked why anyone would want a "disgusting body back after it has died and decomposed or passed through the digestive system of an animal." Christians first answered that a whole human can be made from "just a little bit of semen" (so God creates from very little), then made the leap to "God created the universe out of nothing" — and could therefore reconstitute a body from nothing. Only *after* this did they reread Romans, Colossians, Hebrews, and 2 Maccabees 7:28 (the "out of what is not" texts) as teaching *ex nihilo*, when in their own period "what is not" meant unformed matter in a state of non-being.[^8]

Key pressures that produced the doctrine:
- **Gnostic dualism:** The Gnostic claim that matter is evil required an evil creator (demiurge) distinct from the good God. Creation ex nihilo undercut this by making the one good God the creator of matter itself.
- **Resurrection debates:** Pagan critics like [[Celsus]] mocked the resurrection as impossible — reconstituting a body from scattered, decayed matter. Creation ex nihilo provided the theological counter: if God created matter from nothing originally, reassembling it at the resurrection is a lesser act.
- **The *ex nihilo nihil fit* axiom:** Greco-Roman philosophy universally affirmed "nothing comes from nothing." To assert God's absolute sovereignty, Christian thinkers had to break this axiom — and they did.

### 5. The Pre-Existing Consensus: Creation from Material Substrate

Prior to Tatian/Theophilus/Irenaeus, the conventional wisdom across Hellenistic Judaism and early Christianity was:

> "The conventional wisdom of Greco-Roman period Judaism all the way up to the second half of the 2nd century CE held that all of the world was created out of a material substrate that occupied the realm of non-being. And imposing form and function on that material substrate moved it from the realm of non-being to the realm of being."[^4]

This was the view found in:
- 2 Maccabees 7:28 (see [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]])
- The Shepherd of Hermas[^5]
- Philo of Alexandria (see [[Philo Did Not Endorse Creation Ex Nihilo]])
- [[Wisdom 11.17|Wisdom of Solomon 11:17]] (God created the world "out of formless matter" — ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης)
- **[[Romans 4.17|Romans 4:17]]** and **[[Hebrews 11.3|Hebrews 11:3]]** (discussed below)

### 6. The Shepherd of Hermas Does Not Alter the Picture

Wes Huff cited the Shepherd of Hermas (2nd century) as an additional witness. McClellan responds:

> "The Shepherd of Hermas just appeals to the same kind of language that we find in 2 Maccabees — this 'not out of being' stuff or 'out of non-being' stuff — which is the same idea we already talked about."[^5]

The Shepherd uses the standard pre-ex-nihilo idiom of "out of non-being" (*ek tou mē ontos*) meaning "out of formless matter" — not creation from absolute nothingness.

### 7. Romans 4:17 and Hebrews 11:3 Re-Examined

McClellan notes that these New Testament passages were among those retroactively reinterpreted:

> "They turn around and they look at all the things that came before — from the Shepherd of Hermas, from 2 Maccabees, from Romans, from Hebrews, from Philo, from these other texts — and they reinterpret what was endorsing the conventional wisdom about that pre-existing material substrate occupying the realm of non-being as assertions of creation out of nothing."[^6]

Romans 4:17 speaks of God "who gives life to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not exist" (τὰ μὴ ὄντα ὡς ὄντα). In context, Paul is discussing Abraham's faith in God's promise of a son — the "calling into existence" refers to God bringing about what is not yet, not metaphysical creation from nothing. Hebrews 11:3 states "what is seen was not made out of things that are visible" (μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων) — which speaks of the visible coming from the invisible, consistent with a material-substrate view.

### 8. Gerhard May's Foundational Study Confirms the Timeline

Gerhard May's *Creatio Ex Nihilo* (German 1970s, English 1994) is the standard reference:

> "It is the consensus view of scholars that the concept of creation out of nothing was not articulated until the second half of the 2nd century CE. And that's exactly where we're going to get next with Wes. We have Tatian... and Theophilus of Antioch... and Irenaeus. That is where the doctrine originates."[^2]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

Wes Huff's argument, as McClellan summarizes it:

> "The pre-existing, no pun intended, idea continued to develop over a long period as Jewish and Christian thinkers wrote more on a distinctive scriptural monotheism with clear theological consequences for understanding God's creative act. But the idea that creation ex nihilo was somehow created, dare I say popped into existence in the latter half of the 2nd century AD, seems to not take all the evidence of its ancient articulations into proper account."[^7]

The steelman: The doctrine developed organically from seeds present in Scripture and earlier tradition (2 Maccabees, Shepherd of Hermas, Philo, Romans, Hebrews). To say it "popped into existence" in the late 2nd century ignores the trajectory of development. The late-2nd-century fathers were clarifying and making explicit what was already implicit in the tradition, not inventing something new.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **"Development from seeds" is not the same as "was already taught."** The claim that earlier texts contain "seeds" that later developed into creation ex nihilo concedes the point: the earlier texts do not themselves teach the doctrine. Development requires something new to develop.

2. **The earlier texts consistently reflect the opposite view.** 2 Maccabees uses philosophical language of ordering unformed matter. Philo explicitly posits two principles (active God + passive matter). The Wisdom of Solomon says God created "out of formless matter." These are not ambiguous "seeds" — they are clear endorsements of creation from pre-existent substrate.

3. **The shift coincides with specific philosophical pressures, not organic development.** The emergence of creation ex nihilo in the late 2nd century is not random — it is a direct response to Gnostic and pagan challenges that required a stronger doctrine of divine sovereignty over matter. It was an innovation driven by apologetic necessity, not the flowering of a seed that was always there.[^3]

4. **The originators themselves acknowledge the novelty.** Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus do not say "we are merely clarifying what everyone already believed." They argue AGAINST the prevailing view and propose a new formulation. Theophilus's *Ad Autolycum* is explicitly arguing against the philosophical consensus that matter is eternal.

5. **The retroactive reinterpretation obscures the historical reality.** Because the doctrine became normative and the originators retroactively reread earlier texts through its lens, later readers (including Wes Huff) see creation ex nihilo everywhere — but this is an artifact of the interpretive framework, not evidence the doctrine pre-existed its 2nd-century articulation.[^6]
