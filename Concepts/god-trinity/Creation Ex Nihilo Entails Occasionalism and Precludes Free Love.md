# Creation Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism and Precludes Free Love

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that **creation ex nihilo** (God creating all things from nothing) makes free love impossible, because it entails **occasionalism** — the view that God is the only true cause, recreating every creature and its states in each moment. If so, whatever "love" exists is created by God, so God is merely loving himself. Ostler defends this in volume two of *Exploring Mormon Thought* and argues ex nihilo is a late, non-biblical doctrine. [^1] See [[Love Cannot Be Coerced or Created]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Ex nihilo makes existence precarious** — rate: the metaphysical engine. "If God didn't sustain us in existence in each moment... we would wink out of existence. That means everything we do, everything about us, is recreated in every moment. This is called occasionalism. And I believe occasionalism is entailed by creation ex nihilo." Known as *divine sustenance*: "God must recreate every single thing about me in every moment... in the exact state I am." *(metaphysical argument)* [^2]
2. **Therefore love is God's own product** — rate: the payoff, tied to [[Love Cannot Be Coerced or Created]]. "Love cannot simply be created. It can't be coerced, it can't be forced. And if it were, God would simply be creating whatever love exists by giving it to himself." So ex nihilo is "inimical to the kind of love that God is asking us to give him." *(reductio)* [^3]
3. **The consent objection** — rate: a distinct moral point. Creating persons ex nihilo means "he didn't ask for our consent... that's an unloving act to throw us into a world like this without our consent." *(ethical)* [^2]
4. **Ex nihilo is historically late and non-biblical** — rate: strong, well-attested in scholarship. Ostler argues creation ex nihilo "is not a biblical view and didn't arise until the end of the second century" with [[Theophilus of Antioch]] and [[Tatian]] adopting "a middle platonic view of God which necessitated" it. *(historical-theological)* [^4] See [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]].

**Steelman of the classical theist:** Thomists deny that divine sustenance entails *occasionalism*. On the doctrine of *concurrence* (concursus), God sustains creatures *and* their genuine secondary causal powers simultaneously: God conserves me in being while *I* really cause my own acts as a true (though dependent) cause. Conservation is not re-creation of discrete instants but continuous holding-in-being of one enduring substance, so my free acts remain mine. Thus ex nihilo need not collapse into "God loving himself." Ostler's reply: concurrence is unstable — if my being and every accident (including my willings) are *wholly* derived from God moment-to-moment, the "secondary cause" contributes nothing not first given, and the distinction between concurrence and occasionalism is verbal.
