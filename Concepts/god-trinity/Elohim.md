# Elohim

**Elohim** (אֱלֹהִים) is the most common Hebrew word for "God" in the Hebrew Bible, though it is grammatically *plural* (the singular is *eloah*, and the related *el* means "god/deity"). When it refers to the God of Israel it normally takes singular verbs and adjectives; when it refers to the gods of the nations, deceased persons, or the members of the heavenly court it can take plural agreement. The same lexeme is therefore applied across a *spectrum* of referents — Yahweh, the other gods of the ancient Near Eastern pantheon, the *bene elohim* ("sons of God") who staff the divine council, ancestral/ghostly spirits (e.g. 1 Samuel 28:13), and, on the older interpretation of some passages, human authorities. Critically, *elohim* in the Hebrew Bible marks a being's *role or honor*, not a unique metaphysical "species," which is why the term can stretch so widely.

## Relevance
The flexible range of *elohim* is load-bearing for the vault's **divine-council** and **plurality-of-gods** arguments. If *elohim* names a category that includes the gods of the nations and the council's "sons of God," then Israel's earliest theology is not the later metaphysical monotheism of classical theism but a tiered divine family — exactly the reading the LDS-friendly notes press. It also fuels the rebuttal to [[Michael Heiser]]'s "species-unique" move (the claim that Yahweh alone is *elohim* in kind), since the word's usage of humans, ancestors, and council-members is said to be "powerfully counter-indicated" against any built-in ontological uniqueness. The word's plural form likewise feeds the "let us make man" (Genesis 1:26) divine-council exegesis.
