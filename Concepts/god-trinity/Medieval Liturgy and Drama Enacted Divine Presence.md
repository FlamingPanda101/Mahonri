# Medieval Liturgy and Drama Enacted Divine Presence

## The Point

[[Miranda Wilcox]] argues that medieval Christians "elaborated and expanded the liturgy of ancient Christians to evoke proximity through ritual with God in this life and the life to come" (see [[Luke 24.5-6|Luke 24:5-6]] — "He is not here, but is risen"). [^1] Liturgy structured both the individual life cycle and the communal calendar; sacramental and dramatic ritual became a "mode of becoming" in which participants "became protagonists in Christian history." [^2]

## Evidence

1. **Seven sacraments mapped the life cycle.** By AD 1200 the rites included initiation (Baptism, Confirmation), the Eucharist (Mass), Penance, ordination, anointing of the sick (Extreme Unction), and Matrimony; feast days commemorated Christ's life (Christmas, Epiphany, Easter, Pentecost) and the saints. [^1]
2. **The Mass lay at the heart of liturgy.** "If 'liturgy lay at the heart of medieval religion, the Mass lay at the heart of liturgy.'" Amalarius of Metz (c. 775-850) explained that in the bread and wine "Christ's passion is manifest." Laity were to kneel, change posture at key phrases, and gaze on the elevated Eucharist "with arms raised in divine greeting." [^3]
3. **Liturgical drama reenacted Holy Week.** Christians "processed on Palm Sunday, washed each others' feet on Maundy Thursday, adored the cross on Good Friday, and opened the empty tomb on Easter morning." [^4]
4. **The Quem quaeritis dialogue.** In late-tenth-century Winchester, Bishop Æthelwold's *Regularis Concordia* staged the three Marys meeting the angels at the empty tomb (Luke 24:1-8), the angel singing "Whom do you seek?" and the response "Jesus of Nazareth" — followed by "He is not here; He is risen." [^5]
5. **Civic mystery plays.** Cities like York staged cycles of biblical pageants on wagons during Corpus Christi; the York Resurrection plays end with Mary Magdalene recognizing the risen Christ — "My Lord Jesus, I know now thee" — manifesting "that recognizing Christ comes from being recognized by him." In these plays "the dead come to life in the bodies of the living... a presencing of Christ in the community of the faithful." [^6]

## Best Quotes

> "Medieval Christians realized that a performance is a mode of becoming that endures beyond the performance in the body and consciousness of the participant." [^2]

> "In the York Corpus Christi plays, 'the dead come to life in the bodies of the living. It's a description of resurrection and of theater... It is a presencing of Christ in the community of the faithful.'" [^6]
