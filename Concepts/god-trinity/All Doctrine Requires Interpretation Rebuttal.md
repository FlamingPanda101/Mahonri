# All Doctrine Requires Interpretation (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that because the Trinity is an *interpretation* of scriptural data rather than an explicit teaching, it is improper to make it salvation-deciding (see [[Trinity Is an Interpretation Not Explicit in Scripture]]). The implicit premise is that **explicitness is a necessary condition for a doctrine to be essential**. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Gavin Ortlund]] denies that premise: interpretation is universal, so it cannot by itself demote a doctrine.

1. **Every belief requires interpretation, including agreed essentials.** "Anything we're talking about, there's this human element... we're not infinite robots. So the data comes in and we've got to work through it as finite human beings." He applies this to the **resurrection**: someone might say "we have to interpret this" and find ambiguity, yet both interlocutors hold it essential. [^2] *Rating: strong as a consistency argument* — if interpretation disqualified the Trinity, it would equally threaten the resurrection, exclusivism (Jesus as "the way"), and even "God as Father," all of which Hansen treats as important.

2. **Ortlund turns Hansen's own list against the premise.** Hansen named "Jesus is the way, the truth, and the life," "God is our Father," and a child-of-God ontology as foundational. [^3] Ortlund notes each is *also* a contested interpretation that "lots of liberal-minded people" reject. [^4] "We're differing here in where we set the dials, but not that we both have... first-rank issues." *Rating: an effective tu-quoque* showing Hansen, too, draws interpretive boundary lines.

3. **Steelman of Hansen's reply.** Hansen pushes back that he is *not* really conceding symmetrical boundaries: his "boundaries" are about *progression*, not a torture/bliss binary, and require only openness to "all truth," with backsliding correctable by repentance. [^5] *Rating: a real disanalogy* — Hansen's "boundaries" lack the irreversible, this-determines-your-eternity character of Ortlund's, so the tu-quoque lands only partially.

4. **The deeper point: interpretation vs. inductive discovery.** Ortlund's case is strongest where the *content* of an interpretation is forced by Jesus' own words (the "I am" sayings, [[John 20.28|John 20:28]]); it is weakest where it depends on post-biblical philosophical categories. The honest residue is that the *technical formulation* developed, even if the *substance* is scriptural — a point Ortlund grants (see [[Trinity - Historical Development]]). *Rating: the rebuttal neutralizes the "explicitness" objection but does not by itself prove the Trinitarian reading is the only viable interpretation.*

## Best Quotes
> "Anything we're talking about, there's this human element of we're not infinite robots. So the data comes in and we've got to work through it as finite human beings. But I think that's true for everything." [^2]

> "The resurrection of Christ from the dead... someone might say, well, we have to interpret this... and yet you might come out with the evaluation... this is still essential even though there is an element of interpretation in it." [^6]

> "We're differing here in where we set the dials, but not that we both have, whether you call them first-rank issues or lethal beliefs or whatever you might call them." [^4]
