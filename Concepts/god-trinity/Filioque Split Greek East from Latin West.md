# The Filioque Split Greek East from Latin West (1054)

## The Point
The councils meant to *unite* Christians repeatedly produced *new divisions*. The greatest, the Great Schism of 1054 between Greek Orthodox and Roman Catholic Christianity, was driven in part by a single added Latin clause — *filioque*, "and the Son" — disputing whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from both the Father and the Son. More precision yielded more schism. This is direct evidence that "the Trinity" never produced lasting consensus, contradicting claims of a unified, settled apostolic doctrine.

## Evidence
1. "The intent of these ancient councils was to unite Christians throughout the world, but they often resulted in new divisions."[^1]
2. The Visigoths who sacked Rome were Arian Christians; eastern churches split after Chalcedon over its emphasis on Christ's two natures, holding *miaphysitism* instead.[^2]
3. The Second (553) and Third (680–81) Councils of Constantinople tried to "remedy this rift," the Third concluding Christ had two wills (Luke 22:42) — "but with greater theological precision came additional divisions."[^3]
4. "By AD 1054, Greek Orthodox and Roman Catholic Christians formally divided, in part, over the question of whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or equally from both the Father and the Son (*filioque*)."[^4]
5. Later Protestant councils "reinforced their separation from Roman Catholicism with new creeds of their own."[^5]

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
The relentless fracturing of creedal Christianity — Arian Visigoths, post-Chalcedon miaphysites, the 1054 East-West schism over *filioque*, then Protestant breakaways — illustrates the LDS narrative of a Great Apostasy lacking a living authority to settle doctrine. Where there is no prophet, councils multiply creeds and creeds multiply churches. See [[Trinity - Historical Development]] and [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]].
