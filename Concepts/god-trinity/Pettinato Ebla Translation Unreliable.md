# Pettinato Ebla Translation Unreliable

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Underlying Wes Huff's Argument)

Wes Huff's claim that the Ebla archives affirm creation ex nihilo relies entirely on Giovanni Pettinato's 1981 English translation of tablet TM.75.G1682. The implication is that Pettinato's translation is a reliable rendering of the ancient text, from which theological conclusions can be drawn.

## The Rebuttal

Pettinato's translations of the Ebla texts — particularly TM.75.G1682 — are not reliable. His work was driven by an apologetic agenda to cast Ebla as a precursor to the Old Testament, and his readings have been systematically rejected by the scholarly community. The translation Huff cites is over 50 years old, from a widely-criticized book, and contradicted by subsequent epigraphic work.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Pettinato's Apologetic Program

Giovanni Pettinato's *The Archives of Ebla: An Empire Inscribed in Clay* (English translation, 1981) was written with an explicit agenda of linking Ebla to the Hebrew Bible:

> "This book is notorious for trying to argue apologetically that the Ebla archives are basically a precursor to the Old Testament."[^1]

His specific discredited claims include:
- **Sodom and Gomorrah**: Pettinato argued these cities appear in the Ebla archives. They do not.[^1]
- **Israelite-style kingship**: Pettinato claimed Eblaite royal installation mirrored Hebrew Bible patterns. Scholars rejected this.[^1] Alfonso Archi specifically argued "the kings of Ebla were not anointed like the kings of Israel."[^2]
- **Monotheism**: Pettinato's readings were frequently deployed to argue for early monotheism at Ebla — a reading the evidence does not support.[^3]

### 2. Alfonso Archi's Systematic Refutation

Alfonso Archi, the second epigrapher assigned to the Ebla tablets, published a point-by-point rebuttal in *The Epigraphic Evidence from Ebla and the Old Testament*. His critique of Pettinato's TM.75.G1682 translation:

> "Now this is a Sumerian composition very difficult to understand. TM.75.G1682 runs in four columns... After the first three words, which mean 'king of heaven and earth,' a well-known Sumerian epithet, the text is quite incomprehensible."[^4]

Archi found Pettinato's philological moves unsupported — for example, Pettinato's attempt to derive a Sumerian word from Hebrew *shakar* or Arabic *sahar* (dawn) is described by Archi as "an explanation not requiring comment" — i.e., asserted without evidence.[^5]

Archi's alternate transliteration of column 3 yields a very different sense:

> "Lordship was not in existence and night abundance was not in existence. City was not in existence."[^6]

This reads as a standard ancient Near Eastern cosmogonic catalogue of pre-creation non-order — not a philosophical assertion of creation from nothing.

### 3. Mark Chavalas on the Field's Rejection (2003/2004)

> "The second epigrapher, Archi, spent some effort in refuting many of the premises of Dahoud and Pettinato, ignoring most of the sensationalism, primarily from the American press. He argued that the supposed evidence for Adonai at Ebla was questionable and ambiguous... There is no Genesis creation story. Pettinato 1977 and Ebla place names do not easily correspond to the Bible names or the cities of the plain. The Eblaite connections with the Hebrew language are unclear."[^2]

### 4. Even Matthews & Benjamin's Alternative Translation Diverges

Victor Matthews and Don Benjamin, publishing *Old Testament Parallels* just a few years after Pettinato (and themselves somewhat apologetically oriented), nevertheless offered a different translation of TM.75.G1682 that moves away from Pettinato's explicit ex nihilo reading:

> "You are the creator of the heavens and the earth. There was no earth until you created it. There was no light until you created it."[^7]

McClellan notes: even this "more cautious" translation is "highly problematic" — it still imposes a Genesis-parallel framework on what is, per Archi, a largely incomprehensible text.[^8]

### 5. Pettinato's Translation as Norman Geisler's Pipeline

McClellan observes that Huff's use of this specific Pettinato translation likely comes through [[Norman Geisler]] — one of the few published apologists who has deployed this exact Pettinato passage in defense of creation ex nihilo (and monotheism):[^3]

> "This probably comes from Norman Geisler. That's the only other place I've seen someone actually publish this particular translation of Pettinato in defense of creation ex nihilo. And also it is frequently used to try to defend monotheism as well — which it doesn't do."[^3]

### 6. Translation Variance Signals Unreliability

The fact that competent scholars produce radically different translations of the same text is itself evidence of unreliability:
- **Pettinato (1981):** "Lord of heaven and earth, the earth was not, you created it. The light of day was not, you created it. The morning light you had not yet made exist."[^9]
- **Matthews & Benjamin:** "You are the creator of the heavens and the earth. There was no earth until you created it. There was no light until you created it."[^7]
- **Archi (via McClellan):** "Lordship was not in existence and night abundance was not in existence. City was not in existence."[^6]

When authoritative epigraphers cannot agree on what the text says, it cannot bear the weight of a precise theological claim like creation ex nihilo.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A defender might argue: Pettinato was the ORIGINAL epigrapher assigned to the Ebla tablets and had privileged first access; his readings should be given weight. Even if some of his broader claims were rejected, the specific translation of TM.75.G1682 could still be correct. Multiple scholars offering different translations doesn't mean Pettinato is wrong — translation of fragmentary Sumerian texts always involves interpretive choices.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **"First access" does not confer accuracy.** Pettinato's sensationalism was fueled by his rush to publish apologetically-useful findings, not by superior epigraphic method. Archi, who had equal access and superior methodological rigor, systematically dismantled his readings.[^4]
2. **The text is "quite incomprehensible" per the most cautious epigrapher.** This is not a matter of interpretive nuance — Archi states the text cannot be understood beyond the opening epithet.[^4]
3. **Pettinato's track record is disqualifying.** When a translator claims to find Sodom, Gomorrah, Israelite kingship, Adonai, and monotheism in texts where no other scholar finds them, the pattern indicates motivated reasoning, not superior translation skill.
4. **Huff's use of Pettinato is uncritical and secondhand.** Huff didn't cite Pettinato directly — he likely got it from [[Norman Geisler]], a popular apologist, without engaging the scholarly critique.[^3]
