# Figurative Reading Dissolves the Trinitarian Problem

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Co-host [[Dan Beecher]] raises an outsider's objection: the whole metaphysical problem the Trinity was built to solve only exists if you insist on reading the New Testament's God-language *literally*. Read **figuratively**, the Johannine material makes "perfect sense" — Jesus speaks of God being in him and him in God, of believers being "in God and God in us," language of mutual indwelling and participation that needs no ontological identity.[^1] On this reading, Jesus can "house God's name" or "house God's authority" and possess divinity by designation without *being* God himself.[^2] Beecher: making Jesus literally God "feels actually more problematic... you've created so many more problems than you've solved."[^3]

## The Rebuttal
This is an *objection* to the Trinitarian construction (and a defense of a non-ontological, agency/indwelling Christology), which [[Dan McClellan]] endorses. The "rebuttal" here is therefore the Trinitarian counter to Beecher, steelmanned, followed by why McClellan/Beecher think it fails.

1. **The biblical indwelling language is genuinely pervasive and reciprocal — rate: strong.** John repeatedly frames union in mutual-participation terms ("I in them and thou in me," believers made "one" as Father and Son are one). This is the basis for the [[I and the Father Are One Means Mutual Indwelling]] and [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology]] readings. *Rating: strong; the figurative/participatory sense is exegetically well grounded.*

2. **The Trinitarian rejoinder (steelman).** A defender argues that figurative indwelling is true *but insufficient*: texts like Thomas's "my Lord and my God," the Logos of John 1:1, and the "I AM" sayings push past metaphor toward an identity-claim, and the church's task was to articulate *what kind* of unity could be both real and monotheistic. On this view the Trinity is not a problem manufactured by literalism but the disciplined answer to data that resist a purely figurative reading.

3. **Why McClellan/Beecher think the rejoinder fails.** McClellan agrees with Beecher that designation/agency (Jesus *bears* the divine name and authority — see [[Divine Name Theology]]) accounts for the high-sounding texts without ontological identity, and that [[John 17.3|John 17:3]] explicitly distinguishes the Father as "the only true God" from Jesus. The literalizing move, he argues, is precisely the late systematization that "created so many more problems than you've solved" (the hypostatic union, consubstantiality, the contradiction charge). *Rating: persuasive within an agency Christology; a Trinitarian would reject the premise that designation exhausts the data.* See [[Trinity Is an Interpretation Not Explicit in Scripture]].

4. **The hermeneutical core.** McClellan reframes the historical question: "Did something get more literal in the 4th century?" — yes, he says: an institution with population-wide authority needed everything reduced to literal, line-drawable propositions (see [[Boundary Maintenance Drove Doctrinal Systematization]]). The figurative readings that "resonated" were flattened. *Rating: strong as a description of the pressure toward literalization.*

## Best Quotes
> "He's using language that makes perfect sense to me if we're thinking figuratively. And there's no reason why we can't think figuratively about it. It makes sense — throughout the Bible there's figurative language all the time."[^1]

> "This can be a being that has divinity sort of instilled in him by virtue of his authority, his title... but it doesn't feel problematic to me for him not to be God himself. It feels actually more problematic for him to be God. You've created so many more problems than you've solved."[^3]

> "Did something get more literal in the 4th century or something? ...The church became part of a powerful institution... It all has to be reduced to something that we can draw lines around in a very simple binary way."[^4]
