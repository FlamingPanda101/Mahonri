# A Timeless God Cannot Act or Create

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] presses an *offensive* argument against the classical-theist doctrine that God is strictly timeless (see the defense at [[Divine Eternity as Timelessness - God Exists Outside of Time]]): a genuinely timeless God *cannot act, and in particular cannot create*. The reasoning is that any act — above all the act of creation — requires a *before* and an *after*: "In order for God to make the creative act, there was a time before he acted, and there was a time after he acted." But "in a timeless realm, nothing can happen... for something to happen, that means there was a time before that thing happened and then a time after." So "to be in a timeless realm is to be in a realm that just is" — static, frozen, actless — "and that to me is contrary to the nature of God. God is an act." When [[Daniel Ortner]] replies that God "created time in his act of creation" and is "over and superior to time," Hansen rejoins that this still smuggles in sequence: creating-time *is itself* an act, and an act presupposes the very before/after a timeless God lacks.[^1][^2]

Strikingly, Hansen wields the *same blade against his own tradition*: he explicitly judges **Joseph Smith mistaken** for teaching that existence is "one eternal now." "It's also why I don't like when Joseph Smith said that it was one eternal now. I actually think he's wrong about that." If everything is an eternal now, "every sin you've ever done... has already been committed. There is no future, there is no past... there's no agency." Hansen's positive commitment is therefore a *temporally extended* God and cosmos — real succession, real before-and-after — against both the creedal timeless God *and* a static "eternal now" reading of LDS scripture.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (LDS side — Hansen).** (1) *Action entails sequence.* The intuition is hard to dislodge: "to create" is a verb of change-in-the-world; even granting the world's change is "Cambridge" with respect to God (the classical move; see [[Divine Immutability - God Cannot Change]]), the *bringing-about* relation seems to require that the effect was *not-yet* and then *is* — a structure with temporal direction. A point with no before/after looks like the wrong kind of thing to be an *agent* at all; it is a *state*, not a *doing*. (2) *"God is an act."* A God who genuinely responds to prayer, is moved by suffering, anticipates, remembers, and works out a covenant history is *intrinsically* temporal; strip succession and you strip the personal, narrative God of scripture, leaving an *actus purus* that "acts" only in a redefined, non-sequential sense the LDS finds unrecognizable (cf. [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]], which makes agency *eternal and exercised*, i.e., temporal). (3) *Internal consistency over loyalty.* By correcting Joseph Smith's "one eternal now," Hansen shows the argument is principled, not partisan: he will not accept a *static block* even when an LDS prophet's phrasing seems to license it, because a block universe destroys agency just as surely as creedal timelessness destroys real divine action. The LDS God must be in *time* (or in a real duration with succession), full stop.

**Steelman (classical-theist side — Ortner / the tradition).** (1) *Creating-time is not an act in time.* The standard reply (Feser, Augustine) is precisely that God does not act *at a moment*; rather "God, from outside of time, in a single act causes to exist a temporal world" (see [[Divine Eternity as Timelessness - God Exists Outside of Time]]). The author-of-a-story analogy answers Hansen directly: an author conceives an entire plot "in a single flash" with no before/after *in himself*, while the story he authors is internally sequenced. So "before and after creation" is a fact about *the creation*, not about God — Hansen's argument equivocates between *the act* (timeless, one) and *its term* (temporal, many). (2) *Begging the question.* "An act requires a before and after" is exactly the premise at issue; the classical theist denies that *every* actualizing must be a *change in the agent* (the fallacy-of-accident point: from "every actor we observe also changes" it does not follow that "any possible actor must change"). To assume otherwise is to presuppose temporal causation and then "discover" that timeless causation is impossible. (3) *Cause-and-effect without temporal priority.* Classical metaphysics distinguishes *causal* priority from *temporal* priority — a foot resting eternally in sand could eternally cause the impression with no moment at which the impression "began." So a timeless God's causing of a first moment need not itself occupy a prior moment; "the realm just is" does not entail "nothing can be caused from it." Hansen, the tradition would say, has imported the very notion of time he claims God transcends.

The dispute is thus over whether *agency* is conceptually bound to *succession* (Hansen) or whether there is a coherent notion of *timeless causation* in which a single non-temporal act gives rise to a temporally ordered world (Ortner). Note the rare feature here: Hansen's argument cuts *both ways across the aisle*, indicting the creedal timeless God and a block-universe misreading of Joseph Smith alike, in service of an unabashedly *temporal* LDS deity.
