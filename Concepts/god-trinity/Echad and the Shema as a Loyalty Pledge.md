# Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Shema — "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is **one** (*echad*)" ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deuteronomy 6:4]]) — teaches the *ontological* oneness of the one divine being, and thus undergirds the Trinity.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. *Echad* and the alternate sense "alone"
The Hebrew reads *Yahweh eloheinu, Yahweh echad*. While *echad* means "one," the panel notes the line functions as **"Yahweh is our God, Yahweh alone"** — a statement of exclusive allegiance, not a metaphysical count of beings. *(Moderate: the "alone" rendering is defensible and is reflected in some scholarly translations, though "one" is the more common rendering; the *function* argument carries the weight.)* [^1]

### 2. The Shema was a covenant loyalty pledge, not a metaphysics lecture
In its ancient setting, surrounded by the gods of the nations, Israel's confession meant: "Of all the gods, **we** are the covenant people of *the* God of Israel." It is Israel's pledge of allegiance — like a national loyalty oath — not a claim about the internal constitution of the divine being. *(Strong on the historical-contextual point; coheres with mainstream scholarship that the Shema is covenantal/cultic.)* [^2]

### 3. Reading ontological oneness into it is eisegesis
To find "one being / three persons" in *echad* is to import a 4th-century Greek question into an Iron-Age Hebrew text — anachronism. The ancient Israelite was pledging loyalty to Yahweh alone, not parsing consubstantiality. See [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible]]. [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Trinitarians and classical Jewish readers note that even on the "alone/exclusive" reading, the Shema still asserts there is *only one* God to be loyal to — which the Trinitarian happily affirms and the (apparent) LDS multiplicity of gods must reckon with. Some appeal to *echad*'s use for *composite* unity ("one flesh," "one cluster of grapes") as evidence the term can hold plurality-in-unity — cutting *toward* a multi-personal one God. The LDS reply: composite-unity *echad* supports unity-not-identity (the very point of [[Oneness Means Unity Not Numerical Identity of Being]]) and undercuts a *simple* divine being, while "one God" is handled by the Godhead/"one what" reading (see [[Three Whos and One What Framework]], [[Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists]]).

## Best Quotes
> "The word echad can also mean alone... the Shema in the context of the ancient world was a loyalty pledge, because it wasn't about ontological oneness." [^1]

> "They were saying, of all of the gods of the nations, we are the covenant people of the God of Israel... it's almost like their pledge of allegiance." [^2]
