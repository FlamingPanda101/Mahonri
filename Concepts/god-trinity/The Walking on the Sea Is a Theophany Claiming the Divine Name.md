# The Walking on the Sea Is a Theophany Claiming the Divine Name

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brant Pitre]] holds that the walking-on-the-sea episode (Mark 6 // Matthew 14) is his **strongest** evidence that Jesus claimed divinity. Three Old-Testament cues converge: Jesus (1) takes the divine name — *ego eimi*, "I AM" — (2) adds the standard theophanic reassurance "do not be afraid," and (3) does what Hebrew scripture says *only God* does, tread the waves. It is an "epiphany miracle": an unveiling of his divine identity, not a mere "it's me."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Ego eimi* + "do not be afraid" is theophanic language (strong)
In Mark 6:50 Jesus says "take heart, *ego eimi*, do not be afraid." In a *theophanic* setting (he is walking on water; they think he is a ghost) the bare, non-predicated "I AM" alludes to [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]] and especially to [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43]], where the LORD says "I am he... do not fear." "Do not be afraid" is the recurring response-formula when a heavenly being appears in the Old Testament.[^1]

### 2. Walking on the sea is a uniquely divine act (strong)
[[Job 9]] says God "alone... treads on the waves of the sea" (the Septuagint: walks on the waves "as on dry land"). The act is also a divine prerogative in the Greco-Roman frame: Pitre notes Caligula built a bridge across the Bay of Baiae and rode across it "as befitted his godhead" to manifest divinity. So crossing the sea by one's own power signifies superhuman, divine status in *both* contexts.[^2]

### 3. Non-Christian scholars grant the allusion (medium)
Pitre stresses this is not mere apologetics: the (largely atheist/agnostic) Jesus Seminar read the walking-on-sea as a deliberate allusion to Exodus 3, saying Jesus is "acting like Poseidon." The theophanic reading is available from a secular standpoint.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
O'Connor presses two real difficulties. (1) The **Septuagint problem**: Exodus 3 LXX renders the divine self-identification as *ego eimi ho on* ("I am the One who is"), so a Greek-writing evangelist who wanted the *name* would write *ho on*, not bare *ego eimi* — making the bare phrase look like ordinary "it's me." (2) The **Job context problem**: "God alone treads the waves" is spoken by *Job*, who in surrounding chapters says false, "blaspheming" things about God in his anguish — so quoting Job as an authority on divine prerogatives is shaky. More broadly, *ego eimi* is a commonplace ("it's me" — the blind man in John 9; Jesus warns in Mark 13 that imposters will say *ego eimi*), so the phrase alone cannot carry divinity.

## Counter-Objection
Pitre answers the Septuagint point: it is not only Exodus 3 that matters — [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43]] LXX renders the same Hebrew self-identification with bare *ego*, so the allusion still lands, and meaning is fixed by *context* (the theophanic sea-walking, the "do not fear"). On Job: even if the surrounding chapters are Job's complaint, Job 9:1–10 is a litany of God's *creative attributes* not under dispute, and — decisively — the *ancient cultural background* (Caligula's bridge, Poseidon) shows that walking the sea independently signified divinity, so the claim does not rest on Job's reliability alone. He concedes *ego eimi* is sometimes ordinary, which is exactly why the surrounding theophanic markers are load-bearing.

## Apologetic Engagement
The episode is a textbook case of the [[John 8-58 - Before Abraham Was I Am (Divine Name Agency)|divine-name-agency]] reading the Restoration favors: Jesus is divine *as the authorized bearer of the Father's name*. In ancient Near Eastern thought the divine name could be conferred on a stone, an image, or an agent, charging the medium with the deity's presence — so "I AM" is a claim to *carry* the name and presence of God, which is precisely how a distinct divine Son relates to the Father in LDS theology, not a claim to be numerically identical to him. Pitre's Catholic conclusion (Chalcedonian two-natures) over-reads the same data; the Restoration takes the name-bearing seriously without collapsing Father and Son.
