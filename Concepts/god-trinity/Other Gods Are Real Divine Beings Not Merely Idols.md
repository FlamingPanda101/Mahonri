# Other Gods Are Real Divine Beings Not Merely Idols

## The Claim

When the Hebrew Bible speaks of "other gods," it means **actual divine beings** that Yahweh claims supremacy over — not merely lifeless idols or imaginary nothings. The standard apologetic deflection ("the 'gods' are just idols") fails on the plain wording: the texts say *elohim* ("gods"), and their rhetoric presupposes the other gods exist. [[Dan McClellan]], reacting to a viral video making this case, affirms each proof-text as "totally accurate" and the whole picture as "pretty standard fare" among scholars: the Bible recognizes other gods "from beginning to end." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A command not to prefer them presupposes they exist — rate: strong (lexical)

[[Exodus 20.3|Exodus 20:3]] ("no other gods before me") forbids *ranking* other gods ahead of Yahweh. McClellan: "If other gods didn't exist, then why would Yahweh tell people not to put them before him?" And the text "said gods," not idols. A prohibition on preference is intelligible only if the things prohibited are taken to be real. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the grammar of the first commandment assumes referents, not nonentities.

### 2. The divine-council scene puts Yahweh *among* the gods — rate: strong

[[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82:1]] ("God stands in the divine assembly; he judges among the gods") depicts Yahweh in the council "of the gods... and he's not alone." McClellan grounds the patron-deity reading in his *Journal of Biblical Literature* article. "Did he say demons? No, he said gods." See [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — rests on a peer-reviewed primary publication.

### 3. "God of gods" is comparative rhetoric that needs real rivals — rate: strong

[[Deuteronomy 10.17|Deuteronomy 10:17]] ("God of gods and Lord of lords") works like "King of kings": it only makes sense if there are other rulers to rank above. The rhetoric "would be meaningless if the other gods didn't exist and were entirely fabricated imaginary beings. That would be like saying God is so much better than nothingness — that is rhetorically meaningless." See [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the rhetorical force depends on the rivals being real and powerful.

### 4. "Who is like you among the gods?" is a direct comparison — rate: strong

[[Exodus 15.11|Exodus 15:11]] is "another direct comparison. He's saying he's greater among the other gods, not that they don't exist." This is the ANE rhetoric of incomparability, paralleled in Egyptian and Mesopotamian praise literature for a favored deity. See [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]. [^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the incomparability genre is well attested across the ancient Near East.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The traditional reading holds that the prophets' polemic against the "gods" is really a polemic against *idols* — wood and stone that "have mouths but cannot speak" (Psalm 115) — and that texts like [[Isaiah 44.6|Isaiah 44:6]] ("there is no God besides me") deny any real rival deity, so "gods" elsewhere is either sarcasm or shorthand for impotent idols. McClellan's reply (developed in the linked notes): the "idol" polemic denies the *idol*, not the deity it indexes (see [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]]), and the negation texts assert *incomparability*, not non-existence (see [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]]). The honest reading of the *elohim* language, he argues, is that the authors took the other gods to be real divine beings ranked beneath Yahweh — the same henotheistic picture the [[Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods|Latter-day Saint divine-council reading]] draws on.

## Best Quotes

> "the Bible does explicitly state that there are other gods and not just idols, but actual divine beings that Yahweh claims to be above." [^1]

> "If other gods didn't exist, then why would Yahweh tell people not to put them before him? And before you try to bend this and say that it meant idols — no, it didn't. It said gods." [^1]

> "There is no monotheism anywhere in the Bible, at least to the degree that monotheism is understood as in any way meaning there are no other gods." [^5]
