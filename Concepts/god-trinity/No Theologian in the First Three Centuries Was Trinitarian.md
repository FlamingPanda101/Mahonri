# No Theologian in the First Three Centuries Was Trinitarian

## The Claim
Against the Orthodox priest's assertion that the Trinity is "apostolic in origin," the narrator argues the **scholarly consensus is the opposite**: the codified Nicene Trinity (one God who is tri-personal, three co-equal divine persons) was not taught in scripture and was not held by any theologian in the first three Christian centuries. Pre-Nicene authors who used words like *trias/trinitas* meant a *plurality* (the one God together with his Son and Spirit), "not a triune or tripersonal god," and did not regard the three as equally divine. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A direct scholarly consensus statement.** The narrator quotes (the formulation associated with Trinitarian-studies scholars such as those gathered in works on pre-Nicene theology): "No theologian in the first three Christian centuries was a trinitarian in the sense of believing that the one God is tri-personal, containing equally divine persons, father, son, and holy spirit." *Rating: strong; matches mainstream history-of-doctrine scholarship that full Nicene Trinitarianism is a 4th-century development.* [^1]
2. **The terminology postdates the apostles by ~150 years.** "The terms we translate as trinity, Latin *trinitas*, Greek *trias*, seem to have come into use only in the last two decades of the second century." And even then "such usage doesn't reflect trinitarian belief." *Rating: strong.* [^1]
3. **Pre-Nicene "trinity" language meant plurality, not tri-unity.** These authors "use such terms not to refer to the one God but rather to refer to the plurality of the one God together with his son and his spirit... a trinity, triad or threesome, but not a triune or tripersonal god. Nor do they consider these to be equally divine." *Rating: strong; subordinationism was the pre-Nicene norm — see [[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)]].* [^2]
4. **Creedal apologists concede the long development.** Even defenders admit "the Trinity took a while to develop," which "concedes the point that it wasn't something specifically taught in scripture or by the apostles." *Rating: strong as a dialectical point.* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Trinitarians (and historians like [[Larry Hurtado]] on early high Christology) argue the *worship* of Jesus as divine is demonstrably first-century, and the later creed merely *articulates* in precise vocabulary what the apostolic church already practiced — "seeds" present in the NT that took time to systematize. On this view "no one was a Nicene Trinitarian before Nicaea" is trivially true of any developed doctrine and does not show the doctrine false.

### Counter-objection
The narrator's reply (shared with LDS apologetics): "seeds" is unfalsifiable — any later development can be read back this way — and the specific content that *changed* (from subordinationist plurality to co-equal tri-unity, using Greek philosophical tools) is exactly what Latter-day Saints identify as the [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event|apostasy]]. The development was driven by philosophy, not revelation. See [[William Lane Craig Concedes Logos Christology Is a Platonic Relic]].

## Best Quotes
> "No theologian in the first three Christian centuries was a trinitarian in the sense of believing that the one God is tri-personal, containing equally divine persons, father, son, and holy spirit." [^1]

> "They profess a trinity, triad or threesome, but not a triune or tripersonal god. Nor do they consider these to be equally divine. In other words, the doctrine of the trinity that was codified at Nicaea isn't explicitly taught in scripture, nor was it taught for the first three centuries." [^2]
