# Blasphemy Did Not Require Claiming to Be Yahweh

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"They wouldn't have picked up stones to stone him for blasphemy unless he was claiming to be the very God of Israel." The crowd's lethal reaction in [[John 10.33|John 10:33]] proves Jesus made an explicit claim to be Yahweh.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Blasphemy had a much broader definition (strong)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** calls the inference "asinine" and "simply not true." His correction: "Any speech that was considered to mock, deride or diminish God was considered blasphemy. And for Jesus to say, 'I am one with the father,' could absolutely have been interpreted to have been blasphemy without being interpreted to mean, 'I am the very God of Israel.'" [^1] The stoning reaction is therefore consistent with a charge of *diminishing/usurping* divine prerogatives, not only with a claim to *be* Yahweh.

### 2. The "stoning proves deity" move is a non-sequitur (strong)
The objection assumes a single trigger for blasphemy (claiming to be God). But if many kinds of speech counted as blasphemy, the crowd's reaction underdetermines what Jesus claimed. The reaction is evidence that the crowd took offense, not evidence of the *specific* content they took offense at. McClellan urges reading "these texts seriously on their own terms rather than just imposing your own interpretive lenses on them so that they are only allowed to say what you allow them to say." [^2]

### 3. The grammar settles the content (decisive, cross-ref)
What Jesus was actually accused of is fixed by the Greek of [[John 10.33|John 10:33]] — an anarthrous *theos*, "you, being human, make yourself a god / divine," not "the very God of Israel." See [[Anarthrous Theos Defeats the Trinitarian Reading of John 10]] and [[John 10-33 - Making Yourself God (Missing Article)]].

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
The apologist could argue that within John's Jewish-monotheist setting, *any* self-deification claim would have been heard as a claim against Yahweh's uniqueness, collapsing the distinction McClellan draws. McClellan's reply: Second Temple Judaism comfortably hosted exalted intermediary figures and divine-council beings (cf. [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]], [[Genesis 6.1-4|Genesis 6:1-4]]) who were "divine" without being Yahweh, so the category "a god, but not the God of Israel" was genuinely available — which is exactly what Jesus exploits in his Psalm-82 reply.
