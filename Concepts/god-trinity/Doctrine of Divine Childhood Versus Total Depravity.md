# Doctrine of Divine Childhood Versus Total Depravity

## The Point
The breakdown's ethical climax is a contrast between two anthropologies and the *behavior each produces*. Calvinism's **T** — total depravity — holds that every person, "even infants and drowning little girls," is so corrupt as to deserve eternal torment; [[John Calvin]]'s own words call the newborn "a seedbed of sin... odious and abominable to God." The panel's analytical move is to ask what such a view *motivates* ("why would we give grace to each other... why would we not just rape and pillage?") and to set against it the Restored doctrine that every human being is a literal child of God with "inherent worth, dignity, and destiny." The claim is that **doctrine informs behavior**: a theology of divine childhood yields charity and joyful invitation, while total depravity yields a "dark, negative view of humanity."

## Evidence
1. **Calvin on infants, quoted directly.** "Even infants bring their condemnation with them from their mother's womb. Their whole nature is, as it were, a seedbed of sin, and therefore cannot be but odious and abominable to God." Hansen notes White's softening ("original sin is like a diagnosis... cancer") still makes inherited corruption "the basis for condemnation, even if you are a drowning little girl." [^1]
2. **The behavioral argument.** "If we look at every single body as a sick depraved individual that deserves no grace... why would we give grace to each other? Why would we not just rape and pillage?" The Trinity, the panel adds, "dehumanizes God and deifies us... separates us from our Father"; seeing each person as God's child changes how you treat them. [^2] See [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]].
3. **The canonical rebuttal: God does not joy in destroying children.** [[Moroni 8.12-22|Moroni 8]]: "little children are alive in Christ even from the foundation of the world. If not so, God is a partial God, and also a changeable God, and a respecter of persons." A God who would send unbaptized infants to "an endless hell" is "a partial God" — "it's a hellish thing to believe." [^3] See [[Infant Exaltation]].
4. **C.S. Lewis as the alternative anthropology.** [[C.S. Lewis]]' *The Weight of Glory*: "There are no ordinary people. You have never talked to a mere mortal... immortal horrors or everlasting splendors." The breakdown offers this as "the sort of view we should have of each other," explicitly opposed to "the idea that we are all depraved individuals that deserve everlasting torment." [^4]
5. **The light of Christ as evidence.** Children instantly judge the fisherman evil — "even a child understands it. They have the light of Christ" — which the panel reads as experiential disconfirmation of total depravity: if all were merely corrupt, why the spontaneous moral sense and good choices? [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Even infants bring their condemnation with them from their mother's womb. Their whole nature is, as it were, a seedbed of sin, and therefore cannot be but odious and abominable to God." (Calvin, quoted) [^1]

> "When you really understand that God is our father, then you look at everyone and you say, 'That's God's child'... That's how doctrine informs your behavior." [^2]

> "It's a hellish thing to believe that God would joy in the destruction of children. It's a partial God." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The contrast turns on the [[Anthropomorphic God Grounds Heavenly Mother and Eternal Families|literal divine parenthood]] of the Restored gospel: humans are the same *kind* as God, fallen but not depraved, with a "real nature" to return to ([[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]]). The panel still grants a steelman of depravity — Latter-day Saints affirm we are "fallen... lost without the great mediator" and unworthy of the Atonement — but insists unworthiness "has no bearing on whether he gives it," because grace is offered, not withheld ([[Grace at Gunpoint Is Not Worship|grace freely accepted]]). [[Moroni 8.12-22|Moroni 8]] and [[Infant Exaltation]] supply the doctrinal anchor; the breakdown's closing pairs [[Moses 1.10|Moses 1]] (Moses sees "his own nothingness," yet is made in the image of the Only Begotten) with King Benjamin's [[Mosiah 4.11|sermon on our nothingness]] to frame the bi-natured human as both "nothing" and divine in origin.
