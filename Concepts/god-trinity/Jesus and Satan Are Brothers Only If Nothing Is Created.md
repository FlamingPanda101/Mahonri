# Jesus and Satan Are Brothers Only If Nothing Is Created

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** "Latter-day Saints believe Jesus and Satan are brothers," which (the critic assumes) makes Jesus a *created being* on par with Lucifer — a blasphemous denial of Christ's deity.

**Rebuttal:** [[Ether's Elephant]] grants the kinship language but denies the inference. In LDS ontology *nothing* is created from nothing: God, Christ, angels, humans, and Lucifer are "ontologically the same... at different points in progression." So "brother under God the Father" does **not** entail that Christ is created — Christ is uncreated, "from eternity to eternity," exactly as orthodox Christians affirm. The critic's syllogism smuggles in *creatio ex nihilo*; remove that premise and the scandal evaporates.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The objection only works on creedal assumptions the LDS reject

The interlocutor diagnoses the problem precisely: "they're assuming that Jesus and Satan being brothers would mean that Jesus is a created being... We don't believe that, and we don't even believe that any of us are fully created from nothing." The brotherhood is a statement about a shared *Father*, not about Christ's contingency. The Book of Mormon title page is invoked: "Jesus is the eternal God."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as an internal-coherence move. It correctly identifies that the polemic imports an unshared premise. See [[God and Man Are the Same Species]] and [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]].

### 2. Same base unit, different progression — not different species

For the creedal Christian, angels are a separate created order ("a different base unit"). For Latter-day Saints, "we are all... humans, angels, demons, God... ontologically the same," differing in role and progression. On this view "brother" is literally apt for *all* God's children, Christ included as the firstborn.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Accurately states LDS ontology; the disagreement with classical theism is genuine and located at the Creator/creature distinction (cf. the steelman in [[God and Man Are the Same Species]]).

### 3. God did not author Satan's evil; agency did

Per [[Moses 4]] and [[Isaiah 14.12-15|Isaiah 14]], "God did not create evil or Satan's rebellious nature. Rather, Lucifer chose to rebel." The video marshals [[1 Corinthians 14.33|1 Cor 14:33]] ("God is not the author of confusion") and [[James 1.13|James 1:13]] ("God cannot be tempted with evil, neither tempteth he any man") to argue evil originates in free agents, not in the divine design.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong scriptural grounding for the *agency* claim; the contested step is whether *ex nihilo* creation really makes God evil's author (see steelman).

### 4. The co-eternal model is offered as the better theodicy

If God created everything from nothing, then "he would have created Satan knowing that Satan was going to rebel," even "designing into him" heightened powers for evil — "it almost makes God seem like he created evil." The co-eternal alternative keeps God innocent: "God is not creating evil... but rather evil is created by people who chose to create it," making God "a benevolent being who is there to help us combat the evil that exists external to him."[^5] This is the same engine as [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]] and connects to [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The creedal Christian answers on two fronts. (a) **Christology:** the "brothers" objection was never *only* about createdness; mainstream Christianity holds that the eternal Son is *God*, *homoousios* with the Father, not merely the senior member of a family that also contains Lucifer — to bracket Christ with Satan as fellow "spirit children" still subordinates the Son to a generic divine family and denies his unique, uncreated deity in the Nicene sense (cf. [[Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists]] for the LDS reply). (b) **Theodicy:** *ex nihilo* creation does **not** make God the author of evil — the free-will defense (Plantinga) holds that God can create genuinely free beings whose evil choices are theirs, not God's, even though God foreknew them; foreknowledge is not authorship (see [[Foreknowledge Is Not Causation Objection]]). And the LDS move arguably *relocates* rather than removes the problem: God still chose to organize *these* intelligences — including the one who would become Satan — into *this* world (see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]'s steelman and [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]]). The strongest version of the debate is therefore not "LDS coherent vs creedal incoherent," but a trade between a *constrained, innocent* God and an *omnipotent* God who permits but does not author evil.
