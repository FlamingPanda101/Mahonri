# God's Omniscience as Self-Knowledge

## The Point
Feser argues that God must have intellect and must be omniscient — knowing every truth about everything that exists or could exist, past, present, and future. [^1] Crucially, God does not acquire this knowledge by *observing* the world as history unfolds; he is not in time, has no sense organs, and cannot learn (since learning is change). [^2] Instead, "his knowledge of the world is a consequence of his self-knowledge." [^2] Just as a novelist knows his characters not by watching them but by knowing his own creative act, God knows everything by knowing himself as the timeless cause of it all. [^2]

## Evidence
1. **From the world to its cause (proportionate causality).** Universals like *redness* and *catness* are real (chapter 3), and where they do not exist in concrete things they can only exist as concepts in an intellect. Since everything depends on God's causal activity, the forms and the propositions corresponding to every state of affairs must exist in him as the content of an intellect — so God has intellect. [^3]
2. **Reliable knowledge.** On the standard account, one *knows* p when (a) one thinks p true, (b) p is true, and (c) one thinks so via a reliable process. All three hold of God analogically: he thinks the cat is on the mat, it is so *because he causes it*, and "there can be no more reliable way of determining whether some proposition p is true than being able to make it the case that it is true." [^4]
3. **He knows himself most of all.** Since God is absolutely simple — "his intellect just is his power, which just is his existence" — he cannot know less about himself than about creatures, the way a divided human author might be ignorant of his own body. [^5] Knowing all propositions about himself and everything else, "he is omniscient." [^6]
4. **The prism/dough analogy.** God is pure actuality; each creature is a limited way of being actual. Just as all colors are virtually in white light and all cookie-shapes virtually in the dough, all finite ways of being actual are virtually in God. "Perfectly to know that which is pure actuality would entail knowing all the various limited ways of being actual which might be derived from it." [^7]
5. **Not four-dimensionalism.** That God knows past, present, and future in one act does not collapse time into a static block. That inference would follow only if his knowledge were *observational* — like inferring a fired bullet must be motionless because a still hunter knows in one instant that he fired it. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "It is in knowing himself as so acting that God knows everything that is, has been, and will be. His knowledge of the world is a consequence of his self-knowledge." [^2]

> "There can be no more reliable way of determining whether some proposition p is true than being able to make it the case that it is true." [^4]

> "Perfectly to know that which is pure actuality would entail knowing all the various limited ways of being actual which might be derived from it." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism, Latter-day Saints stand with Feser: God really is omniscient, personal, and possessed of intellect — not the impersonal force or blind cosmos of naturalism. The divergence lies in the *mechanism* and *modality* of that knowledge. Feser's God knows by a single timeless act of self-knowledge precisely because he is pure act, immutable, and outside time ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]); but a God who never learns, never receives, and is in no way affected by what creatures freely do is hard to square with the embodied, temporally-acting, relational Father of the Restoration ([[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]). Some LDS thinkers locate divine foreknowledge in God's exhaustive grasp of all real possibilities and his perfect responsiveness in an unfolding present ([[Divine Foreknowledge as Real-Time Branching]]) rather than in a frozen four-dimensional block — a model Feser explicitly resists. The deeper crux is that Feser derives omniscience from divine *simplicity* (intellect = power = existence), a premise the Restoration questions ([[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]]). In fairness, Feser's self-knowledge model elegantly answers how an immaterial God could know contingents without sense organs, and his refutation of the block-universe inference is careful and apt.
