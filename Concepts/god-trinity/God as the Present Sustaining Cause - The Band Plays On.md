# God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On

## The Point
Turek's conclusion is that God is not a distant "watchmaker" who wound up the universe and stepped away, but a present, sustaining Mind whose power holds creation together "right now."[^1] The argument from final causality is a *here-and-now* dependency: the laws and forces that let you continue to exist are being directed by an intellect at this moment.[^2] His metaphor: "God is as necessary to the universe as a band is to music. Once the band stops playing, the music is over."[^3] Atheists who write books *intending* to argue there is no Mind behind reality are unwittingly "stealing" intentionality and order from God's world while denying its source.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **Sustaining, not merely originating.** Aristotle's case (which Turek adopts) is not that the universe needed a starting push but that "aspects of nature continue to be goal-directed and thus need a continuous, sustaining cause" — "a 'here and now' cause that is powerful, purposeful, and intelligent."[^5]
2. **The biological choreography you don't direct.** Right now your body builds molecular machines and replaces roughly 25 million cells per second; embryonic cells "knew" where to go to become heart or brain with "no known material explanation for their goal-directedness." "If your mind isn't consciously directing all of that goal-directed activity, then what mind is?"[^6]
3. **Stolen intentionality.** Atheists "don't seem to realize that they are displaying intentionality when they write books intending to make the case for atheism. So they unwittingly steal intentionality from God's world while claiming He doesn't exist!"[^4]
4. **Fred Hoyle's concession.** The atheist astronomer, "shaken" by fine-tuning, concluded "a superintellect has monkeyed with physics, as well as with chemistry and biology, and that there are no blind forces worth speaking about in nature."[^7]
5. **The biblical capstone.** Turek closes on the ancient texts: "In him we live and move and have our being," and "In him all things hold together" — God's power "is present to you right now."[^1]

## Best Quotes
> "God is as necessary to the universe as a band is to music. Once the band stops playing, the music is over."[^3]

> "If God were to stop holding the universe and you and me together, we would all cease to exist."[^1]

## LDS Connection
The picture of a God who continuously sustains creation, rather than a deist watchmaker who departed, fits Latter-day Saint theology well: scripture describes Christ as the light and power "which is in all things, which giveth life to all things" and by whom all things are "governed" (D&C 88), and Paul's "in him we live and move and have our being" (Acts 17:28) is read by Latter-day Saints as affirming a living, immanent God — see [[Acts 17.28-29]] and [[Humans Are the Same Species as God (Acts 17 Offspring)]]. Turek's verse [[Colossians 1.16]]–17 ("in him all things hold together") names the sustaining Christ both traditions confess. The Restoration adds that God organizes and sustains *eternal, uncreated* matter, intelligence, and spirit rather than holding in being a creation made from nothing (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]) — but the conviction that the cosmos depends, moment by moment, on a present divine Mind is shared common ground.
