# Lucifer Fell Out of Jealousy of Adam in the Apocrypha

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that a *dominant, near-unanimous* motif across the apocrypha is that **Lucifer fell because he was jealous of Adam** — God created Adam and commanded the angels to worship/honor him; Lucifer refused, and was cast out *because of Adam*. Barnes calls this "the elephant in the room" of the apocryphal corpus and a "giant checkbox" that, for years, he thought Joseph Smith had failed to check (see the companion claims [[Moses 4 and Abraham 3 Are Two Different Premortal Scenes]] and [[Abraham 3 Is the Selection of Adam Not the Redeemer]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Multiple independent texts agree Lucifer envied Adam — rate: strong (textual convergence)
Barnes reads a stack of parallels: God commands every angel to worship Adam; Lucifer says "he should come worship me"; the *Divine Investiture of St Michael* has Michael say "I will not worship him, for he is a man and I am before him"; the Cave of Treasures has Lucifer say "it is meet that he should worship me, because I am fire and spirit; he is a thing of dust"; the *Tales of the Prophets* — "I am better than Adam, you created me from fire and him from clay"; and *Vita Adae* 6:2 — "it is for thy sake, Adam, that I have been hurled from the place… I was hurled out of the presence of God."[^1] The agreement across recensions is genuine and is the strongest part of the case.

### 2. The motif is distinct from the redeemer-contest — rate: moderate (conceptual)
Crucially, the apocryphal fall is over *Adam*, not over a competition to be the savior of mankind. This is what makes it (per Barnes) a separate "checkbox" from the Latter-day Saint council-in-heaven of [[Moses 4]], where Lucifer's rebellion is over the *redeemer* role and the agency of man. The two need not be the same scene. See [[Moses 4 and Abraham 3 Are Two Different Premortal Scenes]].

### 3. A biblical echo: Revelation 12 and the Quranic refusal — rate: moderate (parallel)
[[Revelation 12.4|Revelation 12:7]]'s "war in heaven" (Michael vs. the dragon) is read as the symbolic premortal conflict, and the Islamic tradition's *Iblis refuses to bow to Adam* is the same envy-of-Adam motif in another stream — evidence the theme is broad and old, not a one-off.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The motif is real but its *interpretation* is where the work happens. (a) **The Adam-worship theme has an orthodox reading.** In its native (Jewish and Christian) settings, the angels' refusal to "worship" Adam is usually about *Adam as the image of God* — honoring the image, with Satan's pride as the sin — not a Latter-day Saint premortal genealogy in which Adam/Michael is a quasi-divine first man. Reading LDS metaphysics into it imports a framework the texts need not share (the same caution the vault applies in [[The Divine Investiture of St Michael Calls Lucifer the First-Formed]]). (b) **Dating and direction.** Many of these texts are late (the *Vita Adae* recensions, Cave of Treasures, Tales of the Prophets span the 4th–10th centuries and the Islamic material later still), so they are at best witnesses to *post-biblical* Jewish-Christian-Islamic legend, not to primitive revelation Joseph could be "matching." (c) **Selective harmonization.** The same corpus contains much that is *not* LDS-friendly; lifting the congenial envy-of-Adam thread is parallel-hunting. The disciplined conclusion: there *is* a widespread ancient tradition that Satan fell in connection with Adam, which is a striking and under-appreciated datum; whether it vindicates Joseph Smith depends entirely on the further, contested re-reading of Abraham 3 (next note).
