#type/claim

# The Four Constellations of Deification Belief

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]]'s framing device for the whole deification debate is that **four distinct "constellations of beliefs"** are in play and routinely conflated: (1) the **Mormon** constellation (Jacob Hansen's), (2) the **contemporary evangelical** constellation (Allie Beth Stuckey's), (3) the beliefs **in the biblical literature** itself, and (4) the beliefs **in early Judaism and early Christianity**. None of the four is a monolith; each has *synchronic* (same-period internal) and *diachronic* (across-time) variation. The error he diagnoses in the Hansen–Stuckey debate is that the two living constellations are arguing with *each other* while neither is "adequately representing either the biblical or the early Jewish and early Christian constellations." This is McClellan acting as a **foil to both sides**: he is not defending Mormonism, but he insists the Mormon and the evangelical are each subordinating the ancient data to a modern dogma. It is the deification-specific application of his standing critique — [[Putting Dogma Over Data]] and [[Univocality Forces Centering Aligned Texts and Marginalizing the Rest]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The four are named and kept separate as a method.** "There are four different constellations of beliefs that are going to be relevant to this discussion… Jacob is representing a Latter-day Saint constellation… Allie is representing a contemporary evangelical constellation… Also relevant are the beliefs that we find manifested in the biblical literature. And then the beliefs we find manifested within early Judaism and early Christianity."[^1] *Rating: strong (definitional).* The taxonomy is the load-bearing move; everything downstream is sorted into one of the four.

2. **Each constellation changes over time (diachrony) and internally (synchrony).** "None of these four is a monolith. They all have synchronic as well as diachronic change and differentiation."[^2] *Rating: strong.* This is what licenses his later claim that *biblical* deification and *4th–5th-century* deification are different things (see [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]]).

3. **The debate is a category error.** "What we have in this video is really the Mormon constellation arguing with the evangelical constellation — none of which are adequately representing either the biblical or the early Jewish and early Christian constellations."[^3] *Rating: strong as analysis.* It reframes the disagreement: the question is not "who wins" but "whose modern dogma is being read back into antiquity."

4. **Both interlocutors are charged with the same sin.** McClellan says Stuckey is "putting dogma over data," and that Hansen "is kind of doing the same thing, just unwittingly sharing how he has experienced talking about this with other Mormons without realizing he needs to… actually address the Bible on its own terms."[^4] *Rating: strong.* The symmetry is the point — the framework cuts against the LDS apologist too, which is what makes it a genuine foil rather than a partisan tool.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A confessional reader (LDS or evangelical) can object that this taxonomy quietly privileges a fifth, unnamed constellation — McClellan's own historical-critical, non-confessional reconstruction — as the neutral arbiter of "what the data really say," when it is itself a tradition with commitments (methodological naturalism, a particular dating and source-critical consensus). On that view "the biblical constellation on its own terms" is not a view from nowhere but McClellan's own constellation wearing a lab coat. The reply is that the four-constellation move is *falsifiable and comparative*: it does not claim presuppositionlessness, only that a reading earned across many texts by a tested method is preferable to one fixed in advance by a creed or a couplet, and that anyone can check the priority-ranking against the evidence (cf. the same defense in [[Putting Dogma Over Data]]).

## LDS Relevance

McClellan is not making an LDS argument and explicitly faults the LDS debater — yet the framework is double-edged in a way that helps the careful Latter-day Saint. **Where it cuts against LDS apologetics:** it forbids the move of citing 4th-century fathers (Athanasius, Cyril) or the modern "gods of their own planets" folk-summary as if they were the biblical datum; the Mormon constellation must not be smuggled in as "what the Bible says" (see [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]]; [[LDS Should Lead With John 10 Not Revelation 3 or Patristics]]). **Where it helps:** the same taxonomy demolishes the evangelical assumption that *its* strict monotheism is the biblical baseline. If "no other gods exist" is a post-biblical renegotiation and the biblical/early-Jewish constellations affirmed a divine plurality into which humans could be elevated, then the LDS Godhead-and-deification picture sits *closer* to constellations (3) and (4) than the creedal picture does — a result the vault develops at [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]] and [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]]. The honest LDS gain is methodological, not a free win: argue the ancient data, not the modern dogma.
