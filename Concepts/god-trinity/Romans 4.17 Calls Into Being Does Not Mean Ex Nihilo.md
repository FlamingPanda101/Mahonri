# Romans 4:17 "Calls Into Being" Does Not Mean Ex Nihilo

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Paul Copan]] and [[William Lane Craig]] cite [[Romans 4.17|Romans 4:17]] — God "calleth those things which be not as though they were" (καλοῦντος τὰ μὴ ὄντα ὡς ὄντα) — as a New Testament proof-text for creation out of nothing. [[Blake Ostler]] argues it teaches no such thing, for four converging reasons of grammar, vocabulary, and context.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The verb is καλέω ("to call/summon"), not "to create."** The word *create* does not appear in the verse at all. καλέω means "to call out loud to something, or to invite," and it **presupposes something there to be called to or invited.** God "calls forth" the non-existent by his Word — the same calling-language Paul uses elsewhere (Romans 9:11; 1 Cor 12:3; Gal 5:8; 1 Thess 5:24). One cannot summon or invite sheer nothing.[^2]

2. **The negative is μή, marking relative non-being.** Romans 4:17 uses τὰ **μὴ** ὄντα, not τὰ **οὐκ** ὄντα. As the Greek philosophical tradition used it, μή ὄντα refers to "the previous non-existence of those things which are now brought into existence... There is no direct reference to the absence or presence of a material cause" (Hubler).[^3] See [[Relative Non-Being vs Absolute Nothing - The Greek Distinction Copan and Craig Miss]].

3. **The context is resurrection, which is re-organization, not creation from nothing.** The verse pairs "gives life to the dead" with "calls the things that are not." Just as resurrecting the dead does not presuppose the dead utterly cease to exist or never had bodies, God "calling the things that are not" does not presuppose creation from absolute nonbeing.[^4]

4. **The majority translation is non-creational.** The preferred reading is that God "summons the things that do not yet exist as though they already do" — i.e., God speaks of the future reality (the "many nations" promised to Abraham) as if already present. Douglas Moo: this "fits the immediate context better than a reference to God's creative power." Keith Norman adds that it is contradictory for God to *call to* that which does not exist.[^5]

Ostler's analogy: "If I create a table then I create a table that did not exist before I created it, but it doesn't mean that I create it out of nothing."[^6]

## Supporting Evidence (Copan & Craig, steelmanned)

Copan and Craig marshal a row of evangelical commentators (James Dunn, and others Ostler lists) who read the verse as creation without precondition. Dunn: "As creator he creates without any precondition... he calls into existence where there was nothing at all. Consequently that which has been created... must be totally dependent on the creator."[^7] The New American Bible itself renders it "calls into existence the things which do not exist." On this reading, Paul's whole point is God's unconditioned creative sovereignty, of which raising the dead and bringing nations from a barren couple are instances — and "the things that are not" most naturally means things with no being whatsoever.

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

Ostler answers that these commentators "provide [no] analysis or exegesis beyond asserting that the 'non-existent' must mean that which does not exist in any sense" — they assume the point at issue.[^8] The resurrection pairing actively undercuts them: resurrection is the paradigm of God acting on something that already exists in some mode. And the verse simply does not address the *material* question; it says God *calls* (not *creates*) the *μή*-existent (not the *οὐκ*-existent). Gerhard May agrees the formula fits Hellenistic-Jewish "creation out of non-being" language and means "no more than... creation out of nothing in the strict sense" was never in view.[^9]

## Best Quotes

> "The most natural reading of this text is that the 'non-existent' or μὴ ὄντα refers to a preexisting reality that does not yet exist as God calls it to be. Such a reading has nothing to do with creation out of absolute nothing." — Blake Ostler[^6]

> "The verse's 'non-existent' need not be understood in an absolute sense of non-being. μὴ ὄντα refers to the previous non-existence of those things which are now brought into existence. There is no direct reference to the absence or presence of a material cause." — James Hubler[^3]
