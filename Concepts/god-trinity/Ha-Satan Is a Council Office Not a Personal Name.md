# Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the "secondary use" of *satan* with the **definite article** — *ha-satan*, "**the** accuser" — refers to a divine-council *office* (a prosecutor who works for God), not a being named Satan. It occurs 14 times in [[Job 1]] (Job 1–2) and 3 times in [[Zechariah 3]], and in both it is "not a divine being named Satan... a divine being who is functioning as the divine council's *satan*."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The article marks a title, not a name (rated: strong, grammatical).** Proper names in Hebrew do not normally take the definite article; *ha-satan* ("THE satan") functions like "the accuser" — a description of a role. This is a "later elaboration on the notion of a generic accuser" (see [[Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary]]).[^1]

2. **He works *for* God within the council (rated: strong).** In Job, *ha-satan* is "someone who works for God... likely there to kind of make sure God's rules are followed and to point out when people are not following God's rules," requiring divine permission to act. He is a prosecuting member of the heavenly court, not God's cosmic enemy — "although the precise job description is a little fuzzy."[^1]

3. **Same office in Zechariah (rated: strong).** In Zechariah 3 *ha-satan* stands to accuse the high priest Joshua and is rebuked by Yahweh — again a courtroom accuser within the council, not a rebel leading other angels.[^1] This is the picture catalogued in [[Satan - Development of the Figure]].

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A conservative can argue that an office and a being are not mutually exclusive: the *ha-satan* of Job could be the same malevolent agent later named Satan, performing an accusatory function here — the grammar shows he is *called* "the accuser," not that he lacks a name elsewhere. The cynical, faithless tenor of his speeches in Job (inciting calamity against a blameless man) arguably already shades toward the adversary of later tradition. McClellan's reply is that the text presents a *sanctioned* council functionary acting on God's permission, with no hint of rebellion; the rebel-Satan is read *in* from later development, not found in Job or Zechariah.

## Apologetic Engagement
This blocks the common apologetic claim that Job "proves" a fully-personal Satan in the oldest layers of scripture. The accuser of Job is a court officer under God's authority — the opposite of an autonomous cosmic enemy. Any argument that leans on Job's *ha-satan* as evidence for the developed devil must first explain away the definite article and the council setting.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan is non-confessional and draws no Latter-day Saint conclusion. The finding aligns with the data behind [[The Old Testament Barely Mentions Satan]] — the OT lacks the developed adversary — which Latter-day Saints reframe as loss-and-restoration: the premortal-council rebellion ([[Moses 4]]; cf. the council scene in Abraham 3) is supplied by Restoration scripture. Notably, the LDS reading of a *premortal council* resonates with the Hebrew divine-council backdrop McClellan describes, even though he treats the rebel-Satan as a later innovation rather than restored truth.
