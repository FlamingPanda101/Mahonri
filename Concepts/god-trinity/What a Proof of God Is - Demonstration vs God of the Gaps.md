# What a Proof of God Is - Demonstration vs God of the Gaps

## The Point
Feser insists that the arguments he defends are genuine **demonstrations** — strict, deductive proofs in the tradition of natural theology, not probabilistic inferences to a "god of the gaps." [^1] Natural theology asks what can be known about God's existence and nature "via unaided human reason, apart from divine revelation." [^2] His claim is bold: for over twenty-three centuries a long line of thinkers — "Aristotelians, Neo-Platonists, Thomists and other Scholastics, early modern rationalists" among "pagans, Jews, Christians, Muslims, or philosophical theists" — held that God's existence "can be rationally demonstrated by purely philosophical arguments," and they were right. [^3] The neglect of these arguments tells us "nothing about the proofs themselves and everything about the state of contemporary academic philosophy." [^4]

## Evidence
- This is explicitly **not** a book on Aquinas's Five Ways; only two of the five proofs are found in Aquinas, and Feser presents them in his own form rather than by textual exegesis. [^5]
- Each proof is argued in two stages: stage 1 establishes "the existence of something fitting a certain key description" (e.g., "an uncaused cause of the existence of things"); stage 2 shows that anything fitting that description must have the "key divine attributes" — unity, eternity, immateriality, omnipotence, omniscience, perfect goodness. [^6]
- A demonstration is not a "god of the gaps" appeal to present scientific ignorance; the conclusion follows necessarily from premises about the nature of change and causality, not from what science has failed to explain.
- The real reorientation Feser urges: "The real debate is not between atheism and theism. The real debate is between theists of different stripes" and "begins where natural theology leaves off." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "A long line of thinkers from the beginnings of Western thought down to the present day ... have affirmed that God's existence can be rationally demonstrated by purely philosophical arguments. The aim of this book is to show that they were right." [^3]

> "The real debate is not between atheism and theism. The real debate is between theists of different stripes ... and begins where natural theology leaves off." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Feser's project is a formidable ally against atheism: it claims not merely that theism is probable but that God's existence is rationally **demonstrable**, and it refuses the weak "god of the gaps" posture that secularists love to attack (compare [[God Is Not a God of the Gaps - Henry Ford vs the Laws of Internal Combustion]]). Latter-day Saints can welcome the conclusion that a necessary, sustaining cause of all things exists. The divergence is precisely Feser's own framing of "where natural theology leaves off": he assumes the demonstrated being is the God of *classical theism* (simple, immutable, timeless, purely actual). The Restoration parts company there — the deepest debate, in Feser's own words "between theists of different stripes," is exactly where LDS theology contests the classical attributes (see [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]] and the critique [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]). On method and on the bare existence of a first cause, Feser is a strong friend; the disagreement is over the *nature* the proof allegedly delivers.
