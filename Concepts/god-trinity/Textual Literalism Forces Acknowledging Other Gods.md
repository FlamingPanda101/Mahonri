# Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] uses the Bible's acknowledgement of rival deities as the cleanest proof that self-described literalists are not textual literalists. A genuinely plain-sense reading "results in acknowledging contradiction... [and] perspectives and facts... that don't line up with reality" — including texts that "very clearly acknowledge other gods." [^1][^2] Because inerrantist dogma forbids polytheism, those texts get relabeled: "I guess it's all literal until that point, in which case those are just demons, or that's the devil." [^3] The literal switch is flipped off exactly where doctrine requires.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **[[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3]] reports Yahweh's defeat.** "This is not poetry. This is not a psalm. This is the author... in a slightly reluctant and embarrassed way acknowledging that Adonai got beat by the god of another nation" (Chemosh of Moab). [^4] The plain sense is a territorial-deity defeat. See [[Chemosh Defeats Yahweh Outside His Purview]]. **(Strong — 2 Kings 3:27 is a classic monolatry crux.)**
2. **Exodus credits Egyptian gods with real power.** "You've got all of Exodus talking about all of the Egyptian gods and how they're performing miracles just like Adonai has done" — the magicians replicate Yahweh's signs ([[Exodus 7.9-12|Exod 7:11-12]]). [^5] A literal reading concedes rival supernatural agency. **(Strong.)**
3. **The relabeling tell.** Where the text names other *elohim* (e.g. the [[Deuteronomy 32.17|shedim]]), the literalist suddenly turns figurative-or-demonic. This selective non-literalism is the diagnostic of *ideological* literalism. See [[Shedim Are Foreign Deities Not Demons]] and [[Literalism Is Ideological Not Textual]]. **(Strong.)**
4. **Steelman.** The inerrantist replies that "gods" in these texts are real but *created* spiritual beings (demons / the divine council under Yahweh), not deities ontologically on par with Yahweh — so affirming their existence is not polytheism, and the New Testament's demonization of pagan gods (1 Cor 10:20) is a canonical, not ad hoc, reading. McClellan's rebuttal: the *demon* reinterpretation is precisely the post-biblical, tradition-driven move — the Hebrew authors present these as the gods of the nations with genuine purview, and reading them as "demons" imposes a later theology the text does not carry. See [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]]. **(Moderate-to-strong — the disagreement is about whether "demon" is exegesis or harmonization.)**
