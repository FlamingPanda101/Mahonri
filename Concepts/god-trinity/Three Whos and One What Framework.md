# Three Whos and One What Framework

## The Claim
The entire Trinity debate can be clarified by a single taxonomy: there are **three "whos" (persons) and one "what."** Both Latter-day Saints and Trinitarians agree the word *God* can refer to any one of the three persons *or* to one "what." The only real disagreement is the nature of that one "what": Trinitarians say the one "what" is a single *being*; Latter-day Saints say it is a single *entity* — the **Godhead**. The burden of proof is on the Trinitarian to show from scripture that "one God" means "one being."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Both sides actually agree on the data — only the "what" is disputed
[[Jacob Hansen]] lays out in his opening that the word "God" is **not univocal**: it can name a person ("a who") or the one "what." Trinitarians, he says, will grant "three whos and one what"; LDS grant the same. The fight is solely over whether that "what" = a single being (Trinity) or a single Godhead/entity (LDS). *(Strong: this is a genuine clarification both sides can affirm; it reframes the debate as definitional rather than scriptural.)* [^1]

### 2. "One God" verses do not settle the question
When critics cite [[James 2.19|James 2:19]] ("thou believest that there is one God"), the Shema, or [[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Cor 8:4]], Hansen answers: "I know — we call that one *what* the Godhead." A verse affirming numerical oneness of *God* is compatible with both models, so it cannot adjudicate between them. *(Moderate-strong, but note the Trinitarian rejoinder below.)* [^2]

### 3. The Trinitarian conflation
Hansen charges that Trinitarians **conflate the categories** — sliding from "one God" to "one being" without specifying whether "being" means a *who* or a *what*, and without the scriptural argument that would license it. If "being" is a *who*, then only one of the three could be God; if it's a *what*, LDS already agree. [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The strongest Trinitarian reply, voiced by [[Go With Jordan]] and reported on the stream, is that this framework smuggles in a *non-biblical* "what." Jordan objects: "A court is not a being," and demands that the LDS "Godhead-as-entity" be shown in scripture just as much as the Trinitarian "one being." On the Trinitarian side, *homoousios* is precisely the claim that the one "what" is a *being* (essence) numerically shared — and they argue texts like the Shema and Genesis's singular subject with plural speech encode exactly that. The debate therefore does not dissolve into pure semantics; each side still owes an argument that its "what" fits the Hebrew text (see [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible]] and [[Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge]]).

## Best Quotes
> "They would agree there are three who's and one what. I say yes, there's three who's and one what. The question is — what is the what?" [^1]

> "You have one what, we have three whos... what they do, they conflate the categories to try and say that they are the same being without defining if by a being they mean a what or a who." [^3]

> "The word God can refer to any of the individual three persons, or can refer to the one what — the institution, the Godhead itself." [^4]
