# If God Exists, Miracles Are Possible

## The Point
The third point of the four-point case is that miracles are possible — and Turek argues this follows necessarily once God's existence is granted.[^1] "If there is a God who created the universe out of nothing, then He can do whatever He wants that's not logically impossible inside the universe."[^1] The greatest miracle in the Bible, he says, is not Jonah, the Virgin Birth, water-walking, or even the Resurrection — it is the very first verse, "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth." If that verse is true, every other miracle is "at least believable."[^1]

## Evidence
Turek answers three standard objections to miracles.[^2] First, the claim that miracles "violate the laws of nature" misunderstands those laws: the laws are mathematical *descriptions* of what normally happens when nature is left to itself; they don't cause anything. The four fundamental forces can be overpowered by intelligent agents — catching a falling baseball overpowers gravity without "breaking" any law. If humans can overpower natural forces, so can the God who made them.[^2] Second, the objection that we "don't see biblical miracles today" misunderstands their purpose: the primary purpose of miracles is to *confirm new revelation* ("signs" confirming the message), which is why they cluster around Moses, Elijah/Elisha, and Jesus and the apostles. With the biblical revelation complete, that primary purpose has lapsed — though God can still act narrowly (healing, visions).[^3] Third, miracles must be *rare* to achieve their effect: if resurrections were common, no one would notice them as acts of God. Even in the Bible there are only about 250 miracles spread over ~2,000 years.[^4] He grounds the whole case in the creation itself: since good scientific and philosophical evidence shows the universe began (implying a Beginner with the attributes of a theistic God), "the existence of the universe itself is evidence of a miracle" — so anti-supernatural bias is unreasonable.[^5] He cites C. S. Lewis: "If we admit God, must we admit miracle? Indeed, you have no security against it. That is the bargain."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "If there is a God who created the universe out of nothing, then He can do whatever He wants that's not logically impossible inside the universe. Jonah, water walking, and resurrections are easy for that kind of Being." [^1]

> "A God who has the power to create the universe certainly has the power to intervene in it." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints wholeheartedly affirm the possibility and reality of miracles. The Book of Mormon teaches that God "is a God of miracles" and that miracles cease "only" through unbelief (Mormon 9:15–20; Moroni 7:35–37), and the LDS movement was founded on the conviction that the heavens are open and that signs follow faith. Turek's point that miracles primarily *confirm new revelation* resonates strongly with the Restoration: Saints would gladly extend his own logic — if a God who creates can intervene, He can also speak again and authenticate that word with signs, rather than sealing revelation forever in the first century. Where Turek argues the canon is complete and so sign-miracles have largely lapsed, Saints respectfully diverge: living prophets and continuing revelation mean the confirming purpose of miracles is ongoing, not historical only.
