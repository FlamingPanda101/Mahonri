# The Intuitive Personal God Points Back to the Family of Heaven

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues that the **creedal abstract God** — God as Plato's "form of goodness" and Aristotle's **unmoved mover** — *cannot actually be prayed to*, and that the universal human intuition of God as a **Father who hears and responds** is itself evidence: it is "born into our souls" because our **origin is in the family of God.** The point is devotional-anthropological rather than metaphysical: nobody "in the foxhole" relates to an abstraction, so the God people actually pray to (a personal, relational kinsman) is the older and truer one, recoverable in the Hebrew Bible.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. You cannot pray to the unmoved mover (rating: strong as a phenomenological point)
An **unmoved mover** by definition cannot be moved, so "prayer to an unmoved mover conceptually can't have any impact — your prayer cannot affect God at all." Yet "someone whose child is desperately sick, who's praying to God, is not going, 'Ah, but that unmoved mover will not be moved by my tears.'" The lived practice of petition contradicts the abstraction. [^1] This is the *devotional* counterpart to the metaphysical critique in [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]].

### 2. A corporeal, personal God can be reasoned with — and Scripture shows it (rating: moderate; exegetical)
Unlike an impersonal "universe" or Thanos-like inevitability, "a personal, corporeal God... you can actually reason with." The Old Testament repeatedly shows it: **Abraham bargains** with God over how many righteous would save Sodom ("What if I find twenty, Lord? What if there are ten?"). You cannot negotiate with something that "cannot move." [^2]

### 3. The intuition is inborn and points to origin (rating: moderate; LDS application)
The sense that "God is a being we can talk to and who cares... is intuitive to us, it's born into our souls," and "the idea that gods are beings like us, and that we can communicate with them, is very satisfying." Butler reads this not as wishful projection but as a **pointer**: "your intuitive sense that God is someone you can relate to and who will respond to you is a pointer at the fact that your origin is in the family of God, and your destiny is to return to the family of God." [^3] [^4]

### 4. Even credal Christians pray to the personal God (rating: moderate; observational)
Butler notes that pastors may hold the creed while parishioners "focus on the relationship" — and that on strict credal terms (salvation by *correct* understanding of God) such people would be condemned for "having the wrong idea," which almost no one actually believes. From his Italian mission: people said *credente ma non praticante* and prayed to figures "they feel like they can relate to," because "nobody really has a relationship with that abstraction." The personal God keeps reasserting itself. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Classical theism replies that petitionary prayer is fully coherent without a "movable" God: God timelessly **ordains** that certain outcomes occur *in response to* prayers he eternally foreknew — prayer is a real secondary cause, not a lever that changes an immutable will. Anthropomorphic descriptions (God "relenting," "hearing") are accommodations to human understanding, and the felt intimacy of prayer argues for God's *personal* nature (which classical theism affirms) but not for a *finite, embodied, family-embedded* deity. The intuition of a caring Father is satisfied by Trinitarian fatherhood-by-adoption. Butler's reply: those moves are exactly the "crossing your fingers" abstraction-talk that the praying heart ignores; the simplest account of the God people *actually* pray to is the relational kinsman of [[God Is the Divine Kinsman and Goel-Redeemer]], from whose family humans came.
