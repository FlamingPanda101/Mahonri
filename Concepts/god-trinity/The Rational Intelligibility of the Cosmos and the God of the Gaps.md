# The Rational Intelligibility of the Cosmos and the God of the Gaps

## The Point
[[John Lennox]] argues that the design inference is *not* a god-of-the-gaps move and that theism, far from being science's rival, is its foundation. The deepest issue is not the maths but the **rational intelligibility of the universe**: every scientist is "a person of faith… they believe in the rational intelligibility of the universe," a conviction science assumes but cannot itself supply.[^1] Modern science was born from this belief — "men became scientific… because they expected law in nature, and they expected law in nature because they believed in the legislator."[^2] The real obstacle to following the evidence to a designer is "the domination of naturalism and materialism in the academy," a prior worldview commitment that rules a Creator out of bounds before the evidence is weighed.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Mechanism is not agency (the Ford analogy).** Dawkins' "huge confusion" is treating the God-explanation as a competitor to the scientific one; that is "like saying that the Henry Ford explanation for a motor car is the same as the explanation in terms of physics and automobile engineering." Different *kinds* of explanation are needed for a full account.[^4]
2. **Newton did not stop at "God did it."** On discovering gravitation, Newton "didn't say, now I've got a scientific explanation, I don't need God"; he wrote the *Principia* hoping his research would "lead thinking people to believe in God," reading the fine-tuned solar system as evidence of "an intelligent and powerful being."[^5]
3. **Worldview, not data, sets the limits.** "If I put on my atheist hat… write me an account of the origin of life, I will come up with an evolutionary theory immediately, because that is the only possibility allowed by the naturalistic worldview." The "looming specter… of a coder… that's out of bounds" only because of methodological naturalism.[^6]
4. **Follow the evidence where it leads.** Like Antony Flew — who concluded "there was an intelligent designer… on the basis of DNA" — Lennox insists science is *limited*, not co-extensive with rationality: "history is a rational discipline, so's philosophy." To presume naturalism is to "imprison yourself" in it.[^7]
5. **Science founded by believers.** Oxford's motto *Dominus illuminatio mea* reflects that "the people that founded the great universities… had no problem with the idea of an intelligent designer"; the hostility to design betrays "the spirit that lies behind the modern science that these universities claim to teach."[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "All mathematicians and scientists are people of faith, not necessarily in God, but they believe in the rational intelligibility of the universe." [^1]

> "Men became scientific… because they expected law in nature, and they expected law in nature because they believed in the legislator." (Lennox, quoting C. S. Lewis) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Lennox's claim that rationality and the universe's intelligibility presuppose a mind has firm footing in Latter-day Saint metaphysics, where intelligence is uncreated and mind is not reducible to matter — see the closely related [[Atheism Undercuts the Rationality Science Requires]] and [[The Argument from Reason - Atheism Undermines Reason Itself]]. The "not a gap" point also guards LDS natural theology from the god-of-the-gaps trap: God is inferred as the ground of an *ordered, law-governed* cosmos, not merely as a stopgap for present ignorance. The usual caveat holds — this reasoning establishes a designing/legislating mind, not yet the specific embodied, organizing God of the Restoration.
