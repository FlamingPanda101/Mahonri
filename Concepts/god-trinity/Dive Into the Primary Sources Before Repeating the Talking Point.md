# Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point

## The Point
A recurring shape of online anti-Mormon argument is the **recycled secondhand talking point** — a claim passed around the ex-Mormon subreddit and the CES Letter genre that few who repeat it have checked against the primary record. The panel's prescription: when someone says "I know too much about church history to believe," the answer is usually "No, you don't — dive in deeper." Going to the journals, the dating, and the original documents repeatedly dissolves the slogan. The "Joseph was a Trinitarian" claim is the case study: it survives only at slogan depth and collapses at Doctrine and Covenants 76 (Feb 1832).[^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The pattern named.** "There's always the… ex-Mormon subreddit anti-Mormon trope out there that's recycled… and it's like, 'No, no, go find the primary sources. Go look up the journal entries… Go read the actual literature on it,' and you'll start finding out it's not [what the slogan says]."[^1]
2. **The case in point.** The Trinitarian-Joseph claim depends on a tidy timeline (same-being early, separate-beings mid-1830s) that is falsified by reading one dated primary source — D&C 76. See [[D&C 76 Dates the Distinct-Persons View to Early 1832]].[^2]
3. **A critic who actually dove in reversed himself.** When John Dehlin engaged the actual document instead of the slogan, he conceded the talking point was "incomplete or possibly misleading." The method works even on its opponents. See [[The Hostile Witness - John Dehlin Conceded the Trinitarian-Joseph Talking Point]].[^3]
4. **Why the slogan persists anyway.** The panel notes that demonstrably false items (e.g., a crowdsourced Book of Mormon geography map "everyone knows is garbage") get *kept* because they "broke my shelf" — i.e., emotional and rhetorical utility, not accuracy, sustains them. This is the asymmetry described in [[The Internet Rewards the Short Lie Over the Long Truth]]: a one-line claim travels faster than the paragraph of primary-source context needed to answer it.[^4]
5. **Steelman / self-check.** The same discipline cuts both ways, and a host says so: apologists must not "do the same thing in reverse," assigning causality or imputing motive without evidence — that "makes it very easy for [a critic] to just debunk." The principle is *check the sources*, not *assume the other side is lying*; an apologetic that skips the homework or substitutes character attacks for evidence fails its own test.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Whenever anybody says, like, 'Oh, I just know too much about church history to believe,' I always say, 'No, you don't. Dive in deeper.'… Go find the primary sources. Go look at the journal entries… and you'll start finding out it's not [the slogan]." [^1]

> "We got to be careful here that we're not doing the same thing in reverse… assigning causality… Let's not play the reverse game." [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is a methodological habit more than a single doctrine, and it underwrites much of LDS apologetics: claims about the Book of Mormon, the First Vision, and Joseph Smith's life tend to look strongest in slogan form and weakest under primary-source scrutiny (cf. [[D&C 76 Dates the Distinct-Persons View to Early 1832]], [[Book of Mormon Christology Edits Are Not Evidence of Trinitarian Backpedaling]]). For a believer, the takeaway is twofold: do not be intimidated by confident secondhand claims, and hold your own side to the same primary-source standard.
