# The Hostile Witness - John Dehlin Conceded the Trinitarian-Joseph Talking Point

## The Point
The "Joseph Smith was originally a Trinitarian" argument is a staple of the CES Letter genre. Its evidential force is unusually undercut by the fact that one of its **most prominent former proponents conceded it**. After watching an Informed Saints episode citing Doctrine and Covenants 76, John Dehlin (Mormon Stories) publicly posted that the passage "neutralize[s] many of the concerns" about the 1832-vs-1838 First Vision discrepancy and admitted his own retelling "has been incomplete or possibly misleading." A concession from a hostile witness — someone with every incentive to keep the talking point alive — is strong evidence the point was weak to begin with. Ward Radio dubs Dehlin "the most unlikely apologist."[^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The concession, in his own words.** Dehlin: "I will admit that after all these years, I should understand this issue better, but I clearly don't. And I'm concerned that the way I've been telling the story has been incomplete or possibly misleading. So… where do I have things wrong?"[^2]
2. **He grants the specific rebuttal.** Dehlin acknowledges D&C 76 "was dictated in 1832" and that "at a minimum, they show that in early 1832, Joseph was certainly capable of describing God and Jesus as appearing separately in a vision," then asks "don't these passages sort of neutralize many of the concerns" about the First Vision accounts.[^1]
3. **Why a hostile-witness admission carries weight.** The panel's framing: critics who say "I just know too much about church history to believe" rarely have engaged the primary sources; when one actually does, the talking point can collapse from the inside. This is the evidential logic of an admission against interest — it is costly to the person making it, so it is credible. See [[Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point]].[^3]
4. **Steelman / caution.** Dehlin only conceded that the *specific First Vision discrepancy* argument is weaker than he had presented; he did not renounce his broader critique, and one host explicitly warns the panel not to over-read it or impute motives ("makes it very easy for him to just debunk… how can you trust the rest that they say?"). The clean apologetic use is narrow and powerful: *on this point*, a leading critic agrees the rebuttal lands. Inflating it into "a critic admitted the church is true" overreaches and invites the very motive-imputation the host flags.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "I will admit that after all these years, I should understand this issue better, but I clearly don't. And I'm concerned that the way I've been telling the story has been incomplete or possibly misleading." — John Dehlin, quoted by the panel [^2]

> "At a minimum, they show that in early 1832, Joseph was certainly capable of describing God and Jesus as appearing separately in a vision… don't these passages sort of neutralize many of the concerns voiced with the discrepancies between 1832 and 1838 first vision accounts?" — John Dehlin, quoted by the panel [^1]

## LDS Connection
The point reinforces a recurring apologetic theme: many of the loudest "church history" objections are recycled secondhand and do not survive contact with the primary sources (cf. [[Book of Mormon Christology Edits Are Not Evidence of Trinitarian Backpedaling]] and [[First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels]]). Used carefully — as a narrow concession on the dating, not a conversion story — it is a memorable example for a believer fielding the "Joseph evolved his Godhead" objection.
