# Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the "is and is not God" paradox of the messenger of Yahweh — and, downstream, of Jesus — needs no Trinity to resolve it, because the ancient world already operated with a different model of personhood. On this **cognitive** account, "personhood is partible and permeable, and… vehicles of agency are communicable and can inhabit other entities."[^1] A deity's presence, power, and authority could be borne by a material or animate medium located elsewhere — the **logic of divine images** — so an entity can manifest a god, be addressed as that god, and remain a distinct person. This is a substantively different argument from the *application* of divine-image logic to specific verses (see [[Divine Images Explain Jesus Relationship to God]]): here the focus is the underlying **mechanism** — partible/permeable personhood — and the claim that it is intuitive, cross-cultural, and *still operative today*.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The mechanism: partible, permeable personhood — rating: strong (cognitive anthropology)
McClellan's framework, developed in his open-access monograph *YHWH's Divine Images*, treats personhood not as a bounded individual essence but as something distributable: agency can be lodged in another vehicle without identifying the two persons. "It's the notion that personhood is partible and permeable and that vehicles of agency are communicable and can inhabit other entities such as headstones, which in form and function are virtually identical to divine images."[^1] *Rating: strong within the cognitive science of religion; it reframes the whole debate by denying the Trinitarian's tacit "one person = one bounded self" premise.*

### 2. The headstone-in-the-cemetery proof — rating: persuasive (it shows the logic is live, not exotic)
The framework is "so intuitive that it still goes on today. If you've ever seen someone in a cemetery speaking to a headstone as if they were speaking to the deceased loved one themselves, you have seen the logic of divine images in action."[^2] The headstone is treated as a vehicle that makes an absent person present — "in form and function… virtually identical to divine images." *Rating: a strong rhetorical and phenomenological point; it makes the ancient logic recognizable rather than alien, defusing the intuition that the messenger-paradox requires special metaphysics.*

### 3. The ancient evidence: cult images and the king as ṣalmu — rating: strong (ANE studies)
The logic is attested across Mesopotamia, Egypt, Anatolia, Greece, and Rome.[^3] Kings in Mesopotamia "are sometimes described as the *ṣalmu*, the image of a deity" — the divine image of a given god. In the Hebrew Bible the messenger/angel is "a sentient walking-talking divine image," the same category but animate rather than a statue.[^4] *Rating: strong — the divine-image institution is well documented, and the king-as-ṣalmu datum anchors the "animate image" extension.*

### 4. The activation is the divine name — rating: strong (textual)
What endows the image/agent is the indwelling name: [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20-21]], "my name is in him," lets the messenger exercise an exclusively divine prerogative (withholding forgiveness; cf. Joshua 24:19).[^5] In [[Exodus 3.6|Exodus 3:6]] the messenger says "I am the God of your father." The name is "a communicable vehicle of divine agency, authority and presence."[^5] *Rating: strong; ties the cognitive model to a concrete scriptural mechanism.* See [[Name-Bearing Agency in Exodus 23]] and [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology]].

### Steelman (the Trinitarian reply)
A defender can grant partible-personhood as the *category the texts use* yet deny it exhausts the New Testament's claims about Jesus. Cult statues and angelic agents *manifest* a deity but are not *worshiped as* that deity and are firmly *creatures*; Jesus, by contrast, receives worship he does not refuse, shares the divine throne and the creator-role, and is confessed as "my Lord and my God" ([[John 20.28|John 20:28]]). On a Bauckham-style reading, the divine-image stream is the *vehicle* through which the NT expresses something stronger — inclusion of the Son *within* the unique identity of God — so partible personhood explains the *idiom* without settling the *ontology*. The Trinitarian also notes that "permeable personhood" is a descriptive anthropological model, not itself a normative theology, so it cannot by itself adjudicate whether the Son is consubstantial.

## LDS Relevance
The partible-personhood framework dovetails with the LDS [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead]]: distinct persons can each fully bear divine presence, authority, and the divine name while remaining numerically distinct beings — exactly what the model predicts and what the creedal Trinity must instead explain by one shared *ousia*. If the "is and is not God" pattern is the ordinary ancient logic of communicable divine agency rather than evidence of consubstantiality, then the LDS reading of the Father and Son as two beings "one" in name, glory, and purpose ([[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality|cf. John 17]]) fits the data without importing 4th-century metaphysics. McClellan is doing critical scholarship, not LDS apologetics, but his mechanism removes the chief objection that distinct divine persons "can't" share full deity.
