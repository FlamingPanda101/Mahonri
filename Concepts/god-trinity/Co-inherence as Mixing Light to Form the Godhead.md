# Co-inherence as Mixing Light to Form the Godhead

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] explains the unity of the divine persons not as one substance but as **co-inherence** (the theological term; the Greek tradition's *perichoresis*) — mutual *indwelling*. The persons "take up abode in each other," sharing their light so that, in relationship, they constitute something new: **the Godhead**. The persons remain distinct centers of consciousness; their *oneness* is relational and dynamic. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The biblical term is "indwelling" (rating: strong; textual)
Ostler grounds it in John: the persons "indwell in each other... they actually have a presence in each other." John uses the term for taking up "abode" (*mone*) in a person — "they live inside of each other, they take up abode in each other." The theological term is "co-inherence." See [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]], [[John 14.9|John 14:9]]. [^1]

### 2. The blue/red light analogy (rating: moderate; illustrative)
"You have a blue light coming from one direction, a red light from another, and the two lights mix... they're in each other and they create a new light, a purple." The divine persons "co-here one another, their light mixes, and it creates a new color, the Godhead — because they are something different in relationship than when they are taken individually." [^2]

> "the divine persons share their light with one another. They co-inhere one another, their light mixes, and it creates a new color, the godhead." [^2]

### 3. Eternal mutual love is what binds them (rating: strong; systematic)
"The father, son, and holy ghost have chosen to love one another from all eternity, which is what binds them. They all have the same divine properties. Their divinity is equal in all respects." See [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]] and [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]]. [^3]

### 4. Co-inherence licenses worship of the Son (rating: moderate; systematic)
Because Father and Son are *in* one another and the Son does only what the Father commissions, "they can't act without one another," and worship rightly goes to the Son. See [[Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
*Perichoresis* is a classical-Trinitarian term, and orthodox theologians argue it presupposes *one* divine being (the persons interpenetrate because they share a single *ousia*) — detached from consubstantiality, "mixing lights to make a new color" sounds like three gods producing a fourth composite ("the Godhead"), or like the partim-partim error the Athanasian Creed forbids ("neither confounding the persons nor dividing the substance"). Ostler answers that LDS co-inherence needs no single substance because the unity is constituted by perfect mutual love and indwelling among uncreated persons — a *social* perichoresis he claims is more literal and coherent than a perichoresis hosted by simplicity. See [[Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail]].
