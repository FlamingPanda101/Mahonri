# Forgiving Sins Was a Divine Prerogative in Second Temple Judaism

#type/claim

## The Claim
When Jesus tells the paralytic "your sins are forgiven" (Mark 2), the scribes object, "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" [[Brant Pitre]] argues that — in a first-century Jewish frame — they are **not misreading him**: the positive act of *forgiving* sin (as opposed to mediating sacrifice) was understood as an exclusively divine prerogative. Jesus's reply ("that you may know the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins") therefore *affirms* a divine claim rather than deflecting it.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The divine passive in Leviticus (strong)
In the sin-offering rituals of [[Leviticus 4]], the priest offers the sacrifice and "it shall be forgiven him" — always the *divine passive*, never "the priest forgives." Jacob Milgrom's monumental Leviticus commentary takes this to imply that forgiveness, because sin is *above all* an offense against the LORD, is something only God does; the priest *mediates*, he does not *declare* forgiveness.[^1]

### 2. Isaiah 43 ties forgiveness to exclusive monotheism (strong)
[[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43]] — one of the highest monotheistic passages in the Hebrew Bible ("I, I am He... there is no god beside me") — has the LORD say "I am he who blots out your transgressions." Forgiving sin is affirmed precisely in the context of God's *unique* identity as the one creator.[^2]

### 3. The crowd's reaction and the address "my son" (medium)
The witnesses say "we have never seen anything like this" — implying something *unprecedented*, not merely a healing. And Jesus calls the stranger "my son" (and elsewhere "my daughter"), an oddly paternal address for a bystander, which Pitre reads as another hint that more than an ordinary rabbi is speaking.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
O'Connor's counter is strong and Pitre concedes its reasonableness: (a) there is *no* explicit Hebrew rule "only God can forgive sins"; the scribes — Jesus's perpetually-misunderstanding opponents — may simply be *wrong*. (b) Jesus says "the Son of Man has *authority*... to forgive," language of *delegated* power, and he elsewhere receives authority from the Father. (c) The clincher: in [[John 20.28|John 20]] the risen Jesus *delegates* this very power — "if you forgive anyone's sins, they are forgiven" — "as the Father has sent me, so I send you." So either only-God-forgives is false, or the disciples now do a divine act, which fits a theosis-shaped low Christology. Matthew also *removes* the line "who can forgive sins but God alone" and has the crowd praise God for giving "such authority to *men*."

## Counter-Objection
Pitre grants that divine power *can* be delegated (the apostles in John 20) and that Matthew's "authority to men" is real — but argues this does not erase the divine claim *in this scene*. In its own Second Temple context the scribes rightly perceive Jesus doing what, *heretofore*, only the God of Israel did: not asking God to forgive, not offering sacrifice, but simply *declaring* the sins forgiven on his own authority. Delegation downstream (post-resurrection theosis, [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:4]]) is compatible with — indeed explained by — a divine Messiah who can confer divine prerogatives.

## Apologetic Engagement
This dispute is unusually friendly to the Restoration, because *both* sides end up affirming a Latter-day Saint instinct. Pitre establishes that forgiving sins is a *divine* act; O'Connor establishes that Jesus *delegates* divine acts to his followers ("as the Father has sent me, so I send you"). Together that is the LDS pattern of **divine investiture** and **deification**: God shares his own prerogatives and nature with authorized agents who become "partakers of the divine nature." The Restoration need not choose between "Jesus is divine" and "the disciples receive divine power" — it holds both.

## LDS Relevance
The note pairs with [[Disciples Share the Throne and Divine Glory]] and the deification cluster ([[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]], [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:4]]): authority to forgive flows from the Father, through the divine Son, to commissioned disciples — exactly the priesthood-and-theosis logic of the Restoration rather than a closed, incommunicable divine essence.
