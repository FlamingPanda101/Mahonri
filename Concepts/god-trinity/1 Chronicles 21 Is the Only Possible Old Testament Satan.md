# 1 Chronicles 21 Is the Only Possible Old Testament Satan

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] grants one **debated exception** to "Satan is not in the Old Testament": [[1 Chronicles 21.1|1 Chronicles 21:1]] ("Satan stood up against Israel, and provoked David to number Israel"). Here *satan* appears **without the definite article** (anarthrous), unlike the *ha-satan* office of Job and Zechariah (see [[Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name]]). Some scholars read it as the first occurrence of "Satan" as a personal name; McClellan reads it as a still-generic "an accuser."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It rewrites 2 Samuel to offload blame from God (rated: strong, redactional — McClellan's reading).** 1 Chronicles 21 "is riffing on [[2 Samuel 24.1|2 Samuel 24:1]] where we have the same story but it starts off by saying Adonai was angry with David and *he* incited David to conduct a census" — and then God punishes David for it, so "the narrative doesn't make a whole lot of sense." McClellan: the Chronicler (a "pretty late text") "is trying to offload responsibility from God by introducing this generic noun *satan*, an adversary... We're not naming them, we're just saying, yeah, it was some accuser."[^2]

2. **The angel-as-accuser precedent supports the generic reading (rated: moderate).** "We've already seen that the angel of the Lord can function as an accuser" (Numbers 22 — see [[Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary]]), so the Chronicler "is just kind of punting by saying we don't want people to think it's God, so we'll just have an accuser did this" — without specifying who.[^2]

3. **But respected scholars read it as the name (rated: the live dissent).** McClellan flags the dissent honestly: "there are scholars who interpret it as the very first and the only Old Testament example of the name Satan occurring," and points readers to [[Esther Hamori]]'s "phenomenal book *God's Monsters*" for the case that 1 Chronicles 21 is the first occurrence of *satan* as the personal name of Satan.[^3]

**Steelman of the opposing view (the proper-name reading).** Hamori and others note the **anarthrous** form is exactly what a personal name looks like in Hebrew (names don't take the article), and that a heavenly *adversary* who incites a king to sin already resembles the developing antagonist of Second Temple Judaism; reading it as a proper name is grammatically natural and fits the trajectory toward the New Testament devil. McClellan does not claim certainty — he says "I don't think that's the case," not that it is impossible — so the honest verdict is *contested*: 1 Chronicles 21 is the **single** plausible candidate, and even it is more likely (on McClellan's view) a generic accuser than the name.

## Apologetic Engagement
The concession matters for precision in debate. An apologist cannot point to Job, Zechariah, Genesis 3, Isaiah 14, or Ezekiel 28 for a *named* Old Testament Satan; the *only* candidate is 1 Chronicles 21:1, and its status is genuinely disputed among scholars (McClellan: generic; Hamori: name). The strongest claim anyone can make for "Satan in the Old Testament" rests on this one anarthrous noun in a late text.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan is non-confessional and draws no Latter-day Saint conclusion. For Latter-day Saints the datum is incidental to the larger pattern in [[The Old Testament Barely Mentions Satan]]: even on the most generous reading the OT yields *one* possible naming of Satan, which is why the Restoration supplies the developed premortal-rebellion narrative ([[Moses 4]]) rather than finding it in the Hebrew Bible. The dispute also models good method — McClellan steelmans the scholar who disagrees with him (Hamori) and cites her work, the even-handedness the vault prizes.
