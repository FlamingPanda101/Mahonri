# John 1-1 Deploys the Stoic Logos as a Bridge

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that "In the beginning was the **Logos**" (John 1:1) is not a freestanding oddity but a deliberate deployment of the **Stoic Logos** — the binding, rational principle of the cosmos. To any literate Greco-Roman, the verse would read as "God and the Logos are one… God and logic are one," using a familiar Stoic concept as a *bridge* to introduce Christ to a Greek-speaking audience — the same rhetorical move as Ammon teaching King Lamoni from what was familiar.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Logos was the Stoic cosmic principle (rated: history of philosophy).** For the Stoics, the *logos* is "the fundamental cosmological principle… the binding force of reality" (its etymological root means "to bind, to bring together"). So "there is no way that could be read by anybody who was a Greco-Roman and literate without reading it as a Stoic model."[^1]
2. **The verse is "goofy" without the Stoic background (rated: the argument).** "Why is the word at the beginning? Why is the word with God? Why is the word God? The phrase itself is goofy if you just take it as its own statement — but it's not if you understand it from the Greek."[^2]
3. **A familiarity bridge, then the new claim (rated: rhetorical reading).** The author "takes a Stoic metaphysical posture and then uses that to introduce the gospel" — vv. 14ff ("he became a man… dwelt in a tent among us… those who grasped him received authority to become the sons of God") move from familiar Stoic cosmology to the new, particular claim. "Building relationships of truth by creating objects of familiarity… so the new can be brought up and not totally objectionable."[^3]
4. **An LDS analogue (rated: comparison).** Thomassen compares it to [[Ammon]] teaching King Lamoni — starting from the king's own concept of the Great Spirit and building toward the gospel.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Many scholars trace John's *Logos* primarily to **Hebrew/Jewish backgrounds** — the creative "word of the LORD," personified Wisdom (Proverbs 8), the Aramaic *Memra* of the Targums, and especially [[Philo of Alexandria]]'s Logos (itself a Jewish-Middle-Platonic, not narrowly Stoic, synthesis) — rather than to Stoicism per se. On this reading John uses a term with rich *Jewish* resonance that also happened to be intelligible to Greeks, so "purely Stoic" overstates it. The cross-cultural-bridge function is widely accepted; the specifically *Stoic* attribution is one option among several (and the vault's [[John 1-3 - The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation Not the Originator]] frames the Logos through subordinationist Christology, not Stoic physics).

## Best Quotes
> "Logos is the fundamental cosmological principle within Stoicism. It is the binding force of reality. So what that statement is doing is saying God and the Logos are one — God and logic are one."[^1]

> "He's taking a Stoic metaphysical posture and then using that to introduce the gospel to a Greek-speaking audience… he uses what's familiar to them."[^3]

## LDS Connection
For Thomassen this is the keystone example of Stoicism embedded in the New Testament "from the very beginning" — undercutting the "Hellenization-came-later-and-corrupted-everything" model of the Great Apostasy and instead casting Greek thought as a God-used bridge (a "preparatory gospel"). It complements the vault's Johannine-Logos notes ([[Logos Hymn Belongs to the Original Gospel]], [[John 1-3 - The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation Not the Originator]]) by adding the Stoic-reception angle, and feeds [[Stoicism Was a Theoretical Preparatory Gospel]].
