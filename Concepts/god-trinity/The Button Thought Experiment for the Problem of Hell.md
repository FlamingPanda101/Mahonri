# The Button Thought Experiment for the Problem of Hell

## The Claim
A single intuition pump frames the entire problem of hell. Imagine a button: pressing it brings a brand-new, fully real person into existence. Before you decide, you are shown that person's entire life start to finish — and it ends in eternal conscious torment. You are told three things: (1) you don't have to press it; (2) you lose nothing by walking away; (3) the choice is entirely yours. The claim is that **no morally good agent would press that button**, and that God, possessing perfect foreknowledge and the freedom not to create, is in exactly that position with respect to the damned [^1]. The argument's force is that the moral instinct stays fixed when you swap "you" for "God" — and if it does, the standard picture of God collapses.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The intuition is near-universal and pre-theoretical — rate: strong as a moral premise.** Almost no one says they would press the button. The thought experiment isolates the single morally relevant fact (foreknown, avoidable, gratuitous eternal suffering) and strips away distractions like the victim's later choices, which only arise *after* creation [^1]. **Steelman of the theist:** the analogy assumes God's situation is relevantly identical to the button-presser's. A classical theist can argue (a) creating a free being who can reject God is a different act from manufacturing a doomed outcome, and (b) the value of bringing genuine libertarian agents into existence may outweigh the risk — the button frame smuggles in determinism by *showing* the fixed ending.
2. **The "deathly-allergic meal" parallel — rate: moderate.** If I knowingly serve a guest a dish they are fatally allergic to, "well, they chose to eat it" does not absolve me, because I had the knowledge and the choice not to serve it [^2]. This anticipates the free-will rebuttal (see [[Free Will Cannot Justify the Decision to Create]]) by locating moral responsibility at the upstream decision. **Steelman:** disanalogy — the guest does not *consent* to eating, whereas the damned are said to reject God freely over a lifetime; the theist will press that disanalogy.
3. **It generalizes the existing vault argument.** This is the same logical structure as [[Problem of Hell and Creation]] and [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]], reframed as a vivid first-person decision rather than an abstract syllogism, which is rhetorically the video's main contribution.

## Best Quotes
> "If you press that button, a brand new person comes into existence. A real person. Someone who can think, feel, love, hurt, hope, grieve... And you see how it ends. And for this person, it ends badly... eternal conscious torment forever." [^1]

> "If you do, could you honestly look that person in the eye at the end of their life and say, 'I did this for your own good'?" [^3]

> "If I serve someone a meal and I know they're deathly allergic to what's in it, I can't say, 'Well, they're the one who ate it.' Because I knew what would happen. I didn't have to serve it, but I chose to anyway. Their choice does not erase mine." [^2]
