#type/claim

# LDS Should Lead With John 10 Not Revelation 3 or Patristics

## The Claim

Reviewing Jacob Hansen's case for deification, [[Dan McClellan]] offers what amounts to **debate coaching directed at the Latter-day Saint side**: the strongest *biblical* proof text for deification is **[[John 10.34-36|John 10:34-36]]** (Jesus quoting [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]), and the LDS apologist repeatedly leads with weaker materials instead — **[[Revelation 3.21|Revelation 3:21]]** ("sit with me in my throne"), the **acorn/oak analogy**, and **patristic appeals** to Athanasius. McClellan's verdict is that Hansen "neglected to mention" John 10, "and that's probably the biggest shortcoming in his argument." This is McClellan as a **foil who is nonetheless more useful to the LDS reader than the LDS debater was**: he is not endorsing Mormonism, but he is telling Latter-day Saints which of their own arguments actually survive contact with a non-LDS interlocutor.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Revelation 3:21 is a poor *lead* outside Mormonism.** "When you're in a debate with someone who's not a Latter-day Saint, that's not the passage you should lead with. It's not very convincing to people who have spent centuries interpreting these passages in very different ways."[^1] *Rating: strong as tactics* — McClellan grants there is "a case to make" for Revelation 3 pointing toward deification soteriology, but it is "not the dominant view within critical scholarship, and certainly not within contemporary evangelicalism," so it persuades only the already-convinced. (The substantive throne argument is catalogued separately at [[Disciples Share the Throne and Divine Glory]].)

2. **Repeating in-group talk is not engaging the Bible.** Of Hansen's acorn/oak simile and Athanasius appeal, McClellan says he "retreats to more ways that Latter-day Saints talk to each other about the doctrine of deification" and "doesn't answer" the actual question — *why* read these passages as deification — which his evangelical interlocutor keeps asking.[^2] *Rating: strong* — diagnoses the failure mode as speaking the Mormon constellation's dialect rather than arguing the biblical text (cf. [[The Four Constellations of Deification Belief]]).

3. **Patristic appeals miss on two counts.** Bringing up Athanasius "is not going to be very effective, not only because [the evangelical] is asking for biblical examples… but [because] the later Christian ideas of theosis are also significantly different from what [Hansen] is talking about."[^3] *Rating: strong* — folds in the diluted-patristics point (see [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]]): the fathers are both off-topic (not "biblical") and substantively different.

4. **John 10 is the defensible proof text — and Hansen omitted it.** "There is another proof text that I think does a better job"; Psalm 82 is key "not as it's found in the Hebrew Bible [but] as it's found in Jesus' mouth in John 10:34-36… because the Gospel of John has a divinization soteriology."[^4] McClellan's closing verdict: Hansen "neglected to mention that, and that's probably the biggest shortcoming in his argument."[^5] *Rating: strong* — names the single highest-leverage correction and roots it in [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology|John's divinization soteriology]] and the [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai|Sinai-divinization reading of Psalm 82]].

**Steelman of the opposing view.** An LDS apologist can reasonably defend the "weaker" choices. Revelation 3:21, [[Romans 8.14-17|Romans 8]] joint-heirship, and the throne/glory texts are *cumulative* — they show the *pattern* of shared divine status across the canon, which a single verse cannot; leading with the familiar analogy (acorn/oak) is sound pedagogy for a lay audience even if it is not a proof; and citing the fathers answers the specific charge that deification is a *novel* Mormon invention, which is a different question from "is it biblical." McClellan's reply is contained in his own framing: those moves may comfort the in-group, but against a trained non-LDS interlocutor who keeps asking *why this verse means that*, only the John 10 argument forces the issue — so they are fine as *support*, fatal as the *lead*.

## LDS Relevance

This note is unusual: a Tier-B non-LDS scholar handing the Latter-day Saint apologist a **sharper version of the LDS case** than the LDS debater deployed. The practical takeaway for the vault's apologetics workflow: when defending deification, **open with John 10:34-36** (Jesus authorizing "ye are gods" from [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]] as something the scriptures "cannot be annulled"), develop it through [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology|John's divinization soteriology]] (John 1:12; 17:20-23), and treat Revelation 3:21, the throne/glory texts, the acorn/oak analogy, and the patristic witness as *secondary corroboration*, not openers. The foil cuts both ways and the LDS reader should own both edges: McClellan is *not* conceding the LDS doctrine (he reads John 10 as first-century *Johannine* divinization, not as the King Follett cosmology), but he is confirming that the Latter-day Saint's best biblical ground is real and was simply left unused. See also the methodological siblings [[Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain]] and [[Start From the Critical Reading Then Negotiate]].
