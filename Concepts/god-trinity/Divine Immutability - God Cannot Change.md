# Divine Immutability - God Cannot Change

## The Point
Change, for Feser, just *is* the actualization of a potential. [^1] Since God is pure actuality with no potential that needs to be — or even could be — actualized, "he cannot change. He is therefore immutable or changeless." [^1] Immutability also follows independently from simplicity: a real change means a thing alters in one respect while remaining the same in another, which presupposes parts; a partless God cannot do this. [^2] The apparent counterexample — that God *becomes* Creator when He creates — is dissolved by the distinction between real change and "Cambridge change": God's becoming Creator is a real change only in the world's status, not in God Himself. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **From pure actuality.** "Change … is the actualization of potential. We have also seen that God is pure actuality, devoid of potentiality. Now, if he has no potential that needs to be, or indeed could be, actualized, then he cannot change." [^1]
2. **From simplicity.** "When a thing undergoes a real change … it changes in some particular respect while remaining the same in other respects … But that presupposes that the changing thing is composed of parts … Since God is simple or noncomposite, then, he cannot change." [^2]
3. **The Cambridge-change reply.** Borrowing Geach's distinction (built on by Barry Miller), Feser holds that "Socrates' becoming shorter than Plato" — when only Plato grows — is a mere Cambridge change in Socrates. [^3] Likewise "God's becoming Creator of the world involve[s] a real change only in the world's status, not in God." [^1] Divine simplicity therefore denies God *contingent real* properties, while allowing contingent *Cambridge* properties. [^3]
4. **The fallacy of accident.** The objection "anything that acts must itself change" commits a fallacy of accident — inferring from "every actor we observe also changes" that "any possible actor must change," like inferring from "every professor has been under nine feet tall" that no professor could exceed it. [^4] What is essential to acting is "the bringing about of an effect in another thing, rather than undergoing change oneself." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "If he has no potential that needs to be, or indeed could be, actualized, then he cannot change. He is therefore immutable or changeless." [^1]

> "To say that God is immutable is to say that there is no real change in him, though there may of course be Cambridge changes." [^1]

> "What is essential to acting is the bringing about of an effect in another thing, rather than undergoing change oneself as one does so." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Immutability is one of the precise points where classical theism and the Restoration part ways. Feser's God *cannot* change in any real respect; the Father revealed to Joseph Smith is an exalted, embodied person with a real history — one who "was once a man," who progressed to perfection, and who acts, responds, and relates within time (D&C 130:22; see [[Anthropomorphic God Grounds Heavenly Mother and Eternal Families]]). Latter-day Saints do affirm a kind of constancy: God's character, covenants, and perfections "change not" (Mal 3:6 is congenial in *that* sense). But they reject the strong metaphysical immutability that forbids God any real relation, emotion, or temporal action — precisely the attribute the broader tradition imported from the unmoved mover (see [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and [[Two Defining Features of Deity Eternal and Unchanging]]). The Cambridge-change move is Feser's sophisticated attempt to keep "God creates" true without conceding change in God; the LDS reply is that a God who genuinely *responds* to prayer, is *moved* by His children's suffering, and *acts* at successive moments is undergoing real relation, not a mere Cambridge property — and that wholesale reclassification of God's biblical actions as "Cambridge changes" looks like the same anthropopathic re-reading critiqued in [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]. To be fair to Feser, his account is not crude: it carefully distinguishes acting from changing, and an LDS critic must engage that distinction (see [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]]) rather than assume that all action entails change.
