# Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that if the Trinity were the obvious, natural reading of the Bible, it would not have taken **325 years and decades of further conflict** to formulate. Before Nicaea (325) the *majority* of bishops held the view of [[Arius]], not the homoousian view; the council was a *politically managed* event under [[Constantine]] (who wanted imperial unity, not doctrinal truth); and the controversy continued to rage, with mutual excommunications, for 50–100 years afterward. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **The pre-Nicene majority was Arian.** "Up until Nicaea, the majority of bishops held the view of Arius, not the view that came out of Nicaea." *Rating: broadly defensible* — there was no settled consensus before 325; subordinationist views were widespread (though "majority" is debated). [^3]

2. **The council was imperially controlled.** Constantine "didn't care what the result was, he wanted unity," used his advisor [[Hosius]] as ambassador, convened bishops at his palace, and *invited the previously excommunicated Arians* — so excommunication "didn't mean what we think it means." *Rating: historically grounded* on Constantine's unifying motive and managed proceedings. [^4][^5]

3. **The dispute did not end at Nicaea.** "For the next 50 to 100 years, they continued to fight with one another and excommunicate one another." *Rating: accurate* — the post-Nicene period (Arian ascendancy under Constantius, Councils of Sirmium, etc.) was turbulent until Constantinople 381. [^6]

4. **The "it's the whole Bible" defense is evasive.** When asked *where* the Trinity is taught, the inerrantist says "the whole thing" — but "it doesn't seem so obvious to me if it took them 325 years to come to even a statement." *Rating: a fair rhetorical jab* against appeals to a diffuse, non-specifiable prooftext base (cf. [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]]). [^7]

5. **Steelman of the Trinitarian.** Doctrinal *development* is normal and not evidence of falsehood: the canon, the two-natures of Christ, and even monotheism's precise articulation also took centuries to define against heresies — slow formulation reflects the need to exclude error, not absence from Scripture. High Christology (worship of Jesus, John 1:1, Phil 2) is attested *decades* after the resurrection, long before Arius. And councils being *politically* convened does not make their conclusions *false* (genetic fallacy). *Rating: a strong reply* — the timeline shows the *terminology* was contested, not that the underlying worship of Christ as God was a 4th-century invention. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Up until Nicaea, the majority of bishops held the view of Arius, not the view that came out of Nicaea." [^3]

> "The emperor didn't care what the result was, he wanted unity. He didn't want a division in the territory that he ruled." [^4]

> "When they called the council of Nicaea... they invited the people who'd been excommunicated." [^5]

> "It doesn't seem so obvious to me if it took them 325 years to come to even a statement that would define it in any way and they continued to fight over it for another century." [^7]
