# *Bara* Means to Separate, and Genesis Is Organization Not Creation From Nothing

## The Claim

Genesis 1 does not describe creation from nothing but the *organizing and separating* of pre-existing material. On the cognitive-linguistic reading [[Dan McClellan]] cites from [[Ellen van Wolde]], the verb *bara* — conventionally "to create" — is better understood as "to separate"; the creative acts are a drawing of dividing lines (light from dark, waters above from waters below), and the first human task is to *name* (taxonomize) the animals. Creation as labeling and division presupposes a material substrate, not *ex nihilo* production.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Bara* as "to separate" (van Wolde)
McClellan: van Wolde, "a cognitive linguist," argues "*bara*... doesn't necessarily mean to create but to separate." On this reading the light "must be separated from the dark," and the process is "drawing lines down the middle of things to create two separate categories" — heavens above from heavens below, waters above from waters below. The grammar of Genesis 1 is then *division of stuff*, not summoning of being. See [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — van Wolde's separation thesis is published peer-reviewed work but a *debated minority* position; some philologists retain "create" as the core sense (noted in her People note).

### 2. Genesis 1:2 already presupposes pre-existing material
Even on the traditional translation, [[Genesis 1.2|Genesis 1:2]] describes what already exists "when God began to create" ([[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]] as a temporal clause): "the earth was without form and void, and darkness was upon the face of the deep." McClellan: "God's like, 'All right, I'm ready to create. Here's my stuff that's already here — the darkness, the deep, the earth' — and then the first creative act is 'Let there be light.'" The tohu-wa-bohu, darkness, and deep are the raw materials. See [[Greco-Roman Jewish Creation View Was Material Substrate]].[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the construct reading of 1:1 and the pre-existing waters/darkness of 1:2 are mainstream critical positions (cf. E.A. Speiser, the Anchor Bible).

### 3. Adam's first act is taxonomy, reinforcing the organize-and-label theme
When Adam is created, "what's the first thing that he is asked to do? It's to name the animals. It's to taxonomize" ([[Genesis 2.19|Genesis 2:19]]). The motif of *labeling* extends the creation-as-organization reading: the cosmos and its creatures are sorted and named rather than conjured. (O'Connor frames this via his friend's "God is a mereological nihilist" quip — creation as arranging and labeling matter humans then put names on.)[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — an illustrative thematic point; the naming motif supports the "organization" frame without by itself settling the meaning of *bara*.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The traditional creation-from-nothing reading is defensible. (a) *Bara* is used only with God as subject and never takes a material "out of which," which many scholars take to connote a uniquely divine, effortless making rather than mere separation — so "separate" may capture some uses but not exhaust the verb. (b) Christians have long argued that "in the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" *encompasses everything*, with 1:2 describing an early stage of that creation rather than independent eternal matter, and that later texts (2 Maccabees 7:28; Romans 4:17; Hebrews 11:3) make *ex nihilo* explicit. (c) Van Wolde's separation thesis was sharply contested in the philological literature. McClellan's reply (developed across the linked notes) is historical: *ex nihilo* is a late-2nd-century-CE theological innovation read *back* into Genesis (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]), the construct grammar of 1:1 plus the pre-existing deep of 1:2 fit "organize the substrate" better than "make from nothing," and the "from what is not" texts trade on the Greek convention that *unformed* matter counts as "non-being." The fair conclusion is that Genesis 1, read on its own ancient terms, neither requires nor clearly teaches creation from nothing — whether one accepts van Wolde's stronger "separate" claim or only the weaker "pre-existing material" point.
