# The Old Testament Barely Mentions Satan

## The Claim

[[Ether's Elephant]], citing [[Jonah Barnes]]'s book *Heat of the Keystone*, observes that the Old Testament mentions "the devil only four times," most of those generic ("devils," not "the devil"), so that "the existence of Satan as an adversary to God is apparently absent enough from the Old Testament that some scholars conclude that Satan is an invention of Christianity." The video turns this into a *Restoration* argument: the fuller, coherent picture of Satan's identity was lost and has been "restored today through additional scripture beyond the Bible" (e.g. [[Moses 4]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The figure is thin in the Hebrew Bible.** In the Hebrew Bible *ha-satan* is a *title* ("the accuser") for a member of the divine council who needs God's permission to act ([[Job 1]]) — not a proper name for a cosmic rebel. The video's "only four times" tally reflects this near-absence of a developed adversary.[^1] *Rating: strong and well-supported; this matches mainstream scholarship catalogued in [[Satan - Development of the Figure]].*

2. **Mainstream scholarship agrees the rebel-Satan develops later.** The academic consensus is that the cosmic-rebel Satan emerges through Second Temple literature, especially the Enochic Watcher tradition, and is read back into earlier texts only afterward (Genesis 3's serpent is identified as Satan only by Wisdom 2:24 / Revelation 12:9; Isaiah 14 is a taunt against the king of Babylon). See [[Book of Enoch - Origin of the Satan Figure]].[^1] *Rating: strong; the video's "scholars conclude" gloss is broadly accurate, even if "invention of Christianity" is loose (the development is largely Second Temple Jewish, pre-Christian).*

3. **LDS reads the gap as loss-and-restoration.** "We have a lot of truths that have been restored today through additional scripture beyond the Bible. And so we have a more full picture of Satan's identity." [[Moses 4]] supplies the premortal-rebellion narrative the OT lacks.[^2] *Rating: this is a theological interpretation of the data, not an independent datum; persuasive in-tradition, contestable outside it (see steelman).*

4. **The scholarly anatomy of the "four mentions" (added from [[Dan McClellan - Satan isnt in the Old Testament]]).** [[Dan McClellan]] supplies the lexical detail behind the near-absence: the bare noun *satan* is a generic "adversary/accuser," used mostly of humans and even of the angel of Yahweh (Numbers 22:22) — see [[Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary]]; the definite *ha-satan* of [[Job 1]] (14×) and [[Zechariah 3]] (3×) is a divine-council *office*, not a name — see [[Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name]]; and the classic "Satan" prooftexts (Genesis 3, Isaiah 14, Ezekiel 28) are a snake and two human kings — see [[Genesis 3 Isaiah 14 and Ezekiel 28 Are Not About Satan]]. The *single* debated candidate for a named OT Satan is the anarthrous *satan* of [[1 Chronicles 21.1|1 Chronicles 21:1]] (contested: McClellan generic, [[Esther Hamori]] a personal name) — see [[1 Chronicles 21 Is the Only Possible Old Testament Satan]].[^3] *Rating: strong; this is the mainstream lexical-critical backbone for the video's "only four times" tally.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The same evidence cuts the other way for a critic. (a) If the developed Satan figure is a late, *extra*-biblical accretion (Enochic and Second Temple), then a 19th-century text ([[Moses 4]]) presenting a fully-formed premortal Satanic rebellion looks like it *inherits* the late development, not like it *restores* a primordial truth the OT once held — the very lateness the video cites as a gap is awkward for a "restoration of the original" claim. (b) "Some scholars conclude Satan is an invention of Christianity" is imprecise: the consensus locates the development in pre-Christian Second Temple Judaism (1 Enoch, Jubilees), so the framing slightly overstates the "Christianity invented it" angle. (c) Methodologically, citing critical scholarship's "the OT has no developed Satan" while rejecting that same scholarship's "and the developed Satan is a late legend, not lost revelation" is selective. The strongest LDS reply is that loss-and-restoration is a *theological* reading laid over the agreed historical data — the Restoration claims to recover by revelation what was never fully written, so the absence in the OT is expected rather than embarrassing — but this is a faith commitment, not a result the scholarship yields on its own.
