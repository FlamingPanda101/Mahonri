# Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the Hebrew noun *satan* in the Hebrew Bible is overwhelmingly a **generic common noun** meaning "adversary, accuser, opponent" — used mostly of *humans*, and occasionally of *divine* figures — not a proper name for a cosmic enemy of God. The named character "Satan" did not yet exist when these texts were written.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It mostly describes humans (rated: strong, lexical).** The generic noun *satan* "occurs a number of times in the Hebrew Bible, and overwhelmingly it is used as a generic noun that refers to someone who is acting as an adversary, an accuser or an opponent of someone else... mostly... in reference to humans, whether righteous or wicked."[^1]

2. **It can describe a divine figure — even the angel of Yahweh (rated: strong).** In [[Numbers 22.22|Numbers 22:22]], when the angel of the LORD confronts Balaam, "the text says the angel was a *satan*" — *lə-satan*, "as an adversary." So the word can apply to a *good* divine being doing God's bidding, which is impossible if *satan* were the name of God's enemy.[^1]

3. **The category, not a character (rated: strong, contextual).** The point is that "satan" names a *role* (adversary) filled by various agents — human or divine — not an individual. This sets up the distinction between the generic noun and the later definite-article office *ha-satan* (see [[Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name]]), and the lone debated proper-name case (see [[1 Chronicles 21 Is the Only Possible Old Testament Satan]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A traditional reader can grant the lexical point and still hold that a *referent* (a real, single adversary later known as Satan) stands behind some of these uses, even if the word is generic — much as "the LORD's anointed" is a title that nonetheless designates specific persons. One might also argue the New Testament's retrospective identifications (e.g. the serpent as "that ancient serpent, the devil," Rev 12:9) reveal a unity the OT only implies. McClellan's reply is that reading a later, developed character back into a generic noun is exactly the anachronism at issue; the Hebrew texts give a *role*, and the *character* is a later development (see [[Satan - Development of the Figure]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
The claim undercuts apologetics that locate a fully-formed Satan throughout the Old Testament (the Eden serpent, Isaiah 14, Job's accuser as "the devil"). If *satan* is a generic noun for "adversary" — applied even to the angel of Yahweh — then those readings import a New Testament (or later) figure into texts that do not contain him. It does *not*, by itself, deny the existence of Satan; it dates the *literary character*.

## LDS Relevance
This cuts two ways for Latter-day Saints. (a) It agrees with the academic data the vault already catalogs in [[The Old Testament Barely Mentions Satan]], where the same near-absence is reframed as *loss-and-restoration*: the fuller premortal-rebellion narrative ([[Moses 4]]) is supplied by Restoration scripture, not the OT. (b) But McClellan, non-confessional, treats the developed Satan as a late *innovation* rather than a restored primordial truth — so Latter-day Saints adopt his lexical findings while contesting his inference, holding that revelation recovers what the Hebrew text never fully recorded.
