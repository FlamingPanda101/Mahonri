# Divine Eternity as Timelessness - God Exists Outside of Time

## The Point
Feser insists that God's eternity is not mere unending duration. "It is not mere longevity, but strict timelessness." [^1] God does not simply exist throughout all past and future time; "he exists outside of time altogether." [^1] This follows from both immutability and simplicity: a God in time would constantly add new moments to His life, act differently at different moments, and acquire new knowledge (e.g. "it is now t₁," "it is now t₂") — all of which involve change, which an immutable God cannot undergo; [^1] and a God with distinct temporal stages would have distinct parts, which a simple God cannot have. [^2]

## Evidence
1. **Beginningless and endless first, then timeless.** What comes into being or passes away has parts, potentiality, and contingency and so needs a cause; God has none of these, so "he does not come into being … nor passes away … Hence, God is eternal." [^3] "But God's eternity amounts to more than this." [^1]
2. **Why timeless, not merely everlasting.** If God were *in* time He "would constantly be adding new seconds, days, and years to his life; would be acting at one moment in a way that differs from the way he acts at another moment; and … would constantly be acquiring new pieces of knowledge … But all of this would involve change, and God is immutable." [^1] Feser notes that even William Lane Craig — who *rejects* divine timelessness — grants that "if God is immutable, then he would indeed be timeless." [^1] From simplicity: temporal stages would be "distinct parts," impossible for the noncomposite One. [^2]
3. **The author-of-a-story analogy.** That God causes a thing to exist at t₁ does not mean "*at* t₁, God causes" it — as if He acted serially through time. [^4] Rather, "God, from outside of time, in a single act causes to exist a temporal world." [^4] Compare an author who conceives an entire story "in a single flash of insight": from the characters' viewpoint the middle follows the beginning, "but the author himself … did it all at once." [^4]
4. **The simultaneity objection answered.** The objection that God's eternity being "simultaneous" with both 469 B.C. and A.D. 1961 would make those times simultaneous with each other "misses the point. It treats timeless eternity as if it were a point in time," whereas the whole claim is that God "does not exist at some point in time, but rather outside of time altogether." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "But God's eternity amounts to more than this. It is not mere longevity, but strict timelessness … he exists outside of time altogether." [^1]

> "It is rather that God, from outside of time, in a single act causes to exist a temporal world in which the object exists at times t₁, t₂, t₃, and so on." [^4]

> "It treats timeless eternity as if it were a point in time … But the whole idea … is precisely that he does not exist at some point in time, but rather outside of time altogether." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Timeless eternity is perhaps the cleanest divergence between Feser's classical theism and the Restoration. For Feser, God is wholly *outside* time — there is no before-and-after in the divine life, no sequence of divine acts, no acquisition of new knowledge. Latter-day Saint theology, by contrast, places God *within* time (or within an eternal duration with real succession): the Father progressed to His exalted state (see [[God Was Once a Man]]), acts at successive moments, and works out His purposes through a real history of covenant and response. Intelligences are co-eternal and "eternally" exercise agency — a temporal, not timeless, mode of existence (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). The whole framework of eternal progression presupposes genuine sequence, which strict timelessness forbids. Feser's case deserves to be met at full strength: it is an elegant solution to real puzzles (how an unchanging God relates to a changing world; how His sustaining causation can be one act rather than infinitely many), and the author-of-a-story analogy is a serious answer to the worry that creation forces God into time. The LDS reply is that the price is a God who cannot *literally* respond, remember, anticipate, or progress — a God whose "eternity" is borrowed from the Greek axiom that the truly divine must be changeless and timeless (see [[Two Defining Features of Deity Eternal and Unchanging]] and [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]) rather than from the narrative, time-involved God of scripture. Because Feser derives timelessness *from* immutability and simplicity, the LDS critic who contests those prior attributes ([[Divine Immutability - God Cannot Change]], [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]) is not bound to accept timelessness either.
