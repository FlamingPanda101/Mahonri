# Divine Conservation and Concurrence

## The Point
Feser argues that creation is not a one-time past event but an ongoing act: the world "would be instantly annihilated in the absence of divine causation" — the doctrine of **divine conservation**. [^1] Building on it, he defends **concurrentism**, a middle path between *occasionalism* (only God ever causes anything) and *mere conservationism* (God keeps things in being but they cause independently). [^2] Created things have genuine causal power, yet they cannot exercise it independently of God, who must concur with everything they do. [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Conservation, not existential inertia.** Nothing composite — any mixture of actuality and potentiality, or of essence and existence — can persist on its own; it needs a sustaining cause at every instant. The rival "existential inertia" thesis is asserted without explaining what feature could give a contingent thing such self-sustaining power, and it simply ignores the arguments that led to God as First Cause. [^4] This also rules out pantheism and panentheism: the world is composite, mutable, contingent, and so distinct from the simple, immutable, necessary God. [^5]
2. **Against occasionalism.** Since *agere sequitur esse* (action follows being), a thing with no causal efficacy at all would not truly exist — so occasionalism implies God alone exists. But you know *you* exist (you change, you are not omniscient or omnipotent), so at least one thing other than God exists. [^6] Worse, occasionalism saws off the branch it sits on: we reasoned *to* God only from the real causal activity of creatures, so denying that activity "would be like someone who slowly and carefully climbs a ladder, then pulls out a ray gun and blasts it out from under him." [^7]
3. **Against mere conservationism.** If a thing could cause independently of God, it could *exist* independently of God — an essentially deist picture in which creatures "carry on without him once created." But nothing composite can exist for an instant without God's conserving action; the position is incoherent for the same ladder-blasting reason. [^8]
4. **Secondary causes are real but derivative.** Draw a blue square on a chalkboard: you are the primary cause (that there is a square at all), the chalk a secondary cause (that it is blue) — yet no square appears unless you press the chalk. [^9] God's concurrence with our free acts is like a father guiding a child's hand to write an *A*: the child can resist or submit (a real contribution), but the letter will not appear without the father's guidance. [^10]

## Best Quotes
> "Creation is not a onetime event that occurred at some distant point in the past. It is occurring at every moment." [^1]

> "Secondary causes are true causes insofar as they make a real contribution to the effect. ... Secondary causes are secondary insofar as they would be inert without divine assistance." [^9]

> "It would be like someone who slowly and carefully climbs a ladder, then pulls out a ray gun and blasts it out from under him — he would fall to the ground, making his cautious ascent entirely pointless." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Feser's continuous-conservation picture overlaps strikingly with the scriptural truth that "in him we live, and move, and have our being" — God as the present, moment-by-moment sustainer of all things ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). Latter-day Saints can affirm divine sustaining and the refutation of deism. The hard divergence is the metaphysical engine driving it: conservation here presupposes *creatio ex nihilo*, the claim that creatures have no being of their own whatsoever apart from God's continuous act. The Restoration holds that matter, intelligence, and spirit are *co-eternal* and uncreated — God organized rather than originated them ([[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]). On this reading, things do possess a measure of independent existence and agency that ex-nihilo metaphysics denies, and LDS critics argue ex nihilo collapses toward exactly the occasionalism Feser is at pains to reject ([[Creation Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism and Precludes Free Love]]). Concurrentism is Feser's careful attempt to preserve real secondary causation *within* an ex-nihilo frame; whether it can keep creaturely agency genuinely originative — rather than a derivative trickle of the one true cause — is precisely the point at issue ([[God's Knowledge Does Not Abolish Free Will]]). In fairness, his demolitions of both occasionalism and mere conservationism are rigorous, and his chalk and father-guiding-hand analogies are illuminating models of joint causation.
