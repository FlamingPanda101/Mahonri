# "Without Body, Parts, or Passions" Is a 1530 Protestant Creed, Not Ancient

## The Point
The creedal description of God as "without body, parts, or passions" — one of the beliefs Latter-day Saints find most troubling — does **not appear in any Christian creed until the Lutheran Augsburg Confession of 1530**, more than a thousand years after the councils discussed in this chapter. The First Vision's joke that this God is "incomprehensible" targets a *Protestant* formula, not the Nicene faith. Moreover, the early Church genuinely debated whether God has a body, with major fathers on *both* sides — so divine incorporeality was never the settled, uncontested "Christian" doctrine it is sometimes presented as.

## Evidence
1. "The statement that God is 'without body, parts, or passions' does not appear in Christian creeds until the Lutheran Augsburg Confession (AD 1530) more than a thousand years after the authors and events … discussed in this chapter."[^1]
2. Discussions of God's "impassibility" and "incorporeality" did begin in the 2nd–3rd centuries, rooted in [[Apophatic Theology Describes God by Negation|apophatic (negative) theology]] — defining God by what he is *not* (not passible, not corporeal), extending biblical language like God being "invisible" (Colossians 1:15) and "unsearchable" (Romans 11:33).[^2]
3. **Impassibility's real meaning:** it arose in 2nd-century apologetics contrasting God with the lust- and rage-driven gods of Olympus. The point "was not to argue that God is without feeling but that God is not controlled by base human passions" — God has love but not lust, godly sorrow but not paralyzing grief. "Most Latter-day Saints would likely be sympathetic to this view."[^3]
4. **The body debate had fathers on both sides.** Scripture describes God with a face (Exodus 33:11), a back (Exodus 33:23), wings (Psalm 91:4), and as "spirit" (John 4:24) — which to read literally was contested.[^4]
5. **[[Tertullian]] (God has a body):** closest to Stoicism — "Everything that exists is body of some kind or another. Nothing is incorporeal except what does not exist." Even fire and spirit are bodies.[^5]
6. **[[Origen]] (God has no body):** closest to Platonism — those who think God has a body have "a degraded conception of God," since all bodies are "divisible and material and corruptible."[^6]
7. **Correlation ≠ causation:** the author cautions that philosophy did not simply "corrupt" the Christian idea of God — it parallels *both* the embodied and disembodied readings, and all sides "relied on a careful reading of Christian scripture."[^7] (A nuance against simplistic LDS "Greek corruption" claims; cf. [[David Paulsen]].)
8. Early *art* depicted the Father, Son, and Spirit "anthropomorphically and corporeally" (e.g., the Arles sarcophagus, c. 325, showing the three creating Adam and Eve).[^8]
9. **The phrase crystallized in the Reformed confessional tradition.** The exact triad was carried from the 1530 Augsburg Confession into the Thirty-Nine Articles (1563) and the Westminster Confession (1646, 2.1) — confirming its early-modern, *Protestant* provenance. Its denial of trained *passions* and an *embodied* middle of the person runs against [[The Head-Chest-Belly Anatomy of the Soul|C.S. Lewis's anatomy of the soul]] and [[Thick and Clear Religion - Uniting Head and Belly|"thick and clear" religion]], which dignify the embodied person — close to a Latter-day Saint anthropology that refuses to treat body and passion as defects to transcend.

## Best Quotes
> "The statement that God is 'without body, parts, or passions' does not appear in Christian creeds until the Lutheran Augsburg Confession (AD 1530) more than a thousand years after the authors and events … discussed in this chapter." [^1]

> Tertullian: "Everything that exists is body of some kind or another. Nothing is incorporeal except what does not exist." [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is a powerful apologetic correction: the bodiless, partless, passionless God is a *late Protestant* creedal phrase, and even in antiquity the embodied-God reading had a serious champion in [[Tertullian]] (with [[Origen]] on the other side). Latter-day Saints affirm God has a tangible, glorified body (D&C 130:22) and can be sympathetic to the *moderate* sense of impassibility (not ruled by base passions). The honest nuance — that philosophy did not unilaterally "corrupt" the doctrine — should temper overreaching "Hellenization" arguments. See [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]] and [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]].
