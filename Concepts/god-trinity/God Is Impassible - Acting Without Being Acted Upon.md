# God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon

## The Point
Although Feser does not use the word "impassibility" as a section heading in this slice, he derives its core content within the discussion of immutability and life: God acts upon the world without ever being *acted upon* in return. [^1] A passion, classically, is something undergone — a being-moved by another — and to be moved is to have a potential actualized. Since God is pure actuality, He cannot be acted upon, cannot be moved out of His state, cannot suffer change at the hands of His creatures. The striking payoff is that this does *not* make God inert: Feser argues the immutable God is supremely *alive* and *active*, because what is essential to life is operating from an internal principle rather than being dependent on external forces. [^2]

## Evidence
1. **Acting need not mean being changed.** Against the claim that an immutable God could not act, Feser invokes Brian Davies: "what is essential to acting is the bringing about of an effect in another thing, rather than undergoing change oneself as one does so." [^1] Teaching is essentially causing another to learn, not the teacher's own lecturing or altering. So God "causes the world to exist" without thereby "undergoing change himself." [^1]
2. **The immutable God is supremely alive.** The parallel objection — that an immutable God "could not be said to have life" — also commits a fallacy of accident. [^3] On the Aristotelian account, "what is essential to a thing's being alive … is that the thing operate on the basis of an internal principle, as opposed to being entirely dependent on external forces." [^2] A stone acts only when acted upon (thrown through a window); a living thing acts from its own nature. [^2]
3. **God's life is the fullest possible — precisely because He is unacted-upon.** "As the uncaused cause of all things, God certainly acts in a way which does not require his being acted upon by anything else." [^4] Indeed, unlike creatures who must eat and breathe (and so depend on being acted upon), "God acts in a way which is completely independent of anything else. Hence, he can be said to have life in the fullest way possible." [^4] This independence-from-being-acted-upon *is* divine impassibility.

## Best Quotes
> "What is essential to acting is the bringing about of an effect in another thing, rather than undergoing change oneself as one does so." [^1]

> "As the uncaused cause of all things, God certainly acts in a way which does not require his being acted upon by anything else. Hence, he can certainly be said to have life." [^4]

> "God acts in a way which is completely independent of anything else. Hence, he can be said to have life in the fullest way possible." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This is the sharpest point of contact with the Restoration's distinctive doctrine that God has "passions." Feser's God is impassible in the strict sense: nothing the creature does can move Him, grieve Him, or alter His state, because to be so moved would be to have a potential actualized in a purely actual being. Latter-day Saints reject this directly. The Father is a God "of body, parts, **and passions**" (D&C 130:22) — the explicit negation of the creedal "without … passions," a formula that, as scholars note, is itself a 1530 Protestant addition rather than ancient doctrine (see [[Without Body Parts or Passions Is a 1530 Protestant Creed]]). The biblical Yahweh is grieved, angered, and moved with compassion; LDS theology takes this as literal and central, not as accommodative anthropopathism (see [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]] and [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]]). It is worth representing Feser at full strength: his impassibility is *not* the claim that God is cold or unloving — classical theists affirm God *is* love — but that God's love is His pure, unchanging act rather than a reactive emotion He is buffeted by. And his point that the impassible God is *supremely alive* genuinely answers the caricature of classical theism as deism. The remaining LDS objection is that a God who cannot in any way be *affected* by His children is not the relational, responsive Father of the scriptures or of the Restoration — that genuine relationship requires the capacity to be moved, and that classical impassibility, however it protects divine perfection, empties the word "love" of its ordinary, responsive meaning (the apophatic over-extension critiqued in [[Apophatic Theology Describes God by Negation]]).
