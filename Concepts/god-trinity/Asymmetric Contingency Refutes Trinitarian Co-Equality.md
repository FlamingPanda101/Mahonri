# Asymmetric Contingency Refutes Trinitarian Co-Equality

#type/claim

## The Claim

Creedal Trinitarianism asserts two things that cannot both be true: (1) the Son is **eternally begotten** of the Father — and therefore *contingent on* the Father for his existence — and (2) the three persons are **co-equal**. [[Travis Anderson]] presses the asymmetry: the Son is said to depend on the Father, but the Father does not depend on the Son. "If you reverse that, is the father contingent on Jesus? No, he's not. So then how can they be co-equal?" [^1] A genuinely co-equal relation must be symmetric; "eternally begotten" makes it one-directional, so co-equality fails on the Trinitarian's own terms.

A second horn targets **immutability**. The same theology calls God timeless, spaceless, unchanging, and bodiless — yet on the creedal account the Son is begotten, "enters time," "enters physicality," takes a body, and then exits mortality at the resurrection. A being that begins-from-another, enters time and matter, and changes state cannot be the immutable, timeless ground the classical definition requires. "I have not had a good satisfactory answer for how they really reconcile that." [^2] Unlike the existing LDS *defensive* notes that merely show subordination is *compatible* with full divinity (e.g. [[Ontological Equality With Relational Subordination]], [[Begotten Implies Source and Origin]]), this argument is *offensive*: it turns the creed's own commitments — eternal generation **plus** co-equality **plus** immutability — into a self-contradiction.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Contingency is asymmetric, co-equality is symmetric (rating: strong; internal critique)
"Eternally begotten from the father" means "he is a contingent being on the father." Co-equality among the persons is supposed to be mutual and without rank. But the dependence runs only one way — Father → Son, never Son → Father — so the two doctrines describe incompatible relations. The contradiction is internal to creedal theism and needs no LDS premise to generate it. [^1]

### 2. Immutability cannot survive entering and exiting time and body (rating: strong; internal critique)
"If God is unchanging and immutable and they're co-equal and they're all the same thing... How does Jesus Christ have a body and he's fully God and he's one in the same with the spirit and the father, and yet God is not a physical being?" A timeless, immutable God who is begotten, enters time and physicality, and then "exits that as well" has changed and is therefore not immutable — or was never timeless to begin with. This dovetails with [[Embodied Risen Christ Strains the Trinity]], which presses the body/consubstantiality tension from the resurrection side. [^2]

### 3. The Father-greater texts read naturally on the LDS model (rating: moderate; scriptural)
Jesus repeatedly subordinates himself — "my Father is greater than I" ([[John 14.28|John 14:28]]), and after the resurrection "I ascend to my God and your God, my Father and your Father" — language in which the Son *has* a God. On the LDS Godhead these are literal relational facts between distinct beings; on co-equal consubstantiality they must be qualified away as referring only to the incarnate state. The plain reading favors asymmetry, which is exactly what defeats co-equality. [^3]

### 4. The classical definition of God is itself imported, not biblical (rating: supporting)
The whole problem arises because creedal theism defines deity as the "unmoved mover... immutable force," outside time, space, and matter. The hosts deny this is the biblical God and treat it as Hellenistic metaphysics (see [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]], [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]). Drop the Aristotelian definition and the contradictions in 1-2 dissolve — but then the creedal Trinity, which is built on that definition, loses its footing. [^4]

### Steelman (the Trinitarian reply)
The careful Nicene/Thomist answer denies that "eternally begotten" entails *contingency* in any dependence-of-being sense. Eternal generation is a **relation of origin** *within one identical, eternal essence*: the Son receives the *same* undivided divine being the Father has, timelessly and necessarily, so there is no moment at which the Son might not exist and no superiority of being in the Father — only an order of *processions*. Co-equality is preserved precisely because what is communicated is the *whole* divine nature, not a lesser one. The asymmetry is logical/relational, not a chain of dependence among separate beings (to make the persons separate beings, the Trinitarian says, is to misread "begotten" as production). On immutability, Chalcedon locates all change in the assumed *human* nature: the divine nature neither begins, enters time, nor acquires a body; the one person of the Son does so *according to his humanity* while the divine essence remains timeless and impassible. So eternal generation, co-equality, and immutability are mutually consistent once "contingent," "begotten," and "enters time" are not read univocally with creaturely cases. The LDS rejoinder is that "same numerical essence shared by three persons, one of whom is timelessly produced by another" is not a coherent notion but a label pinned over the very tension at issue (cf. [[The Western Athanasian Trinity Is Self-Contradictory]], [[Three Persons Sharing a Nature Are Still Three Beings]]), and that the two-natures escape is itself a Greek-metaphysical artifact rather than anything the biblical authors taught.
