# Who Designed the Designer - God Is Uncreated

## The Point
The central argument of *The God Delusion* is the "who designed the designer" objection. [[Richard Dawkins]] writes that "a designer God cannot be used to explain organized complexity because any god capable of designing anything would have to be complex enough to demand the same kind of explanation in his own right. God presents an infinite regress." To explain complexity, he insists, "we have to go back to simplicity"; postulating a complex designer "explains precisely nothing."[^1] [[John Lennox]] replies that the objection assumes God is *created* — "I'm not surprised therefore that you call the book the God delusion because created gods are by definition a delusion." But "none of us believe in created gods. Jews, Muslims or Christians." The God of theism "was not created. He is eternal. This is the fundamental distinction between God and the universe. It came to exist. He did not."[^2]

## Evidence
Lennox turns the demand for "simplicity" against Dawkins, arguing that explanation in science routinely runs from simple to complex. An apple falling is simple; its explanation by Newton's law stretches the mind, and its explanation by a warp in spacetime stretches "the cleverest" — "simplicity isn't the only criterion of truth."[^3] His signature illustration: an archaeologist who sees two scratches on a cave wall and infers the Chinese character for "human being" is not refuted by the protest that "you're postulating something as complex as a human brain to explain two scratches." We rightly infer intelligence from the scratches because they "carry meaning" — they have "a semiotic dimension."[^4] Dawkins counters that the scratches' simplicity is irrelevant: the human who made them is complex and "we would both need an explanation for where that complex living being came from"; you cannot "duck the issue by saying God was always there."[^5] Lennox presses back that Dawkins, as a materialist, presumably believes "matter and energy and the laws of nature were always there" — so he too "believes in something eternal"; the real difficulty is "believing in an eternal *person*."[^6] On the fine-tuning that frames this exchange, see [[Anthropic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer]] and [[Multiverse as Ad-Hoc Escape from Fine-Tuning]].

## Best Quotes
> "The God who created the universe... was not created. He is eternal. This is the fundamental distinction between God and the universe. It came to exist. He did not." [^2]

> "You're postulating something as complex as a human brain to explain two scratches... Now it seems to me that's exactly what you're saying in your book. The reason we can deduce something as sophisticated as human intelligence from two scratches on a cave wall is because they have a semiotic dimension." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology answers the "who made God?" regress from a different direction than classical theism but reaches the same anti-regress conclusion: there are uncreated realities. The Restoration affirms that "intelligence... was not created or made, neither indeed can be" ([[D&C 93]]:29), and the [[King Follett Discourse]] teaches that the fundamental constituents of reality are eternal — never brought into being from nothing. Where Lennox locates the regress-stopper in an eternal, uncreated God, the LDS view locates eternality in God *and* in co-eternal intelligence and matter, equally refusing Dawkins' assumption that everything (including any designer) must be a created, contingent thing demanding a prior cause. Both reject [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation ex nihilo]] as the unexamined premise smuggled into the objection.
