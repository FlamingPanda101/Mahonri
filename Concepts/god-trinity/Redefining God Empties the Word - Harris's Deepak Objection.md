# Redefining God Empties the Word — Harris's Deepak Objection

## The Point

[[Sam Harris]] objects that [[Jordan Peterson]]'s evolutionary-psychological definition of God (see [[God as the Highest Value and Truthful Speech]]) "robs the traditional noun of its traditional meaning." By redefining God as something he can accept, Peterson lets believers think their ordinary supernatural faith is being vindicated when it is not — a rhetorical move Harris likens to Deepak Chopra's.

## Argument (steelman)

1. **The ghost analogy.** Most people mean something specific by "ghost"; if Harris says "I don't believe in ghosts" and is told "you do — a ghost is your relationship to the unseen," he can agree to the redefinition while noting "that simply isn't what most people mean by a ghost." Same with God.[^1]

2. **The God Peterson described isn't the God of the pews.** "I was not hearing a personal God who can possibly hear anyone's prayer, much less answer them" — nor one who could "care if anyone masturbated." Harris asks what percentage of religious people have *that* God in mind.[^2]

3. **The Deepak Chopra comparison.** "It's a little bit the kind of thing I get into with Deepak Chopra" — letting an audience believe everything they already accept is "justifiable by his reading of quantum spookiness." Peterson half-accepts the analogy as "more invidious than it sounds."[^3]

4. **"Own it."** Harris's frustration with the applause: if Peterson is "not playing a religious fundamentalist game," he should make that unmistakable, not let believers who think "Jesus is very likely coming back in their lifetime" hear validation. "What's all the applause about? Own it."[^4]

5. **The resurrection litmus.** Harris presses the "40 hours to answer" anecdote: a clear-cut "almost certainly not" on the literal resurrection would be a fine answer; Peterson's hedging is precisely what worries him. Peterson replies it "isn't obvious in the biblical account that Christ was literally resurrected," so it "isn't simple."[^5]

6. **Peterson's defense.** He never claimed to be describing the same God others mean; the Old Testament itself warns against "defining it too tightly" (not uttering the divine name), so demanding "the classical definition of God" is "a historical non-starter."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "By shifting the definition, you have robbed the noun, the traditional noun, of its traditional meaning."[^1]

> "You're not playing a religious fundamentalist game — so what's all the applause about? Own it."[^4]
