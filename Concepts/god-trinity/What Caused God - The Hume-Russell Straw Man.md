# What Caused God - The Hume-Russell Straw Man

## The Point
**Objection:** "If everything has a cause, then what caused God?" — presented by the New Atheists (Dawkins, Dennett, Harris, Hitchens) as a devastating retort.[^1] **Reply:** None of the serious cosmological arguments rests on the premise that "everything has a cause"; some of their defenders explicitly *deny* it.[^2] The whole point of the arguments is to establish that there must be something that not only lacks a cause but *could not even in principle have had one* — precisely because it lacks the feature (potentiality, composition, a real distinction of essence and existence, contingency) that makes other things need a cause.[^3] So the question, fairly read, amounts to "What caused the thing that cannot in principle have had a cause?" — which is incoherent.[^4]

## Evidence
- Each proof gives a *principled*, non-arbitrary reason God needs no cause while other things do: the **Aristotelian** — what is purely actual has no potentials to be actualized; the **Neo-Platonic** — what is absolutely simple has no parts to be composed; the **Thomistic** — what *is* existence has no essence to be conjoined to existence; the **rationalist** — what is necessary could not have been otherwise.[^3]
- The objection is so common because it is aimed at a "nearly omnipresent straw man" — the so-called "basic cosmological argument" ("everything has a cause, so the universe has a cause, namely God"), which *no major defender in the history of philosophy actually advanced* (not Aristotle, Plotinus, Avicenna, Aquinas, Leibniz, Clarke, or any other).[^5] Even Le Poidevin, who frames it this way, admits "no-one has defended a cosmological argument of precisely this form."[^6]
- Feser traces the straw man's genealogy via Thomist W. Norris Clarke: Hume confused two senses of "cause" — *efficient cause distinct from its effect* versus *sufficient reason* — misreading Descartes' and Spinoza's claim that "everything has a [sufficient-reason] cause" (which makes no exception for God) as the claim that "everything has an [efficient] cause."[^7] He then attributed this hybrid "metaphysical monster" to cosmological arguments generally, and Russell, Mill, and the textbook tradition repeated it ever since.[^8]
- A serious critic must grapple with the details and try to show the relevant notions (pure actuality, absolute simplicity, the identity of essence and existence) are incoherent; "he cannot short-circuit them with a single smug question."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Part of the point of the arguments is to establish that there must be something that not only lacks a cause but could not even in principle have had one, precisely because it lacks the very feature that makes other things in need of a cause." [^3]

> "Not one of the many prominent defenders of the cosmological argument in the history of Western philosophy ever actually put forward anything like this so-called 'basic cosmological argument'." [^5]

> "Thus the First Cause argument for the existence of God which the Hume-Russell tradition so devastatingly attacks is indeed an inviable metaphysical monster. But it is a monster of their own fabrication, not that of any reputable theistic philosopher." (W. Norris Clarke, quoted by Feser) [^9]

## LDS Connection
This reply is a strong shared weapon against atheism: the "What caused God?" gotcha collapses for *any* theism that affirms an uncaused, self-existent God — Restoration theism included. Latter-day Saints hold God to be without beginning and self-existent, and they can press exactly Feser's point that the regress must stop at something that could not have been caused (compare the parallel Turek treatment, [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]]). The honest divergence is *which feature* makes God uncausable. Feser's answer is the classical package — pure actuality, absolute divine simplicity, essence identical to existence — and the Restoration rejects those attributes for an embodied, anthropomorphic God (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]] and [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]). An LDS thinker can locate God's beginninglessness instead in His being eternal alongside eternal matter and intelligences (D&C 93:29), rather than in *actus purus*; cf. [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]. The logical core — a coherent stopping-point that by its nature needs no cause — is fully available to the Restoration; only the metaphysics of that stopping-point differ.
