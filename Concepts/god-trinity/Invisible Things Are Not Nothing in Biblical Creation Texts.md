# Invisible Things Are Not Nothing in Biblical Creation Texts

#type/claim

## The Claim

Several creation texts say God made the visible "from the invisible" or "from things not seen." [[Paul Copan]] and [[William Lane Craig]] treat "invisible things" as equivalent to **absolute nothing**, yielding *creatio ex nihilo*. [[Blake Ostler]] argues the opposite: in the ancient world "invisible things are still things that are simply not seen" — an already-existing, formless, pre-cosmic substrate not visible to mortal eyes. Reading "invisible" as "nonexistent" is a category error that the texts themselves refute.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Hebrews 11:3.** "The worlds were framed by the word of God, so that things which are seen were not made of things which do appear" (μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων — "out of unseen things"). The verb is καταρτίζω — to frame, organize, or put in order what is not yet organized — not to create from nothing. The "invisible things" are not nothing; they already exist.[^2] Notably, the Septuagint renders the precreation state of Genesis 1:2 (*tohu wa-bohu*) as ἀόρατος καὶ ἀκατασκεύαστος ("**invisible** and unformed") — so "invisible" is the LXX's own word for the unformed *matter* God shapes, not for nonexistence.[^3]

2. **2 Peter 3:5–6.** Heaven and earth were "formed out of water and through water by the Word of God" — the verb συνεστῶσα (from συνίστημι) means to organize by combining together, not to create from nothing. The double "out of / through" water emphasizes a material substrate; the very next verse identifies that water with the chaotic "deep" of Genesis 1:2 that destroys the world in the flood.[^4]

3. **2 Enoch and the Odes of Solomon.** God "moved around in the invisible things" before any visible thing existed, and is himself "invisible among the invisible things." One cannot move among what does not exist; the invisible things "coexisted with God." The Odes likewise has God *investigate* "that which is invisible" before creating — a potency that absolute nothing could not possess.[^5] See [[Two-Stage Creation Is an Ad Hoc Rescue Device]].

4. **Colossians 1:16 and 2 Corinthians 4:18.** "Everything visible and everything invisible — thrones, dominions, principalities, powers" plainly includes existing realities in heaven; "the things which are not seen are eternal." Invisible ≠ nonexistent.[^6]

5. **Hubler on the philosophical fit.** "The notion of creation μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων was comfortable for Platonic dualists or Stoics, because it lacked all qualities" — i.e., the formula naturally denotes formless, quality-less matter, exactly the substrate of *creatio ex materia*.[^7] See [[Relative Non-Being vs Absolute Nothing - The Greek Distinction Copan and Craig Miss]].

## Supporting Evidence (Copan & Craig, steelmanned)

Copan and Craig argue (citing Paul Ellingworth and William Lane) that Hebrews 11:3's denial that the visible came "from things that appear" is meant to exclude *any* observable pre-material, and that creation "by the word of God" connotes effortless, unconditioned origination — God speaks, and it is. They grant that "invisible things" can exist (angels, thrones) but contend the author's *point* is that the cosmos did not arise from any prior stuff at all; otherwise the contrast "not from what is visible" would be trivial. They then apply their two-stage reading: even the invisible substrate was itself first created from nothing.

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

The decisive datum is the Septuagint: Genesis 1:2's *matter* is literally called "invisible" (ἀόρατος), so a first-century Jewish author writing "the visible was made from the invisible" is most naturally invoking that unformed-but-real substrate, not nothingness.[^3] Creation "by God's word" does not entail *ex nihilo* — 2 Peter 3:5 has God creating *from water* by his word, and Egyptian, Philonic, and rabbinic parallels all use word-creation over pre-existing matter.[^4] And the two-stage rescue collapses on 2 Enoch and the Odes, where God demonstrably acts *among* and *investigates* the invisible things — they are there before he creates. Far from teaching *ex nihilo*, "Hebrews 11:3 implicitly assumes creation of the earth out of a preexisting substrate not visible to us."[^8]
