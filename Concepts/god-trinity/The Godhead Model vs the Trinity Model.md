# The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model

## The Claim

There are two competing models of divine plurality-in-unity. The **Trinity model** says the three persons are *the same being* (one substance). The **Godhead model** says the three persons are *distinct beings* who share the divine nature and have *voluntarily united their wills* into one will — forming a single governing entity (a "council" or "court") that is ultimate and supreme. Hansen argues the Godhead model fits all the biblical data without contradiction, while the Trinity model contradicts it. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The council/court analogy.** Three persons can form one entity that takes singular pronouns: "the court has found him guilty," "the council has decided," "the opinion of the court." We do this in *every* language for kingships, quorums, and councils. So three divine *whos* forming one *what* (the Godhead) is ordinary usage, not a metaphysical paradox. (Evidence: solid linguistic point; the singular-of-a-collective is cross-linguistically common.) [^2]
2. **United will, not numerically identical will.** The Godhead's oneness is a *unity of will* — "three wills combined into one will because we all want the same thing" — modeled in real time when Jesus submits his will to the Father's, and into which disciples are invited (John 17). This explains the oneness texts without collapsing the persons. [^3]
3. **Steelman.** The Trinitarian objects that a merely *united* will is exactly tritheism with good manners — three gods who happen to agree — and that the creeds insist on *numerical* oneness of essence precisely to rule this out; a "council" of three omnipotent beings is still three beings. Moreover, mainstream social trinitarians (Swinburne, the Cappadocians on some readings) occupy nearly the same logical space, so the LDS "Godhead" may differ from "social Trinity" mostly in vocabulary and in denying *creatio ex nihilo*/consubstantiality. (Evidence: a fair challenge — the line between "social Trinity" and "Godhead/tritheism" is genuinely contested.) The LDS reply distinguishes the models by their treatment of *being* and *will*: the Godhead never claims one substance, so it owes no account of how one being has three wills. [^4]
4. **The deciding question.** "Which one better lines up with scripture?" Hansen's wager: the Godhead model makes sense of Gethsemane, "my God my God," "the Father is greater," "the only true God," and the begetting language; the same-being model contradicts each. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "The Godhead model says there are three persons who share the divine nature. ... there's not — in essence, there are three wills, but they're three wills combined into one will cuz we all want the same thing." [^3]

> "We use the word Godhead to refer to this council of three that is the ultimate governing authority over all things. The one what... We have one what, we have three who's. This makes total sense." [^2]

> "The Trinity model says that those three persons are the same being. We just say that the three persons form an entity like a court, which has singular pronouns. Again, which makes more sense?" [^1]
