# Ancient Israelite Henotheism Was Not Mormon Henotheism

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] grants the scholarly point that "the early Israelites believed there were many gods and Yahweh was the only one that they should worship," but argues this **early henotheism was categorically unlike Mormon henotheism**. The ancient Israelites, he says, worshipped Yahweh because they believed he was "the incomparable Creator" — "unique and supreme among all beings, earthly and celestial" — and the other deities were "lesser, subordinate beings... called sons of God who we now recognize to be angels." Crucially, "Yahweh did not used to be a man who was exalted or divinized by some other being." So when Israel "eventually learned about his unique supremacy in full monotheism," that was a development *toward* the truth that Yahweh always uniquely was God — whereas Mormon henotheism (on Horn's reading) makes God one exalted man among an infinite series. He clinches it with Isaiah 44:8 ("Is there a God besides me? I know not any") and Isaiah 43:10 ("before me no God was formed").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, concession-based)** Horn does not deny the divine-council data; he reinterprets its trajectory: ancient Israelite belief "was not a mere henotheism that arbitrarily favored Yahweh over other gods."[^1] This is a more sophisticated move than flat denial and engages the same texts Latter-day Saints cite.
2. **(Moderate, scriptural)** Isaiah 44:8 and 43:10 are read as Yahweh's claim to be uncreated and unique: "If the God of this world were omniscient, wouldn't he know about the God that he worshiped when he was a man?"[^2] See [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]].
3. **(Moderate)** The "sons of God = angels" identification reduces the other elohim to creatures, blocking any inference from the divine council to a plurality of true gods. Compare the LDS reading in [[Bene Elohim Are Sons Not a Different Species]] and [[Other Gods Are Real Divine Beings Not Merely Idols]].

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Latter-day Saint (and much of the critical-scholarly) reply is that Horn is reading the *latest* monotheizing layer of the Hebrew Bible back onto the earliest. On this view, pre-exilic Israel genuinely affirmed a divine spectrum with a high god and subordinate deities ([[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]], [[The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh]], [[Deuteronomy 32 Dead Sea Scrolls Monolatry]]), and the strict-monotheism declarations of Isaiah 40–55 are a Deutero-Isaiah development, not the original baseline. The "sons of God = mere angels" equation is itself the anachronism — the Septuagint's angelic re-rendering is later ([[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]]), and Psalm 82 reads more naturally as a divine council than as a tribunal of human judges ([[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]). The Isaiah "no God formed" formula targets manufactured idols in a courtroom polemic, not the existence of other divine beings ([[Isaiah 43-10 - No God Formed Before Me Rebutted]]).

## Counter-objection
Even granting Horn's developmental story, it does not by itself rebut the LDS model so much as restate classical theism's conclusion: that *real* divinity is incommunicable and uniquely Yahweh's. The LDS distinctive is precisely that divinity *is* communicable — that the "sons of God" share the Father's kind, and that exaltation extends that kind to the redeemed ([[Humans Are the Same Species as God (Acts 17 Offspring)]], [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]]). Horn's reading also has to absorb the awkward fact that the very texts most useful to him (Deutero-Isaiah's negations) are framed as boasts of *superiority* over rivals, a rhetorical form attested in ancient Near Eastern taunts ([[Babylon Taunt Formula Shows Negation Is a Boast of Superiority]]) rather than as bare metaphysical census-taking. So the disagreement again resolves into *which* layer of Israelite religion is normative and whether divinity is the sort of thing that can be shared.

## Apologetic Engagement
Horn's version is the best Catholic answer to the divine-council argument: concede the data, contest the trajectory. Latter-day Saints should not treat him as denying the *bene elohim* exist; they should contest (a) the dating that makes strict monotheism original and (b) the claim that the council members are mere creatures. The relevant vault clusters are the divine-council notes and the Deutero-Isaiah material.

## LDS Relevance
This note is the henotheism half of Horn's "Reason 1" (the metaphysics half is [[Mormonism Rejects Classical Theism So the God Objection Bites]]). Its payoff for Latter-day Saints is the reminder that the strongest critics no longer deny the divine council outright — so the live argument is developmental and exegetical, handled by [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]], [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]], and the Isaiah rebuttals.
