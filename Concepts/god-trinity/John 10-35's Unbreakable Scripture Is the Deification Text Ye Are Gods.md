# John 10:35's Unbreakable Scripture Is the Deification Text Ye Are Gods

#type/claim

## The Claim

When Evangelical apologist [[Frank Turek]] quotes **"the scripture cannot be broken"** (John 10:35) to defend biblical inerrancy, LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] (on [[Ward Radio]] with [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Jonah Barnes]]) points out that the scripture Jesus is calling unbreakable in that very verse is **"ye are gods"** ([[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]], quoted in [[John 10.34-36|John 10:34]]). So an inerrantist who treats this clause as decisive is unwittingly leaning on a **deification proof-text** while omitting its actual content. The verse most often used to prooftext "the Bible can't be wrong" is, in context, Jesus defending the claim that humans can be called "gods."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "unbreakable scripture" just is Psalm 82:6 — rate: strong (exegesis)
In [[John 10.34-36|John 10:34-36]] Jesus replies to the blasphemy charge by citing "your law": *"I said, Ye are gods"* ([[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]). His logic — "If he called them gods, unto whom the word of God came; and the scripture cannot be broken" — makes the deification statement the premise the inerrancy-claim is protecting. The clause Turek isolates is grammatically subordinate to, and rhetorically in service of, "ye are gods."[^1]

### 2. The apologist quotes the wrapper but drops the content — rate: strong (rhetorical critique)
Carroll's point is that selectively quoting "the scripture cannot be broken" for inerrancy, while ignoring *which* scripture, is a textual sleight of hand: you cannot invoke the unbreakability of a verse to win a debate about the Bible's authority while suppressing the very assertion that verse certifies. If "the scripture cannot be broken," then neither can "ye are gods."[^1]

### 3. It fits the broader double-standard thesis — rate: moderate (framing)
The episode's larger argument is that apologists like Turek argue *as believers* (inference to the best explanation from witnesses) for the resurrection but switch methods when the same standard is turned on Latter-day Saint claims. The John 10:35 catch is a specimen of that selectivity at the level of a single citation — see [[Apologists Drop the Resurrection Evidential Standard When Judging the Gold Plates]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A Trinitarian has replies. (a) Jesus's use of Psalm 82 is widely read as an *a fortiori* argument ("if mere men/judges addressed by God's word could be called 'gods,' how much more the Son"), not as an endorsement of human deification — so quoting "the scripture cannot be broken" for inerrancy need not commit Turek to LDS theosis. (b) "Ye are gods" in Psalm 82 may address Israel's unjust *judges* (or the divine council) under judgment, a reading that blunts the exaltation gloss — the vault treats the divine-council reading separately in [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]. What survives is narrower but pointed: the inerrantist's go-to verse is not a neutral statement about scripture's reliability — it is embedded in Jesus's appeal to a "ye are gods" text, so the apologist cannot wield the clause while pretending its surrounding claim is innocuous.

## Best Quote
