# The Trinity Cannot Count to Three

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The doctrine that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three distinct persons in one substance/being. [[Blake Ostler]] endorses the quip that "trinitarians don't know how to count to three" and argues the standard construals collapse into recognized heresies. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The trilemma (rating: strong; logical)
Take three claims: Yahweh is the Father; Yahweh is the Son; the Son is distinct from the Father. "Any three of them is going to entail a well-worn heresy." [^1]
- **Identify** Father and Son → modalism.
- **Distinguish** them as separate gods → polytheism (which Trinitarians reject).
- **Make them "the exact same being"** → you must then *define* "being," which reopens the problem. [^2]

> "if the father is the father and Yahweh is the son and the son is distinct from the father, then those three claims are inconsistent. Any three of them is going to entail a well-worn heresy." [^1]

### 2. "One substance" only escapes by equivocation (rating: strong; philosophical)
The escape "they are one substance" depends entirely on what *substance* means. Aristotle uses "substance" for each individual human, so on that usage the three persons would be *three substances*, not one. The word is "equivocal." See [[Substance Is Equivocal in the Trinity]]. [^3]

### 3. The Trinity is contrary to reason on its own admission (rating: strong; uses their authorities)
Citing [[Edward Feser]] and [[Thomas Aquinas]], Ostler notes classical theologians admit the Trinity is a *revealed* (not reasoned) doctrine and even "contrary to reason": "Nobody just by reasoning is going to coherently come up with one being that is three beings." If "being" is used univocally, "that's clearly a contradiction," like saying "there are three chairs and there's one chair." See [[Trinity Is a Scholarly Creation Not a Revealed Doctrine]]. [^4]

### 4. The "you can't expect me to understand it" out (rating: strong; rhetorical)
When pressed on coherence, the Trinitarian retreats to mystery ("you can't expect me to understand the Trinity"). Ostler's rejoinder: "Then you can't expect me to understand what you're saying — or to believe that it's actually what is being taught. How could I be wrong about it if you don't understand it?" [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The orthodox reply is that "person" (*hypostasis*) and "substance" (*ousia*) are *not* used in the same sense, so "three persons, one substance" is no more contradictory than "three sides, one triangle"; the charge of "counting to three" allegedly conflates the categories the doctrine carefully separates. Defenders (e.g., relative-identity or constitution accounts) argue the formula is mysterious but not formally inconsistent. Ostler grants the categories *can* be separated but argues that once "substance" is given any determinate sense, either it yields three substances (Aristotelian) or, under divine simplicity, it cannot host three really-distinct persons. See [[Substance Is Equivocal in the Trinity]] and [[Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail]].

## Best Quotes

> "The claim is often made that trinitarians don't know how to count to three. It's a valid claim." [^1]

> "Nobody just by reasoning is going to coherently come up with one being that is three beings... if being is used in a univocal sense, that's clearly a contradiction." [^4]
