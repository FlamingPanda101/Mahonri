# Origen on Postmortem Progression in the School for Souls

## The Point
Origen of Alexandria (c. 185-254) taught that **human progression toward God continues after death** — but eventually ends. Becerra reports that, for Origen, after death the spirits of the good remain in a "paradise" he describes as a "school for souls," where the departed learn the nature and destiny of the earth, the stars, and "the various works of God," progressing in knowledge until they "ascend to see the face of God." [^1] Critically, the absence of a physical body lets a person "learn differently," growing "not as it grew in this life in the flesh... but increasing in mind and intelligence." [^2] At the journey's end the person becomes one with God — but progression *terminates*, "because it has an end." [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Paradise as a classroom.** "He [God] will show to them, as to sons, the causes of things and the perfection of his creation, teaching them why one star is placed in its particular position in the sky." [^1]
2. **Learning here equips learning there.** "The more one learns in mortality, the better equipped one will be to participate in this kind of learning in the afterlife," though some learning requires being free of the "carnal senses." [^2]
3. **Union is intellectual, and final.** For Origen unity means God will be "all in all" (1 Corinthians 15:28): "the mind will no longer be conscious of anything besides or other than God." There is "a point at which humans will stop progressing and rest in God." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "[The departed] will be taught about... the things which are not seen as they progress in knowledge and will eventually ascend to see the face of God." [^1]

> "[A person grows] not as it grew in this life in the flesh... but increasing in mind and intelligence, progressing toward perfect knowledge and the contemplation and understanding of God." — Origen [^2]

## LDS Connection
The "school for souls" — postmortem **learning and progression**, with mortal study aiding afterlife growth — is remarkably close to LDS teaching on the spirit world and continued progression (D&C 130:18-19, "whatever principle of intelligence we attain... will rise with us"). Becerra explicitly contrasts the LDS sense of oneness (united "in purpose and power," possessing "all that the Father hath," similar celestial bodies — D&C 84:38) with Origen's purely *intellectual* union. [^5] Origen's view that progression has an **end** stands opposite the open-ended LDS model — compare [[Gregory of Nyssa's Epektasis - Eternal Progression Toward God]] and [[Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends]].
