# Divine Attributes Follow From Pure Actuality

## The Point
Feser's strategy is to derive the traditional divine attributes not by stipulation but as logical consequences of one premise: the first cause is *purely actual*. Once the regress argument has reached an actualizer "devoid of potentiality," Feser holds that this being thereby possesses "the attributes definitive of God — unity, immateriality, eternity, perfection, omnipotence, and so forth." [^1] An actualizer that still had unrealized potentials "will not be a purely actual actualizer, and thus will not have many of the attributes definitive of God." [^1] So the whole list of classical attributes stands or falls with the claim that the first actualizer has no potentiality whatsoever. [^2]

## Evidence
1. **The attributes are bundled to pure actuality.** Feser explicitly ties having the divine attributes to *being purely actual*: deny pure actuality and you forfeit the attributes; affirm it and "we have to acknowledge an actualizer that has all the divine attributes which follow from being purely actual." [^2] The attribute-derivations (developed in the remainder of the chapter and later chapters) are therefore corollaries of one metaphysical result, not independent natural-theology arguments.
2. **Why limitation tracks potentiality.** The reason the causes of our experience are themselves moved/limited is "precisely because they are limited in the various ways entailed by being mixtures of actual and potential." [^3] An arm is "actually at one point in space and only potentially at another," so its potential must be actualized by something else. A being "which is pure actuality, devoid of all potentiality, would have no such limitations." [^3] This is the seed of immutability (no potential to change), immateriality (matter is the principle of potency), and perfection (no unrealized good).
3. **Immutability already in view.** Because change just is the actualization of a potential, and the first actualizer has no potential, it cannot change — Feser's reply to the "anything that actualizes must itself change" objection turns precisely on this point (a purely actual cause need "not be actualized itself as it is actualizing other things"). [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "As long as it has them, it will not be a purely actual actualizer, and thus will not have many of the attributes definitive of God — unity, immateriality, eternity, perfection, omnipotence, and so forth." [^1]

> "And thus we have to acknowledge an actualizer that has all the divine attributes which follow from being purely actual." [^2]

> "Something which is pure actuality, devoid of all potentiality, would have no such limitations." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The unity and omnipotence on Feser's list are congenial to Restoration theism, and against atheism the move from a self-sufficient cause to a being of supreme power is welcome. But the package is sold as inseparable: pure actuality is meant to yield *immutability*, *timeless eternity*, and *immateriality* in one stroke — and these collide head-on with Latter-day Saint doctrine. The Father has "a body of flesh and bones" (D&C 130:22), is moved by love and sorrow, and progresses; an immaterial, impassible, changeless absolute is the very profile the Restoration identifies as Hellenized accretion ([[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]], [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]). The LDS counter is structural: because Feser bundles every attribute to pure actuality, an LDS critic who rejects the pure-actuality step ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]) is not obligated to accept *any* of the downstream attributes as Feser frames them, and can hold instead that God is supremely powerful yet genuinely conditioned by eternal law ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). To be fair to Feser, the deductive architecture is elegant and avoids the charge of arbitrarily stipulating God's nature — which is exactly why the LDS objection must engage the metaphysics of act and potency, not merely cite proof-texts. (Note: in this slice Feser *names* and motivates these attributes; the detailed derivations of simplicity, eternity, and the rest are developed later in the book.)
