# Trinity as Identity Marker and Costly Signal

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that because the Trinity cannot actually be rendered coherent, affirming it functions less as a conclusion and more as a **social/identity function**: it became "a central identity marker, a costly signal, a credibility enhancing display, a way to police and to structure power and values and boundaries." [^1] To belong, one must be able to say "I can see the circle being squared" — i.e., profess to perceive the resolution that he argues isn't actually there. [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Costly-signaling theory.** In the cognitive/evolutionary science of religion (Sosis, Henrich, etc.), professing a belief that is costly, counter-intuitive, or hard to fake reliably signals group commitment. McClellan applies this: affirming an admittedly "mysterious"/paradoxical doctrine is exactly such a hard-to-fake signal. *Rating: a coherent application of an established theoretical framework; explanatory rather than strictly demonstrative.* *Steelman:* that a true belief *also* functions as an identity marker does not make it false — this is the genetic fallacy if pressed too far. McClellan's argument only bites *given* his prior conclusion that the doctrine is incoherent (see [[Trinity Is Fundamentally Contradictory]]).

2. **The "credibility enhancing display" (CRED) concept.** Drawn from Joseph Henrich's work: people adopt beliefs more readily when others display costly commitment to them. McClellan frames the demand to affirm the Trinity as functioning this way within Christian identity-policing. *Rating: plausible sociological framing.*

3. **Perpetual, unresolved debate is the predicted outcome.** "You have people who can say, 'I see it. The square has become a circle.' And you have a bunch of other people saying, 'no it hasn't.'" [^3] If the doctrine were coherent and inductively true, McClellan implies, this stalemate would not persist; identity-marking explains why people still affirm it anyway. *Rating: consistent with his thesis, contestable on the inference from "debated" to "false."*

## Best Quotes
> "It became a central identity marker, a costly signal, a credibility enhancing display, a way to police and to structure power and values and boundaries." [^1]

> "That's why you have people who can say, 'I see it. The square has become a circle.' And you have a bunch of other people saying, 'no it hasn't.'" [^3]

> "We've got all these complex philosophical and novel frameworks that get us almost all the way there and that last little bit you just have to arbitrarily accept." [^2]
