# The Divine Investiture of St Michael Calls Lucifer the First-Formed

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] highlights a pseudepigraphal text he calls the **Divine Investiture of St Michael** (drawn from the Vatican collections) for two Latter-day Saint resonances. First, it teaches **premortality**: God creates Adam, turns to Jesus and warns the creation "is going to be pretty painful," and Jesus answers "I know — I'll go down there and save him," a premortal council scene. Second, and more unusually, it calls **Lucifer the "first-formed"** — the *protoplast*, the first thing God formed — who upon his fall is *demoted* from the title "first-formed" to "the accuser." Barnes flags this as fascinating (he is non-committal on its truth) because it fits a biblical *birthright* logic in which the firstborn can forfeit the birthright to a younger, chosen heir.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. An explicit premortal volunteering scene — rate: moderate (clear parallel motif)
The text's Father–Son exchange before the creation/fall — the Son foreseeing the cost and volunteering to redeem — parallels the Latter-day Saint premortal council (cf. [[Jesus and Lucifer Were Two Plans in the War in Heaven]]). It is one more apocryphal instance of premortal-deliberation imagery the canon only hints at.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the volunteering motif is a recognizable parallel, though premortal-council scenes are diffuse across late apocrypha.

### 2. "First-formed" (protoplast) demoted to "accuser" — rate: moderate (distinctive datum)
The claim that Lucifer was the *protoplast* and was renamed upon falling is unusual and specific. Barnes frames it through the Bible's recurring **birthright-forfeiture** pattern: Esau, Reuben, Manasseh, and others lose the birthright to a younger brother — so a "first-formed" who loses his standing to the chosen Christ would be coherent with scriptural type, not alien to it (cf. [[Two Brothers Where the Younger Is Chosen Are Types of Christ and Lucifer]]).[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a striking single detail; its evidential force depends on the text's date and reliability, which Barnes does not establish.

### 3. Barnes flags the tension with primogeniture Christology — rate: moderate (honest internal caveat)
He notes the parallel "might not be well received," because Latter-day Saints normally call **Christ** the *primogenito* (firstborn). His reconciliation: Jehovah was a co-creator with the Father, so "first-formed" may mean formed by the Father alone vs. by the Father-and-Son — and birthrights in scripture are famously *transferable*. He explicitly leaves the question open.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a candid acknowledgment that the text cuts against a standard LDS title; the harmonization is plausible but speculative.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Provenance and date are unestablished.** A late, obscure pseudepigraphon from "the Vatican vaults" carries little weight for primitive belief; without a critical edition and dating, the "first-formed Lucifer" line is an isolated curiosity.
- **It contradicts the LDS firstborn Christology.** Mainstream LDS teaching makes *Christ* the firstborn of the Father's spirit children; a text crowning *Lucifer* as first-formed is in tension with that, and Barnes's "co-creator" fix is ad hoc.
- **"First-formed" need not mean a literal birth order.** In many ancient angelologies a chief angel is "first" by rank or splendor, not by a Mormon-style spirit-birth sequence — so reading it as LDS premortal genealogy imports a framework the text need not share.
- **Selective resonance.** The same document doubtless contains much that is *not* LDS-friendly; lifting two congenial motifs is parallel-hunting.

**What survives:** the premortal-volunteering scene is a real, if diffuse, apocryphal echo of the LDS council in heaven. The "Lucifer as first-formed" datum is genuinely distinctive but doctrinally double-edged — it resonates with the biblical *forfeited-birthright* type while sitting awkwardly beside the usual LDS title of Christ as firstborn. Barnes's own posture (interested, explicitly uncommitted) is the right calibration: a suggestive text to think *with*, not a load-bearing proof. For the underlying LDS framework see [[Jesus and Satan Being Spirit Brothers Follows From Rejecting the Trinity]] and [[Spirit Birth and Intelligences]].
