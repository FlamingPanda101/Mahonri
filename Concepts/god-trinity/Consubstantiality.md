# Consubstantiality (Homoousios)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The doctrine of consubstantiality — that the Father and Son are of the *same substance* (*homoousios*) — is a biblical teaching, inherent in texts like [[John 10.30|John 10:30]] ("I and the Father are one") and [[John 1.1|John 1:1]].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Consubstantiality is a 4th-century philosophical solution
The term *homoousios* (same substance/essence) was formalized at the [[Council of Nicaea]] (325 CE) in response to the [[Arius|Arian]] controversy. It is not a biblical term, nor is it a concept the NT authors possessed. It is a solution developed using Greek philosophical categories to answer a philosophical problem: how can Jesus be divine without there being two Gods?[^1]

### 2. The philosophical assumptions were Greek, not Jewish
The doctrine relies on concepts from Greek philosophy (particularly Middle Platonism):
- That God is **immutable** (cannot change)
- That God is **simple** (not composed of parts)
- That divine beings are **eternal** (no beginning)
- That there is one divine "substance" (*ousia*) that can have multiple "persons" (*hypostaseis*)

These are Greek philosophical commitments, not teachings of the Hebrew Bible or the New Testament.[^2]

### 3. It was controversial even at Nicaea
The difference between *homoousios* (same substance) and *homoiousios* (LIKE substance) was a single iota — and this letter difference became a matter of exile. One bishop allegedly tried to sneak in the iota change and was banished. This is not a dispute that arises naturally from Bible reading; it's a dispute born of philosophical precision demands.[^3]

### 4. The "persons vs. being" distinction is a post-biblical innovation
The Nicene framework separates psychological identity ("person") from ontological being ("substance") — saying three persons share one being. This is a novel philosophical move: "when we think of a person today, we think of a being who also has a psychological unity... and what they did was basically divide the idea of the psychological identity and the being and say now that they're different."[^4]

### 5. The NT authors work with agency/representation, not substance
New Testament Christology operates in the framework of divine agency and representation (see [[Divine Name Theology]]) — Jesus does what God does, speaks for God, bears God's name, manifests God's presence. This is a relational/functional Christology, not an ontological/substance Christology. The shift from agency to substance is the shift from the NT to the creeds.[^5]

## Best Quote
> "The doctrines of Christianity develop out of trying to reconcile everything that's going on under this social institution while also trying to make it sound defensible to the broader intellectual world, which is deeply entrenched in Greek philosophy." [^5]
