# Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum

## The Claim

Ancient Jewish "monotheism" was not a denial that other divine beings exist but an affirmation of Yahweh's unique *supremacy*. Divinity was conceived as a *spectrum* (degrees of glory) rather than a binary (God vs. not-God). This plurality-in-unity is what Joseph Smith restored as the Godhead. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The scholarly roster.** [[Joshua Sijuwade]] cites [[Peter Hayman]], [[Nathan McDonald]], [[Paula Fredriksen]], [[Michael Heiser]], [[Larry Hurtado]], and [[Richard Bauckham]] as arguing that ancient Jewish monotheism affirmed Yahweh's unique supremacy rather than the non-existence of other divine beings. (Evidence: strong attribution — these are leading scholars of Second Temple Judaism and early "monotheism"; the characterization fits Hayman's and Heiser's published positions, though Bauckham and Hurtado emphasize Yahweh's *unique* identity/worship more than a flat "spectrum," so the roster is somewhat flattened — see steelman.) [^1]
2. **Qualitative, not numerical.** "The key thing is monotheism was not a numerical oneness. It was a qualitative concept." The one-ness asserted is Yahweh's incomparable *status*, not a head-count of one. [^2]
3. **Sijuwade's Islam contrast.** Islam claims to restore Abrahamic monotheism but actually *reverses* Judaism's core claim — moving from "multiple divine beings under one supreme God" to "no other divine beings at all." So strict numerical monotheism is non-biblical. [^3]
4. **Textual anchors.** [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8-9]] (sons of God / divine allotment) and [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82]] ("ye are gods") supply the divine-council backdrop; the [[Bene Elohim Are Sons Not a Different Species|bene elohim]] are God's *sons*, members of the same family/species at differing degrees of glory. [^4]
5. **Steelman.** The "inclusive monotheism" critics (and even some on the cited roster) push back two ways. (a) Bauckham and Hurtado argue ancient Jews drew a *sharp* line — Yahweh alone is creator and alone receives worship — so "spectrum" understates a real Creator/creature divide; the other "divine beings" are subordinate creatures, not lesser gods of the same kind. (b) Reading this as vindicating *Joseph Smith's* Godhead is a further leap: scholarly "divine plurality" describes an angelic/council hierarchy, not three co-equal-in-nature persons or exalted humans becoming gods. (Evidence: a substantial caution; the LDS use is a constructive extension beyond what most of the cited scholars claim.) The LDS reply: the line Bauckham draws is precisely the *creatio ex nihilo* assumption LDS theology rejects, and the council texts are read as the same divine family. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Ancient Jewish monotheism was not a denial of other divine beings' existence, but rather an affirmation of Yahweh's unique supremacy. Christianity conceived of divinity as a spectrum, rather than a binary." [^1]

> "Monotheism was not a numerical oneness. It was a qualitative concept. Monotheism is something which came later in the 17th century." [^2]

> "Christianity conceived of divinity as a spectrum. That's precisely what Joseph Smith restored." [^5]
