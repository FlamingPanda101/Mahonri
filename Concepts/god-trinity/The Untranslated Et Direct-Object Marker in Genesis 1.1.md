# The Untranslated Et Direct-Object Marker in Genesis 1:1

## The Claim

Genesis 1:1 has **seven words in Hebrew, and two of them are never translated into English at all** — the particle אֵת (*et*), which appears twice: בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים **אֵת** הַשָּׁמַיִם וְ**אֵת** הָאָרֶץ, "[God] created **et** the heavens and **et** the earth."[^1] *Et* is the Hebrew **definite direct-object marker**: a grammatical signal placed before a *specific, definite* object to flag that you are about to "conjure up a specific object rather than a concept or abstract." Magnify glosses it as a kind of "a-to-z" word, because it is spelled with **aleph** (the first letter of the Hebrew alphabet) and **tav** (the last) — "a to Z, or a to zed."[^1]

The apologetic-linguistic point is twofold. First, the English translators silently decided this information was **irrelevant** — "really fascinating to already in the first verse decide that there's information that's irrelevant," especially "if you're claiming to be translating the inspired word of God."[^1] Second, on the mystical reading Magnify reports, *et* is "more than a procedural word; it's a ceremonial word": every time you mark a definite object you "take a moment to remember the a-to-z's, the universe of things from which you have called out that object" — an object "exists as part of a universe of things you are pulling it out from."[^1] That framing dovetails with O'Connor's [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading|pre-existing-matter]] reading of creation as the *delineation* of objects out of an undifferentiated whole, rather than their origination from nothing — see [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The grammatical fact is real.** אֵת is the standard sign of the definite direct object in Biblical Hebrew, and it does occur twice in Gen 1:1 (before *ha-shamayim* and, with the conjunction, before *ha-aretz*) — visible in the consonantal text recorded at [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]]. English has no equivalent particle (object-hood is shown by word order), so it is genuinely untranslatable word-for-word. *(Evidence rating: strong for the morphology — *et* + definite object is uncontroversial Hebrew grammar.)*

2. **The "omission" charge is fair but mild.** That English drops *et* is not a *mistranslation* so much as a structural necessity; the loaded claim is the *inference* that something meaningful is therefore lost. Magnify concedes modern Hebrew speakers "use *et* with as much thought as we use 'a' and 'the.'"[^1] *(Evidence rating: moderate — the loss is real but the burden is on showing the lost content was ever doctrinally load-bearing.)*

3. **The ceremonial/mystical reading is tradition, not lexicon.** The "remember the universe of things" gloss is attributed to "the mystics or the rabbinical scholars… going back a thousand years," not to the grammar itself; it is a *kabbalistic-style* devotional reading of a routine particle. *(Evidence rating: weak as exegesis of authorial intent — it is reception history, valuable as a window on how the marker was spiritualized, not evidence the Priestly writer meant it so.)*

**Steelman of the traditional reading:** Dropping *et* in English costs nothing doctrinally — it is a syntactic placeholder with no semantic content of its own, exactly like declining to "translate" English word-order into a particle-marking language. Every competent translation omits it for the same reason the Septuagint and Vulgate did, and no major theology has ever turned on it. The mystical "a-to-z / universe of things" reading is a later homiletic overlay (the same impulse that finds cosmic meaning in letter-shapes), not a recovery of original meaning; treating its absence as a translation *failure* over-presses a grammatical commonplace.

## Best Quotes

> "Out of the seven words in Genesis 1:1 in Hebrew, two of them are not even translated into English… there's a special kind of word in ancient Hebrew that you use any time you want to specify a specific object in the world — you preface it with a word that signals that you're about to conjure up a specific object." — Christopher Magnify[^1]

> "It's two letters, *et*, made up of aleph, the first letter in Hebrew, and tav, the last — so it's a little bit like saying A to Z, or A to zed, every time you call out a specific object." — Christopher Magnify[^1]

> "Every time you're stopping to reference a specific object you take a moment to remember… the universe of things from which you have called out that object… that's definitely been lost to time." — Christopher Magnify[^1]
