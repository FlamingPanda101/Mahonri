# Jesus and Satan Being Spirit Brothers Follows From Rejecting the Trinity

#type/claim

## The Claim
Host **Hayden Carroll** argues that the standard evangelical "gotcha" — voiced by apologist [[Cliff Knechtel]] — that "Mormonism teaches Jesus and Lucifer are brothers" only sounds damning *on Trinitarian premises*. For a Trinitarian, Father and Son are the *same being*, so an uncreated Christ obviously could not be the brother of a created Lucifer. But Latter-day Saints reject that premise: Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are **three distinct beings one in purpose**, and Christ, Lucifer, and humans are all **spirit children of God the Father** — so Christ and Lucifer being spirit brothers is coherent, while in no way implying they are *equals* (Christ is the chosen, righteous Only Begotten; Lucifer the rebel cast out for pride). The oneness of [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] is read as *unity of purpose* extended to disciples, not numerical identity of being.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The charge presupposes the Trinity.** Hayden: "the reason why Protestants and Catholics will never be able to understand this... is because they believe in the Trinity. The idea that the Father and the Son are literally the same being... so there's no way that Christ could be the brother of Lucifer." Remove that premise and the contradiction dissolves. See [[The Court Analogy for the Godhead]]. *Rating: correctly identifies that the objection is premise-relative; a fair dialectical point.*[^1]
2. **John 17 reads oneness as purpose.** [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] — "that they all may be one as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee" — applies the *same* oneness to believers, which (Hayden argues) cannot be numerical fusion but unity "in purpose." See [[I and the Father Are One Means Mutual Indwelling]]. *Rating: a standard and defensible LDS reading of John 17; the disciples-included logic has real force against strict numerical identity.*[^2]
3. **Not equals — distinct destinies.** Hayden stresses the brothers-claim "is not saying they are on the same level": just as no one thinks Cain and Abel were "spiritual equals," Christ (chosen for surpassing light and righteousness) and Lucifer (cast out for pride in a [[Revelation 12.4|premortal council]]) are radically unequal. The objection trades on conflating *kinship* with *parity*. See [[Two Brothers Where the Younger Is Chosen Are Types of Christ and Lucifer]]. *Rating: a clarification that defuses the rhetorical force of the "brothers" framing.*[^3]
4. **The Trinity is post-biblical (the weaker leg).** Hayden locates the doctrine at Nicaea (he says "Constantinople, the emperor of Rome gathers everyone... 325 AD") as philosophy read back onto scripture. *Rating: the broad point (the developed Trinity is post-NT) is defensible, but the details are loose — Constantine (not "Constantinople") convened Nicaea, which produced *homoousios* against Arius rather than the full Trinitarian formula; the vault treats this more carefully at [[Homoousios Was Coined to Refute Arius]].*[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A Trinitarian grants the LDS view is *internally* coherent given its premises and still rejects it: (a) Scripture and the earliest creeds confess Christ as *uncreated* ("begotten, not made"), the agent *through whom* all things including angels were made (John 1:3; Colossians 1:16) — so the Creator/creature line falls between Christ and Lucifer, not above both; (b) "spirit brothers" is not merely unequal but *category-confusing*, placing the eternal Word in the same created class as a fallen angel; (c) the historical-development point cuts both ways — that the *word* Trinity is post-biblical does not show the *teaching* is, since pro-Nicene theology claimed to articulate what the apostolic witness already implied. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the dispute is precisely *whether* Christ is uncreated-in-the-creedal-sense or the firstborn of the Father's spirit children, and the "brothers" charge cannot settle that by assuming the creedal answer; LDS theology preserves Christ's unique divine sonship and saving role while denying only the metaphysics of *homoousios* — so the polemic, stripped of its Trinitarian assumption, reduces to a restatement of the very point in dispute (cf. [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]]).

## Best Quotes
> "The reason why Protestants and Catholics will never be able to understand in the same way that we do this sort of doctrine is because they believe in the Trinity... so on its face, yeah, it makes no sense. But if you don't accept the Trinitarian view... then all of a sudden we have something we can make sense of." — Hayden Carroll[^1]

> "We do believe that Christ is a spirit son of God the Father, and that we are also spirit sons and daughters of that same Father, and that Lucifer was one of them who went rogue and was cast out." — Hayden Carroll[^3]
