#type/claim

# Ancient Jewish Readings of Genesis 1.1 Make Elohim the Object Created by the Head God

## The Claim

In the [[King Follett Discourse]], [[Joseph Smith]] re-parsed the Bible's first verse so that *Elohim* ("gods") is the **object** of the verb *bara*, not its subject: dropping the prefixed *bet*, he read *reshit bara Elohim* as "the head one of the gods brought forth the gods" (and "the heaven and the earth"). [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues this is not Joseph "making it crazy" but **converges with a stream of genuinely ancient Jewish readings** that likewise refused the conventional "In the beginning God created" and instead made the gods the thing produced — exactly the [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We|plural-Elohim / divine-council]] picture Latter-day Saints affirm. This is a *different* argument from the [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading|construct-state grammar case]] (which targets *ex nihilo* by making vv. 1–2 a dependent clause); here the point is **who acts and what is brought forth** — a head God bringing forth other gods.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A Targum reads "with wisdom God created" (rated: strong, textual).** Butler reports a Targum (he tentatively names Onkelos) rendering the opening not "in the beginning" but *bahukama* — Aramaic for "with wisdom" — i.e. parsing *ba* as the instrumental "with," *reshit* as "the principal one," and the feminine *-īt* termination as pointing to a feminine being: "with the great feminine one… who is that? That's wisdom." He cross-references [[Proverbs|Proverbs 3:19]], "The LORD by/with wisdom (*behokmah*) founded the earth," where the same *be-* may be instrumental ("with wisdom") and wisdom is also the tree of life.[^2]

2. **The *Sefer Bahir* makes the subject implied in the verb, with *Elohim* as object (rated: strong, textual).** Butler cites the early Jewish-mystical *Sefer Bahir* reinterpreting Genesis 1:1 so that "*Elohim* is not the subject of the sentence; the subject is implied in the verb" — grammatically possible because the *qal* perfective *bara* alone implies a third-person "he/she." The sense becomes "In the beginning **He created** *Elohim*, the heavens and the earth," the implied subject being the *Ein Sof*, "the aspect of God that exists… God beyond God."[^3]

3. **The *Zohar* gives a third "by means of a beginning it created the Elohim" (rated: moderate, textual).** Mike Day adds the *Zohar*'s reading — "by means of a *reshit* (a beginning), it created the *Elohim*" — as a further old Jewish witness that *Elohim* is what is produced. Butler's summary: "These are all old Jewish readings… this is not Mike and Dave sitting around the Institute building going, 'How can we make this crazy?'"[^4]

4. **Joseph triangulates but keeps his own reading (rated: contextual).** Butler notes Joseph "triangulates between" these — asserting (without grammatical proof) that the *bet* was added by "an old Jew without authority," making *reshit* ("the head") the subject who created the gods, the heavens, and the earth. The hosts flag that Joseph's transcript **omits** the feminine force of the *-īt* termination; the friendly Latter-day Saint amendment is that if pressed, the implied agent is "the great woman, the principal woman" — Wisdom / [[Wisdom Personified as the Mother in Heaven|the Mother in Heaven]].[^5] A plausible channel for Joseph's exposure is his Kirtland Hebrew instructor **Joshua Seixas** (a Sephardic Jew at the School of the Prophets), though Butler allows it may equally be Joseph's own experimentation or revelation.[^6]

**Steelman of the conventional reading:** The standard "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" — with *Elohim* as a grammatically singular-construed plural **subject** — is the reading of the Septuagint, Vulgate, and most Masoretic-based translations, and is defended by major Hebraists. The Targumic "with wisdom" and the Bahir/Zohar object-readings are *minority* and largely *mystical/esoteric* traditions, not the plain consensus of the Masoretic vocalization; that ancient readers **could** parse *Elohim* as object shows the consonantal text is underdetermined, not that the object-reading is correct. The Wisdom/feminine inference from the *-īt* ending is a further interpretive layer the texts themselves do not make explicit.

## Best Quotes

> "He says get rid of the *bet* and the translation is the head god… *reshit bara Elohim* — the head God created gods, meaning the other gods, and the heaven and the earth." [^5]

> "One of the Targums… instead of 'in the beginning,' the first word is *bahukama* — with wisdom God created the heavens and the earth… the Targumist looked at *bereshit* and said 'with the great feminine one,' and said, 'who is that?' That's wisdom." [^2]

> "The *Sefer Bahir* says the subject of the verb *bara* is not *Elohim*; *Elohim* is not the subject of the sentence. The subject is implied in the verb… in the beginning **He created** gods, the heaven, and the earth." [^3]

## LDS Connection

This supplies an *ancient-Jewish* pedigree for the King Follett reading that a "head of the gods" brought forth the gods — independent of, and reinforcing, the [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We|divine-council reading of Genesis 1:26]] and the broader claim that [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible|a divine family was native to the Hebrew Bible]] before the Deuteronomistic and Hellenistic collapse. It runs parallel to, but is distinct from, the grammatical [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading|construct-form]] and lexical [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention|bara-as-organize]] arguments against *ex nihilo*: those concern *how* creation happened (organizing pre-existent matter); this concerns *what was produced* (gods) and *by whom* (a head God, possibly with Wisdom/the Mother as agent).
