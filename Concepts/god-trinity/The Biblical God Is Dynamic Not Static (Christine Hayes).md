# The Biblical God Is Dynamic Not Static (Christine Hayes)

## The Claim
The narrator enlists Yale professor of religious studies [[Christine Hayes]] to show that the "immutable, impassible, omniscient" God students *expect* is a Greco-Roman philosophical inheritance, not the God of the Hebrew Bible. Students are "shocked or scandalized" that the biblical God investigates, changes course, and negotiates — but Hayes argues that *dynamism*, not Greek *stasis*, is the genuinely biblical and Jewish picture of the divine. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "static" God is a Greco-Roman import.** Hayes: the image of God as "omniscient... omnipotent... [who] never changes his mind" is "really an outcome of a certain western philosophical tradition that's grounded very much in Greco-Roman notions of the divine as that which is static, immutable, unchanging." "God is the unmoved mover... perfection is understood to be stasis." *Rating: strong; a credentialed academic stating the mainstream history-of-ideas point.* [^1]
2. **Scripture shows a God who changes course.** Students protest that [[Exodus 32.14|Exodus 32:14]] "say[s] that God changes his mind," that God "go[es] down to investigate complaints" (Genesis 18), and asks why he is "thwarted." Hayes: "that is not the God that we meet in biblical and Jewish tradition. The God that we meet in biblical tradition is a dynamic God... intimately involved in creation... learning... changing course in response to their actions." *Rating: strong; an accurate reading of the Hebrew narrative.* [^2]
3. **The lens, not the text, produces the scandal.** "We sometimes wear a set of lenses that have been handed to us through the western philosophical tradition... and we sometimes then read biblical stories or midrashim and are sort of shocked or scandalized by what we see there." *Rating: strong; reframes the "anthropomorphism problem" as a problem of imposed Greek expectations.* [^3]
4. **What is divine is the dynamism itself.** Hayes: "for the Bible and for the rabbis, what's divine about God is precisely not that he's static — that which is static is dead — but precisely that he's dynamic and alive and interacting with humans." *Rating: strong; positively states the alternative.* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A classical theist replies that the dynamic, "mind-changing" language is **anthropomorphic accommodation**: Scripture speaks of God *as if* he repents (Exodus 32) so finite readers can grasp his unchanging purposes, while texts like Malachi 3:6 ("I the LORD do not change") and Numbers 23:19 ("God is not a man, that he should... repent") teach genuine immutability. Divine impassibility, properly understood, denies only *passions that control* God, not love or care — a point even many Latter-day Saints share (see [[Without Body Parts or Passions Is a 1530 Protestant Creed]]).

### Counter-objection
Hayes (and the narrator) would respond that the accommodation move *begs the question*: it assumes the Greek static God as the literal truth and then demotes the abundant dynamic texts to figure — exactly reversing the priority. If one lets the Hebrew narrative set the baseline, the "anthropomorphisms" are the revelation and the static abstraction is the imported gloss. This is the same logic as [[The Creedal God Is the Platonic One Not the God of the Bible]].
