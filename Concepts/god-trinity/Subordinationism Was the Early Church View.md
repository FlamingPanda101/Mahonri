# Subordinationism Was the Early Church View

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that **subordinationism** — the view that the Son is genuinely subordinate to the Father (lesser in rank/authority, derivative in being) — was "a common understanding of the relationship between the Father and the Son in the early church," and that it "got put away" at the [[Council of Nicaea|Council of Nicaea]] (325 CE) when the consubstantial Trinity was adopted.[^1] He grounds it in Jesus' own words: the Father is "the only true God," the Father is "greater than I," Jesus does not know what the Father knows, and Jesus submits a *different will* in Gethsemane ("not my will but yours").[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The scriptural data is real — rate: strong
The "subordination texts" Carroll cites are genuine and have long troubled Trinitarian exegesis: [[John 14.9|John 17:3]] (Father = "only true God"), John 14:28 ("the Father is greater than I"), Mark 13:32 (the Son does not know the day/hour), [[Matthew 22.30|Mark 14:36]] / Gethsemane (distinct will), [[Luke 18.19|Luke 18:19]] ("none is good but God"). These are standard NT christological tension points. See existing [[John 14.9]], [[Isaiah 43-10 - No God Formed Before Me Rebutted]].

### 2. Early subordinationism is historically defensible — rate: strong, with nuance
Mainstream patristics scholarship agrees that *ante-Nicene* theology was frequently **subordinationist** in a broad sense: the Son was widely regarded as derived from and ranked beneath the Father (e.g., Justin Martyr's "second God"/Logos, Origen's eternal-generation-but-subordinate Son, [[Arius|Arius']] more radical creaturely Son). The Nicene *homoousios* ("same substance") was a development that suppressed the harder forms of subordination. **Steelman of the Trinitarian:** orthodoxy distinguishes *ontological* subordination (heresy — the Son lesser in being, condemned at Nicaea) from *functional/economic* subordination (orthodox — the Son obeys the Father in the work of salvation while equal in essence). On this view, "the Father is greater" refers to the incarnate Son's *role*, not his nature, and Gethsemane shows two wills united in the hypostatic union — not two unequal beings. Carroll collapses these, treating all subordination data as anti-Trinitarian; a Trinitarian would say he is attacking a strawman of "ontological" subordination that orthodoxy *also* rejects.

### 3. The Nicaea narrative is broadly accurate but compressed — rate: moderate
Carroll's account — Constantine convenes the council because bishops "can't agree on anything," no central authority exists, they "debate and vote," and the Nicene Trinity "wins" — is a reasonable popular summary, though it flattens the theological substance into pure politics.[^3] See [[Council of Nicaea]], [[Trinity - Historical Development]]. **Caution:** "the modern Trinity was not found in large part before Nicaea" is defensible for the *formal Nicene definition* but overstates if taken to mean no proto-Trinitarian devotion existed earlier (binitarian/triadic worship patterns are attested well before 325). The vault's existing notes ([[Divine Mediation Predates the New Testament]], [[Trinity - Historical Development]]) give the fuller picture.
