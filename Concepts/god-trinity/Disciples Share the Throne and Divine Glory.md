# Disciples Share the Throne and Divine Glory

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that the New Testament promises *the disciples* the very same throne, glory, and divine attributes given to Christ — the heart of the LDS "Mormon God" Shamoun demanded from the Bible. [[Revelation 3.21|Revelation 3:21]]: the one who overcomes sits *with Christ on his throne* as Christ sat with the Father. [[1 John 3.1-3|1 John 3:1-3]]: "we shall be like him." [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] and [[John 1.12|John 1:12]]: the glory the Father gave the Son is shared with believers, who become children of God. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **Revelation 3:21 puts believers on the divine throne.** "I will give the right to sit with me on my throne, just as I... sat down with my Father on his throne." Ostler reads one shared throne: "we're all sitting on the same throne together... equal in power and authority." *Rating: strong* — to be given a throne was to be given "all the attributes of the king, in this case God." [^3][^4]

2. **1 John 3:2 — "we shall be like him."** The Greek (*homoiōma*) is a *likeness*; "we're going to be what he is in the sense of sharing in this glory." Ostler is careful: it is *like* (homoiōma), deliberately *not* *homoousios*. *Rating: precise and honest* about the Greek; see [[1 John 3.1-3|1 John 3:1-3]]. [^5][^6]

3. **Two senses of "children of God."** [[John 1.12|John 1:12]] / 1 John 3 distinguish generic children (same *kind*) from *adopted heirs* of the household who inherit "all that the Father has" via covenant with Christ. *Rating: a coherent LDS framing* of theosis-by-adoption (cf. [[Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis]]). [^7]

4. **Steelman of the classical reading.** Sharing Christ's throne and glory is *grace and adoption*, not *deification into the divine nature*: believers reign *with* Christ as glorified creatures, "like him" morally and in resurrection-glory, while the Creator/creature line (creatio ex nihilo) holds. *homoiōma* itself signals analogy, not identity of essence — the very word choice the classical reader leans on. Patristic theosis ("partakers of the divine nature," 2 Peter 1:4) was always *by participation/grace*, not by becoming a god of the same kind. *Rating: a strong, mainstream rebuttal* — the texts establish exalted participation, which Christianity affirms; whether that crosses into same-kind godhood depends on the prior ontology, not these verses. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "I will give the right to sit with me, with Christ, on my throne, just as I was victorious and sat down with my Father on his throne." [^1]

> "We're all sitting on the same throne together. We're equal in power and authority." [^4]

> "When it says we will be like him, the Greek is actually saying in a literal sense we're going to be what he is in the sense of sharing in this glory." [^5]

> "To be given a throne was to be given all the attributes of the king, in this case God, who is fully divine." [^9]
