# Deification Diminishes God Not at All but Glorifies Him

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Evangelical critics caricature LDS exaltation as humans **dethroning God** — kicking him off his pedestal to take his place. [[Jonah Barnes]], [[Luke Hansen]], and [[Cardon Ellis]] answer that *neither* the apocrypha, *nor* Satan, *nor* the church fathers ever imagined dethroning the Father; deification is a son rising to the glory of the Father, which **glorifies** God rather than diminishing him — "having made thee God unto his glory."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. No source teaches dethroning (rated: strong as stated)
"Nowhere does it say, nowhere does it even imply that we're going to dethrone God ... not even Satan, not even Lucifer was that stupid, that brazen, to make a claim like that." Across the Old Testament apocrypha, lowercase gods are everywhere, but the move is always "you can rise up and ascend up to be with God," never displace him.[^1] The "I want my own planet" parody is "an unnatural misrepresentation — who says that about their own father?"[^1]

### 2. The patristic principle (rated: moderate, primary source)
[[Hippolytus of Rome]]: "for the deity, by condescension, does not diminish ... the dignity of his divine perfection, having made thee God unto his glory." Just as Christ's condescension didn't diminish his divinity, God raising humans to godhood doesn't diminish God — "it actually is unto his glory."[^1]

### 3. The Pauline anchor (rated: moderate)
[[Philippians 2.6-7|Philippians 2:6]] says Christ "thought it not robbery to be equal with God." Barnes argues Paul says this precisely to *rebut* people calling deification robbery: "the whole point of Paul saying this is to rebut the very same argument evangelicals use against us today — that it's not robbery to believe that we can rise up to become the sons of God."[^1] [[Romans 8.14-17|Romans 8:16-17]] then makes believers "joint-heirs with Christ."

## Counter-arguments / related
A critic will say the rebuttal disposes of a *strawman* (no serious evangelical claims Mormons literally plan a coup against God) while leaving the real objection untouched: that exalted humans becoming Gods who organize their own worlds still posits an *expanding plurality of deities*, which classical monotheism rejects on ontological, not pecking-order, grounds. See [[Gods of Their Own Planets Is a Caricature of Exaltationist Theosis]] for the LDS framing of that worry, and [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]] for the genuine point of difference.
