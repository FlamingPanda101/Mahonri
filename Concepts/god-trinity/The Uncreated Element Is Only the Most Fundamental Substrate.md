# The Uncreated Element Is Only the Most Fundamental Substrate

#type/claim

## The Claim

When [[Joseph Smith]] taught that "the elements are uncreated," he was not claiming that *every* observable thing is eternal. On the reading [[Kaden Alvie]] advances (work he says he is helping a BYU professor develop), "element" — contemporary to Joseph — means *whatever is physically most fundamental*: not this table, not its atoms or molecules, but the deepest, most-basic physical stratum that cannot be further broken down. On this view there is "an open view to Latter-day Saints where most everything we see could be considered created, but whatever is the most fundamental thing was not created."[^1] This dovetails with the in-principle scientific possibility — argued by [[J.P. Moreland]] — of "some kind of quantum foam," a pre-Big-Bang "quantum soup" that is the most fundamental physical thing.[^2]

The move reconciles two LDS commitments: (1) that God *organized* a pre-existing cosmos rather than creating from nothing (see [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]]), and (2) the strong intuition, shared with Catholics, that "as far as what we can observe, everything's created" — because the uncreated substrate is, by hypothesis, below the threshold of observation.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It is a narrowing, not an expansion, of the LDS claim (moderate).** Instead of asserting the eternity of ordinary objects, it isolates eternity to the lowest physical substrate, leaving the entire observable order open to being "created." This blunts the caricature that Mormons think God merely stuck existing planets together — "No, no, no, that's not what God did"; he took "the unformed chaos, the waters of chaos, and made it formed."[^4] It pairs naturally with [[D&C 93]]'s "the elements are eternal."
2. **It claims mainstream cosmological cover (moderate, contested).** Moreland — co-editor with [[William Lane Craig]] of *The Blackwell Companion to Natural Theology* and author of a book on scientism — grants it is "theoretically possible for there to be something that happened before the Big Bang, some kind of quantum foam."[^2] The hosts read this as a respected Christian philosopher conceding a most-fundamental pre-cosmic substrate. (Caveat: Moreland is defending the *bare possibility* against scientism's overreach, not endorsing eternal matter or LDS cosmology; and the note's speaker openly says "I still need to do a lot of work to prove this view.")
3. **It harmonizes with the Hebrew-Bible reading (supporting).** If Genesis describes God ordering pre-existent "waters" (see [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]) and *bara* means "organize" (see [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]]), then "uncreated element" naturally refers to that unformed substrate, not to formed creatures.

### Steelman of the Classical-Theism Reply

The classical theist answers that this does not rescue the LDS God's ultimacy but merely relocates the problem. *Whatever* is most fundamental and uncreated — quantum foam, primordial matter, "intelligence" — is then a brute, eternal, independent given that God did not bring into being. That is precisely what *creatio ex nihilo* denies in order to secure divine **aseity**: God as the absolute, unconditioned source of *all* being (see [[Divine Attributes Follow From Pure Actuality]]). A God who must work with an eternal substrate he did not author is, on classical premises, a metaphysically smaller God — an organizer constrained by a co-eternal "stuff," not the ground of existence itself. Moreover, identifying Joseph's 1844 "element" with 21st-century quantum-field vocabulary risks anachronism: the concept of a quantum vacuum was unavailable to Joseph, so the reading is a charitable modern reconstruction, not a recovery of his intended referent — which the speaker concedes is still a "hunch."

## Best Quotes

> "Whatever is the most fundamental physical thing is what's uncreated. So there's an open view to Latter-day Saints where most everything we see could be considered created, but whatever is the most fundamental thing was not created."[^1]

> "JP Morland… in his book on scientism argues that it's theoretically possible for there to be something that happened before the big bang, some kind of quantum foam. And so if there's like this quantum soup, that's the most fundamental thing."[^2]

> "As far as we can tell, everything's created from nothing — as far as what we can observe, what's discernible to us."[^3]
