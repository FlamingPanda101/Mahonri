# The Creation Dilemma Is an Internal Catch-22

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that "the creation dilemma" is a strict *internal* critique of creedal Christianity, not an external one. He explicitly refuses the [[Trent Horn]]-style move ("if my church is true, yours is false"); instead he *grants* the creedal worldview and shows it is self-contradictory. The dilemma: if you affirm all the classical attributes of God (all-powerful, sovereign, unbound, knows the future, creates from nothing) plus "all good," you get a God who is not actually good; but to escape, you must revise one attribute — at which point you have retreated from the creedal God. Either horn is a loss, which is why it is a *dilemma* / catch-22.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The internal-critique framing is the argument's main strength — rate: strong rhetorically.** By assuming the opponent's premises, Carroll avoids begging the question against creedalism and forces the interlocutor to do the work of identifying which premise to drop.[^2] **Steelman of the creedal theist:** the dilemma only bites if "all good" is *univocal* between God and creatures. Classical theists (Aquinas) hold that goodness is predicated of God *analogically*, not univocally; God's goodness is His own simple essence, not conformity to a creaturely moral standard. On that view "an all-good God creates the damned" is not obviously a contradiction the way "married bachelor" is — it is a claim about a being whose goodness is not measured by our intuitions. Carroll's reply (implicit in the panel) is that this just relocates the problem: a "goodness" so unlike ours that it permits gratuitous eternal torment is not recognizably goodness.
2. **It reframes existing vault arguments as a forced dilemma — rate: structurally sound.** This is the same logical core as [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]] and [[Problem of Hell and Creation]], but cast as a closed decision tree (affirm all → lose; revise any → retreat). See also [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]] for the syllogistic version and [[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]].
3. **The catch-22 depends on the modal "had to retreat" — rate: contestable.** A Molinist or open theist will say revising foreknowledge is not a "retreat" but a more accurate doctrine of God (see [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]], [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]]). Carroll concedes open theism *would* dissolve the dilemma, which shows the second horn is real, not merely rhetorical.

## Best Quotes
> "In order... you either have to accept that God is not all good, which is not an attribute of God, right?... and in order to fix the dilemma, you have to change another attribute. That's why it's a dilemma. It's a catch-22. No matter what you do, if you accept all the attributes, you lose. If you change the attributes, you lose. You retreat from the creedal God." [^1]

> "It's an internal critique. That's one of I think the biggest strengths of the book... I'm not saying because I'm right, you're wrong. I'm saying assuming your own worldview, you have a contradiction." [^2]

> "Once you start to try to solve this problem, you are now retreating from the classical view of God." [^3]
