# Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] uses an **acorn-to-oak-tree** analogy to express the LDS doctrine that humans and God are the same *kind* of being at different stages of development. "We believe that we fundamentally are of the same kind as Jesus, and we are like acorns where they are like oak trees." [^1] Every truth a person accepts is like soil, water, or light that helps the acorn grow toward its potential; asking which doctrines are "first tier" is like asking which gardening acts matter — "they all matter and some have a bigger effect on us than others." [^2][^3] Some errors, however, can "kill the acorn" or "stop its growth," analogous to severance from God. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (and steelman of the critique)

1. **The analogy encodes a same-kind ontology.** The acorn already *is* an oak in essence; it only needs to develop. This presupposes humans are ontologically continuous with God — "you are not ontologically a different kind from God." [^5] *Rating: a clear, vivid expression of LDS theosis;* it stands or falls with the underlying ontology (see [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]]).

2. **It reframes "first-rank" doctrines as growth-accelerators, not gatekeepers.** "Everything that I do to that acorn to help it progress is part of the process." [^2] *Rating: coherent within the progression model* (see [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]]) but it dissolves the very category of *necessary* belief that [[Gavin Ortlund]] is pressing.

3. **Ortlund's counter (steelmanned acceptance + redirection).** Ortlund accepts the metaphor and turns it: "there are some things that can kill the acorn... the scripture is replete with warnings that there are some things that can sever us from God." [^4] *Rating: a shrewd move* — it concedes the imagery while reintroducing a decisive threshold (death of the acorn = severance/hell). Hansen agrees an acorn's progress can stop, relocating the dispute to *what counts as fatal.* [^6]

4. **Steelman of the classical-theism objection.** On creatio ex nihilo, an acorn can never become the gardener; creature and Creator are different in kind, so no amount of growth crosses the ontological gap. The analogy therefore *begs the question* against classical theism by assuming shared substance. *Rating: a fair philosophical rejoinder* that again routes back to [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]].

## Best Quotes
> "We believe that we fundamentally are of the same kind as Jesus and we are like acorns where they are like oak trees." [^1]

> "An acorn can become an oak tree... everything that I do to that acorn to help it progress is part of the process that helps this acorn become what it has the potential to be." [^2]

> "You are not ontologically a different kind from God. I think that's massively important because it says something about who you are as a child of God." [^5]

> "There are some things that can kill the acorn. Just like in nature, something can cause the acorn to stop growing." [^4]
