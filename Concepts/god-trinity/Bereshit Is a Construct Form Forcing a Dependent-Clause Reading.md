# Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading

## The Claim

The grammar of the very first word of the Bible undercuts *creatio ex nihilo*. בְּרֵאשִׁית (*bereshit*) is not the absolute "In the beginning" but a **construct form** — literally "in (the) beginning **of**" — and, decisively, it **lacks the definite article** (it is not vocalized *ba-reshit*, "in *the* beginning"). A construct noun cannot stand alone; it must govern what follows. Consequently the verb בָּרָא (*bara*) that comes next cannot be read as a finite perfect ("[God] created") but functions within a **construct chain** — effectively an infinitive ("of God's creating"). The whole of Genesis 1:1–2 then resolves into a single long **dependent / circumstantial clause**: "When God began to create the heavens and the earth — the earth being *tohu wa-bohu*, darkness over the deep, the wind of God hovering over the waters — God said, 'Let there be light.'"[^1]

On this reading the *tohu wa-bohu*, the darkness, the *tehom*, and the primordial waters of [[Genesis 1.2|Genesis 1:2]] are the **pre-existing state of things before the first act of creation**, not products of it. The familiar independent-clause translation ("In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth") is the *later* reading: it "dates to the Hellenistic period (as in the Septuagint), when the details of classical Hebrew grammar had been forgotten," and "the idea of *creatio ex nihilo* is dependent on the later rendering."[^1] This is the Latter-day Saint claim that the Hebrew itself — not LDS dogma — yields creation from organized matter. The argument is associated above all with the medieval Jewish commentator **Rashi** (Rabbi Solomon ben Isaac, 1040–1105), whom several of the quoted academic authorities credit with first making the case.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The morphology: construct + missing article.** Marvin Sweeney's close analysis of *bereshit bara Elohim* states the words "cannot be read as 'in the beginning G-d created,' because the term *bereshit* is a construct form that lacks a definite article. The verb, *bara*, cannot be read as a perfect verb, but it must be rendered as an infinitive that forms a construct chain with the terms that precede and follow." The verse "must be read as, 'in (the) beginning of G-d's creating the heavens and the earth, the earth was formless and void.'"[^1] *(Evidence rating: strong — the absence of the article on the first word and the construct state of *reshit* are hard grammatical facts repeatedly affirmed by the cited specialists.)*

2. **A convergent witness list.** The blog stacks independent scholars making the same grammatical point: **Ronald Hendel** ("the Hebrew word for 'beginning' (*reshit*) required a dependent relation… The traditional rendering, 'In the beginning, God created,' dates to the Hellenistic period… The idea of *creatio ex nihilo* is dependent on the later rendering"); **John Levenson** (Rashi "made a case that the verse functions as a temporal clause… 'When God began to create heaven and earth'"); **Ziony Zevit** ("a circumstantial clause in a long, complicated sentence spread out over three biblical verses"); **Edwin Good** ("In beginning of," the natural continuation being "Elohîm's creating," requiring the verb be read as the infinitive *berō'*); **John Collins** (the consonantal text "can also be translated as 'In the beginning, when God created…'").[^1] *(Evidence rating: strong as convergence — these are Yale, Brill, and major study-Bible scholars, several of them Jewish or secular, not LDS apologists.)*

3. **The pattern matches ANE cosmogony.** Several note that beginning a creation account with a temporal/dependent clause is exactly how *Enuma Elish* and the second creation account at Genesis 2:4b begin — so the dependent-clause reading is not a strained import but the native ancient Near Eastern form.[^1] *(Evidence rating: corroborating — comparative-literary support, not proof of the grammar, but it removes the charge that the reading is ad hoc.)*

4. **Distinct from the bara-functional and Masoretic-vowel arguments.** This is a grammatical claim about the **construct state of the first noun**, separate from the lexical claim that [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention|*bara* denotes function rather than material origination]] and from the [[Masoretic Vowels Reversed the Active Verb to Passive|Masoretic-vocalization argument about *ra'ah*]]. The arguments reinforce but do not duplicate one another. *(Evidence rating: clarifying.)*

**Steelman of the traditional (ex-nihilo / independent-clause) reading:** The independent-clause rendering "In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth" is itself ancient and well-pedigreed — it is the reading of the Septuagint, the Vulgate, and "a tradition over two millennia old" (Levenson) — and remains grammatically defensible: a few commentators (e.g., Benno Jacob) still hold v. 1 to be a complete sentence, and Gordon Wenham judges that "the phraseology leaves the author's precise meaning uncertain."[^1] More fundamentally, even granting the dependent-clause syntax, the most one can squeeze from it is that the biblical writer **does not affirm** *creatio ex nihilo* here — not that he denies it. As Mangum, Custis, and Widder and as Gary Anderson stress, the Priestly author was simply **not asking** where matter ultimately came from; reading either ex nihilo or its refutation into the syntax over-presses a text indifferent to the question. The grammar settles a translation dispute, not the whole metaphysical one.
