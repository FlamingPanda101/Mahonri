# The Creedal God Is the Platonic One Not the God of the Bible

## The Claim
The God defined by the creeds — perfectly transcendent, simple, immaterial, timeless, spaceless, without parts or passions — is not the God revealed in scripture but **Plato's "the One"** (and Aristotle's "unmoved mover") re-read into the Bible through the Hellenization of the ancient world. The narrator argues that if you "just read the Bible," you would never conclude God is passionless, immutable, or immaterial; that picture "emerged from the creed tradition" and is "the God that many Christians read back into the Bible." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The attributes of "the One" and the creedal God are identical.** In Platonism the first principle is "perfectly transcendent... unchanging, simple, without parts and passions, immaterial, spaceless, timeless, and the source of being" — a list that matches the creedal God point for point. The narrator asks why "Platonism's the One sound[s] exactly like the God of creed Christians." *Rating: strong as a description; the parallel is widely granted even by orthodox historians (the "Hellenization" debate).* [^1]
2. **Hellenization is a documented historical phenomenon.** Greek philosophy "dominated intellectual thought from around 300 BC until the fall of the Roman Empire," and thinkers like [[Philo of Alexandria]] had already begun "attributing these same qualities to the God of Israel" before Christ. *Rating: strong; Philo's fusion of Torah with Middle Platonism is uncontested.* [^2]
3. **Jesus's bodily resurrection cut against the Greek frame.** Jesus "rose from the dead... not as an abstraction, but as a physical, glorified, and embodied person," creating the very christological problem the Fathers then tried to solve with Greek categories — "how is it possible that the risen Jesus is God if God is a timeless, spaceless, immaterial substance." *Rating: moderate-strong; frames the christological debates accurately.* [^3]
4. **Paul warned against exactly this.** [[Colossians 2.8|Colossians 2:8]] — "Beware lest any man spoil you through philosophy and vain deceit after the tradition of men... and not after Christ." *Rating: contested as proof (Paul's target is debated) but rhetorically apt.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A serious creedal defender (against [[Adolf von Harnack]]'s "Hellenization thesis") argues the Fathers *baptized* Greek vocabulary to **protect** biblical content, using philosophy as servant not master: the terms are Greek but the substance is the worship of Father, Son, and Spirit found across the NT. Second Temple Judaism was *already* Hellenized (Philo), so "Greek categories were alien to the Bible" is overstated; later precision can legitimately articulate what the texts entail. The live dispute is whether the Greek frame *preserves* or *distorts* the Hebrew data. See [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible]] for the same fault line.

### Counter-objection
The narrator's reply: this is not a quarrel over vocabulary but over **content** — the creedal God is positively *contradictory* (embodied yet immaterial; impassible yet feeling), and these contradictions "only arise due to the post-biblical mixing of philosophical and Christian principles," not from anything revealed. See [[The Hypostatic Union Is a Greek Solution to a Greek Problem]] and [[The Creeds Built a Self-Contradictory God]].

## Best Quotes
> "Why does Platonism's the One sound exactly like the God of creed Christians? If you just read the Bible, would you come to the conclusion that God had no passions? That God never changed in response to human behavior? That God was actually immaterial? No. The God that emerged from the creed tradition is not the God of the Bible. It is however the God that many Christians read back into the Bible." [^1]

> "Instead of letting Jesus and the Bible reveal the nature of God, they tried to figure out how to shove Jesus into the box definition of the Greek philosophers' abstract first principle." [^3]
