# Mormonism Rejects Classical Theism So the God Objection Bites

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that his single biggest reason for not being Mormon is that, on his analysis, **Mormonism rejects the existence of God** — not because Latter-day Saints deny that *a* god exists, but because the LDS deity is "a super being in the sky," an exalted, embodied, formerly-mortal person, rather than the God of classical theism: pure actuality, simple (not composed of parts), the necessary, uncaused cause of being itself. Horn's move is that only the classical-theist God answers the atheist's "what created God?" objection; a god who is "a being among other beings that could fail to exist" leaves that objection in force, so the atheist's comparison of such a god to the Flying Spaghetti Monster would have a point. Because he believes reason (arguments from motion, contingency, and causal finitude) demonstrates the classical God, he concludes "the gods of Mormonism do not exist," and that a changing, limited human "can never be that which is pure actuality."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, internal to classical theism)** Horn states the metaphysics crisply: "God just is being itself... the infinite undivided eternal all-powerful act of being. This means God is simple; he's not composed of parts... all of his temporal life exists in one eternal now."[^2] If one grants Thomistic classical theism, an embodied once-mortal deity does not satisfy the description.
2. **(Strong, dialectical)** The "who made God?" leverage: "If God were merely a being among other beings that could fail to exist, atheists would have a good point when they say why do we need God to explain the universe."[^3] Horn ties theism's defensibility to divine necessity/simplicity — see [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]] and [[What Caused God Is a Straw Man - The No Special Pleading Reply]].
3. **(Moderate)** Divine unity: Horn argues there can be only one God, "because if there were more than one God both beings would be limited in some way, or they would exist in a framework more fundamental than either of them, and that would be... truly God."[^1] Compare [[Divine Unity - There Can Be Only One God]] and [[Two Self-Existent Realities Cannot Coexist]].
4. **(Moderate, scriptural)** He adds the biblical monotheism texts (Isaiah 43:10; 44:8; Romans 16:27; 1 Timothy 6:16) but insists he "would be a monotheist even if I wasn't a Christian or a Jew" — the metaphysics is primary.[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Latter-day Saint reply does not concede that "God" means "being itself." On the LDS account, classical theism is a Hellenistic overlay (the unmoved-mover reshaping the biblical God), and the God of the Hebrew Bible is anthropomorphic, dynamic, and embodied — see [[The Biblical God Is Dynamic Not Static (Christine Hayes)]], [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]], and [[Perfect-Being Theology Reads the Bible Through a Greek Lens]]. Latter-day Saints answer the "what created God?" worry not with simplicity but with the **uncreated, self-existent** status of God and of intelligence/element ([[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]], [[The Uncreated Element Is Only the Most Fundamental Substrate]]): the Father is *necessarily* divine and was never created ([[LDS Father Was Always Fully Divine (No Eternal Regression)]]), so the regress Horn fears does not actually run. And the LDS "Father was once a man" teaching is, on the strongest reading, esoteric rather than load-bearing for the doctrine of God ([[God Was Once a Man Is Esoteric Not Core Doctrine]]).

## Counter-objection
Horn's argument turns entirely on a *definitional* premise — that "God" just means the simple, purely-actual being of classical theism. That premise is exactly what is in dispute, so the argument risks begging the question against a tradition that holds classical theism is itself an unbiblical philosophical construction ([[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]], [[The Creedal God Is the Platonic One Not the God of the Bible]]). Moreover, the "only one purely-actual being is possible" inference does not by itself touch a model on which the Father is the one *unoriginate* God who shares his nature with the Son and with deified humanity ([[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]]); to the Latter-day Saint, "limited" gods who depend on the Father are not rival ultimate explanations but participants in his life. The dispute is therefore at the level of *which* doctrine of God the data (biblical and rational) actually support, not whether Latter-day Saints "believe in God."

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a clean instance of the vault's central fault line: classical (Greek/creedal) theism versus the restored, embodied, social model of deity. Horn deploys the strongest Catholic version of the cosmological-argument-plus-divine-simplicity package; the LDS responses are already catalogued under [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]], [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible]], and the Godhead-vs-Trinity map ([[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]]). An effective LDS engagement concedes the *force* of "what created God?" as a constraint, then shows the Restoration meets it with divine self-existence rather than simplicity.

## LDS Relevance
For Latter-day Saints, Horn's reason is not that the evidence is thin but that the *concept* of God is wrong — making this a metaphysics dispute, not an evidential one. The rebuttals are: (1) deny that "God" must mean "being itself" ([[Perfect-Being Theology Reads the Bible Through a Greek Lens]]); (2) ground theism's answer to the regress in the **uncreated** Father rather than in simplicity ([[LDS Father Was Always Fully Divine (No Eternal Regression)]]); and (3) read Isaiah's "no God formed before me" as a courtroom polemic against manufactured idols, not a denial of the divine council ([[Isaiah 43-10 - No God Formed Before Me Rebutted]], [[Ancient Israelite Henotheism Was Not Mormon Henotheism]]).
