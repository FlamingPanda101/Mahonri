# The Brothers Slur Twists Universal Divine Sonship

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The hosts argue that the "Jesus and Lucifer are brothers" attack is a *deliberate twisting* of one of the most beautiful Latter-day Saint doctrines: that **all of humanity are literally the spirit children of God**. If God is the Father of all His children, then everyone — Mother Teresa and Hitler alike — shares that divine parentage, which "yields some awkward connections," including that Lucifer, as a premortal spirit child, is in that limited genealogical sense a "brother." The slur, they say, weaponizes universal divine sonship by stripping it of context and presenting the genealogical kinship as a begotten-equality. [[Jacob Hansen]] then exposes the deeper asymmetry: *traditional Christianity does not even hold that all people are God's children.*[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Universal divine sonship is the doctrine being twisted — rate: moderate (LDS-internal)
Cardon's framing: "if you believe God created everything and that we are all children of God, then you also believe that, unfortunately, the worst among us are also children of God" — Mother Teresa, an abusive husband, even Hitler. Put the fatherhood of God "into practice" and "one could say Jesus and Lucifer are brothers" in that limited, premortal-spirit-child sense — but that is an outgrowth of "the most fundamentally beautiful aspect of God, his love for all mankind," not a demotion of Christ.[^1] The Only Begotten remains unique (see [[Moses 1 Shows Satan Falsely Claims to Be the Only Begotten]]).

### 2. The hidden asymmetry: creedal Christianity denies universal sonship — rate: moderate-to-strong (comparative theology)
Jacob's intervention sharpens it: "you guys are making the assumption that traditional Christianity teaches that all people are children of God — that is not the position of traditional Christianity. You become an adopted son of God when you accept him. We are not all brothers and sisters; we are all *image bearers*." So the very critics who scoff at "we are all children of God" actually hold the *narrower* view (sonship by adoption upon belief; otherwise mere image-bearers).[^1] The hosts press the oddity: a parent who claimed only the "successful" children as his own and called the rest "image bearers… not in the will" would be monstrous — yet that is the adoption-only model.

### 3. The trope's pedigree confirms it is a twist, not a tenet — rate: moderate (provenance)
A super-chat (relayed by the hosts) traces the "Jesus and Satan are brothers" trope to the Tanners and Ed Decker (*The God Makers*) — evangelical/ex-Mormon polemicists — i.e. it is an outside *construction*, "their weird evangelical twisting of the basic concept that all of us are children of God," not a phrase Latter-day Saints teach as doctrine.[^2] See [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]] and [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The creedal Christian has principled replies. (a) **"Image bearers, not children" is a *theological* distinction, not a slight.** Mainstream theology grounds universal human dignity in the *imago Dei* (Genesis 1:27) and reserves "children of God" for the redeemed (John 1:12, "to them gave he power to become the sons of God") — a reading with strong scriptural warrant; it is not obviously the "monstrous" parent the hosts caricature, since adoption is offered to *all* who will receive it. (b) **The asymmetry is the point, not a gotcha.** Precisely *because* creedal Christianity does not make everyone an ontological child of God, bracketing Christ (the unique, eternal Son) with Lucifer (a creature) in one "family of spirit children" is, for the critic, a category error that levels the Creator/creature distinction — so the "brothers" objection stands on the creedal premises even if it mishears the LDS ones. (c) **Limited-sense concessions can mislead evangelistically.** Knechtle's worry is pastoral: an audience hearing "yes, in a sense Jesus and Lucifer are brothers" will not parse the premortal-genealogy qualifier, so the slogan, even when technically defended, communicates the very thing the LDS deny. The honest reading: the hosts are right that the trope distorts a genuine and beautiful LDS doctrine (universal divine parentage) and right that creedal Christianity holds the narrower adoption-only sonship — a real and underappreciated asymmetry — but the underlying creedal objection (that even a *limited* shared family subordinates the Son and blurs Creator/creature) is coherent on its own premises and is engaged directly in [[Jesus and Satan Being Spirit Brothers Follows From Rejecting the Trinity]].
