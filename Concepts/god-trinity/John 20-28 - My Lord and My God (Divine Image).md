# John 20:28 — "My Lord and My God" (Divine Image Theology)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[John 20.28|John 20:28]] — Thomas says to Jesus, "My Lord and my God" — is the climactic confession of John's Gospel, proving Jesus IS God.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. This is an adaptation of the Shema — but through divine image theology
Thomas's confession echoes the Shema ("the Lord our God, the Lord is one") and Paul's expansion ("one Lord... one God"). But [[Dan McClellan]] argues this reflects **divine image theology**, not Trinitarian ontology.[^1]

### 2. Divine images could be addressed AS the deity
In the ancient Near East, a divine image (cult statue, standing stone) properly consecrated with the **divine name** could be **addressed as the god it represented**, because it manifested that god's presence. If the divine image was sentient, it could even speak as the god. McClellan cites [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]] as a biblical parallel: "the angel of the Lord" speaks as God — "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, the God of Jacob."[^2]

### 3. Jesus as the authorized bearer of the divine name
Thomas is recognizing Jesus as the one who bears the divine name and manifests God's presence — "functioning like a divine image." Jesus "could even go so far as to identify as God," but this is an identification through **agency and presence**, not through ontological identity.[^3]

### 4. There is still distance from Trinitarian confession
McClellan notes three layers of distance from a Trinitarian reading:[^4]
- It is **Thomas** (a follower) speaking, not the narrator and not Jesus himself
- Jesus nowhere says "I am God" in John's Gospel
- The philosophical framework needed to express co-equality/co-eternity ([[Consubstantiality|homoousios]]) did not exist when John was written

### 5. John gets "right up next to" Trinitarianism without arriving
"The Trinitarian concept of God is a very different concept from what we have in the Gospel of John — even though it gets right up next to it, it does not have the philosophical frameworks available to it yet to assert Jesus is God the way the Trinitarians would later determine."[^4]

## Best Quote
> "Thomas saying 'my Lord and my God' is appealing to this idea that Jesus is functioning like a divine image. Jesus as the authorized bearer of the divine name is bearing the presence of God, is manifesting the presence of God, and could even go so far as to identify as God — but again, there's still some squishiness here because Jesus is not saying 'I am God.' Jesus is allowing a follower to say that." [^3]
