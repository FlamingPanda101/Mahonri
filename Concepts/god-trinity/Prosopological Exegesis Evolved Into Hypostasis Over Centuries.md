# Prosopological Exegesis Evolved Into Hypostasis Over Centuries

#type/claim

## The Claim
The Trinity could not be expressed quickly because the *philosophical vocabulary* it requires did not exist and had to be invented incrementally. McClellan traces a specific multi-step lexical and conceptual evolution: early Christians began with **prosopological exegesis** (treating Father, Son, and Spirit as different *faces / personae*), passed through a stage where *ousia* (being) and *hypostasis* (substance) were used **interchangeably**, then **deliberately disentangled** the two terms under middle-Platonic influence, and only at the very end — after overcoming the subordinationism still held by Origen — arrived at the horizontal, co-equal, consubstantial Trinity of Nicaea. The point against [[Wes Huff]]: this is not a doctrine that "was known all along" and merely awaited articulation; the *concept itself* was being constructed step by step over centuries, so the NT authors could not have held it.

## Supporting Evidence
- **It is counter-intuitive, so it was not "on the menu."** "It is not intuitive to think of one human being as three simultaneous persons who are distinct but also entirely unified. That wasn't on the menu yet."[^1] You had to *start* somewhere more primitive.
- **Step 1 — prosopological exegesis (faces / personae).** "*Prosopon* is a Greek word for face, and it can refer to a role that you play or a persona." Father, Son, and Spirit are approached "as different faces or persona," "united not by the same essence but by the same mind or agency or will."[^2]
- **[[Justin Martyr]]'s formulation.** Justin presents the three as "distinct in number… different beings, but… united by the same will or mind or agency."[^3] (This is the same Justin who, in the sibling cluster, calls Jesus "another God" — explicitly *not* co-equal.)
- **Step 2 — the apologists drop *prosopon*.** The 2nd-century apologists "move away from the use of *prosopon* because it seems to reflect kind of an external role or persona rather than the actual substance of the person," and move toward appropriating *hypostasis*.[^4]
- **Step 3 — *ousia* and *hypostasis* were interchangeable.** In "the first and early 2nd centuries CE," *hypostasis* "is really used interchangeably with the concept of *ousia*." Only gradually do thinkers "begin to disentangle these."[^5]
- **Step 4 — the middle-Platonic gradient is *vertical*, not horizontal.** They "adopt a middle Platonic notion of multiple hypostases within a single *ousia*. But within middle Platonism that is a *vertical gradient* — that is levels of being. It's not a horizontal three hypostases that are equal and consubstantial. That concept still didn't exist."[^6] This is the decisive point: even once the words existed, they encoded *subordination*, not equality.
- **Step 5 — Origen completes the disentangling but stays subordinationist.** "By the time of Origen you really get *hypostasis* as now entirely distinct from the notion of *ousia*. So now you can work towards what we get articulated at Nicaea. But Origen and those around him were still subordinationists. The son was still subordinate to the father."[^7] See [[Origen]].
- **The endpoint only appears in the 4th century.** Full co-equality and consubstantiality "really isn't fully developed until the early 4th century CE… a framework that takes centuries to develop."[^8] There is "absolutely no case to make that the New Testament authors already had this concept in the bag, but just couldn't figure out how to express it."[^9]
- **Why it matters for [[Matthew 28.19|Matthew 28:19]].** Because this apparatus did not exist, the "one name… of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit" cannot be read as Trinitarian; it reflects the older notion that "the son bears the name of the father" — image christology, not consubstantiality.[^10] (Complements, but is distinct from, [[Trinity - Historical Development]], which stresses canonization and imperial enforcement; this note isolates the internal *philosophical-vocabulary* mechanism.)

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint Godhead — three distinct persons/beings united in will and purpose rather than in one numerical substance — corresponds closely to the **early, pre-Nicene** stage McClellan describes (Justin's "distinct in number, united by the same will"; the *vertical* gradient that preserves the Son's subordination). On this telling, the LDS view is not a deviation *from* original Christianity but a preservation of a stage that institutional pressure later overwrote. The consubstantial, horizontal Trinity is the *innovation*; "united by the same will or mind or agency" is the older datum.
