# Special Pleading and God Logic

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"We can't be multiple persons, but God can" — humans are one being / one person, yet God is one being / three persons. (The Trinitarian asserts the rule holds for creatures but a unique exception holds for God.)

## The Rebuttal

### 1. It is textbook special pleading
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s prepared question: "Can you explain to the audience what special pleading is?" Special pleading is invoking an **arbitrary, unjustified exception** to a rule for one's own case. "We can't be multiple persons, but God can" is dogmatic assertion of exactly such an exception, with no independent justification offered. *(Strong as a naming of the fallacy, *if* no principled reason is given.)* [^1]

### 2. "It's a mystery / God's ways are higher" is not a justification
The exception is usually defended by "it's a mystery," which Hansen parodies: "In my case, 2 + 2 can equal 37 because he's God." Appealing to divine incomprehensibility to license a *logical* contradiction is the special-pleading move dressed as piety. See [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]. *(Moderate-strong; sharp, though it discounts the serious theological category of mystery.)* [^2]

### 3. The brand-name irony
The panel needles that "God Logic" relies on suspending logic — "maybe that's God logic, but actual logic, it doesn't work." The substantive point under the joke: a doctrine that requires arbitrary exceptions to the laws of identity and non-contradiction has abandoned argument for assertion. *(Opinion-weighted; rhetorical, D-leaning.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The Trinitarian denies it is *arbitrary*: the exception is grounded in God's being *sui generis* — the uncreated, infinite ground of being is not a member of any genus, so creaturely intuitions about how "person" and "being" must relate need not transfer to him. That is not special pleading (an *unprincipled* exception) but a *principled* one: a justified reason (God's transcendence and uniqueness) is offered for why the creaturely rule doesn't bind. They would add that "mystery" is not a license for contradiction but an admission that an infinite reality may exceed finite comprehension without being incoherent — and that the LDS view itself relies on its own non-negotiables (eternal intelligences, embodied deity) that resist further explanation. Whether transcendence *justifies* the exception or merely *relabels* it is the crux (cf. [[Trinity Is Fundamentally Contradictory]]).
