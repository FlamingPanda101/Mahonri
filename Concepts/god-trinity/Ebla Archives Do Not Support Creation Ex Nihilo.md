# Ebla Archives Do Not Support Creation Ex Nihilo

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Being Rebutted)

Wes Huff asserted on X/Twitter that "one of the oldest extrabiblical recorded statements on creation, the 4,000-year-old Ela archives explicitly affirms creation from nothing," quoting what he designated as "EA 250": *"Lord of heaven and earth, the earth was not, you created it. The light of day was not, you created it. The morning light you had not yet made exist."*[^1]

## The Rebuttal

The Ebla archives do NOT support creation ex nihilo. Huff's claim rests on multiple layers of error: (1) he misidentified the tablet designation (EA250 is an Amarna letter, not an Ebla text); (2) he relied on a widely-discredited apologetic translation by Giovanni Pettinato from 1981; (3) contemporary epigraphers find the text largely incomprehensible; and (4) the scholarly consensus has thoroughly rejected Pettinato's apologetic readings of the Ebla material.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Correct Tablet Identification: TM.75.G1682, Not "EA 250"

Dan McClellan flags that "EA" is the prefix designation for the Amarna tablets — diplomatic correspondence from 14th-century BCE Egypt — and has nothing whatsoever to do with the Ebla archives.[^2] The correct tablet designation is TM.75.G1682, a Sumerian composition from the Ebla archives spanning four columns on a larger tablet (with a smaller parallel, TM.75.G2500, covering column 1 with many variants).[^3]

> "EA250 has absolutely nothing whatsoever to do with creation."[^2]

The misidentification alone signals unfamiliarity with standard ancient Near Eastern epigraphic conventions.

### 2. Pettinato's Translation Is Discredited Apologetics

The translation Huff used comes from Giovanni Pettinato's 1981 English work *The Archives of Ebla: An Empire Inscribed in Clay*.[^4] McClellan notes:

> "This book is notorious for trying to argue apologetically that the Ebla archives are basically a precursor to the Old Testament."[^5]

Pettinato made several claims that scholars immediately rejected:
- That Sodom and Gomorrah are mentioned in the Ebla archives (they are not)[^5]
- That Eblaite kingship parallels the Hebrew Bible (it does not)[^5]
- That the Ebla texts support biblical monotheism (they do not)[^5]

### 3. Alfonso Archi's Epigraphic Refutation

Alfonso Archi, the second epigrapher to work on the Ebla tablets, published a detailed refutation in *The Epigraphic Evidence from Ebla and the Old Testament*. His analysis of TM.75.G1682:

> "Now this is a Sumerian composition very difficult to understand. TM.75.G1682 runs in four columns... After the first three words, which mean 'king of heaven and earth,' a well-known Sumerian epithet, the text is quite incomprehensible."[^3]

Archi tentatively proposed an alternative reading of column 3:

> "Lordship was not in existence and night abundance was not in existence. City was not in existence."[^6]

This is a cosmogonic tradition enumerating what did not yet exist — a literary pattern found across ancient Near Eastern creation texts — NOT an affirmation of creation from nothing.

Archi also noted Pettinato's philological overreach, attempting to derive a Sumerian word from Hebrew *shakar* / Arabic *sahar* (dawn) — "an explanation not requiring comment" (i.e., without argumentative basis).[^7]

### 4. Matthews & Benjamin's Different Translation

Just a few years after Pettinato, Victor Matthews and Don Benjamin offered a more cautious translation in *Old Testament Parallels: Laws and Stories from the Ancient Near East*:

> "You are the creator of the heavens and the earth. There was no earth until you created it. There was no light until you created it."[^8]

McClellan points out this translation "moves away from Pettinato's more explicit attempt to try to read creation ex nihilo into the text" — but notes even this translation is "highly problematic" because it still slants the text toward a Genesis-parallel reading.[^9]

### 5. Scholarly Consensus: Ebla Doesn't Defend the Bible

Mark Chavalas, in *Mesopotamia and the Bible: Comparative Explorations* (2003/2004), summarized the field's mature assessment:

> "The second epigrapher, Archi, spent some effort in refuting many of the premises of Dahoud and Pettinato, ignoring most of the sensationalism... He argued that the supposed evidence for Adonai at Ebla was questionable and ambiguous, and that the kings of Ebla were not anointed like the kings of Israel... There is no Genesis creation story... Ebla place names do not easily correspond to the Bible names or the cities of the plain."[^10]

> "The excitement concerning the Ebla material has somewhat died down... In the past 15 years, only a minuscule amount of effort has been put to comparisons of Ebla and the Bible compared to the large amount of work on the civilization of Ebla itself. This is preferable."[^11]

### 6. Pattern: The "Not-Yet" Creation Motif Is Common ANE, Not Ex Nihilo

The pattern of listing things that "were not" before creation is a well-known ancient Near Eastern literary trope, appearing in the *Enuma Elish* (Babylonian), hymns to Ptah and the Aten (Egypt), and Genesis 1. Matthews and Benjamin explicitly note the parallel pattern, not a unique theological claim.[^8] "Not yet existing" in these cosmogonies refers to a state of formlessness or absence of function — not philosophical non-being from which matter is generated.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

The strongest version of Huff's argument would be: the Ebla text uses the language of things "not existing" before God created them, which at minimum gestures toward creation-without-pre-existing-materials and contradicts the claim that creation ex nihilo was a late invention with no ancient Near Eastern precursors. The phrase "the earth was not, you created it" could be read as God bringing the earth into being where there was previously nothing — not merely ordering pre-existing matter.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **The text is too fragmentary and obscure** for any confident theological reading. Archi, the foremost epigrapher of Ebla, says it's "quite incomprehensible" beyond the first three words.[^3]
2. **"Not existing" =/= "nothing"** in ancient Near Eastern thought. The standard cosmogonic pattern describes a state of non-order (formlessness, darkness, absence of named entities), not philosophical non-being. Genesis 1:2 itself has *tohu wa-bohu* — formless and void — with waters already present.
3. **The translation Huff used is a known apologetic distortion.** Pettinato's entire project of reading the Bible back into Ebla has been rejected by the field for decades.[^10]
4. **Pattern parallels abound.** The "things that were not" formula appears in multiple ANE texts that clearly do NOT teach creation ex nihilo (e.g., Enuma Elish, where Marduk creates from Tiamat's corpse).
