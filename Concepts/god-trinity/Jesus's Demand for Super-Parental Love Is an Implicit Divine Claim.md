# Jesus's Demand for Super-Parental Love Is an Implicit Divine Claim

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brant Pitre]] argues that one of the most overlooked but forceful divine self-claims is Jesus's demand that disciples love him **more than father and mother** ([[Matthew 10.34-36|Matthew 10:37]]: "anyone who loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me"). In a first-century Jewish frame — where honoring parents (the Decalogue's *kabod*) was second *only* to honoring God — demanding super-parental love is an implicit claim to the place that belongs to God alone.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Parents rank second only to God (strong)
Pitre cites Philo and Josephus on the fourth/fifth commandment ("honor your father and mother," Hebrew *kabod* = "glorify"). Philo: parents stand "on the borderline between humanity and divinity." Josephus: "the law ranks parents as second only to God in honor." Honoring parents tops the "love your neighbor" half of the Decalogue precisely because they are co-creators of life.[^1]

### 2. A contemporary rabbi concedes the implication (strong)
Jacob Neusner's *A Rabbi Talks with Jesus*, reaching this saying, concludes: "only God can ask of me what Jesus is asking here." A practicing rabbi reads the demand as one only God is entitled to make — independent confirmation that the claim's *implication*, in its Jewish context, is divine.[^2]

### 3. The early church *loved* Jesus, not just obeyed (medium)
Pitre notes that Ephesians and 1 Peter speak of *loving* Jesus — language he finds paralleled in the *Shema*'s command to love God ([[Deuteronomy 5.7|Deut 6]]; [[Mark 12.30-31|Mark 12:30]]) but *not* in Greco-Roman piety toward the gods (which speaks of honoring, serving, sacrificing, but not loving). The demand for, and practice of, love toward Jesus echoes the love commanded toward the God of the *Shema*.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
O'Connor's reply (offered tentatively): Koine Greek distinguishes loves — *agape*, *eros*, *storge* (familial), *philia* (friendship). Matthew 10 uses the *phileo* root. Perhaps Jesus demands a *different kind* of love than the familial *storge* owed to parents — you still love parents most *familially*, but love Jesus more as a confided-in teacher/friend (*philia*). On that parse the saying is an extraordinary discipleship demand, not necessarily a demand for the love owed to God.

## Counter-Objection
Pitre answers that the *agape/philia* boundary is far softer than C.S. Lewis's *The Four Loves* suggests: John 20 uses *phileo* and *agapao* almost as synonyms, and they function interchangeably in the Gospels. So the lexical distinction cannot carry the deflationary reading. What is load-bearing is not the *word* but the *comparison*: Jesus places himself *above father and mother*, which — per Philo, Josephus, and Neusner — is a place reserved for God. The same pattern recurs in "let the dead bury their dead; follow me," another extraordinary demand that overrides the Decalogue's filial obligation.

## Apologetic Engagement
This argument is congenial to the Restoration because it grounds Jesus's divinity in **relational, covenantal** categories (love commanded by the *Shema*) rather than in metaphysical simplicity. A Latter-day Saint can affirm wholeheartedly that Jesus rightly demands the love owed to God — because the divine Son shares the Father's name, authority, and glory and is "one" with him in will and purpose ([[John 17.20-23|John 17]]). The claim does not require, and is not best explained by, numerical identity of Father and Son; it is explained by a unified Godhead in which the Son legitimately stands in the Father's place for the disciple's devotion.

## LDS Relevance
Pairs with [[Jesus Claimed Divinity in a Jewish Way Through Riddle and Allusion]] and the *Shema*/great-commandment cluster ([[Mark 12.30-31|Mark 12:30]]). It strengthens the case that Jesus is worshipped and loved *as God* in the New Testament — but as the divine Son of a [[The Father and Son Distinct - Even Trinitarians Deny Jesus Is the Father|distinct Father]], which is the Restoration's reading.
