## The Point
As early Christians gradually separated from the sacred spaces of first-century Judaism, New Testament writers and their successors drew on social metaphors to forge identity and cohesion. One central metaphor cast the movement as a new "family" or "household of God" to which the faithful belonged as daughters, sons, sisters, and brothers — regardless of natural familial connections. This metaphor was not merely rhetorical: it was embodied in the domestic settings of house-church worship, which produced a tangible social equalization among members.

## Evidence
1. Paul tells the Ephesians they are "no longer strangers and foreigners, but fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God" ([[Ephesians 2.19-22|Ephesians 2:19–22]]).
2. New religious movements often pair the creation of new spaces with a new sense of belonging; the "household of faith" metaphor served exactly this community-formation function.
3. The domestic setting and relationship dynamics of house-church gatherings showed "a remarkable degree of social equalization among the men, women, children, and slaves of the group" (see [[Galatians 3.28]]).
4. This equalization gave concrete reality to the "family" and "household of faith" metaphors used to bind believers together in a sometimes hostile cultural environment (Galatians 6:10; 1 Thessalonians 2:1–17).
5. A parallel metaphor cast the church as a new "temple" indwelt by God's spirit (see [[The Church as the New Temple Replacing Jerusalem]]).

## Best Quotes
> "One such metaphor described the movement as being a new 'family' or 'household of God' to which the faithful belonged as daughters, sons, sisters, and brothers (regardless of their natural familial connections)." [^1]

> "This latter feature of early Christian gatherings added a tangible reality to the 'family' and 'household of faith' metaphors being used to forge community cohesion among believers in a sometimes hostile cultural environment." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The image of belonging to God's family regardless of natural kinship resonates with Latter-day Saint covenant theology of being adopted into the family of Christ, a theme Grey ties to the broader value of appreciating early Christian community formation.
