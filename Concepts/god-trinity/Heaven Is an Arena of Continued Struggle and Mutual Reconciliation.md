# Heaven Is an Arena of Continued Struggle and Mutual Reconciliation

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] proposes that, under the "theology of ascent," the next life "is going to look a lot more like this one than maybe we've thought." Because "we're not going to become more like God by just sitting in celestial schoolrooms and learning more theology," and because progress requires "opposition in all things" — "alternatives and opportunities to choose and contending imperatives" — heaven must be "a continuing arena of struggle and challenges." He pairs this with a striking moral picture: drawing on a play by LDS writer **Thomas Rogers**, he envisions an afterlife where "we find no God who's going to serve as referee" to "litigate these differences," and invokes Joseph Smith's "most enigmatic" saying — "if you have no accusers, you will enter heaven" — to conclude that exaltation waits on being "forgiven, not just by God, but by every person who has a grievance with me."[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Progress requires opposition — rate: coherent with LDS theology.** Givens grounds the claim in the principle that "there have to be alternatives and opportunities to choose and contending imperatives," so the mechanism of growth (free moral struggle) cannot simply switch off at death. Heaven is more of the same *kind* of becoming, not its cessation — the ascent/theosis logic of [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]] and [[Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis]].[^1]
2. **Not learning-by-lecture but moral labor — rate: a pointed contrast.** "We're not going to become more like God by just sitting in celestial schoolrooms and learning more theology." Christlikeness is acquired through "the same hard work of introspection and repentance and change that we're engaged in here." Givens elsewhere insists "I don't think correct belief gains us any standing anywhere" — formation, not information.[^1][^4]
3. **No God-as-referee; reconciliation is horizontal — rate: theologically distinctive.** Rogers's play depicts the dead "expecting that God is going to come to litigate these differences," but finding none. Combined with Joseph Smith's "if you have no accusers, you will enter heaven," Givens infers that admission to heaven requires being forgiven *by the people one has wronged* — "that may take a long time to work out." Heaven is where "all conflicts will have to be resolved."[^2][^3]
4. **An ancient Christian analogue — rate: supportive parallel.** The notion that the blessed do not arrive static but progress endlessly into God has a patristic pedigree in [[Gregory of Nyssa's Epektasis - Eternal Progression Toward God|Gregory of Nyssa's epektasis]] — eternal stretching-forward. Givens's "continued struggle" is a moralized, agency-heavy version of the same intuition that heaven is dynamic, not a frozen beatific stasis.[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing (classical) side:** The historic Christian hope is *rest* and the *beatific vision* — to "see God as he is" (1 John 3:2) and be perfected, not to keep wrestling temptation forever; "contending imperatives" in heaven seems to smuggle the possibility of sin into glory, undercutting assurance. Salvation is by grace through Christ's finished work, so making exaltation wait on securing forgiveness "from every person who has a grievance" risks a works-righteousness that no one could ever complete (the very burden the gospel lifts). Gregory's epektasis, defenders add, is *joyful* unending growth in love, not perpetual moral *struggle* against opposition — Givens has fused two different ideas.

**Counter-objection (Givens's / LDS rejoinder):** "Rest" need not mean inertia, and a heaven without genuine choice would be a heaven without the very agency that makes love and growth possible — a return to the determinism the Restoration rejects. The horizontal-reconciliation requirement is not works-righteousness but the *content* of being Christlike: one cannot be exalted while unreconciled to those one has harmed, because exaltation just *is* perfected charity (cf. [[The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric|the God who weeps in solidarity]]). Christ's grace enables and completes that reconciliation; it does not render it unnecessary.

## Best Quotes
> "We're not going to become more like God by just sitting in celestial schoolrooms and learning more theology. So I think we have to anticipate... a continuing arena of struggle and challenges."[^1]

> "We all look about us expecting that God is going to come to litigate these differences. And in his depiction, we find no God who's going to serve as referee."[^2]

> "If you have no accusers, you will enter heaven... then we're being told, no heaven for me until I've been forgiven — not just by God, by every person who has a grievance with me."[^3]

> "I don't think correct belief gains us any standing anywhere... theology matters because what we believe about God shapes how we act."[^4]
