# Semantic Gerrymandering Redefines God to Exclude Pre-Existing Matter

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (a rebuttal of a Catholic objection)

The Catholic objection ([[Noah Biser]]): "If matter is co-eternal with God and has always existed alongside him, then the Mormon God is not even truly a God," because being the sole eternal existence is one of the divine attributes — so admitting co-eternal matter "removes one of the divine attributes from the Godhead."[^1]

[[Baylor McKay Johnson]]'s rebuttal names the move **semantic gerrymandering**. Just as political gerrymandering redraws district lines "so you ensure a victory," semantic gerrymandering "is when you redraw the contours of your definition to exclude people… to ensure a victory."[^2] The objection works only by first loading "God" with the contested attribute (being the *only* eternal thing) and then deducing that anyone who denies that attribute "doesn't believe in God." That is question-begging: it smuggles classical theism's conclusion into the definition.

The parody that exposes it: "All right, God by definition is perfectly loving and doesn't damn people to hell for all eternity. Noah, you believe people are damned to hell for all eternity. Therefore you don't believe in God." Baylor grants this would be "dumb," and that Biser's version is more sophisticated — but the *form* is identical: win the dispute by definition rather than by argument.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The supplied definition is not neutral (strong).** Biser displayed a dictionary-style gloss — "God: the supreme or ultimate reality… supreme over everything else." The hosts note this generic definition "doesn't really get us anywhere," because it does not entail being the *sole* eternal existent; that further premise is the disputed Thomistic attribute (unmoved mover, without parts or passions), not a lexical fact about the word "God."[^4]
2. **The ordinary referent of "God" does not include the contested clause (moderate).** "If you said God to any person on the street, they're not going to think, 'Oh yeah, God, the one thing that… wasn't there with other things ever.'" And the scriptural authors "certainly wouldn't think that, because they thought waters were there" — the unformed chaos of [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]]–2.[^5] So the exclusionary definition is neither common usage nor the biblical usage.
3. **It diagnoses a recurring apologetic pattern (supporting).** The same maneuver underlies several "five-attribute" deductions against the LDS God; naming it lets the LDS apologist reframe the debate as *which* attributes belong in the definition, rather than conceding the word outright. (See [[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]], [[Two Defining Features of Deity Eternal and Unchanging]].)

### Steelman of the Catholic View

The classical theist replies that this is not arbitrary line-drawing but a principled entailment of what "ultimate reality" *means*. If God is the supreme, ultimate ground of all being, then nothing can be ontologically independent of him — otherwise that independent thing limits him and is co-ultimate, and "ultimate" is false of God. The attribute of **aseity** (self-existence as the source of all else) is not an extra clause bolted on to win a debate; it is analytically contained in calling God *ultimate*, and it is defended on independent grounds (contingency arguments, the need for a non-derivative ground of being; see [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]] for the LDS counter). On this view the LDS deity, constrained by co-eternal matter and intelligences, is genuinely a different *kind* of being — a maximally great organizer within reality rather than the source of reality — and using the same word "God" for both is the real equivocation. The dispute is therefore substantive, not merely verbal; the charge of "gerrymandering" only lands if aseity is *not* part of the concept, which is exactly what is at issue.

## Best Quotes

> "There's a word for this. I call it semantic gerrymandering… when you redraw the contours of your definition to exclude people… to ensure a victory."[^2]

> "God by definition is perfectly loving and doesn't damn people to hell for all eternity… therefore you don't believe in God. That would be dumb, and you would rightly call me out for being dumb."[^3]

> "If you said God to the authors of scripture, they certainly wouldn't think that, because they thought waters were there — formless of chaos."[^5]
