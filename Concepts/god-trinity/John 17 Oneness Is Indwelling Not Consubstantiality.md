# John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that the unity of the Father and the Son in [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] is *indwelling/inherence* — the Father in the Son and the Son in the Father — not a unity of substance (*homoousios*/consubstantiality). The proof is that Jesus prays for his disciples to share the *same* oneness ("that they may be one, as we are one"), which would be incoherent if oneness meant numerical identity of substance. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **The unity is *defined by demonstration* in the text.** John 17 repeats the "one as we are one" formula and explains it as mutual indwelling: "just as you are in me and I am in you. May they also be in us." *Rating: strong textual case* — the passage itself supplies the definition, and *homoousios* never appears in the Bible. [^1][^3]

2. **The same oneness is extended to disciples.** "I in them and you in me so that they may be brought to complete unity." If the Father–Son oneness were consubstantiality, the disciples would be folded into the divine substance — which no Trinitarian wants to say. *Rating: a genuine pressure point* on a substance reading; it favors a relational/glory model. [^2]

3. **Glory, not substance, is the bond.** The Son had glory "before the world began" (17:5), now restored by the Father; the shared glory ("the glory you gave me") is what makes them one. *Rating: coherent within the source's pleroma framework* (see [[The Pleroma Exchange Formula]]); it dovetails with [[John 14.28|John 14:28]] and [[John 17.3|John 17:3]]. [^4]

4. **Steelman of the Trinitarian.** A classical reader can grant indwelling (*perichoresis*) yet insist it presupposes one divine essence — the persons can co-inhere *because* they share one being, and the disciples' unity is *analogical* (union by grace, not by nature), exactly the distinction 1 John 3 draws with *homoiōma* ("like him") rather than *homoousios*. *Rating: a strong reply* — John 17 underdetermines the metaphysics, so the passage does not by itself refute consubstantiality; it shows oneness is *relational at minimum*, which is all Ostler strictly needs. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The oneness that we're talking about between the father and the son is not consubstantiality. It's not homoousios... That term is never used in the Bible." [^3]

> "The father and the son are one because they dwell in each other. The picture that is being given is there's a glory, a light that interpenetrates them mutually and that is also shared with his disciples." [^6]

> "When the Bible speaks of unity, it's not speaking about substance or consubstantiality or homoousios. It is speaking of an indwelling glory that arises from loving each other." [^7]
