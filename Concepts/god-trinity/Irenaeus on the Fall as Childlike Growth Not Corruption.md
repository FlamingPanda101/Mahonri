# Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth, Not Corruption

## The Point
[[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (c. 130–202) read the Garden of Eden not as a catastrophic corruption of human nature but as a stage of *immaturity*. God created Adam and Eve with "an innocent and childlike mind" who "had need to grow." Their eating of the fruit was an act of *ignorance*, not rebellion; the real "head and fount of sin" was Satan, not Adam. Human nature is therefore seen across a *process of time* — created, growing, recovering, glorified — culminating in being "glorified" and seeing God. This developmental, theosis-shaped view has striking parallels with the Book of Mormon's doctrine of opposition and agency.

## Evidence
1. Irenaeus's five-volume *Against Heresies* (c. 185) refuted groups who taught (a) the body is evil and humans are saved only when "flesh has been stripped off," and (b) some humans were created with inherently good or bad natures, the latter beyond salvation.[^1]
2. Against the second error he insists on free will: God wants obedience "voluntarily, and not by compulsion. For there is no coercion with God." If natures were fixed good or bad, there would be neither merit nor blame.[^2]
3. God created humans with a *neutral* nature, "not as fully developed beings" — Adam and Eve were children who "had need to grow so as to come to [their] full perfection."[^3]
4. Their disobedience was thus "an act of ignorance," not rebellion; being innocent, they were "easily led astray" by Satan, who envied God's favors to humanity.[^4]
5. Since Adam *repented*, Irenaeus makes Satan, not Adam, "the head and fount of sin"; the serpent is "the author and originator of sin."[^5]
6. The serpent was even partly right that they would "be as the gods, knowing good and evil" (Genesis 3:5) — "the problem was in the timing." Drawing on Paul's milk-before-meat metaphor (1 Corinthians 3:2), they needed to grow first.[^6]
7. Consequences were real (expulsion, toil, loss of God's presence, and a spiritual loss of the "likeness"), but God cursed the earth and Satan, not Adam and Eve.[^7]
8. Recovery is twofold: humans using free will to receive God's word, *and* Christ's incarnation restoring "what we had lost in Adam … to be according to the image and likeness of God."[^8]
9. The grand sweep: man "making progress day by day, and ascending towards the perfect … created … should receive growth … strengthened … abound … recover … be glorified; and being glorified, should see his Lord."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> Adam and Eve's thoughts "were innocent and childlike" and they "had need to grow so as to come to [their] full perfection." [^3]

> man "making progress day by day, and ascending towards the perfect, that is, approximating to the uncreated One. … having recovered, should be glorified; and being glorified, should see his Lord." [^9]

## LDS Connection
The text explicitly pairs Irenaeus with Lehi: "there is an opposition in all things … righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness" (2 Nephi 2:11). Irenaeus's progressive, agency-centered, theosis-oriented anthropology — innocence in Eden, growth through opposition, glorification in seeing God — closely tracks the LDS "fortunate fall" and eternal-progression framework, and stands opposed to [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development|Augustine's inherited guilt]].
