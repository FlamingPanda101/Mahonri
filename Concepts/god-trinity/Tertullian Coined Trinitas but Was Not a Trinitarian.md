# Tertullian Coined Trinitas but Was Not a Trinitarian

## The Claim
[[Tertullian]] is often cited as proof the Trinity is early because he was the first to use the Latin word *trinitas* (late 2nd / early 3rd century). The narrator concedes the coinage but denies the inference: **coining the word is not holding the doctrine.** Tertullian's view of God is "not the same as the later Nicene Trinity," as documented by the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's dedicated treatment of him. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The word is not the doctrine.** A creedal interlocutor in the video grants "the first person to use the word *trinitas* is Tertullian," while arguing the "roots for trinitarian theology are there." The narrator accepts the *word* but presses that it took the church "a long time to figure out the most coherent way to explain that... using the philosophical tools and language that was on hand" — an admission of development, not apostolic deposit. *Rating: strong as a dialectical point.* [^1]
2. **Stanford Encyclopedia distinguishes Tertullian from Nicaea.** The narrator points to the SEP entry on Tertullian, which lays out a representation of *what Tertullian taught* against *what the creeds teach about the Trinity* — "there's quite a difference." Tertullian's theology is **subordinationist** and economic (the Son derived from and subordinate to the Father), not the co-equal, co-eternal, consubstantial tri-unity of Nicaea. *Rating: strong; reflects standard history-of-doctrine scholarship.* [^2]
3. **It fits the broader pattern.** Tertullian belongs to the pre-Nicene authors who used *trias/trinitas* for a *plurality*, not a tri-personal God — consistent with [[No Theologian in the First Three Centuries Was Trinitarian]]. *Rating: strong.* [^2]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A defender argues Tertullian already states the core grammar later canonized — *tres personae, una substantia* (three persons, one substance) — so he is a genuine proto-Trinitarian whose subordinationism is a refinement issue, not a different religion. On this reading the "roots" objection stands: the conceptual seed is unmistakably present by ~200 AD.

### Counter-objection
The narrator replies that the *substance* of Nicaea — co-equality and consubstantiality of three persons — is precisely what Tertullian lacks; reading his *una substantia* as Nicene *homoousios* is anachronistic. And the very fact that the grammar had to be "figured out" over centuries "using philosophical tools" is what Latter-day Saints deny was inspired. See [[William Lane Craig Concedes Logos Christology Is a Platonic Relic]].

## Best Quotes
> "Tertullian was not trinitarian. Yes, he was the first to use the word *trinitas*, but he wasn't a trinitarian. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy has a section specifically on Tertullian, showing how his view of God is not the same as the later Nicene Trinity." [^1]

> "It is exactly this time it took to develop the idea of the trinity and this use of philosophical tools that we Latter-day Saints question. We don't believe these developments that were made by using the philosophical tools of the day were inspired." [^2]
