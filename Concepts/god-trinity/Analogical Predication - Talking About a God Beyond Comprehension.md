# Analogical Predication - Talking About a God Beyond Comprehension

## The Point
If God is absolutely simple, how can we ascribe to Him distinct attributes like power, intellect, and goodness without making Him composite? [^1] Feser's answer, following Thomas Aquinas, is that such terms are used neither *univocally* (the same sense as in us) nor *equivocally* (a wholly different sense), but *analogically* — a middle usage where the senses are related but not identical. [^2] When we say God is powerful or good, we mean there is in God *something analogous to* what we call power or goodness in us, not the very same thing. [^3] This is to be expected, Feser argues, because in reasoning to the most fundamental level of reality we must stretch ordinary concepts to their limit. [^4]

## Evidence
- The objection: aren't omnipotence, intellect, etc. "different attributes, and thus different parts of the One?" [^1]
- First reply (Frege): different *senses* can share one *reference* — "the morning star" and "the evening star" both denote Venus; likewise the One's omnipotence and intellect refer to one simple reality. [^1]
- The three modes: *univocal* ("Rover is a dog," "Fido is a dog"), *equivocal* ("swung the bat" vs. "a bat in the attic"), and *analogical* ("the wine is good," "George is a good man") — the last being literal, not metaphorical. [^2]
- Applied to God: "there is in God something analogous to what we call power in us, something analogous to what we call intellect in us, and something analogous to what we call goodness in us" — related to ours but not identical, and in God all one thing viewed from different angles. [^3]
- The wave-particle analogy: just as physics forces us to stretch everyday concepts to describe phenomena remote from experience, reasoning to the simple first cause "even farther from experience than anything physics describes" should be expected to yield conclusions "hard to grasp." [^4]
- Hence "Reason itself thus tells us that there is a level of reality that reason can only partially comprehend." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "We should understand them instead in what Thomas Aquinas called an analogical sense." [^2]

> "Reason itself thus tells us that there is a level of reality that reason can only partially comprehend." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism, analogical predication is a sophisticated defense: it shows that affirming a transcendent, simple God need not collapse into either anthropomorphism or empty equivocation, and that some incomprehensibility at the foundation of reality is exactly what we should expect. The doctrine of analogy is, however, *driven by* divine simplicity (see [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]) — its whole purpose is to preserve God's partlessness against the "attributes are parts" objection. That is where the Restoration parts ways. Because Latter-day Saints worship an embodied Father "of body, parts, and passions" (D&C 130:22) with *really distinct* attributes (see [[God Was Once a Man]]), they need not reinterpret God's power, knowledge, and goodness as "all one and the same thing." Indeed, LDS scripture stresses that humans are made in God's literal image and that His attributes are genuinely communicable to His children — a continuity that classical analogy, premised on an utterly simple Absolute, must deny. The deeper reshaping is traced in [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]].
