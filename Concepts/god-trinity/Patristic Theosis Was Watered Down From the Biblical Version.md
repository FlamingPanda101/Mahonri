#type/claim

# Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the **deification of the 4th–5th-century fathers is a heavily qualified, diluted version** of a more radical, ontological divinization found in the New Testament (especially John) and the earliest Jewish and Christian authors. Three datable shifts caused the dilution: (1) the rise of a **philosophically strict monotheism** ("only one entity can be called God"), tied to the rising salience of the Trinity; (2) the development of **creatio ex nihilo** (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]); and (3) **Jerome's *Hebraica Veritas*** at the end of the 4th century — the turn from the Septuagint to the Hebrew, which "removed a lot of the proof texts" earlier Christians used for human–divine relationship. The upshot is a **double-edged warning that is mostly a foil to LDS apologetics**: it is a mistake to cite Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, or other patristic theosis language as a proof text for *Mormon* deification, because (a) the fathers' theosis is "passive participation in the divine qualities… [rather] than actual ontological divinization where you can speak of humans as becoming gods," and (b) it differs from *both* the biblical picture *and* the LDS picture — "three very distinct ideals." McClellan notes he made exactly this argument as a Latter-day Saint in 2007.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The fathers' theosis is qualified participation, not ontological god-making.** Of Athanasius and Cyril's "gods and sons" language, McClellan says "their idea was heavily qualified. It was more of a passive participation in the divine qualities, in divinity, than it is actual ontological divinization where you can speak of humans as becoming gods."[^1] *Rating: strong* — it concedes the *vocabulary* survives while denying the *content* is the same, which is the whole point.

2. **The vocabulary is vestigial by the 4th–5th centuries.** "Some of the language is still vestigially around in the 4th and 5th centuries in Athanasius and Cyril of Alexandria, but it has been divested of a lot of the content that would have characterized the same discussions in the 1st or early 2nd century CE."[^2] *Rating: strong* — names the mechanism (diachronic change, cf. [[The Four Constellations of Deification Belief]]) and the direction (loss of content).

3. **The Hebraica Veritas removed earlier proof texts.** "We have the introduction of the *Hebraica Veritas*, the turn away from the Septuagint and towards the Hebrew… that Jerome effected at the end of the 4th century CE, and that is going to remove a lot of the proof texts that were used in earlier Christian understanding of the relationship of humanity to deity."[^3] *Rating: strong* — a concrete, datable, text-critical cause (e.g. Proverbs 8:22 "created me" → "begat me"; see [[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)]]).

4. **Three distinct ideals — patristic ≠ biblical ≠ Mormon.** From his 2007 paper "Latter-day Saints and Patristics," McClellan recalls "a whole section dedicated to the doctrine of deification where I talk about how the patristic idea of deification is not what's in the Bible and it's not what's in Mormonism, and these are three very distinct ideals."[^4] *Rating: strong (and notable provenance)* — a former-insider verdict that directly forbids the naive LDS use of the fathers. He points to Norman Russell's *The Doctrine of Deification in the Greek Patristic Tradition* as the best academic treatment of the later development.

5. **Bringing up the fathers does not answer the biblical question.** "Bringing up patristic appeals to theosis is not going to be very effective… because [the evangelical] is asking for biblical examples… [and] the later Christian ideas of theosis are also significantly different from what [the LDS apologist] is talking about."[^5] *Rating: strong* — connects the historical claim to the debate tactic (see [[LDS Should Lead With John 10 Not Revelation 3 or Patristics]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** An LDS (or Orthodox) apologist can answer that the *continuity* matters more than McClellan allows: the same technical word — *theosis / theopoiēsis* — runs unbroken from Irenaeus and Justin to Athanasius, Cyril, and Maximus, and the "exchange formula" ("God became man that man might become god," [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]]) is preserved verbatim. That the fathers later *guarded* it with the essence/energies distinction (see [[The Essence-Energies Distinction Distinguishes Orthodoxy From Pantheism]]) is a refinement under philosophical pressure, not necessarily a betrayal; and an honest LDS case already concedes the *ontology* differs while claiming the *tradition* (see [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]]). So patristic theosis is still a legitimate witness that *some* real deification was always Christian. McClellan's reply is that "legitimate witness to *a* deification" is not "witness to the *biblical* or the *Mormon* deification" — and conflating the three is precisely the category error his four-constellation frame is built to expose.

## LDS Relevance

This note is the clearest place where **McClellan's scholarship cuts against LDS apologetics**, and it should be filed honestly as such. The common Latter-day Saint apologetic — "even the Church Fathers taught that men become gods" — is, on his analysis, only half-true: the fathers used the *words*, but by the 4th century they meant participation-without-ontological-godhood, a third thing distinct from both the New Testament's more radical divinization and LDS exaltation. The disciplined LDS takeaway is twofold. **Concession:** stop deploying Athanasius/Cyril as if they proved the LDS doctrine; that is the very [[Putting Dogma Over Data|dogma-over-data]] move applied in reverse. **Opportunity:** McClellan's own claim is that the *earlier* (NT and early-Jewish/Christian) deification was *more*, not less, radical than the creedal version — which relocates the strongest pro-deification evidence to exactly the texts the LDS reading prizes (John 10; Psalm 82; the divinization soteriology of [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology|John]]). So the corrective points the apologist *toward* a better argument: argue the first-century data (and [[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)|the first-century definition of "god"]]), not the watered-down fourth-century gloss.
