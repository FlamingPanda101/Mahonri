# D&C 76 Dates the Distinct-Persons View to Early 1832

## The Point
The ex-Mormon argument depends on a **timeline**: Joseph Smith supposedly held a Trinitarian/modalist "same-being" view through the early 1830s and only *evolved* a separate-beings Godhead in the **mid-1830s**, which then drove the Book of Mormon edits and the two-personage 1838 First Vision. That timeline breaks on a single dated document. Doctrine and Covenants **76** was dictated in **February 1832** — months *before* the 1832 First Vision account (written summer 1832) — and already describes the Father and the Son as **distinct persons appearing together**. So the distinct-persons view is documented in early 1832, not invented mid-decade. The dated text "softens, complicates, or possibly kills" the evolution timeline.[^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The text itself.** D&C 76:20, 23: "we beheld the glory of the Son on the right hand of the Father, and received of his fulness… and we saw him, even on the right hand of God… he is the only begotten of the Father." Two distinct persons, one on the right hand of the other — the host notes it "sounds like Stephen" (Acts 7:55–56, the archetypal vision of the Son standing at God's right hand).[^2]
2. **The dating is the whole argument.** Because the critic's case is specifically that separateness emerged *after* the 1832 First Vision account, a separate-persons vision *predating* that account in February 1832 directly contradicts the proposed sequence. At minimum it shows Joseph "was certainly capable of describing God and Jesus as appearing separately in a vision" in early 1832.[^3]
3. **It dovetails with the unedited Book of Mormon evidence.** This is a second, independent line alongside the Book of Mormon data: even the 1830 text stages the Father and Son speaking separately (2 Nephi 31), which is hard to read modalistically. See [[Book of Mormon Christology Edits Are Not Evidence of Trinitarian Backpedaling]].[^4]
4. **Steelman.** A careful critic will note that D&C 76 shows Joseph *could describe* two persons in a vision, but does not by itself prove a fully worked-out metaphysics of two *separately embodied, ontologically distinct* beings — the later King Follett / plurality-of-gods teaching is a further development. The honest LDS reply is the one in [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]]: D&C 76 defeats the *specific dated claim* ("separateness only appears mid-1830s") without needing to assert that every later refinement was already present in 1832.

## Best Quotes
> "And we beheld the glory of the Son on the right hand of the Father and received of his whole fullness, and we saw him even on the right hand of God… And we heard the voice bearing record that he is the only begotten of the Father." [^2]

> "Since that 1832 first vision account was apparently written in the summer of 1832, these passages soften, complicate, or possibly kill the idea that Joseph Smith didn't develop the belief that God and Jesus being separate until the mid-1830s." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints hold that the Father and the Son are distinct, embodied persons (Article of Faith 1; the First Vision). D&C 76, the "vision of the glories," is itself a Father-and-Son vision and is canonized scripture. Apologetically, the value of this concept is its **precision**: it is a dated primary source that targets the critic's *chronology* directly, rather than a general appeal to doctrine. Pair it with [[First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels]] for the parallel objection about the 1832-vs-1838 accounts.
