# Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that where the Hebrew Bible uses *elohim* for non-Yahweh beings, these are simply what Christians call **angels** — created, subservient, worshiping Yahweh — citing that the Septuagint renders *elohim* as *angeloi* (e.g., Psalm 8:5), and that angels do not threaten monotheism.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The angelic reading is a late, datable development (rating: strong; literary-historical)
"Squishing down" the rest of the Israelite pantheon into angelic/demonic status happened in the **post-exilic, Persian, and Hellenistic periods** — not in the earliest literary layers. Reading it back across the prior ~1,000 years presupposes univocality. [^1] See [[Synchronic Change in the Conception of Deity]].

> "the squishing down of the rest of the Israelite Pantheon apart from Adonai... into cile Angelic demonic status is something that occurs in the postexilic period into the Persian period into the helenistic period" [^1]

### 2. The Septuagint dates ~3rd century BCE — it cannot retroject onto 700 prior years (rating: strong; the central methodological point)
Appealing to the Septuagint's *elohim* → *angeloi* translation (c. 3rd century BCE) is appealing to an event "that is not indicative of how the word Elohim was interpreted in the 700 years prior." [^2]

> "your appealing to an event that took place around the 3r century BCE that is not indicative of how the word Elohim was interpreted in the 700 years prior to the translation of the Hebrew Bible into Greek" [^2]

### 3. Same move with Psalm 8 / "heavenly beings" (rating: strong)
Psalm 8:5's *elohim*, rendered "angels/heavenly beings" by the Septuagint and the Syriac and Aramaic traditions, again "dates to probably around the middle of the 3rd century BCE or after" — not evidence for how *elohim* was understood centuries earlier. [^3]

### 4. Psalm 82 is the test case (rating: strong; cites McClellan's JBL article)
[[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82]] has God standing in the divine council "in the midst of the gods" and sentencing them to death. The angelic reading (Heiser's divine-council-of-angels) is "one possible way to read" it, but it is a later overlay; McClellan develops the alternative in his 2018 *Journal of Biblical Literature* article "The Gods' Complaint." [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund/Heiser can argue that the *concept* of subordinate divine beings serving Yahweh is present early (the divine council appears in pre-exilic texts), so calling them "angels" is a fair functional gloss rather than a foreign import, and that Nehemiah 9 already has Yahweh creating and being worshiped by the host of heaven. McClellan's reply: the *terminological reduction* to "angels/demons" — stripping these beings of genuine deity status — is what is anachronistic, and the same created-and-worshiping language is said of the gods relative to Amun-Ra without yielding monotheism (see [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]). [^5]

### 6. The angel-rendering cuts the *other* way at Deuteronomy 32:8 (rating: strong; text-critical)
Speaking to [[Alex OConnor]], McClellan turns the Septuagint's habit into evidence *for* an earlier divine-plurality reading: the LXX majority text of [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8]] reads "according to the number of the **angels of God**," but "the translators of the Septuagint really like to take the Hebrew *Elohim* and translate it as *angels*," so "angels of God" most likely reflects a Hebrew *Vorlage* that said **"sons of God."** A scholar (Chris Hansen) has catalogued Septuagint and later Latin manuscripts that read "sons of God" outright, and a 1960s Dead Sea Scrolls Deuteronomy fragment preserves exactly that Hebrew reading — "about as close as you can get to a slam dunk" against the Masoretic "sons of Israel." So the angelic rendering is not evidence of original monotheism; it is a translational softening of an older "sons of God" text. See [[Deuteronomy 32 Dead Sea Scrolls Monolatry]].[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "what the argument from here is going to do is take that postexilic identification of the Gods of the Nations as angels and then just kind of arbitrarily extend it to cover all of the time that came before without argument" [^2]

> "this is I would argue methodologically problematic and is presupposing univocality is presupposing a lot of dogmas that can only be read into the text and cannot be critically identified or detected in the text" [^6]
