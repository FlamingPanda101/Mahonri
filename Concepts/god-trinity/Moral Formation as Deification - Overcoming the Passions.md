# Moral Formation as Deification - Overcoming the Passions

## The Point
For ancient Christians, becoming like God was not only a past event (the Incarnation) but a **present process**: "living a moral life was essential to becoming like God." [^1] This meant keeping the commandments and cultivating virtues (faith, hope, charity, humility, patience, wisdom, temperance) by overcoming the "passions" or "vices" — the desires that lead to sin. Christians described moral growth as "regaining God's 'image' and/or 'likeness'" (Genesis 1:26) and "imitating Christ and the life of heavenly angels." [^1] Becerra frames the gospel itself as "a system of becoming, intended to transform disciples into more godlike and Christlike persons." [^2]

## Evidence
1. **Origen distinguishes "image" from "likeness."** The image is humans' *potential* for growth; the likeness is the *realized* perfection, which humans "acquire for themselves by their own earnest efforts to imitate God." [^3] (See [[Image of God - Patristic Interpretations]].)
2. **Monasticism as a "university of virtue."** Monks (*monachos*, "solitary") and monastic communities living under a "rule" with "all things in common" (Acts 4:32) were "laboratories for intellectual and spiritual formation... a paradigm of what heavenly life might be like." [^4]
3. **Evagrius of Pontus — eight vices and *apatheia*.** Evagrius (c. 345-399) catalogued eight vices (gluttony, lust, avarice, sadness, anger, despondency, vainglory, pride) — later revised into the "seven deadly sins" — and prescribed remedies (prayer, fasting, scripture study, psalmody, almsgiving, service). Against Origen's *metriopatheia* (merely controlling sinful inclinations), Evagrius taught *apatheia*, the complete eradication of the passions — the "health of the soul." [^5]
4. **Gregory of Nyssa — the Beatitudes as a ladder.** In *Homilies on the Beatitudes*, Gregory saw the eight beatitudes "like so many steps" of ascent to godliness, the goal being "communion with the Godhead," i.e., "likeness to God" and the "highest form of freedom," self-mastery. [^6]
5. **The grace/agency dispute reappears.** Augustine (original sin: humans cannot even *desire* righteousness without grace) vs. Pelagius (humans born sinless, morally self-sufficient with external help). [^7] (See [[The Pelagian Controversy on Grace and Human Ability]].)

## Best Quotes
> "The gospel of Jesus Christ is more than a system of belief or a code of conduct. It is a system of becoming, intended to transform disciples into more godlike and Christlike persons." [^2]

> "Humankind received the honor of God's image in their first creation; whereas the perfection of God's likeness was reserved for them at the consummation... by their own earnest efforts to imitate God." — Origen [^3]

## LDS Connection
"A system of becoming" is strikingly congenial to LDS soteriology, where the point of mortality and ordinances is to be *changed into* a Christlike, exalted being, not merely declared righteous. Origen's image/likeness distinction (potential realized through effort) mirrors the LDS view that exaltation is a developmental attainment — compare [[Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis]]. Evagrian *apatheia* and the monastic "university of virtue" resonate with the LDS emphasis on enduring discipline, repentance, and progressive sanctification rather than a single forensic moment.
