# Gods of Their Own Planets Is a Caricature of Exaltationist Theosis

## The Claim

The stock anti-Mormon one-liner is that "Mormons believe that humans can actually one day become **gods of their own planets**." In the [[All Those In Favor - Three Tactics to Evangelize Mormons|All Those In Favor]] episode, the Roman Catholic commentator **Noah** ("Jesus and whatnot") singles this out as a line he himself had used and now retracts: it is "a gross misrepresentation," "a cartoony version of their theology," on par with saying "Christians believe they get to jump around on clouds every day and wear angel wings."[^1] The accurate description, he says, is **"exaltationist theosis"** — the exalted "partake in the divine glory and power, and then share that power with more creation" — and "simply saying that they become their own gods is just a terrible misrepresentation."[^2] The hosts hold this up as a model of a critic who actually learned the doctrine before attacking it.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The "own planet" image is folklore, not the taught doctrine
What the Church actually teaches is participation in God's glory and creative work, framed as becoming like God — see [[Human Deification in Mormonism]]. The "personal planet" picture is a 19th-century speculative gloss and a Hollywood shorthand, not the catechetical content; attacking it is attacking a cartoon. Noah's own correction supplies the steelman: he grants there is "so much theology behind the celestial kingdoms" that he should "never have described it the way I did."[^3]

### 2. Theosis is a respectable, ancient Christian category
By naming it *theosis*, Noah locates exaltation inside the patristic and Eastern Orthodox tradition of deification rather than treating it as a Mormon eccentricity — compare [[Theosis as Likeness and Union So Far as Possible]] and [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]]. The LDS distinctive is the *ontology* (humans are the same kind as God — see [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis]]), not the bare idea of being deified, which historic Christianity already affirms.

### 3. The scriptural footing is real
Noah anticipates the [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]] objection ("before me there was no God formed, neither shall there be after me") and concedes the LDS reply — that it addresses Israel's loyalty, not a metaphysical census — and points to [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]] ("ye are gods"), which "literally calls other humans gods."[^4] He still disagrees with the theology, but he argues against the real position, urging fellow critics to "stop making such low-tier arguments… and start actually understanding what our brothers and sisters believe."[^5]

### Steelman (the critic's side)
A critic who keeps his disagreement can still object that "exaltationist theosis" is a softening label: LDS exaltation includes spirit-children, eternal increase, and a plurality of gods that goes materially beyond Orthodox *theosis*, which guards an absolute Creator/creature line (participation in divine *energies*, never the divine *essence*). So the "own planet" phrasing, while crude, gestures at a genuine difference — exalted beings who organize and preside over creation — that the polite relabeling can obscure. Noah grants exactly this by *retaining* his disagreement; his point is only that the difference must be stated accurately to be argued honestly, and "becoming gods of their own planets" is not an accurate statement of it.

## Best Quotes

> "Mormons believe that humans can actually one day become gods of their own planets — that is a gross misrepresentation of what Mormons actually believe."[^1]
