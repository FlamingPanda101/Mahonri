# Exodus 21–22: *Ha-Elohim* Means "the Gods," Not "the Judges"

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

In the slave-law and oath passages of Exodus 21–22, the servant is brought "to *ha-elohim*" ([[Exodus 21.6|Exodus 21:6]]) and disputed property cases come "before *ha-elohim*," who "condemns" the guilty party (Exod 22:8–9). Many translations (KJV, NIV) render *ha-elohim* there as "the judges." [[Dan McClellan]] argues this is a misreading: *elohim* never means human judges; the passages refer to **the gods** (household deities indexing the divine presence before whom oaths were sworn), and the grammar forces it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The plural verb in Exodus 22:8–9 forces a plural-gods reading
McClellan: the verb is plural — "the one whom *ha-elohim* condemn" (plural) — "it's very clearly plural here in 22:8 and 9." Because the plural is unambiguous there, it should govern the reading of the parallel singular-looking case in 21:6 as well. The "judges" gloss has to *override* this plural to fit a human tribunal.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a concrete grammatical datum (plural verb agreement) that the "judges" reading must explain away.

### 2. *Elohim* never lexically means "judges"
The "judges" sense is, on McClellan's account, a post-biblical renegotiation (2nd–3rd c. CE) born of discomfort with divine plurality, not an attested meaning of the word in the biblical period. The household *teraphim* "index the presence of a deity or other deities," so oaths and adjudication "before the gods" is the natural sense. See [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on the chronological/lexical claim (it matches McClellan's documented academic-consensus argument elsewhere in the vault).

### 3. The NET note cites Gordon and Draffkorn *against* their own conclusion
McClellan flags a striking case of mishandled scholarship: the New English Translation's note grants the "judges" reading while conceding "others have made a stronger case that Elohim refers to human judges," then cites [[Cyrus Gordon]] and [[Anne Draffkorn]] — two scholars who, "in their own papers separated by decades, argue strenuously that it does *not* mean judges, it means gods." So "whoever edited that note was either just straight up lying or wildly misunderstood those papers or did not read them at all."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a checkable claim about a specific translation note and two named, published papers (Gordon and Draffkorn are credentialed A-tier Near Eastern scholars).

### 4. The "judges" reading rode in on a human reinterpretation of *bene elohim*
McClellan situates the shift historically: in the Greco-Roman period the gods got "squished down into angelic status"; then, to relieve the scandal of angels fathering children in Genesis 6, some Jewish thinkers re-read the *bene elohim* as *humans* (the line of Seth). That human reading then "cascades down" as the interpretive lens later applied to Psalm 82 and Exodus 22 — yielding "*elohim* = mighty ones / judges." It is a downstream harmonization, not the original sense.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — a coherent reconstruction of the interpretive history that explains *why* the "judges" gloss arose late.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The "judges" reading is not arbitrary. (a) Exodus 18 and 23 present Moses and human representatives administering God's justice, so reading the tribunal *ha-elohim* as God-authorized human judges has a contextual rationale — the elders judge "as God." (b) Ancient and rabbinic interpreters (and the Targumim) took *elohim* here as magistrates, so the reading is old and within Jewish tradition, even if not as old as the text. (c) A defender of "judges" can argue the plural verb is a frozen idiom or honorific plural rather than evidence of multiple deities. McClellan's reply is chronological and lexical: the *attested* "judges" sense post-dates the biblical period and arises precisely when divine plurality became uncomfortable; the plural verb, the household-teraphim background, and the parallel with Psalm 82 (see [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]) all point to *gods*. What survives is a genuine interpretive fork — "gods" vs. "judges" — but the burden, he argues, lies on the "judges" reading to supply a biblical-period attestation it does not have.
