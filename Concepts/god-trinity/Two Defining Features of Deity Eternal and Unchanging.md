# Two Defining Features of Deity Eternal and Unchanging

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the entire shift from acceptable subordinationism to mandatory consubstantiality was driven by two **Greek-philosophical axioms about what deity must be**: (1) deity is *eternal* — it has always existed — and (2) deity is *immutable* — there is no change, no hierarchy, no alteration within it.[^1] [[Arius|Arius']] claim that "there was a time when Jesus did not exist" and that the Son is subordinate to the Father directly violated both axioms, which is why a position that "was actually pretty standard in the 3rd century CE" became intolerable by the 4th.[^2] The driving concern was keeping the gospel "palatable to Greco-Roman intelligentsia" whose received Greek philosophy these axioms encoded.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The two axioms are genuine features of Greek metaphysics — rate: strong
Eternality and immutability as marks of the divine are standard in Platonic, Middle/Neoplatonic, and Aristotelian thought: the truly real is unchanging and timeless, while the mutable belongs to the realm of becoming. The unmoved mover of Aristotle and the eternal Forms of Plato encode exactly the axioms McClellan names. *Rating: strong; this is mainstream history of philosophy.* See [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]].

### 2. They explain the timing of the Arian controversy — rate: strong
McClellan's point is that subordinationism was not refuted by new exegesis but ruled out by a hardening commitment to the philosophical profile of deity: "by the 4th century CE we're exalting Jesus more and more," so a created, subordinate Son became "problematic" precisely because it implied the Son was not eternal and stood in a hierarchy.[^2] *Rating: strong; tracks the standard account that Nicaea's *homoousios* protected the Son's full, eternal, unchanging deity.* **Steelman:** an orthodox theologian agrees God is eternal and immutable but would say these are also *biblical* attributes (Mal 3:6, "I the LORD change not"; Ps 90:2), not merely Greek imports — so applying them to the Son is faithfulness to revelation, not capitulation to philosophy. McClellan would counter that the *combination* with strict numerical oneness, and the demand that the Son share these attributes without subordination, is what generates the distinctively Nicene puzzle.

### 3. The axioms are the engine, not just background — rate: persuasive
On this reading the axioms are causal: they are the constraints that make the Trinity *necessary as a construction*. If deity must be one, eternal, and unchanging, yet Father, Son, and Spirit are all divine and distinct, the only escape is to split "being" from "person" and assert consubstantiality (see [[Consubstantiality]]). *Rating: persuasive as an explanatory model; it coheres with [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].*

## Best Quotes
> "One of the main two features of deity is, one, that it is eternal — it has always existed — and two, that there is no change. There is no hierarchy. There is no alteration in deity. It is always the same and it can never change."[^1]

> "It begins to become problematic to say there was a time when Jesus didn't exist and that Jesus is subordinate."[^2]

> "It was undermining the attempt to make the gospel palatable to Greco-Roman intelligentsia if their concept was flatly contradicting the received wisdom of Greek philosophy."[^3]
