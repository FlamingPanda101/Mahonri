# The Armenian Adam and Eve Says God Was Once a Man

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the **Armenian** apocryphal *Adam and Eve* literature — translated and edited by the specialist [[Michael Stone]] in the late 20th century, roughly 150 years after Joseph Smith — preserves the most distinctive Latter-day Saint teaching about God. In several Armenian manuscripts of the Fall, when the serpent tempts Eve, he says not merely "you shall not die" but that **God himself was once a man who became God by eating of the fruit / gaining the knowledge of good and evil**. Barnes reads this as a *prolepsis*: the [[God Was Once a Man|King Follett doctrine]] that God is an exalted man, surfacing in an ancient text Joseph Smith could not have read.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The serpent's line states the doctrine almost verbatim — rate: moderate (striking textual parallel)
Barnes quotes the Armenian serpent: "Not so, you won't die — because God himself was a man like you when he ate of the fruit... and he became God of all because of that. He said not to eat it because he knows when you eat of it you will become a god, his equal." The convergence with Joseph Smith's "God himself was once as we are now and is an exalted man" is close and specific, not generic.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the quoted line genuinely makes the claim; weight depends on the recension's date, the translation, and whether the motif is isolated or widespread.

### 2. Multiple manuscripts and recensions, not a single outlier — rate: moderate (breadth within the corpus)
Barnes stresses the idea recurs "over and over... different manuscripts, different time periods" within the Armenian Adam corpus, which (if accurate) makes it a stable tradition rather than a one-off scribal flourish.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — breadth claimed but not here documented; the corpus is real and Stone's editions are citable, but the apologist's "over and over" needs verification against the editions.

### 3. The source was inaccessible to Joseph Smith — rate: strong (chronology and language)
The Armenian texts were edited and translated into English only in the modern period; Joseph Smith had no access to Armenian pseudepigrapha in the 1840s. So the parallel cannot be explained by borrowing, fitting the [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data|unique-data]] pattern.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on availability; the live question is what the parallel *means* (see steelman).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **The serpent is a liar in the narrative.** The line is spoken by Satan as a deception, so even granting the text, it attests an ancient *temptation motif* ("you'll be like God"), echoing Genesis 3:5, not a doctrinal affirmation that God was literally once a man. The Genesis serpent already says "ye shall be as gods" — the Armenian is an expansion of a canonical lie, not a new revelation.
- **Late, Christianized, and fluid.** Armenian Adam literature is medieval in its surviving form and heavily reworked; a motif in a late recension is weak evidence for a primitive pre-Christian belief.
- **Deification ≠ "God was once a man."** Patristic *theosis* ("God became man that man might become god," see [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]]) is ubiquitous and orthodox; an apocryphal echo of *becoming* divine does not establish the specifically LDS claim that *the Father* progressed from mortality.
- **Cherry-picking.** Selecting the one serpent-line that matches while passing over the rest of the text risks parallelomania.

**What survives:** the **availability** point holds — Joseph Smith did not read Armenian pseudepigrapha — so a genuine verbal parallel is a real curiosity. But because the words are placed in the *serpent's deceiving mouth*, the text is better read as an ancient *form of the temptation* ("you can become as God did") than as a creedal statement of divine autobiography. The defensible claim: the King-Follett intuition that humanity and divinity differ in degree, not kind, has surprising ancient echoes; the strong claim that this apocryphon *teaches* "God was once a man" overreads a tempter's lie.
