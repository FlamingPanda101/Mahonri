# Three Persons Sharing a Nature Are Still Three Beings

## The Claim

Sharing one divine *nature* does not make three persons numerically one *being*. Three persons who each fully possess the divine nature are three divine beings, just as three persons with human nature are three humans. The Trinitarian inference from "one nature" to "one being" is a non sequitur. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The counting argument by parallel cases.** Three persons with human nature = three humans; three animals with canine nature = three dogs; three shapes with triangle nature = three triangles. By identical logic, three persons with the divine nature = three gods (or, in the LDS framing, three divine persons forming one Godhead) — *not* one thing. "To just say that you have three persons who all share a divine nature, that doesn't mean you only have one thing." (Evidence: strong as ordinary-language logic; this is the standard "counting problem" pressed against the Trinity in analytic philosophy of religion.) [^1]
2. **The general principle, stated formally.** "When three distinct things are each fully X, we count three X's, not one." Treating the divine case as the lone exception is what requires special pleading. [^2]
3. **Steelman.** The Trinitarian answers that the divine nature is not a *universal* shared by instances (like "humanity," exemplified by many) but a single *concrete individual* nature, numerically one and wholly possessed by each person — so the counting analogy fails because there are not three divine natures but one. Relative-identity theorists add that "is the same being as" and "is the same person as" are distinct sortal relations: the Son is the same *being* as the Father but a different *person*. (Evidence: these are serious moves; classical theism denies the divine essence is a universal.) The LDS reply: a single concrete individual nature with one will and one mind just *is* one person (modalism); and relative identity must still explain how three things each fully divine count as one without abandoning the transitivity of identity used everywhere else. [^3]
4. **Why "being" must mean something.** If the Trinitarian says the persons are "the same being" but then concedes "being means nothing or anything we want it to mean," the term ceases to do work; the only non-trivial reading ("same nature/properties") is one the LDS side accepts without conceding one *being*. [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "If we have three beings, three persons who have the divine nature, how many do we have? ... No, you have one. You have one God. ... Bad at math." [^1]

> "Everyday reasoning: when three distinct things are each fully X, we count three X's, not one. Calling this a mystery is not humility, it's special pleading." [^2]

> "If you mean by that that they share the same nature, that they have the same properties, like, yeah, we agree. ... But that doesn't mean that they are the same being." [^4]
