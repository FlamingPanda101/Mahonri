# The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the Jewish establishment could not recognize Jesus partly because the **Deuteronomists** — "a school of scribes, priests, and theologians operating primarily during the reign of King Josiah" (~640–609 BC) — had reshaped the older Israelite tradition and "flattened and removed" the **divine council** theology that made a pre-existent heavenly Messiah intelligible.[^1] The older "first temple religion" held that "Yahweh presided over an assembly of divine beings, that there were heavenly figures called the sons of God... and that the coming deliverer of Israel would be a pre-existent heavenly being among those gods" — "exactly what made sense of a pre-existent divine heavenly messiah, which is precisely what Jesus claimed to be."[^1] When that framework "was edited out of the mainstream canon, the figure that Jesus was describing became much harder for people immersed in that canon to actually recognize."[^1] Arnold grounds the editorial program in mainstream, non-LDS scholarship — [[Richard Elliott Friedman]] (*Who Wrote the Bible?*), [[Israel Finkelstein]] & Silberman (*The Bible Unearthed*), and [[Frank Moore Cross]] of Harvard — and stresses "this is all mainstream academic consensus, not LDS scholars or just fringe theories."[^1] The textual fingerprint is [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8]]'s "sons of God" (older) vs. "sons of Israel" (edited). See [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]] and [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Deuteronomistic school is real and mainstream.** The J/E/P/D source analysis and a Josianic Deuteronomistic redaction are standard in critical scholarship; [[Frank Moore Cross|Cross]]'s double-redaction model and [[Richard Elliott Friedman|Friedman]]'s popularization are widely taught. *Rating: solidly mainstream as a framework (individual reconstructions are debated).* [^1]
2. **The divine-council suppression has textual evidence.** [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8]] ("sons of God," 4QDeut/LXX, vs. Masoretic "sons of Israel") and [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82]]'s divine assembly are concrete data of a council theology later muted — corroborated by Michael Heiser's work and the vault's [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]]. *Rating: the variant is solid; the inference to deliberate Messianic suppression is Arnold's synthesis.* [^1]
3. **It supplies a mechanism, not just a motive.** "Jews missed Jesus" gains explanatory depth if the *conceptual category* (a pre-existent heavenly deliverer) had been editorially marginalized — connecting non-recognition to a documented textual process rather than to bare stubbornness. *Rating: a coherent and distinctive explanatory move.* [^1]
4. **It coheres with the wider First-Temple case.** The same reform is the vault's hinge for the Book of Mormon's pre-exilic theology ([[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]], [[The Surviving Old Testament Is the Southern Kingdom's Edition]]). *Rating: well integrated.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **"Mainstream consensus" is overstated for the strong claim** — that *there was* Deuteronomistic editing is mainstream; that it *deliberately deleted a pre-existent-Messiah doctrine* is not the consensus, and figures like Heiser argue the divine council survives *in* the canon (Ps 82; Deut 32) rather than being successfully erased — which, if true, means the establishment *had* the texts and still read them non-messianically, so the problem is interpretation, not deletion. (b) **The divine council ≠ a dying, incarnate Messiah** — even granting a council and "sons of God," that is a long way from the specific Christian claim of an incarnate, crucified, risen deliverer; Second-Temple Jews who *did* hold high-mediator views (Enochic circles, Philo's Logos) still did not become Christians, so the missing-framework theory under-determines non-recognition. (c) **Jewish non-recognition has simpler explanations** — a crucified Messiah was a genuine scandal (1 Cor 1:23) and Jesus' messianic claims were historically contestable, independent of any editorial history. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is *probabilistic and cumulative*, not that one doctrine was surgically removed — the *marginalization* of the divine-mediator stream in the standardized canon, plus the documented "sons of God→Israel" edit, plus the *pattern* that the communities who kept the older texts recognized Jesus first (see [[The Communities That Preserved Older Texts Recognized Jesus First]]), together make non-recognition a *predictable* outcome of textual narrowing rather than mere hard-heartedness; and that the divine council *survives in fragments* is itself evidence an editor tried, imperfectly, to suppress it.
