# Being and Person Are the Same in Aristotle

## The Claim
To be your own *person* is to be your own *being*. On [[Aristotle]]'s own terms, an individual person *is* an individual substance, so "three persons sharing one substance/being" would have been incoherent to him. The Fathers therefore did not merely borrow Greek language — they **coined new definitions** for Greek words so the doctrine could be stated at all.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Aristotle: an individual person = an individual substance
For Aristotle, a primary substance is the individual. "Three persons who share one substance" is not what *ousia* meant; Aristotle "would have looked at that and been like, no, that isn't what we mean by that." What makes you your own person *is* having your own being/existence. *(Moderate-strong: faithful to Aristotle's primary-substance doctrine, where the individual is the paradigm substance.)* [^1]

### 2. The Fathers redefined the terms to fit
Greek philosophy "at its core would not allow for contradictions such as that," so the Fathers "used Greek philosophical language but actually came up with new definitions" — making it sound rigorous enough that "the higher class would take Christianity seriously." The technical *hypostasis* / *ousia* distinction is a Christian innovation, not classical usage. *(Strong: the persons/essence (*hypostasis*/*ousia*) distinction is indeed a 4th-century Cappadocian refinement.)* [^2]

### 3. Therefore "one being, three persons" multiplies persons without their own being
If a person just *is* a distinct being, then either there are three beings (and "one being" fails) or one being (and you have one person — modalism). "You don't have a single being that can be multiple persons." A water bottle is one being with no persons; you cannot make it "two persons." *(Moderate: this is the LDS/contradiction reading; the Cappadocians explicitly deny that *hypostasis* reduces to *ousia*, which is the crux — see steelman.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The Cappadocian Fathers ([[Basil the Great]], [[Gregory of Nyssa]]) anticipated exactly this objection and answered it: they distinguished *ousia* (what a thing is — the shared "what") from *hypostasis* (who a particular is — the "who"), arguing that *person* and *being* are **not** synonyms, so one *ousia* in three *hypostaseis* is not a contradiction but a refinement. They would say the LDS argument simply *assumes* Aristotle's collapse of person and individual substance and then declares the Trinity incoherent — begging the question. The doctrine's coherence stands or falls on whether that ousia/hypostasis distinction is intelligible, which the Fathers (and modern analytic "social" and "relative-identity" Trinitarians) defend. The LDS counter is that the distinction is a bespoke patch with no purchase outside the doctrine it was built to save (cf. [[Trinity Is Fundamentally Contradictory]]).

## Best Quotes
> "A substance, for Aristotle — an individual person was an individual substance... Aristotle would have looked at [three persons, one substance] and been like, no, that isn't what we mean by that." [^1]

> "They used Greek language to make it sound complex... so that the higher class would take Christianity seriously, but they created new definitions." [^2]

> "What makes you your own person is the fact that you have your own being or existence." [^3]
