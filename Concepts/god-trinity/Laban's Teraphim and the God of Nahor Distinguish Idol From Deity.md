# Laban's Teraphim and the God of Nahor Distinguish Idol From Deity

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

A common apologetic reply to the Bible's "other gods" is that they are *only idols* — lifeless wood and stone — so polytheism is illusory (the proof-text is [[2 Kings 19.18|2 Kings 19:18]], where the nations' gods "were not gods, but the work of men's hands"). [[Dan McClellan]] answers that the Laban–Jacob narrative shows the biblical authors themselves distinguishing the *idol* from the *deity it indexes*: Laban calls his stolen household images "my gods," yet separately swears by a real, non-idol "God of Nahor." The idol-polemic denigrates the statue without denying the god behind it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Laban calls the teraphim "my gods" — the images index deities
When Rachel steals Laban's *teraphim* ([[Genesis 31.19|Genesis 31:19]]) and Laban pursues, he demands, "Why did you steal *my gods*?" (Hebrew *elohai*). McClellan reads the *teraphim* as "probably representative of ancestral deities" — physical objects that *index* a divine presence, the same logic by which idols function. (Even David has *teraphim* in his house, 1 Sam 19.) So "my gods" applied to the images is not a denial that gods exist; it is precisely the idol-as-pointer relationship. See [[Teraphim Were Normative Divine Images Never Condemned]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the text plainly has Laban name the images "my gods"; the indexing function is standard ANE understanding of cult images.

### 2. Laban *also* swears by the God of Nahor — a separate, non-idol deity
In the covenant scene (Gen 31:53), "May the God of Abraham and the God of Nahor judge between us," with a **plural verb** ("let them judge," *yishpetu*). Laban is "obviously swearing by the actual separate entity that the idol only indexes" — a deity he no longer possesses any statue of, since the teraphim were taken. So within a single episode the narrator has Laban refer both to images-as-gods *and* to a real deity distinct from any image.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the plural verb and the distinct "God of Nahor" (vs. the God of Abraham) are textual; the implication that idol ≠ deity is hard to evade.

### 3. The same logic runs into the New Testament
McClellan notes the pattern is consistent across the canon: Paul says "an idol is nothing in the world," yet also says that those who sacrifice to idols are sacrificing "to demons" — "gods that they did not know." So "idols are nothing, but the thing the idols represent are still something." The idol-polemic targets the object, not the existence of the indexed power. See [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]].[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — 1 Corinthians 8–10 explicitly pairs "an idol is nothing" with "they sacrifice to demons," exactly the idol/deity split.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The traditional reading has real resources. (a) "God of Abraham and God of Nahor" may be a single God known under two ancestral relationships — much as Jesus speaks of "my God and your God" without implying two deities — so the plural verb could be a grammatical convention rather than proof of distinct gods. (b) 2 Kings 19:18's "they were no gods" can be read as the narrator's *own* verdict that the nations' deities were ontological nullities, with "gods" used loosely for the idols people mistakenly served. (c) Calling the teraphim "my gods" may simply report Laban's pagan viewpoint, which the inspired narrator does not endorse. McClellan's reply is that this is rhetoric, not metaphysics: the biblical writers *denigrate* rival gods (calling them nothing, or collapsing them into their impotent idols) as a polemical move, while elsewhere plainly presupposing those deities are real entities indexed by the images — so "they're just idols" cannot be lifted out as the Bible's settled ontology (cf. [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]]). The honest reading, he argues, is that idol-talk and real-deity-talk coexist precisely because the idol *points to* a god the authors took to exist.

## Best Quotes

> "We've got Laban talking about his gods in reference to these idols. But then we've also got... Laban talking about the God of Nahor that he's swearing by, which is obviously not the idol that he no longer possesses. He's got to be swearing by the actual separate entity that the idol only indexes." — [[Dan McClellan]] [^2]

> "Even Paul says an idol is nothing in the world... but then there's another part where Paul says when they worship idols, they're actually worshiping demons, gods that they did not know. Idols are nothing, but the thing that the idols represent are still something." [^3]
