# The Abolition of Man as Prophecy

## The Point
In his conclusion, Ward synthesizes *The Abolition of Man* under four headings — **prophecy, poetry, participation, and power** — and argues that the book's enduring power is first of all prophetic.[^1] Like the great prophets, Lewis works "on two fronts at once": he both *tells forth* (critiques his own culture's subjectivism) and *foretells* (charts where that subjectivism must lead).[^2] What proves the prophecy genuine, Ward says, is not that Lewis predicted the future but that his 1943 message "resonated with its first hearers and has only attracted further attention as the decades have passed."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward classifies the book as "largely a jeremiad, largely a negative case" — "chiefly a philosophical forecast, intellectual in intent," describing the logical endpoint of unchecked subjectivism more than prescribing a cure.[^4] Lewis deliberately ends on a "hollow note," depicting moral blindness ("to 'see through' all things is the same as not to see"), because his aim is "less to change our destination than to predict our destiny."[^5] Ward captures the conditional logic: "This philosophical error leads to sub-humanity and if a sub-human fate is what we want, that's the fate we'll get."[^6] He notes how uncharacteristic this bleakness is — Lewis usually "rounds off his works on bright notes" — and suggests the downbeat peroration follows from his writing *philosophy* rather than *theology*, so "the theological virtue of hope need not be brought into the picture."[^7] The chief weakness Ward concedes is density: as three high-level philosophy lectures compressed into Lewis's "slimmest publication," it "tries to pack too much in," moving "too quickly" over its hardest point.[^8] Yet students still find it "continuingly relevant and (therefore) unsettling," which Ward treats as confirmation of its prophetic accuracy.[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "The old Sunday School definition can hardly be improved on: prophets tell forth and foretell." [^2]

> "What marks out his message as genuinely prophetic is that it resonated with its first hearers and has only attracted further attention as the decades have passed." [^3]

> "He is simply charting the likely course of unchecked subjectivism, saying in effect, 'This philosophical error leads to sub-humanity and if a sub-human fate is what we want, that's the fate we'll get.'" [^6]

## LDS Connection
Ward's framing of Lewis as a prophet who both diagnoses his age and forecasts its trajectory honors a category central to Latter-day Saint thought: the prophet who "tells forth and foretells." Latter-day Saints likewise read present moral decline as the predictable fruit of abandoning **eternal, objective moral law** — the warning of 2 Nephi 2 that agency turned against eternal law is self-destructive, and the modern apostolic counsel that a society which calls "evil good, and good evil" (Isaiah 5:20; 2 Nephi 15:20) is charting its own ruin. Lewis's insistence that "the choice is ours" mirrors the LDS emphasis on **agency**: the prophecy is conditional, a foretold consequence we remain free to avert. This is the same subjectivist drift that secular projects like [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] now try to reverse from within naturalism, while Lewis insists the correction can only come from within the [[The Tao - Lewis's Universal Doctrine of Objective Value|Tao]] itself (see [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
