# Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory

## The Point
Harris argues that moral relativism — the view that moral truths hold "only relative to a specific cultural framework" — refutes itself, because "this claim about the status of moral truth purports to be true across all possible frameworks."[^1] In practice relativism reduces to a demand for tolerance, "but this commitment to tolerance is not put forward as simply one relative preference among others"; tolerance is treated as "more in line with the (universal) truth about morality than intolerance is."[^1] He goes further: "given how deeply disposed we are to make universal moral claims, I think one can reasonably doubt whether any consistent moral relativist has ever existed."[^1]

## Evidence
Harris's case is as much diagnostic as logical. He targets the anthropological cult of the "noble savage," citing Robert Edgerton's demonstration that influential anthropologists (Boas, Mead, Benedict) "systematically exaggerated the harmony of folk societies and ignored their all too frequent barbarism."[^2] He exposes a **double standard**: dissenters who reject our *scientific* goals have no standing in science, yet dissenters from our *moral* goals are taken to render moral truth unspeakable.[^3] His most damning illustration is the academic who, asked whether a culture that ritually plucked out the eyes of every third child "on the basis of religious superstition" could be called wrong, answered: "Then you could never say that they were wrong."[^4] He quotes Donald Symons: when one person mutilates a girl's genitals it is a crime, but "when millions of people do this… suddenly it becomes 'culture,' and thereby magically becomes less, rather than more, horrible."[^5] He frames relativism as "an attempt to pay intellectual reparations for the crimes of Western colonialism," whose real-world cost is "abject failures of compassion" — Western intellectuals who "won't even blink" before defending acid attacks, compulsory veiling, or genital cutting.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Moral relativism, however, tends to be self-contradictory. Relativists may say that moral truths exist only relative to a specific cultural framework—but this claim about the status of moral truth purports to be true across all possible frameworks." [^1]

> "I think one can reasonably doubt whether any consistent moral relativist has ever existed." [^1]

> "When millions of people do this, instead of the enormity being magnified millions-fold, suddenly it becomes 'culture,' and thereby magically becomes less, rather than more, horrible." [^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
On the destructive half of the argument, the theist and Harris are firm allies: relativism is incoherent, the self-refutation charge is sound, and the moral cowardice it breeds is real. The disagreement is over what the refutation *proves*. **(1) The argument is double-edged.** Harris's most effective examples — that mutilating a child is wrong even if a whole culture sanctifies it — appeal to moral intuitions that are *universal, exceptionless, and binding regardless of consequences*: in other words, they sound exactly like the "intrinsically wrong actions" Harris explicitly disowns (p.26). The natural-law theist says he is borrowing precisely the realism he denies himself ([[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]; on objective value, [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]). **(2) Refuting relativism doesn't establish *his* alternative.** Showing that "anything goes" is false does not show that *well-being-maximization* is the standard; it leaves wide open the theistic option that the universal moral order is grounded in the nature of the human person and ultimately in God ([[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]]). **(3) On who is "left supine."** Harris blames *both* religion and secular relativism for cultural paralysis (see [[Two Catastrophes - Dogmatic Absolutism and Moral Relativism]], [[The Problem Is Dogmatism Not Religion]]). But historically it is robust moral realism — often theological — that fueled abolition and human-rights movements, while it is the *loss* of a transcendent ground that produced the relativist drift he laments. His own argument that "we simply must stand somewhere" is an admission that morality needs a fixed point; the theist agrees and offers a metaphysically adequate one rather than a stipulated definition. Fairly read, Harris has written a powerful brief *against* relativism — and most of its force is on loan from moral realism his naturalism cannot fund.
