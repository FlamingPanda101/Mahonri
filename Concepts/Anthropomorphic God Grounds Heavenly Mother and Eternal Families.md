# Anthropomorphic God Grounds Heavenly Mother and Eternal Families

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Casey Paul Griffiths]] argues that the King Follett picture of an embodied, fatherly God is not an embarrassing oddity but a generative principle: if God is "the best form of what a human being can be," then distinctively LDS doctrines — a Mother in Heaven, eternal marriage, and the persistence of loved-one relationships into the next life — follow as "anthropomorphic consequences."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A relational, loving God vs. an impersonal force.** The doctrine produces "a warm association between Latter-day Saints and God where they don't see God as a mystical energy field that surrounds us and penetrates us... he's not the Force, basically. We see God as a figure who loves us and cares about us and as an idealized father."[^2] The deliberate *Star Wars* contrast underlines the rejection of impersonal-deity theism.
2. **The downstream doctrines.** "If God's a human being, just the best form of what a human being can be — how do human beings have children? They fall in love, they have a counterpart. This leads to the idea of a mother in heaven, of the idea that you can gain exaltation without losing the things on earth that make life worth living."[^3] Exaltation is thus *additive*: "we take what's best with us into the next life, and that includes our relationships."[^4]
3. **Purpose-driven cosmology.** Griffiths ties this to [[Moses 1.39|Moses 1:39]] ("this is my work and my glory — to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man"), calling it the most-quoted scripture in the LDS canon and the reason members can say "I know what he's going for."[^5]
4. **Evidence rating.** This is a coherentist argument — it shows how LDS distinctives hang together once anthropomorphism is granted. Its strength is internal consistency; it does not by itself prove the premise (that God is embodied), which rests on the [[King Follett Discourse]] and prior revelation.
5. **Steelman of the opposing view.** Classical theists argue that biblical anthropomorphisms (God's "arm," "face," "passions") are *accommodations* — figurative language for an incorporeal God, not literal anatomy — and that pressing them literally yields a finite, gendered deity unworthy of worship. They further contend a Heavenly Mother and divine procreation have no clear biblical warrant and import pagan-style theogony. Griffiths' counter is that the scriptures plainly depict a passionate, involved, embodied God and that denying "body, parts, or passions" is itself an unscriptural Hellenistic imposition (he calls it "nonsense" at [05:00]-range in the closing apologetic).

## Best Quotes

> "They don't see God as a mystical energy field that surrounds us and penetrates us and is inside of us... he's not the Force, basically. We see God as a figure who loves us and cares about us and as an idealized father."[^6]

> "How do human beings have children? They fall in love, they have a counterpart. This leads to the idea of a mother in heaven... that you can gain exaltation without losing the things on earth that make life worth living."[^7]
