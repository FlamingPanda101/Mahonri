# Nahom Need Not Lie on the Red Sea Coast

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Dan Vogel]]'s strongest argument is that First Nephi keeps Lehi's party "in the borders near the Red Sea" in "the more fertile parts," implying a *coastal* route — so Nahom should sit on the Red Sea coast, not 80–100 miles inland on the Frankincense Trail. [[Neal Rappleye]] answers that the text's last reference to "the borders near the Red Sea" comes at **Shazer** — roughly 1,000 miles north of Nahom — and the entire broken-bow and Nahom account that follows never mentions the sea, the shore, or fertile parts. The text *permits* an inland turn, and the real-world geography *requires* one. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The last coastal reference is at Shazer.** Rappleye locates the final "more fertile parts of the wilderness which were in the borders near the Red Sea" in [[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:14]], as the party *departs Shazer* (~Wadi Sharma in northwest Arabia). That is the last sea reference in the whole account — about 1,000 miles from Nahom. [^2]

2. **The broken-bow / Nahom account omits the sea entirely.** Across the broken-bow narrative and the Nahom narrative (~1 Nephi 16:17–39), there is no reference to the sea, the borders of the sea, or the shore — and none to "fertile parts." On the contrary, the suffering from hunger and thirst, and Nephi having to climb "to the top of the mountain" to hunt rather than simply slay food "by the way," signal a *more arid* inland environment by that point. [^3]

3. **"South-southeast" is a general bearing, not a coast-hugging mandate.** The skeptic must take the directional statements (south-southeast in 16:13–14; "nearly the same course" in 16:33) to mean the party stayed glued to the coast the whole way. But real travel winds; Rappleye's "I'm going south to Vegas" analogy notes the road can run southwest for stretches. He documents four scholar-attested routes from the coastal side over the mountains to the inland trail, all *trending* south-southeast — so an inland shift is fully consistent with the stated bearing. [^4]

4. **The eastward turn forces an inland position.** [[1 Nephi 17.1|1 Nephi 17:1]] has them travel "nearly eastward from that time forward," and only the inland Frankincense Trail system offers eastward branches (around the Wadi Jawf) leading to a Bountiful candidate. To make that turn at all, Nahom *must* be inland. Even the Hiltons' older coast-then-inland reconstruction ends up turning east in the same approximate area. The burden is on the critic to give a positive argument that Nahom is coastal — which the silent text does not supply. [^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Vogel's case is genuinely the best of the bunch, and Rappleye says so. The text *does* twice tie the journey to "the borders near the Red Sea" and to "fertile parts," and some early scholars *did* first reconstruct a coastal route, only later shifting inland when a pure coast route proved unworkable — which can look like accommodating the data after the fact. A critic can argue the natural inference from "they kept the same course" is continuity along the same coastal corridor, and that the inland turn is read in to reach a predetermined Nahom. Rappleye's reply is essentially evidential silence plus geographic necessity: the text stops mentioning the sea long before Nahom, positively signals aridity at the broken bow, and gives an explicit eastward turn that *only* an inland trail can satisfy — so the inland reading is not imposed but is the only one consistent with both the (silent) text and known Arabian routes. He grants that none of this is forced *if* one refuses to treat First Nephi as a real account of real travel — but then, he notes, there is no point correlating it with geography at all.

## Best Quotes

> "That last mention of the borders near the Red Sea... it's verse 14 when they're departing Shazer... That's a thousand miles away from Nahom right now. That is the last mention of the borders near the Red Sea in the entire account." [^2]

> "You will not find any reference to the sea or the borders of the sea or the shore in those accounts" — i.e., the broken-bow and Nahom narratives. [^3]

> "I would say the text allows for them to have traveled more inland, and the data from ancient Arabia tells us they did travel more inland — if we're taking this seriously even a little bit as an actual account of real people." [^5]
