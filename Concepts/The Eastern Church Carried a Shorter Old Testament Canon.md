# The Eastern Church Carried a Shorter Old Testament Canon

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that the Christian **East** preserved a dominant tradition of a **22-book Hebrew Old Testament** (numbered by the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet) that is *very close to the Protestant canon* and excludes most of the deuterocanon. Far from the Protestant canon being a 16th-century novelty, this shorter list "tends to predominate for some time in the East" across [[Cyril of Jerusalem]], [[Athanasius]], [[Gregory of Nazianzus]], Amphilochius, Epiphanius, and — as late as the 7th/8th century — John of Damascus, much of it flowing out of the Synod of Laodicea. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Orthodox/Catholic reply
The Eastern witness is not cleanly "Protestant." (a) These lists carry **wrinkles** that are not in the Protestant Bible: Baruch and the Letter of Jeremiah folded into Jeremiah, the Greek **additions to Daniel** (Susanna, Bel) retained, Ruth attached to Judges, and Esther sometimes *omitted entirely*. (b) The Eastern Orthodox church to this day **reads and venerates** the broader "anagignoskomena" in its liturgy, so a few patristic enumerations hardly prove the East rejected them. (c) Counting books by the Hebrew alphabet is an inherited Jewish convention, not necessarily a Christian dogmatic boundary. So the East shows *fluidity*, which the wider-canon traditions can claim as easily as the narrower one.

### Ortlund's reply
- **The core 22-book list is genuinely shorter.** Cyril of Jerusalem's *Catechetical Lectures* enumerate exactly these books — "no Tobit, no Judith, no Maccabees, no Wisdom of Solomon" — and Athanasius's 367 festal letter gives a near-identical list. The "majority of the deuterocanonical books are excluded in this Eastern tradition." [^2]
- **Cyril forbids the apocrypha on apostolic grounds.** Cyril tells catechumens to "read the 22 books, but have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings," reads "only" what is read in church, grounds the list in books the apostles and bishops "handed down," and calls the list authoritative ("statutes... trench not upon" them). This is church practice + apostolic tradition + authority, not mere private scholarship. [^3]
- **The wrinkles are conceded but symmetrical.** Ortlund grants the East is "not exactly identical" to the Protestant canon — but notes the *wider* tradition is also not identical to Trent: the 4th-century councils of Hippo/Carthage used a Septuagint **1 Esdras** that Trent later excluded. "So it's simply false that Trent reiterated exactly the earlier tradition." Minor variation is everywhere; it cannot be used selectively against only the shorter list. [^4]
- **The pattern is breadth, not a lone voice.** The same list recurs in Gregory of Nazianzus, his cousin Amphilochius, Epiphanius, and John of Damascus — a *tradition*, not an idiosyncrasy. Combined with [[Jerome]] in the West, the shorter canon has "important early testimonies in its favor." [^5]

## LDS Relevance
Ortlund uses the Eastern data to defeat the *unanimity* claim — that the wider canon was the settled, universal Christian Bible until Protestants pruned it. That demolition serves the LDS open-canon view *negatively*: it confirms there was no monolithic, Spirit-sealed canon in the early centuries, undercutting any tradition's claim to an unbroken received list. But the Eastern witness also pressures the LDS position, because what the East transmits is still a *short, Hebrew-bounded, "closed"* canon defended by appeal to apostolic tradition and liturgical practice — categories closer to the Catholic/Orthodox authority model than to continuing revelation. The East is thus a useful ally against Roman canon claims and a reminder that "the canon was fluid" does not by itself yield "the canon is open."

## Best Quotes
> "In the East you actually have much more strong support for a shorter Old Testament canon... a 22-book Hebrew canon based upon the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The only exceptions... are that Baruch is included as part of Jeremiah, and... sometimes Esther is omitted." [^1]

> "Read the 2-and-20 books, but have nothing to do with the apocryphal writings... for the apostles and the bishops of old time, the presidents of the church who handed down these books." — Cyril of Jerusalem, read by Ortlund [^3]

> "There are also smaller differences between the Old Testament canon accepted by Augustine and the councils of Hippo and Carthage... versus the later Old Testament accepted by the Council of Trent. They weren't identical." [^4]
