# Early Christian Persecution and the Hope for a Better World

## The Point

[[Nicholas J. Frederick]] uses the martyrdom of **Perpetua and Felicitas** to show *why* the literal, premillennial hope had such grip on early Christians: persecution made the longing for a better, redeemed world urgent and concrete. [[Tertullian]]'s warning that the antichrist "is now close at hand and gasping for the blood... of Christians" hints at "the increased persecution Christians were facing," recalling Jesus's prophecy that Christians would be "delivered up and killed" before his return (see [[Matthew 24.30|Matthew 24:9]]). [^1]

## Evidence

1. **Perpetua's defiant confession.** In Carthage (modern Tunisia), the twenty-two-year-old noblewoman Perpetua — newly married, having just given birth — was arrested, tortured, and killed in the arena for refusing to deny Christ. "I cannot be called anything other than what I am, a Christian," she declared. [^1]
2. **Communal solidarity in death.** Saturus, Felicitas, and others died with her: "they were stripped naked, placed in nets and thus brought out into the arena." When a crazed heifer tossed Perpetua and crushed Felicitas, Perpetua "went over to her, gave her her hand, and lifted her up. Then the two stood side by side." [^1]
3. **The rhetorical force of the example.** Frederick's conclusion: "How could Christians not hope for a better world in the face of such torture and tragedy!" [^1]
4. **A note on historicity.** Frederick cautions that "while it has been common in the past to interpret such tales of Christian persecution and martyrdom as historical fact, we should exercise caution in how much historicity we attribute to such stories," citing Candida Moss. [^2]
