# A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity

## The Point
This is the headline claim [[Jacob Hansen]] made when [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] chose him for a one-on-one: "the restored gospel, also known as Mormonism, provides [a] conception of God that is more rationally defensible than any other creedal Christian sect."[^1] The argument is a *finitist theodicy*. Because the restored gospel rejects creation *ex nihilo* and holds that God "creates and operates within a set of constraints" — "we don't believe God can simply make whatever he wants"[^2] — God genuinely *could not* have brought His children (and the created order) to their divine potential by fiat. Exaltation requires becoming "as he is," and that requires freely passing through a real "world of injustice." So the suffering O'Connor calls gratuitous is, on this model, *logically necessary* to God's ends, not a free choice an omnipotent God could have skipped. Hansen's frame: the problem isn't suffering per se but **injustice**, and a world without the *possibility* of injustice is a world in which divine potential is unreachable.

## Evidence
- **Reframing the problem as injustice + necessity.** Hansen distinguishes justified from unjustified suffering with a medicine analogy: suffering to gain a better outcome is justified; "that same medicine for no reason at all" is not.[^3] The real charge to answer is therefore *unnecessary* suffering — and the LDS claim is that this suffering is necessary.
- **The constrained-God core.** "We reject creation from nothing... we believe God creates and operates within a set of constraints, and so because he could not simply make us gods like him, he thus had to create a plan."[^2] This is the same metaphysic the vault develops from the O'Connor/Collins fine-tuning exchange — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and its soteriological edge [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]] — here applied directly to the problem of evil.
- **Premortal consent.** Against O'Connor's "you chose to put them in that world," Hansen invokes the pre-existence: "human beings, knowing the unjust nature of reality, have the choice... they choose to enter into the mortal state."[^4] This is [[Hayden Carroll]]'s "Creation Dilemma" move deployed live — see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]] and [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]].
- **The scriptural engine — opposition in all things (2 Nephi 2).** Hansen says he wished he'd had the text handy: "it must needs be that there is an opposition in all things... if not so... righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness, neither holiness nor misery."[^5] He claims "essentially the entire chapter of [[2 Nephi 2.8|2 Nephi 2]] in the Book of Mormon is an answer to Alex's question," because "the definition of something is created by its contrast against something else."[^6] Rate: **the load-bearing distinctive** — it converts "why must we know injustice?" into a metaphysical necessity of contrast, not a divine preference.
- **O'Connor's "square circle" pushback.** O'Connor repeatedly presses *why* it is logically necessary: "you're just sort of restating that he can't do it, I want to know why... which laws are relevant here?" He doubts that creating "a totally unjust world" is on a par with the law of non-contradiction.[^7] Rate: **the strongest objection in the exchange** — Hansen asserts logical necessity ("I would say that is a logical contradiction") but, by his own later admission, did not fully articulate *why* a free, loving relationship with God could not be had in a world with far less injustice. The burden of showing the *necessity* (not merely the *possibility*) of opposition is real and only partly discharged on tape.
- **The decisive late turn.** Hansen lands the point that creedal Christianity's God *does* create the conditions requiring "the shot," whereas the LDS God "does not create the conditions where the shot is necessary." O'Connor concedes the conditional: "you're correct on creedal Christianity, it does, and that's the biggest flaw with it."[^8] Rate: **a genuine dialectical win on the comparative claim** — O'Connor grants the constrained model escapes an objection that sinks the unconstrained one, even while rejecting theism overall.
- **Steelman the critic.** (1) Aseity cost: a God bound by external eternal law is no longer the absolute ground of being — O'Connor can ask "what grounds the eternal law?" (2) The necessity gap above. (3) Animal suffering, which the consent-and-progression story fits awkwardly (handled in [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]]). Hansen openly accepts the smaller-God trade-off as the price of a coherent theodicy.

## Best Quotes

> "God does not create the conditions where the shot is necessary." / "You're correct, and you're correct on creedal Christianity — it does, and that's the biggest flaw with it." [^8]

## LDS Connection
This is the most explicitly Latter-day Saint argument in the episode, and a textbook deployment of LDS finitist theology: rejection of *ex nihilo*, co-eternal intelligences and matter, a premortal council and consent, theosis ("become as he is"), and 2 Nephi 2's doctrine that opposition is a precondition of any meaningful good. Hansen's strategic claim is comparative — not that theism is proven, but that the *restored* gospel answers O'Connor's problem of evil where *creedal* Christianity (with its unconstrained, omnipotent God) cannot. The companion notes [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]] develop the same metaphysic from a different O'Connor exchange.
