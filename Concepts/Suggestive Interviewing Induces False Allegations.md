# Suggestive Interviewing Induces False Allegations

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"Children don't lie about abuse, so the child testimony in the satanic-panic cases must reflect real events."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Unqualified interviewers, conflict of interest (strong)
[[Kathleen McFarlane]], director at the Children's Institute International, "sold herself as a psychotherapist but lacked any professional licenses." Her practice "ballooned with new patients" as panicked parents arrived — a direct financial incentive to find abuse [^1]. See [[The Satanic Panic Was Driven by Financial and Reputational Incentives]].

### 2. The methods were coercive, not neutral (decisive)
McFarlane invented the "anatomically correct doll" and her interviewing was "highly controversial and highly suggestive": she told each isolated child "what other children had already said happened" and invited children "to pretend or speculate about supposed events" [^2][^3].

### 3. The transcript proof (decisive; verbatim)
Researchers Garven and Wood analyzed the McMartin therapy transcripts and found the therapists' techniques "substantially more effective than simple suggestive questions at inducing preschool children to make false allegations." The McMartin method produced accusations **58% of the time vs. ~17% on average**; "social influence and reinforcement" beat simple suggestion [^4]. A sample exchange: the therapist repeatedly presses a child who denies "naked pictures" — "Why don't you think about that for a while... Your memory might come back to you" [^5].

### 4. The child cannot escape the pressure (strong)
"What if there were no naked pictures? The child is literally incapable of satisfying the pressure of the therapist. Their only way out is to give the therapist what they want" [^6].

### 5. The general science backs it (strong)
Since McMartin, researchers have shown adults and *especially* children "can be coerced into rehearsing false memories after conditioning and coaching" [^7]. See [[False Memory Syndrome Explains the SRA Accusations]].

## Best Quotes
> "The McMartin interrogations produced accusations 58% of the time compared to just 17% on average." [^4]

> "Therapist: 'Why don't you think about that for a while, okay? Your memory might come back to you.' The obvious problem with this method of questioning is, what if there were no naked pictures? The child is literally incapable of satisfying the pressure of the therapist." [^6]

> "McFarland and the therapists... began by telling each isolated child what other children had already said happened. She invited children to pretend or speculate about supposed events." [^3]
