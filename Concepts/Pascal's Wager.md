# Pascal's Wager

## Overview

**Pascal's Wager** is Blaise Pascal's argument that, under uncertainty about God's existence, the rational course is to live as if God exists, because the expected payoff of belief dominates that of unbelief. This is an overview note linking the existing cluster.

## The Claim

The vault's careful framing is that the Wager is **a decision under uncertainty, not a proof**: it does not purport to demonstrate that God exists, but to guide action when the evidence is genuinely uncertain ([[Pascals Wager Is a Decision Under Uncertainty Not a Proof]]). Read as a proof it fails (and invites the many-gods objection); read as decision theory it has real force.

The strongest form is the **dominance** version ([[The Dominance Version of Pascals Wager]]): if one option is at least as good as the alternative under every state of the world and strictly better under at least one, it dominates — and belief is argued to dominate once the payoff matrix is set up honestly. The Wager also surfaces in adjacent arguments about why [[Natural Selection Selects for Religiosity|religiosity is naturally selected]], where wager-like expected-value reasoning appears in the evolutionary account of belief.
