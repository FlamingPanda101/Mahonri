#type/claim

# Objective Meaning Requires a Self-Justifying Non-Contingent Terminus

## The Claim (skeptical)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] defines **meaning as a "reason to act or to be,"** then observes that every reason is *contingent* on a further reason (I reach for the water *because* I'm thirsty *because* of biology *because* of evolution…). Genuinely **objective** meaning would therefore require the chain to terminate in a **non-contingent, self-justifying principle** — a reason such that asking "but why?" becomes inappropriate, "it just is."[^1] The nihilist's wager is that this regress either (a) bottoms out in something "completely arbitrary" (which "feels meaningless… because it could have easily been something else"), or (b) "goes back forever," or (c) terminates in a self-justifying principle that, on inspection, always turns out to be a *subjective preference* (children, art, "big questions," and for the religious, God). Because the felt terminus is preference-dependent — "you don't care about other people's children in the same way" — O'Connor concludes there is no *objective* meaning, only universally-felt subjective meaning.[^2] The argument is striking because it is structurally **the same regress** O'Connor wields for God in [[Horizontal vs Hierarchical Causation - O'Connor's Sustaining-Cause Argument]], now turned on value: meaning, like causal power, must either ground out in something self-existent or evaporate.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Meaning = reason; reasons are contingent.** "To have meaning is to have some kind of purpose. And purpose is something like reason to act or reason to be." But "that reason will be contingent on something else… So to find an objective meaning at the root of life would mean finding a non-contingent reason to act or to be."[^1] *(A clean parallel to contingency arguments — the very move the vault catalogues in [[The Leibnizian Cosmological Argument from Contingency]], now applied to axiology.)*

2. **The "why?" regress and its three exits.** "It will either have to bottom out in something completely arbitrary… or this contingent chain of reasoning just essentially goes back forever, which seems implausible, or it terminates in something which is self-justifying."[^1] *(The trilemma mirrors the cosmological PSR trilemma: brute fact, infinite regress, or necessary being.)*

3. **People do reach a felt terminus — but it's subjective.** "'Why?' 'What do you mean why? That's it, that's what it's about.'" That moment "where that question why just seems inappropriate, that's where you've bottomed out." For most it is "relational… children or spouses"; "for the religious, it will be God… all for the glory of God."[^2] *(He grants the phenomenology of meaning is real and near-universal — an honest concession a theist can build on.)*

4. **Why the religious terminus is "objective" but the secular one isn't — on his telling.** Caring for your children "is dependent on you… you prefer the wellbeing of your children over the wellbeing of other children." For it to be *objective* "would mean that it is insensitive to preference" — true "even for somebody who just had different preferences." Only God (or a built-in *teleology in nature*, which he "struggle[s] to grapple with") could supply a preference-independent terminus.[^2][^3] *(Crucially, O'Connor concedes that theism — or natural teleology — is the kind of thing that* could *ground objective meaning; he denies only that it obtains.)*

5. **The universality of felt meaning "raises eyebrows."** He grants a real argument the *other* way: "there does seem to be this universal compulsion towards meaning," and "it does also seem completely bewildering that we would just exist for absolutely no reason… There needs to literally be a reason why things exist." So "there is an inherent implausibility to nihilism too."[^4] *(O'Connor explicitly holds the case in tension — a model foil who refuses to overstate the nihilist conclusion.)*

## Apologetic Engagement

This is the rare skeptic who *builds the theist's argument for them*. O'Connor concedes (i) objective meaning requires a non-contingent, self-justifying terminus; (ii) a subjective/preference terminus is not objective meaning; and (iii) God is exactly the kind of terminus that *would* be preference-independent. That is the structure of the apologetic meaning-argument, granted on the foil's own terms.

- **Craig's version is the direct counter.** [[The Absurdity of Life Without God]] argues that significance "cannot be answered without first answering 'Does God exist?'" — meaning, value, and purpose all require a transcendent ground. O'Connor's regress *is* Craig's point, reached independently: a self-justifying terminus is precisely what God supplies and naturalism cannot. Where Craig says atheism entails the loss, O'Connor says the loss is *real but he is not certain it obtains* — the gap is his agnosticism, not a disagreement about the logic.
- **The contingency parallel is the strongest engagement.** O'Connor's "non-contingent reason to act" is the axiological twin of the cosmological "necessary being" ([[Why the Necessary Being Must Be God Not the Universe]]). If he accepts a non-contingent *sustaining cause* for *being* (which he does, enthusiastically), consistency presses toward a non-contingent ground for *value* — and a *mind* that intends purposes is a far more natural terminus for *reasons to act* than an impersonal law. The apologetic move is to run his two regresses together: the One who sustains existence is the candidate to ground meaning.
- **Naturalism's terminus self-destructs.** [[Heat Death of the Universe Voids Naturalistic Meaning]] and [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]] press that an *evolutionary* terminus ("you act because natural selection favored it") is exactly the *arbitrary* exit O'Connor calls "meaningless… it could have easily been something else." His own analysis convicts the naturalistic answer.
- **The Restoration's preference-independent terminus.** [[Evolution Points to Family as the Locus of Meaning]] meets O'Connor on his own chosen ground — *children/relationship* — and argues that in LDS theology relational meaning is *not* merely subjective preference but is "baked into the metaphysical structure of reality": eternal sealing, eternal increase, the intrinsic worth of every co-eternal intelligence. That converts O'Connor's strongest *subjective* terminus (family) into an *objective* one, answering his demand that meaning be "insensitive to preference."

**Steelman the foil.** O'Connor's sharpest residual point is the "universally-subjective-is-enough" move: even if meaning is only universally *felt* (like everyone preferring blue), "in practice that sort of works the same as an objective truth." The apologetic burden is to show why a universal *illusion* of meaning is not the same as meaning — that if the felt purpose has "none of the properties meaning requires" (a persisting self, genuine agency, preference-independence), calling it "meaning" is, as the vault puts it, "a semantic trick."

> "To find an objective meaning at the root of life would mean finding a non-contingent reason to act or to be. There would just need to be a self-justifying principle where if you ask 'But why?' the answer… would be it just is."[^1]

> "For there to be an objective meaning would mean that it is insensitive to preference… even for somebody who just had different preferences."[^2]
