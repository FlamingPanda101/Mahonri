# Three Narrative Modes of Christian History

## The Point
Jason R. Combs opens the volume by classifying how Christian history has been told over two thousand years into three broad narrative modes: (1) **the triumph of Christianity**, (2) **the decline of Christianity**, and (3) **the varieties of Christianity**. Each mode is not neutral reporting but an interpretive framework that decides in advance what counts as success, failure, or apostasy. Understanding which mode a given history operates in is prerequisite to evaluating any claim about a "Great Apostasy."

## Evidence
1. **Triumph of Christianity (ancient).** The earliest histories were tales of triumph. The Acts of the Apostles is structured to show the systematic fulfillment of Jesus's words in Acts 1:8 — the gospel preached "first in Jerusalem and Judea (Acts 2–7), then in Samaria (Acts 8), and then to the uttermost part of the earth (Acts 9–28)." Despite persecution, martyrdom, famine, and shipwreck, "the gospel message proceeds triumphant." [^1]
2. **Eusebius as the great triumphalist historian (c. AD 324).** [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] has been called "the first great historian of Christianity." His *Ecclesiastical History* narrates how the Church "triumphed over heretics, apostates, and persecutors" so that "no unhallowed hand can stop the work of the Lord from progressing." Combs cites Eusebius's account (Eccl. Hist. 5.28.1–12) of a Christian led astray into a heretical sect who was chastised by an angel in a night-long dream and repented. This triumphalist mode dominated for the next thousand years. [^2]
3. **Decline of Christianity (Renaissance/Reformation).** The 1400s humanists invented a tripartite periodization — ancient/classical, the medieval "Dark Ages," and the Renaissance/modern era — casting the middle period as barbaric. By the 1500s Protestant Reformers fused this scheme with an ecclesial claim: the Church itself had fallen into darkness and needed restoration. Some dated the loss of original purity to Emperor Constantine (c. AD 300), others to the Christianization of England (c. AD 600–700). [^3]
4. **Varieties of Christianity (modern scholarship).** Today's scholars decline to dismiss whole epochs as corrupt or to adjudicate which form of ancient Christianity is "most true." Instead they "work to understand ancient Christians on their own terms," situating them in their social and cultural contexts. This is the mode the volume adopts. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "The earliest histories of Christianity were tales of triumph." [^1]

> "In Eusebius's narrative, no unhallowed hand can stop the work of the Lord from progressing." [^2]

> "Today, scholars approach the study of ancient Christians in a more positive way. Rather than dismissing entire epochs as corrupt or identifying which forms of ancient Christianity are most true, today we work to understand ancient Christians on their own terms." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The phrase "no unhallowed hand can stop the work" — used here to summarize Eusebius's triumphalism — is itself famous Latter-day Saint language (Joseph Smith / Wentworth Letter), implicitly inviting LDS readers to notice that triumphalist historiography is a temptation their own tradition shares. Combs uses the typology to set up his argument that the Restoration does not require LDS readers to adopt the Reformation-era "decline/apostasy" narrative.
