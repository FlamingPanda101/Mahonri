# Paul Pioneered Mutual Bodily Ownership

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]]: in [[1 Corinthians 7.3-5|1 Corinthians 7]], Paul makes a genuinely revolutionary move — granting the *wife* equal claim over the *husband's* body, inverting the one-directional Greco-Roman norm. "The husband's body does not belong to him; it belongs to his wife." Scrivener reads this as Christianity "thrusting consent into the bedroom."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The reciprocity is the radical part.** In the ancient world, a wife's body belonged to her husband; Paul's symmetrical clause ("likewise the husband has not power of his own body, but the wife") was countercultural.[^1] *(Strong — the mutual-ownership symmetry is a real and notable feature of the text.)*

2. **O'Connor concedes the innovation.** "I am willing to grant that that is genuinely revolutionary and radical and progressive and brilliant for women."[^2] *(Major concession — the dispute is not whether 7:4 is progressive, but whether 7:5 [abstention by mutual consent] yields the *modern* consent concept.)*

3. **The marriage-and-family program.** [[Joseph Henrich]]'s *WEIRDest People in the World* pairs 1 Cor 7 with [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew 19]] as the "Christian sexual revolution" that "upended our ideas of the human person, the individual, and equality." Scrivener: "I don't think you get feminism of the kind we have without this intellectual genealogy."[^3] *(Strong scholarly support [Henrich]; ties to [[The Christian Origin of Equality]].)*

4. **Steelman of O'Connor — it doesn't reach modern consent.** O'Connor agrees 7:4 is brilliant but argues Scrivener "overstates the case": Paul does **not** arrive at the modern principle that "my wife's body belongs to her and she can say no for any reason." Because abstention requires *mutual* consent ([[1 Corinthians 7 Consent Is to Abstain Not to Have Sex|7:5]]), a unilateral refusal is excluded. So Christianity "did a really good thing here, but it hasn't gone all the way — we've needed other factors to come in" for the modern concept of consent.[^4] *(Sharp — distinguishes "mutual ownership/obligation" [Pauline] from "bodily autonomy / unilateral veto" [modern], and these are genuinely different.)*
