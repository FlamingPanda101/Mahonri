# Scientism Cannot Explain the Origin of Information

## The Point
The hinge of the Meyer-Atkins debate is the distinction between **order** and **specified information**. Atkins argues that information needs no intelligent agent: structures emerge spontaneously all the time, and "information be it... the information encoded in DNA is just a kind of structure," produced by the second law of thermodynamics as the universe falls into disorder and throws off "local abatements of chaos."[^1] Meyer's reply is that this equivocates. He grants that energy flow through a system can produce *order* — a vortex in a draining bathtub, a spiral wave — but the sequence-specific, functional information in DNA is not that kind of order at all.[^2] The chemical bonds of the DNA backbone permit *any* base to attach at *any* position, so chemistry does not determine the sequence; the information is therefore not the product of physical or chemical necessity, and self-organization theories (including Prigogine's dissipative structures) fail to account for it.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Atkins' thermodynamic proposal.** "One of my favorite laws of nature is the second law of thermodynamics... it accounts for the emergence of structures and structures include information without an internal agency... those local abatements of chaos constitute information... DNA is a sequence of bases [as] a physical way of carrying that information... it has grown through evolution."[^1]
2. **The order/information equivocation.** Meyer: "structure and information is an equivocation. There are lots of structures that do spontaneously arise through self-organizational processes — if you drain a bathtub you get a nice symmetrical vortex" — but that is not "functionally specified digital information."[^4]
3. **Crick's sequence hypothesis (1957).** The key was not merely the double-helix structure but that along its spine four chemicals function "like alphabetic letters in a written language or digital characters." The *origin of that information* is what has never been explained.[^5]
4. **Chemistry doesn't fix the sequence.** "The chemical bonds that allow for the structure of the molecule as a whole do not determine the sequence of the bases." The glycosidic bonds "allow any one of the four bases to attach... at any place, so there's no physical or chemical determinism that's responsible for the sequencing of the critical information-bearing bases."[^3]
5. **Spontaneous order ≠ information.** Meyer concedes Prigogine-style work shows "energy flow through a system can produce spontaneous order," but "spontaneous order is not the same thing as information" — the order of a vortex is symmetrical and repetitive, whereas DNA exhibits an asymmetrical, aperiodic sequence like a message text.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Spontaneous order is not the same thing as information... the kind of order that you find in a spiral wave or in a vortex is not the same kind of order that [is] in a DNA molecule, which is not order but specified complexity." [^2]

> "There's no physical or chemical determinism that's responsible for the sequencing of the critical information-bearing bases along the spine of the molecule." [^3]

> "Information be it... the information encoded in DNA is just a kind of structure." (Atkins) [^1]

## LDS Connection
The information-from-a-mind inference is congenial to Latter-day Saint natural theology, which is comfortable with a designed, ordered cosmos sustained by intelligence and "light and truth" (D&C 88:6–13). The fit is broad, not specific: the argument concludes only to an "intelligent source," not to the distinctive LDS picture of a God who *organizes* eternal, uncreated matter and intelligences rather than creating from nothing — and ID by design argues for a generic designer, not the embodied, organizing God of the Restoration. The Restoration is also squarely anti-scientism: it pairs knowledge gained "by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118) rather than collapsing all knowing into the lab.
