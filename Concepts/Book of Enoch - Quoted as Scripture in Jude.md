# Book of Enoch — Quoted as Scripture in Jude

## The Claim

The Epistle of Jude directly quotes 1 Enoch 1:9 as authoritative prophecy, referring to "Enoch, the seventh from Adam." This demonstrates that at least one New Testament author — and the community for which he wrote — regarded 1 Enoch as inspired, authoritative, and historical scripture.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Quotation Is Verbatim
> "The Epistle of Jude, that we find in the New Testament, directly quotes from First Enoch and it says 'Enoch the seventh from Adam said' and then directly quotes a passage from First Enoch." [^1]

The quotation in Jude 1:14–15 matches 1 Enoch 1:9 in both the Ethiopic and surviving Greek witnesses. This is not a loose allusion or oral tradition — it is a direct literary quotation from the extant text. The phrase "the seventh from Adam" is a precise Enochic marker drawn from Genesis 5 (Seth's genealogy), distinguishing this Enoch from Cain's son in Genesis 4.

**Evidence rating: primary** — the textual correspondence is unambiguous and universally acknowledged by scholars

### 2. The Author of Jude Uses Prophetic Terminology
Jude introduces the quotation with "prophesied" (προεφήτευσεν, proephēteusen) — the standard verb for canonical prophetic utterance. This indicates the author considered Enoch's words to carry prophetic authority, not merely historical interest. "At least the author of the Epistle of Jude considered it to be authoritative, considered it to be historical." [^1]

**Evidence rating: primary** — the Greek verb is unambiguous

### 3. The Enoch Quotation Is the Centerpiece of Jude's Argument
Jude is not making a passing reference; he builds a theological argument on Enoch's authority. The quotation serves as the climactic proof that judgment comes upon the ungodly — the very point Jude is making about the false teachers infiltrating the church. This is not decoration; it is structural.

**Evidence rating: primary** — observable from the text's rhetorical structure

### 4. Qumran Attestation Confirms 1 Enoch's Pre-Christian Authority
The fact that 1 Enoch was widely copied at Qumran (11 fragmentary manuscripts, outnumbered only by Genesis, Deuteronomy, Isaiah, and Psalms) confirms that it held scriptural-level authority in at least some Second Temple Jewish communities. The author of Jude was not an outlier — he was drawing on a text that was already treated as authoritative in his milieu. [^2]

**Evidence rating: primary** — material evidence from Qumran

## Steelman Objection

**"Jude quoting 1 Enoch does not make 1 Enoch canonical. Paul quotes pagan poets — Epimenides (Titus 1:12) and Aratus (Acts 17:28) — without canonizing them. The inspiration belongs to Jude's epistle, not to his source material. The church, guided by the Holy Spirit in its canonical process, rightly discerned that 1 Enoch, however useful, is not inspired scripture."**

This objection is the standard Christian response and has strong historical precedent. Augustine and Jerome both argued that apostolic citation does not imply canonicity — an inspired author can quote a true statement from any source without elevating that source to scripture.

However, this objection has weaknesses:
1. Jude does not quote Enoch as a "pagan poet" making a true observation — he presents Enoch as a *prophet* who *prophesied*. The category is fundamentally different from Paul's rhetorical use of pagan authors.
2. The objection assumes the post-4th-century canonical boundaries were obvious to 1st-century Christians — a circular argument. The very fact that canonical debates continued for centuries demonstrates the boundaries were not self-evident.

## Related Debate Questions

- If Jude considered 1 Enoch authoritative, on what basis did later Christians overrule an apostle's (or apostolic associate's) judgment?
- Does Jude's use of 1 Enoch strengthen or weaken the case for a fixed, closed canon determined in the apostolic age?
