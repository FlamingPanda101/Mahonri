# Intentionality and Final Causality Require a Mind

## The Point
Turek argues that even if biological evolution were entirely true, God would still be necessary — a point he traces to Aristotle.[^1] *Intentionality* (mind acting toward a goal) and *final causality* (goal-directedness) pervade all of reality, conscious and unconscious: hearts pump, acorns become oaks, electrons orbit, planets keep precise paths.[^2] But nothing can be directed toward an end "unless that end exists in an intellect," and the layers of goal-directed causes cannot regress forever; they terminate in an uncaused "unmoved mover" — a present, sustaining Mind, not a distant past cause.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Aristotle's four causes and the recovery of final causes.** Turek (with Edward Feser) revives Aristotle's *final cause* — the end or purpose for which a thing exists — against the modern denial of purposes. Final causes are "everywhere"; minds act toward goals, "and so does unconscious nature."[^4]
2. **Goal-directedness top to bottom.** In the living world the heart is directed to pump and an embryo to become an adult; in the nonliving world electrons orbit, atoms form specific molecules, planets follow precise orbits. This "regularity is what Aristotle would call 'final causality.'"[^2]
3. **The descending regress to an unmoved mover.** To choose anything your neurons must fire; for them to fire, atoms must act precisely; for atoms to do so, the four fundamental forces must hold. "The layers of explanation can't go on forever." They terminate in an uncaused first cause Aristotle called the "unmoved mover."[^3] This is a "here and now" sustaining cause, traced "downward at the present moment," not backward in time.[^5]
4. **Evolution itself presupposes goal-directedness.** Even granting unguided evolution, mutation and natural selection "rely on the goal-directedness of the material world." Mutations may be random, "but the natural forces that produce the mutations are not random." Atheists cannot even *detect* randomness except "against the backdrop of order and goal-directedness."[^6]
5. **Denying intentionality is self-defeating.** "The only way atheists can get rid of intentionality is to deny it. But that would involve the intention to deny it. So to deny it is to affirm it!" Writing a book for atheism is itself an act of intentionality.[^7]
6. **Final causality grounds science.** Final causality is "more fundamental" than any scientific finding — it "explains why the laws of nature exist in the first place," so more science (which presupposes an ordered, goal-directed reality) cannot explain it away.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "It is impossible for anything to be directed toward an end unless that end exists in an intellect, which directs the thing in question toward it." (Edward Feser, quoted by Turek)[^3]

> "The only way atheists can get rid of intentionality is to deny it. But that would involve the intention to deny it. So to deny it is to affirm it!"[^7]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints can wholeheartedly affirm the core insight — that reality is ordered and goal-directed, and that mind, not blind chance, is its foundation — which resonates with the scriptural conviction that "all things denote there is a God" (see [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]). Honesty requires a real caveat about the *metaphysics*: Turek's conclusion is Aristotle and Aquinas's "unmoved mover," a completely actualized being with "no potential for improvement or change of any kind." The vault elsewhere notes that this classical, immutable, timeless construct is itself a Hellenized reshaping of the biblical God, not the embodied, dynamic Heavenly Father of Restoration theology (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]] and [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]). Latter-day Saints embrace a *sustaining* Mind behind the cosmos while declining the package of pure actuality and changelessness that Aristotelian metaphysics attaches to it.
