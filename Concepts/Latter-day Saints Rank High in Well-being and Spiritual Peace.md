# Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace

## The Claim
On measures of happiness, well-being, spiritual peace, and family-life satisfaction, Latter-day Saints rank at or near the top among U.S. religious groups — consistent with the dose-response benefit of high religious engagement.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Top-tier on spiritual peace (rated: strong, Pew).** On the Pew data, Latter-day Saints are "in a statistical tie with evangelicals, who are the highest, in saying that they feel a deep sense of spiritual peace and well-being."[^1]

2. **High family-life satisfaction (rated: strong, Pew).** They are "one of the highest in saying that their family life is very good or excellent," and LDS millennials are "the highest in these areas as well."[^2]

3. **Tied to high practice rates (rated: moderate, correlational→causal).** Latter-day Saints are highest in weekly attendance, prayer, and scripture study, and also high on well-being indices; combined with longitudinal tracking, "we could infer that there is a causal relationship," since earlier participation predicts later well-being.[^3] See [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]]. *Steelman:* survey self-reports of happiness can reflect social desirability and in-group framing; "we could infer" causation is a measured claim, and even the longitudinal evidence cannot fully rule out selection (happier or better-resourced people may both practice more and report more well-being).

4. **Benefit persists across belief states (rated: moderate).** Whatever one's level of religiosity tends to associate with better mental health, even after leaving. See [[Religiosity Benefits Mental Health Even After Leaving]].[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "They're, if you win, in a statistical tie with evangelicals — who are the highest — in saying that they feel a deep sense of spiritual peace and well-being."[^1]

> "Latter-day Saints are at the top or very near the top in experiencing those things. And again, the higher demand the religion, the more you're going to be deriving benefits of that religion."[^3]

> "The more religious you are earlier tends to relate to increases in well-being across time."[^4]
