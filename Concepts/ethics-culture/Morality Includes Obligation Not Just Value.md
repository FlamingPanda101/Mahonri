# Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] draws a sharp distinction between **value** and **obligation** and argues that morality requires *both*. "Good and bad are concepts of value," and morality includes them — "but it also includes obligation in relation to the good." Morality "does not just say what is good; it says that you are obligated to do what is good and not what is bad." Hence: "Any moral formulation that doesn't include obligation simply isn't describing morality — it's just describing value." [^1] This distinction is the hinge that forces the next question — *what is an "ought"?* — answered in [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]].

## Argument
1. **Value and obligation are different things.** "Good and bad are concepts of value." A theory can rank states of affairs as better or worse without yet saying anyone *must* pursue them. *(conceptual distinction)* [^1]
2. **Morality adds obligation on top of value.** "Morality includes value… but it also includes obligation in relation to the good. Morality does not just say what is good; it says that you are obligated to do what is good." *(definitional)* [^1]
3. **Without obligation, you have not described morality at all.** "Any moral formulation that doesn't include obligation simply isn't describing morality — it's just describing value." Moral language "describes what a person ought or ought not do, what they should or should not do." *(test for adequacy)* [^1]
4. **This shifts the burden to analyzing the "ought."** Having located the distinctive ingredient — obligation — the live question becomes "what is this notion of the ought or obligation?", which the video answers directionally. *(setup)* [^1]

**Steelman of the critic:** A non-cognitivist or a value-theorist could grant the value/obligation distinction but deny that Hansen's framework can *generate* the obligation half. Describing what conduces to well-being (value) does not by itself yield a categorical "you must" (obligation); that is the classic is–ought / open-question worry. Hansen's eventual move is to make the ought *hypothetical* ("if you desire this end, you ought…"), which arguably re-describes obligation as mere instrumental necessity rather than the binding, categorical force most people mean by "moral obligation." So a critic may say he has smuggled the harder notion (categorical ought) out and replaced it with an easier one (means–end ought). See [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]].
