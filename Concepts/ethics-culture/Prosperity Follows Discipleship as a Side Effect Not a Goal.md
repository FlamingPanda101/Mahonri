# Prosperity Follows Discipleship as a Side Effect Not a Goal

## The Claim

[[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Jonah Barnes]] distinguish the Latter-day Saint pattern — that temporal prosperity *follows* discipleship as a **side effect** of seeking God and serving others — from the **prosperity gospel** (e.g., televangelists) that makes money the *goal*. They also rebut the inverse charge that tithing *impoverishes* members and merely enriches a greedy institution.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Seek ye first the kingdom" — prosperity is a byproduct — rate: moderate (scriptural framing)
The Book of Mormon promise "keep the commandments and ye shall prosper in the land" appears as a *side benefit*, never the aim; the goal is serving God and neighbor, with temporal blessing following ("seek ye first the kingdom of heaven, and all these things shall be added"). The hosts contrast this with prosperity preachers (Kenneth Copeland's "put your hand on the television") whose focus *is* the money — which they read as priestcraft. See [[Church Wealth and Welfare Are Evidence Not Scandal]].[^2]

### 2. The "tithing makes you poor" charge is backwards — rate: moderate (rebuttal)
Against the claim (attributed to John Dehlin) that the Church "takes your tithing" to keep members poor and enrich "LDS Inc.," the hosts note the Church frequently *spends more* supporting struggling members (rent, groceries) than those members tithe, runs a **self-reliance program**, and — per the "Light and Truth Letter" — could fund itself in perpetuity on its reserves' interest alone, so it does not *need* members' tithing for income. The point of tithing is "changing your hearts," with temporal stability following. See [[Mormonism as a Sociological Cure for Poverty]].[^3]

### 3. Tithing is a fixed, modest discipline — rate: weak/illustrative
The hosts frame the "strictness" Cowen praises as basic healthy living: 10% tithing, fidelity, sobriety, not wasting money — a small, predictable discipline whose *cumulative* effect is financial stability, not deprivation.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The distinction is real but not airtight. (a) The line between "prosperity as side-effect" and "prosperity gospel" can blur in practice: a tradition that repeatedly promises "keep the commandments and prosper" and points to members' upward mobility as validation is, functionally, *also* linking faith to wealth — critics argue the difference from Copeland is one of degree and decorum, not kind. (b) The "tithing doesn't impoverish because the Church helps the needy" reply does not address the member who is *not* in crisis: a struggling family still pays 10% off the *top* (gross, before other needs), which can be a real burden regardless of the institution's reserves — and "we could fund ourselves on interest alone" arguably *sharpens* the critic's question of why tithing is still required. (c) Pointing to John Dehlin as the foil risks attacking the messenger rather than the strongest form of the financial-burden argument. (d) The self-reliance and welfare programs are genuine goods, but their existence does not by itself refute the claim that tithing is regressive for low earners. So the defensible core is theological and motivational — Latter-day Saint teaching *frames* prosperity as a fruit of discipleship rather than its purpose, distinguishing it from money-centered prosperity preaching — while the *distributional* fairness of a gross-income tithe on the poor, and the family-budget cost of the discipline, remain legitimate questions the "it changes your heart" answer does not fully settle.
