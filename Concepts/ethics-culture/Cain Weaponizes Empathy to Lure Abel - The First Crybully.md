# Cain Weaponizes Empathy to Lure Abel - The First Crybully

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] (citing host [[Cardon Ellis]]) argues that the apocryphal account of how Cain lured the wary Abel down to the field is the **first recorded "weaponization of empathy"**: Cain, already plotting murder and cast out for it, feigns brotherly *need* — "come bless my fields and my flocks… for you are righteous and I love you much, oh my brother, but you have alienated yourself from me" — exploiting Abel's godly compassion to draw him to the kill-site. Barnes quotes Cardon by name in his book on this point: "this is the first instance of what LDS filmmaker Cardon Ellis calls the weaponization of empathy."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The lure is an appeal to empathy and grievance — rate: moderate (textual, apocryphal)
In the apocryphal tradition Abel has been warned to stay on the holy mountain; Cain must coax him down. He does it not by force but by feigned vulnerability and an inversion of blame — *you've alienated yourself from me; won't you come bless my flocks?* As a righteous man, "Abel feels it's his duty to serve his brother… and Cain is abusing that."[^1] The structure — aggressor poses as the wounded party to disarm the good — is the "crybully" pattern.

### 2. The blame-inversion matches a recognizable rhetorical move — rate: moderate (analogy, the note's interpretive payload)
Cardon and Barnes map the "you've alienated yourself from me / why are you so mean to me?" move onto a contemporary rhetorical tactic: the party in the wrong reframes accountability as the victim's intolerance ("you don't want equity… you must *hate* X"). The structural point — *grievance deployed offensively to extract compliance from the conscientious* — is the same whether the target is ancient Abel or a modern interlocutor. This is the moral-psychology cousin of [[Empathy Does Not Scale - The Identifiable Victim Effect]], which treats empathy's exploitability from the cognitive-bias side.

### 3. The irony: Cain is the one under restraining order — rate: moderate (narrative)
The bite of the reading is that Cain has *already* been expelled for plotting murder, yet casts himself as the ostracized victim. Barnes' gloss: "did you threaten to burn down the church? Is that why there's a restraining order? …don't pretend like it's us." The aggressor's self-pity is the tell.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is a *homiletic application*, not an exegetical result, and it should be held loosely. (a) **The text is late and non-canonical.** The "bless my flocks" lure comes from the Adam-and-Eve apocrypha (Egyptian Life of Adam and Eve ch. 78, Cave of Treasures), not Genesis, so "the first weaponization of empathy" is a reading of a 4th–7th-century legend, not a datum about the historical Cain. (b) **"Weaponization of empathy" is a contested polemical frame.** Critics of the phrase argue it is often used to *dismiss* legitimate moral appeals by recasting compassion itself as manipulation; importing today's culture-war vocabulary into Genesis risks anachronism and motivated reading. (c) **Empathy is a virtue the same texts commend.** Abel's compassion is presented as *righteous*; the lesson cannot be "beware empathy" but at most "beware deceivers who exploit it" — a narrower and less novel claim. The disciplined version: the apocryphal Cain illustrates that genuine virtue can be *exploited* by bad actors posing as victims — a timeless caution — without licensing a general suspicion of compassion.
