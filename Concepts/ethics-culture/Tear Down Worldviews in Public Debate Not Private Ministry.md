# Tear Down Worldviews in Public Debate Not Private Ministry

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] attaches an *ethical* governor to the powerful method of exposing a worldview's inconsistencies (see [[Bahnsen's Method - Arbitrariness, Presuppositions, Inconsistency]]). Because "logically tearing up another person's worldview usually does more harm than good," it "should be avoided" in private, one-on-one ministry. But in public advocacy or debate "where there is an audience watching," a "calm, civil, and unrelenting deconstruction" of a misguided worldview through strategic questioning "may be exactly what is needed."[^1] The governing principle is Christ's counsel to be "wise as serpents but harmless as doves" — pairing intellectual rigor with charity, deploying the sharp tool only where it edifies an audience rather than wounding an individual.[^2] This is the charitable-but-critical posture applied to apologetic combat (compare [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]]).

## Evidence
1. **The tool is genuinely powerful — and dangerous.** "This sort of critical logical analysis of another person's worldview can be incredibly powerful in ripping apart people's bad ideas... asking people how they resolve obvious contradictions in their views can really tie them into knots, and usually they get quite annoyed by this."[^1]
2. **Private vs. public distinction.** "In matters of private ministry, logically tearing up another person's worldview usually does more harm than good and therefore should be avoided. But on the other hand, in public advocacy or in public debate where there is an audience watching..."[^1]
3. **The Christlike constraint.** "Christ wanted us to be wise as serpents but harmless as doves."[^2]
4. **Believers play too much defense.** Hansen argues Believers "spend so much time defending our beliefs that we never take a critical lens to the alternative worldviews on offer," and should be willing to examine rivals — while granting that "the believing worldview has its challenges."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "In matters of private ministry, logically tearing up another person's worldview usually does more harm than good and therefore should be avoided. But on the other hand, in public advocacy or in public debate where there is an audience watching, a calm, civil, and unrelenting deconstruction of a person's misguided worldview using strategic questioning may be exactly what is needed." [^1]

> "Christ wanted us to be wise as serpents but harmless as doves. I really think that believers play too much defense." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is a practical ethic of apologetics rooted in charity: the same critic-engaging method that exposes [[Bill Reel's Free-Will Double Standard - Determinism Cannot Ground Moral Blame|a critic's double standard]] is restrained out of love when the only "audience" is the person being questioned. The Latter-day Saint mandate to teach "with all long suffering" and to reprove "betimes with sharpness... showing forth afterwards an increase of love" (D&C 121:43) maps onto Hansen's public/private distinction — boldness calibrated to context, never contention for its own sake (3 Nephi 11:29). The aim is not to win but to "build the kingdom," which means knowing when a teardown edifies onlookers and when it merely "ties someone into knots." See also [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]].
