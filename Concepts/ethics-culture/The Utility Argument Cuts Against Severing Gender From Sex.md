# The Utility Argument Cuts Against Severing Gender From Sex

## The Claim
[[Steven Woodford]] holds there is "no such thing as a correct definition" — definitions are judged only by *utility* — and argues that treating "Jane" (a CAIS person) as a man would be cruel and "untenable," so the more useful move is to count her a woman by gender while granting her sex is male. [[Trent Horn]] turns the utility test against him. Even granting that definitions are judged by usefulness, "one could still show that adult human female is a far more useful definition of woman than any other competing definition." Woodford only weighs the *downsides* of Walsh's definition (the difficulty for someone like Jane) while ignoring the far greater downsides of severing gender from sex: admitting biological males with few or no female characteristics into women's locker rooms, prisons, and sports. "Even if we looked at this from a strictly utilitarian perspective, the classical view of sex wins out."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, internal critique)** Horn grants Woodford's framework *arguendo* and shows it backfires: the comparison must weigh *both* columns, and the harms of an open-ended "woman" definition (safety, privacy, fairness) dwarf the harm of telling a rare intersex person an unwelcome truth.[^1]
2. **(Strong, the gap)** Jane's case does nothing to license the broader claim, because Jane has *many* female characteristics while most transgender women have *none* — "this definition does absolutely nothing to show that most transgender women are women."[^2] So the compassionate intersex case is not generalizable to the policy conclusion Woodford wants.
3. **(Moderate, the ledger of harms)** Horn enumerates the costs the spectrum view imposes: children scarred by "psychological, chemical, and surgical mutilation," female athletes who lose to higher-bone-density competitors, and women who "live in fear of someone with a penis invading their personal space."[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The strongest utilitarian case for Woodford is that the population of intersex and transgender people who suffer under a strict definition is real and chronically harmed (discrimination, mental-health burdens), whereas the feared harms (sports, locker rooms) are statistically rare or policy-addressable without redefining "woman" wholesale. A consequentialist could argue that a flexible, identity-respecting definition maximizes well-being across the affected population, and that "fairness in sport" and "safety in prisons" are downstream policy problems solvable case-by-case rather than reasons to deny the social reality of trans women.

## Counter-objection
The utilitarian ledger does not favor severing gender from sex once *both* sides are tallied. First, the move generalizes badly: a definition crafted to be kind to Jane (who has female anatomy) is then extended to people with no female characteristics, so the "compassion" rationale and the policy payoff come apart — you cannot get from "be kind to the intersex person" to "any self-identified man may enter women's spaces" without a separate argument the case does not supply. Second, the harms on the other side are neither rare nor cleanly policy-addressable: they fall on entire classes (female athletes, incarcerated women, children subjected to irreversible interventions) and are *caused by* the redefinition, not incidental to it. Third — and decisively for a non-consequentialist — *utility was Woodford's own chosen metric*; Horn need not defend consequentialism to win, only show that on Woodford's terms the classical definition scores better. And once the definition has no non-arbitrary threshold, it collapses into self-identification, which is *maximally* dis-useful for any purpose that needs the category to track reproductive biology (medicine, sport, safeguarding) ([[Boundary Cases Do Not Dissolve the Sex Binary - The Fallacy of the Beard]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
A shared natural-law point. The effective engagement is to (a) accept the utility frame *for the sake of argument*, (b) insist that *both* harm-columns be weighed, and (c) note that the intersex sympathy case does not reach the trans-inclusion conclusion. This is the practical-stakes companion to the metaphysical arguments in [[Intersex Conditions Do Not Prove Gender Is Separate From Sex]] and [[Appearance Is a Defeasible Guide to Sex Not Its Definition]].

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration's stake in stable, embodied sex and the protection of women's distinctive goods aligns with Horn's ledger — see [[Male and Female Balance Is Required for the Divine Image and Human Flourishing]]. More broadly, the note models the vault's preferred tactic of *meeting a relativist on his own ground*: just as a moral relativist's framework refutes itself ([[Subjectivism Refutes Itself]], [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]), a "definitions are mere utility" framework can be turned to favor the very definition it was meant to unseat.
