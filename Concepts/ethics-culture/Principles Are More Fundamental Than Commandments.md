# Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s central distinction is that **specific commandments are time- and place-bound expressions of deeper, more fundamental principles**. "Specific commandments are really just expressions of deeper principles and values given to a particular person or group of people at a particular time"; it is "these deeper principles that make up the building blocks of the algorithm and ultimately are more fundamental than any particular commandment given in a particular time and place." [^1] A commandment ("thou shalt not lie") is a surface application of a principle (honesty); the principle is the durable thing. Crucially, Hansen argues this is "the moral model that Jesus taught as he rejected the law-based moral legalism of his day." [^2] This is the engine behind [[Algorithmic Morality - Principles as Code Converting Situations to Right Action]] and what makes [[A Value Hierarchy Resolves Conflicts Between Moral Principles]] possible.

## Evidence
- **Principles are "concentrated truth packaged for application."** "As you seek spiritual knowledge, carefully search for principles. Separate them from the detail used to explain them. Principles are concentrated truth packaged for application to a variety of circumstance. A true principle makes decisions clear even under the most confusing and compelling circumstances" — an apostolic teaching Hansen reads but does not name in the video. [^3]
- **Jesus reaches past the commandment to the principle.** On murder, Jesus moves from "thou shalt not kill" to the deeper principle: "whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment" (Matthew 5:21-22). On the Sabbath, against the ruler of the synagogue's legalism, He defends loosing "a daughter of Abraham whom Satan hath bound, lo these eighteen years" (Luke 13:14-16). "Jesus' ultimate concern is with the principle behind the commandment." [^2][^4]
- **The Pharisees' error was confusing the two.** "The confusion about the differences between principles and commandments was the essence of Jesus' message to the Pharisees, who had confused and distorted what values were higher in the hierarchy because they were obsessed with the universal application of commandments. They placed a higher value on the letter of the law over the spirit of the law." [^5]
- **C.S. Lewis: Christianity gives principles, not a program.** [[C.S. Lewis]] (from *Mere Christianity*'s "Social Morality"): Christianity "has not and does not profess to have a detailed political program for applying 'do as you would be done by' to a particular society at a particular moment... It is meant for all men at all times, and the particular program which is suited to one place or time would not suit another... When it tells you to feed the hungry it does not give you lessons in cookery." [^6]
- **Latter-day prophets on the permanence of principles.** [[Wilford Woodruff]]: the principles of "the salvation and exaltation of the children of men are principles you cannot annihilate... beyond the reach of man to handle or to destroy." A second (unattributed) apostolic teaching contrasts changeable "organization, programs, procedures, policies" with unchanging principles: "procedures, programs, the administrative policies, even some patterns of organization are subject to change... but the principles never change." [^7][^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Specific commandments are really just expressions of deeper principles and values given to a particular person or group of people at a particular time. However it is these deeper principles that... are more fundamental than any particular commandment given in a particular time and place." [^1]

> "Notice how Jesus' ultimate concern is with the principle behind the commandment." [^2]

> "They placed a higher value on the letter of the law over the spirit of the law." [^5]

> "When it tells you to feed the hungry it does not give you lessons in cookery." (Lewis) [^6]

## LDS Connection
This is a sharply Latter-day Saint reading of the Sermon on the Mount: the Restoration's "spirit over letter" hermeneutic treats the gospel's *principles* (charity, honesty, the law of love) as eternal, while the *commandments and programs* that express them are adapted "to a particular person or group of people at a particular time." It dovetails with the restored gospel's rejection of divine-command voluntarism — commandments are not arbitrary fiat but applications of eternal law that God Himself embodies ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]; [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]) — and with the LDS emphasis on living truth over mere credal correctness ([[Orthopraxy over Orthodoxy in LDS Salvation]]). The danger Hansen flags in the Pharisees — elevating the letter and "the universal application of commandments" over the principle — is the same warning latter-day teaching gives against checklist religiosity that forgets the heart of the law.
