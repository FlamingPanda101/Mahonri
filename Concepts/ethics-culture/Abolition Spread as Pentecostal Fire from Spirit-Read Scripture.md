# Abolition Spread as Pentecostal Fire from Spirit-Read Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] gives a specific *mechanism* for the second abolition of slavery: a radical-Protestant hermeneutic — "the Spirit descends on you and enables you to read scripture as it's meant to be understood" — let Quakers and evangelicals declare slavery wrong *even though the Bible nowhere says it is*. This conviction "spread like wildfire" (conceptualized by Christians as Pentecostal fire), birthed the first mass activist movement, and ultimately generated **international law** as a framework of principles transcending confessional lines — later turned against the Muslim slave trade.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Bible never says slavery is wrong — so the verdict came from the Spirit, not the page.** Holland is emphatic: "nowhere in the Bible does it say that slavery is wrong"; slavery is taken for granted as part of the human condition. Radical Protestants in the 18th–early 19th centuries said "it may not say this, but I feel the Spirit is telling me slavery is wrong." This makes abolition a *theological* development driven by a particular doctrine of Spirit-illumined reading, not a plain proof-text. See [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]].[^2] *(Strong — Holland concedes the proof-text problem and locates abolition in hermeneutics + conscience.)*
2. **Industrialization + radical Protestantism = the conjunction.** The English Civil-War/Commonwealth tradition of Spirit-reading was transported to the Caribbean and American colonies just as *plantation* slavery industrialized human exploitation. The collision of intensified cruelty with the "spirit-tells-me-it's-wrong" conviction lit the fuse; Quakers and evangelical Anglicans led.[^2] *(Plausible historical synthesis.)*
3. **The first activist movement and Vienna.** It produced petition campaigns and street demonstrations in London demanding abolition — so "unignorable" that at the 1814–15 Congress of Vienna, Lord Castlereagh had to press the powers to abolish the slave trade. Catholic powers, unmoved by Protestant arguments, drew instead on the [[Bartolome de las Casas|Las Casas]] tradition; the radicalism of the French Revolution (which had abolished then re-imposed slavery under [[Napoleon]]) added a third stream.[^3] *(Documented; the three-stream account is a clean synthesis.)*
4. **Three streams pool into international law.** Protestant, Catholic, and Enlightenment-revolutionary traditions — all "bred of the marrow of Christian Europe" — pooled to create the *concept of international law*: principles that transcend religious doctrine, so a Protestant and a Catholic can equally accept them. This is what gave the Royal Navy a legal rubric to stop Portuguese/Spanish slave ships and, in the 19th century, to confront the **Muslim** slave trade — which required Muslim powers to accept the primacy of international law over a God-given legal corpus, "a massive deal."[^4] *(A strong, distinctive thesis about the Christian genealogy of international law.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The materialist counter (voiced by Holland's frequent foil Alex O'Connor elsewhere, and a live position among historians): abolition tracks *economic* conditions — slavery became less profitable as industrial wage-labor rose — and Christianity supplied the *rhetoric* after the fact, not the cause; moreover Christians defended slavery "just as strongly" from the same Bible, so scripture under-determines the outcome. Holland partly grants this (the Bible's silence, the reversibility of French abolition) but argues the *direction* of moral travel and the activist energy are unmistakably Christian in origin — and crucially, the standard by which we now condemn slavery as a unique evil is itself a Christian inheritance (see [[Wokeness Is a Christianity That Jettisoned Its Roots]]). The deeper rebuttal: if abolition were merely economic, it would not have been framed, fought, and felt as a *sin* to be expiated.

## LDS Relevance
Latter-day Saints inherit this abolitionist moral grammar while carrying their own complicated 19th-century racial history (the priesthood-and-temple restriction, lifted 1978). Holland's argument is double-edged but usable: it warns against reading present moral consensus back as timeless obvious truth (the Bible's own silence on slavery shows how *developed* the abolitionist conscience is), which is exactly the charitable-historiography move LDS apologists urge when critics judge past leaders by present standards. It also undercuts the "Christianity is just a slavery religion" polemic by showing Christianity as the engine of slavery's abolition and the seedbed of international human-rights law.
