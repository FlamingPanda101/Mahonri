# The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues there is exactly *one* law of morality — the **law of love** — and that this answers both the Calvinist charge that "the LDS God is subject to law" and the metaethical grounding question. Love is "the single law of morality... the basis of morality," and it is defined not by God's fiat but by what love intrinsically *is*. Christ taught it ([[John 13.34|"a new commandment I give unto you, that ye love one another"]]; [[Matthew 22.37-39|love God and neighbor]]) and *modeled* it rather than decreeing it. [^1] See [[Divine Command Theory Is Metaethics Not Ethics]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Love is a brute, unalterable reality** — rate: the metaethical foundation; honest about its primitiveness. "Love is what it is. God could not define love as killing little children just for the fun of it and it still be loving. Something doesn't become loving simply because God says so." When pressed whether this is "a brute fact," Ostler grounds it in relationality: "the greatest good in the universe is love." *(metaethical)* [^2]
2. **A functional definition** — rate: usable, consequence-flavored. Love is "anything that fosters, heals, creates, brings about the flourishing of relationships," and evil is whatever "destroys relationships... makes us sick in our relationships." This gives a content-full criterion divine command theory lacks. *(definitional)* [^3]
3. **Love is learned and grows** — rate: experiential support. "I have more capacity to love my wife now after 47 years of marriage than I did when I first knew her... I learned how to love from her." Capacity for love is "a growing reality about us." *(experiential)* [^4]
4. **Christ grounds it relationally, not by command** — rate: exegetical. "He didn't say, 'You should love each other because I command you to.' ...Love me the way the Father loved me... I'm the guide here. Watch me." Love "inheres in the relationship between us and God." *(scriptural)* [^5] See [[Love Transcends Moral Obligation]].
5. **Why it works ontologically in Mormonism** — rate: distinctive metaphysics. Because persons are "eternal uncreated beings... intelligences" who "exist in the eternal relationality," flourishing (of persons, communities, nations, the world) just *is* the love given to one another. *(theological)* [^6] See [[Uncreated Intelligences Make Free Love Possible]].

**Steelman of the critic:** A divine command theorist or natural-law theorist replies that "love" is too thin to *ground* morality without smuggling in prior moral content — "fostering flourishing" presupposes we already know which states count as flourishing and which relationships are worth fostering (a torturer and victim have a "relationship"). Calling love a "brute fact" arguably just relocates the Euthyphro problem (why is *love* the standard rather than, say, justice or holiness?). Ostler's answer is that love is self-evidencing in action ("you'll know it because it's what will make your relationships flourish") and that no grounding chain avoids terminating in a brute given — see [[Love Transcends Moral Obligation]].

## Best Quotes
> "The law of love is the single law of morality. It is the basis of morality. It's not independent of God because Godhood is totally dependent on God being loving." [^1]

> "Love is what it is. God could not define love as killing little children just for the fun of it and it still be loving. Something doesn't become loving simply because God says so." [^2]

> "We can define love as anything that fosters, heals, creates, brings about the flourishing of relationships." [^3]

> "He didn't teach this is love because he commanded it... I'm the guide here. Watch me. And you'll know it because it's what will make your relationships flourish." [^5]
