# Objective Morality Is Known Through Moral Experience

## The Point
Craig's second premise — *objective moral values and duties do exist* — is defended not by a further argument but by appeal to experience that is properly basic. "Just as our sense experience convinces us that the physical world is objectively real, our moral experience convinces us that moral values are objectively real."[^1] We do not infer the external world from prior premises; we simply perceive it and are rational to trust the perception absent a defeater. Moral perception works the same way: the felt wrongness of cruelty is a direct datum, so the burden falls on the skeptic to overturn what we plainly apprehend.

## Evidence
The everyday evidence is moral indignation: "Every time you say hey that's not fair! That's wrong; that's an injustice! You affirm your belief in the existence of objective morals."[^2] Craig points to acts whose wrongness is not seriously in doubt — "we're well aware that child-abuse, racial discrimination, and terrorism are wrong, for everybody, always" — and asks whether this is "just a personal preference or opinion? No."[^3]

The clinching analogy compares moral error to mathematical error: "The man who says that it is morally acceptable to rape little children is just as mistaken as the man who says two plus two equals five."[^4] The point is epistemic confidence — we are *more* sure that such acts are wrong than we could be of any premise marshalled to deny objective morality. From this premise plus the first, the argument closes: "If God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist. But objective moral values and duties do exist. Therefore, God exists."[^5]

## LDS Connection
This properly-basic defense of the second premise dovetails with LDS theology's account of *how* every person has moral knowledge: the Light of Christ "giveth light to every man that cometh into the world" (cf. D&C 84:46), so the universal moral experience Craig appeals to is, on the Restoration view, the inward witness of that light rather than mere evolutionary conditioning. It also reinforces the related grounding cluster: knowing morality (epistemology) is distinct from grounding it (ontology), and the atheist who feels the wrongness of injustice is drawing on a reality his worldview cannot supply ([[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]; [[Borrowing Objective Morality to Condemn Evil]]). The "rape is as mistaken as 2+2=5" move parallels the apologetic point that moral skepticism rests on premises less certain than the moral facts themselves (cf. [[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]]), and the values it knows are grounded where premise one locates them ([[Moral Values From God's Nature, Moral Duties From God's Commands]]).
