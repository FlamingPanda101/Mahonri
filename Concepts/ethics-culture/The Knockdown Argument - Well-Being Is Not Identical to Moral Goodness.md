# The Knockdown Argument - Well-Being Is Not Identical to Moral Goodness

## The Point
Craig's most technical move in the debate is a **modal argument** that Harris's identity claim — that the property *being good* just *is* the property *creaturely flourishing* — is logically false. Identity is a *necessary* relation: "there is no possible world in which some entity A is not identical to A. So if there's any possible world in which A is not identical to B, then it follows that A is not in fact identical to B."[^1] Craig then supplies the possible world from Harris's own book: on the next-to-last page Harris concedes that if rapists, liars, and thieves could be "just as happy as good people," his moral landscape "would no longer be a moral landscape" but merely "a continuum of well-being whose peaks are occupied by good and bad people alike."[^2] Harris also reports that roughly three million Americans are psychopathic and can enjoy inflicting pain. So there is a *conceivable world* in which the peaks of well-being are occupied by evil people — meaning well-being and moral goodness come apart *there*, and therefore (since identity is necessary) they are *not* identical in the actual world either.[^3] Craig: "it's not often in philosophy that you get a knockdown argument against a position, but I think we've got one here."[^3]

## Evidence
The argument targets the strong metaphysical thesis behind [[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]] and the [[The Moral Landscape Metaphor - Peaks and Valleys of Well-Being]] — not merely that well-being *correlates with* or *constitutes evidence for* goodness, but that the two are the *very same property*. If they were identical, no possible scenario could separate them; Harris's own admission that they *could* separate (evil people on the peaks) is, on Craig's reading, fatal. Craig frames it as the decisive support for his larger thesis: "on atheism there's just no reason to identify the well-being of conscious creatures with moral goodness."[^4] Notably, Harris **never directly answers** this argument in the debate — Craig repeatedly points out that "none of these have been responded to," listing the knockdown argument alongside the is/ought and ought-implies-can problems.[^5]

**Assessing it.** The argument is strongest against Harris's *identity* formulation specifically. A defender of Harris might retreat from strict property-identity to a weaker *constitution* or *supervenience* claim (well-being is what goodness consists in, without being a synonym), which the modal argument would not touch — but that retreat concedes Craig's underlying point in [[Redefining Good as Well-Being - The Stipulation Objection]]: that the move from "is" (well-being) to "ought" (goodness) is not a definitional identity and so still needs a bridge ([[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]]). The conceivability of evil-on-the-peaks is exactly the "open question" — that some flourishing lives are *not good* — which a pure naturalist reduction cannot rule out.

## Best Quotes
> "Since it's possible that human well-being and moral goodness are not identical, it follows necessarily that human well-being and goodness are not the same as Dr. Harris has asserted in his book." [^1]

> "The peaks of well-being could be occupied by evil people. But that entails that in the actual world, the continuum of well-being and the moral landscape are not identical either. For identity is a necessary relation." [^3]

> "By granting that it's possible that the continuum of well-being is not identical to the moral landscape, Dr. Harris's view becomes logically incoherent." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The Latter-day Saint shares Craig's conclusion that flourishing and goodness, while deeply intertwined, are not *identical* — and locates the difference in an objective moral order that outranks subjective well-being. On the Restoration view the good is fixed by **eternal law** that God honors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]; [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]), so a flourishing-but-wicked life can be genuinely *contra* the moral order even if it tops the well-being curve — precisely the "evil people on the peaks" case. This also clarifies a real point of LDS agreement *with* Harris: ultimate human flourishing and goodness do finally coincide, because the eternal law is calibrated to the true end (the "joy" of [[2 Nephi 2|2 Nephi 2:25]]) — but they coincide *because* goodness grounds and orders well-being, not because the two are the same property (cf. the LDS teaching that "men are, that they might have joy" — 2 Nephi 2:25). Craig's modal argument is thus a useful tool for showing that any purely naturalistic reduction of goodness to a brain-state continuum cannot be the *foundation* of morality, only at best a downstream measure of it ([[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]).
