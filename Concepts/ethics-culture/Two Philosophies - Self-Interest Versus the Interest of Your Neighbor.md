# Two Philosophies - Self-Interest Versus the Interest of Your Neighbor

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]] reduces "all the scriptures" to a single recurring choice present "since the Council in Heaven": you either *prosper by managing the creature* (the philosophy of Korihor — "you prosper because of the management of the creature") or you *seek the interest of your neighbor* (the philosophy of Jehovah in the Doctrine and Covenants). Every small daily decision instantiates one or the other, and that choice is what makes one "a humanitarian… a disciple… a Christian — or… just worried about myself."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The two-philosophy frame (strong, scriptural).** Eubank explicitly contrasts Korihor's creed (Alma 30) with the Lord's "let every man esteem his brother as himself, and… every man seek the interest of his neighbour" (D&C 82:19), reading the whole canon as the working-out of these two options.[^1]
2. **The rich fool as the self-interest endpoint (strong).** She invokes [[Luke 12.16-21|Luke 12:16-21]]: the man stored up goods "for himself," planned to "live an easy life," and died "that very night." "What was all that for? Nothing." Self-management terminates in emptiness.[^2]
3. **Choice is granular, not heroic (interpretive).** "Every day in tiny little decisions, we make those choices." The two philosophies are decided in ordinary moments, which links this concept to the doctrine of small acts (see [[Small Acts Are the Only Thing We Have]]).[^1]
4. **Cost to self is built in (moderate).** Seeking the neighbor's interest is "even at the cost of myself" — the philosophy is sacrificial, not merely enlightened self-interest dressed up.[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A defender of rational self-interest (Smithian or Randian) would reject the binary: well-ordered self-interest, channeled through markets and voluntary exchange, has lifted more people from poverty than charitable altruism ever has, and "seeking your neighbor's interest at the cost of yourself" can be both economically naive and personally unsustainable (the burnout of the chronic giver). Framing self-concern as the Korihor-pole demonizes the ordinary, legitimate care for oneself and one's family that makes any giving possible.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank is not condemning self-care or productive enterprise — elsewhere she insists givers must "eat too" and that money "creates opportunity" — but is targeting *ultimate orientation*: whether the self or the neighbor is the end. The rich-fool parable's point is not that storing grain is wrong but that a life *aimed* at the self is finally empty, a claim about meaning that markets do not address. The "binary" is offered as a moral-theological orientation (whom do you live *for*?), compatible with prudent self-provision as a means, not a competing terminus.

## Best Quotes
> "There's just two philosophies going on… you prosper because of the management of the creature, Korihor, or every man seeking the interest of his neighbor, and that's Jehovah."[^1]

> "That very night… he dies. Well, what was all that for? Nothing."[^2]
