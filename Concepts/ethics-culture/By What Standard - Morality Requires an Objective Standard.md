# By What Standard - Morality Requires an Objective Standard

## The Point
[[Greg Bahnsen]] distinguishes an **argument** from an opinion by a single feature: "an argument is backed by some kind of a standard and reasons why a particular view conforms to that standard."[^1] This yields his master diagnostic question — **"by what standard?"** — which he urges the apologist to "remember and use often" as "a test to see if someone is being arbitrary or not."[^2] Applied to ethics it has teeth: "even if you are not religious at all you can't have a coherent moral or ethical philosophy of any kind without some kind of standard or authority by which you judge something as more or less right."[^3] The conclusion is blunt: "an arbitrary morality is no morality at all."[^4] This is the transcendental edge of presuppositional apologetics — not merely that the unbeliever *lacks* a standard, but that moral judgment is *impossible* without one, so the very act of moral condemnation presupposes a standard the arbitrary worldview cannot supply.

## Evidence
Bahnsen targets the inconsistency of those "who don't believe in an objective standard for moral values and duties" yet pronounce on "how things should be": "if there is no objective standard for what is right and what is wrong then how can one say what is right or what is wrong?"[^5] Without grounding, "it's arbitrariness all the way down."[^6] He frames the worldview-comparison version of the question too: "if all worldviews are not equal, by what standard do we evaluate if one worldview is superior to another?"[^7] The test is meant to be applied relentlessly: "as you test people by asking them by what standard are they judging this or that to be true, you will be shocked at how much of what people believe and say is totally arbitrary or based on very flawed standards."[^2] [[Jacob Hansen]] generalizes the closing thesis: "if a person cannot provide a standard by which a belief can be judged as true or false then their belief is arbitrary — it has no foundation."[^8] The deeper companion problem (worldview *incoherence*) is flagged for the sequel.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "An argument is backed by some kind of a standard and reasons why a particular view conforms to that standard." [^1]

> "An arbitrary morality is no morality at all... unless you can ground your claims in something beyond mere opinion." [^4]

> "Remember these words and use them often: by what standard?" [^2]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology supplies exactly the objective standard Bahnsen's question demands, but locates it in **eternal moral law that God himself honors** rather than in a bare divine decree. God acts according to eternal principles — there are "irrevocable" laws upon which all blessings are predicated (D&C 130:20–21) — so the standard against which actions are "more or less right" is real, eternal, and mind-independent (compare [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]] and the existing [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]] / [[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]]). This both validates the transcendental move — moral judgment really does presuppose an objective standard no arbitrary worldview can ground (see also [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]]) — and refines it: the LDS answer to "by what standard?" is not "whatever God arbitrarily wills" but "the eternal law of righteousness that God perfectly embodies and reveals," so divine authority and objective moral order coincide without collapsing into either voluntarism or human relativism.
