# Mere Opinion Is Intellectually Worthless - The Diabetes Analogy

## The Point
The most obvious form of arbitrariness, [[Greg Bahnsen]] argues, is offering a **mere opinion** in place of an argument. "When someone says it's my opinion that has no weight intellectually... opinions are a dime a dozen — actually it's not even a dime a dozen, it doesn't cost anything and it isn't worth anything."[^1] In a debate, the moment an unbeliever retreats to "that's just my opinion," "they have conceded the case, they've lost."[^2] The reason is that every department of inquiry asks the same question of any claim — *"why? what's your evidence? what's your proof?"* — and "until somebody is going to go beyond mere opinion and offer an argument, some line of proof or evidence for their views, they haven't got anything worth hearing."[^3]

## Evidence
Bahnsen illustrates with two appeals to authority and a personal **diabetes analogy**. First: if it is "your opinion against the opinion of the 12 best brain surgeons in the world on the topic of what to do with your brain cancer," the experts' voice is obviously more authoritative — opinions are not equal.[^4] Then he recounts his own diabetes diagnosis: he questioned the doctor ("I'm a doctor too and it's my job to ask questions"), but concedes that refusing to *think of himself* as diabetic would change nothing — "that'd be preposterous."[^5] The medical case exposes the double standard: "why is it when it comes to something medical we all realized... you'd be a fool" to deny the diagnosis, yet in religion and morality people "think they have the right to just believe whatever they want."[^6] [[Jacob Hansen]] sharpens the worldview point: people who deny an objective standard for moral values still pronounce on "how things should be," but with no standard "their opinion about the way the world should be is arbitrary... not based on anything but their mere opinion."[^7] Hansen also turns the critique inward, noting Christians tolerate arbitrary opinion partly because Bible studies often model "everybody kind of shares their opinion" rather than submitting to authority.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "You need to know that when someone says it's my opinion that has no weight intellectually. Opinions are a dime a dozen... it doesn't cost anything and it isn't worth anything." [^1]

> "The minute an unbeliever says 'well you've got all those arguments but my opinion is this,' they have conceded the case. They've lost. That's it, it's all over." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Bahnsen's "by what authority?" challenge dovetails with the Restoration's emphasis on **authoritative revelation over private opinion**. Latter-day Saints hold that the "general mind and will of God for the world" is not discerned by each person's arbitrary preference but through living prophets and revelation — the collective "voice of God's prophets and Revelations" carries more authority than one person's notion, exactly as the brain-surgeon analogy implies. Yet the LDS frame adds a crucial counterweight to pure deference to experts: each member is also entitled to **personal revelation** to confirm truth (Moroni 10:4–5; cf. [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]). This is not the arbitrary "mere opinion" Bahnsen condemns — it is a Spirit-given witness tied to an objective reality and tested against the standard of revealed truth, the opposite of "believe whatever you want."
