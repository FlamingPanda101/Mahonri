# Men Without Chests - The Trained Emotions

## The Point
Lewis's central image gives the first chapter its title. The human person, on the old picture, is tripartite: **cerebral reason** (the head), **visceral appetite** (the belly), and between them the **"chest"** — *trained emotion*, the stable, educated sentiments. The chest is "the indispensable liaison officer between cerebral man and visceral man," the seat of "Magnanimity," of "emotions organised by trained habit into stable sentiments." Reason cannot govern appetite directly; only rightly trained feeling can translate the verdicts of reason into the motions of desire. To educate well is therefore "to irrigate deserts" — to cultivate the just affections by which a child comes to *love* what is good — not merely "to cut down jungles" of excess feeling.[^1]

## Evidence
Ward situates the chapter's argument as Lewis's "segue from the aesthetic question he opened with... to his larger theme, which is the grounds human beings have for moral behaviour."[^2] The Green Book's error is to attack the chest itself: fearing that "youth is sentimental," Gaius and Titius "conclude that the best thing they can do is to fortify the minds of young people against emotion."[^3] But this misdiagnoses the patient. Ward draws out Lewis's metaphor of the heart as a desert: "In Lewis's view, students are more likely to live lives from which the Muses are absent than to live lives in which the Muses are too dominant. The average pupil... tends towards sterility of sentiment, not excessive fertility of sentiment."[^4] The remedy is *amousia*-avoidance — Lewis's master Smewgy taught "never let us live with *amousia*," the absence of the Muses[^5] — because a heart starved of trained feeling has no organ left to bind reason to action: "Both deserts and jungles need correction, but it is the former which is the more common problem."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> The chest is "the indispensable liaison officer between cerebral man and visceral man." (Lewis, as glossed by Ward) [^1]

> "The task of the modern educator is not to cut down jungles but to irrigate deserts." [^1]

> "The average pupil... tends towards sterility of sentiment, not excessive fertility of sentiment." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The "chest" — trained sentiment mediating reason and appetite — names something Restoration discipleship targets directly: the formation of a *new heart* whose desires are aligned with truth. The gospel does not merely inform the intellect or restrain the appetite; it works to "sanctify... your hearts" (Helaman 3:35) until the Saints can say "we have no more disposition to do evil, but to do good continually" (Mosiah 5:2) — a description of the chest fully irrigated. This is why charity, "the pure love of Christ," is something one must *pray* to be "filled with" (Moroni 7:48): right feeling is cultivated, not assumed. Lewis's diagnosis — that modern education *starves* the heart — parallels the warning that in the last days many will be "without natural affection" (2 Timothy 3:3). And it answers the materialist who treats moral motivation as mere neurochemistry: the chest must be *educated toward objective good* ([[The Doctrine of Objective Value - The Tao]]), not just stimulated, which is the burden of the [[Reason Presupposes Emotion - The Neuroscience Reply to Materialism]] critique of [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]].
