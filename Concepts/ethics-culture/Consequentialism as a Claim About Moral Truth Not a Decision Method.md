# Consequentialism as a Claim About Moral Truth Not a Decision Method

## The Point
In Chapter 2 [[Sam Harris]] states the metaethical commitments behind his project: **moral realism** ("moral claims can really be true or false") and **consequentialism** ("the rightness of an act depends on how it impacts the well-being of conscious creatures").[^1] His key clarifying move is that consequentialism is **not a procedure for answering moral questions** but **a claim about the *status* of moral truth** — namely, that all value reduces to actual or potential changes in conscious experience.[^2] The practical impossibility of calculating outcomes (the "Three Mile Island Effect," population-ethics paradoxes) therefore does no damage to the view, because difficulty in *knowing* the answer is not evidence that there is *no* answer or some *non-experiential* basis for value.[^3]

## Evidence
- **All value presupposes possible experience.** "All questions of value... depend upon the possibility of experiencing such value. Without potential consequences at the level of experience... all talk of value is empty."[^4] Harris claims he is "unaware of any interesting exception."[^4]
- **Even religion is covertly consequentialist.** Pleasing God assumes God is conscious, cognizant of behavior, and able to be suffered or enjoyed; the suicide bomber's logic is "entirely the point" — he seeks consequences (paradise for himself and seventy relatives). So "even within religion... consequences and conscious states remain the foundation of all values."[^5]
- **Realism does not require uniformity.** Against Joshua Greene and Jonathan Haidt, Harris argues that lack of consensus no more refutes moral truth than disagreement about relativity or evolution refutes physical truth: "the conversation must continue."[^6] He grants there may be multiple equivalent "peaks" on the moral landscape, yet there remain right and wrong *directions* of movement.[^7]
- **The calculation problem is conceded, not fatal.** Parfit's "Repugnant Conclusion" and average-vs-total paradoxes show "we cannot rely on a simple summation or averaging," yet "at the extremes... human welfare must aggregate in some way."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "This commits me to some form of moral realism (viz. moral claims can really be true or false) and some form of consequentialism (viz. the rightness of an act depends on how it impacts the well-being of conscious creatures)." [^1]

> "Consequentialism is less a method of answering moral questions than it is a claim about the status of moral truth." [^2]

> "Even within religion, therefore, consequences and conscious states remain the foundation of all values." [^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's framing is honest and avoids the crude charge that consequentialists must run impossible utility sums. But two theistic responses press hard.

**First, the smuggled premise.** Calling consequentialism "a claim about the status of moral truth" does not *establish* that status; it *asserts* it. Why think value just *is* change in conscious experience? That equation is itself a substantive value-judgment that no fact about experience can supply — the is/ought gap reappears one level up. This is precisely Peterson's objection that one needs a prior interpretive frame to select which facts count as good (see [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]] and [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]]). A theist locates that frame in God as the source and standard of the good; Harris must either treat "well-being is the good" as a brute axiom (no longer "science") or argue for it (and then he is doing the moral philosophy he disparages). Grounding-of-value resources develop this: [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] argues that intelligibility and goodness require a necessary being, and [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] presses that objective moral *obligation* cannot be read off natural facts at all.

**Second, "even religion is consequentialist" proves less than claimed.** That actions have consequences in conscious experience is uncontroversial; that consequences *exhaust* what makes an act right is the disputed thesis, and the religious cases do not show it. Latter-day Saint ethics is teleological without being merely consequentialist: the end is a *kind of person in a kind of relationship* — charity, the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4), exaltation in loving union — not a quantum of pleasant experience. On that view the suicide bomber is wrong not because he miscalculated a reward (Harris's reading) but because murder is intrinsically a violation of agency and love; getting the *consequences* right would still leave the act evil. See [[Uncreated Intelligences Make Free Love Possible]] and [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]. Harris's own later admission of irreducible "peaks" and intuitions he cannot derive (sins of commission vs omission, loss vs forgone gain) quietly concedes that experience-aggregation is not the whole story.
