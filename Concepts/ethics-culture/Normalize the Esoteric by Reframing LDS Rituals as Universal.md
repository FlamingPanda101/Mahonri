# Normalize the Esoteric by Reframing LDS Rituals as Universal

## The Claim

On [[Ward Radio]], [[Kwaku El]], [[Austin Falter]], and [[Cardon Ellis]] propose a **rhetorical strategy** for the "vibe check" problem: when social-media critics call baptism for the dead "weird," do not try to sanitize the ordinance (you can't) — instead **own the weirdness and reframe ritual as universal**, "gaslighting" the audience into recognizing that they too perform strange rituals. The flagship example: a Texas high-school football crowd weeping through the national anthem is a *ritual* whose unstated religion is "American exceptionalism." If all cultures ritualize, then a font and white jumpsuits are not uniquely odd.[^1] Two corollary moves: (i) a **rebrand** — Austin Falter's "ancestral baptism," likening proxy baptism to East/Southeast Asian ancestral veneration so "all the vibe-check problems go away";[^2] and (ii) an **audience pivot** — Kwaku's pitch to court the large "New Age" community by translating restored-gospel concepts into its idiom (priesthood power ≈ energy healing, seer stones ≈ crystals, Book of Mormon ≈ Atlantis/Lemuria, D&C ≈ channeled texts), on the principle that in an anti-authority age "the idea has to win first" and cohesion "will sort itself out."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The ritual-is-universal observation is sound — rate: strong (descriptive)
Anthropologically, ritual and the sacred are human universals; civic rites (anthems, pledges, sports liturgies) really do function ritually. Reframing baptism for the dead as one ritual among many is a defensible move that lowers the "weirdness" differential rather than denying it.[^1]

### 2. The strategy keeps the doctrine rather than hiding it — rate: moderate
The panel's healthier instinct is "lean into the doctrine": be "straight up front" that the prophet calls the youth to "gather scattered Israel on both sides of the veil," offered as a *choice*. This pairs the normalization tactic with candor, avoiding the trap of the critic's gotcha video — and connects to the substantive defense in [[The One Ambiguous Verse Objection to Baptism for the Dead Fails]] and [[Every Person Gets a Fair Opportunity Through Proxy Ordinances]].[^1]

### 3. The "ancestral baptism" rebrand has explanatory value — rate: moderate
Naming the rite for its *purpose* (acting on behalf of ancestors) communicates more than the opaque "baptism for the dead," and the cross-cultural analogy to ancestral veneration is pedagogically useful — though Cardon's caution (below) limits it.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Several cautions, some voiced by the panel itself. (a) **"Gaslighting" is the wrong frame and the wrong tactic** — telling people their patriotism is "secretly a religion" is a tu quoque that may irritate rather than persuade, and branding one's own apologetic as gaslighting concedes bad faith; the move proves "you're weird too," not that the ordinance is *true*. (b) **A rebrand can sever the biblical anchor** — Cardon's own objection: dropping "baptism for the dead" loses the King James wording of [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]], "the most unexplainably biblical" ritual that "no other church can explain" (and that [[Tertullian]] witnesses); the apologetic strength is partly *in* the strange biblical phrase. (c) **The New Age pivot risks syncretism** — equating priesthood with "energy healing" and seer stones with "magic crystals" to win an audience could blur real doctrinal distinctions, and "cohesion will sort itself out" understates the problem of importing incompatible beliefs. (d) **Cardon/Austin's mission experience cuts against it**: it is "easier to relax an uptight person than to discipline a chaotic and non-disciplined person," so converts won by a maximally-loose framing may lack the structure to endure. A measured synthesis: normalization is a legitimate *opening* move to defuse the weirdness reflex, but it works only when chained to candor about the doctrine and its biblical warrant — not as a standalone vibes play.
