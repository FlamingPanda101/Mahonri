# God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro

## The Point
Turek answers the Euthyphro dilemma — *Does God will something because it is good (so the standard is above God), or is it good because God wills it (so morality is arbitrary)?* — by denying it is a real dilemma at all. A genuine dilemma is "A or non-A"; Euthyphro offers "A and B," leaving room for a third alternative, C: **God's own nature is the standard of Good.**[^1] God neither consults an external measure (which would make *that* God) nor invents morality arbitrarily, because "there is nothing arbitrary about an unchanging standard of Good." The buck "stops at God's unchanging moral nature."[^2]

## Evidence
On Turek's account, "the standard of rightness we know as the Moral Law flows from the nature of God Himself — infinite justice and infinite love."[^2] Because morality is grounded "in the object known as God's nature, it is unchangeable and authoritative" and "unaffected by our opinions about it."[^3] This also dissolves the "arbitrary tyrant" caricature: "God doesn't arbitrarily make up moral commands. He's not an exasperated parent who justifies everything with, 'Just do it because I said so!' God's commands are consistent with His moral nature."[^4]

Turek ties value to *source*. An atheistic universe "can't account for the objective value of human beings" — we'd be "overgrown germs," each "objectively worth zero," and "if you add up a bunch of zeroes, the total worth is still zero."[^5] But "God is the ground and source of ultimate value, and He endows us with His image," so human lives have "objective value, meaning, and purpose."[^6] He illustrates with auction prices — Elvis's hair, Monroe's dress — whose worth derives not from raw materials but from *source*: "if Christianity is true, your value is intrinsic because you are connected to God... You are literally sacred."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "The third alternative is that God's nature is the standard. God Himself is the unchanging standard of Good. The buck has to stop somewhere, and it stops at God's unchanging moral nature." [^2]

> "There is nothing arbitrary about an unchanging standard of Good." [^1]

> "The infinite God has endowed you with immeasurable worth. The majestic heavens aren't made in His image, but you are! That's why you have moral rights." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Turek's "third horn" is broadly compatible with LDS thought, but the Restoration sharpens it in an important way. Turek grounds morality in God's *nature* (a "nature" horn shared with Craig and classical theists). LDS theology adds that this moral order is *eternal law* — co-eternal with God, which God himself honors and administers rather than authoring from nothing ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]). This avoids pure divine-command voluntarism even more decisively than the "nature" reply alone, and it is the affirmative counterpart to the critique that the *Calvinist* divine-command view collapses under Euthyphro ([[The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory]]). The "value from source / image of God" theme matches the LDS doctrine of intrinsic human worth ([[Genesis 1 Image of God as Seedbed of Rights]]), and Turek's "unchanging standard of Good" is functionally Lewis's Tao — the one objective value system that is the sole source of all value ([[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]; [[The Tao Is the Sole Source of All Value]]).
