# Temples Treat Social Pathology at the Root, Not the Leaves

## The Claim
[[Keystone]] argues that the common complaint — temple money "would be better spent on social issues" — misreads what temples *do*. Borrowing a medical analogy, it contends that poverty, addiction, crime, and self-harm are *symptoms* whose underlying *disease* is the breakdown of marriage and the family; the temple's family-sealing ordinance is an intervention at the root, so "temples do address social issues — they're just doing it at the root instead of the leaves."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The pneumonia analogy.** Keystone recounts his own untreated cough: cough drops "were helpful in the moment, but I was treating symptoms, not the underlying and infinitely more serious sickness." Only "once I treated the pneumonia" did the symptoms resolve. Social pathologies likewise need their root cause addressed, not just their surface.[^2] *Rating: strong as an illustration; it frames the argument's logic clearly without claiming to prove it.*

2. **An outside authority names the root.** Non-Latter-day Saint professor [[Nathan Schluter]], in *Crisis* magazine: "the single greatest contributor to our social pathologies such as addiction, mental illness, suicide, and violent crime is the breakdown of marriage and the family." Children of family disruption face elevated risk of "violent crime, drop out of school, child abuse, self harm, depression, drug abuse, jail time, teen pregnancy, lower GPA, health problems, poverty, and homelessness."[^3] *Rating: moderate — a real (non-LDS) claim, but cited from a popular magazine, and "single greatest contributor" is a strong causal assertion the social-science literature would qualify; see [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]].*

3. **The temple targets exactly that root.** Of the temple ordinances, eternal marriage is "the capstone of the holy endowment" ([[James E. Faust]]) and "the crowning ordinance of the gospel" ([[Bruce R. McConkie]]); [[Russell M. Nelson]]: "salvation is an individual matter; exaltation is a family matter." So the institution most criticized as wasteful is precisely the one aimed at strengthening lasting families.[^4] *Rating: moderate — establishes the doctrinal aim; the causal efficacy is argued elsewhere via outcomes (see [[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]]).*

4. **Both/and, not either/or.** "Of course, this isn't a this-or-that situation. It's a this-and-that situation. Yes, let's absolutely feed the hungry and clothe the naked... But lasting societal healing isn't going to happen until we battle the underlying issues." Critics "might be playing checkers while the church is playing chess."[^5] *Rating: strong as framing — it pre-empts the false dilemma that temple spending and humanitarian aid are rivals.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies that this is a sophisticated rationalization of opportunity cost: even granting that family stability is upstream of social problems, it does not follow that *temple buildings* (as opposed to, say, marriage education, poverty relief, or mental-health services) are the most effective intervention, and "we're treating the root" is unfalsifiable if no spending tradeoff could ever count against it. One can also note that Schluter's "single greatest contributor" is contested — many social scientists weight poverty, policy, and structural factors as causes *of* family instability, reversing the arrow. The disciplined conclusion: the analogy legitimately reframes temples as *aimed at* a real upstream factor and dissolves the lazy "spend it on the poor instead" dilemma, while it does not establish that temple-building is the *optimal* use of resources or settle the causal direction between family structure and social outcomes.
