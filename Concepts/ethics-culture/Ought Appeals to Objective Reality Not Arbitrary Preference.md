# Ought Appeals to Objective Reality Not Arbitrary Preference

## The Point
This is the constructive payoff of Part 2, and the answer to the cliffhanger left by [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]. Because suffering is objectively real (see [[Suffering Is Objectively Real Not a Preference]]), [[Jacob Hansen]] argues that moral statements are "more than mere statements of preference": there is "a rational basis for saying you ought to do X rather than saying I prefer that you do X." [^1] "I prefer that you do X is an appeal to your arbitrary preferences"; morality, by contrast, "says that you ought to do X and is an appeal to the objective reality of suffering and the inherent desire of all sane creatures to avoid their own suffering." [^1] The *ought* gets its bite from two real things — the reality of suffering and a universal desire to avoid it.

## Evidence
The argument chains the two prior moves into a contrast between two grammars of guidance. From the reality of pain: "we rightly say that you ought not put your hand on that stove because we know that burns and the suffering they cause are real even if the experience of that objective reality come through your perceptions." [^1] He then states the thesis of the whole series: "what I'm postulating in these videos is that a real basis for moral statements exist and that makes them more than mere statements of preference." [^1] The decisive contrast: an "I prefer" sentence is "an appeal to your arbitrary preferences," answerable to nothing outside the speaker; an "ought" sentence is "an appeal to the objective reality of suffering and the inherent desire of all sane creatures to avoid their own suffering." [^1] So morality is not a louder preference but a different *kind* of claim — one with a truth-maker. Hansen then opens the next problem: if everyone desires to avoid suffering, "why is there so much disagreement about morality?" — deferred to the following video. [^2]

**Steelman of the critic:** The same two gaps that shadow the Part 1 derivation reappear. (1) *Hypothetical, not categorical.* "You ought not burn yourself" is prudential advice grounded in *your* desire to avoid *your* suffering; deriving a duty toward *others* needs a further bridge the argument has not yet built (the agent-relativity gap noted in [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]). (2) *Real desire ≠ binding obligation.* That all sane creatures *do* desire to avoid suffering is a fact about desires; the move to what one *morally must* do is the classic is–ought step. Hansen's account makes morality objective in the sense of *fact-anchored and non-arbitrary*, which already defeats pure subjectivism — but a theist will add that the *obligatory force* of well-being still needs grounding in something like eternal law or the law of love ([[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]), not merely in shared desire.

## Best Quotes
> "What I'm saying is that there is a rational basis for saying you ought to do X rather than saying I prefer that you do X." [^1]

> "Saying I prefer that you do X is an appeal to your arbitrary preferences. Morality on the other hand… says that you ought to do X and is an appeal to the objective reality of suffering and the inherent desire of all sane creatures to avoid their own suffering." [^1]

> "We rightly say that you ought not put your hand on that stove because we know that burns and the suffering they cause are real." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The restored gospel frames commandments not as arbitrary divine preferences but as expressions of an objective, eternal order God himself conforms to ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]) — exactly the difference between "you ought" and "I prefer." Where Hansen anchors the ought in suffering and universal desire, Latter-day Saint thought locates its binding force in eternal law oriented to real flourishing and charity ([[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]). Either way, moral language is treated as describing reality, which is why conscience (the Light of Christ given to every person) can be trusted as perception of a real moral order rather than mere feeling.
