# Identity Reconstruction at 20-Something Is Ordinary Not Cult Damage

## The Claim
The [[Salty Saints - Fact-Checking Johnny Harris's Harm Story|Salty Saints]] panel argues that [[Johnny Harris]]'s framing of his twenties — "I went to college and realized there's a lot more history… I was finally ready to question it" — describes **normal young-adult development**, not escape from a uniquely controlling religion. "You're 22 — welcome to the human race." Every young adult, in or out of any church, renegotiates inherited belief; ex-members err by treating a universal life stage as evidence of "high-control religion."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The universality point (strong, common experience).** "Every single one of us, when we hit our 20s, [starts] going, 'a second, I need to figure some stuff out for myself.'… Your perception of reality changes drastically and you have to try to figure out what's true." This happens "whether or not you're in the church."[^1]
2. **Misattribution of the ordinary to the sinister (interpretive).** "Ex-members of the church think this is unique to Mormons. No — it's called being a 20-year-old." Framing a developmental commonplace as cult-exit smuggles in the conclusion (that the religion was the problem).[^1]
3. **Harris kept the formation he disowns (moderate, cross-reference).** That Harris simultaneously credits the Church with his virtues undercuts the "I was deformed and had to rebuild" narrative; the rebuilding is the ordinary integration every adult does. See [[The Net-Positive Ledger Rebuts Johnny Harris's Harm Thesis]].
4. **Maturation requires renegotiation (interpretive).** "Life is bigger than what I thought it was when I was in high school" is presented as the normal expansion of perspective, not trauma recovery.[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
The steelman of Harris is that *not all belief systems are equal* in how they handle the normal twenties renegotiation: a "high-demand" religion with strong social and (he alleges) institutional costs for doubting — temple recommends, BYU honor code, family expectation — can make the ordinary process *far more costly and frightening* than it is for, say, a lapsed mainline Protestant, and that added cost is the harm he's naming, not the mere fact of questioning. The panel's "everyone does this at 20" reply is true but *incomplete*: the disputed question is whether the *exit costs* are abnormally high, and waving the universality flag does not address that. A rigorous response would grant that questioning is universal while arguing the LDS exit costs are not uniquely severe (most who leave are not shunned; President Nelson's counsel is to keep loving them) — an argument the panel gestures at elsewhere but does not develop here.

## LDS Relevance
The concept supplies a useful corrective for Latter-day Saints encountering deconstruction memoirs: the *experience* of "everything I thought is up for grabs" in one's twenties is developmentally normal and not, by itself, proof that the faith was a cult or that leaving is maturity. It pairs with the broader vault discussion of healthy vs. unhealthy deconstruction (see [[Swimmers vs Ship-Builders - The Ethics of Deconstruction]]) and with the demographic point that most 20-somethings of every background pass through this. The honest version concedes that exit *can* be socially costly and focuses the conversation there, rather than denying the experience.
