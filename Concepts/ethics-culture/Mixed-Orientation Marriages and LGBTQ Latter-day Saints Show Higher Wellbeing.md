# Mixed-Orientation Marriages and LGBTQ Latter-day Saints Show Higher Wellbeing

## The Claim
[[Timber Harvard|Tember Harward]] and the Ward Radio hosts argue that the dominant narrative — that LGBTQ Latter-day Saints are destined for divorce and misery — is empirically false: a substantial share of same-sex-attracted Latter-day Saints "are genuinely happier and do better in the church than out of the church," and mixed-orientation marriages (a same-sex-attracted member married to an opposite-sex spouse) succeed at higher-than-assumed rates. Harward presents himself not as a one-in-a-million outlier but as "representative of the majority."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (as presented)

### 1. Harward's lived experience
Harward came "from gay relationships" and married a woman "because I knew that is God's standard." He still has challenges but says "this is where I'm happiest," and contrasts present access to grace and "eternal worth" with two suicide attempts while "living a secular lifestyle." He frames himself as a deliberate, informed covenant-keeper, not a repressed casualty.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as testimony (a firsthand counterexample to the "all are miserable" universal); anecdotal as data (one case).

### 2. Cited marriage and youth statistics
The hosts relay (secondhand) figures attributed to **Mike Goodman** and **Justin Dyer** (*Deseret News*) and **Jeff Bennion / North Star**: mixed-orientation marriages in the Church lasting "twice as long" / "half the divorce rate," and LGBTQ LDS youth "more than twice" as well-adjusted (mental health, sense of community) as secular peers.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate as relayed. The named researchers and outlets are real; but the figures are recalled loosely on-air ("he said... I think"), undated, and not directly verified — see the caution in [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]].

### 3. Secular corroboration on religiosity
Harward stresses the supporting studies "aren't just religious studies, they're secular studies" showing religious LGBT individuals fare better than non-religious ones — consistent with the broader literature catalogued in [[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]].[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate. The general religiosity-wellbeing correlation is well-supported; its specific application to *mixed-orientation LDS marriages* is where the data thin out.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic (and the cited ex-Mormon panelists) would press several real points. (a) **Selection/survivorship bias**: those who remain in mixed-orientation marriages and speak publicly are the ones for whom it worked; the divorced and the departed are, by definition, underrepresented — so "twice as long" can partly reflect who is left to count. (b) **Disclosure and measurement**: self-reported wellbeing in a high-demand community can mask distress, and "twice as well-adjusted" depends heavily on instrument and comparison group. (c) **The autonomy concern is symmetric**: the ex-Mormon side argues the Church's teaching itself constrains the choice set, so framing the marriage as freely "chosen" understates social pressure. (d) **Correlation ≠ causation**: religiosity tracks many confounds (family stability, community, baseline temperament). The honest, defensible residue is significant nonetheless: the universal claim that SSA Latter-day Saints are *uniformly* worse off is refuted by even one thriving counterexample, and the secular religiosity-wellbeing literature makes "religion is bad for gay people, full stop" untenable. The narrower, supportable version — *many* SSA Latter-day Saints report flourishing, and outcomes are far better than the doom narrative implies — stands; the precise multipliers ("twice," "2.5×") are tier-C as relayed and need their primary sources checked (see [[Dehlins Faulty-Pool Study Skews LDS LGBTQ Outcomes]] on why the comparison pool is decisive).

## Best Quotes
> "There's a lot more of us who are thriving in the church than are trying to get out of it... I came into this knowing what the church doctrine was, and I chose to marry a woman because I knew that is God's standard."[^2]

> "You're not just the one in a million gay LDS guy that made it — all the studies show that the majority make it. You have a majority chance of success."[^1]

> "They need to acknowledge that some of us, many of us, are genuinely happier and do better in the church than out of the church — but they don't like to acknowledge that."[^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the empirical core of the Latter-day Saint pastoral case that covenant-keeping is a viable, even flourishing, path for members who experience same-sex attraction — deployed here against a *Jubilee Media* "Mormons vs. Ex-Mormons" segment. It connects to the vault's well-being literature ([[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]], [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]]) and depends, as the steelman and the companion note [[Dehlins Faulty-Pool Study Skews LDS LGBTQ Outcomes]] show, on getting the comparison population right. Tier-C as relayed; the underlying religiosity-wellbeing correlation is the stronger general claim.
