# Substitutional Patriarchy - The Government Replaces the Husband

## The Claim
Reacting to [[Trent Horn]]'s *Council of Trent* video, [[Jacob Hansen]] and the Ward Radio panel endorse Horn's thesis of **"substitutional patriarchy"**: as feminism vilified men and devalued marriage, the **government** has stepped into the provider/protector/leader role that a husband and father were meant to fill — so young women increasingly "marry the state." The hosts apply this to declining Latter-day Saint retention among women and argue the cure is re-centering family and restoring male leadership (servant-leadership, not tyranny).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Patriarchy defined as male *leadership*, glossed as servant-leadership — rate: moderate (scriptural + interpretive)
Horn defines patriarchy as the claim that men are "especially suited to lead in their relationships with women," citing [[Ephesians 5.23|Ephesians 5:23]] (husband as head, as Christ is head of the church) and Pius XI's *Casti Connubii*. The hosts immediately qualify: leading means "the first into the trenches," blazing a trail "in full consultation with my wife," not "the boss on top of the wagon whipping" — "in Christianity, leaders are servants."[^1] This tracks the vault's [[The Patriarchal Order Is the Order of Fatherhood Not Male Rule]] and [[Genesis 3.16 Means Rule Not Preside]].

### 2. The "government as husband" pattern — rate: moderate (sociological, second-hand data)
Horn's core illustration is the Obama-campaign "Life of Julia" infographic, in which government funds Julia's preschool, birth control, and child-rearing with "no husband or man ever mentioned" — "her dad and her husband are her uncle Sam." The replacement claim is that "you can replace the man's responsibility with the government." The hosts call this the "OG great replacement."[^2] *(The supporting statistics — abortion as the #1 issue for women under 30, married women least likely to vote Democrat at 45% vs. never-married at 72%, 87% of abortions to unmarried women — come from Horn's video citing Pew and various studies and should be verified against primary sources before reuse.)*

### 3. The LDS application: women's retention and the "therapizing" worry — rate: moderate (LDS-internal)
The hosts note women are leaving the Church at historically high rates (cf. [[Younger Women Are Disaffiliating at Higher Rates]]) and fault a cultural "therapizing of the gospel" that centers the self over the family. Their proposed structural counterweight is *priesthood keys* as the reminder that men must "lead out," and the conviction that "if men will go, women will follow." They explicitly exempt faithful single members who desire marriage, targeting only intentional self-prioritization.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is a politically loaded thesis and the strongest objections are substantive, not merely partisan. (a) **Correlation, causation, and reverse causation.** That unmarried women favor more government provision may reflect that they *lack a second income and caregiving partner* (a rational response to real economic precarity) rather than that government "replaced" a husband they would otherwise have; the arrow may run from economic structure → policy preference, not feminism → state-as-husband. (b) **"Substitution" frames public goods as emasculation.** Paid family leave, childcare, and healthcare are supported by many *married* people and in most peer democracies; casting the welfare state as a usurping pseudo-husband is an ideological reading, not a neutral finding. (c) **Selection and sourcing.** The statistics are relayed from an apologetic video, not examined at the source; figures like voting splits have multiple confounds (age, education, urbanity, religiosity) that the "substitutional patriarchy" story collapses into one cause. (d) **The leadership claim is contested even within the tradition.** Reading male "presiding" as divinely mandated headship is disputed; the vault's own [[Masculinity Should Preside Within Each Person Not Just Between the Sexes]] relocates "presiding" to an *intra-personal* virtue, and critics note that empirically equal partnerships also produce stable, happy families. (e) **The "if men lead, women follow" maxim risks instrumentalizing women's agency.** The disciplined version: the descriptive observations — a real political-gender divergence among the young, declining marriage rates, and genuine male disengagement — are worth taking seriously and are partly documented; but the *explanatory frame* ("the government is replacing men") is a contestable interpretation that outruns the cited data, and the prescriptive payload (restore male leadership) imports a particular and disputed theology of gender. Best treated as a provocative cultural thesis to examine, not an established sociological result.
