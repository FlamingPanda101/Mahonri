# Womanism Is Misnamed Masculinism That Devalues the Feminine

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jacob Hansen]], building on an analysis by James Cussen (Living Philosophy), argues that **what we call "feminism" has, since roughly the first wave, been *misnamed*.** Femininity and masculinity are psychological principles (compare yin/yang) that every person mixes, but that statistically correlate with the sexes.[^1] Industrial society — capitalist West and socialist East alike — selects for *masculine* traits: competitiveness, ambition, dominance, the "numbers-oriented," "thing-oriented," "deconstructive" cluster.[^2] In such a value landscape men come out on top "by the setup of the landscape," and this is misdiagnosed as "patriarchy" oppressing women, when the deeper problem is that **the society does not value femininity at all.**[^3]

The historical women's movement — which Hansen/Cussen propose renaming **"womanism"** — won a real structural victory (women entering the workplace, law, medicine, the vote) but left the *value system itself untouched*. It told women to become more competitive, ambitious, and dominant — i.e., **more masculine** — and chastised men for expressing the very masculinity women were now told to adopt. The result: "nothing has done more to devalue the feminine than so-called feminism." Far from liberating feminine values (care, cooperation, connection, nurture, mercy), womanism "consummated industrial society's apotheosis of masculine values while putting the final nail in the coffin of feminine values."[^4] The constructive proposal is a genuine *second move* — a "revaluation of the feminine" itself, rather than the mere integration of women into a masculinist paradigm.[^5]

This is offered as the substantive reply to [[Julie Hanks]]: granting that first-wave feminism was "great — a movement about women's liberty and equality under the law," Hansen insists Hanks collapses feminism's multiple, evolving waves into one undifferentiated good, missing that the later movement turned "against valuing women and femininity."[^6]

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman)

1. **The yin/yang / trait-cluster premise is widely shared.** That masculine and feminine principles can be abstracted from biological sex (effeminate, mannish), that everyone is an admixture, and that the traits correlate-but-don't-determine, is "intuitively grasped in language" and tracks the Bem Sex-Role Inventory categories (yielding, compassionate, tender vs. assertive, analytical, dominant).[^1][^7] *(Strong as a framing device; the trait lists are contestable and partly culturally constructed.)*

2. **The "value landscape" reframing of patriarchy is genuinely clever.** Oxford defines patriarchy as society "ruled or controlled by men." Cussen's move — that conflating *men* with *masculinity* obscures the real issue — lets him say the highest-paid roles (CEO, coder, Wall Street) are defined by *masculine traits*, so men's statistical dominance is downstream of the values, not merely of male power.[^3][^8] *(Strong — it reframes an outcome (male over-representation) as a symptom of a value system rather than the disease itself. Critics will object that "masculine value landscape" is doing a lot of unfalsifiable work and can absorb any data.)*

3. **The "be more masculine, then get blamed for masculinity" double-bind.** The claim that the post-womanist world "has never been so masculinist" — a few more women in power, identical value structure — names a real tension progressives also worry about (girlboss critique, the corporatization of feminism).[^4] *(Moderate-to-strong; rhetorically forceful, empirically hard to quantify.)*

4. **The well-being data is the strongest empirical leg.** Martin Seligman (*Authentic Happiness*, 2002): depression is "ten times as prevalent as in 1960" with onset dropping from ~29 to ~14½, even as every *objective* indicator improved.[^9] A University of Pennsylvania study found women, *happier than men* in the 1960s, are now the *less* happy sex despite massive gains in workplace and social representation.[^10] Hansen is careful: this does not "fully explain" rising depression, but it is "extremely compelling" that a society which minimized feminine virtues became unbalanced.[^11] See [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]]. *(The "paradox of declining female happiness" (Stevenson & Wolfers, 2009) is real and cited across the spectrum; the *causal* attribution to "devaluing the feminine" is an interpretation, not a finding.)*

5. **The in-house LDS rebuttal to Hanks.** The earliest Restoration revelation to a woman — [[Doctrine and Covenants 25|the 1830 charge to Emma Smith]] "to expound scriptures and to exhort the church" — and the founding of the [[Eliza R. Snow|female Relief Society]] as "a kingdom of priests," plus 19th-century LDS women like Ellis Reynolds Shipp earning a medical degree, are marshaled as evidence the Restoration valued women's public voice *before and independently of* secular feminism's later waves.[^12] *(Genuine counter-evidence to a flat "the Church only silenced women" narrative; defenders and critics dispute how much "ordained to expound" implies.)*

**Honest counterweight.** This is a *positive cultural thesis*, not a knock-down apologetic, and it leans heavily on one podcaster's framework. The atheist objections catalogued in [[Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine]] and [[Modern Gender Equality Is Not Grounded in the Bible]] cut the other way: the concrete liberties Hansen's own female friends enjoy were largely *won by* women's movements, which is precisely Hanks's point. The strongest version of the disagreement is narrow and possibly reconcilable: Hanks credits feminism for women's *permissions*; Hansen credits "first-wave" feminism for the same permissions but charges *later* feminism with devaluing femininity. Whether the later movement actually "abandoned feminine values" (vs. expanded the options available to women) is exactly what is in dispute.

## Best Quotes

> "Feminism has had multiple waves since the 1890s and has evolved over time into a movement that is actually against valuing women and femininity." [^6]

> "Womanism brought women more and more into the masculine value system. The integration of women into the masculine value sphere was the ultimate victory for what we might call masculinism." [^4]

> "Nothing has done more to devalue the feminine than so-called feminism." [^4]

> "What feminism does is it tells women to be more masculine and then chastises men as oppressive if they express their own masculinity." [^13]
