# Rival Value Systems Are Only Fragments of the Tao

## The Point
Lewis denies that there has ever been a genuinely *new* system of value. "What purport to be new systems… all consist of fragments from the Tao itself, arbitrarily wrenched from their context… swollen to madness in their isolation, yet still owing to the Tao and to it alone such validity as they possess."[^1] The rebel against traditional morality never escapes it; he simply seizes one of its precepts, absolutizes it, and turns it against the rest. There is no fresh moral colour to be invented: "The human mind has no more power of inventing a new value than of imagining a new primary colour."[^2]

## Evidence
Ward connects Lewis's "swollen to madness" to G. K. Chesterton's *Orthodoxy*: "The virtues have gone mad because they have been isolated from each other and are wandering alone."[^3] Lewis's standing example of a value swollen to madness is racial prejudice. He lists five well-grounded forms of special beneficence — duties to those we have promised (Justice), who have benefited us (Gratitude), who are weak (Mercy), who share our citizenship (Patriotism), and to whom we are related (Familial Affection) — and warns that the last two must not be allowed to outweigh the first three: "Racialism, setting up to be the supreme duty, is the rebellion of one particular moral law against moral law in general."[^4] He dramatizes the same point in *Out of the Silent Planet*, where Weston has let "love of kindred… which is not one of the greatest laws" justify flouting "pity and straight dealing and shame," and now "can do nothing but obey it… though if we ask you why it is a law you can give no other reason for it than for all the other and greater laws which it drives you to disobey."[^5] By contrast Nietzsche's "Campaign against Morality" is, in Lewis's verdict, "a mere innovation" — not a new value at all.[^6]

## LDS Connection
That every supposedly "new" morality is a stolen fragment of the one true moral order coheres with the Latter-day Saint conviction that right and wrong are not human inventions but features of an objective, eternal law known to all through the **Light of Christ** (Moroni 7:16-19). Ideologies that absolutize a single value — race, nation, class, even survival — are, in this light, rebellions of a part against the whole, and they "borrow" their force from the very moral law they distort. This is why naturalist reconstructions such as [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] cannot generate genuinely new foundations: they too can only select an existing value (well-being) and elevate it, while the deeper question of *why we ought to honor it* still presupposes the prior moral order (see [[The Innovator Secretly Smuggles In the Tao]]). Lewis's argument also exposes the incoherence of the relativism beneath such projects: to call one value-fragment "supreme" is already to measure it against the larger law (cf. [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
