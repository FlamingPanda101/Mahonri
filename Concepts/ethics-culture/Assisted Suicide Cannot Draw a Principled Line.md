# Assisted Suicide Cannot Draw a Principled Line

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] argues (per the summary) that legalized assisted suicide is a genuine **slippery slope**: once the *justifying principle* is "relieve suffering by ending the sufferer's life," there is no principled stopping point at the terminally ill, because suffering is not confined to the dying. His sharpest framing is a question to proponents — **"Where do you draw the line?"** Society ordinarily goes to great lengths, including physical force, to stop a person from jumping off a bridge; to permit assisted suicide for *some* is therefore to declare that **"some lives are worth saving from suicide, but others are not."** Horn likens that judgment to Nazi Germany's category of **lebensunwertes Leben — "life unworthy of life."** Marketed for the terminally ill in severe pain, the practice "cannot logically stop there"; it expands to chronic pain, disability, and depression — which, he says, is *already happening*.[^1]

This is not a bare "things will get worse" prediction (the *fallacious* slippery slope) but a **conceptual** slope: the criterion offered to license the first step (subjective unbearable suffering) **logically contains** the later steps, so the line-drawing is exposed as arbitrary. Horn supports it with trend data, not just intuition.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The expansion is documented, not hypothetical (rate: strong).** Horn cites (per the summary): the Netherlands assisted a **physically healthy 29-year-old autistic woman** in dying for *mental* suffering; in **Canada**, assisted death rose from **1 in 50 deaths in 2019 to 1 in 25 by 2022** — fast enough to rank as a leading cause of death, surpassing car accidents and influenza. A doubling of share in three years, reaching psychiatric and non-terminal cases, is the empirical shape a real slope predicts.[^1] *(The factual trajectory is the load-bearing evidence; the autistic-woman and Canada-ratio cases are the concrete anchors.)*
2. **The inconsistency argument is internally valid (rate: strong).** If the state forcibly prevents the despairing bridge-jumper *because his life is worth saving*, but funds death for the despairing disabled or chronically ill, it has implicitly **ranked** lives by worth-of-saving. Horn's point is that no neutral suffering-criterion can justify intervening in one case and assisting in the other without smuggling in a judgment that some lives are not worth preserving.[^1]
3. **The "life unworthy of life" parallel names a real precedent (rate: moderate, rhetorically strong).** *Lebensunwertes Leben* was the actual slogan of the pre-Holocaust German "euthanasia" (T4) program that began with the disabled and mentally ill. Invoking it is an argument that *this exact criterion* — judging a life not worth living — has a historical trajectory. See [[Darwinian Ethics Licenses Eugenics and Tribe-Supremacy]] for the broader pattern of worth-of-life rankings. *(Rated moderate because a Nazi analogy is rhetorically potent but logically only shows the criterion is *dangerous*, not that every assisted death is genocidal; the force is in the shared premise, not the body count.)*[^1]

### Steelman of the pro-assisted-suicide side

The strongest reply is that **a principled line exists and is routinely drawn elsewhere**: competent, informed, voluntary adult consent — the same standard that distinguishes licit surgery, fasting, or hazardous occupations from things done *to* a person against their will. On this view the bridge-jumper is stopped not because his life is "worth more" but because suicidal crisis is typically **impulsive, treatable, and non-autonomous**, whereas a settled, repeatedly-affirmed request from a competent patient is an exercise of autonomy, not its failure — so intervening in the first and assisting the second is *consistent*, tracking capacity-for-autonomous-choice rather than worth-of-life. Defenders add that "slippery slope" is a fallacy absent a causal mechanism, that strict safeguards (waiting periods, multiple assessors, capacity review) can hold a line, and that jurisdictions like Oregon have (they argue) kept eligibility narrow for decades. The autism and Canada cases, they say, are **policy-design failures** — arguments for *better safeguards*, not against the practice itself. The Nazi comparison is, on this account, a category error: the T4 program was **involuntary state killing**, the opposite of a patient-requested death.

### Counter-objection (Horn's rejoinder)

Horn's reply (developed across the video) is that the consent line does **not** in fact hold, *because the same suffering-relief logic that justifies the practice also undermines the voluntariness of the consent*. (a) The expansion to the autistic 29-year-old and to psychiatric suffering shows the "settled competent request" standard already bending to admit conditions where competence and treatability are precisely in dispute. (b) The [[The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving|abuse cases]] show consent **manufactured by deprivation** — offer death to the man losing his housing, the untreated veteran, the woman denied chemo, and the "choice" is coerced by the cheaper alternative. A consent that the system has financial reason to elicit is not the clean autonomy the steelman assumes. (c) Thus the Nazi parallel is about the *criterion* ("this life is not worth sustaining"), which can be reached by social pressure just as surely as by state decree.

## Apologetic Engagement

The note's value is methodological: it models the **conceptual** slippery slope (the justifying principle contains the feared consequence) and pairs it with **trend data** so the argument is not the fallacious "things will slide" version. Against the autonomy rebuttal, the move is to show the autonomy is **eroded by the very conditions the policy creates** — push the debate from *principle* (consent vs. worth-of-life) to *evidence* (what the suffering-criterion actually licenses, and whom). The Nazi analogy should be deployed as a **shared-criterion** argument ("judging a life unworthy is the premise, however reached") rather than a guilt-by-association swipe, or it backfires.

## LDS Relevance

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints **opposes euthanasia and assisted suicide** as elective acts: deliberately taking a life to end suffering "violates the commandments of God." The handbook simultaneously holds that a person is **not** required to accept *extraordinary* means to prolong life and may receive pain relief even at risk of hastening death — the same killing/allowing-to-die line Catholic teaching draws (see [[Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer]]). The slippery-slope worry resonates with the LDS conviction that **human worth is intrinsic and eternal** — grounded in divine parentage and a pre-mortal identity (cf. [[LDS Abortion Position Follows From Pre-Existence Not Cultural Caving]]), not in a life's measured quality, productivity, or cost. From that vantage, the "where do you draw the line?" challenge cuts the same way: a worth that is conferred by God cannot be revoked by a suffering-calculus, so any criterion that ranks lives by worth-of-saving is already off the rails.
