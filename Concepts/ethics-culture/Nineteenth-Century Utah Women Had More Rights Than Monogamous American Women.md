# Nineteenth-Century Utah Women Had More Rights Than Monogamous American Women

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The misconception is that "monogamy is fair and polygamy is unfair" — one man with several wives is self-evidently the unjust arrangement.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Monogamy in the period was not "fair" either
"If we look back at 19th-century monogamy, it was just unfair all around." Monogamous American women's "legal rights... were paralyzing": they often could not own property, "had no recourse if there was domestic violence," and on divorce "would lose custody of their children." Suffragist [[Elizabeth Cady Stanton]] "used the fury that was pointed at polygamy" to argue that "marriage in general is unfair," not polygamy specifically.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the legal disabilities of 19th-century married women are well documented; Stanton's redirection is historical).

### 2. Utah women actually had *more* rights — partly because of polygamy
"Women in Utah had more rights than monogamous women," and this was "largely because of the practice of polygamy." Because a plural wife might keep her own household while her husband lived elsewhere, women could **own property**; they could **divorce and keep custody**; they received the **vote in 1870** ("which they were very proud of"); and they could pursue **higher education** "which was not accessible for women everywhere."[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (Utah women's 1870 suffrage and liberal divorce/property arrangements are documented; the causal tie to polygamy is a defensible historical claim Nash frames carefully).

### 3. The honest framing
Nash does not claim polygamy was therefore good: "for all of its faults and difficulty," it "in an interesting way expanded the possibilities for women in Utah." The point is comparative and corrective, not a defense of the institution.[^4] This is a case of rights tracking material/social conditions rather than the abstract fairness of a marriage form — compare [[Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the qualification disciplines the claim against overreach).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies that conferring property and voting rights inside a system that still structurally subordinated women to a shared husband is a low bar — and that Utah's woman suffrage (1870) was partly a political maneuver, later revoked by federal anti-polygamy legislation, not a pure feminist gain. One can also argue the comparison cherry-picks: the average plural wife's autonomy varied enormously, and "more rights than the worst-off monogamous women" is not "fair." The measured conclusion: the *contrast* is real and refutes the lazy "monogamy fair / polygamy unfair" binary, while it does not establish that plural marriage was, on net, *good* for women — a judgment Nash explicitly declines to make.

## Best Quotes
> "If we look back at 19th-century monogamy, it was just unfair all around. The legal rights of women were paralyzing — they couldn't own property... they had no recourse if there was domestic violence."[^2]

> "Polygamy did, for all of its faults and difficulty, in an interesting way expand the possibilities for women in Utah."[^4]
