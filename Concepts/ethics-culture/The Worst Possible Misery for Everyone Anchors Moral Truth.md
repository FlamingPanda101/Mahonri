# The Worst Possible Misery for Everyone Anchors Moral Truth

## The Point
This is Harris's argumentative bedrock — the single fixed point from which he says the whole edifice of moral realism follows. Even if each conscious being has a unique "nadir," we can conceive "a state of the universe in which everyone suffers as much as he or she (or it) possibly can," which he calls "the worst possible misery for everyone."[^1] His challenge: "If you think we cannot say this would be 'bad,' then I don't know what you could mean by the word 'bad' (and I don't think you know what you mean by it either)."[^1] Once we grant that this extreme and "absolute flourishing" are different and "dependent on facts about the universe," then "we have admitted that there are right and wrong answers to questions of morality."[^2]

## Evidence
Harris designs the thought experiment to be culture-neutral: the descent toward misery "need have nothing to do with people enforcing their culturally conditioned moral precepts" — imagine merely "a neurotoxic dust" falling from space so that "everyone loses a little, or a lot, without there being compensatory gains."[^1] The modest premise — "we simply must stand somewhere" — is that "a universe in which all conscious beings suffer the worst possible misery is worse than a universe in which they experience well-being," and "this is all we need to speak about 'moral truth' in the context of science."[^2] He scales it with the **Adam and Eve** scenario: two people alone could maximize well-being or "smash each other in the face with a large rock," and "there are, by definition, paths that lead to the worst misery and paths that lead to the greatest fulfillment."[^3] He then asks: "Why would the difference between right and wrong answers suddenly disappear once we add 6.7 billion more people to this experiment?"[^4] This grounds his rejoinder to the moral skeptic who demands to know why well-being "ought to matter" — the worst-possible-misery case is meant to make "bad" undeniable without prior moral commitments.[^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is Harris's most persuasive move, and its persuasiveness is exactly the point of contention. **(1) "Bad" is doing two jobs.** That maximal universal misery is *worse* (a comparative, factual claim about quantities of suffering) does not by itself yield that it is *morally* bad in the obligation-bearing sense (that we *ought not* bring it about). The slide from "this state is worse" to "there are right and wrong *answers to questions of morality*" trades on the ambiguity the is–ought gap names (see [[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]]). **(2) Axiom, not fact.** Even sympathetic interlocutors grant the floor is bad but deny it is a *fact* in the scientific sense — it functions as a foundational moral *intuition* or axiom presupposed by all value talk, not a finding of neuroscience (the precise dispute logged in [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]]). If it is an axiom, then Harris has *assumed* moral realism at the foundation rather than *derived* it from facts — which is what the theist claimed all along. **(3) Whose intuition, and why trust it?** The intuition that suffering-for-everyone is objectively bad is exactly the kind of universal, mind-independent moral perception that fits a created moral order far better than a naturalist ontology in which it is a contingent feeling bequeathed by selection (see [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] on borrowed moral capital, [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]] on the objectivity of value, [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] on grounding goodness in God). The Latter-day Saint can stand with Harris in calling absolute misery objectively bad — and then point out that "we simply must stand somewhere" is itself the confession that morality rests on a foundation facts cannot provide.
