# The Value of Human Life Is a Faith Claim Not a Rational One

## The Point

The conviction that every human being is infinitely precious cannot be
established by reason alone. It rests on the faith claim that humans are created
in God's image. Strip that away and reason can be marshalled to justify killing
the weak — as a Princeton philosopher's defense of infanticide illustrates.

## Argument

1. Asked whether he would kill a disabled baby, a "distinguished professor of
   philosophy at Princeton" ([[Peter Singer]]) answered, "Yes, if that was in
   the best interests of the baby and the family as a whole" [^1].
2. Prager challenges the listener: "Can you offer a purely rational reason why
   the professor is wrong?" [^1].
3. His answer: the only reason he can offer is "a belief that all human beings
   are created in God's image and are therefore infinitely precious" — and "the
   preciousness of all human life is a belief, not an assertion of reason" [^1].
4. Therefore the moral floor that protects the weak is supplied by faith, not by
   rationality; reason left to itself can endorse a utilitarian calculus that
   treats a disabled infant as a net burden.
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) Secular ethicists reply that human dignity
   can be grounded without God — in rationality, sentience, or the capacity for
   interests — and that "best interests of the baby" language is itself a moral
   premise, not raw self-interest. Prager's rejoinder is that each such
   criterion is itself a chosen premise reason does not compel, and that
   capacity-based criteria are exactly what license Singer's conclusion when the
   capacity is absent.
