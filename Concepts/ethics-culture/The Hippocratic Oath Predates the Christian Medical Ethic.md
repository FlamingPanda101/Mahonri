# The Hippocratic Oath Predates the Christian Medical Ethic

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (Scrivener): the egalitarian impulse — treating all persons equally regardless of status or sex — is uniquely Christian, exemplified by [[Galatians 3.28|Galatians 3:28]] ("neither male nor female").

## The Rebuttal

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: the same egalitarian medical ethic appears in the [[Hippocrates|Hippocratic Oath]], centuries before Christianity, in language strikingly parallel to Galatians.

1. **The text.** The Oath pledges that "whoever's house I walk into, rich or poor, male or female, I am to serve them."[^1] *(Strong — explicit, pre-Christian, status- and sex-blind.)*

2. **The double-standard test.** O'Connor: "I have absolutely no doubt that if that prescription showed up in the letters of Paul, Christians would be jumping on it as evidence that our modern medical ethic is completely indebted to Christianity."[^1] *(Powerful rhetorical move — exposes selective crediting. This is an instance of the general [[Western Values Predate or Arise Independently of Christianity|"values predate Christianity"]] argument applied to medicine.)*

3. **Steelman of Scrivener — concede the value, claim the institution.** Scrivener does not deny the Oath; he says "yay and amen to Hippocrates." His counter is that Hippocratic medicine and Galen's manuals never *institutionalized* care for "the poor and the sick as sick" — only Christianity scaled it into hospitals. See [[Institutionalization Is the Unique Christian Contribution]].[^2] *(This is the strongest available reply, and O'Connor concedes the hospital point — but argues it shrinks the thesis from "values are Christian" to "Christians built institutions for pre-existing values.")*

4. **"Where did Hippocrates get it?"** Scrivener appeals to "common grace."[^3] *(Theological answer that concedes the historical point — see [[The Common Grace Concession Undercuts the Causation Claim]].)*

## Best Quotes

> "Whoever's house I walk into, rich or poor, male or female, I am to serve them... I have absolutely no doubt that if that showed up in the letters of Paul, Christians would be jumping on it."[^1]

> "We look at Galatians and see 'neither male nor female' and say look how radical this was for Paul — when hundreds of years earlier the Hippocratic Oath says the same thing. It literally says male or female."[^4]
