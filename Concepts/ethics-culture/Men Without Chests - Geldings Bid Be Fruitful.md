# Men Without Chests - Geldings Bid Be Fruitful

## The Point
Lewis closes his first lecture with one of the most quoted passages in twentieth-century apologetics — the indictment that gives the chapter its title. A culture that teaches schoolchildren to debunk all sentiment, then demands they behave virtuously, is performing a self-defeating act of mutilation: "**We make men without chests and expect of them virtue and enterprise. We laugh at honour and are shocked to find traitors in our midst. We castrate and bid the geldings be fruitful.**" [^1] Having atrophied the very organ of trained sentiment that connects reason to conduct, modern education then expresses surprise that the resulting man cannot do what only a man *with* a chest could do.

## Evidence
Ward notes the deliberate craft: Lewis "combines anaphora and antithesis of thought" in this passage, each clause naming a faculty destroyed and then a fruit absurdly expected from the destruction. [^1] The wartime backdrop sharpens "traitors in our midst": in the years just before the lectures, Britons George Armstrong (1941) and Duncan Scott-Ford (1942) had been executed for treason, and William Joyce ("Lord Haw-Haw") would hang in 1946. [^2] A "gelding," Ward glosses from the OED, is "a castrated animal, especially a horse" — the image fuses the breeder's barnyard with the moral classroom. [^3] The same diagnosis runs through the wordplay a few pages earlier: "the old [education] was a kind of propagation — men transmitting manhood to men; the new is merely propaganda." [^4] In Lewis's bleaker moods (e.g., the 1964 poem "Re-Adjustment") these chestless products become "ex-men," separated from the living by a barrier "across which no voice can ever carry"; Ward observes it is left unclear whether Lewis thinks the Tao can truly be *eradicated* from a person or only suppressed so thoroughly as to appear so. [^5]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's warning is a warning about *formation* — that you cannot harvest righteousness from people in whom the capacity for righteous feeling has been schooled away. Latter-day Saint teaching makes the same demand from the constructive side: parents are charged to "bring [children] up in light and truth" (D&C 93:40) and to "train up a child in the way he should go" so that the affections, not merely the intellect, are shaped toward the good. The "geldings bid be fruitful" image is the photographic negative of the covenant ideal of generative, virtue-transmitting households — manhood and womanhood handed on rather than cut off. A gospel reduced to slogans without the cultivation of the heart would, on Lewis's logic, breed exactly the sterile virtue it laments.
