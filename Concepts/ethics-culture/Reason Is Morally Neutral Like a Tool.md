# Reason Is Morally Neutral Like a Tool

## The Point

Reason by itself is not a source of goodness. It is an instrument — "no more
intrinsically moral than a knife" — that serves whatever end the agent already
desires. Because reason can be marshalled just as effectively for evil ends as
for good ones, the Enlightenment hope that reason *alone* will make people and
societies good is mistaken.

## Argument

1. Since the 18th-century "Age of Reason," educated opinion has held that reason
   alone leads to goodness, with no need for God or religion [^1].
2. But reason can argue for doing good *or* for doing bad with equal ease;
   "reason leads to good only when you want it to. Just as it leads to bad when
   you want it to" [^2].
3. Therefore reason is a *tool*, not a moral compass. Like a knife — which can
   murder and torture, or in a surgeon's hand save lives — its moral valence
   comes entirely from the end it is put to [^2].
4. This is shown by the fact that reason can equally defend opposite ends: it is
   rational to fight and die for liberty *and* rational to fight and die for a
   fascist, Communist, or Islamist dictatorship, depending on what you value
   [^3].
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) A defender of Enlightenment rationalism
   would reply that genuine, fully-informed reason, applied impartially, does
   converge on moral conclusions — e.g., that one's own interests carry no
   special weight, which grounds fairness and benevolence. Prager's response is
   that this still presupposes a prior commitment (impartiality, the value of
   others) that reason itself does not supply; the premise is smuggled in.

## Best Quotes

> "Reason is just a tool. It is no more intrinsically moral than a knife. A
> knife can be used to murder or to torture people. But in the hands of a
> surgeon, it can be used to save lives."[^2]

> "Reason leads to good only when you want it to. Just as it leads to bad when
> you want it to."[^2]
