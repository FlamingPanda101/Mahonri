# Science Can Determine Human Values

## The Point
Harris's signature and most provocative claim: "morality should be considered an undeveloped branch of science."[^1] Because well-being "entirely depends on events in the world and on states of the human brain," there "must be scientific truths to be known about it."[^2] He is explicit that this is not the banal claim that science can *describe* what people call morality, nor merely help us get what we want; rather, "science can, in principle, help us understand what we should do and should want."[^3] So "there are right and wrong answers to moral questions, just as there are right and wrong answers to questions of physics."[^3]

## Evidence
Harris defines "science" broadly, "our best effort to understand what is going on in this universe," not a few of its tools like mathematical modeling.[^4] He distinguishes two senses of "objective" (borrowed from John Searle): epistemological (unbiased, evidence-respecting) versus ontological (mind-independent). Moral facts are *ontologically subjective* — they concern experience — but can still be studied with *epistemological objectivity*, exactly as a neuroscientist studies depression by correlating first-person reports with brain states.[^5] He insists our inability to answer a question in practice "says nothing about whether the question itself has an answer," using the example of how many people were bitten by mosquitoes in the last minute: unanswerable in practice, yet it "admits of simple numerical answers."[^6] He offers candidate scientific moral questions — would a billion dollars do more good against racism or malaria? are "white" lies or gossip more harmful to relationships?[^7] — and points to existing findings (e.g. corporal punishment is "a disastrous practice") as down payments.[^8] He also rebuts the consensus double standard: scientific controversy is taken to mean "further work remains," but moral controversy is taken to prove "there can be no such thing as moral truth," even though "truth has nothing, in principle, to do with consensus."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "I will argue that morality should be considered an undeveloped branch of science." [^1]

> "I am arguing that science can, in principle, help us understand what we should do and should want… My claim is that there are right and wrong answers to moral questions, just as there are right and wrong answers to questions of physics." [^3]

> "Our inability to answer a question says nothing about whether the question itself has an answer." [^6]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's strongest insight is the *answers-in-practice vs answers-in-principle* distinction — a theist can grant it wholesale. The trouble is the leap from "there are objective moral facts" to "*science* determines them." **(1) Science measures the means, not the end.** Once you fix well-being as the goal, empirical inquiry can indeed tell you how to reach it — but Harris concedes science "cannot tell us why, scientifically, we should value health," only that *given* the goal we can pursue it (pp.29–30). That concession is fatal to the bold thesis: the *foundational* value is imported from outside science, so it is not science that "determines values" but a prior, un-argued axiom. **(2) The "values built into objectivity" admission cuts the other way.** Harris grants that the very practice of science "has values built into it" — logical consistency, reliance on evidence, parsimony (p.13). But these epistemic norms are themselves *oughts* the naturalist cannot derive from any brain-scan; they are presupposed, which is precisely the theist's point that reason and value are more fundamental than matter (compare [[Reason Presupposes Emotion - The Neuroscience Reply to Materialism]] and the regress problem). **(3) Category, not maturity.** Calling morality an "undeveloped branch of science" assumes the only thing missing is data. But "ought," obligation, and the dignity that grounds duties are not the sort of thing a microscope finds; they are metaphysical, and on theism they are grounded in God as the source of being, intelligibility, and goodness ([[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]], [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]). Just as Harris rightly says there is no "Christian physics," the theist replies there is likewise no *merely empirical* ethics — moral truth is real (here Harris and the natural-law tradition agree, see [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]) but it is known by reason reflecting on the human good, not discovered the way cholera is.
