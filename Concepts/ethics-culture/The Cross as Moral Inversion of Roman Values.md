# The Cross as Moral Inversion of Roman Values

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] argues the cross effected a total inversion of the ancient world's value system. Crucifixion was the most agonizing and *humiliating* Roman death — reserved paradigmatically for slaves, a public billboard of state power over the powerless. For Christianity to claim that the *crucified* man triumphs over his crucifier, and is in fact God, turned the strong-over-weak ethic of antiquity on its head. The scandal was so deep that even early Christians were embarrassed by it, and Western moderns are now so *desensitized* to the cross that they no longer feel the inversion that produced their own moral instincts.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Crucifixion was maximal humiliation, not just pain.** Holland insists on the unsanitized reality: the victim was stripped fully naked (the loincloth in art is a later softening), suspended as a public spectacle, unable to ward off birds pecking at his eyes, left "like a slab of meat." It was "the most agonizing but also the most humiliating form of death imaginable," visited especially on slaves. So the claim that such a victim is Lord is not merely surprising — it is, by Roman lights, insane.[^2] *(Strong — accurate to Roman penal practice; the humiliation dimension is central and often forgotten.)*
2. **Paul is himself embarrassed by the cross.** The earliest Christian writer, [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]], puts the crucified Christ at the center of his preaching while conceding it is "a stumbling block" to Jews and "foolishness" to Greeks ([[1 Corinthians 1.23|1 Cor 1:23]]); for Romans the madness was not that a man could be a god (Caesar was deified) but that a man who died a *slave's* death could be. Holland reads Paul as "constantly trying to preempt objections" — direct evidence the inversion was felt as scandalous at the origin.[^3] *(Strong — a defensible reading of Paul's rhetoric.)*
3. **Art was reluctant to depict the reality for ~500 years.** Even after Constantine, Christian art shied from portraying crucifixion's horror: the British Museum ivory casket shows a buff, relaxed, "Instagram-influencer" Christ "winning first prize at the Olympics." The first depictions of Christ *dead* on the cross appear only just before the first millennium; only in the high Middle Ages does Latin Christendom become "obsessed with the sufferings of Christ." The long desensitization since is why moderns treat the cross as an "abstract" logo.[^4] *(Strong as art-historical observation.)*
4. **Nietzsche alone among atheists "gets" the cross.** Holland — himself agnostic — argues [[Friedrich Nietzsche]], "the most brilliant of all atheist writers in the West," grasped what is radical and subversive about the cross better than centuries of theologians: he saw it as the charter of a "slave religion" exalting the weak over the strong, mingling resentment with hostility toward the strong — and *hated* it for exactly that reason, holding that strength and health should be celebrated. Nietzsche's revulsion is backhanded testimony to the reality of the inversion. See [[The Dominion Thesis - Western Values Are Christianized]].[^5] *(A rhetorically powerful move: the inversion is confirmed by its sharpest enemy.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A skeptic replies that (a) value-inversions of "the last shall be first" type appear in other traditions (the Hebrew prophets, some Buddhist and Stoic strands), so the cross is not uniquely responsible; and (b) Christianity's *practice* often re-inverted the inversion — crusades, inquisitions, and a church allied with worldly power hardly embodied exaltation of the weak, which suggests the "value system" was more aspirational than operative. Holland grants both that Christianity has "many rivers" feeding it and that its history is a "pulsing matrix of paradoxes" practiced monstrously at times; his claim is genealogical (the *source* of the modern instinct that the victim deserves sympathy) not a clean-hands historical record. The reductio for the skeptic is that the very standard used to condemn Christian cruelty — that the powerful must not crush the weak — is itself the cross's inheritance.

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration intensifies rather than dilutes the inversion: the atonement is framed as the infinite God descending below all things (D&C 122:8, "the Son of Man hath descended below them all"), and Book of Mormon Christology centers a suffering, condescending Christ (1 Nephi 11; Mosiah 3). Holland's thesis equips the apologist to argue that the modern moral reflexes critics use against the Church (concern for the marginalized, suspicion of power) are downstream of the very cross the Church preaches. Compare the institutional-fruits version of the argument in [[The Compassion Revolution of the Cross]].
