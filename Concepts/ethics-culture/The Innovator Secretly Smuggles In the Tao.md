# The Innovator Secretly Smuggles In the Tao

## The Point
Lewis's name for the would-be reformer who claims to ground morality on something other than the *Tao* is "the Innovator."[^1] The Innovator tries to rebuild ethics on a "scientific" footing — typically on **instinct** (e.g., the herd or self-preservation instinct) or on "**the good of society**" / "the preservation of the species."[^2] Lewis's reply is that the Innovator always *smuggles the Tao back in*: instinct can tell you that you *do* feel an urge, but it cannot tell you that you *ought* to obey it; and "the good of society" is only a goal worth pursuing if you already grant that you *ought* to pursue it. That "ought" is never supplied by instinct or by facts about society — it is borrowed, silently, from the very traditional morality the Innovator means to replace.[^3]

## Evidence
Ward notes that "almost a third of this second lecture, on 'The Way,' discusses instinct," prompted partly by listeners who had written to Lewis after his 1941 BBC talks insisting instinct was the real basis of morality.[^4] Lewis presses the dilemma in stark either-or form: "Either these words conceal a value judgement passed upon the instinct… or else they merely record its felt intensity."[^5] To say we *ought* to follow an instinct is already to stand within the *Tao*; to say only that the instinct feels strong is to say nothing moral at all. Ward records that even Gaius and Titius, and the wider debunkers, "have in the background values of their own which they believe to be immune from the debunking process" — values they treat as objective even while declaring all value subjective.[^6] J. R. Lucas (quoted by Ward) sees the strategic point: Lewis "cites the impossibility of deriving an 'ought' from an 'is' in order to discredit all alternative systems of values that start out by denying the validity of value judgments generally so as to sweep away traditional morality and leave the field clear for the new system."[^7] Micah Watson adds that once Gaius and Titius "are forced to explain why posterity or economic success are goods and some other good is not, they are back in the arena of objective morality."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Either these words conceal a value judgement passed upon the instinct… or else they merely record its felt intensity." (Lewis) [^5]

## LDS Connection
The Innovator's hidden borrowing is precisely the trap that catches modern naturalist ethics. [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] tries to ground morality on a scientific fact — the well-being of conscious creatures — yet must still smuggle in the unproven value-premise that we *ought* to maximize that well-being (the is/ought problem; see [[You Cannot Derive an Ought From an Is]] and [[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]]). Latter-day Saint thought locates the missing "ought" where Lewis's argument points: in an objective, eternal moral law written on the conscience of every person by the **Light of Christ**, which "giveth light to every man that cometh into the world" and lets all "judge… that which is good" (Moroni 7:16-19). Because that standard is real and God-given, the debunker who appeals to "society's good" is, without admitting it, leaning on the very moral law he means to deny — exactly Lewis's charge.
