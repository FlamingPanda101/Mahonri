# Noticing Compassion Elsewhere Is Itself Christian

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (O'Connor): compassion and care for the weak appear in many cultures (Buddhism, Confucianism, Hippocrates), so these values cannot be uniquely Christian — see [[Western Values Predate or Arise Independently of Christianity]].

## The Rebuttal

[[Glenn Scrivener]] argues this very act of *selecting* the admirable elements of other cultures is performed through a Christianized lens. "The fact that we notice examples of compassion in other cultures is a profoundly Christian thing itself."[^1]

1. **The lens argument.** We praise Buddhist "compassion" and Confucian "right relationship" but recoil from "Spartan brutality" and Roman "dominance." Scrivener: "What are the lenses we're looking through in order to cite other moral sources? I would say those are being profoundly Christianized."[^2] *(Subtle and strong — our criteria for what counts as "the good bits" of a culture may themselves be inherited.)*

2. **The Good Samaritan as paradigm.** In [[Luke 10.30-37|Luke 10:30-37]], Jesus makes the outsider (the Samaritan) the moral exemplar over the "people of the book." So the Christian frame intrinsically *commends* compassion wherever found: "I want to say yay and amen to Hippocrates."[^3] *(Strong internal consistency — the thesis is not "only Christians are good," which would be easily refuted.)*

3. **Steelman of O'Connor's reply.** O'Connor: "I'm only noticing that there because *you're* telling me it's indebted to Christianity. So of course I'm going to go and look elsewhere and say, look, it shows up here."[^4] More decisively, he argues the *existence* of the parallel value defeats the *uniqueness* claim **regardless** of why he noticed it — the lens argument explains his attention, not the historical facts.[^5] *(This is the key rejoinder: even granting the Christianized lens, Hammurabi's code still says what it says. The lens argument risks being unfalsifiable in the way O'Connor objects to elsewhere — see [[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]].)*

4. **Where did Hippocrates get it?** Scrivener's deeper move: "But where did *he* get that idea from? There is such a thing as common grace."[^6] *(This concedes the value pre-exists Christianity historically while attributing it to God theologically — which O'Connor notes undercuts the *historical-causation* version of the thesis. See [[The Common Grace Concession Undercuts the Causation Claim]].)*
