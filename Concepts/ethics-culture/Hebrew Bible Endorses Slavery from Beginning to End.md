# Hebrew Bible Endorses Slavery from Beginning to End

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] affirms — agreeing with Joshua HS on this single point — that "the Bible does endorse slavery from beginning to end. If it doesn't endorse it, it at the very least condones it." Where Joshua leverages this to argue slavery "is not inherently evil," McClellan argues the opposite conclusion: the Bible is morally wrong on this matter, and "we know better now."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Explicit chattel permission (rated: strong; primary text).** [[Leviticus 25.44-46|Leviticus 25:44-46]] authorizes buying foreign slaves as heritable "possession" passed to one's children "for ever." This is unambiguous endorsement, not mere description.[^2] See [[Hebrew Bible Endorses Chattel Slavery of Foreigners]].

2. **Two institutions, both condoned (rated: strong; primary text).** The Hebrew Bible regulates both Israelite debt-bondage ([[Leviticus 25.39-43|Leviticus 25:39-43]], [[Deuteronomy 15.12-15|Deuteronomy 15]]) and foreign chattel slavery (Lev 25:44-46). Neither is condemned; both are legislated as normal.[^3] See [[Two Distinct Slaveries Debt-Bondage and Chattel]].

3. **Permitted abuse (rated: strong; primary text).** [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]] permits beating an enslaved person who survives "a day or two," with no punishment, "for he is his money."[^4] See [[Bible Permits Beating Slaves Short of Immediate Death]].

4. **Steelman of the apologetic.** The strongest version of Joshua's case is that Israelite debt-bondage included real protections — Jubilee release, a release-gift, eye/tooth manumission — that were genuinely more humane than later slaveries. McClellan grants such protections existed **for Israelite debt slaves** but shows they did not extend to foreign chattel slaves, who were the heritable property the law explicitly allowed to be ruled "with rigour."[^3]

5. **Scholarly framing (rated: moderate; consensus).** McClellan's position reflects mainstream historical-critical scholarship: the biblical legal corpora reflect ancient Near Eastern slave law and do not abolish or condemn the institution.[^1]

## Best Quotes

> "He's right to point out that the Bible does endorse slavery from beginning to end. If it doesn't endorse it, it at the very least condones it. So Joshua is not off the mark in acknowledging that. However, we know better now. The Bible was wrong."[^1]

> "The Bible just does not say that... No, the Bible doesn't say that. And the Bible is wrong just like you are wrong. And it's up to us to determine what's right."[^5]
