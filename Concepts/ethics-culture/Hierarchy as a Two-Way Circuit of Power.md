# Hierarchy as a Two-Way Circuit of Power

## The Point
The Head-Chest-Belly model is *hierarchical*, and Ward pauses to rehabilitate the idea of "rule" for modern readers who hear in it only domination. Lewis's vision of the human person is "essentially a unity, but a unity of unlikes, such as a family" — neither a chaos of competing elements, nor a flat uniformity, nor "a stultifying hegemony of one element over the others," but **unity in diversity**, the parts "hierarchically arranged but equally necessary." [^1] Properly understood, power is not a thunderbolt hurled from high to low but "an energy continually surging back and forth between two parties," a two-way street, "a kind of electrical circuit or magnetic field, something in which the south pole is as necessary as the north pole." [^2]

## Evidence
Ward (quoting his own essay on Lewis and love) lays out **four corruptions of power**, all departures from the hierarchical principle: *tyranny* (ruling natural equals), *servility* (obeying natural equals), *rebellion* (failing to obey a natural superior), and *remissness* (failing to rule a natural inferior, as when a teacher lets a class run riot). [^2] Tellingly, tyranny — the abuse that dominates modern fears — "is only the flip-side of servility": tyrant and slave, rebel and negligent ruler, feed off one another. [^2] This model has deep roots: it mirrors St. Paul's image of the body, where every part is needed and "those parts which are thought less honourable" are "invested with the greater honour" (1 Corinthians 12:12-26), and Lewis fictionalizes it in the peacefully stratified planet of *Out of the Silent Planet*, with its three different but harmoniously related races. [^1] Judith Wolfe, quoted by Ward, presses the point: hierarchical structure, "far from necessarily being external to (and imposed upon) a person's identity, is the condition of personhood as such, and is reciprocal in its workings." [^3] Relationships between unequals — parent and child, governor and governed — are not inherently evil; rightly exercised, "power really does work to the benefit of everyone." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "What defines the person is neither a centrifugal heterogeneity of elements, nor a radical uniformity of elements, nor a stultifying hegemony of one element over the others, but unity in diversity, in which the elements are hierarchically arranged but equally necessary." [^1]

> "it is better to think of power not as a thunderbolt, striking down randomly, violently, mercilessly from above, but as a kind of electrical circuit or magnetic field, something in which the south pole is as necessary as the north pole." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's reciprocal hierarchy maps closely onto the Doctrine and Covenants' theology of authority: priesthood power can be exercised "only by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness" (D&C 121:41), and "amen to the priesthood" of any man who attempts to "exercise control or dominion or compulsion" in the spirit of the tyrant (D&C 121:37). The Restoration's ideal of presidency, like Lewis's "two-way street," is service that flows both directions and benefits the governed — the opposite of "unrighteous dominion." Paul's body image that Ward invokes also undergirds the LDS view of differentiated callings within one Church: a "unity of unlikes," every member indispensable, hierarchy ordered to love rather than to power for its own sake.
