# Reason Can Justify Immoral Acts When You Can Get Away With Them

## The Point

If self-interest is the standard, then doing wrong is often perfectly
*rational*. When the benefits of an immoral act outweigh the risk of getting
caught, a purely cost-benefit calculation recommends the wrong action. So
rationality and morality come apart: the rational choice and the right choice
are frequently not the same.

## Argument

1. Cheating on a test is clearly *wrong*. But is it *irrational*? Not obviously —
   if you can get away with it and it means a better school or job, cheating
   "may well be reasonable" [^1].
2. The same logic generalizes: a shady-but-lucrative business deal, or marital
   infidelity, can be rational whenever you can get away with it and judge the
   benefits to outweigh the risk of being caught [^2].
3. So the question "why not do it?" has no purely rational answer once the only
   inputs are personal benefit and probability of detection [^2].
4. Therefore reason does not by itself forbid wrongdoing; morality must come from
   somewhere other than rational self-interest.
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) A sophisticated rationalist (e.g., a
   contractarian or rule-consequentialist) argues that it is *not* in fact
   rational to cheat, because honesty as a stable disposition serves one's
   long-run interests, sustains the cooperative institutions one depends on, and
   protects against the risk and anxiety of exposure. Prager's reply is that
   this works only probabilistically — where detection is genuinely impossible
   and the gain is large, the calculus flips, and "rational" still points the
   wrong way.

## Best Quotes

> "If you can get away with it, and it might mean the difference between getting
> into a great school or getting a great job, cheating on a test may well be
> reasonable."[^1]

> "If you know you can get away with it, or simply judge that the benefits of
> doing something illegal or immoral outweigh the risk of being caught, why not
> do it?"[^2]
