# Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology

## The Point
Turek's central move in the Morality chapter is to distinguish how we *know* the moral law (epistemology) from why the moral law *exists* and has authority over us (ontology). Atheists can perfectly well know that rape and murder are wrong without believing in God — but that does nothing to explain the *ground* of that wrongness.[^1] The book-without-an-author analogy frames the whole chapter: "You can know what a book says while denying there's an author. But there would be no book to know unless there was an author. Likewise, atheists can know objective morality while denying God exists, but there would be no objective morality unless God exists."[^2]

## Evidence
Turek explicitly denies the strawman that "you have to believe in God to be a good person" or that "you need the Bible to know basic right and wrong." He grants that "everyone knows basic right and wrong whether they believe in God or have the Bible or not" and notes this is what Romans 2 already teaches.[^3] What he claims instead is narrow: atheists "can't *justify* morality" — they can make moral judgments but "can provide no objective basis for those judgments."[^4]

He charges Sam Harris with exactly this confusion: *The Moral Landscape* answers the question of *method* (how science can discover what promotes well-being) but never the question of *ground* (why a moral law exists at all and why it binds us). "Harris seems to think that because we can know objective morality (epistemology), that explains why objective morality exists in the first place (ontology)."[^5] One may learn morality "from parents, teachers, society, your conscience"; none of those routes of *knowing* supplies a *foundation*.[^6] Likewise he tells atheist David Silverman he is "confusing how we know the moral standard (epistemology) with the existence of a moral standard (ontology)."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "atheists can know objective morality while denying God exists, but there would be no objective morality unless God exists." [^2]

> "What I am saying is that atheists can't justify morality. They can act morally and judge some actions as being moral and others immoral... But they can provide no objective basis for those judgments." [^4]

> "The issue isn't how we know what's Right, but why an authoritative standard of Rightness exists in the first place." [^8]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint thought affirms both halves cleanly. On the epistemic side, the Light of Christ is "given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16) — the LDS analogue to Turek's "everyone knows basic right and wrong" and to Paul's law "written in their hearts" ([[Romans 2.6-13]]). On the ontological side, the moral law is grounded not merely in a divine decree but in *eternal law* that God himself honors and administers ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]) — so the LDS "ground" is even less arbitrary than Turek's bare formulation, while landing on the same conclusion against the naturalist. This is the same objective-value case Michael Ward's gloss on Lewis makes for the Tao: the moral law can be *argued from* but never *argued to*, and recognizing it is recovery, not invention ([[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]; [[The Tao Cannot Be Argued To Only Argued From]]; [[The Tao as Homecoming - Objective Morality Recovered, Not Invented]]).
