# The Tao Cannot Be Argued To Only Argued From

## The Point
Ward identifies the single positive thesis hidden beneath *The Abolition of Man*'s mostly negative case: there is only one ethical system and **we already stand within it**.[^1] Borrowing from Lewis's essay "On Ethics," Ward states the point in Kantian terms — "ultimate ethical injunctions have always been premises, never conclusions… the imperative is categorical. Unless the ethical is assumed from the outset, no argument will bring you to it."[^2] The *Tao* is therefore, in Ward's striking phrase, "a defence of the indefensible" — "indefensible" because it "cannot be argued *to*, it has to be argued *from*."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward explains the logic with two images. The *Tao* "grounds all our morality, even our immoral attempts to overturn it, just as the Earth upholds us as we dig our own graves."[^4] And any attempt to step back and inspect the ground we stand on "will turn into an infinite regress unless we realise that a fundamental truth of this kind ('I am standing on the Earth' — 'I have a moral nature') is different from all subsequent truths, which is why it is rightly called fundamental."[^5] Ward reports that students "often have difficulty in grasping this point, because they presume that all points must be equally graspable," whereas "this fundamental point is qualitatively unique"; when they finally see it, "it is a breakthrough moment, a paradigm shift."[^6] Lewis's "overarching and undergirding purpose," Ward writes, "is to demonstrate that the Tao grasps us; we are already within the Tao, and always remain within it, often without realising it, whether we like it or not."[^7]

Ward links this to Lewis's later book *Miracles*, where he faced "a similar problem about how to communicate an inescapable, preconditioning status" — there, reason itself, "that immaterial medium which carries and colours all our rational thoughts."[^8] Lewis likens reason to native grammar (effortless in your mother tongue), to healthy breathing (unnoticed until you are ill), and to bats' eyesight in daylight, to show how we always already "Enjoy" reason rather than "Contemplate" it.[^9] The same is true of the moral *Tao*: it is "a truth too close to observe."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Ultimate ethical injunctions have always been premises, never conclusions. Kant was perfectly right on that point at least: the imperative is categorical. Unless the ethical is assumed from the outset, no argument will bring you to it." (Lewis, "On Ethics") [^2]

> "The Tao cannot be argued *to*, it has to be argued *from*. The Tao grounds all our morality, even our immoral attempts to overturn it, just as the Earth upholds us as we dig our own graves." [^3]

> "The Tao grasps us; we are already within the Tao, and always remain within it, often without realising it, whether we like it or not." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Ward's point that the moral law "grasps us" before we ever reason about it parallels the Latter-day Saint doctrine of the **Light of Christ**, the moral knowledge given to "every man that cometh into the world" so that all "may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16-19) — a light that is itself "the law by which all things are governed" (D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]). On this view conscience is not a conclusion we build but a premise God plants: the moral sense is **received, not engineered**. This directly exposes the limit of naturalist projects like [[Science Can Determine Human Values]] and [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]], which attempt to *argue to* objective value from neutral facts about well-being; Lewis and Ward reply that no chain of factual premises can reach a first moral premise — value must be argued *from*, never *to* (cf. [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
