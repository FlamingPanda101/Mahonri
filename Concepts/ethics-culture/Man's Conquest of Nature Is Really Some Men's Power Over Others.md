# Man's Conquest of Nature Is Really Some Men's Power Over Others

## The Point
In the climactic third chapter of *The Abolition of Man*, Lewis dismantles the comfortable phrase "Man's conquest of Nature." As Ward glosses it, what is really happening whenever a new power is acquired is that *some* men gain power over *other* men, with Nature merely the instrument through which that power passes.[^1] The individual who flies, listens to the radio, or uses a contraceptive is not exercising his own private power over Nature but is depending on "other men" who hold the levers.[^2] The phrase "Man's conquest of Nature" conceals this by abstraction.

## Evidence
Ward stresses that Lewis is at this stage being **descriptive, not evaluative** — there is nothing wrong in itself with the airplane, the wireless, or the contraceptive; the human race as a whole has genuinely gained "power over gravity, power over radio waves, and power over fertility."[^2] The danger is not that "wicked airlines and broadcasters and contraceptive manufacturers have made a power-grab."[^3] The problem arises only once these powers are exercised **outside the Tao**: at that moment "the actual location of these new powers is exposed as residing with some people, not equally with all people," and those with their hands on the levers become "Conditioners."[^3] Ward notes that before this epoch of lawlessness, the powerful still held themselves "under a responsibility to use those powers for the common good" — *noblesse oblige* — but the new situation "will be one not of inequality but of anarchy."[^4] The shift is not a change in moral *weather* but a change in moral *climate*: "It will never again be warm. We have moved to the North Pole."[^4] Ward draws on Filostrato in *That Hideous Strength* ("There is no such thing as Man — it is a word. There are only men") as the nominalist error Lewis opposes: Lewis holds that "Man" is a real universal whose integrity "is founded on life within the Tao."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "While we speak from within the Tao we can speak of Man having power over himself in a sense truly analogous to an individual's self-control." (Lewis) [^5]

> "What looked like a power-grab by elites is really a shared lawlessness: the elite and the masses together are complicit because of their shared disregard for the Tao." (Ward's paraphrase of Lewis) [^3]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's warning that humanity can be reduced to an instrument for the will of a powerful few resonates with the Latter-day Saint conviction that every person possesses a **divine nature and eternal worth** that no one — not even a benevolent elite — may rightfully override. The "Conditioners" who manipulate others mirror Satan's plan to compel and remake souls "without their concurring voice," whereas the plan of the Father preserves **moral agency** as the very condition of human flourishing (cf. 2 Nephi 2:27; Moses 4:1-4). Lewis's insistence that "Man" is a real family bound by a shared moral law parallels the doctrine that all are children of one Heavenly Father, each illuminated by the **Light of Christ**, so that the powerful are not owners of the powerless but stewards under a law that "can over-arch rulers and ruled alike." Against [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]], which would entrust human values to expert measurement of well-being, Lewis answers that any power exercised outside an objective moral order collapses into coercion.
