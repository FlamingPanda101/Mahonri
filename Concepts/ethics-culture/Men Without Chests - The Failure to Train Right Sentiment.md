# Men Without Chests - The Failure to Train Right Sentiment

## The Point
"Men without chests" is Lewis's image for what a subjectivist education produces: people with intellect ("head") and appetite ("belly") but no trained moral sentiment ("chest") to mediate between them.[^1] Ward explains that for Lewis the "chest" is the seat of stable, rightly ordered emotions — the faculty by which a person feels approval of what truly merits approval — and that it is precisely this faculty that the philosophy of "The Green Book" destroys.[^2]

## Evidence
Ward presents Lewis's anthropology as a sketch in three parts: in "the head" we have thoughts, in "the belly" sensations, and only when the rational and the sensual are integrated "in stable sentiments, located in 'the chest,'" do we "really discover ourselves as human beings."[^3] The philosophy of Gaius and Titius is "dehumanizing," Lewis maintains, "because it makes for 'men without chests,' people who have heads and bellies but no capacity to unite their cerebral self and their visceral self in their thorax, which is the definitively human faculty."[^4] Ward distills the lecture in E.M. Forster's phrase "Only connect": "Only connect the angel and the animal and you will have the anthropological."[^5] The deep point is that emotions are not morally neutral; they can be trained to correspond to reality. A man *ought* to feel that a waterfall is sublime or that dying for a good cause is meritorious — and an education that tells him such feelings are "nothing but feelings" leaves him incapable of the very responses that make him human, while still demanding (absurdly) that he be brave, just, and self-sacrificing. Lewis grants that the subjectivists are inconsistent — "they are better than their principles" — since they still want us to grant that they have said something *true*, not merely that they "feel that way."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> A subjectivist education makes for "men without chests, people who have heads and bellies but no capacity to unite their cerebral self and their visceral self." [^4]

> "Only connect the angel and the animal and you will have the anthropological. The human being is a synthesis of the human brain and the human belly in the human breast." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's "chest" — the cultivated capacity to love what is good and recoil from what is evil — corresponds to the Latter-day Saint understanding of **conscience** informed by the **Light of Christ**, by which a person is "enticed to do good... and to love God" and can "judge... that which is good" (Moroni 7:16-19). The claim that right feeling can and must be *trained* to match an **objective, eternal moral law** fits the LDS emphasis on developing Christlike virtue and using moral **agency** to align the heart with God's will, not merely the intellect. A culture that produces "men without chests" — articulate but morally hollow — is, in LDS terms, one that has dimmed the light of conscience God gives to all. This directly answers the naturalist assumption behind [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] that moral motivation can be reduced to brain-states alone: Lewis insists the *ordering* of the affections toward a real good outside us is what makes a human being whole.
