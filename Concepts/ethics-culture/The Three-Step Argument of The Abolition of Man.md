# The Three-Step Argument of The Abolition of Man

## The Point
Ward summarizes Lewis's book as a single argument unfolding across its three lectures — "Men Without Chests," "The Way," and "The Abolition of Man" — which Ward frames as a check-up, a diagnosis, and a prognosis.[^1] Step one: there is an objective moral order (the *Tao*), and to deny it produces "men without chests." Step two: that order is universal and can only be reformed from the inside. Step three: a civilization that finally abandons objective value will not liberate humanity but abolish it.[^2]

## Evidence
**Step 1 — Men Without Chests.** Against the subjectivism of "The Green Book," Lewis argues that some objects genuinely merit certain responses; a waterfall really can be sublime. The properly formed person unites "head" (reason) and "belly" (appetite) through trained sentiment located in "the chest." Strip that away and you get "men without chests" — "for by his intellect he is mere spirit and by his appetite mere animal."[^3]

**Step 2 — The Way (the *Tao*).** Lewis contends that moral reality is not only objective but universally recognized. Even Gaius and Titius, who say value is subjective, act as though it is not.[^4] He marshals "a diverse chorus of voices" — Plato, Aristotle, Horace, Jesus, St. Paul, Locke, Confucius, Shelley — to show "the human family is a real family, with certain core values in common."[^5] The *Tao* "is not something that human beings simply make up; it is something they discover," and it can be refined only from within: "As soon as one steps outside the Tao, one has stepped into the void."[^6]

**Step 3 — The Abolition of Man.** Here Lewis "shifts up a gear into full prophetic mode."[^7] Once objective value is discarded, the solution to life's problems is sought not in "knowledge, self-discipline, and virtue" but in "willpower, technological control, and surgical alteration of nature."[^8] A managing minority of "Conditioners" will admit no moral constraint, and the conditioned masses will have "no grounds for disputing any enormity." Stepping outside the *Tao*, the Conditioners "ceased to be human"; their subjects become not men but "artefacts."[^9] In conquering human nature, man defeats himself, "for this time the being who stood to gain and the being who has been sacrificed are one and the same."[^10] The remedy Lewis prescribes is blunt: "dogmatic belief in objective value," needed "to the very idea of a rule which is not tyranny or an obedience which is not slavery."[^11]

## Best Quotes
> "It is by this middle element that man is man: for by his intellect he is mere spirit and by his appetite mere animal." (Lewis) [^3]

> "For this time the being who stood to gain and the being who has been sacrificed are one and the same." (Lewis) [^10]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's three steps map closely onto Latter-day Saint moral theology. The **objective, eternal moral law** of step one corresponds to the truth that right and wrong are real and God-given, not human inventions; the universal **Tao** of step two parallels the **Light of Christ**, which "giveth light to every man" so that all people in every culture can know good from evil (Moroni 7:16-19; D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]). Step three — the warning that abandoning that law abolishes our humanity — resonates with the LDS conviction that moral **agency** exercised against eternal law is self-destructive (cf. 2 Nephi 2). Where [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] and [[Science Can Determine Human Values]] try to rebuild objective ethics on neuroscience and well-being alone, Lewis insists no such reconstruction can stand "outside the Tao" — value must be received, not engineered.
