# The Adverbial Ethic - Intent Makes the Sacrifice Acceptable

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the Stoics held an **adverbial ethic**: an action (e.g. helping an old woman across the street) has no moral standing in itself "until you understand the intent" — whether it was done lovingly, sincerely, or begrudgingly. The *adverb* makes the act moral. He matches this to the New Testament's emphasis on the heart over the outward deed (the widow's mite; intent over the size of the sacrifice).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The adverb makes the act (rated: the framework).** "If we designate an action X as moral… for a Stoic, that action in and of itself does not have moral standing until you understand the intent… The adverb is what informs the action and makes it moral or not."[^1]
2. **Seneca on sacrifice (rated: textual parallel).** Seneca: "the honor paid to the gods lies not in the victim for sacrifice, though they be fat and glitter with gold, but in the upright and holy desire of the worshipper. Good men… are pleasing to the gods with the offering of meal and gruel; bad men do not escape impiety, although they dye the altars with streams of blood."[^2]
3. **The widow's mite as the Christian parallel (rated: textual).** Luke 21: the rich cast in of their abundance, but the poor widow "hath cast in all she had" — "it's the intent, the sacrifice, that's the important thing. That is a Stoic idea."[^3]
4. **The Sermon-on-the-Mount intensifications (rated: the reading).** "You have heard… thou shalt not kill… but I say, whoever is angry…"; "whoever looks on a woman to lust… hath committed adultery in his heart" — apparent impossibilities that, for a Stoic, are easy: the point "is not about the listing of the things, but about having the right orientation at the very beginning" (the *kathēkon*, proper function), from which right action follows.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** The priority of inward disposition over mere outward conformity is deeply rooted in the **Hebrew prophets** (1 Samuel 15:22 "to obey is better than sacrifice"; Hosea 6:6 "I desired mercy, and not sacrifice"; Psalm 51:16–17 "a broken and contrite heart"; Isaiah 1 against empty offerings) — so the "intent over sacrifice" theme need not be borrowed from Stoicism at all. Convergent moral insight across traditions is the more cautious explanation than literary dependence. The Stoic parallels (Seneca on sacrifice) are genuine; the claim that the NT *got* the adverbial ethic *from* Stoicism is the contested step.

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
The adverbial ethic supports Thomassen's "preparatory gospel" thesis by showing the heart-over-ritual emphasis Christians prize was already a Stoic principle ([[Stoicism Was a Theoretical Preparatory Gospel]]). It resonates with the Restoration's "broken heart and contrite spirit" replacing animal sacrifice (3 Nephi 9:20) and with the vault's treatment of reason and disposition in morality ([[Reason Is the Organ of Morality]]).
