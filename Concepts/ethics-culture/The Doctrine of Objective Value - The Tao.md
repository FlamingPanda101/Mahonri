# The Doctrine of Objective Value - The Tao

## The Point
Behind Lewis's critique of the Green Book lies his positive thesis: the **doctrine of objective value** — "the belief that certain attitudes are really true, and others really false, to the kind of thing the universe is and the kind of things we are." Things possess qualities that *demand* our approval or disapproval; value is discovered, not invented. Lewis names this shared moral vision the ***Tao***, drawing the term from Confucius (the book's first epigraph: "He who sets to work on a different strand destroys the whole fabric") to signal that it is no parochial Western or Christian code but the common inheritance attested across the great traditions.[^1] Ward notes that the *Tao* "may be self-evident, but it is not obvious, and must be carefully taught."[^2]

## Evidence
Ward argues that one of Lewis's "keen insights" is the **seamlessness of the normative** (Cuneo): subjectivism "is really a thesis about a whole array of values and not merely moral ones." Practical rationality, prudence, morality, aesthetics, epistemology, and logic "are all normative domains and are thus so similar, that, on pain of arbitrariness, if one is a subjectivist about one domain one has to be a subjectivist about all the other domains."[^3] So the *Tao* is not one rulebook among many but the precondition of any reasoning whatever. Ward is careful that affirming objective value does *not* mean denying the subject: Lewis "is careful to avoid" reinforcing the "subject/object and value/fact divisions" that *generate* subjectivism. Citing Paul Holmer, Ward shows Lewis rejecting positivism's claim "that the language of science is uniquely objective" — "the fact-value distinction is, therefore, abrogated," for "to read [the world] with everything moral and esthetic abstracted out is to make it a caricature of itself."[^4] Notably, Lewis argues the case on purely philosophical grounds: he does "not rest the argument of Abolition on belief in God."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The Master said, He who sets to work on a different strand destroys the whole fabric." (Confucius, the book's epigraph) [^1]

> "If one is a subjectivist about one domain one has to be a subjectivist about all the other domains." [^3]

> "To read [the world] with everything moral and esthetic abstracted out is to make it a caricature of itself; to read it with these factors admitted is to see the very logic and sense of the world." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's *Tao* — an objective moral order written across all cultures — is a striking philosophical analogue to the Restoration's teaching of **eternal law** and the universal **Light of Christ**. Latter-day Saint scripture affirms moral law as objective and eternal: "all kingdoms have a law given" (D&C 88:38), and "there is a law, irrevocably decreed in heaven before the foundations of this world, upon which all blessings are predicated" (D&C 130:20–21). The mechanism by which the *Tao* is broadly available maps onto the Light of Christ, "given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16) and which "lighteth every man that cometh into the world" (D&C 93:2; John 1:9). Lewis's deliberately non-confessional argument therefore functions as natural-law evidence the Latter-day Saint can own: it is the head-on alternative to the naturalist project of *manufacturing* value from brain states ([[Science Can Determine Human Values]], [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]), and it dovetails with the [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]] that an objective moral order points beyond mere matter.
