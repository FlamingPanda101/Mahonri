# Suffering Is Objectively Real Not a Preference

## The Point
Having distinguished perception from preference (see [[Subjective Perception vs Subjective Preference]]), [[Jacob Hansen]] argues that suffering lands decisively on the *perception* side: it is interpreted through perception but not created by it. Suffering and its opposite are "arguably the most real thing that exists," the "ocean in which we swim as sentient beings." [^1] His proof is ostensive — put your hand on a hot stove and the effects "objectively exist neurologically physiologically psychologically." [^1] Calling that a mere "subjective happening" is not skepticism but absurdity. This supplies the objective anchor the metaethics needs (compare [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]]).

## Evidence
The argument is an appeal to the undeniability of pain. Hansen first locates suffering at the deepest level of conscious existence: "suffering and its opposite are arguably the most real thing that exists as it literally makes up the ocean in which we swim as sentient beings." [^1] Then the test case: "if you don't think so then just go place your hand on a hot stove. The effects objectively exist neurologically physiologically psychologically etc. Are we really going to act as though it was merely a subjective happening? No." [^1] The rhetorical force is that *no one actually lives* as though their burns were preferences. He grants the subjectivist's only true premise — "even if the experience of that objective reality come through your perceptions" — while denying the conclusion: experiencing X through perception does not make X a creation of the mind. [^1] Suffering is therefore a fact, and facts can ground "you ought" rather than "I prefer" (see [[Ought Appeals to Objective Reality Not Arbitrary Preference]]).

**Steelman of the critic:** Two objections remain. (1) *From real pain to objective morality is still a leap.* That my burn is neurologically real shows pain *exists*; it does not show anyone is *obligated* to prevent it, nor that *your* pain gives *me* a reason — the is–ought and agent-relativity gaps flagged against the Part 1 argument ([[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]) still apply. (2) *The hot-stove case is the easy one.* Physical pain has clear physiological markers, but much of morality concerns contested goods (justice, dignity, flourishing) whose "objective reality" is far less stove-like; the vividness of the example may not transfer. Hansen's own closing question — why is there "so much disagreement about morality"? — concedes that the move from undeniable suffering to a shared, action-guiding moral standard is not yet complete.

## LDS Connection
Restoration theology takes embodiment and felt experience as real and weighty, not illusory — "men are, that they might have joy" (2 Nephi 2:25), and joy's opposite is equally real ("it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things," 2 Nephi 2:11). Suffering and well-being are genuine features of the created order God himself answers to as eternal law ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]). That makes the reality of suffering a fit foundation for objective moral claims rather than a private preference — the same realist instinct behind treating the Light of Christ / conscience as perception of a real moral order.
