# Old Testament Repudiates Antebellum Slavery Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Joshua HS argued that the three things people associate with antebellum American slavery — slaves were (1) **kidnapped**, (2) had **no rights**, and (3) were **abused** — are "all three explicitly repudiated by the Old Testament law."[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **"Kidnapped" mischaracterizes the antebellum South too (rated: strong; historical).** McClellan: most enslaved people in the pre–Civil War South were not kidnapped but **bred** or purchased from prior enslavers; the kidnapping image is itself inaccurate. And the biblical kidnapping ban protects only Israelites.[^2] See [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]].

2. **"No rights" is the only partly-true item — and cuts both ways (rated: strong).** Enslaved people in the antebellum South "did actually have rights... laws on the books that were supposed to protect their rights, just like the Bible." The Bible gives us only the ostensible laws, not what happened on the ground.[^3] See [[Laws on the Books vs Facts on the Ground]].

3. **"Abuse" — the Bible permits it (rated: strong; primary text).** Far from repudiating abuse, [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]] permits beating a slave to death so long as death is delayed a day or two.[^4] See [[Bible Permits Beating Slaves Short of Immediate Death]].

4. **Sojourner texts are misread (rated: strong).** Joshua's "rights/love the sojourner" proof-texts concern free resident aliens, not slaves, and his appeal to "Leviticus 24" is hallucinated.[^5] See [[Sojourner Passages Are Misread as Slavery Protections]].

5. **Steelman.** The honest core of Joshua's case is that biblical law contains some humane provisions (manumission for maiming, Jubilee, kidnapping bans). McClellan grants these exist but shows each is narrower than claimed (Israelites only, or unenforced ideals), so they do not amount to a repudiation of slavery as practiced.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "All three of those are explicitly repudiated by the Old Testament law. — Uh, not really. The only one that I would say is the notion that they had no rights. But that's also a misrepresentation of what went on in the pre–Civil War American South because enslaved folks did actually have rights."[^3]

> "Joshua seems to be squinting too hard at passages about sojourners and imagining that they're talking about enslaved folks when they're not."[^5]
