# All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] builds his metaethics on a descriptive premise about being itself: conscious existence is lived "in a tension between two poles — suffering and its opposite, which… we'll call well-being." Every sane action is "consciously or subconsciously some move" away from suffering and "towards some conception of increased well-being." [^1][^2] This eudaimonist / well-being-first picture (drawn in the style of [[Sam Harris]]'s *Moral Landscape*) is the empirical anchor that the rest of the argument — [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]] — needs in order to ground morality objectively.

## Argument
1. **Being is the most basic fact, and for conscious creatures it is polarized.** "We exist" is maximally self-evident; but conscious being "seems to exist at all times in a tension between two poles: suffering and… well-being." Life is "flapping their wings against the constant gravitational like pull of suffering." *(phenomenological premise)* [^1]
2. **Every action is a move toward well-being.** Eating lunch (else "you would suffer"), watching Netflix (avoiding "the suffering of boredom"), helping a neighbor (avoiding a grinding conscience or expecting future benefit) — "keep trying to find an example… I have yet to find one." *(inductive challenge)* [^2]
3. **The great philosophers agree.** [[Blaise Pascal]]: "all men seek happiness. This is without exception… even those who hang themselves." [[Aristotle]]: "happiness therefore being found to be something final and self-sufficient is the end at which all actions aim." *(appeal to authority)* [^3][^4]
4. **Sacrifice is not a counterexample.** "No one ever chooses suffering for suffering's sake"; one sacrifices "because they believe that doing so will ultimately bring them more well-being," even if that payoff is "in an afterlife." A mother dies for her children because she "would rather die than live with herself" otherwise — her well-being is served. *(rebuttal of objection)* [^5]
5. **The contrary would be insanity.** "If any action meets the definition of insanity, it's to choose suffering with no expectation that suffering will lead to greater well-being in the future." So the thesis holds across "all sane human beings." *(reductio)* [^5]

**Steelman of the critic:** This is a version of **psychological egoism / hedonism**, and philosophers have long flagged two problems. (1) *Unfalsifiability:* by redefining every act — even genuine self-sacrifice — as secretly self-interested ("she'd rather die than live with herself"), the thesis can absorb any counterexample, which makes it look true by stipulation rather than by evidence. (2) *The is–ought gap:* even granting that we all *do* pursue well-being, it does not follow that we *ought* to, nor that *my* well-being gives *you* a reason. Hansen partly anticipates this by routing obligation through a hypothetical "if you desire X" (see [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]), but the critic notes this yields only hypothetical, agent-relative oughts unless a further bridge to *other people's* well-being is supplied — and Hansen's own closing question ("isn't well-being subjective?") concedes the load-bearing step is still owed.

## Best Quotes
> "In every action you and everyone around you is flapping their wings against the constant gravitational like pull of suffering and trying to fly higher and higher into the Skies of well-being." [^1]

> "Why did you watch Netflix last night? To avoid the suffering of boredom… keep trying to find an example of when you did something that can't ultimately be attributed to your quest for increased personal well-being. I have yet to find one." [^2]

> "All men seek happiness. This is without exception… even those who hang themselves." (Pascal) [^3]

> "No one ever chooses suffering for suffering's sake. They sacrifice because they believe that doing so will ultimately bring them more well-being." [^5]
