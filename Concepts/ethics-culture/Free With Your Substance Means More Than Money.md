# Free With Your Substance Means More Than Money

## The Claim
Reading her favorite verse, [[Jacob 2.17|Jacob 2:17]], [[Sharon Eubank]] argues that "be familiar with all, and free with your substance, that they may be rich like unto you" is not primarily about money. "Substance" includes "the dignity, the agency, the friendship, the prayer," and "rich like unto you" means rich "in friendship, in resources, and opportunities, and talent" — the gospel reorienting disciples "away from worrying about physical things to worrying about each other as human beings."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The audience makes it radical (strong, exegetical).** Eubank stresses Jacob addresses his own relatives — including the line of Laman and Lemuel, who had tried to kill Nephi. "Think of your brethren like unto yourselves" is the golden rule extended even to adversaries: "have that openness… I don't agree with you, but I'm willing to engage with you."[^1]
2. **"Free with your substance" is gift-broad (interpretive).** "Of course we think money… but also the dignity, the agency, the friendship, the prayer. I'm going to be free with the gifts that I've had with you." The verse authorizes giving the *non-material* goods that the exchange trades in.[^1]
3. **Reciprocity, not one-way charity (moderate).** "That they may be rich like unto you" frames the goal as the recipient's *enrichment to parity* — friendship, opportunity, talent — which Eubank ties to "reciprocity… pay it forward… the only thing we can give is our love and care."[^2]
4. **Coheres with the prayer story (strong, anecdote).** The friend who prayed nightly with Eubank gave "substance" that was not money at all — hearing another "pray for you… those words that you wish you would say for yourself" lifted a months-long spiritual paralysis. The gift was prayer, dignity, presence.[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A skeptic could read this as a convenient spiritualizing of a verse that, in context (Jacob 2 condemns the Nephites' love of riches and inequality), is pointedly *material*: Jacob's burden is that the rich share their literal wealth so "ye may be rich like unto you" temporally. Expanding "substance" to "friendship and prayer" risks letting the affluent off the economic hook the prophet is actually setting, substituting cheap intangibles for the costly redistribution Jacob demands.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank's reading does not *delete* the material sense — she repeatedly affirms money "creates opportunity and resource" and that "free with your substance" includes being free with money ("of course we think money, and money is good to be free"). Her addition is that the verse's *telos* ("rich like unto you") is holistic enrichment, of which material parity is one component alongside dignity and opportunity; this is an *expansion*, not a replacement, and it is anchored in the verse's own relational language ("be familiar with all"). The material demand stands; she insists it is necessary but not sufficient.

## Best Quotes
> "Free with your substance — of course, we think money… but also the dignity, the agency, the friendship, the prayer."[^1]

> "That they may be rich like unto you — rich in friendship, in resources, and opportunities, and talent."[^1]
