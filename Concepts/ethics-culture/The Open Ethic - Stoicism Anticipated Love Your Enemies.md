# The Open Ethic - Stoicism Anticipated Love Your Enemies

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the Stoics invented the **open (unbounded) ethic** — a universal obligation not tied to in-group relationships — and that this, not Judaism, is the closest precedent for the New Testament's "love your enemies." Where most ancient ethical systems (including the Mosaic law) are *closed* (defined by the in-group/out-group, e.g. Israelite vs. Gentile), the Stoics extended moral obligation to all humanity, which is why they alone used the abstract, relationship-free word *agape*.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Open vs. closed ethics (rated: the framework).** "Most ethical models are a closed moral system. Judaism is an example… the house of Israel and the Gentile and the obligations between the one and the other." The Stoics "are the first to create and put forward an open ethic, which means an unbounded ethic… the obligation for a Stoic is going to be universal."[^1]
2. **"Love your enemies" has no Old Testament precedent (rated: Thomassen's claim).** "There is not a single verse in the Old Testament that says love your enemies"; the closest is "love thy neighbor" (Leviticus 19:18), which is still in-group. By contrast Matthew 5:43–44 ("love your enemies… pray for them which despitefully use you") matches the Stoics.[^2] See [[Matthew 5.43-45]].
3. **The Stoic parallels predate the Gospels (rated: textual comparison).** [[Seneca]] (decades before Matthew, ~90 AD): "if you are imitating the gods, give benefits also to the ungrateful, for the sun also rises upon the wicked" (cf. Matthew 5:45, the sun rising "on the evil and on the good"); "return [an injury] with kindness"; "we shall never quit working for the common good, helping everyone, even our enemies." [[Epictetus]]: "one must love the one who flogs him as if he were the father or brother."[^3][^4]
4. **Why the "anemic" word *agape* (rated: lexical argument).** Of eight Greek words for love, the NT writers chose *agape* — "the least used word," with "no cache for the standard Greek audience" — precisely because, unlike the relational terms (*eros*, *philia*, *storge*), it is an abstraction fitting a universal, relationship-free obligation; and *agape* is "the fundamental word [the Stoics] use for ethics."[^5] See [[The Agape-Phileo Exchange in John 21 Reflects Stoic Vocabulary]].
5. **Open to all classes (rated: corroboration).** Stoicism, like early Christianity (and unlike elite Mithraism), "was open to all classes — slave or consul of Rome"; Epictetus himself was a former slave.[^6]

**Steelman of the critic.** The claim that the Old Testament contains *no* universal-love impulse is overstated: Leviticus 19:34 commands love of the resident alien "as yourself," Proverbs 25:21 ("if thine enemy be hungry, give him bread"), and the Jonah and wisdom traditions push beyond the in-group; Second-Temple Judaism is not uniformly "closed." Critics also note that parallel maxims (Seneca, Epictetus) show a shared moral *milieu* or convergent insight, not necessarily literary *dependence* of the Gospels on Stoicism (Thomassen himself jokes "Paul was plagiarizing the Stoics… no, he was in dialogue with them"). The Stoic parallels are real and striking; "Judaism had nothing like it" and "the NT borrowed it from Stoicism" are the contested over-reaches.
