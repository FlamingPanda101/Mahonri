# Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: female suffrage and feminism succeeded because women *asserted their own interests* when material conditions allowed — not because Christian men, after centuries of reading the Bible, suddenly concluded women should vote.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The timing problem.** If churchgoing produced feminism, "why didn't they do it 500 years ago? Why then?" Feminism "happened to coincide with technological developments" that freed women to organize. "The washing machine transformed feminism."[^2] *(Strong — the coincidence with industrialization and labor-saving technology is real and demands explanation; doctrine alone doesn't explain the timing.)*

2. **Suffrage required female agitation.** "Do you think men on their own... would have just sat around and gone, you know what, I think we should give women the right to vote? Without the protests of women?" The vote came because women "threw themselves under horses until we got it."[^3] *(Strong — suffrage was won by movements, not granted by theological reflection.)*

3. **The oppression-and-opportunity model.** "People living in oppressive conditions will, when they have an opportunity, try to overcome that, regardless of whether they're Christians." This unifies feminism, Magna Carta, and the Haitian revolt under one mechanism. See [[Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming]] and [[Abolition Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Conviction]].[^4] *(Coherent materialist account.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener.** (a) Why assume the women agitating were *not* Christian-motivated? "What's your evidence that the only person who'd agitate for female suffrage would not be driven by Christian convictions?"[^5] (b) The book of Acts trajectory (prophesying daughters, Junia, Phoebe) and [[Joseph Henrich|Henrich's]] "Christian sexual revolution" supply the genealogy. (c) "Who has overturned patriarchy more than Western societies?" — the comparative record favors the Christianized West. (d) Technology happened *within Christendom*, raising the further question of why the scientific revolution occurred there.[^6] *(Strong — Scrivener rightly notes O'Connor smuggles in the assumption that material drivers and Christian motivation are mutually exclusive; "I think it's a bit of both.")* O'Connor concedes it may be "both," but insists the material driver is doing real, often decisive, work.

## Best Quotes
