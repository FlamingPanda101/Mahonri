# Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**The objection:** "If you oppose assisted suicide, you must just **want people to suffer** — you'd rather they be kept alive in agony than let them go."

**Horn's rebuttal** (per the summary) has two prongs. (1) **No duty to over-treat.** Catholic teaching does **not** require **extraordinary, burdensome, or disproportionate** treatment to keep a person alive artificially; **discontinuing overzealous treatment is acceptable**. So the opponent of assisted suicide is **not** committed to maximal life-prolongation at all costs — there is a vast, licit middle ground between "kill the patient" and "ventilate them forever." (2) **Suffering is not necessarily pointless.** Citing [[Karol Wojtyla (John Paul II)|John Paul II]], suffering can be **united to Christ's suffering** for a greater spiritual good, so enduring suffering is not the meaningless evil the objection assumes. The rebuttal thus denies both that opposing euthanasia *entails* prolonging agony and that suffering *must* be pointless.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The ordinary/extraordinary distinction is the heart of it (rate: strong).** Catholic moral theology (and broadly, mainstream medical ethics) distinguishes **ordinary/proportionate** means — which one ought normally to use — from **extraordinary/disproportionate** means, which one is **free to forgo** when the burden outweighs the benefit. Withdrawing a ventilator, declining a fourth round of futile chemo, or signing a DNR is **not** suicide or euthanasia; it is **allowing a disease to take its natural course**. This dismantles the dilemma the objection presupposes ("either kill or torture"). See [[Dignity Is How You Face Death Not the Manner of It]] for the parallel double-effect point on pain relief.[^1]
2. **Killing vs. allowing-to-die is a real moral line (rate: strong, conceptual).** The objection collapses *not actively killing* into *forcing suffering*. But there is a genuine difference between **intending death** (assisted suicide) and **accepting death** as the outcome of stopping a disproportionate intervention. The first makes death the *means/goal*; the second permits a death one does not intend. Horn's opponents typically concede this line elsewhere (e.g., in supporting a patient's right to refuse treatment), which makes its denial here ad hoc.[^1]
3. **The meaning-of-suffering claim has theological depth, not just assertion (rate: moderate–strong within a Christian frame).** John Paul II's *Salvifici Doloris* develops the idea that human suffering, joined to Christ's redemptive suffering, acquires salvific meaning. Within a Christian worldview this is a serious, structured answer — suffering can be **transformative** (of the sufferer and of those who love and serve them), not bare negativity. See the vault's problem-of-evil treatments [[God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ]] and [[Suffering as the Frame That Displays the Painting]]. *(Rated lower than the first two because it is **worldview-dependent**: it persuades inside a Christian frame and is meant to, but it does not by itself bind a secular interlocutor — though it does rebut the claim that the believer's position is *incoherent* or merely cruel.)*[^1]

### Steelman of the pro-assisted-suicide side

The strongest reply concedes the ordinary/extraordinary distinction and then **presses the residual case**: granting that we may withdraw burdensome treatment and maximally medicate pain, there remain conditions — certain neurodegenerative diseases, intractable suffering that sedation only blunts at the cost of consciousness — where the choice is between **prolonged, conscious anguish** and **terminal sedation into oblivion**, and the patient may reasonably judge a swift death more humane than either. In those cases, the defender argues, the "vast licit middle ground" runs out, and insisting the patient *endure* it **does** amount to choosing their suffering over their settled wish. On meaning: the redemptive-suffering claim is **the believer's to make for themselves**, not to **impose** on a person who does not share the theology — to tell a dying unbeliever that their agony is "salvific" is to conscript their body into a religious narrative they reject. And the "killing vs. allowing" line, they add, can feel like a **technicality** to the person actually suffering: dead is dead, and dressing the distinction in intention-language does not lessen the pain the line requires them to bear.

### Counter-objection (Horn's rejoinder)

Horn's reply (per the summary, extended): (a) The genuinely hard residual cases are **rarer than the rhetoric** and are precisely where modern **palliative care and proportionate sedation** do their work — the existence of a hard tail does not justify a *general* license whose **predictable effect** is the [[The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving|coercion of the poor and disabled]]; you do not build a society-wide policy around the edge case while ignoring whom the policy actually kills. (b) The meaning-of-suffering point is **not** wielded to *force* a theology on the dying, but to rebut the specific charge that the believer's opposition is **sadistic or incoherent** — the believer can *coherently* hold that suffering need not be pointless, which is all the rebuttal needs; it answers "you just want people to suffer," not "everyone must interpret their suffering as I do." (c) The killing/allowing line is no technicality precisely because **what the agent intends is part of what the agent does** (the same point as double effect in [[Dignity Is How You Face Death Not the Manner of It]]); a medicine and a poison can be the same molecule, and the difference is real. The merciful response remains **relieving** suffering and **accompanying** the sufferer (cf. [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]]), not eliminating the one who suffers.

## Apologetic Engagement

This note neutralizes the most **emotionally loaded** objection in the debate ("you just want people to suffer"), and its leverage is showing that the objection **rests on a false dilemma**. The first prong — *no duty to over-treat* — is the one to lead with, because it is **common ground** with most secular interlocutors and immediately defuses the caricature: the pro-life position permits DNRs, withdrawal of futile treatment, and aggressive palliation. Only *then* introduce the second prong — *suffering can have meaning* — and introduce it carefully and **non-coercively**, as a rebuttal to the charge of cruelty/incoherence rather than as a demand that the sufferer adopt the theology. Citing John Paul II / *Salvifici Doloris* signals this is a developed position, not a brush-off. Keep the **killing-vs-allowing** and **double-effect** distinctions explicit, since the whole argument stands on them; if the interlocutor rejects those, the disagreement has moved to action-theory and should be argued there.

## LDS Relevance

LDS practice maps closely onto Horn's first prong: the Church teaches that a dying person is **not** obligated to be kept alive by **extraordinary** means and that decisions to forgo such measures are a **family-and-physician matter under the Spirit**, while **deliberately ending life to relieve suffering is condemned** — the same ordinary/extraordinary and killing/allowing lines. On the second prong, Latter-day Saint theology has its **own** robust account of redemptive and instructive suffering that parallels (without being identical to) John Paul II's: adversity is part of a **probationary mortal estate**, the Atonement means Christ "descended below all things" and **suffers with** His people (cf. [[God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ]]), and tribulation can sanctify and refine (e.g., D&C 122:7, "all these things shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good"; Romans 8:18). The **agency-and-eternal-perspective** frame reinforces both prongs: mortal suffering is bounded and meaningful within an eternal plan, so the merciful response is to **relieve** and **accompany** — adequate pain control, declining disproportionate treatment, ministering to the dying — rather than to take the life God authored. (See also [[Suffering as the Frame That Displays the Painting]].)
