# Atheist Atrocities and the Logical-Path Argument

## The Point
Dawkins' fourth thesis is that "Christianity is dangerous" — even "mild and moderate religion helps to provide the climate of faith in which extremism naturally flourishes."[^1] His core claim is asymmetric: there is "a logical path from religion to doing terrible things" because "if you really really really believe that your god... wants you to do something, you'll go to paradise if you do it, then it's possible for an entirely logical rational person to do hideous things." But "I cannot conceive of a logical path that would lead one to say because I am an atheist, therefore it is rational for me to kill."[^2] [[John Lennox]] answers with the twentieth century: "imagine with John Lennox a world without atheism, with no Stalin, with no Mao, with no Pol Pot... no gulag, no cultural revolution, no killing fields."[^3]

## Evidence
Lennox charges Dawkins with "airbrushing out the atrocities of the communist world," noting that "atheism was not peripheral to Marxism. For Marx the criticism of religion was the foundation of all criticism."[^4] He quotes Dawkins' dismissal — that Hitler and Stalin "both also had mustaches" — and retorts: "all three of them had noses in common with the rest of us, but what kind of reasoning is this? We're not talking about shared characteristics in general, but the motivating ideology that drove these men to murder millions in their attempt to get rid of religion."[^5] He recounts the thousands of churches demolished in Stalin's Russia and, from his own visits to the GDR, a brilliant 13-year-old girl denied further education because she would not "swear public allegiance to the atheistic state" — "I would call that intellectual murder... committed many times in the name of atheism."[^6] His decisive fairness move: "You rightly expect me to differentiate between atheists. I would like you to write another book in which you differentiate between religions because they are not all the same."[^7] Dawkins concedes "it may even be that atheism was an integral part of the Marxism which led them to do terrible bad things," but holds that Stalin's evil "followed from something horrible within him," not from his atheism, whereas the 9/11 hijackers "were well educated, rational people" for whom atrocity "follows logically" once you grant the premise of their faith.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "We're not talking about shared characteristics in general, but the motivating ideology that drove these men to murder millions in their attempt to get rid of religion, whether Jewish, Christian, or anything else." [^5]

> "You rightly expect me to differentiate between atheists. I would like you to write another book in which you differentiate between religions because they are not all the same." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Lennox grants Dawkins' indictment of *fanatical* religion — and, importantly, distinguishes Christ's explicit teaching from the violence done in his name: the Crusaders "were not following Christ, but... actually disobeying his explicit command," for Christ "prohibited very famously his followers from using physical weapons" ("My kingdom is not of this world").[^9] This dovetails with the Latter-day Saint reading of the Book of Mormon, which repeatedly condemns those who take up the sword in God's name and praises the Anti-Nephi-Lehies who buried their weapons rather than shed blood (Alma 24). The deeper apologetic point — that an ideology denying objective good and evil cannot itself condemn atrocity — is developed in [[No One Needs God to Be Moral - The Grounding Problem]].
