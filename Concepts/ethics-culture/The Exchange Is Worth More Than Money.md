# The Exchange Is Worth More Than Money

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]] names the heart of ministering "the exchange" — a reciprocal human encounter in which both parties give and receive dignity, not a one-way transfer of goods. Money "creates a lot of opportunity… but it isn't the main driver"; the driver is "honoring somebody's choice… respecting them… giving them dignity, not making them feel like a victim." What is exchanged is being *seen*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The transactional-vs-relational contrast (strong).** A Middle Eastern friend told her, "You Americans… try to use relationships to get your projects done. In the Middle East, we do projects to build our relationships." She resolves to make the person, not the project, primary: "the exchange is the real thing that's going on."[^2]
2. **Marilyn the panhandler (strong, confessed blind spot).** Eubank avoided a panhandler for years; her colleague Elizabeth had befriended the same woman, learned her name and her children's school, and received from Marilyn a saved piece of red velvet cake as a Christmas gift. "She's a giver too… It wasn't about the cake." The exchange was *dignity*.[^3]
3. **Reciprocity heals the giver too (moderate).** The retired woman who watched a widow's children for two years, the friend who prayed nightly with Eubank during a spiritual crisis — in each, "it isn't just the receiver, it's the givers as well… an exchange that blesses everybody."[^4]
4. **Christological grounding (interpretive).** "Jesus didn't have a fund… He's not passing out money to people. He's seeing people… 'You're valuable.'" The model is Christ seeing the Samaritan, the tax collector, the soldier.[^5]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic of "relationship-first" aid can argue it risks moralizing poverty: the poor overwhelmingly need *material* resources — cash, housing, medicine — and elevating "the exchange" over money can become a pious excuse for under-funding, or a way for the comfortable to feel virtuous while withholding the dollars that would actually change a life. Dignity talk can also be paternalistic, substituting the helper's emotional needs ("I want to know you") for the recipient's stated material ones.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank explicitly guards this flank — "I don't want to give the impression that money isn't important. Money creates a lot of opportunity and resource" — so the claim is *priority*, not *exclusion*: relationship is what makes material help land as respect rather than as a verdict of failure (the women's-shelter Christmas that shamed the mothers is her own cautionary case). And the reciprocity she describes (Marilyn *giving* cake) is the opposite of paternalism: it restores the recipient as a giver, which is the dignity the money alone cannot deliver. See [[Ask What People Want Instead of Assuming Their Need]].
