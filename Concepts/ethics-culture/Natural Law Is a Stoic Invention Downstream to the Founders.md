# Natural Law Is a Stoic Invention Downstream to the Founders

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the concept of **natural law** — a universal, eternal moral order binding even the divine — was *invented by the Stoics*, and that the chain runs from the Stoics to Aquinas (13th c.) to the Enlightenment (Locke, Montesquieu) to the American Founders. So when Latter-day Saints invoke natural law (as they often do in connection with the Founding and the Constitution), they are "ipso facto referencing a Stoic cosmology."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Stoics created it (rated: history of philosophy).** "Natural law is not a concept that exists within the Old Testament anywhere… The Stoics are the ones who created the concept of natural law."[^1]
2. **Cicero as the primary witness (rated: textual).** [[Cicero]]: "Law is something eternal that directs the whole world by its wisdom in ordering and prohibiting."[^2]
3. **A binding order over the divine (rated: the framework).** The Stoics paired natural law with "a universal morality that is so binding that it will even bind the divine itself," which is why one can have confidence in it; the *logos* root "means to bind, to bring together."[^3]
4. **The transmission chain (rated: intellectual history).** "When we read Locke or Montesquieu making references to natural law, you can trace that back to Aquinas, 13th century — but where did Aquinas get it from? The Stoics. That is ground zero."[^1] See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]].

**Steelman of the critic.** The pedigree is broadly right (Stoic *koinos nomos* → Cicero → Roman law → Aquinas → modern natural-law theory), but two cautions: (a) natural-law thinking also has **non-Stoic** roots (Aristotle's natural justice; the Hebrew prophets' appeal to a moral order over the nations; Romans 1–2's "law written on the heart"), so "the Stoics invented it" is too strong; (b) Thomassen's "binds even the divine" formulation is a *Stoic* construal that classical Christian natural-law theory (Aquinas) explicitly rejects — for Aquinas natural law is a *participation* in God's eternal law, not a constraint *over* God. The Stoic origin of the *technical term* is defensible; the universal-claim and the "binds the divine" gloss are contestable.

## Best Quotes
> "Natural law is not a concept that exists within the Old Testament anywhere… The Stoics are the ones who created the concept of natural law."[^1]

> "When we read Locke or Montesquieu… you can trace that back to Aquinas, 13th century — but where did Aquinas get it from? The Stoics. That is ground zero for the concept of natural law."[^1]

## LDS Connection
This matters for the Latter-day Saint reading of the Founding (the Constitution as inspired, natural rights as God-given): Thomassen's point is that the very framework is a Stoic inheritance, reinforcing his thesis that Greek thought was a providential "preparatory" vehicle rather than a corruption. It connects to the vault's [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]] and the LDS appeal to a divinely-grounded moral order, while the steelman flags that the "binds the divine" version is a notable departure from classical theism.
