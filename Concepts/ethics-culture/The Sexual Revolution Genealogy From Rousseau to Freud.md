# The Sexual Revolution Genealogy From Rousseau to Freud

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that the deepest driver of abortion is not "women's choice" as such but a **modern "social imaginary"** that makes *sexual autonomy* the defining feature of personal identity — and that this imaginary has a traceable intellectual genealogy from [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] through the Romantics, [[Friedrich Nietzsche]], Sigmund Freud, and Karl Marx. People absorb these ideas "in the water that they swim in" without ever reading their sources. The gospel offers a rival account: human worth rests on relationship to God and covenant with Christ, not on sexual expression. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The genealogy, stage by stage.** Hansen sketches the shift (echoing Carl Trueman's *The Rise and Triumph of the Modern Self* and Charles Taylor's "social imaginary"): medieval people located the self as a *participant* in society and before God; **Rousseau** made one's inner feelings and "truth" primary; later thinkers internalized identity further; **Freud** placed the *sexual drive* at the summit of human happiness, so that "your sex drive makes you a person... gives you your worth"; **Marx** (and successors) recast the self as locked in an oppressor-versus-oppressed political struggle. The upshot: sexual identity becomes a political battle to be defended at all costs. *Rating: moderate-strong as intellectual history at the popular level; it compresses contested scholarship but tracks Trueman/Taylor faithfully.* [^2]
2. **The "social imaginary": ideas without sources.** "People have ideas and concepts of how they view the world even if they've never read the people where those ideas came from. Most people haven't read Kant, Rousseau, Nietzsche, Freud, Marx, but they have their ideas because it's been passed down... through things like even Ariana Grande songs." The fish "doesn't see the water." *Rating: strong as a description of how worldviews propagate pre-reflectively — Taylor's actual concept.* [^3]
3. **Abortion as the defense of sexual autonomy.** On this analysis, "abortion is about birth control because people want to be sexually promiscuous and would end up in a bad spot if they got pregnant." The real commitment being protected is unrestricted sexual expression as identity-constituting — which is why, Hansen argues, the debate so rarely stays on the personhood question. *Rating: moderate; a motivational claim, persuasive within the frame but not a knockdown of any individual's reasoning.* [^4]
4. **The gospel's rival anthropology.** "The gospel... does not place your sexual satisfaction as the divining, defining characteristic of who you are... Our value is placed in our relationship to God as his children and to each other as brothers and sisters who enter into covenants with Jesus Christ." So Latter-day Saints "shouldn't feel a need to protect free sexual expression at all costs." *Rating: strong as a statement of the LDS alternative — connects to [[Child Rights Confront Sexual Autonomy as the Highest Value]].* [^5]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Several pushbacks. (a) The genealogy is **reductive**: Rousseau, Nietzsche, Freud, and Marx held deeply *incompatible* views, and stringing them into one "expressive individualism → sexual revolution" line is a tidy story that flattens real disagreement and ignores material/medical drivers (contraception, women's economic entry, maternal-mortality concerns). (b) It risks the **genetic fallacy**: even if support for abortion rights *did* descend from these thinkers, that would not make the pro-choice position false — the personhood and bodily-autonomy arguments must be met on their merits. (c) A secular ethicist can ground a right to abortion in bodily autonomy or in a developmental account of personhood *without* making sexual self-expression the highest value, so the "it's really about sexual autonomy" diagnosis may be a strawman of the most serious pro-choice arguments.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: the genealogy is offered as *diagnosis of a cultural mood*, not as a syllogism against any particular argument — Taylor's "social imaginary" is precisely about pre-theoretical background assumptions, not formal philosophy, so internal disagreements among the sources don't defeat the claim that a shared *imaginary* trickled down. He explicitly engages the personhood question elsewhere (the sacred-body-in-development account), so he is not substituting genealogy for argument. The honest core: the genealogy illuminates *why* the debate resists staying on personhood, while the substantive case still has to be made (and Hansen makes it separately). See [[The Pro-Life-as-Anti-Feminism Narrative Is Dogma Not Data]] for the symmetrical charge that opponents' framing is ideologically driven.
