# Redefining Good as Well-Being - The Stipulation Objection

## The Point
Craig's central attack on Harris's positive case is that Harris has not *solved* the value problem but merely *redefined* it away. Harris stipulates that "good" means "that which supports the well-being of conscious creatures"; so when Harris says it makes no sense to ask whether maximizing well-being is good, that is only because "he's redefined the word good to mean the well-being of conscious creatures." The question "why is maximizing creatures' well-being good?" then collapses into "why does maximizing creatures' well-being maximize creatures' well-being?" — "just a tautology… nothing but word play."[^1] On Craig's reading, Harris "isn't really talking about moral values at all. He's just talking about what's conducive to the flourishing of sentient life on this planet."[^2] This is the live-debate form of the is/ought objection logged in [[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]] — Harris doesn't bridge the gap, he *defines* it shut, and Moore's "open question" simply migrates to the definition ("but is *well-being* what we ought to maximize?").

## Evidence
Craig reinforces the charge by collecting the many **non-moral** uses of "good" in ordinary English — "a good move in chess," "Notre Dame is a good team," "that's a good way to get yourself killed," "the sunshine felt good," "she's in good health" — to argue that Harris's "good life vs. bad life" contrast "is not an ethical contrast… It's a contrast between a pleasurable life and a miserable life. And there's no reason to identify pleasure / misery with good and evil, especially on atheism."[^3] He drives the is/ought point with science's limits (quoting Jerry Fodor: "science is about facts, not norms… it wouldn't tell us what is wrong with how we are"), concluding that science "cannot tell us that we have a moral obligation to take actions which are conducive to human flourishing."[^4]

**Harris's reply (stated fairly).** Harris denies the move is arbitrary. Consciousness is not an arbitrary starting point — "imagine a universe entirely constituted of rocks… value judgments don't apply"; value requires that something *matter to* a conscious system.[^5] And the tautology charge cuts at all knowledge: "every branch of science must rely on certain axiomatic assumptions"; you cannot prove you *should* value evidence or logic without already using them, yet that does not make science unscientific.[^6] He turns Craig's accusation back: Craig "has merely defined God as being intrinsically good… that is a definitional move that he has made" — so if stipulation is a sin, "the shoe's on the other foot."[^7] (See [[The Worst Possible Misery for Everyone Anchors Moral Truth]] and [[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]] for the book-length versions.)

## Best Quotes
> "So Dr. Harris has quote-unquote solved the value problem just by redefining his terms. It's nothing but word play." [^1]

> "His so-called solution is just a semantical trick of an arbitrary and idiosyncratic redefinition of the terms good and evil in non-moral vocabulary." [^2]

> "Dr. Craig has merely defined God as being intrinsically good… if you want to charge someone with merely semantic games, the shoe's on the other foot as well." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The Latter-day Saint can stand with Craig's *logical* point while noticing that the tu-quoque exposes the real fork. Both men must "stand somewhere": Harris's bedrock is the bare axiom that well-being matters; Craig's is that goodness is identical to God's nature. The Restoration locates the standard one step deeper than either bare stipulation — in **eternal moral law** that God himself conforms to and reveals ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]; [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]) — so the LDS "ground" is neither an arbitrary divine fiat nor a contingent fact about primate brains. The Light of Christ already gives every person the *knowledge* that flourishing is good (Moroni 7:16), but knowing is not grounding ([[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]): Harris's redefinition supplies a *method* for tracking well-being while leaving untouched the question of why well-being carries *moral authority* — exactly the a-priori structure he must presuppose ([[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]]).
