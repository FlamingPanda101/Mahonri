# Subjective Perception vs Subjective Preference

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s whole reply to "isn't morality subjective?" turns on a single distinction he calls "critical." Granting that suffering and well-being are "experienced subjectively" only states the obvious, because *all* of reality is known through subjective perception — yet "all perception [being] by definition subjective" does not make reality itself subjective. The real question is "if that perception is purely the creation of the mind or if it is a reflection of an objective reality outside of the mind." Subjective *perception* interprets a real world; subjective *preference* ("I like green") describes nothing outside the head. [^1] Conflating the two is the equivocation behind moral subjectivism.

## Evidence
Hansen lays the two categories side by side. **Perception:** "subjective perception is the way that we subjectively interpret a reality that exists outside of ourselves which may or may not be accurate" — it has a truth-value because it answers to something external (my perception of a tree can be right or wrong about the tree). **Preference:** "if I say I like green and you say that you like blue these are not a description of a reality outside of one's head they are merely statements of subjective preference… purely a mental construct without a right or wrong answer because it is not a description of anything in objective reality." [^1] The subjectivist's slogan — "morality is just how it's experienced" — illegitimately files moral claims under *preference*, when the prior question is whether moral claims (like perceptual claims) are *about* a reality outside the mind. This sets up the next move: suffering is on the perception side of the line, not the preference side (see [[Suffering Is Objectively Real Not a Preference]]).

## Best Quotes
> "All perception is by definition subjective but the question is if that perception is purely the creation of the mind or if it is a reflection of an objective reality outside of the mind." [^1]

> "Subjective perception is the way that we subjectively interpret a reality that exists outside of ourselves which may or may not be accurate; however subjective preference is purely a mental construct without a right or wrong answer because it is not a description of anything in objective reality." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration assumes exactly this realism about perception: revelation and the witness of the Spirit are treated as *perceptions of something real* — "the knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) — not as private preference. A moral order that God himself conforms to (the eternal law behind the rejection of divine-command voluntarism, [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]) is precisely a reality "outside the mind" that our moral perceptions can track truly or falsely. The distinction also disarms the relativist's self-defeating move: a worldview that collapses all moral claims into preference saws off its own branch (compare [[Subjectivism Refutes Itself]], [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
