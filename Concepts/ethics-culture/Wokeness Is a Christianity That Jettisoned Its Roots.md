# Wokeness Is a Christianity That Jettisoned Its Roots

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]] reads the 2020 racial-justice upheaval as "a deeply Christian movement" — Quaker-descended activism that has *jettisoned* its Christian scaffolding (scripture, practice, self-identification) while retaining Christian moral forms. The result treats slavery as an indelible *original sin*, stages secular versions of Protestant ritual (toppling idols, penitence), and, severed from its roots, "mutates very fast."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The line of descent from the Quakers is "very clear."** Holland argues the activism of 2020 is recognizably what 18th-century Quakers were doing — the same moral energy against oppression — but stripped of the Christian framework that gave it structure. Having "jettisoned the Christian scripture, the Christian practice, the Christian self-identification," it had to "construct its own traditions." See [[Abolition Spread as Pentecostal Fire from Spirit-Read Scripture]].[^2] *(Strong genealogical claim from a historian of exactly this tradition.)*
2. **The rituals are Protestant forms in secular dress.** Toppling statues is "a very Protestant thing to do" (toppling idols); "taking the knee," and white people offering themselves "as penitentials," are "very very Christian forms of practice" divorced from their institutional birthplace.[^3] *(A pointed observation about the religious morphology of secular protest.)*
3. **Slavery reframed as original sin.** The woke treatment of slavery mirrors the doctrine of original sin: "we are born into this sin and we are never going to be washed clean... an indelible stain on our reputation and fundamentally our souls." Holland: slavery comes "to be seen as the great sin," but "we judge that by Christian standards" — across global history, exploitation (chattel slavery, caste, serfdom, industrial proletariats) was "always" present and "not in any way a moral given." The conviction that it is a *stain to be eradicated* is "very, very novel" and specifically Christian.[^4] *(Strong — connects the secular discourse to a recognizably Christian theological structure.)*
4. **Cut from its roots, it mutates fast.** Because "once you unmoor ideas and stories and traditions from the context that gave them birth, they can mutate very, very quickly," the movement's trajectory is unstable and unpredictable — a freed "head of the rocket" that has jettisoned everything beneath it. This is the same borrowed-capital dynamic as [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]], applied to activist politics.[^5] *(Coherent with the broader Dominion thesis.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Critics on the left reply that anti-racism is grounded in *secular* commitments (Enlightenment equality, social-scientific analysis of structural injustice, lived experience of the oppressed), not crypto-Christianity, and that calling it a "Christian heresy" is a rhetorical move to claim ownership of moral progress for the church that historically *enslaved* people. Holland's answer is that the Enlightenment commitments themselves are post-Christian (see [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]]) and that the *specific shape* of the discourse — collective inherited guilt, penance, purity, an unforgivable stain — is theological, not merely sociological. A fair concession: Holland is offering a genealogy of *form and energy*, not denying that secular actors sincerely hold secular reasons; one can grant the Christian ancestry of "the marginalized matter" while disputing that any particular 2020 policy follows from it.

## LDS Relevance
This is a defensive *and* offensive apologetic tool. Defensively, it warns Latter-day Saints (and all Christians) that a free-floating moral fervor unmoored from doctrine "mutates very fast" — an argument for keeping moral commitments rooted in revealed theology rather than the shifting consensus. Offensively, it disarms the secular-progressive critique of the Church: the very moral instincts deployed against the Church's record (concern for the oppressed, demand for repentance) are themselves Christian inheritances, so the critic stands on borrowed ground. Compare [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]] and [[Western Secularism Is Post-Christian Not Non-Christian]].
