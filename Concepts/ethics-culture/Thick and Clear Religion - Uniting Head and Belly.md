# Thick and Clear Religion - Uniting Head and Belly

## The Point
Ward connects the chest's work of *uniting* the head and the belly to a theological distinction Lewis drew in his 1945 essay "Christian Apologetics," where he sorted religions into the **"thick"** and the **"clear,"** as if they were kinds of soup. [^1] "Thick" religions are "orgiastic, ecstatic, mysterious, attached to particular localities" — the religion of the belly and the savage. "Clear" religions are "ethical systems and universalizing philosophical principles" — the religion of the head and the citizen. [^1] True religion, Lewis argues, must contain *both*, "for the true God must have made both the child and the man, both the savage and the citizen, both the head and the belly." [^1]

## Evidence
Lewis judged that most traditions hold the two elements apart. Hinduism contains both Thick and Clear strands but "combines them imperfectly," because "the Clear religion of the Brahmin hermit in the jungle and the Thick religion of the neighbouring temple go on side by side," never truly engaging each other. [^1] Christianity, by contrast, "fully succeeds in combining both elements": the universal ethic of Christ's teaching is fused with "the mysterious physical ritual of eating his body and drinking his blood." Christianity "really breaks down the middle wall of the partition" between the two compartments — Lewis quoting Ephesians 2:14. [^1] Ward presents this as the *theological* counterpart to the philosophical anatomy of "Men Without Chests": the same refusal to let pure intellect float free of embodied, ritual, affective life that the Chest performs within the individual soul, Christianity performs within the structure of religion itself. [^1]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration is conspicuously a religion of *both* head and belly in Lewis's sense. On the "clear" side it offers a rationalized cosmology and ethic — eternal law, agency, covenant theology, a God who reasons with His children ("Come now, and let us reason together," Isaiah 1:18). On the "thick" side it is intensely embodied and local: temple ritual, the laying on of hands, anointing, sacred clothing, gathering to consecrated places, a sacrament of bread and water taken into the body. Where the historic creeds pushed toward a purely "clear" God "without body, parts, or passions" (see [['Without Body, Parts, or Passions' Is a 1530 Protestant Creed, Not Ancient]]), Latter-day Saint worship deliberately reunites mystery and ethics, ordinance and doctrine — embodying within its practice the very integration of head and belly that Lewis says marks true religion.
