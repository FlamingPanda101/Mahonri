# Beliefs Are Intrinsically Epistemic - There Is No Freedom of Belief

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that we cannot knowingly believe a proposition for bad reasons, because **belief is intrinsically epistemic**: to believe something "is also to believe that we are in touch with reality in such a way that if it were not true, one would not believe it."[^1] Hence there is no genuine "freedom of belief." Confronted with overwhelming evidence, "I am a slave to the evidence"; one cannot simply *choose* to believe George Washington was not the first president — and if the evidence changed, one would be "helplessly stripped" of the belief, "through no choice of my own."[^2]

## Evidence
Harris dramatizes the point with absurd counterexamples no sane person utters: a man claiming to believe "robbing others is a harmless activity" or that he is "destined to marry Angelina Jolie" *merely because* the beliefs feel good.[^3] Against his interlocutor Philip Ball — who held that a well-informed person should be "free to choose" beliefs that make him feel better — Harris imagines a Christian who believes in the virgin birth and resurrection *only* because it feels good, then drops the belief upon meeting an admired Muslim woman.[^4] "Has a person like this ever existed? I highly doubt it," because beliefs "purport to represent the world as it is," and this believer makes "no effort to stay in touch with the features of the world that should inform his belief."[^4] Harris allows real mental freedom (choosing which facts to *attend to*) and concedes people do believe comforting things — but "not in the full light of consciousness"; self-deception "is not at all the same as knowingly believing a proposition simply because one wants it to be true."[^5] He reframes "my daughter is the loveliest in the world" as honestly meaning "the loveliest girl in the world *for me*," a special attachment, not an objective claim.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Choosing beliefs freely is not what rational minds do." [^2]

> "To really believe a proposition — whether about facts or values — we must also believe that we are in touch with reality in such a way that if it were not true, one would not believe it." [^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's core insight — that belief aims at truth and is not voluntarily willed in defiance of evidence — is one Latter-day Saint epistemology can affirm wholeheartedly, and it actually *refutes* a common anti-religious caricature. Many secularists assume believers "knowingly believe things for reasons that have nothing to do with their perception of the truth"; Harris's own argument shows this is psychologically impossible — believers, like everyone, take themselves to be tracking reality. The Restored Gospel does not ask for evidence-immune wishful thinking but for an evidence- and experience-responsive trust that can mature into knowledge (cf. [[Belief as Acceptance - The Mental Act of Grasping a Proposition as True]], [[Know and Believe Mark Points on a Confidence Spectrum]]). The genuine disagreement is over the *evidence base*: theists hold that moral experience, the contingency and intelligibility of the cosmos, and personal spiritual witness are real data the honest mind must weigh, not feelings to be bracketed (cf. [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]], [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]]). Harris's caricatured "I believe because it feels good" Christian is a straw man: the thoughtful believer claims, precisely as Harris says all believers must, to be in touch with how things are — and invites the same scrutiny of evidence Harris demands (cf. [[Inherited Belief Is Fragile - Examine Each Proposition Independently]]).
