# Relativism Kills Itself - The Hitler Argument Against Self-Created Reality

## The Point
[[Greg Bahnsen]] identifies **relativism** — "that's true for you, it's not true for me" — as the second great form of arbitrariness, and argues it is both **self-refuting** and **morally catastrophic**.[^1] It is self-refuting because "true for you is actually a paraphrase of the verb to believe": saying "it's true for me" just means "I believe it," so relativism reduces every truth-claim to a mere report of belief and thereby dissolves the very notion of being right or wrong.[^2] And the slogan "there is no absolute truth" collapses under one question — *"is that absolutely true?"* — putting the relativist "on the horns of a dilemma": deny it and absolute truth is conceded; affirm it and you have asserted an absolute truth, contradicting yourself.[^3]

## Evidence
Bahnsen's decisive move is moral, drawn from an international religious-liberty conference in Moscow where participants defended liberty by appeal to relativism ("if your view of God helps you feel better then that's true for you").[^4] He replied that "religious liberty cannot be founded on relativism," because **the same principle licenses atrocity**: "if you believe that everyone creates their own reality then Hitler creates his own reality too, and Hitler therefore in terms of his reality has the right to kill the Jews."[^5] Relativism does not protect the persecuted; it "condones" genocide, so "relativism kills itself."[^6] He reinforces the point with vivid imagery — reality is not a "smorgasbord" you fill by preference, and God is not a "Mr. Potato Head" you assemble to taste ("if God is whatever you want him to be, he is not God").[^7] He also exposes the social motive: relativism is "a lot more comfortable" because then "you can go to cocktail parties and everybody's welcome" — Jesus included, "as long as Jesus didn't say he was the only truth."[^8] [[Jacob Hansen]] applies this directly to the Church, warning against members who "downplay our claim to be the one true church" and treat "inclusion" as a "soft entry to moral and cultural relativism," while affirming we should be kind without pretending "all worldviews are worthy of equal respect."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "If everybody's right then clearly nobody's wrong, including the Idi Amins of the world, the Hitlers, the David Koreshes and all the rest. Relativism kills itself." [^6]

> "When someone says there is no absolute truth, you're going to say: is that absolutely true?" [^3]

> "True for you is one of the most asinine statements in the English language. Truth is not person-relative." [^10]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration is emphatically anti-relativist about ultimate truth: it claims to be "the only true and living church" (D&C 1:30) and defines truth as objective "knowledge of things as they are" (D&C 93:24), so Bahnsen's "truth is not person-relative" is fully at home here. Hansen's worry that a modern "obsession with inclusion" can soften into relativism is a pointed in-house application: charity ("we should be kind to all") must not be confused with the relativist claim that all worldviews are equally true. The LDS frame also supplies the *ground* the relativist lacks — an eternal, objective moral order God himself honors ([[Subjectivism Refutes Itself]]; [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]) — so that condemning the genocide in Bahnsen's example is not arbitrary but anchored in real, mind-independent moral law.
