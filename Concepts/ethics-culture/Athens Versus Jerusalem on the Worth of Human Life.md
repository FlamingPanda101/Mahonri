# Athens Versus Jerusalem on the Worth of Human Life

## The Point

Western civilization has two parents — reason-based Athens and faith-based
Jerusalem — and they reached opposite conclusions about the worth of weak human
lives. Greek reason found it reasonable to expose sickly infants to die;
biblical faith taught the world to keep them alive. The humane valuation of
every life is thus an inheritance from faith, not from reason.

## Argument

1. The Greeks, "the founders of Western reason," thought it "quite reasonable to
   leave sickly babies to die of exposure," since such a baby "would just be a
   burden on the parents and the state" [^1].
2. By contrast, "it was faith-based Jerusalem, the other parent of Western
   civilization, not reason-based Athens, that taught the world to keep sickly
   babies alive" [^1].
3. So on the concrete test case of vulnerable infants, reason and faith diverge,
   and it is faith that produced the humane answer.
4. This grounds the broader claim (see [[The Value of Human Life Is a Faith Claim Not a Rational One]]) that the preciousness of human life is a
   faith-based belief, not a deliverance of reason.
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) A critic notes the framing is a simplified
   trope: Greek thinkers also produced humane ethics (Stoic cosmopolitanism), and
   biblical texts contain harsh commands, so the clean Athens-vs-Jerusalem split
   is rhetorical. Prager's narrower point survives the objection: on the
   specific practice of infant exposure, the influence that ended it in the West
   was the biblical valuation of every life, not Greek rationalism.

## Best Quotes
