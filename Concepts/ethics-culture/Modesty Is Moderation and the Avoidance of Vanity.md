# Modesty Is Moderation and the Avoidance of Vanity

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues modesty is **fundamentally about moderation and the avoidance of vanity and pride** — "the opposite of vanity... the opposite of calling attention to oneself for self-aggrandizement" — not primarily about sexually revealing clothing. The word is tied to *moderation* (the middle), so it applies as much to men as to women and to *any* attention-seeking extreme. Reducing modesty to "are a woman's shoulders showing" cheapens "this deep and meaningful principle." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The etymology/concept points to the mean.** "The idea of modesty is connected to the idea of moderation or the middle." Vanity (excessive self-admiration, showing off, seeking the praise of men) is the real target, and "both sexes are prone to" it "though often in different ways." *Rating: moderate-strong; aligns the virtue with the classical doctrine of the mean and broadens it beyond female dress.* [^1]
2. **Both extremes are immodest.** "On one extreme we can have a woman in a burka; on the other hand... clothing that is barely holding on to anything at all. But... both of these are immodest because moderation, the middle, exists between the extremes." Crucially, the *over-covered* extreme can also be immodest when it is a prideful display: "wear[ing] that pioneer dress to the dance... to call attention to one's own piety is just as prideful and lacking in moderation as the girl who wants to show off her body." *Rating: strong; the symmetry (both extremes miss the mark) is the distinctive move.* [^2]
3. **Context determines the mean.** Because modesty is a principle of moderation applied to context (not a fixed rule), "wearing tight spandex shorts may be totally appropriate in a volleyball game but immodest when giving a talk in church." This is why apparent inconsistencies (BYU athletic uniforms; a 1950s church-sold swimsuit; shoulders fine at the beach) are not arbitrary — see [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]]. *Rating: moderate-strong; consistent with the principles-over-rules framework.* [^3]
4. **Dress is one expression among many.** "Modesty is not fundamentally about sexually revealing clothing; it is about excessive self-admiration and pride... about the problem of showing off." Dress is "one of the ways we can be immodest," not the whole of it. *Rating: moderate-strong; reframes the virtue as interior.* [^1]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Two challenges. (a) From the **traditionalist** side: this redefinition risks *diluting* practical guidance — youth need concrete standards, and "it's about your heart, judge the context" can become a license to push boundaries while claiming pure motives; the old *For the Strength of Youth* rules existed precisely because principles under-determine hard cases. (b) From the **critical-scholarship** side (cf. [[Biblical Modesty Refers to Wealth Not Sexuality]]): [[Dan McClellan]] argues *biblical* modesty language is about avoiding displays of *wealth/status*, not sexuality at all, and that lust is the looker's fault — so even Hansen's "anti-vanity" framing, while closer to the text than "cover up so men don't stumble," still imports a sexual-signaling concern the biblical texts do not place on women's clothing.

### Counter-objection
Hansen partly *agrees* with the wealth/anti-vanity reading — his thesis is precisely that modesty is about pride and the praise of the world, not female bodies — and he explicitly retains rules as sometimes "necessary" while teaching the *why* behind them ("rules will be necessary, but the principle of modesty is about avoiding vanity and pride"). The aim is to inspire internalized virtue "so that they will want to be modest in all ways," which he argues is *more* powerful than rule-policing, not a loophole for it. See [[Sexual Signaling in Moderation Is God-Designed and Good]] for the positive account that keeps attractiveness in view without vanity.

## Best Quotes
> "Modesty is the opposite of vanity. It is the opposite of calling attention to oneself for self-aggrandizement... we cheapen this deep and meaningful principle when we reduce it to simply being about if a woman's shoulders are showing." [^1]

> "A desire to use an extreme... to call attention to one's own piety is just as prideful and lacking in moderation as the girl who wants to show off her body to get attention. Both of these extremes miss the mark." [^2]
