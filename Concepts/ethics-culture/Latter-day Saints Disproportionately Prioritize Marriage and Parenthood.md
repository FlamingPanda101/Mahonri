# Latter-day Saints Disproportionately Prioritize Marriage and Parenthood

## The Claim
[[Keystone]] argues that the temple's doctrinal emphasis on eternal families shows up in measurable attitudes: in U.S. survey data, Latter-day Saints are far more likely than the general public to rank a successful marriage and good parenthood among life's most important goals — "the statistics do indeed seem to reflect the doctrine."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Pew valuation gap.** A Pew Research survey asked Americans how important various life goals were. "Only 34% of the general public considered having a successful marriage to be one of the most important things in life. Among Latter-day Saints, that percentage more than doubles, coming in at 73%." For "being a good parent," it was 50% of Americans versus "81%" of Latter-day Saints.[^2] *Rating: moderate–strong — a real Pew dataset on stated values; Keystone reports the figures without on-screen citation, so the exact survey/year is not verified in the video.*

2. **Behavior tracks the values.** "At least in the United States, Latter-day Saints are more likely to get married, stay married, and be happier in marriage." Keystone is careful: a temple marriage "doesn't guarantee a successful marriage," divorce is allowed, and "some marriages are happier than others" — "but generally speaking, the statistics... reflect the doctrine."[^3] This pairs with the outcome data in [[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]]. *Rating: moderate — the directional claim is consistent with other survey work; the hedge is appropriate.*

3. **The honest scope.** "I'm of course not saying that Latter-day Saints are the only ones who care about marriage and parenthood — that's certainly not true. But the data does indicate that Latter-day Saints tend to value [them] more than the general public," and "temples are a big reason for that."[^4] *Rating: strong — the claim is explicitly comparative and disclaims exclusivity.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A skeptic notes that *stated* prioritization in a survey is cheap and may reflect social desirability within a high-demand religious culture rather than distinctive outcomes; that valuing marriage highly is not self-evidently good (it can pressure people into early or unhappy marriages); and that attributing the gap specifically to *temples* (rather than to general religiosity, community, or regional culture) is an inference the data cannot isolate — the perennial limit catalogued in [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]]. The fair reading: the valuation gap is real and sizable, and it is consistent with the temple-centered family theology, while the causal credit assigned to temples specifically is an interpretation rather than a finding.
