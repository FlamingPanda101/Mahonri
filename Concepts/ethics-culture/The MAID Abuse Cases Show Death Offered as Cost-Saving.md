# The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] argues (per the summary) that assisted suicide, once normalized, becomes the **cheapest "treatment"** a strained system can offer — so vulnerable people are nudged toward death not by their illness but by **poverty, denied care, or institutional convenience**. He supports this not with theory but with a dossier of reported cases in which death was *offered to* people who had not sought it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the cases, per the summary)

1. **Housing, not illness — St. Catharines, Ontario (rate: strong).** A man applied for MAID **not because he wanted to die** but because he was about to lose his housing; his ~**$1,200/month** disability payments could not prevent homelessness — and his doctor signed off. The request was driven by a *social* deprivation the state could have remedied far more cheaply than it can remedy nothing; instead the lethal path was approved.[^1] *(The clearest illustration that "consent" can be manufactured by deprivation.)*
2. **The veteran offered death instead of care (rate: strong).** A Canadian **veteran** who called **Veterans Affairs** seeking treatment for **combat PTSD and traumatic brain injury** was instead **offered medically assisted death**. The person sought help to live; the system volunteered death.[^1] *(Shows the offer originating from the institution, not the patient — the inversion of "patient autonomy.")*
3. **The insurer's letter — Oregon (rate: strong).** A woman **denied coverage for a life-prolonging chemotherapy drug** received an insurance letter offering to **pay for physician-assisted suicide** instead. The same payer that refused the expensive cure offered the cheap death.[^1] *(The cost-saving incentive made explicit on paper.)*
4. **Forced transfer — Providence Healthcare, British Columbia (rate: moderate).** A **Catholic hospital** is being **sued** for requiring MAID-seeking patients to **transfer to another facility** rather than perform the procedure on-site. Horn frames this as the practice now coercing *objecting institutions*, eroding conscience protection.[^1] *(Rated moderate as an "abuse" case — it is a conscience-rights conflict rather than a coerced patient, but it shows the normalization pressuring dissenters.)*

Together the cases form a pattern: in each, the **structural incentive runs toward death** because death is cheaper than housing, psychiatric care, or chemotherapy. Horn's inference is that this is not incidental misuse but the **predictable equilibrium** of a system that has made killing a fundable option — which is exactly the mechanism the [[Assisted Suicide Cannot Draw a Principled Line|slippery-slope]] argument predicts.

### Steelman of the pro-assisted-suicide side

A defender replies that **anecdotes are not the system**: each case is a **failure of safeguards or of the social safety net**, and the remedy is to *fix those* — guaranteed housing, funded psychiatric care, mandatory screening that rules out applicants whose suffering is **remediable** — not to ban a practice that serves genuinely dying patients well. The St. Catharines and veteran cases, they argue, show that **the safeguards worked or should have** (the request should have been refused / the offer was an individual employee's error, since disavowed). The Oregon letter reflects a **bureaucratic coverage algorithm**, not a coordinated push to kill the sick. And the hospital suit is about **patient access**, not coercing patients to die. None of this, they say, touches the core case: a competent, dying, suffering adult who freely chooses death.

### Counter-objection (Horn's rejoinder)

Horn's reply (developed across the video) is that "fix the safety net first, then talk" is exactly the point: **as long as the safety net is broken, the lethal option is the one being funded** — so the practice does not operate in the idealized conditions its defenders describe; it operates in *these* conditions, where the cheapest exit is death. "Better safeguards" presumes the institution wants to spend more to keep the person alive than to end them — but the **cost gradient runs the other way**, which is why the offers keep coming unbidden. A right to die, in a system with a financial interest in your death, decays in practice into a **duty to consider dying**. The anecdotes matter precisely because they reveal the **incentive structure**, and incentive structures, not intentions, govern outcomes at scale.

## Apologetic Engagement

This note arms the **empirical** wing of the assisted-suicide critique. Its rhetorical strength is that it does not require the interlocutor to accept any religious premise about the soul or intrinsic dignity — it appeals to a **secular** worry any honest proponent shares: that the poor, disabled, traumatized, and uninsured will be the ones for whom "choice" becomes pressure. The discipline is to **steelman the safeguards reply fairly** (these are real policy failures, and proponents do not *want* coerced deaths) and then press the structural point: the cost gradient is not a bug to be patched but the **standing condition** under which the practice runs. Pair with [[Assisted Suicide Cannot Draw a Principled Line]] (the conceptual slope) — together they argue the slope is both *logical* and *already-instantiated*.

## LDS Relevance

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints opposes elective euthanasia and assisted suicide, and these cases sharpen *why* from an LDS angle: when human worth is treated as a function of **cost, productivity, or being wanted**, the poor and disabled are devalued first — the precise inversion of the doctrine that every person has **intrinsic, eternal worth** as a child of God (cf. [[We Show Humans More Mercy Than Animals Not Less]]). The Church's parallel social ethic — care for the poor, the sick, and "the least of these," and a welfare apparatus aimed at sustaining life and dignity — is the affirmative answer the abuse cases demand: the response to a man losing his housing is *housing*, to an untreated veteran is *treatment*, not a lethal injection. (The Church does permit declining **extraordinary** measures and accepting pain relief that may hasten death; see [[Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer]] — a categorically different thing from being *offered death in lieu of care*.)
