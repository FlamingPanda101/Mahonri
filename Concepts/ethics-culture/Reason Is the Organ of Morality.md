# Reason Is the Organ of Morality

## The Point
Lewis insists that our judgments of good and evil are not feelings but acts of **reason** — specifically what older thinkers called **Practical Reason**: "We must therefore… extend the word Reason to include what our ancestors called Practical Reason."[^1] Until modern times, he notes, "no thinker of the first rank ever doubted that our judgments of value were rational judgements or that what they discovered was objective… in temptation passion was opposed, not to some sentiment, but to reason."[^2] Morality is thus the *perception* of real moral truths by reason, not the projection of subjective taste — which is why the moral law can be discovered rather than invented.

## Evidence
Ward traces Lewis's medieval distinction between two faculties of the Rational Soul: *intellectus*, which "enabled the perception of a self-evident truth," and *ratio*, which "enabled the taking of a series of logical steps in order to arrive at a truth which was not self-evident."[^3] First moral principles are grasped by *intellectus*; only afterward can *ratio* reason from them. Hence Lewis's pivotal axiom: "If nothing is self-evident, nothing can be proved."[^4] No chain of deduction can establish that "A is good for its own sake"; that "must be accepted from some other source before the reasoning can begin."[^5] Ward shows Lewis catching even Samuel Johnson in the slippage: Johnson's *Dictionary* defines reason narrowly as mere deduction (*ratio*), yet immediately illustrates it with Richard Hooker's older, larger sense — "reason is the director of man's will, discovering in action what is good."[^6] The shrinking of "reason" to bare calculation, Lewis argues, is itself a symptom of the subjectivism he opposes: once people doubted "that moral judgements are… rational," the word's meaning contracted to *ratio* alone.[^7] Ward links Lewis's position to Aquinas: "the precepts of the natural law are to the practical reason what the first principles of demonstrations are to the speculative reason, because both are self-evident principles."[^8]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's claim that reason perceives objective moral truth — rather than morality being grounded in bare will — aligns with the Latter-day Saint refusal of [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory|divine command theory]]: good is not good merely because it is commanded; it is recognized as good against a real, **eternal moral law** that even God honors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). Scripture frames moral knowledge as exactly this kind of rational perception: the **Light of Christ** "giveth light to every man" so that all may "judge… that which is good… even the light of Christ" (Moroni 7:16-19) — the *intellectus* of the moral law written on every conscience. This is also the crucial reply to naturalist projects like [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]: because first moral principles are self-evident perceptions and not conclusions deduced from facts, no inventory of brain-states or well-being data can *generate* an "ought" (see [[You Cannot Derive an Ought From an Is]]). Value is something reason apprehends in a moral cosmos, not something neuroscience manufactures.
