# Meet People Where They Are Without Surrendering the Doctrine

## The Claim
[[Tikla Fe]] opens the matrimony episode with a pastoral caveat: Latter-day Saints should "meet people where they are" — even doing something like wearing rainbow socks to help an LGBTQ person feel welcome — *without* that meaning any retreat from the family proclamation or one's covenants. Crucially, she argues, the community should stop **vilifying those who try to make others feel welcome**, treating them "as the enemy" rather than recognizing they are "just trying to make space."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Grounded in a real relationship (strong, personal).** Tikla frames it through her late aunt, a "huge founding member of the lesbian community in Portland" who did "incredibly wholesome good things" (a counseling center for displaced homemakers, elder counseling, an improv group); meeting such a person "where she is" might mean a small gesture of welcome that does not compromise belief.[^2]
2. **"Aimed up" matters (interpretive).** "My aunt was aimed up, and that matters… she was living a life that will prepare her to be at home in heaven" — a degrees-of-glory framing that lets Tikla honor genuine goodness without collapsing doctrine.[^2]
3. **Welcomers are not the enemy (interpretive).** "We sometimes vilify the people who are trying to make other people feel comfortable… instead of recognizing maybe what they're trying to do is just help people feel welcome." She warns against turning people "into lepers with something contagious when really there's healing that's required."[^1]
4. **A genuine in-house disagreement (moderate).** Sam pushes back that church/temple deliberately *minimize* identity markers (white shirts, temple whites) in favor of a shared child-of-God identity, and that rainbow symbols carry contested meanings — so the panel models the tension rather than resolving it cleanly.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
An affirming critic will note the obvious tension the episode does not fully resolve: an entire episode titled "Why Gay Temple Marriage Will Never Happen" is, for many LGBTQ listeners, the opposite of "making space," so the opening charity can read as a disclaimer that softens a hard conclusion. The deeper objection is that "meet people where they are, but the doctrine cannot move" still tells a gay member their covenant path cannot include a sealed relationship — which is precisely the wound, not its healing; warmth about socks does not address that. A traditional defender's honest reply is that *charity and doctrinal fixity are not the same axis*: one can fully love, befriend, and welcome a person whose life one believes is "aimed up" without affirming every choice, and the alternative (making welcome contingent on doctrinal change) collapses pastoral care into agreement. The episode is strongest where it rebukes *Latter-day Saints* for treating welcomers as traitors, and weakest where it underestimates how the headline conclusion lands on the very people it says to welcome.

## LDS Relevance
The concept names a real intra-LDS fault line: the suspicion that members who signal welcome to LGBTQ people (rainbow socks, "all are welcome here") are compromising the faith. Tikla's contribution is to insist they usually are not — welcome and doctrinal conviction can coexist — and to model honoring the genuine good in a person's life via the degrees-of-glory frame. Its limit, which a careful Latter-day Saint should hold, is that warmth does not dissolve the doctrinal claims defended in the same episode ([[Matrimony Means the Making of a Mother]], [[The Sealing Ordinance Is Worded for Man and Woman]]); the pastoral and the doctrinal must both be carried honestly rather than letting either erase the other.

## Best Quotes
> "We need to be careful not to vilify those who are attempting to do things to make people feel welcome… there's a real need for that."[^1]

> "My aunt was aimed up, and that matters to me. She was living a life that will prepare her to be at home in heaven."[^2]
