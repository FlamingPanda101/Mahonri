# The Moral Lawgiver Argument

## The Point
Turek's concluding formulation of the moral argument is a short syllogism: **every law has a lawgiver; there is an objective moral law; therefore there is an objective moral Lawgiver.**[^1] Restated in terms of prescription: "every prescription has a prescriber," and since there is "a prescription for human behavior that is universally binding, there must be someone with the power and means to prescribe it, and enforce it, now and after the grave." Only God — "an unchanging, authoritative moral Being" — has the attributes to be that lawgiver and enforcer.[^2]

## Evidence
Turek stresses that the argument needs only a *single* clear moral fact to go through: "we don't need to know the morally right action for every possible situation. Moral ambiguity in rare situations doesn't negate moral clarity in many others. If there's just one action that's objectively right or wrong, then God exists" — e.g., that "it's objectively wrong to torture babies for fun or to rape and murder little girls."[^3]

The chapter's title question — *What's More Obvious, Morality or Atheism?* — frames his closing epistemic point. Some atheists, seeing that objective morality can't be grounded without God, "abandon belief in objective morality" rather than abandon atheism; Turek calls this "the height of unreasonableness," since "it's far more certain that raping and murdering little girls is wrong than any argument one could offer for atheism."[^4] He clinches it with a concession from atheist philosopher Louise Antony in her debate with William Lane Craig: "Any argument for moral skepticism will be based upon premises which are less obvious than the existence of objective moral values themselves."[^5] Turek also separates grounding from legislation: "you don't need to be religious to know [moral laws] or to support them. You need God to justify them, but not to legislate them."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Every law has a lawgiver. There is an objective moral law. Therefore, there is an objective moral lawgiver." [^1]

> "If there's just one action that's objectively right or wrong, then God exists." [^3]

> "Any argument for moral skepticism will be based upon premises which are less obvious than the existence of objective moral values themselves." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The syllogism is fully usable by Latter-day Saint apologists as a theistic argument shared across the Christian tradition (cf. the existing [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]). The LDS nuance enters at "lawgiver": Restoration theology does not make God the *inventor* of morality by fiat but the perfect embodiment and administrator of eternal moral law that he himself honors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]) — so "Lawgiver" is best read as the supreme moral agent who reveals and enforces an eternal standard, not a voluntarist whose bare will makes things right (see [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]). Turek's "one clear moral fact is enough" move parallels Lewis's appeal to the cross-cultural, self-evident Tao ([[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]; [[Illustrations of the Tao - Cross-Cultural Evidence for Universal Moral Law]]), and his point that we can *know* and *legislate* morality without religion but cannot *justify* it without God matches the LDS distinction between the universally given Light of Christ and the divine ground of the law it reveals.
