# The Pipeline and Sugarcoating Charges Against the Church Are Overstated

## The Claim
The [[Salty Saints - Fact-Checking Johnny Harris's Harm Story|Salty Saints]] panel pushes back on two of [[Johnny Harris]]'s recurring charges: that the Church ran him through a coercive "pipeline," and that it "sugarcoated" its history. On the first, they note Harris's own clarification reveals the dread "pipeline" was simply *attending BYU* — "that's it." On the second, they question whether "sugarcoating" really means the Church should teach contested historical episodes (e.g., Fanny Alger) to twelve-year-olds, given the few instructional hours available.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "pipeline" deflates to BYU (strong, from the source).** Harris's ominous "out of the pipeline of information and social and psychological ramifications" resolves, in his next breath, to "I'd left BYU." The panel: "He graduated from BYU. That's it… The church did not try and get me in that pipeline — I was trying to get in that pipeline" (many are routed to BYU-Idaho or BYU-Hawaii instead).[^2]
2. **Church ≠ Provo (moderate).** Treating BYU/Provo culture as "the pipeline" or "the culture of the church" ignores the global Church — "all the people I taught in the mountains of Guatemala, they're not in the pipeline."[^2]
3. **Limited instructional hours (moderate).** With ~52 Sundays a year (formerly ~104 hours of instruction when church was three hours), the panel argues finite time is better spent teaching people to "connect with God… utilize the atonement" than "random polygamy facts or timelines" — and that topical curricula (Old Testament year, etc.) make in-depth church-history coverage situational, not concealment.[^3]
4. **A parenting analogy for "sugarcoating" (interpretive).** One host notes his own parents never sat him down to detail their marital fights or failures — not deception, just not a useful focus for children — questioning the expectation that the Church narrate every controversy to minors.[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's case is stronger than the "pipeline = BYU" reduction admits, and a fair steelman must engage it: the "pipeline" he describes is not merely a campus but an *integrated system* — seminary, mission, BYU, marriage, calling — with real social and economic stakes for stepping off, and the "sugarcoating" complaint is not "teach Fanny Alger to 12-year-olds" but that the *correlated* curriculum he grew up on omitted or smoothed material (multiple First Vision accounts, seer-stone translation, polygamy's scope) that he later found jarring, fueling a sense of betrayal. The "limited hours" reply is partly fair but partly a dodge: the issue is *what* is taught in those hours and whether hard topics are acknowledged at all, not raw airtime. The strongest honest concession (made by faithful responders elsewhere) is that earlier curricula *did* underemphasize hard history, that the Church has since published more transparent materials (Gospel Topics Essays, Saints, the Joseph Smith Papers) — which Harris himself acknowledges — and that "more transparent now" is the right response to a real past gap, not a denial that the gap existed.

## LDS Relevance
The concept helps Latter-day Saints separate a fair point from an overreach. Fair: "the Church controlled my whole life via a pipeline" is hyperbole when the named pipeline is voluntary university attendance, and the Church is far larger than Provo. Overreach to avoid: dismissing the curricular concern entirely — the more credible posture grants that correlated lessons underemphasized hard history, points to the Church's now-published transparent resources (which Harris concedes), and reserves "limited hours" for *why* Sunday instruction centers on Christ rather than as a blanket excuse.
