# The Occasion of The Abolition of Man - The Green Book

## The Point
Lewis wrote *The Abolition of Man* (delivered as the 1943 Riddell Memorial Lectures at Durham, in the depths of the Second World War) as a reply to a single school textbook he had been sent for review.[^1] He calls it "The Green Book" and its authors "Gaius and Titius" to spare their blushes; in fact it was *The Control of Language* (1939) by Alec King and Martin Ketley.[^2] What alarmed Lewis was that this book quietly taught children that all value-statements are merely reports of the speaker's private feelings — and did so without argument, smuggled into a book on a different subject.[^3]

## Evidence
Ward shows that the textbook is only a "foil," a convenient opponent through which Lewis can establish his anti-subjectivist case by contrast.[^4] Gaius and Titius take the example of a man calling a waterfall "sublime" and insist that "he was not making a remark about the waterfall, but a remark about his own feelings"; the remark has, they say, no referential meaning, only emotive meaning.[^5] Lewis traces a chain of transmission: King and Ketley's subjectivism popularizes the literary theory of **I.A. Richards** (whom Lewis names as his "main target"), which itself echoes the wider movement of logical positivism associated with **A.J. Ayer**, who held that moral statements are "pseudo-concepts" expressing nothing but feeling.[^6] Lewis confirmed the lineage in a 1947 letter to Dorothy L. Sayers: asked whether Richards was "the precursor of my Green Book villains," he answered "yes."[^7] What especially offends Lewis is the irresponsibility of teaching this to children "for whom it is far more dangerous," when the same idea at the professorial level was at least "above-board, a theory advanced for adult consideration and argued for."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Actually, he was not making a remark about the waterfall, but a remark about his own feelings." (King and Ketley, quoted by Lewis) [^5]

> Lewis's concern is for "intellectual honesty and self-awareness on the part of academics, especially in writing for children." [^8]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's complaint is that "Gaius and Titius" teach the young to disbelieve in any **objective, eternal moral law** — exactly the inheritance Latter-day Saints hold sacred. Scripture affirms that every person is born with a true moral sense: the **Light of Christ** "giveth light to every man that cometh into the world" and enables all to "judge... that which is good... even the light of Christ" (Moroni 7:16-19); that light is "the law by which all things are governed" (D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]). Because this moral knowledge is given by God and not invented by the speaker, the schoolbook's claim that value is "only a feeling" is, in LDS terms, a denial of conscience itself. This is the same naturalist move answered today by [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]], which likewise must wrestle with whether values are objective; Lewis's diagnosis exposes the self-refuting character of the subjectivism beneath it (see [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
