# You Are Powerful Where You Live

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]]'s organizing thesis: real power in helping others is *local*. "You're powerful because you speak the language, you understand the culture, you're there every day… These are your people." A neighbor who is present continuously can do what an outside NGO with funds cannot — and the truest power is not money or position but **faith expressed in daily solidarity**.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Shanta watershed (strong, anecdote).** After the 2004 tsunami in Sri Lanka, Eubank — representing a "global NGO" with "a big humanitarian pot of money" — stood "paralyzed," unable to help grieving families. Her interpreter Shanta kicked a soccer ball with the kids, praised a woman's bread, and helped another find washing powder. "Shanta is more powerful than I am… he's there every single day, and I'm not."[^1]
2. **Power redefined as solidarity (interpretive).** "It's not money, it's not power, it's faith." The power is "that solidarity among human beings" — "I can't fix everything, but what I can do, I'll do."[^2]
3. **Covenant amplifies locality (moderate).** A locally present person who is "covenanted… I will seek the interest of my neighbor" becomes "the most powerful person in the world… there's no way to stop a person like that."[^3]
4. **Scales through councils (moderate).** Multiple local "Shantas" who "choose to be connected" form a council (HOA, PTA, ward) that holds itself accountable; money then "percolated through" that committed local group gains far more leverage than outside spending.[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A development economist could object that *systemic* problems — famine logistics, vaccine supply chains, disaster finance — are precisely what local presence *cannot* solve and what large, capitalized, technically expert institutions are built for. Romanticizing the powerful local neighbor risks under-resourcing the infrastructure (clinics, water systems, cash transfers) that actually moves the needle on poverty and mortality; "faith and presence" do not rebuild a thousand homes.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank is not opposing institutions to locals — she *ran* the institution building the thousand homes, and her point is that the two operate at different layers: the NGO supplies resource and scale; the local actor supplies the relationship, trust, and moment-to-moment ministering that resource alone cannot buy, and through which resource is best *delivered* (her "percolate money through local councils" model). The thesis corrects an *over*-reliance on money/scale, not a denial of it, and is pitched to the ordinary person asking what *they* can do.

## Best Quotes
> "You're powerful where you live. You're powerful because you speak the language, you understand the culture, you're there every day… These are your people."[^1]

> "That person is the most powerful person in the world. It's not money, it's not power, it's faith."[^3]
