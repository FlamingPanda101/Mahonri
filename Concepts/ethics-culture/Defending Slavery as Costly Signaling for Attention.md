# Defending Slavery as Costly Signaling for Attention

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that Joshua HS's defense of biblical slavery is not driven by careful exegesis but functions as **costly signaling** — taking a deliberately offensive, countercultural stance to demonstrate group loyalty and attract attention.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The provocation structure (rated: moderate; argued).** The original viral claim escalates deliberately — "the institution of slavery is not inherently evil... It is not inherently evil to own another human being... I know, just wait" — a rhetorical pattern engineered to provoke.[^2]

2. **Costly signaling defined (rated: moderate; argued).** "What Joshua is doing is trying to leverage the Bible in order to just endorse a countercultural ethical stance as a piece of costly signaling so he can get attention." The very offensiveness is the point: it signals commitment that cheaper claims could not.[^1]

3. **Continuity with the modesty episode (rated: moderate).** McClellan frames this within a pattern of "ill-advised things on the internet," including the earlier modesty screeds, suggesting a habit of provocation over substance.[^3] See [[Biblical Modesty Refers to Wealth Not Sexuality]].

4. **Connection to identity politics (rated: moderate).** The signaling reinforces in-group identity, dovetailing with McClellan's broader thesis in [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]].[^1]

5. **Steelman.** Joshua would presumably say he is not seeking attention but defending biblical authority against cultural compromise — that affirming hard texts is faithfulness, not signaling. McClellan's rebuttal is empirical: the affirmation is paired with demonstrable ignorance of the texts (see [[Apologist Misremembers and Hallucinates Bible Passages]]), suggesting the stance precedes and outruns the exegesis.[^1]

## Best Quotes

> "What Joshua is doing is trying to leverage the Bible in order to just endorse a countercultural ethical stance as a piece of costly signaling so he can get attention."[^1]
