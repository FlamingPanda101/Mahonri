# Western Secularism Is Post-Christian Not Non-Christian

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Tom Holland]]'s synthesis: a modern Western atheist who explicitly rejects God, Jesus, and "all of that" is *still a product of Christianity* and "looks at the world through Christian eyes" whether he accepts it or not. The dominant civic creed — "be kind, we're all basically the same, there are many roads to God" (Holland's "Paddingtonism") — is not a neutral non-religious baseline but a *mutated post-Christianity*. The West is therefore at a "great choke point" with several incompatible futures simultaneously in play.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The atheist still sees through Christian eyes.** The hosts call this the heart of the conversation: even the self-described atheist who rejects the creed "is still a product of Christianity, and as a result looks at the world... through Christian eyes" — and "people don't quite understand what that means," especially when they look at Islam or other cultures and assume "we're all the same."[^2] *(Strong restatement of the core Dominion thesis; see [[We Are Goldfish Who Cannot See the Christian Water]].)*
2. **"Paddingtonism" is a religion that won't admit it is one.** The reigning ideology — "be kind, diversity is wonderful, we're all basically the same, there are many roads up the mountain" — has its "paradigmatic pulpit" in BBC Radio 4's *Thought for the Day*, where clergy of every faith "preach exactly the same message" with different garnishings. Asked if that pluralist creed is *true*, Holland (the agnostic) flatly says "No, I don't think it's true," because the study of history reveals "the infinite number of ways there are to be human" — every society wrongly assumes its own way is the obvious standard.[^3] *(A striking concession from a non-believer that the pluralist civic religion is a contingent faith, not a neutral fact.)*
3. **The borrowed-rocket image and the "choke point."** Holland offers three (then four) futures: (a) post-Christian "Paddingtonism" as a rocket-head that has jettisoned the Christian "baggage" of Bible and church and now flies on self-evidently-good liberal principles; (b) a return of "might is right" — Nietzsche's prophecy realized in fascism/Nazism, whose fading bogeyman-power (Hitler "has taken the place of the devil") has kept liberals "on the straight and narrow" but is wearing off; (c) a genuine return to Christianity, with "tentative signs"; plus (d) the growth of Islam. Holland sees "signs of all three [four] happening at once" — Britain on "the verge of going through something" like the Norman Conquest or the Reformation.[^4] *(A serious civilizational forecast; the "Hitler replaced the devil" line is a memorable diagnosis of secular moral motivation.)*
4. **Progress and arrogance are themselves Christian.** Even the *idea* of being "progressive" — progressing ahead of everyone else — is "a very Western thing" with "a sense of arrogance baked into" it; the conviction that one's secular liberal society is "uniquely advanced" is not a view from nowhere but a culturally specific inheritance.[^5] *(Reinforces that the secular self-image is itself a tradition, not the absence of one.)*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A secularist replies that genealogy is not justification: even if liberal values *historically* descend from Christianity, they may now stand on independent rational grounds (autonomy, well-being, the social contract), so the "you're still Christian" point is a genetic observation that does no normative work — cut flowers can be replanted. Holland's rejoinder (shared with [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]]) is that the attempted re-grounding has largely failed — 150 years of moral philosophy "trying to hold on to Christian inheritance without rooting it in Christian theology" — and that when the Christian inhibitions fully fade, "what's wrong with doing what I like if I'm rich and powerful?" has no obvious secular answer. The honest middle: the genealogy is well-evidenced and humbling, but whether the values *need* their root to survive is the open empirical question Holland frames as the "choke point."

## LDS Relevance
This is the master-key apologetic frame the Restoration can deploy against secular critique: the critic's own moral vision is borrowed Christian capital, so the debate is not "neutral reason vs. religion" but "which account best *grounds* the values we already share." The Restoration answers the grounding question concretely — the worth of souls in literal divine kinship (D&C 18:10), continuing revelation to re-anchor moral commitments a "mutating" post-Christianity cannot stabilize, and a frank historicism (every age wrongly universalizes itself) that supports charitable reading of both scripture and the past.
