# Zealous Ex-Mormon Atheists Engage the Church More Than Members Do

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]], reading [[Ryan Burge]]'s data on the most hostile cluster of the Nones (the "Zealous Atheists," whom Burge himself calls "Reddit atheists"), highlight a paradox: this group **still interacts with religion more than the merely "done" group does** — ~17% attend a service yearly and pray "a little" — *not* out of residual faith but, in Burge's own explanation, because "they still interact with religion enough to remind themselves why they dislike it so much." The hosts apply this to online ex-Mormon culture (e.g., r/exmormon), claiming such critics scour General Conference, lesson manuals, and their believing relatives' lives for grievance fuel — engaging the Church, in some respects, more intensely as opponents than they did as members.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Burge's data shows residual engagement in the most hostile cluster — rate: solid (tier-A source)
Among "zealous atheists," ~75% had tried to talk someone out of their religion in the past year, yet ~17% attended a house of worship once a year or more and a similar share prayed a little — anomalous for a group defined by hostility. Burge's stated interpretation: continued contact (a religious spouse/parent, Christmas Eve mass) keeps the antipathy fresh. *Strong: this is Burge's published finding and his own gloss, not the hosts' invention.*[^2]

### 2. The pattern fits observed ex-Mormon online behavior — rate: plausible but anecdotal
The hosts argue r/exmormon visibly tracks LDS General Conference and Come Follow Me lesson manuals and reacts to believing relatives' words — i.e., heavy *engagement* with the institution they left. They do a live "audit" of the subreddit's top posts to illustrate. *Moderate-to-weak as evidence: a real and recognizable phenomenon, but the demonstration is impressionistic (cherry-picked posts), and Burge's data is about American "nones" broadly, not ex-Mormons specifically — the mapping from "zealous atheists" to "ex-Mormon subreddit" is the hosts' inference.*[^1]

### 3. Even a non-Mormon observer flags the toxicity — rate: rhetorically useful
Barnes notes Burge (no Latter-day Saint) independently describes this cluster as "often angry, caustic, and eager to get into an argument," which the hosts take as outside corroboration that the dynamic they experience is real. *Moderate: a neutral third party characterizing the group similarly is mild corroboration of tone, not of the specific ex-Mormon claim.*[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A fair critic pushes back. (a) **Over-generalization / selection bias.** A subreddit's most-upvoted posts are not a representative sample of people who left the Church; most disaffiliates are the quiet "done" or "raised-in-it" types who never post, so foregrounding the loud minority caricatures ex-members and commits a survivorship/visibility error. (b) **Engagement has benign explanations.** Continued contact is often *relational*, not obsessive — people attend a parent's funeral, a sibling's baptism, a child's blessing; reading Conference may be processing a high-control upbringing, grieving, or staying connected to believing family, not "scouring for grievance." (c) **Motivated framing.** The episode is partly a morale piece for an apologetics audience; labeling critics "toxic bedfellows" can be a way to dismiss substantive grievances (e.g., about history or policy) rather than engage them. (d) **Symmetry.** Apologists also engage critics intensely (this very episode "audits" r/exmormon); high engagement with the other side is a feature of *committed partisans on both sides*, not uniquely of ex-members. The defensible core: Burge's data genuinely shows the most hostile "nones" retain more religious *contact* than the indifferent "dones," and his explanation (contact sustains antipathy) is reasonable; the loud, conference-tracking ex-Mormon archetype is real — but it is a *subset*, and extrapolating from upvoted posts to "ex-Mormons engage more than members" overstates a claim the underlying data does not establish about Latter-day Saints in particular.

## Best Quotes
> "What's noteworthy about this group is that they actually engage with religion more than [the dones]… 17% attend a house of worship once a year or more."[^2]

> "They still interact with religion enough to remind themselves why they dislike it so much… they go just so they can remind themselves how much they hate it."[^2]

## LDS Connection
A pastoral/apologetic application of [[Ryan Burges Four-Part Typology of the Nones]]: the "zealous atheist" cluster, not the quiet "done," is the one believers most often meet online. It pairs with the vault's LDS-specific work on *why* people leave ([[Four Distinct Groups of Those Who Leave the LDS Church]], [[Politics Predicts Religious Disaffiliation]]). Best read with the steelman foregrounded: the data point is sound, the ex-Mormon application is a recognizable but anecdotal and partly polemical extension that should not be mistaken for a measured finding about disaffiliated Latter-day Saints as a whole.
