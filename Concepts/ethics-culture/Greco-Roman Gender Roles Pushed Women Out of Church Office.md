# Greco-Roman Gender Roles Pushed Women Out of Church Office

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] argues women's early leadership was eroded not by a sudden doctrinal decree but by the **gradual import of Greco-Roman gender norms** as the church institutionalized. As upper-class Roman men joined and filled offices, they brought public/private distinctions in which women belonged to the silent, private "household" and men to the public sphere — and the church increasingly *became* a public, governing institution.

## Evidence

### 1. The shift from private house to public basilica
In the 3rd–4th centuries, church meetings physically relocated "from private houses to basilicas and other public buildings." Roman public space was the man's domain; women were expected to keep modesty and silence in public and could not hold public office. So "it was easy for Tertullian and other later Roman Christian writers to conclude that women had no rights to speak or act in the 'public' of the Church." [^1]

### 2. Paul already accommodates the head-covering norm
Even while permitting women to pray and prophesy, 1 Corinthians 11 insists "the head of the woman is the man" and that women cover their heads — Paul "shows continuing concern for maintaining traditional Greco-Roman customs." [^2]

### 3. The silencing texts and disputed authorship
1 Corinthians 14:34–35 ("Let your women keep silence in the churches") is, "many scholars have argued," a later non-Pauline addition, since it contradicts Paul's earlier permission of women's prophesying and floats in the manuscripts. The same anti-woman thrust appears in the **disputed** epistles (Ephesians, Colossians, 1 Timothy): wives subject to husbands, women to "learn in silence with all subjection" and not "to usurp authority over the man" (1 Tim 2:11–12). [^3]

### 4. 1 Timothy disciplines "unruly" single women
1 Timothy defines qualifications for bishops and deacons (good household managers, married once) and seeks to bring widows under control — only widows over 60, married once, with good works qualify for the church's list; younger widows "should instead marry again, have children, and take up their proper role as household managers." The text "uses stereotypes common to Roman culture about single women behaving badly." [^4]

### 5. The new elite brought Roman organizational instincts
"More male members of the upper-class ruling elite became Christians and began to fill the leadership roles … They brought with them notions of organization, leadership, and order that stemmed from Roman public life." Leadership shifted from "a special commission to preach the gospel" to governing "the established body of the Church" — and women lost their avenue of visible participation. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "common cultural attitudes about women and proper gender roles increasingly determined the ways in which women were viewed by Church leaders and how they were allowed to contribute." [^5]

> "when the qualifications for Church leadership shifted from a missionary to a Roman imperial government model, women lost this avenue to visible participation and influence in the Church." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Laughton resists reading this purely as "a narrative of loss" or "a decline and fall into apostasy"; she frames it as "also one of grace," with modern LDS developments (home-centered worship, women's expanded callings) "circling back toward" the ancient pattern. The cultural-erosion thesis lets a [[Latter-day Saints|Latter-day Saint]] reader see the suppression as social/cultural rather than as Christ's original design. [^6]
