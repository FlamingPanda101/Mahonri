# Biblical Slavery Is Like Credit Card Debt Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
On the podcast, one speaker analogized owning a debt slave to modern **credit card debt** ("which is credit card debt today, just so you know"), with Joshua agreeing ("Yeah. No, that's exactly right").[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **Ownership of a body, not a balance (rated: strong; argued).** "The ownership of a debt slave is in no way, shape, or form whatsoever comparable to credit card debt." A creditor does not "control what you're doing every hour of the day. It does not control your body."[^2]

2. **No legal right to abuse (rated: strong).** A credit card company "is not able to physically abuse you without any punishment" — whereas a master could beat an enslaved person ([[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]]).[^2]

3. **Forced breeding (rated: strong; harrowing).** A creditor cannot "bring a woman to you and say, 'Breed me additional enslaved people that I'm going to keep even after you go free. Oh, and I'm keeping the woman, too.'" Slave offspring were the master's property.[^2]

4. **Steelman.** The analogy's grain of truth is that both debt slavery and credit-card debt are economic obligations one can be released from by repayment. McClellan grants debt slavery was "an economic arrangement" but argues the comparison collapses the moment you account for bodily ownership, legal violence, and heritable offspring.[^2]

## Best Quotes

> "The ownership of a debt slave is in no way, shape, or form whatsoever comparable to credit card debt. Yes, credit card debt sucks, but the credit card company does not control what you're doing every hour of the day. It does not control your body. It is not able to physically abuse you without any punishment."[^2]

> "It is not able to bring a woman to you and say, 'Breed me additional enslaved people that I'm going to keep even after you go free. Oh, and I'm keeping the woman, too.'"[^2]
