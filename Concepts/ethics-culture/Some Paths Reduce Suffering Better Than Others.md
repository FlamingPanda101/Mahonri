# Some Paths Reduce Suffering Better Than Others

## The Point
This is [[Jacob Hansen]]'s answer to the subjectivity objection raised at the end of Part 1 (see [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]). A critic grants the universal desire to escape suffering but says the *desired end* is "ultimately a matter of preference and therefore strictly subjective." Hansen concedes we have "different notions about what will move us the farthest away from ultimate suffering," but denies the conclusion: "that does not mean that all roads will indeed actually lead us equally away from suffering," and "we can objectively say that some paths are better or worse than others in relation to the universal goal of avoiding our own suffering." [^1] Disagreement about the route does not make the destination subjective.

## Evidence
Two moves carry the argument. **(1) Moral systems can be ranked.** "Communism, fundamentalism, utilitarianism, humanism may be moral insofar as they take us away from the worst conceivable suffering, and some of these systems may be more moral than others in the sense that they move us farther away from suffering." [^2] So objectivity does not require a single right answer at every point — it requires that there be *better and worse* answers, which there demonstrably are. **(2) The health analogy.** "Just because there is much disagreement about what brings about better physical health does not mean that physically better or worse health is a subjective phenomenon." His test: "go down to your local hospital and meet people getting treatments for severe obesity and diabetes" — since no sane person wants those "hellish treatments," "we can say that you ought to eat healthy and exercise." [^3] The body's functionality is "governed by laws that exist independent of anyone's personal opinions about those laws," and "in like manner all aspects of our suffering and well-being are based on proper relationship to the laws of reality." [^4] Hence the punchline: "suffering doesn't care about your arbitrary opinions; if you put your hand on a hot stove, suffering will result." [^5]

This generalizes the well-being framework shared with [[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]] and Harris's "moral landscape," while insisting (against pure subjectivism) that the landscape has real peaks and valleys.

## Best Quotes
> "While we may have different notions about what will move us the farthest away from ultimate suffering, that does not mean that all roads will indeed actually lead us equally away from suffering — and we can objectively say that some paths are better or worse than others." [^1]

> "Just because there is much disagreement about what brings about better physical health does not mean that physically better or worse health is a subjective phenomenon." [^3]

> "Suffering doesn't care about your arbitrary opinions. If you put your hand on a hot stove, suffering will result." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The "laws of reality that govern suffering and well-being" map onto the restored gospel's conviction that morality is anchored in **eternal law** rather than divine fiat — God himself conforms to the celestial order. Just as the laws of health "exist independent of anyone's personal opinions," the moral order is discovered, not invented; the work of moral life is coming into "proper relationship to the laws of reality," which on the eternal scale means proper relationship to God and creation (see [[Pure Rationality Makes Morality Selfish - Only God Connects All Well-Being]]).
