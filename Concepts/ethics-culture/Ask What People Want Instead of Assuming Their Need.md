# Ask What People Want Instead of Assuming Their Need

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]] argues the "premier skill" of helping is the opening question — "What do you want? What's going on in your life? What help do you need from me?" — and that **"making an assumption about what people need is always going to be wrong."** Honoring the recipient's agency (even letting them choose poorly and learn) is more effective and more dignifying than imposing the helper's diagnosis.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The soccer-field lights (strong, anecdote).** A community offered a health center said they wanted lights for their soccer field; once helped to get what *they* wanted, they had a safe, lit place for kids at night — and were then ready to tackle the clinic. "Making an assumption… is always going to be wrong."[^2]
2. **The women's-shelter Christmas (strong, anecdote).** A ward's lavish Christmas-Eve gift-giving made the sheltered mothers "feel like failures." The next year they let the mothers choose and wrap gifts from a little store, sacrificing the donors' "emotional kick" so the *mothers* could give to their own children — also accommodating non-Christian families' holidays.[^3]
3. **Agency over outcome (interpretive).** Eubank insists this is not naive "give them whatever they ask." There is "a skill to… asking somebody to ask for something and helping them think through that" — walking through "what happens when it runs out?" while honoring the final choice: "I'm going to honor whatever you say, but let's walk through it."[^4]
4. **Mistakes are part of the method (moderate).** Even when someone spends the $50 on cigarettes, "that's the way the Lord works with us. You made a mistake, and I'm still going to help you" — the relationship absorbs the failure and learns from it.[^5]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A paternalist (and much of public-health practice) replies that *experts often do know better*: people in crisis may request the lit soccer field while their children die of malaria, and "honor whatever you say" can subsidize self-destructive choices (the cigarettes) or culturally entrenched harms. Sometimes the responsible, compassionate act is to provide the bed-net or the clinic the community *didn't* ask for, because immediate preference is a poor guide to genuine welfare.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank's model already contains the expert's concern as a *conversation*, not a veto: she walks the recipient through longer-term tradeoffs ("between those two things, which has the longer-term benefit?") before honoring their choice, and the soccer-field case shows that meeting the felt want first *builds the trust and buy-in* that later makes the clinic possible — imposed solutions that ignore agency tend to be rejected or unused. The deeper claim is that respecting agency is itself part of the welfare being delivered (dignity, ownership, capacity), not a sentimental abdication of judgment. See [[The Exchange Is Worth More Than Money]].
