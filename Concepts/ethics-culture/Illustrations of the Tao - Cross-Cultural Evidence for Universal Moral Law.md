# Illustrations of the Tao - Cross-Cultural Evidence for Universal Moral Law

## The Point
Lewis closes *The Abolition of Man* with an Appendix titled **"Illustrations of the Tao"** — a catalogue of moral precepts drawn from "a vast array of cultures," including Norse, Egyptian, Babylonian, Hebrew, Greek, Roman, Hindu, and American Indian sources.[^1] The recurrence of the same core duties across civilizations that never influenced one another is presented as **empirical evidence** that the moral law is "not something that we construct but something... built into the order of the universe."[^2] It is the natural-law apologist's exhibit A for a universal morality.

## Evidence
Ward catalogues 122 sources, organized into **eight "laws"** in two quartets: General Beneficence, Special Beneficence, Duties to Parents/Elders/Ancestors, Duties to Children and Posterity; then Justice, Good Faith and Veracity, Mercy, and Magnanimity.[^3] He notes how comparatively *few* of the citations come from the Judaeo-Christian tradition — only twelve from the Old Testament and five from the New — undercutting any charge that Lewis simply favoured his own scriptures.[^4] Ward records two important qualifications Lewis himself makes. First, Lewis explicitly **disclaims the argument from common consent**: "I am not trying to prove its validity by the argument from common consent. Its validity cannot be deduced. For those who do not perceive its rationality, even universal consent could not prove it."[^5] The Tao rests on "preconceptions... per se nota, basic, self-evident, axiomatic."[^5] Second, Ward cites Basil Mitchell: a closer survey "would reveal considerable differences between the various ethical traditions," yet Lewis "is right nevertheless in stressing what they do have in common," explicitly rejecting J.L. Mackie's *Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong*.[^2] Wesley Kort, quoted by Ward, adds a postcolonial defense: Lewis's lines of similarity offer "a salutary recognition that other peoples are not only different from us but also like us."[^6] Ward observes that Lewis deliberately ends the whole book on three "successive chords" about good death — Plato's *Phaedo*, the Norse *Hávamál* ("myself offered to Myself"), and Christ's words in John 12:24 — quietly marking where Lewis believed wisdom "had reached its highest point."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "I am not trying to prove its validity by the argument from common consent. Its validity cannot be deduced. For those who do not perceive its rationality, even universal consent could not prove it." (Lewis) [^5]

> "Morality is not something that we construct but something, as it were, built into the order of the universe." — Basil Mitchell, on Lewis [^2]

## LDS Connection
The Appendix is perhaps the strongest natural-law parallel to a distinctive Restoration doctrine: the **Light of Christ**, "the Spirit [that] giveth light to every man that cometh into the world" (D&C 84:46; John 1:9), by which "every thing which inviteth to do good... is sent forth by the power and gift of Christ" and may be known "with a perfect knowledge" (Moroni 7:16-19). Lewis's empirical finding — that Norse, Hindu, Egyptian, and Hebrew peoples independently affirm the same duties of justice, mercy, honesty, and care for parents and posterity — is exactly what one would expect if a single divine light illumines *every* conscience, not merely those within the covenant. The Restoration teaches that God "speaketh unto all nations" and inspires truth wherever it is found (cf. 2 Nephi 29:7-12; Alma 29:8). Lewis's caution that the Tao must be *perceived*, not proved by majority vote, also matches the LDS insistence that moral and spiritual truth is recognized by an inward, Spirit-given light rather than demonstrated by external consensus. This is the positive answer Lewis offers where naturalistic projects like [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] must still build morality from scratch (see [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]).
