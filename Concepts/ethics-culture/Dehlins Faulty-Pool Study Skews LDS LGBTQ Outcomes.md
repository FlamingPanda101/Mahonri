# Dehlins Faulty-Pool Study Skews LDS LGBTQ Outcomes

## The Claim
[[Timber Harvard|Tember Harward]] (with Jeff Bennion) argues that [[John Dehlin]]'s research on LGBTQ Latter-day Saint outcomes is methodologically faulty: it draws participants "from a pool of a bunch of people who basically think the same" (a non-representative, disaffected sample), and then generalizes from that skewed pool to "the fate of all LGBT members of the church." A larger, more balanced study — the "Four Options" study by **Tyler Mansfield** (a faithful, same-sex-attracted member married to a woman) paired with **Tyler Lefever** (an ex-Mormon, gay, pro-LGBT researcher) — reaches very different conclusions from a wider participant pool.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (as presented)

### 1. Sampling bias drives the result
The core methodological charge: a sample recruited largely through disaffected / ex-Mormon networks will over-represent negative outcomes, so Dehlin's pessimistic conclusions partly reflect *who answered*, not the underlying population. Harward also notes the suicide statistic Dehlin advanced is challenged by a *Deseret News* guest opinion (Goodman & Dyer) citing secular data that religious LGBT individuals fare better than non-religious ones.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate as a methodological principle (non-representative sampling genuinely biases estimates); weak as adjudication (the on-air description of Dehlin's actual sampling frame is secondhand and uncharitable).

### 2. The balanced-team study as a corrective
Harward's reason for trusting the "Four Options" study is its adversarial collaboration: a faithful SSA researcher and a gay ex-Mormon researcher *together*, with "a bigger pool," reducing the chance that one side's priors drive the design — a structural safeguard against the bias alleged against Dehlin.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate. Adversarial collaboration is a real bias-reducer; the specific study's methods/sample are not detailed on-air, so this is a pointer, not a verified comparison.

### 3. The "in-person" disconnect
Secondary point: the hosts argue Dehlin's professed allyship sits oddly with how Harward was treated in the Jubilee segment (cut off, dog-piled), framing it as evidence the conclusions flow from a stance, not curiosity. (Rhetorical, not statistical.)[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak (ad hominem-adjacent; relevant to motive, not to data quality).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Dehlin's defenders make fair replies. (a) Convenience and network sampling is *endemic* to this entire research area, including the faith-affirming studies Harward prefers — Latter-day Saint LGBTQ samples are hard to recruit randomly, so "your pool is biased" cuts at North Star / Bennion / Mansfield data too, not just Dehlin's. (b) Some of Dehlin's work (e.g., large LGBTQ-Mormon surveys) reports real, replicated distress signals — high rejection, faith-sexuality conflict, family strain — that should not be waved away merely because the messenger is a critic. (c) Calling a study "faulty" because you dislike its conclusion, without engaging its actual sampling and statistics, is itself a bias. The honest synthesis: **both** sides' datasets are vulnerable to selection effects, so the methodological high ground belongs to *adversarial, pre-registered, representative* designs — which is exactly why the "Four Options"-style balanced collaboration Harward cites is the right instinct, and why confident multipliers from *any* single non-representative survey (Dehlin's pessimistic ones included) are tier-C. The defensible claim is narrow and symmetric: no single convenience-sampled study settles "the fate of all LGBT members," and the most-cited pessimistic figures rest on pools no more representative than the optimistic ones (see [[Mixed-Orientation Marriages and LGBTQ Latter-day Saints Show Higher Wellbeing]] and [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]]).

## Best Quotes
> "The way he conducted the study was from a pool of a bunch of people who basically think the same, and so he got these bad answers on the study and concludes... the fate of all LGBT members of the church is this — but it's a faulty study."[^1]

> "There's this other study called the Four Options study by Tyler Mansfield — a faithful same-sex-attracted member married to a woman — paired with Tyler Lefever, an ex-Mormon gay married to a guy... a lot more well-rounded, with a bigger pool."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the methodological backbone behind [[Mixed-Orientation Marriages and LGBTQ Latter-day Saints Show Higher Wellbeing]]: the wellbeing case lives or dies on whether the comparison population is representative. The note documents the Latter-day Saint critique of [[John Dehlin]]'s LGBTQ research and the appeal to adversarial-collaboration data — while the steelman flags that the selection-bias sword is double-edged and that the rigorous standard (representative, pre-registered, balanced) indicts confident numbers on *both* sides. Tier-C as relayed.
