# The Poor Are Partially Complicit in Their Own Fate

## The Claim

[[Tyler Cowen]] (relayed by [[Ward Radio]]) argues that the reason people resist the "Mormonism cures poverty" thesis is that it "makes the poor look less like victims and more like a group **partially complicit in their own fate**" — i.e., it locates *some* causal power over poverty in personal habits and choices, not only in external imposition. The hosts ([[Jonah Barnes]], [[Cardon Ellis]]) endorse the *partial*, agency-affirming version while explicitly rejecting a "just stop being poor" caricature.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Partially," not "entirely" — rate: moderate (careful framing)
Cowen's wording is deliberately bounded: poverty is *partially* within one's influence. The hosts stress he is *not* saying "you're poor, that's your fault" — external factors and real unfairness are granted — but that *operating as if poverty is purely externally imposed* tends to keep people poor, whereas an internal locus of control ("I am master of my own fate") improves outcomes.[^2]

### 2. Agency is empowering, not blaming — rate: moderate (psychology)
The point is framed as good news: believing you *can* affect your circumstances motivates the education, thrift, and family stability that change them. A faith that instills "I'm a steward of my time and my life" gives people back a sense of efficacy that pure-victim framings remove. This is the mechanism behind [[Mormonism as a Sociological Cure for Poverty]].[^3]

### 3. The Benin barber anecdote — rate: weak/illustrative
Barnes recounts his father's charity work in Benin, where a barber closed at noon because he "made enough," with no concept of saving, reinvesting, or scaling — illustrating (the hosts argue) how an absent *culture of increase/ambition*, not just lack of money, perpetuates poverty. Offered as a vivid story, not data.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the most ethically and empirically fraught claim in the set. (a) "Culture/agency causes poverty" has a long history of sliding into *blaming the poor* and rationalizing the withdrawal of help; the "partially complicit" frame can be weaponized to discount structural causes (wages, discrimination, health shocks, lack of capital) that are demonstrably powerful and not chosen. (b) The Benin-barber anecdote is exactly the kind of story critics flag as *imposing a Western growth/ambition norm* as if it were a universal good — "contentment at a sufficiency" is a defensible value, not a pathology, and reading it as a poverty-cause begs the question. (c) Locus-of-control research is real but bidirectional: chronic poverty *erodes* sense of control (scarcity taxes cognition), so an external locus can be a *consequence* of deprivation, not merely a cause — making "act as if you have control" hard to enact without first relieving the deprivation that policy targets. (d) The word "partially" does a lot of load-bearing work that the rhetoric can quietly drop. So the defensible core is narrow: personal agency is a *real and non-zero* factor in economic outcomes, and frameworks that restore a sense of efficacy can help — while honesty requires holding the "partially" firmly, resisting the slide to victim-blaming, and conceding that structural drivers remain large and are precisely what external policy exists to address.

## Best Quotes

> "Proposing Mormonism as a fix makes the poor look less like victims and more like a group partially complicit in their own fate."[^1]

> "It's not like, oh, you're poor, that's your fault... but he is saying the reason people don't like this solution is because what you're addressing is the culture and the soul of the person."[^2]

> "What would I do with twice as much money? ...the idea of eternal increase, of ambition, wasn't a thing... on his radar."[^4]
