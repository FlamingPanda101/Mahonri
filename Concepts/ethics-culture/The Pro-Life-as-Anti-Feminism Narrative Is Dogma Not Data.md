# The Pro-Life-as-Anti-Feminism Narrative Is Dogma Not Data

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Dan McClellan]], on a large platform, framed the pro-life movement as (1) a **backlash against feminism** — a way to "push back against women advocating for rights when it comes to abortion" — and (2) historically a project of Jerry Falwell and others who, having lost the fight for racial segregation, "settled on abortion" to galvanize conservatives and elect Ronald Reagan. On this telling, opposition to abortion is fundamentally about suppressing women and is downstream of segregationist politics. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues McClellan's account is **"dogma, not data"**: it ignores the actual moral stakes (the unborn, including "little girls," who are killed), conflates the distinct *waves* of feminism, and reflects a pattern in which McClellan reverse-engineers history to whatever "pisses off white evangelical men." [^2]

1. **It erases the unborn entirely.** "Has he ever considered that maybe they exist? The way that a guy like him will pretend that those babies don't exist, that those little girls don't exist, is just revolting." The narrative treats abortion purely as a women's-rights battle, assuming away the entity whose moral status is the whole question. *Rating: strong as an internal critique — the framing presupposes the pro-choice answer to the personhood question.* [^3]
2. **It conflates the feminist waves.** Hansen: "You don't distinguish between the different waves, Dan. Do you even know the different waves?" First-wave feminism (suffrage, legal equality) "we all are on board with"; later waves carry contested philosophical commitments. Lumping all "feminism" together lets the rebuttal smuggle in a strawman ("pushing back on feminism" = wanting to "take away women's right to vote"). *Rating: moderate-strong; the wave distinction is real and the equivocation is a genuine flaw.* [^4]
3. **Rights have a grounding the narrative ignores.** "Does he even know where rights come from?... The government does not grant women rights." If one claims a *right* to abortion, one must defend that it is a real right (and from what source), not assert it — "defend the claim that women have a right to murder the infants inside of them at any point in pregnancy." *Rating: moderate; shifts the burden to ground the asserted right.* [^5]
4. **A diagnosed motivational pattern.** Hansen claims McClellan "is being driven by his dogma that white Christian men are the problem in the world," and that "the only data he cares about is data that aligns with that goal" — so the Falwell-segregation story is selected to fit the thesis. *Rating: moderate; an ad-hominem-adjacent charge, but framed as a falsifiable prediction about McClellan's output — cf. [[Putting Dogma Over Data]].* [^2]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
McClellan's case has real historical support. (a) Historians (e.g., Randall Balmer) *do* document that the politicized white evangelical "Religious Right" mobilized in the late 1970s initially around defending segregated institutions' tax status, with abortion becoming the galvanizing issue somewhat later — so the Falwell narrative is not invented. (b) Some strands of anti-abortion rhetoric *have* been entangled with controlling women's autonomy. (c) "Where do rights come from?" cuts both ways — a secularist can ground rights in social contract or human dignity without a creator, so "the government doesn't grant rights" does not by itself establish a fetal right to life. McClellan would say *he* is the one following the historical and textual data while Hansen supplies the dogma.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: even granting the *political-history* point about the Religious Right's origins, it is a **genetic fallacy** to infer that the pro-life *argument* is false or merely instrumental from the movement's messy political genesis — the moral question (is the unborn a person?) stands on its own and is exactly what the narrative refuses to engage. And the suspicious-origins move is reversible: one could equally narrate the *pro-choice* movement's entanglements. The honest core: McClellan's framing may capture real political history while still committing the error of treating a contested moral claim (no person is present) as settled background. See [[Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine]] for the related claim that feminism tracks material conditions rather than doctrine.
