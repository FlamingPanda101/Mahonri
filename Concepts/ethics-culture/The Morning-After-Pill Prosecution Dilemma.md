# The Morning-After-Pill Prosecution Dilemma

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] presses a consistency dilemma on the strict "life begins at conception, abortion is always murder" position: if a newly fertilized egg is a full person, then a woman who *knowingly* takes the morning-after pill (understanding it ends that life) has committed murder — and should therefore be prosecuted and punished **exactly like any other murderer** (life in prison, or on a strict reading, execution). Hansen reports that confronting his own unwillingness to do this is what "shook [him] out of" the conception-equals-personhood view: "I was not going to treat a woman who had taken a morning after pill the same way as I treat a woman who shoots her baby." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The argument is a clean modus tollens.** *If* conception = a person, *then* knowingly killing it = murder, *and* murderers are jailed/executed; the hard pro-lifer affirms the antecedent but recoils from the consequence (jailing morning-after-pill users), so something in the premise set must give. "If you want to say it's murder, act like it's murder." *Rating: strong as a dilemma; its soundness is contested (see steelman).* [^2]
2. **The recoil reveals a latent spectrum.** Hansen argues that even committed Catholics "don't act like they're murdering people because deep down... you don't think they actually are. You recognize there's a difference between taking a morning after pill [after] a rape and shooting a one-year-old." The differential *intuition* is evidence that personhood is graded, not binary at conception. *Rating: moderate-strong; an argument from moral phenomenology.* [^3]
3. **It is explicitly *not* pro-choice.** Hansen stresses he holds the Church's position — abortion is "a grievous sin at any point" with only narrow exceptions, and after certain developmental points he would call it murder deserving the seriousness of murder. The dilemma targets the *metaphysics* (binary ensoulment at conception), not the wrongness of abortion. *Rating: strong as a scope clarification — mirrors [[The IVF Reductio Against Conception-as-Ensoulment]].* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The pro-life reply (paralleling [[Joe Heschmeyer]] in the sister debate) does **not** flinch: it bites the bullet via the [[Homicide Is Not Always Murder - The Mens Rea Distinction|mens-rea distinction]]. *Culpability* requires knowledge and intent; many women who abort or take the morning-after pill act under ignorance, social pressure, or cognitive dissonance, so they are not *first-degree murderers* even though the act objectively kills a person — exactly as the law distinguishes degrees of homicide. The guest "Luke" makes this move (comparing it to a negligent drug-maker). So the asymmetric *punishment* intuition is fully explained *without* abandoning conception-personhood; one can hold the embryo is a person *and* that prosecution should track intent. The dilemma therefore shows a disanalogy in *culpability*, not in *personhood*.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's rejoinder: he stipulates the *knowing* case — "a person who grew up Catholic... knows exactly what they're doing... your priest told you" — precisely to remove the ignorance escape, and even *then* the strict pro-lifer hesitates to demand a murderer's sentence. That residual hesitation, he argues, is the conception view's intuitions quietly conceding a developmental spectrum. The honest core: the mens-rea reply rescues the *punishment* asymmetry but Hansen claims it cannot fully explain why the *knowing* morning-after-pill case still feels categorically lighter than infanticide.
