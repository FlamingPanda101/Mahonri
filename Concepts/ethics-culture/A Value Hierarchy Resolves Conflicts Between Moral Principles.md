# A Value Hierarchy Resolves Conflicts Between Moral Principles

## The Point
Because commandments are situational expressions of deeper principles ([[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]]), two genuine duties can collide — and [[Jacob Hansen]] argues the resolution is to **rank the competing principles in a context-sensitive value hierarchy**. "This is where the algorithm helps: it takes all of these principles and arranges them into a value hierarchy. This allows your wife's self-esteem and your relationship to be worth more than brutal honesty at all times, and can actually justify a white lie." [^1] The "game of morality" is therefore "how you order the principles," because "not all values or principles are equal in all contexts." [^2] Hansen openly concedes the move is "a dangerous slippery slope" but answers "what's the alternative?" — since "life is too complex for a complete list of do's and don'ts." [^3]

## Argument
1. **Real moral conflicts exist.** Charity vs. honesty: a wife "dealing with serious issues of body insecurity asks if she looks fat in the dress and you think that she does." Property vs. life: "you've been told not to steal but... told that you should protect the life of your child who is starving — do you steal the loaf of bread from the rich man to save your baby?" *(dilemma setup)* [^4]
2. **A rigid rule-list cannot resolve them.** "It's obvious that all commandments cannot be universally applied in every situation. Life is too complex for a complete list of do's and don'ts." *(rejection of legalism)* [^3]
3. **Order the principles by context.** "The game of morality is a game of understanding what values and norms will bring you toward the desired outcome. Most importantly it's about how you order the principles. Not all values or principles are equal in all contexts." *(the mechanism)* [^2]
4. **A ranked hierarchy yields the right output — even a "white lie."** Ordering the wife's self-esteem above "brutal honesty at all times" "can actually justify a white lie"; ordering a child's life above property can justify the theft. *(application)* [^1][^4]
5. **The slippery-slope cost is accepted as unavoidable.** "But is this a dangerous slippery slope? Yeah it is. But what's the alternative?" The risk of misordering is the price of moral agency in a complex world. *(concession + rhetorical defense)* [^3]
6. **This is the very point of agency.** "Knowing the principles, properly ordering them, and manifesting that order in your mode of being is the game of morality and ultimately... the very reason you have moral agency in the first place." *(theological payoff)* [^2]

**Steelman of the critic:** Critics of situationism press exactly the slippery slope Hansen concedes: once "white lies" are licensed by a self-constructed hierarchy, the same machinery rationalizes self-serving lies, since the agent both sets the ranking and judges the outcome — the worry raised in [[Reason Can Justify Immoral Acts When You Can Get Away With Them]]. A defender of exceptionless norms (e.g. classical natural law, or Kant's famous refusal to lie) argues some acts are wrong *in kind* regardless of the projected outcome, precisely to remove that discretion. Hansen's reply is structural: he denies there is any escape from ordering values — "everyone, whether explicitly or implicitly, does this because it is requisite to act in the world" — so the only real question is whether one's hierarchy is *well* calibrated toward the objective end (see [[The Moral Absolute Is the Universal End Not the Particular Rule]]), not whether to have one. The disagreement is thus over whether *any* principle sits at the absolute top, exceptionlessly.

## LDS Connection
Hansen ties the ranking of principles directly to the Latter-day Saint doctrine of moral agency: agency is not merely the freedom to obey a checklist but the capacity to "know the principles, properly order them, and manifest that order in your mode of being." This reframes moral life as wisdom and becoming rather than rule-keeping (cf. [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]], [[Orthopraxy over Orthodoxy in LDS Salvation]]). The scriptural precedent for principles overriding lower commandments is strong in LDS thought — Nephi slaying Laban "that a nation [perish] not in unbelief" (1 Nephi 4) is the canonical case of a higher principle (covenant life of a people) outranking "thou shalt not kill" under divine direction. The concession that this is "a dangerous slippery slope" is also why the tradition couples agency with revelation and conscience (the Light of Christ) as calibration against self-serving misordering.
