# The Moral Absolute Is the Universal End Not the Particular Rule

## The Point
This is [[Jacob Hansen]]'s direct answer to the video's title question. The mistake, he argues, is to look for moral absolutes in the form of *exceptionless rules* ("never lie," "never steal"). On his model there are no such universal rule-absolutes — but morality is **not** thereby relative, because there *is* an absolute: the **universal, objective end** that every value hierarchy is calibrated toward. "Does this mean that morality is relative or subjective...? No, because we all ultimately value the same thing above all else: we desire to escape suffering. In fact this is the very bedrock of all moral reasoning and language." [^1] The variability is only in *application* — how we order principles in a given situation — while the *target* (escape suffering, approach well-being) is fixed for "all sentient sane creatures." So the answer to "is there such a thing as a moral absolute?" is: yes — the end, not the rule.

## Argument
1. **Values vary, so isn't morality relative?** The objection: since "what we value varies from person to person," and we reorder principles by context ([[A Value Hierarchy Resolves Conflicts Between Moral Principles]]), morality looks relative or subjective. *(objection)* [^1]
2. **No — the ultimate value is shared.** "Because we all ultimately value the same thing above all else: we desire to escape suffering... we all want to get as far away from suffering as possible and approach some notion of ultimate well-being." This is the universal end from earlier in the series ([[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]]). *(the fixed point)* [^1][^2]
3. **That shared end is the bedrock of moral language itself.** It is "the very bedrock of all moral reasoning and language" — without it, "ought"-talk would have nothing objective to refer to (cf. [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]). *(grounding)* [^1]
4. **The value hierarchy is a road map to the fixed end.** "The way we arrange the hierarchy of values is an expression of the road map we implicitly build for how we reach that end. Everyone, whether explicitly or implicitly, does this because it is requisite to act in the world." *(reframing variability)* [^3]
5. **So the only real question is calibration, not relativity.** "The only question is if one's hierarchy of values is properly calibrated towards ultimate well-being." A hierarchy can be objectively *miscalibrated* — which is exactly what relativism cannot say. *(payoff)* [^3]
6. **The absolute is the goal, applications are contingent.** Particular commandments are contingent ([[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]]); the goal is the absolute. "The nature of well-being and our desire to find it is... the heart of our deepest existential longings and debates." *(conclusion)* [^4]

**Steelman of the critic:** A relativist grants that humans *desire* to avoid suffering but denies this yields a *moral absolute* — it gives a universal *motivation*, not a universal *obligation* (the is–ought worry flagged in [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]]). Two further pressures: (1) *Whose well-being?* A shared desire to escape *one's own* suffering does not obviously bind me to *others'* well-being, so the "absolute" may ground only agent-relative prudence unless a bridge to impartial concern is added. (2) *Calibration needs a metric.* Saying a hierarchy can be "properly calibrated" presupposes an objective standard of well-being to calibrate against — the very thing critics say cannot be read off the facts without a prior evaluative structure (cf. [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]]). Hansen's strongest ally here is the destructive case against relativism: like [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]], he notes everyone *must* "stand somewhere" and rank values "because it is requisite to act in the world," so thoroughgoing relativism is performatively impossible.

## LDS Connection
Hansen's "absolute end, contingent applications" answer mirrors the Latter-day Saint conviction that eternal law is fixed while its commandments are administered to particular peoples in particular times ([[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]]). The fixed end — flight from suffering toward ultimate well-being — is given theological depth by a faith whose very name for the goal is *eternal life* and *exaltation*, and whose God is not the arbitrary author of the moral order but its embodiment ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]; [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]). Against relativism, the Restoration holds that there really are "right and wrong answers" about what conduces to that end, so a value hierarchy can be eternally *miscalibrated* (sin) or rightly ordered (righteousness) — which is the moral realism a consistent relativist cannot afford (compare [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]).
