# Exaltation Is Found in Banal Earthy Work Not the Monastery

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] offers a practical reading of apotheosis: the great takeaway is **not** "I can dethrone God," but that the most godlike acts are **banal, earthy, everyday** ones — weeding a garden, raising children, fixing a faucet, nursing a baby — because in them you bring order and life to the world the way God does. He contrasts this with the monastic ideal (sitting in a cold cell poring over scrolls) and argues that *denying* deification quietly devalues the ordinary labor of family life.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Godliness in ordinary creative work (rated: theological)
"The most godly things that you do are banal, earthy things — when you're weeding in your garden or you're fixing a leaky faucet, you're bringing order and organization to the universe. When you're bearing children and ... raising them up ... you're doing godly things. ... For women, when you're suckling your baby, that is divinity."[^1] This extends the LDS theme that ordinances and covenants **recontextualize ordinary actions** as sacred (see [[Ordinance Creation Recontextualizes Ordinary Actions]]).

### 2. A critique of the monastic ideal (rated: polemical)
Barnes argues the rival view makes "the most godly thing you can do ... sit in a cold monastery and look over scrolls all day," and that by ruling out deification "you're diminishing all of the earthy day-to-day activities that men and women do to raise families." Cardon partly pushes back, defending Catholic contemplative knowledge-seeking — the note preserves both voices.[^1]

### 3. Continuous with the Restoration's exalting of the everyday (rated: moderate)
"This is what Joseph Smith taught — he exalted the everyday." The claim links deification to the LDS theology of embodied, familial, materially-engaged divinity rather than disembodied contemplation.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic answers: (a) the monastic tradition does **not** despise ordinary labor — Benedictine *ora et labora* sanctifies manual work, and contemplative prayer is itself framed as love of God and neighbor, so the contrast is a caricature; (b) classical theosis (participation in God by grace) is fully compatible with valuing family and work, so deification-as-same-species is not *required* to dignify the mundane; and (c) "godlike because it brings order" risks proving too much — every productive act would be "divinity," draining the term. Cardon's in-episode defense of the contemplative Catholic ideal is the steelman the hosts themselves voice. The LDS point stands best as a *theology of embodiment* (cf. [[Anthropomorphic God Grounds Heavenly Mother and Eternal Families]]) rather than a knock against monasticism.
