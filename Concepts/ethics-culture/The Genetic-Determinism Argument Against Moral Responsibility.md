# The Genetic-Determinism Argument Against Moral Responsibility

## The Point
The critic in this clip argues from biology to the abolition of moral responsibility. The structure: (1) a geneticist asserts it is "demonstrable that it is genetic or epigenetic" that some people are predators, so "child predators aren't choosing to do that; they are predisposed genetically to doing such"; (2) if such inclinations are not chosen, then (the argument generalizes) *no* disposition is ultimately chosen — "if I was born into a family where I had the predisposition to be a serial killer... I might turn into a serial killer with no choice of my own"; (3) therefore "if there is no agency there just is no agency and we have to deal with that," and moral responsibility has no coherent ground. [^1][^2] The hardest possible case (child predation) is chosen deliberately because its moral horror makes the determinist conclusion feel forced: surely *no one* defends it, so if even *this* is "just genetics," responsibility looks like an illusion everywhere.

## Evidence
- **The appeal to scientific authority.** "As a geneticist it is demonstrable that it is genetic or epigenetic — genetic meaning... passed from semen and egg, epigenetic meaning something else is going on in the womb... that now has that person having this predisposition." [^1]
- **From predisposition to no-choice.** "Child predators aren't choosing to do that; they are predisposed genetically to doing such." [^1]
- **Generalization to all character.** The racist "didn't choose that"; given the wrong genes and a broken home "I might turn into a serial killer with no choice of my own." [^3]
- **The anti-LDS payoff.** "The science says at best your agency is nothing like what Mormonism taught you," tied to the historical shift "from free agency to moral agency" as evidence that "Mormonism... seems unable to discern" right and wrong. [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Child predators aren't choosing to do that; they are predisposed genetically to doing such." [^1]

> "If there is no agency there just is no agency and we have to deal with that... at best your agency is nothing like what Mormonism taught you." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration grants the *premise* about constraint while denying the *conclusion* about agency — the same modest-vs-strong distinction that defeats [[Sam Harris]]'s parallel argument (see [[Moral Responsibility Without Free Will - Harris's Reconciliation]]).

**1. A predisposition is not a determination.** That a desire is unchosen does not entail that the *act* expressing it is unchosen — a confusion the critic himself stumbles over moments later when he concedes that some people *can* "choose whether or not to actually have sex with children" (see [[Inclination Is Not Action - The Predisposition Does Not Compel the Act]]). Latter-day Saint anthropology expects the natural man to arrive with unchosen inclinations (Mosiah 3:19); the moral drama is precisely the agent's response to them.

**2. The argument overreaches its evidence.** Heritability and epigenetic loading raise the *probability* of a trait; they do not demonstrate that the agent is a "phenomenological glockenspiel played by an unseen hand" (the materialist picture, see [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]). Even Harris's neuroscience of psychopathy ([[Evil as a Natural Phenomenon - Harris on Psychopathy]]) concedes humanity has "grown steadily less violent" — nature is not destiny.

**3. It proves too much, and self-destructs.** If genes-plus-upbringing fully determine action, they equally determine the critic's *own* deconstruction — which then is not a reasoned insight but one more neural weather-pattern, undercutting the argument's claim on us. The LDS reply roots real agency in the uncreated intelligence that "act[s] for itself" ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:29–30]]; [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]), escaping the materialist *caused-or-random* dilemma the argument silently assumes.

**4. The "free agency → moral agency" jab misfires.** The vocabulary shift refined, not retracted, the doctrine: "moral agency" stresses that agency is exercised *within* an eternal moral law toward accountability, not that agency was downgraded — and accountability presupposes agency rather than denying it ([[Alma 41.3-7]]).
