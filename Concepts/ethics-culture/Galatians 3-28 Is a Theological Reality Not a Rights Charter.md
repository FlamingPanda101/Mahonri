# Galatians 3:28 Is a Theological Reality Not a Rights Charter

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]] argues Christians "lean too much on [[Galatians 3.28|Galatians 3:28]]" as if the verse "spawned by itself the rights revolution." Properly, it describes "an incredible metaphysical reality" — unity in Christ — not a political program. The rights and equality movements flow not from the proof-text but from centuries of meditation on the *theology* it reflects.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The verse reflects, not legislates.** "Galatians 3:28 is a reflection on some profound theological truths... what people have been meditating on, and that has rippled out — rather than going to the Bible as a proof-text, pick-a-mix."[^1] *(Consistent with Scrivener's general [[The Narrative Reading of Scripture vs Proof-Texting|anti-proof-text hermeneutic]]; honest about not over-reading a single verse.)*

2. **The theological engine: incarnation and shared nature.** The intellectual firepower of the early church and medieval period concentrated on Christ (Trinity, incarnation): "Jesus of Nazareth became man for all, therefore all humans are saved in the man Christ Jesus" — so men and women "must share an identical human nature."[^2] This grounds radical equality "at the level of ultimate reality," while preserving male/female *difference*.[^3] *(Strong — explains why equality got embedded rather than floating as a Platonic ideal; ties to [[Institutionalization Is the Unique Christian Contribution]].)*

3. **The grammatical detail.** Scrivener notes the conjunction for "male and female" (καὶ, echoing Gen 1:27) differs from the "neither/nor" of Jew/Greek and slave/free — so Paul is "clearly not a gender abolitionist." Both interlocutors agree on this.[^4] *(Accurate; defuses the over-literal egalitarian reading.)*

4. **Steelman of O'Connor.** O'Connor agrees Paul is not a gender abolitionist — which is exactly his point: if the verse is *not* a charter for societal equality, then it cannot be the source of *modern* gender equality (equal social standing), which is what needs explaining. See [[Modern Gender Equality Is Not Grounded in the Bible]]. He also presses: "But where did they get this *value* from?" — to which Scrivener answers via incarnation theology, not the verse.[^5] *(Fair — Scrivener's reading is theologically robust but, by his own admission that it's not a rights charter, concedes the verse doesn't directly yield the modern political claim.)*

## Best Quotes
