# No One Needs God to Be Moral - The Grounding Problem

## The Point
Dawkins' fifth thesis is that "we do not need God in order to be good or evil." He offers an evolutionary account: there was a time we lived in small kin groups "where good deeds could be expected to be reciprocated," and under those conditions "we developed a kind of lust to be good which was parallel to the lust for sex." On top of this sits a "shifting moral zeitgeist" that changes decade to decade — and "it clearly has nothing to do with religion because it doesn't come from scripture."[^1] [[John Lennox]] accepts the *psychological* claim but rejects the *metaethical* one: "Of course we can be good without God in the sense of our personal behavior. But I'm not sure whether we can find foundations for the concept of being good without God."[^2] The dispute is not whether atheists behave well, but whether atheism can *ground* morality at all.

## Evidence
Lennox's leverage is Dawkins' own writing. From *River Out of Eden*: "in a universe of blind physical forces and genetic replication... [there is] no rhyme or reason in it, nor any justice... no design, no purpose, no evil, and no good, nothing but blind, pitiless indifference. DNA neither knows nor cares. DNA just is. And we dance to its music."[^3] Lennox presses: if good and evil "don't exist... how can it possibly make sense to talk of the evils of religion or of the good of atheism?" — "you remove from yourself the categories you're using to discuss morality."[^4] If Pol Pot or the 9/11 terrorists "were simply dancing to their DNA," in what sense are they evil?[^5] He invokes Hume's is/ought gap — "you cannot get an ought from an is... you cannot derive morality and ethics from matter and energy" — and Dostoevsky: "if God does not exist everything is permissible," which Lennox glosses not as "people can't be good" but as "the foundations of morality are removed."[^6] Dawkins replies that the bleakness of a view is no evidence against its truth ("maybe the world is a hideous world... doesn't make it not true") and that we "rise above" our Darwinian dictates every time we use a contraceptive.[^7] Lennox's rejoinder: if "we are nothing but our genes and dancing to the tune of our DNA, what part of us can rebel against them?"[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Of course we can be good without God in the sense of our personal behavior. But I'm not sure whether we can find foundations for the concept of being good without God." [^2]

> "If good and evil don't exist, there is no evil and no good, nothing but blind, pitiless indifference, how can it possibly make sense to talk of the evils of religion or of the good of atheism?" [^4]

## LDS Connection
Lennox grounds objective morality in our being "made in the image of God," reading the cross-cultural "common core of morality" as "evidence for the truth of the biblical claim that we are moral beings made in the image of God."[^9] Latter-day Saint theology deepens this with an account of moral agency that is eternal rather than emergent: humans possess a "light of Christ" that gives every person a moral sense ([[Moroni 7]]:16, "the Spirit of Christ is given to every man, that he may know good from evil"), and agency itself is rooted in eternal law and the uncreated intelligence of the soul ([[D&C 93]]:29–30). Where Dawkins' zeitgeist is contingent and could have evolved otherwise, the Restoration locates moral truth in "things as they really are" (Jacob 4:13) and in a God constrained by eternal law — supplying exactly the objective foundation Lennox argues naturalism cannot.
