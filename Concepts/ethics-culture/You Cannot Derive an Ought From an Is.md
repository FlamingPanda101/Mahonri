# You Cannot Derive an Ought From an Is

## The Point
At the logical heart of "The Way" is Lewis's charge that "the Innovator is trying to get a conclusion in the imperative mood out of premisses in the indicative mood."[^1] You can pile up any number of factual statements ("there is the door"; "the species will survive if you do X") and you will never validly reach a command ("shut the door"; "you ought to do X") — unless a value-premise is already present.[^2] This is the famous **is–ought problem**, and Ward identifies its source: David Hume, in *A Treatise of Human Nature* (1739-40), observed that moralists slide imperceptibly from "is" and "is not" to "ought" and "ought not" without explaining "how this new relation can be a deduction from others, which are entirely different from it."[^3] Lewis's conclusion: an "ought" is irreducible and "must not be dismissed because it cannot produce some is as its credential."[^4]

## Evidence
Ward unpacks the grammatical core: from an indicative premise ("There's the door") "we can never get a conclusion in the imperative mood ('Shut the door!') unless we connect them in logic with 'The door ought to be shut.'"[^5] That connecting premise is itself a value drawn from the *Tao*; it cannot be manufactured from facts. Ward links Hume's point to G. E. Moore's "naturalistic fallacy" in *Principia Ethica* (1903): it is fallacious "to define that which is 'good' reductively in terms of natural properties such as desirability or pleasantness," for "good" is indefinable.[^6] Lewis's own use of the principle is, Ward notes via Lucas, strategic rather than merely technical: he "does not actually convict the Innovator of the naturalistic fallacy, but he cites the impossibility of deriving an 'ought' from an 'is' in order to discredit all alternative systems of values that start out by denying the validity of value judgments generally."[^7] Because no factual "is" can yield a moral "ought" on its own, the would-be debunker who has thrown out the *Tao* has thrown out the only thing that could ever license a moral conclusion — and is left, Lewis says, with premises that "already concealed an imperative or the conclusion remains merely in the indicative."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "The Innovator is trying to get a conclusion in the imperative mood out of premisses in the indicative mood." (Lewis) [^1]

> An "ought… must not be dismissed because it cannot produce some is as its credential." (Lewis) [^4]

> "Either the premisses already concealed an imperative or the conclusion remains merely in the indicative." (Lewis) [^8]

## LDS Connection
The is/ought gap is the standing rebuttal to every attempt to ground morality in mere nature or utility, and it is exactly the gap that [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] tries to leap. Harris stipulates that "good" *means* "what supports the well-being of conscious creatures," but this only relocates Hume's question one level up — "but *is* well-being what we ought to maximize?" remains perfectly coherent ([[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]]; [[You Cannot Get From Facts to Values Without an Interpretive Structure]]). Latter-day Saint thought supplies the value-premise that facts cannot: an objective, **eternal moral law** that even God honors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]), made known to every soul through the conscience / Light of Christ (Moroni 7:16-19). On naturalism the foundational "ought" is brute and arbitrary; on the restored gospel it is the lawful structure of a moral universe, received rather than invented — which is why Lewis insists Reason itself, not bare fact, is "the organ of morality" ([[Reason Is the Organ of Morality]]).
