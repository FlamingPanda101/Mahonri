# Universal Human Dignity Comes from Genesis Not the Greeks
## The Point
The belief that *every* human being shares one common humanity and possesses inherent, universal dignity is not a self-evident truth of reason and was not bequeathed by classical antiquity. It traces specifically to Genesis — God creating man and woman "in his own image" ([[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]]) — and to Paul's declaration that in Christ "there is neither Jew nor Greek... slave nor free... male nor female" ([[Galatians 3.28|Galatians 3:28]]). Secular humanism quietly assumes this Genesis-derived dignity while denying its source.

## Evidence
1. **Not from Greece or Rome.** Asked where the conviction that "humanity somehow has a kind of universal dignity" comes from, Holland answers plainly: "it doesn't come from the Greeks, it doesn't come from the Romans, it comes essentially from Genesis... because in Genesis it says that God creates man and woman in his own image and so for Jews and Christians man and woman has an inherent dignity that is much greater than that of any other civilization."[^1]

2. **A universal humanness that the ancients lacked.** The idea of a single human species deserving equal treatment "is something that actually even in the 18th century they struggled with," e.g. debates over "whether the American Indians had souls." Paul's "neither Jew nor Greek" in [[Galatians 3.28|Galatians 3:28]] is named "the single most influential phrase for understanding why we have a notion of a kind of common humanity."[^2]

3. **Pinker's secular rejoinder, and the reply.** [[Steven Pinker]] argues we need only "recognize our universal humanity... we're all part of the same species, [and] that gives us every grounding we need for treating each other with dignity." The counter: "who thought like that in the first century?" — a universal humanness inferred from mere biology is itself a post-Christian intuition, not a deliverance of species-membership.[^3] Compare [[Athens Versus Jerusalem on the Worth of Human Life]], where Greek reason found infant exposure "quite reasonable."

4. **Alien to non-Western civilizations.** The goal of a society ordered toward *universal* human dignity "would have been totally alien to the Romans, the Hindus, the Greeks, the Aztecs, the Mongols, the Vikings, and essentially every other major civilization in history apart from the West."[^4] Its rarity argues against self-evidence and for a specific (biblical) seedbed — the same logic as [[Genesis 1 Image of God as Seedbed of Rights]].

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology intensifies the *imago Dei*: humans are not merely *like* God by analogy but are literally His offspring, spirits begotten before mortality, with the capacity to grow into His nature (theosis). This gives the strongest possible grounding for the very universal dignity Holland traces to Genesis — worth that is ontological and eternal, not conferred by culture or inferred from biology.
