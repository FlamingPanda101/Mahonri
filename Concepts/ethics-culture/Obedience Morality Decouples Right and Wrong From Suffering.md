# Obedience Morality Decouples Right and Wrong From Suffering

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that because most religions "conceive of morality as a matter of being obedient to the word of God (generally for the sake of receiving a supernatural reward)," their precepts "often have nothing to do with maximizing well-being in this world."[^1] This "pious uncoupling of moral concern from the reality of human and animal suffering," he claims, lets believers brand contraception, masturbation, and homosexuality "immoral" without ever showing these "actually cause suffering" — and even to "pursue aims that are flagrantly immoral... while believing that these actions are morally obligatory."[^1] On his consequentialist premise (see [[Consequentialism as a Claim About Moral Truth Not a Decision Method]]), any morality that floats free of consciousness and consequences is empty.

## Evidence
- **The structural charge.** Obedience-morality "often [has] nothing to do with maximizing well-being in this world"; believers assert immorality "without ever feeling obliged to argue that these practices actually cause suffering."[^1]
- **The faculty that judges scripture is *not from* scripture.** "The faculty we use to validate religious precepts, judging the Golden Rule to be wise and the murder of apostates to be foolish, is something we bring to scripture; it does not, therefore, come from scripture."[^2]
- **Scripture's own moral failures.** "The scriptures of many religions, including the most well subscribed (i.e., Christianity and Islam), countenance patently unethical practices like slavery"; the texts that obsess over menstruation and diet "view slavery as morally unproblematic."[^3]
- **Conservative-Islam exhibits.** Demonizing homosexuals, stoning adulterers, veiling women, soliciting the murder of artists (the Westergaard/Danish-cartoon case), and celebrating suicide bombers "will [not] move humanity toward a peak on the moral landscape" — "as objective a claim as we ever make in science."[^4]
- **Many people are simply *wrong* about morality.** "If morality is a system of thinking about (and maximizing) the well-being of conscious creatures like ourselves, many people's moral concerns must be immoral."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "This pious uncoupling of moral concern from the reality of human and animal suffering has caused tremendous harm." [^1]

> "The faculty we use to validate religious precepts... is something we bring to scripture; it does not, therefore, come from scripture." [^2]

> "If morality is a system of thinking about (and maximizing) the well-being of conscious creatures like ourselves, many people's moral concerns must be immoral." [^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris here aims at a real target — moralism that fetishizes rule-keeping while ignoring human misery — and Latter-day Saint scripture aims at the *same* target ("they draw near to me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me," Joseph Smith—History 1:19; cf. Isaiah 1:11–17; Matthew 23:23). The disagreement is over his three premises.

**1. The dichotomy is false: theistic ethics is not "obedience *instead of* well-being."** Harris assumes divine commands are arbitrary fiats unrelated to flourishing. But on a Latter-day Saint reading, God's commands just *are* the revealed map to flourishing — "wickedness never was happiness" (Alma 41:10), and the law exists "for the benefit and learning" of agents (2 Nephi 2). Obedience and well-being coincide because the Lawgiver is also the source of the good (cf. [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]]). Harris's framing only works against a *voluntarism* (sheer-will divine-command theory) that the Restoration does not hold — God himself is subject to eternal law (D&C 130:20–21).

**2. The "faculty we bring to scripture" cuts toward theism, not away.** Harris is right that we judge the Golden Rule wise and apostate-killing foolish by a moral sense prior to any text. But where did *that* normative faculty come from, and why is it authoritative? On naturalism it is an evolved heuristic with no claim to track moral *truth* (a point Harris himself presses against Greene and Haidt — see [[Consequentialism as a Claim About Moral Truth Not a Decision Method]]). The reliability of an innate moral light is far more at home in a worldview where humans bear the image of God and "the Spirit of Christ is given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16) than in one where it is the residue of out-group raiding. This is the grounding problem [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] develops.

**3. "Scripture endorses slavery / many believers are immoral" — granted in part, but it does not reach the conclusion.** That *people* and even *texts* get moral questions wrong shows fallible interpreters and progressive revelation, not that *theism* decouples morality from suffering. Harris's own admission that "many people are simply wrong about morality" requires an objective standard by which they err — which is exactly what his naturalism struggles to supply and what theism provides. And note the asymmetry he overlooks: his consequentialism reduces wrongness to *suffering caused*, but much of what we (and he) condemn — humiliating the dead, betraying a confidence, the racist's *pleasure* — is wrong independent of net suffering, which is why morality includes *obligation*, not merely welfare-aggregation. See [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]].

**A fair concession.** Religion *can* curdle into the suffering-blind moralism Harris decries, and his examples from conservative Islam are not strawmen. The Restoration's answer is not to abandon revealed commands for welfare-calculus, but to read the commands as *given for the flourishing of agents* and tested by their fruits (Matthew 7:16–20) — keeping both the obligation Harris's view loses and the compassion his critique rightly demands. Compare the related, debate-sourced [[Religion Gives Bad Reasons to Be Good]].
