# Mormonism as a Sociological Cure for Poverty

## The Claim

[[Ward Radio]] ([[Jonah Barnes]], [[Cardon Ellis]]) showcases the non-LDS Yale/George-Mason economist [[Tyler Cowen]], who argued (c. 2011) that a mass conversion of low-income people to Mormonism would, "as a viable solution... actually, yes," reduce poverty — because the faith supplies a **sociological/cultural fix** (habits, family structure, education, community, self-reliance) rather than a cash transfer, addressing the *roots* of poverty that money alone does not.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A cultural fix outperforms external policy — rate: moderate (economist's claim)
Cowen's thesis is that anti-poverty *policy* (cash, tax tweaks, benefits) is by design "agnostic to culture and values," yet culture and values are "the driving force." Mormonism changes how a person views life — stewardship, eternal perspective, obeying laws, supporting family, fidelity, service — and Cowen reports the *effects* track upward mobility. He calls Mormonism's anti-poverty agenda "better than... the progressive left."[^2]

### 2. It targets the documented levers — rate: moderate (mechanism)
The hosts connect Cowen's point to concrete LDS programs that hit the levers economists agree break poverty cycles: the global **Pathway** online education system (education is among the few proven cycle-breakers) and the Church's **self-reliance** curriculum on managing money and stabilizing families. This is "fighting poverty at the root."[^3]

### 3. "Mormons will inherit the earth" — rate: weak/illustrative (rhetorical)
On a radio show, Cowen quipped that as ever-more-addictive substances and distractions proliferate, the most temptation-resistant, still-reproducing group — Mormons — "are going to inherit the earth," and that he encourages productive people "at the margin to become more Mormon." Offered as a provocative framing of the resilience point, not a rigorous result.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The argument is suggestive, not demonstrated, and faces real objections. (a) **Selection vs. treatment**: people who convert to and stay active in a high-demand religion may already differ (conscientiousness, social capital) from those who do not — so the *correlation* between Mormon affiliation and mobility may reflect *who joins and remains*, not a *causal* effect of the religion; a mass forced conversion need not reproduce the outcomes of self-selected converts. (b) Cowen himself frames this as a blog/interview *opinion*, not peer-reviewed research; the hosts' "studies show / Yale economist hath spoken" framing overstates its evidential weight. (c) "Culture causes poverty" is contested in economics precisely because it can shade into blaming the poor and discounting structural barriers (redlining, wages, health shocks) that policy targets for good reason — the very critique Cowen anticipates. (d) Confounders abound: Utah's relative prosperity also reflects demographics, two-parent household rates, and regional economics that are not simply "the gospel." So the honest claim is modest and genuinely interesting: a credentialed *outsider* economist judges that the *behavioral package* associated with Latter-day Saint practice (thrift, fidelity, education, sobriety, community) plausibly improves economic outcomes — a real challenge to purely external anti-poverty models — without that observation rising to a controlled demonstration that the religion *causes* the mobility.

## Best Quotes

> "A viable policy — mass conversions to Mormonism? No. But a viable solution? Actually, yes."[^1]

> "Mormonism and other relatively strict religions can have big anti-poverty effects... I now think that Mormonism has a better anti-poverty agenda than does the progressive left."[^2]

> "It's kind of the case that the Mormons are going to inherit the earth, because whoever is the most resistant to those temptations and still wants to have children..."[^4]
