# Participation - To Be Truly Human Is to Participate in the Tao

## The Point
Ward's third synthesizing heading is **participation**, anchored in what he calls one of the most important lines in the book: the *Tao* is "the concrete reality in which to participate is to be truly human."[^1] Ward's insight is that Lewis does not merely *write about* participation; he *exemplifies* it in the very form and manner of his argument.[^2] Because the reason Lewis defends is "Practical" rather than "Theoretical," it is fitting that "there [is] a practical spirit evident in the way he develops his thesis" — beginning, tellingly, with a quotation from a school textbook about "the daily activity of raising the next generation."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward shows Lewis admitting his own person into the argument — drawing on his "own experience as a teacher," and even confessing, "I myself do not enjoy the society of small children: because I speak from within the Tao I recognize this as a defect in myself."[^4] The *Tao* "is as inescapable as oxygen"; it "has an unavoidably personal resonance," a "moral ecology that embraces him, his experiences, preferences, needs, desires, foibles."[^5] To avoid centring himself, Lewis "corrals numerous historical figures" and "more general exemplars of the participatory spirit" — Plato, Augustine, Locke, Traherne, Shelley; Confucians treading the Way, Hindus conforming to the *Rta*, a cardplayer who knows not to cheat, a Roman father raising his son to love his country.[^6] "To be moral is to be human, and to be human is to belong to a family, a community, a race, a species."[^7]

The great irony Ward presses is that **even those who deny the *Tao* participate in it**: "Innovators inherit shreds of the Tao," and subjectivists like Gaius and Titius are "better than their principles," their textbook turning out to be "practical in intention."[^8] He compares this to the "observer effect" in physics: "the act of observing a thing cannot be kept separate from the thing that is being observed," so "the very fact of making value judgements, even judgements that run counter to the Tao, involves one necessarily in the Tao… there is no other ethical air to breathe."[^9] Crucially, Ward argues this means morality is "not an abstract picture of goodness, an external standard to which everyone must conform," but "the road of active adventure in the drama of character."[^10] Quoting Paul Holmer, moral culture "is not a matter of fitting particulars to universals… instead, it involves the actual development of wants and desires, hopes and cares."[^11] "One becomes good not by assenting to ideas, but by actualising one's identity as either courageous or not, either chaste or not, either honest or not."[^12]

## Best Quotes
> "[The Tao is] the concrete reality in which to participate is to be truly human." (Lewis, quoted by Ward) [^1]

> "There is no other ethical air to breathe, or at least not for as long as one remains discernibly human." [^9]

> "One becomes good not by assenting to ideas, but by actualising one's identity as either courageous or not, either chaste or not, either honest or not. Morality is less an onerous imposition upon one's nature and more a kindly outfitting of one's nature." [^12]

## LDS Connection
Ward's portrait of morality as "the actual development of wants and desires" and the "actualising [of] one's identity" rather than mere assent to rules maps directly onto the Latter-day Saint understanding of the **formation of virtue** and **agency**: salvation is not a verdict pronounced over a passive soul but a becoming, the gradual sanctification by which one's very nature is "outfitted" toward the divine (cf. Moroni 7:48; Mosiah 3:19, putting off the natural man). Lewis's claim that "to be truly human is to participate" resonates with the LDS doctrine of **human divine potential** — that humanity is of God's family and grows toward His likeness by exercising real choices in a real "drama of character." The note that even the *Tao*'s deniers cannot escape it echoes the scriptural teaching that the Light of Christ is given to all and cannot be wholly extinguished (Moroni 7:16-19; D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]).
