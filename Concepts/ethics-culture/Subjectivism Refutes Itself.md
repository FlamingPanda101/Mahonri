# Subjectivism Refutes Itself

## The Point
A recurring charge Lewis brings against the subjectivist — the "debunker" who treats all values as mere feeling — is that the position is **self-defeating**. "To live as a thoroughgoing subjectivist who debunks everything means that one has to debunk all one's own beliefs too, including the supposed value of that very debunking," a rigour "hard to keep up for long." [^1] The same blade cuts logical positivism: its founding rule that metaphysical statements are meaningless is itself a metaphysical statement, incapable of scientific verification, and so meaningless by its own standard. [^2]

## Evidence
Ward traces how Owen Barfield convinced the young Lewis that "if thought were a purely subjective event," there could be no trustworthy awareness of truth, goodness, or beauty — pushing Lewis to admit "that mind was no late-come epiphenomenon; that the whole universe was, in the last resort, mental; that our logic was participation in a cosmic Logos." [^3] This realization of "his own inconsistency" was a chief step toward writing *Abolition*. [^1] The point proved durably persuasive. Lewis's student Paul Piehler recalled being struck "by Lewis's point that skeptical materialists never seemed able to take in the awkward fact that their denial of the possibility of objective truth cut away the validity, or objective truth, of their denial itself." [^4] Confronting dominant positivism at Dartmouth, Piehler asked whether "the fundamental positivist principle 'that metaphysical statements are meaningless' [is] itself a metaphysical statement… incapable of scientific verification. Is it not therefore meaningless?" — and met only silence. [^2] The philosopher John Lucas judged that the intervening years "have largely vindicated Lewis," whose arguments against logical positivism his own generation "only gradually stumbled on." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Is not the fundamental positivist principle 'that metaphysical statements are meaningless' itself a metaphysical statement? It is obviously a principle that is itself incapable of scientific verification. Is it not therefore meaningless?" [^2]

> To debunk everything "means that one has to debunk all one's own beliefs too, including the supposed value of that very debunking." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration rests on the reality and knowability of truth — "the knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) — and on a moral order God did not invent but expresses. The self-refutation argument defends that ground: a worldview that denies objective truth saws off the branch it sits on, including its own claim to be true. Latter-day Saints can wield it to clear space for the deeper witness that real knowledge of God and of good is possible.
