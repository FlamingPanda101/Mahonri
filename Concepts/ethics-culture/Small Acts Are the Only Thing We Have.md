# Small Acts Are the Only Thing We Have

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]] answers the paralysis of "what difference can I possibly make?" with the principle that **"small acts are the only thing we have. Big acts are just a lot of small acts strung together."** Scaled humanitarian impact is not an alternative to small individual acts but their aggregation; the antidote to feeling overwhelmed is to do one prompted thing at a time.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Scripture frames the principle (moderate).** "By small and simple means are great things brought to pass" (Alma 37:6); the Lord "links you in an organized faith" so individual capacity can act "at scale and scope." Eubank, as a Charities director, sees small donations from "my six-year-old nephew and my grandparents" aggregated into global projects.[^1]
2. **The one-scholarship story (strong, anecdote).** A young woman hoping to fund 15–20 scholarships raised enough for one — and the Spirit told her "don't wait… you can be generous right now." Reframing "one" as "one person's life… completely changed" converts a felt failure into a real good.[^2]
3. **A concrete daily practice (moderate).** Her closing counsel: pray each morning for "one thing I can do today," then do it — over a lifetime "16,000 divine things," each "a small thing being done with love." If unsure, offer God a short list and ask which to do.[^3]
4. **Names the real enemy (interpretive).** "One of the easiest things that Satan can do is to just get us to feel overwhelmed." The doctrine of small acts is explicitly a remedy for spiritual paralysis, not a counsel of mediocrity.[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The effective-altruism critique pushes back hard: if the goal is actually to reduce suffering, then *scale, leverage, and cost-effectiveness matter enormously* — a bed-net program saves vastly more lives per dollar than scattered local kindnesses, and "do one small thing" can rationalize complacency or feel-good gestures that leave the largest, most tractable problems unaddressed. Telling people small acts are "the only thing we have" understates the genuine difference that strategic, large-scale giving makes.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank does not deny that money and scale "create opportunity and resource" (she ran a global NGO); her claim is about the *building block*, not a rejection of leverage — "big acts are just a lot of small acts strung together," and she shows money working best when "percolated through" committed local councils. The framing targets the *individual's* live question ("what can *I* do?"), for whom strategic mega-philanthropy is not an available option, and answers the empirically real problem of compassion fatigue and the identifiable-victim effect (see [[Empathy Does Not Scale - The Identifiable Victim Effect]]). Small and strategic are complementary, not rivals.
