# Algorithmic Morality - Principles as Code Converting Situations to Right Action

## The Point
Facing the conundrum "is it always wrong to lie?", [[Jacob Hansen]] proposes a model he calls **algorithmic morality** — his preferred name for a corrected, "often misunderstood notion of situational ethics." An algorithm "will give you an output based on inputs according to a given formula in order to achieve a desired outcome"; morality, likewise, is "a complex algorithmic formula that leads us away from suffering and towards ultimate well-being." [^1] The deep *principles* are "the hard code that we take into ourselves," and when that "coding is properly installed we process external inputs... into the proper outputs," i.e. "the morally correct response to that situation." [^2] The model presupposes the series' foundation — that all action aims at escaping suffering ([[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]]) — and converts the directional "ought" ([[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]) into a working decision procedure.

## Argument
1. **An algorithm maps inputs to outputs via a formula toward a goal.** "Think of it this way: an algorithm will give you an output based on inputs according to a given formula in order to achieve a desired outcome." Some are simple — "if x then y." *(definition)* [^1]
2. **Morality is such a formula, aimed at well-being.** Morality "should be thought of as a complex algorithmic formula that leads us away from suffering and towards ultimate well-being" — the universal end established earlier in the series. *(thesis)* [^1]
3. **Principles are the code; situations are the input.** Principles "act like the hard code that we take into ourselves," and "when this coding is properly installed we process external inputs... into the proper outputs." *(mechanism)* [^2]
4. **Spiritual development = installing the code.** "Spiritual development is when we encode these principles into our psyche and soul." Moral formation is internalizing the algorithm so that right responses follow even "under the most confusing and compelling circumstances." *(application)* [^2][^3]
5. **The label is rehabilitated situational ethics.** Hansen explicitly grounds the model in "situational ethics" but renames it because the common version is "often misunderstood" — the point is not that anything goes, but that the *same* principles yield *different* right actions across different situations. *(clarification)* [^1]

**Steelman of the critic:** "Algorithmic morality" is essentially **rule-sensitive consequentialism**, and two classic worries apply. (1) *Where does the formula come from?* Calling principles "code" does not by itself say which principles are loaded or in what order — the normative content is smuggled in rather than derived, and the bridge from "all sane creatures avoid their own suffering" to *my obligation to your* well-being is still owed (the gap flagged in [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]]). (2) *Calculability.* Treating moral judgment as running a "complex algorithmic formula" toward well-being inherits consequentialism's epistemic problem: agents rarely can compute downstream well-being, which is partly why moral traditions issue near-exceptionless rules. Hansen partly answers this by relocating the fixed point from rules to the *end* (see [[The Moral Absolute Is the Universal End Not the Particular Rule]]), but the critic notes the "algorithm" is a metaphor for practical wisdom, not a literal procedure.

## Best Quotes
> "While this model's roots are based on an often misunderstood notion of situational ethics, I prefer the term algorithmic morality." [^1]

> "I contend that morality should be thought of as a complex algorithmic formula that leads us away from suffering and towards ultimate well-being." [^1]

> "Principles act like the hard code that we take into ourselves, and when this coding is properly installed we process external inputs... into the proper outputs — in other words, the morally correct response to that situation." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The "encode these principles into our psyche and soul" framing is a software metaphor for what Latter-day Saint theology calls becoming — moral agency exists so that, by ordering and living true principles, a person's "mode of being" is transformed (cf. [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]]). The restored gospel's rejection of divine-command voluntarism ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]) is what licenses this model: if God Himself conforms to eternal law rather than inventing it, then commandments can be understood as situational expressions of deeper, unchanging principles that the disciple internalizes — not arbitrary fiat to be applied identically in every case.
