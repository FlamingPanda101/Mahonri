# Abortion Politics Is a Game of Inches Not Purity

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that Latter-day Saints who refuse to support any pro-life law that does *not* perfectly match the Church's position (e.g., one lacking a rape exception) thereby **vote for the worse status quo**. Because "politics is a game of inches," the moral task is to "move the ball in the right direction" by choosing the lesser of two available evils — not to hold out for an unattainable perfect law. He applies this against purists on *both* sides: the progressive Latter-day Saint who sides with pro-choice politics because the pro-life law is imperfect, and the absolutist who rejects incremental gains. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Refusing to choose is still a choice — for the status quo.** "If you say I'm not going to vote... unless it matches the church perfectly, that means you accept the status quo. You are voting for the status quo over the change." Abstention or third-party purity defaults to whatever law currently stands. *Rating: strong; a standard point about binary-choice voting.* [^2]
2. **The lesser-evil comparison is unavoidable.** Given a choice between a law permitting killing "during the first year" and a law without a rape exception, "obviously the rape is just going to not be on the table anymore... Let's save the babies." One weighs the *total* moral cost of each option, including the cost of leaving current law unchanged. "Welcome to politics, guys... you might have to choose between the lesser of two evils." *Rating: moderate-strong; a consequentialist weighing the marginal effect of the vote.* [^3]
3. **The "exceptions" objection does not flip the calculus.** Pointing to rare hard cases (a mismanaged miscarriage under a pro-life law) does not justify rejecting the law, any more than rare deaths caused by *speed limits* (a patient delayed en route to the ER) justify abolishing speed limits. "That is absolutely not how law works." Bad outcomes at the margin are often the fault of *misinterpretation/propaganda*, not the law itself. *Rating: moderate-strong; the speed-limit analogy isolates the marginal-harm fallacy.* [^4]
4. **Allies, not purity tests.** The Church's resources make it a valuable ally to the pro-life cause; treating the small LDS-Catholic differences as disqualifying ("if they won't give me everything I want, then I'm going to vote with the enemy") is self-defeating. *Rating: moderate; a strategic/coalitional point — see [[LDS Abortion Position Follows From Pre-Existence Not Cultural Caving]].* [^5]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Several serious replies. (a) A **principled** objection: incrementalism can entrench compromise (a law that *codifies* exceptions one believes are usually unjustified may normalize them), and some hold that voting *for* a law that still permits some killing makes one formally complicit. (b) A **pro-choice-leaning LDS** reply: the Church's own exceptions and its "left to prayerful consideration" language suggest genuine moral complexity that a blunt criminal ban may handle worse than the status quo, so the "lesser evil" is not obvious. (c) A **prudential** worry: the speed-limit analogy understates real, documented harms when vague statutes chill emergency care — those are not mere "propaganda" in every case.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: incrementalism is how *all* durable moral progress is legislated, and refusing partial gains to keep one's hands clean simply leaves more children dead while changing nothing — the *marginal* question ("is this law better than what it replaces?") is the only honest one a single vote answers. On complicity, supporting the *better available* option is not endorsing its imperfections. He grants hard ER cases exist but argues they are vastly outweighed by the lives at stake and are often driven by fear-mongering rather than the statute itself. See [[The Pro-Life-as-Anti-Feminism Narrative Is Dogma Not Data]] for the parallel charge that opponents argue from ideology rather than the actual stakes.
