# Divine Command Theory Is Vacuous as a Practical Guide

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
If God's commands are the standard of morality, then Scripture (God's commands) gives us the moral guidance we need to know right from wrong. [^1] See [[Divine Command Theory Is Metaethics Not Ethics]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Massive under-determination** — rate: strong. "God has given precious few commands. How are we supposed to know?" The Bible "never actually addresses abortion... never addresses in vitro fertilization," nor countless ordinary moral situations. As a practical guide "it is vacuous." *(scope)* [^2]
2. **The commands we have are mostly superseded** — rate: solid, with a doctrinal claim attached. "Most of the commands are in the Old Testament that were overridden by a single new law — the law of love." In the [[Matthew 5.43-45|Sermon on the Mount]] "the old law is done away" and replaced by an *intensification*. *(redemptive-historical)* [^3] See [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]].
3. **Commands conflict, requiring further commands to interpret them** — rate: strong, with a concrete textual case. "I need another command to clarify what the command is." On divorce, "in [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew]] you can get divorced only if your wife commits adultery; in [[Mark 12.30-31|Mark]] and Luke you can't get divorced at all, even if there's adultery. So which command am I supposed to follow?" The Bible gives "indications that are not consistent." *(internal contradiction)* [^4] See [[All Doctrine Requires Interpretation Rebuttal]].
4. **Verdict** — rate: summary judgment. "As a metaethical theory, it's arbitrary and capricious; as a practical guide, it is vacuous." *(conclusion)* [^5]

**Steelman of the sufficiency reply:** The Reformed answer is that Scripture is sufficient *by good and necessary consequence* (Westminster Confession 1.6): not every case is addressed explicitly, but moral principles deduced from Scripture plus the general equity of the law cover new situations (abortion via "thou shalt not kill," etc.). On divorce, harmonizers argue Mark/Luke state the general rule and Matthew supplies the one exception, so there is no genuine contradiction. Ostler's rejoinder: the very need to *deduce*, *harmonize*, and *select* among texts concedes his point — the commands alone do not deliver the guidance, an *interpreter* does, so "the commands are the standard" is doing no real work.
