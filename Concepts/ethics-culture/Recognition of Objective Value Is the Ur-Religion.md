# Recognition of Objective Value Is the Ur-Religion

## The Point
*Abolition* is not "religious" in the ordinary sense, yet Ward argues it is profoundly religious in the word's strict etymological sense: *religio*, "to bind." [^1] Behind the diversity of moral traditions Lewis cites lies "a strong, central, unifying principle — namely, objective value, a universally recognised moral code, which requires obedience from all human beings." [^1] This recognition of objective value "precedes what we ordinarily call 'religion'" — Paganism, Judaism, Buddhism, Christianity, Islam — and is "a kind of ur-religion underlying them all." [^1] Long before we are Christian or Stoic or Platonist, "we are human by dint of this belief." [^2]

## Evidence
For Lewis the acceptance of "the ultimate platitudes of Practical Reason" is what "makes us distinctively human"; this belief binds together both the perspectives within each tradition and the people across traditions "who believe that reality can be objectively known and intelligently spoken about." [^2] It does not dissolve every dispute — "It is moral codes that create problems of casuistry, just as the rules of chess create chess problems" — but only a shared bedrock of objective value gives any "grounds for hoping that a resolution of moral differences can be obtained through reasonable and peaceful means." [^2] To deny this core principle is to descend below even Paganism: a state "so ethically immature it does not even rise to the level of Paganism." [^3] Lewis had "a sincere respect for Paganism," noting in "A Christmas Sermon for Pagans" (1946) that Pagans believed in an "Objective" Right or Wrong, something "which Man had not invented but had found to be true," so that "we shall have to set about becoming true Pagans if only as a preliminary to becoming Christians." [^4] Ward frames such moral and philosophical "lights" — from any tradition — as witnesses to "common grace" and to "the value of natural theology," since for Lewis God is "the Father of lights." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Recognition of objective value is a kind of ur-religion underlying them all, and indeed all traditional philosophies and coherently logical systems of thought whatsoever." [^1]

> Without a shared premise as bedrock, "we cannot dispute matters rationally with one another, but only assert our particular subjective preferences and try to shout down those whose preferences conflict with our own." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The claim that one must "first [be] a human being" — sharing in an objective moral order — before being a religious human being harmonizes with the Restoration teaching that all people are children of God who carry the Light of Christ and a native moral sense (Moroni 7:16; Romans 2:14–15). Latter-day Saint scripture likewise locates real, eternal moral law beneath every covenant tradition, and Lewis's "Father of lights" echoes the conviction that "every good thing cometh of Christ" (Moroni 7:24). The book's vision of a single binding moral order under one God dignifies, rather than relativizes, the truth scattered across the world's traditions.
