# Beliefs Have Consequences - Religion as a Real Cause of Behavior

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] attacks the fashionable academic move — most associated with the anthropologist Scott Atran — of denying that religious *beliefs* actually cause religious *behavior*. Against Atran's claim that "core religious beliefs are literally senseless and lacking in truth conditions" and so cannot influence conduct, Harris insists that "beliefs have consequences," that people generally "believe exactly what they say they believe," and that taking believers at their word is not bigotry but basic intellectual honesty.[^1] The thesis matters because it underwrites Harris's broader argument that religion's specific doctrines — not merely tribal sociology — are doing real and often harmful work in the world.

## Evidence
Harris's central case is Muslim suicide bombing. Atran says the best predictor of whether a Muslim moves from supporting jihad to perpetrating violence "has nothing to do with religion, it has to do with whether you belong to a soccer club" — reducing martyrdom to bonding among "fictive kin."[^1] Harris notes that Atran's *own* interview data undercut him: would-be martyrs uniformly say "duty to God cannot be postponed," that even an attack killing no one but oneself is fine because "God will love you just the same," and (per the alleged Emir of Jemaah Islamiyah) that martyrdom is an "inescapable individual obligation" trumping four of the five pillars of Islam.[^2] To call this unrelated to belief, Harris says, is "like saying that honor killings have nothing to do with what their perpetrators believe about women, sexuality, and male honor."[^2] He multiplies examples: the trade in albino body parts in Tanzania (fishermen weave albino hair into nets to catch more fish) and the historical Western use of "mummy paint," sold in the Merck catalog as late as 1908. Each is intelligible *only* through "the content of people's beliefs."[^3] He charges social scientists with a "perverse inability to accept that people often believe exactly what they say they believe."[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Beliefs have consequences." [^3]

> "To say that the behavior of Muslim jihadists has nothing to do with their religious beliefs is like saying that honor killings have nothing to do with what their perpetrators believe about women, sexuality, and male honor." [^2]

> "Many social scientists have a perverse inability to accept that people often believe exactly what they say they believe." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The theist can grant Harris's narrow point gladly — beliefs *do* have consequences, and the reductive sociology that explains them all away is condescending and false. The disagreement is over what *follows*. (1) **Harris's principle cuts both ways.** If we take believers at their word, then we must also take seriously the testimony of the saint, the martyr-for-charity, the abolitionist, and the hospice founder who say their self-giving flows directly from belief in a God who commands love of neighbor. Harris reads the causal arrow only where doctrine produces harm; consistency requires reading it where doctrine produces the heroic altruism that, on his own later admission, naturalism struggles to explain. (2) **"Religion" is not a single variable.** The albino-flesh trade, transubstantiation, and jihadist martyrdom are not one phenomenon; lumping them as "religion" is the very over-generalization the vault flags in [[The Problem Is Dogmatism Not Religion]]. What Harris's examples actually indict is *false belief held immune from correction* — a vice that, as the New Atheists' own track record shows, is not unique to the religious. (3) **Cause is not warrant.** Showing that a belief *moves* people says nothing about whether it is *true* or *justified*; treating "it has bad effects" as evidence of falsehood is the genetic move examined in [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]]. The Latter-day Saint position agrees with Harris that doctrine has teeth — which is precisely why it insists on *true* doctrine, continuing revelation, and a God whose character is the standard of the good, rather than leaving "what God commands" as an arbitrary trump. On grounding that standard see [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] and [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]]; on whether the goods religion delivers can be detached from belief, the foil's own argument is in [[Religion Gives Bad Reasons to Be Good]].
