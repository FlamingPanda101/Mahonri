# We Show Humans More Mercy Than Animals Not Less

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**The objection:** "We put suffering animals out of their misery — isn't it *more* cruel to deny dying humans the same mercy?" The analogy treats euthanizing a human as an act of compassion modeled on what any kind person does for a hurting pet.

**Horn's rebuttal** (per the summary): the analogy is **backwards**. We do **not** euthanize humans *precisely because* we owe them **more** mercy than animals, not less. Animals are routinely euthanized for being **unwanted or too expensive** — convenience and cost, not the animal's own good, drive most of it. To extend "the same mercy" to humans would be to extend the same **disposability**: it would make a human life forfeitable when it becomes inconvenient or costly to others. Human dignity is **intrinsic** — not determined by whether the person is wanted or by the price of their care — so the very thing that distinguishes our treatment of humans is that we *refuse* to put them down.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The premise of animal euthanasia is exactly what we deny for humans (rate: strong).** A shelter euthanizes healthy, unwanted animals; owners euthanize pets whose treatment is unaffordable. The operative criteria are **wantedness and cost** — the two criteria a sanctity-of-life ethic insists must *never* govern whether a human may be killed. So importing "the same mercy" imports those criteria, which is the opposite of mercy toward the vulnerable.[^1] *(The rebuttal turns on naming what animal euthanasia is actually *for*.)*
2. **It connects to the abuse cases (rate: strong, corroborating).** The [[The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving|MAID dossier]] is precisely the "too expensive / unwanted" logic arriving for humans — the man losing housing, the woman denied chemo. The animal-mercy frame, applied consistently, *predicts* those cases; that they occurred is evidence the frame devalues exactly whom it touches.[^1]
3. **Dignity is not a function of function (rate: strong, conceptual).** The deeper basis is that human worth does not rise and fall with usefulness, wantedness, or capacity — the same *what-we-are* (not *what-we-can-do*) grounding Horn uses elsewhere. See [[The Brainless Organ-Farm Reductio Against the Consciousness Criterion]] and [[Universal Human Dignity Comes from Genesis Not the Greeks]]. *(Anchors the rebuttal in a positive theory of dignity, not just a rejection of the analogy.)*[^1]

### Steelman of the pro-assisted-suicide side

The strongest reply **repairs the analogy** rather than abandoning it. A defender concedes that *much* animal euthanasia is for convenience — and says that is **irrelevant**, because the analogy appeals only to the **best** case: the beloved pet in untreatable agony, whom a loving owner releases **for the animal's own sake**, at real emotional and financial cost *to themselves*. There the motive is pure mercy, and the intuition is near-universal that prolonging the animal's suffering would be the cruelty. The argument is then: if we recognize a duty of mercy toward a suffering creature that **cannot consent**, how is it *more* respectful to deny that release to a suffering human who **can** consent and explicitly **asks** for it? Far from treating the person as disposable, honoring their settled request **treats them as a rational agent** — the very dignity Horn invokes — rather than as a patient whose suffering we are entitled to override "for their own good." On this view it is the *refusal* that is paternalistic.

### Counter-objection (Horn's rejoinder)

Horn's reply (per the summary, extended): even granting the loving-pet case, the **disanalogy is decisive** because the pet **cannot refuse, cannot reconsider, and has no future of meaning to forfeit**, whereas a human's request is formed *inside* the very social pressures the [[The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving|abuse cases]] document — so "they asked" is not the clean autonomy the steelman assumes. More fundamentally, the rejoinder reframes mercy itself: the merciful response to human suffering is to **relieve the suffering** (palliative care, pain control even at risk of hastening death — see [[Dignity Is How You Face Death Not the Manner of It]]), not to **eliminate the sufferer**. We can do for humans what we cannot do for animals — accompany them, medicate them, give their suffering a context of meaning (see [[Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer]]) — and *that* expanded toolkit is the "more mercy." Killing is the option you reach for when you have **only** the animal's toolkit; offering it to humans is a *downgrade* of mercy dressed as an upgrade.

## Apologetic Engagement

The rebuttal's power is the **reversal**: it accepts the analogy's surface (yes, we euthanize animals) and weaponizes the *content* the objector glossed over (we euthanize them for convenience and cost). The discipline is to grant the steelman's best case honestly — the loved pet released purely for its own sake is a *real* and sympathetic intuition — and then locate the disanalogy in (a) the human's capacity for **meaning, reconsideration, and accompaniment**, and (b) the **social coercion** that contaminates "consent." The decisive move is redefining mercy as *relieving suffering*, not *removing the sufferer*, which simultaneously answers this objection and sets up the dignity and double-effect notes. Avoid the temptation to deny that animal mercy is ever real — that concedes too little and looks callous; the stronger line is "we owe humans the mercy we *can't* give animals."

## LDS Relevance

Latter-day Saint teaching grounds human worth in **divine parentage and eternal identity**, not in wantedness, usefulness, or cost — the exact contrast the rebuttal draws. Scripture frames human life as uniquely the object of God's purposes ("this is my work and my glory — to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man," Moses 1:39), which is precisely the **future of meaning** that distinguishes a person from a suffering animal and that euthanasia forecloses. The Church's practical ethic matches Horn's "more mercy" reframing: it **opposes** elective euthanasia/assisted suicide while affirming compassionate care — adequate pain relief, the liberty to decline **extraordinary** measures, and ministering to the suffering and dying (see [[Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer]]). Mercy, on the LDS view as on Horn's, is to *sit with* and *relieve* the sufferer, not to end them because their care has become burdensome.
