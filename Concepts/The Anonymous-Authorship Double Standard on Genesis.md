# The Anonymous-Authorship Double Standard on Genesis

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] exposes an inconsistency in how the presuppositionalist handles biblical authorship. [[Jeff Durbin]] insists that [[Moses]] wrote the book of Genesis — and when asked for evidence, ultimately grounds it in *Jesus' quotations* of "Moses" (e.g. "have you not read what was spoken to you by God"). Carroll's counter has two prongs: (1) the text itself betrays multiple, post-Mosaic authorship; and (2) even granting Durbin's worry, **Durbin already accepts anonymous books as scripture** — most obviously Hebrews — so *why must Genesis be Mosaic at all?* "Jeff believes in some books as scripture that have anonymous authorship. So why does he need the book of Genesis to be written by Moses?" The demand for a named author is applied selectively, only where it props up the apologetic.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The internal seams of the Pentateuch.** Carroll marshals the standard composite-text evidence: [[Deuteronomy 34|Deuteronomy 34]] narrates Moses' own death and burial "unto this day"; the J and E sources are visibly **woven together** in [[Genesis 37|Genesis 37]], producing the Ishmaelite-versus-Midianite contradiction (who pulled Joseph from the pit and sold him to Egypt) that resolves cleanly *only* when the two source-strands are separated. This is the documentary hypothesis in miniature. See [[Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch]] and [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration]].[^3]

2. **Durbin's evidence regresses to bare authority.** Pressed for evidence that Moses wrote Genesis, Durbin says "I don't know... Jesus," i.e. Jesus referred to Moses. Carroll's reply: Jesus quoting "Moses" is a way of citing the Torah by convention, not a claim about compositional history — "can you please show me Jesus or any biblical author claiming that *Moses wrote the book of Genesis*?" The appeal substitutes assertion of authority for historical evidence.[^4]

3. **The double standard is the kill-shot.** Protestants receive **Hebrews** (and other anonymous works) as canonical and inspired without a named author. So authorship-anonymity is plainly *not* disqualifying for Durbin in general — it becomes disqualifying only when applied to the Pentateuch by his opponents. Carroll's point is consistency: you cannot make Mosaic authorship a load-bearing requirement for Genesis while waving anonymity through for Hebrews. This dovetails with the broader canon problem ([[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]]): the Protestant has no principled, author-based criterion for what belongs in the canon. As Latter-day Saints, Carroll notes, "we don't deal with this problem" because authority rests in living apostles and prophets, not in reconstructing ancient authorship.[^1]

**Steelman of the traditional view.** A defender can argue the cases are disanalogous: Hebrews is anonymous but *apostolic-era* and never attributed by scripture to a specific named figure, whereas Genesis is positively *ascribed* to Moses by later scripture (Jesus and the apostles say "Moses said/wrote"). So denying Mosaic authorship is, for him, contradicting the New Testament's own testimony, not merely tolerating anonymity. Classic readers also long granted that *some* verses (Deuteronomy 34) were completed by a later hand (Joshua/Ezra) without surrendering substantial Mosaic authorship. Carroll's rejoinder is that "Moses said" is a citation formula for the Torah, not a compositional claim, and that the *composite* evidence (Genesis 37's doubled narrative) reaches well beyond a few appended verses — so the New Testament's idiom cannot bear the inerrantist weight Durbin places on it.
