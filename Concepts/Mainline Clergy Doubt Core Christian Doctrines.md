# Mainline Clergy Doubt Core Christian Doctrines

#type/claim

## The Claim
Survey data on U.S. clergy shows that large minorities of mainline Protestant and Catholic clergy do **not "definitely believe"** in doctrines the panel treats as traditional markers of Christian faith — the literal existence of [[Adam]] and Eve, [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]]'s bodily resurrection, hell, and miraculous healing. The hosts argue that lay believers in the pews often hold "stronger, deeper beliefs than the guy they're paying."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Belief in God itself (strong evidence — primary survey).** On "which statement comes closest to your belief about God," only ~70% of mainline clergy reported a *certain* belief in God; roughly 30% fell into "belief with doubts" or lower, and ~15% of Catholic clergy reported belief-with-doubts.[^2] *Rating: strong as a data point, but see the wording caveat below.*

2. **Adam and Eve (strong contrast).** Share of clergy who "definitely believe" Adam and Eve existed: Catholic ~23%, mainline ~24%, Evangelical ~80%, Black Protestant ~88%.[^3] The denominational gap (~60 points) is far larger than any plausible survey margin of error, which is the panel's strongest evidentiary point.

3. **Bodily resurrection (the panel's headline).** Evangelicals ~99% definitely believe in Jesus's bodily resurrection; mainline clergy only ~75% — i.e., about one in four mainline clergy do not definitely affirm it.[^4] [[Jonah Barnes]]: "A fourth of all mainline clergy don't definitely believe in the bodily resurrection of Jesus?"[^5]

4. **Miraculous healing (weaker, more wording-sensitive).** 53% of mainline clergy do *not* definitely believe in miraculous healing; only 47% do.[^6] *Rating: moderate — this is the item most vulnerable to the "definitely" wording objection, since "definitely" is a high bar for an inherently contingent claim.*

5. **Cross-checking the source.** The data are drawn from the **National Survey of Religious Leaders**, analyzed by political scientist [[Ryan Burge]] (graphsaboutreligion.com).[^7] Burge is a tier-A quantitative scholar of religion, which raises the evidentiary value above mere anecdote.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The skeptical host ("Ryland") argues the gap may overstate genuine disbelief: (a) respondents may balk at the word "definitely" rather than the doctrine; (b) mainline Protestantism explicitly tolerates doctrinal latitude on non-creedal items, so a "soft" answer reflects ecclesial culture, not apostasy; (c) some "no" answers may be theologically *sophisticated* hesitations (e.g., a youth pastor unsure whether affirming a *bodily* risen Christ commits a Trinitarian heresy).[^8][^9] On this reading the data show a *spectrum of belief and humility*, not hollow faith.

**Scholarly/linguistic context.** Bodily resurrection is creedally central to Nicene Christianity and is the load-bearing claim of [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Corinthians 15]] ("if Christ be not raised, your faith is vain"). Mainline Protestant theology since the 19th–20th century (Bultmann's demythologization, etc.) has produced clergy who reinterpret resurrection symbolically — so a non-trivial mainline "no" is historically plausible and not merely a survey artifact.
