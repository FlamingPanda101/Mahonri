# The LDS Godhead Sidesteps the Named Heresies

## The Point
The creedal Trinity is defined negatively, by the errors it forbids. Knowing the definitions is half the apologetic:

- **Modalism / Sabellianism** — Father, Son, and Spirit are not three persons but three *modes/manifestations* of one person.
- **Arianism** — the Son is a *creature*, "there was a time when he was not."
- **Tritheism** — the three are *three gods*.
- **Partialism** — each person is only a *part* of God (the shamrock/egg analogies).
- **Subordinationism** — the Son is *ontologically lesser* than the Father.
- **Apollinarianism** — Christ has a human body but *no human mind* (the divine Logos replaces it).

Orthodoxy must affirm one substance *and* three persons *and* full deity of each *and* the Father as sole source — while denying every item above. The episode's running demonstration is that this ridge is so narrow that a *sola scriptura* defender keeps falling off it (Apollinarianism, then *autotheos*). The **LDS Godhead** — three distinct divine persons united in one purpose/will — simply does not generate the paradoxes, because it never asserts the one-substance premise that makes them paradoxes. It is not modalism (the persons are really distinct), not Arianism (the Son is eternally divine), not tritheism (they are one Godhead, one in will), not partialism (each is fully a person, not a fraction), and it can affirm Christ's *full* human mind (answering Apollinarianism via Gregory's "what is not assumed is not healed").

## Evidence
1. **The recurring contrast — distinct persons, one in purpose:** when Jacob Hansen described the Godhead, even his Trinitarian opponent conceded it "sounded Trinitarian... it is — definitionally, of divine persons," differing only over *one being* vs. one purpose.[^1]
2. **The heresy inventory is the episode's spine:** Apollinarianism is named and dated (Constantinople 381) as the lead example of an error the creedal defender hit; *autotheos* is flagged as yielding "two gods separately existing."[^2] See [[Avery Tripped the Apollinarian Wire]], [[Autotheos Collides With Eternal Generation]].
3. **Why sola scriptura cannot thread it:** "how many minds does Christ have? You cannot even come close to answering that through the Bible" — the metaphysical precision the heresies require is not in the text.[^3] See [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]].
4. **The monarchy is retained, not flattened:** the Nicene "one God the Father" already reserves *ho theos* for the Father — the same Father-as-source the LDS keep, paired with genuinely distinct persons.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Jacob described his view of the Godhead, and Avery thought it sounded Trinitarian... it is, definitionally, of divine persons. It's just not his view of the Trinity per se."[^1]

> "Apollinarianism was a fourth-century heresy that said that Christ had a human body but not a human mind."[^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the constructive payoff of the whole episode: the named heresies are *the cost of insisting on one substance*. Remove that single creedal commitment — keep three distinct persons, the Father as source, and oneness as unity-of-will — and modalism, tritheism, partialism, subordinationism-as-defect, and the one-mind/two-natures tangle all evaporate at once. This is the standard LDS Godhead model (see [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]], [[Three Whos and One What Framework]], [[The Court Analogy for the Godhead]], [[Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists]], [[John 17 Oneness Is Unity Not Shared Being]]). The honest counterweight: Trinitarians answer that a merely *united* will is social tritheism with good manners, and that "one God" in Scripture means one *being* — the live dispute logged in [[Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists]].
