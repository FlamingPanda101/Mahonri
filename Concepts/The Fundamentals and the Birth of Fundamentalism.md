# The Fundamentals and the Birth of Fundamentalism

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] identifies *The Fundamentals* (a series of publications, "between 1910 to 1917ish") as "the main pivot towards what we might call fundamentalist Christianity." [^1][^2] The series was a defensive program "lashing out against the historical critical method, against critical scholarship," drawing "the line in the sand" with a checklist of beliefs "you have to accept... to be on our side of things." [^2] It "doubled down on some concept of a literal approach to scripture," developing "the inerrancy, the historicity, the univocality" of scripture as "firm identity markers." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The series defined the in-group.** Its function was boundary-marking against rising critical scholarship — accept the fundamentals or you are outside. This is apologetics as identity politics, the show's recurring theme. See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]] and the existing [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]]. **(Strong.)**
2. **It coined the now-standard inerrancy vocabulary.** *Inerrancy*, *historicity*, and *univocality* are deployed not as conclusions reached by study but "as a way to object to rising critical scholarship." [^3] McClellan contrasts this with the "hippie-dippy Origen... just relax about these contradictions" posture of the early church. [^4] **(Strong as characterization.)**
3. **The Scopes Monkey Trial dramatized it.** The 1925 trial (whose centennial McClellan dates to July 2024) adjudicated "whether or not evolution could be taught in schools" — really "how literally should we be interpreting the Bible and should that literal reading govern... public education." [^5][^6] Out of this milieu the fundamentalist package was asserted: six 24-hour days, the global flood, historical Adam and Eve, literal [[Genesis 10|table-of-nations]] genealogies. [^7] **(Strong historical reference.)**
4. **Steelman.** A fundamentalist would say *The Fundamentals* did not *invent* these beliefs but *defended* historic Christianity against genuinely corrosive 19th-century skepticism — a legitimate apologetic response, not a novel dogma. McClellan grants the defensive intent but argues the response over-corrected into a brittle literalism that the tradition had never required, and which "is not giving any wiggle room to the scriptures" but "dictating to the Bible what it is allowed to be." See [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]]. **(Fair — disagreement is over whether the defense distorted the tradition.)**
