# Athanasius Cited a Noncanonical Book to Defend the Rule of Faith

## The Point
[[Athanasius]] of Alexandria — the very figure whose AD 367 festal letter fixed the 27-book New Testament — openly **quoted a book he admitted was *not* in the canon** (the *Shepherd of Hermas*) to defend orthodox doctrine against Arians. This demonstrates [[Thomas A. Wayment]]'s central thesis: in this period canon debates were about protecting the **rule of faith**, so a "noncanonical" text that *supported* the rule of faith could still be marshalled in its defense. Canon status and doctrinal usefulness were not the same thing.

## Evidence

### 1. Athanasius's canon list (AD 367)
In his festal letter establishing the date of Easter, Athanasius "put into writing what had become the officially recognized, although not clearly voted upon, canon" — the 27 NT books in their current order, plus a 22-book Old Testament. He was not the first to write about canonical books but "represents a turning point" from defending a rule of faith to fixing a collection of books that supported it. Notably his 22-book OT *rejected seventeen books canonized by Christians today*. [^1]

### 2. Quoting Hermas while denying its canonicity
Against Arians on the eternal sonship of the Son, Athanasius wrote: "In the Shepherd it is written, since they accept this book, even though it is not of the Canon, 'First of all believe that God is one, who created all things … and brought all things from not being into being.'" He used the book's affirmation that God is creator — a tenet of the rule of faith — even while branding it extracanonical. [^2]

### 3. The Shepherd had already been rejected as spurious
Tertullian (*On Modesty* 10.6) and Irenaeus (*Against Heresies*) had condemned the *Shepherd of Hermas* as extracanonical/apocryphal. Yet it was bound into the mid-4th-century **Codex Sinaiticus** alongside the NT, showing it was still being read and copied. [^3]

### 4. Councils begin formally excluding books
The Council of Laodicea (AD 363–64) issued decrees using the terms *canonical* and *noncanonical*; its 59th canon (relatively secure) forbade reading certain books in the liturgy, and the (disputed) 60th canon listed noncanonical books — its list even omitted the Book of Revelation and gave a 22-book OT slightly different from Athanasius's. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "In the Shepherd it is written, since they accept this book, even though it is not of the Canon, 'First of all believe that God is one, who created all things, and arranged them, and brought all things from not being into being.'" — Athanasius, *Defense of the Nicene Definition* 18 [^2]

> "When a noncanonical … text supported the rule of faith, Athanasius was happy to cite it in defense of his own beliefs." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Wayment places his LDS application here at the same point: the 1921 **decanonization of the Lectures on Faith** from the Doctrine and Covenants shows that "the influence of doctrine on the shape of canon" operates in the restored church too — the Lectures' "personage of Spirit" language was dropped because it conflicted with the later revelation that "the Father has a body of flesh and bones" ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]). Canon follows doctrine. [^5]
