#type/claim

# Orthographic and Textual Variants Are Separate Lists in DJD 32

## The Claim

The frequently repeated apologetic move — that the ~2,600 differences between the Great Isaiah Scroll ([[Donald W. Parry|1QIsaᵃ]]) and the Masoretic Text are "mostly spelling," so the figure is "potentially very misleading" — is, in [[Dan McClellan]]'s words, "entirely false." Both [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] and [[Gavin Ortlund|Gavin Ortlund]] made this same mistake. The published critical edition of the Cave 1 Isaiah scrolls — *Discoveries in the Judaean Desert*, volume 32 (DJD 32) — keeps **two distinct lists**: one of *orthographic* (spelling / meaning-neutral) variants and a *separate* one of *textual* variants. The 2,600-plus figure that apologists cite is the **textual** list, which by the editors' own definition contains **meaning differences, not spelling**.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The editors' explicit definition.** DJD 32 defines the textual-variant list as readings in which 1QIsaᵃ "differs from another Qumran scroll or Masoretic manuscripts or the Targumim in **more than mere orthography or meaning-neutral morphology**, though ambiguous cases are included." McClellan: "what that means is that this list is explicitly meaning differences," with only "a negligible number" of cases the editors were unsure about.[^1] *(Evidence rating: strong — this is a direct appeal to the published critical edition's own stated methodology, verifiable in print.)*

2. **There is a separate orthographic list.** Because DJD 32 also publishes a dedicated list of orthographic variants for the same scroll, conflating the two is not a matter of interpretation but of not having checked the edition. "There are separate lists of orthographic variants and textual variants. You can go look in DJD 32."[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong and falsifiable — the structure of the volume settles it.)*

3. **"Slip of the pen" is an irrelevant qualifier.** A defender might grant the variants are textual but say many are scribal slips. McClellan: whether a change is "a slip of the pen or whether it's intentional… it doesn't matter… it's still a variation, the text has still been changed." A slip still produces a different reading.[^3] *(Evidence rating: sound — intentionality is orthogonal to whether the texts differ.)*

4. **Both a skeptic and an apologist made the same error.** The same correction lands on O'Connor (who called the variants "tiny issues like spelling") and Ortlund (who called the 2,600 figure misleading for that reason). McClellan: "I don't know where they're getting their information from," implying both inherited it secondhand rather than from the edition.[^4] See [[1QIsaiah-a Aligns With the Masoretic Text Under 92.5 Percent]].

**Steelman of the apologetic reading:** It is *true* that many of the textual variants are minor — single-word or sub-word differences — and that the Masoretic and Qumran Isaiah traditions agree the overwhelming majority of the time. So "most differences are small" is defensible. The error is the *category claim* that the cited number is a spelling count; smallness of meaning-change is not the same as orthography, and DJD 32's separate lists make the distinction non-negotiable.

## LDS Relevance

Latter-day Saint apologists also appeal to DSS Isaiah — but in the opposite direction, citing genuine *textual* variants where Book of Mormon Isaiah agrees with Qumran against the KJV/Masoretic base (see [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Matches the Dead Sea Scrolls]]). McClellan's correction is methodologically helpful to that case: it establishes that the 2,600-plus differences are real *meaning* variants, not spelling noise, which is the premise the LDS anachronism argument needs. The lesson cuts both ways — any side quantifying the scroll for rhetorical ends should cite the textual-variant list from DJD 32, not a conflated total, and should distinguish minor from major meaning differences as the editors do.

## Best Quotes

> "This is entirely wrong. Both Alex and Gavin make this mistake, and I don't know where they're getting their information from, but the notion that the list of more than 2,600 textual variants includes spelling differences — what textual critics refer to as orthographic variants — is entirely false."[^4]

> "Listed as variants are those readings in which 1QIsaᵃ differs from another Qumran scroll or Masoretic manuscripts or the Targumim in more than mere orthography or meaning-neutral morphology, though ambiguous cases are included."[^1]

> "It doesn't matter whether it's a slip of a pen or whether it's intentional, it's still a variation. The text has still been changed."[^3]
