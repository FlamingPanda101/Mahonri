# 1 Corinthians 7 Consent Is to Abstain Not to Have Sex

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: Scrivener claims [[1 Corinthians 7.3-5|1 Corinthians 7]] "thrust consent into the bedroom." But read carefully, the mutual consent applies only to *abstaining* — so neither spouse can unilaterally refuse sex. "It's not consent to have sex; it's consent to *not* have sex." This structure arguably licenses marital rape.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The grammatical hinge.** Paul: "Do not deprive one another, except by mutual consent for a time... for prayer." The exception clause governs the *deprivation/abstention*. O'Connor: "If there is not mutual consent to *not* have sex — if I want sex and she doesn't — we don't have mutual consent to withdraw, so she still has to have sex with me. There's a word for that."[^1] *(Strong reading of the syntax — the only sanctioned abstention is *mutual*; unilateral refusal is excluded.)*

2. **The historical effect.** O'Connor: this has "probably been a justification for marital rape until it was illegalized relatively recently."[^2] *(Plausible — spousal-rape exemptions persisted in Western law into the late 20th century; this verse was among the texts cited for marital sexual obligation.)*

3. **Steelman of Scrivener — the suspicious reading.** Scrivener: "That is the most suspicious reading, and unnecessarily suspicious." He argues the mutual-consent clause applies to *both poles* — the chapter is a pro-sex "marriage and family program" ([[Joseph Henrich|Henrich's]] "Christian sexual revolution"), encouraging mutual self-giving, not licensing coercion. The wife's equal claim over the husband's body is the radical, liberating innovation. See [[Paul Pioneered Mutual Bodily Ownership]].[^3] *(Defensible — the verse's *thrust* is mutual obligation, and "you must satisfy your spouse" is symmetric; but O'Connor's point is that *symmetric* obligation without a unilateral veto is precisely what fails the *modern* consent standard.)*

4. **The "concession not command" coda.** Scrivener cites v. 6 ("I say this by way of concession, not command") and Paul's preference for celibacy, arguing the whole passage is permissive/pastoral, not a rule licensing demand. O'Connor: that concession is about marriage vs. celibacy, not about overriding a spouse's refusal.[^4] *(The exchange ends unresolved; Brierley calls Scrivener's reading "slightly tendentious" too. Scrivener said he'd "go back and think harder about 1 Corinthians 7.")*
