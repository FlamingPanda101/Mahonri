## The Point
Historians debate whether baptism in the early centuries was usually immersion (plunging, perhaps not always a full submersion), sometimes affusion (pouring) or aspersion (sprinkling), or a combination. Pouring as a method emerged as early as the late first century, primarily as an accommodation when running water or full immersion was not possible — especially for the gravely ill.

## Evidence
1. The Didache (late first/early second century) encouraged immersion in running water, but if none was available, "then pour water on the head three times in the name of Father and Son and Holy Spirit."
2. The fifth-century Ravenna baptistery mosaic over its octagonal font shows Jesus's baptism; nineteenth-century restoration rendered John pouring water on Jesus's head, but the original probably portrayed John's hand on Jesus's head, as elsewhere in early Christian art.
3. Possible combinations existed, such as kneeling "immersed" in water while receiving pouring.
4. Affusion particularly served those at the point of death, including infants whose parents urgently wished baptism before death.
5. Cyprian (mid-third century) defended pouring or sprinkling as "divine abridgements" (*compendia*) acceptable "when necessity compels," citing Ezekiel 36:25–26 ("then will I sprinkle clean water upon you ... a new heart also will I give you").
6. Octagonal fonts and baptisteries became popular from the fourth century, the eighth side recalling the "eighth day" of Christ's resurrection — symbolizing rebirth and new creation.

## Best Quotes
> "Historians debate whether the practice in the early centuries was usually immersion (plunging, perhaps not always a full submersion), sometimes affusion (pouring) or aspersion (sprinkling), or a combination of these." [^1]

> "Cyprian ... defended accommodations such as pouring or sprinkling with water, calling them 'divine abridgements' (compendia) that were acceptable 'when necessity compels.'" [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison notes that "we Latter-day Saints can easily recognize differences between our norms and ancient practices," but affirms "God has never stopped loving and blessing people who desired to turn to him" — modeling charitable engagement with divergent modes of baptism. [^2]
