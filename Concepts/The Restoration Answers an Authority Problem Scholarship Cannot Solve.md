# The Restoration Answers an Authority Problem Scholarship Cannot Solve

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] closes by arguing that the loss he has documented is "not fundamentally a textual problem. It's an **authority problem.**"[^1] "The question of which texts are scripture, which readings are authentic, and which interpretations are correct... cannot be resolved by the texts themselves, because the texts are what's in dispute. You cannot use the canon to establish the canon."[^1] Better scholarship cannot fix it: "the Protestant Reformation demonstrates this clearly" — Luther, Calvin, and the reformers, "serious and scripturally rigorous people working from the same biblical texts... produced not unity, but fragmentation. 40,000 Christian denominations today, each reading the same 66 books and reaching different conclusions."[^2] "More careful interpretation of a corrupted and incomplete text does not produce certainty. It produces more denominations."[^2] The LDS answer is "what God has always used when the texts become corrupted and authority becomes confused, a **prophet**" (Amos 3:7) — "Moses was not a reformer of existing Egyptian religion... Lehi left Jerusalem precisely because the institutional religion of his day had drifted."[^3] The evidential hook: the Book of Mormon (produced 1829) and 1 Nephi 13's "great and abominable church" taking away "many parts that are plain and most precious" *predicted* the editorial narrowing that 20th-century scholarship (Dead Sea Scrolls 1947; [[Bart Ehrman]]'s *Misquoting Jesus*, 2005) would later document — "Joseph Smith either predicted what the scholars would spend their careers confirming, or the text is just what it claims to be."[^3] See [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]] and [[LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "can't use the canon to establish the canon" problem is real.** Every reader presupposes judgments about translation, manuscript family, and which books count — judgments not derivable from the text alone. The vault develops this in [[The Canon-as-Article-of-Faith Dilemma]] and its canon cluster. *Rating: a genuine and widely acknowledged dilemma for *sola scriptura*.* [^1]
2. **Reformation fragmentation is a fact (if not the exact number).** That shared texts + private interpretation yielded enormous denominational diversity is historically undeniable; "40,000 denominations" is a rounded, much-quoted figure of disputed precision but the *phenomenon* is real. *Rating: the trend is solid; treat the number as rhetorical.* [^2]
3. **The biblical pattern of prophets is genuine.** Scripture does present God acting through called prophets (Amos 3:7; Moses; the prophets to Israel), so "a prophet, not a committee" is a scripturally grounded model of authority. *Rating: a fair appeal, though it presupposes the LDS reading of apostasy/restoration.* [^3]
4. **The predictive framing is the load-bearing evidential claim.** 1 Nephi 13's "plain and precious things... taken away" (1829) does describe, in outline, the editorial-loss thesis later detailed by critical scholarship — the same "direction of evidence" structure as the temple argument. *Rating: rhetorically strong; its force depends on how *specific* one judges 1 Nephi 13 to be (see steelman).* [^3]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** (a) **The authority problem cuts both ways** — appealing to a living prophet relocates, but does not dissolve, the problem: one must still decide *which* prophet is genuine, by *what* criteria, using *texts and judgment* — so the LDS solution faces its own version of "you can't use the prophet to establish the prophet" (rival restorationist claimants exist). (b) **Protestants have answers** — the *perspicuity* of scripture on *essentials* (vs. disputable secondary matters), the rule of faith, and the Spirit's witness mean fragmentation is over *non-essentials*, not the gospel core; "40,000 denominations" notoriously double-counts independent congregations and is not 40,000 *theologies*. (c) **1 Nephi 13 is vague enough to fit many outcomes** — "plain and precious things taken away" is general, and reading it as a prediction of 20th-century textual criticism is *retrospective* pattern-matching; the Book of Abraham and other cases are often cited as evidence cutting the *other* way. (d) **Catholic/Orthodox answer the canon problem** via apostolic Tradition and Magisterium without needing new revelation. A Latter-day Saint reply: the prophet model is *not* circular in the same way, because it claims an *external*, ongoing divine voice plus *evidential* anchors (the Book of Mormon as a testable artifact — Nahom, chiasmus, Dead Sea Scrolls convergences) to break the loop, whereas *sola scriptura* offers no external arbiter at all; perspicuity has *empirically* failed to produce unity even on baptism, the nature of God, and salvation; and the point is comparative — the Restoration at least *names* an authority that can settle the canon, where "the Bible alone" cannot. See [[LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity]].
