# God Was Once a Man

The most concrete and radical teaching from the [[King Follett Discourse]]: that God
the Father was once a mortal human being who progressed through exaltation to become
God. This directly collapses the Creator/creature distinction that is foundational
to mainstream Christian theism.

## The Claim

[[Joseph Smith]] taught explicitly in the King Follett Discourse that God is an exalted,
embodied man — and that this was the "great secret" of Mormon theology.[^1]

## Best Quote

> God himself was once as we are now and is an exalted man and sits enthroned in
> yonder heavens. That is the great secret. If the veil were rent today and God
> were to make himself visible... if you were to see him today, you would see him
> like a man in form like yourselves in all the person image and the very form
> as a man.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Corporeal God:** Smith declared in Nauvoo that he had been teaching a corporeal
   (embodied) God for "over a dozen years," though [[Benjamin Park]] notes the historical
   record is "a bit more nuanced" — early revelations like the Book of Ether (Book of
   Mormon) and Book of Moses hint at embodiment, but may have used the language of
   "social trinitarianism" (common among evangelicals of the day) where God having
   "body and parts" was meant metaphorically, not literally.[^3]

2. **Earlier hints:** Pre-Nauvoo revelations like [[D&C 76]] (exaltation) and [[D&C 93]]
   (spirits existing "in the beginning with God") laid groundwork. Smith's early-1830s
   "Specimen of Pure Language" used terms like *Aman God* and *Aman Christ* which
   "seem to imply that God and Christ are part of the same lineage as human beings."[^4]

3. **Contextual statement:** The sermon opens with: "If men do not comprehend the
   character of God, they do not comprehend themselves" — a direct assault on mainstream
   Christianity's doctrine that God is ultimately unknowable.[^5]

## Counter-arguments

- Mainstream Christianity holds that God is uncreated, eternal, immaterial spirit
  ([[John 4.24]]: "God is a Spirit"), and that collapsing God into a progressed human
  being undermines the transcendence and uniqueness of the Creator.
- Some LDS leaders and scholars (e.g., [[Gordon B. Hinckley]], in a 1997 interview)
  have distanced the church from the discourse, saying it "gets into some pretty
  deep theology that we don't know very much about."[^6]
- See [[Human Deification in Mormonism]] for the inverse claim (man becomes God)
  and its objections.
