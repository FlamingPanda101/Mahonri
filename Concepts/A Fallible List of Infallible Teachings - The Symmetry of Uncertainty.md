# A Fallible List of Infallible Teachings — The Symmetry of Uncertainty

## The Claim
In cross-examination [[Gavin Ortlund]] presses a precise question: if Catholicism has a body of infallible teachings, "the least we can do is identify what it is." How many *ex cathedra* papal definitions have there been? [[Trent Horn]] concedes "theologians debate that — there's not a set number," and that there is likewise "no fixed number of sacred traditions or infallible papal pronouncements that everyone agrees upon." Ortlund draws the summary admission: **"a fallible list of infallible teachings."** Horn: "sure."[^1]

This is the magisterium-side analogue of the famous Protestant canon problem (R.C. Sproul's "fallible list of infallible books"): the Catholic, too, cannot produce an *infallibly defined inventory* of which dogmas are infallible — so the supposed advantage of certainty is undercut at the level of the *list itself*. Ortlund's broader point: "having a purportedly infallible magisterium does not remove ambiguity on important questions" — "if I ask 10 Roman Catholics am I saved and why, I get eight or nine different answers."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

**Steelman (Ortlund / Protestant):**
- **The infallibility is inaccessible if its scope is undefined.** If you cannot say *with certainty* which teachings are infallible, then "infallibility" cannot do the disambiguating work it is advertised to do; you are back to *fallible judgment* about where to find the infallible content — structurally identical to the Protestant reading Scripture. This is the [[The Infinite Regress of Infallible Interpreters|regress]] applied to the *catalog* of dogmas.
- **Catholics disagree on essentials too.** Ortlund's recurring parity move: "neither do Catholics agree on the essentials — look at the breadth between the arch-conservatives and the liberals." So the "Protestants can't agree" charge is "not particular to sola scriptura; it's just a human problem."[^3]

**Steelman (Horn / Catholic counter — the symmetry cuts both ways):**
- **The Protestant has the *worse* version of every item.** Horn's tu quoque: "the same problem affects *your* knowledge of what is and isn't scripture." There is a fallible list of infallible *books* (longer ending of Mark? the woman caught in adultery?), a fallible list of *essential doctrines* (Ortlund "had to write a whole book" on which doctrines are first-rank, and Protestants still disagree), and even an unsettled text (Protestants "disagree about textual variants, some important").[^4] So a fuzzy *boundary* is a universal feature of finite knowers — but the Catholic still has a *living* authority that can, when needed, sharpen the boundary ("break glass in emergency"), whereas the Protestant has no such mechanism. See [[A Living Magisterium Settles What Sola Scriptura Cannot]] and [[The Fallible Canon Is Hostage to Critical Scholarship]].
- **A core can be certain even if the periphery is fuzzy.** Horn distinguishes "second-order objects of infallibility" (this council is valid) from defined dogmas (the Divinity of Christ, the perpetual virginity of Mary) — the *core* dogmas are identifiable even if no exhaustive numbered list exists, just as the Protestant *homologoumena* are secure even though the *antilegomena* were disputed.

## LDS relevance
The symmetry argument is **grist for the LDS "no human system terminates the regress" thesis** (see [[The Infinite Regress of Infallible Interpreters]]). If *both* the Protestant canon-list and the Catholic dogma-list are fallible lists of infallible items, the Restoration's response is to relocate ultimate authority from any *static list* to a *living God who still speaks* through called prophets — [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]. The LDS would press Horn exactly as Ortlund does: an open-ended, undefined inventory of binding teachings is functionally the *open canon* Catholics deny having — and an open canon is what the Restoration affirms outright.
