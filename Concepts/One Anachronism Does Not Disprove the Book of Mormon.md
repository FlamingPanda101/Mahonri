# One Anachronism Does Not Disprove the Book of Mormon

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

A common critic move: a single genuine anachronism in the Book of Mormon — one
plant, animal, or technology that demonstrably did not exist in the ancient
Americas — is enough to prove the text is a 19th-century fabrication and "ride out"
the whole book.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The standard collapses on a translated text

The only Book of Mormon we possess is an English text dated to 1829. Even if it is
a divine translation of an ancient record, translation introduces anachronism (see
[[Translation Introduces Anachronism Even in Divine Translation]]). So the bar of
"zero anachronisms" was never appropriate to the artifact we actually have.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a structural point about what kind of document is being judged).

### 2. "Zero anachronisms" really means "nothing unique"

Past the blatant cases (an iPhone, an automobile), an "anachronism" is often just
information a long text preserves that no other source corroborates. Demanding none
of that from a text the size of the Book of Mormon would be "insane" and would
discredit nearly every real ancient document. See
[[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]].[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (applies a uniform historical standard).

### 3. A claimed anachronism must be *certain*

For "one does it in" to hold, the one has to be airtight — "an iPhone." But the
list keeps shrinking as evidence accrues (scimitars, fortifications, cement), so any
given survivor may simply be undiscovered, not impossible. See
[[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]] and
[[Only Two Percent of the Americas Has Been Excavated]].[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (depends on the trajectory data holding).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The strongest version of the objection: probability is cumulative — many
independent "trending" items each with weak evidence do not add up to a likely
ancient origin, and a single *truly* impossible item (e.g., a clearly post-Columbian
technology with no diffusion path) would indeed be decisive. Rappleye concedes this:
it would have to be the equivalent of an iPhone, and the 1830s "smoking guns" critics
cited (gunpowder, cannons, steamboats) are simply not in the text.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "I actually want to directly respond to this idea that one anacronism does the Book of Mormon in... there are some challenges that make that hard to accept."[^1]

> "You have to really know... you have to be absolutely certain that it's a legitimate anacronism for that to be true. It has to be an iPhone, right? It has to be an automobile."[^4]

> "When you say it can't have any anacronisms, what you're kind of saying is there can't be any unique information that's uncorroborated by anything else... that would be insane for a text as long as the Book of Mormon."[^3]
