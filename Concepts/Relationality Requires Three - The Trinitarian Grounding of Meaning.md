# Relationality Requires Three — The Trinitarian Grounding of Meaning

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brother Oz (Austin)]]'s master argument for the Trinity is not exegetical but metaphysical: **relationality cannot exist below the number three.** Because "God is love" ([[1 John 4.8|1 John 4:8]]), and love is irreducibly relational, there must necessarily be — eternally, within God — "the lover, the love, and the beloved... the knower, the knowledge, and the known."[^1] He fuses this with a **Logos** claim: because Christ is the underlying logic of reality through whom "all things are made" ([[Colossians 1.16|Colossians 1:16]]; [[John 1.1|John 1:1]]), meaning, identity, and purpose are *baked into reality itself* rather than socially constructed.[^2] The payoff is sweeping: a triune God is the single source of "all relationships, identities, and substances at once," so that "ontology, epistemology, and all of ethics" emerge from the one brute fact that *God is love*.[^3] His charge against the LDS Godhead is that it leaves this mystery "unsolved" — "there is no sun behind the horizon," no ultimate ground for why anything means anything.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Evangelical (Brother Oz)
- **Love is triadic, not dyadic.** Oz's sharpest move is that a bare monad cannot *be* love in itself (it has no beloved), and even a dyad collapses lover and beloved into a closed exchange; genuine relational love requires a third — the love itself, or the shared object of love — so the structure of love mirrors the structure of a tripersonal God. Spoken form: "the lover, the love, and the beloved."[^1]
- **Meaning = purpose = identity, grounded in the Logos.** Oz argues that to categorize anything ("this cup") requires a *purpose*, and "meaningful is the degree to which something is purposeful." The most meaningful human experiences — marriage, children, love — are where people say "this is exactly what I'm supposed to be doing." If Christ is the Logos, then purpose and identity are objective features of reality, not postmodern "power games."[^5] This connects to the vault's natural-theology case that meaning and abstracta require a divine Mind — see [[Abstract Objects Must Exist in an Infinite Divine Intellect]].
- **The "tripartite reality" intuition.** Oz observes that we always confront three mysteries at once: identity, the external world (substance), and the relationship between them. He claims this triune structure of experience is "answered in Christ" — a Trinitarian reality reflecting a Trinitarian God.[^6]
- **The closing thesis.** "God is love is the only brute fact necessary, and from that emerges ontology, epistemology, and all of ethics."[^3] As an account that posits *one* foundational fact and derives everything from it, it has genuine explanatory economy — the same parsimony virtue Oz weaponizes against LDS "brute facts" of eternal matter and law.

### Steelman of the Latter-day Saint (Hayden Carroll)
- **"Why must relationality require three rather than two?"** Carroll's direct rebuttal: he grants love is foundational but denies the numerical claim. Lover and beloved are *two*; "love" is their relation, not a third *being*. He repeatedly resists Oz's reification — "love is not a being, right?" — and suspects no authoritative source defines "being" so as to force the count to three.[^7] On the LDS view, the relational fullness Oz wants is supplied by the **plurality of the Godhead** (Father, Son, Spirit as distinct beings unified in love) *and* by the whole human family — see [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]], [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]].
- **Mind/intelligence already grounds identity.** Carroll proposes that **mind** fulfills the grounding role Oz assigns the Trinity, and that reality (space, time, matter, law) can exist as a brute fact without being authored — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]], [[The Laws of Nature Are a Brute Fact]]. He turns Oz's parsimony charge around: positing *all* of physics-plus-meaning inside one simple God is not obviously fewer brute facts than positing an eternal reality.[^8]
- **Common ground, different metaphysics.** Notably, Carroll *agrees* that relationship is "the foundation of all things" and a "solid pillar of Latter-day Saintism" — exaltation just *is* being "perfectly aligned and connected and bound to God."[^9] The dispute is narrow: whether that relational foundation entails *numerical* threeness within a single divine substance (Oz) or a society of distinct divine and human persons (Carroll). The LDS objection is that Oz smuggles the conclusion (three) into the premise (love) without argument for *why three and not two-or-more*.

**Honest assessment.** The argument is elegant but proves less than it claims. Even if love requires a beloved, that yields a *minimum of two*, not exactly three; the move from "lover/love/beloved" to "three co-equal persons of one substance" is a verbal trinity that does not obviously pick out the Nicene Trinity over (a) a binitarian God, (b) a society of distinct beings (the LDS Godhead), or (c) God-plus-creation. Oz's deeper point — that *meaning must be grounded in a divine Logos* — is strong against atheism and is one the Restoration can largely affirm (intelligence and truth are divine), so the real fork is metaphysical (one simple being vs. embodied society), not whether meaning needs a divine ground.
