# Jacob of Serugh and the Veil of Moses Typology

## The Point
The fifth-century Syriac preacher [[Jacob of Serugh]] held that "there is not even one line in the Old Testament that does not proclaim the way of the Son." In his metrical homily *The Veil on Moses' Face* (Exodus 34:29–35), Jacob interprets the veil Moses wore as a symbol that "the words of prophecy are veiled" — the Old Testament hides Christ in types until the coming of Christ removes the veil and reveals him shining through every page.

## Evidence
1. **Jacob's context and output.** He preached in Syriac, "an Aramaic dialect similar to the one spoken by Jesus," was educated in Edessa nearly a century after Egeria, and "over four hundred of his sermons survive, many on the Old Testament." [^1]
2. **The veil = veiled prophecy.** Answering why Moses veiled his face, Jacob says the veil means "the words of prophecy are veiled." The Old Testament "hides its content in parables so that they might not be recognized … so that the world might not become openly aware of the Son of God." [^2]
3. **Veil over the prophets' words.** "Through the Spirit, … the prophets brought news to the entire world of the Son in secret, and the veil which was on Moses' face was spread over their words whenever they spoke of the Only-Begotten." Indeed "the whole Old Testament is veiled after the fashion of Moses," yet "within the veil which lies over scripture there sits resplendent Christ as judge." [^2]
4. **Christ removes the veil.** "Only in the life and ministry of Christ did the entire Old Testament become comprehensible." When Christ came "he uncovered Moses' face" and let his light "shine out over the whole earth," explaining the "symbols and figures and parables" of the Old Testament — for "the radiance of Moses was in fact Christ shining in him." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "For Jacob of Sarug, there is not even one line in the Old Testament that does not proclaim the way of the Son." (Armando Elkhoury, quoted) [^1]

> "Within the veil which lies over scripture there sits resplendent Christ as judge." (Jacob of Serugh) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Jacob's reading echoes the New Testament's own veil typology (2 Corinthians 3:13–16, where the veil over Moses's face is removed "in Christ") and exemplifies the Christocentric reading affirmed in 2 Nephi 11:4 — that the Old Testament is saturated with types of Christ. Heal notes the homily also "exhibits anti-Judaism that was … foundational to Syriac Christianity," a candid acknowledgment of the tradition's darker side.
