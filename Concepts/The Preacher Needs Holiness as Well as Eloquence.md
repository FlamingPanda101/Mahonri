# The Preacher Needs Holiness as Well as Eloquence

## The Point
[[Jerome]], in *Letter 52 to Nepotian* (AD 393), gives advice to a young preacher that sharply subordinates rhetorical performance to scriptural mastery and personal righteousness. The aim of preaching is to win "the hearts of the congregation for God," not "the applause of the congregation for the speaker." A preacher should be "an exceptionally well-trained expert on the deep things and mysteries of … Scripture" and, above all, a man of "deep personal righteousness."

## Evidence
1. **Tears, not applause.** "When you preach in church, let not applause … be elicited; let the tears of your listeners be your accolades." [^1]
2. **Against the windbag.** "I do not want you to be a declaimer, a ranter, or a windbag, but an exceptionally well-trained expert on the deep things and mysteries of your God's Scripture. Reeling off words and speaking briskly to win admiration from the unwashed masses are the ploys of ignorant men." [^1]
3. **Gregory of Nazianzus's quip.** Jerome recounts asking his teacher Gregory of Nazianzus the meaning of the "second-first" (*deuteroprōton*) sabbath in Luke 6:1; Gregory craftily replied he would explain it "in church," where the applauding crowd would force Jerome either to pretend he understood or be thought "a fool" — exposing how easily crowds are deceived. [^2]
4. **Righteousness as the defining qualification.** Knowledge alone is insufficient: "Jerome believed that deep personal righteousness acquired through Christian discipleship and diligent right-living should be the defining feature of the Christian preacher." More seekers, "like Augustine, would be drawn to the Christian message through the goodness and kindness of the preacher than by his profound eloquence alone." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Let not applause … be elicited; let the tears of your listeners be your accolades." (Jerome, *Letter 52*) [^1]

> "There is nothing as easy as deceiving the illiterate rabble with a slick style of speaking, for whatever they do not understand they admire all the more." (Jerome, *Letter 52*) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Jerome's insistence that the preacher's deep righteousness outweighs his eloquence — and that genuine preaching is "seasoned by his reading of Scripture" — parallels D&C 50:17–22 (preach "by the Spirit of truth") and the Latter-day Saint suspicion of "priestcraft," i.e., preaching to be praised rather than to edify (2 Nephi 26:29).
