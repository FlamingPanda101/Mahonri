# The Online Echo Chamber Accelerates Disaffiliation

## The Claim
Interest-based social-media algorithms sort people into ideological silos that feed increasingly anti-religious content to those drifting left (and tribalizing content to those drifting right), reinforcing disaffiliation and substituting online political activism for in-person community.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Feeds amplify the direction you're already moving (rated: moderate).** A young person whose religious identity is declining and political identity rising "enters a bit of an echo chamber"; if trending liberal, "they're going to start seeing far more things that are negative towards religion because that's the direction that has moved."[^1] *Steelman:* Dyer guards against overclaiming — many religious people are liberal — and frames this as a macro tendency, not a deterministic rule.[^2]

2. **Activism moved from bodies to feeds (rated: moderate).** "The political activism is not happening as much in groups and persons, but it's just happening online." Where criticizing someone's politics once required showing up or writing a letter, "now I can do that in a public, open public sphere," eroding the in-person bonds that hold congregations together.[^3]

3. **Algorithms are interest-based, not relationship-based (rated: moderate).** The host notes that because feeds are now interest-based rather than friend-based, "people are just living in a lot of ideological silos," rarely encountering those different from them.[^4]

4. **The geographic ward as antidote (rated: moderate, normative).** Because wards are organized by geography, members worship across class and political lines, "forcing" cross-difference relationships — the structural opposite of an echo chamber.[^5] *Steelman:* the host concedes geography "can have political implications sometimes," so wards are not perfectly heterogeneous.

## Best Quotes

> "You enter a bit of an echo chamber... they're going to start seeing far more things that are negative towards religion because that's the direction that has moved."[^1]

> "The political activism is not happening as much in groups and persons, but it's just happening online... now I can do that in a public, open public sphere."[^3]

> "If you don't like a person's political opinion, then you distance yourself... the online world would have you feel like you should disconnect from that individual. And so that's one reason also that we're fracturing more as a people, and that's pulling us away from religion."[^6]
