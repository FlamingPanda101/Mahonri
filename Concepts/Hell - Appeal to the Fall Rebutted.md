# Appeal to the Fall — Rebutted

## The Defense Being Rebutted

When Christians face the problem that God seems responsible for creating
depraved humans who can't avoid damnation, they often appeal to the **Fall of
Adam and Eve**:

1. **Because of the Fall**, humans exist in a less-than-ideal state lacking
   attributes or beliefs required to avoid damnation
2. **The Fall is the result of Adam's choice**, not God's actions
3. Therefore, **God is not responsible** for individuals lacking what's
   required to avoid damnation [^1]

This argument attempts to put the blame back on humanity, often appealing to
[[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12]]: "Wherefore, as by one man sin entered into the
world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have
sinned."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. God Created Adam with the Capacity to Fall

God still created Adam with the attributes of being imperfect and sinful, and
sustained him in existence with the properties of being able to sin and cause
humanity to fall. Would not this response **limit God's power** in addition to
making him unjust? [^2]

### 2. The Perpetual Creation Problem

Even if Adam fell, God still **perpetually creates depraved souls from nothing**
for the rest of human existence. Why would Adam's one-time fall result in God
continuing to create **trillions** of beings worthy of damnation? He could
create them in such a way as to avoid being worthy of damnation. [^2]

### 3. The Selective Salvation Problem

Some Christians might say God *could not* create humans any other way — but
God certainly does create at least **some** humans capable of avoiding
damnation (those who accept Christ, believe in the Trinity, etc.). If the
Fall doesn't prevent God from creating **some** humans who meet the
requirements for salvation, why doesn't he create **all** humans this way? [^2]

### 4. The Impotence Dilemma

> "The fall objection cannot explain why the perfectly loving God of
> Christianity would continue to create humans from nothing in this fallen
> state."

Either:
- **(a)** God is **bound by the effects of Adam's actions**, which would make
  him impotent, OR
- **(b)** God is creating souls from nothing with the exact properties they
  have, **knowing they will be worthy of eternal torment** — in which case he
  either should have created them differently or is not *able* to create them
  differently at all [^2]

Neither option preserves the classical theist conception of God.
