#type/historical

## The Point
With Constantine's favor toward Christianity in the early fourth century (legalization and protected status) and Theodosius's late-fourth-century edicts (making Christianity the official imperial religion), believers shifted within two generations from a persecuted minority to a state-supported network. To mark this new status, monumental church construction adapted the basilica — a prominent Roman civic building form — into structures meant to house God's presence. Christian basilicas became, in effect, the new temples of Christianity.

## Evidence
1. The Roman basilica was a large structure divided by columns into three aisles with an elevated platform (bema) at one end of the central nave, designed for royal audiences, judicial hearings, and assemblies before state officials.
2. Constantine, his successors, and other officials commissioned basilical churches at prominent civic locations throughout the empire.
3. Christian basilicas followed a general template: a longitudinal (east-west) axis, colonnaded atrium, covered narthex, central nave, side aisles, and a raised bema with a semicircular apse (the sanctuary) containing the Eucharistic altar and a semicircle of clergy benches (the central seat being the cathedra, the bishop's seat).
4. Architectural variations included a transept (creating a cruciform church), a centralized sermon platform (common in Syrian churches), and — especially in the Byzantine east — a large dome over the juncture of nave and sanctuary.
5. By dedicating the basilica to Christ, framing the apse with a triumphal arch (associated with imperial military victory), and adorning it with art highlighting Jesus's divinity (Christ enthroned, or Christ Pantocrator in the dome), monumental churches declared Jesus the cosmic ruler, eschatological judge, and ultimate mediator.
6. Imperial patrons sometimes built churches over destroyed pagan temples or converted such temples, declaring Christianity's triumph over Greco-Roman polytheism.

## Best Quotes
> "In short, Christian basilicas were to become the new temples of Christianity." [^1]

> "Within two generations, believers went from belonging to a persecuted minority group to being members of a state-supported Church network that granted them empire-wide patronage, privilege, prestige, and position." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Grey notes that early churches "demarcated zones of sacred space through the use of doors, veils, and chancel screens," "used altars and apses to represent the divine presence," and "relied on priestly personnel (wearing sacred liturgical clothing)" — features offering conceptual vocabulary that can enrich the Latter-day Saint temple experience even though early Christians built no temples separate from their churches.
