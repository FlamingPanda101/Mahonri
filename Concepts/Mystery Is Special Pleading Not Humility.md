# Mystery Is Special Pleading Not Humility

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection: the Trinity's apparent contradictions are a "mystery" — beyond human comprehension — and accepting it is an act of humility, since "God is not like anything else." [^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **"Mystery" here is special pleading, not humility.** "Calling this a mystery is not humility, it's special pleading. The Trinity survives only by asking believers to abandon the very logic they rely on everywhere else." It singles out one case for an exemption from the law of non-contradiction. (Evidence: a sharp framing; the force depends on whether the Trinity is a *contradiction* or merely *above* reason — see steelman.) [^1]
2. **Special pleading defined.** "Special pleading is just fancy talk for excuse" — applying a rule everywhere except where it would defeat your own position (the sun rises in the east *every* day "except this one day because I said so"). The Trinitarian, when cornered, "axiomatically declares that contradictory concepts are not contradictory." (Evidence: correct definition; whether it applies turns on the contradiction question.) [^2]
3. **Belief by coercion, not coherence.** Hansen argues no one finds the Trinity intuitively coherent ("yeah, I don't fully understand it, but I have to believe it"); it is held because the creedal tradition makes assent a condition of being Christian/saved, and to exclude Latter-day Saints. (Evidence: a sociological/rhetorical claim, partly true of popular belief but not a refutation of the doctrine itself.) [^3]
4. **"God is unlike everything" cuts against the gospel.** Against "why expect God to be like anything else?", Hansen replies the issue isn't *what* God is but the *incoherence*; and Christ's whole message ("this is life eternal, to *know* the only true God"; the incarnation: "I'm like you, you're like me") is an affront to a God declared an unknowable mystery. (Evidence: theological argument; coheres with the LDS knowability emphasis.) [^4]
5. **Steelman.** The classical theist distinguishes a *contradiction* (which even God cannot be) from a *mystery above reason* (a truth whose mode we can't fully grasp but which entails no formal contradiction). The Trinity, properly stated, is claimed to be the latter: the persons are one *as to essence* and three *as to person* — different respects, so no "A and not-A in the same respect." Analogies (a divine "incomprehensibility" already granted for omnipresence, timeless eternity, etc.) show Christians accept above-reason truths elsewhere without special pleading. (Evidence: this is the strongest orthodox reply — the "same respect" qualifier is exactly what blocks a formal contradiction *if* "person" and "being" are genuinely different respects.) The LDS rejoinder: the [[The Who or What Test for the Word God|who/what test]] is designed to test whether "person" and "being" really are different respects or a relabeling, and on the LDS analysis they collapse, leaving a true contradiction, not a mere mystery. [^5]
