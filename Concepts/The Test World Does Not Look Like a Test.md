# The Test World Does Not Look Like a Test

## The Point
In closing, Seth presses the problem of evil as a *predictive* test and then turns it specifically against the most common theodicy — the free-will "mortal test" defense. First pass: if God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, "we should find no evil," yet we observe evil, so that definition of God is falsified.[^1] When the theist upgrades to "God set everything up to be a test... giving everyone free will," Seth runs the same method on the new definition: "if God is a god of tests, what should the world look like?" His answer is that the world conspicuously does *not* look like a fair test — dying babies who never start it, people "born in the wrong nations with the wrong textbook," unequal advantages, and randomly distributed disabilities and trauma.[^2] The LDS-specific replies in [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]] and [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]] answer exactly this "unfair test" objection, though Hansen himself brackets evil as a theological question outside his ontological argument.

## Evidence
1. **The basic prediction.** "If God is all-powerful, if he knows everything, if he is omnibenevolent, then what should we find in the world? We should find no evil. And so now we test... we look around and we find evidence of evil — that debunks the definition of God."[^1]
2. **The test fails its own description.** "We find babies and children that are dying before they even get the test started... people born in the wrong nations that have the wrong textbook for the test... people born into positions of advantage that give them an unfair advantage."[^2]
3. **What is even being tested?** Seth argues there is no uniform exam: "does everyone have the same tests in this life? Some people have unique disabilities... some are born with unfortunate circumstances that give them trauma." He adds that "a rigorous study of the concept of free will will prove that it doesn't even exist," removing the point of a test.[^3]
4. **Everything looks random, not designed-for-testing.** "If we have an unbiased look at the world we'll find that everything kind of looks random — people have random experiences, random diseases... all the different planets seem random, they're not fine-tuned for anything except being a void of nothing."[^4] This dovetails with his fine-tuning rebuttal in [[The Multiverse and Cosmic Selection Reply to Fine-Tuning]].

## Best Quotes
> "If God is a god of tests, what should the world look like? Let's make a prediction and test it... when we look into the world we find babies and children that are dying before they even get the test started." (Seth) [^2]

> "Are we testing heroism? Is there a universal test of heroism?... I think if we have an unbiased look at the world we'll find that everything kind of looks random." (Seth) [^4]

> "Don't get distracted by... the argument from evil — those are theological questions. This debate is not about the nature of God." (Hansen, declining to engage) [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the objection LDS theodicy is unusually well-positioned to answer: a *premortal existence* in which spirits consented to mortal conditions reframes apparent unfairness (short lives, unequal circumstances) as agreed-upon and educative rather than arbitrary, removing God's culpability without denying the reality of evil. See [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]] and [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]. Notably, Hansen does not deploy this in the debate, choosing to keep the argument ontological rather than theological.
