# Religion as Anachronistic Category

## The Claim
"Religion" is a modern category that did not exist as a discrete sociocultural dimension in the ancient world. The biblical authors had no word for "religion" and would not have understood their texts as "scripture for a religion." To speak of the Bible becoming a "religion" is to impose a category that developed between the Renaissance, Reformation, and Enlightenment onto ancient communities.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **No Hebrew Word for "Religion":** When asked "what is the Hebrew biblical Hebrew word for religion?" [[Dan McClellan|McClellan]] responds: "That's the thing — religion is something that developed between the Renaissance, the Reformation, and the Enlightenment … this is not a discrete sociocultural dimension that existed in the ancient world. So to speak of this becoming a religion is thoroughly [wrong]."[^2]

2. **Emic vs. Etic Categories:** "Emically" (how the communities describe themselves) there is no concept of religion in the biblical texts. The Hebrew terms *amim* (peoples), *goyim* (nations), and *mamlakah* (kingdom) are political/ethnic categories, not religious ones.[^3]

3. **The Wrong "What" Question:** Wright argues that by focusing on "what the Bible teaches" (the religious question), we miss what the biblical authors were actually doing: "connecting stories of rival communities, bringing a defeated traumatized community together around text, refocusing attention on the lives of average folk, re-envisioning gender roles, making wisdom available for the whole people not just for the elites."[^4]

4. **Global Appeal Without Religion:** Wright notes that students from Muslim countries (Iran, Iraq, Saudi Arabia) as well as China and India connected with the *how* and *why* questions — how you create a community around text — while finding "what the Bible teaches" uninteresting ("thou shalt not kill — we all agree on that, great, we don't need the Bible to tell us that").[^5]

## Best Quote

> "Emically there's nothing — there's no concept, there's no word for religion. But there's something greater at stake than that, and that is we miss that the making of the Bible and the 'why' question … should occupy our attention as societies, as communities, more than the 'what' question — what the Bible teaches."[^6]
> — [[Jacob Wright]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-Arguments
- Even if the *word* "religion" is modern, the *concept* of organized worship, priesthood, temple, and sacred texts clearly existed — these are the components of what we call religion.
- The Hebrew term *dat* (found in Esther and Daniel) can mean "law/decree/religion" and is used in later Jewish literature for religious practice — the concept may be emerging even if our modern category is imprecise.
