# Decreeing All Things Makes God the Author of Sin

## The Point
The hinge of the debate is whether God's decreeing "whatsoever comes to pass" (Westminster; Ephesians 1:11) makes God the *author of sin*. [[James White]] bites the bullet: God decrees all things, and when asked point-blank in cross-examination whether that includes rape, he answers "Yes," reasoning that an undecreed evil "is an element of meaningless evil that has no purpose." [^1] [[Jacob Hansen]] presses the Eden case as a reductio: God knowingly "put the tree there" intending Adam and Eve to eat, so "in what meaningful sense is the creature the ultimate origin of evil rather than God?" [^2] White answers with **primary vs. secondary causation** — God is the primary cause ordaining all things "toward the glorification of God," while creatures are the secondary causes who actually commit the sin and bear the guilt; his proof-text is Acts 4, where "Herod, Pontius Pilate, the Romans, and the Jews" did "what your hand and your purpose predestined to take place," calling the crucifixion "the greatest act of evil in all human history" yet predestined by God. [^3] Hansen counters with Jeremiah 19: God says of child sacrifice to Baal, "which I did not decree, nor did it come into my mind." [^4]

## Evidence
- **White affirms the strongest form of the doctrine without flinching.** On rape: "Yes... otherwise it's purposeless." [^1] On Eden: God put the tree there "because it was central to the accomplishment of his redemptive purposes," and could have created man without a fallen nature but "then he wouldn't have accomplished the redemption of mankind." [^5]
- **The primary/secondary causation distinction is White's load-bearing move.** "You need to understand the difference between primary and secondary causes... the primary cause is always focused toward the glorification of God." [^3] Compare the steelman in [[Meticulous Providence Makes God the Author of Evil]]: God authors the *plan*, not the *sin* (Gen 50:20, "ye thought evil... but God meant it unto good").
- **Hansen's "poisoned cookie" analogy and White's rejection.** Hansen: if I leave a poisoned cookie with my child knowing they'll eat it, am I innocent? White rejects it flatly — Adam "was made in the image likeness of God and was representing all of humanity," not "a little child in the room with the cookie," and dismisses the analogy as "foolish." [^6] The disanalogy White presses: Adam is a covenant head, not an innocent.
- **The Jeremiah 19 exchange.** Hansen: God says child sacrifice "did not come into my mind" — so God plainly does *not* decree all evils. [^4] White's reply: the phrase "all he's saying is I never commanded you to do it," not that it fell outside His decree. [^7] This is a genuine exegetical standoff over what "did not come into my mind" entails.

## Best Quotes
> "Did God decree all things? Yes, because if he didn't decree child rape, then that rape is an element of meaningless evil that has no purpose." — White (as quoted/affirmed) [^1]

> "In what meaningful sense is the creature the ultimate origin of evil rather than God if God was the one who put the tree in the garden in the first place with the intention that they ate of it?" — Hansen [^2]

> "Herod, Pontius Pilate, the Romans, and the Jews were gathered together... to do what your hand and your purpose predestined to take place. That, by the way, was the greatest act of evil in all human history." — White [^3]

> "They have built high places of Baal to burn their sons with fire... which I did not decree, nor did it come into my mind." — Hansen, quoting Jeremiah 19 [^4]

## LDS Connection
This is the doctrinal core that the Restoration answers directly, and the vault already catalogs the rebuttal under [[Meticulous Providence Makes God the Author of Evil]] (Blake Ostler's prep for this debate) and [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]]. The LDS move is to deny the premise that *every* event flows from an exhaustive divine decree: God works *with* eternally self-existent agents whose intelligences He did not create ([[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]), so genuine evil originates in the misused agency of creatures, not in God's causal will. White's own admission that an undecreed evil would be "meaningless" exposes the cost of his system — it purchases a tidy theodicy by making God the determiner of every rape and genocide, which Hansen argues is precisely "morally reprehensible." The Restoration also escapes the [[Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy|moral-quietism]] trap: if not everything is decreed for the best, fighting evil is not fighting God's secret will. In fairness to White, the primary/secondary distinction is the historic Reformed answer and is not obviously incoherent — and the LDS critic must reckon with Acts 4, which does say the crucifixion was "predestined to take place." The Restoration reading distinguishes God foreknowing-and-incorporating an evil freely chosen by others from God *decreeing it into being*, which is the very distinction Ostler labels "pre-ordination, not predestination."
