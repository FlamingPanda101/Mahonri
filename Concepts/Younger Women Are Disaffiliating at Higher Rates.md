# Younger Women Are Disaffiliating at Higher Rates

## The Claim
Retention is roughly flat across age cohorts for LDS men, but for women the youngest generation (Gen Z) appears to be disaffiliating at a notably higher rate than older women — a gendered generational divergence.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Men are retained evenly across ages (rated: moderate).** "Independent of age, men are being retained in the church at about the same rate" — younger and older generations stay at roughly equal rates, "maybe the older generations a little bit more, but there's not much of a difference."[^1]

2. **Younger women diverge (rated: moderate).** "For the women it is very different. For the youngest generations, the Gen Z, they seem to be disaffiliating at a much higher rate than the women of older ages."[^2]

3. **A genuine generational decline overall (rated: strong).** Separately from gender, younger people today stay less than younger people did in the 1980s: "younger people from previous generations were more likely to stay Latter-day Saint than younger people today are."[^3] *Steelman:* Dyer is careful that the *gender-by-generation* breakdown rests on thin cells — "there's just too few in these data sets to really be able to see that. That's why in our report, we don't break it out by gender." The women-specific claim is therefore tentative relative to the cross-age men finding.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Independent of age, men are being retained in the church at about the same rate... there's not much of a difference."[^1]

> "By gender, we really don't know — there's just too few in these data sets... That's why in our report, we don't break it out by gender."[^4]
