# Every Person Gets a Fair Opportunity Through Proxy Ordinances

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the LDS model is the only one that "actually resolves" the problem of the unevangelized "instead of just *hmm*-ing at it." The setup is the **thief on the cross** — the *sola fide* "trump card" (saved with no works, no baptism). Arnold's replies: the thief died *before* Christ did, and "a covenant takes effect only when the one who made it dies" (Hebrews 9:16-17), so the new-covenant ordinances of [[Acts 2.38|Acts 2:38]] and [[John 3.5|John 3:5]] "did not yet exist as binding covenant requirements"; and Jesus, being God, had "direct authority to make whatever promises he chose" (he forgave the paralytic, the woman caught in adultery, Zacchaeus) — "you don't build a systematic theology from exceptions to the rule."[^1] The constructive claim: God "has always had a plan for every category of person who has never had a fair shot" — children who die before accountability (covered automatically; infant baptism is "an abomination" precisely *because* grace already covers them, Moroni 8); the cognitively disabled ("God does not hold people accountable for covenants that they were never capable of making"); and "the billions who never heard the gospel," through **proxy baptism for the dead**, where the person "retains agency to accept or reject it in the spirit world."[^2] The biblical anchor is [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]] — Paul references baptism for the dead "as an established practice... He doesn't condemn it"; Arnold notes "Protestant scholars have produced over 200 different interpretations of this verse specifically to avoid what it plainly says."[^3] He contrasts this with the Catholic abandonment of **limbo** (2007) — "that's not a doctrine, that's simply a shrug" — and strict-Calvinist double predestination, arguing only the LDS plan is "completely coherent and directly cited in Paul."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The thief-before-the-covenant argument is structurally sound.** Hebrews 9:16-17 does tie a *diathēkē* (covenant/testament) to the death of the one who made it; the thief's pardon predates Pentecost's baptismal preaching, so using him to *abolish* new-covenant ordinances is, at minimum, contestable. *Rating: a real argument, though "the thief shows grace can save apart from ordinances" remains a serious Protestant point the LDS view answers via proxy work rather than denial.* [^1]
2. **1 Corinthians 15:29 is a genuine crux.** Paul's matter-of-fact appeal to baptism "for the dead" — without explanation or rebuke — is hard to square with a flat denial of the practice, and the vault documents an early-Christian rite behind it in [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]]. *Rating: strong proof-text for *a* vicarious practice; its exact nature is debated, and "200 interpretations" is a rhetorical figure for "many."* [^3]
3. **The fairness/theodicy payoff is distinctive.** A system that gives "every single person... a real opportunity" addresses the unevangelized more directly than limbo (abandoned 2007) or unconditional reprobation. *Rating: a strong comparative-coherence argument; it presupposes the LDS spirit-world and proxy framework, which a critic need not grant.* [^2]
4. **It keeps grace primary.** Baptism here is "the door" and "a handshake that seals the agreement," not a work that earns — consistent with [[LDS Salvation Is by Grace Not Earned by Works]] and [[Sola Fide vs Faith Plus Ordinances]]. *Rating: coherent within the grace frame.* [^2]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A critic replies: (a) **the thief still proves the point** — Jesus promised him paradise *that day* with no ordinance, showing grace through faith suffices, and the "covenant not yet in force" move proves too much (it would also void the thief's *need* for any future ordinance); (b) **1 Corinthians 15:29 is obscure on purpose** — Paul uses a third-person "they" ("what shall *they* do"), possibly describing a fringe Corinthian practice he neither endorses nor institutionalizes, and the many readings reflect real exegetical difficulty, not evasion; the *normative* NT pattern is baptism of living believers; (c) **proxy baptism for the dead is otherwise unattested as apostolic** and appears mainly among groups (Marcionites) the early church treated as heterodox; (d) a **Protestant inclusivism** (God justly judges the unevangelized by the light they had, Rom 2:14-16) can answer the fairness problem without postponed ordinances. A Latter-day Saint reply: the thief is an *exception under Christ's personal authority*, not a *system*, and proxy work is precisely how the Restoration extends the *same* grace to those who lacked access — without either damning the unevangelized or pretending ordinances do not matter; and an honest reading of 1 Cor 15:29 should at least concede Paul argues *from* the practice as something his readers accept, which fits an actual rite better than a fringe error he silently tolerates mid-argument for the resurrection. See also [[God Meets the Sincere Heart Outside His Jurisdiction]].

## Best Quotes
> "He has always had a plan for every category of person who has never had a fair shot. ... that's where proxy baptisms for the dead comes in. The ordinance is offered. The person retains agency to accept or reject it in the spirit world. So, everyone gets a real opportunity." [^2]

> "Protestant scholars have produced over 200 different interpretations of this verse specifically to avoid what it plainly says. ... the willingness to accept any interpretation except the obvious one tells you something about the conclusion that someone is trying to avoid." [^3]

> "The ordinance doesn't earn salvation. It's just a handshake that seals the agreement." [^2]
