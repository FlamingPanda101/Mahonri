# Nazarene Prophecy Has No Known Hebrew Bible Source

## The Claim

[[Matthew 2.23|Matthew 2:23]] says Jesus settled in Nazareth "that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the prophets, He shall be called a Nazarene." But no such prophecy exists in the Hebrew Bible. The two traditional apologetic explanations (nazirite connection and Isaiah 11:1's _netzer_/"branch") are strained and do not hold up to scrutiny.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. There is simply no such prophecy

McClellan states it plainly: "There's no part of the Hebrew Bible that says he would be called a Nazarene. So that's a reference to a prophecy that we have no knowledge of."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (textual). The Hebrew Bible can be comprehensively searched; no verse says the Messiah will be called a Nazarene.

### 2. The Nazirite connection is conceptually unrelated

"Nazarene" and "Nazirite" are "not even related conceptually" — a Nazirite is someone under a temporary vow (Numbers 6), not someone from Nazareth. Furthermore, "there's nothing in the Hebrew Bible that says the Messiah would be a Nazirite either." The only link is that the Greek transliterations are "very close together."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (conceptual). The categories are distinct in both Hebrew and Greek, and no messianic Nazirite prophecy exists.

### 3. The Isaiah 11:1 netzer connection is strained

The Hebrew word for "branch" in [[Isaiah 11.1|Isaiah 11:1]] is _netzer_ — which sounds somewhat like "Nazareth." But the connection requires collapsing two distinct Hebrew roots and assuming Matthew's author engaged in wordplay that no ancient reader could be expected to catch. McClellan: "It's still squinting at this pretty hard to try to make a connection there."[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (interpretive). The phonetic similarity is real but the connection requires motivated reasoning; _netzer_ (branch, from נצר) and the root of "Nazareth" (possibly נזר or נצר — debated) may not even share the same etymology.

### 4. Matthew may have invented or referenced a lost prophecy

"It might be that Matthew was like, 'We got to have a prophecy here. Come on, let's find something.'" Or "it could have been something that was in some text that we just don't have anymore." Either way, no verifiable source exists.[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (speculative). Both explanations are possible but neither provides evidence for the prophecy's authenticity.

## Best Quote

> "There's no part of the Hebrew Bible that says he would be called a Nazarene. So that's a reference to a prophecy that we have no knowledge of." — [[Dan McClellan]][^6]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- [[Tim Chaffy]] (Answers in Genesis) presents both the Nazirite and _netzer_ options as "plausible solutions" — but McClellan notes this standard ("plausible" = "that tiny little sliver of not impossible that apologetics is concerned to gin up") is far below what would count as evidence.[^7]
- See also [[Bethlehem Birth Is Likely an Invention to Fulfill Prophecy]] for the broader pattern of Matthew inventing or misapplying prophecies.
