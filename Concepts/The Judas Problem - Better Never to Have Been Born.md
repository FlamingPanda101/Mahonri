# The Judas Problem - Better Never to Have Been Born

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that Jesus himself acknowledged the creation dilemma when he said of Judas in [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]], "it had been good for that man if he had not been born." If existing-and-going-to-hell (becoming a son of perdition) is *better than not existing at all*, you can affirm the common free-will defense ("God loves you enough to give you free will, and existing is better than not"). But Jesus' statement directly contradicts that: there are outcomes so horrible that non-existence is preferable. Therefore even Christ "saw the tension here," and the creedal God who creates Judas anyway — when not creating was an option — faces the dilemma head-on.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **It turns a creedal prooftext into a creedal liability — rate: strong.** The free-will defender's premise is "existence + free will is a gift worth giving even if misused." [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] denies that premise for at least one person from the mouth of Jesus.[^1] Carroll uses it as a vise: either disagree with Jesus (existence is always better) or concede that creating the foreknown-damned can be a net harm — "if not being born is better than being born and going to hell, how do you square that?"[^2] **Steelman of the creedal theist:** the verse may be hyperbole / Semitic idiom of woe (cf. Job 3, Jeremiah 20) expressing the gravity of Judas's betrayal, not a literal metaphysical ranking of existence vs. non-existence. Many exegetes read it as a pronouncement of doom, not a considered claim that annihilation would have been objectively preferable. Carroll's reply: even read as hyperbolic woe, it presupposes that *some* fate is worse than never existing — which is all the argument needs.
2. **It connects foreknowledge to the upstream choice — rate: coherent.** Carroll notes he is "not saying he necessarily foreknew" Judas's fate as Creator, but that *even Christ* recognized the principle; this keeps the point about the *structure* of the dilemma rather than relying on contested claims about Jesus' omniscience.[^1] See [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]].
3. **Depends on the badness of hell — rate: load-bearing.** The "better not born" reading only stings if hell is genuinely worse than non-existence; the panel ties this to rejecting the "watered-down hell" solve and even granting the deprivation model while insisting hell remains worse than heaven.[^2] See [[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]], [[The Six-Child Analogy for the Creation Dilemma]].

## Best Quotes
> "How do you square that with Jesus in Matthew 26:24 when he said it would have been better for Judas if Judas was never born?... If existing and going to hell, so being a son of perdition, is better than not being born, you have to disagree with Jesus." [^1]

> "Even Jesus Christ saw the tension here... there are outcomes that are so horrible, hell we'll say, that it would have been better for the individual." [^1]

> "Why is God creating people knowing they're going to go to hell when he doesn't have to if... not being born is better than being born and going to hell. Like, how do you square that?" [^2]
