# Exodus as Post Hoc Memorial Narrative

## The Claim
McClellan argues that the pervasive "memorial" language in the hardening texts — the insistence that this is so "your children and your grandchildren" will "know that I am the Lord" — is internal evidence that the Exodus story was **composed centuries after the ostensible events, by later generations, for the purpose of communal memory.** "The entire story of the Exodus is crafted with its rhetorical utility as a memory in mind... which is indicative that the story has been written by later generations."[^1] He adds the historical claim that "the Exodus is not historical. There are no data that support the historicity of the Exodus. In fact, the data unilaterally indicate it did not happen."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Memorial framing saturates the text.** Repeatedly the stated goal is that future generations "will remember what happened, will know that I am the Lord." A story so oriented toward its own future commemoration is plausibly *crafted as* a commemoration.[^1] (Moderate-to-strong as a literary inference; it is suggestive rather than dispositive on its own.)
2. **Composed for the circulating community.** "The story was crafted post hoc... for the benefit of the people who are circulating it precisely as a memorial."[^3]
3. **Lack of historical data for the Exodus.** McClellan asserts the absence of supporting archaeological/textual data and positive indicators against a large-scale historical Exodus.[^2] (This is the mainstream minimalist/consensus position in much of academic archaeology; cf. [[William Dever]], [[Israel and Judah Never United]]. Note: stated here as McClellan's assertion in the clip, not independently footnoted to a study.)

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Conservative scholars argue (a) memorial framing is a normal feature of ANE foundation narratives and does not by itself prove late composition; covenant-renewal liturgy could preserve genuinely early events; (b) absence of evidence for a nomadic group in the Sinai is weak ("argument from silence"); and (c) a smaller, historical kernel could underlie a later literary elaboration. McClellan elsewhere grants that an [[Argument from Silence Is Not Always a Fallacy]] cuts both ways. The honest position: the *literary* argument here is suggestive, and the *historicity* claim rests on the broader archaeological consensus rather than on this clip.

## Best Quotes
> "Everything is oriented toward how this story is going to function among later generations, which is indicative that the story has been written by later generations." [^1]

> "This is a story that was written centuries after the ostensible fact. Now the story never actually happened. The Exodus is not historical. There are no data that support the historicity of the Exodus. In fact, the data unilaterally indicate it did not happen." [^2]

> "The story was crafted post hoc... for the benefit of the people who are circulating it precisely as a memorial, as a way for people to remember what happened." [^3]
