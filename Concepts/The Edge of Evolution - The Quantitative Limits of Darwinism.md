# The Edge of Evolution - The Quantitative Limits of Darwinism

## The Point
Even setting aside whole machines, [[Michael Behe]] argues that the *numbers* defeat Darwinism wherever more than one coordinated mutation is required. Natural selection can do "baby stuff" — break a gene, change a bear's coat color — but each *additional* needed mutation multiplies the odds against it exponentially, so the creative reach of mutation-plus-selection has a hard quantitative limit, an "edge."[^1] The deeper asymmetry: it is "a whole lot faster and easier to get rid of stuff" than to build it, so the Darwinian mechanism mostly subtracts information, while new species and new body plans require *adding* it — exactly what the mechanism cannot supply.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Chloroquine resistance.** To resist the anti-malarial drug chloroquine, it took "trillions of malarial cells" (*Plasmodium falciparum*) to acquire "two crummy mutations." Each further coordinated mutation costs "another factor of a trillion."[^3]
2. **Scale against the flagellum.** Compared to two mutations costing trillions of organisms, the flagellum needs ~30 proteins and ~12,000 amino acids of coordinated information — "truly… beyond Darwinian processes."[^3]
3. **Lenski's E. coli experiment.** In Richard Lenski's long-term bacterial cultures, one of the first beneficial mutations *deleted* the genes for the bacterial flagellum — improving growth by **dropping** information, not adding it.[^4]
4. **Breaking is easier than building.** Like stones nicking a car's paint, "random things can happen," and a broken gene can even drive a brown bear toward a white polar bear; but no such loss explains the "elegant machinery… at the foundation of life."[^5]
5. **New capabilities require new proteins.** Meyer reinforces it: "Adding new capabilities requires new proteins, which requires solving this combinatorial problem" — the needle-in-a-haystack search for rare functional sequences among vast "gibberish."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "It took trillions of malarial cells… to get two crummy mutations. Two — and we're talking about 12,000 for the flagellum… and for each extra mutation, it goes up exponentially." [^3]

> "It's oftentimes a whole lot faster and easier to get rid of stuff and improve a species' chance to survive and prosper than to build stuff." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The edge-of-evolution argument is a probabilistic natural-theology resource: it presses the limits of undirected mechanism without, by itself, naming the designer. That restraint matters for honest Latter-day Saint use — the argument leaves room for "a large degree of natural selection" (Behe grants antibiotic and pesticide resistance, antifreeze proteins) while denying selection can explain *everything*. It infers a generic intelligence, not the embodied, organizing God of the Restoration, and is fully compatible with an old earth and common descent.
