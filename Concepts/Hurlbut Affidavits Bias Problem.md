# Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics cite the "1834 affidavits" — statements from Smith-family neighbors describing laziness, drunkenness, and elaborate occult practices — as documentary proof of Joseph's bad character and folk-magic activity. The rebuttal: these are biased, commissioned, late statements that fail basic source-critical tests.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Commissioned to "get the dirt" (decisive bias).** [[Philastus Hurlbut]] was hired in 1833 by Eber D. Howe, a hostile freethinker near Kirtland, specifically to gather derogatory statements; "Hurlbut is hired to go get the dirt — that was his mission."[^2] For an academically trained historian, "one of the main things they're looking at is trying to evaluate bias," and here "we have a major problem."[^3] *Rating: strong — provenance fatally compromises neutrality.*

2. **Not formal affidavits.** Despite the popular "1834 affidavit" label, they are interview statements; the few notarizations only confirm the person's identity, not the truth of the contents (Christian, a former notary, confirms notarization "is not a statement of this is true").[^4] *Rating: strong — corrects a common misframing.*

3. **Leading the witness (method critique).** The statements read like guided testimony — "sounds like he's leading the witness ... that would be an objection in court."[^5] *Rating: moderate-to-strong.*

4. **Contradicted by counter-testimony and facts.** Lorenzo (or Benjamin/Orlando) Saunders, a Smith neighbor, said Hurlbut "wasn't able to get me to say the things that he got other people to say."[^6] The "lazy Smiths" charge is falsified by the documented hard work clearing their farm — property tax valuations rose as they improved the land.[^7] *Rating: strong — external corroboration cuts against the statements.*

5. **Steelman.** A critic can note that bias does not automatically make a statement *false*, and that hostile witnesses sometimes report true facts; the seer-stone and treasure-digging claims in these same statements turn out to be largely accurate. Mark agrees: "You can't just outright completely dismiss it as impossible ... but on its own, it's highly problematic" — the statements require corroboration, not wholesale acceptance *or* rejection.[^8]

## Best Quotes

> "Hurlbut is hired to go get the dirt. That was his mission."[^2]

> "If they show their ID, you can notarize anything. It's not a statement of 'this is true.' It's just this person is who they say they are."[^4]

> "Sounds like he's leading the witness a little bit. That would be an objection in court."[^5]
