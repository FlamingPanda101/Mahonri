# Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy in Canon Criteria

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
One escape from naked self-authentication is to offer *objective criteria* by which Scripture authenticates itself — e.g. apostolic authorship, or "does it preach Christ?" — such that exactly the canonical books pass [^1]. [[Trent Horn]] and [[Joe Heschmeyer]] reply that this is the **Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy**: you fix the target list first, then draw criteria around it [^2].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Devising criteria is itself non-self-authenticating — rate: strong
"Of course it isn't self-authenticating — you're coming up with the criteria." The moment a human selects which qualities count as marks of inspiration, the canon is being established by human judgment, not by the text speaking for itself [^1].

### 2. No criterion reverse-engineers the exact canon — rate: strong
- **Apostolicity** fails: Mark, Luke, and the anonymous author of **Hebrews** were not apostles, yet are canonical.
- **"Preaches Christ"** fails: [[3 John|3 John]] never explicitly preaches Christ, yet is canonical.
- "Any objective thing you'll never reverse engineer back into the canon we have now" [^1]. **Steelman:** a Protestant can combine criteria (apostolic *circle* + orthodoxy + reception) rather than any single test — but Heschmeyer's point holds that the combined net is gerrymandered after the fact to fit a predetermined list.

### 3. It is explicit post hoc rationalization — rate: strong
Horn names it: "you're throwing the darts and then painting the bullseye around them. That is the Texas sharpshooter fallacy." You start from "we want exactly these 66 books in" and ask "what rules do we need to create to make sure these books and no others make it through the door?" — inventing a route to a conclusion you already hold, not following a principled standard [^2].

> "You're trying to invent a route that would've gotten you to a predetermined conclusion... it's blatantly post hoc rationalization." [^3]
