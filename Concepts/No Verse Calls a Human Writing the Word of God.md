# No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] makes a sweeping lexical claim: in the roughly **50 occurrences** of "word of God" in the Bible (almost all in the NT), the phrase is *never* used of a particular human writing, even though it is freely used of particular human *speech*.[^1] This is the empirical backbone of the whole episode: it is what blocks the Protestant escape "Scripture says it is the word of God."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Old Testament: "word of God" = a message to a prophet — rate: strong
In the OT the phrase usually denotes a message God spoke to a prophet — Nathan ([[1 Chronicles 17.3|1 Chr 17:3]]), Shemaiah ([[1 Kings 12.22|1 Kgs 12:22]]) — and the same of John the Baptist ([[Luke 3.2|Luke 3:2]]).[^1] Strikingly, [[1 Samuel 9.27|1 Samuel 9:27]] has Samuel say he will "make known to you the word of God," then *anoint Saul with oil* — not read a text.[^2] The referent is a divine *communication event*, not a scroll.

### 2. The New Testament: "word of God" = apostolic preaching — rate: strong
In the NT the term usually denotes a message about God preached by an apostle or someone with apostolic authority.[^2] The decisive case is [[1 Thessalonians 2.13|1 Thessalonians 2:13]]: believers received "the word of God, which you heard from us… not as the word of men but… the word of God." Horn stresses there is **no parallel description of the written words of men being the word of God** — only spoken words.[^3] And 1 Thessalonians was likely the first NT book written, so the "word of God" preached there could not be a recitation of (a not-yet-existing) NT.[^4]

### 3. Other referents: God's salvation-history utterances and Christ himself — rate: strong
The phrase also denotes specific things God *spoke* in salvation history (e.g. the Ten Commandments recited to Moses) and, supremely, **Christ himself** — the Word made flesh ([[John 1.14|John 1:14]]).[^4] None of these is a human writing. See [[Word of God Means Prophecy Preaching or Christ Not Scripture]].

### 4. The strongest counter-data are reread, not ignored — rate: contested
**Steelman of the Protestant:** Mark 7, Matthew 15, John 10, Romans 3:2, and Hebrews 4:12 are routinely cited as Scripture being called "the word of God." Horn does not dismiss these; he argues each refers to oral commandment, prophecy, or Christ — see [[Mark 7 and Matthew 15 Concern Oral Commandments Not Scripture]], [[Romans 3.2 Says Oracles Logia Not Words Logos]], [[John 10.35 Cannot Be Broken Means Cannot Be Nullified]], and [[Hebrews 4.12 Word of God Is Christ or Gods Commands Not Scripture]]. He also reports that colleague [[Jimmy Akin]] independently and "at least tentatively" reached the same conclusion: Scripture never unambiguously calls human writings the word of God, though it unambiguously calls human *speech* the word of God.[^5] The honest weakness Horn concedes is that this is an argument partly from the *absence* of an unambiguous verse, which is strong only insofar as the surveyed counter-texts genuinely fail — the contested exegesis carries the weight.

## Best Quotes
> "There is no passage of scripture that says any human writings are the word of God, even though scripture calls other human realities the word of God."[^1]

> "In the Bible, the phrase word of God occurs about 50 times, almost always in the New Testament."[^1]

> "Word of God is never used in either the Old Testament or the New Testament in reference to a particular human writing, even though that term is used to describe particular human speech."[^3]

> "Scripture never unambiguously calls human writings the word of God, though it does unambiguously call human speech the word of God as we saw in 1 Thessalonians 2:13." — Jimmy Akin, as reported by Horn[^5]
