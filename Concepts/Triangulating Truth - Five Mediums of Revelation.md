# Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of Revelation

## The Point
To pre-empt the charge that Latter-day Saint epistemology is "feelings alone" (a "burning in the bosom" treated as infallible), Carroll lays out **five God-given mediums of communication**, all encompassed by the Holy Spirit. We "triangulate truth" by comparing them: the more independent witnesses overlap on a claim, the more confident we can be. [^1]

## Evidence
The five mediums: [^1]
1. **Senses** — the body God gave us to perceive and interpret the world, including His word.
2. **Reason** — which "can detect self-defeating ideas such as Sola Scriptura."
3. **Intuition** — which proclaims self-evident truths ("I don't need the Bible to tell me that physical child abuse of an infant is wrong").
4. **Moral outcomes** — the observable fruits of a person, group, or ideology (governments, ideologies — "by their fruits").
5. **Authority** — God can give authority to prophets and apostles to write scripture or declare His mind and will.

- **The Spirit is the binding ground.** "Everything we learn of and from God is grounded in the fact that His Spirit is the means by which we learn those truths." [^2]
- **Triangulation, not proof-by-feeling.** "As we use these sources... we are able to triangulate truth... The more often they overlap, the more sure we can be of the truthfulness of their claims." [^2] The reality of gaps and contradictions among witnesses is exactly why faith is still required. [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "We do not believe... that a simple 'burning in our bosom' makes something infallibly true in the actual world. That's not how this works. We reject that." [^1]

> "As we use these sources of communication from God, we are able to triangulate truth." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is a careful statement of Latter-day Saint spiritual epistemology that answers the "just feelings" caricature: spiritual confirmation operates with reason, senses, conscience, fruits, and authority, all under the Spirit — a convergent, fallibilist method rather than a single emotional trigger. Compare Blake Ostler's [[Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture]].
