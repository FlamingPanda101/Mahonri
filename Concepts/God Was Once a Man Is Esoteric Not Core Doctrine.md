# God Was Once a Man Is Esoteric Not Core Doctrine

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Critics charge that Mormonism's defining heresy is that "God the Father was once a man," treating it as a load-bearing dogma. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] does not deny the teaching but reframes it: it is *esoteric* doctrine — believed but largely unexplained — not a core salvific teaching.[^1] (For the underlying claim see [[God Was Once a Man]].)

## The Rebuttal

1. **The "what if he means Jesus?" deflection is rejected.** Griffiths honestly forecloses an easy out: some try to "wrangle it" by saying Joseph meant Jesus (whose incarnation all Christians accept), "but if you go back and look at the original sermon notes it's clear that he's saying God the Father was once a man — and that's pretty controversial to begin with."[^2] This concession strengthens his credibility; he does not soften the data.
2. **The couplet asymmetry.** The Gospel Topics essay "Becoming Like God" quotes [[Lorenzo Snow]]'s couplet ("As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may become") and then states "we know a lot more about the second line than we do about the first" — Griffiths reads this as the church gently labeling line two core and line one esoteric.[^3]
3. **The substance is "same species," not biography.** What Joseph is establishing, per Griffiths, is that "God and man are the same species, the same type of being" — the acorn-and-oak relation (see [[God and Man Are the Same Species]]). The specifics (God's planet, his parents, his childhood) are simply unknown.[^4]
4. **Steelman of the critic.** Calling a founding-prophet teaching "esoteric" can look like motivated minimization: it was preached at general conference, popularized in a memorized couplet, and taught by apostles for over a century. A critic may reasonably say the doctrine is too historically central to be quarantined as a footnote, and that the "we don't know much about it" move shifts ground without retracting the claim. Griffiths' answer is that *uncertainty about specifics* is genuine and that responsible teachers should name what they do not know rather than overspeculate.
5. **Evidence rating.** As a description of current LDS emphasis this is well-supported (Hinckley, the essay, ordinary Sunday curriculum). As a *defense* against the heresy charge it is partial: it lowers the temperature without conceding to classical theism's uncreated, immaterial God.

## Best Quotes

> "If you go back and look at the original sermon notes, it's clear that he's saying God the Father was once a man, and that's pretty controversial to begin with."[^5]

> "The idea that God was once a man is an esoteric teaching that we believe but we don't know very much about if we're being totally honest with ourselves."[^6]

> "It does seem like the substance of what Joseph Smith is saying is that God and man are the same species, they're the same type of being."[^7]
