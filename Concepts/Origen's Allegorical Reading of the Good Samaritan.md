# Origen's Allegorical Reading of the Good Samaritan

## The Point
In a homily delivered AD 233–234 (part of a series on Luke), [[Origen]] of Alexandria — "perhaps the greatest scriptural interpreter in the early Church" — preaches the parable of the good Samaritan (Luke 10:25–37). He begins with the plain ethical meaning, relays a traditional allegory from "one of the elders," then extends it with his own interpretive gifts, drawing out both a salvation-history reading and a renewed ethical application.

## Evidence
1. **Origen's stature.** "It was through [Origen] that exegesis and preaching were so firmly united that throughout the history of the ancient church and long afterwards they remained intertwined." Though his works were later condemned, "his influence reverberated throughout the early Christian world for centuries." [^1]
2. **The plain reading first.** Origen summarizes the parable and its context, clarifies the text, cites intertexts, and states its purpose: the beaten man was a neighbor only to the one "who willed to keep the commandments and prepare himself to be a neighbor to everyone who needs help." [^2]
3. **The traditional allegory (from "one of the elders").** "The man who was going down is Adam. Jerusalem is paradise, and Jericho is the world. The robbers are hostile powers. The priest is the Law, the Levite is the prophets, and the Samaritan is Christ. The wounds are disobedience, the beast is the Lord's body, the *pandochium* (stable/inn) … is the Church. … The two denarii mean the Father and the Son. … The Samaritan promises he will return represents the Savior's second coming." [^3]
4. **Origen's extension — the Samaritan came prepared.** Origen notices the Samaritan carried bandages, oil, and wine, not for one man only "but also on behalf of others who … had been wounded." This was "a deliberate act of kindness, rather than a random act." The wounded man becomes "all humankind … wounded by vices and sins, pains and sorrows." [^4]
5. **Renewed ethical call.** Origen applies it to disciples who would "go and do likewise" (Luke 10:37): "it is possible for us to imitate Christ and to pity those who 'have fallen among thieves.' We can go to them, bind their wounds, pour in oil and wine." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The man who was going down is Adam. Jerusalem is paradise, and Jericho is the world. … The Samaritan is Christ. … The two denarii mean the Father and the Son." (the elder's allegory, via Origen) [^3]

> "It is possible for us to imitate Christ and to pity those who 'have fallen among thieves.' … Go and do likewise." (Origen) [^5]

## LDS Connection
Heal footnotes a Latter-day Saint reading in the same tradition — John W. Welch, "The Good Samaritan: A Type and Shadow of the Plan of Salvation" (*BYU Studies* 38.2) — showing modern LDS scholarship consciously drawing on Origen's allegorical typology of the parable.
