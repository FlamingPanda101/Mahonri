# Simple Foreknowledge Makes God a Prisoner Not Sovereign

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Jacob Hansen]] (as [[James White]]) presses: if God knows the future perfectly, then future events are fixed; if people go to hell, God already knows it, so it is *predestined*. To deny this is to deny God's sovereignty, omniscience, and omnipotence. [^1] See [[Foreknowledge and Free Will Are Logically Incompatible]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Distinguish *simple foreknowledge* from *predestination*** — rate: a clarifying and decisive move. [[Blake Ostler]]: the objection as stated assumes "*simple foreknowledge*" — "the future simply there, and God is creating us the way it has to be." But then "God doesn't have any sovereignty at all." *(distinction)* [^2]
2. **What God foresees is logically prior to his seeing it** — rate: the heart of the argument. "He sees it because it's already there in some sense. God can't do anything about what he sees. He can't change it. God is a prisoner to what he sees... shackled to a future he can't work with or change or be sovereign over." So simple foreknowledge, far from securing sovereignty, *destroys* it. *(modal argument)* [^3]
3. **The Calvinist who adopts simple foreknowledge is inconsistent** — rate: an internal critique. "A number of [Calvinists] do [adopt simple foreknowledge], and it is inconsistent, because simple foreknowledge makes the reality God sees logically prior to what God sees. That's how he knows it." *(consistency)* [^4]
4. **The only consistent Calvinist alternative is worse** — rate: sets up [[Meticulous Providence Makes God the Author of Evil]]. If instead "everything causally follows from his decree," then God directly causes every evil — escaping the prisoner-problem only by making God the author of evil. *(dilemma)* [^5]

**Steelman of the foreknowledge defender:** Many classical theists deny the "prisoner" framing: on a *timeless* view God does not passively *foresee* an independently fixed future but eternally *knows-and-wills* the whole of history in one act, so there is no temporal gap in which he is "stuck." On Molinism, God's *middle knowledge* of counterfactuals lets him sovereignly *select which world to actualize* among feasible worlds — robust providence without causing sin. Both deny Ostler's premise that "what God sees is logically prior to his seeing." Ostler's reply (developed elsewhere) is that timelessness still faces the incompatibility argument and that Molinist counterfactuals lack grounding — so the "shackled God" result stands for any view that affirms a fixed future of genuinely free acts without divine causation.

## Best Quotes
> "If that's the case, God doesn't have any sovereignty at all. He foresees a reality, but God is stuck with what he foresees because it's logically prior to what he sees." [^3]

> "God is a prisoner to what he sees as already just given... that shackles God to a future he can't work with or change or be sovereign over." [^3]

> "Simple foreknowledge makes the reality that God sees logically prior to what God sees. That's how he knows it." [^4]
