# Hardened Heart Apologetic Shifts Blame to Pharaoh

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The apologetic move's real payload, McClellan argues, is *relocating moral responsibility*. By framing hardening as an automatic response of a clay-filled heart to God's unchanging glory, the apologist makes it "Pharaoh who is putting his own heart in a position to be hardened under God's glory."[^1] God remains the passive, immutable sun; Pharaoh is the active agent who chose the clay. This protects God from the charge of being a tyrant who manipulates a human "like a marionette in their own narrative."[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The text names God as the agent, not Pharaoh.** [[Exodus 4.21|Exodus 4:21]]: "I will harden his heart so that he will not let the people go." The grammar makes God the subject and cause; the apologetic must invert the agency the text assigns. (Strong — direct contradiction of the proof framework by its own proof text.)[^3]
2. **The text states God's goal would otherwise fail.** "The idea here is clearly that if God does not do this, Pharaoh will let the people go. God must do this in order to prevent the people from being released."[^3] Blame cannot rest on Pharaoh if, absent divine intervention, Pharaoh would have complied.
3. **Steelman of the blame-shift.** Compatibilist and Reformed readers (e.g., Romans 9:17-18, "he hardens whomever he wills") would say *both* are true: Pharaoh's prior self-hardening earns a judicial hardening by God, so blame is justly his while God's sovereignty stands. This is a coherent theological position. **But** it is not the wax/clay analogy, which denies any divine action at all; and it concedes McClellan's main point — that God really does the hardening. The dispute then becomes theodicy (is judicial hardening just?), not whether God overrides agency.
4. **Function over evidence.** McClellan's diagnosis: "all this does is shifts the blame from God to Pharaoh" — the analogy's purpose is exculpatory, not exegetical.[^4]
