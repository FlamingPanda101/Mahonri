# The Shadows Prove the Sunshine - Evil Backfires on Atheism

## The Point
The problem of evil is widely thought to be atheism's strongest argument, but Turek argues it actually boomerangs into an argument *for* God. To call anything **objectively** evil presupposes an objective standard of good; objective good in turn requires God. So if evil is real — and "we all know it is" — then God exists.[^1] Evil may show there is a devil loose in the world, but it cannot disprove God; it can only count against Him on the left side of the evidence ledger, not the right.[^2]

## Evidence
Turek frames the logic as a chain of entailments: there can be no objective evil unless there is objective good, and no objective good unless God exists; therefore the reality of evil establishes the reality of God.[^3] He illustrates with the image of shadows and sunshine — there can be sunshine without shadows, but no shadows without sunshine; likewise there can be good without evil, but no evil without good, and no objective good without God.[^4] He notes the dilemma this poses for the atheist who wants it both ways: in one breath denying that good, evil, and justice are real ("we just dance to the music of our DNA"), and in the next breath being morally outraged at evils done in religion's name. Either evil exists or it does not. If it does not, the complaint is empty; if it does, it points to God.[^5] In the book's summary chart of evidence for and against God, *every* line — the universe's beginning, fine-tuning, reason, information, life, mind, free will, objective morality — sits on the "Yes" side; only "Evil" sits on the "No" side, and even that, Turek argues, ultimately migrates back to the left.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "There can be no objective evil unless there is objective good, and there can be no objective good unless God exists. If evil is real—and we all know it is—then God exists." [^3]

> "The shadows prove the sunshine. There can be sunshine without shadows, but there can't be shadows without sunshine." [^4]

> "Good reason provides all the information we need to see that the very existence of evil is a contradiction for atheism. If evil is real, then atheism is false." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints can affirm Turek's central move wholeheartedly: objective evil presupposes objective good, and the existence of a real moral order testifies of God. The Restoration adds a distinctive and arguably stronger grounding for the *opposition* Turek's "shadows" image depends on. Lehi teaches that "it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things" — that without the bitter the sweet cannot be known, and that a world with no possibility of evil would be "a thing of naught," "no purpose in the end of its creation" (2 Nephi 2:11). Where Turek treats evil's contrast with good chiefly as an epistemic and metaphysical proof of God, the LDS framework treats genuine opposition as a *necessary condition of a meaningful, agency-bearing mortal probation* — built into the plan of salvation rather than merely tolerated. The two views complement: Turek shows evil cannot be wielded *against* God, while the Restoration explains *why a God who is not the author of evil* would permit a world in which real opposition exists.
