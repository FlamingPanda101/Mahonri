# After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do

## The Point
2 Nephi 25:23 — "it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do" — is the single verse most often cited to prove Latter-day Saints believe works earn salvation. Read in context, it teaches the opposite: **after / in spite of / regardless of** all our efforts, we are *still* saved by grace. The phrase marks the insufficiency of human effort, not a list of works that must be completed before grace activates. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **The grammatical/contextual reading.** Hayden Carroll glosses it: "that is — *regardless* of all we can do. Or, *in spite* of all we can do. Or, after all we can do, we are *still* saved by the grace of God." [^2]
2. **Same author, same book, clarifies it.** The very next chapter: "we talk of Christ... that our children may know to what source they may look for a remission of their sins" (2 Nephi 25:26); and "it is only in and through the grace of God that ye are saved" (2 Nephi 10:24). The Nephites "did not suppose that salvation came by the law" (Alma 25:15). [^1]
3. **A symmetry argument.** Reading 2 Nephi 25:23 (or Moroni 10:32, or Article of Faith 3) as "works-salvation" is the same fallacy as using James 2:24, Matthew 7:21, or Acts 2:38 to accuse Protestants of works-salvation — see [[The Prooftext Double Standard on Grace and Works]]. [^1]

## Best Quotes
> "we know that it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do." (2 Nephi 25:23) — read as: "in spite of all we can do, we are still saved by the grace of God." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This verse is a frequent flashpoint with Evangelical critics; the contextual reading dissolves the objection and aligns the Restoration squarely with salvation by grace ([[LDS Salvation Is by Grace Not Earned by Works]]). Some Latter-day Saints themselves misread it as "grace fills the gap after I've done my part," which the cited prophets (Oaks, McConkie, Uchtdorf) and these scriptures correct.
