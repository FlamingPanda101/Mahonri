# Trained Emotions Are the Liaison Between Reason and Appetite

## The Point
Lewis's load-bearing claim is that bare reason cannot, by itself, restrain appetite: "**Without the aid of trained emotions the intellect is powerless against the animal organism.**" [^1] The Chest is not a luxury but the indispensable transmission by which the Head can actually govern the Belly. Strip out trained sentiment and you are left not with a purely rational agent but with an intellect that capitulates the moment instinct presses hard. [^1]

## Evidence
Ward shows how Lewis dramatizes this thesis in his fiction. In *That Hideous Strength* the villains who take refuge in "the Head" (a literal decapitated head kept artificially alive) are torn apart by animals — appetite overrunning disembodied intellect. [^1] More subtly, in *Till We Have Faces* the Fox, an embodiment of rationalism, abandons his sceptical principles before a baying, famine-maddened mob, conceding that perhaps "some hands can heal." His "intellect is powerless against his instinct for self-preservation because he does not have the trained emotion of fortitude." [^2] The reason the chest matters, Ward (quoting Wesley Kort) explains, is that it "mediates a relationship between bodily drives and intellect, and the person becomes both more moral and more him- or herself as that relationship emerges and is secured." Virtues are the stabilized fruit of that relationship — "integrity and moral reliability." [^3] Lewis is not anti-emotion: emotions and sentiments are *alogical*, but "they can be reasonable or unreasonable as they conform to Reason or fail to conform. The heart never takes the place of the head: but it can, and should, obey it." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Without the aid of trained emotions the intellect is powerless against the animal organism." [^1]

> "The heart never takes the place of the head: but it can, and should, obey it." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration teaches that discipleship is not the suppression of the body's appetites by raw willpower but the *training* of desire itself — "deny yourselves of all ungodliness" (Moroni 10:32) accomplished as the natural man is "put off" through Christ and the affections are remade (Mosiah 3:19). Alma's counsel to "bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love" (Alma 38:12) is precisely Lewis's point: the passions are not to be severed but harnessed, the bridle being the cultivated, just sentiment of the Chest. A merely intellectual conviction of the gospel, ungoverned by trained affections, is the LDS equivalent of Lewis's helpless Head — knowing the good yet "powerless against the animal organism."
