# Discernment Comes From Prior Experience of God's Glory

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that Moses 1 teaches a method of spiritual discernment by **contrast with a prior, genuine experience of God**. When Satan appears as "an angel of light" ([[2 Corinthians 11.14|cf. 2 Corinthians 11:14]]) and demands "worship me," [[Moses]] is not deceived — and Moses *tells us why*: he had already stood in God's presence, transfigured by God's glory ([[Moses 1.1-2|Moses 1:2]]), and he simply does not experience that same holiness/light from the impostor ("I'm not experiencing the light that I experienced from God... You are a false god"). The generalizable principle: "if we fill our lives with experiences of the good... of God, then when we encounter evil, we are prepared to distinguish [it]." Discernment is the felt *difference* between the counterfeit and a previously known original, which is why **the order matters** — you must first know God, then you can detect what is not God.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Moses gives the reason in the text
Moses to Satan: "I couldn't stand in the presence of God unless His glory came upon me and transformed me." Having experienced that, "I'm not experiencing that with you." Bradley: "the key is the contrast... because he already had experiences with God." The transfiguration that opened the chapter ([[Moses 1.1-2|Moses 1:1-2]]) becomes the very calibration that defeats the temptation later in the chapter.[^1] *Rating: a faithful reading of the Moses-Satan exchange; the discernment principle is drawn directly from Moses' stated reasoning.*

### 2. The forewarning is the preface's purpose, not a scare
This sits inside the larger preface argument ([[Moses 1 Is the Lost Preface and Hidden Key to the Bible]]): the Lord's preface warns "there is a villain in your story" precisely so the reader can "have discernment" — "it's obviously not telling us this to scare us." Notably it is "a story of how he was able to discern the wrong... and overcome it," not a story where Satan wins. (Satan's own origin/pride is developed further in [[Moses 4|Moses 4]].)[^3] *Rating: a defensible characterization of the narrative's function within LDS theology of opposition.*

### 3. A practical corollary: immersion builds the baseline
Bradley applies it pastorally: "fill my life with those experiences of the good, the true, the spiritual, the divine — immersing myself in the scriptures, in the temple, in prayer, in serving other people" — "then I will be ready when temptation comes." Discernment is trained by accumulated exposure to the genuine.[^4] *Rating: a coherent spiritual-formation claim; experiential and not independently testable.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The contrast principle is psychologically plausible but **double-edged**, and a critic will press the symmetry problem the vault raises elsewhere (see [[Praying to Know Truth and the Discernment-of-Spirits Objection]]): if "I recognize God because it matches my prior experience of God" is valid, then someone with vivid prior experiences inside a *different* tradition will, by the same rule, "discern" *their* claimant as true and the LDS claimant as the counterfeit — the method validates whatever baseline one already has, so it cannot adjudicate between rival revelations. It also risks **circularity**: the "genuine" experience of God is itself identified by the same felt-quality that is supposed to do the discerning, with no independent check, which is exactly the worry behind "Satan as an angel of light" — a sufficiently good counterfeit *feels* right. And the textual case rests on [[Moses 1.1-2|Moses 1]], a Restoration passage an outsider does not grant as ancient. The fair statement: as *spiritual formation* the principle is sound and unobjectionable (habituation to the good aids recognition of the bad), but as an *epistemology of revelation* it is non-transferable — it explains how an insider stays oriented, not how a neutral inquirer could tell the true light from a convincing forgery.
