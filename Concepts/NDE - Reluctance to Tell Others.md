# NDE - Reluctance to Tell Others

## The Point
Although experiencers are utterly certain their NDE was real — emphatically *not* a dream, hallucination, or fantasy — they almost universally **keep silent** about it.[^1] Anticipating (or having met) ridicule, they tell at most one trusted relative, having learned that "people just kind of look at you like you're crazy."[^2] This reticence has apologetic weight: it tells against the charge that NDE reports are attention-seeking confabulations, and it means most experiencers feel utterly *alone*, convinced no one else has been where they have been — until they discover otherwise.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Certain it was real, not a hallucination.** "It was nothing like an hallucination. I have had hallucinations once, when I was given codeine in the hospital... this experience was nothing like the hallucinations, nothing like them at all."[^4] Moody notes his interviewees are "functional, well-balanced personalities" who relate the events "as real events which actually happened to them."[^5]
2. **Fear of being thought insane.** "I didn't tell anyone about it for a long, long time... I was afraid that nobody would think I was telling the truth."[^6]
3. **Rebuffed when they tried.** "I tried to tell my minister, but he told me I had been hallucinating, so I shut up."[^7] Nurses told one patient "not to talk about it, that I was just imagining things."[^8] A popular student found people would listen, then say behind her back, "She has really flipped out."[^9]
4. **No proselytizing.** "No one... has built himself a portable lectern and gone out to preach about his experience... No one has seen fit to proselytize."[^10] "You simply don't jump up on a little soapbox and go around telling everyone these things."[^11]
5. **A crushing sense of uniqueness — and relief.** "I have been somewhere nobody else has ever been," one man said.[^3] On learning others reported the same events, subjects expressed "profound feelings of relief": "Now I know I'm not crazy."[^12]

## Best Quotes
> "It was nothing like an hallucination... this experience was nothing like the hallucinations, nothing like them at all." [^4]

> "It is a very interesting thing to find out that other people have had the same experience... Now I know I'm not crazy." [^12]

## LDS Connection
The pattern of a profound, certain spiritual experience met by a skeptical world that brands the witness "crazy" is deeply familiar to Latter-day Saints, who read Joseph Smith's own words after the First Vision: men "treated my communication... with great contempt," yet "I had seen a vision; I knew it, and I knew that God knew it, and I could not deny it" (Joseph Smith—History 1:21–25). That sincere, sober people will endure ridicule rather than recant a sacred experience — and decline to "proselytize" or argue — fits the LDS understanding that genuine spiritual knowledge is personal, hard to convey, and not subject to public proof. It also illustrates why a hostile cultural environment, not the absence of the experience, can explain widespread silence about the afterlife. See [[NDE - Corroboration of Veridical Details]].
