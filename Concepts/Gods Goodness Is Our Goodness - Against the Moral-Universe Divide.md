# Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] calls this his "most scathing critique of Christianity gone awry": the doctrine that **"God's goodness is not our goodness,"** or, as he attributes to [[Augustine]], that **"his justice is not our justice."** Severing continuity between God's moral nature and ours, Givens argues, is "morally incomprehensible" and "intellectually indefensible," and — crucially — it is the move that made historical atrocity *sayable*: a "whole history of abuses became possible once we say there is no continuity between God's nature and our nature, between his moral universe and our moral universe." He frames it with [[Marcion|Marcion]]'s original question and Elie Wiesel's: "If God's moral universe isn't our moral universe, then what's the use of any of this?"[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **It licenses atrocity by redescription — rate: strong moral argument.** Givens: "Why are you burning him at the stake? Well, God's love looks... Why are you damning infants to hell? Well, we don't understand God's justice. We're doing it lovingly." If "good" can mean anything when applied to God, then crusades, inquisitions, and terrorizing "dissenting Christians" can all be re-narrated as love. See [[Hell for Gods Glory Is Narcissism Not Love]].[^2]
2. **Backed by a non-LDS historian — rate: strong as cited authority.** Givens quotes [[Tom Holland|Tom Holland]]: "moral chaos results when love is cut loose from its theological foundations." The point: you cannot "just do anything and say, well, that's just a different kind of love."[^2]
3. **Wiesel's Holocaust formulation — rate: rhetorically powerful.** "The most powerful counter... comes from Elie Wiesel in response to the Holocaust, where a character in one of his plays says: if God's moral universe isn't our moral universe, then what's the use of any of this?" For Givens "the fact that that question can even be in dispute is the most severe evidence of a going off the rails of Christianity."[^1]
4. **It is the deep root, not a side issue — rate: structural claim.** Givens dates the dominance of "the incomprehensibility, the hiddenness of God's justice" as "the dominant strain until the 17th century," making it not a marginal excess but the mainline. He treats restoring moral continuity (God is good in the sense we mean) as central to the Restoration's recovery of a God "worthy... of our adoration."[^3]

**Steelman of the opposing (classical / Thomist) side:** No serious classical theist says God's goodness is the *opposite* of ours; the claim is that "good" is predicated of God **analogically**, not univocally — God *is* Goodness itself, the standard human goodness participates in, so human goodness is the *image*, not the measure, of divine goodness (see [[Analogical Predication - Talking About a God Beyond Comprehension]]). Creaturely minds cannot fully comprehend infinite goodness any more than they can comprehend infinite being; "his justice is not our justice" can mean *transcends*, not *contradicts*. And appeals to mystery about *particular* providences (why this child suffers) need not collapse the *concept* of goodness. The atrocities Givens names, the classical theist replies, were violations of the church's own moral theology (which always affirmed the natural law binding on God's image-bearers), not entailments of analogical predication. The Euthyphro third-horn reply — God's nature *is* the good — is the systematic version Givens does not engage here.

**Counter-objection (Givens's / the LDS rejoinder):** Analogical predication is exactly the slide that "empties the word *good* of meaning" — if the analogy stretches far enough to permit damning infants "lovingly," then "good" no longer does any work and "mystery" is doing all of it (the same dynamic the vault documents in [[The Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good of Meaning]] and [[Good Becomes Synonymous with Whatever God Does]]). Givens demands a *univocal core*: whatever else divine goodness is, it cannot include acts we would unhesitatingly call monstrous in a human. The LDS payoff is a God genuinely bound by an [[Independent Justice Refutes Whatever God Does Is Good|independent moral order]], whose goodness is therefore trustworthy because it is recognizably *good*.
