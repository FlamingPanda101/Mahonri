# Evolution Points to Family as the Locus of Meaning

## The Point

If evolution is not purely random, then the things it most powerfully shaped are clues to our purpose — and what it shaped most powerfully, [[Samuel T. Wilkinson]] argues, is the family. The extreme helplessness of human infants drove the evolution of profoundly deep parental love (attachment), and that parent-child bond was biologically "exapted" to make long-term romantic pair-bonding possible. The deepest human satisfactions reliably come through family relationships. Wilkinson reads this as nature *implying* the centrality of family: "evolution is very pro-family," and the same logic scales up to the "good society." For him this resonates so strongly that "scientifically you can show that we're designed for" the LDS *Family Proclamation*.

## Evidence

- **Helpless offspring built deep love.** Human infants sit at the extreme end of immaturity at birth, so parents "over evolutionary time... have developed this incredibly deep sense of love and loyalty and care" — the root of attachment theory. "The deepest form of love... in the flesh comes from the way that nature has shaped the relationship between parent and child." [^1]
- **Parental love exapted for monogamy.** The same machinery was "exapted" for pair-bonding: oxytocin, which binds mother to child "in pretty much every mammal," was "relatively recently in evolutionary terms... adapted to also allow for long-term pair bonding" in the few monogamous species, including humans. [^2]
- **"You were created to love your children."** To the cynic who says "I love my kids because they have my genes," Wilkinson replies: "that is how you were created — you were created to love your children, that is in your DNA." On a non-random reading, this is design, not accident. [^3]
- **Family scales to society.** The strongest pro-social dispositions "have their origins in the flesh in the way that nature shaped our relationships," strongest "with our kin"; linking men to their biological children (culturally, through marriage) makes them "behave in ways that are better for themselves and better for society." [^4]
- **The Dorothy Day quote.** Wilkinson reads Day's description of the love at her first child's birth — "no human creature could receive or contain so vast a flood of love and joy" — as a witness that family love opens "another chamber in your heart that you didn't know existed." [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "From one frame of reference, evolution is very pro-family... that infant that is so helpless needs the care and concern of a parent, and... human parents have developed this incredibly deep sense of love and loyalty and care and concern for their children. This is written into the way that evolution works." [^1]

> "Some people might take a cynical view of this and say, oh, are you just saying I love my kids because they have my genes? And I would say, well, that is how you were created — you were created to love your children, that is in your DNA." [^3]

## LDS Connection

This is the most explicitly Latter-day Saint payoff of the interview: Wilkinson recounts seeing a framed copy of *The Family: A Proclamation to the World* and feeling "an intense confirmation" that "scientifically you can show that we're designed for that Proclamation." [^6] The claim that family is the locus of meaning aligns with the LDS conviction that exalted, sealed family relationships are the very substance of eternal life, and that the dissolution of the divine family was central to the apostasy (see [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]]) while its restoration — and the gathering of believers into "the household of God" (see [[The Church as the New Family and Household of God]]) — is central to the gospel. Nature, on this reading, points beyond itself to the eternal family as humanity's ultimate purpose.
