# Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine

#type/claim

## The Claim
Prophets are not divine or perfect — only Jesus Christ was. Therefore the fact that [[Joseph Smith]] held era-bound cultural beliefs and used culturally embedded tools (seer stones, divining rods) is no objection to his prophetic calling; God works through fallible men.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Only Christ was perfect (premise).** "There's only been one person on earth who's lived who was perfect and divine, and that was Jesus Christ"; prophets are not divine.[^2] *Rating: strong (as LDS/Christian doctrine).*

2. **Scriptural demonstration.** "If you want to know if prophets are divine or not, just read the Old Testament" — and Peter in the New Testament: "Peter messed up, and we're okay with that. We're okay with fallible men being used as tools for God."[^3] *Rating: strong — direct scriptural appeal.*

3. **Distinguish core from culture (links to method).** This pairs with [[Seer Stones and Divining Rods as Revelatory Means]]: Latter-day Saints must own the revelatory *core* (God revealing through means) without owning every *cultural* belief attached to it, precisely because the prophet is a culturally situated, fallible human.[^4] *Rating: strong.*

4. **Steelman.** A critic argues this is unfalsifiable insulation: any failed prophecy or false belief is excused as "fallibility," so the prophet can never be tested. The believer's reply is that fallibility is itself a *scriptural* expectation (the Old Testament prophets, Peter), not an ad hoc patch; the relevant question is the genuineness of the revelatory gift, not the prophet's cultural inerrancy. The concept does not claim Joseph was never wrong — it claims being wrong about cultural matters does not disqualify a prophet.

## Best Quotes

> "There's only been one person on earth who's lived who was perfect and divine, and that was Jesus Christ."[^2]

> "Peter messed up ... We're okay with fallible men being used as tools for God."[^3]
