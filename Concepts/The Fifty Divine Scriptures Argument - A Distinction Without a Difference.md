# The Fifty Divine Scriptures Argument — A Distinction Without a Difference

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] dismantles two of Wes Huff's defenses of the great codices (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, Alexandrinus, Washingtonianus) — the claim that they "*contained* scripture" rather than "*were* scripture," and the claim that "inclusion in a codex ≠ scriptural reception." McClellan's reply to the first is that in the 4th century this is **a distinction without a difference**: [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] reports [[Constantine]] commissioning 50 copies of "the divine scriptures," while [[Athanasius]] describes sending volumes "containing the scriptures" to Constantine — the *same commissioned volumes*, described in both idioms.[^1] His reply to the second is that bare codex-inclusion is indeed not determinative, but the *rest of the data* show several "non-canonical" texts really were treated as scripture by early Christians.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Were scripture" vs "contained scripture" is a modern back-projection — rate: strong
McClellan concedes the modern distinction is real *for us*, who already possess a canonical/non-canonical boundary — but it "wouldn't really have worked in the 4th century CE," demonstrated by the fact that "the two different things that Wes is saying are not the same in this clip are references to the exact same texts of scripture that were produced for Constantine."[^3] He explicitly brackets the separate question of whether Sinaiticus/Vaticanus descend from those 50 volumes ("that's not the point"); the point is that *Eusebius* and *Athanasius* apply both phrasings to one referent.[^1] This is the canon-specific case of his anti-anachronism method (see [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]]).

### 2. Codex-inclusion alone is not determinative — and McClellan grants it — rate: fair concession
Unlike a maximalist, McClellan concedes Huff's narrow point: "It is true that inclusion in a codex in and of itself is not determinative of the canonical status of a text."[^2] The great codices "operated as many ancient libraries" and hold Christian books no one canonizes today. His objection is not the principle but the *use* Huff makes of it: Huff "collapses a lot of chronological distance" and lumps disparate texts together "in order to dismiss them all" without case-by-case analysis.[^2]

### 3. The other data show specific books *were* received as scripture — rate: strong, graded
This is the substantive core. McClellan insists the texts be weighed individually, and the evidence is graded, not uniform:
- **[[Shepherd of Hermas]]** — the strongest case. "Copied more than any other text of the Old or New Testament except for Psalms, Matthew, and John," and [[Tertullian]] quite explicitly refers to it as scripture.[^4]
- **Epistle of Barnabas** — "other early Christians referring to [it] as scripture."[^5]
- **1 Clement** — "there's a case to make that a lot of early Christians understood First Clement as inspired scripture."[^6]
- **2 Clement** — weaker: "there's not as good a case to make for Second Clement" (cf. [[2 Clement Is the Earliest Surviving Christian Sermon]]).[^6]
- **4 Maccabees** — ambiguous: "some places where it could be interpreted as being referred to as scripture," but no strong case.[^7]

The conclusion: "it's a lot messier than Wes makes it sound,"[^8] and grouping the texts together to dismiss them en masse ignores that "the data indicate the situation was a lot more complex."[^9] **Steelman of Huff:** Huff's challenge — "unless you're willing to argue that books like 4 Maccabees, Hermas, Barnabas, and 1–2 Clement should be in *your* Bible" — has rhetorical force because no modern church canonizes them, suggesting the early "scripture" citations were loose. McClellan's answer: that is "a distinction without a difference... it's leveraging a Protestant's desire to appear orthodox," and a book's *modern* exclusion "[has no] bearing on whether or not early Christians considered any or all of these texts to be scripture."[^10] This is the same defect the two-tier model exposes from the Protestant side ([[The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha]]): you cannot read modern canonical status back onto ancient reception. Where McClellan and Ortlund differ: Ortlund preserves a permanent edifying *tier two*; McClellan declines to tidy the messiness into any fixed tier, treating "scripture" as a contested, graded status full stop.

## LDS Relevance
The argument removes a favorite Protestant firewall — "those books were only ever *bound with* scripture, never *received as* scripture." If first-century-through-4th-century Christians demonstrably *did* receive books outside the modern 66 as scripture (Hermas, Barnabas, 1 Clement, 1 Enoch via [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude|Jude]]), then the boundary of "scripture" was historically larger and more fluid than the closed Protestant canon — congenial to the LDS open-canon claim that valuable scripture extends beyond it (compare [[Gavin Ortlund - Did Protestants Remove Books From the Bible]]). The caution mirrors the two-tier note: the LDS tradition does not want a *permanent* second tier either — living-prophet revelation can promote or demote — so the takeaway is the shared insight that ancient canonicity was graded and contested, not the adoption of a fixed tier system.
