# Passibility - Mainstream Theology Now Affirms a God Who Suffers

## The Point
Passibility is "the concept of being affected — that God can be affected" by what is outside Him, that He "can hear your prayer and feel an emotion about it," as opposed to being only "the unmoved mover."[^1] [[Brian Thomassen]] argues this is one of the clearest cases of mainstream Christianity converging on a Restoration position. [[Joseph Smith]]'s scriptures present an emotionally moved God: in Moses 7 "the God of heaven looked upon the residue of the people, and he wept," and Alma 7:12 has Christ taking on infirmities "that his bowels may be filled with mercy."[^2] The creedal "logic of perfection" had to reject this — "if something is perfect... it cannot be affected by that which is inferior to it"[^3] — yet from the early 20th century forward, theologians across the mainstream began affirming a God who genuinely suffers.

## Evidence
- **The 19th-century divergence.** Thomassen samples the hostile reaction across the century: Leroy Sunderland's *Mormonism Exposed* called the doctrine "monstrous and absurd" (1838); *The Living Age* (1854) called the claim "that God does possess passions... not only retrogressive but profane"; Robert B. Neil (1898) reduced it to "Mormonism tells you that a man is our God."[^4]
- **The 1912 turn.** The convergence quote (Baron von Hügel's circle) reads: "Theology has no falser idea than that of the impassibility of God... A God who cannot suffer is poorer than any man, but the one who cannot suffer cannot love either." Thomassen dates the shift here: "that's when we start to see this on a theological level that people are challenging this passibility idea."[^5]
- **Now common — and self-contradictory for the creeds.** A passible God "is not uncommon in the theological space within larger Christianity," which, the hosts note, "directly contradicts their creeds of God having no body parts or passions."[^6] The "without... passions" formula traces specifically to the Westminster Confession and is "not universally held" — "primarily Presbyterians."[^7] See [[Without Body Parts or Passions Is a 1530 Protestant Creed]].
- **The internal tension the move exposes.** The hosts press the obvious biblical point: Jesus is "repeatedly described as having his bowels moved by compassion," which classical theology can only reconcile through the two-natures split of Chalcedon (451) — a being who feels emotion fused with a divinity that "obviously cannot."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Passibility is the concept of being affected — that God can be affected... who can hear your prayer and feel an emotion about it, as opposed to being only the unmoved mover." [^1]

> "Theology has no falser idea than that of the impassibility of God... A God who cannot suffer is poorer than any man, but the one who cannot suffer cannot love either." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology takes the biblical God's grief, anger, and compassion as literal and central: the Father is a God "of body, parts, **and passions**" (D&C 130:22) — the explicit negation of the creedal "without... passions." This note is the convergence-side companion to the natural-theology entry [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]], which represents the classical case at full strength (an impassible God who is nonetheless supremely alive and *is* love). Thomassen's contribution is historical rather than philosophical: he shows that the mainstream has been quietly migrating onto the LDS side of this exact divide for over a century. The deeper LDS objection — that genuine love and relationship require the capacity to be moved — is developed in [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]]. The standing counter-argument is that affirming divine *pathos* does not entail Joseph's further claims (embodiment, an exalted-man God); a theologian can grant a suffering God while rejecting the rest of the package.
