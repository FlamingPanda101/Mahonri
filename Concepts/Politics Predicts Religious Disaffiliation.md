# Politics Predicts Religious Disaffiliation

#type/claim

## The Claim
One of the strongest predictors of leaving a faith — including the LDS Church — is political identity. When political identity is *forefronted* above religious identity and becomes the lens through which one views one's religion, an eventual clash between politics and faith resolves in favor of politics.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Burge's general finding (Evidence: strong, cited scholar).** [[Justin Dyer]] cites [[Ryan Burge]] — "one of the most important scholars in this area" — that "one of the best indicators of ... disconnecting from faith is your politics," especially at the extremes, "either extremely conservative or extremely ... liberal."[^1]

2. **Identity displacement mechanism (Evidence: clear causal model).** "When we start to tie our identities to our politics ... people's politics have become more important to their identity than their religion. And ... at some point, your politics and your religion are probably going to clash." When that happens, "you're going to follow your politics rather than your religion."[^2]

3. **Asymmetric but U-shaped pattern (Evidence: moderate, LDS-specific).** Left-leaning members are more likely to leave than right-leaning ones, **but the far right is more likely to leave than the moderate right** — implicating *extremity* on either pole, not merely one direction.[^3]

4. **Confirmed as a top youth predictor (Evidence: longitudinal).** In Dyer's panel tracking 16-year-olds into their early 20s, "feeling like their politics didn't match up ... with the church's views ... was a really big predictor" of de-identifying.[^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Correlation is not causation — politics may be a *proxy* for prior loss of belief rather than its cause; people whose convictions already shifted may adopt extreme politics and leave for independent reasons. The framing can also function to delegitimize sincere moral or doctrinal objections by recoding them as "you just made politics your idol," sidestepping whether the member's political critique of the institution was correct. Self-reported "politics didn't match" may itself encode genuine ethical disagreement.

## Best Quotes

> "One of the best indicators of ... disconnecting from faith is your politics ... particularly when you get extreme."[^1]

> "If you've ... forefronted your political identity, and that's the lens through which you see your religion ... you're going to follow your politics rather than your religion."[^2]

> "Those on the far right are actually more likely to leave than those who are say moderate right."[^3]
