# NDE - The Life Review

## The Point
Often immediately after the being of light poses its question, the dying person undergoes a **panoramic life review**: an extraordinarily vivid, instantaneous replay of the events of his life.[^1] Subjects describe it as three-dimensional, in color, moving, and absolutely accurate — "just like you walked outside and saw them"[^2] — yet compressed into seconds. The review is not random; when the being directs it, it deliberately highlights certain scenes, "stressing the importance of love" and of the pursuit of **knowledge**, and presenting failures not for condemnation but as occasions of learning.[^3] Crucially, this is **not** the cartoon Judgment of reward and punishment: the emphasis is self-recognition and growth, and subjects emerge wishing they had loved more, not terrified of a sentence.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **Total, vivid recall — in order.** A young woman's review began with childhood by the creek and ran chronologically through kindergarten, Girl Scouts, grammar school, junior high honors, graduation, and college — "the things that flashed back came in the order of my life, and they were so vivid... completely three-dimensional, and in color. And they moved."[^2]
2. **A third-person vantage.** She watched her childhood self "like the little girl I saw was somebody else, in a movie... Yet, it was me."[^2]
3. **The being already knows; the point is *your* recall.** "It's not like he was trying to see what I had done—he knew already—but he was picking out these certain flashbacks of my life and putting them in front of me so that I would have to recall them."[^3]
4. **Love and knowledge are the lessons.** "All through this, he kept stressing the importance of love"; he also "kept on pointing out things that had to do with learning, and... said that I was going to continue learning... even when he comes back for me... there will always be a quest for knowledge."[^3]
5. **Instantaneous yet complete.** A truck driver in a crash saw his whole life flash "in less than a second... It probably didn't last but a split second," though recounting it would take fifteen minutes.[^5] A dentist saw it "like looking through a volume of my entire life and being able to do it within seconds... like a motion picture that goes tremendously fast, yet I was fully able to see it, and able to comprehend it."[^6]
6. **Regret, not damnation.** "I thought about my mother, about things that I had done wrong... I wished that I hadn't done these things, and I wished I could go back and undo them."[^7] Yet others passed through "with no regrets, no derogatory feelings about myself at all."[^8]
7. **Can occur without the being.** Reviews are reported even when no being of light appears, though when the being "directs" it the review is "a more overwhelming experience."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "The things that flashed back came in the order of my life, and they were so vivid. The scenes were just like you walked outside and saw them, completely three-dimensional, and in color. And they moved." [^2]

> "It's not like he was trying to see what I had done—he knew already—but he was picking out these certain flashbacks of my life and putting them in front of me so that I would have to recall them. All through this, he kept stressing the importance of love." [^3]

> "It was so rapid it was like looking through a volume of my entire life and being able to do it within seconds." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The instantaneous, total replay of one's life is a vivid analogue to the Book of Mormon's repeated promise of a **"perfect remembrance"** at the judgment. Alma teaches that after death "our words will condemn us... and our thoughts will also condemn us... we shall not dare to look up to our God... if our minds are pure and white" — and elsewhere that the wicked "shall have a bright recollection of all their guilt" ([[Alma 11.43|Alma 11:43]]; cf. [[Alma 5.18]]). Nephi likewise foresees that "we shall have a perfect knowledge of all our guilt, and our uncleanness, and our nakedness; and the righteous shall have a perfect knowledge of their enjoyment" ([[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9:14]]). The NDE life review even mirrors Alma's principle of **restoration** — that what we send out returns to us, "good for that which is good... merciful unto that which is merciful" ([[Alma 41.3-7|Alma 41:3–7]]) — for the survivors do not face an arbitrary verdict but *recognize themselves*, judged by their own loving or selfish acts. The continual stress on love and on a "quest for knowledge" that "goes on after death" resonates strongly with the Restoration teaching that "whatever principle of intelligence we attain unto in this life, it will rise with us in the resurrection" ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:18–19]]).
