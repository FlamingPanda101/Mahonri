# Book of Enoch — Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation

## The Claim

The discovery of 1 Enoch fragments among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran — 11 separate fragmentary manuscripts — confirms that 1 Enoch was one of the most important and widely copied texts in Second Temple Judaism, arguably treated with scriptural-level authority by the Qumran community.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Scale of Qumran Attestation
> "We have 11 different manuscripts — fragmentary manuscripts, just shards and fragments, but different manuscripts — that were found at Qumran. Meaning, of all the books of the Bible, the only books that were better represented than the Book of Enoch... would be like Genesis, Deuteronomy, Isaiah, and Psalms." [^1]

This is a staggering statistic. 1 Enoch at Qumran was MORE frequently copied than:
- Exodus
- Leviticus
- Numbers
- All the prophetic books except Isaiah
- All the wisdom literature

This level of attestation indicates that the Qumran community treated 1 Enoch as functionally equivalent to scripture in importance, even if they may not have used the later rabbinic category of "canonical."

Dan Beecher: "Wow — so Isaiah or First Enoch is big time." [^1]

**Evidence rating: primary** — the manuscript count is documented in the DJD (Discoveries in the Judean Desert) series

### 2. The Aramaic Fragments Confirm Original Language
> "Originally written in Aramaic and then later translated into Greek... the first Aramaic fragments that were discovered among the Dead Sea Scrolls... were published [in] a 1976 volume edited by Joseph Milik." [^2]

Before Qumran, scholars hypothesized an Aramaic original based on linguistic features in the Greek and Ethiopic translations. The Qumran fragments — all in Aramaic — confirmed this hypothesis. [[Joseph Milik]]'s 1976 DJD volume remains the critical edition of these fragments.

**Evidence rating: primary** — material evidence

### 3. Multiple Recensions at Qumran
The 11 Qumran manuscripts do not all represent the same textual tradition. They show variation, confirming that 1 Enoch circulated in multiple recensions simultaneously during the Second Temple period. This matches McClellan's description of the text "accreting more and more chapters" while "still in circulation." [^3]

### 4. The Calendar Connection
The Qumran community's adoption of the 364-day solar calendar — described in [[Book of Enoch - 364 Day Solar Calendar|1 Enoch's Book of Luminaries]] — may explain the text's high status. The calendar dispute with the Jerusalem priesthood was a major factor in the Qumran community's separation, and 1 Enoch provided authoritative support for their calendrical practice.

### 5. Scope of the Fragments
McClellan cautions about the fragmentary nature: "The stuff we found at Qumran makes up, you know, 1% — if that — of the entire 108 chapters." [^4] The Qumran fragments are invaluable for confirming the text's antiquity and original language, but they do not provide a complete text. The medieval Ethiopic manuscripts remain our primary witnesses.
