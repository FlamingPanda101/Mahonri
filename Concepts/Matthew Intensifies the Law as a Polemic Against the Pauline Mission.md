# Matthew Intensifies the Law as a Polemic Against the Pauline Mission

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that [[Matthew 5.17-20|Matthew 5:17-20]] is best understood not as a record of a historical Q&A but as the **author of Matthew (~80-90 CE) pushing back against the law-free [[Paul the Apostle|Pauline]] mission to the Gentiles** (Paul active ~50s-60s CE). On this reading, Matthew represents the *Torah-intensifying* end of a first-century spectrum of opinion about Gentile obligation. When Jesus warns that "whoever breaks one of these least commandments and *teaches others to do the same* will be called least in the kingdom of heaven," the unnamed target is precisely the kind of teacher Paul was. Matthew is one "volley" in an intra-Christian argument, not a neutral report.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The driving issue is social (Gentile inclusion), not abstract exegesis (rating: moderate-strong; historical)
McClellan locates the dispute in the practical crisis of admitting Gentiles: by Matthew's day the gospel is being preached among Gentiles who balk at circumcision and kosher law. The question "do we still keep all the law?" is forced by *who is joining the movement*, which was already "Paul's big challenge decades before these texts were written." The first-century church ran the full spectrum from "keep every law" to "keep none." See [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]]. [^1]

### 2. Relative dating makes Matthew a *response* to Paulinism (rating: moderate; standard critical chronology)
Paul wrote in the 50s-early 60s CE; Matthew is usually dated ~80-90 CE. By then Paul's followers were numerous, so Matthew "is probably pushing back from the other end of the spectrum" — having Jesus declare that anyone teaching others to relax the law is "least in the kingdom." McClellan reads this as a reasonable (if not provable) reference to the Pauline party. [^2]

### 3. Matthew uniquely preserves pro-Torah material (rating: strong; redaction-critical)
Matthew alone has Jesus *endorse continued tithing* — "you tithe mint and dill... but do not neglect the weightier matters... these you ought to have done, **without leaving the other undone**" (Matt 23:23). Nowhere in Matthew does Jesus straightforwardly *abolish* a Mosaic law; the "you have heard... but I say unto you" antitheses ([[Matthew 5.43-45]]) **reinterpret and intensify** ("not even angry," "not even lustful"), never repeal. The other gospels lack this dill-and-cumin material. The distinctive Matthean tendency is law-strengthening. [^3]

### 4. "Exceed the Pharisees" is a clearable, polemical bar (rating: moderate; Second Temple background)
In early Jewish sources the Pharisees were sometimes mocked as *relaxed* interpreters (the Essenes were the rigorists). So "unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees" is rhetorical escalation aimed at maximal observance, consistent with Matthew's overall law-intensifying program rather than a hint that the law is passing away. [^4]

### 5. The author is composing, not transcribing (rating: moderate; method)
McClellan stresses it does not matter whether a historical Jesus said this; what matters is that *the author* wants to achieve something — sliding the saying into the Sermon on the Mount "soapbox" to make a case about the responsibilities of Jesus-followers. Reading it as authorial argument (a "discursive volley" answering Mark, against Luke/John and against Paulinism) is the historically responsible move. [^5]

### 6. Steelman of the harmonizing / traditional reading
A traditional Christian (and much non-"anti-Pauline-Matthew" scholarship) holds that Matthew and Paul are **complementary, not combatant**: Paul opposed *imposing* the ceremonial law on Gentiles, while affirming the law's goodness and its moral core (Rom 7:12; Rom 3:31, "we establish the law"); Matthew affirms the law's abiding authority for the same God's people, fulfilled and reinterpreted through Christ. On this view, "least commandments" and "exceed the Pharisees" target hypocritical lawlessness and externalism, not Paul, and the Acts 15 council shows the early church *resolving* the Gentile question rather than warring over it. Some scholars also caution that an explicitly *anti-Pauline* Matthew is a reconstruction, not a consensus. McClellan concedes the precise scholarly state of play is beyond his specialty but holds the anti-Pauline reading is "a reasonable way to interpret what's going on."

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint tradition reads Acts 15 and the Paul/Gentile-law debates as a live picture of an early church that was **plural and in tension** — which fits the LDS narrative of a developing church that ultimately required restored authority to settle doctrine. LDS theology sides materially with the *Pauline* outcome (the law of Moses fulfilled; Gentiles not bound to circumcision/kosher) while, unlike Paul, retaining an ongoing law-giving function through living prophets and an open canon (e.g., restored tithing in D&C 119). The "Matthew vs. Paul" framing therefore does not threaten LDS commitments so much as illustrate the kind of unresolved early-church dispute that, on the LDS view, motivates the need for continuing revelation. See also [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]].
