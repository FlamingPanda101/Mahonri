# Personages vs Subsistent Relations - What Person Means

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that when Trinitarians say the divine "persons," they cannot mean what ordinary usage (and Latter-day Saints) mean by *person* — "a separate center of consciousness." Classical theology must "highly qualify" *person*, even reducing it (with [[Thomas Aquinas]]) to a *subsistent relation*. [[Joseph Smith]] wisely used **"personages"** precisely to insist on full-blooded persons. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. LDS "person" = separate center of consciousness (rating: strong; definitional)
Joseph Smith's term "personages" ensures "we're talking about a person in a full-blooded sense — a separate center of consciousness, somebody who has their own [mind/will]." Thus when "Jesus ate, they're not saying the trinity ate... His bodily actions and what he does are distinct." See [[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:20-21]]. [^1]

> "Joseph Smith uses wisely the term personages to make sure that what we understand is we're talking about a person in a full-blooded sense — a separate center of consciousness." [^1]

### 2. Aquinas reduces person to subsistent relation (rating: strong; historical-philosophical)
"Thomas Aquinas reduced the term person to substantial relations. He would say the son is the property *filiation*, the father is the property *paternity*." But "a relationship requires two things to be in relation, and given his doctrine of simplicity, there can't be two distinct things... So he has to make them solely relations." See [[Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail]]. [^2]

### 3. "Are you assuming divine simplicity?" — the diagnostic (rating: strong; debate strategy)
Ostler's opening question to a Trinitarian: "Are you assuming the doctrine of simplicity in your description of the Trinity? Because that's going to change things greatly." If yes → the Latin incoherence; if no → a social model closer to the LDS view. [^3]

### 4. "God ate / God wept" creates ambiguity (rating: moderate; linguistic)
Because Jesus is God, one *could* say "God ate," but that "would create an ambiguity," since the bodily action belongs to the *person* of the Son, not to the Father or "the trinity." Hence one must always ask which sense of "God" is meant — the persons or the whole. See [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The classical theist argues that *person* in Trinitarian usage is a *technical* term (*hypostasis* / *persona*) deliberately distinct from the modern "individual center of consciousness," and that importing the modern psychological sense is the anachronism — three modern "centers of consciousness" sharing one will would be tritheism, the error the tradition avoids by the subsistent-relations account. They contend "personages" as separate minds is exactly what makes the LDS Godhead three gods. Ostler's reply: a "person" that is merely a relation, with no distinct center of will or consciousness, is not a *person* in any meaningful sense and cannot ground the I-Thou love the Gospels portray; the tradition pays for monotheism with an emptied notion of personhood. See [[Co-inherence as Mixing Light to Form the Godhead]].
