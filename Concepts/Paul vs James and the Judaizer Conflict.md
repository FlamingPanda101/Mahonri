# Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict

## The Claim
The earliest church contained two genuinely divergent forms of Christianity in open conflict: [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]]'s law-free Gentile mission ("you don't have to follow the 613 laws of Torah; believe in Jesus") versus [[James the brother of Jesus|James]]'s Torah-observant Jerusalem community, with [[Peter]] caught in between. This is not a polite difference of emphasis — it is a vicious, unresolved dispute preserved in scripture.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Paul rebukes Peter to his face (rating: strong; canonical)
[[Galatians 2.14|Galatians 2:11-14]]: Peter (Cephas) ate with Gentiles until "certain men came from James," then withdrew "fearing those of the circumcision." Paul opposes him publicly for being two-faced — keeping kosher table-fellowship only when James's people are watching. [^1]

### 2. Paul's wish that the Judaizers castrate themselves (rating: strong; the harshest evidence)
[[Galatians 5.12|Galatians 5:12]] — read in the NASB ("I wish that those who are troubling you would even mutilate themselves") via [[Peter Enns]] — shows Paul expressing the hope that his pro-circumcision Christian opponents (including, by extension, James's party) would emasculate themselves. Butler stresses this is "not different perspectives... they are at war with each other." The KJV "cut off" and a footnote glossing it as "excommunication" obscure the Greek *apokopsontai* ("amputate"). [^2]

### 3. James, not Paul, holds Jerusalem (rating: moderate; historical reconstruction)
Per [[Robert Eisenman]] and [[Raymond Brown]], James was the Torah-observant leader holding the Jerusalem church together — beloved by Jews for his law-keeping and by Christians for loving Jesus. "Men from James" carried enough authority to make Peter change his behavior, implying a Jerusalem-headquartered hierarchy. See [[Acts 15 Shows James Deciding Not Peter]]. [^3]

### 4. The dispute is sociologically intelligible, not merely doctrinal (rating: moderate; charitable reading)
Day refuses to "diss either guy": if you lived in Jerusalem you *had* to keep Torah or risk death; if you preached to Greeks, demanding circumcision killed the mission. Each leader was reading his room. The conflict reflects two viable survival strategies, not one apostate and one faithful party. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A harmonizing reading (which Butler explicitly critiques via [[Hayden Carroll]]'s interlocutors) holds that Paul and James were not contradicting each other — Paul opposed *imposing* the law on Gentiles while James affirmed it for Jewish believers, and Acts 15 resolves the matter amicably. On this view the "war" language is Paul's rhetorical heat, not a doctrinal schism, and James's letter (faith without works is dead) and Paul's gospel are complementary. Butler counters that the castration wish and the Antioch rebuke are too sharp to reduce to "different perspectives."

## Best Quotes

> "He is expressing hope that his fellow Christians, including James the brother of Jesus, cut off their testicles. So this is not that they are sharing different perspectives... they have a really strong disagreement." [^2]

> "Paul and James kind of represent what I would term a couple divergent views of Christianity." [^4]

> "It honestly shares books with different perspectives, but those perspectives are at war with each other." [^5]
