# The Problem of Suffering Reframed to Disarm the Standard-of-Evil Retort

## The Claim (skeptical)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] names the **problem of suffering** "undoubtedly" the strongest argument *against* God as traditionally conceived. His distinctive contribution is rhetorical-strategic: he deliberately **renames** the classic "problem of evil" the "problem of *suffering*" to pre-empt the standard theistic counter that an emotivist/atheist has no objective standard of *evil* with which to indict God. "A lot of the time… a religious person will say, 'How can you call anything evil if you've got no standard of goodness?' Okay, let's not say evil then. Let's say suffering."[^1] Whatever one's metaethics, the *suffering* is undeniable, so the argument proceeds without conceding the moral-realism premise the theist hoped to extract. He grants the argument does not *disprove* God and tells only against a *good* God — but holds that, especially via evolutionary suffering, "for most people it's very powerful."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The reframe itself, as a metaethical firewall.** By trading "evil" for "suffering," O'Connor blocks the [[Moral Argument Against Atheism|moral-argument]] judo move ("you're borrowing a standard of good you can't ground") *before* it lands. He is not claiming the suffering is objectively *evil* — only that it exists and is unexpected given a loving, omnipotent creator.[^1] *(A genuinely clever framing: it relocates the dispute from metaethics to the bare evidential datum of pain.)*

2. **Evolution is the sharpest form.** "Natural selection is survival of the fittest. Survival of the fittest is the same thing as the destruction and death and suffering of the weakest." For "billions of years" life existed "in a brutal competition for survival, with predation and disease," 99.9% of species "wiped from existence, usually in a pretty dismal manner" — and "all of this is built into the very mechanism by which God chose to bring about human life on Earth."[^2] *(This is [[Paul Draper]]'s evidential terrain; see [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]].)*

3. **The "unfathomable" animal datum.** "It is unfathomable, truly, the amount of suffering that these animals… meaningless torment for those animals who don't get to inherit eternal life." On the traditional view "humans are very special… all of this has occurred for our sake," which makes the pre-human ages of torment "a little unexpected."[^2] *(O'Connor presses the animal version specifically because it resists soul-making and free-will theodicies.)*

4. **Honest about scope — it argues against a *good* God only.** "Of course, this doesn't disprove the existence of God. It's not like logically contradictory to say that God did this for some reason." It "only really does anything to argue against the existence of a good God. And some people will then just say, 'Well, maybe God is evil.'"[^3] *(He concedes the logical problem fails and frames his as evidential/probabilistic — and even names the evil-god escape route.)*

5. **Where it bites.** The argument forces a choice on traditions that have *proven* God's goodness a priori (e.g. from pure actuality) or that confess it doctrinally: "they would at least have to give up that" goodness if they accepted the argument's plausibility.[^3] *(Correctly identifies that the cost is borne specifically by classical theism's omni-attributes.)*

## Apologetic Engagement

The vault already engages this cluster densely, and O'Connor's own evolutionary version is *already cited* in [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]] — so the new material here is the **reframe** (suffering-not-evil) and the **evil-god aside**, not the evolutionary datum itself.

- **The reframe cuts both ways.** O'Connor renames "evil" to "suffering" to avoid borrowing a moral standard — but [[Suffering Is Objectively Real Not a Preference]] presses the counter: if his emotivism is true, "this suffering is *bad*" is itself only "Boo, suffering," a feeling with no truth-value, so the argument loses its *normative force* (it shows pain exists, not that a good God *ought* to have prevented it). The theist can accept "there is suffering" and deny that the inference "therefore probably no good God" carries any objective weight on O'Connor's own metaethics. The reframe buys evidential neutrality at the cost of normative bite.
- **The evil-god aside is already neutralized.** O'Connor's "maybe God is evil" is exactly [[The Evil God Hypothesis Neutralizes the Argument from Suffering]]: the symmetry between a good-God-plus-evil and an evil-God-plus-good shows the *distribution* of goods and evils underdetermines the deity's character, blunting the argument as a disproof of theism per se.
- **Logical vs. evidential.** O'Connor concedes the logical problem fails ("not logically contradictory") — matching [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]] — and runs an *evidential* version, where the reply [[We Cannot Judge That God Lacks Reasons for Suffering]] (the no-see-um / skeptical-theist move) applies directly: a finite mind is poorly placed to judge that the pre-human ages of suffering are *gratuitous*.
- **The Restoration's distinctive answer.** The strongest engagement is the finite-theist relocation already worked out in [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]]: under a constrained, non-ex-nihilo God who "works with an eternal law that he didn't author," evolutionary suffering becomes "the cost of a constrained process" rather than "a chosen design feature of an all-powerful being." That note candidly concedes this does not *dissolve* Draper's evidential point — but it answers O'Connor's specific complaint ("God *chose* this mechanism") by denying the blank-slate omnipotence the complaint presupposes.

**Steelman the foil.** O'Connor's animal version is the genuinely hard case, and the vault grants it: non-human animals are not candidates for theosis, so "suffering as the cost of growth toward deification" fits human suffering far better than hundreds of millions of years of pre-human animal death. The honest apologetic is to *concede the gap* (as [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]] does) while denying that the gap supports atheism over a constrained theism more than it supports either over the other.
