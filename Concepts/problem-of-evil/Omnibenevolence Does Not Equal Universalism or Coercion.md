# Omnibenevolence Does Not Equal Universalism or Coercion

## The Claim
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]] draws a sharp definitional fence: omnibenevolence ("without exception willing the good of every moral subject") is *not* the claim that all are saved (universalism), nor that God forces the good upon anyone (coercion). God "wills the good of all but does not override freedom, because forced love is not love." Salvation can be genuinely offered to all yet not universally accepted.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Conceptual cleanliness** — rate: strong and correct as a distinction. Willing X's good is about *intention/orientation*, not about the *outcome* X freely chooses. The resolution is explicitly about *willing* the good, so non-universal salvation does not by itself falsify it. *(definitional)* [^1]
2. **Free-love premise** — "forced love is not love" is the standard free-will-defense move: a world with libertarian creatures who can refuse God is more valuable than coerced compliance. *(philosophical)* [^1]

**Hayden's decisive counter (steelman of the negative):** Hayden *grants* this entire distinction and says free will is "a red herring." His dilemma does not require God to coerce or save all; it bites *before* freedom operates — at creation. Even a perfectly free-willed person whose damnation is foreknown poses the question: why create *that* person at all, when God could refrain and lose nothing? So conceding "omnibenevolence ≠ coercion" does nothing to rescue the resolution. See [[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]] and [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]].

## Best Quotes
> "Omni benevolence does not equal universalism. Omni benevolence does not equal coercion. God wills the good of all but it does not override freedom because forced love is not love."[^1]

> "My argument has nothing to do with free will. I don't have a problem with someone choosing hell." — Hayden, granting the distinction and redirecting to creation [^2]
