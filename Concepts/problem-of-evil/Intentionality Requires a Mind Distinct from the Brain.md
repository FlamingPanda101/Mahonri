# Intentionality Requires a Mind Distinct from the Brain

## The Point
Craig backs his rejection of neuroscientific determinism with a positive argument from *intentionality* — the "aboutness" or "of-ness" of mental states.[^1] Thoughts are *about* or *of* something; but the brain, considered purely physically, is just "a glob of tissue," and a glob of tissue is not about or of anything.[^2] Therefore, to have intentionality at all you must have a mind that is distinct from the brain — a self, a soul — even though that mind uses the brain as an instrument for thought.[^3] This is why mental states cannot simply be reduced to the brain states with which they correlate.

## Evidence
1. **Mental states are intrinsically directed.** The argument from intentionality "shows that there have to be these mental states," and they are "the properties of a self, a soul, a mind."[^1]
2. **Matter as such lacks aboutness.** "The brain is just a glob of tissue, and a glob of tissue is not about something or of something."[^2] Physical stuff, by itself, has no intrinsic reference to anything beyond itself.
3. **The inference to a distinct mind.** "To have intentionality you've got to have a mind that is distinct from the brain, even if it uses the brain as an instrument for thought."[^3] Distinctness is compatible with dependence-in-use.
4. **Why this defeats the reductive reading.** If intentionality is real and matter cannot supply it, then a purely physical (brain-only) account of the person is incomplete — the agent who decides and is *about* things is the mind/soul, not merely the tissue.

## Best Quotes
> "To have intentionality you've got to have a mind that is distinct from the brain, even if it uses the brain as an instrument for thought." [^3]

> "The brain is just a glob of tissue, and a glob of tissue is not about something or of something." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Craig's conclusion — that the thinking, intending agent is a mind/soul distinct from the body it uses — harmonizes with Restoration anthropology, in which the person is an eternal spirit ("intelligence") that pre-exists and operates through a physical body (D&C 93:29; D&C 88:15; cf. [[Consciousness Precedes Existence - Neuroscience and Pre-Existence]]). Because the agent is a real spirit and not an epiphenomenon of tissue, genuine moral agency — to "act and not be acted upon" (2 Nephi 2:26) — is preserved against neural determinism. The structure also parallels the argument from reason (see [[The Argument from Reason - Atheism Undermines Reason Itself]]): just as rational inference cannot be reduced to blind physical causes, neither can the *aboutness* of thought, both pointing beyond mere matter to mind.
