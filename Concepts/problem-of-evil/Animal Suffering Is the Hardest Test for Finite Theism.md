# Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism

## The Point
[[Alex Arnold]] names animal suffering as "the version that philosophers say is the hardest answer, and I agree" — "hundreds of millions of years of predation and death before any human being existed."[^1] [[Emerson Green]] and [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] both point to evolutionary suffering as "the hardest version of this problem for finite theism," and [[Paul Draper]] (Purdue) "has pressed this carefully, saying that the distribution of pain and pleasure in the natural world tracks biological fitness, not moral purpose. The pattern is more consistent with atheism than with any form of theism."[^2] The note Arnold strikes here is the episode's methodological signature: rather than claim the objection is already answered, he concedes "LDS theology doesn't claim to have resolved this," and argues only that the finite-theist framework gives a "meaningfully different account."[^3]

## Evidence
- **[[Paul Draper]]'s evidential argument.** Draper's is not the logical problem of evil but an *evidential* one: the actual *distribution* of pain and pleasure — closely tracking what aids survival and reproduction — is what we would antecedently expect on indifference, and is surprising on theism. Arnold quotes the thrust: pain and pleasure "track biological fitness, not moral purpose," so "the pattern is more consistent with atheism than with any form of theism."[^2] This is the strongest single objection in the episode and Arnold flags it as such.
- **The finite-theist move, stated precisely.** Under classical theism, "if God is omnipotent in the classical sense and created from nothing, then every feature of this universe, including all of its suffering, is a direct choice from a blank slate." Under LDS finite theism, "God works with an eternal law that he didn't author" — "the laws of physics, logic, and mathematics are eternal and not created." If "the laws of biology that govern the development of embodied mortal growth-capable beings capable of genuine deification... require an evolutionary process, then the suffering embedded in that process reflects the constraints of the mechanism, not the preference of an all-omnipotent designer."[^4] This is the same constrained-God metaphysic argued in [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]] and [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **What the framework explicitly does *not* claim.** Arnold: "We don't say, 'God has mysterious reasons,' or 'Suffering is good for you.' But God didn't design this from unlimited options. He worked within a structure that he didn't author."[^5] He then *concedes the gap*: "There's still a question about why the eternal constraints are structured the way that they are, and whether a God working within them might have found a less costly path. Unfortunately, LDS theology doesn't claim to have resolved this."[^3]
- **Independent corroboration of the finite-theist response.** [[David Ray Griffin]] "argued this from process philosophy," and Harold Kushner "also arrived at finite theism from Jewish theology and personal experience, independently and decades earlier" — so even the *response* to the hardest objection is one non-LDS thinkers reached on their own.[^6]
- **The honest-apologetics contrast.** Arnold sets his approach against traditions that "treat every objection as if it's already answered, every philosophical difficulty as resolved, and every critic [as] simply confused" — "that's not what careful or honest thinkers do, and it's not what this channel is about."[^7] The relocation of suffering "from being a chosen design feature of an all-powerful being to being the cost of a constrained process" is offered as a real difference "even without the whole answer."[^8]

## LDS Connection
This note is the deliberate weak point of the convergence case, and its candor is the apologetic. The finite-theist framework central to the Restoration — co-eternal matter, uncreated eternal law, theosis through an embodied mortal process — relocates animal suffering from a free design choice to a structural cost, which is a genuine advance over an unconstrained ex-nihilo God who chose predation "from a blank slate." But it does not *dissolve* [[Paul Draper]]'s evidential point, and Arnold says so. The companion vault notes [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]] and [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]] carry the same unresolved tension — whether a loving, if constrained, God "might have found a less costly path."

**Steelman the critic.** (1) *The constraint is asserted, not shown* — that deification "requires an evolutionary process" of this severity is precisely what Draper's data calls into doubt; finite theism still owes an account of *why* the eternal laws make so much pre-human suffering necessary. (2) *Fitness-tracking is the live problem* — relocating suffering to "the mechanism" does not explain why the mechanism's pain/pleasure distribution looks exactly like indifference rather than design. (3) *Animals and theosis* — non-human animals are not candidates for deification on the LDS account, so "suffering as the cost of growth-capable beings becoming like God" fits human suffering far better than the hundreds of millions of years of animal death that predate humanity.
