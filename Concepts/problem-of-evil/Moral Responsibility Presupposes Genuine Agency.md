# Moral Responsibility Presupposes Genuine Agency

## The Point
Moral responsibility is *conceptually parasitic* on genuine choice: you can only be blamed or praised for what you actually choose. Both speakers in this clip grant the premise — "people are only morally responsible for the choices that they make... if you don't make a choice... you're not responsible for it because you didn't make the choice." [^1] The critic then runs the premise as a *weapon*: "let's get rid of agency altogether," so that no one ever "make[s] the choice," and concludes that coherent talk of moral responsibility becomes impossible. [^2] The argument is valid — which is exactly why it is so revealing. If hard determinism is true, responsibility does collapse; so anyone who wishes to keep responsibility (as nearly everyone does in practice) must reject the elimination of agency. The Latter-day Saint reply does not dispute the conditional; it denies the antecedent, grounding real agency in the uncreated intelligence.

## Evidence
- **The shared premise.** Responsibility tracks choice: no choice, no responsibility. [^1] This is the hinge on which the whole exchange turns, and it is uncontested by either party.
- **The eliminativist move.** "Now let's get rid of agency altogether... you don't even make the choice that you like." The racist "didn't choose that," and given the wrong upbringing "I might turn into a serial killer with no choice of my own." Therefore "how can we have a coherent discussion about moral responsibility... if you ultimately don't make choices?" [^2]
- **The conclusion is meant to be deflationary.** The critic's payoff is not "be compassionate about constraints" but the stronger claim that responsibility-talk is a fiction we cannot ground — "if there is no agency there just is no agency and we have to deal with that." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "People are only morally responsible for the choices that they make... if you don't make a choice... you're not responsible for it because you didn't make the choice." [^1]

> "How can we have a coherent discussion about moral responsibility... if you're not morally responsible for any of your choices because you ultimately don't make choices?" [^2]

## LDS Connection
Restoration scripture makes *agency* — "to act and not to be acted upon" — the defining feature of personhood, which is precisely what underwrites responsibility. Lehi teaches that men are "free to choose liberty and eternal life... or to choose captivity and death" and are "agents unto themselves" (2 Nephi 2:27; see [[2 Nephi 2.8]] on the broader chapter). Accountability follows directly: "men will be punished for their own sins" (Articles of Faith 1:2), and judgment is "according to their works" and "the desires of their hearts" ([[Alma 41.3-7]]). Crucially, the LDS metaphysic answers the determinist's strongest move — the materialist dilemma of *caused-or-random* — by denying that persons are caused configurations of matter at all: the intelligence is uncreated and "act[s] for itself" ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:29–30]]; see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]). Where the critic says the self is merely "acted upon" by genes it never chose, scripture says the self genuinely *acts*. This is the same fault line where [[Sam Harris]]'s honest hard determinism ([[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]) tries — and arguably fails — to keep responsibility without agency ([[Moral Responsibility Without Free Will - Harris's Reconciliation]]); and it mirrors why classical creation *ex nihilo* faces a parallel "robot running a program" worry ([[Ex Nihilo Determinism Negates Moral Praise]]).
