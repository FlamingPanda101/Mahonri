# Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy

## The Point
The single most common reply to the problem of evil is the *skeptical-theist* move: God is so far above us that He must have morally sufficient reasons for permitting any evil, reasons we cannot fathom. [[Blake Ostler]] grants that the premise is *true* — but argues that, for a God who causes or foreknows everything, it is self-defeating. If every actual evil is permitted because God foresaw a greater good it secures, then **stopping** an evil would thwart God's plan. The defense therefore licenses what Ostler calls **moral quietude**: a serene refusal to act against any evil, because "if it occurs, God must have his reasons for letting it occur." That conclusion "makes nonsense of moral freedom, moral accountability, and any kind of motivation to do any good," and so "can't be a Christian response to the problem of evil." [^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The skeptical-theist premise, fairly stated.** Ostler steelmans it with the dog-at-the-vet analogy: the dog cannot understand germ theory and so cannot grasp why its owner permits the pain of shots; the cognitive distance between us and God "is at least that great," so "we can't even begin to fathom the reasons that God could possibly have." Ostler concedes "in a sense it is a good argument because it is true that God knows a lot more than we do." [^3]
2. **The reductio — sitting and watching.** "I come upon the little girl while she's being brutalized and I think to myself, you know, if this happens God must have a very good reason for allowing it... so I'm just going to sit and watch it because God is so good. Well that's just nonsense." If any evil that *actually happens* is part of a greater-good plan, "there's no moral obligation to do anything other than what actually happens." [^4]
3. **It depends on your doctrine of God.** The trap closes hardest on those for whom God causes/wills everything — "if you're a Calvinist, if you follow what Thomas Aquinas teaches about God, then you've got a real problem here, because what you're saying is virtually everything that occurs is God's will." [^5] See [[Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism - The Hidden Determinism of Classical Theism]].
4. **The fatalism parallel.** Ostler ties moral quietude to Islamic *inshallah* fatalism: in a thoroughly deterministic world, the Twin Towers coming down "was God's will," and so were the deaths of the Muslims killed in retaliation — "none of that makes any sense. What we're doing now is calling things that are clearly evil the greater good." [^6]
5. **The "God will act through me" rejoinder fails too.** If one says God would prevent the evil by working through a bystander, then the evil's *occurrence* proves God did *not* will its prevention — "the fact that she was killed says it was God's will that she died." The escape collapses back into quietude. [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "The real problem with that view is it leads to what I call moral quietude... it would justify us in allowing virtually anything to happen, or doing literally anything that's evil, because if it occurs God must have his reasons for letting it occur." [^1]

> "No matter what evil I do was all part of God's plan, he knew all about it... The problem is it makes nonsense of moral freedom, moral accountability, and any kind of motivation to do any good. And that can't be a Christian response to the problem of evil." [^2]

> "If something happens it's all for the best, it's all for the greater good — that's why that argument will work [against the classical theist]." [^8]

## LDS Connection
This argument is the negative half of the distinctively LDS theodicy: it shows the *cost* of the classical, all-determining God, clearing the ground for a God who does **not** author every event. In Latter-day Saint theology God organizes co-eternal matter and uncreated [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency|intelligences]] who possess their own libertarian agency, so a genuinely evil choice is the agent's, not God's plan — escaping quietude because not everything that happens is God's will. Ostler notes the trap can be dodged only by *open theism* (denying exhaustive foreknowledge) or by his organizer model; classical theism's omni-causal God (see [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]) cannot. This is why rejecting [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation ex nihilo]] matters morally, not just metaphysically: only a constrained, [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|non-all-determining God]] preserves the moral obligation to fight evil rather than baptize it. The LDS framework also preserves the *truth* in skeptical theism — God really does know more than we do (see [[The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil]]) — without letting that truth metastasize into quietude, because human evils trace to free agents God did not program.
