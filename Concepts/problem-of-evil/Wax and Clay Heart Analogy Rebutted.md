# Wax and Clay Heart Analogy Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The apologetic illustration McClellan is responding to runs as follows: picture God as **the sun** — unchanging in love, justice, and mercy. Picture human hearts as **vessels** that we fill up by our own free will. In the world there are life-giving things (like **wax**) and destructive things (like **clay**). If you fill your heart with wax, then "when God shines on your heart, it's going to be softened." If you fill your heart with clay, then "when the same God shines on us, that heart is going to be hardened." The punchline: "God's character stays the same. Our choices change our response to him."[^1] The analogy is designed to reconcile the Exodus hardening texts with the doctrine that God never overrides human agency.

## The Rebuttal
1. **It is wholly fabricated — no textual basis.** McClellan: "there's nothing anywhere in the Bible that supports this notion that God treats everyone the same and our hearts are either softened or hardened based on what we have done." (Strong — the analogy cites no verse; it is an external interpretive frame, not exegesis.)[^2]
2. **Steelman of the apologist.** The wax/clay model is a sophisticated attempt to preserve two cherished intuitions at once: (a) God's immutability — the "sun" never changes — and (b) genuine human moral responsibility. It also resonates with real biblical sun-and-heat imagery and with the Pauline tradition that the same gospel is "a fragrance from death to death" or "from life to life" depending on the hearer (2 Cor 2:16), and with the medieval idea that the same divine love is felt as bliss by the saved and torment by the damned. The instinct — that the meaning of an encounter depends on the state of the one encountered — is not silly. **But** none of that is what *Exodus* says is happening, and the apologist smuggles in the conclusion (Pharaoh is the cause) rather than deriving it.
3. **It is motivated reasoning from presupposition.** The framework "was necessary when you come to this text presupposing the univocality of the Bible and presupposing that the Bible would never represent God robbing a human of their agency in order to manipulate that human like a marionette." Because that presupposition forbids what the text says, the apologist must "fabricate and jin up scenarios and frameworks and principles."[^3] (See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]] and [[Putting Dogma Over Data]].)
4. **It contradicts the text's own causation.** The analogy makes hardening an *automatic, constant* effect of glory on a clay-filled heart; but Exodus presents hardening as *selective and time-specific* divine action with stated rhetorical purposes. See [[God Explicitly Hardens Pharaohs Heart in Exodus]].
5. **It is self-defeating given Pharaoh's own hardening.** If hardening were just glory acting on a badly-curated heart, [[Pharaoh Also Hardens His Own Heart]] would be incoherent.

## Best Quotes
> "Picture God like the sun, unchanging in his love, justice, and mercy. And picture our hearts like vessels that we can fill up... If we fill our hearts with wax, then when God shines on our heart, it's going to be softened. However, if we fill our hearts with destructive things like clay, then when the same God shines on us, that heart is going to be hardened." [^1]

> "This is obviously an apologetic attempt to sidestep the fact that the book of Exodus repeatedly and explicitly describes God hardening Pharaoh's heart in order to bring about God's own rhetorical goals." [^4]

> "There's nothing anywhere in the Bible that supports this notion that God treats everyone the same and our hearts are either softened or hardened based on what we have done. And all this does is shifts the blame from God to Pharaoh." [^2]

> "A ridiculous bit of apologetic misinformation that was just made up so that people who already want that to be true can feel justified in thinking that it's true because it feels truthy to them." [^5]
