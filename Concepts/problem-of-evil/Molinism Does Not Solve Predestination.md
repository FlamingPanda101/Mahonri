# Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A sophisticated creedal response to the predestination problem is **Molinism** (after Luis de Molina): God possesses *middle knowledge* — knowledge of all counterfactuals of creaturely freedom (what any free creature *would* freely do in any possible circumstance) — logically prior to His creative decree. This is offered as the "best solve" because it appears to combine exhaustive divine foreknowledge with genuine libertarian free will, dissolving Calvinism's hard determinism.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Middle knowledge preserves exhaustive foreknowledge, so predestination survives — rate: strong.** [[Luke Hansen]] concedes Molinism allows free will but stresses it "doesn't say that God doesn't have exhaustive foreknowledge. He actually does know... but in Molinism even, he still knows the actual outcome." Since God, knowing every counterfactual, *chooses which world to actualize*, He selects the precise set of people who will be damned — the predestination problem returns at the level of the creative decree.[^2] Hansen calls it "whistling past the graveyard": Molinism addresses Calvinism's *rejection of free will* but not the *predestination* aspect that drives the creation dilemma.[^3] See [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]. **Steelman of the Molinist:** the Molinist replies that God does *not* invent the counterfactuals — they are pre-volitionally true, so God is constrained by which "feasible worlds" are available. He cannot necessarily actualize a world where everyone freely accepts Him (that world may not be feasible — Plantinga's "transworld depravity"). On this view God actualizes the *best feasible* balance of saved-to-lost, and the damned freely damn themselves, so God is not the author of their loss. This is a serious answer that the panel acknowledges but does not refute in detail.
2. **Carroll claims the book pre-rebuts it — rate: asserted, not demonstrated here.** Carroll says he "has a section on Molinism" and that "any response that you could get to this is addressed."[^4] On the stream itself the rebuttal stays at the level of (1): even granting feasible-worlds constraints, God still *chose* to actualize a world containing the damned when (per the Mary problem) He could allegedly have actualized one without them — see [[The Mary Problem for Catholic Theism]], [[Why Doesnt God Only Create the Saved]].
3. **Contrast with open theism — rate: clarifying.** Open theism *does* fully dissolve the dilemma (no foreknowledge of the actual future), which is why Carroll grants it works even though he rejects it on scriptural grounds. Molinism, by retaining foreknowledge, keeps the dilemma alive. See [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]].
