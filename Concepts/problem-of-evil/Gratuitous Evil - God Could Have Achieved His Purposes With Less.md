# Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less

## The Point
The hardest form of the problem of evil is not "why any evil?" but "why *this much*?" [[Blake Ostler]] argues that an omnipotent creator faces a *gratuitous-evil* problem the free-will defense cannot touch: for any actual evil, an all-powerful God could have secured the very same good with *less* suffering — and the surplus that serves no greater good is **gratuitous evil**. Worse, any attempt to license evil by appeal to greater goods ends up "justifying evil," which "is a bad thing to do." Natural evils (disease, accidents) are especially resistant, because agency has nothing to do with them. [^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The aneurysm thought-experiment.** Even granting that free will is precious, omnipotence has a free-will-preserving escape from moral evil: to stop the murder of the little girl, "what he does is he causes small aneurysms in the brain of the murderer... in the area of the brain where he doesn't desire to do any of those things. Well that doesn't take away his free will, it just directs him." So "it's easy to come up with explanations where God could have done without the evil to still achieve the purpose." [^3]
2. **The smallpox arithmetic.** Smallpox is "by far and away the most virulent killer in the history of the world" — about four billion dead, "preying on younger little children," leaving survivors blind and scarred. Ostler presses: "Did we need to have 4 billion die? Could we have done three billion nine hundred million... could we have saved a hundred million lives? Was it really that number exactly? The last person who died — was that really necessary?" [^4][^5]
3. **Defining gratuitous evil.** Gratuitous evil "is not necessary... we can't fathom any way that it leads to a greater good." The standard greater-good reply is structurally self-undermining: "if you try to justify evil by saying it leads to a greater good, you end up justifying evil." [^6]
4. **Natural evils break the agency defense.** The three test cases — a five-year-old kidnapped and murdered (moral evil), a child crushed by a rolling car (negligence + natural law), and smallpox (pure natural evil) — show that for storms, cancer, ALS, "agency has nothing to do with those things," so "natural evils are not really amenable to explanation by free will." [^7]
5. **The creator-is-responsible turn.** "If God causes the world... it's very hard to figure out [how] he's not responsible, because he could have caused it in some other way" where the victim "didn't get ALS and die" — and the reply to whether God could have done so easily is "easily... just about anything with omnipotence has an easy explanation to avoid it that doesn't ruin free will." [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "It's easy to come up with explanations where God could have done [away] with the evil to still achieve the purpose." [^3]

> "Did we need to have 4 billion die? Could we have done three billion nine hundred million... The last person who died, was that really necessary to cause that suffering?" [^5]

> "If you try to justify evil by saying it leads to a greater good, you end up justifying evil. It's a bad thing to do." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The gratuitous-evil problem is a knife aimed at the *omni-causal* God of classical theism — the God who, having created everything ex nihilo, is "the prime mover... the cause of everything, including the bad stuff." It does far less damage to the LDS God, who **organizes** a world out of co-eternal matter and uncreated [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency|intelligences]] under [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|eternal law He did not author]]. Because the LDS God works with genuinely free agents and resistant ("recalcitrant") materials rather than designing every constant from nothing, much apparent gratuity is the byproduct of a fallen, chaotic arena agents *premortally consented* to enter (see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]), not a quantity God dialed in. Ostler's own constructive answer — the [[The Agape Theodicy - Evil as the Arena for Learning Divine Love|agape theodicy]] — does not pretend each particular evil is individually necessary; it locates meaning in how free agents respond within an unfair world, framing surplus suffering as *injustice* and chaos rather than divine design. Note the honest tension Ostler concedes elsewhere: the classical theist can still retreat to "we just don't get it" ([[The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil]]) — but that retreat is exactly what triggers the [[Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy|moral-quietude]] objection.
