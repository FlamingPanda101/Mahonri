#type/claim

# The Satanic Fall May Have Influenced the Natural Order

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] names pre-human natural evil — "death, disease, pain, and apparent futility" across hundreds of millions of years before any human existed — as his "personal greatest intellectual struggle in my journey of faith."[^1] He frames it precisely so it cannot be evaded by rejecting evolution: "even if you're just an old-earth creationist, same problem," so the difficulty is "the problem of pre-human natural evil," not merely a problem for theistic evolution.[^2] The standard theodicies fail here because, as [[Trent Horn]] grants, "the traditional theodicies or responses to the problem of evil don't always apply to the same degree — in some cases they don't apply at all — to the animal realm, because animals are before the human fall."[^3] Free-will defenses in particular do not cover animals, who "don't have free will like humans."[^4]

Ortlund's distinctive proposal: **a fall of the angels prior to and independent of the human fall may have disordered the natural world itself.** "I wrote an article many years ago saying the satanic fall may have influenced the natural order, and that's the approach I take."[^5] The decisive advantage he claims is conceptual room: "you don't necessarily have to say this is exactly how God set up the world... we all believe there's been another fall before the human fall, we all think evil began before human beings, and so that at least gives you that conceptual space for a kind of fallenness theodicy for pre-human natural evil."[^6] It answers the otherwise-fatal rejoinder — *why didn't God just set up a world with no crocodile slowly killing a gazelle?* — by allowing that the world's predatory structure need not be God's direct, unfallen design.[^7]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
- **A serious historical pedigree, not a fringe move.** Ortlund anticipates the "as crazy as that seems" reaction by listing major figures who held it: "Catholic theologians, Von Balthazar, one of the greatest Catholic theologians of the 20th century, he advocated for that; [[Alvin Plantinga]], one of the greatest Christian philosophers, advocated for that; [[C.S. Lewis]]; Tolkien's *Silmarillion* basically envisions that — it's the evil angels fighting with the good angels and that affects nature."[^8] The view has Catholic (Balthasar), analytic-philosophical (Plantinga), and imaginative-literary (Lewis, Tolkien) support.
- **It exploits a premise the objector already shares.** Every orthodox Christian already affirms an angelic fall *before* humanity. So the move does not invent new machinery; it draws a consequence from a commitment the interlocutor holds, supplying "conceptual space" for pre-human fallenness without positing anything novel.[^6] This is the same fallen-angels-as-origin-of-evil logic developed independently in [[Book of Enoch - Problem of Evil Through Fallen Angels]].
- **It is offered with explicit epistemic humility and a defined role.** Ortlund flags it as "at least a possibility" he holds "as a Christian," not a demonstrated fact — and crucially restricts its use: "that's not my argument in actual apologetics with a skeptic, because it's not going to gain much."[^9] It functions as an internal consolation that defeats the *defeater* (it shows the theist need not say predation is God's unfallen design), rather than as positive evidence for theism to a non-believer. This mirrors the modest, distribution-of-burden approach in [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]].
- **It pairs with a narrower scriptural point on death.** Horn adds that the apparent biblical obstacle is limited: "Scripture teaches that human death came into existence at the fall, not necessarily death itself," so animal death before the human fall is not a straightforward contradiction of Revelation.[^10] [[Thomas Aquinas]] is invoked on the same side — he "believed that they retained their carnivorous [nature]... predators existed [as] part of the [created order]," i.e. carnivory predates the human fall.[^11] That carnivory-before-the-fall is not a settled doctrinal problem is argued in [[No Death Before the Fall Is a Twentieth-Century Interpretation Not Settled Doctrine]].

## Best Quotes
> "I think the problem of animal suffering is actually one of the toughest ones to respond to, because... the traditional theodicies don't always apply to the same degree — in some cases they don't apply at all — to the animal realm." [^3]

> "I wrote an article many years ago saying the satanic fall may have influenced the natural order... we all believe there's been another fall before the human fall... so that at least gives you that conceptual space for a kind of fallenness theodicy for pre-human natural evil." [^6]

> "That's not my argument in actual apologetics with a skeptic, because it's not going to gain much. But as a Christian, I actually think that there's at least a possibility there." [^9]
