# The Negative Distance Test for a Broken Theology

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s recurring heuristic — highlighted by the *Stick of Joseph* panel as the breakdown's best single argument — is the **cannonball / negative-distance** analogy: if you solve a physics problem for how far a cannonball traveled and your answer comes out *negative*, you know with certainty you erred somewhere, even before you can locate the mistake, because a displacement cannot be negative. Likewise, a chain of theological reasoning that terminates in "a God who loves tormenting little girls" has produced a *morally* negative result, which is a reliable signal that one of the premises is false — regardless of how airtight each individual step felt. The test reverses the usual debate dynamic: instead of contesting each verse, you reason backward from an absurd conclusion to a corrupt premise.

## Evidence
1. **The analogy stated.** "If you're doing a math problem... calculate the distance and you go through page after page of math and you get to the end and you go, 'The answer is a negative number.' You know you did something wrong cuz you can't get a negative distance... you don't know where you went wrong, but you know that this isn't right" — so "I should probably go back to the beginning." [^1]
2. **Applied to Calvinism.** "For Calvinists or people who end up with a God who loves tormenting little girls, something went wrong along the way." The morally monstrous output functions as the negative number. [^2]
3. **It diagnoses the *whole* spectrum, not just Calvinism.** The panel extends it: the non-Calvinist classical theist's equation also yields a wrong answer — just "not so obviously wrong," not a flagrant negative — because they made "a mistake later on in the equation" while keeping the same starting premise (creation ex nihilo). Calvinism is simply the version honest enough to print the negative sign. [^3] See [[Calvinism Is Consistent but Built on a Single Bad Premise]].
4. **The premise it sends you back to.** The breakdown locates the original error at creation *ex nihilo* — from which omniscient authorship of every choice, and thus the loss of free will, follows. Swap in creation *ex materia* and the negative result disappears. [^4] See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]].

## Best Quotes
> "You get to the end and you go, 'The answer is a negative number.' You know you did something wrong cuz you can't get a negative distance... I can't see where I went wrong right now, but I know that this isn't right, and I should probably go back to the beginning." [^1]

> "It's not so obviously wrong, but it's still wrong, and they're just doing it by making a mistake later on in the equation." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The test is a vernacular form of *reductio ad absurdum* that privileges the moral conscience (the [[Light of Christ]]) as a check on speculative theology — paralleling the Book of Mormon's own logic in [[2 Nephi 2.11-13|2 Nephi 2]] (a world without real opposition reduces to "a thing of naught") and [[Alma 42.13|Alma 42]] (a God who destroyed justice "would cease to be God"). It also names *which* premise to discard: by trading creation ex nihilo for ex materia and uncreated intelligences, Latter-day Saints get a positive answer — free agents who can genuinely love. Compare the formal treatment of premise-hiding in [[The Logical Problem of Evil Hides Two Unprovable Premises]].
