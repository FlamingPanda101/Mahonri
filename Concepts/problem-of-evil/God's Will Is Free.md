# God's Will Is Free

## The Point
Because there is intellect in God, there must also be something analogous to *will* — rational appetite, an inclination toward the good that he apprehends. [^1] Feser argues this will is **free**: nothing external compels God (everything else depends on him), and nothing internal compels him either, since the concepts of all possible creatures exist in his intellect and "before creation a world with unicorns in it was as possible as a world with lions in it." [^2] He need not create at all, and there is no obligatory "best of all possible worlds" forcing his hand. [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Will follows intellect.** Trees and dogs incline (non-rationally) toward realizing their natures; humans incline rationally (will). God, who has intellect, must have "something like the limit case of rational appetite." He apprehends all that could exist and causes some things while refraining from others, so "there must exist in him something analogous to willing the former and not willing the latter." [^1]
2. **Free from external and internal compulsion.** Nothing outside God could compel him, since all else depends on him. And nothing inside the concepts necessitates his choices: there is nothing in the concept of a lion that makes lions necessary, nor in the concept of a unicorn that forbids unicorns. "If there is nothing either external to God or internal to him compelling him to act as he does, then his will is free." [^2]
3. **Reply: simplicity does not bar freedom.** The objection that free action requires actualizing one potential rather than another (which a purely actual God lacks) commits a *fallacy of accident*: our free acts involve actualizing potentials, but freedom *as such* does not require it, any more than acting as such requires changing. [^4] It is admittedly easier to state divine freedom apophatically — God is *not compelled* by anything — than to give it positive content. [^5]
4. **Reply to Leibniz / "best possible world."** The claim that God must create the best world assumes (a) one is always obliged to bring about the best one can, and (b) there is a best possible world. Both fail. God wrongs no one by not creating, since "what does not exist in the first place cannot be wronged"; and there is no best world, since for any world with *x* forests there is a better one with *x + 1*. [^6]
5. **Perfection follows from being, not doing.** That God could create any of infinitely many worlds does not mean he must create any particular one, or any at all — "a perfect diamond is a perfect diamond whether it cuts glass ... or indeed whether it ever cuts any glass at all." [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "If there is nothing either external to God or internal to him compelling him to act as he does, then his will is free." [^2]

> "He need not create any world at all, let alone a world with trees in it. ... What does not exist in the first place cannot be wronged." [^6]

> "God's perfection ... follows from what he is. ... He thus has it whatever he creates, or indeed whether he creates anything at all." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Against the atheist's blind, necessitated cosmos, Feser's case for a free, choosing divine will is a strong ally: creation is a free act of a personal God, not an automatic emanation. Latter-day Saints can affirm a free divine will heartily. The divergences are characteristically about *grounds*. Feser secures divine freedom while insisting God is purely actual and absolutely simple ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]) — and the resulting "limit case of rational appetite," which Feser concedes is best stated only negatively, sits uneasily with the embodied, deliberating Father of the Restoration who genuinely intends, relates, and acts in time ([[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]). The tension between simplicity and a real will is one instance of the broader worry that the classical omni-attributes do not cohere ([[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]]). His "no best possible world" argument and the diamond analogy are, in fairness, elegant and persuasive, and undercut a common atheist objection that a good God would have been compelled to make a perfect world. The remaining LDS question is whether a God whose freedom must be described almost entirely apophatically is the living, relational God of scripture or a philosophically necessary abstraction.
