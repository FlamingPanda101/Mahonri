# The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil

## The Point
Turek calls this "the revolutionary insight" that resolved his own doubts about seemingly senseless evil. As finite, time-bound creatures we can see why God allows evil *in general*, but not why He allows each *particular* evil — because every event sets off a **ripple effect** through countless future events and people that only an infinite, timeless God could trace.[^1] So the gratuitous-evil objection fails: the atheist cannot prove that no good will ever come from a given evil, which is why "most philosophers agree that the existence of evil is not incompatible with the existence of God."[^2]

## Evidence
Turek first deflates the "sum of human suffering" objection: no one actually experiences the aggregate. If it's 80° in Brooklyn, 82° in Manhattan, and 84° in the Bronx, "no New Yorker experiences the cumulative 246 degrees"; each person experiences only the suffering present to him.[^3] To the charge that appealing to unseen goods is a "cop-out" ("God works in mysterious ways"), he replies it is not a *lack* of evidence but an *extrapolation* from evidence — we routinely watch good come out of evil, and simply recognize our finitude regarding outcomes we cannot yet see, "not here or in the afterlife where perfect justice will ultimately be done."[^4] He illustrates with **It's a Wonderful Life**: George Bailey is shown how much worse his town would be had he never existed — a glimpse of the timeless perspective only God truly has, seeing how "trillions of free choices and events can interact ultimately for good."[^5] Critically, this is *not* "the ends justify the means": "God is not doing evil so that good may result. In fact, God is not doing evil at all—we are."[^6] He guards the chapter's spine — evil's reality argues *for* God — and closes with the asymmetry of mystery: "Christianity has partial mysteries. Atheism has complete contradictions."[^7] The chapter's two human bookends embody the thesis: a widow, Buff Winter, grown "closer to Christ than I've ever been" through her husband's death; and Pastor Gary Bingham, conceived in rape, telling a hostile atheist, "My mother turned evil into good, and God can too."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "As finite beings constrained by time, we can see why God allows evil in general to occur, but not why He allows each specific evil to occur. Only the infinite God, who can see every event at once, could know the good reasons for allowing each evil event to run its course." [^1]

> "There is a big difference between a mystery and a contradiction. Christianity has partial mysteries. Atheism has complete contradictions." [^7]

> "Sometimes you don't have an answer, just a perspective. From our limited perspective, the world can look like a confusing puzzle. But from the infinite God's perspective, every piece ultimately fits together." [^9]

## LDS Connection
Turek's appeal to God's timeless, all-encompassing vantage resonates strongly with the Latter-day Saint conviction that "all things are present" before God and that He sees the end from the beginning (cf. D&C 38:2; Moses 1:6). The Restoration enriches the ripple-effect argument with an **eternal horizon for justice and growth**: because mortal life is one stage of an ongoing existence — premortal, mortal, and postmortal — the "story isn't over yet" that Turek invokes is, for Latter-day Saints, a literal eternal arc in which compensation, healing, and soul-making continue beyond death (Alma 40–42; the work for the dead in the spirit world). Where Turek says we lack the information to trace particulars "yet," LDS theology supplies a robust *mechanism* for that "yet": a God who is not the author of evil, who honors agency, and who through the Atonement of Christ can consecrate even the worst afflictions for our gain (2 Nephi 2:2 — affliction "consecrated... for thy gain"). The shared insight is that finite creatures should withhold the verdict of "purposeless"; the LDS plan of salvation explains why patient trust is warranted — every piece "ultimately fits together" in an eternity expressly designed for our growth toward God.
