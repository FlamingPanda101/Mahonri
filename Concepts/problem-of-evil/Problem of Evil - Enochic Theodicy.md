# Problem of Evil - Enochic Theodicy
#type/claim

## The Claim
1 Enoch offers one of the earliest sustained Jewish answers to the problem of evil — an "Enochic theodicy" that locates the origin of evil not in human disobedience (as Genesis 3 does) but in the free, deliberate rebellion of heavenly beings. The Watchers covenant together to descend, take human wives, and teach forbidden arts; their monstrous offspring and corrupting knowledge flood the world with violence. Evil thus enters creation through the choices of created angelic agents, which preserves God's goodness while explaining the pervasiveness of evil.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Evil as angelic free choice.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], 1 Enoch uses the Watchers myth "as a lens for contemplating the problem of evil... it comes from these angels who make this decision — in fact they make a pact, a covenant, to descend to Earth." The deliberateness (a covenant, not deception) is the point: evil is freely chosen by creatures, not authored by God. Developed in [[Book of Enoch - Problem of Evil Through Fallen Angels]].
- **Civilization as corruption.** The Watchers teach warfare, metallurgy, cosmetics, and sorcery (1 Enoch 8) — so the theodicy is distinctively pessimistic about human "progress," framing technology itself as angelic contamination. Wisdom personified even abandons the earth, and the flood becomes cosmic cleansing of angelic corruption; the Watchers' dead spirits persist as the demons.
- **Distinctiveness among theodicies.** It is unusual in placing evil's origin in *heavenly* rather than earthly rebellion, contrasting with the Genesis 3 (human sin), Augustinian (original sin), and Irenaean (soul-making) frameworks. Its account of a personal adversary and fallen spirits fed directly into New Testament demonology (see [[Christology - Enochic Background]]).
- **Steelman / caveats.** Critics note the Enochic theodicy only relocates the question (why did God make angels able to rebel, or permit it?), so it does not dissolve the problem of evil so much as push it up a level — the same regress that attends any free-will defense. It also does little to address natural evil (disease, disaster) not traceable to the Watchers. Its value is historical and conceptual: it shows how Second Temple Judaism imagined evil's source, and it shaped later Jewish and Christian thought, whatever its philosophical adequacy.
