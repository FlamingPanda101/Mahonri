# The Agape Theodicy - Evil as the Arena for Learning Divine Love

## The Point
[[Blake Ostler]]'s constructive answer to the problem of evil — his **agape theodicy** — holds that the purpose of existence is to be brought into the divine relationship of perfect mutual love (the *coinherence* the Father, Son, and Spirit already enjoy), and that **love cannot be coerced**. It must be a "full-blooded choice." Therefore free agents must be placed in a world with genuine evils — an arena calling for compassion, forbearance, and the love of enemies — where, by their own free response, they learn to love as God loves. The towering good of that relationship "would justify any finite pain or evil that we had to endure in order to realize it," provided it is not eternal. This is *soul-making* grounded in the LDS doctrines of uncreated agents, premortal consent, and an afterlife. [^1][^2]

## Evidence
1. **The goal: coinherence, not mere agreement.** The Godhead is "an eternal unity"; in every moment they could choose to love perfectly, "they've done so," and that perfect love "gives rise to the divine power, knowledge, intelligence." The aim of life is to make us "fit to be in that relationship." Ostler grounds this in John 17's "co-inheritance" — "they actually live their lives in each other" — and insists it is far more than a Biden-style "unity speech." Sharing a *purpose* is not enough: "I played football, we all had the purpose of winning the game; it did not make us divine." [^3][^4]
2. **The hinge premise: love can't be coerced.** "Love can't be coerced, it can't be something that's chosen for you by somebody else; it has to be your full-blooded choice." Hence "free will per se may not be valuable, but free will toward the choice of a loving relationship... that's valuable, and it's worth anything that we need to confront to gain it." [^5][^6]
3. **Why a world with real evils is required.** Christ asks us to love our *enemies* — "I don't learn anything" by loving the lovable. Learning to overcome "my desire to just knock their block off" requires people who are hard to love and injustices that are hard to bear, so the world is "set up to teach us how to love in the way that we would be fit to enter into that relationship." [^7]
4. **The unfairness/opposition frame.** Host [[Jacob Hansen]] reframes evil as *injustice* — an unfair, fallen, chaotic arena (the jiu-jitsu analogy: children must be "sent into the lone and dreary world" to grow); Ostler folds this into the agape theodicy via "the necessity of opposition" and Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning*: "a human can survive any evil if they have a purpose in surviving." Looking back, "the toughest times... were the greatest blessings." [^8][^9]
5. **The hardest case — children who die young — and the LDS answer.** Roughly a third of humanity has died before age two and cannot "benefit" from soul-making in mortality. Ostler answers that any adequate theodicy *requires an afterlife* — and Joseph Smith uniquely supplies **both** an afterlife (an epilogue) **and** a premortal existence (a prologue): "we chose to come here, we chose to confront the evils — it was a free choice." Like consenting medical volunteers (vs. the non-consenting victims of Nazi experiments), spirits consent to enter a specific family with its known propensities. Children who die still lived valuable, consented lives and continue soul-making beyond the veil. [^10][^11][^12]

## Best Quotes
> "Love can't be coerced, it can't be something that's chosen for you by somebody else; it has to be your full-blooded choice." [^5]

> "There's something that is worth confronting anything you can think of in order to realize it, as long as it's not eternal... it would justify any finite pain or evil that we had to endure in order to realize it." [^1]

> "Not only [an] epilogue — you gave a prologue to life. We chose to come here, we chose to confront the evils. It was a free choice... this makes all the difference in the world." [^11]

## LDS Connection
The agape theodicy is *the* distinctively Latter-day Saint theodicy, and it works only because of the restored gospel's metaphysics. Its hinge — "love cannot be coerced" — is the same logic as [[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy|free will being the price of love]], but Ostler can wield it without contradiction because LDS theology grounds free will in **uncreated [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency|intelligences]]**, not in beings God created ex nihilo (which would collapse into the determinism of [[Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism - The Hidden Determinism of Classical Theism|occasionalism]]). The theodicy's two load-bearing moves are uniquely LDS: a **premortal prologue** in which agents freely *consent* to a mortal arena and even to a specific family (see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]] and [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]]), which removes God's culpability for "assigning" suffering; and a **post-mortal epilogue** that lets soul-making continue for those (like children) who get no start here (see [[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]]). It rests on the [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer|organizer model]] of God: a lovingly *ordered* (not recalcitrant) world, governed by [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|eternal law]], whose evils are the byproduct of agency and chaos rather than divine design — which is also why it need not claim each particular evil is individually necessary (see [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]]). Ostler concedes the honest soft spot — the suffering of those who cannot soul-make in mortality is "the toughest part of any theodicy" — and answers it with Joseph Smith's prologue/epilogue rather than pretending it away.
