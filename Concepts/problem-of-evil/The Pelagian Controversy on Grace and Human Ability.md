# The Pelagian Controversy on Grace and Human Ability

## The Point
In the late 4th–early 5th century, Augustine of Hippo and the monk Pelagius disputed **how an individual obtains** the salvation Christ's atoning work made possible. Both presupposed Christ's atonement; they disagreed about grace and human ability. [^1] **Pelagius** held a "perfectionism": humans, made in God's image, possess complete freedom and are "left without excuse if they sin." Grace for Pelagius is "external and passive" — natural faculties plus enlightenment (commandments and Jesus's example). [^2] **Augustine** countered that the Fall wounded human nature, so grace must be "internal and active" — "the real, redeeming presence of God in Christ within us"; even the faith by which we are justified is itself a gift. [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Pelagius: "God has not willed to command anything impossible."** He mocked the excuse "we are only human" as "blind madness," accusing it of charging God with ignorance of his own creation and commands. McGrath summarizes: "since perfection is possible it is obligatory." [^2]
2. **Augustine on grace as healing.** God "is striving to heal and restore wounded human nature"; grace "is not bestowed as a reward for merits, but is given freely, which is why it is called grace." When Paul says we are justified by faith, Augustine takes him to mean God gives "the very faith by which we believe." [^4]
3. **The pendulum metaphor.** Peek frames the whole chapter as a swing: Paul's free grace → fathers fearing cheap grace and emphasizing repentance/obedience → Pelagius swinging too far toward human self-sufficiency → Augustine recalling and amplifying Paul. [^5]
4. **Note the chronology of "original sin."** Augustine's robust doctrine of inherited sinful incapacity was the Western development that framed this debate; it was *not* the universal early consensus (see [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]]).

## Best Quotes
> "We cry out at God and say, 'This is too hard!... We cannot do it!' What blind madness!... God has not willed to command anything impossible." — Pelagius [^2]

> "This grace of Christ, without which neither infants nor grown persons can be saved, is not bestowed as a reward for merits, but is given freely, which is why it is called grace." — Augustine [^4]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints sit between the poles and consciously so. Peek notes some Latter-day Saints, "wary of cheap grace," emphasize works and the real transformative effects of the Atonement, while others warn that overemphasizing works yields "toxic perfectionism." [^5] The LDS reading of [[2 Nephi 25.23|"after all we can do"]] (see [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]]) is essentially an attempt to honor Augustine's free, internal grace *without* Pelagian self-salvation or the doctrine of total inability. Robinson's *Believing Christ* and Uchtdorf's "The Gift of Grace" are cited by Peek as modern LDS contributions to this very current. [^5]
