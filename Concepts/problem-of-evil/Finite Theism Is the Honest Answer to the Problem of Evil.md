# Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil

## The Point
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the problem of evil "chafes classical theism specifically because of creation *ex nihilo*": if God made everything from literally nothing "with no pre-existing matter and no external constraints," then "every earthquake, every child born into suffering, and every act of cruelty" was "chosen from an infinite range of alternatives with nothing constraining the choice."[^1] Remove *ex nihilo* and "you change the character of the problem of evil fundamentally." Under LDS eternal matter and **finite theism**, "the suffering and mortality simply reflects the constraints of a process, not unconstrained omnipotent preference."[^2] The striking claim is that non-LDS philosophers keep reaching this *exact* solve independently: [[David Ray Griffin]], [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]], [[Philip Goff]], and the rabbi Harold Kushner all conclude that a God who "works within an eternal law that he didn't author" is more defensible than the omnipotent God of the creeds.

## Evidence
- **The structural defect Arnold targets is unconstrained omnipotence.** He notes the problem "hasn't been resolved, it's just been managed" since [[J.L. Schellenberg|J.L. Mackie]] laid out its logical form in 1955 (Arnold cites Mackie's 1955 paper); the standard free-will/soul-making replies "only address the moral evil" and not natural evil "like animal suffering before humans existed."[^3] The diagnosis is that an ex-nihilo God "had cosmically unlimited alternatives," so any suffering is a free choice. (Note: the philosopher is J. L. Mackie, distinct from the vault's existing [[J.L. Schellenberg]] note on divine hiddenness.)
- **[[David Ray Griffin]] — finite theism from process philosophy.** Griffin, emeritus at Claremont, "concluded that finite theism, or a God who works within eternal law rather than above it, is the only intellectually honest answer to the problem of evil," in *God, Power, and Evil* (1976) — "decades before LDS apologists were framing [it] in those terms with no engagement with LDS theology."[^4]
- **[[Philip Goff]] — fine-tuning points to a constrained God.** Goff (Durham) moved "from atheism toward theism" because fine-tuning suggests "a life-centered purpose." Crucially, "Goff's move is specifically toward a God who works within mathematical law rather than above it," since "a God who simply chose those constraints from unlimited alternatives faces the same arbitrariness problem as ex nihilo creation." Arnold names this "the connection to LDS cosmology... eternal law that God works within rather than authors."[^5]
- **[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] — the comparative concession.** O'Connor "acknowledged the LDS framework offers a more defensible answer to the problem of evil than classical Christianity" after engaging it directly through the Jubilee format and the follow-up with [[Jacob Hansen]].[^6] This is the same concession documented at length in [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **Harold Kushner — finite theism from grief.** A rabbi who "arrived at finite theism from Jewish theology and personal experience, independently and decades earlier," writing his best-known book while grieving his son's death — a fourth independent vector, from outside both philosophy and Christianity.[^7]
- **Greg Boyd and Clark Pinnock — the open-theist edge.** From evangelical biblical study, both "concluded that a God genuinely constrained by eternal relationship is more consistent with scripture than classical omnipotence" — and "were pushed to the margins of Evangelical Christianity for saying it."[^8] The vault develops the same move in [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]].

## Best Quotes
> "Under ex nihilo, God had unlimited options with no constraints. So every instance of suffering is a direct design choice from a blank slate. Under LDS eternal matter, the suffering and mortality simply reflects the constraints of a process, not unconstrained omnipotent preference." [^2]

> "David Ray Griffin... concluded that finite theism — or a God who works within eternal law rather than above it — is the only intellectually honest answer to the problem of evil. His 1976 book, God, Power, and Evil, made the argument decades before LDS apologists were framing [it] in those terms." [^4]

> "Goff's move is specifically toward a God who works within mathematical law rather than above it, because the physical constraints are themselves the structure, not just free choices made from a blank slate." [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the philosophy-side companion to the vault's existing finitist theodicy. The metaphysic — co-eternal matter and intelligences, a God who *organizes* rather than creates from nothing, and therefore a God genuinely *constrained* — is exactly what [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer]] catalogs and what [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]] argue dissolves the problem of evil that classical theism can only "manage." The convergence is that Griffin, Goff, O'Connor, Kushner, Boyd, and Pinnock reach the constrained God from process philosophy, cosmology, analytic philosophy of religion, Jewish theology, and evangelical exegesis respectively — none of them Latter-day Saints. The remaining frontier, animal suffering, gets its own note: [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]].

**Steelman the critic.** (1) *The aseity cost* — a God bound by external eternal law is no longer the absolute ground of being, and a critic can ask "what grounds the eternal law?" Arnold treats the constraints as simply eternal and unauthored, which trades a smaller God for a coherent theodicy. (2) *Why these constraints?* — relocating suffering to "the constraints of a process" still leaves open why the eternal structure is arranged so costly a process is required; Arnold concedes finite theism "doesn't claim to have resolved this." (3) [[Paul Draper]]'s evolutionary objection (next note) presses whether *any* theism, finite included, fits the data better than indifference.
