# The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God

## The Point
**Objection:** The reality of suffering and evil shows God does not exist — either as a strict logical contradiction (J. L. Mackie: an omnipotent, perfectly good God would eliminate all evil) or as evidence making God improbable (William Rowe's "evidential argument": some suffering, like a fawn burned in a forest fire, seems pointless).[^1] **Reply:** Feser supplies a *defense* (showing evil is consistent with God), which is enough to rebut the objection even without a full theodicy.[^2] Omnipotence does not extend to the self-contradictory, and a good being need not eliminate evils whose removal would also remove greater goods that presuppose them; and since we have *independent* demonstrations that a good God exists, we already know there must be such goods, whether or not we can see them.[^3]

## Evidence
- **Against Mackie's logical argument.** His second premise fails twice. (1) Omnipotence has no power over contradictions — "even God cannot make a round square." So if some goods logically require tolerating certain evils, even God cannot have the goods without the evils.[^4] (2) "It is not true to say that a good thing will eliminate all evil as far as it can," since removing an evil can remove a dependent good — like a parent who could spare a child the unpleasantness of homework only by preventing the child from learning.[^4]
- **The goods that presuppose evil.** Feser concedes (with Mackie) that God *could* have made free creatures who never sin. But such a world could not contain *all* the moral goods that actually exist: forgiveness and mercy presuppose people who really do wrong; courage presupposes danger; compassion presupposes others who suffer; self-sacrifice presupposes a real good given up.[^5] Mackie himself later conceded the problem of evil is no "conclusive disproof of traditional theism."[^6]
- **Against Rowe's evidential argument.** Rowe admits we "cannot know with certainty" there is no greater good in the fawn's suffering, but thinks it rational to believe there is none. Feser's rebuttal: that is rational "only if we don't already have independent reason to think that God exists." But we do — the five demonstrations — so we have independent reason to conclude there *is* such a good. For the evidential argument to bite, its defenders "would first have to provide an independent refutation of the arguments of this book. And if they could do that, they wouldn't need the 'evidential argument' in the first place."[^7]
- **Why we cannot see the goods.** That we don't know God's reason for every evil "is precisely what we should expect." Given an immortal soul and an endless afterlife, even the worst sufferings of this life (which "do not last") can be outweighed; and "we are no more in a position to understand what the afterlife is like than a small child is in a position to understand what adulthood is like."[^8] The mere *possibility* of such outweighing goods suffices for the defense.[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "It is not true to say that a good thing will eliminate all evil as far as it can. For to get rid of some evils may entail also getting rid of the goods that presuppose those evils." [^4]

> "Acts of forgiveness and mercy are not possible unless there are people who actually do evil things for which they can be forgiven ... You cannot have courage unless there is danger ... You cannot show compassion unless there are people who have suffered." [^5]

> "For the 'evidential argument' to succeed as a challenge to those arguments, its defenders would first have to provide an independent refutation of the arguments of this book. And if they could do that, they wouldn't need the 'evidential argument' in the first place." [^7]

## LDS Connection
Feser's defense is a strong ally against the atheist who wields suffering as a disproof, and much of it is common ground: the goods-presuppose-evils insight, the free-will dimension, and the soul-making appeal to an afterlife all resonate with Restoration teaching (compare [[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy]] and [[The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil]]). The deepest divergence is in *how each tradition gets God off the hook*. Feser, as a classical theist, holds that God creates everything — including every soul and every free will — **ex nihilo**, so his defense must rest on permission plus outweighing goods. The Restoration cuts the knot differently: because human intelligence is **uncreated and co-eternal**, God did not author the wills that choose evil, so He is not the ultimate source of human sin in the first place (see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]] and [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]]). Where Feser leans on the immortality of the soul as a *future* outweighing, LDS theodicy adds a *prior* dimension — a pre-mortal estate in which agents consented to mortality. Critically, the LDS move flows from rejecting creation ex nihilo (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]) and from God's being constrained by eternal law and eternal materials rather than unconstrained (see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). Be fair to Feser: his "defense vs. theodicy" distinction is methodologically careful, and his point that demonstrated theism reverses the burden of proof on evil is sound on its own terms; the Latter-day Saint simply has an *additional* resource — uncreated agency — that the classical theist's ex nihilo commitment forecloses.
