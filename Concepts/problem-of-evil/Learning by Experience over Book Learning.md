# Learning by Experience over Book Learning

## The Claim
God permits a messy, error-prone mortality — for prophets and ordinary people alike — because experiential learning produces "a richer, deeper, more fundamental knowledge than book learning." He could have issued "a manual about mortality" and skipped Earth, but "we don't learn that way." Knowing something by having done it (and suffered its consequences) differs in kind from knowing it abstractly.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Knowing-by-doing vs knowing-about (rated: strong; experiential epistemology).** "My sense of knowing something is wrong is very different... after I've done it and discovered the consequences were really bad." Reading about France differs from going to France.[^2] Even Christ "learned by His experience... to succor people and to feel with them in their affliction" — Peterson doubts the Lord "could have come here having read a book about the mortal experience."[^3]

2. **The driving / power analogy (rated: strong within LDS framework).** God wants to give us "all His power and authority," but cannot safely give it to someone "who has never exercised any independent judgment." You don't refuse to ever let a child practice driving, then hand over the keys — "A child would end up in a ditch." See [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]].[^4]

3. **Satan's counterfeit: reward without sacrifice (rated: moderate; theological).** The host frames the Fall narrative: Satan tempted Adam and Eve "to partake of the fruit without sacrifice" — to "have without experience," which "means nothing." Growth requires the experiential cost. [[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:10-11]] has Adam and Eve bless the Fall precisely because without it they "never should have known good and evil."[^5]

4. **But experience alone is insufficient (rated: important qualifier).** Peterson guards against the opposite error: "Book learning isn't enough" does *not* mean experience replaces scripture. "We can be pretty dense and not learn from experience very quickly." The productive method is the *interplay*: read scripture → act → mess up → return to scripture and see something new. Boyd K. Packer: a needed verse "was not in the Book of Mormon any previous time I'd read it" — i.e., experience made him finally see it.[^6]

5. **Marked-scriptures illustration (rated: illustrative).** Peterson's college scriptures show passages he marked at 17–18 that no longer seem important, and passages he "sailed right past" (e.g., Jacob's lament about being "despised of our brethren") that only later trials made vivid. Experience changes what scripture says to you.[^7]

6. **Institutional version (rated: applied).** Colonizing Utah, the Saints learned by experiment which crops worked; leaders like Elder Perry and Harold B. Lee tried programs and kept what worked. "The Lord has never dictated every step to us. ... He doesn't want little robots."[^8] Backed by [[D&C 121.39]] ("learned by sad experience").

## Best Quotes

> "The learning from experience is a richer, deeper, more fundamental knowledge than book learning."[^1]

> "If you read all about France, then go to France, it's different than it was reading about it."[^2]

> "It's a combination of experience and book learning. ... you read the scriptures, you go out, you try to implement them, you mess up, you come back, you read the scriptures, you'll see something else that you hadn't realized."[^6]
