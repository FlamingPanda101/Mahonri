# We Cannot Judge That God Lacks Reasons for Suffering

## The Point
The evidential (probabilistic) problem of evil concedes that God and suffering *can* coexist, but argues there is so much apparently pointless suffering that God's existence is rendered improbable.[^1] Craig's first reply targets the load-bearing inference — *from "this suffering looks pointless to me" to "God probably has no good reason for it."* That inference assumes we occupy a vantage point we do not have. We are "limited in space and time, and in intelligence and insight," whereas God "sees every detail of history from beginning to end, and orders it through people's free decisions and actions in order to achieve his purposes."[^2] So suffering that "appears pointless within our limited scope of understanding may be seen to have been justly permitted by God within his wider framework."[^3] The atheist is not entitled to the confident probability judgment the argument requires.

## Evidence
The core is an argument from human cognitive limitation, not a claim to know God's specific reasons. Because God "may have to allow a great deal of suffering along the way" to achieve purposes routed through billions of free choices, the causal threads connecting any one evil to later goods quickly outrun finite tracing — a point Frank Turek develops as the **ripple effect**, that only "the infinite God, who can see every event at once, could know the good reasons for allowing each evil event to run its course" (see [[The Ripple Effect - Why We Cannot See the Good in Every Evil]]). Craig's analogy is perspectival: "Sometimes what we experience makes no sense until we gain a wider perspective and see the big picture designed by the Creator."[^4] This shifts the burden: the evidential argument needs the premise that God *probably* lacks morally sufficient reasons, but a finite knower is in no position to assert that probability with confidence — the same move Edward Feser presses against William Rowe's "fawn in the forest fire" (see [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]]). Note what the reply does *not* claim: not that we can see the reason, only that our failure to see one is poor evidence that none exists.

## Best Quotes
> "We are not in a position to say with any confidence that God probably lacks reasons for allowing the suffering in the world. The problem is that we're limited in space and time, and in intelligence and insight." [^2]

> "Suffering which appears pointless within our limited scope of understanding may be seen to have been justly permitted by God within his wider framework." [^3]

> "Sometimes what we experience makes no sense until we gain a wider perspective and see the big picture designed by the Creator." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The appeal to a limited mortal vantage sits naturally within the Restoration's framing of this life as a **finite probation set inside an eternal scope**. Latter-day Saints affirm that God "knoweth all things, for all things are present before mine eyes" (Moses 1:6; cf. D&C 38:2), so the gap between His vantage and ours is not merely quantitative but woven into the plan: mortals "see through a glass, darkly" by design, walking by faith precisely because the wider framework is withheld for now. The Restoration adds resources Craig's general appeal leaves implicit. First, **opposition in all things** — "it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things" (2 Nephi 2:11) — means apparent pointlessness can be the very friction agency requires, not a defect. Second, the eternal horizon stretches *both* directions: a pre-mortal estate in which agents consented to mortality and a post-mortal continuation in which "all things shall be restored" (Alma 40:23; Alma 42), so the "wider framework" is a literal eternal arc, not a hopeful gesture. Be careful not to overclaim: this reply does not *explain* any particular evil, and Latter-day Saints, like Craig, should resist glib answers to specific suffering — the point is epistemic humility about our vantage, paired with trust grounded in independent knowledge that God is good (see [[Probability of God Must Be Weighed Against All the Evidence]]).
