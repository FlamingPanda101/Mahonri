# Calvinism Consistency with Atheism on Free Will

## The Claim

[[Calvinism]] is the most logically consistent form of creedal Christianity on
the question of free will — because it concedes what atheism also concedes:
that libertarian free will does not exist. Calvinists and atheists agree that
human choices are ultimately determined by something outside the individual
(God's sovereign decree in Calvinism; physical causes in atheism). However,
Calvinism harbors an internal inconsistency regarding the nature of the human
"will" — claiming we have a will (enslaved to God or Satan) but not a *free*
will, a distinction that collapses under scrutiny.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Calvinists and Atheists Agree: No Libertarian Free Will (Evidence Rating: Primary — Theological)

[[Hayden Carroll]] identifies Calvinism as uniquely honest among creedal
Christians:

> "There is one group of Christians who do agree with the atheists and it's the
> Calvinists... They're very consistent in their soteriology and in their view
> of free will. They say, 'God — everything that happens happens because God
> wanted it to happen.' And so in that way they would say, 'Yeah, we don't have
> libertarian free will.'"[^1]

This is a striking argument: rather than using Calvinism as a foil, Carroll
*credits* Calvinists for their logical consistency. They see the implications
of divine sovereignty + *creation ex nihilo* and accept them. This is
preferable (from a logical standpoint) to Arminians who try to hold both divine
sovereignty and libertarian free will in tension.

### 2. Calvinists Are "in the Atheist Camp as Far as Free Will Goes" (Evidence Rating: Primary)

> "They're in the atheist camp as far as free will goes."[^1]

The common ground: both worldviews hold that your choices are ultimately
determined by something you didn't choose and can't control. For the atheist,
it's physical causation. For the Calvinist, it's God's eternal decree. The
*experience* of choosing is preserved, but the *freedom* to have done otherwise
is denied.

### 3. The Internal Inconsistency: "Enslaved Will" vs. Divine Determinism (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Critique)

Carroll identifies a remaining inconsistency:

> "Now, let me just clarify. They wouldn't say we don't have a will. They would
> say we don't have a free will. We're either enslaved — we talked about last
> time — to the devil or to God. But God's grace is irresistible. So you cannot
> resist it."[^2]

The problem:[^3]
1. Calvinists say we have a "will" — but it's enslaved (to sin or to God).
2. But who determines whether you're enslaved to sin or to God? God does,
   through unconditional election.
3. If God predetermines everything, including whether you're "enslaved" to Him
   or to Satan, then the distinction between having a "will" and being a puppet
   collapses.
4. Carroll: "Either in the end God will either choose us to be enslaved to him
   or we will be enslaved to [Satan]... So there is an inconsistency."[^3]

### 4. The Performative Nature of Calvinist Preaching (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

David Butler adds: "What's the point of any of this? It's all very performative
their preaching and stuff because... God created them. This is what God
wanted."[^1]

If everything is predetermined by God, then preaching, evangelism, and moral
exhortation are theater — God has already determined who will respond and who
won't. The Calvinist response (that God ordains the means as well as the ends,
so preaching IS the means by which God saves the elect) preserves consistency
but at the cost of making human action purely instrumental.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Compatibilist Free Will Is a Genuine Form of Freedom
**Steelman (Calvinist):** Calvinists are compatibilists. They affirm that humans
make real choices according to their desires and nature — and that this is the
only kind of freedom worth having. Libertarian free will (the ability to have
done otherwise) is incoherent; no one, not even God, can make a choice
uncaused by their nature. Freedom is doing what you want without external
coercion, not freedom from causation.

**Response:** This is the strongest Calvinist response and Carroll's video
doesn't fully engage it. However, the LDS counter would be: (a) If God
determined your nature without your input, your "free" choice according to that
nature is still downstream of God's determination. (b) Compatibilism may be
sufficient for legal/moral responsibility in human courts, but it's
insufficient for ultimate moral responsibility before a God who designed you to
sin and then punishes you for it.

### Objection 2: Calvinism's "Will" vs. "Free Will" Distinction Is Coherent
**Steelman:** Calvinists distinguish between *voluntary* action (you act
according to your desires without external compulsion) and *libertarian*
freedom (the ability to choose contrary to your nature). The former is real;
the latter is a philosophical fiction. When Calvinists say we have a "will"
that is "enslaved," they mean our desires are bound to sin (or to God) — but we
still act voluntarily. This is not inconsistency; it's precision.

**Response:** Carroll's point is narrower: if God predetermined whether your
will is enslaved to sin or to God, then the "voluntary" nature of your actions
is downstream of a determination you had no role in. The distinction between
"God made me do it" and "I did it voluntarily because God made me the kind of
person who would voluntarily do it" is a distinction without a difference for
ultimate moral responsibility.
