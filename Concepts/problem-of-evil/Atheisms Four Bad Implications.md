# Atheism's Four Bad Implications

## The Claim

[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that atheism, when followed to its logical
conclusions, entails four implications that most people — including many
atheists — find repugnant or contrary to their lived experience:

1. **No Free Will** — Materialist determinism eliminates libertarian
   free will. Human choices are the product of prior physical causes (genetics,
   environment, brain chemistry) over which we have no control.
2. **No Objective Morality** — Without a transcendent moral lawgiver,
   "right" and "wrong" are human constructs, preferences, or social
   conventions. There is no action that is *actually* wrong — only actions we
   happen to dislike.
3. **No Genuine Consciousness** — If matter is all there is, and
   non-conscious matter preceded consciousness, it is inexplicable how
   consciousness could arise. The consistent atheist must deny consciousness is
   real (as [[Daniel Dennett]] does).
4. **Nihilism (No Purpose)** — Without God, free will, morality, or
   consciousness, human existence has no objective meaning or purpose. We are,
   in [[Richard Dawkins]]' words, products of "pitiless indifference."[^1]

Carroll calls atheism "stupid" not because atheists are unintelligent (he
acknowledges many are "really intelligent"[^2]) but because atheism "lacks
common sense" — it requires denying things that every person intuitively knows
to be true about their own experience: that they make real choices, that some
things are truly wrong, that they are conscious, and that their lives have
meaning.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Atheist Thought Leaders Concede These Implications (Argument weight: Secondary — Concession from Opponents)

Carroll's argument draws much of its force from the fact that prominent atheist
intellectuals *agree* with these implications:

- **No Free Will:** "The real strong hitters... [[Sam Harris]], [[Christopher Hitchens]], [[Alex O'Connor]], they all agree that yeah, we actually don't
  have free will. If atheism is a correct description of reality."[^3]
- **No Morality:** [[Richard Dawkins]]: "The universe we observe has precisely
  the properties we should expect if there is at bottom no design, no purpose,
  no evil, no good, nothing but pitiless indifference."[^1] [[Alex Rosenberg]]:
  "There's no moral difference between the acts of Mother Teresa and those of
  Hitler."[^4]
- **No Consciousness:** [[Daniel Dennett]]: "Consciousness is an illusion...
  we're not actually conscious. We're just — it's an illusion. We're just
  playing out. There is no self. It's a trick the brain plays on us."[^5]
- **Nihilism:** [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]: "Nihilism as a psychological state
  will have to be reached... the recognition of the long waste of strength, the
  agony of the in vain insecurity, the lack of any opportunity to recover or to
  regain composure."[^6]

### 2. The Implications Are Interconnected (Argument weight: Tertiary — Structural)

Carroll notes these four are not independent problems but form a chain:[^7]
- If there's no free will, moral responsibility collapses (you can't blame
  someone for what they were causally determined to do).
- If there's no morality, the concepts of "better" and "worse" lose meaning,
  which undermines purpose.
- If there's no consciousness, there's no *subject* to whom purpose or morality
  could matter.
- Nihilism is the cumulative endpoint: nothing matters because there's no one
  to whom it could matter, no standard by which it could matter, and no choice
  that could change anything.

### 3. Most Atheists Are Inconsistent — They Live As If These Things Are Real (Argument weight: Tertiary — Pragmatic)

> "Most atheists who don't know a lot about the implications don't think that
> way."[^3]

Carroll's argument is that the *average* atheist believes in free will,
morality, consciousness, and purpose — and in doing so, they are borrowing from
a theistic worldview they claim to reject. They are "living on borrowed
capital," in the phrase often associated with [[C.S. Lewis]] (though Carroll
doesn't use that exact phrase here). The consistent atheist — a Sam Harris or
Daniel Dennett — embraces the four implications. The inconsistent atheist — who
says "of course I have free will" — hasn't thought through the logical
consequences of their materialism.[^8]

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Compatibilism — Free Will and Determinism Can Coexist
**Steelman:** Many atheist philosophers (e.g., Daniel Dennett himself) are
compatibilists: they argue that free will is compatible with determinism. Free
will doesn't mean your choices are uncaused; it means your choices flow from
your own desires, character, and reasoning — even if those are themselves
causally determined. This is the kind of free will worth wanting.

**Response (from the video's perspective):** Carroll and the hosts consistently
assume *libertarian* free will (the ability to have done otherwise) as the only
meaningful kind. Compatibilism, on their view, is simply determinism with nicer
language. If your "choice" was inevitable given prior causes, it's not a choice
in any morally significant sense. The Calvinist parallel is instructive: see
[[Calvinism Consistency with Atheism on Free Will]].

### Objection 2: Atheists Can Ground Morality in Human Flourishing
**Steelman:** Sam Harris (*The Moral Landscape*) argues that morality can be
grounded in the objective fact of conscious creatures' well-being. We don't
need God to know that suffering is bad. Moral truths are facts about what
promotes or diminishes well-being, discoverable by science.

**Response:** Carroll doesn't engage Harris's moral landscape directly in this
video. However, the implicit LDS counterargument would be: (a) "well-being" is
a value judgment that itself requires justification — why *ought* we maximize
well-being? (b) Without free will, the concept of "ought" is incoherent — you
can't have moral obligations if you can't choose otherwise. (c) Even Harris's
framework can't ground the *objective wrongness* of acts that increase
well-being for some at the expense of others — the is-ought gap remains.

### Objection 3: Calling Atheism "Stupid" Is Rhetorical, Not Philosophical
**Steelman:** The banner "Atheism is Unreasonable and Stupid" is designed to
provoke, not illuminate. By Carroll's own admission, "stupid" means "lacking
common sense" — but many things that lack common sense (quantum mechanics,
relativity) are true. Argument from incredulity is a fallacy.

**Response:** Carroll distinguishes: "I think a lot of atheists are actually
really intelligent. I think atheism if you want to believe it even though the
data that we have actually goes against it... Once you accept it, you have to
accept these... implications of atheism that go against common sense."[^2] The
"stupid" label attaches to the worldview's implications, not to atheists as
people.
