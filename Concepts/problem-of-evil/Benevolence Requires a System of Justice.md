# Benevolence Requires a System of Justice

## The Claim
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]]'s closing-stretch claim: omnibenevolence *entails* a system of justice, including hell. "The concept of hell is to be just, and God being just is part of his love. There is no love when a God is indifferent... That's the way God shows his love — through a system of justice." Asked directly whether benevolence and justice are separable, he affirms "benevolence requires a system of justice."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Indifference objection** — rate: moderate. A god who never opposed evil would be *indifferent*, not loving; so the *capacity* to punish is arguably part of perfect love. *(moral)* [^1]
2. **Justice as love's mode** — Gavin frames retributive justice as an expression, not a limitation, of benevolence. *(theological)* [^2]

**Steelman of the negative (Hayden):** the move equivocates on the debate term. The resolution is *omnibenevolence* ("willing the good of every moral subject without exception"), and Hayden gets Gavin to concede that "is it worse to go to hell than to go to heaven? — Yes." If eternal hell is *worse for that person*, then subjecting a foreknown-damned creature to it — even justly — does not *will that person's good*; it wills their just *destruction*. A coherent justice can require punishing *existing* wrongdoers, but it cannot by itself justify *creating* a person whose foreknown end is that punishment. So "benevolence requires justice" is true but does not rescue *omni*benevolence in the face of [[The Creation Dilemma]]: justice toward the guilty ≠ willing the good of *each* subject without exception. There is also a distinct sub-question (untouched here) of whether *infinite/eternal* punishment for *finite* sin is *just* at all — see [[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]].

## Best Quotes
