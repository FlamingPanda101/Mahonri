# The Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good of Meaning

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The final fallback when argument runs out: "We just can't understand God's ways. It's a mystery." The contradiction between divine goodness and eternal torment is a mystery beyond human comprehension, to be accepted in faith rather than solved.

## The Rebuttal
1. **There is a difference between hard-to-understand and meaningless — rate: strong.** Mystery is legitimate when a claim still *makes sense* but the mechanism is opaque. The doctor analogy: "This medicine will heal you, but I can't fully explain how" is a real mystery — *healing* still means something. But "This medicine will kill you, but it's actually healing you in a higher sense" is not a mystery; it has *emptied the word healing of meaning* [^1]. Likewise, saying "God is good but knowingly creates people for eternal suffering" does not deepen the meaning of "good" — it voids it [^2]. **Steelman of the theist:** classical theism distinguishes *univocal*, *equivocal*, and *analogical* predication. Aquinas holds "good" applied to God is *analogical* — neither identical to nor wholly unlike human goodness. On this view divine goodness can transcend human moral categories without being meaningless, because it is the *eminent source* of goodness. The video's dichotomy ("either it means what we think or it means nothing") arguably ignores the analogical middle.
2. **It reduces "God is good" to "God is God" — rate: strong.** If "good" can include foreknowingly creating people for hell, then to call God good is just to say "whatever God does, we label good by default." That is not a defense of goodness but a surrender of the word [^2]. This is a charge of *vacuity*: a goodness that forbids nothing predicts nothing.
3. **Self-undermining for apologetics — rate: framing.** The video grants real mystery exists ("there are things about God I can't fully grasp") to pre-empt the strawman, then argues the mystery card is being misused to shield a *contradiction*, not an unknown — the same distinction it presses in [[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]] ("that is not a mystery to be accepted; that is a contradiction to be acknowledged").
4. **The opponent in fact retreats here — rate: observed.** [[Hayden Carroll]] presses the identical argument against [[Gavin (Catholic Debater)|Gavin]] and predicts mystery will be the fallback; under cross-examination, asked how creating someone foreknown for hell wills their good, Gavin answers "that's where I would leave it at mystery… we will not know in this life," confirming the prediction. [^3] The collapse of "God is good" into "God is God" is developed in [[Good Becomes Synonymous with Whatever God Does]], and the whole exchange is the mystery exit of [[The Creation Dilemma]].

**Reply to the analogical-predication steelman (above):** Carroll grants that "good" said of God may be *analogical* (Aquinas), but insists analogy still requires that divine goodness be *recognizably continuous* with the human concept — otherwise "analogy" has quietly become *equivocation*. A "goodness" that does what no good human agent could do (knowingly create a person for hell) has crossed that line; legitimate mystery is ignorance of *how*, not a redefinition that contradicts the term's core content. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "If the doctor says, 'This medicine will kill you, but it's actually healing you in a higher sense,' well, that's not a mystery anymore. That's the word healing being completely emptied of meaning." [^1]

> "If good can include that, well then good doesn't actually mean anything. You're not saying God is good. You're just saying God is God and whatever he does, we call it good by default. That's not a defense of goodness. That's giving up on the word." [^2]

> "Mystery is not the same as meaninglessness… it becomes synonymous with whatever God does. Instead of saying that God is good, it's like saying God is God. It renders the word meaningless." [^4]

> "That's where I would leave it at mystery. I just don't know why this prophecy was written so a man like Judas would end up in hell." — Gavin [^3]
