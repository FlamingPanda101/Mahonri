# God Works Through Fallible Prophets

## The Claim
God deliberately accomplishes His work through flawed, fallible human beings — including prophets — rather than through "robots" or "automatons." This is not a defect in the plan but the design: "The Lord uses flawed people. He doesn't have much alternative," and more strongly, He *chooses* to, so that humans (and prophets) can grow.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Every biblical/Restoration prophet is shown erring (rated: strong, scriptural).** "Moses has all sorts of issues and is rebuked on occasion." Even [[Joseph Smith]] was slow: he received the [[D&C 76|three-degrees-of-glory vision]] in February 1832 but it "didn't really impact him for several years"; he lost the 116 pages after repeatedly pestering the Lord until allowed to "flub up and mess up big time."[^2] Peterson reads Joseph's slowness as evidence the doctrine "didn't come from his mind" — it took years to sink in.

2. **Schindler/Oskar Schindler as the limiting case (rated: moderate, illustrative).** A "bad man in a lot of ways" who nonetheless rose to genuine heroism saving Jewish lives — God can use a person who is heroic in one domain and ordinary or worse in others.[^3] *Steelman:* a critic may say this proves too much — if any flaw is compatible with prophethood, the office loses evidential force. Peterson bounds this with [[Prophets Are Fallible Today]] (no "gross" error that leads the Church astray).

3. **The free-will / agency rationale (rated: strong within LDS framework).** God could remove our bad instincts but "it would be a violation of my agency." Learning to overcome flaws *is* the point of mortality; a God who dictated every step would produce beings who never exercised independent judgment — to whom He could never safely give "all His power and authority."[^4] The driving analogy: you don't refuse to ever let a child practice driving and then hand over the keys.

4. **The Fall as plan, not failure (rated: strong, scriptural).** [[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:10-11]] has Adam and Eve *bless* the transgression as the precondition of seed, knowledge of good and evil, and "the joy of our redemption." The temple makes provision for a Savior "before we've really done much" — the plan always anticipated stumbling and rescue, not flawless lives.[^5]

5. **Brigham as case study (rated: applied).** Brigham's "unique set of skills" came bundled with rough edges; "you pick up both sides of the stick." The receptiveness and devotion God chose, not a guarantee of perfection.[^6] See [[Brigham Young Saved the Church Through the Keys]].

## Best Quotes

> "He works through human beings. He always does. ... But He doesn't work through robots. He doesn't work through automatons. He works through people who sometimes are dense and don't get it."[^7]

> "He could come in and take away all my bad instincts... but it would be a violation of my agency to do that. I need to learn, and part of my growth is learning to overcome those things."[^4]

> "How can He give [all His power] to somebody who has never exercised any independent judgment before? ... You don't say, okay, I refuse to ever train you to drive the car... Now, here are the keys. A child would end up in a ditch."[^4]
