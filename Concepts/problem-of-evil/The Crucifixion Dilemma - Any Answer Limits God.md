# The Crucifixion Dilemma - Any Answer Limits God

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] presses a two-horned dilemma designed to show that *even the traditional Christian* is committed to a limited God — using the cross itself as the lever. If God is genuinely omnipotent, "Why isn't there a snap of the fingers and it's eternal bliss for everyone forever? ... Why is Jesus dying on a cross?" Hansen argues that every possible answer collapses into one of two options: "Jesus is either dying on that cross because God wants him to die on that cross, or he's dying on that cross because there's no other way for God to do it. If God *wants* Jesus to suffer on that cross — meaning there were all sorts of other ways to do this without him dying — then God is a perverse psycho. Or two, if you say that no, this is the only way he can do what he needs to do — as soon as you say that's the only way, you're saying there's some sort of a limit on what God is able to do." Therefore "any answer they're going to give is going to put some kind of a limit on God."[^1] [[Philip Goff]] concurs that this lands: "So we're of one mind then that to solve the problem of evil and suffering, you have to limit God in some ways."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
- **The dilemma is exhaustive over divine motive.** Either the suffering of the cross was **necessary** (no other route to the goal) or it was **gratuitous** (other routes existed but God chose suffering anyway). There is no third option for an action God performs. The gratuitous horn makes God morally monstrous ("a perverse psycho"); the necessary horn concedes a real constraint on omnipotence. **Rate: a tight, genuinely forced choice** — its force is that it does not attack God's *existence* but extracts *limitation* from the believer's own central symbol.
- **It turns the atonement into evidence for finitism.** The cross is the one event traditional Christianity *cannot* call optional theater — it is "the only way" by the logic of substitution and justice. But "the only way" *is* the admission Hansen wants. So the more seriously the creedal Christian takes the necessity of Calvary, the more firmly they have conceded that God's power is bounded by what genuinely must be (cf. [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]]: an omnipotent God who could save by fiat makes the atonement gratuitous). This generalizes the move beyond the cross to **character-formation**: "Why doesn't God snap his fingers and develop my character? ... Any answer they're going to give is going to put some kind of a limit on God."[^3]
- **Goff supplies the parallel for natural evil.** Goff extends the same logic to creation: "the only way God could create intelligent life is by creating a universe with the right numbers... God knew it was going to be messy... it's that or nothing." Both interlocutors thus arrive at the identical structure — *the good was unobtainable without the cost* — applied to the cross (Hansen) and to natural selection (Goff). See [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **Steelman the traditional reply.** The classical theist has answers the dilemma must engage. (1) *Fittingness, not necessity:* the cross was not the *only* possible means but the most *fitting* revelation of divine love and justice — God chose it freely without other means being equally good, so neither "psycho" nor "limited" follows cleanly. (2) *The limit is God's own nature, not an external cap:* God "cannot" act against his goodness or deny himself (2 Tim 2:13), which classical theists insist is not a deficiency in power but its perfection. Hansen's rejoinder is that "fittingness" still implies the alternatives were *worse* — i.e. constrained by something — and that a self-limit grounded in nature is exactly the move he is forcing the traditionalist to make explicit (developed in [[A Primitive Limit in God's Nature vs an External Eternal Constraint (Goff vs LDS)]]).

## LDS Relevance
This is a compact **apologetic wedge**: rather than defending LDS finitism on the back foot, Hansen shows that the *creedal* Christian's own soteriology already presupposes a God who cannot reach his ends costlessly — so the disagreement with the Restoration is not *whether* God is limited but *where the limit is grounded*. For the Latter-day Saint the answer is clean: God is constrained by **eternal law and the nature of free agency**, so the cross, the fall, and mortal struggle are all "the only way" to bring agents to their divine potential without coercing them (see [[An Omnipotent God Cannot Force a Free Creature to Choose Good]] and [[God Will Not Force You Into Heaven Against Your Will]]). The dilemma also exposes why an *unconstrained* God is theologically corrosive: if God could have produced exaltation by fiat, then every cost — Gethsemane included — becomes a gratuitous cruelty rather than a necessity of love, which is precisely the reductio the vault runs in [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]]. Hansen's move lets the LDS apologist convert the most cherished datum of mainstream Christianity — the necessity of the atonement — into a premise for the constrained God the Restoration teaches.
