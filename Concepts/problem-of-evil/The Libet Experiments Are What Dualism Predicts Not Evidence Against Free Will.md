# The Libet Experiments Are What Dualism Predicts Not Evidence Against Free Will

## The Point
The most famous neuroscientific challenge to free will is Benjamin Libet's button-press experiment, in which the brain signal to move the hand appears a fraction of a second *before* the subject reports being aware of deciding to press.[^1] Some (not Libet himself) read this as proof that motor functions cause the action prior to any conscious choice, eliminating free will.[^2] Craig answers that this inference fails: because nerve signals travel at a finite velocity, a time delay between the soul's decision and the *conscious awareness* of that decision is exactly what we should expect — so the data is precisely what a dualist-interactionist would predict, not evidence against freedom.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **What Libet actually found.** Subjects were asked to press a button; the neural signals in the brain to move the hand occurred "a fraction of a second before the agent was aware of making the choice to push the button."[^1]
2. **The deterministic spin is an interpretation, not the result.** "Some — although not Libet himself" — tried to read this as evidence against free will, claiming the brain's motor functions caused the press "prior to the conscious decision."[^2]
3. **Finite signal velocity dissolves the puzzle.** Because neural signals travel "at a finite velocity... along our nerves," it is "no surprise at all" that there is a lag between a decision and conscious awareness of it — awareness "arises through brain states" that take time to transmit.[^3]
4. **The sequence a dualist predicts.** First the soul makes a free decision; "a split second later" the brain state that brings the decision to consciousness occurs; *then* the muscular contractions fire and the hand moves. The experiment is "exactly what a dualist interactionist would expect to happen."[^4]
5. **No elimination of freedom.** Therefore the experiments do nothing "to eliminate freedom of the will."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "These experiments are exactly what a dualist interactionist would expect to happen — namely, the soul would make a free decision, and then a split second later the brain state which brings this decision to consciousness would occur, and then after that the muscular contractions would take place and the hand would move." [^4]

> "Because of the finite velocity at which neural signals travel through the brain along our nerves, it's no surprise at all that there would be a time delay between the decision of the soul or the mind to do something and the consciousness... of that state." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Restoration theology rests on moral agency — the capacity "to act and not to be acted upon" (2 Nephi 2:13–14, 26), grounded in the eternal, uncreated intelligence of each person ("Man was also in the beginning with God. Intelligence... was not created or made," D&C 93:29). A neuroscience that reduced choice to prior brain causes would gut the accountability the plan of salvation presupposes (see [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]). Craig's reply matters to Latter-day Saints because it shows the headline experiment does not establish that conclusion at all: the soul as a genuine agent acting *through* the brain (an instrument) is fully consistent with — even predicted by — the Libet data, paralleling the broader case that the mind is not the brain (see [[The Mind Is Not the Brain]]).
