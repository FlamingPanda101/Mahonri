# Moral Responsibility Without Free Will — Harris's Reconciliation

## The Point
Having denied free will, [[Sam Harris]] tries to preserve moral responsibility without it. To "take responsibility" for an act, he says, is just for the act to be "sufficiently in keeping with [one's] thoughts, intentions, beliefs, and desires to be considered an extension of them"; responsibility tracks "the overall complexion of one's mind, not... the metaphysics of mental cause and effect."[^1] We may therefore still condemn an intention to do harm and judge degrees of guilt by the facts of a case — "without any recourse to notions of free will."[^2] But Harris concedes one casualty: while responsibility survives, **retributive justice does not**, because "the urge for retribution... depend[s] upon our not seeing the underlying causes of human behavior."[^3]

## Evidence
- **Responsibility = fit between act and character.** If Harris found himself "standing in the market naked, intent upon stealing... anchovies," he would feel "not responsible," because the act is out of character; responsibility is about whether behavior expresses the global properties of one's mind.[^1]
- **The five-shooters thought experiment.** Five killings, identical outcome, differing background (a 4-year-old; an abused 12-year-old; an abused adult; a non-abused psychopath; the same psychopath with a golf-ball MPFC tumor). Our graded blame tracks the *intention* and the conditions affecting it, not any libertarian free will.[^4] "What we condemn in another person is the intention to do harm."[^2]
- **Why conscious planning is most blameworthy.** Consciousness "is the context in which our intentions become completely available to us"; a deliberated murder "really reflects the sort of person you are," so "it makes sense for the rest of society to worry about you."[^5]
- **Containment, not desert.** "Clearly, we need to build prisons for people who are intent upon harming others. But if we could incarcerate earthquakes and hurricanes for their crimes, we would build prisons for them as well."[^6] Death-row inmates have "some combination of bad genes, bad parents, bad ideas, and bad luck — which of these... were they responsible for?"[^6]
- **The "cure for evil."** If psychopathy could be cured "like vitamin D," withholding the cure as punishment would be senseless — exposing the retributive impulse as resting "on a cognitive and emotional illusion."[^7] Harris allows that a *"sham form of retribution could still be moral"* if it deters, but only as social engineering, not desert.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "To say that I was responsible for my behavior is simply to say that what I did was sufficiently in keeping with my thoughts, intentions, beliefs, and desires to be considered an extension of them." [^1]

> "If we could incarcerate earthquakes and hurricanes for their crimes, we would build prisons for them as well." [^6]

> "A retributive impulse, based upon the idea that each person is the free author of his thoughts and actions, rests on a cognitive and emotional illusion — and perpetuates a moral one." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the load-bearing reconciliation of Harris's project, and the place where the cost of denying agency comes due.

**1. He keeps the word "responsibility" but empties it.** Harris redefines responsibility as a *descriptive fit* between an act and one's mental states — exactly the compatibilist move. But mere fit is also true of a malfunctioning machine: a virus-laden program "expresses" its code. What is missing is the *answerability* that responsibility ordinarily means — that the agent *could have done otherwise* and so is fittingly praised or blamed. By his own lights ("our choices merely appear... sprung from the void," see [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]), the murderer no more authored his intention than his birth. Latter-day Saint theology supplies what Harris removes: agents are "free to choose liberty... or captivity" (2 Nephi 2:27), so blame and praise attach to genuine self-determination, not to a causal résumé. See [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]].

**2. The "earthquakes and tumors" leveling is double-edged.** Harris uses the brain-tumor case (example 5) to argue that *all* wrongdoing is, at bottom, tumor-like — fully caused, hence never truly culpable. But the very fact that we *do* distinguish the tumor-driven killer from the freely deliberating one is evidence *for* agency, not against it: we excuse the tumor *because* it bypassed the agent's will, which presupposes that ordinarily the will is operative. Harris must explain away a near-universal moral datum; the agency view explains it directly.

**3. Justice, not just deterrence.** Conceding that only a *"sham"* retribution can be justified guts desert, fairness, and the dignity of treating persons as authors of their deeds — reducing punishment to behavior-management of "neuronal weather patterns." Restoration theology insists, by contrast, that "men will be punished for their own sins" (Articles of Faith 1:2) precisely because they are real agents; mercy and the Atonement presuppose culpable-yet-free persons who can repent, not malfunctioning mechanisms to be recalibrated. This is the loss of objective morality flagged in [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]]: if no one is ever truly blameworthy, "justice" collapses into engineering. Grounding resources: [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]], [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]].

**4. A fair concession.** Harris is right that bad genes, abuse, and circumstance *mitigate* guilt, and Latter-day Saint thought agrees — agency operates within real constraints, and God judges "according to... the desires of their hearts" and their light and opportunity (D&C 137:9; Alma 41:3). The dispute is not whether circumstances reduce culpability but whether they *eliminate* agency altogether. Harris needs the strong claim; the moral life only needs the modest one.
