# Evil as a Natural Phenomenon — Harris on Psychopathy

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that "evil" is not a metaphysical category but **a natural phenomenon with neural causes**. Psychopathy is "a nearly perfect dissection of conventional morality": studying brains that *cannot* care about others reveals that ordinary moral cognition runs on emotional machinery (especially the amygdala and orbitofrontal cortex) which psychopaths lack.[^1] On this view the worst human cruelty is, in principle, a *pathology* — and predatory violence is partly innate, an evolved legacy we must consciously oppose, since "there is a difference between what is natural and what is actually good for us."[^2]

## Evidence
- **Psychopathy is a brain difference, not a free choice.** Psychopaths show "significantly less activity in regions of the brain that generally respond to emotional stimuli," lack normal anxiety and fear (the "anchors to social and moral norms"), and characteristically "cannot learn to care about others."[^3] They retain intact "theory of mind" yet "simply [do] not care," and uniquely fail to recognize *fear and sadness* in others — "the difference that makes all the difference."[^4]
- **Conventional vs moral transgression.** Normal children past ~39 months treat "hit another student" as intrinsically wrong even if a teacher permits it, while "eat at your desk" is merely conventional; children at risk for psychopathy "view these questions as morally indistinguishable," guided by no awareness of potential suffering.[^5]
- **Evil is on a continuum and partly innate.** Game theory predicts a stable "permanent defection" strategy that "begins to sound like garden-variety psychopathy"; Hare estimates ~3 million psychopaths in the U.S.[^6] Predatory violence is innate, but humanity "ha[s] grown steadily less violent" (Diamond), so nature is not destiny.[^2]
- **The grizzly analogy — why we *don't* call a bear "evil."** A bear caged with you is "morally insane" in the same way a psychopath is, yet we never call the bear "evil" — signaling that, on a fully naturalistic view, "evil" is a description of a malfunctioning mind, not a transgression against a moral law.[^7]
- **Cure, not condemnation.** If "evil is now nothing more than a nutritional deficiency," the retributive frame collapses (see [[Moral Responsibility Without Free Will - Harris's Reconciliation]]); "soul/body dualism has been the enemy of compassion."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Some people cannot learn to care about others." [^3]

> "We must continually remind ourselves that there is a difference between what is natural and what is actually good for us. Cancer is perfectly natural, and yet its eradication is a primary goal of modern medicine." [^2]

> "Evil is now nothing more than a nutritional deficiency." [^8]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's neuroscience of psychopathy is genuinely illuminating, and Latter-day Saint thought need not deny that vice has biological correlates or that some predispositions are innate. Several tensions remain.

**1. The grizzly proves too much.** Harris's own analogy concedes that a thoroughgoing naturalism cannot sustain the word *evil* — a bear is merely dangerous, not wicked, and if humans are just more complex animals "blown about by an internal wind" (see [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]), then neither is the child-torturer *evil* in any sense beyond *broken*. Yet Harris cannot help reaching for the moral term, because the horror of the case (which he quotes at length) is not the horror of a malfunction but of a *wrong done by someone who should not have done it*. The persistence of that intuition is evidence that "evil" names a real category his metaphysics cannot hold — what theists call sin against a moral order grounded in God. See [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] and [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]].

**2. "Natural vs good" reopens the is/ought gap he elsewhere closes.** Harris insists we must *oppose* natural tendencies like rape and territorial violence because they are not "good for us." But this presupposes a standard of the good *above* what nature delivers — the very transcendence naturalism is meant to avoid. Once "natural" and "good" come apart, the question "good *by what measure?*" cannot be answered by more facts about brains; it requires the prior value-frame Harris denies needing.

**3. Theodicy, not just neurology.** Harris treats evil as defect-to-be-cured; the Restoration treats moral evil as the misuse of real agency within a purposeful mortality. Lehi teaches that "opposition in all things" (2 Nephi 2:11) is the condition of growth, and Irenaeus' developmental reading — [[Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth Not Corruption]] — frames evil's possibility as the price of agents maturing toward the divine nature, not a glitch in the food supply. The "cure for evil" thought-experiment is telling: a species *engineered* to be incapable of cruelty would also be incapable of freely chosen love — the very good Harris's own ethics most prizes (cf. [[Uncreated Intelligences Make Free Love Possible]]).

**4. A fair point conceded.** Harris is right that stigmatizing mental illness as moral failure is itself unjust, and Latter-day Saint teaching agrees that God judges according to capacity, light, and circumstance (D&C 137:9; Moroni 8:8 on those "not capable of committing sin"). Diminished capacity genuinely *mitigates* culpability. The disagreement is whether *all* wrongdoing reduces to diminished capacity — which would, as [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]] notes, abolish moral evil altogether rather than explain it.
