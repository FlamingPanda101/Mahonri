# The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] (chapter six of *The Creation Dilemma*) argues that the morally decisive act is **not** the creature's later rebellion but **God's prior choice to create that creature** out of nothing, without consent, foreknowing damnation. "The first moral act is not the rebellion of the person. It's God choosing to create that person in the first place." Because God was unbound (He did not have to create), lost nothing by abstaining, and the soul "didn't ask to be here," the moral weight lands upstream on the Creator's free decision.[^1] This is why Carroll calls it the *creation* dilemma — the dilemma lies in God choosing to create you knowing your outcome when He didn't have to.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **It distinguishes the dilemma from the ordinary problem of evil — rate: strong and clarifying.** Carroll explicitly says this is "the problem of evil on steroids." The classical problem of evil ("why do bad things happen?") has a Christian solve — the atonement redeems/justifies all suffering. But the creation dilemma asks a *different* question: why create at all, given a foreknown, unredeemable end? The atonement cannot answer it, because the very person could simply never have been created.[^3] See [[Problem of Hell and Creation]], [[Hell - Free Will Defense Rebutted]]. **Steelman of the theist:** the value of *existence itself* may be an intrinsic good — Augustine and the Thomist tradition hold being is good as such, so even a life ending in loss instantiates a real good (a free, rational creature in God's image). On that view the "first moral act" produces a genuine good, not a pure harm.
2. **The non-consent premise carries real load — rate: contestable but pointed.** Carroll concedes he "doesn't spend too much time on" non-consent but says "that makes a big difference": the created soul never agreed to play a game it cannot opt out of, ending in eternal conscious torment.[^4] **Steelman:** consent is incoherent as a precondition for *coming into existence* — there is no prior subject to consent. The LDS reply (Carroll's own preferred solve) sidesteps this by positing a *premortal* self who *did* consent — see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]. This is precisely why the non-consent objection is fatal to creedalism but not to LDS theology.
3. **Anchored in Jesus' own words — rate: strong prooftext.** Carroll points to [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] ("it had been good for that man if he had not been born") as evidence that *Christ himself* recognized outcomes so bad that non-existence is preferable — the upstream judgment is therefore biblical, not merely philosophical.[^5] See [[The Judas Argument - Better Never to Have Been Born]].
