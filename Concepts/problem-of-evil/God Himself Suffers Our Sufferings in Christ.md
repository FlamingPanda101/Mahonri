# God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] answers the problem of evil not with a complete theodicy but with the claim that in Christ, **God enters and shares human suffering** — so "we do not suffer alone." He grants he doesn't "have the answers" for why particular evils occur, but anchors hope in the one thing he says we *do* know: that God came down and took our pains upon himself.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Book of Mormon prophecy of a suffering God (strong, scriptural).** Bradley cites Abinadi and King Benjamin, who both foretell that "God himself shall come down among the children of men and… take upon him our sufferings," and the title page's "Jesus is the Christ, the eternal God" — God, not merely a man, bearing the pains.[^1]
2. **Suffering shared, not merely observed (interpretive).** The consolation is participatory: "God in Christ is suffering our sufferings," so the sufferer is accompanied. This is offered against the deist picture of a distant God who only watches.[^1]
3. **Witnessed in his son's own faith (strong, first-person).** Months before he died, Donnie wrote in a gratitude journal "how much sense it made to him and how grateful he was that Christ suffered all his pains" — Donnie "suffered many pains," and the doctrine that "we do not suffer alone" was lived, not theoretical.[^2]
4. **Paired with eschatological victory (moderate).** Christ "didn't just come to suffer our sufferings… he also came to declare an end to those things," linking present accompaniment to the future new creation. See [[Resurrection Is the Beginning of a New Creation]].[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic can object that divine co-suffering, however consoling, does not solve the problem of evil: an omnipotent God who merely *joins* the suffering he could prevent has explained nothing about why he permits it — companionship is not justification. Indeed, a passible God who suffers raises its own difficulties for classical theism (immutability, impassibility), and to the bereaved "God suffers with you" can feel like a substitute for the missing answer to "why my child?"

## Counter-Objection
Bradley explicitly does *not* present co-suffering as a logical theodicy; he concedes the whys are "known to God" and that he is "still working out" his thoughts. The claim functions as *pastoral* ground — solidarity that makes the suffering bearable and non-isolating — alongside, not in place of, the eschatological hope that evil will end. As to impassibility, the LDS frame (an embodied, relational God) already affirms divine passibility, so the objection that lands against classical theism is less forceful here. Whether companionship "discharges" the intellectual problem is a separate question from whether it is true and sustaining, which is what he claims.
