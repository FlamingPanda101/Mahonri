# Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality

## The Claim

Materialist atheism entails determinism: if humans are purely physical beings
whose thoughts and actions arise from prior physical causes (genetics,
environment, brain chemistry), then libertarian free will is an illusion.
Leading atheists like [[Sam Harris]] honestly concede this. However, LDS
theology uniquely solves the determinism problem through the doctrine of
**co-eternality** — the teaching that human intelligences (the core of
personal identity) are uncreated, co-eternal with God, and thus not caused by
anything outside themselves. We are "uncaused causes" in the relevant sense.
Remarkably, mainstream Christian *creation ex nihilo* faces the SAME
determinism problem as atheism — making LDS theology the only consistent basis
for genuine free will.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Sam Harris and the Case for Determinism (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Cited Opponent)

[[Hayden Carroll]] discusses [[Sam Harris]]'s argument from his book *Free
Will* (2012):

> "He talks about in the book, right, he says that our choices and intentions
> they arise from like prior causes — your genetics, your environment,
> unconscious brain. And he does these little studies about how before someone
> says something, things happen in their brain first. Like before things are
> even consciously even thought about."[^1]

Harris's argument draws on the Libet experiments and related neuroscientific
findings showing that brain activity associated with a decision occurs before
the subject is consciously aware of having made the decision. On a materialist
framework, this is strong evidence against libertarian free will: your
conscious "choice" is a post-hoc rationalization of unconscious neural
processes.

Carroll steelmans the atheist position: "If you eat food, why did you eat food?
Because you were hungry. Well, why were you hungry? Because your biology and
chemical [processes] made you. It leads you to do things."[^2]

### 2. The Leading Atheists All Concede No Free Will (Evidence Rating: C — paraphrased by a tier-C source)

> "The real strong hitters, right, Sam Harris, Christopher Hitchens, Alex
> O'Connor, they all agree that yeah, we actually don't have free will. If
> atheism is a correct description of reality. If there is no God, if we are a
> materialist, if we were caused by something that we had no control over, we
> have no free will and consciousness."[^3]

This is significant because it's not just one atheist's view — it's the
consistent position of the most prominent atheist intellectuals. They recognize
that materialism + causal determinism = no libertarian free will.

### 3. LDS Co-Eternality as the Unique Solution (Evidence Rating: C — commentary, not primary evidence)

The LDS solution rests on [[Joseph Smith]]'s teaching that human intelligences
are co-eternal with God:

> "As Latter-day Saints we would say that we are actually not caused, right?
> Because we're eternal beings, right? Joseph taught that very clear, that
> we're co-eternal with God."[^4]

The logic:
1. If you are caused by something outside yourself (whether God or matter),
   your choices are ultimately determined by that cause.
2. If you are uncaused (co-eternal), your capacity to choose is fundamental and
   not derivative.
3. LDS doctrine teaches that the core of your identity — your intelligence or
   spirit — was not created but has always existed.
4. Therefore, LDS theology provides a metaphysical basis for genuine
   libertarian free will that neither atheism nor *ex nihilo* Christianity can
   provide.

The D&C connection: [[D&C 130.20-21]] teaches that eternal law is
"irrevocably decreed in heaven before the foundations of this world." Moral
agency operates within an eternal framework of law — not as a arbitrary grant
from a sovereign God who could revoke it.[^5]

### 4. An Unusual Alliance: LDS and Atheists Agree on Eternal Matter (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

> "This is what's I love so much about the LDS doctrine and what Joseph Smith
> put forth is we're able to align with... they'll be like 'matter was neither
> created nor destroyed, neither can it be.' Like exactly, we actually do
> believe that and we believe in God."[^6]

Atheists often appeal to the law of conservation of mass-energy ("matter can
neither be created nor destroyed") as a scientific principle that rules out
*creation ex nihilo*. LDS theology agrees: matter is eternal, uncreated. The
difference is that LDS theology also affirms that *intelligence* and *spirit*
are eternal, providing a basis for consciousness and free will that atheism
lacks.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Co-Eternality Doesn't Solve Determinism
**Steelman:** Even if intelligences are eternal, they still have a *nature*
— dispositions, inclinations, tendencies. If your choices flow from your
eternal nature, and you didn't *choose* that nature (it's just what you
eternally are), then your choices are still determined — just by your eternal
character rather than by physical causes. You're no freer than a rock is "free"
to act according to its nature.

**Response:** This objection conflates *determination by nature* with
*determination by external cause*. The LDS position is that the intelligence
*is* the agent — not that the agent is a product of the intelligence. Your
eternal nature is *you*, not something external constraining you. Furthermore,
LDS theology posits that intelligences can grow, learn, and change through
agency — the eternal nature is not a fixed program but a capacity for
self-determination. This is closer to agent-causal libertarianism than to
compatibilism.

### Objection 2: LDS Theology Still Has Determinism Through God's Foreknowledge
**Steelman:** If God knows with certainty what you will choose (as LDS doctrine
affirms), then your choice is fixed — you cannot do otherwise. Foreknowledge
entails determinism.

**Response:** This is the classic theological fatalism objection, which applies
to all theistic traditions, not just LDS. The standard response (from Boethius
through modern philosophy) is that knowledge does not *cause* the event — God's
knowledge of your future choice is chronologically prior but logically
posterior to the choice itself. God knows what you will freely choose; He does
not determine it. LDS theology's emphasis on co-eternality strengthens this
response: God's foreknowledge is of the choices of eternally existing agents,
not of creatures He designed with predetermined outcomes.

### Objection 3: Libertarian Free Will Is Incoherent Regardless of Worldview
**Steelman:** Even without materialism, libertarian free will faces the "luck"
objection: if your choice is not determined by your reasons, character, or
prior states, then it's random — and a random choice is no more "free" than a
determined one. Libertarian free will is simply an incoherent concept.

**Response:** This is a deep philosophical challenge. LDS thought responds by
locating agency in the eternal intelligence itself, which acts for *reasons*
but is not *compelled* by them. Reasons incline without necessitating. This
isn't randomness — it's the exercise of a fundamental capacity for
self-determination that is not further reducible. The cost of denying this
capacity (as materialists do) is the loss of moral responsibility, rational
discourse, and the very concept of "you" as an agent.
