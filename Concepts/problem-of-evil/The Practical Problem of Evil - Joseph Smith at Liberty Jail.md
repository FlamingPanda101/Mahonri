# The Practical Problem of Evil - Joseph Smith at Liberty Jail

#type/claim

## The Claim

The third problem [[David Paulsen]] treats is the **practical** problem of evil: not whether belief in God is *rational* amid suffering, but how to "live trustingly and faithfully in the face of what personally seems to be overwhelming evil." Here Paulsen answers less with argument than with **Joseph Smith's own life**, which "speaks more powerfully than the words." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Joseph was no stranger to sorrow.** Tarred and feathered, repeatedly jailed, impoverished, often homeless; he buried four of his eleven children (including twin sons) and a fifth at fourteen months; lost brothers Alvin and Don Carlos and his father; was betrayed by friends and finally martyred. "Deep water is what I am wont to swim in" (D&C 127:2). [^2]
2. **Liberty Jail, winter 1838–39.** After the Saints were driven from Missouri — Haun's Mill massacre, the sack of Far West, women raped to death — Joseph was confined for four months in a dungeon, abused, "fed human flesh." From the depths he prayed, "**O God, where art thou?**" ([[D&C 121.7-8|D&C 121:1–2]]). Even Joseph "experienced the troubling sense of God's absence." [^3]
3. **God's answer reframes the suffering.** "My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment; and then, if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high... all these things shall give thee **experience**, and shall be for thy good. The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" ([[D&C 121.7-8]]; [[D&C 122.7-8]]). [^4]
4. **The practical resolution.** "Confronted with what seemed to be overwhelming evil, Joseph found meaning in his suffering, maintained hope, trusted God, and kept the faith. And God spoke peace." The instrumentalist theodicy ([[Lehi's Theodicy - Opposition in All Things]]) is here lived, not just argued. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Know thou, my son, that all these things shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good. The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" (D&C 122:7–8) [^4]

> "Joseph found meaning in his suffering, maintained hope, trusted God, and kept the faith. And God spoke peace." [^5]
