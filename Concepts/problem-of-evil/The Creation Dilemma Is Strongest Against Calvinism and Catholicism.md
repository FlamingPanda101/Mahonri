# The Creation Dilemma Is Strongest Against Calvinism and Catholicism

## The Claim

The force of [[The Creation Dilemma]] varies by theological system: it is **weakest against nuanced Evangelical philosophers** (who hold many escape positions) and **strongest where there is "no wiggle room"** — Calvinism and Catholicism. [[Hayden Carroll]] and the Evangelical Molinist hosts of Freethinking Ministries *agree* on this ranking. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Calvinists "bite the bullet" — rate: strong (they own the conclusion)

Carroll: the book "is not really written to Calvinists, because Calvinists read the book and they say, 'Yeah, so what? My God does that, and he's still good.'" On Calvinism the dilemma's conclusion (God wills the damned to hell) is *affirmed* rather than resisted — Carroll adds the sharper layer that "God wants them to go to hell." So Calvinism does not refute the dilemma; it accepts it. See [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]] and [[Decreeing All Things Makes God the Author of Sin]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — Calvinist compatibilism plus double predestination concedes the structure outright.

### 2. Catholicism has the least room to maneuver — rate: moderate-to-strong

Both Carroll and host Tim agree it is "probably strongest against Catholicism... they have no wiggle room," because Catholics "can't go outside of their dogma." Carroll quips that Protestants "can commit heresy without being killed," so most Protestants he meets still won't budge from the contested attributes either. The point: dogmatic fixity removes the escape routes (deny *ex nihilo*, deny eternal torment, limit foreknowledge). [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong — a claim about doctrinal latitude, not a proof; some Catholic philosophers would dispute that their tradition is so boxed in.

### 3. It is weakest against nuanced Evangelical philosophers — rate: moderate

Tim notes that taking this argument "to the Evangelical Philosophical Society conferences" would "get excoriated," because so many Evangelical philosophers "take so many nuanced positions" (Molinism, annihilationism, open theism, traducianism). Carroll grants "it's stronger on some worldviews than others, for sure." So the dilemma is a *worldview-comparison* tool, not a universal refutation. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — both interlocutors concede the asymmetry.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A Calvinist would say "biting the bullet" is not a weakness but a refusal of the dilemma's hidden premise that God's goodness must be *measured by our intuitions about creaturely flourishing*; on a theocentric account, God's glory in both mercy and justice is the standard, so creating vessels of wrath is not a defect (Romans 9). A Catholic would deny being boxed in: Thomistic and Molinist resources within Catholicism (permission vs. intention, the distinction between antecedent and consequent will) are precisely the "wiggle room" Carroll says is absent. The Molinist hosts press their own escape — there may be no *feasible* world with the saved but without the lost (see [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]] and [[God Could Have Created Only the Saved]]) — which Carroll answers by appeal to an *unbound* God who could create "one at a time."
