# A Reason for an Action Is Not Proof It Was Caused

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim

[[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]] argues that the determinist's central
inference is **invalid as stated**: it establishes at most the *possibility* of
determinism, never a demonstrated causal link. "The argument only provides the
possibility for determinism. No direct link can be shown that our choices are
directly the result of past events."[^1] The key move: "If one has a reason for
his actions, that doesn't automatically mean that reason caused his action. In
understanding why we make certain choices, that doesn't mean we are unable to
make choices at all."[^2]

The point targets the determinist's rhetorical question — "where else could our
choices come from if they are not the result of prior causes?"[^3] Jones'
response is that the existence of *reasons, motives, and antecedent conditions*
for a choice is fully compatible with the choice being *free*: a reason can
**incline** without **necessitating**. To slide from "there was a prior factor"
(genes, hunger, upbringing) to "that factor *determined* the choice" is to assume
the very causal closure under dispute. Determinists, Jones says, treat the
inference as inescapable — but it is not; identifying *why* someone chose as they
did does not show they *could not have chosen otherwise*.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Possibility ≠ actuality.** That a deterministic story *can* be told for a
   choice does not show it is the *true* story. The determinist offers a
   coherent redescription, not evidence that excludes agent causation. "No direct
   link can be shown that our choices are directly the result of past
   events."[^1] **Rating: a clean logical point** — it correctly identifies that
   the burden of proof is on the one asserting causal closure, which is a
   metaphysical thesis, not a datum of observation.

2. **The reason/cause distinction.** Having a reason explains a choice
   *rationally* without thereby explaining it *mechanically*. "If one has a
   reason for his actions, that doesn't automatically mean that reason caused his
   action."[^2] **Rating: strong** — it tracks a real and standard distinction in
   action theory between rationalizing and efficient-causal explanation, and
   dovetails with [[Free Will Is the Choice to Focus the Mind - Ayn Rands Account]], where reasons "motivate" without compelling.

3. **Understanding a choice ≠ abolishing it.** "In understanding why we make
   certain choices, that doesn't mean we are unable to make choices at all."[^2]
   Explanation and freedom are not rivals. **Rating: corrective** — it punctures
   the assumption that to *explain* a decision is automatically to *explain it
   away*, an assumption that quietly drives much determinist rhetoric.

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)

The determinist has a serious reply: this is **a god-of-the-gaps move for
agency**. Yes, today we cannot trace every choice to its antecedents — but the
direction of all evidence (neuroscience, the universal success of physical
explanation) is that mental events *are* brain events with sufficient prior
causes; the burden is on the libertarian to exhibit even one choice that is
*uncaused-yet-not-random*, and none has been produced. The reason/cause
distinction, the determinist adds, may be merely epistemic: a "reason" just *is*
a brain state doing causal work, so a reason that genuinely figures in producing
action *is* a cause. To insist that "incline without necessitate" names a real
third category — neither determined nor random — is to assert exactly the
contested thing without showing it is coherent (the **luck/sourcehood
dilemma**: anything not fixed by prior states looks like chance). On this view
Jones has shifted the burden but offered no positive mechanism.

## Counter-Objection

The steelman is the strongest determinist card, and it shows this argument is
*defensive* — it neutralizes the determinist's claim to have *proven* its case,
but does not by itself establish libertarian freedom. That is the right division
of labor: this note clears the ground (possibility is not proof), and the
positive work is carried elsewhere. Three replies. (1) The "direction of all
evidence" claim overreaches: the universal success of physical *prediction* fails
precisely at the quantum level, where the future is not fixed by the past — so
the closure premise the determinist needs is itself empirically contested (see
[[The Conway-Kochen Free Will Theorem - Indeterminism Preserves Freedom]]). (2)
The "incline-without-necessitate" category is not incoherent if the agent is a
*non-reducible source* rather than a configuration of caused matter: an
agent-cause selects among reasons without being pushed by them, which is neither
prior-determination nor chance — the position developed in
[[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]. (3) Charging "gaps"
cuts both ways: the determinist's confident causal closure is *also* a promissory
note, not a completed reduction, and it carries the heavy cost of dissolving the
rational authorship its own argument presupposes (cf.
[[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]). The Restoration
grounds the missing source in an uncreated intelligence that "act[s] for itself"
(D&C 93:29–30), so reasons can rationally explain a free choice without
mechanically producing it.
