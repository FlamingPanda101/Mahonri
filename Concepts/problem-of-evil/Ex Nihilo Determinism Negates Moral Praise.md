# Ex Nihilo Determinism Negates Moral Praise

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Classical Christianity holds that God created the human soul *ex nihilo*. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] turns this into a problem for the classical view: if God made you from nothing, then every good thing you do was effectively pre-programmed by God, so you cannot be genuinely praiseworthy — you are "a robot running a program."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The robot dilemma.** "If you were created ex nihilo, then everything that you do was pre-programmed; you're a robot that's running a program that God placed in you, and you're not really a good person or a bad person — you're just doing what you're programmed to do."[^2] On co-eternality, by contrast, "there's a part of you that God didn't create; therefore if you make a good decision it's not something you were programmed to do, it's something that you learned to do."[^3]
2. **Symmetry with the problem of evil.** This is the mirror image of [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]: the same uncreated agency that exculpates God for our *evil* also makes our *good* truly our own, enabling real moral progress toward becoming like Christ and ultimately God.[^4]
3. **Evidence rating.** As an intuition pump this is forceful and tracks a real worry about theological determinism. But it is a *philosophical* argument, not a scriptural proof; its force depends on assuming that creation *ex nihilo* entails determinism.
4. **Steelman of the opposing view (important).** Most classical theists deny the very premise: creating a soul *ex nihilo* does not entail *determining* its choices. Molinists, Arminians, and many Thomists hold that God can create genuinely free agents whose libertarian choices he does not pre-program — origination is not the same as causal determinism of every act. So the "robot" charge arguably attacks hard theological determinism (some forms of Calvinism) rather than *ex nihilo* per se. The strongest critical reply: the LDS scheme buys libertarian freedom at the price of God's aseity (co-eternal souls), whereas classical theism claims to keep *both* a free creature *and* an ultimate Creator. Griffiths' framework wins only if one grants that made-from-nothing means programmed-from-nothing.

## Best Quotes

> "If you were created ex nihilo, then everything that you do was pre-programmed. You're a robot that's running a program that God placed in you, and you're not really a good person or a bad person."[^5]

> "Joseph Smith would argue no, there's a part of you that God didn't create; therefore if you make a good decision it's not something you were programmed to do, it's something that you learned to do."[^6]
