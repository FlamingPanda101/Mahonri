# God Will Not Force You Into Heaven Against Your Will

## The Point
Turek's conclusion turns from evidence to the will. Having argued that atheism is intellectually self-defeating and that "mere Christianity" is true, he asks why everyone doesn't simply accept it — and answers that our deepest obstacle is not the evidence but ourselves.[^1] "Our biggest obstacle is ourselves. We want to go our own way and do not want to be encumbered by anything that smacks of divine authority."[^2] Belief, in the end, requires more than the intellect; it requires the will. And because love cannot be coerced, "God will not force you into heaven against your will."[^3]

## Evidence
Turek distinguishes **two kinds of faith**: *belief that* (assent based on reason and evidence — a matter of the head) and *belief in* (trust, the response of the heart and will).[^4] Evidence can establish belief *that* Jesus is the Savior, but salvation requires moving to belief *in* Him; "even the demons believe that God exists, but they shudder" — they have head-knowledge without trust.[^4] He illustrates with a courtship: a man cannot force a woman to love him, and if he truly loves her he will eventually leave her alone.[^5] So with God — He makes Himself known through nature, scripture, His Spirit, and other believers, but if we keep refusing, "He eventually leaves us alone to pursue our own sinful desires" (Romans 1).[^5] Answering atheist Eddie Tabash's question about his Holocaust-survivor mother, Turek says God "did not force her into heaven against her will. If she did not want Jesus on earth, she will not want Him in eternity. God respects our choices."[^6] He frames hell as *self-chosen separation* from God, quoting C. S. Lewis: "There are only two kinds of people in the end: those who say to God, 'Thy will be done,' and those to whom God says... 'Thy will be done.' All that are in Hell, choose it."[^7] He invokes Augustine — "We love the truth when it enlightens us. We hate the truth when it convicts us" — to locate unbelief in moral resistance rather than lack of proof.[^1]

## Best Quotes
> "God did not force her into heaven against her will. If she did not want Jesus on earth, she will not want Him in eternity. God respects our choices." [^6]

> "Love, by definition, must be freely given; it cannot be coerced." [^5]

> "There are only two kinds of people in the end: those who say to God, 'Thy will be done,' and those to whom God says, in the end, 'Thy will be done.' All that are in Hell, choose it." [^7]

## LDS Connection
This conclusion is profoundly congenial to Latter-day Saint theology, which makes moral agency — the freedom to choose God or refuse Him — central to the entire plan of salvation; Saints likewise hold that God will never coerce love or faith. Turek's two-kinds-of-faith distinction maps closely onto Alma's teaching that faith begins with *desire*: "if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you, even until ye believe" — faith is an experiment the will must choose to perform (compare [[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32:27-43]]). The LDS distinctive enriches rather than contradicts Turek: because agency is so sacred, the plan of salvation envisions a richer range of outcomes than a simple heaven/hell binary (degrees of glory), so that God's perfect justice and mercy give each soul exactly the eternity its desires fit. On the core claim — that God honors the will and "sends" people only where they have chosen to go — Turek and the Restoration speak with one voice.
