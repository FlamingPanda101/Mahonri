# The Logical Problem of Evil Hides Two Unprovable Premises

## The Point
The **logical** problem of evil — framed classically by Epicurus and pressed in the twentieth century by J. L. Mackie — claims that the propositions "an all-powerful, all-loving God exists" and "suffering exists" are strictly *logically inconsistent*, so the undeniable reality of suffering disproves God.[^1] Craig's reply, following [[Alvin Plantinga]]'s Free Will Defense, is that there is **no explicit contradiction** between those two statements. Unlike "David is married" and "David is a bachelor," neither one is the denial of the other.[^2] To generate a contradiction the atheist must add two *hidden* assumptions: (1) an all-powerful God can create *any* world he wants, and (2) an all-loving God *prefers* a world with no suffering. The whole argument stands or falls on these buried premises — and neither can be shown to be *necessarily* true.[^3]

## Evidence
- **Two consistent statements are not a contradiction.** Craig contrasts "God exists" / "suffering exists" with a genuine contradiction ("David is married" / "David is a bachelor"). Because the first pair has no *explicit* contradiction, "there must be hidden assumptions behind this argument that would bring out the alleged contradiction."[^2]
- **The smuggled bridge premises.** Spelled out, the hidden assumptions are: *"If God is all-powerful, he can create any world he wants,"* and *"if God is all-loving, he prefers a world without suffering."* Only with both does the conclusion "so suffering does not exist" follow.[^3]
- **The burden is "too heavy to bear."** Because the claim is one of strict *logical* impossibility, the atheist must prove far more than that the premises are plausible — he must show it is *logically impossible* both that free will exists and that God has morally sufficient reasons for permitting suffering. Craig judges this "burden of proof... too heavy to bear," so "it's quite possible that God and suffering both exist."[^4]
- **Even atheists have conceded the point.** Craig closes by quoting Plantinga and Mackie's own admission: "We can concede that the problem of evil does not after all show that the central doctrines of theism are logically inconsistent... It's now acknowledged on almost all sides that the logical argument is bankrupt." He is careful to add this is "hardly the end of the discussion" — the *probability* (evidential) version remains live.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "There is no explicit contradiction between these two statements, so there must be hidden assumptions behind this argument that would bring out the alleged contradiction." [^2]

> "For the logical problem of suffering to succeed, the atheist would have to show that it's logically impossible that free will exists, and that it's logically impossible that God has good reasons for permitting suffering. This burden of proof is too heavy to bear." [^4]

> "This is why philosophers, even atheist philosophers, have given up on the logical problem of evil... It's now acknowledged on almost all sides that the logical argument is bankrupt." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration is well stocked to supply exactly the two things Craig says the atheist cannot rule out — genuine free agency and a morally sufficient reason for permitting evil — and arguably grounds them more securely than creation-ex-nihilo theism. Latter-day Saint scripture builds the whole plan of salvation on libertarian agency: humanity is "free to choose liberty and eternal life... or to choose captivity and death" (2 Nephi 2:27), and "opposition in all things" is presented as a *necessary* condition for any meaningful world (2 Nephi 2:11). Two distinctive resources fortify the defense. First, because intelligence is **uncreated and co-eternal**, God did not author the wills that choose evil, so He is not the ultimate source of human sin to begin with — the Free Will Defense gets a metaphysical footing the ex-nihilo theist must work harder to secure (see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]). Second, a **pre-mortal estate** in which spirits consented to a probationary mortality supplies a candidate "morally sufficient reason": mortality with its risks was chosen, not merely imposed (compare [[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy]]). One honest caveat: Craig is offering only a *defense* (showing the atheist's logical claim fails), not a full theodicy; the LDS account goes further and ventures an actual explanation, which is a heavier claim to defend but answers a deeper question than mere logical consistency.
