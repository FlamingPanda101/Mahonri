# The Contradiction Test for God's Existence

## The Point
Seth's logical centerpiece — which he calls in closing "the most devastating argument against theism" — is an application of the law of non-contradiction: "anything that violates the... law of non-contradiction, it can't exist."[^1] He therefore issues a standing challenge: "come up with a definition of God and test it; see if you can find any definition that's not a contradiction." If every conception of God contains a contradiction, then "if gods don't exist, theism is not understanding reality."[^2] He supports this with a catalog of alleged contradictions — "at least 637" documented in the Bible, plus contradictions in Hinduism (form vs. formless, creates vs. doesn't) and Islam (made from clay or not, Allah forgives or doesn't).[^3] The standard reply, captured in [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]], is that an apparent tension is not a genuine logical contradiction, and Hansen separately dismisses these as *theological* questions irrelevant to his ontological argument.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The test stated.** "Things can only be true or false, you can't be both... so the question for theism is: is your definition of God a contradiction? If so, your God does not exist."[^1]
2. **Sample "contradictions."** "Does God love children or does God hate children?" (the Egyptian firstborn); does God "punish people for their own sins or for someone else's sins"; "God being the same yesterday today and forever" set against changes in his dealings — plus parallel lists for Hinduism and Islam.[^3]
3. **The if-then thought experiment.** Seth generalizes the method: "if God is good, what should the world look like? And we know that evil exists, so lots of definitions of God get debunked — that's a problem of evil."[^5] See [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]] and [[The Test World Does Not Look Like a Test]].
4. **Hansen's response: category error.** Hansen repeatedly sets these aside as outside the debate: "don't get distracted by... the argument from evil or that kind of stuff — those are theological questions; this debate is not about the nature of God."[^4] He also notes he does not "endorse religion" but "what is true," granting that religions "have had various models" without conceding the contradiction charge.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Is your definition of God a contradiction? If so, your God does not exist, and then we have to look for some definition of God that isn't a contradiction — and we'll see how hard that is." (Seth) [^1]

> "I would challenge anyone to try to come up with the definition of God that doesn't have a contradiction under this framework of testing." (Seth) [^5]

> "Don't get distracted by... the argument from evil or that kind of stuff — those are theological questions. This debate is not about the nature of God." (Hansen) [^4]

## LDS Connection
The contradiction challenge bites hardest against *creedal* (classical-theist) definitions of God, and Latter-day Saint thought already presses many of the same tensions from the other direction — arguing the creeds built "a self-contradictory God" while the LDS Godhead avoids them. So an LDS apologist can grant Seth's method while denying that the *LDS* conception fails it. See [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]] and [[The Debate Is About Coherence Not the LDS God]].
