# Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism

## The Claim

Mainstream Christianity's doctrine of *creation ex nihilo* (creation out of
nothing) leads to the SAME determinism problem as atheism. If God created you
from nothing — designing your nature, your capacities, your inclinations — then
your choices are ultimately causally traceable to God, not to yourself. On this
view, you are "just an effect of dominoes in the line of dominoes that you
didn't cause, because God caused you."[^1] Only LDS theology's doctrine of
co-eternality (that human intelligences are uncreated) provides a genuine basis
for libertarian free will. Atheists and *ex nihilo* Christians are, ironically,
aligned against LDS theology on the free will question.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Logic of Creation Ex Nihilo (Evidence Rating: C — commentary, not primary evidence)

[[Hayden Carroll]] lays out the argument:[^1]

1. *Ex nihilo* creation means God brought everything into existence from
   nothing — including human souls.
2. If God created your soul, He determined its nature, capacities,
   dispositions, and initial conditions.
3. Your choices flow from your nature.
4. Your nature was determined by God.
5. Therefore, your choices are ultimately determined by God.
6. Determinism-by-God is still determinism — your choices are not free in the
   libertarian sense.

This is not a straw man. Carroll explicitly aligns this with the atheist
position:

> "If you want to accept that presupposition of God, which we reject... you run
> into the same issue that the atheists have, which is we're caused by
> something out of our control. And so effectively, Sam Harris is right and I
> would say the creedal Christians are in the same camp."[^1]

### 2. Calvinism as the Consistent Ex Nihilo Position (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Theological)

[[Calvinism]] explicitly embraces this logic: if God created everything and
ordains everything, then human choices are determined by God. Carroll notes:

> "There is one group of Christians who do agree with the atheists and it's the
> Calvinists... They're very consistent. They say, 'God — everything that
> happens happens because God wanted it to happen.'"[^2]

See [[Calvinism Consistency with Atheism on Free Will]]. The Calvinist is the
honest *ex nihilo* Christian — they recognize that creation out of nothing +
divine sovereignty entails determinism.

### 3. Non-Calvinist Christians Are Inconsistent (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Pragmatic)

Most non-Calvinist Christians affirm both *creation ex nihilo* AND libertarian
free will — but Carroll argues they haven't thought through the logical
implications:

> "They're not thinking about the logical implications. They're not [seeing]
> why creation, why *ex nihilo* creation, necessitates there is no free
> will."[^3]

The standard Arminian/Molinist responses (middle knowledge, prevenient grace,
simple foreknowledge) attempt to preserve free will within an *ex nihilo*
framework, but Carroll's claim is that these are ad-hoc patches that don't
address the root problem: if God created your nature, He caused your choices.

### 4. The Atheist-Creedal Christian Alignment (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

Carroll finds it "actually hilarious when you think about it, how at the end of
the day the atheist worldview of no free will and those who believe in *ex
nihilo* creation, they align perfectly."[^3]

This rhetorical point serves to: (a) Show that the free-will problem isn't
unique to atheism — it's a problem for *any* worldview where humans are created
beings whose natures are determined by something outside themselves. (b)
Position LDS theology as the unique resolution.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: God Can Create Free Beings
**Steelman:** An omnipotent God can create beings with libertarian free will.
The fact that God created their nature doesn't mean He determined their choices
— He created them *with the capacity to choose freely*. This is like an author
creating characters who make free choices within a narrative; the author's act
of creation doesn't remove the characters' agency.

**Response:** The author analogy actually cuts against the *ex nihilo*
position: an author DOES determine every choice their characters make — the
characters have no independent existence. If God created your nature *and*
knows with certainty what you'll choose (given that nature in given
circumstances), and He chose to create you with that nature in those
circumstances, then He chose the outcome. This is the "problem of divine
freedom" that even non-Calvinist philosophers like [[Alvin Plantinga]]
acknowledge as a serious challenge. LDS co-eternality avoids it by positing
that God didn't create your fundamental nature — He's working with eternally
existing intelligences.

### Objection 2: This Argument Misunderstands Creatio Ex Nihilo
**Steelman:** *Creatio ex nihilo* means God created the *substance* of the soul
from nothing, not that He programmed every choice. The soul, once created, is a
genuine substance with its own causal powers. God creates the *being*; the
*being* creates its choices. Creation of substance ≠ determination of every
subsequent act.

**Response:** The distinction between creating the substance and determining
the acts is real but incomplete. If God creates a substance with a specific
nature, and that nature (combined with circumstances God also created) fully
determines what choices the substance will make, then God has determined the
choices by choosing the nature and circumstances. For libertarian free will to
be genuine, the agent must be the *ultimate source* of their choices — not just
the proximate source downstream of a divinely-designed nature. LDS theology
locates that ultimacy in the eternal intelligence itself.

### Objection 3: LDS Theology Has Its Own Determinism Problem
**Steelman:** LDS theology still posits that God organized our spirits from
eternal intelligence. If God "organized" you, didn't He still shape your nature
in a way that determines outcomes? And doesn't God's foreknowledge of the
pre-mortal "war in heaven" and who would follow Satan imply some form of
determinism?

**Response:** LDS theology distinguishes between the eternal intelligence
(uncreated, the core "I") and the spirit body (organized by God). The
intelligence — the fundamental agent — is NOT created by God and thus not
causally determined by Him. God's foreknowledge is of the free choices of these
eternal intelligences. The spirit body provides a vehicle for progression and
embodiment but does not overwrite the intelligence's fundamental agency. This
is a real metaphysical difference from *ex nihilo* — though whether it fully
resolves the determinism problem is debated even within LDS thought.
