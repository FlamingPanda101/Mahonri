# Suffering as the Frame That Displays the Painting

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brother Oz (Austin)]]'s theodicy is **aesthetic and exhibitionist**: if "all things exist for the glory of God," and glory just means the *exhibition of God's attributes* "by compliment or by contrast," then nothing in a Christian cosmos is meaningless — including suffering and hell.[^1] His controlling images: his physical strength displays God's strength "the same way that a frame displays a painting"; the endurance of a sufferer puts "the purity of their heart on display" the way striking a steel beam as hard as you can and watching it stand reveals its strength; and hell is "the exhibition of the judgment of God," the fire being the felt presence of a holy God to one who refuses union with him.[^2] The argument is wielded **offensively against the LDS Godhead**: a finite God bound by eternal law leaves "tons of suffering that doesn't mean anything," whereas Oz claims he can root *every* instance of suffering in the nature of God himself, so that the cosmos is meaning-saturated and "literally nothing exists that doesn't mean something."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Evangelical (Brother Oz)
- **Meaning-saturation as the real payoff.** Oz's strongest framing is comparative, not deductive: he is not first arguing *why* a baby's cancer is good, but that his metaphysics *guarantees a meaning exists* for it (rooted in God's glory), while the LDS metaphysics, by making God finite and matter eternal, allows a residue of genuinely *purposeless* suffering. "I cannot justify what suffering objectively means... because your God is powerful enough to stop any bullet."[^4] This is the [[Hell - For Gods Glory Defense Rebutted|"for God's glory"]] defense, deployed as a *contrastive* advantage rather than a pastoral answer.
- **Joy is not the point — meaning is.** Oz explicitly rejects joy/happiness as the purpose of life: if it were, "I would just make myself into a drug addict." Christ is "the man of sorrows... acquainted with grief"; he suffers for the beloved because "the pain in and of itself means something," and "the sacrifice of Christ echoes throughout all of creation."[^5] This ties the theodicy to [[Relationality Requires Three - The Trinitarian Grounding of Meaning]]: meaning, not pleasure, is the currency of a Logos-grounded reality.
- **Aesthetic theodicy has a real pedigree.** The "attributes displayed by contrast" move and the felt-fire-of-God's-presence reading of hell echo a recognizable Reformed and Eastern-Orthodox tradition (the river-of-fire view; "the heavens declare the glory of God"). Oz is candid that this is "the most touchy subject you can ever possibly touch with a 10-foot pole," and concedes he would *not* say to a specific suffering child "this is for God's glory" — locating the claim at the level of metaphysics, not pastoral speech.[^6]

### Steelman of the Latter-day Saint (Hayden Carroll)
- **The exhibition theodicy "treats people as tools."** Carroll's pressure (and the vault's [[Hell as a Greater Good Treats People as Tools]], [[Hell for Gods Glory Is Narcissism Not Love]]) is that making a raped-and-murdered child's agony an *instrument* for displaying God's justice is precisely the morally intolerable result — and worse on Oz's view, since his unbounded God *could* have stopped the bullet and chose not to. "Are you really saying there's a purpose for some child being sexually assaulted a hundred times... and then they get killed?"[^7]
- **The Calvinist tu quoque.** Carroll repeatedly notes Oz's logic is "very Calvinistic... Romans 9 / TULIP" — God creating people he foreknows will go to hell, for his glory.[^8] The LDS alternative dissolves the dilemma at the front end: God is [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|constrained by eternal law]] (so he genuinely *cannot* stop every bullet), persons [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability|consented premortally]] to enter a world of injustice, and the Atonement *compensates* unjust suffering rather than retroactively *justifying* it. See [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **LDS suffering is not meaningless — it is unmerited-yet-redeemed.** Carroll concedes "some suffering actually on the micro level probably is unjustified," but insists it is "made right through the Atonement of Jesus Christ" — Christ "makes up for" it.[^9] He flips Oz's charge: the design of existence *is* joy (2 Nephi 2:25; Moses 1:39), and matter that looks purposeless will "eventually... be used for a purpose." So the LDS view does not claim every pain is *deserved* or *instrumental*; it claims every pain is *answerable* and *compensable* — arguably a more humane structure than "your torture displays God's glory."

**Honest assessment.** This is the debate's deepest exchange and neither side lands a knockout. Oz's argument has real force as a *meaning* claim — a finite-God cosmos does risk leaving brute, unredeemed suffering, and the LDS appeal to "eventually used for a purpose" is a promissory note. But Oz's view pays for total meaning-saturation with a steep moral cost: on his own unbounded-God premises, every horror was permitted by a God who could have prevented it, which is exactly the engine of the problem of evil and slides toward the Calvinism Carroll names. The LDS view trades some metaphysical tidiness (a constrained God, brute eternal reality) for a cleaner exoneration of God from authoring evil. The fork is value-laden: maximize *meaning* (Oz) or maximize *God's moral innocence* (Carroll).
