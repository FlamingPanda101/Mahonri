# Maximal Autonomy Is Not Free Will — Plantingas Bait and Switch

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim

The determinist case against free will — paradigmatically [[Sam Harris]]'s in
*Free Will* — trades on an equivocation. [[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]],
following [[Alvin Plantinga]]'s reply article "Bait and Switch," argues that
Harris **refutes a kind of freedom no one ever claimed** and then advertises it
as a refutation of the freedom that actually matters. Harris reasons that
because we do not choose our genes, past, character, and neurology, we are not
free.[^1] But that assumes freedom would require choosing all those things —
what Plantinga calls **maximal autonomy**: "I act freely on a given occasion
only if I myself freely choose to have the desires and affections I then act on.
And furthermore, I myself freely bring it about that I do have them."[^2] So
defined, acting freely even once would require performing "an infinite number of
actions," each setting up the prior set of desires — an obvious impossibility.[^3]

Jones' point: advocates of free will **never** defined it as maximal autonomy.
"Advocates of free will have never concluded free will means we get to pick our
past, our genes, our character, place of birth, and so on."[^4] What is required
for moral responsibility is *ordinary* free will — the everyday capacity to make
genuine choices — and Harris's demolition of maximal autonomy leaves that
entirely untouched. As Plantinga puts it, "What we have here looks like a
classic bait and switch."[^5]

A telling reinforcement: **not even God has maximal autonomy.** Because God is
necessarily all-knowing, all-powerful, and perfectly good, "there never was a
time at which he had both the power to bring it about that he had that character
and also the power to bring it about that he did not have that character."[^6]
If maximal autonomy were the standard for freedom, a perfect God would fail it —
which shows the standard is the wrong one, not that freedom is impossible.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The infinite-regress reductio (decisive against maximal autonomy).** On
   Harris's implicit standard, to choose freely now I must have earlier freely
   chosen the desires I now act on — and freely chosen *those*, and so on without
   end. "If Harris is right, it is possible that I act freely only if it is
   possible that I perform an infinite number of actions... Clearly, no one has
   time, these busy days, for that."[^3] The standard self-destructs, so failing
   it says nothing against ordinary freedom. **Rating: decisive** — it is a
   logical, not empirical, point and Harris's own framing supplies the premise.

2. **The advertised conclusion ≠ the proven conclusion (the bait and switch).**
   Plantinga: "Announce that you will show we do not have freedom in the ordinary
   sense required by moral responsibility, and then proceed to argue that we do
   not have freedom in the sense of maximal autonomy."[^5] The equivocation is on
   the word *freedom*. **Rating: strong** — it identifies a genuine scope-shift
   in the argument, though it requires that Harris really *needs* the ordinary
   sense for his ethical conclusions (which he does, since his project is about
   moral responsibility).

3. **The theological parity case.** "It is certainly true that we do not have
   freedom in that sense. Not even God could have that kind of freedom."[^6]
   Since no one thinks God is *unfree*, maximal autonomy cannot be what freedom
   means. **Rating: illuminating** — a clean intuition pump that isolates the
   conceptual error.

4. **Harris does also target ordinary freedom — but only asserts it.** Jones
   concedes Harris "argues, as do most determinists, that we do not have freedom
   in the ordinary sense," yet says "the argument only provides the possibility
   for determinism. No direct link can be shown that our choices are directly the
   result of past events."[^7] So once maximal autonomy is set aside, the
   remaining case is the weaker possibility-argument handled in
   [[A Reason for an Action Is Not Proof It Was Caused]]. **Rating: moderate** —
   it correctly notes the burden shifts back to Harris, though it postpones
   rather than refutes the neuroscientific case.

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)

The determinist can press back hard. **First**, the "ordinary free will" Jones
wants to protect may be a moving target: if it is just *compatibilist* freedom
(acting on one's own desires without external compulsion), then Harris largely
*grants* it — but denies it amounts to the deep, could-have-done-otherwise
authorship people actually mean when they assign ultimate praise and blame. So
the dilemma re-forms: either "ordinary free will" is the thin compatibilist
notion (which Harris concedes and which arguably does not support *desert*-based
responsibility), or it is a thicker libertarian notion (which faces exactly the
sourcehood and luck problems Harris raised). Calling maximal autonomy a straw man
does not by itself locate a coherent third option.

**Second**, Harris's core worry was never really "you didn't pick your genes" as
such; it was the **sourcehood** point — that wherever the causal chain behind a
choice is traced, it bottoms out in factors the agent did not author (prior
causes *or* chance). Plantinga's regress is a fair hit on one *formulation*, but
the underlying sourcehood challenge survives reformulation and is not answered
merely by renaming the target.

## Counter-Objection

The steelman shows what Jones still owes — a *positive* account of the ordinary
freedom he says survives — but it does not rescue Harris's original argument,
which really did run through the maximal-autonomy premise (the "we can't pick our
past" move at the top of *Free Will*). The deeper sourcehood worry is legitimate,
but it is a **different and harder** argument than the one Plantinga catches, and
it cuts against *materialist* accounts specifically: if persons are
configurations of caused matter, sourcehood is indeed hopeless. The
Latter-day-Saint reply locates the source in an uncreated, co-eternal
intelligence that is itself the terminus of explanation — an agent-causal answer
to sourcehood rather than a dodge of it (see
[[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]] and
[[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]). Jones' own next move,
quantum indeterminism, is the *weaker* leg here, since Harris already argued
randomness is not authorship — the tension tracked in
[[The Conway-Kochen Free Will Theorem - Indeterminism Preserves Freedom]] and
[[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]].
