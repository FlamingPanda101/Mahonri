# Evil Is a Privation Not a Thing

## The Point
Turek revives Augustine's classical answer to the charge that a good God created evil. The objection runs: God created all things; evil is a thing; therefore God created evil.[^1] Augustine's solution is to deny the second premise. Evil is real, but it is not a "thing" with independent existence — it is a **lack, deficiency, or privation of good** in something that is itself good. Evil "doesn't exist on its own. It only exists as a lack or a deficiency in a good thing."[^2] This dissolves the dilemma: God need not be the author of evil, because evil is not a created substance at all.

## Evidence
Turek traces the argument to Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430), who initially thought evil impugned God's nature before realizing the flaw in the syllogism.[^1] He supplies two homely analogies. Evil is like **rust in a car**: remove the rust and you have a better car; "take the car out of the rust, you have nothing." Evil is like a **cut in a finger**: remove the cut and you have a better finger; remove the finger from the cut and nothing remains.[^3] The point is that evil is parasitic — it has no positive being of its own and "only makes sense against the backdrop of good." This is why our moral vocabulary is so often built from negations: we call people *im*moral, *un*just, *un*fair, *dis*honest — naming the absence of a good rather than a thing in its own right.[^4] Because evil is a deficiency in good, and good itself requires God, the privation analysis dovetails with Turek's larger claim that evil ultimately points back to God rather than away from Him.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "While evil is real, it's not a 'thing.' Evil doesn't exist on its own. It only exists as a lack or a deficiency in a good thing." [^2]

> "Evil is like rust in a car: If you take all of the rust out of a car, you have a better car; if you take the car out of the rust, you have nothing." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The privation view is congenial to Latter-day Saint theology insofar as it protects a foundational commitment: **God is not the author of evil.** Scripture affirms that God "cannot look upon sin with the least degree of allowance" and that all good comes from Him, while that which is evil "cometh of the devil" (cf. Moroni 7). Where the two frameworks differ in *emphasis*: classical privation theory is built on a Neoplatonic metaphysic in which good is being and evil is non-being, often paired with *creatio ex nihilo*. LDS thought, by contrast, holds that matter and intelligence are eternal and uncreated, and tends to locate evil less in a metaphysical "lack of being" and more in the **misuse of moral agency** by free beings — Satan and fallen mortals choosing against light. The accounts are complementary rather than identical: Turek's privation analysis answers the *metaphysical* worry ("did God make an evil thing?"), while the LDS free-agency theodicy answers the *moral* worry ("where does wickedness come from?") by pointing to genuine creaturely choice in a world of real opposition (2 Nephi 2:11). A Latter-day Saint can gladly borrow Augustine's insight that evil is a corruption of good without adopting the ex-nihilo metaphysics sometimes packaged with it.
