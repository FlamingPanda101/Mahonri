# Problem of Evil and the Harms of Religion - Craig vs Hitchens

## The Point
Two related strands run through the debate's later half: the **problem of evil** and the **social harms of religion**. On evil, a student asks Craig how an omnipotent, omniscient God can fail to intervene for children born with AIDS. Craig distinguishes the *intellectual* from the *emotional* problem. The *logical* version (Epicurus) fails because God and evil are not explicitly contradictory, and "no philosopher in the history of the world has ever been able to identify" the hidden premise that would make them so; adding "God has morally sufficient reasons for permitting the evil" shows their compatibility. The *probabilistic* version fails because the atheist cannot know God lacks such reasons — perhaps only "a world... suffused with natural and moral suffering" yields "the maximum number of people" freely saved.[^1] For the *emotional* problem, Craig points to the cross: God "enters into human history in the person of Jesus Christ" and "suffers."[^2]

On religion's harms, Hitchens presses his signature indictment — apartheid was "a creation of the Dutch Reformed Church," fascism was Catholic-aligned, and there are "specific biblical scriptural injunctions to do evil" (the command to exterminate the Amalekites, slavery, genital mutilation). He insists these are "not an aberration of religion" but scriptural.[^3] Craig's reply is a sharp methodological move: "you cannot judge the truth of a worldview by its social impact" — quoting Bertrand Russell that this "cuts both ways." He concedes the Amalekite command is troubling but reframes it as "a question about *biblical inerrancy*," not about God's *existence*: did the ancient Israelites rightly hear God, "or did they in their nationalistic fervor think God is on our side?" — "not an issue between atheism and theism."[^4]

Notably, Hitchens largely *concedes* the intellectual problem of evil as an argument: "If I wasn't a believer I would not feel God owed me an explanation. I'm not one of those atheists who thinks you can go around... complaining." His objection is moral revulsion, illustrated by the Fritzl case, not a disproof.[^5]

## Evidence
**Hitchens (negative):**
- Religion causes specific evils by scriptural command — the Amalekite genocide, slavery, mutilation — "not an aberration... it is a scriptural injunction."[^3]
- Atrocity regimes were religious or church-backed (apartheid, fascism, emperor-worship Japan).[^6]
- Yet on the abstract problem of evil he is candid: he would not say "God owed me an explanation"; his complaint is moral, e.g., the Fritzl horror.[^5]

**Craig (affirmative):**
- The logical problem of evil fails (no demonstrable contradiction; "morally sufficient reasons" possibly true); the probabilistic version fails (the atheist can't show God would prevent the evil).[^1]
- "You cannot judge the truth of a worldview by its social impact" (Russell cuts both ways); social harm is "irrelevant" to truth.[^4]
- The Amalekite issue is about *inerrancy and inspiration*, not God's existence: did Israel hear God rightly, or rationalize conquest?[^4]
- The cross meets the *emotional* problem: God himself suffers in Christ.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "The atheist would have to show that it is logically impossible for God to have morally sufficient reasons for permitting the evil and suffering in the world — and no atheist has ever been able to do that." [^1]

> "There are specific biblical scriptural injunctions to do evil... it is not an aberration of religion, it is a scriptural injunction." (Hitchens) [^3]

> "You cannot judge the truth of a worldview by its social impact... Russell said you cannot assess the truth of a worldview by seeing whether it's good for society or not. I think Russell's point cuts both ways." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Craig's free-will/soul-making defense — that God may permit suffering to bring "the maximum number of people freely into his kingdom" — is broadly compatible with Latter-day Saint theology, which adds distinctive resources. The Restoration's doctrine of a **pre-mortal existence** frames mortality (with its suffering) as a chosen, purposeful estate of growth ([[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]]), and the rejection of creation *ex nihilo* means God works with **co-eternal** matter, intelligences, and law rather than being the sole author of every condition of existence ([[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]) — which softens the "why did a from-nothing Creator design it this way" force of Hitchens' indictment. On the harms of religion, Craig's "you can't judge a worldview by its social impact" is sound, and his relocation of the Amalekite problem to *inerrancy* is congenial to Latter-day Saints, who already hold scripture to be true only "as far as it is translated correctly" (Articles of Faith 1:8) and so are not committed to defending every Old Testament war command as a literal divine decree.
