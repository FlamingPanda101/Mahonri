# The Creation Dilemma Targets Five Divine Attributes Not the Trinity Specifically

## The Claim

[[Hayden Carroll]]'s *Creation Dilemma* is widely thought to be an argument against the **Trinity** (his campus banner reads "the Trinity is evil"). Carroll clarifies that the banner is bait; the argument actually targets a **bundle of five divine attributes**, not the Trinity as such. It applies to *any* God — Trinitarian Christian, Muslim, or Jewish — who is said to (1) know the future, (2) be all-powerful, (3) be unbound, (4) create *ex nihilo* (from nothing), and (5) send people to hell. "If you accept that as a Trinitarian, then yes; if not, then no." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The banner is rhetorical bait, not the thesis — rate: strong (author's own framing)

Carroll admits the "Trinity is evil" banner exists only "to draw people in," because "if I said perfect being theology is evil, people don't know what I'm talking about." Most Trinitarians he meets *do* affirm the five attributes, "and that's what I'm critiquing." So the title attacks a familiar label while the argument attacks the attributes underneath it. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — Carroll states this explicitly and repeatedly.

### 2. The argument is cross-traditional — rate: moderate-to-strong

"I'm critiquing any God — and this may be the case for Islam and Judaism as well — who affirms that God knows the future, is all-powerful, is unbound, creates from nothing, and where people go to hell. It's those attributes." So a Trinitarian who *rejects* one of the five (e.g., open theists who deny exhaustive foreknowledge, or annihilationists who deny eternal torment) escapes the argument. This is the same five-claims structure formalized at [[The Five Incompatible Claims of Creedal Theism]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong — the cross-traditional scope follows directly from targeting attributes rather than the doctrine of God's internal structure.

### 3. It does not depend on consubstantiality or persons-vs-being — rate: moderate

Although the conversation opens on whether social Trinitarianism is coherent (Carroll grants it is not *logically* incoherent), the dilemma never relies on any feature of how the three persons relate. It is indifferent to whether God is unitary or triune; what matters is the five-attribute profile. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a clarifying point that defuses the "this is just anti-Trinitarian polemic" objection.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A Trinitarian philosopher can respond that bundling "unbound" and "creates ex nihilo" with omniscience and hell smuggles in a *particular* (often Calvinist or popular) construal of those attributes that sophisticated Trinitarians do not hold — e.g., Molinists deny that omnipotence can actualize "only the saved" (see [[God Could Have Created Only the Saved]]), and many deny that foreknowledge is morally equivalent to intending an outcome. On this reading the argument does not touch "the Trinity" or even classical theism per se, only a contestable package of attribute-interpretations. Carroll partly concedes this — the argument "is stronger on some worldviews than others," strongest against Calvinism and Catholicism (see [[The Creation Dilemma Is Strongest Against Calvinism and Catholicism]]) — which is precisely his point that it tracks attributes, not labels.

## Best Quotes

> "I'm critiquing any God... who affirms that God knows the future, is all-powerful, is unbound, creates from nothing, and where people go to hell. It's those attributes." [^2]

> "When I go to Arizona State... it does say 'the Trinity is evil.' And the only reason... is to draw people in... most Trinitarians I talk to will affirm particular attributes of God. And that's what I'm critiquing." [^1]
