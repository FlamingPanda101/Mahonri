# The Two Great Wrong Turns - Overthrow of Origen and Pelagius

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] argues that "much of Christianity goes awry" at **two datable points**, each marked by the church repudiating a major figure: (1) the anathematization of [[Origen]] — "the most important theologian in early Christianity" — for his **premortality of human spirits** and his **universalism**; and (2) the condemnation of [[Pelagius]] (c. AD 400), after which "free will becomes subordinated to grace," and "salvation contracts from universal to a limited few, the elect." On Givens's reading the theology of *ascent* (humans rising toward God) was replaced by a theology of *descending grace*, and a generous, expansive plan of salvation was traded for one that "condemn[s] the vast majority of his creations to hell."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Origen's centrality and posthumous condemnation — rate: accurate (intellectual history).** Givens stresses Origen "was widely viewed as the most important voice in the Christian tradition until you get to the sixth century, and then he becomes anathematized" for premortality and universalism. This tracks the historical Origenist controversy culminating in the anathemas associated with Constantinople II (553). See [[Origen's Apokatastasis Taught Universal Restoration]] and [[Origen on the Preexistence and Fall of Souls]].[^1]
2. **A non-LDS scholar's verdict — rate: strong as cited authority.** Givens invokes Duke historian [[Elizabeth Clark]] ("I don't think she was even a believer"), endorsing her judgment that "Christianity chose a tragic path when it chose the path of [[Augustine]] over the path of Origen."[^3]
3. **The Pelagian turn is datable "to the very year" — rate: moderate (a deliberate simplification).** Givens: when [[Augustine]] "succeeds in getting Pelagius condemned... it's at that moment, I mean we can track it to the very year, when free will becomes subordinated to grace." Pelagianism was condemned at the First Council of Ephesus (431). See [[The Pelagian Controversy on Grace and Human Ability]].[^2]
4. **The LDS self-location — rate: candid.** Givens notes "some theologians today refer to Latter-day Saints as Pelagian... because we so emphasize freedom of the will," and treats this as a feature, not a bug, of the Restoration's recovery of the theology of ascent.[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing (proto-orthodox / Augustinian) side:** Origen and Pelagius were not arbitrarily silenced; each was judged to threaten core gospel commitments. Origen's premortal-fall cosmology imports a Platonic transmigration scheme foreign to the biblical doctrine of creation, and his apokatastasis arguably empties final judgment of seriousness. Pelagius, in [[Augustine]]'s eyes, made grace dispensable — if we *could* in principle save ourselves by free choice, the cross becomes advisory rather than necessary (Augustine's stated alarm that Pelagius "depreciate[d] grace as essential"). On this telling the church corrected real errors, and "ascent" theologies recurrently underrate human bondage to sin (Rom 7). The strongest version notes that Pelagius himself was likely more moderate than the "Pelagianism" condemned, so "the very year" oversimplifies a long, multi-synod process.

**Counter-objection (Givens's rejoinder):** Even granting genuine dangers, the *cost* of the settlement was a God who "foreordained vast multitudes to hell," which Givens treats as a worse error than the one it cured — and the very fact that universalism is "an outlier" no longer (it now has "strong proponents" in "every faith tradition alive in America," and [[D&C 76]] is "a pretty clear affirmation of... quasi-universalism") suggests the church over-corrected. He reframes universalism not as "automatic salvation" with "no accountability" but as "an unlimited opportunity extended to all" — his friend's "no final buzzerism."[^4] See [[Premortality Is the One LDS Doctrine Mainstream Christianity Will Not Move Toward]].

## Best Quotes
> "Origen, who is the most important theologian in early Christianity... was widely viewed as the most important voice in the Christian tradition until you get to the sixth century, and then he becomes anathematized for two of his notorious teachings. One was the premortality of human spirits, and the second was his universalism."[^1]

> "She said Christianity chose a tragic path when it chose the path of Augustine over the path of Origen. And I think that's absolutely the case."[^3]

> "[Augustine] succeeds in getting Pelagius condemned. And it's at that moment — I mean, we can track it to the very year — when free will becomes subordinated to grace. Salvation contracts from universal to a limited few, the elect."[^2]

> "A better term would be no final buzzerism."[^4]
