# Faith for the Things We Cannot Change

## The Claim
[[Sharon Eubank]], from decades of humanitarian work, names "a really hard reality": there are evils "I cannot change" — global conflicts in Ukraine, Russia, Gaza, Israel — that exceed any individual's power. Her response is neither despair nor pretense of control but **faith and prayer**: doing what can be done locally, and entrusting the rest to a God "much bigger than I am" who "will make all those things right… It might not be in this world, but he will do it."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Honest limits, not learned helplessness (strong).** "I cannot stop the conflict… I don't have the ability to change a global conflict. I'm just one person." Eubank, who *runs* global relief, models accepting the boundary while still acting where she can.[^1]
2. **Mother Teresa as the paradigm (moderate, illustrative).** Mother Teresa "cannot change the fact that they're going to die… but she can make sure that they won't be alone." Much of the work is "I cannot change the reality of this situation, but I'll be with you." (Cf. the divine co-suffering in [[God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ]].)[^2]
3. **Prayer as real action (moderate, scriptural).** She cites James 5:16 ("the effectual fervent prayer… availeth much") and the Bethlehem nun who kneels 24/7 praying for peace; to the objection "what good is that?" she answers that drawing on faith "is one of the great consolations… God will look over the things that we can't do anything about."[^3]
4. **Interior change precedes exterior change (interpretive).** Quoting Ezra Taft Benson and Mother Teresa, she holds that helping people gain confidence and agency ("interior remodeling") is what finally lets them "take themselves out of the slums" — faith works by transforming people, not only circumstances.[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The problem-of-evil objection presses hard here: appealing to a God who "will make it right… maybe not in this world" can look like a way of *tolerating* present atrocity, and prayer for distant wars is precisely the kind of action a skeptic calls inert — "a woman kneeling at an altar and not doing anything." If God has the power to stop Gaza and does not, faith-as-consolation neither explains the inaction nor relieves the victims.

## Counter-Objection
Eubank concedes the tension rather than dissolving it: she "feel[s] the burden" of what she cannot change and offers faith as *consolation and orientation*, not as a theodicy or a substitute for action where action is possible (her whole thesis is doing the local thing you *can* do). Her claim is bounded — for the genuinely-beyond-reach, the alternatives are despair, denial, or trust, and she chooses trust grounded in the conviction that the present world is not the last word (see [[Resurrection Is the Beginning of a New Creation]]). Whether that satisfies the intellectual problem is separate from whether it sustains a humanitarian who has stared at mass suffering for a career.

## Best Quotes
> "I cannot change the reality of this situation, but I'll be with you. I'll sit by you. I can soften this."[^2]

> "All I can do is turn to faith and ask God in his mightiness… to intervene and to work on that and to have the patience for that."[^1]
