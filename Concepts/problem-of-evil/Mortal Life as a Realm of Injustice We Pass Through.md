# Mortal Life as a Realm of Injustice We Pass Through

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] frames the LDS answer to gratuitous, undeserved suffering not as the claim that the world is secretly *just*, but as the claim that mortality is **deliberately a realm of injustice** that agents must pass through to become as God is. "This life is a chance for us to pass through a realm of injustice. Now I say a realm of injustice very specifically, because an unjust world is a world where anything can happen, where an innocent person can suffer for no apparent reason at all." Crucially, when confronted with horrific, unfair suffering, the LDS theist does **not** explain it away: "When you see the suffering in things and it's totally unfair, I throw up my hands and I go, 'Exactly. It is chaos, and it was stupid, and it happened, and that is unfortunately what this world is like.'" The fallen world's *capacity to be unjust* is the point — it is what makes the moral test, and therefore exaltation, possible. Hansen ties this to a **fortunate-fall** theology (Adam and Eve's fall as "part of the plan") and to **opposition in all things** ([[2 Nephi 2.11|2 Nephi 2]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
- **The parental "leave the house" analogy.** Hansen's load-bearing illustration: a loving father takes care of his children but "you don't want your children to live in your house with you forever where you take care of all their needs, because they will never grow and progress. There are laws of reality such that you as a father cannot make your children, with the snap of your fingers, into what you are. They have to go out into this world and have experiences." Mortality is the children being sent *out of the house* — given freedom, and with it the genuine possibility of walking away.[^2] This grounds the theodicy in **agency**: a realm where nothing unjust could happen would be a realm without real freedom or real stakes.
- **It concedes gratuitousness instead of denying it.** The distinctive feature is that Hansen *refuses* the classical "God has a hidden reason for each evil" move. The injustice is not individually purposed; it is the **systemic price** of a world genuinely open to free action. This sidesteps the standard objection to greater-good theodicies (that they make God complicit in each particular evil — cf. [[Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy]] and [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]]) by relocating the justification from *each instance* to *the existence of the arena*.
- **Distinct from generic soul-making.** Goff hears the parallel and names it: "what you said sounds a little bit like the soul-making theodicy of John Hick." But Hansen's version adds two things Hick's lacks (see [[Soul-Making Theodicy - Hick and Joseph Smith]]): (1) **premortal consent** — agents agreed to enter this realm, which answers "the innocent never signed up" ([[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]); and (2) **a genuinely constrained God** for whom the realm-of-injustice is "the only way," not a route an omnipotent God merely preferred. Goff's own pushback to Hick ("but does it have to be *this* gruesome?") is exactly what the constrained-God premise answers: given eternal law, it does.[^3]
- **Steelman the objection.** A critic replies that calling injustice "the only way" still leaves the *degree* unexplained — childhood cancer, predation, "creatures that paralyze their prey and slowly eat them alive" (Goff's examples). Why this *much* horror for the test? Hansen's two-part answer: (a) because God did not author the base reality *ex nihilo*, the raw material's chaotic character is not God's free choice but what he organizes within ([[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]); and (b) "this is the best possible world that could exist *in order to achieve God's purposes*" — best-relative-to-the-end of producing gods, not best in comfort. The note's honest residue: Hansen grants the problem "is still a very difficult issue... I won't say that it's simple," claiming only *better* answers, not a dissolution.[^4]

## LDS Relevance
This reframing is a sharp apologetic asset because it **stops trying to win the argument the atheist is strongest on**. Rather than insisting the world looks fair (it does not — see [[The Test World Does Not Look Like a Test]]), the Latter-day Saint *agrees* with the skeptic that the world is full of senseless, undeserved suffering, then argues that a *realm capable of injustice* is exactly what a free, soul-making probation requires — and that an unfallen, micromanaged world would defeat the entire purpose of mortality (cf. [[The Dual Potential of Human Nature as Moral Test]] and [[Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth Not Corruption]]). It coheres with the broader Restoration package: a **fortunate fall**, premortal consent, opposition in all things, and a God who **organizes** rather than creates *ex nihilo* — so the chaos is not a flaw in God's free design but the medium he redeems. The move converts the problem of evil from a defeater into a feature: a world where the innocent can suffer "for no apparent reason at all" is the only kind of world in which acorns can freely become oaks.
