# Discovering a Miracle's Mechanism Does Not Make It Less Miraculous

## The Claim

[[Ether's Elephant]] argues that finding the natural mechanism by which a miracle occurred does not dissolve the miracle. Both sides of a familiar debate err: religious people who assume "miracles cannot be explained," and scientists who assume that explaining the mechanism means "it was just science," not God. On the Latter-day Saint view, God ordinarily works *through* natural processes, so a discovered mechanism is the *how* of the miracle, not its disproof — God "set that process in motion."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The lightning-as-AED parable.** A man in cardiac arrest spontaneously recovers; later it is found that lightning struck "at precisely the right time and spot to replicate an AED." The religious onlookers stop calling it a miracle; the scientists declare victory. The video says both responses are mistaken — "discovering how the miracle happened does not make it less miraculous."[^1] *Rating: strong as a conceptual point; the timing/precision is exactly the sort of thing a providential reading highlights, though a skeptic will call it coincidence (see steelman).*

2. **Knowing the mechanism is compatible with divine action.** "It doesn't negate my belief in God because I can explain it. God put us here with a brain to learn and to understand how God's creation works."[^2] The dichotomy "either God or a mechanism" is false; secondary causes do not crowd out a primary cause. *Rating: strong; this is the mainstream theistic account of providence and concurrence.*

3. **Scripture commands confessing God's hand in *all* things.** The video quotes [[Moroni 7]]/Alma-style language: "in nothing does man offend God... save those who confess not his hand in all things." A naturalistic mechanism is therefore not a rival to God but an instance of his hand.[^3] *Rating: strong as LDS grounding; it is a scriptural premise, persuasive to the in-tradition reader.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The skeptic presses two points. (a) **Verificationist worry:** if a miracle survives the discovery of a complete natural mechanism, then *nothing* could ever count as showing an event was *not* a miracle — the claim becomes unfalsifiable, and an unfalsifiable "miracle" does no explanatory work. (b) **Ockham:** once lightning fully explains the recovery, "God set it in motion" is an idle wheel — true perhaps, but adding nothing the physics did not already supply, exactly the [[Miracle-Tests for God Commit the God-of-the-Gaps Fallacy|god-of-the-gaps]] structure in reverse. The strongest reply concedes that *this kind* of event is not **evidence** for God to a neutral party (the video agrees: such experiences are "for you and only you"), while maintaining that mechanism and providence are *compatible* — discovering the one does not refute the other. The claim is a defense of coherence, not an apologetic proof.

## Best Quotes

> "Discovering how the miracle happened does not make it less miraculous."[^1]

> "It doesn't negate my belief in God because I can explain it. God put us here with a brain to learn and to understand how God's creation works."[^2]

> "Or that God, if you're a person of faith, didn't set that process in motion."[^1]

## LDS Connection

The note pairs with the Restoration rejection of the [[The Warfare Model of Science and Religion Is a Myth|science-vs-religion warfare model]]: because God works through law and natural process, learning the mechanism is itself an act of confessing his hand ([[Moroni 7]]). It also guards against the inverse mistake of resting faith on the unexplained, which would make God a [[God of the Gaps - Demonstration vs Scientific Hypothesis|God of the gaps]] who shrinks with every discovery.
