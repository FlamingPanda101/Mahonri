# The Drowning-Girls Analogy Against TULIP

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] frames his entire affirmative case with one intuition pump: a fisherman finds several little girls drowning, "randomly saves three," then "sat and watched" the rest die before driving the saved ones to shore. Nearly everyone calls the fisherman evil — and Hansen argues that fisherman *is* the God of TULIP. [^1] He maps each petal onto the story: **T** (total depravity) — even "infants and drowning little girls" deserve not just death but "eternity of conscious torment"; **U** (unconditional election) — "no condition... affects the fisherman's selection of which of the drowning girls will live or die"; **L** (limited atonement) — "the fisherman could save all the girls, but he limits how many"; **I** (irresistible grace) — once chosen "they have no choice in the matter"; **P** (perseverance) — "your fate... and the fate of those two dead girls in the lake was fixed from the very beginning of time." [^2] [[James White]] rejects the analogy as emotional manipulation: the girls are "not innocent little eight-year-olds drowning in a lake," but "creatures of God in rebellion against their creator," so the true picture is rebels who seized a castle and killed the king's son, to *some* of whom the king then shows undeserved mercy. [^3]

## Evidence
- **Hansen's TULIP walkthrough is the spine of the affirmative.** He devotes roughly eight minutes to expounding each petal through the fisherman, quoting John Calvin that even infants are "odious and abominable to God." [^2] The rhetorical claim: if the *story* is monstrous, and the story just *is* the system, then the system is monstrous.
- **The arbitrariness charge.** "If two little girls are equally lost, equally desperate, and equally in need, and God saves one and not the other, on what basis is that just?... is that justice? Or is that arbitrariness?" [^4] This connects to [[Calvinism Consistency with Atheism on Free Will]] and the broader [[Arbitrariness and Inconsistency - The Two Intellectual Sins]].
- **White's counter-analogy relocates the moral baseline.** "Rebels take over a castle... the king sends his son... and they kill the son. And then the king comes and he shows mercy to some of those rebels, undeserving rebels." [^3] The move is to deny premise one of the story — the victims' innocence — so that *any* mercy is supererogatory grace, not owed justice.
- **White's standing reply to all such stories: "I cannot win an emotion war and I'm not going to try."** [^5] He insists the resolution is decided by exegesis (Romans 9), not by intuition pumps "meant to poison your mind."

## Best Quotes
> "He randomly saved three of the girls and then drove a little ways away and sat and watched... the cries of the desperate 8- and 9-year-old were eventually muffled by the sound of gurgling water." — Hansen [^1]

> "If two little girls are equally lost, equally desperate, and equally in need, and God saves one and not the other, on what basis is that just?" — Hansen [^4]

> "They're not innocent little eight-year-olds drowning in a lake. They're creatures of God in rebellion against their creator and God in his mercy shows love and saves undeserving sinners." — White [^3]

## LDS Connection
The drowning-girls analogy is a popular-level cousin of [[The Button Thought Experiment for the Problem of Hell]] and Blake Ostler's indictment in [[Meticulous Providence Makes God the Author of Evil]] (the debate-prep recording for this very debate). The Latter-day Saint upshot is not merely that the analogy stings but that the Restoration *dissolves* the dilemma it exploits: because agency is rooted in the **uncreated, co-eternal intelligence** ([[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]), God does not unilaterally "select which girls live or die" — salvation is a genuine, mutual relationship the creature can accept or reject. The binary that makes the story horrifying (saved vs. drowned-and-damned) is replaced by degrees of glory and ongoing progression ([[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]]; [[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]]). White's rebuttal — that the victims are guilty rebels, not innocents — itself rests on imputed Adamic guilt, which the LDS view rejects: humans inherit the *effects* of the Fall and need healing, not condemnation for "what they are." Note White's strongest fair point, which the LDS apologist must answer rather than dodge: an intuition pump is not an exegesis of Romans 9, and "I feel this is unjust" is not yet an argument that it *is* unjust — which is why the companion grounding-of-goodness dispute ([[Whose God Can Ground Morally Reprehensible]]) matters.
