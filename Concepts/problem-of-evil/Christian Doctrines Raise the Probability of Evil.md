# Christian Doctrines Raise the Probability of Evil

## The Point
Craig's third reply turns the probabilistic argument on its head. The objection treats "God exists" as a bare hypothesis and asks how likely suffering is on it. But the actual hypothesis under attack is *Christian* theism, and "Christianity entails doctrines that increase the probability of the coexistence of God and suffering."[^1] That is, the conditional probability of evil given Christianity is much higher than its probability given generic theism — so once we condition on the *real* content of the faith, the existence of suffering stops being surprising. "If Christianity is true, it's not at all improbable that suffering and evil should exist."[^2]

## Evidence
Craig names four doctrines, each of which raises the probability of evil:
1. **The purpose of life is to know God, not to be happy.** People assume God's job is "to create a comfortable environment for his human pets," but Christianity holds the goal is knowing God, and "suffering is one of the ways God can draw people freely to himself" — even noting that "countries that have endured the most hardship often show the highest growth rate for Christianity." Craig quotes Lewis: "God whispers to us in our pleasures ... but shouts in our pains. It is his megaphone to rouse a deaf world."[^3]
2. **Humanity is in rebellion against God.** Terrible human evils testify to "man's depravity, a consequence of his alienation from God." So "the Christian isn't surprised at moral evil in the world; on the contrary, he expects it."[^4]
3. **God's purpose spills over beyond the grave.** This life is "the entry way to an unimaginable, never-ending life." Paul, himself a sufferer, calls present affliction "slight" and "momentary," "preparing us for an eternal weight of glory beyond all comparison."[^5]
4. **The knowledge of God is an incomparable good.** Knowing God is "an infinite good," so "the person who knows God, no matter how much he has suffered, can still say God is good to me."[^6]

The free-will dimension threads through doctrines 1–2 — God "having given us free will, is drawing as many people as he can into his unending Kingdom" — which connects to the broader free-will theodicy (see [[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy]]). Logically, this reply complements the other two: where reply one denies we can judge God lacks reasons (see [[We Cannot Judge That God Lacks Reasons for Suffering]]) and reply two insists on the full evidence base (see [[Probability of God Must Be Weighed Against All the Evidence]]), reply three supplies positive background that makes evil *expected* on Christianity.

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
Each of Craig's four doctrines is affirmed and *deepened* in Restoration theology. The purpose of life as soul-making rather than comfort is the heart of the plan: mortality is a probation in which "we will prove them herewith, to see if they will do all things whatsoever the Lord their God shall command them" (Abraham 3:25), and "opposition in all things" makes suffering structurally necessary to growth rather than a regrettable side effect (2 Nephi 2:11). On humanity's fallen state, Latter-day Saints affirm a real Fall while reframing it as a foreseen, even forward step ("Adam fell that men might be"; "men are, that they might have joy," 2 Nephi 2:25) — so moral evil is expected without a doctrine of total depravity. The "spills over beyond the grave" point is vastly enlarged: the eternal scope runs both directions, from a pre-mortal estate of agency to a post-mortal continuation where wrongs are restored (Alma 40–42), so Paul's "momentary affliction" is a finite mortal probation framed by eternity. And the knowledge of God as the incomparable good aligns with "this is life eternal, that they might know thee" (John 17:3) and the doctrine of eternal progression toward divine nature. A point of integrity: Craig's appeal works *given* Christianity, so it persuades only one who grants the framework; its job is not to convert the skeptic from scratch but to show that suffering is no evidence *against* a faith that already predicts it — and the Restoration, with uncreated agency and a literal eternal arc, predicts it even more strongly.
