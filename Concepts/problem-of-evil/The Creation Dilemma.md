# The Creation Dilemma

## The Claim (Objection to Trinitarian Omnibenevolence)
[[Hayden Carroll]]'s central negative argument: under classical creedal trinitarian theism, the resolution "the triune God is omnibenevolent" cannot be maintained without contradiction. The dilemma, stated tightly: **God knows who will be eternally tormented; God could refrain from creating them; God creates them anyway; and God is still said to will their good.** These claims "cannot all be true at the same time." Something must give — and creedal Christianity insists nothing can. This is the formal-debate elaboration of the vault's existing [[Problem of Hell and Creation]] and [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]].[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The button thought-experiment** — rate: strong intuition pump. You hold a button that creates a conscious person; you are shown their entire life and certain final outcome (eternal conscious torment); you are under no obligation and lose nothing by refraining. Do you press it? Hayden: if being born were truly better for them all-things-considered you'd press without hesitation — but you hesitate, because creating someone whose definite outcome is hell "feels incompatible with willing their good." *(thought experiment)* [^2] This mirrors the roller-coaster/created-person pump in [[Problem of Hell and Creation]].
2. **Knowledge + freedom = responsibility** — the moral engine. When an agent *freely* brings about a *foreknown* catastrophic outcome that didn't have to occur, we hold them responsible everywhere else (defective-product company, bridge engineer who builds knowing it will collapse). God has both perfectly. See [[Creation Is a Free Morally Evaluable Act]] and [[Foreknowledge Plus Power Turns Permission Into Choice]]. *(moral-parallel)* [^3]
3. **Creation precedes freedom** — the free-will defense misfires because "the first morally decisive moment is not human rebellion; it is creation itself." See [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]]. *(logical-ordering)* [^4]
4. **The five claims** — Hayden formalizes creedal theism into five mainstream claims (omniscience, omnipotence/freedom, creation ex nihilo, some go to hell, God wills all good) that cannot be jointly held. See [[The Five Incompatible Claims of Creedal Theism]]. *(systematic)* [^5]
5. **Scriptural self-witness** — Jesus says of Judas it "would have been better... if he had not been born" ([[Matthew 26.24|Matt 26:24]], [[John 17.12|John 17:12]]). If non-existence is preferable, why create him? See [[The Judas Counterexample]]. *(scriptural)* [^6]

**Steelman of the affirmative (the five escape-routes Hayden pre-blocks):** (a) limit foreknowledge (open theism) — abandons omniscience; (b) limit freedom — abandons the free-will defense; (c) reject creation ex nihilo — abandons a creedal pillar (this is the LDS exit, [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]]); (d) deny eternal torment (annihilationism/universalism); (e) redefine "good" (mystery — [[The Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good of Meaning]]). Hayden's claim: "there is no sixth option," and each route revises a doctrine the resolution presupposes.

## Best Quotes
> "God knows who will be eternally tormented. He could refrain from creating them. He creates them anyway. And he's still said to will their good. These claims cannot all be true at the same time."[^1]

> "If being born were truly better for them, all things considered, you would press the button without hesitation. But you hesitate... because you would be bringing into existence someone whose definite outcome is eternal conscious torment."[^2]

> "These claims cannot live in the same house."[^5]

> "This debate is not about winning arguments. It's about whether the word good still means what we say it means when we apply it to God."[^7]
