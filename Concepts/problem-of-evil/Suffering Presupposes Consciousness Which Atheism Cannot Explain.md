# Suffering Presupposes Consciousness Which Atheism Cannot Explain

## The Point
This is [[Hayden Carroll]]'s opening reversal of [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]'s claim that "suffering makes God's existence unlikely." Instead of defending a theodicy on O'Connor's terms, Hayden re-routes the problem of evil into the problem of consciousness. The move has three steps, each of which O'Connor concedes on tape: (1) consciousness is *necessary* for suffering — "if you don't have consciousness you don't have suffering"; (2) atheism does not obviously account for consciousness — O'Connor allows it "may seem to imply the existence of a conscious creator of the universe"; therefore (3) the very phenomenon O'Connor weaponizes against theism (suffering) rides on a phenomenon (consciousness) that, by O'Connor's own admission, fits *theism* better than atheism.[^1] The strategic payoff: the problem of evil cannot be cashed out without smuggling in consciousness, and consciousness is itself evidence for a mind behind the cosmos.

## Evidence
- **The concession chain.** O'Connor agrees consciousness is necessary for suffering ("probably yeah"), then agrees "if there is no consciousness there is no suffering... that's probably true," and finally — pressed on whether atheism accounts for consciousness — says "perhaps not."[^1] Hansen flags this as "a big admission."
- **Why it bites.** Hansen's narration sharpens the logic: in "a purely material universe" suffering is *not* what we should expect, because suffering is "a manifestation of consciousness," and a mindless material substrate gives no reason to expect inner experience at all.[^2] So the argument from suffering quietly presupposes exactly the kind of fact (irreducible first-person experience) that the *theist* predicts and the naturalist struggles to derive from particles.
- **O'Connor's escape — separate the two debates.** O'Connor's defense is that this "shifts the goalposts": the original claim assumed a material world of conscious creatures, and whether *consciousness* is better explained on atheism is a *different* argument. "If you don't want to grant the existence of the material world before we start... we can do an argument from consciousness, but then we need to debate whether consciousness is explicable on atheism."[^3] Rate: **a fair procedural objection** — Hayden has changed the subject from theodicy to philosophy of mind. But it is also a concession that O'Connor cannot run the argument from evil *without* first being granted consciousness, which is the very thing in dispute. This is the standard theistic "argument from consciousness" (cf. [[Consciousness and First Cause Problem for Atheism]], [[The Cosmic Conscious Argument for God]]).
- **Steelman the naturalist.** A committed physicalist would simply deny step (2): consciousness is a high-level product of evolved nervous systems, fully natural, so granting "a material world of conscious creatures" is no concession to theism at all. On that view Hayden has won only a verbal point — O'Connor's "perhaps not" reflects honest uncertainty about the hard problem, not an admission that theism explains mind. The exchange therefore shows the *cost* of the argument from suffering (it presupposes consciousness) without settling whether consciousness needs God.

## Best Quotes
> "Why is he asking this? Because suffering is a manifestation of consciousness. Put simply, if you don't have consciousness you don't have suffering." [^2]

> "Consciousness may seem to imply the existence of a conscious creator of the universe." [^1]

> "What I'm going to say is this... if you don't want to grant the existence of the material world before we start talking about this, we can do an argument from consciousness, but then we need to debate whether consciousness is explicable on atheism." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The argument is general theism, but it sets up the specifically Latter-day Saint payload that [[Jacob Hansen]] delivers later: if consciousness (intelligence) is fundamental, the restored gospel already holds that intelligences are *uncreated and co-eternal* with God (D&C 93), so mind is not a late accident of matter but a basic feature of reality. That makes the LDS finitist model especially comfortable saying both "consciousness is basic" and "God works within eternal law" — see [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
