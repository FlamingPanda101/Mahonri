# The Pace Memo Recycled Existing Satanic Panic Tropes

## The Claim
Nothing in the Pace memo is original. Its imagery and even its quantitative assumptions are lifted from the existing satanic-panic literature — above all *Michelle Remembers* — making the memo a "late-stage synthesis" of well-circulated tropes rather than an independent discovery.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The motifs are stock genre material (strong)
Pace's "baptized by blood," doctrinal inversion ("black is white and white is black"), "spiritual grooming of children," "mock burial rituals," and "buried, fragmented, and layered memories" were "all very common phrases popularized in the media" and "common motifs in the sensational books of the 1980s." *Michelle Remembers* "described an oath to the devil or being given to the devil, just like in Pace's memo" [^1].

### 2. The "coven of 13" is a direct borrowing (strong — specific tell)
Pace assumes "each of the 60 victims comes from a coven of 13, which is straight from *Michelle Remembers* by Pazder and Smith." The same coven-of-13 figure appears in *The Satanic Underground* and in [[Mike Warnke]]'s *The Satan Seller*. The shared, arbitrary number is the fingerprint of copying. See [[Lauren Stratford Was a Serial Fabricator of Victim Identities]] [^2].

### 3. Stylometric convergence (moderate — AI analysis)
"An analysis by AI shows that *Michelle Remembers* has the most influence on the language contained in the Pace memo, followed closely by the media coverage of the McMartin trial." The memo "recycles evangelical anti-cult theology, recovered memory therapeutic discourse, media-amplified legal cases, and popular SRA memoir templates" — "a product of convergence and not origination" [^3].

### 4. Utah was late, not first (moderate — chronology)
"Pace and the state of Utah were actually quite late to the satanic panic game." Far from breaking news, the memo arrived after the tropes were fully established nationally, which is why it reads as derivative. See [[Michelle Remembers Sparked the Satanic Panic]] [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender might respond that genuine satanic cults could share real practices, so overlapping motifs need not mean copying — a true phenomenon would *also* produce recurring descriptions. The rebuttal: the recurring elements here (an arbitrary coven size of exactly 13, the same literary phrasings, the same media-trial vocabulary) track the *publication history of specific books*, not any independently verified practice, and the most probable source is shared cultural transmission. When the unique "tell" is a number invented in a discredited memoir, convergence on it points to the memoir, not to Satan.
