# The Mary Problem for Catholic Theism

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that Roman Catholics face the creation dilemma more acutely than any other creedal group because of the **Immaculate Conception** dogma. Catholic teaching holds that God preserved Mary free from original sin from conception, and that she then *of her own free will* lived sinlessly her entire life. Carroll's challenge ("the Mary problem"): this proves God *can* create a person without sin who freely chooses good — so why does He use that power only once in human history? If God can make sinless, freely-good people, "why doesn't God only create people he knows will go to heaven?"[^1] The dogma converts the dilemma from "could God have done otherwise?" into "by your own theology, He demonstrably could — and didn't."

## The Rebuttal
1. **It weaponizes a Catholic dogma as an internal proof — rate: strong against Catholics specifically.** Because the Immaculate Conception is *de fide* dogma, the Catholic cannot deny that God exercised exactly the power in question; the move is a clean internal critique (see [[The Creation Dilemma Is an Internal Catch-22]]).[^1] [[Luke Hansen]] generalizes it: "the creedal God could just have created those who make it to heaven already in heaven" — the only thing stopping Him was Himself.[^2] See [[Why Doesnt God Only Create the Saved]]. **Steelman of the Catholic:** the Immaculate Conception is *not* a case of God overriding freedom — Mary's preservation was a singular grace ordered to the Incarnation (she had to bear the sinless Christ), not a template for ordinary creation. Catholics can argue God wills a world containing *libertarian* agents whose free response to grace is genuinely their own; a world entirely of preserved-sinless people would lack the good of self-determined virtue won against real temptation. Mary's sinlessness was still freely *maintained*, but her unique mission, not arbitrariness, explains the singularity.
2. **The Catholic counter-replies the panel reports — rate: the panel finds them weak.** Carroll says the most common Catholic answer is "it's actually more loving for God to create them and let them go to hell than not create them," which he answers with the wood-chipper/lava-pool analogy (see [[Free Will Defense Fails - The Lava Pool Analogy]]).[^3] **Steelman:** the "existence is a good" reply (Thomist: being is good as such) is a serious position, not a dodge — Carroll's rebuttal assumes hell makes existence a net harm, which the Thomist denies.
3. **A related Catholic angle the panel raises — rate: contextual.** Luke notes some Catholics hold **double predestination** (God positively chooses both the saved and the damned), though he then corrects that this is *not* official Catholicism (it was condemned).[^4] This shows the panel trying to source positions accurately rather than strawman (cf. [[Calvinism Is the Logical Conclusion of Classical Theism]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Catholics... struggle with this because they have their dogmas that define what God is and they have the Mary problem where God apparently can create people who are created without sin... and who of their free will and choice are sinless... Why doesn't God only create people he knows will go to heaven?" [^1]

> "They gave God this power that he can use, and then he only uses it once in human history... How about you just use that every time?" [^5]

> "Most of the arguments I get from Roman Catholics are, 'Well, it's actually more loving for God to create them and go to hell than not create them.'" [^3]
