# The Universal Desire to Fulfill the Dead's Wishes Witnesses to Objective Moral Truth

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]], reflecting after his son's death, offers an argument from lived behavior to moral realism: "almost every normal person," when a loved one dies, "wants to fulfill the wishes of that person" — yet "if you really believe that person no longer exists, what difference does it make?" The near-universal impulse to honor the dead's hopes reveals that people *live out* a truth (that goodness is real and that the dead still matter) which their stated naturalism cannot ground.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The performative contradiction (strong, observational).** "No matter what we say we believe, we still are living out a different truth" — that there is "such a thing as goodness," "such a thing as moral truth," and that "our loved ones continue and therefore it matters… if we do things on their behalf."[^1]
2. **Atheists who live as moral realists (moderate, illustrative).** Bradley notes there are naturalists who *deny* ultimate moral truth yet "live as if they did," suggesting "a disconnect between what they're saying they believe and the fact that on some level they clearly believe something."[^2]
3. **The love-of-truth argument, parallel form (interpretive).** He runs a sister argument: humans have an implanted "love of the truth," even painful truth — but "why do we have this love of the truth if the truth isn't somehow… ultimately the good?" Both the truth-drive and the honor-the-dead drive point beyond a "particles and forces" ontology in which "where is goodness?"[^3]
4. **Grounded in his own case (strong, first-person).** This is not abstract: Bradley is editing and publishing his late son Donnie's poetry and starting a memorial fund — concretely "living out the truth that our loved ones continue."[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A naturalist can fully account for honoring the dead without any objective morality or survival: evolved kin-altruism, grief as an attachment-system misfire, social signaling, and the psychological comfort of continued ritual all explain the impulse as *adaptive sentiment*, not evidence of moral facts or an afterlife. That people behave *as if* the dead matter shows the strength of evolved bonds, not the existence of a transcendent good — deriving an "ought" or an ontology from a feeling is the naturalistic fallacy.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley's point survives as an argument from *fittingness/abductive coherence* rather than deductive proof: theism predicts and explains both the love of truth and the conviction that persons endure, whereas thoroughgoing naturalism must treat these deep, universal commitments as systematic illusions we cannot help living against. The "it's just evolved sentiment" reply concedes the phenomenon while relocating it to a story in which our most serious moral experience is fiction — a cost the theist says is higher than admitting the experience tracks something real. (See the broader pattern in [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]].)
