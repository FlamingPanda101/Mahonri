# Foreknowledge and Free Will Are Logically Incompatible

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]], an **open theist**, argues that exhaustive divine foreknowledge of *future free acts* is logically incompatible with those acts being free. He notes there is no unified LDS position — "not all Mormons are open theists" — but he holds that God "doesn't know the entire future because future free acts would be incompatible with being known beforehand," and he has a whole volume defending it. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The elegant incompatibility argument** — rate: a clean statement of the standard open-theist argument. "If God knows in 1900 that I am going to rob a 7-Eleven tomorrow, then in order to do other than I do, I would have to have power to change that fact that God knew that. But I have no such power. Therefore foreknowledge and free will are not logically compatible." *(modal-logical argument)* [^2]
2. **All escape-routes fail, including timelessness** — rate: he claims comprehensive coverage. "I go through a number of iterations of this argument, including timelessness and so forth, and demonstrate that none of them can resolve this incompatibility." *(philosophical)* [^3]
3. **It is a feature, not a defect, of God** — rate: connects to sovereignty (see [[Simple Foreknowledge Makes God a Prisoner Not Sovereign]]). Not knowing future free acts is no imperfection because such "knowledge" would be of something logically impossible to know; God instead knows all *possibilities* and has power to fulfill his word. *(theological)* [^4] See [[God Fulfills Prophecy as a Master Chess Player]].

**Steelman of the foreknowledge defender:** (1) *Boethian/timeless* solutions hold God is outside time, so he does not *fore*-know but *eternally sees* free acts as present; there is no "fact at 1900" that constrains tomorrow, only timeless observation, so freedom is preserved. (2) *Molinism* posits divine "middle knowledge" of true counterfactuals of creaturely freedom — God knows what any free agent *would* freely do in any circumstance, grounding both foreknowledge and providence without determinism. Ostler explicitly claims to refute the timeless move and elsewhere argues middle-knowledge counterfactuals lack truth-makers; the foreknowledge defender denies that "power to change a past fact" is the right test, since on these views the past truth *depends on* the future free act, not vice versa.
