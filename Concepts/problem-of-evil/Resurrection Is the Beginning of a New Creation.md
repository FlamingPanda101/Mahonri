#type/claim

# Resurrection Is the Beginning of a New Creation

## The Claim
Grieving the death of his son Donnie, [[Don Bradley]] grounds Christian hope not in a present explanation of suffering but in an **eschatological** one: Christ's resurrection is "the beginning of a new creation," a *prolepsis* (a present event that portends the future) showing that "the forces of evil don't win." The fallen world's death, illness, and "gratuitous suffering" are "not the last word" and "not forever"; what looked like the empire crushing a backwater peasant was God's first act of remaking the world as it is meant to be.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The eighth-day / first-day motif (moderate, typological).** Bradley notes the resurrection occurred "on the morning of the first day" — when, in the creation pattern, God *begins* creating — so Easter is the "eighth day," a new creation cycle, not merely a Sabbath rest.[^2]
2. **Resurrection as prolepsis (interpretive).** He uses the technical term: Christ's rising is "the prolepsis of the future… it shows us how things are going to be," extending to all the dead the victory already won in Christ — "they didn't win over God's son; they won't win over my son."[^1]
3. **Embodied, not merely spiritual, hope (moderate, comparative).** Bradley distinguishes the Restoration's promise of *bodily* resurrection from reincarnation or purely-mental afterlives: family bonds depend on shared bodies and shared experience, so "you have to have a body to make it family." The hope is concretely embodied reunion.[^3]
4. **Christ shares the suffering first (strong, scriptural).** The new creation does not bypass the cross: drawing on Abinadi and King Benjamin, Bradley affirms "God himself shall come down… and take upon him our sufferings," so believers "do not suffer alone." See [[God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ]].[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A skeptic (the very position Bradley once held) can reply that eschatological hope is unfalsifiable consolation: postponing the resolution of evil to a future no one can verify does nothing to answer *why* a good, omnipotent God permits a child's senseless suffering now. Appealing to a coming new creation re-describes the hope without discharging the logical or evidential problem of evil; it asks the sufferer to trust rather than supplying a theodicy.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley concedes he has "no answer" to all the whys and does not offer the resurrection as a theodicy but as a *ground of hope* compatible with unresolved anguish — he reports screaming and being angry at God while still hearing himself say "I trust you." His case rests not only on the future but on the present claim that God *enters* the suffering in Christ ([[God Himself Suffers Our Sufferings in Christ]]), so the sufferer is accompanied, not merely promised. Whether that satisfies the intellectual problem is separable from its function as lived hope, which is all he claims for it here.

## Best Quotes
> "Christ's resurrection is the beginning of a new creation… It's God's first act of this new creation, and that shows us how things are going to be — that the forces of evil don't win."[^1]

> "There will be no more death. There will be no more tears. There will be no more gratuitous suffering."[^2]
