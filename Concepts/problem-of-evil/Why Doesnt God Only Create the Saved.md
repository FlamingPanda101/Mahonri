# Why Doesnt God Only Create the Saved

## The Claim
A pointed sub-argument of the creation dilemma: if God is omnipotent and sovereign, He could simply have **created only the people He foreknew would be saved** — or, as a viewer (Swenson Bailey) put it, "the creedal God could just have created those who make it to heaven already in heaven. The only one stopping him from doing that was himself." Carroll affirms this is "part of the book."[^1] Combined with the Mary problem (God demonstrably *can* make sinless, freely-good people), the argument is that the existence of the damned is gratuitous: an omnipotent God had a fully available alternative and chose not to take it.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **It removes every external excuse — rate: strong against omnipotence claims.** Once God is granted full sovereignty and the power to create whomever He wishes (including the sinless, per [[The Mary Problem for Catholic Theism]]), no *external* constraint explains the damned; the only "thing stopping him was himself."[^1] This converts the problem of hell from a problem about *human choices* into a problem about *God's creative selection*. See [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]]. **Steelman of the creedal theist:** the Molinist "feasible worlds" reply (see [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]]) denies the premise — a world where God creates *only* the freely-saved may be infeasible if those very persons would *not* freely accept God absent the surrounding circumstances (including the existence of others who reject Him). On this view God cannot "just create the saved" because their free acceptance is counterfactually entangled with the whole world. Additionally, the Thomist holds that a universe displaying both mercy (the saved) and justice (the damned) manifests God's glory more fully than a uniform one — the "greater good" defense.
2. **The "greater good / God's glory" reply is pre-rebutted in the book — rate: handled at the structural level.** Carroll lists the greater-good defense (the damned "used as tools" — "it's utilitarianism essentially") as one of the chapters of rebuttals.[^3] The panel's counter elsewhere: appealing to God being "glorified in your annihilation" if not "in your salvation" yields "really dark sound bites" that conflict with the moral law "circumscribed on our hearts."[^4] See [[Hell - For Gods Glory Defense Rebutted]], [[The Six-Child Analogy for the Creation Dilemma]].
3. **It is the inverse of the Mary problem — rate: tightly linked.** Where the Mary problem says "God *can* make the sinless," this says "so why not make *only* such people?" Together they form a pincer that the panel deploys especially against Catholics (whose dogma supplies the first premise).[^2]

## Best Quotes
