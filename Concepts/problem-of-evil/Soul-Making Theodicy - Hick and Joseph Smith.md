# Soul-Making Theodicy - Hick and Joseph Smith

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[David Paulsen]] observes that [[John Hick]]'s **soul-making (Irenaean) theodicy** — developed in *Evil and the God of Love* (1966), "the watershed work on the problem of evil" — is "highly reminiscent of Joseph Smith's revelation." Both hold that God's purpose in creating us is (a) to let morally immature agents made in God's image **grow into God's likeness**, and (b) to let us enter a *free and uncompelled* relationship of love with Him. The striking parallels make the **one structural difference** — creation ex nihilo — all the more decisive. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Shared mechanism: epistemic distance.** To keep our freedom genuine, God sets us at an "epistemic distance" from Himself. Hick effects this distancing by having us emerge as self-centered creatures from a naturalistic evolutionary process; **Joseph Smith** by "veiling" our memory of our premortal existence. [^2]
2. **Shared mechanism: a rudimentary light.** Hick says God endows us with a faint awareness of Him and a slight tendency toward moral self-transcendence; Joseph Smith identifies this as the **light of Christ**, the Spirit which "enlighteneth every man through the world" ([[D&C 84.46]]). [^3]
3. **Shared goal: growth through hardship.** For both, soul-making "occurs as we overcome our self-centeredness by making moral choices within an environment fraught with hardship, pain, and suffering" — the instrumentalist reading of evil. Compare [[The Agape Theodicy - Evil as the Arena for Learning Divine Love]]. [^4]
4. **The decisive divergence.** Hick **affirms** creation out of nothing; Joseph Smith **denies** it. Because Hick keeps ex nihilo (plus absolute foreknowledge), he inherits [[Flew's Divine Complicity Argument]] and must escape via universalism — which Paulsen argues is incoherent (see [[Hick's Universalism Collapses Into Determinism]]). Joseph Smith's denial of ex nihilo lets the soul-making picture stand *without* making God the author of evil. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "The soul-making component in Hick's theodicy is highly reminiscent of Joseph Smith's revelation." [^1]

> Soul-making "occurs as we overcome our self-centeredness by making moral choices within an environment fraught with hardship, pain, and suffering." [^4]
