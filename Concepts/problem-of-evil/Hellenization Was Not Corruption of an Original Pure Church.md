# Hellenization Was Not Corruption of an Original Pure Church

## The Point
A common engine of the "decline" narrative is the claim that Greek and Roman culture "crept into an originally pure Church and corrupted it" — that Greek philosophy distorted the Christian doctrine of God, or that pagan practices displaced biblical ones. Combs argues this "philosophical and pagan corruption" thesis is "far too simplistic to be an accurate representation of historical events." Influence and engagement are not corruption; Greco-Roman culture was part of Christianity *from the beginning*.

## Evidence
1. **Jews were already Hellenized before Jesus.** "Already by the time of Jesus, Jews had been engaging with Greek thought and culture for hundreds of years longer than the United States of America has existed as a country." To imagine the Jewish authors of the New Testament were uninfluenced by Greco-Roman thought is, Combs argues, like claiming Native American authors such as N. Scott Momaday or Sherman Alexie are uninfluenced by Western culture because of their heritage. [^1]
2. **Greco-Roman influence is visible in the first-century texts themselves.** Combs cites scholarship (Aune; Engberg-Pedersen, *Paul and the Stoics*; Robyn Faith Walsh) showing the New Testament was written within Greco-Roman literary culture. [^2]
3. **Anti-Catholic motive behind the corruption thesis.** The Hellenization-as-corruption model (associated with Edwin Hatch and Adolf von Harnack) was itself shaped by nineteenth-century anti-Catholicism, per Elizabeth A. Clark's work on the rise of the discipline. [^3]
4. **Philosophy is not inherently anti-Christian.** Colossians 2:8 ("Beware lest any man spoil you through philosophy") is "not a critique of philosophy in general" — substitute "religion" for "philosophy" and it reads as a warning against misuse, not condemnation of the category. Both Judaism and Christianity "were understood to be philosophies in the ancient world" (cf. Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.1.2–6; [[Justin Martyr]], *Dialogue with Trypho* 8). [^4]
5. **Self-critique of LDS scholarship.** Combs notes that some LDS scholars "uncritically adopted these early academic ideas," citing Richard R. Hopkins, *How Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God*, and Stephen E. Robinson's identification of the great and abominable church with "Hellenized Christianity." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Influence and engagement are not corruption, and Greco-Roman culture was part of Christianity from the beginning." [^1]

> "Historians today have come to realize that this narrative of philosophical and pagan corruption is far too simplistic to be an accurate representation of historical events." [^1]

## LDS Connection
In the box "Unto [Our] Understanding," Combs grounds the point in LDS scripture: God speaks "according to their language, unto their understanding" (2 Nephi 31:3). The 1978 First Presidency statement affirmed that "philosophers including Socrates, Plato, and others, received a portion of God's light." A teaching's similarity to philosophy is therefore "not sufficient to prove any particular teaching right or wrong" — Latter-day Saints, he notes, would not want their temple ordinances dismissed merely because they resemble Masonic rites.
