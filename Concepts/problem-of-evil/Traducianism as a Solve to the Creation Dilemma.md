# Traducianism as a Solve to the Creation Dilemma

## The Claim

The Freethinking Ministries hosts (Evangelical Molinists) float **traducianism** — the view that human souls arise through ordinary human generation rather than direct *ex nihilo* creation by God — as a possible escape from [[The Creation Dilemma]]. If a person's soul comes about through their parents' free procreative choice, then God does not directly create each damned soul from nothing; he only "started the process" with Adam and Eve. [[Hayden Carroll]] answers that this concedes a *limited* God, which is exactly the God his argument does not target. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the exchange)

### 1. The traducian setup — rate: presents the position fairly

Host Tim (who says "we lean traducian," with [[J.P. Moreland]] and others): souls "arise through human generation, rather than direct divine creation." So for everyone after Adam and Eve, "God only plays a necessary condition, but not sufficient" — the parents' free choice supplies the rest, "so you don't get determinism there." The illustration: God could not create "Sam" without also creating "Sam's atheist parents," since Sam is generated from them. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Of the position as stated — internally coherent; whether it actually blocks the dilemma is the live question.

### 2. Carroll's rebuttal: an unbound God needs no parents — rate: strong (within his framing)

Carroll: "If I really wanted Sam to exist and to go to heaven, I would just snap my fingers and I would create him. I don't need his parents." Because his argument targets a God who is *omnipotent and unbound*, appealing to traducianism only works by positing a God who *cannot* directly create Sam — i.e., a limited God. "If you're positing a God that can't do that, then I'm not critiquing that God." So traducianism does not refute the dilemma; it changes the subject to a different (limited) deity. See [[The Creation Dilemma Targets Five Divine Attributes Not the Trinity Specifically]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the rebuttal correctly identifies that traducianism, to do work, must deny omnipotence-to-create-directly.

### 3. The "import the memories" wrinkle — rate: moderate

Tim presses that what makes Sam *Sam* is partly being raised by his particular parents — so a God who "snapped" Sam into existence directly would not be creating the same person (you would have to "import memories"). Carroll's reply: an omnipotent God "could do anything you want," including that. The deeper disagreement is whether personal identity is bound to one's actual causal history (Molinist "feasible worlds") or is freely fixable by omnipotence. See [[God Could Have Created Only the Saved]]. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — turns on contested intuitions about personal identity and transworld identity.

## Steelman of the Traducian View

The traducian (and Molinist) can answer that the constraint is not on God's *power* but on what counts as a *feasible* creation: it may be metaphysically true that *this* person — with *this* libertarian-free trajectory toward salvation — exists only given a particular ancestry and history, such that "snap Sam directly into heaven" produces a *numerically different* being, not Sam. On this view God is not "limited" in the pejorative sense; rather, "create Sam-who-freely-loves-God" and "create Sam without his actual history" may be as incompossible as a married bachelor. Carroll's reply is that this still concedes a God constrained by something — which Latter-day Saints affirm openly (eternal law) but most creedal Christians say is "not possible," thereby owning the unbound God his dilemma critiques. See [[Origins of the Soul - Traducianism vs Creationism vs Preexistence]].
