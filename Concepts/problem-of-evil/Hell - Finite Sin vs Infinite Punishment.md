# Hell — Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Defenders ([[Jonathan Edwards]], [[William Wainwright]]) hold that **all sin warrants eternal punishment because it is committed against an infinite being** (God) — so any sin, from doubting the Trinity to genocide, justly merits infinite torment.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Violation of proportionality
All mortal actions receiving the identical infinite punishment contravenes the near-universal maxim that punishment should fit the crime.

> "Retribution consisting in unending suffering imposed as recompense for the actions of a finite intellect and will must be by any sound definition disproportionate, unjust, and at the last nothing more than an expression of sheer pointless cruelty." — [[David Bentley Hart]] [^1]

Only an *infinite crime* could warrant infinite punishment — and no finite mortal action is infinite [^2].

### 2. The creation problem
God supposedly created depraved beings in a system where infinite sins are *remarkably easy* — even failing to *understand* the Trinity — and committable unknowingly. He "created such beings from nothing in a system that endows them with infinite guilt... if they make even one single mistake" [^3].

### 3. The impassibility problem
If God is **impassible** (cannot be affected by anything), then sin cannot harm him — making eternal punishment for an act that leaves God entirely unmoved even more unjustifiable [^4].

## Best Quote
> "Why are the actions of a finite being with finite consequences being punished eternally?" — [[David Bentley Hart]] [^1]
