# The Wheat and Tares Grow Together as a Reply to the Problem of Evil

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] read the "let both grow together until the harvest" of [[D&C 86]] / [[Matthew 13.24-30|Matthew 13:24-30]] as an answer to the perennial question, "Why does God allow wicked people to carry out their works?" The Lord tells the angels who are "ready and waiting... to reap down the fields," "Pluck not up the tares while the blade is yet tender... lest you destroy the wheat also." The striking implication is that "the wheat needs the tares to fully mature" — removing the wicked too soon "might actually damage the righteous." Evil is permitted, in part, because moral growth requires opposition.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The text ties God's restraint to protecting the wheat (rating: strong; scriptural).** D&C 86:6-7: "Pluck not up the tares while the blade is yet tender, for verily your faith is weak, lest you destroy the wheat also. Therefore, let the wheat and the tares grow together until the harvest is fully ripe."[^1]
2. **Omniscience is not the issue; agency and accountability are (rating: moderate; theological).** The hosts insist the puzzle "is not whether or not God is omniscient... but it's about the agency and the accountability of mankind." God foreknows who the wheat and tares are, "but we don't" — so He grants time to "use our agency to develop and become what we choose to be."[^2]
3. **Wheat and tares look identical when young (rating: moderate; illustrative).** A botanist friend's point: early on "they look almost identical," so a good person and a bad person "put together as children" may be indistinguishable; God "has to allow us time" to become what agency makes us.[^2]
4. **Opposition is structurally necessary (rating: moderate; scriptural cross-support).** The hosts connect this to the Book of Mormon's "opposition in all things" (2 Nephi 2) and D&C 29:39 ("it must needs be that the devil should tempt the children of men... for if they never should have bitter, they could not know the sweet"), plus a resistance-training analogy: muscles and bones strengthen by having "something to push against."[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A philosophical critic raises the very question the hosts pose: is it *just to the wicked* — and to the innocent who suffer at their hands — for God to let evil run its course "only for the benefit of the righteous"? Using the tares (real persons) as instruments for the wheat's maturation looks like treating people as mere means. Moreover, a soul-making theodicy of this kind struggles with *horrendous*, growth-destroying evils that crush rather than refine, and with the suffering of those who never consented to be others' "opposition."

## Counter-Objection
The hosts do not claim the tares exist *merely* to serve the wheat; the tares "have to have the time to fully become tares" by their own agency, just as the wheat does — each becomes what it freely chooses, and both are finally judged. So the model is agency-respecting on both sides, not instrumentalizing: God's restraint preserves the conditions of genuine moral becoming for everyone. Griffiths concedes the hard residue openly — "I know that there's people out there right now that are struggling with the consequences of bad decisions that other people have made" — and locates the comfort not in a tidy explanation but in the testimony that growth often comes precisely "when I was suffering because of the wickedness of other people." The note thus offers a *partial*, agency-based theodicy congenial to the LDS premortal-agency framework, not a complete solution to horrendous evil — which it does not claim to resolve.

## Best Quotes
> "It's interesting that the passage even suggests... that the wheat needs the tares to fully mature... if the wicked were immediately removed from the equation, it might actually damage the righteous — which is an interesting take on the problem of evil."[^1]

> "If we never should have bitter, they could not know the sweet... in basic weight training, you've got to have something to push against in order to develop and grow the muscle."[^3]
