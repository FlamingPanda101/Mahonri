# An Omnipotent God Cannot Force a Free Creature to Choose Good

## The Point
The logical problem of evil leans on the hidden premise that an all-powerful God "can create any world he wants."[^1] Craig (following [[Alvin Plantinga]]) shows this is false: if God wills a world of creatures with **genuine free will**, "it's logically impossible for God to force someone to freely choose to do good."[^2] A *forced free choice* is a contradiction in terms — like a square circle. This is no defect in God's power, because omnipotence has never meant the ability to do the logically impossible; "it's not that God lacks the power to perform the task; it is that the supposed task itself is just nonsense."[^3] Therefore it may not be **feasible** for God to create a world of free creatures who *always* freely choose the good, and the first hidden premise collapses.

## Evidence
- **Forcing a free choice is incoherent.** "What if [God] wants a world populated by people who have free will? It's logically impossible for God to force someone to freely choose to do good. Forcing free choices is like making a square circle; it's not logically possible."[^2] The two notions — *forced* and *freely chosen* — cancel each other out.
- **Omnipotence excludes the nonsensical, not the difficult.** Craig draws the classic distinction: the failure here is not a shortfall of divine power but the emptiness of the alleged "task." "It's not that God lacks the power to perform the task; it is that the supposed task itself is just nonsense."[^3] (Compare Feser: "even God cannot make a round square," in [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]].)
- **Feasible vs. merely possible worlds.** It follows that "it may not be feasible to create a world populated by people who always freely choose to do what is morally good." A sinless free world is *describable*, but it may not be one God can actualize without overriding the very freedom that defines it — "so the first assumption is not necessarily true. Therefore, the argument fails."[^4]
- **Pairs with the second premise's failure.** This handles only the first hidden assumption; Craig separately shows the second (that a loving God would *prefer* a suffering-free world) is also unprovable, since we ourselves permit suffering for a greater good (see [[The Logical Problem of Evil Hides Two Unprovable Premises]]).[^5]

## LDS Connection
This is common ground between Craig and the Restoration, and Latter-day Saint metaphysics arguably gives the point deeper roots. The Book of Mormon makes inviolable agency the architecture of mortality — without the freedom to choose evil there could be "no good" and the creation would have "no purpose in the end of its creation" (2 Nephi 2:11–13). Where the LDS view sharpens Craig's argument is in *why* free choices cannot be coerced even by omnipotence: because the agent's **intelligence is uncreated and co-eternal**, the will is not a divine product God could simply have programmed differently. God works *with* eternally self-existent agents rather than authoring their choices from nothing, which is precisely why a "forced free choice" is impossible for Him (see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]] and [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]]). The Restoration also reframes God's relation to His own power: He is "constrained by eternal law," not an unconstrained will that could abolish the logical structure of agency at whim. One point to flag fairly: Craig, as a classical theist, still holds that God creates each soul ex nihilo, so for him the impossibility is purely logical (you cannot *describe* a forced-free choice); the LDS adds an *ontological* reason (God did not create the agent's will in the first place) — a stronger claim that the classical theist's commitments do not require.
