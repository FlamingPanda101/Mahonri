# Justice Does Not Negate Omnibenevolence

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]]'s rebuttal line: God's punishment of evil is an *expression* of, not a contradiction to, his benevolence. "Justice does not negate omnibenevolence. Justice answers evil; love answers persons. God punishes evil because he wills the good of persons harmed by it. A judge who refuses to oppose evil is not benevolent but indifferent." He cites [[Hosea 11.8|Hosea 11:8]] ("How can I give you up?") to show God's benevolence precedes and survives covenant-breaking.[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Indifference vs. love** — rate: moderate, and partly true. The point that a good agent must *oppose* evil (not be indifferent) is sound; benevolence toward victims can require justice against perpetrators. *(moral)* [^1] Gavin reinforces it in closing: hell is "to be just, and God being just is part of his love... there is no love when a god is indifferent." See [[Benevolence Requires a System of Justice]].
2. **[[Hosea 11.8|Hosea 11:8]] for prevenient love** — God's relenting toward Israel shows benevolence is not earned by obedience. *(scriptural)* [^2]

**Why it misses the dilemma (Hayden's steelman of the negative):** Hayden's argument *concedes* God is just; the resolution is specifically about *omnibenevolence*, not justice — "would you agree this debate is not on whether God is just but whether he is omnibenevolent?" The justice reply answers a *different* objection (the problem of evil/suffering of victims), not the *creation* objection. Justice can explain *post-creation* punishment of the guilty; it cannot explain why a benevolent God would *create* a person foreknown to incur that justice eternally when refraining would harm no one. Retributive justice operates on *existing* persons; the dilemma operates *before* anyone exists to be justly punished. Calling eternal-conscious-torment-of-a-foreknown-creature "justice" still has to show it *wills that creature's good* — which justice, by itself, does not.

## Best Quotes
> "Justice does not negate omnibenevolence. Justice answers evil. Love answers persons... A judge who refuses to oppose evil is not benevolent but is indifferent."[^1]

> "Even when Israel breaks their covenants, God says, 'How can I give you up?' God's benevolence precedes any kind of covenant obedience."[^2]

> "Would you agree this debate is not on whether God is just but whether he is omnibenevolent?" — Hayden's reframe [^3]
