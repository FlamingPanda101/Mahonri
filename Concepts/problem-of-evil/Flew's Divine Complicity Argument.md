# Flew's Divine Complicity Argument

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[David Paulsen]] argues that [[David Hume|Hume]]'s classic statement of the logical problem of evil is **too narrow** — it rests only on God's (i) perfect goodness and (ii) omnipotence. Traditional Christian theology adds two premises that *intensify* the problem: (iii) God created all things **absolutely, out of nothing**, and (iv) God has **absolute foreknowledge** of all the outcomes of His creative choices. [[Antony Flew]] takes these extra premises into account and concludes that reconciliation is impossible: an omnipotent, omniscient creator-from-nothing "must be an **accessory before (and during) the fact to every human misdeed**." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The four-premise intensification.** Apologists have labored to square evil with God's goodness and power, but "have often overlooked the much more difficult task" of squaring it with **absolute creation and absolute foreknowledge** as well. Those two premises are what make the problem look like a flat contradiction rather than a mere paradox. [^2]
2. **Why the extra premises bite.** "We cannot say that [God] would like to help but cannot: God is omnipotent. We cannot say that he would help if he only knew: God is omniscient. We cannot say that he is not responsible for the wickedness of others: **God creates those others.**" If God makes finite agents out of nothing while foreknowing exactly what they will do, He is "ultimately responsible for every moral and nonmoral defect in the universe." [^3]
3. **The hell aggravation.** And if (as some believers hold) some agents suffer endlessly in hell, God is "at least jointly responsible for these horrendous outcomes" — deepening the tension with perfect love. See [[Problem of Hell and Creation]]. [^4]
4. **The Restoration's answer.** Paulsen's thesis is that Joseph Smith's denial of creation ex nihilo defeats premise (iii): within "a framework of eternal entities and structures that God did not create and that He cannot destroy," it does not follow that God is "the total or even the ultimate explanation of all else," so the complicity charge fails. See [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]], [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]]. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Indeed an omnipotent, omniscient God must be an accessory before (and during) the fact to every human misdeed; as well as being responsible for every non-moral defect in the universe." — Antony Flew [^3]

> "From Joseph Smith's theological platform, it does not follow that God is the total or even the ultimate explanation of all else." — David Paulsen [^5]
