# Every Dispensation Opens With God Declaring Divine Sonship

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues there is a **recurring opening formula at the head of each of the seven dispensations**: God reveals *who He is* and then declares *who the prophet is to Him* — "I am God; you are my son." The order is fixed (deity first, sonship second), and Bradley walks all seven: [[Adam]] ([[Moses 6.51|Moses 6:51-52]]), [[Enoch]] (Moses 6:27), [[Noah]] (Moses 8:13), [[Abraham]] (Book of Abraham — "Abraham, my son... my son, my son"), [[Moses]] ([[Moses 1.1-2|Moses 1:3-7]]), [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] at his baptism ([[Mark 1.11|Mark 1:11]], "Thou art my beloved Son"), and [[Joseph Smith]] in the 1832 First Vision account ("Joseph, my son, thy sins are forgiven thee"). The decisive observation: **every one of the pre-Christian examples survives only in the scriptures of the Restoration** (Book of Moses, Book of Abraham) — this is "a lost key" that the Bible largely lost.[^1][^2]

This pairs with but is distinct from [[Priesthood Keys Frame the Seven Dispensations]]: that concept tracks *authority/keys* across the dispensations; this one tracks the *relational declaration of sonship* that opens each.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The pattern is textually explicit in the Restoration scriptures
For Adam: God "called upon our Father Adam by his own voice, saying, I am God" and, after baptism, "thou art one in me, a son of God" ([[Moses 6.51|Moses 6:51-52]]). For Enoch: "Enoch, my son, prophesy unto this people" (Moses 6:27). For Noah, "the case that's least clear," it still says he and his sons "were called the sons of God" (Moses 8:13). Each conversion of a dispensation head carries the formula.[^1] *Rating: internally consistent — the verses say what Bradley says; it is a real pattern within the LDS canon.*

### 2. The "my son, my son" intensification ties Abraham to David's lament
In the Book of Abraham the Lord stretches out his hand and says "My son, my son" — a doubling Bradley notes appears only one other place in all scripture: [[David]]'s lament over [[Absalom]] ("O Absalom, my son, my son"). He reads this as paternal tenderness, not bare title — "those are some of the most tender words spoken in the entirety of scripture."[^3] *Rating: an evocative intertext; the parallel is verbal and thematic, not a demonstrable literary dependence.*

### 3. God models the formula on his own Son
At Jesus's baptism — in [[Mark 1.11|Mark]], "the earliest gospel" — the Father says "Thou art my beloved Son" at the event that *begins* the ministry, and Jesus then "goes and teaches others" that God is Father. So God applies to the dispensation heads the same declaration he makes over Christ; "that's even how God does it... He emphasizes that right away, not only to Joseph, but he does it to his Son in that order."[^4][^5] *Rating: a typological reading that presupposes the LDS framing of Jesus as model-Son; see Steelman.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic notes the pattern is **only as old as the Restoration texts that contain it**: six of the seven instances (everyone before Jesus) come from the Book of Moses and Book of Abraham, documents a non-LDS scholar dates to the 1830s, so the "lost key restored" claim is unfalsifiable from outside — the evidence for the ancient pattern *is* the modern revelation. The Hebrew Bible's call narratives (Moses at the bush, the prophets) emphasize commission and divine name, not a standardized "you are my son" formula; "son of God" language for Israel/king exists (Exodus 4:22; Psalm 2:7) but is corporate or royal, not a per-prophet opening. The Mark 1:11 voice is Christologically unique — it identifies the *one* beloved Son — and reading it as the template for a class of prophet-sons is precisely the LDS move other Christians reject. The fair conclusion: *within* LDS scripture the pattern is real and theologically rich (identity precedes commandment), but it is a structure of the Restoration canon offered as revealed theology, not a feature recoverable from the Bible alone.

## Best Quotes
> "If we look elsewhere at the beginning of each dispensation, we actually find the same pattern. Every time... Adam... Moses 6:51. I am God, starts at the exact same place with who He is."[^1]

> "Notice that all these examples that I've given... they're all from the scriptures of the Restoration... This is knowledge that was lost. This is a lost key."[^2]

> "So count them, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, six, seven. There's not another one. We didn't skip any. That's all the dispensations. They all start with the revelation... I'm God. You're my son."[^6]
