# Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue

## The Point
Answering "how should a fair critique actually be structured?", [[Braden Gospel Guardian|Braden]] contrasts the **straw man** (dumb down, re-word, or decontextualize an argument so it's weaker than the one actually made) with the **steel man** (put yourself in your opponent's shoes and state their case as strongly and honestly as you can, *then* give valid criticism). The diagnostic for a *bad-faith* critic is the **Gish gallop**: piling on accusation after accusation without pausing for an answer — explicitly "what this book [*Kingdom of the Cults*] is doing, what the CES letter does." The remedy is to **take control of the conversation**: answer point one fully before allowing point two, ask questions (the questioner controls the pace), and disengage if the other side ignores your answers.[^1][^2][^3]

## Evidence
1. **Straw man vs. steel man defined.** A straw man is "taking an argument and dumbing it down, taking it out of context, changing the wording so that it is a weaker argument." A steel man is: "if there's a view that you disagree with, you put yourself in their shoes and try and defend it in your mind the best and most honestly as you can," which lets you give criticism that's fair to how they actually believe.[^1] *Rating: strong; textbook definitions matching [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]].*
2. **The Gish gallop named.** "It's just throwing one argument after another. Did you know that this happened and then this person did this… They don't give you a second to breathe… to defend your points." It is "what this book is doing, what the CES letter does," and "a Gish gallop is sure evidence that they're not sincere."[^2] *Rating: strong; names the tactic and its tell.*
3. **The sincerity test is how they treat your answer.** A good-faith critic responds "okay, interesting — what about this?"; a bad-faith one ignores the answer and "just move[s] on to anti-Mormon point number two." "The person asking the questions is the one controlling the conversation."[^3] *Rating: strong; practical and reciprocal.*
4. **Don't crash out — and don't humiliate.** Engage sincere doubters; for public bad-faith actors, "you don't want to make them to look like a fool" but use "a little bit of the Socratic method" to let them discover their position's weakness. Bad actors "actually want the gratification of you crashing out," so refusing to is itself a win.[^4] *Rating: moderate-to-strong; a temperament guideline more than an argument.*

## Best Quotes
> "A steel man is… if there's a view that you disagree with, you put yourself in their shoes and try and defend it… the best and most honestly as you can." [^1]

> "A Gish gallop is just throwing one argument after another… They don't give you a second to breathe… A Gish gallop is sure evidence that they're not sincere." [^2]

> "The person asking the questions is the one controlling the conversation… if you're the one asking the questions you retain control." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the conversational discipline LDS apologists prescribe for the *manner* of engagement, complementing the *evidentiary* discipline of [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]]. It pairs naturally with the panel's broader read that much anti-Mormonism is [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea|character attack rather than argument]], and it answers a Gish gallop the way the witness tradition answers hearsay: slow down, isolate one claim, and hold the other side to a consistent standard.
