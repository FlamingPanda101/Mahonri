# Chalcedon Hypostatic Union as Mystery Defense

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that after Nicaea settled "Jesus is God," a new controversy arose — Jesus is also human, so "how's that work? Is it 50/50?" — which was resolved a century later at the **Council of Chalcedon (451 CE)** with the *hypostatic union*: Jesus is "100% God but Jesus is 100% human."[^1] McClellan's polemical point is that when pressed on *how* this is coherent, the answer is essentially "shut up... it's a mystery. It just is the way it is."[^2] He reads this not as humble apophaticism but as an admission that the framework was engineered to be "plausible enough that everybody can be like, 'yeah, I can get on board with that'" and then enforced by exile.[^3] See the standalone [[Hypostatic Union]] note for the doctrinal definition.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Chalcedonian Definition is historically accurate as summarized — rate: strong
Chalcedon (451) affirmed Christ as one person "in two natures," fully God and fully man, "without confusion, change, division, or separation." McClellan's "100% God, 100% human" gloss, and his note that this resolved a controversy "settled a century later" than Nicaea, is correct.[^1] *Rating: strong; standard church history.*

### 2. "It's a mystery" is in fact the orthodox stance — rate: strong, but cuts both ways
McClellan accurately reports that the union is classically held to be a mystery exceeding rational comprehension; the councils define *that* it is so, not *how*. *Rating: strong description.* **Steelman:** for the orthodox, appeal to mystery is principled, not evasive — finite minds should expect the inner life of God and the God-man to transcend comprehension, just as physics tolerates wave-particle duality without a fully intuitive model. The councils used *apophatic* (negative) boundary-language ("without confusion... without separation") precisely to fence off error while refusing to over-specify. On this view "shut up, it's a mystery" is a caricature of a serious epistemic humility.

### 3. McClellan's "engineered plausibility" charge — rate: persuasive as critique, contestable as history
His claim is that the formula's function was social ("just need to make it work" so "everybody can get on board") rather than truth-tracking, and that dissent was met with exile rather than argument.[^3] This is continuous with [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]] and [[Trinity Is Fundamentally Contradictory]]. *Rating: persuasive as a rhetorical critique; a defender would respond that the conciliar debates (Cyril vs. Nestorius, the Tome of Leo, the rejection of monophysitism) show genuine theological reasoning, not mere managerial compromise.*

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus is God but Jesus was also a human. How's that work? Is it 50/50? And that's where they came up with what's called the hypostatic union, where Jesus is 100% God but Jesus is 100% human. How does that work? Shut up."[^1]

> "It's a mystery. It just is the way it is. And what you see in these arguments is basically we just need to make it work... come up with an argument that is plausible enough that everybody can be like, 'yeah, I can get on board with that.'"[^2]

> "Doesn't make a ton of sense. It makes enough sense that we can threaten people if they don't agree with it."[^3]
