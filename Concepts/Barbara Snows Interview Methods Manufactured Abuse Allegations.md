# Barbara Snows Interview Methods Manufactured Abuse Allegations

## The Claim
Therapist [[Barbara Snow]]'s interview techniques — leading questions, prolonged isolation, refusal to record, and conditional affection — generated abuse accusations rather than uncovering them, making her the engine of the Utah ritual-abuse cases.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Cascading accusations from a single starting point (strong)
A Lehi mother worried her children talked too freely about sex; Snow interviewed them, concluded they were abused by their babysitter (the bishop's daughter), then interviewed other babysat children until accusations "started to pile up," eventually reaching 40 adults in the Lehi 8th Ward [^1].

### 2. Coercive technique observed on video and in person (strong — multiple eyewitnesses)
Recordings showed Snow drawing stick men on a chalkboard and telling children "Here are the perverts and they are smiling because they are getting away with it"; she sat a reluctant boy on her lap, took away dolls a child was playing with, and asked leading questions. [[Wayne Watson]], who watched a session, was "appalled" [^2].

### 3. Conditional warmth as reinforcement (strong)
Per Watson, Snow stayed "formal and stiff" when a child reacted negatively but became "warm and loving," holding the child and praising her, when the child indicated something had happened — operant conditioning toward disclosure, not neutral inquiry [^3].

### 4. No recordings, only notes (moderate)
Snow refused to allow sessions to be recorded, assuring skeptics she took "copious notes." She rationalized the absence of early symptoms by claiming children "made significant increases during the disclosure process" — an unfalsifiable frame [^4].

### 5. A controlled test confirmed implantation (decisive — added from Part 4)
In 2007 the Utah Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing charged that Snow "had implanted false memories of sexual abuse into her patients." To test it, "law enforcement fed Dr. Snow false information that immediately began appearing in the children's accounts" — a near-experimental demonstration that her process generated content. She accepted probation and license suspension. The Utah State Court cited "the suggestive and coercive nature of Dr. Snow's techniques" in reversing a Hadfield-related conviction, and the 10th Circuit called her conduct "disturbing and irresponsible." See [[Leading SRA Therapists Were Discredited Sued or Delicensed]] [^5][^6].

### 6. A grown interviewee describes the coercion firsthand (strong — eyewitness)
[[David Cox]], taken to Snow as a child, recalls: "Barbara vigorously tried to get me to believe in fictitious memories that I had been sexually abused by my father. She would yell, threaten, and berate me … She came up with the most outlandish ideas and then tried to convince me that they were my own memories … She does use recorders, but conveniently loses them when it makes her look bad" [^7].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender would say child-abuse interviewing is genuinely hard: traumatized children disclose slowly, need rapport, and may deny abuse initially — so warmth and patience are legitimate, and disclosure "increasing over time" can reflect real trauma processing. Modern forensic-interview protocols (e.g., NICHD) exist precisely because of cases like this. But those protocols mandate the very things Snow refused — neutral, non-leading questions and recording — and the total absence of physical corroboration across dozens of accused undercuts the trauma-disclosure reading here.
