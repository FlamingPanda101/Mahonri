# Bible as Pedagogical Curriculum

## The Claim
Wright's second major thesis: the Bible is fundamentally a *pedagogical project* — an educational curriculum designed to form a people without a kingdom. It is not scripture for a religion but a "curriculum" that shapes identity through shared texts, laws, songs, love poetry, and wisdom. Education replaces military might as the key to enduring identity, and wisdom is democratized from elite court training to universal family life.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Proverbs as Democratized Wisdom:** In Egyptian wisdom literature, the "father" was a court tutor and the "son" a student training for elite careers. In the Book of [[Proverbs]], this shifts: "my son, listen to the words of your father and the Torah/instruction of your mother" — mother and father are now the biological parents, and wisdom has moved from the court to the family. "This wisdom is being democratized … it's become an educational curriculum for average folks."[^2]

2. **Deuteronomy's Vision:** Wright points to [[Deuteronomy]]'s claim that "what's going to make us wonderful and honored on the world stage is not our military power but our wisdom and our devotion to learning."[^3]

3. **[[Song of Songs]] as Educational:** Wright addresses the objection — what does love poetry have to do with education? His answer: Song of Songs models an "I-Thou relationship, a real intimacy between two individuals in which the woman is equal with the man." A society "cannot exist unless two people can really come together in some deep intimacy that you can't have between three people." Poetry itself has "educational capacity" — it "shapes us as readers in a deep way."[^4]

4. **Pedagogy from the Margins, Not Hellenistic Influence:** Wright builds on David Carr's work (*Writing on the Tablets of the Heart*) that the Bible is an educational text, but pushes back against Carr's thesis that it's a Hellenistic elite project for training male elites. Wright argues the pedagogical impulse emerges from the margins: "people on the margins are always going to gravitate to the necessity and the advantages of education. Think of colonized peoples and how they turn to educational projects because they need to come together around something."[^5]

5. **From Battlefield to Text:** The orientation shifts "from the battlefield where one gets honored by being just a great soldier to one who is really well educated — or scribes or teachers or parents and families." Participation in public life requires drawing on shared traditions, laws, and texts — not martial prowess.[^6]

## Best Quote

> "Education is the key to the kingdom if you will … to be able to participate in public life one needs to be able to draw on various traditions and laws and stuff that public appreciates, and that then changes the orientation from the battlefield where one gets honored by being just a great soldier to one who is really well educated."[^6]
> — [[Jacob Wright]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-Arguments
- [[David Carr]]'s thesis that the Bible is Hellenistic-era elite literature — Wright acknowledges the influence but argues for an earlier, more populist origin.
- The "curriculum" framing may be anachronistic in its own right — applying a modern educational category to ancient texts.
