# Abolition Was Read Into the Bible From the Outside

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the moral conviction that slavery is wrong **developed independently of the Bible** and was only later **imposed upon** the text — first "in the 19th century," roughly "1,500 years after the completion of the New Testament," once "a critical mass of folks" decided to stop endorsing the practice. The anti-slavery reading is "a renegotiation" the culture "agreed to accept," not a meaning written into Scripture: "It can be read into the Bible, but there's nothing remotely approximating a plausible case... that there is any syllable of the Bible that was written with the intention of... questioning, condemning, or prohibiting the practice itself of enslavement."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The morality is external, then retrojected (rated: strong; argued).** "The notion that that morality that was developed independent of the Bible can be found in the Bible is nonsense. It can be read into the Bible" — abolitionist ethics arrived from outside and were mapped onto passages like the kidnapping ban.[^1]
2. **A specific verse was repurposed (rated: moderate; argued).** [[Exodus 21.16|Exodus 21:16]] is, on McClellan's account, "one of the passages that was misunderstood in that otherwise well-meaning attempt to overrule the morality of the Bible" — a reinterpretation, not a recovery of authorial intent. See [[The ESV Mistranslates Exodus 21.16 to Criminalize Unwitting Buyers]].
3. **The reinterpretation has been forgotten as such (rated: moderate; argued).** "We're far enough removed from it now that interpreters don't realize that what they imagine to be the actual meaning of the Bible is really just the renegotiation that we all agreed to accept."[^1]
4. **Steelman / counter-voice.** Christian scholars argue the imago Dei and Exodus trajectory genuinely furnished abolition (see [[The Old Testament Was a Hammer for Abolitionists]]); McClellan's claim is the narrower historical-critical one that *authorial intent* never aimed at abolition, conceding only that the text could be *read* that way after the fact.

## LDS Relevance
Cuts against any "the Bible alone, plainly read, gave us our morality" apologetic and indirectly supports the Latter-day Saint case for **continuing revelation**: if even the abolition of slavery had to be supplied from outside an inerrant-text framework and read back in, then a closed canon is an insufficient moral compass, and the Restoration's claim that God keeps speaking — and corrects earlier accommodations — addresses exactly the gap McClellan identifies. Contrast the faithful "constructive renegotiation" framing in [[Biblical Interpretation Has Always Renegotiated Under New Cosmologies]].

## Best Quotes
> "1,500 years after the completion of the New Testament... there were renegotiations and reinterpretations. And this is one of the passages that was misunderstood in that otherwise well-meaning attempt to overrule the morality of the Bible."[^1]

> "Interpreters don't realize that what they imagine to be the actual meaning of the Bible is really just the renegotiation that we all agreed to accept."[^1]
