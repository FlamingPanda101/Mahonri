# Rejecting Polygamy Unravels Sealing and the Plan of Salvation

## The Claim
[[Christopher Blythe]] observes a recurring historical pattern across the breakaway factions: a movement that rejects or "denies" plural marriage almost inevitably loses the doctrine of eternal **sealing** along with it — and, in the RLDS case, eventually loses much of the restored **plan of salvation** too. The two doctrines are so logically intertwined that "when you start picking away at the Restoration, you're going to lose stuff." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The logic: sealing implies the possibility of plural marriage (rated: strong, doctrinal).** Blythe's explanation of why the Cutlerites lost sealing: the earliest 1840s defenses of plural marriage argued that if a man's wife dies and he remarries, he should be eternally married to *both* — so "the logic of eternal marriage was so intertwined with plural marriage... that they discarded both." [[Alpheus Cutler]] suspended plural marriage "until Jesus Christ returns," even arranging the remarriage of his own remaining plural wife (a former wife of [[Orrin Porter Rockwell]]), and thereafter lived "as if he was always a monogamist." See [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]]. [^2]

2. **The pattern repeats across factions (rated: strong, comparative).** The same dynamic appears in [[Sydney Rigdon]]'s and [[William Bickerton]]'s polygamy-denying movements and in the RLDS. [[Cardon Ellis]] generalizes from his own observation: "every time" a group — or someone inside the church — tries "to get rid of the polygamous history... it almost always ends up to also pulling out sealing with it." [^3]

3. **In the RLDS the unraveling reached the whole plan of salvation (rated: strong, firsthand).** Blythe's missionary anecdote: an RLDS "high priestess" couple had no pre-existence "the way that we believe it," and when she said she did not know how God would save those who never heard the gospel, he answered "that's what baptisms for the dead are for" — and "saw the lights go on." With the rejection of polygamy came "a loss of our whole understanding of the plan of salvation." This tracks the broader 1960s RLDS drift (questioning the apostasy, demoting baptism for the dead to a "local commandment," moving D&C sections to an appendix and then deleting them). See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]]. [^4]

**Steelman of the other side.** A reorganized-tradition believer would say dropping plural marriage was a *recovery* of New Testament monogamy, not a loss, and that sealing and an elaborate plan of salvation were Nauvoo-era accretions rightly pruned. The LDS reply embedded here is structural rather than moral: historically, the factions did not stop at polygamy — the same hermeneutic that removed it kept going (sealing, then the pre-existence, then baptism for the dead), which the panel reads as evidence the doctrines stand or fall together as one revealed system.

## Best Quotes
> "The fact of the matter is that sealing implies the possibility of plural marriage... I think the logic of eternal marriage was so intertwined with plural marriage that they discarded both." [^2]

> "Every time that I've seen any group... try to get rid of the polygamous history that we have, it almost always ends up to also pulling out sealing with it." [^3]

> "When you start picking away at the Restoration, you're going to lose stuff. That's how it works." [^1]

> "With the rejection of polygamy generally comes this loss of some kind of sealing. And in tandem with that... it also included a loss of our whole understanding of the plan of salvation." [^4]
