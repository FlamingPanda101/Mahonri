# Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]]'s one developed argument: the boxcar/contingency argument. Each contingent thing depends on a prior one (boxcars need an engine); an infinite regress of contingent things never reaches the "ground of existing," so there must be one *necessary* being "whose essence is existence." This takes both atheism *and* Mormonism "off the table," because Mormons make God material, embodied, and themselves contingent on a prior God ("turtles all the way down") — "just more boxcars," never the engine.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The argument does not establish *one* engine (rated: moderate, philosophical).** [[Luke Irwin]]: "Why can't there be more than one engine on these box cars?" The contingency argument, even if sound, gets you to *a* necessary ground — it does not by itself prove monotheism (singular) versus a plural necessary ground.[^2] Extending Irwin's train metaphor, [[Cardon Ellis]] notes that real freight trains commonly need *two or three* diesel engines to pull the same load a single steam engine once pulled — "if you want to make the contingency argument and say God is the train pulling all the cars, you just made a real good case for a plural Elohim."[^3] This is rhetorical, but it presses the genuine logical gap between "a necessary being exists" and "exactly one necessary being exists."

2. **The official Church takes no position on God's God (rated: strong, doctrinal correction).** [[Jasmine Rapley]]: the Church's official position today does *not* affirm an infinite regress of Gods. "The church does not take a position about whether God has a God." For LDS purposes, "God the Father has created everything... he is the creator of all things." The "turtles all the way down" caricature attributes to current doctrine what is at most King Follett-era speculation. So the objection's premise (that LDS *affirm* an infinite contingent regress) misstates the doctrine.[^4] See [[Doctrine Is Defined by the Unified Voice of the Highest Authorities]].

3. **LDS posit a real engine plus pre-existing track (rated: moderate, analogical).** Rapley: LDS hold "that God is the engine on the train, but we also recognize there's *tohu* before that — there's track someone had to make, there's coal." LDS affirm God as the organizer/creator of all *we* know, while not claiming He created the raw substrate (matter, intelligences) ex nihilo. See [[Greco-Roman Jewish Creation View Was Material Substrate]] and [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]].[^5] *Steelman:* the Thomist rejoinder is that a *plurality* of necessary beings is incoherent (necessary being, being pure existence with no potency, cannot be individuated — there can only be one), so "two engines" misunderstands the argument: contingent diesel engines are themselves contingent and would need their own ground. The LDS reply is that this presupposes precisely the divine-simplicity metaphysics under dispute (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]); LDS reject that God must be *actus purus*, so the uniqueness proof does not bind them.
