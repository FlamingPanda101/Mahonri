# Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship

## The Claim
Mainstream Christian scholarship increasingly recognizes that the feminine divine was an important part of worship in the ancient Middle East, with books now published under titles like *God Had a Wife* and *Did God Have a Wife?*.[^1] This academic recognition aligns with the LDS doctrine of a Heavenly Mother and feminine divine, challenging the exclusively masculine picture of deity inherited from later creedal Christianity.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Book Titles Signal Mainstream Interest:** The source notes that "you can even find books with the title *God Had a Wife* or another one *Did God Have a Wife*" — book titles that would have been unthinkably heretical in prior generations of Christian publishing.[^1] The existence of these works in mainstream (not just LDS) publishing indicates a shift in what is considered a legitimate topic of inquiry.

2. **Recognition of Ancient Near Eastern Context:** The video frames this as part of "the recognition that in the ancient Middle East the feminine divine was an important part of worship."[^1] This situates the feminine divine not as a Mormon innovation but as a recovery of an ancient Israelite (and broader ANE) religious reality later suppressed by monotheistic reform movements.

3. **Convergence Pattern:** The feminine divine is one of several "uniquely Mormon heresies" now being engaged seriously by mainstream Christian scholarship — part of the broader pattern identified by [[Terrell Given]] where LDS doctrines "outside the margins of mainstream Christianity are now being reinvestigated just vigorously by theologians."[^2]

4. **Archaeological and Textual Evidence:** While not detailed in this video, the broader scholarly conversation (reflected in books like [[William Dever]]'s *Did God Have a Wife?*) draws on archaeological finds — particularly the Kuntillet Ajrud and Khirbet el-Qom inscriptions referencing "Yahweh and his Asherah" — and textual evidence of goddess worship in pre-exilic Israel.

## Best Quote

> "There's a recognition that in the ancient Middle East that the feminine divine was an important part of worship and it's not just limited to academia either."[^1]

## Counter-Arguments

- **Asherah was a Canaanite goddess, not a "Heavenly Mother":** The archaeological evidence shows syncretistic worship of Asherah as a consort of Yahweh — condemned by the Hebrew prophets. Critics would argue this is evidence of Israelite apostasy, not evidence for a revealed feminine divine.
- **Book titles don't prove endorsement:** A book titled *Did God Have a Wife?* asks a question it may answer in the negative. Without knowing the authors' conclusions, the titles alone prove only that the topic is discussed, not that scholars endorse a feminine divine.
- **Creaturely vs. divine distinction:** Even if ancient Israelites believed in a divine consort, this could reflect cultural assimilation (as the prophets charged) rather than theological truth recoverable by Latter-day Saints.
