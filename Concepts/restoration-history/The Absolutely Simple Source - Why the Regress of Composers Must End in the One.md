# The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One

## The Point
If every composite thing requires a composer, and that composer is itself composite, it too requires a composer — generating a hierarchical regress. [^1] Such a regress cannot go on without end; it must have a first member. [^1] But the first member cannot itself be composite, for then it would need a cause of its own and so not be first. [^1] Therefore the regress terminates in something *absolutely simple* — having no parts of any kind, material or metaphysical. Following Plotinus, Feser calls this noncomposite ultimate cause **the One**: the here-and-now source of the being of every composite thing. [^2]

## Evidence
- The series of composers is *hierarchical*, and (as argued in the previous proof) a hierarchical series must have a first member. [^1]
- "the first member cannot itself be composite, for then it would require a cause of its own and thus not be first." [^1] So it must be "utterly simple in the sense of having no parts of any kind—no material parts, and no metaphysical parts like form and matter or essence and existence." [^1]
- The dependence is *here and now*, not backward in time: "the claim is not that the chain of causes of composite things traces backward into the past… Rather, the claim is that it traces here and now to a simple or noncomposite cause." [^3]
- The One must be *unique*: two simple causes would need some differentiating feature F, but having F-versus-not-F would make each a "simple cause plus a part," contradicting their simplicity. So there can be only one. [^2]
- The One is the source of *all else*: "since it is unique, anything other than itself is composite, and we have already seen that anything that is composite must ultimately depend for its existence on the One." [^4]
- The "What caused God?" retort fails: the One needs no cause "not… that he is an arbitrary exception to a general rule, but rather that only what is in some way composite needs to have, or indeed could have, a cause." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "there must also exist here and now a noncomposite or utterly simple ultimate cause of their existence—a cause which, following the Neo-Platonic philosopher Plotinus, we might call the One." [^2]

> "Something absolutely simple or noncomposite not only needs no cause but could not have had one." [^3]

## LDS Connection
As an answer to atheism this is formidable: it shows the chain of dependent, composite things cannot be self-sustaining and must rest *now* on a non-derivative source — and it dismantles the popular "Who made God?" objection by showing only composites can have causes. Latter-day Saints affirm a God who is the ground and sustainer of the cosmos. The sharp divergence is the *character* of that source: Feser's One is **absolutely without parts** (see [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]), whereas the Restoration confesses an exalted, embodied Father "of body, parts, and passions" (D&C 130:22) — see [[God Was Once a Man]]. The proof also assumes a single ultimate that is *unique by simplicity*; LDS theology instead situates the Father within co-eternal realities He did not create from nothing — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]], [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]], and the broader critique in [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]].
