# Pancho Villa's Posthumous Visitation and Temple Work

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Luke Hansen]] relays a faith-promoting account — sourced to a 2019 *Meridian Magazine* article by Anne Hinton Pratt and to his wife's family history — that the Mexican revolutionary **[[Pancho Villa]]** (Doroteo Arango), decades after his 1923 assassination, appeared to former mission president **[[James Bevan Weetman]]** pleading for help to enter "a beautiful city" he could not enter alone. Weetman is said to have obtained First Presidency authorization and performed Villa's temple work at the Mesa Arizona Temple. The story is offered as a vivid illustration of the Latter-day Saint doctrines of **vicarious work for the dead** and **post-mortal repentance** — that even a violent man may, after death, seek and receive the saving ordinances (cf. [[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]], [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]]).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A named firsthand source with a traceable chain of custody — rate: weak-to-moderate (family tradition)
The account is attributed to Weetman himself, and the hosts stress that the article's author is married to Weetman's grandson, so the report is (they argue) close to its source rather than free-floating folklore. The prior encounter is also somewhat documented: Weetman and a stake president were genuinely captured by Villa's men during the Revolution.[^2]

### 2. The visitation fits the established Latter-day Saint pattern of postmortal appeals — rate: moderate (doctrinal coherence)
Weetman's reported reply — that president Bentley "is on the other side of the veil with you, he can help you" — presupposes the LDS framework in which the dead labor for one another and await proxy ordinances. The hosts liken Villa "standing above the ground at the foot of his bed" to the [[Angel Moroni]] visitation pattern, and connect it to [[Wilford Woodruff]]'s well-known account of the "eminent men" (the U.S. founders) appearing to request their temple work.[^3]

### 3. The "beautiful city" matches LDS afterlife geography — rate: weak (suggestive imagery)
Villa's described inability to enter the city "without your help" is read as spirit-world dependence on living proxies for ordinance work, and the hosts riff on heaven imagined "as some sort of library" / a city with thresholds — an afterlife of degrees and prerequisites rather than a binary saved/damned. See [[LDS Afterlife - Degrees of Glory]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Single late source, no documentation.** The visitation and temple-work narrative rests entirely on one popular-magazine retelling decades after the fact; there is no contemporaneous record, and family transmission is exactly the channel in which pious legends accrete.
- **The reputation-change is better explained by 1960s revisionism.** The hosts themselves concede that Villa's rehabilitation from "villain" to "liberator/hero" coincided with a broad mid-century wave of revisionist Mexican historiography — a sufficient secular cause that needs no posthumous-ordinance explanation.
- **Subjective experience proves nothing to an outsider.** Even granting Weetman's sincerity, a dream or vision of a deceased man does not establish that the visitation was real or that the ordinance "took"; the account is evidence of LDS *belief and practice*, not of the metaphysics it assumes.
- **Selection and embellishment.** The dramatic details (the sadness, the exact dialogue, the "you can ride a pig" colonist anecdote) bear the marks of a story polished in the retelling.

**What survives:** the episode is best read not as *evidence* but as *catechesis* — a memorable dramatization of two genuine Latter-day Saint commitments: that the gospel is preached to the dead and that no one is beyond the reach of repentance and vicarious ordinances. Its apologetic weight is doctrinal-illustrative, not evidential.
