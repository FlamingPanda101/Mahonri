# Yeast Working Through Dough — Christianity's Slow Abolition

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (O'Connor): [[The New Testament Does Not Teach Abolition|Paul should have condemned slavery outright]] if Christianity is responsible for abolition; he didn't, so Christianity isn't.

## The Rebuttal

[[Glenn Scrivener]]: Paul did "what you'd expect a missionary to do to a brutal culture — work within the structures and sow the seed." Abolition is "yeast working through dough," a slow, transformative leaven, not an immediate decree.[^1]

1. **Manumission was illegal en masse.** Roman law (c. 2 BC, 6 AD) made it "literally illegal to manumit your entire slave population." So a Pauline abolition decree was not legally or socially actionable; "the social upheaval of abolition" was vast (Britain paid off its abolition compensation debt until 2015).[^2] *(Strong — the legal constraint is real; immediate abolition was not a live option in the 1st century.)*

2. **The structural-subversion reading.** Within the church, slave and free "eat together, kiss one another, are brothers and sisters, with no Lord but Jesus" — planting the dignity-seed that eventually dissolves slavery. See [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]].[^3] *(Coherent; the early church's social practice was genuinely subversive of status hierarchy.)*

3. **Steelman of O'Connor — the illegality tu quoque.** O'Connor: Christianity *itself* was illegal, and refusing the imperial cult was punishable — yet Christians defied Rome on idolatry "because they thought it was the right thing to do." So legality didn't stop them when conviction was strong; the absence of an abolition stand suggests abolition wasn't yet a Christian conviction, not merely that it was illegal.[^4] *(Effective — the "it was illegal" defense is weakened by the church's documented civil disobedience on worship. Scrivener's reply leans on the "yeast" model: not every reform comes by frontal decree.)*

4. **The "ought he to have written it?" exchange.** O'Connor: if Paul is "in part responsible for the abolitionist tendency, then you have to say that's what he should have written." Scrivener: "No, because it's yeast working through dough."[^5] *(The disagreement is whether delayed, indirect causation counts as the text being "responsible" — O'Connor wants a direct condemnation; Scrivener accepts indirect leaven as genuine causation.)*

## Best Quotes

> "I think he does what you'd expect a missionary to do to a brutal culture — work within the structures and sow the seed."[^1]

> "I don't have to do that, because it's yeast working through dough."[^5]

> "You know what else was illegal in the early Roman Empire? Christianity. But they still practiced it because they thought it was right." — O'Connor[^4]
