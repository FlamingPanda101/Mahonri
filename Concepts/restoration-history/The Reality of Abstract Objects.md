# The Reality of Abstract Objects

## The Point
The world contains not only particular things (this billiard ball, that pool cue) but also the **abstract, general patterns** they instance — what philosophers call **universals** (triangularity, redness, roundness).[^1] Alongside universals stand three further kinds of abstract object: **propositions** (the truth that "snow is white," distinct from any sentence expressing it), **numbers and mathematical objects**, and **possible worlds** (ways things could have been).[^2] None of these can be perceived by the senses or picked up off a table, yet we genuinely know things about them.[^3] The central question of the chapter is not *whether* they are real but *in what mode* they exist.[^4]

## Evidence
Feser argues these four classes of abstract object are not reducible to the concrete particulars or labels we associate with them:
- A universal is not its instances: you can erase every triangle and triangularity could be exemplified again in a new one.[^1]
- A proposition is not the sentence that expresses it: "Snow is white" and "Schnee ist weiss" are different sentences expressing the *same* proposition; erasing every such sentence would not make the proposition false.[^5]
- A number is not its numeral: the mark "2," the Roman "II," are mere labels; erasing them would not make 2 + 2 = 4 false.[^2]
- A possible world is a genuine way-things-could-be (Romney winning, Mars with three moons), constrained not by physics but by logic — there is no possible world where 2 + 2 = 5 or round squares exist.[^6]

This yields three rival positions, and Feser frames the debate as a choice among them: **realism** (abstract objects are real, neither material nor mere mental constructs), **nominalism** (they are not real — only general names exist), and **conceptualism** (they are real but wholly built by the human mind).[^4] The whole Augustinian proof is built on first establishing realism, then asking *where* such objects can exist.

## Best Quotes
> "Such patterns are called universals by philosophers, and they are 'abstract' in the sense that when we consider them, we abstract from or ignore the particular, individualizing features of the concrete objects that exhibit the patterns." [^7]

> "There are compelling reasons to think that none of these answers can be correct—to conclude instead that abstract objects of the sort we've been considering are real, and neither reducible to anything material nor sheer constructs of the human mind. This is a view known as realism." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Against atheist materialism, Feser's catalogue of abstract objects is a powerful ally: a "matter in motion" universe has no resources to ground universals, propositions, numbers, or modal truths, yet these are presupposed by science and reasoning itself (compare [[The Laws of Logic Are Immaterial and Point to a Mind]]). The chapter's payoff — that such realities must be anchored in a Mind — fits theism broadly. The interesting convergence with the Restoration is that Latter-day Saint theology *already* treats certain truths and laws as eternal and not invented (D&C 88:13; D&C 93:36), so the existence of mind-independent necessary truth is unsurprising on LDS terms. The eventual divergence, fair to Feser, is over the *mode* of grounding: Feser will argue these objects exist as ideas in a single, absolutely simple, purely actual God, whereas the LDS picture is comfortable with eternal law and intelligence that God himself honors rather than authors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]).
