# Justin Martyr's Account of the Sunday Worship Service

## The Point
The earliest surviving description of a Christian worship service comes from [[Justin Martyr]] (*First Apology* 67, c. AD 150), writing roughly a century after Jesus preached at Nazareth. It already shows an ordered weekly liturgy with an offices/hierarchy — a presiding authority, readers, and deacons — and the now-familiar sequence of scripture-reading followed by an admonishing sermon.

## Evidence
1. **Dating and significance.** Justin "died before AD 167, a Palestinian Christian who lived not much more than one hundred years after Jesus preached in Nazareth." His account is the earliest extant description of the service. [^1]
2. **An ordered assembly with offices.** "In Justin's time there appears to be an order to the weekly worship, as well as a hierarchy, with a presiding authority and other offices, including readers and deacons." [^1]
3. **The quoted liturgy.** "On the day which is called Sunday we have a common assembly of all who live in the cities or in the outlying districts, and the memoirs of the Apostles or the writings of the Prophets are read, as long as there is time. Then, when the reader has finished, the one presiding over the assembly verbally admonishes and invites all to imitate such examples of virtue." [^1]
4. **The sermon's purpose: imitation of virtue.** In Justin's description the sermon is "a robust admonition, intended to inspire the congregation to imitate the examples of virtue that they have found in the scriptures." Early preaching was "rooted in scripture but related directly to the spiritual life of the congregation … intentional and invitational." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "On the day which is called Sunday we have a common assembly … and the memoirs of the Apostles or the writings of the Prophets are read, as long as there is time. Then, when the reader has finished, the one presiding over the assembly verbally admonishes and invites all to imitate such examples of virtue." (Justin Martyr, *1 Apology* 67) [^1]

## LDS Connection
Justin's "presiding authority," "readers," and "deacons," meeting on Sunday with scripture reading followed by exhortation, offer a striking second-century parallel to Latter-day Saint sacrament-meeting structure and lay offices — an instance of the volume's strategy of finding recognizable continuity rather than apostasy.
