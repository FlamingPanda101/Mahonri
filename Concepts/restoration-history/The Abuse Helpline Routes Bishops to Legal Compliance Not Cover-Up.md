# The Abuse Helpline Routes Bishops to Legal Compliance Not Cover-Up

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Michael Rezendes]] characterized the LDS abuse helpline as a mechanism to "divert abuse allegations away from law enforcement and instead to church attorneys who may bury the problem, leaving the victim in harm's way" — i.e. an institutional cover-up tool. The objection is that the helpline *exists to protect the institution, not the child*.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The helpline exists because lay bishops face a 50-state legal patchwork (strong)
"These bishops aren't trained counselors, nor are they law enforcement. So, the church created a hotline for these men. All of these men … are volunteers." Reporting law "varies from state to state"; the helpline "complies with all the various laws surrounding child abuse and informs clergy" what their jurisdiction requires. Its function is legal triage for untrained volunteers, not suppression [^1]. See [[The Spotlight Analogy Equates a Celibate Cardinal With a Volunteer Bishop]].

### 2. It pushes *toward* reporting and counseling (strong)
The helpline "encourages victims and family members to seek professional counseling and to self-report their abuse to the authorities." In the Adams case the bishop, after calling the helpline, "advised the couple to call the police themselves" — the opposite of diverting them from police [^2].

### 3. It mandates direct reporting when a child is in danger (strong)
"If a child is ever in imminent danger, the hotline will directly report any abuse to the authorities." The cover-up reading cannot accommodate a protocol that escalates imminent-danger cases straight to law enforcement [^3].

### 4. It is staffed by survivors and former abuse prosecutors (strong)
"The hotline is frequently staffed by actual abuse survivors, by parents of abuse survivors, or grandparents of abuse survivors. Even former government child abuse investigators and prosecutors work the hotline." The staffing is inconsistent with an instrument designed to bury abuse [^4].

### 5. The legal context is the Catch-22, not concealment (strong)
The helpline is the church's answer to symmetric liability — sued if it reports (the [[Timothy Johnson]] case) and sued if it doesn't. "As long as the church is wealthy, and as long as it is involved in regular people's lives, it will be the target of lawsuits." The helpline routes volunteers through that minefield lawfully [^5]. See [[The Reporting Catch-22 and the Johnson Case]].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
The strongest counter: routing a bishop's first call to *church attorneys* creates a structural conflict of interest, since counsel's duty is to the institution's liability, not the child's safety; "complies with state law" can mean "does the legal minimum," which in a confession-exemption state (like Arizona in the Adams case) means *not* reporting; and a helpline can be well-staffed and still, in a given case, produce an outcome where abuse continued. These are real tensions. The rebuttal's reply is that the protocol nonetheless *urges* self-reporting and counseling, *mandates* reporting in imminent-danger cases, and exists against a genuine two-sided legal threat — so "designed to bury the problem" mischaracterizes a system whose documented default is to push victims toward police and professional help. Whether mandatory clergy reporting should override confessional privilege "is a debate to be had."
