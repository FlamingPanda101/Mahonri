# Cirillo Catalogs Substantial Enoch Parallels Beyond Dispute

## The Claim
[[Salvatore Cirillo]], a non-Latter-day Saint scholar, wrote a master's thesis comparing [[Joseph Smith]]'s Enoch material (the "extract of the prophecy of Enoch" in [[Moses 6|Moses 6]]–[[Moses 7|7]], 1830–1832) with the ancient Enoch corpus and concluded the overlaps are "beyond dispute." His verdict: "individually these parallels may appear small, but when viewed together these comparisons prove substantial similarities between the Book of Enoch and Joseph's rendering in the Pearl of Great Price which are beyond dispute."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the catalogued parallels)

1. **Enoch directly linked to the Son of Man (rate: strong, distinctive).** In the *Book of Parables* (part of [[1 Enoch]]) the Son of Man is a pre-existent heavenly judge, and it is "only in 1 Enoch that Enoch himself is directly linked to this Son of Man figure" — and Joseph's Book of Moses "makes that same unique connection." See [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]].[^2]

2. **Enoch foresees Noah's birth and the flood (rate: strong, specific).** The Bible relates Enoch and Noah only as great-grandfather and great-grandson, but Moses has Enoch receive visions of Noah and the flood and the covenant usually given to Noah — and 1 Enoch likewise has Enoch witness Noah's birth and foretell the flood. See [[Book of Enoch - Birth of Noah as Wonder Child]].[^3]

3. **The personified, pained earth (rate: moderate, thematic).** Both 1 Enoch and the Book of Moses personify the earth, giving it human emotion — pained by wickedness and needing cleansing — a motif absent from the Genesis flood narrative.[^3]

4. **The Mahijah / Mahawai name (rate: strong, low-probability).** A figure named Mahijah/Mahujah questions Enoch in Moses, matching Mahawai (MHWY) who journeys to question Enoch in the *Book of the Giants*. Treated in full at [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch]].[^4]

5. **Enoch called a "lad" despite his age (rate: moderate, verbal).** 2 and 3 Enoch describe the 65-year-old Enoch as a "lad"; Joseph's Book of Enoch uses the same odd descriptor — and this is not found in 1 Enoch, the only version available in Joseph's day.[^5]

6. **Further verbal/thematic hits (rate: moderate, cumulative).** Rivers changing course as a display of divine power; the phrase "open thy mouth and it shall be filled" (2 Enoch); secret oaths and "secret works of darkness" against Enoch (Book of Giants); the **book of remembrance**; Enoch's homeland as a "land of righteousness"; and Enoch's enthronement/exaltation into the divine council ("thou hast given unto me a right to thy throne").[^6]

7. **God weeping over humanity (rate: moderate, theological).** Moses 7's weeping God parallels the *Midrash Rabbah on Lamentations*, where the exalted Enoch (as Metatron) comforts a weeping God — see [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology]] and [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]].[^7]

> The cumulative force is the point: each parallel alone is dismissible, but the *stack* of unique, role-matched correspondences is what Cirillo calls "beyond dispute." This is an instance of [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]].

## Steelman of the Critic
A skeptic (and the video's own framing concedes alternatives) can reply: (a) **parallelomania** — list-making without textual-dependence analysis carries no probative weight; loose thematic overlaps between religious texts are easy to manufacture (see [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]] and [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]]). (b) Some parallels are **generic** (a weeping God, a personified earth, secret oaths) and appear across ancient literature. (c) The presentation is an **AI narration** of the thesis, so wording and emphasis may overstate Cirillo's hedged academic claims. (d) Mahijah aside, several "hits" rest on contested datings of which Enochic layer is early. The strongest single item remains the role-matched proper name; the rest are corroborating rather than independently decisive. The availability question — whether Joseph *could* have reached these sources — is carried by [[The Enoch Rediscovery Timeline Postdates Joseph Smith]].
