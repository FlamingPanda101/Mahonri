# Infinite Monkey Theorem Against Random Chiasmus

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection: *chiasms are statistically inevitable* — give a monkey a typewriter (or run enough random text) and ABCBA patterns "are going to have to pop up," so finding chiasmus in the Book of Mormon proves nothing. The hosts raise this themselves and then rebut it [^1][^2].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The real math kills the monkey.** [[Cardon Ellis]] cites a BBC-reported study by two Australian mathematicians who called the infinite-monkey theorem "misleading": with realistic limits (every chimpanzee that could ever exist, one keystroke per second, until the heat death of the universe) the monkeys would *never* type Shakespeare — only a ~5% chance one chimp types "bananas" in its lifetime, and odds of ~1 in 10 million billion billion for a sentence like "I chimp therefore I am" [^2][^3]. *Rating: the cited study is real (2024, Stephen Woodcock & Jay Falletta, University of Technology Sydney) and accurately summarized; the point that bounded randomness is hopeless is correct.*
2. **Inference to design.** From this the hosts conclude "God's creation is greater than random selection," and tie it to [[Stephen C. Meyer]]'s argument that coded information in DNA points to an intelligent source (his books *Signature in the Cell*, *Darwin's Doubt*, *Return of the God Hypothesis*) [^4]. So ordered linguistic structure (chiasmus) is offered as a small-scale instance of information that randomness cannot generate [^5].
3. **Steelman of the opposing view.** The monkey rebuttal is **directed at the wrong target.** No critic claims Book-of-Mormon chiasms arose from *random keystrokes*; the live alternative is a literate 19th-century author (or dictation) shaped by deep immersion in KJV parallelism — a *non-random, intelligent* process that easily yields inverted parallelism. Defeating blind randomness does nothing to defeat human authorship. Likewise, transplanting an Intelligent-Design/DNA argument onto a literary-form question conflates biological information theory with the much easier task of a person producing patterned prose. The hosts present this as a flourish (and immediately joke about interviewing the AI/chiasmus-hunting "Ryan Lether's elephant"), not a rigorous defense [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "Even if all the monkeys in the world were locked in with all the typewriters in the world till the end of the universe, they would never type Shakespeare." [^3]

> "So God's creation is greater than random selection apparently... which is actually Stephen C. Meyer's argument about coding in DNA." [^4]

> "At some point there is a limitation to a theory... given enough time and enough pages, okay, kayasmus are going to have to pop up." [^1]
