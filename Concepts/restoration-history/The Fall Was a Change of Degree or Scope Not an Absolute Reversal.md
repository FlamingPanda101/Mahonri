# The Fall Was a Change of Degree or Scope Not an Absolute Reversal

#type/claim

## The Claim

Read closely, the Fall is "a change of degree or scope, not an absolute monumental shift in the laws of the universe."[^1] The four conditions usually said to *begin* at the Fall — death, reproduction, choice, and experience — are all already operating *inside* the garden, just on a smaller scale. So the Fall is better described as "a process than as an event":[^2] not a snap of the fingers in which mortality, fertility, and knowledge switch on, but a maturing and an enlargement of scope. [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] argues this both fits the text better and dissolves the "evolution vs. the Fall" tension, since Adam need not "absolutely bring death to the world" if death already existed in limited form.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **Limited death is already in the garden.** Adam and Eve eat, "and that's a kind of death." Spackman notes [[J. Reuben Clark]] (First Presidency, 1934–1961) argued in a dispute with Joseph Fielding Smith that even vegetarian animals eating plants involve "a kind of death," limiting "no death in the garden" to the humans only. Crucially, "there is nothing in Genesis that says Adam and Eve are immortal until they eat the fruit. That's an extrapolation." They are told they may eat from *all* the trees, including the tree of life — so it may be that "eating from the tree of life kept them alive," not an inherent immortal state.[^3]
- **Limited reproduction is implied.** If Adam, Eve, and the animals strip the trees, do the plants grow back? If yes, that is plant reproduction in the garden. A 1973 church-magazine article by the chairman of correlation evaluation, Eldredge — "the embodiment of orthodoxy" — taught that the scriptures "do not say Adam and Eve *could not* have children" but that they "*would not* have had children" while in a state of innocence; they were "physically capable of having children in the garden." If humans were physically capable, plants and animals presumably were too.[^4]
- **Choice and experience are presupposed.** Eating itself presupposes "Oh, I need to eat... I need to stop eating" (and a "tummy ache" if you don't). When Eve "perceives that it is good for food," that "presupposes an ability to recognize on a spectrum"; the fruit being "pleasant to the eyes" presupposes an aesthetic — "she's making a judgment call." These faculties were active *before* she ate.[^5]
- **The conclusion.** "If the fall is not an absolute 180-degree flipping of universal conditions... it would seem that the fall, even reading scripture closely, is a change of degree or scope." This "opens the door" to scenarios outside the garden and "gets us back to the evolution and the fall question" — Adam need not "absolutely bring death to the world in the same way as we have imagined."[^1]
- **"Knowing good and evil" = maturation.** The same reasoning extends to knowledge: in Isaiah 7 and Deuteronomy 1 "knowing good and evil" marks the move from childhood to adulthood, so eating the fruit is "less of a snap of the fingers download than a maturing into adulthood" — they "grew up." This is "still a very historical, very literal reading," just not an instantaneous one.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "It would seem that the fall, even reading scripture closely, is a change of degree or scope, not an absolute monumental shift in the laws of the universe."[^1]

> "Scripture seems to present fall more as a process than as an event... They don't eat the fruit and then snap, they're mortal. They know stuff... They've already been vaguely aware of this stuff and they eat the fruit and they're out of the garden and things change."[^2]

> "There is nothing in Genesis that says Adam and Eve are immortal until they eat the fruit. That's an extrapolation... It may be that... eating from the tree of life kept them alive."[^3]

## LDS Connection

This is a textual companion to [[No Death Before the Fall Is a Twentieth-Century Interpretation Not Settled Doctrine]]: that note traces the *doctrinal history* of "no death before the Fall," while this one makes the *exegetical* case from Genesis itself that death, reproduction, and judgment operate in limited scope in Eden. By reframing the Fall as enlargement-of-scope rather than cosmic reversal, it removes the supposed conflict with [[Nothing Has Been Revealed Concerning Evolution|an old, death-filled earth]] and resonates with [[Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth Not Corruption|the patristic reading of Eden as immaturity that needed to grow]]. The cost is low because, on Spackman's account, the gospel does not hang on an "absolute fall" — "I'm not sure our theology is wedded to that as much as we have imagined."[^2]
