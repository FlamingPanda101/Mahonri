# Latter-day Saints Are Radically Non-Dogmatic With Strong Orthopraxy

#type/claim

## The Claim
Discussing the temple-recommend interview, [[Jonathan Stapley]] and his hosts argue that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has a **minimal doctrinal "creed" but strong orthopraxy**: what is required is assent to a few essentials (God, Jesus Christ, the Holy Spirit; the divine origins of the Restoration; that church leaders have the right to govern) plus *covenant practice* — while leaving a "huge diversity of belief" on most theological questions. The slogan from the conversation: Latter-day Saints are "radically non-dogmatic." A practical apologetic payoff follows: critics who say "Mormons are damned by their own doctrine" cannot pin the church to a doctrinal monolith, because Latter-day Saints do not all believe the same things and "don't believe in salvation by theology."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The recommend "creed" is thin (strong)
To enter the temple one assents to belief in God, Christ, and the Spirit, to the divine origins of the Restoration, and to leaders' governing authority — "a very conservative, essentially, creed," but a *short* one. Within that boundary belief varies widely.[^1]

### 2. Orthodoxy thin, orthopraxy strong (strong)
The hosts frame it as orthodoxy (correct belief) vs. orthopraxy (correct practice): LDS orthodoxy on, e.g., the fine metaphysics of God or exaltation is limited and multivocal, but its orthopraxy — covenants kept, ordinances performed, "promising to do and to be" — is "strong with us."[^2]

### 3. Multivocality is a feature, rooted in Joseph Smith (medium)
Stapley calls the historical "multivocality" on hard topics one of "the best things about being a Latter-day Saint," and the hosts cite Joseph Smith: "I never thought it was right to call up a man and try him because he erred in doctrine... Methodists have creeds which a man must believe or be kicked out... I want the liberty of believing as I please." Hence: "It doesn't prove that a man is not a good man because he errs in doctrine."[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies that this is overstated and selectively deployed: the church *does* enforce doctrine (excommunication for apostasy; required assent in the recommend interview; correlated curriculum), and "radically non-dogmatic" sits awkwardly beside a living prophet whose pronouncements members are told to follow "in all things." The move also looks tactically convenient — invoked to dodge doctrinal critique ("you can't pin us down") while elsewhere insisting on distinctive, binding truth-claims (the First Vision, the Book of Mormon, exclusive priesthood authority).

## Counter-Objection
The claim is not that *nothing* is binding but that the binding *core* is small and concrete (a few credal essentials + covenant practice), with wide latitude on speculative theology — which is exactly what the history shows: first-generation leaders (the Pratts, Brigham Young, B.H. Roberts) publicly disagreed about exaltation, the origin of spirits, and Adam-God without being unchurched, and several of their positions were later set aside (see [[Was Adam-God Official Binding Teaching]], [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]]). The tension with "follow the prophet" is real but is itself contested terrain within the tradition (see [[The Living Prophet Versus the Standard Works Are Two Competing LDS Authority Traditions]]). And the apologetic use is legitimate: it blocks the *strawman monolith*, not honest engagement with the actual core claims (which the church does press).

## Best Quotes
> "We're radically non-dogmatic... There's a huge diversity of belief... we don't believe in salvation by theology."[^2]

> "It doesn't prove that a man is not a good man because he errs in doctrine." (Joseph Smith)[^3]
