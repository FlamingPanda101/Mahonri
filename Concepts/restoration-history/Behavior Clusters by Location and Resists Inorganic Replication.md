# Behavior Clusters by Location and Resists Inorganic Replication

> **Note:** This is a secular economics/sociology concept with no direct apologetic or Latter-day Saint application. It is catalogued for completeness because the source video contains no scripture, theology, or Restoration content. Filed under restoration-history only as the vault's catch-all domain.

## The Claim

Economic and behavioral success concentrates in particular places ("location inequality"), and these clusters are extraordinarily hard to manufacture from the top down. Beyond ordinary economic *clustering* (talent and customers attracting more of the same), there is a deeper phenomenon — "small area variation" — in which specific *behaviors* concentrate by location and are absorbed by newcomers almost subconsciously, which is why governments that spend billions to copy New York or Silicon Valley usually fail.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Clustering produces self-reinforcing hubs
If a place already has the talent pool and customers a firm needs, the firm goes there, and others follow — a virtuous cycle that built New York (finance), Silicon Valley (tech), and Boston (biotech). But clustering alone cannot explain why a *super*cluster ignites in one location and not in another with equal assets (Boston, Dublin), which the video treats as the real puzzle.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — clustering is a well-established economic mechanism; the video's pop-level treatment is directionally sound but not rigorously sourced.

### 2. "Small area variation" — behavior follows place, not the person
A cardiologist relocating from Boulder to Buffalo rapidly adopts local treatment rates rather than carrying his own habits; Vietnam veterans' heroin addiction largely dissipated on returning home (~5% relapse). The video draws these cases from Malcolm Gladwell's *Revenge of the Tipping Point* and the medical small-area-variation literature: behaviors cluster by location and integrate newcomers without their noticing.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the cardiology and veteran cases are real and cited in the literature; the sweeping "something weird about locations" framing is the video's editorializing.

### 3. Clusters resist inorganic creation
Dublin's tax incentives drew Apple, Meta, and Amazon yet it doesn't rank in Europe's top 10 for startups; East Palo Alto stays poor despite sitting inside Silicon Valley — a legacy of redlining that persists as a behavioral pattern even after the policies ended. Switzerland's centuries-long focus on watchmaking (seeded by a 1500s Calvinist jewelry ban) shows the converse: doubling down on an existing local strength works better than chasing the economic fad of the day.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — illustrative case studies rather than controlled evidence; the persistence-of-redlining point is well documented, the Swiss-watch causal story is simplified.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can argue the video overgeneralizes from anecdotes: "small area variation" in medicine reflects identifiable factors (training networks, reimbursement incentives, malpractice climates) rather than a mysterious "something in the water," and the cardiologist-relocation effect plausibly tracks local institutional pressures, not place-magic. Some inorganic clusters *have* succeeded (Shenzhen, Bangalore, Tel Aviv) given the right policy and timing, so "you can't build one" is too strong. The defensible core that survives is narrower: organic behavior clusters are *path-dependent* and resist quick top-down replication, which is a real and useful caution for policymakers — but the video's "weirdly, it might be something in the water" framing is rhetorical color, not a causal finding.

## Best Quotes

> "They started acting like Buffalo, New York cardiologists... It happens almost entirely by itself overnight. There's something weird about locations." [^2]

> "It's nearly impossible to create these behavior clusters inorganically." [^3]
