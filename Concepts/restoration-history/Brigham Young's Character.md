# Brigham Young's Character

## Overview

This is a hub note for the apologetic defense of **Brigham Young's character** against the charge that he was a brutal, autocratic, racist "monster." The case (as marshaled by Daniel Peterson on Ward Radio) is that Young was a good, sincere, loyal man whose rough edges have been exaggerated and whose complex record on race, polygamy, and doctrine has been distorted by **presentism** (judging a 19th-century figure by 21st-century norms) and **cherry-picking**. A linchpin evidentiary claim is LeGene Kuth's finding that George D. Watt systematically edited the *Journal of Discourses* to make Young sound more authoritarian than he was — so the harshest "Brigham quotes" may be an editor's artifact.

The cluster notes that the *same* attacks on Young's character were already deployed in the 1850s by RLDS splinter groups and are now resurgent online — a "cycle" recurring 160 years later — which the apologists treat as evidence the critique is polemical rather than novel discovery.

## The Steelman

The critic's strongest form: presentism cannot do *all* the work. Young's recorded teachings on blood atonement, race, and Adam-God are not merely editorial exaggerations but appear in multiple sources, and a man claiming to speak for God is fairly held to a higher bar than a typical contemporary. The apologetic reply distinguishes a *man of his time* who erred from a *monster*, and shifts the weight onto sincerity and the institutional fruits of his leadership rather than the perfection of his words. See [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].
