# Church Is Hemorrhaging Members at a Unique Rate (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A widespread online narrative holds that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is "losing members in droves," "hemorrhaging at a large rate," and is *uniquely* failing to retain its people. The factual seed is real: in the 1980s about 80% of those raised Latter-day Saint still identified with the Church as adults, but today that figure is closer to 50% — a genuine, large drop.[^1] The objection then infers that the Church is in special, self-inflicted crisis.

## The Rebuttal

1. **The decline is real but not unique (rated: strong, multi-dataset).** Dyer concedes the retention drop outright — "have we declined in retention over time? The answer is yes" — but the inference of *uniqueness* fails once the comparison set widens. The same question that motivated the paper ("how would we find that out?") was answered by pulling multiple large datasets rather than anecdote.[^2] *Steelman:* a 30-point drop in lifetime retention is objectively severe and would alarm any institution; the rebuttal does not minimize it, only the claim that it is anomalous.

2. **It tracks a society-wide collapse in connectedness (rated: strong).** Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone* documents a broad American decline in civic, philanthropic, and religious participation; "religion hasn't been spared those general trends," and the LDS Church "follow[s] the same general trends."[^3] See [[Decline in Religion Tracks the Broader Collapse of Social Connectedness]].

3. **LDS retention is comparatively high, especially active retention (rated: strong, Pew).** Pew data show Latter-day Saints near the top for retaining members who both still identify *and* still attend regularly; the decline is "not as much as most other Christian traditions."[^4] See [[LDS Retention Is Higher Than Most Other Christian Traditions]].

4. **The narrative is amplified by online selection effects (rated: moderate).** The loudest voices online are activist ex-members, distorting public perception of who actually leaves and why. See [[The Four Groups of LDS Disaffiliates]] and [[The Online Echo Chamber Accelerates Disaffiliation]].

## Best Quotes

> "In the 1980s, 80% of the people who were raised Latter-day Saints still identified with the church as adults. But today, that number is closer to 50. That is huge. And it's reinforcing this narrative online that the church is uniquely losing members at an alarming rate."[^1]

> "You really do... have to zoom out and you have to take a broader view of what's going on in the United States in general."[^2]

> "Have we declined in retention over time? The answer is yes. Not as much as most other Christian traditions in particular. And particularly with active retention, we're at... the top of that list."[^4]
