# Why a Hierarchical Series Cannot Regress to Infinity

## The Point
Feser sharpens *why* a hierarchical (essentially ordered) causal series must have a first member, while a linear series need not: it is not about simultaneity but about *derived* versus *underived* causal power. In a hierarchical series every member except the first "can act only insofar as its power to act is imparted to it from outside." [^1] Until we reach something that "can impart causal power without having to derive it," we have "just keep[t] passing the explanatory buck." [^1] Crucially, this holds *even if the series were infinitely long*: "an infinitely long paintbrush handle could not move itself," and "the length of the handle is irrelevant." [^2] So the impossibility of regress is not a claim that you cannot have infinitely many causes — it is that instruments with no ultimate source explain nothing.

## Evidence
1. **Instrumental vs. inherent power is the whole hinge.** Linear series (Al begets Bob begets Chuck) can regress to infinity because each member has its causal power "inherently rather than derivatively" relative to the others — Bob can beget whether or not Al is still alive. [^3] Hierarchical series cannot, because each later member's power is borrowed; a "hierarchical series without such a first member would be like an instrument that is not the instrument of anything." [^1]
2. **The mirror and paintbrush illustrations.** A mirror reflecting a face in another mirror, *ad infinitum*, still requires "the face itself — which could impart the content of the image without having to derive it." Otherwise there would be "only mirror images and never any actual face." [^2] An infinite series of instrumental causes "could not exhibit any causality at all unless there were something beyond the series whose instruments they were." [^2]
3. **Reply to Oppy (infinite series).** Pointing to infinite *mathematical* series is a non-answer: they "do not involve instrumental and primary causes, or indeed any causality at all." [^4] Aristotelians don't reject infinite series as such — they even grant that linear causal series and a beginningless universe/multiverse are *possible*; so "cosmological models favoring a universe... without a beginning" miss the point. [^4]
4. **Reply to Sobel (begging the question).** Calling a cause "instrumental" does not smuggle in the conclusion: it "is merely to say that it derives its causal power from something else." Even a skeptic grasps that "a stick cannot move the stone under its own power" without yet conceding that the regress must terminate. [^5]
5. **"First" reconceived.** A first cause is one with *primary/underived* causal power, not the one at the temporal head of the queue; even an infinite hierarchical series "would still have to be a source of causal power outside the series to impart causal power to the whole." [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Even an infinitely long paintbrush handle could not move itself, since the wood out of which it is made has no 'built-in' power of movement. The length of the handle is irrelevant." [^2]

> "What there could not be is only mirror images and never any actual face." [^2]

> "To characterize something as an instrumental cause is merely to say that it derives its causal power from something else." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this is decisive work: it blocks the naturalist's hope that an infinite regress of dependent causes could be self-explanatory, forcing the question of an underived source — a conclusion every theist, Latter-day Saint included, can affirm. The argument coheres beautifully with the scriptural picture of God as the present, underived sustainer "in [whom] all things hold together" ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). The Restoration divergence is not with the regress logic but with what the terminus *is*. Feser will identify the source of underived causal power with the purely actual, immaterial, immutable absolute ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]); a Latter-day Saint can grant a primary, underived sustaining cause while denying the Aristotelian metaphysics that makes that cause potency-free and bodiless ([[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]). Indeed, LDS theology locates underived realities plurally — God plus co-eternal intelligence, matter, and law ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]) — so it need not read "first member" as a lone simple absolute. In fairness, Feser's instrumental/primary distinction is genuinely strong and exposes shallow "but maybe it's infinite" rejoinders; the honest LDS engagement is to accept the regress argument and contest only the further identification of the terminus.
