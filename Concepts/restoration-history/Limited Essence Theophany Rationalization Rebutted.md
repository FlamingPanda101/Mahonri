# Limited Essence Theophany Rationalization Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Apologists claim Genesis 32:30 and John 1:18 do not contradict because the OT figures who "saw God" only saw a limited, diminished manifestation — not God's full divine essence. The analogy given: "The same way you can see sunlight without staring into the sun itself."[^1] People saw a revelation or manifestation, while John 1:18 refers to seeing God in His full, unlimited essence — a claim the OT itself rules out ("no man shall see me and live," Exodus 33:20).

## The Rebuttal — Why This Rationalization Fails

### 1. It Presupposes Univocality (Begging the Question)

The "limited essence" argument only exists because the apologist has ALREADY decided the Bible cannot contradict itself. McClellan explains: "As long as we assume that and we see passage A and passage B and they seem to conflict, well, we have just misunderstood them and we need to seek out rationalization C — the separate third thing that functions as a unifying framework."[^2] The conclusion (the Bible doesn't contradict itself) is assumed in the premise. This is circular reasoning. See [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]].

### 2. The Hebrew Bible Contains People Looking Directly at God's Face

The rationalization asserts that OT theophanies were dimmed-down, partial encounters. But McClellan points out: "You do have people who look directly at God's face. That is the full essence of God. So the notion that in the Hebrew Bible they're not getting the full essence, but in the New Testament they're talking about seeing the full essence, that is a rationalization that is rather naive and sophomoric."[^3]

Jacob explicitly says "I have seen God face to face." In the Hebrew Bible, the "face" (*panim*) IS the source of all the divine glory — the most dangerous, most complete manifestation. To see the face is to encounter the fullness. There is no textual basis for saying Jacob saw a "dimmed-down" version.

### 3. The Concept of "Full Essence" Is a Later Theological Import

"There is absolutely nothing in the Hebrew Bible about 'the full essence' of God versus a partial manifestation. That is a concept imported from Greek philosophical categories and later Christian theology."[^4] The Hebrew Bible operates with physical, spatial categories: God has a body, a face, a back, hands, and feet. The abstract philosophical category of "essence" is anachronistic when applied to these texts on their own terms.

### 4. Nothing in the New Testament Supports the "Partial Vision" Carve-Out

McClellan argues: "Absolutely nothing in the New Testament suggests that people saw God, but just not the full send God. That's not anywhere in the New Testament. In the New Testament, you cannot see God. Period."[^5] The NT makes no distinction between "full essence" and "limited manifestation." John 1:18 says NO ONE has seen God — no qualifier, no exception, no carve-out. The apologist must invent the distinction from outside both texts.

### 5. Later Editors Themselves Saw the Problem

The Hebrew Bible's own editorial history reveals awareness of the tension: "You have a number of other stories where later editors have come through and written *mal'ak* to make it seem like they were actually just talking with a messenger of Adonai or a messenger of God."[^6] The scribes who transmitted these texts already tried to soften the direct divine encounters — evidence that the contradiction was felt within the tradition itself, long before modern apologetics.

### 6. Exodus 33 Only Restricts the FACE, Not the Body

The key proof-text for the "limited essence" argument — Exodus 33:20-23 — actually undermines it. God tells Moses he cannot see God's FACE and live, but God HAS a face, a back, and a hand that physically covers Moses. God passes by and Moses sees God's "back parts" (*achor*). This is NOT a passage about "God is an invisible essence that cannot be perceived." It is a passage about a corporeal God whose face specifically is lethally radiant. The whole scene presupposes a physical, visible deity with spatial orientation.

## Best Quotes

> "The notion that there were appearances of God where the glory was ratcheted down and they didn't see the full essence, which is a concept that you cannot find anywhere in the Hebrew Bible, is that separate third thing. That's the rationalization that you don't find anywhere in the Bible because you do have people who look directly at God's face. That is the full essence of God." [^3]

> "According to Numbers, according to Genesis, according to Exodus, you have figures who look at God's face and that is the source of the full essence of God. And also absolutely nothing in the New Testament suggests that people saw God, but just not the full send God." [^7]

> "This is not paying attention to the text. Is not allowing the text to operate on their own terms. But is imposing upon the text a unifying framework to try to make two contradictory texts agree with each other." [^8]
