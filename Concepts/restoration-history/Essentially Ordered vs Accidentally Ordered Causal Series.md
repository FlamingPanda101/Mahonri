# Essentially Ordered vs Accidentally Ordered Causal Series

## The Point
The hinge of the Aristotelian proof is a distinction between two kinds of causal series. A **linear** (accidentally ordered, *per accidens*) series extends backward in time, and each member retains its own causal power: the air conditioner keeps cooling the room "even if you left the house or dropped dead." [^1] A **hierarchical** (essentially ordered, *per se*) series is one in which every member except the first has its causal power only *derivatively*, here and now, from a higher member. [^2] Feser's key claim: a linear series might in principle regress to infinity, but a hierarchical series **cannot** — it must terminate in a first member with "built-in" causal power. [^3]

## Evidence
- The cup sits three feet up only because the desk holds it, the desk only because the floor holds it, the floor only because of the foundation, then the earth — all "at that same moment." [^4] The lamp hangs only because of the chain, fixture, ceiling, walls, earth — simultaneously. [^4]
- What makes a series hierarchical is "this instrumental or derivative character of the later members." The desk, floor, chain "are in that sense like instruments" — "just as it is not a brush which paints a picture but rather the painter who uses the brush as an instrument." [^5]
- "First" does **not** mean first in time or even finite. A paintbrush "would remain powerless to move itself even if its handle were infinitely long"; likewise "an infinite series of desks ... would be as powerless to hold it up as a single desk would be." [^6]
- The hierarchical series is more fundamental: "every series of the linear sort presupposes series of the hierarchical sort." [^7] Applied to *existence itself*: the coffee exists only because the water does, the water only because its atoms' potential is actualized, the atoms only because of subatomic particles — a hierarchical series terminating in a sustainer of existence. [^8]
- Formal premise 11: "such a series cannot regress infinitely." [^9] Feser's footnote identifies this as the *per accidens* vs. *per se* distinction. [^10]

## Best Quotes
> "What makes a hierarchical series of causes hierarchical, then, is this instrumental or derivative character of the later members of the series." [^5]

> "A paintbrush has no power to move itself, and it would remain powerless to move itself even if its handle were infinitely long." [^6]

## LDS Connection
This distinction is **largely neutral metaphysics** and is one of the most useful tools in the theistic arsenal. It neutralizes the favorite atheist retort "but the universe could be infinitely old" — Feser grants an infinite past *for the sake of argument* and shows it is irrelevant, because the proof concerns a here-and-now sustaining (essentially ordered) series, not a temporal (accidentally ordered) one (compare [[The Cosmological Argument - The Universe Began to Exist]], which takes the other route of arguing the universe began). Latter-day Saints, who affirm eternal matter and intelligences, can wield the *per se* argument without committing to a temporal beginning. The divergence is downstream: Feser will argue the first member of the *per se* series must be purely actual and thus immutable/timeless (see [[The Purely Actual Actualizer - Aristotle's Unmoved Mover]]); an LDS reader can accept a primary, non-derivative sustaining cause while denying that "first" entails the static, simple deity of classical theism (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]).
