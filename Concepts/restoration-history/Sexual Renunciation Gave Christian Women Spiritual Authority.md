# Sexual Renunciation Gave Christian Women Spiritual Authority

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] documents a paradox: as ordinary women lost church office, women who **renounced marriage and sexuality** (virgins and once-married widows) gained extraordinary spiritual prestige and practical freedom. Ascetic women were said to transcend their "femaleness" and become "manly," and many — Macrina, Melania, Marcella, Olympias — wielded real influence as scholars, patrons, and founders of monasteries.

## Evidence

### 1. Celibacy became the marker of priestly authority
Spurred by Paul's counsel that the unmarried stay unmarried (1 Cor 7:8), "renunciation of second marriages and of marriage and sexuality in general quickly became the marker of priestly masculinity and authority." Those who renounced remarriage or retained virginity "were considered to have a higher degree of spiritual authority." [^1]

### 2. Thecla: the virgin who baptizes herself
In the 2nd-c. *Acts of Paul and Thecla*, the virgin Thecla survives the arena twice by miraculous divine protection, **baptizes herself** in a pool of seals (a baptism Paul later accepts), dresses as a man, travels as a missionary, and performs healings, exorcisms, and intercession for the dead. "Thecla's virginity grants her divine favor and the ability to assume the 'masculine' virtues of courage and self-control." [^2]

### 3. Virgins "cease to be women"
4th-c. writers extolled virginity in gendered terms. [[Jerome]] of Stridon asserted a woman who prefers Christ over marriage "will cease to be a woman and will be called a man." [[Ambrose of Milan]] claimed virgins transcend "the bonds of female flesh" and become superior even to married men — "only Christ is the head of the virgin." Yet they were simultaneously called "brides of Christ," reaffirming subordination to a heavenly husband (Athanasius, Gregory of Nyssa, Ambrose, using the Song of Solomon). [^3]

### 4. Real-world influential ascetics
**Macrina** (AD 330–379) managed vast estates, founded a women's monastery, and converted her brother Basil to the ascetic life. **Melania the Elder** (c. 350–410) founded a convent and monastery on the Mount of Olives and mentored clergy. **Marcella of Rome** (325–410) "publicly refuted heretics, including priests and monks" and was praised by Jerome for her scriptural scholarship. **Olympias** (c. 361–408) was a deaconess, friend of John Chrysostom, and patron of a hospital, orphanage, and monastery. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Because female virgins renounced to some extent their 'femaleness' … some ancient Christian writers extolled the 'manliness' of these women and praised them for exercising privileges and authority usually reserved for male clerics." [^1]

> a woman who prefers Jesus over marriage "will cease to be a woman and will be called a man." — Jerome of Stridon [^3]

## LDS Connection
Laughton presents asceticism's self-denial as broadly analogous to LDS practices of fasting, tithing, and missionary service — acts of self-denial "with similar hopes of gaining greater spiritual strength." But she also notes the irony for [[Latter-day Saints]]: a tradition centered on eternal marriage and family will read the early church's exaltation of perpetual virginity as a striking departure, even as it confirms that women *did* hold authority by some recognized path. [^5]
