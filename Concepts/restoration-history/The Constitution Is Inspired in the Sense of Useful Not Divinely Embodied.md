# The Constitution Is Inspired in the Sense of Useful, Not Divinely Embodied

## The Claim

When Latter-day Saints say "the Constitution was inspired," [[Brian Thomassen]] proposes a reading that preserves the word while constraining its meaning: the Constitution is "inspired" in the sense of the Greek ***theopneustos*** — **life-giving / useful** — not in the sense of the *enthos* ("the god is in it," an embodiment of the divine will). It is inspired *because of what it does*: it "creates the space from which men can then make free choices, from which they can then be judged." So one can answer "yes, it was inspired" and then specify a **utility** meaning rather than divine dictation.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The biblical "inspiration" prooftext uses *theopneustos*, which means useful
The only verse in the whole biblical corpus calling scripture "inspired" is [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]] — "All scripture is given by inspiration of God." The Greek is *theopneustos* ("God-breathed"), but the Greek word for *inspiration* proper is *entheos* (whence "enthusiasm," "god-in-it"). *Theopneustos* in early imperial usage described *life-giving* things with no divine connection — sandals (no sores on your feet), the ointments used to make a corpse look alive, a spring you can drink from. The "inspired" gloss is a 3rd-century innovation by [[Origen]] (defending poorly-written Christian Greek against classical critics). This is the same lexical point made in [[Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible]].[^2][^3]

### 2. "Wise men" signals utility, not dictation
[[D&C 101]]:80 says God "established" the Constitution "by the hands of **wise men** whom I raised up." Webster's 1828 glosses *established* as "affirm / confirm / appoint," and **wisdom** means "the proper application of something" — i.e., a *utility* is in play. The wise men took principles that *predated* them ([[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]], [[John Locke|Locke]], [[Montesquieu]]) and crafted them into a working system. See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]].[^4]

### 3. The utility is the free space for agency and judgment
Why is the Constitution useful? Because it "creates the space from which men can then make free choices… so that they can then be judged justly" — explicitly tied to [[D&C 101]]:77, "that every man may act… according to the moral agency which I have given unto him… that every man may be accountable for his own sins in the day of judgment." This connects the inspiration reading to [[America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration]].[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** Thomassen concedes others "can disagree" and "go full bore into the inspiration in the sense of divine," and that many Latter-day Saints will say "Bro, it's inspired." His reading is a *deflationary* one offered as a defensible option, not a proof. The *theopneustos* = "useful/life-giving" gloss is contestable: the dominant scholarly translation (e.g., B.B. Warfield) is the active "breathed-out by God," and "useful" partly leans on the *separate* clause "and is profitable" (ὠφέλιμος) in the same verse. The strength of the move is the negative point — the word does not *lexically* entail divine embodiment — more than the positive "useful" definition. See the parallel caution in [[Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible]].

## Best Quotes

> "The reason that we could maybe say that the Constitution is inspired is in the same sense of the theopneustos — that it is useful. That's how I'm going to interpret it… which doesn't require the entheos, that it is an embodiment of the divine will per se, but rather it is useful."[^6]

> "Why is it useful? Because of what it does. It creates the space from which men can then make free choices, from which they can then be judged."[^5]
