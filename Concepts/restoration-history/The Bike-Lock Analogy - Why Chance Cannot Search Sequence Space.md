# The Bike-Lock Analogy - Why Chance Cannot Search Sequence Space

## The Point
The Veritasium cartoon leans on "just so happens" — by pure chance the right shapes form, given enough shuffling.[^1] Meyer answers with a probability argument made vivid by a homely image: searching for a functional protein by chance is "like a great big bike lock with only one combination," and "you're turning, turning, turning dials." Even if every event in the history of the universe since the Big Bang (13.8 billion years ago) had been devoted to trying amino-acid combinations, all those random events "would have searched but a tiny fraction" of the possibilities for a single modest protein about 150 amino acids long. The ratio is roughly 1 functional arrangement per 10^77.[^2] So even granting a maximally favorable shuffling environment, chance has nowhere near enough trials.

## Evidence
1. **The cartoon's "just so happens."** The animation repeatedly relies on lucky coincidences — "just so happens and just so happens" — to bridge each step.[^1]
2. **The combinatorial space is astronomical.** "The information content of modest length proteins is so immense" that the whole history of the universe could explore only "a tiny fraction of the total combinatorial space associated with a single modest length protein of about 150 amino acids long." Meyer does the math in *Signature in the Cell*.[^2]
3. **The 1-in-10^77 ratio.** "It's about 1 over 10 to the 77th power possibilities for every one that will perform a biological function."[^3] (This is the same rarity treated, from the protein-engineering angle, in [[The Improbability of New Functional Proteins - Combinatorial Inflation]].)
4. **The bike-lock image.** "It's like having a huge bike lock, only one combination, and you're turning, turning, turning dials. Even 13.8 billion years is not enough time to have a realistic chance of finding the one or very few combinations that will perform biological functions."[^4]
5. **This is independent of the decay problem.** It holds "even without the James Tour consideration" that molecules degrade faster than they assemble — i.e., it assumes a *favorable* shuffling environment and chance still fails.[^5] See [[Time Is Not the Hero - Decay Outruns Assembly]].

## Best Quotes
> "It's like a great big bike lock with only one combination... you're turning turning turning dials. Even 13.8 billion years is not enough time to have a realistic chance of finding the one or very few combinations that will perform biological functions." [^4]

> "It's about 1 over 10 to the 77th power possibilities for every one that will perform a biological function." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The argument bears on the adequacy of *chance* as the mechanism for life's information, not on the age of the earth or the reality of natural law — on which Latter-day Saint thought takes no fixed stance. It supplies a natural-theology resource consonant with the LDS picture of a God who *organizes* eternal, uncreated matter through ordered processes: if the functional arrangements are too rare for chance to find, that points toward an organizing intelligence rather than toward creation *ex nihilo*. The inference lands on a generic designing mind, which an LDS reader can affirm while leaving the biology open. See [[Unlimited Chances Destroy Probability - The Royal Flush Objection]].
