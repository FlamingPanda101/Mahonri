# The Breeder Myth Was Invented to Explain Missing Bodies

## The Claim
The satanic "breeder" narrative — women secretly impregnated to produce unregistered babies for sacrifice — was a post-hoc patch generated to answer the panic's fatal evidence gap: if babies were being sacrificed, where were the birth certificates and bodies? The story arose to dodge the question, then collapsed under investigation.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. It answered a specific skeptical challenge (strong — causal)
"But what about the babies that were sacrificed? Where were the birth certificates? In response to this question, [[Lauren Stratford]] wrote her book *The Satanic Underground*, in which she claimed to have been used as a breeder." The babies "would never have had a birth certificate" — conveniently removing them from the documentary record [^1].

### 2. The pattern: stories mutate to evade falsification (strong — diagnostic)
"Breeders is an example of how the stories merely adjusted to dodge the skepticism, and also how the stories began to collapse under their own weight." As each checkable claim failed, a new unfalsifiable claim replaced it [^2].

### 3. Investigators found nothing (strong — [[Massimo Introvigne]])
Introvigne: "the local police and FBI, despite extensive investigations, have never found the slightest evidence of breeding practices having actually taken place." A 1993 *Christianity Today* article by two evangelical professors likewise reported "no evidence for the allegation of large-scale baby breeding … human sacrifice, and organized satanic conspiracies" [^3][^4].

### 4. The same move recurs across the panic (moderate — pattern)
The task-force chair's "complicit morticians" explanation for missing adult bodies is the structural twin of the breeder myth for missing infants: each invents an unverifiable mechanism to preserve belief against absent evidence. See [[Noemi Mattis Turned From Skeptic to True Believer on the Ritual Abuse Task Force]] [^5].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender could argue that genuinely clandestine crime *would* leave little documentary trace — unregistered births and concealed bodies are exactly what one expects from a competent conspiracy — so the absence of records is consistent with, not contrary to, real abuse. The rebuttal: a hypothesis that predicts the same observation (no evidence) whether it is true or false has no evidentiary content; and here the specific witness who personified the breeder claim (Stratford) was independently proven never to have been pregnant in the relevant years, while exhaustive police/FBI investigation turned up nothing. The myth is not merely unprovable but, in its concrete instances, falsified.
