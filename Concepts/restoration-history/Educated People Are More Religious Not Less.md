# Educated People Are More Religious Not Less

#type/claim

## The Claim
Against the anti-Mormon trope that *"you're stupid if you believe"* — that education predictably erodes faith — the hosts of [[Ward Radio]] argue the U.S. survey data shows the **opposite**: as educational attainment rises, the share of people who are irreligious *falls* and the share who attend services weekly *rises*.[^1] Reading political scientist [[Ryan Burge]]'s charts from graphsaboutreligion.com, [[Jonah Barnes]] reports that the "none" (atheist/agnostic/nothing-in-particular) share drops monotonically from **39% among those with no high school to 31% among those with postgraduate education**, and that the inverse weekly-attendance curve climbs with education.[^2] Crucially, the pattern is presented as *robust*: it persists when broken out by race (white, Black, Hispanic, Asian, all-others) and by age band (20s through 70s), and even holds inside the Church on an LDS-only chart of education versus attendance.[^3] The thesis: smart people "listen to data," and the data says the educated *graduate into* higher religiosity, not out of it.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The monotonic gradient on a large instrument (rated: moderate — real data, read secondhand).** Burge's aggregate bar graph shows irreligiosity declining across the full education ladder (39% → 34% → 36% → 33% → 33% → 31%), with the inverse attendance chart rising in step.[^2] Burge is a tier-A quantitative scholar of American religion using national-survey data, which blunts the "internet poll" dismissal — the same source-credibility point the vault makes in [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]].

2. **Robustness across subgroups is the signal (rated: strong, methodologically).** The trope-buster's strongest move is that the trend "persists" in every racial cut and every age decade. A spurious or noise-driven result would not reproduce a *consistent direction* across independent slices; a "boring," flat-everywhere replication is exactly the fingerprint of "a very, very strong and robust correlation," as Barnes puts it.[^3] This is the same fixed-effects-beat-noise logic the vault develops in [[Variance Between Groups Is Real Signal Rebuttal]]: shared bias washes out, structured cross-cutting patterns do not.

3. **The pattern localizes inside the tradition (rated: moderate).** Burge's LDS-only chart shows attendance climbing with years of education for both sexes (women out-attending men until the most-educated 17+ band).[^5] The finding is therefore not merely an artifact of comparing high-education-but-secular groups against low-education-but-devout ones across traditions — it holds *within* a single high-demand church. This dovetails with [[Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity]].

4. **A direct rebuttal to the "uneducated believer" caricature (rated: rhetorical).** The data supplies an empirical counter to the long-running smear that faith is for the unlettered — the modern cousin of the claim the vault treats in [[The Consensus of Joseph Smith's Contemporaries That He Was Uneducated]]. The sound-bite the hosts offer the listener: *"When you graduate — especially as a man with your juris doctorate — statistically you graduate into higher church attendance, not out of it."*[^4]

## The Steelman of the Secular Critic
A careful skeptic can grant every chart and still deny the trope is refuted — and the hosts hand him most of his ammunition:

- **It is correlation, by their own admission.** Barnes and the panel repeatedly concede the relationship is "just correlation," explicitly naming a plausible confounder: a *stable, married, two-parent family and good community* tends to produce **both** more education and more religiosity, so the education→religion link may be largely a spurious by-product of family background, not a causal effect of schooling.[^6] The episode's own causal gloss — that religion supplies the "diligence and persistence to finish post-graduate work" — is unsupported by the data and reverses just as easily.
- **"Religious" here is mostly *behavioral*, not cognitive.** The charts measure *affiliation* and *attendance*, which are heavily driven by community, marriage, and social capital — precisely the goods the well-educated accumulate. They do not measure literal *belief* in supernatural claims, so the data do not actually show that smarter people find theism more *credible*; a critic can hold that attendance rises with social integration while assent to specific doctrines does not.
- **Composition and selection effects.** A secular reader can note the gradient is shallow in aggregate (a 39→31 swing of eight points), that "some college" actually *bumps up* irreligiosity before it falls, and that the steep Black-respondent curve plausibly reflects the central social role of the historically Black church (a community/cultural variable), not an education-causes-belief mechanism.
- **The opposing literature exists.** The well-known finding the hosts themselves invoke — that more education tracks with more *liberal* politics, which tracks with *less* religiosity (see [[Politics Predicts Religious Disaffiliation]]) — runs the other way, and a fuller treatment would have to reconcile the two rather than display only the favorable chart. The hosts grant this is where "corruption in data or misinterpretation" is likeliest.
- **Caveat on sourcing.** Every figure is relayed off a screenshot; the underlying graphs are never opened on-air, so the exact percentages should be verified against Burge's published work before being cited as fact.

The defensible residue, even granting all of the above: the data are flatly *inconsistent* with the strong trope ("education reliably destroys faith") — which is the only claim the episode actually needs to defeat.
