# Physiological Anoxia Explanation of NDEs Rebutted

## The Point
The most frequently proposed physiological explanation is that, because the brain's oxygen supply is cut off during clinical death, the NDE must be "some sort of last compensatory gasp of the dying brain" [^1] — the so-called "dying brain" or anoxia/hypercarbia hypothesis. Moody's reply is short and empirical: a great many NDEs occur **before any such physiological stress takes place**, and in some cases there was no bodily injury at all — yet these uninjured subjects report every single element seen in cases of severe injury [^2]. If the same full experience appears with and without oxygen deprivation, oxygen deprivation cannot be its cause.

## Evidence
1. The hypothesis: "since the oxygen supply to the brain is cut off during clinical death and some other kinds of severe bodily stress, the phenomena perceived must represent some sort of last compensatory gasp of the dying brain" [^1].
2. Moody's rebuttal: "many of the near-death experiences happened before any physiological stress of the required type took place. Indeed, in a few cases there was no bodily injury at all during the encounter" [^2].
3. The clincher on uniformity: "every single element which appears in cases of severe injury can also be seen in other instances in which injury was not involved" [^2].
4. The argument parallels his rebuttal of the drug theory: the explanatory variable (anoxia, like medication) is simply absent in a large class of full-featured cases, so it cannot be the generating cause.

## Best Quotes
> "The main thing wrong with this hypothesis is simply this: As can easily be seen from a survey of the dying experiences reported earlier, many of the near-death experiences happened before any physiological stress of the required type took place." [^2]

> "Yet, every single element which appears in cases of severe injury can also be seen in other instances in which injury was not involved." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The "dying brain" theory is materialism's strongest card: consciousness is just a flicker of failing neurons. Moody's observation that the identical experience occurs in subjects under no physiological distress undercuts that reduction, leaving room for the spirit's genuine separation from the body taught in Restoration scripture (Alma 40; Alma 42:22–25). If the experience is not manufactured by a hypoxic brain, the natural reading — that something in the person actually departs the body — regains its plausibility.
