# Pre-existence Was a Mainstream Early-Christian Belief Later Purged

## The Claim

On [[Ward Radio]], [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Tikla]] argue that belief in a human **pre-existence** (premortal life of the soul) was held by major early-Christian thinkers and was only later "sterilized" out of Christian thought — and that this excision is "proof positive that a great apostasy happened." Cardon names [[Clement of Alexandria]], [[Justin Martyr]], and [[Origen]] as having "believed in a pre-existence that was basically Mormon doctrine," adding that some Fathers were "posthumously excommunicated... hundreds of years after" for it. He frames the loss as a "Greco-Roman sterilization of Christian thought" that betrays the religion's founders, and argues the doctrine is indispensable because it alone answers the questions of where we came from and why mortality (with its trials and its premortal "informed consent" to the plan) is meaningful.[^1] Tikla adds the irony that Latter-day Saints are "woefully undereducated about early Christianity" yet "overeducated about it accidentally through the restoration" — encountering ancient traditions (e.g., Adam's "coats of skins") that they already possess "new and revealed and restored."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Origen did teach a form of pre-existence — rate: strong (historical, with caveats)
[[Origen]] (c. 184–253) genuinely held a doctrine of the pre-existence of souls, and Origenist positions were anathematized in the 6th century (the controversies surrounding the Second Council of Constantinople, 553). This is the strongest leg of the claim and is uncontested history. *Caveat:* Origen's pre-existence is a Platonizing scheme (souls falling from a prior contemplative unity), not identical to the LDS premortal council; the parallel is real but not exact. See [[The Two Great Wrong Turns - Overthrow of Origen and Pelagius]].[^1]

### 2. The broader convergence is documented elsewhere — rate: moderate
The vault's [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]] records [[Terryl Givens]] and [[David Bentley Hart]] engaging premortal-existence themes, supporting the narrower point that the idea has ancient and reviving Christian currency rather than being a Mormon invention.[^1]

### 3. The "informed consent" payoff is internally coherent — rate: moderate (theological)
Cardon's argument that pre-existence supplies the *reason* for mortality — premortal agency and consent to a plan involving a real adversary — is a coherent theodicy move, matching the vault's [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability|premortal-consent]] material, though it is an argument *from* the doctrine's explanatory power, not independent evidence that it is ancient.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A historian-critic pushes back on three fronts. (a) **Attribution is loose.** Clement of Alexandria and Justin Martyr are far weaker cases than Origen; Cardon himself hedges ("I believe Justin Martyr"). Justin's anthropology is not clearly a premortal-soul doctrine, and lumping the three together overstates a consensus that did not exist. (b) **Even Origen's pre-existence is Platonic, not Restoration doctrine** — it serves a cyclical fall-and-return cosmology the LDS plan rejects, so its later condemnation is not straightforwardly the suppression of "basically Mormon doctrine." (c) **A single condemned strand is not a lost mainstream.** That Origenism was *debated and rejected* shows the early church engaging the question, not that premortality was the universal apostolic teaching later erased; the New Testament itself is, at best, ambiguous on human premortal existence. A measured Latter-day Saint reply: the apologetic does not need a universal consensus — only that a serious, respected pre-existence tradition existed and was driven out, which Origen alone substantiates; and the vault elsewhere candidly notes [[Premortality Is the One LDS Doctrine Mainstream Christianity Will Not Move Toward|premortality is the one doctrine the modern mainstream will not move toward]], so this is held as a genuine distinctive rather than an easy win.
