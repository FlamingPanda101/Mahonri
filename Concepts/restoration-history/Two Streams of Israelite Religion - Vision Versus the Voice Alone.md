# Two Streams of Israelite Religion - Vision Versus the Voice Alone

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] holds that a dividing line runs "right through" the Hebrew Bible: the **Deuteronomist** stream insists you *cannot see* the Lord and rejects incarnation, while the **first-temple / Isaiah** stream is built on the *vision* of God in human form. Christianity, she argues, is rooted in the visionary stream — so the "contradiction" atheists cite is really two theologies preserved side by side, and we can tell which one Christianity inherited.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Deuteronomist denial of vision (rated: textual).** [[Deuteronomy 4.12|Deuteronomy 4:12]]: at Horeb "you heard the sound of words, but you saw no form; there was only a voice." Barker: "for the Deuteronomists they're absolutely clear, you can't see the Lord," and "they didn't believe in what we would call incarnation."[^1]
2. **The visionary counter-witness (rated: textual).** Isaiah 6 and Ezekiel 1 see the Lord (or "the likeness of the glory of the Lord") in human form; the Beatitude "blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God" puts Jesus "into the first temple… not of the Deuteronomy-inspired second temple."[^2] The vault documents the same tension in [[Conflicting Traditions on Seeing Gods Face]] and [[Pilgrimage Commandment Was to See Gods Face]].
3. **The "age of wrath" and its reversal (rated: Barker's reading of *1 Enoch*).** Per *1 Enoch* 93, the turning point of Israel's history is not the Babylonian destruction (587) but the moment, just after Isaiah ascends, when "the people in the temple… lose their spiritual sight" and "forsake wisdom"; then begins the "age of wrath" in which the Lord hides his face. The New Testament reverses it — "we beheld his glory" (John 1), and on the last page [[Revelation 22.4|Revelation 22:4]] "they shall see his face."[^3][^4]
4. **The Deuteronomist suppression of secret knowledge (rated: textual).** Deut 29:29 ("the secret things belong to the Lord… but the things revealed," i.e. the law) and Deut 30:11–14 ("not in heaven… the word is very near you") forbid the ascent-to-heaven the older faith prized — yet John 3:31 presents Jesus as "he who comes from above," bearing witness to "what he has seen and heard."[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** A non-Barkerian scholar reads the same texts as a single tradition's internal diversity (anthropomorphic theophany alongside aniconic emphasis), not two rival cults, and notes that the "two streams" framing is Barker's interpretive construction. Critics also point out that Deuteronomy's "you saw no form" is plausibly an *anti-idolatry* polemic (no *image* of God), not a denial that God can be seen at all. The data (the texts genuinely diverge on seeing God) are not in dispute; the two-religions explanation is.

## Best Quotes
> "There is this dividing line that goes right through… one of the keynotes of the Deuteronomist is that you cannot see the Lord. They didn't believe in what we would call incarnation… Isaiah sees the Lord in human form… but the Deuteronomist said no, no, no, you just hear the words."[^1]

> "We know the pure in heart see God, from the Beatitudes. We know which stream Christianity is rooted in."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The visionary stream is the one the Restoration claims: an embodied God who is seen, prophets who ascend and receive sacred knowledge, and the beatific vision as the temple's goal (see [[Beatific Vision in the Israelite Temple]] and [[Pilgrimage Commandment Was to See Gods Face]]). It is also the theology the vault argues was edited out of the Messiah — [[The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah]] — and which the Book of Mormon, beginning with Lehi's throne-room vision, restores.
