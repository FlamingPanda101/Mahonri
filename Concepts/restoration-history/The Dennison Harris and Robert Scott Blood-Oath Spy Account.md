# The Dennison Harris and Robert Scott Blood-Oath Spy Account

## The Claim
Kimberly Watson Smith recounts a little-known episode: in spring 1844, two teenagers — **Dennison L. Harris** and **Robert Scott** — alerted [[Joseph Smith]] to a secret conspiracy to kill him and other leaders. At Joseph's direction the boys attended the secret meetings as spies. Before the third meeting Joseph warned them their blood might be shed and that if they had to die, to "die like martyrs." At the meeting, over 200 men and women took an oath on the Bible — each holding the Bible in his right hand while Brother Higbee asked him to swear "before God and all holy angels ... that you will give your life, your liberty, your influence and all for the destruction of Joseph Smith and his party." The boys refused the oath, were nearly murdered in a cellar, and were saved when a conspirator argued their fathers would come looking; they were then threatened at the Mississippi River, where Joseph was hidden listening.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **It coheres with the documented conspiracy (rated: moderate).** The named conspirators (the Higbees, [[William Law]], Wilson Law, Joseph H. Jackson) and the oath-bound character match Joseph's own "Conspiracies of Nauvoo" discourse, where he named men who had "received their endowments" and said William Law offered Jackson $500 to kill him — so the spy account is not free-floating; it dovetails with discourse and trial material. See [[The Martyrdom Was a Coordinated Political Assassination Not a Mob]].[^2]
2. **It illustrates the "secret combination" framing (rated: moderate, thematic).** The Bible-oath ritual to destroy a prophet maps onto the Book of Mormon's Gadianton "secret combination" motif — a theological resonance the panel draws out, and a reason the Saints viewed the plot as more than ordinary political opposition.[^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This is the **weakest-sourced strand** of the episode and should be flagged as such. The Harris/Scott narrative descends from a single late account: Dennison Harris reportedly told the story decades later (recorded c. 1881 by Horace Cummings), long after the events and after every other participant was dead — the classic [[The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims|late-reminiscence problem]]. The specific figures (200+ oath-takers, the cellar near-murder, Joseph hidden on the riverbank overhearing the threat) are uncorroborated by contemporary documents and have the shape of a dramatized faith-promoting tale. A fair reading: it *may* preserve a real core (there genuinely were oath-bound conspirators, per the trial and Joseph's discourse), but the cinematic details rest on one reminiscence and cannot bear the evidentiary weight the panel gives them. Treat it as devotional history, not as documented fact on par with the *Carthage Conspiracy* trial record.
