# Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Joshua HS argued the death penalty for man-stealing (which he cited as "Deuteronomy 21:16") proved the Bible repudiated kidnapping-based slavery: a buyer "found in possession" of a kidnapped person was also liable to death, so slave-masters had to ensure their slaves were not stolen.[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **Citation is wrong (rated: strong).** The verse is **[[Exodus 21.16|Exodus 21:16]]**, not Deuteronomy 21:16, though a parallel exists at [[Deuteronomy 24.7|Deuteronomy 24:7]].[^2]

2. **It does not make possessors liable (rated: strong; primary text).** The text reads "the stealer of a man, whether he has sold him or he has found in his hands, will be put to death." The clause modifies the **kidnapper** (whether he still holds the victim or has sold him), not a third-party buyer.[^2]

3. **It protects native Israelites only (rated: strong; primary text).** Exodus 21 (the Covenant Code) uses *ish* for a native Israelite man. The kidnapping prohibited is of freeborn Israelites. [[Deuteronomy 24.7|Deuteronomy 24:7]] makes this "quite explicit," naming "his brethren of the children of Israel."[^3] See [[Covenant Code Lexical Precision on Social Status]].

4. **Does nothing against the slave trade (rated: strong; analogy).** "That is no different from the illegality of kidnapping a white man in the pre–Civil War American South. That in no way undermined their practice of slavery."[^4]

5. **Steelman.** Joshua's intuition — that a law against man-stealing shows moral concern about coerced enslavement — is reasonable on its face. McClellan's rebuttal is that the law's scope is the in-group only, exactly paralleling slave societies that criminalized kidnapping free members of the dominant group while licensing the enslavement of out-group people.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Exodus 21:16 is widely agreed to be a prohibition on kidnapping freeborn Israelite men. This doesn't have anything to do with mitigating or prohibiting the slave trade."[^4]

> "That is no different from the illegality of kidnapping a white man in the pre–Civil War American South. That in no way, shape, or form whatsoever undermined their practice of slavery."[^4]
