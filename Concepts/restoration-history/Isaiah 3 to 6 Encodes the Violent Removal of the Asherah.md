# Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] reads [[Isaiah]] 3–6 as a single chiastic unit — crisis (3) → response (4) → crisis (5) → response (6) — that, beneath the King James surface, laments the **violent removal of the Asherah** (the tree-goddess / divine Mother) from the temple by the reforming party. Where [[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah]] makes a *dating* argument (the polemic against Asherah is late), this is a *literary* argument that the earliest Isaiah actually *defends* her and condemns her destroyers. It supplies the prophetic backdrop for [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Isaiah 5 opens with a pun on Asherah.** The first word of Isaiah 5:1 is *ashira*, "I will sing / let me sing" — "a yod off" from *Asherah*. In a verse "talking about a vineyard [a standard temple image]... and trees," Butler reads the opening word as a deliberate call-out to the goddess. [^1]
2. **Isaiah 6:13 ends with the goddess "returning."** The remnant "shall return and... be eaten as a *teil* tree and as an oak." Butler notes *elah* (rendered "teil/terebinth") "straight up means goddess" (Hebrew counterpart of Ugaritic *Athirat/Elat*), and "oak" (*allon/elon*) puns the high god *El*. So the unit that *starts* with *ashira* *ends* with "Asherah the goddess... will come back." [^2]
3. **The wicked drag out the "Tree Lady" — and Isaiah calls it rape.** In Isaiah 5:18-19 sinners "draw iniquity with cords... with a cart rope," crying "let him hasten his work... and let the **counsel** (*etsah*) of the Holy One of Israel draw nigh, that we may know it." Butler: *etsah* (counsel) puns *ets* (tree) and, with its feminine ending, can be read "the Tree Lady" or "her tree." Worse, "let her... come that we may know it" reuses the exact words of the men of [[Genesis 19.5|Sodom]] — "bring them out unto us, that we may **know** them" — so Isaiah depicts the dragging-down of the divine being as a **sexual assault / rape**. (Butler says reading this in Hebrew made him cry.) [^3]
4. **"Calling evil good" and moving light/dark and bitter/sweet = the reformers.** Isaiah 5:20's woes — "call evil good and good evil... put darkness for light... bitter for sweet" — Butler reads literally as cultic vandalism: moving the lamp (the menorah-tree of light) so the holy of holies that *should* be light becomes a dark cave; and "bitter for sweet" alludes to [[Exodus 15.23-25|Exodus 15]], where a tree makes bitter water sweet, so swapping sweet for bitter = *removing the tree* (see [[The Tree Sweetens the Bitter Waters So the River Is Both Love and Filth]]). [^4]
5. **The "daughters of Zion" and the seven-fold woman.** Isaiah 3's judgment on the bejeweled "daughters of Zion" Butler reads not as misogyny but as the apostasy of a **women's priesthood** (sometimes the "daughter of Zion" is a divine person / the Virgin on the holy mountain, Isaiah 37; 7:14). [[Isaiah 4.1|Isaiah 4:1]]'s "seven women shall take hold of one man" is then not polygamy but the **seven-branched menorah-tree-woman** — the recurring sevenfold woman (Hannah's "seven sons," Bathsheba "daughter of seven," Mary Magdalene's seven spirits) — perhaps a "last faithful core of the old priesthood of Asherah" who choose/anoint a Melchizedek priest (cf. Psalm 110). [^5]
6. **The "calling evil good" datum is the strongest hook, per the hosts.** [[Hayden Carroll|Hayden]] singles out Isaiah 5:20 as "the strongest case," matching the triumphalist praise of [[Hezekiah]] and [[Josiah]] for cutting down the Asherah ([[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]]) — reformers "doing good things... and Isaiah is mad at someone telling a big moral lie." Butler: "Can't prove it's Hezekiah, but I think so." [^6]

**Steelman.** Butler is emphatic these are "hypotheses... beyond what we can prove," "deep in the shadows," and that [[Mike Day|Mike Day]] is "very right" to flag them. A critic will say: standard commentary reads Isaiah 3 as judgment on real wickedness and Isaiah 5:1's "song" as a vineyard parable, not a goddess-pun; *etsah* means "counsel" and the Sodom echo may be coincidental; and a prophet who "made the people cry" by *defending* Asherah cuts against the canonical Isaiah who elsewhere mocks idols. **But** the reading's strength is the *converging chiastic frame* — a unit bracketed by *ashira* (5:1) and *elah*/Asherah-returns (6:13), with bread/clothing/fruit echoes across 3–4 — plus the verbatim Genesis 19 quotation, which is harder to dismiss than any single pun; and it coheres with the [[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah|late-dating]] of the anti-Asherah polemic.

## Best Quotes
> "The first word in this verse — 'Now I will sing' — that is *ashira*... it's a yod off from [Asherah]. How do you know it means that? Look at the context: it's talking about a vineyard, it's talking about trees, it's talking about a temple, and it starts out *ashira*." [^1]

> "[They're] dragging out something that is the Tree Lady of the Holy One of Israel... 'send her out that we may know her' — it's the same words the men of Sodom did when they wanted to rape the angels. Isaiah sees it as a sexual assault on a divine being. It is a rape." [^3]

> "'Woe unto them that call evil good and good evil... that put darkness for light.' They're moving the lamp... they think they're doing some kind of reform; what they're actually doing — good is evil." [^4]

## LDS Connection
If the earliest [[Isaiah]] *defends* the tree-goddess and brands her removers as rapists and liars, then [[Nephi]]'s preservation of the tree-of-life-as-divine-woman ([[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]]) is squarely Isaianic — explaining *why* Nephi quotes these very chapters and "likens" his people to Isaiah as an oppressed remnant guarding "the precious remembrance of the tree of life." It deepens [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]] and [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]] from a removal *reported* to a removal *protested* from within scripture.
