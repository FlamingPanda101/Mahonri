# Plural Marriage and the Law of Consecration Are the Same Law

## The Claim
[[Selene Anderson]] proposes that **plural marriage is the feminine counterpart of the law of consecration** — "the masculinized version of it" is consecration; the two are "basically the same law." Both are demanding ideals that the Church *covenants* toward without currently living their full economic/marital form, and (she argues) Latter-day Saints should extend to plural marriage the same relaxed acceptance they already extend to consecration ("we don't practice it now... but we believe in it").[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Both are bounded by justice, not appetite (rated: textual/interpretive).** Plural marriage carries built-in limits: [[Exodus 21.10|Exodus 21:10]] forbids diminishing the first wife's "food, raiment, and duty of marriage," so "if you can't deal justly with multiple women, do not marry multiple women." The caricature of unlimited wives is a misreading.[^2]

2. **It is structurally woman-driven (rated: the just-making condition).** In the sealing ceremony of the period "the first wife would give the hand of the new wife to the husband" — "the women doing the giving to a man." Anderson reads [[Doctrine and Covenants 132]]'s "if... given unto him" through *who does the giving*, and notes the section requires Emma to "administer... as Sarah administered the law unto Abraham," i.e. women administering the covenant. The best historical outcomes occurred when both the existing wife and the new wife consented (see [[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent]]).[^3]

3. **Both are "millennial" ideals held in abeyance (rated: the apologetic payoff).** Anderson grants it is "totally fair to criticize" whether either law can be lived optimally in this life or is millennium-only — but says that uncertainty should produce the *same* comfort Latter-day Saints feel about consecration, not anxiety. Rejecting plural marriage outright, by contrast, threatens the sealing theology (see [[Rejecting Polygamy Unravels Sealing and the Plan of Salvation]]).[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The analogy is imperfect in a morally decisive way: consecration asks people to surrender *property*; plural marriage asks (mostly) women to share a *spouse* — a difference in kind, not degree, with direct bearing on sexual autonomy and emotional welfare. One can affirm consecration as a pure ideal while holding that plural marriage's costs fell asymmetrically on women and produced documented suffering. "We don't live consecration now either" reassures about an economic ideal; it does not obviously transfer to an intimate one. The pairing is a suggestive theological intuition, not an established doctrine.

## Counter-Objection
A critic: "Calling polygamy 'the same law' as consecration smuggles its moral acceptability in by association." Anderson's reply is that the *form* is parallel — both are consecratory surrenders of self-interest under covenant, just-making only when fully consented — so the discomfort with one should be examined with the same tools as the (accepted) other, not exempted.

## Best Quotes
> "The law of consecration is in a sense a similar principle to plural marriage. ... It's the masculinized version of it."[^1]

> "If he take him another wife; her food, her raiment, and her duty of marriage, shall he not diminish. ... if you can't deal justly with multiple women do not marry multiple women."[^2]

> "We don't practice [consecration] now... even though we believe in it... I would love if we could feel the same sort of comfort with plural marriage because they are basically the same law."[^4]
