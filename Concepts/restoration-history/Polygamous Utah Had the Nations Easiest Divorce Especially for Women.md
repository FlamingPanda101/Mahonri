# Polygamous Utah Had the Nation's Easiest Divorce — Especially for Women

## The Claim
Against the image of plural wives trapped in marriages, [[Keystone]] argues that nineteenth-century *polygamous* Utah had unusually easy ecclesiastical divorce — easier than the rest of the United States, and especially easy when a *woman* initiated it. Plural marriage was hard on many people, but women were not powerless to leave.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The national contrast.** In the 19th-century United States divorce was "relatively difficult, especially if initiated by a woman." In polygamous Utah "the opposite was true. Getting an ecclesiastical divorce was relatively easy, especially if initiated by a woman."[^2] *Rating: strong — the comparative claim is the historical consensus and is the note's core point.*

2. **Brigham Young's own view.** [[Brigham Young]] held that a woman should "live with her husband as long as she could bear with him, and if her life became too burdensome, then leave and get a divorce." In an 1861 speech he said that "when a woman becomes alienated in her feelings and affections for her husband, it is then his duty to give her a bill of divorcement and set her free."[^3] *Rating: strong — direct quotation of the practice's chief authority.*

3. **A woman's right, with property attached.** [[George Q. Cannon]] (1879): "the liberty upon this point of divorce rests with the woman," and if she desires a separation "not only is the man bound to respect it, but he is bound also to give her and her offspring a proportionate share of his whole property."[^4] *Rating: strong — couples the right to leave with an economic settlement, unusual for the era.*

4. **The measurable outcome.** By 1870 "Utah likely had the highest divorce rate in the country." Even the leaders were not exempt: "[[Brigham Young]], [[Wilford Woodruff]], and [[Joseph F. Smith]] all had wives who divorced them."[^5] *Rating: strong — a behavioral outcome, not just stated policy; the leaders' own divorces show the right was exercised.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic notes that easy *ecclesiastical* divorce is not the whole story: a woman leaving a plural marriage still faced economic precarity, social stigma, and the loss of a sealing she may have believed was eternal; "highest divorce rate" can also be read as evidence of how much unhappiness plural marriage generated. Keystone itself grants that "polygamy in Utah wasn't butterflies and rainbows... it was understandably heart-wrenching for a lot of people, and mistakes were certainly made."

## Counter-objection
The note's claim is comparative and narrow: relative to contemporary American women, Utah women had *more* exit options, not perfect ones. Conceding that divorce remained painful does not undercut the documented fact that it was procedurally easier and financially protected here than elsewhere — which is precisely what refutes the "trapped powerless wife" image. The high divorce rate cuts both ways but is consistent with the thesis that women *could and did* leave. See [[Nineteenth-Century Utah Women Had More Rights Than Monogamous American Women]].
