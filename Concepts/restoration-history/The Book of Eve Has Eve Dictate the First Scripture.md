#type/claim

# The Book of Eve Has Eve Dictate the First Scripture

## The Claim
In several recensions of the *Life of Adam and Eve*, it is [[Eve]] — not [[Adam]] — who gathers the children as Adam is dying and recounts the Creation and Fall, dictating what amounts to "the first scripture." [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]] dubs this "the Book of Eve" and argues it carries two distinctively restoration-friendly features: (a) a woman delivering the foundational sacred narrative, and (b) an explicit, ingenious concern to *preserve the record* against destruction — a preoccupation the Bible lacks but the Book of Mormon shares.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Eve, not Adam, delivers the foundational narrative
As Adam dies, Seth asks why; Adam defers to Eve ("Eve, tell him"), and Eve gathers the family and recounts being in the garden, the Fall, and the loss of the tree of life dooming them to death. Barnes calls Eve "a really unsung heroine... the one who figures out the plan and gets the ball rolling." The hosts tie this to the *criterion of embarrassment* used for the Resurrection: an ancient text crediting a woman with first witness/first scripture is unexpected in a patriarchal milieu and so less likely to be a later invention.[^2][^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the female-delivered-narrative motif is genuinely present in the recensions; "first scripture" is Barnes's framing).

### 2. A dual-medium preservation contingency
The *Vita* (Barnes cites "chapter 15") has Eve instruct: "Make ye then tables of stone and others of clay, and write on them all my life and your father's... If by water the Lord judge our race, the tables of clay will be dissolved but the tables of stone will remain; and if by fire, the tables of stone will be broken up and the tables of clay will be baked hard." Barnes: a deliberate two-way "dead-man's switch" so that whether the world ends by flood or fire, one medium survives.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the quotation is specific and checkable; its evidentiary weight is the analogy, not a direct Joseph-Smith parallel).

### 3. Record-preservation is a Book of Mormon signature, not a biblical one
Barnes's interpretive payoff: "the Bible has no concern with preservation of documents... the Book of Mormon is obsessed with it" — Christ reprimanding the Nephites for omitting [[Samuel the Lamanite]]'s words, and the plates themselves as "a metallic contingency plan" against scribes who "destroy all the scrolls and change everything." A primeval text fixated on durable record-keeping is, he argues, "very Mormon."[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate as a thematic resonance; it is a vibe-level parallel, not a textual dependence.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The "Book of Eve" is legendary apocryphal expansion of uncertain date and provenance, and the stone/clay-tablet motif is a known ancient topos (Josephus reports the children of Seth inscribing astronomy on two pillars of brick and stone against fire and flood — *Antiquities* 1.2.3), so it need not indicate anything uniquely restored. Reading Eve's role through a modern lens ("unsung heroine," "you can't be what you can't see") is anachronistic, and the host repeatedly hedges ("this is just what the texts say... I don't know"). The thematic "very Mormon" link to Book-of-Mormon record-keeping is a resemblance, not evidence Joseph knew the text. The honest, narrower point: the Adam apocrypha do contain a tradition of Eve delivering the primal narrative and of deliberate record-preservation — features that *rhyme* with Latter-day Saint emphases on the divine feminine and on scripture as a guarded deposit — but the case is suggestive, tier-C, and not a demonstration.

The "Book of Eve" reinforces Latter-day Saint readings of [[Eve]] as a wise initiator of the plan rather than a culpable dupe (cf. [[Eve Completes Adam's Broken Chiasmus in Moses 5]]) and of the divine feminine generally.
