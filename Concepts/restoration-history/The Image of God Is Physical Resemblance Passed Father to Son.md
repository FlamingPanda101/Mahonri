# The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son

#type/claim

## The Claim

The *tselem* ("image") and *demut* ("likeness") of [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]] connote a genuine **physical, anthropomorphic resemblance** between humanity and God — not merely a moral, rational, or functional likeness. [[Stephen Smoot]] argues this is clinched by [[Genesis 5.18-24|Genesis 5:3]], where Adam fathers Seth "in his own likeness, after his image" (*bidmuto ketsalmo*) — the *identical* terms in reverse order. Because Seth shares Adam's image the way any son shares a father's physical form, the logic is generational: divine similitude is passed from God to humanity as a parent's likeness is passed to a child, implying "a continuity of nature and form" between God and humanity. This grounds the Latter-day Saint doctrine that the Father "has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's" ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]) and that humans are literally his offspring. This concept is **distinct** from the vault's [[Image of God - Patristic Interpretations|patristic-reception]] and general [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible|anthropomorphism]] notes: its load-bearing move is the specific Gen 1:26-27 → Gen 5:3 lexical-generational argument.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Lexical baseline.** *Tselem* occurs ~17× in the Hebrew Bible, most often for a cast or carved image, statue, or effigy; *demut* occurs ~25× for a form, shape, model, or visual resemblance — used vividly in Ezekiel's vision of "the *demut* of a man" enthroned in heaven, understood to be God. The plain semantic range is concrete and visual, not abstract. [^1] *(Strong: the words' ordinary usage is for physical likenesses.)*

2. **The Genesis 5:3 generational parallel.** The exact terms of 1:26-27 reappear in [[Genesis 5.18-24|Genesis 5:3]] to describe Adam begetting Seth "in his image and likeness." Smoot: "Divine similitude is passed down as a parent's image is passed to a child," suggesting "not only a relational bond between God and humanity, but also a continuity of nature and form." [^2] *(This internal-to-Genesis link is the strongest exegetical lever; cf. J. Kathleen McFarlane / Garr on inherited image.)*

3. **Cross-confessional scholarly concession.** Smoot cites three scholars from different traditions conceding a physical sense:
   - **Richard J. Clifford** (Catholic, Jesuit): "the most natural explanation … is that humans resemble God because they resemble heavenly beings who resemble God."
   - **[[Benjamin Sommer]]** (Jewish): the terms "pertain specifically to the physical contours of God" (from *The Bodies of God*).
   - **David Noel Freedman** (Protestant): "certainly the intention of Genesis 1:26 is to say that God and man share a common physical appearance." [^3] *(Strong: the convergence across Catholic, Jewish, and Protestant scholars blunts the charge that this is special-pleading LDS apologetics.)*

4. **Pervasive anthropomorphism elsewhere.** God has a face, hands, and feet throughout the Hebrew Bible (Francesca Stavrakopoulou, *God: An Anatomy*), at times appearing so humanly that he is mistaken for a man. These are "not isolated poetic devices" but a foundational biblical conception. (See [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]].) [^4]

5. **The royal/democratized nuance, not a replacement.** Smoot grants the *second* dimension (elevated status/vocation): in Egypt and Mesopotamia the king was "the image of God" by royal office; Genesis "democratizes" this so all humans are "royal images of God" called to rule and steward creation (Gen 1:26-28). Humans are "little gods and viceroys" sharing both God's nature *and* his vocation. He insists this is additive — the physical resemblance is not thereby erased. [^5]

6. **LDS payoff.** This converges with Joseph Smith: "God is a man like one of yourselves possessing the person, image, and very form of man," and [[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]: "The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's." (See [[King Follett Discourse]].) [^6]

## Steelman of the Traditional / Creedal Reading

The dominant creedal interpretation — that the image is *non*-physical (rationality, moral agency, dominion, relationality, or the soul) — is not arbitrary, and an honest treatment must grant its real strengths:

- **Patristic weight and theological motive.** Major fathers ([[Origen]], [[Augustine]]) explicitly denied the body could bear God's image, locating it in the reasoning soul; this flowed from a sincere reading of God as spirit ([[John 4.24|John 4:24]], "God is a spirit") and the prohibition of divine images (Exodus 20:4) — making a *bodily* imago look like the very idolatry the Torah forbids. (See [[Image of God - Patristic Interpretations]].)
- **The "likeness" softener.** Some traditions read *demut* as deliberately *qualifying* *tselem* — adding "after our likeness" precisely to block a crude statue-identity and signal mere analogy ("like, but not identical").
- **The functional/royal reading stands on its own.** A large body of ANE scholarship grounds the imago in the *royal-functional* role (image = representative/viceroy) without requiring physical resemblance at all — which is exactly the "dominion" half Smoot himself concedes.
- **Anthropomorphism as accommodation.** The creedal tradition can read God's "hands" and "face" as condescension to human language (Aquinas's analogical predication), so their pervasiveness need not entail literal corporeality.
- **Gen 5:3 cuts both ways.** A defender can say Adam passes to Seth a *fallen human* nature; the verse establishes that Seth is in Adam's image, but whether that warrants reading *God's* image as physical is an inference, not a statement of the text.

The genuinely contested point is therefore narrow: even granting the royal/functional sense, can one *exclude* the physical sense? Smoot's answer — and the cross-confessional citations — is that the lexicon and Gen 5:3 make exclusion untenable, whatever else the image also means.
