# Recovered Memory and Coercive Therapy Produce False Accusations

## The Claim
The Utah ritual-abuse cases demonstrate that suggestive, coercive "recovered memory" therapy can implant detailed false accusations — including memories of satanic cult initiation that never occurred.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Recovered memories" of satanic cults (strong)
[[Barbara Snow]] claimed Hadfield's children "had recovered memories of being initiated into satanic cults and compelled to worship Satan" — the recovered-memory paradigm applied to fantastical content [^1].

### 2. Children pressured into accusations they first denied (strong)
The Hadfield children "all initially denied the allegations but eventually succumbed to pressure from Dr. Snow." A child testified under oath she was "intimidated by Snow into offering an accusation"; another called the scandal a "fairy tale concocted to satisfy Snow" [^2].

### 3. Stories homogenized after therapy (strong — diagnostic of contamination)
Accounts that began "wild and fantastical" became "homogeneous" only after sustained therapy, and identical drawings appeared across unconnected cases — the fingerprint of suggestion, not memory. See [[Identical Cross-Case Allegations Point to the Interviewer Not the Crime]] [^3].

### 4. Independent expert verdict (strong)
Sociologist [[Anson Shupe]] concluded "the psychotherapists' conjecture was the source of it all" [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
Defenders of recovered-memory therapy argue that trauma can be dissociated and later genuinely recalled, and that blanket skepticism risks silencing real victims. The dissociation debate is real — but the content here (satanic cult initiation, infant sacrifice), the documented coercion, the recantations citing the interviewer, and the cross-case homogenization together fit fabrication far better than recovery. The broader scientific literature (Loftus and others) likewise establishes that suggestive interviewing can implant vivid false memories, especially in children.
