# Marcionites Practiced Proxy Baptism for the Unbaptized Dead

## The Point
Patristic sources report a concrete **proxy baptism** rite among the followers of [[Marcion]]: a living believer would lie *beneath* the deceased, answer the baptismal questions on their behalf, and receive the water as a stand-in. As described by [[John Chrysostom]] (and discussed by [[Jeffrey Trumbower]]), the rite was performed for **catechumens** — people who already had faith but died before completing baptism. This is the most explicit description of vicarious baptism mechanics in the early church, even though it was a group later deemed heretical.

## Evidence
1. **Tertullian's hint.** [[Tertullian]] "hints at the practice of vicarious baptism being a ritual carried out for family members by some who also honor their dead during the traditional Roman festivals, such as Parentalia" [^1].
2. **The Marcionite mechanism.** Chrysostom "describes the Marcionite practice being performed for uninitiated believers or catechumens who died before receiving baptism. A living Marcionite could act as proxy by laying underneath the deceased, answering the baptismal questions, and then receiving the rite by water" [^2].
3. **The faith contingency.** "This act was completed for those who already had faith and were simply not yet baptized. The contingency here was that the benefits of baptism were still reserved for those who had accepted the gospel of Jesus while they were alive" [^2].
4. **Heresy label is later.** The Marcionites were "deemed heretical by Tertullian and others some two centuries later" — i.e. the polemical framing postdates the practice [^2].

## Best Quotes
> "A living Marcionite could act as proxy by laying underneath the deceased, answering the baptismal questions, and then receiving the rite by water. This act was completed for those who already had faith and were simply not yet baptized." [^2]

> "Tertullian hints at the practice of vicarious baptism being a ritual carried out for family members by some who also honor their dead during the traditional Roman festivals, such as Parentalia." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The mechanics — a living proxy physically standing in for the dead in the baptismal rite — strikingly prefigure LDS temple baptism for the dead. The "already had faith" contingency is the key difference: the ancient rite required prior mortal faith, whereas the Restoration pairs proxy baptism with postmortem preaching of the gospel (D&C 138) so that the dead may *accept* the rite performed on their behalf.
