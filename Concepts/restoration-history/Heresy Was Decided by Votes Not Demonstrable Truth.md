# Heresy Was Decided by Votes Not Demonstrable Truth

## The Claim
What counted as "heresy" versus "orthodoxy" in the early church was frequently settled by **votes and political power**, not by a demonstration of which view was actually true. "Heresy a lot of times isn't really a matter of if it was true or not — it's just did they get the votes."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "I only need three votes" anecdote (rating: illustrative; Day's framing device)
Day recounts a city-council colleague who, mid-debate, said "Who cares [what's right]? I just need three votes, dude." He uses this to argue ecclesiastical councils worked similarly — adjudicating doctrine by majority and influence. [^1]

### 2. Catholic and evangelical scholars concede political decisions (rating: moderate; appeal to authority)
Day claims "there are Catholic scholars and there are evangelical scholars that agree... there were times when there was political decisions made" in determining orthodoxy. [^1]

### 3. Near-winners who lost the vote (rating: moderate; historical examples)
Marcionites may have outnumbered the orthodox at points; [[Valentinus]] was "very influential for a few decades and then lose out"; Arianism "almost came back" before later creeds cemented the opposite. Orthodoxy is the position that survived the contest, not self-evidently the true one. See [[Docetism and Early Christological Diversity]], [[The New Testament Canon Is a Reaction to Marcion]]. [^2]

### 4. Councils proceed by debate, not revelation (rating: moderate; per Donald McKim)
Day (via [[Donald McKim]]) stresses that what you *don't* find in the councils is "counseling of prayer, or seeking revelation, or prophetic instruction" — only "crisis, debate, and decision." Even Acts 15, the conciliar template, is "much disputing" before a verdict. See [[Nicene and Athanasian Creeds Apply Labels Not Explanations]]. [^3]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The traditional reply (Catholic and Orthodox) is that conciliar votes were the Spirit-guided means by which the church recognized — not invented — the truth (cf. Acts 15:28, "it seemed good to the Holy Ghost, and to us"). A vote can be both political *and* providentially correct; the survival of Nicene orthodoxy across radically different cultures is itself offered as evidence of its truth, not merely its political muscle. Day's counter: the explicit absence of prayer/revelation language in the council records, and the frank political horse-trading, undercut the "Spirit-guided recognition" account.

## Best Quotes

> "Heresy a lot of times isn't really a matter of if it was true or not. It's just did they get the votes?" [^1]

> "'Who cares? I just need three votes, dude.' And so... we just need to accept this." [^1]

> "One thing you read that you don't find in these councils is like counseling of prayer or seeking revelation or prophetic instruction." [^3]
