# The Priesthood Ban Was 1850s False Doctrine From Protestant Race Theory

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues the priesthood/temple restriction on people of Black African descent was a **practice** that the Church adopted in the 1850s, justified by a **false doctrine** (linking Black Africans to the curses of Cain and Ham) borrowed from **Protestant racial theory** of the age of exploration — not from scripture or the collective prophetic witness. The 1978 revelation corrected the practice *back* to its original state under Joseph Smith, and the Church has since disavowed the justifying doctrine. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The practice originated mid-century and reversed an earlier one.** "In the 1850s the church changed its original practice under Joseph Smith to allow blacks the priesthood. Then in 1978 that practice was changed back to the way things were originally under Joseph Smith." Black men *were* ordained under Joseph Smith (e.g., Elijah Abel). *Rating: strong; historically documented — ordinations predate the ban.* [^1]
2. **The justifying doctrine had no scriptural basis.** "No one in scripture who was proclaimed as cursed by God with blackness or dark skin were subsaharan Africans." The only Sub-Saharan Africans in scripture are Ethiopians, "welcomed eagerly into the fold" in Acts 8. *Rating: strong; the Cain/Ham-to-Sub-Saharan-Africa link is an interpretive overlay, not a scriptural statement.* [^2]
3. **The source was Protestant race theory, not revelation.** During "the age of exploration and the discovery of less developed civilizations, Protestant Christianity began asserting various racial theories, including the theory that subsaharan Africans were a lesser race because they were descendants of Cain and Ham. And early church members adopted this view as well." *Rating: strong; the "curse of Ham" justification for African slavery is well-attested in antebellum Protestant thought.* [^3]
4. **The Egyptian counter-example.** Critics cite Egypt's "curse of Ham," but "the Egyptians are not and were not subsaharan Africans, and the church never imposed a ban on people of Egyptian lineage" — showing the ban did not actually track the scriptural curse texts. *Rating: strong; internal inconsistency in the proof-texting.* [^4]
5. **The correction realigned with the collective witness.** "It was the collective witness that showed us that the priesthood ban was wrong" — a one-dispensation teaching with no cross-temporal support was corrected back toward the diachronic consensus. *Rating: strong within the model — see [[The Collective Prophetic Witness]].* [^5]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Two serious replies: (a) a *believing* critic notes that prophets and apostles for over a century *taught* the ban as God's will (Brigham Young especially), so calling it "false doctrine they adopted from Protestants" still concedes prophetic fallibility on a matter long defended as revelation — which is itself unsettling; (b) a *defender* of the ban argues God may have had purposes in permitting or instituting it (a view Hansen explicitly allows others to hold while declining it himself). Either way, "it was always just culture" can look like a retroactive rescue.

### Counter-objection
Hansen grants the fallibility point openly — "our prophets in one particular era were fallible and made a mistake and we had to make a correction... this is something we already believe." The model does not require prophetic infallibility, only that the *collective* witness across dispensations be the more reliable guide; a single-era error corrected back toward that witness is exactly what the [[Change Is Not the Same as Contradiction|change-not-contradiction]] framework predicts. Crucially, this is why the ban is **disanalogous** to a hypothetical change on marriage. See [[Gay Marriage Is Not Analogous to the Priesthood Ban or Polygamy]].

## Best Quotes
> "No one in scripture who is cursed was a subsaharan African. With the exception of the Ethiopians, there are essentially zero references to subsaharan Africans anywhere in the scriptures." [^2]

> "The church in the 1850s under the influence of the culture of that time adopted a bad interpretation of scripture which led to the adoption of false doctrine. And we know it's false because they adopted something that was out of line with the collective witness of God's prophets." [^3]
