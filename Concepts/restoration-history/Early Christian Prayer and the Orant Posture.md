## The Point
Prayer was a central early Christian practice, both private and communal. The most common and most-depicted posture was the *orant* pose: standing, with arms raised and hands open, eyes looking upward — "lifting up holy hands." What distinguished Christian prayer from that of neighbors was the way believers sought connection with God *through Jesus Christ as mediator*. The famous third-century *Donna Velata* fresco in Rome's Priscilla catacomb depicts a veiled woman in exactly this pose.

## Evidence
1. The orant posture reflects [[1 Timothy 2.8|1 Timothy 2:8]] ("lifting up holy hands"; cf. 1 Kings 8:22; Mark 11:25) and was one of the most popular forms of early Christian portraiture.
2. Prayer — "approaching God with empty, outstretched hands in reflection of a yearning heart and mind" — expressed the believer's faith and devotion.
3. Other biblical postures included kneeling (Luke 22:41; Acts 9:40; 20:36) and full prostration with the face to the ground (Matthew 26:39); the modern posture of folded hands originated only in the late tenth century.
4. Early Christians prayed facing east — the biblical location of Eden, the direction of the rising sun, and the direction from which Christ (the Light of the World) was believed to return.
5. Observant Jews recited the shema morning and evening and prayed three times a day (Daniel 6:10); the Didache encouraged thrice-daily prayer, and Tertullian encouraged prayer at even more frequent intervals.
6. In monastic settings, Christians developed the Liturgy of the Hours (Divine Office); Benedict of Nursia's Rule (AD 516) prescribed eight prayer hours (Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline, Midnight) to live the ideal "pray without ceasing" (1 Thessalonians 5:17).

## LDS Connection
Ellison cites Basil of Caesarea's vision of unceasing prayer as uniting oneself to God "through all the course of life," a devotional ideal that parallels Latter-day Saint emphasis on continual, sincere prayer. [^3]
