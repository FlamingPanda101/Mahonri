# The Pace Memo Names No Perpetrators Making Prosecution Impossible

## The Claim
Despite implicitly accusing more than 800 people of the gravest crimes, the Pace memo deliberately records no names, dates, or places — making any actual investigation or prosecution impossible and exposing the gap between the alleged horror and the author's passivity.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Pace refused to take perpetrators' names (strong — self-described)
Pace writes: "I go out of my way to not let the victims give me the names of their perpetrators. I told them my responsibility is to help them with spiritual healing and that the names of perpetrators should be given to therapists and law enforcement officers." Only community *positions* (patriarchs, stake presidents, Young Women's leaders, Tabernacle Choir members) are mentioned [^1].

### 2. Over 800 nameless people implicitly accused (strong)
"In all, over 800 nameless people were implicitly accused" — yet "no actual names are given," making "actual investigation and prosecution an impossibility" [^2].

### 3. No dates, no places (strong)
"Pace provided no dates, no places, no names. A serious investigation would have been referred to law enforcement or at least church discipline." The memo "suggests no action" [^3].

### 4. The passivity is incongruent with the alleged stakes (moderate — argument)
"Why would you not ask for names? If they are committing grievous sins, if they are endangering children, shouldn't that be the number one priority?" "The more serious Pace makes the abuse sound, the more absurd his passivity in his memo appears" — "something so evil should be excised without detour" [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender could argue Pace was acting within proper ecclesiastical limits: a welfare officer's role is spiritual healing, and steering victims to therapists and police for the criminal dimension is arguably *correct* boundary-keeping, not negligence — and victims may have been unwilling to name names. Granting that, the point stands on the evidentiary plane: a document that by design contains no checkable specifics cannot function, as critics use it, as proof that named institutions harbored a satanic abuse ring. Proper pastoral restraint and probative evidence are different things, and the memo has the former at the cost of the latter.
