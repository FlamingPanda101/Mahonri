# Messenger Shaliach Tradition Explains Bearing the Divine Name

## The Claim
In the ancient world a *shaliach* (Hebrew, "messenger / sent one") could speak in the first person *as* the king whose name and authority he bore. [[Blake Ostler]] argues this is the key to Jesus' divine-name and "I am" speech: Christ is the Father's sent agent who, bearing the divine name, can say "I am Yahweh" and "I am the first and the last" without being the Father — and without being merely a created angel. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The king's messenger speaks as the king (rating: strong; ANE documents)
Ostler: "a king who has a son... bears the royal name... so he can speak as if he were the king." He cites ancient documents where a messenger arrives announcing, in the king's own words, "Behold, I am the great Xerxes. There is no other god than me. I am the first and the last" — while being "just a messenger." [^2]

> "the messenger will show up and... 'Behold I am the great Xerxes. There is no other god than me. I am the first and the last.' And he's just a messenger." [^2]

### 2. Revelation shows the pattern explicitly (rating: strong; textual)
In Revelation an angel "shows up and claims, 'I am the first and the last. I am God,'" so John falls to worship — and the angel rebukes him: "I'm not God... I'm a fellow servant like you." This proves bearing divine claims does not make one God. See [[Revelation 22.8-9|Revelation 22:8-9]], [[The Angel in Revelation Who Refuses Worship]]. [^3]

### 3. The Angel of Yahweh (Malak Yahweh) tradition (rating: strong; textual)
"The angel appears... he begins saying 'I am Yahweh... I am the first and the last'... and we find out later in the text, it's just an angel. It's not really Yahweh at all. And it happens over and over again." This is "the thought world we're dealing with." [^4]

### 4. *Shaliach* and the consistent direction of sending (rating: strong; lexical/textual)
*Shaliach* is "Hebrew for messenger sent." Crucially, "it's clear that the father has sent the son. It is never ever stated that the son has sent the father" — a fixed asymmetry establishing the Father's primacy and the Son's agency. The gift of the name was "part of an adoption legal process... the greatest honor one can give." See [[Name-Bearing Agency in Exodus 23]]. [^5]

### 5. Revelation 1: the dead one bears the title (rating: moderate; exegetical)
At [[Revelation 1.8|Revelation 1]] the "Alpha and Omega" speaker is Yahweh, yet later "the first and the last" is "he who was dead." Ostler asks: when was Yahweh ever dead? The shared title is messenger-speech, and he rejects retreating to the hypostatic union since the text ascribes the title to the one who died. [^6]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
Trinitarians answer that the NT crosses a line no mere *shaliach* crosses: Jesus receives *worship* (which the Revelation angel forbids), is the *agent of creation* (a divine prerogative, not a herald's role), and is confessed "my Lord and my God" (John 20:28). On Bauckham's reading these mark inclusion in the divine identity, not delegated agency. Ostler's counter is that worship of the Son follows from the Father's exaltation and mutual indwelling, not from the Son being numerically identical to the Father. See [[Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling]] and [[John 20-28 - My Lord and My God (Divine Image)]].

## Best Quotes

> "maybe like a king who has a son who can bear his royal name when he goes out to the people to do the work of the king... he can speak as if he were the king." [^2]

> "He's the one who has sent. And it's clear that the father has sent the son. It is never ever stated that the son has sent the father." [^5]

> "when Christ is using the divine name for himself, he's not claiming to be the father... it's imperative to understand how these names are actually functioning." [^7]
