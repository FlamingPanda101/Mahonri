# King Follett Discourse

The most famous sermon of [[Joseph Smith]], delivered at the April 1844 general
conference in Nauvoo, Illinois, just months before his death. It was originally
a funeral sermon for [[King Follett]], a close associate who died in a well-digging
accident on March 9, 1844. The discourse became the most consequential sermon in
Mormon history — and one of the most controversial.

## The Claim

Joseph Smith used the occasion to deliver his most explicit and radical theological
teachings, fundamentally challenging Christian orthodoxy on the nature of God and
humanity. He collapsed the sacred distance between Creator and creature, teaching
that God was once a mortal man who progressed to godhood — and that humans can
follow the same path.[^1]

## Setting and Delivery

- Delivered outdoors west of the Nauvoo Temple construction site, in windy conditions.
  Smith had to tell the audience to "give profound attention" and spoke loudly over
  the wind.[^2]
- The sermon invoked Masonic imagery — advancement from "stage to stage," "station
  to station" — making the setting "seem more appropriate for a Masonic lodge."[^3]
- Smith addressed both the Follett family directly ("I'm trying to help you compensate
  for your loss") and challenged Christian orthodoxy explicitly, calling out
  "learned men and the doctors and lawyers of scripture," specifically citing
  [[Alexander Campbell]].[^4]
- Four contemporaneous scribes recorded it: [[Thomas Bullock]] (shorthand), [[William Clayton]]
  (longhand), [[Willard Richards]] (minutes-style), and [[Wilford Woodruff]] (diary).[^5]

## Immediate Reactions

The response was sharply polarized:
- Some believers called it the greatest sermon Smith ever preached. [[Joseph Fielding]]
  declared that nobody hearing it could conclude Smith was a fallen prophet.[^6]
- Dissenters like [[William Law]] saw it as evidence of heresy. The *Nauvoo Expositor*
  (published weeks later) included details from the sermon as proof Smith was
  teaching blasphemy.[^7]

## Enduring Significance

[[Harold Bloom]], a non-Mormon Yale literary critic, called the King Follett Discourse
"one of the truly remarkable sermons ever preached in America."[^8] [[Wilford Woodruff]]
recalled it decades later as "the most powerful spiritual oration he had ever
heard," citing it when he dedicated the Salt Lake Temple.[^9]
