# Secularized Founders Used Christian Language

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]] argues that the American founders — even the avowedly non-Christian ones — operated with "profoundly Christian ideas" recast in secular vocabulary, the paradigm of the "air we breathe."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **"Sacred and undeniable" → "self-evident."** [[Thomas Jefferson]]'s first draft read "we hold these truths to be sacred and undeniable"; [[Benjamin Franklin]] struck this for "self-evident." Scrivener: that edit *is* "the move to the air we breathe" — turning a supernaturally grounded claim into an unexamined assumption.[^1] *(Strong and well-documented historical detail; the rhetorical reading is apt.)*

2. **"Endowed by their Creator."** Jefferson "didn't edit out that we are endowed by our Creator with certain unalienable rights." Scrivener also claims Jefferson wrote "created" not "creator," with "creator" added later (by Congress) — a *re-*Christianizing edit.[^2] *(Accurate that the Declaration retains "Creator"; the precise editorial history Scrivener cites is a finer point.)*

3. **[[Thomas Paine]] echoing [[Micah 6.8|Micah 6:8]].** Paine — who wrote "thoroughly against Christianity" — summarized his philosophy as "act justly, love mercy, and work for human flourishing," the first half lifted from Micah 6 with a "humanistic gloss" replacing "walk humbly with thy God."[^3] *(Striking — a clear case of secular self-understanding using biblical cadence.)*

4. **Steelman of O'Connor — the founders saw themselves as working *contra* Christianity.** [[Thomas Jefferson|Jefferson]] held 600 slaves and was "famously anti-Christian"; Gouverneur Morris (who penned the Constitution's preamble) called it "the work of plain, honest men," denying any "hand of God"; [[Thomas Paine|Paine]] died in exile, his bones lost. O'Connor: "these men at least saw themselves as working contra" Christianity.[^4] Scrivener's reply: "When they say *rights*, they are showing us the intellectual genealogy" — psychological self-understanding doesn't override conceptual origin.[^5] *(Genuine standoff: O'Connor cites the actors' stated intentions; Scrivener cites the conceptual vocabulary they could not help but inherit. O'Connor charges this lets Scrivener claim better access to the founders' "psychological motivations than they do.")*

## Best Quotes

> "Franklin puts six lines through 'sacred and undeniable' and just says 'self-evident.' ... that move to the air we breathe."[^1]

> "Act justly, love mercy — that's Micah chapter 6 — and then he adds the humanistic gloss."[^3]

> "These men at least saw themselves as working contra... you could say you have a better grasp of their psychological motivations than they do." — O'Connor[^4]
