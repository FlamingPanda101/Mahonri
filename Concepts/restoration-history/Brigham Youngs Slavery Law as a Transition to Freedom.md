# Brigham Youngs Slavery Law as a Transition to Freedom (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The standard objection: [[Brigham Young]] "said all that stuff about slavery being ordained of God and made Utah a slave state" — therefore early Mormonism was pro-slavery.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The pro-slavery rhetoric was political cover to win statehood (rated: moderate, historical/interpretive).** [[Jacob Hansen]] argues Brigham's 1852 legalization of slavery in Utah was a deliberate strategy to "appease the slave states" and secure votes for Utah statehood. Quotes from earlier in Young's life show he had spoken *against* slavery; the inflammatory pro-slavery speeches were instrumental, not sincere conviction.[^2] See [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]].

2. **The law was structured to end slavery in one generation (rated: moderate, textual — but contested).** Hansen: the Utah law provided that "all of the children of the slaves would be born free," that slaves had to *consent* to come to Utah, receive proper treatment, compensation, and medical care, and would be freed for any mistreatment — effectively reducing slaves to "indentured servants for one generation." He calls it "a transition plan couched in pro-slavery language" and claims that had the U.S. adopted Brigham's model, it could have ended slavery without a civil war.[^3] See [[Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery]]. *Steelman:* this is a strong apologetic reading that historians do not uniformly endorse — the 1852 Act *did* legalize chattel-style servitude, Brigham Young *did* teach the curse of Cain and that slavery was divinely sanctioned, and "it was actually brilliant, I make no apologies" risks minimizing real harm. The defensible core is narrower: the law contained genuinely ameliorative provisions atypical of Southern slave codes; the grander "would have prevented the Civil War" claim is speculative.

3. **Native American slavery was acquired by coercion (rated: moderate, historical).** [[Cardon Ellis]] adds that much "slavery" among Utah Saints came from Indian slave-traders who would bring captive children and threaten to kill them if the Mormons refused to buy them — a buy-or-watch-them-die dilemma, not a slave market of choice.[^4] See [[Native American Slavery and the Buy-or-Watch-Them-Die Dilemma]].

## Best Quotes

> "It was a transition plan that he couched in pro-slavery language to actually end slavery by legalizing it for one generation and giving that generation protections under the law."[^3]

> "You can find quotes from earlier in his life when he said that he was against slavery. So what changed in that context?"[^2]

> "The Indians... would grab children and bring them to Mormons and say, 'You want to buy these kids?' ... 'If you don't buy them, we'll kill them.'"[^4]
