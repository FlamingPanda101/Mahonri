# NDE - Ineffability

## The Point
The single most uniform feature of the near-death experience (NDE) is that those who undergo it find it **ineffable** — literally inexpressible in ordinary language [^1]. Moody argues this is exactly what one would predict: human language is built on a shared community of experience, and the events of dying lie outside that common experience, so survivors lack adequate words [^1]. Far from undermining the reports, the recurring complaint "there are no words" is itself a striking commonality across otherwise independent accounts [^1].

## Evidence
1. Survivors "uniformly characterize their experiences as ineffable, that is, 'inexpressible'" [^1].
2. Typical remarks include "There are just no words to express what I am trying to say" and "They just don't make adjectives and superlatives to describe this" [^1].
3. One woman framed the difficulty geometrically: our world is three-dimensional but the next one "definitely isn't," so every word she knew was inadequate to the reality she had entered [^1].
4. The same inadequacy of language recurs as the chief obstacle when survivors try to describe the new "spiritual body" — "almost everyone... at some point became frustrated and said, 'I can't describe it'" [^2].

## Best Quotes
> "Now, there is a real problem for me as I'm trying to tell you this, because all the words I know are three-dimensional.... our world—the one we're living in now—is three-dimensional, but the next one definitely isn't. And that's why it's so hard to tell you this." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The ineffability of the next realm resonates with scriptural descriptions of post-mortal glory as beyond mortal comprehension and beyond what language can convey (cf. the things "which no man was permitted to utter," and glory "incomprehensible"). The persistent witness that consciousness enters a fuller, higher-dimensional mode of existence at death is consonant with the LDS conviction that the spirit world is a real place where the spirit lives on, awaiting fuller revelation (Alma 40; D&C 138).
