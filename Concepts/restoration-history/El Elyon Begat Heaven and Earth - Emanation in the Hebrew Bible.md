# El Elyon Begat Heaven and Earth - Emanation in the Hebrew Bible

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the *emanation* model of creation — the world proceeding *out of* God as his offspring — is not merely a Greek option but is already present in the Hebrew Bible, at [[Genesis 14.19|Genesis 14:19]]. There, in the Melchizedek–Abram blessing, *El Elyon* ("God Most High") is titled in the KJV "possessor of heaven and earth," while other translations read "**maker** of heaven and earth." Thomassen explains the divergence as two different Hebrew roots: the "possessor" rendering reflects *qanah* (קנה, "to buy / acquire / own" — God owns the earth in the sense that he *bought* it), while the "maker" rendering reflects a root meaning **to beget**. On that second reading, *El Elyon* "begat heaven and earth" — i.e. the cosmos *emanated from* God as his child.[^1]

This matters because it lands one of the two logically permissible creation models (organization vs. emanation — see [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]]) squarely *inside* the Old Testament: "there is the idea that the world emanated from God as his child." The Hebrew text therefore "mirrors" the Greek and other ANE systems — and, conspicuously, leaves no room for creation from nothing.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The translation split traces to two roots
Genesis 14:19 in the KJV blesses Abram by "the most high God, possessor of heaven and earth"; other versions read "maker." Thomassen: "it's two different words with the same roots." The "possessor" word is *qanah*, "which means actually to buy in modern Hebrew... God owns earth in the sense that he bought it." The alternative root behind "maker" "actually means to beget" — so "the translations that say El Elyon is the maker of heaven and earth, what they're really translating is that he *begat* heaven and earth."[^1]

### 2. This is the emanation model in Hebrew dress
The point is offered as direct confirmation of the two-model framework: "this is apropos of your slide that says, look, there's two ways — either you can organize or it can emanate from a being." Genesis 14:19, on the "beget" reading, is the *emanation* horn: "there is the idea that the world emanated from God as his child."[^2]

### 3. The Hebrew thereby mirrors surrounding traditions — and still excludes ex nihilo
With organization established from Genesis 1 (*bara*, *tohu wa-bohu*; see [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]) and emanation surfacing in Genesis 14, "the Greek modeling fits — it mirrors with modeling from other systems" across the ancient Near East. Both biblical strands are *something-from-something*; neither is creation from nothing. (This dovetails with the vault's [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son]], where divine "begetting" language is read literally rather than as bare metaphor.)[^2]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A traditional Hebraist would push back hard on the lexicography. The participle in Genesis 14:19/22 is *qoneh* (קֹנֵה), from *qanah* — and the relevant ancient sense is not "buy" but "**create / bring forth**," as in the Ugaritic title of the mother-goddess Asherah, *qaniyatu ʾilima* ("creatress of the gods"), and as most lexicons gloss the verb in this cultic context. "Possessor / maker / creator" are thus all renderings of the *same* root *qanah*; there is no separate "begetting" root in the verse. So the "emanation" reading rests on a root-confusion. Moreover, even if *qanah* can shade toward "beget," Hebrew routinely uses parental/birth imagery for God's relation to *Israel* and to *kings* (Deuteronomy 32; Psalm 2:7) **metaphorically**, without implying that the physical cosmos literally proceeded out of God's substance. To build an emanationist cosmology on one ambiguous participle over-reads a poetic blessing.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **Even the "create" gloss kills ex nihilo.** The strongest scholarly reading of *qanah* here — "creator / bringer-forth," paralleling Asherah *qaniyatu* — is itself a *production/emanation* word drawn from the same birth-and-craft semantic field, not a "make from nothing" word. Whichever way the participle is taken (own, fashion, beget, bring forth), it presupposes the surrounding ANE pattern of forming/birthing from a prior reality, which is exactly the claim being defended.
2. **The metaphor objection still concedes a model the tradition denies.** Whether the begetting is "literal" or "metaphorical," Genesis 14 frames God's relation to heaven and earth in *generative* terms — and *generation* (emanation) is one of the two Greek-permissible models. *Ex nihilo* is neither generation nor organization; it is the option that has *no* biblical foothold, literal or figurative.
3. **The argument doesn't stand alone.** It is one data point alongside the organization reading of Genesis 1; its job is only to show emanation is *also* native to the text, reinforcing that the Hebrew Bible operates entirely within the something-from-something space (see [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]]).
