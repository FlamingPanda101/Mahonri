# Brigham Young Was Not the Autocrat of Utah

## The Point
The caricature is that [[Brigham Young]] was an iron-fisted, humorless theocratic tyrant — "the führer of the Great Basin," "Cowboy Hitler" — with total control over a little Utah theocracy, where nothing happened without his decree. Dr. [[Daniel C. Peterson]] answers on two fronts: (1) Brigham was far wittier and kindlier than the meme allows, and (2) his will was demonstrably *not* absolute, even on Church-owned buildings and in the territorial legislature.

## Evidence
- **He had a real sense of humor and was widely loved.** People called him "Brother Brigham"; he was "approachable," "kindly," and "blunt-spoken," and admitted his own tongue "was the most unruly member." When a woman complained her husband had told her to "go to hell," Brigham deadpanned, "Well, don't go." When a young, needling Mark Twain visited, Brigham ignored him, then at the end patted him on the head: "Cute child you have. Is it a boy or is it a girl?"[^1]
- **His will was overruled on his own temple and tabernacle.** Brigham hated the squat tower of the original St. George Temple and wanted it changed — but "he didn't get his wish"; only after he died was a taller tower built (the St. George joke: "Well, he's arrived"). At the St. George Tabernacle, Brigham and architect Miles Romney could not agree on the pulpit height, so they reached a *compromise* by which one must climb the stairs and then step back down to the pulpit. "Brigham Young, the tyrant, the autocrat, did not get his wish there. And this is on a church-owned building."[^2]
- **The territorial legislature genuinely debated and constrained him.** New research on the early territorial legislature shows that "at no point does Brigham Young just unilaterally step in as the dictator of Utah." They went "through the democratic process as an elected legislature," with real arguments. On the 1852 slavery statute (documented in Paul Reeve and W. Jean Garrard's *This Abominable Slavery*), Orson Pratt *opposed* the bill, others wanted stronger pro-slavery rules, Brigham wanted a middle ground — and the outcome came "not by simply issuing a fiat and sheer force of will."[^3]
- **Communication itself made micromanagement impossible.** "The communications in Utah weren't that good" — it was a big territory and horseback was the fastest travel. The notion that "everything in Utah happened with his direct supervision" is physically implausible.[^4] (This same fact is decisive for [[Brigham Young Did Not Order the Mountain Meadows Massacre]].)

## Best Quotes
> "He's the führer of the Great Basin... I call him Cowboy Hitler... So, what can you say about that? / Well, first of all, I don't think he's as grim and humorless as many people think him to be." [^1]

> "Brigham Young, the tyrant, the autocrat, did not get his wish there. And this is on a church-owned building." [^2]

> "At no point does Brigham Young just unilaterally step in as the dictator of Utah and say this is how we're going to do it... it's not the sense of the führer steps up to the Reichstag and just declares what's going to happen." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The "autocrat" meme is one of the load-bearing distortions behind the harsher charges against Brigham: if he controlled everything, then he must own every bad thing that happened in Utah. Dismantling it both humanizes the prophet and removes the premise for blaming him for events like Mountain Meadows. The print record that fuels the meme is itself unreliable — see [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]] — and the kindlier man recovered here matches the pastoral Brigham of the primary sources.
