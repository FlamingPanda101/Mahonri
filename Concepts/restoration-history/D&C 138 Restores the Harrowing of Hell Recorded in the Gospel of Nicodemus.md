# D&C 138 Restores the Harrowing of Hell Recorded in the Gospel of Nicodemus

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]] argue that **[[D&C 138]]** — [[Joseph F. Smith]]'s 1918 vision of the redemption of the dead — restores the ancient, once-universal doctrine of the **harrowing of hell** (Christ's descent to the spirit world to preach to and free the dead), and that it is the "**fullness of the record of John**" promised in **[[D&C 93]]**. They identify that "John" as **John the Baptist**, the forerunner who crossed the veil before Christ to prepare the way among the dead, and they point to a parallel table (by LDS apologist [[Brett McDonald]]) setting D&C 138 beside the apocryphal **Gospel of Nicodemus**, which shows the same gathered prophets and the same descent scene.[^1][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The descent is genuinely well-attested in antiquity — rate: strong (already documented in the vault)
[[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3:18-20]] ("preached unto the spirits in prison") and [[1 Peter 4.6|1 Peter 4:6]] anchor the doctrine in the New Testament; it recurs in the Apostles' Creed ("he descended into hell"), the Gospel of Nicodemus / Acts of Pilate, the Armenian "History of John," and patristic and liturgical sources. The vault's [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested]] (from a Maxwell Institute volume) independently establishes that the harrowing was "ubiquitous from 1 Peter to the end of the fourth century." *Strong: the antiquity and breadth of the descent tradition is not in dispute.*[^3]

### 2. D&C 138 and the Gospel of Nicodemus share scene and roster — rate: striking parallel, partisan table
[[Brett McDonald]]'s "A Document Without Parallel" lines up D&C 138 with the Gospel of Nicodemus: both open with the righteous dead gathered "in one place," list named patriarchs (Adam, Eve, Abel, Noah, Shem, Abraham, Isaac…), and narrate Christ's arrival and the dead rejoicing/singing. D&C 138:12 ("an innumerable company of the spirits of the just") is set beside Nicodemus's "multitude of the saints… gathered into one place." *Moderate-to-strong as a parallel; the table is apologetic, but the structural overlap (gathered just dead → Christ descends → liberation) is real.*[^4]

### 3. "The record of John" reading gives D&C 93's cryptic promise a referent — rate: plausible but interpretive
[[D&C 93]] promises the faithful "the fullness of the record of John." Barnes argues John the Baptist uniquely "bridged the veil" (knew Christ in mortality and went before him into the spirit world), so D&C 138 — a vision of that spirit-world ministry — *is* that fullness; he notes the Armenian harrowing text is literally titled the "History of John." *Weak-to-moderate: an attractive harmonization, but "the record of John" is traditionally read as John the Evangelist/Beloved (cf. D&C 93:6-18, which quotes John 1), so the identification is contested even within LDS interpretation.*[^1][^2]

### 4. The loss is explained by creedal anthropology — rate: thematic
The hosts attribute the doctrine's eclipse to the Greco-Roman/Platonic devaluation of the body and an over-transcendent God who could not descend — the same impulse behind the hypostatic union. *Moderate as a historical-theological narrative; it is the vault's standard "great apostasy via hellenization" frame.*[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A careful critic grants the descent's antiquity but presses the specifically LDS moves. (a) **The Gospel of Nicodemus is late.** Its *Descensus* sections are a 4th-5th-century Christian composition; a 1918 revelation resembling it shows continuity with *Christian tradition*, not necessarily restoration of a lost *first-century* text — and since the descent was always in the Apostles' Creed and 1 Peter, no "restoration" of a *forgotten* doctrine is required (mainstream Christianity never lost the *descensus*; it is in the Creed every Catholic and many Protestants recite). (b) **The "record of John = John the Baptist = D&C 138" chain is a triple interpretive leap**; the canonical context of [[D&C 93]] points to John the Evangelist, and equating the promised record with Section 138 is post hoc. (c) **The parallel roster proves little** — any descent narrative will gather the famous Old Testament dead in genealogical order, so shared names are expected, not evidential. (d) **The "no hell, only spirit prison" framing** (see [[Hell Is Not in the Bible It Is the Spirit World Awaiting Judgment]]) overstates a complex NT picture (Luke 16's chasm; Gehenna sayings). The defensible core is solid and ecumenically interesting: D&C 138 articulates, in detail, an intermediate-state postmortem mission that the earliest church *did* teach (Christ-as-preacher to the dead, even baptism for the dead in the Shepherd of Hermas), so the Restoration's spirit-world theology has real ancient pedigree (cf. [[Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection]]) — the overreach is the precise "fullness of the record of John" identification and the implication that the descent was wholly *lost*.

## LDS Connection
A popular-level companion to the vault's scholarly [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested]]: where that note (from *Ancient Christians*) documents the breadth of the *descensus*, this one ties it to the distinctly LDS texts ([[D&C 138]], [[D&C 93]]) and to [[Brett McDonald]]'s comparison table, and pairs with [[Hell Is Not in the Bible It Is the Spirit World Awaiting Judgment]]. Its strongest contribution is reframing Saturday (Holy Saturday) as the day of spirit-world preaching and temple/genealogy work. Its weakest link is the "record of John = Section 138" identification, which even sympathetic readers may not grant; and, as with the other apocrypha cases, the force depends on [[Dating an Idea Differs From Dating Its Manuscript|how one dates the idea versus the manuscript]].
