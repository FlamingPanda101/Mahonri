# Calvinism Is the Logical Conclusion of Classical Theism

## The Claim
The panel argues that Calvinism is simply classical/perfect-being theism followed to its honest end. [[John Calvin]] — "a lawyer, very logical thinker" — saw the implications: if God is all-powerful, sovereign, omniscient, and creates from nothing, He chooses who is saved and who is damned. Calvinists "bite the bullet" and accept it; the non-Calvinist classical theist holds the *same* attributes but recoils. [[Luke Hansen]] says: "the fact that we have the Calvinism versus Arminian debate, I think proves that the creeds got it wrong, yet they all affirm the creeds."[^1] The most fruitful conversation, Carroll says, is therefore with the *non*-Calvinist classical theist — show them that to keep the God of classical theism, "you kind of actually have to be a Calvinist."[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The Calvinist-Arminian impasse as evidence of a defective doctrine of God — rate: suggestive, not decisive.** The panel treats the centuries-old, unresolved split as a symptom: both sides affirm the creeds, yet derive opposite soteriologies, and the Bible itself supplies prooftexts both ways (Calvinist election texts vs. [[1 Timothy 2.4|"God desires all to be saved"]]).[^3] **Steelman of the classical theist:** a live theological dispute does not prove the shared premises are false — Christology, Trinity, and free will are all perennially debated without that disproving the underlying doctrines. The Arminian/Molinist holds a *coherent* third option (libertarian freedom plus exhaustive foreknowledge) that the panel concedes exists; they merely judge it unstable (see [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]]).
2. **Calvinism is presented as the *consistent* position — rate: a notable rhetorical concession.** [[Cardon Ellis]] notes the panel "respects" Reformed theology for honesty: "at least they're admitting the logical conclusion of their ideas and embracing the darkness."[^4] This is a steelman of the opponent's strongest form — and it sharpens the dilemma, because the soft middle (Arminian) is portrayed as inconsistent and the consistent end (Calvinist TULIP, limited atonement: "if you don't believe that Jesus didn't die for everyone, you're not reformed") as morally unacceptable.[^5] See [[Calvinism Consistency with Atheism on Free Will]].
3. **Authority/sourcing of the Calvinist position — rate: fairly handled.** Ellis asks how they know this is *actually* what Calvinists believe (wary of strawmanning, as anti-Mormons strawman LDS). Hansen answers correctly that Protestantism has no central magisterium, but the **Westminster Confession** and **London Baptist Confession** are the widely held standards, and self-identified "reformed" means accepting **TULIP** / the five points.[^6] He honestly notes Calvinists are likely a minority of evangelicals, most of whom simply "don't think about the implications of their perfect being theology." See [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].
4. **One overstatement flagged — rate: a real distinction missed then recovered.** Ellis correctly pushes back that Calvinists go *further* than the dilemma requires: they typically reject libertarian free will even in this life (God grants faith), whereas the bare three-facts argument only forces predestination of *final outcomes*. Hansen concedes the outcome is identical "no matter how you cut it."[^7]
