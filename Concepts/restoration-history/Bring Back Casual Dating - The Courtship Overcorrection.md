# Bring Back Casual Dating - The Courtship Overcorrection

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] argues that conservative Christian culture, reacting against the hookup culture of the 1980s–2000s, **over-corrected into a "courtship" model that suppressed ordinary dating** — and that this is now a major driver of the marriage decline among exactly the young people who most want to marry. "Dating is a skill. Dating well is a skill. And nobody has practiced."[^1]

Her diagnosis: after the church "saw the dangers of the hookup culture… the casual sex… the anonymous sex," it said "no more dating — courtship. You're not going to go on a date until you're ready to get married."[^2] The unintended message became: "I should not ask a girl on a date… unless I think she's marriage material," and a girl "won't say yes to a guy unless she thinks, 'Would I marry him?'" The result is that young adults "knock tons of people out of the running" before a first coffee. Her prescription is a return to low-stakes practice: "Bring back casual dating, because you need practice and you need a lot of face-to-face contact." Concretely — "Girls, if a guy is brave enough to say, 'Do you want to go out for coffee?'… don't plan your whole wedding in the middle of that coffee date. And don't say no to him if you can't see yourself walking down the aisle. Just say yes… reward his courage."[^3]

She layers on a second culprit: **dating apps**, which "elevate the hotties" — "the hottest girls and the hottest guys get the most attention," while "89% of people on the dating apps who are gems… just get swiped through" because they are not "a 10," when "you need to be looking for worldview alignment and character, and you can't see that on an app."[^4] Even in-person church or social events skew toward the outgoing and funny, leaving "quieter guys who are incredible quality" off the radar. Her structural fix: a **matchmaker in every church** — "an older married woman" — pairing people who have "worldview alignment, good character… and are willing to go on some dates." She has started a recommendation-only matchmaking service herself.[^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The courtship-overcorrection critique names a real cultural episode (rate: moderate, real phenomenon).** The 1990s–2000s "courtship/purity" movement in conservative evangelicalism (e.g., the *I Kissed Dating Goodbye* era) did discourage casual dating in favor of marriage-intentioned courtship, and a visible wave of later reflection — including by figures once central to it — has argued it produced anxiety and delayed relationship formation. Faust's "won't ask unless she's marriage material" mechanism is a fair characterization of a documented dynamic.[^2]
2. **The "dating as a skill" point aligns with developmental common sense (rate: moderate).** That relational competence is built through low-stakes practice and repeated face-to-face interaction is intuitively plausible and consistent with how other social skills develop; treating every first date as a marriage audition plausibly raises the stakes past the point where practice can occur.[^1]
3. **The app critique tracks known dynamics (rate: moderate-to-strong).** The observation that swipe-based apps concentrate attention on a small number of highly-rated profiles (a skewed attention distribution) is consistent with widely reported patterns in online-dating behavior, and the point that character and worldview are hard to assess from a profile is well-taken.[^4]

### Steelman of the alternative view

A fair pushback: (a) **Casual dating carries its own risks the courtship movement was right to fear.** "Go on lots of casual dates" can re-open doors to emotional churn, sexual pressure, and the very hookup dynamics the courtship model arose to resist; defenders of intentional dating would say the answer is *purposeful* dating with boundaries, not a return to volume-dating. (b) **"Reward his courage / just say yes" can cut against women's discernment.** A norm that women should say yes to most invitations to "reward courage" could pressure women into unwanted dates and underrate the legitimacy of an early no; the consent-and-comfort framework would resist making acceptance a quasi-obligation. (c) **The matchmaker model has trade-offs.** Outsourcing pairing to "an older married woman" embeds particular gendered and communal assumptions and may not transfer outside tight-knit religious communities; it also concentrates significant influence over others' romantic lives in a single gatekeeper. (d) **Structural drivers may dominate culture.** Faust herself elsewhere concedes economic factors (housing, cost of family formation); declining marriage may owe more to those and to shifting values than to dating *technique*, so "bring back casual dating" may be necessary-but-insufficient. Faust's reply is that she is *not* endorsing recreational hookup-dating but low-stakes, character-oriented practice with face-to-face contact, and that a trusted community matchmaker is precisely a safeguard *against* the app-driven superficiality she critiques.

## LDS Relevance

This is the most *practical* and least doctrinally freighted argument in the episode, and it maps onto live concerns in Latter-day Saint culture, where leaders have publicly worried about delayed marriage and have explicitly encouraged *casual, low-pressure dating* over premature pairing-off or non-committal "hanging out." (Elder Dallin H. Oaks's widely circulated counsel to young adults distinguished group "hanging out" from real dating and urged simple, inexpensive, get-to-know-you dates — the same corrective Faust urges.) The argument coheres with [[The Law of Chastity as a Family-Stability Engine]]'s positive framing ("get married, be faithful, have children, in that order") by addressing the *upstream* bottleneck: people cannot marry well if the culture has made the first date too high-stakes to attempt. The matchmaker-in-every-congregation idea has a natural analogue in the ward as a marriage-supporting community (cf. [[The Church as the New Family and Household of God]]). For a Latter-day Saint, the takeaway is that defending marriage requires not only *teaching* its value but rebuilding the ordinary on-ramps — practiced dating — that make it reachable.
