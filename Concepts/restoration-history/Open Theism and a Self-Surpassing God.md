# Open Theism and a Self-Surpassing God

## The Point
Classical theism holds God to be timeless and immutable — outside time, incapable of change, "the fairest and the best that is conceivable" who could "only change for the worse."[^1] [[Brian Thomassen]] argues that 20th-century process theology and open theism broke with this and moved toward [[Joseph Smith]]'s picture of a God who is "eternally self-surpassing" and acts genuinely within time. Joseph's Moses 1 has God declare "there is no end to my works, neither my words... for my works are without end" — an open, ongoing, expanding creation rather than a finished, static perfection.[^2]

## Evidence
- **The classical divergence — the "big guns."** Thomassen lines up the tradition deliberately to show continuity: Plato (a perfect God who, if he changed, "can only change for the worse"), [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]] ("nothing future to God"), [[Thomas Aquinas]] (God supremely immutable and therefore eternal, outside time), and [[John Calvin]] (to God "nothing is future or past, but all things are present").[^1][^3]
- **The process-theology convergence.** Against that lineage he sets three thinkers: William James ("the world is still in the making and God himself may be growing"; God "works in an external environment, has limits, and has enemies"); Alfred North Whitehead, "the founder of process theology" ("it is as true to say that God creates the world as that the world creates God"); and Charles Hartshorne ("God is not surpassed by any other being yet he is self-surpassing," *The Divine Relativity*).[^4][^5][^6]
- **Academy first, pulpit later.** Thomassen concedes these are philosophers, not all confessionally Christian, but argues the academic shift "naturally feeds into the religious space," so "you'll start seeing pastors and other ministers that will start speaking in these terms" — "whoever controls education controls the future."[^7]
- **Why a God in time is required.** The hosts argue an eternally self-surpassing God *must* be temporal: "if Joseph is right and he is eternally self-surpassing, he has to exist in time," and creation itself "by definition is a process," which a strictly timeless God cannot perform. They tie this to a critique of the timeless-God idea as a "word game" — defining a being into existence rather than describing a real, acting one who "has acted in time."[^8][^9]

## Best Quotes
> "The world is still in the making and God himself may be growing... God is real since he produces real effects. He works in an external environment, has limits, and has enemies." — William James [^4]

> "It is as true to say that God creates the world as that the world creates God." — Alfred North Whitehead [^5]

> "God is not surpassed by any other being, yet he is self-surpassing." — Charles Hartshorne, *The Divine Relativity* [^6]

## LDS Connection
A self-surpassing, temporally-active God is close to the Restoration's distinctive theology: Joseph's God progresses, creates endlessly, and acts genuinely in time rather than contemplating a frozen eternal present. This connects to the LDS rejection of the abstract "superlative God" (see [[The Superlative God Deflection Collapses Into Nothing]]) and underwrites the move toward a [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity|constrained, relational God]] whose engagement with evil and agency is real. It is also the direct foil to classical immutability and impassibility — represent the strongest classical case at [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]] before leaning on this convergence. Two honest qualifications: process theism is not LDS theism (Whitehead's God is bound up with the world process in ways Latter-day Saints would not affirm, and these figures are philosophers rather than churchmen), so this is convergence in *direction* — away from the timeless, immutable absolute — not arrival at the Restoration's God.
