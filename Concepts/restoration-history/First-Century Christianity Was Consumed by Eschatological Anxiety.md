# First-Century Christianity Was Consumed by Eschatological Anxiety

## The Point

To be a Christian in the first century, [[Nicholas J. Frederick]] argues, "was to be consumed by the anxiety of eschatology" — the study of the "end times" or "last days." [^1] The earliest believers urgently sought answers to questions Jesus's resurrection and promised return had raised: When is Jesus returning? When will the righteous be resurrected? What will the millennial earth look like? How will God judge the righteous and the wicked? These concerns saturate Paul's letters, the Gospel of Mark, and the book of Revelation. The crisis driving the anxiety was the perceived *delay* — Jesus had been crucified, resurrected, appeared to hundreds (see [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Corinthians 15]]), and promised to return (see [[Matthew 24.30|Matthew 24:27, 30]]; [[Acts 1.11|Acts 1:11]]), yet the world carried on as before.

## Evidence

1. **The word *eschatology* is post-biblical but names a biblical preoccupation.** The term "does not appear in the Bible but has become a critical part of the theological study of the Bible and early Christian thought." [^1] Frederick uses it as an organizing lens for how early Christians processed Jesus's absence.
2. **The questions were existential, not academic.** Frederick frames the anxiety in terms a Latter-day Saint reader will recognize: the LDS tradition is "robust with a belief that Jesus Christ will physically return," yet believers "often feel anxiety because of our unsurety of when, exactly, Jesus will return." [^2]
3. **The same anxiety recurs across centuries.** Christians of the first century, Latter-day Saints of the nineteenth century, and believers today have all "had to grapple with the unresolved tension of eschatological hope and the perceived delay in Christ's coming." [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "To be a Christian in the first century was to be consumed by the anxiety of eschatology." [^1]

> "Christians who lived during the first centuries following the ministry of the Savior faced the same questions. By studying the various ways in which early Christians approached the questions and timing surrounding Jesus's return, we can better grapple with our own anxieties and concerns over 'The End.'" [^2]

## LDS Connection

Frederick draws the parallel explicitly: early Latter-day Saints lived in a "millenarian world" (Grant Underwood's phrase), and the Lord's repeated promise to Joseph Smith that he would "come quickly" (see Doctrine and Covenants 33:18; 34:12; etc.) "served to stoke the eschatological fire." [^3] Studying how earlier believers adjusted to a world that did not end as expected models how to "set their sights on God's work in the present rather than focusing too much on the future."
