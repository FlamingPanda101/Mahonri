# The Word of Wisdom Was Not Received in a Cultural Vacuum

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] argue that the [[Word of Wisdom]] ([[D&C 89]]) is often imagined as "a lightning bolt out of the blue," but in fact it "wasn't created in a vacuum." There are two contexts: an *immediate* one (heavy tobacco use in the School of the Prophets, plus Emma Smith's complaints about cleaning tobacco juice, prompting Joseph to "inquire of the Lord") and a *broader* one (the 1830s American temperance and health-reform ferment). The revelation's wide range of topics "directly reflect some of the prevailing health concerns and recommendations of the day," so it is best read as the Lord responding to live debates in Joseph Smith's own time and place.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The immediate tobacco context (rating: moderate; a later reminiscence).** [[Brigham Young]]'s 1868 account describes School-of-the-Prophets elders lighting pipes and chewing tobacco, spitting "all over the room," with Emma's complaints driving Joseph to inquire. Young candidly notes he "was not present at the time," but knew the participants well.[^1]
2. **The temperance movement was already in the air (rating: strong; historical).** The American temperance movement began officially in 1826; a temperance society existed in Kirtland by 1830, before most Saints arrived. The 1830s saw "broad and intense public discourse on diet, health, and temperance," including reaction against "harsh practices of conventional medicine" like bloodletting and calomel (mercury), the latter implicated in Alvin Smith's death.[^2]
3. **[[Sylvester Graham]] parallels the revelation's content (rating: strong; close textual parallel).** Graham, a Presbyterian minister (and Graham-cracker inventor), preached against coffee, tea, meat, spices, condiments, and alcohol in favor of grains, fruits, and vegetables — ideas circulating at Oberlin College, with which Church members (e.g. Lorenzo Snow) were affiliated. "Most of the topics that are covered in this revelation directly reflect" these recommendations.[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A faithful reader (or a critic) might worry the "cultural context" framing dissolves the revelation's divine origin into mere borrowing from Graham and the temperance societies — if Joseph could read the same pamphlets, why call it revelation? More pointedly, a triumphalist apologetic has historically claimed the Word of Wisdom was *ahead of its time*, foreseeing dangers science only later confirmed; conceding it merely "follows the currents of the time" appears to surrender that apologetic.

## Counter-Objection
The hosts pre-empt this: revelation routinely comes "according to [people's] language and to their understanding" (2 Nephi 31:3; D&C 1:24), so the Lord engaging a live cultural conversation is the *normal* pattern, not a defeater — "no church member should assume that any revelation in the Doctrine and Covenants comes out of nowhere." Recovering the context also corrects a false apologetic ("people didn't even know alcohol was bad for them") that the historical record refutes — there *were* contemporaries pushing against these substances. The distinctive claim is not that the topics were unprecedented but that the *particular synthesis*, framed as binding counsel "adapted to the capacity of the weak," carried prophetic authority. The divine and the cultural are complementary, not rivals (cf. [[The Word of Wisdom Reflects Its 1833 Context Including Outdated Health Counsel]]).
