# The Church Kept Most of Adam-God, Rejecting Only Specific Tenets

## The Claim
Against the framing that Adam-God was wholesale repudiated, Brad Whitfield (walking through a **Mormonr.org table** of "Brigham Young's teachings" vs. "the church's response") argues the church has **accepted the large majority** of the package and **rejected only a handful of specific tenets**. "We've accepted an awful lot of it actually." The disagreement with critics is therefore not "was Brigham wrong about everything" but "*which* of these specific claims did the church keep and which did it drop" — a nuanced, point-by-point sort the critics refuse to make [^1].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The accepted column is long (strong — the core move).** Per the table, the church **accepts**: heavenly parents and a premortal life; that "earth was created from pre-existing matter"; that Adam and Eve "were our first parents"; that **Adam is the ancient of days**; that "Jesus is the foreordained savior of his father's children"; and that "we will be exalted if we follow the pattern of Adam and Eve." Whitfield notes the ancient-of-days acceptance is loaded, "because of how people understood the ancient of days to equal God the father" [^2].

2. **The rejected column is short and specific (strong — defines the actual heresy).** The church **rejects** only: that "Adam is our God and father" *in the sense of being our Father in Heaven*; that "Elohim is the spiritual grandfather of Adam" and "Jehovah (not to be identified with Jesus Christ) is the spiritual father of Adam"; and that "Adam is the father of Jesus's spirit and body." So the precise error is the *identification of Adam with Heavenly Father and as the father of Jesus*, not Adam's exalted/resurrected status as such [^3].

3. **A residual, defensible "Adam is a god" survives (moderate — the nuance).** Even on "Adam is our God and father," Whitfield keeps a qualified yes: "we still view Adam as a resurrected being and a God, but not in the same way as... heavenly father." Just as humans "have the potential to become like God," Adam "has been resurrected," so "there are some ways you could refer to him as a god." [[Joseph Fielding Smith]] is cited preserving this as Adam being "the general... under Jesus" — a chain of command, not deity-supremacy. Blyth flags one further wrinkle Brigham taught (that Adam "came resurrected"), which he lists as "another aspect" [^4].

### Steelman of the critic
The table is itself an apologetic instrument, and the host concedes elsewhere the critics aren't even making this argument — "this ain't what the anti-Mormons are saying." A critic can reply that the *only* tenets that made it distinctively "Adam-**God**" — Adam = Heavenly Father, Adam = father of Jesus — are exactly the ones rejected, so listing the surrounding, uncontroversial LDS beliefs (heavenly parents, ex-materia creation) as "kept Adam-God" inflates continuity. The honest residue is "we kept an exalted Adam; we dropped Adam-as-God," which is closer to the standard repudiation than "we kept most of it" suggests [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "The way you were just talking about it as if Brigham Young brought up this thing that is a thing we've kind of rejected and thrown out — well, we really haven't. We've accepted an awful lot of it actually." — Brad Whitfield [^1]

> "While the teaching that Adam and Eve are our heavenly parents is rejected, the teaching that we have heavenly parents is accepted... Adam is the ancient of days — we accept that." — Brad Whitfield, reading the table [^2]

> "We still view Adam as a resurrected being and a God, but not in the same way as... heavenly father... there are some ways you could refer to him as a god." — Brad Whitfield [^4]
