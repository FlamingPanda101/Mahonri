# The Word of Wisdom Was a Gradual Living Commandment Not an Instant Prohibition

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] argue that strict abstinence from alcohol, tobacco, coffee, and tea was *not* the original force of [[D&C 89]]. The revelation was explicitly given "not by commandment or constraint," observance "varied during the lifetime of Joseph Smith," and the binding, temple-recommend-gating standard emerged through "a gradual implementation... that does take almost a century." It is best understood as a *living commandment* — a foundational text whose application Church leaders have "refreshed" over time (most recently adding vaping, e-cigarettes, green tea, coffee-based products in 2019).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The text disclaims commandment status (rating: strong; textual).** Verse 2 frames the revelation "not by commandment or constraint, but by revelation and the word of wisdom." [[Joseph F. Smith]] explained this was mercy: a binding command would have "brought every man addicted to the use of these noxious things under condemnation," so the Lord "gave them a chance to overcome before he brought them under the law."[^1]
2. **A documented century-long escalation (rating: strong; dated record).** Brigham Young admonished abstinence in 1851 and 1870 sermons (framed around health and longevity); ~1905-06 Joseph F. Smith substituted water for sacramental wine and instructed leaders to abstain; 1908 — non-observers barred from local leadership; 1913/1919 — required for missionary service; ~1921 under [[Heber J. Grant]] — required for a temple recommend, with Grant teaching it as "a binding commandment, not a guideline."[^2]
3. **Joseph Smith practiced moderation, not abstinence (rating: strong; primary).** Journals record him drinking wine and beer (Carthage Jail, Zion's Camp, "small beer"); Brigham Young made and drank his own wine and "openly drank tea and coffee"; Wilford Woodruff's journal records taking tea; coffee was on the Nauvoo-to-Utah pioneer packing list. What was taught was moderation; "I don't think anything we have indicates that Joseph Smith drank to excess."[^3]
4. **Still living and adjusting (rating: strong; current).** The August 2019 First Presidency statement adds vaping, green tea, and coffee-based products, and treats marijuana/opioids as medicinal-only — demonstrating the revelation "is still growing."[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic presses the tension the hosts themselves flag elsewhere ([[The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom]]): if the standard is whatever current leaders say, then the appeal to "it's not in D&C 89" (used to permit caffeine) and the appeal to ongoing prophetic interpretation (used to ban tea/coffee) are wielded selectively, and the "living commandment" framing can rationalize almost any shift. A traditionalist Christian might add that a code reversed and re-defined over a century looks less like fixed divine law than evolving institutional policy.

## Counter-Objection
The "living commandment" reading does not claim the *text* changed (the hosts stress "they haven't changed any of the text"), only its *application* — which is how a body governed by continuing revelation is expected to administer a standard. The selective-appeal worry is real but concerns *which authority is decisive* (the subject of the caffeine-reversal note), not whether gradual development occurred; on the historical facts, the escalation from moderation to abstinence is simply documented. That Joseph Smith drank wine in moderation without rebuke, while modern members abstain entirely, is not a contradiction but the signature of a floor-plus-ongoing-interpretation model.

## Best Quotes
> "When the Word of Wisdom was originally given, it wasn't seen as a binding commandment for the Church... observance of the Word of Wisdom varied during the lifetime of Joseph Smith."[^1]

> "There was a gradual implementation of the Word of Wisdom that does take almost a century."[^3]
