# Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion

## The Point
[[Austin Fife]] turns the standard "by their fruits" test back on the critics. Each hostile origin story for the Church predicts a particular harvest — but the predicted harvest never appears. "If a bunch of sexual deviants founded the Church, how come we don't see the fruits of that today? Why are Latter-day Saint families and marriages stronger?"[^1] Run every critical narrative — Joseph Smith as power-hungry, the Church as abusive, treasure-digging as its legacy — to its logical conclusion, and reality contradicts each one: "If I believed the stories about the Church from critics, why do we not see what we would expect based on their narratives?"[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The argument is a modus tollens on the critics' own theory.** If theory T (deviant/abusive/exploitative founding) were true, we would expect bad fruits F. We do not observe F. Therefore T is in trouble. Fife lists the missing fruits: stronger families, high sexual standards for both sexes, leaders who marry once and stay married, no epidemic of the violence or abuse the narratives imply.[^1]

2. **The measured fruits run the opposite direction.** Fife's conclusion catalogs them: the Church is "highly successful at generating stronger families, less divorce, longer lives, higher life satisfaction, healthier people, better outcomes for adolescents and young adults, less domestic abuse, less sexual violence, less juvenile delinquency, less depression, more community, less loneliness, more purpose, better outcomes for sexual minorities, and higher rates of reported happiness."[^3] These claims connect to [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]] and [[Religiosity Benefits Mental Health Even After Leaving]].

3. **To keep the narrative, critics must discard the data.** "To make their ideas work, critics must ignore mounds of evidence. They must constantly adapt and adjust as their theories fall out of favor."[^4] Fife frames this as blaming the human condition on the Church "despite the Church improving the human condition by measurable standards."[^4]

4. **It complements, rather than replaces, the theology-vs-fruit debate.** Where [[Fruit Versus Theology - Are They Separable]] asks whether good fruit can be separated from doctrinal truth, Fife's move is narrower and evidential: a *false* origin story should produce *bad* fruit, and the absence of that fruit is itself evidence against the story.

## Best Quotes
> "Most critical narratives make no sense when I follow them to their logical conclusion. If a bunch of sexual deviants founded the Church, how come we don't see the fruits of that today?" [^1]

> "If I believed the stories about the Church from critics, why do we not see what we would expect based on their narratives?" [^2]

> "Throw all that away, the critics insist. For what? ... A nihilistic life without meaning? A life without hope? Without Christ? Is that what the critics suggest is the good life?" [^5]

## LDS Connection
Fife is applying the Savior's own diagnostic — "by their fruits ye shall know them" (Matthew 7:16-20), echoed in Moroni 7:5-17, that a corrupt tree cannot bring forth good fruit. The restoration stakes part of its claim on observable outcomes: a tree planted by a fraud should rot, yet the Church's measurable fruits are healthy. The argument pairs with [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught]] — both refuse to let an unproven hostile premise stand in for evidence.
