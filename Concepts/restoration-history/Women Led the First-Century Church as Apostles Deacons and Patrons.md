# Women Led the First-Century Church as Apostles Deacons and Patrons

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] documents that in the first-century church — before offices hardened — women "actively led and participated … as missionaries, prophets, teachers, and patrons." The loosely-defined leadership structure plus the relative legal freedom of Roman women combined to open real leadership to women. The New Testament names women as **apostle** (Junia), **deacon** (Phoebe), missionaries (Prisca), and **patrons/heads of house churches** (Lydia, Nympha, Mary mother of John Mark, Tabitha).

## Evidence

### 1. Romans 16 portrays a vibrant body of co-laboring women
Paul's extended greeting names Phoebe ("a deacon of the church at Cenchreae"), Prisca and Aquila, Mary "who has worked very hard," and Andronicus and Junia "prominent among the apostles." The salutations give "the impression of a busy, vibrant body of Saints … men and women alike … working together side by side." [^1]

### 2. Women as missionaries traveling in pairs
Following Christ's pattern of sending the Twelve "two by two" (Mark 6:7), early missionaries traveled in pairs. Paul names male-female couples (Prisca/Aquila, Andronicus/Junia, Philologus/Julia) and women like Euodia and Syntyche who "struggled beside me in the work of the gospel" (Philippians 4:2–3). Prisca and Aquila were teachers and spiritual mentors to the eloquent Apollos (Acts 18:24–26); Prisca's name precedes her husband's four of six times, suggesting she may have been the principal teacher. [^2]

### 3. Phoebe as a recognized office-holder
Paul calls Phoebe a *diakonos* "of the church at Cenchreae" (Romans 16:1) — a title he uses of his own ministry and of Apollos, Tychicus, Timothy, and Epaphras. The Greek structure and her designation to a *particular* church indicate "a recognized position of honor and responsibility." She was likely the bearer of the letter to Rome and Paul's *prostatis* (benefactor/patron). [^3]

### 4. Female patronage as leadership
In the Roman world "a person of higher social status … could function as mediator and dispenser of favor regardless of sex." Women patrons hosted the earliest gatherings in their homes — Lydia (Acts 16), Nympha (Col 4:15), Mary mother of John Mark (Acts 12:12), Prisca (Rom 16:5). Lydia "insists that Paul and Silas accept her hospitality," effectively "electing them to preside over the Church in her house" and likely presiding herself when they were absent. Tabitha (Acts 9) was a patron to a community of widows. [^4]

### 5. Women prophesied and prayed aloud in church
Paul's instruction in 1 Corinthians 11:5 — "every woman that prayeth or prophesieth with her head uncovered dishonoureth her head" — *assumes* women actively prayed and prophesied in the house churches. Philip's four daughters had the gift of prophecy (Acts 21:8–10). [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The New Testament clearly shows that women actively led and participated in the early Church as missionaries, prophets, teachers, and patrons." [^1]

> "Some women such as Junia, Phoebe, Tabitha seem to have held special callings of office, status, or leadership in the early Church." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Laughton ties this to Article of Faith 6 ("the same organization that existed in the Primitive Church") and to Elder [[Dallin H. Oaks]]'s 2014/2018 teachings that women exercise priesthood authority in their callings and that priesthood power operates in the family without keys. The first-century picture "confirms the authority and leadership that ancient Christian women exercised as missionaries, teachers, deacons, and apostles (in Paul's broad sense)." [^6]
