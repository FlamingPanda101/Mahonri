# Follow the Prophet in All Things Is a Slogan That Gets Weaponized

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] observes that statements urging members to obey the prophet "in all things and at all times" are rarely *qualified* — they function as powerful, head-nodding talking points — and that, precisely because they are unqualified, they get **weaponized** to browbeat people into compliance and to settle disputes by authority rather than reason. The slogan's force comes from its vagueness; pressed for what "all things" concretely means, its users seldom say.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The unqualified statements (strong)
Hatch cites Ezra Taft Benson ("if there's ever a conflict between earthly knowledge and the words of the prophets, you stand with the prophet") and David A. Bednar (members must "promptly and quietly obey the counsel of the prophet in all things and at all times"). He notes these are almost never qualified — "all things and at all times" is left undefined, covering civic matters, politics, family, and finances without specification.[^1]

### 2. Weaponization in practice (strong)
Hatch gives lived examples: a relative ending a COVID-vaccine discussion with "the prophet said it, case closed"; a meme overlaying President Nelson's face with "the prophet has spoken, the thinking has been done"; and, most seriously, the move from rhetoric to *policy* — California Prop 8, where members who declined to campaign were in some cases reprimanded or released, and "leadership abuse" occurred under appeal to prophetic direction.[^2]

### 3. The "President vs. Prophet" gambit (medium)
Hatch recounts asking whether a given counsel came from "Prophet Nelson" (speaking prophetically) or "President Nelson" (administrative/advisory) — and being eye-rolled as a "liberal professor." The point: people collapse the distinction to make a contingent counsel *binding*, weaponizing the prophetic office to win arguments and shape identity politics.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A defender replies that "follow the prophet in all things" is not a manipulation tactic but a covenant of trust: the unqualified form is appropriate because faith means following even when one cannot see the reason, and Benson's "stand with the prophet over earthly knowledge" expresses exactly the priority a believer in continuing revelation should hold. Hatch's worry about weaponization, on this view, risks corroding the very trust that makes prophetic leadership effective; abuse by individuals is a misuse, not a feature, of a sound principle.

## Counter-Objection
Hatch's reply is that the principle is sound *within its proper scope* but corrosive when left unbounded — which is why he proposes the stewardship boundary (see [[Prophetic Stewardship Is Over the Institution and Ordinances Not Over All Truth]]). Unqualified "all things at all times" rhetoric is precisely what enables the abuse: it supplies no criterion to distinguish binding counsel (church order, ordinances) from advisory counsel (a civic vote, a health decision), so it can be deployed to coerce wherever a leader wishes. And the abuse is not merely individual misuse — it became *policy* (Prop 8 reprimands), which is the predictable institutional fruit of an unbounded slogan. The corrective is not distrust of prophets but *clarity about scope*, exactly what Oaks ("not meant to be binding") and George Albert Smith ([[The Thinking Has Been Done Was Repudiated by George Albert Smith]]) supplied. Compare the priesthood's own self-limiting principle in [[Unrighteous Dominion Condemns the Holder Not the Ordinance]].

## Best Quotes
> "It's just sort of a talking point that's powerful and that people nod their head... but when the rubber hits the road, what are we talking about?"[^1]

> "People can use this to bludgeon or to browbeat people into doing what you want them to do by appealing to a prophet in a certain context."[^3]
