# Book of Enoch — Enoch as the Son of Man

## The Claim

In the Book of Parables (1 Enoch 37–71), Enoch is taken to heaven and identified AS the Son of Man — the heavenly mediator figure from Daniel 7 who comes to the Ancient of Days and receives eternal dominion. This identification has significant implications for early Christology, since Jesus's preferred self-designation ("the Son of Man") draws from this same Danielic/Enochic tradition.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Danielic Source Text
Daniel 7:13–14 presents a vision of "one like a son of man" (Aramaic: כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ, k'var enash) coming with the clouds of heaven to the Ancient of Days and receiving everlasting dominion. In Daniel's vision, this figure represents the righteous remnant of Israel receiving the kingdom after the four beast-empires are judged.

1 Enoch takes this visionary figure and personalizes him — the Son of Man becomes a specific individual with a name and identity.

### 2. Enoch's Ascent and Identification
> "Enoch is taken from the earth... Enoch summarizes his ascent to heaven and presentation as the Son of Man." [^1]

In 1 Enoch 71:14, the angel declares to Enoch: "You are the Son of Man who was born to righteousness." The antediluvian patriarch — who "walked with God" and was "taken" without dying ([[Genesis 5.18-24|Genesis 5:18–24]]) — is identified as the heavenly mediator who will execute final judgment.

**Evidence rating: primary** — explicit in the text

### 3. The Textual Variant in Daniel's Old Greek
McClellan highlights a significant textual variant:
> "In the Old Greek... it says 'the son of man came to the Ancient of Days.' Well, that preposition is altered by one letter so that it says in one version of the Old Greek 'the son of man came AS the Ancient of Days.' And suddenly it's like — oh, the son of man IS the Ancient of Days! What's going on here?" [^2]

This one-letter variant (πρός / ὡς) blurs the distinction between the Son of Man and God himself — a theological ambiguity that 1 Enoch exploits (Enoch is both a human who was taken up AND the divine mediator) and that early Christians would later apply to Jesus.

**Evidence rating: primary** — the textual variant is documented in the manuscript tradition

### 4. The Son of Man in 1 Enoch vs. the New Testament
The Enochic Son of Man shares key features with the New Testament's presentation of Jesus:
- **Preexistence**: The Son of Man is "named in the presence of the Lord of Spirits" before creation (1 Enoch 48:3)
- **Judgment**: He sits on the "throne of glory" and judges the wicked (1 Enoch 45:3, 69:27–29)
- **Worship**: He receives worship alongside God (1 Enoch 48:5)
- **Hiddenness**: He is concealed with God until revealed at the eschaton (1 Enoch 62:7)

> "It is so significant to the discussion of early Christology because of the Son of Man character." [^3]

### 5. Implications for Jesus's Self-Designation
Jesus's consistent use of "the Son of Man" (ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου) as his preferred self-designation draws from this Danielic/Enochic conceptual pool. The title carries:
- **Authority**: The Danielic figure receives dominion
- **Humility**: "Son of man" also means "human one" (Ezekiel's usage)
- **Hidden identity**: The Enochic figure is concealed until revelation
- **Judgment role**: The Son of Man is the eschatological judge

Jesus's audience — familiar with the Danielic tradition and possibly the Enochic tradition (attested at Qumran, quoted by Jude) — would recognize these resonances.
