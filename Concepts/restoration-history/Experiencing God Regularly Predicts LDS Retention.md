# Experiencing God Regularly Predicts LDS Retention

#type/claim

## The Claim
Among 16-year-olds tracked into their early twenties, **regularly experiencing God in daily life** was a leading predictor of *staying* in the Church; church history was a predictor of leaving but did not rank among the top predictors.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Longitudinal design (Evidence: strong methodology).** Dyer's panel followed youth "when they're about 16 and tracked them until they were just after ... 20," then modeled which age-16 factors predicted later de-identification.[^1] Prospective tracking is stronger than retrospective self-report for causal inference.

2. **The protective factor (Evidence: strong within model).** "A big predictor of staying in the church was experiencing God on a regular basis."[^2] Daily lived relationship with God, not merely belief assent, is the retention driver.

3. **Church history is real but secondary (Evidence: candid concession).** "Church history was on there. It didn't end up in the top predictors, but you could see ... how ... difficulties with church history might keep people from engaging ... and then feeling God's presence in their daily life."[^3] Dyer concedes history *can* indirectly erode the daily-experience factor.

4. **The practical pipeline (Evidence: host application).** Daily experience of God is produced by ordinary devotional practice — scripture reading, prayer, church attendance, fulfilling a calling — which is "what our leaders are drilling ... for decades."[^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** "Experiencing God" is self-reported and circular as a retention predictor — those on the way out naturally report fewer spiritual experiences, so this may describe disaffiliation rather than cause retention. Treating church-history difficulties as merely a downstream dampener of "feeling God's presence" can minimize substantive historical objections (polygamy, translation, Book of Abraham) by psychologizing them. A skeptic would argue that for some, honest engagement with history *removes* the felt experience of God for good reason.
