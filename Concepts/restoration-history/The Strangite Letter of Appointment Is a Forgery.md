# The Strangite Letter of Appointment Is a Forgery

## The Claim
[[Christopher Blythe]] and the Ward Radio hosts argue that James Strang's "Letter of Appointment" — the document Strangites cite as Joseph Smith's written designation of [[James Strang]] as successor — is "an obvious forgery," widely rejected as such "by historians and non-Strangites." The case rests on a handwriting/signature mismatch and on the implausibility of the underlying scenario, not on hostility to Strang (whom the hosts admire). [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The signature does not match Joseph's hand (rated: strong, paleographic).** Blythe issues a live challenge: pull up the Letter of Appointment (dated "Nauvoo, June 18th, 1844") on Wikipedia, then compare its signature to any verified Joseph Smith signature in the Joseph Smith Papers. The hosts do so on air and react to the letter's signature — "that looks a little janky" — which Blythe calls "the scholarly term for probably a forgery." There are also disputes over the postmark and what was actually mailed. [^2]

2. **The four-months / one-meeting problem (rated: strong, circumstantial).** Strang converted only **four months** before Joseph's death and had met him **once**. The hosts press the absurdity by analogy ("how long would you want someone to have worked for Ward Radio before they become your successor?"): it is implausible that Joseph would hand a newly-baptized stranger a letter of appointment over the established Quorum of the Twelve. [^3]

3. **"Too many succession plans," not too few (rated: moderate, historiographical).** The hosts grant Strang is not alone — there were succession gestures toward Sidney Rigdon, toward Hyrum Smith (the document destroyed at his martyrdom), and toward the Twelve. [[D. Michael Quinn]] was "the first person to kind of" catalogue these competing succession orders, and [[Don Bradley]] is quoted: "the problem with Joseph Smith was not that he had too little succession plans but he had too many." On the LDS reading, the resolution is not a secret letter but the **keys** resting with the Quorum of the Twelve (D&C 107). See [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]]. [^4]

4. **Even granting the letter, it never says "successor" (rated: noted concession).** Blythe observes some defenders argue the document "doesn't actually say successor anywhere on it," so even a genuine letter would not establish Strang's claim. [^5]

**Steelman of the Strangite view.** Blythe is careful to say he "loves" the Strangites and finds Strang's overall case "the most compelling" of the rivals; the letter's interest is precisely that it parallels Joseph's own prophetic pattern. A Strangite could note the forgery verdict turns partly on a low-resolution Wikipedia image and on assumptions about how quickly Joseph might act. The LDS reply is that the paleographic mismatch is corroborated independently of the image quality and that the keys-based succession (not any letter) is what actually settles 1844.
