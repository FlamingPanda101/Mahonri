# The Creeds Won by State Power Not Persuasion

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that the trinitarian creed prevailed **not because it convinced people but because the church and the governments that adopted it had the power to enforce it**. When [[Constantine]] made Christianity the religion of Rome and found the factions warring, the largest faction's view was set in stone as the Nicene Creed and pressed on the world "via the state" for ~1500 years; dissenters — including pre-Nicene fathers like [[Origen]] — were *posthumously excommunicated*. The creed is "incoherent" yet endured by coercion, not argument.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Constantine standardized one faction's view by fiat — rate: moderate (historical)
Hansen: "Constantine takes on Christianity as the official religion of Rome… he gets the largest faction together and they come up with, okay, we're going to set in stone what is orthodox… we're going to impose it via the state." The Nicene Creed (and later creeds) drew the line "you are a Christian or you're not," and "they never resolved the christological debates — they just imposed a creed of one group."[^1] The vault's [[St Nicholas Slapped Arius - The Creeds Were Forged in Contention]] and [[Heresy Was Decided by Votes Not Demonstrable Truth]] record the contentious, vote-driven character of the councils.

### 2. It grew by power, not persuasion — rate: moderate (causal claim)
"The Christian conception of God as this Trinity that no one can understand didn't become popular because so many people were convinced by it. It became popular and grew because the church that owned it and the governments that owned it took over the world."[^2] The marrying of imperial power to doctrine is, on this view, the decisive corruption — cf. [[Marrying Imperial Power to Religion Is the Decisive Corruption]].

### 3. Posthumous excommunication of dissenters — rate: moderate (specific, checkable)
Hansen and host [[Cardon Ellis]] claim the creedal church "posthumously excommunicated the early church fathers who didn't believe in these creeds," naming **Origen** ("go Google Origen excommunication") — a man "practicing Christianity 400 years before any of y'all" condemned for not matching creeds formulated after his death.[^2] The vault treats the related condemnations in [[The Two Great Wrong Turns - Overthrow of Origen and Pelagius]]. (Historically: Origen was anathematized at the Second Council of Constantinople, 553 — roughly three centuries after his death.)

### 4. Enforcement persists socially today — rate: moderate (observation)
"The creeds are still in force today… when we challenge the doctrine of the Trinity, they don't say 'but the Trinity makes perfect sense.' The argument is simply to look at you and say, you reject the Trinity, therefore you're not a Christian — by fiat." Disqualification-by-label, not by demonstration.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The historical picture is more complex than "Caesar imposed it." (a) **Nicaea was not simply Constantine's decree.** The emperor convened and pressured, but the *content* was hammered out by bishops, and Nicene orthodoxy *lost* state favor repeatedly afterward (pro-Arian emperors — Constantius II, Valens — backed the other side for decades; Athanasius was exiled five times). If raw state power decided doctrine, Arianism, not Nicaea, should have won; that Nicene theology ultimately prevailed *despite* swings in imperial backing is evidence it had broader ecclesial conviction behind it, not merely coercion. (b) **"No one finds it coherent" is polemic.** Sophisticated trinitarian theology (Cappadocians, Augustine, Aquinas) offers reasoned articulations; one may reject them, but "incoherent nonsense that only survived by force" caricatures a tradition that has argued the position for 1,700 years. (c) **Posthumous condemnation ≠ the creed's *origin*.** Origen's 553 anathematization concerned specific later "Origenist" propositions (pre-existence of souls, apokatastasis) amid the Origenist controversies — a real and harsh episode the LDS can cite, but it postdates Nicaea and does not show the *Nicene* creed itself was manufactured by power. (d) **Establishment cuts both ways.** State support spread Christianity generally (including the scriptures the LDS revere); singling out the Trinity as the power-imposed part is selective. The disciplined version: it is *true and important* that imperial establishment entangled doctrine with coercion, that councils were contentious and political, and that dissenters were sometimes condemned posthumously — all of which fits the apostasy thesis; but it is an overreach to say the creed prevailed *purely* by force, given that Nicene theology survived sustained imperial opposition, which points to genuine theological conviction as part of the story.
