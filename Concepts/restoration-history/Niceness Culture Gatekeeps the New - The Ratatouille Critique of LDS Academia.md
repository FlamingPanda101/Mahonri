# Niceness Culture Gatekeeps the New - The Ratatouille Critique of LDS Academia
#type/claim

## The Claim
LDS academic culture cultivates a polished "niceness" — credentials, civility, brand-building ("master learner," "receive truth let it come from whence it may") — that in practice *gatekeeps* novel ideas from outside the guild rather than engaging them. The hosts of a Ward Radio episode frame this through Anton Ego's closing monologue in *Ratatouille*: "In many ways, the work of a critic is easy... but the bitter truth we critics must face is that, in the grand scheme of things, the average piece of junk is more meaningful than our criticism designating it so... **the new needs friends.**" The charge is that credentialed scholars now police *narratives* rather than test *knowledge*, dismissing popular apologists unread while branding themselves as open-minded.

## Supporting Evidence
- **A concrete trigger.** The episode reads an email in which BYU Marriott School professor [[Taylor Halverson]] declined to read [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]]'s book, calling himself "highly skeptical that Jonah has said anything new or meaningfully accurate" — before reading it. Juxtaposed with his "receive truth let it come from whence it may" self-branding, the hosts charge hypocrisy. (The substantive claims at issue are catalogued in [[The Life of Adam and Eve Fills the Gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses 5]].)
- **The Ratatouille frame.** Anton Ego — the feared critic disarmed by a meal from an unlikely (rodent) chef — supplies the moral: criticism is cheap, creation is costly, and gatekeepers should befriend rather than reflexively crush the new. The hosts apply it to credentialed scholars who "lord over" popular apologists.
- **A partial concession (the steelman, made by the hosts themselves).** They explicitly grant that academia is genuinely needed — "we need good academia... falsifiable results" — and distinguish legitimate rigor from "cruel hypocrisy." They even offer Halverson $500 to charity to read the book and verify (or fail to disprove) one claim, and cite insiders like [[Mike Day]] and Dave Butler as scholars unafraid of "the new," noting Joseph Smith and Brigham Young (the university's namesake) held no degrees.
- **Steelman of the academics.** A scholar can fairly respond that finite time forces triage: declining to read every self-published claim is not gatekeeping but stewardship, and "niceness" (peer review, civility, credential-checking) exists precisely to filter unfalsifiable or low-quality work — exactly the kind comedic apologetics often produces. The disagreement is partly about who bears the burden of proof: the popularizer says "engage my evidence," the academic says "meet basic methodological standards first." The note's value is cataloguing the *critique* (a real cultural tension in LDS intellectual life), not adjudicating it; the source is openly non-scholarly and half-roast.
