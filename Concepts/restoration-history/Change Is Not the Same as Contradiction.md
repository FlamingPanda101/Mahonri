# Change Is Not the Same as Contradiction

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"Isn't the collective-witness model just an LDS *sola scriptura* that won't allow anything to change?" And conversely, "the church changes all the time, so any current teaching could be reversed." [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Jacob Hansen]] draws a sharp line: **there is a difference between a change and a contradiction.** The restoration "is a restoring and expanding on ideas already in the prophetic tradition, not a contradiction of it." Practices, policies, and even false doctrines adopted at one time can be corrected — *so long as the correction moves back into harmony with the collective prophetic witness rather than contradicting it.* [^2]

1. **The distinction stated.** Quoting [[Joseph Fielding Smith]]: if a leader writes something "in conflict with the standards which are accepted, with the revelations the Lord has given, then we may reject what he has said no matter who he is." The point is not that nothing changes, but that change must stay "in harmony with the unchanging principles... already revealed." *Rating: strong; a real LDS hermeneutic — cf. [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]].* [^2]
2. **New revelation is expansion, not reversal.** The model expects "new revelations and expansions in doctrine" while they "remain in harmony with past truths revealed." A genuine *contradiction* of the collective witness would signal error; an *expansion* (or a correction back toward it) does not. *Rating: strong.* [^3]
3. **The law of witnesses governs the difference.** To trust a single leader *against* the collective witness "would be to blindly follow a single person and reject God's law of witnesses." Even Jesus said, "If I bear witness of myself, my witness is not true" ([[John 5.31|John 5:31]]; cf. [[Deuteronomy 19.15|Deuteronomy 19:15]]). *Rating: strong; ties the distinction to [[The Law of Witnesses Establishes Truth]].* [^4]
4. **Why it is not *sola scriptura*.** Unlike a closed-canon view, the model permits continuing revelation and the correction of past error — it simply forbids *contradiction* of the diachronic prophetic consensus. *Rating: moderate-strong.* [^2]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic replies that "change vs. contradiction" is a **distinction without a difference** in practice: ending polygamy after sermons that it could "never ever change," or reversing the priesthood ban after it was preached as God's will, *look* like contradictions, and relabeling them "changes back into harmony" is exactly the unfalsifiable move that lets any reversal be re-described as fidelity. The line is drawn by the same authority whose consistency is in question.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: the ruler is not the *current* leadership's say-so but the **cross-dispensational** witness, which is public and checkable. Measured against it, polygamy's *doctrine* never changed (monogamy-norm-with-authorized-exceptions held throughout — see [[Polygamy Doctrine Did Not Change Only the Exaltation-Requires-Polygamy Idea Did]]), and the priesthood ban was a *return* to the collective witness, not a contradiction of it (see [[The Priesthood Ban Was 1850s False Doctrine From Protestant Race Theory]]). The distinction does real work precisely because some proposed changes (e.g., gay marriage) *would* contradict the witness and so are ruled out.

## Best Quotes
> "There is an important distinction to make between a change and a contradiction. I'm going to say that again. There is an important distinction to make between a change and a contradiction." [^2]

> "The restoration is a restoring and expanding on ideas already in the prophetic tradition, not a contradiction of it. So please don't ever forget that there is a difference between a change and a contradiction." [^2]
