# Defining Death - Clinical Versus Biological Death

## The Point
Before one can ask "what is it like to die?" one must ask what *death* even is — and the boundary is not a single sharp line. Moody distinguishes **clinical death** (a person "thought, adjudged, or pronounced clinically dead" by physicians, e.g., absence of vital signs, who is then resuscitated) from **biological death** (the irreversible destruction of the body) [^1][^2]. His strongest cases involve "an apparent clinical death" that "actually occurs," yet from which the person returns — precisely the window in which patients report conscious experience [^3]. The fact that consciousness is reported *after* clinical death has been pronounced is what makes the phenomenon theologically and scientifically provocative.

## Evidence
1. **Talking about death is hard for two reasons.** First, "the subject of death is taboo" psychologically; second, language itself fails, because "death… is something which lies beyond the conscious experience of most of us because most of us have never been through it" [^4].
2. **Clinical death as a category.** Moody's first and most dramatic case-type comprises "persons who were resuscitated after having been thought, adjudged, or pronounced clinically dead by their doctors" [^1].
3. **A continuum, not a wall.** Cases of a close brush with death "are not different from, but rather form a continuum with," cases in which a clinical-death event took place — the experiences reported are "remarkably similar" across the boundary [^5].
4. **"Biological death" named directly.** Moody describes including "readings on the subject of human survival of biological death" in his philosophy courses — distinguishing survival of *biological* death from the recoverable *clinical* state [^2].
5. **The corroborating witnesses' testimony.** Kübler-Ross notes Moody's patients "continue to have a conscious awareness of [their] environment after being pronounced clinically dead" — the conscious report straddles the clinical-death line [^6].
6. **Why the boundary matters.** The whole evidential force depends on the death being real enough to count (pronounced clinically dead) yet reversed enough to report — which is exactly why modern resuscitation made the data available [^7].

## Best Quotes
> "Death… is something which lies beyond the conscious experience of most of us because most of us have never been through it." [^4]

> "The experiences of persons who were resuscitated after having been thought, adjudged, or pronounced clinically dead by their doctors." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The clinical/biological distinction maps loosely onto the LDS understanding that the death of the body is *not* the death of the person: the spirit separates from the body and continues consciously (Alma 40:11–14), awaiting the resurrection (Alma 40:23; D&C 138). NDE testimony of awareness persisting past pronounced clinical death is an honest empirical echo of that doctrine — though the LDS claim concerns the spirit's survival of *bodily* death, not merely a recoverable clinical pause.
