# Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] identifies **open theism** as a clean solve to the creation dilemma: open theism holds "that God can only know things that are possible, and the future doesn't exist, so God cannot know the future." [[Luke Hansen]] confirms this is "very good" as a steelman.[^1] If God lacks exhaustive foreknowledge of free choices, He cannot be culpable for creating the foreknown-damned — He didn't foreknow. This is the denial of *premise #1* in [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]. Carroll grants "if open theism is true, great... that solves a lot of problems" — but personally rejects it because he "can't reconcile it with scripture and prophecy," preferring the material-creation/premortal-consent route.[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Open theism dissolves the dilemma but at scriptural cost — rate: Carroll's honest trade.** The dilemma's force depends on God *foreknowing* the bad outcome; remove foreknowledge and the moral charge evaporates. Carroll concedes the route works, which strengthens his internal-critique claim (there really are multiple exits, see [[The Creation Dilemma Is an Internal Catch-22]]).[^2] But he won't take it because fulfilled prophecy implies God knows the actual future (see [[The 116 Pages as Evidence of Foreknowledge]]). **Steelman of the open theist:** much prophecy is *conditional* or expresses God's settled *intentions* (which He can guarantee by His own action) rather than foreknowledge of others' free acts; open theists (Pinnock, Boyd) argue this is compatible with biblical prophecy and better fits texts where God "repents," tests to "know," or is surprised. Carroll's rejection is a personal scriptural judgment, not a refutation of open theism's coherence.
2. **Carroll's own view: foreknowledge without timelessness — rate: a middle position.** Carroll affirms God *does* know the future (D&C 1:38 "past, present, future... before him"; D&C 130:7 on God's time) but denies God is *timeless* — "time is relative and he can view time differently than us," while admitting "I don't claim to know how that works."[^3] [[Cardon Ellis]] floats the C.S. Lewis "God in all times at once" view; Carroll says "it depends what he means."[^4] This keeps premise #1 (foreknowledge) intact, forcing his solve onto premise #3 (deny *ex nihilo*) rather than #1. See [[God Found Spirits He Did Not Create - Book of Abraham Solve]].
3. **Contrast with Molinism — rate: clarifying.** Open theism removes foreknowledge entirely; Molinism retains it. Only the former dissolves the dilemma — which is why Carroll treats open theism as a genuine solve and Molinism as a non-solve (see [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Open theism... means that God can only know things that are possible, and the future doesn't exist, so God cannot know the future." [^1]

> "We have open theism as an option... if open theism is true, great. You know, that I think that solves a lot of problems. I can't reconcile it with scripture and prophecy, but if I'm wrong, so be it." [^2]

> "I think that God knows the future because there are scriptures about his ultimate foreknowledge... I don't believe God is timeless. I believe God's time is different cuz time is relative... Now, I don't claim to know how that works." [^3]
