# Modern Medicine Made Death Taboo and Set the Stage for Life After Life

## The Point
For tens of thousands of years humans accepted death as a natural part of life; then, around the turn of the twentieth century, a "brutal revolution" made death "unnatural, dirty, medicalized, and hidden from the public view" [^1]. Where most people once died at home, by mid-century most died in hospitals subjected to interventions that stripped the dying of dignity and control [^1]. Two cultural wounds — the loss of shared "myths" about death, and "the systematic devaluation of anything to do with the spiritual side of humans" — created the vacuum that *Life After Life* later filled [^2]. Elisabeth Kübler-Ross's *On Death and Dying* (1969) cracked the taboo a decade before Moody; her foreword to *Life After Life* frames the book as the next step [^3].

## Evidence
1. **A historical reversal.** "Whereas most people died at home in the 1800s, by the mid–twentieth century most people died in hospitals," and "aggressive end-of-life interventions" cost the dying "dignity and control"; even the American Medical Association eventually concluded patients faced "humiliating and unnecessary medical interventions" [^1].
2. **The "loving lie."** In 1965 death "was not discussed"; dying patients "were not told the truth about their medical situation," though "dying patients always knew they were going to die anyway." The lie "only served to insulate the medical profession and society from the facts of death" [^4].
3. **Kübler-Ross broke the silence.** She "dared to actually talk to dying patients about their feelings," generating "enormous hostility" from medical staff, and documented the emotional stages of dying — "denial, isolation, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance" [^4][^5].
4. **The two cultural problems the book addressed.** *Life After Life* succeeded because it answered "(1) the loss of collective societal myths having to do with death and dying, and (2) the systematic devaluation of anything to do with the spiritual side of humans" [^2].
5. **The taboo persists at the personal level.** Moody himself notes in Chapter 1 that "the subject of death is taboo" because contact with it "confronts us with the prospect of our own deaths"; even medical students feel "uneasiness" at the anatomy lab, seeing "a symbol of my own mortality" [^6].
6. **Irony of technology.** "The same medical technology that contributed to the degrading and humiliating conditions of dying patients allowed us to successfully resuscitate people so that they could report their near-death experiences" [^7].

## Best Quotes
> "Death became unnatural, dirty, medicalized, and hidden from the public view." [^1]

> "Life After Life was hugely successful because it addressed two major problems in twentieth-century Western Civilization: (1) the loss of collective societal myths having to do with death and dying, and (2) the systematic devaluation of anything to do with the spiritual side of humans." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The modern medicalized denial of death — and the spiritual impoverishment Morse blames for "homelessness, depression, drug abuse… and gun violence" rooted in "a lack of spiritual wisdom" [^8] — is the cultural condition LDS afterlife theology directly answers. The Plan of Salvation reframes death not as a humiliating extinction but as a purposeful passage of the conscious spirit into the spirit world (Alma 40), where labor and progression continue (D&C 138). The book's recovery of death as "a spiritually dynamic time with life-transforming insights" [^9] honestly parallels that restored frame.
