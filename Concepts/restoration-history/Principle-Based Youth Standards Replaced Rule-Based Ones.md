# Principle-Based Youth Standards Replaced Rule-Based Ones

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Johnny Harris]] mocks the old *For the Strength of Youth* booklet as a controlling "manual on how you are to behave and dress and look," quoting dated lines (especially for girls) about hemlines, sleeves, and figures.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Keystone concedes the dated content
"I grew up with this same booklet of standards... It includes some awesome stuff that was a great protection to me as a teenager. There are also some sections that could have been improved." Keystone does not defend the old wording; it agrees some of it warranted change.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the concession is candid and disarms the "apologists defend everything" expectation.

### 2. The booklet was deliberately revised to be principle-based
"That's probably exactly why the church doesn't use this booklet anymore. Instead of spelling out specific dress and grooming rules... the current booklet is much more principle-based." Example: rather than "don't date until you're 16," it now says "16 is a good guideline, but counsel with your parents and leaders," leaving "a lot more room for personal interpretation than there used to be."[^3] This is the youth-standards instance of principle over rule — compare [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the 2022 *For the Strength of Youth* revision is a documented, public shift to a principles framework.

### 3. Self-correction is a virtue, not a liability
"I'm grateful to belong to a church that is willing to examine itself and improve over time. I think that's admirable, and frankly, the new book of principle-based standards doesn't get enough credit." The change refutes the "rigid control" frame: the institution loosened prescription in favor of conscience.[^4] It also fits the broader pattern that forms change while the covenant core holds — [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the normative read ("admirable") is the apologist's; the underlying fact (the revision happened, toward more latitude) is solid.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Harris can reply that a *principle-based* booklet is not obviously less controlling — vaguer standards can expand a leader's discretion and internalize self-policing more deeply than explicit rules — and that praising the Church for fixing its own dated guidance still concedes the guidance *was* controlling and harmful to some who lived under it. The honest reply: the critique lands against the *old* booklet (which Keystone grants), but the *current* approach genuinely shifts authority toward the individual and parents, which is the opposite of tightening control; whether "principles" in practice empower or merely re-route control is a fair open question, not a refutation.

## Best Quotes
> "Instead of saying 'don't date until you're 16,' it says 16 is a good guideline, but counsel with your parents and leaders. By design, there is a lot more room for personal interpretation than there used to be."[^3]

> "I'm grateful to belong to a church that is willing to examine itself and improve over time... the new book of principle-based standards doesn't get enough credit."[^4]
