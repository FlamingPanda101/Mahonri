# Apocryphal Traditions That Satan Tricked Abraham Into the Sacrifice

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] presents a speculative reconstruction, assembled from several apocryphal sources, that the command to sacrifice Isaac may have come from **Satan impersonating the Lord**, not from God. On this reading the Binding of Isaac ([[Genesis 22]]) is a satanic trap: Satan overhears Isaac and [[Ishmael (son of Abraham)|Ishmael]] arguing over the covenant, disguises himself as the Lord to command the sacrifice, then appears *as himself* to denounce it — playing both sides so that whether Abraham obeys or refuses, Satan "wins." God intervenes with the ram; Abraham is credited not for completing a wrongful act but for his faith. Barnes presents it candidly as a "might be totally wrong" theory.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Multiple apocrypha attribute the idea to Satan (rated: moderate for attestation, speculative for truth)
Drawing on the *Book of Jubilees*, the *Book of Jasher*, pseudo-Jubilees (Qumran), the targums, and the untranslated **Old Slavonic Palaea**, Barnes counts roughly six accounts of the Akedah, of which "four of them say it was Satan's idea."[^1] In Jubilees/pseudo-Jubilees the episode opens, Job-like, with Satan ("Mastema") going "to and fro" and provoking the test — "so it's still Satan's idea."[^1]

### 2. The both-sides trap (rated: literary)
In the reconstruction Satan, disguised, commands the sacrifice; then, as himself, catches up to Abraham and calls it evil ("art thou silly or brutish ... this thing cannot be from the Lord"). Because Satan opposes it, Abraham concludes the command must be *from God* — "he plays both sides ... he's a Democrat and Republican." Satan even snags the ram in the thicket and races to tell Sarah the deed was done, hoping to drive her to despair.[^1]

### 3. The poetic fit with Abraham's own life (rated: thematic)
[[Cardon Ellis]] adds that Abraham had *fled* a culture that practiced child sacrifice (Abraham 1), so a test in which God seems to ask "the very thing that you knew was wrong" is dramatically apt — though he then "stands for the Canon," noting the canonical Akedah carries its own meaning regardless of source.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A serious reply: (a) these apocryphal works are late, legendary, and mutually divergent — Jasher is a much later compilation, and the Slavonic Palaea is medieval — so "four of six say Satan" counts *texts*, not independent early witnesses; (b) attributing the command to Satan **contradicts** the plain canonical text, where "God did tempt [tested] Abraham" (Gen. 22:1) and the angel says "now I know that thou fearest God" — making the trick-theory a rewrite, not a recovery; (c) it risks dissolving the very point of the Akedah (radical obedience / the Christ-type, see [[The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ]]); and (d) Jubilees' Mastema framing parallels Job precisely because it is a *theological retelling*, not a historical correction. Barnes concedes the speculative status; the strongest defensible claim is the narrower one in [[The New Testament Draws on Non-Canonical Abraham Traditions]] — that NT authors knew an *expanded* Abraham tradition — not that Satan literally issued the command.

## LDS Connection
A speculative apocryphal reading that enriches the [[Binding of Isaac]] material in the vault. It is offered alongside the canonical type-of-Christ reading, and its load-bearing apologetic value lies in the companion notes on James ([[James Says Abraham Believed Not That He Completed the Work]]) and on the New Testament's use of non-canonical Abraham tradition.
