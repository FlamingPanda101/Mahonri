# The Sacred Garment Is a Credibility-Enhancing Display

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues — relaying the sociology of religion — that the temple garment is not a liability but a **source of religious identity and intergenerational transmission**, and that its very privacy is an asset. Three strands:

1. **CREDs (credibility-enhancing displays).** In the empirical study of religious behavior, "the most accurate predictor... of whether a child will grow up and continue in the religion of their parents is how many of these credibility-enhancing displays they see" — costly, sincere behaviors that show the parents *actually believe* what they teach. Barnes's own childhood example: "I knew that my parents wore the garment... that showed me that they truly believe what they are doing," even though "they never spoke a word about it to me." [^1]
2. **It anchors identity (the NRM scholar's verdict).** Barnes recounts the world's leading scholar of new religious movements ([[Lorne Dawson]], an agnostic) telling him that being a little "ashamed" / reticent to discuss the temple "is a sign that you have something worthwhile going on" — "if you can tell anyone on the subway in five minutes everything about your religion, then you probably don't have much to offer." Dawson, citing a *New York Times* piece speculating the garment might fade, said that from a sociological view its loss "would be absolutely terrible... that is a source of religious identity that you are drawing on." [^2]
3. **Eliade's "return to creation" (the devotional complement).** [[Dave Butler]] adds [[Mircea Eliade]]: in sacred space one re-enacts the creation and is "made new"; donning a new garment symbolizes a healed, re-created self — a both-and with the covering and veil readings. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **CREDs are a real, peer-reviewed construct.** "Credibility-enhancing displays" is Joseph Henrich's term (and a developing literature) for costly behaviors that make belief credible to observers; the finding that parental CREDs predict children's religious retention is genuine social science, not a slogan. *Rating: solid empirical grounding.* [^1]
2. **The "smallness/secrecy as identity asset" point matches the vault's data.** It aligns with [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]] and [[Smallness as Asset for Enduring Identity]]-type findings: distinctive, costly markers strengthen group cohesion and retention. *Rating: coheres with established sociology of religion.* [^2]
3. **It reframes a perceived weakness as a strength on neutral terms.** Having an *agnostic* NRM scholar say the garment is sociologically valuable is a non-defensive, outside-view argument — the same move as [[The Mockery of the Most Religious Is a Costly Signal of Faith]] and [[Trinity as Identity Marker and Costly Signal]] (costly markers signal commitment). *Rating: rhetorically strong.*
4. **Eliade is cited responsibly as a lens.** Butler frames the "return to creation" as *one* multivalent reading, matching the vault's [[Mircea Eliade]] note (influential but contested). *Rating: appropriately hedged.* [^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This is a **functional/sociological defense, not a truth-claim** — showing the garment *works* (aids retention, anchors identity) says nothing about whether the practice is divinely instituted; a committed critic can grant every datum and reply that **costly markers strengthen *any* group**, true or false (the same CRED logic supports cults and harmful sects, which is partly why Dawson studies NRMs). "Anything mockable must be valuable" is a non-sequitur if pressed too far: obscurity and costliness are not evidence of *truth*, only of *bindingness*. And invoking [[Mircea Eliade]] inherits his contested universalism (J.Z. Smith's critique). A measured reply: the claim is explicitly *not* offered as proof the garment is true — it answers a *different* objection (that the garment is an embarrassing liability the Church should shed) by showing that, even by secular lights, it is a wellspring of identity and transmission; the truth-question is handled elsewhere (covering, name-bearing, veil), so the sociological point stands on its own modest terms.
