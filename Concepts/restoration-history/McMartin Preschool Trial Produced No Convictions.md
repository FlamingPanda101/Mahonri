# McMartin Preschool Trial Produced No Convictions

## The Claim
The McMartin preschool case (Los Angeles, 1983–1990) — the longest and most expensive criminal trial in American history — ended in zero convictions and no evidence of abuse, exposing the satanic panic as a self-fueling hysteria built on coached children and a mentally ill accuser.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. How it started (strong; documented)
[[Judy Johnson]] accused workers at the McMartin preschool of satanic ritual abuse. Police mailed **200 parents** a letter saying their children "might have been sexually abused" and asking them to question their kids — predictably sending the community "into a frenzy," even though the letter admitted "there is no evidence as of yet" [^1][^2].

### 2. The accusations metastasized (strong)
Around 400 children were interviewed; workers Raymond Buckey and Peggy McMartin were charged with 100 counts; the alleged-victim count ballooned to **over 1,400 children** as claims "grew and grew and spread and spread" [^3].

### 3. The stories were impossible (decisive)
In private the children gave conflicting accounts and "testified to supernatural things" — they named impossibly unconnected Hollywood action stars as abusers, described lavish zoo field trips, giant underground tunnel systems, "levitating abusers, witches on broomsticks," and children "being fed to sharks." The supernatural material was quietly stripped out before the jury heard the abuse accusations [^4][^5].

### 4. The accuser was a paranoid schizophrenic (strong)
Original accuser [[Judy Johnson], who started the entire ordeal, "was revealed to have been a diagnosed paranoid schizophrenic" [^6].

### 5. The outcome (decisive)
The deputy DA, Glenn Stevens, publicly resigned with a newspaper editorial doubting the evidence [^7]. The trial ran **7 years, cost ~$15M (1980s dollars), and produced no convictions**; all charges were dropped in 1990. Apart from the Children's Institute International doctors, none of 124 witnesses or 800 exhibits offered corroborating evidence [^8][^9]. The Institute still operates but no longer interviews children or determines abuse [^10].

### Steelman
A defender argues that failure to convict is not proof of innocence and that child victims are often disbelieved. The rebuttal: the *content* of the testimony (sharks, levitation, Hollywood stars) was physically impossible, and the production method (see [[Suggestive Interviewing Induces False Allegations]]) demonstrably generated the claims.
