# The Satanic Panic Was Driven by Financial and Reputational Incentives

## The Claim
The satanic panic persisted not because the evidence was strong but because money, media ratings, and professional reputation rewarded keeping it alive. Roughly 12,000 unsubstantiated SRA cases were pursued while fortunes changed hands.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The scale of the unsubstantiated wave (strong)
"Fully 12,000 unsubstantiated cases of satanic ritual abuse were brought forward to fervent fanfare. Families were destroyed, institutions rose and fell, men and women were imprisoned, and millions upon millions of dollars in legal fees, book deals, TV shows, and fundraisers changed hands" [^1].

### 2. The originators cashed in (strong)
[[Lawrence Pazder]] and [[Michelle Smith]] "divorced their spouses and married each other, pocketing millions of dollars in royalties and book deals without ever proving one case of abuse." Pazder ran police ritual-abuse seminars and *Michelle Remembers* became social-worker training material [^2][^3]. See [[Michelle Remembers Sparked the Satanic Panic]].

### 3. Therapists had a direct payday (strong)
Unlicensed interviewer [[Kathleen McFarlane]]'s practice "ballooned" overnight [^4]. The episode asks the reader to "imagine the payday for a pediatric therapist who suddenly received 16 new clients overnight, all of them in need of regular therapy sessions" — plus TV interviews, talk shows, book deals, and government agencies "all clamoring and paying for new training" [^5]. See [[Suggestive Interviewing Induces False Allegations]].

### 4. Institutional momentum (strong)
"Once a case has been taken up by law enforcement, dropping it is an embarrassment. The therapists were also motivated to prove their theories on memory recovery and to show their usefulness" [^6]. The McMartin trial alone cost LA County $15M in 1980s dollars, excluding news ad revenue, private investigators, and thousands of families' therapy fees [^7]. See [[McMartin Preschool Trial Produced No Convictions]].

### Steelman
A defender might say public concern for child safety, not greed, drove the response. The rebuttal: genuine concern explains the *start*, but the documented financial and reputational structure explains why cases continued long after the evidence collapsed.
