# The Burning-Building Argument for Urgency Cuts Against the Critic

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] turns the stakes of exclusivism back on the critic. If a fellow Christian sincerely believes a Latter-day Saint is bound for "an eternity of torment in hell," then their *behavior* should match that belief — as one would scream and act frantically to pull a friend out of a burning building. The mismatch between the critic's professed belief (Mormons are hell-bound) and their casual conduct signals that they do not actually hold it, which collapses back into the no-true-church dilemma.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Belief implies proportionate action (rate: moderate, behavioral).** "If I were in a building that was on fire, would you not be screaming and shouting and going crazy trying to get me out of that building? And certainly dying in a building on fire is a much smaller circumstance than an eternity of torment in hell."[^2]

2. **The reductio (rate: moderate).** "If you really believe that I'm going to hell because of my involvement in the Church ... then shouldn't you be doing everything you can to get me out of it?" Casual indifference is evidence the critic does not truly believe the damnation claim — so why press it?[^3]

3. **Connects to the dilemma (rate: framing).** The argument feeds [[The No-True-Church Critic Smuggles In a Creedal Minimum]]: either the critic genuinely holds a creedal-minimum true church (and should act with urgency) or they do not (and should drop the damnation talk). It is the practical, behavioral edge of the same trichotomy in [[The Three Options for the Seeker - True Church Other Church or No True Church]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The inference from calm demeanor to disbelief is weak. (a) Many exclusivists *do* evangelize earnestly; a single relaxed conversation is not proof of insincerity. (b) Belief in a bad outcome does not require frantic affect — people hold grave beliefs (mortality, long-term risk) while remaining composed; emotional restraint can reflect tact or hopelessness about persuasion, not absence of conviction. (c) Some theologies hold that postmortem outcomes are God's business and human frenzy changes nothing, so equanimity is consistent with sincere exclusivism. The argument lands as rhetorical pressure exposing *inconsistent* critics, not as a demonstration that exclusivism is false.
