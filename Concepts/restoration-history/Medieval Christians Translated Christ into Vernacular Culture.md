# Medieval Christians Translated Christ into Vernacular Culture

## The Point

[[Miranda Wilcox]] argues that medieval Christians made the mystery of the Incarnation — the union of Christ's human and divine natures (see [[1 Timothy 2.5|1 Timothy 2:5]]) — comprehensible by *translating* it into their own vernacular languages and cultural paradigms. This cultural translation, she suggests, is "analogous to Christ's mediation between God and humanity in which he translates God to us in human form thereby transforming humanity." [^1] Monastics in early medieval England initiated this work after sixth- and seventh-century missionaries from Ireland, Italy, and France evangelized the pagan Germanic peoples of England. [^2]

## Evidence

1. **[[Bede]] translated and interpreted scripture for his people.** The "most prolific and learned scriptural exegetes of the early medieval period," Bede (AD 673-735) made 1 Timothy 2:5 one of his favorite texts, frequently quoting it in his homilies on Christ as "the very Mediator between God and human beings." [^3]
2. **Cædmon turned scripture into Old English poetry.** Bede related how Abbess Hild of Whitby recruited the cowherd Cædmon, divinely commanded in a dream to "Sing creation." Monks "taught Cædmon the scriptures, and he transformed them into Old English poems" — scripture rendered in native verse. [^4]
3. **The Ruthwell Cross fused Latin scripture and Old English heroic idiom.** The eighteen-foot Ruthwell monument (eighth century, Scotland) bore a panel quoting "The Dream of the Rood," depicting Christ "as a heroic prince hastening to leap upon the cross and die to save humanity" — the crucifixion narrated in the cultural language of Germanic warrior heroism. [^5]
4. **Christ depicted as warrior and physician.** The early English imagined Christ both as "a heroic warrior freeing humanity from the bonds of the devil" and as "a physician freeing humanity from the disease of sin." [^6] (See [[Old English Named Jesus the Healer Haelend]].)

## Best Quotes

> "These few examples illustrate how medieval monastics translated their new faith into their cultural media and how Christianity transformed it — analogous to Christ's mediation between God and humanity in which he translates God to us in human form thereby transforming humanity." [^1]

> "It has always been my delight to learn or to teach or to write." — Bede [^7]
