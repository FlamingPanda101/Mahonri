# Yahweh and His Asherah Inscriptions
#type/claim

## The Claim
A small corpus of Iron Age Hebrew inscriptions — most famously from **Kuntillet Ajrud** (~8th c. BCE) and **Khirbet el-Qom** — contain blessing formulas invoking "Yahweh... and his Asherah." Read at face value, they attest that some ancient Israelites/Judahites paired Yahweh with a consort or cult-symbol named Asherah (a known Canaanite mother-goddess and/or her wooden cult pole). Together with the abundant Judean pillar figurines, the inscriptions are primary evidence that pre-exilic Israelite religion was less uniformly monotheistic and aniconic than the later biblical editors portray, and that a feminine divine element stood beside Yahweh.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The epigraphic data.** The Kuntillet Ajrud pithoi and the Khirbet el-Qom tomb inscription preserve "Yahweh and his Asherah" blessings; the Kuntillet Ajrud drawings (often read with Bes-style imagery) are argued by some, per [[Dan McClellan]], possibly to depict the deity. The Judean pillar figurines corroborate a widespread household feminine-divine practice.
- **Bearing on aniconism.** Within the [[De Facto Aniconism Mettinger]] discussion, these inscriptions illustrate that early representation of the divine was symbolic and that a consort-figure was tolerated — supporting the view that strict aniconism and exclusive monotheism were a *later development* (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]), not the original baseline.
- **LDS appropriation.** In the Stick of Joseph reading, the same "Yahweh and his Asherah" attestation undergirds the claim that the Book of Mormon's tree-of-life is the goddess Asherah / divine Mother — Nephi knows and does not condemn Asherah symbolism (see [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]] and [[Daniel C. Peterson]]'s "Nephi and His Asherah," 1998).
- **Steelman / caveats.** The reading is genuinely contested. (1) Hebrew grammar normally resists a proper name taking a possessive suffix, so "his Asherah" may denote the *cult object* (a wooden pole/shrine) rather than a personal goddess-consort. (2) The Kuntillet Ajrud figures' identification with Yahweh is debated. (3) Folk practice attested in inscriptions need not equal official or normative theology. The strong-claim version ("Yahweh had a wife") therefore outruns what the epigraphy strictly proves; the cautious version ("an Asherah symbol/figure was associated with Yahweh in some Israelite cult") is well grounded.
