# The No-True-Church Critic Smuggles In a Creedal Minimum

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] exposes an inconsistency in a common critic posture. Many Christians tell Latter-day Saints two things at once: (a) "there is no true church" (so denomination is just preference), yet (b) "Latter-day Saints will end up in hell unless they accept trinitarian theology." Hansen argues (b) secretly establishes a true church after all — "the true church are the people who assent to ... certain theological minimums" — so the critic cannot consistently hold both. Either there is a theological-minimum true church (option 1 or 2), or salvation does not hinge on creed and Mormons are fine where they are.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The two claims are jointly inconsistent (rate: strong, logical).** If salvation requires assent to a trinitarian minimum, then there exists a bounded set of saved believers defined by doctrine — i.e. a true church by theological criterion. Saying "no true church" while damning the doctrinally non-conforming "sets up a True Church" by the back door.[^2]

2. **It forces a dilemma (rate: strong).** "So which is it? Is there a true church, or am I fine staying with the religion I prefer? Is Jesus really that concerned with your theology, or are good people from all churches going to be saved?" The critic must pick a horn.[^3]

3. **The Catholic-brother anecdote (rate: illustrative).** Asked at his Catholic high school whether he would go to hell for being Mormon, a Catholic Brother answered, "Not if you're a good Mormon" — locating salvation in discipleship rather than creed, which concedes Hansen's point.[^4]

4. **The LDS resolution dissolves the dilemma (rate: framing).** Latter-day Saints "don't have this problem" because heaven is not binary: good Christians "through their faith inherit a level of glory beyond their imagination," and all are "closer or farther from God in a journey of eternal progression." See [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A thoughtful critic can escape without inconsistency. (a) The genuinely *consistent* pluralist drops (b) entirely: no true church, and Mormons are not damned — the dilemma never touches them. (b) A consistent *exclusivist* drops (a): there *is* a true church (a creedal one), and says so plainly — again no inconsistency, just disagreement to be argued on the merits (which then revives the [[Name the True Church or Concede the Apostasy - the Negative's Burden|true-church adjudication]] question). (c) One could distinguish "true church" (institutional) from "saved individuals" (a looser set), holding that no institution is uniquely true while some doctrinal content is still salvifically necessary — a position that is coherent even if Hansen finds it unattractive. So the argument convicts the *muddled middle*, not every non-LDS position; its real payoff is rhetorical pressure plus the LDS degrees-of-glory off-ramp.

## Best Quotes

> "It becomes really weird when in one breath people say that there is no true church but also then hold the idea that Latter-day Saints will end up in hell unless they accept a trinitarian theology. What they don't realize is that they are setting up a true church."[^2]

> "Do you really think a just God would send all of your kind and deeply Christlike Mormon friends to an eternal lake of torturous fire forever simply because they thought the trinitarian philosophy did not make sense?"[^3]
