# Hell Is Not in the Bible It Is the Spirit World Awaiting Judgment

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]] argue that the popular "hell" — a fiery, devils-and-pitchforks place of eternal torment — is *extra-biblical*, a creedal/Dantean import. What scripture actually describes, they say, is the **spirit world**: *sheol* / the realm of the dead, an **intermediate state before judgment** in which no one has yet been finally sentenced. Until Christ's descent it was undivided "spirit prison"; his harrowing opened it into spirit paradise and spirit prison. "Hell," Barnes claims, even derives etymologically from a Germanic goddess of death, and *gehenna* names the Land of the Dead, not eternal fire.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The NT word-field is *sheol/hades/gehenna*, not a single "hell" — rate: largely sound philology
The English "hell" flattens distinct terms: Hebrew *sheol*, Greek *hades* (the abode of the dead), and *gehenna* (the Hinnom valley, a judgment image). Translating all as "hell" smuggles in a later, unified picture. The hosts' core point — that the New Testament's afterlife vocabulary is not the medieval inferno — is broadly defensible. *Strong on the lexical claim; the "German goddess Hel" etymology is real (Norse Hel) but is about the English *word*, not the biblical concept, so it is a rhetorical flourish, not an argument about doctrine.*[^2]

### 2. The intermediate state is biblical and patristic — rate: strong (vault-documented)
Christ "preached unto the spirits in prison" ([[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3:18-20]]); the earliest Christians widely held an intermediate state between death and final resurrection/judgment (see [[Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection]]). The descent doctrine presupposes the dead are *awaiting*, not yet finally judged. *Strong: an intermediate state has solid scriptural and early-Christian support.*[^3]

### 3. No final judgment has occurred, so "no one can be in hell yet" — rate: coherent on LDS premises
Because the last judgment is future, the panel reasons, the post-mortem state cannot yet be the final "hell" of torment; it is provisional (spirit prison/paradise). *Moderate: internally consistent with an intermediate-state model and with [[D&C 138]]; it is a theological inference, and traditions that locate a real (if not yet final) suffering before judgment — e.g., Luke 16 — will resist the strong "no one can suffer yet" form.*[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The traditional Christian grants the lexical point but pushes back on the theology. (a) **The NT does depict pre-resurrection suffering and separation.** Luke 16:19-31 (the rich man "in torments" in hades, a "great gulf fixed") shows an intermediate state that is *already* one of conscious suffering for the wicked, not a neutral waiting room; Jesus' Gehenna sayings (Matthew 5:29-30; 10:28; Mark 9:43-48, "where their worm dieth not") use vivid fire/judgment imagery. So "hell isn't in the Bible" overcorrects: the *word* is a translation artifact, but the *realities* of post-mortem judgment and punishment are taught. (b) **The Hel etymology is a red herring** — that the English word descends from Norse "Hel" says nothing about whether the underlying biblical concept is real, any more than "Thursday" (Thor's day) implies pagan theology. (c) **Final vs. provisional** — most Christian traditions already distinguish the intermediate state (hades) from the final lake of fire (Revelation 20:14, where "Death and Hades" are themselves cast in), so the LDS picture is less unique than framed; the genuine distinctive is the *missionary* and *probationary* character of spirit prison (a second chance to accept the gospel), which traditional Christianity largely denies (cf. Hebrews 9:27). The defensible core: the medieval/Dantean "hell" is indeed not the Bible's unified teaching, and an intermediate, not-yet-final state is well grounded — the overreach is the strong claim that there is *no* pre-judgment suffering at all.

## Best Quotes
> "There is no hell — hell's not a thing, it's not in the Bible, it's extra-biblical, it's from the creeds. Gehenna, the Hebrew word for the Land of the Dead — we believe in that. But there's no [fire-and-brimstone] hell."[^1]

> "This is a spirit world before nobody's been judged — judgment hasn't happened yet, so nobody can go to hell yet… it's spirit prison, and it was only spirit prison until Christ came and made it spirit prison and spirit paradise."[^1]

## LDS Connection
Sets up the harrowing-of-hell argument in [[D&C 138 Restores the Harrowing of Hell Recorded in the Gospel of Nicodemus]]: if the post-mortem realm is a probationary spirit world rather than final hell, then Christ's descent to *preach* (and the living's vicarious temple work) makes sense. It coheres with the vault's documentation of an early-Christian intermediate state ([[Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection]], [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested]]). The honest takeaway: the lexical and intermediate-state points are strong; the rhetorical "there is literally no hell in the Bible" is the part a traditional Christian will (fairly) contest with Luke 16 and the Gehenna sayings.
