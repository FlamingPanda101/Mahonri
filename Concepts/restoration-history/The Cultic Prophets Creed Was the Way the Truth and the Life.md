# The Cultic Prophets Creed Was the Way the Truth and the Life

## The Claim
[[Aubrey Johnson]] concludes — only in his book's epilogue — that the recurring doctrine of the cultic prophets, embedded in the Psalms, is **"the way, the truth, and the life"** plus **"the righteous shall live by his faith."** This, he argues, is a creed that bears on the birth of Christianity "to a far greater degree than is commonly recognized," because these are the very formulas Jesus and the New Testament take up.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The repeated Psalmic motif (rated: Johnson's thesis).** Johnson hits the point "over and over again": the goal is *life*, reached by following *the way* (Hebrew *derek*) through fidelity to covenant (*truth*/faithfulness). The host: this is "strictly consistent with the Sermon on the Mount, where you pass through the strait and narrow gate into the presence of life, which is a tree with good fruits."[^1]
2. **"The righteous shall live by his faith" (rated: textual).** Johnson pairs the way-truth-life motif with Habakkuk 2:4 / the Psalmic principle "the righteous will live by his faith" as the cultic prophets' watchword — the same text Paul builds on in Romans.[^2]
3. **Christianity rooted in the first temple, not the law (rated: Johnson's epilogue).** Johnson's payoff: "the roots of Christianity… are in the first temple, but not necessarily in following the law of Moses; they're in some other part of the first temple" — the Psalms and the cultic prophets. His epilogue (read aloud) says the Hebrew scriptures "had a direct bearing upon the birth of Christianity to a far greater degree than is commonly recognized in the contemporary field of New Testament scholarship."[^3][^4]
4. **A "covenant path" gloss (rated: LDS application).** The hosts note the way-truth-life pattern reads as "the covenant path" — staying on the way by keeping covenants and entering "the presence of life."[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** Johnson's epilogue conclusion — that a unified "creed" of the cultic prophets directly shaped Christianity — is his interpretive synthesis, and the "way, truth, life" reading of scattered Psalmic vocabulary is more thematic than a formal creed. A critic would say John 14:6 ("I am the way, the truth, and the life") and Habakkuk 2:4 are independently famous without needing a hypothesized guild-creed connecting them. The Psalmic vocabulary is real; the "this is the taproot of Christianity" claim is Johnson's (and is, as the hosts note, deliberately understated until his last page).

## Best Quotes
> "The repeated theme of the Psalms… is the way, the truth, and the life… and the truth is faithfulness to your covenants. So once you understand the way, you stay on the way by keeping the commandments."[^1]

> "The Hebrew scriptures have had a direct bearing upon the birth of Christianity to a far greater degree than is commonly recognized."[^4]

## LDS Connection
The cultic prophets' creed maps onto Latter-day Saint "covenant path" language and onto the temple reading of the Sermon on the Mount (the strait gate to the tree of life) the vault develops in [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms]]. Like [[Margaret Barker]]'s thesis, it locates the roots of Christianity in a first-temple stream distinct from the law-centered, Deuteronomist reform — see [[The Psalms Predate and Were Not Rewritten by the Deuteronomists]].
