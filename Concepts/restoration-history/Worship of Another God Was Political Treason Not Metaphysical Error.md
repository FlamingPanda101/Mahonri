# Worship of Another God Was Political Treason Not Metaphysical Error

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Objection: when Yahweh says "I alone am the creator" and "besides me there is no God," he is making a *metaphysical* claim that only one divine being exists — so the LDS "many gods" view contradicts the text. The interlocutor presses: "the text doesn't say you have to be allegiant to me; it says I alone am the creator." [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Every city-state had its god; Israel's claim was allegiance, not non-existence (rating: strong; comparative-ANE)
[[Blake Ostler]] explains that in the ancient world "every city state had its own god," and the Hebrews "didn't so much think that they didn't exist as that they were inferior to Yahweh, their god." Yahweh "demanded sole political allegiance." [^2]

> "the Hebrews didn't so much think that they didn't exist as that they were inferior to Yahweh their god and their god demanded sole political allegiance if you worshiped another god in essence you were betraying all of Israel" [^2]

### 2. Worshiping another god = treason (rating: strong; cultural)
To worship another god was to betray Israel — "you were committing treason," even if they lacked that exact legal category. The "no other God" claims are thus claims of preeminence and exclusive loyalty, not denials of existence. [^3]

### 3. Humans co-create as near-divine regents (rating: moderate; textual)
The ANE thought-world "did not have ontological categories." They did not think God "was the sole creator of the world — he said 'let us make.'" Humans "also participate; they're made co-regents, near divinities, to have lordship over the world with God." So "I alone created" reads as a claim of sovereign priority and allegiance within a shared-agency cosmos. See [[Genesis 1.24-26|Genesis 1:24-26]]. [^4]

### 4. "I alone am the one to whom you give allegiance" (rating: strong; Ostler's paraphrase)
Ostler renders the claim: "Allegiance to anyone else is not merely a betrayal. It's a failure to recognize me as the sole god who has any claim on you." [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian/Heiserian reply is that Deutero-Isaiah's creation language ("I, Yahweh, make all things; who was with me?") goes *beyond* allegiance-rhetoric to assert that Yahweh is the sole, uncaused source of all that exists — a genuinely ontological monotheism that allegiance-only readings under-describe. Ostler answers that this imports the later category of *creatio ex nihilo* and metaphysical uniqueness back onto texts whose own genre and "let us make" cosmology resist it. See [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]] and [[Isogesis - Reading Metaphysical Monotheism Back Into the Text]].

## Best Quotes

> "So that's how that functions in the Old Testament. What he's saying is I'm claiming I'm incomparable and if you contest that, you're committing treason." [^3]

> "I alone am the one that to whom you are to give allegiance. Allegiance to anyone else is not merely a betrayal. It's a failure to recognize me as the sole god who has any claim on you." [^5]

> "they didn't think that God was the sole creator of the world he said let us make... because humans also participate. They're made co-regents, near divinities." [^4]
