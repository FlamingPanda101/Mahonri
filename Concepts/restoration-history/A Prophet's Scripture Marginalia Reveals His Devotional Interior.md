# A Prophet's Scripture Marginalia Reveals His Devotional Interior

## The Claim
The personal scripture annotations of a prophet — marking patterns, brackets, highlights, and handwritten and typewritten notes — are faith-promoting evidence of authentic, intense devotion and study, humanizing the prophet while showing the depth of his engagement with scripture. "The prophets mark their scriptures just like we do," yet [[Spencer W. Kimball]] went far beyond ordinary marking: bracketing whole prophetic blocks, highlighting his own notes, and gluing in some **50 pages of typewritten commentary** that functioned as "his internet" — "with this he could give a talk on any subject."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The marking patterns show systematic, percentage-level engagement (rated: strong, quantified).** Collector Reid catalogued Kimball's Book of Mormon: most verses marked overall in **Alma** (the longest book); highest *percentage* in a long book is **[[3 Nephi]]** (55%, ~405 of ~750 verses); highest percentage overall is **[[Enos 1.1-2|Enos]]** at **91%** (27 of 30 verses). The most verses marked *in a row* is **89**, all of [[Helaman 13.31|Helaman 13–15]] — the prophecy of Samuel the Lamanite — bracketed and then highlighted within.[^2]

2. **He added original material, not just markup (rated: strong, specific).** "It's not just unique because he's annotated and highlighted it. He's actually adding his own material." The ~50 typewritten pages are glued in "like an old-school post-it note," and "he even highlights his own notes" — a layered personal commentary, his portable reference library for talks and funerals.[^3]

3. **The notes betray a devotional, sometimes playful interior (rated: moderate, illustrative).** Beside [[Helaman 13.31|Helaman 13:31]]'s warning that riches "become slippery," Kimball wrote a single wry word with a question mark (the panel guesses "Taxes?"). Beside [[D&C 7|D&C 7]], where John the Beloved asks to tarry and bring souls to Christ, Kimball wrote "**I desire the same thing**," echoing the verse's own verb — a glimpse of personal aspiration. His funeral-prep notes cluster at [[Alma 40.11|Alma 40]] (the state of the dead) and Alma 32, with annotations on children who die young "so mothers will have a chance."[^4]

4. **The apologetic payoff: prophets are relatable yet deep (rated: moderate, devotional).** Reid's collection is explicitly "meant to be faith-promoting"; 75,000 visitors a year, including missionaries, are shown the artifacts. Seeing a future prophet study, wonder, joke, and aspire in his own margins is offered as evidence of sincerity — the same register as [[Prophets Point to Christ Through Their Failings|prophets pointing to Christ through their humanity]]. *Steelman:* a skeptic may answer that heavy marking and copious notes prove diligence, not divine calling — any devout scholar's Bible looks similar — and that a private collector's curated "cool stuff" is selected to promote faith, not to test it. The claim's force is devotional and characterological, not evidential of prophetic office.
