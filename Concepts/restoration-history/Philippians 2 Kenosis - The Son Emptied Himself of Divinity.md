# Philippians 2 Kenosis - The Son Emptied Himself of Divinity

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] reads [[Philippians 2.5-8|Philippians 2:5-8]] as a *kenotic* Christology: the pre-existent Son who was "in the very nature (form) of God" *emptied himself* (Greek *kenoō*) of full divinity to take the nature of a servant and become mortal, and was then *exalted* and *given* "the name above every name" ([[Philippians 2.9-11|Philippians 2:9-11]]). Ostler argues this self-emptying is *inconsistent with classical immutability* — a radically unchanging God cannot empty himself of anything. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **The verb is kenosis/*kenoō* — "to empty, let go, set aside."** "He's emptying himself of full divinity by becoming a servant." The hymn frames a *descent then ascent*: form of God → emptying → servant/death → exaltation → name above every name. *Rating: a defensible (and historically real) reading* — kenotic Christology is a recognized minority tradition; Ostler concedes "not everybody agrees with this reading, but this is the majority reading" of Phil 2's preexistence. [^1][^3]

2. **It presupposes a genuinely pre-existent person who can change.** "Christ was once had an equality with God... and then he emptied himself of this divinity." *Rating: coherent within the source's mutable-God model*; it is the crux of [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]] (LDS reject strong immutability/impassibility). [^4]

3. **Exaltation = a *given* status.** "God exalted him... and gave him the name that is above every name" — read as the Father conferring on the Son something he did not then possess as a mortal. *Rating: strong link* to [[The Divine Name Yahweh Was Given to Jesus]] and the enthronement motif. [^5]

4. **Hansen's immutability objection.** "If God is radically unchanging, how does the son empty himself of anything?" *Rating: a real tension for classical theism* — though defenders answer that the Son empties himself by *addition* (assuming a human nature) rather than *subtraction* of divinity, which preserves immutability. [^2]

5. **Steelman of the classical/Reformed reading.** *Morphē theou* ("form of God") may denote Christ's actual divine status, and "did not consider equality with God *something to be grasped/exploited*" can mean he *already possessed* equality but refused to exploit it. On the "non-kenotic" reading the Son empties himself not of deity but of *glory's prerogatives*, veiling divinity under flesh — so Phil 2 affirms, not denies, his full deity. *Rating: a strong, mainstream rebuttal*; the participle and the meaning of *harpagmos* are genuinely disputed, so the verse does not unambiguously teach loss of divinity. [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The verb here is is a form of the Greek verb kenosis. It means to empty or let go or set aside... he's emptying himself of full divinity by becoming a servant." [^1]

> "Christ was once had an equality with God. He was in the form of God. And then he emptied himself of this of this divinity." [^4]

> "If God is radically unchanging, how does the son empty himself of anything?" [^2]

> "You can't make sense of it in a trinitarian context... This is a kenotic theory of Christology... simply inconsistent with the classical view of Christology." [^7]
