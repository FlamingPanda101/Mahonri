# The Layman Groups Latter-day Saints as Christian

## The Claim
The hosts seize on a revealing fact about the underlying PBD clip: the **Protestant interlocutor**, host Patrick Bet-David, frames his own question "within Christianity… the difference between non-denominational Christians, Catholicism, and LDS/Mormonism" — treating Latter-day Saints as one of the three branches of Christianity alongside Catholics and Protestants — and [[Cliff Knechtel|Cliffe Knechtle]] never corrects him. The hosts argue this is the *self-evident, layman's* classification (by their fruits, Latter-day Saints are obviously Christian), and that only creedal gatekeeping overrides it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The interlocutor's framing groups them as Christian — rate: strong (textual fact about the clip)
Bet-David asks, in his own words, "within Christianity, let's break that down — the biggest difference between non-denominational Christians versus Catholicism versus LDS Mormonism." The premise of the question already places Latter-day Saints inside Christianity, as a parallel branch — "Mormon, Catholic, and Protestant" — and Knechtle "to his credit never corrected him on that."[^1] The non-theologian's intuitive taxonomy is the LDS-friendly one.

### 2. "By their fruits" is the layman's test — rate: moderate (scriptural framing)
The hosts argue the plowboy/fifth-grader sees Latter-day Saints as Christian "because by their fruits ye shall know them" ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]]) — Christlike conduct and good fruit — whereas the creeds have "put in a blender" the obvious thing. This is the same fruit-vs-creed move developed in [[Fruits Means Outcomes Not Right Belief in the Trinity]] and the definitional argument in [[Define Christian First and the Concession Follows]].[^2]

### 3. It reframes the burden — rate: moderate (rhetorical)
If the *default, untutored* classification includes Latter-day Saints, then excluding them requires an extra, learned premise (the creed). The hosts use the interlocutor's own framing to shift the burden onto the gatekeeper — connecting to the consistency/bigotry argument of [[Creedal Latitude on Baptism but Not the Trinity]].[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The argument leans on a colloquial classification doing theological work. (a) **Lay usage is not a doctrinal criterion.** That a podcast host casually lists "Mormonism" under "Christianity" reflects ordinary cultural shorthand (self-identification, Jesus-language, cultural Christianity), not a considered judgment about whether LDS theology meets the historic definition; the creedal Christian distinguishes *sociological* Christianity (anyone who claims Christ) from *theological* Christianity (Nicene orthodoxy), and only the latter is at issue. (b) **"By their fruits" was about false prophets, not creedal boundaries.** Matthew 7:15-20 is a warning to *detect false teachers*, and the creedal reply is that good moral fruit is real but does not settle *doctrinal* identity — heretical groups can be admirable people (the point conceded in the vault's own [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]]). (c) **Silence is not endorsement.** Knechtle's failure to correct Bet-David's framing in a fast-moving interview is weak evidence he *accepts* it; he may simply be answering the question as posed. (d) **The definition is exactly what's disputed.** Whether the right definition of "Christian" is "follower of Christ / good fruit" or "Nicene confession" is the whole debate — so appealing to the layman's broad usage assumes the broad definition the critic rejects (see [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]]). The disciplined version: the interlocutor's spontaneous grouping is a *nice rhetorical datum* that the intuitive, fruit-based classification favors the LDS, and it usefully shifts the burden onto whoever wants to draw a narrower line; but it does not *settle* the question, which turns on the contested definition of "Christian" handled directly in the related notes.
