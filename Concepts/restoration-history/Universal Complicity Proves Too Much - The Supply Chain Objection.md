# Universal Complicity Proves Too Much - The Supply Chain Objection

## The Point
When "profiting from war" is treated as moral guilt by association, it generalizes uncontrollably. In a modern economy every actor is several steps removed from some downstream harm, so a standard that condemns the Church for holding a defense stock would also condemn nearly every investor, fund, and supplier on earth. The objection **proves too much**: if it convicts the Church, it convicts everyone, which means it is not really tracking anything distinctive about the Church. The hosts press the critic's own question back on him — "if we were to do a moral audit and ethical audit on every single company, which companies would be left over to invest in?"[^1]

## Evidence
1. **The line-drawing problem in the supply chain.** [[Jonah Barnes]], drawing on having worked for a defense contractor, traces the chain: his company made parts for planes (commercial and military); it bought from a mom-and-pop fabricator in Phoenix that was "90% of their business," whose vendors were in turn 90% dependent on it. "So, in the supply chain, at what point do you say all of you are benefiting from... war? ...at what point do you cut it off?" The steel fabricators "don't know" the end use three or four steps down the line.[^2] If complicity propagates up the chain, it never stops.

2. **Even blue-chip darlings fail an ethics audit.** A source at Ensign Peak's founding made the point with Apple — "one of the most successful companies on planet Earth" — repeatedly cited for labor conditions in manufacturing iPhones. "If we were to do a moral audit... on every single company, which companies would be left over to invest in?"[^1] The screen that would purge defense stocks would, applied consistently, purge the entire index.

3. **The Good Place illustration.** The hosts invoke the show *The Good Place*: a man buys an apple and his moral score drops — the apple was grown with child slave labor, and the truck's fuel was drilled by a warlord financing violence. The lesson: "you see this complex society that we've built... nothing is as black and white as you think."[^3] Modern consumption and investment are saturated with distant, untraceable harms; isolating the Church for one such linkage is arbitrary.

4. **The defense-tool argument cuts both ways — and is conceded honestly.** The hosts do not pretend the industry is clean. They grant weapons "are used by the United States government," that they personally accept "violence... can and should be committed in defense of innocence," but also concede the tools have at times been used "unrighteously" (e.g., the 2000s Middle East invasions under false WMD pretenses).[^4][^5] The honest tension actually strengthens the rebuttal: even granting real moral concern about the *users* of weapons, that concern does not transfer to a passive minority shareholder via a chain of intermediaries.

5. **Selective application is the tell.** The same logic that condemns the Church's $25M defense holding would condemn anyone holding an S&P 500 index fund, every pension, and BlackRock itself. When a principle indicts the entire economy but is wielded against one institution, the principle is a pretext, not the actual grievance — "if we're just tearing down every institution on the planet, then just come out and say it."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "In the supply chain, at what point do you say all of you are benefiting from... bombs or from war? ...at what point do you cut it off?" [^2]

> "If we were to do a moral audit and ethic[al] audit on every single company, which companies would be left over to invest in?" [^1]

> "Nothing is as black and white as you think." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The supply-chain rebuttal is a structural cousin of the broader pattern critics use against the Restoration — building an indictment from associations and "ominous-sounding" framing that, examined, applies to everyone (compare [[The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit]]). The Church's actual posture — screening out direct participation in alcohol, tobacco, gambling, and pornography while accepting unavoidable broad-market exposure — is a defensible stewardship stance under the [[parable of the talents]], not the hypocrisy the "proves too much" charge needs it to be.
