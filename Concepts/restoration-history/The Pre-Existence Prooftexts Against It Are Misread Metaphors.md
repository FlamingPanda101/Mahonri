# The Pre-Existence Prooftexts Against It Are Misread Metaphors

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The evangelical channel "God Loves Mormons" argues the Bible *denies* a premortal human existence, citing [[1 Corinthians 15.45-46|1 Corinthians 15:45-46]] ("not the spiritual… but the natural first") and a cluster of "no one has seen the Father" / "you are from below, I am from above" texts ([[John 5.37-38|John 5:37-38]]; John 8) to show Jesus alone is heavenly while humans are merely earthly. [[Cardon Ellis]], [[Jonah Barnes]], and [[Oak Norton]] answer that each prooftext is a **metaphor or a context-bound rebuke being read as a metaphysical absolute**: Paul's Adam/Christ contrast is not a creation-mechanics claim, and the "no one has seen God" texts cannot be literal given the Old Testament theophanies — so they do not bear on whether spirits pre-existed.[^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Natural first, then spiritual" is the two-Adams typology — rate: strong
[[1 Corinthians 15.45-46|1 Cor 15:45-47]] contrasts "the first man Adam" (a living soul, "of the earth, earthy") with "the last Adam"/"second man… the Lord from heaven" — i.e., **mortal Adam vs. the resurrected/heavenly Christ**, in a chapter about *resurrection*, not premortality. Paul is not narrating the order of creation of human spirits; importing "therefore no pre-existence" mistakes a resurrection typology for a denial of premortal life. "Did Paul ever talk in metaphor?"[^1] *Strong: the immediate context (vv. 42-54, the resurrection body) governs.*

### 2. "No one has seen the Father" can't be flatly literal — rate: strong, internal-consistency
[[John 5.37-38|John 5:37]] ("ye have neither heard his voice… nor seen his shape") and John 1:18 collide with Moses ([[Exodus 33.11|Exodus 33:11]], "face to face"), [[Isaiah the Prophet|Isaiah]] (Isa 6), and [[Jeremiah]] seeing/hearing God. So the verse is either (a) about a **distinct divine Son** the people had not seen — which, the panel notes, actually *undercuts* strict Trinitarianism — or (b) a **rebuke to a particular unbelieving audience** ("ye have not his word abiding in you"), not a universal metaphysical claim. Either way it says nothing against premortal existence.[^2] *Strong as a tu-quoque on the critic's own canon.*

### 3. "You are from below, I am from above" is moral/spatial idiom — rate: moderate
In John 8 Jesus tells hostile opponents "ye are from beneath; I am from above… of this world / not of this world" — the same idiom as "ye are of your father the devil" (8:44). It contrasts *spiritual allegiance and origin-in-Christ*, not biology or providence; read literally it would also have to deny that Jesus had an earthly mother. "If God were your Father, ye would love me" likewise means *be God-like*, not *be biologically descended*.[^2] *Moderate: a reasonable contextual reading, though "from above/below" does carry a real origin sense the panel underplays.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The evangelical can answer: (a) **the burden is on the affirmative** — even if these verses don't *deny* pre-existence, the Bible never clearly *teaches* premortal human existence either, so the doctrine is an LDS import, and the natural-before-spiritual ordering at least cuts against it as a general principle of how God brings persons into being; (b) **"from above/below" is genuinely about origin** — Johannine theology really does mark Jesus as uniquely *katabaino* (descended from heaven) in contrast to everyone else (John 3:13, "no man hath ascended… but he that came down"), which is precisely a claim that Jesus' heavenly origin is *not* shared by ordinary humans; (c) the theophany texts can be harmonized (people saw God's glory, an angel, or the pre-incarnate Son, not the Father's essence), so John 5:37 stands as a statement about the Father specifically. A Latter-day Saint reply (the episode's): this note's burden is only *defensive* — to show the anti-pre-existence prooftexts don't prove their point — and the *positive* case is made separately from the divine-sonship and "knew thee before the womb" texts (see [[The Canon Affirms Literal Divine Sonship and Premortal Existence]]); moreover the harmonization that John 5:37 means "no one saw the *Father*, only the Son" concedes a real personal distinction between Father and Son, which is the Latter-day Saint point, and premortality was in fact an early-Christian belief later purged (see [[Pre-existence Was a Mainstream Early-Christian Belief Later Purged]]).

## Best Quotes
> "He's talking about Jesus Christ… that's the second Adam. Obviously the comparison here isn't between the pre-existence and our mortal existence — it's between mortal man and the second man from heaven."[^1]

> "Has anybody in the scriptures ever heard the voice of God or seen God? Adam, Noah, Abraham, Jacob, Moses on Mount Sinai, Isaiah, Jeremiah all saw God and heard his voice."[^2]

> "He's not making a comment on ontology. He's making a comment on who is your father — like 'who's your daddy,' the Dodgers."[^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the *defensive* half of the vault's pre-existence apologetic — clearing away the texts critics deploy against premortality — paired with the *offensive* half in [[The Canon Affirms Literal Divine Sonship and Premortal Existence]]. It sits beside the vault's broader case that premortal existence was an early-Christian belief later edited/purged ([[Pre-existence Was a Mainstream Early-Christian Belief Later Purged]]) and the related [[Pre-Existence Implied by John 9]]. Honest takeaway: the readings of 1 Cor 15 and John 5:37 are solid defensive points; "from above/below" in John carries more of a genuine origin-claim than the panel allows, so it is the verse a sharp interlocutor will press.
