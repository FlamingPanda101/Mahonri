# God as Head Coach and the Prophet as Offensive Coordinator

#type/claim

## The Claim
Revelation usually operates by **delegation**, not dictation. God is the head coach who owns the team's overarching vision and philosophy; the prophet is the offensive coordinator (OC) who calls the individual plays — working out how to apply that vision "on the field." The coach generally trusts the OC and "isn't going to micromanage every decision," overruling only on occasion. Because the OC genuinely decides plays, not every play succeeds — yet members rightly **sustain** the OC even when a play fails or when they would have called a different one, in respect for the authority the head coach delegated.[^1] This reframes prophetic mistakes: they are the expected by-product of a real delegation of judgment, not evidence the channel is broken.

The model replaces the "Neo from the Matrix" picture the host grew up with — God directly uploads the play and the prophet executes it flawlessly and "almost without thought." Clear, direct revelation "can of course happen," but it is the exception, not the normal mode.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Vision is fixed; the play-calling is delegated (rated: core of the claim).** "It is [the OC's] job to apply [the] coach's vision on the field." The head coach "offer[s] counsel and might sometimes overrule the OC, but generally he has confidence that the OC is going to do his job."[^1] God supplies the unchanging principle ("our bodies are gifts from God... we should respect and take care of them"); the prophet grapples with "what does that look like on the field? What plays are we going to run to bring that vision to life?"[^3]

2. **The Word of Wisdom as a worked example (rated: strong, illustrative).** "Some early prophets said, 'we're going to advise that members only consume alcohol, coffee, and tea in moderation.' Later offensive coordinators said... 'abstain completely.'" Same coach's vision (care for the body), different plays by different OCs.[^3] This is the policy-development point also reached by [[Mistakes Versus Leading Astray]] and [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context]].

3. **Sustaining while disagreeing (rated: applied, distinctive).** "Some past and present prophets have called for plays that I have sometimes disagreed with. But I still sustain and respect those leaders even while disagreeing because they're the offensive coordinator and I trust that they're doing their best to capture the head coach's vision."[^4] Sustaining is grounded in **delegated authority**, not in agreement with every call — the same outcomes-and-trust logic as [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]].

4. **God's foreknowledge does not collapse the delegation (rated: qualifier).** Unlike a real coach, God "already knows exactly how the game is going to end." So why not dictate the optimal play every time? Because He "is more interested in seeing his players learn and improve," which is impossible "without practice and opposition."[^5] This hands off to the agency/soul-making rationale in [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]] and [[Lehi's Theodicy - Opposition in All Things]].

5. **Steelman — the infallibility critic's reply.** A critic holds that *if a prophet genuinely speaks for God, the "plays" should not fail*: an OC analogy works for a fallible human coach precisely because the coach himself cannot foresee outcomes, but an omniscient coach who lets His coordinator run a play He knows will injure people (the priesthood ban, Adam-God) is differently situated. Either the bad plays are not really "from the coach" (in which case the prophet's authority adds nothing evidential), or the coach is endorsing known harm. The believer's answer is twofold: (a) the analogy is explicitly bounded — "all metaphors break down," and the relevant promise is reliability, not that every play scores (see [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]]); and (b) a known-but-permitted bad play is the standard agency trade-off, with the Atonement as the provided remedy for those hurt (the "medic"). The critic's rejoinder — that this makes the office unfalsifiable, since success proves divinity and failure proves "soul-making" — is the live cost of the model and is addressed only by an independent fruits/outcomes test, not by the analogy itself.

## Best Quotes

> "When it's game time, the head coach is actually not generally the one who decides what each individual play is going to be. That's what the offensive or defensive coordinator does as he tries to bring the coach's vision to life."[^1]

> "Some past and present prophets have called for plays that I have sometimes disagreed with. But I still sustain and respect those leaders even while disagreeing... I respect the authority they have been given by the head coach to act in that role."[^4]

> "Instead of focusing on scoring points, our head coach is more interested in seeing his players learn and improve. And you can't do that without practice and opposition. Without opposition, there is no game."[^5]
