#type/claim

# Extreme Anti-Mormonism Is Reactionary Hatred for Belief Alone

## The Claim
Reading from a column by the conservative commentator Jonah Goldberg, [[Jacob Hansen]] frames a distinctive diagnosis of anti-Mormon animus that surfaced amid the Grand Blanc / Michigan shooting and the "you're not Christians" rhetoric that followed: extreme anti-Mormonism is almost uniquely *theological and abstract*, directed at Latter-day Saints "solely for doctrines and not for what they actually do." [^1] Because its haters cannot point to any harmful conduct — no Mormon terrorism, crime wave, or welfare fraud — the hatred reduces to animosity for belief as such, which Goldberg calls "the most reactionary form of hatred in America." [^2] The claim matters apologetically because it isolates and names a double standard: other group hatreds at least *pretend* to indict actions, whereas anti-Mormonism, stripped of any behavioral charge, stands exposed as bigotry over ideas alone.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It is "overwhelmingly theological and abstract."** Goldberg: "What I find particularly fascinating about extreme anti-Mormonism is that it is overwhelmingly theological and abstract... there is something distinctly off-putting, one might even say evil, when theological differences morph into hatred towards a whole class of people solely for doctrines and not for what they actually do in service to those doctrines." [^1] *Rating: strong;* it concedes that doctrinal disagreement is legitimate (cf. [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea]]) while locating the wrong precisely in the leap from idea to contempt-for-persons.

2. **The disanalogy with other hatreds.** Most hatreds claim, "often dishonestly but not always," to link beliefs to actions: racists and antisemites "will make claims about what the blacks or the Jews *do*"; critiques of communism "hinge on the things communists actually do, have done, or want to do in the real world." [^2] By contrast, "Mormon haters can't point to Mormon terrorism or make specious individual-guilt-by-association claims the way that racists and antisemites routinely do." [^2] *Rating: strong;* the asymmetry is the load-bearing move — it grants the partial coherence of action-linked animus only to highlight that anti-Mormonism lacks even that pretext. This inverts the usual "beliefs have consequences" lens (see [[Beliefs Have Consequences - Religion as a Real Cause of Behavior]]): here the *absence* of bad consequences is what indicts the hater.

3. **The 2012 Romney experience as data.** Goldberg recalls that when National Review endorsed Mitt Romney in 2012, the email he received from "self-professed evangelical Christians spewing the rankest bigotry" was, jokes about undergarments aside, "all about heresy, demonic this, Antichrist that, and various theological crimes — but not anything that Mormons actually or even allegedly do. They just believe the wrong thing." [^3] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* a single anecdotal correspondence is not a survey, but it is concrete, dated testimony that the animus tracked theology rather than conduct.

4. **The historical analogy: wars of religion.** Hating people "solely for what they believe is the closest we get to the ideas that powered the wars of religion in Europe, not to mention atrocities like the Albigensian Crusade. It's premodern, tribal, reactionary, and evil." [^4] Hansen responds, "Amen." [^4] *Rating: rhetorically strong, historically freighted;* the comparison is deliberately severe and frames belief-only persecution as a regression to pre-liberal intolerance.

**Steelman of the critic.** A thoughtful opponent could answer that theology is not "mere" abstraction — Goldberg himself grants "abstractions can be extremely important" [^1] — and that warning against doctrines one regards as soul-endangering is an act of *concern*, not hatred (compare the demonic-framing debates and [[See Mormons as Victims Is Condescending Not Loving]]). The claim's reply is built in: the target is not doctrinal critique at all but the slide into contempt for "a whole class of people," the move from "I think this teaching is false" to demonizing the persons who hold it. The line, as in [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea]], is contempt, not intensity.
