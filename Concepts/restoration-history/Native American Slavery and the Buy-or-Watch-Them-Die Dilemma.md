# Native American Slavery and the Buy-or-Watch-Them-Die Dilemma

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
That Latter-day Saints — including [[Brigham Young]], who acquired at least one Native American slave — were straightforward slaveholders, and that the obvious moral response ("simply say no") was easy and was wrongly refused.[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **The understudied context: Ute slave-trading (rated: strong; historical).** "The biggest slave-trading operation in the American West was the Ute Indian tribe," seizing children from other tribes and selling them across a range "from Texas... to California." When the Saints arrived, the traders saw "a new market."[^2] This reframes the issue from Saints-as-initiators to Saints-as-confronted.

2. **The coercive dilemma (rated: strong; harrowing).** Traders would offer captured children and threaten to kill them if not bought. In at least one documented case, when a couple refused, the trader "took one of the little girls and dashed her head against a rock and killed her," at which point they bought the other girl to save her life.[^3] The "just say no" prescription ignores that refusal could mean watching a child murdered.

3. **The supply-and-demand trap (rated: moderate; honest concession).** Peterson grants the bind cuts both ways: "once you start buying slaves, if there's demand, there will be supply." Buying to rescue could incentivize more raiding.[^4] This is the steelman *against* the rescue rationale, which Peterson voices himself rather than hiding.

4. **Brigham's own case (rated: moderate; mitigating).** Brigham bought at least one Native American girl but "didn't treat her as a slave" — she lived in his house as a servant, not chattel.[^5] This dovetails with the servitude-vs-slavery framing of the [[Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery]] and with [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]].

5. **Scope (rated: contextualizing).** Black chattel slaves in Utah were few — "probably at most 30... at any given time" (e.g., Green Flake, Hark Lay) — so the Native American slavery problem was arguably "a bigger issue" practically, though both were minor relative to Brigham's settlement agenda.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "The biggest slave trading operation in the American West was the Ute Indian tribe. They would go out and capture people from other... tribes, and then offer them for sale."[^2]

> "Well, if you don't buy them, I'm going to kill them. ... the Indian slave trader then took one of the little girls and dashed her head against a rock and killed her. At which point... the couple... said, Okay, okay. We'll buy the other girl."[^3]

> "Oh, it wasn't that easy when you were on the ground facing that situation. You really want to say no, maintain your purity, and watch a little child have its head dashed against a rock?"[^7]
