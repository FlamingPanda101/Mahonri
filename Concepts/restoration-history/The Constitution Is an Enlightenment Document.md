# The Constitution Is an Enlightenment Document

## The Claim

The ideas informing the U.S. Constitution "are fundamentally tied to the Enlightenment… It is an Enlightenment document at its core."[^1] [[Brian Thomassen]] dates the Enlightenment from the mid-17th to the early 19th century and identifies both its **foundational texts** and its **intellectual dispositions**, which together stamp the American founding. This matters for the Restoration thesis: the Enlightenment's *limiting-principle* character is what makes America [[America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration|the seedbed of the Restoration]], in contrast to the [[Rousseau Is the Anti-Enlightenment Lynchpin of the French Revolution|Romantic/Rousseauian]] roots of the French Revolution.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Foundational texts
- [[Francis Bacon|Bacon]], *Novum Organum* (1620) — expands [[Aristotle]]'s inductive logic into the scientific method.
- [[Galileo Galilei|Galileo]], *The Assayer* (1623) — "mathematics is the language of science," giving scientific statements force "immune to ecclesiastical or political authority."
- [[René Descartes|Descartes]], *Discourse on Method* — the philosophical foundation for induction and modern natural philosophy.[^2]

### 2. Intellectual dispositions of the Enlightenment
- **Skepticism toward truth claims** — nothing is accepted merely because someone asserts it; it must justify itself (the same move Thomassen applies to "the Constitution is inspired").
- **Reason as the mode of inquiry** — not appeals to authority.
- **Mathematics as the language of science.**
- **Empiricism** — appeals to *efficient* and *material* causality, which means **abandoning final causes** (no teleological explanations).[^3]

### 3. The abandonment of teleology, illustrated
The clearest case is [[Charles Darwin|Darwin]] vs. [[Jean-Baptiste Lamarck|Lamarck]]: Darwin's natural selection and random mutation have "no purpose behind the change," whereas Lamarck's model makes intent drive change (the giraffe stretching for leaves). [[Isaac Newton|Newton]]'s gravitation ("action at a distance," criticized for lacking a mechanism) is offered as a related, more tentative example. The Enlightenment keeps efficient/material causes and drops final causes — a decisive break from the Aristotelian model.[^4]

### 4. Enlightenment focal points feeding the Constitution
Appeal to rational first principles; the **social contract**; and the questions "What is the basis for political authority? How does it justify itself? What are the obligations of the citizen?" These are developed by [[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]], [[John Locke|Locke]], and [[Montesquieu]] and adopted by the founders. See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]].[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** The claim that the American founders drew heavily on Enlightenment political philosophy (especially Locke and Montesquieu) is mainstream and well supported. The sharper, more contestable move is the *dichotomy* that makes the American founding purely Enlightenment and the French Revolution purely anti-Enlightenment/Romantic — historians note Enlightenment *philosophes* among the Revolution's sources and Christian/classical-republican strands in the American founding. Thomassen acknowledges he is "conflating" and bracketing for the sake of the argument.

## Best Quotes

> "I'm going to argue that the ideas that inform the Constitution are fundamentally tied to the Enlightenment. It is an Enlightenment document at its core."[^1]

> "Under the Enlightenment principle, final causes are going to be removed, which means there are no teleological explanations."[^3]
