# Book of Enoch - Birth of Noah as Wonder Child
#type/claim

## The Claim
The closing chapters of 1 Enoch (the Birth of Noah, 1 Enoch 106-107) narrate Noah's birth as that of a miraculous "wonder child." The newborn is so extraordinary — radiant, with eyes that light up the house — that his father Lamech fears the boy is not his own but the offspring of the fallen Watchers (the angelic transgressors of the Genesis 6 story). Lamech sends to Enoch, who reassures him the child is legitimate and destined to survive the coming flood. The episode shows 1 Enoch elaborating the bare Genesis genealogy into a vivid annunciation-style birth narrative.

## Supporting Evidence
- **It belongs to the Watchers expansion.** 1 Enoch repeatedly enlarges the terse Genesis 6 account of the "sons of God" taking human wives; the Noah birth story is the bookend of that project — the anxiety that a child might be Watcher-fathered only makes sense against the Watchers myth the book has been developing. See [[Book of Enoch - The Watchers and Genesis 6 Expansion]].
- **A "wonder child" type-scene.** The luminous newborn whose paternity is questioned and then divinely vindicated is a recognizable ancient birth-of-a-hero pattern. Per [[Dan McClellan]]'s treatment, the Genesis Apocryphon from Qumran preserves a closely related version, showing the tradition circulated in multiple forms in Second Temple Judaism.
- **Conceptual influence.** Such miraculous/contested-paternity birth narratives form part of the wider Second Temple repertoire that later Christian nativity material draws on, consistent with the view that Enoch is conceptually formative rather than historical (see [[Book of Enoch - Not Historical but Conceptually Influential]]).
- **Steelman / caveats.** A reader who takes 1 Enoch as ancient revelation might treat the radiant-Noah account as genuine supplementary history. The academic framing resists that: the Birth of Noah is a legendary expansion, valuable for what it reveals about how Second Temple Jews imagined the antediluvian world, not as a factual record of Noah's infancy.
