# Catechetical Instruction Before Baptism

## The Point
Candidates for baptism (catechumens) underwent an intensive, systematic course of scriptural and doctrinal instruction during Lent, culminating in baptism on the eve of Easter Sunday. Egeria describes this catechesis at Jerusalem; the surviving lectures of [[Cyril of Jerusalem]] (AD 348) likely preserve the very kind of teaching she heard.

## Evidence
1. **A catechumen defined.** "A catechumen is a disciple or student of the Christian gospel who has yet to be baptized. The formal teaching of such disciples is called catechesis." The terms derive from the Greek verb *katechein*, "to instruct orally." [^1]
2. **Forty days through the whole Bible.** Per Egeria: "During the forty days [the bishop] goes through the whole Bible, beginning with Genesis, and first relating the literal meaning of each passage, then interpreting its spiritual meaning. He also teaches them at this time all about the resurrection and the faith." [^2]
3. **The Creed taught article by article.** After five weeks, candidates "receive the Creed, whose content he explains article by article in the same way as he explained the Scriptures, first literally and then spiritually." Teaching ran "from six to nine in the morning all through Lent, three hours' catechesis a day." [^2]
4. **Cyril's elevated prologue.** The intensity is conveyed in Cyril of Jerusalem's procatechesis: "Already, my dear candidates for Enlightenment (i.e. Baptism), scents of paradise are wafted towards you … you have enlisted; you have been called to the Colors (i.e. as part of Christ's army against Satan)." The candidate's resolve must be true: "It is the sincerity of your resolution that makes you 'called'" (cf. Romans 8:28). [^3]
5. **Sermons as lectures.** In these catechetical sermons "the boundaries between preaching and teaching are blurred"; they are "systematic, focused on scripture and the core doctrines of Christianity," reorienting candidates to "their new lives as Christians." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "During the forty days [the bishop] goes through the whole Bible, beginning with Genesis, and first relating the literal meaning of each passage, then interpreting its spiritual meaning." (Egeria, quoting catechesis at Jerusalem) [^2]

> "You have enlisted; you have been called to the Colors … with a holy resolve also, and the confident hope which that brings in its train." (Cyril of Jerusalem) [^3]

## LDS Connection
The structured pre-baptismal teaching program — covering scripture, the resurrection, the faith, and covenant resolve before the ordinance, with the deepest "mysteries" reserved for after — parallels the LDS pattern of missionary discipleship lessons preceding baptism and of progressively revealed sacred teaching.
