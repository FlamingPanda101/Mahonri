# Is Mormonism Just an American Religion

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common assumption is that Mormonism is essentially an American — even a Utah — phenomenon. [[Kyle Beshears]] answers no: "approximately half of all Latter-day Saints live outside of the United States." Yet he qualifies the answer, judging that Mormonism still *struggles* to be a true "world religion." [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The demographic correction.** The stereotype is "somebody who lives in a suburb outside of Salt Lake City," but Latter-day Saints are "all over the globe," with more in South America than Asia. His memorable datum: "there are literally more Latter-day Saints in the state of Indiana than there are [in] the entire country of India." [^3] *Rating: vivid and substantially true* — LDS membership in India is tiny relative to its population.

2. **The "world religion" criterion is not size.** "Size doesn't really matter... there are less Jews than there are Southern Baptists" yet Judaism is a world religion. The real test is whether a religion "can exist and thrive in multiple cultures simultaneously" — Christianity, Islam (Morocco to Indonesia), Judaism. [^4] *Rating: a thoughtful, non-numerical definition.*

3. **Why Mormonism still struggles by that test.** "I think it exports a lot of norms from Utah... the Intermountain West," which "perpetuates the fact that when you think about Mormonism, you're thinking about America." [^5] *Rating: a fair, contestable observation* — critics inside the LDS church make the same "Utah cultural export" critique.

4. **Eschatology drives missiology.** "One's eschatology is one's missiology." Early LDS missions were *gathering* missions ("come here, leave Scandinavia, leave Britain") — converts were sent *to* America, the reverse of the standard indigenous-leadership model. He notes LDS missionaries reached the Pacific Islands before Florida. [^6] *Rating: a genuinely illuminating historical-theological link.*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A Latter-day Saint could argue the gathering theology has long since shifted to "build Zion where you are," that international temples and local leadership now anchor the church globally, and that exported "Utah norms" are no different from any global faith carrying a cultural matrix. Beshears's claim is comparative and tendency-level, not a denial of LDS global presence.

## Best Quotes

> "One's eschatology is one's missiology. So what you believe about the end times will affect how you behave in your activity as a church." [^6]
