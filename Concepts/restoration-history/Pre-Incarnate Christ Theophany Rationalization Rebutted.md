# Pre-Incarnate Christ Theophany Rationalization Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The most common Christian apologetic rationalization for OT theophanies is that those who "saw God" actually saw the **pre-incarnate Christ** — God the Son, the Logos/Word — not God the Father. Since John 1:18 says "no one has seen God" (the Father) but then says "the only begotten Son... he hath declared him," the argument goes that OT figures encountered the Son, while John's absolute prohibition applies only to the Father. This harmonizes the contradiction: they DID see God (the Son) and they did NOT see God (the Father).[^1]

## The Rebuttal — Why This Rationalization Fails

### 1. It Is Built on the Same Circular Presupposition

McClellan identifies this as the MOST common rationalization but emphasizes it shares the same fatal flaw as the limited-essence argument: "like the pre-incarnate vision of the Word rationalization, this is also built on a foundation of the presupposition of univocality. In other words, it's begging the question because the conclusion is built into the premise that supports the argument, and the conclusion is that the Bible doesn't contradict itself."[^2]

The apologist starts by assuming the Bible cannot contradict itself. Faced with two texts that appear to contradict, they invent a third entity (Christ as intermediary) to bridge them. But if you do not presuppose inerrancy, there is no need for the bridging entity — the texts simply reflect different theological stages. See [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]].

### 2. The OT Texts Never Identify the Being as "The Son" or "The Word"

"The people who wrote about Jacob and Moses and others seeing God face to face didn't have a problem with that."[^3] The authors of the OT theophany accounts believed they were describing encounters with YHWH/Elohim — not a second divine person who would only be identified centuries later. The Christophany reading is a retroactive reinterpretation, not something discoverable in the text on its own terms.

### 3. It Parallels the Early Christian / Scribal Pattern

McClellan connects this to a pattern visible in the Hebrew Bible's own editorial history: "This is related to the way early Christians would say, 'Oh, no, that's Jesus that they saw. It's not actually God.'"[^4] Just as later editors inserted *mal'ak* ("angel/messenger") into theophany accounts to soften the direct divine encounter, early Christians retrojected Christ into those same accounts. Both moves reveal discomfort with the original, more direct conception of divine-human encounter.

### 4. The "Invisible God" Is a Late Development, Not the Original Framework

The Christophany argument assumes the pre-existence of a Trinity framework in which the Father is invisible and the Son is the visible manifestation. But this framework postdates the OT by centuries. McClellan's historical reconstruction shows the opposite trajectory: "We went from the notion that God was anthropomorphic, corporeal, male-presenting and visible in the earliest parts of the Hebrew Bible through to the influence of Greek philosophy and the notion that deity is something that is distinct from the visible and the corporeal and the fleshly world."[^5]

The original conception was NOT of an invisible Father with a visible Son mediating encounters — it was of a single, visible, corporeal deity who could be directly encountered. The "invisible Father" is the innovation, not the baseline.

### 5. John 1:18's Own Logic Undermines the Christophany Reading

The closest the NT gets to offering a "seeing God through Jesus" framework is John 14:9 ("he that hath seen me hath seen the Father"). But McClellan argues this operates on ancient "divine image" logic: "It's not because Jesus is the Father. It's because Jesus bears the Father's presence just like a divine image. And so to look at Jesus is to look at God... You're not actually seeing God's own self."[^6]

In other words, even the NT's own framework for "seeing God through Jesus" is fundamentally different from the OT's direct, unmediated theophanies. The two operate on incompatible theological premises.
