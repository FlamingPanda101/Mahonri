# Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]]'s decisive move: when "literalism" forces a choice between two contradictory plain senses, the literalist must elevate one as "really literal" and reinterpret the other. The principle that picks the winner is *not in the Bible* — it is the reader's interpretive tradition. "Really what the authority is is not the Bible in its literal sense. The authority is your tradition and whatever interpretive tradition is propping it up." [^1] Inerrancy thus "demonstrates where the real authority rests — with the tradition... It does not rest with the Bible." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Contradictions force a non-textual tiebreaker.** "When you have two passages where a critical approach would say the literal sense of this one flatly contradicts the literal sense of this one... you've got to pick one. You say this one's really literal and the other is the figurative one." [^3] The selection criterion is supplied from outside the text. **(Strong.)**
2. **The "who decides" question has only one honest answer.** "Who gets to decide when it is or isn't? Well, obviously I do." [^4] The decider is the interpreter — i.e., Driscoll, or Franklin Graham, or Billy Graham, or whoever holds the mic. [^5] See [[There Is No Such Thing as a Biblical Literalist]]. **(Strong.)**
3. **Inerrancy dictates to the Bible.** The Chicago Statement "is dictating to the Bible what it is and is not allowed to be, to do, and to say... which demonstrates where the real authority rests." [^6] McClellan's example: a textual literalist must concede [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3]] and Exodus acknowledge rival gods, but tradition forbids it, so the texts get relabeled (demons, the devil). See [[Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods]]. **(Strong.)**
4. **It mirrors the Protestant adjudication problem.** This is the inerrantist version of the question "who is the final interpreter?" — the very problem sola scriptura claimed to solve by removing the church. See [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]] and [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]]. **(Strong cross-link.)**
5. **Steelman.** An inerrantist replies that adjudicating two apparently conflicting texts by the "analogy of Scripture" (letting clearer texts interpret obscurer ones) is a principled, text-internal method, not an external imposition. McClellan's rebuttal: deciding *which* text is "clearer" is itself the unexplained, tradition-driven judgment — the method names the problem rather than solving it, and in practice the "clearer" text is always the one that fits prior dogma. **(Moderate-to-strong — turns on whether "analogy of Scripture" is principled or circular.)**
