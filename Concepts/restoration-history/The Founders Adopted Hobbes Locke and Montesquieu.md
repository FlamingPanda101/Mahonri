# The Founders Adopted Hobbes, Locke, and Montesquieu

## The Claim

The intellectual genealogy of the Constitution runs through three Enlightenment political philosophers whose writings the founders had read — [[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]] → [[John Locke|Locke]] → [[Montesquieu]] — each refining social-contract theory toward the American model. Following [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s principle that ideas "don't appear in a vacuum," [[Brian Thomassen]] traces the lineage that culminates in the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution. All three reject the **divine right of kings** (itself a late, forgery-based medieval innovation).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Hobbes invents the social contract
Writing amid the English Civil War and the Thirty Years' War, Hobbes argues from a **state of nature** that is a "war of all against all" — life "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Rational self-preservation drives men to surrender self-government to a **sovereign** with the enforcing power, since "covenants without the sword are but words." The social contract "does not exist before him." Obligation lasts only as long as the sovereign can protect (so it is not absolutism).[^2]

### 2. Locke shifts to natural law, property, and the right to change government
Locke (context: the Glorious Revolution of 1688) holds that "natural man is free and equal," that self-protection and **property** predate the state ("Government has no other end but the preservation of property"), and — crucially, against Hobbes — that **the people retain the right to change their government** if it fails their interest. This is "embedded in those founding documents."[^3]

### 3. Montesquieu invents separation of powers
Montesquieu (context: the absolutism of Louis XIV/XV), a jurist and historian of Rome, argues "power should be a check to power" and invents the division of **executive, legislative, and judicial** — the checks-and-balances scheme the founders adopt directly.[^4]

### 4. The backdrop: divine right of kings is a late forgery
Thomassen argues the divine right of kings is *not* ancient continuity. Rome made no claim to divine kingship (the *imperator* ruled by brute force; [[Constantine]] himself won a civil war), so medieval Europe inherited a **gap**. The papacy filled it via the 8th-century **donation of Constantine** — a forgery (exposed by its medieval/dark-age Latin) — used to claim authority to crown Germanic kings (Pepin, Charlemagne; earliest case the Visigothic king Wallia, also 8th century). The Enlightenment thinkers "across the board" reject divine right as "bogus."[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** That the founders drew on Locke and Montesquieu is mainstream; the Hobbes-as-origin-of-the-social-contract claim is defensible but glosses earlier contractarian threads (e.g., natural-law and conciliar traditions). The donation of Constantine *is* a genuine medieval forgery (definitively exposed by Lorenzo Valla in 1440 on philological grounds — the talk gets the *mechanism* right but does not name Valla). The sweeping "Rome had no divine kingship" claim brackets the imperial cult and *divus* apotheosis, which the speakers set aside as not "divine right of kings" in the medieval sense.

## Best Quotes

> "The social contract is a Hobbesian idea. It does not exist before him. He invents this concept."[^2]

> "Montesquieu is going to be the fellow that invents the idea of separating the executive, the legislative and the judicial — that there has to be checks on power… which the founding fathers are going to adopt."[^4]
