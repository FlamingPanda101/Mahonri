# Subsistent Existence Itself - Ipsum Esse Subsistens

## The Point
The series of here-and-now imparters of existence cannot regress infinitely; it must terminate in a first cause that can impart existence without having existence imparted to it. [^1] Such a cause "can only be something the very essence of which is identical to existence, something which just is existence itself." [^2] Its existence is "built in"; it is not "merely one existing thing alongside others but what Thomas Aquinas calls 'subsistent existence itself'" — *Ipsum Esse Subsistens*. [^2] In God, essence and existence are not really distinct but **identical**: this is the doctrine of **divine simplicity**, the convertible flip-side of the whole proof. [^3]

## Evidence
Stage 2 unpacks what Subsistent Existence Itself must be: [^4]
- **Unique**, since (as shown) there can only be one thing whose essence is its existence; therefore everything else is essence-existence composite and depends on it, so it is "the cause of everything other than itself." [^5]
- **Necessary and uncaused**: since its very essence is existence, "it would not and indeed could not fail to exist," nor could it have its existence imparted to it. [^6]
- **Purely actual**: essence is to existence as potentiality is to actuality; a thing whose essence just *is* existence "could not have some potentiality for existence that needs to be actualized," so it is Pure Act. [^7] This makes the Thomistic terminus the *same one cause* as the Aristotelian proof's purely actual Unmoved Mover, "arrived at from different starting points." [^8]
- Hence it inherits all the divine attributes argued in chapter 1: "immutable, eternal, immaterial, incorporeal, perfect, omnipotent, fully good," plus (via proportionate causality, as cause of all things) "intellect and omniscience." [^9]

"Hence, that which is subsistent existence itself must be one, necessarily existing, the uncaused cause of everything other than itself, purely actual, immutable, eternal, immaterial, incorporeal, perfect, omnipotent, fully good, intelligent, and omniscient. It is, in short, God." [^10] Feser stresses this also disarms "What caused God?": God lacks a cause not as an arbitrary exception but because "there is no real distinction in him between essence and existence" — so He needs none and could not have one. [^11]

## Best Quotes
> "The first or primary cause in the present series ... can only be something the very essence of which is identical to existence, something which just is existence itself ... It is not merely one existing thing alongside others but what Thomas Aquinas calls 'subsistent existence itself'." [^2]

> "That which is subsistent existence itself must be one, necessarily existing, the uncaused cause of everything other than itself, purely actual, immutable, eternal, immaterial, incorporeal, perfect, omnipotent, fully good, intelligent, and omniscient. It is, in short, God." [^10]

> "The reason God does not have a cause is not that he is an arbitrary exception to this general rule, but rather that there is no real distinction in him between essence and existence." [^11]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism, *Ipsum Esse Subsistens* is the proof's triumphant payoff: a necessary, uncaused, all-causing ground of being — exactly the kind of self-existent first principle that blocks every "the cosmos is a brute fact" maneuver. But this conclusion is also the **sharpest single point of LDS divergence in the whole book.** To say God *is* Existence Itself, with essence and existence identical and no potentiality whatsoever, entails the full apparatus of **divine simplicity**: no parts, no body, no real internal distinctions, immutable and timeless. The Restoration confesses instead an **exalted, embodied** God the Father — "The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's" (D&C 130:22) — a personal Being who acts, relates, and is glorified in time, and alongside whom matter and intelligence are themselves eternal and uncreated (D&C 93:29). On the LDS reading, "God is Being Itself" is not the God of the Hebrew scriptures but a Hellenistic overlay (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]] and [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]); the identification of the demonstrated first cause with a *simple* God is precisely the inference [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]] contests. Be fair to Feser: granted his premises about essence/existence composition and pure actuality, the conclusion follows rigorously, and as a refutation of atheism it stands. The Restoration's quarrel is not with "there is a necessary first cause" but with "that cause must be metaphysically simple Being Itself rather than an exalted personal God."
