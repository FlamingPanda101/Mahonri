# Kenotic Christology - Divine Properties in Potentiality

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] defends a **kenotic Christology**: in becoming mortal the Son "empties himself" of his divinity ([[Philippians 2.9-11|Philippians 2]]), so that while incarnate he possesses the divine properties "only in potentiality," not in full actuality — remaining dependent on the Father for knowledge, power, and re-exaltation. Christ was genuinely divine on earth, but not *fully actualized* divine. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Kenosis* in Philippians 2 (rating: strong; textual/lexical)
The term is from the Greek verb *kenoo* in Philippians 2: "he empties himself of his divinity to become mortal." When the divine persons leave "the unity of this divinity, they have the divine properties only in potentiality. They still have them, but they're dependent on one another." [^1]

> "the term used in Philippians 2 is that he empties himself of his divinity to become mortal... they have the divine properties only in potentiality. They still have them, but they're dependent on one another." [^1]

### 2. The Son is dependent on the Father in the Gospels (rating: strong; textual)
"Christ is dependent on the father to express the properties of divinity. He's dependent on the father for his knowledge. It's stated no less than nine times in the gospel of John that it's the father who reveals to the son." He is also dependent on the Father "for his exaltation to become divine again. He can't just take it up again at will." [^2]

### 3. The infant analogy (rating: moderate; illustrative)
Asked whether Jesus was divine on earth, Ostler says "of course" — but distinguishes *mere* from *full* humanity/divinity. Newborns "are fully human" yet lack developed human properties (can't walk, can't do math); the properties exist "in potentiality," in "dynamic growth." Likewise Christ has "full divinity in potentiality, however not in actuality." [^3]

> "So I distinguish between mere humanity and full humanity. Full humanity is to be divine. Mere humanity is to have the properties that could be developed." [^4]

### 4. Properties are dynamic, not static Platonic forms (rating: moderate; metaphysical)
Ostler rejects the "Platonic notion of properties" as "static immutable timeless" possessions held all at once; to be human (or divine) is to be "in growth and in process." This connects to [[Divine Properties Are Dynamic Like Baseball Not Golf]]. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Classical Christology objects that strong kenoticism imperils the immutability and full deity of the Son: if Christ literally *divested* divine attributes (omniscience, omnipotence), then either God changed (against divine immutability) or the incarnate Son was not truly God during the incarnation — the very Nestorian/Arian dangers Chalcedon guarded against via the *communicatio idiomatum* (the Son retains the divine nature; the human nature is the locus of growth and death). Ostler answers by relocating the divine attributes in *potentiality plus relationship* rather than abandonment, and by rejecting the Platonic immutability premise outright. See [[Hypostatic Union]] and [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]].
