# Prōtē Could Mean "Before" Quirinius (Apologetic Objection)

## The Objection

Luke 2:2 uses the Greek word _prōtē_ (πρώτη), which can mean "first." Apologists argue it can also mean "earlier" or "before," and that the passage should be translated: "This was the registration _before_ Quirinius began to govern in Syria." This would resolve the chronological contradiction — a census occurred during Herod's reign, before Quirinius's later (well-known) census of 6 CE.[^1]

## Why It Fails

### 1. The grammar doesn't support this reading

McClellan explains: "_Prōtē_ — spelled slightly differently when it's used to mean 'earlier' or 'before' — is followed up immediately after by a noun or a participle or a phrase that is in the genitive case. And that is not what we see in Luke 2:2." The construction in Luke does not match the grammatical pattern that would be required for "before" to be the intended meaning.[^2]

### 2. Scholars reject it as apologetic special pleading

While McClellan acknowledges it's "not an utterly impossible reading," he states the scholarly consensus: "It is not a plausible reading and is clearly just apologetic in origin. It is just an attempt to sidestep the fact that Luke 2:2 would have to have this census and Jesus's birth dating to 6 CE, which directly contradicts Luke 1."[^3]

### 3. Even if accepted, it wouldn't solve the other historical problems

Even if _prōtē_ meant "before," the census mechanism itself remains historically impossible — no empire-wide Roman census required travel to ancestral hometowns, and Herod's client kingdom wasn't subject to direct Roman censuses (see [[Census of Quirinius Is Historically Unworkable]]).[^4]
