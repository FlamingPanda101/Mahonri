# Son of Perdition Requires the Highest Bar of Knowledge

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The second-most-quoted part of the [[King Follett Discourse]] defines the "son of perdition" who commits the unpardonable sin. Some members fear (and some critics allege) that ordinary people who leave the church are branded sons of perdition. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] rebuts this: the bar is so high that almost no one clears it, and Smith's warning was aimed at specific blood-seeking dissenters.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The "sun is shining" standard.** Smith: a man must "have the heavens opened to him and see God and Christ" and then deny it — "you have to see the sun shining and say that it's not there."[^2] Griffiths: "this is such a high bar, I don't know how anybody could ever clear it."[^3]
2. **The precise canonical text is [[D&C 76]].** Since King Follett is an amalgamation, Griffiths anchors the doctrine in section 76, where a son of perdition is one who would "crucify Christ afresh." He is "not even comfortable saying someone is a son of perdition... other than Cain."[^4]
3. **Aimed at a named faction, not generic ex-members.** Two and a half months before the martyrdom, the warning targeted [[William Law]] and the Foster and Higbee brothers — the future *Nauvoo Expositor* publishers — who "thirst for my blood." "You could make the case these ideas get him killed."[^5]
4. **A scaled standard of apostasy.** "There's a level of apostasy that's measured against the amount of light and knowledge that a person possesses." The "uncle who left and posts anti-Mormon memes... probably isn't denying that God and Jesus Christ still exist; they just have issues with the church." So the term must be used cautiously.[^6]
5. **Steelman of the critic.** A critic can note the rhetorical danger remains: a prophet publicly linking "many apostates of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints" to the unpardonable sin in a mass sermon predictably bleeds into how members treat all who leave — and Griffiths concedes "I feel like it's applied that way sometimes." The critic may also argue the convenient move is to retroactively narrow a sweeping public condemnation to a few villains once it proves harsh. Griffiths' answer is that the *textual* standard (D&C 76) is genuinely extreme and that Smith "didn't use it lightly," evidenced by the fact that the targets literally sought his life.
6. **Evidence rating.** Strong as exegesis of D&C 76 and good as historical contextualization of the 1844 setting; weaker as a guarantee against the pastoral misuse Griffiths himself admits occurs.

## Best Quotes

> "You have to see the sun shining and say that it's not there... this is such a high bar, I don't know how anybody could ever clear it."[^7]

> "I'm not even comfortable saying someone is a son of perdition that I know for sure, other than Cain."[^8]

> "We're talking about people who sought his life and wanted to spill his blood — that's how serious the stakes were."[^9]
