# Polygamy Raised Fertility by Raising the Marriage Rate

## The Claim

A common anti-Mormon soundbite holds that 19th-century plural marriage could not have served its stated purpose of "raising up seed" because plural wives bore *fewer* children than monogamous wives — so polygamy did not raise the birth rate. Demographer [[Stephen Cranney]] argues this is a unit error: the relevant variable is not children-per-wife but children-per-*society*, and polygamy raised the **marriage rate** to near-universal for women. In a 19th-century context where childbearing happened almost entirely within marriage, eliminating unmarried women raises total fertility even if each plural wife individually has fewer children. Cross-cultural data confirm that polygynous societies have higher fertility, so the pro-natal effect is, in his phrase, "ipso facto."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The critic's premise is true but irrelevant: per-wife fertility vs. societal fertility
Cranney concedes second and third wives tend to have fewer children than first or monogamous wives. But "the part of that equation they don't take into account is that it increases the marital rate" — and in the 1800s you were "only at risk of getting pregnant... if you're in a marital state." Removing unmarried women from the population raises aggregate births.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a demographic argument — the per-woman vs. per-population distinction is exactly the standard polygyny-fertility result.

### 2. Plural marriage made never-marrying women vanishingly rare
[[Kathryn Daynes]]'s study of Manti found essentially **one woman** who died single across the decades examined — i.e. the marriage rate for women approached 100%. Near-universal female marriage is the mechanism by which societal fertility rises.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a specific, attributed demographic finding directly supporting the marriage-rate mechanism.

### 3. Cross-cultural data: polygynous societies have higher fertility
"When you look at societies that practice polygamy and societies that don't... those that practice polygamy do in fact have more babies." Combined with the theoretical mechanism, Cranney calls the pro-natal conclusion "ipso facto."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong — broad comparative regularity; transfer to 1800s Utah specifically is an inference (see his own caveat below).

### 4. The data limitation is conceded honestly
Cranney stresses a clean causal proof is impossible: "you'd have to have... an experimental setup where we randomly assign people" to polygamy or not. The honest conclusion is "as much evidence as you could hope for" short of a randomized trial, not a definitive demonstration. The early-Mormon **Utah Population Database** (one of the best historical demographic datasets in the world, used by cancer geneticists) gives unusually precise data on wife-number, fertility, and endowment status.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on candor; the concession bounds the claim appropriately.

### 5. The stated purpose was openly scriptural, not hidden
The episode opens by rebutting the "secret reason" trope: [[Jacob 2.30|Jacob 2:30]] and [[Doctrine and Covenants 132.63|D&C 132:63]] state the purpose ("raise up seed," "bear the souls of men") in plain text, and the demographic finding is consistent with that purpose.[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on the textual point; the link from "stated purpose" to "achieved effect" is what the demography supplies.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can grant the demography and still object on relevance and ethics. (a) **The mechanism is the marriage rate, not polygamy as such:** monogamous societies can also achieve near-universal female marriage, so the result shows that *high nuptiality* raises fertility, not that *plural* marriage is uniquely or necessary pro-natal — the same total births could come from universal monogamy. (b) **Per-woman fertility decline is a real cost:** Cranney himself notes plural wives had fewer children, which complicates the "raise up seed" rationale at the individual level and raises questions about why the institution was needed if monogamy plus encouragement to marry would do. (c) **The cross-cultural correlation may be confounded:** polygynous societies differ from monogamous ones on many fertility-relevant variables (mortality, age at marriage, economy), so "polygyny → more babies" is not cleanly causal — which Cranney's own randomized-trial caveat concedes. (d) The whole framing measures polygamy by aggregate fertility and brackets the coercion and inequality concerns the vault treats elsewhere (see [[First Wives Could Be Coerced by the Law of Sarah]]). A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument is narrow and defensive — it only rebuts the specific "polygamy didn't even raise the birth rate" soundbite — and on that narrow question the theory and the data line up about as well as historical demography allows.

## Best Quotes
