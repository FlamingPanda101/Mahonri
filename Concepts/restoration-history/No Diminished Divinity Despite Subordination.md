# No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection (raised "for shock value"): "So you Mormons think that Jesus is a lesser god." [[Blake Ostler]] calls this the **problem of diminished divinity** — and answers that there is no diminished divinity whatsoever, even though the Son is subordinate to the Father in a specific, limited sense. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Son shares every divine property (rating: strong; systematic)
"Every single divine property that the father possesses, the son also possesses. Every divine property that the father possesses, the holy ghost also possesses." What is *required* to be fully divine "they all have in common." If "one essence" means the properties necessary to be divine, "they all share those equally." [^2]

> "there's no diminished divinity whatsoever. Every single divine property that the father possesses, the son also possesses." [^2]

### 2. The subordination is of source and honor, not kind (rating: strong; systematic)
In what sense is Jesus "lesser"? Only that "it's the father who initiates the relationship and the son gives all glory and honor to the father" — the Father delivers the kingdom to the Son ([[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Cor 15]]), exalts him ([[Philippians 2.9-11|Phil 2]]/Acts). "Honor and glory don't mean you're a different kind of being." See [[The Son Bears Yah but Never El Elyon]]. [^3]

### 3. Adoption analogy: no change of ontological order (rating: moderate; ANE)
"When a benefactor adopted someone as a son, it didn't mean that he was a different ontological order than the one he's adopting." So the Father's primacy "no more implies that in the Christian usage." [^4]

### 4. Brian Leftow's objection is named and answered (rating: moderate; scholarly)
Ostler notes the diminished-divinity argument was pressed by Brian Leftow against the social Trinity "promulgated by a number of Protestants," and that he answers it in chapter 9 of volume 3 of *Exploring Mormon Thought*. [^1]

### 5. The Son's mutual dependence is real but symmetrical in divinity (rating: moderate; systematic)
Even the Father is "as dependent on them as they are dependent on the father for their divinity, because he has to be loving and in loving relationships in order to be divine." So subordination of *honor* coexists with equality of *divinity*. See [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]]. [^5]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
The critic (e.g., Leftow, or a Nicene Trinitarian) argues that if the Son *derives* his divinity from the Father — is *made* divine, exalted, re-divinized after kenosis — then his divinity is contingent and derivative, hence *lesser in kind*, which is precisely Arian subordinationism the councils condemned; "equal in properties but unequal in source" allegedly cannot avoid a tiered deity. Ostler replies that *source* is not *kind*: each person is uncreated and possesses every property necessary for full divinity, so the asymmetry is one of relational honor and gratitude, not ontological rank. See [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]].
