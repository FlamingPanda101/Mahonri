# Even Lighthouse Ministries Doubted the Pace Memo's Credibility

## The Claim
The very anti-Mormon publication that broadcast the leaked Pace memo — [[Lighthouse Ministries]] — privately doubted its central claim, conceding that 45 alleged human sacrifices "would be very difficult to cover up." That a hostile witness balked is strong evidence the memo's content is not credible on its face.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The hostile source conceded implausibility (strong — adversarial admission)
On receiving the memo, "even they balked at Pace's hyperbole," writing: "Many rational people will have a difficult time believing the statement that 45 of the 60 victims allege witnessing and or participating in human sacrifice. Although we would not want to claim that this would be impossible, it does seem that it would be very difficult to cover up that many murders" [^1].

### 2. They published anyway for opportunistic reasons (strong — motive named)
"Lighthouse Ministries doubted the authenticity of the memo, but it was simply too good an opportunity to attack and embarrass the LDS Church to be skeptically dismissed." They published it "far and wide to peddle the narrative that seemingly peaceful Latter-day Saints were actually sacrificing and eating babies" [^2].

### 3. The cover-up problem is real and unanswered (moderate — argument)
The narrator agrees with Lighthouse's own logic — "Yes, it would be" difficult to hide that many murders — which dovetails with the funded crime unit's near-total failure to substantiate any such killings. See [[Ritual Abuse Crime Unit Found Ninety-Nine Percent Wild Goose Chases]] [^1].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A critic could argue that Lighthouse's caveat was narrow — doubting the *number* of undiscovered murders, not the existence of abuse — and that an opponent publishing damaging material does not make the material false; opponents publish true embarrassing documents all the time. Granted; the argument here is not that Lighthouse's hostility proves falsity, but that when even a motivated adversary, eager to wound the Church, pauses to flag a claim as something "rational people will have a difficult time believing," that is independent corroboration of the claim's prima facie implausibility — which the later evidentiary collapse confirmed.
