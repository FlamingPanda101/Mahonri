# Emma's Difficult Childbirth Is the Immediate Context of the Word of Wisdom

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] supplies a "deeper context" for the [[Word of Wisdom]] beyond the familiar tobacco-spitting story: the revelation ([[D&C 89]]) came in February 1833, **less than four months after** [[Emma Smith]] nearly died bearing [[Joseph Smith III]] and while she still struggled with her postpartum health. Read against that household crisis, the revelation's promises — "health in the navel, marrow in the bones," strength to "run and not be weary" — answer a concrete, present need, and likely required sacrifices of *both* Joseph and Emma.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The near-fatal labor (strong, biographical).** Emma had "an extremely difficult labor with Joseph III… so difficult that she nearly died"; Joseph, returning from Boston/New York, "just barely" missed the birth and came home to find her "close to death," struggling for months afterward.[^1]
2. **The loss of household help compounded it (moderate, biographical).** Around this time the Smiths' "house girl" died and another servant married and moved out, so Emma faced housework and a nursing infant in poor health — Joseph "watching Emma struggle day by day" is part of the revelation's setting.[^2]
3. **Likely caffeine dependence (moderate, inference).** Bradley infers Emma, a former Methodist teetotaler, was probably leaning on tea/coffee for energy during exhaustion — "a losing battle" because the body habituates — so the hot-drinks counsel addressed a real struggle, not an abstract rule.[^3]
4. **A shared sacrifice (interpretive).** Where the men of the School of the Prophets threw their pipes in the fire (the excavated pipe-stems corroborate the story), Emma was probably "making sacrifices too" — giving up caffeine — making the revelation a mutual household reform.[^2]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Emma-centered reading is a *plausible reconstruction*, not a documented cause: no source has Joseph saying the revelation was given because of Emma's health. The traditional Brigham Young account ties the Word of Wisdom to the tobacco mess in the council room, and Bradley's caffeine-addiction inference about Emma is explicitly speculative ("very good chance," "we don't know"). The chronological proximity could be coincidence.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley presents the childbirth context as *additional* to, not a replacement for, the School-of-the-Prophets setting, and grades his own claims (the labor is documented; Emma's caffeine dependence is inference). The proximity-plus-content fit (a health revelation arriving amid a health crisis, promising exactly bodily strength and marrow) is a reasonable abductive link even if not a proof — and it materially enriches how the revelation's *positive* promises are read. See [[The Word of Wisdom Is Positive Health Counsel Not Just a List of Prohibitions]].

## Best Quotes
> "Just four months before the Word of Wisdom is given… Emma had had a child… an extremely difficult labor… so difficult that she nearly died."[^1]

> "The Word of Wisdom promises health in the navel, marrow in the bones, the ability to run and not be weary and walk and not faint."[^1]
