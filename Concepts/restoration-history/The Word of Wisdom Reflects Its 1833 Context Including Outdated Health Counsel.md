# The Word of Wisdom Reflects Its 1833 Context Including Outdated Health Counsel

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] candidly grant that parts of [[D&C 89]] "fit comfortably into the 1833 context... but they're scientifically outdated now." Two examples: the verse calling tobacco "an herb for bruises, and all sick cattle" reflects 1830s folk-medicine poultices that modern veterinary and medical consensus rejects; and the prohibition of coffee and tea is not supported by current science, which finds moderate consumption harmless or beneficial. Their resolution: the Word of Wisdom's authority is not ultimately *scientific* but *spiritual* — a test of faith and obedience, given by a God who "speaketh unto men according to their language and to their understanding" (2 Nephi 31:3).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Tobacco for bruises/cattle has not aged well (rating: strong; the hosts concede it).** Griffiths "did a little deep dive" and reports modern consensus: topical tobacco on bruises causes skin irritation, introduces carcinogens, impairs healing; and "there is no use for tobacco in livestock treatment." The 1833 poultice practice was real but is now disavowed.[^1]
2. **Coffee and tea lack a clear health rationale (rating: strong; the hosts concede it).** "There is no credible medical evidence today that shows that moderate consumption of coffee or tea is harmful"; black/green tea and coffee are even linked to reduced cardiovascular disease and lower all-cause mortality. By contrast, the alcohol counsel "has aged really well" (modern "safe level... is zero").[^2]
3. **Coffee/tea function like pork did for ancient Israel (rating: moderate; analogical-theological).** Griffiths frames abstention as obedience without a scientific basis — "coffee and tea may be to us what pork was to the ancient Israelites" — and notes other Restoration churches (Community of Christ) drop the tea/coffee provision precisely because "they've taken that scientific consensus to be the Word of Wisdom."[^3]
4. **Divine accommodation explains the dated counsel (rating: moderate; interpretive).** As with biblical "four corners of the earth" cosmology, the Lord meets 1833 worldviews where they are — affirming the harmless folk practice (tobacco poultice) while nudging toward what matters (don't ingest tobacco). The "enduring value... lies in its call to temperance," good judgment, and trust in God.[^4]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic argues this concession quietly abandons the strongest historical apologetic for the Word of Wisdom (that it was prophetically ahead of the science) and replaces it with an unfalsifiable move: when a provision is vindicated (alcohol, tobacco's carcinogenicity), it proves divine foresight; when it is refuted (coffee/tea, tobacco poultices), it is reclassified as a non-scientific "test of obedience" or "accommodation." That asymmetry, the critic says, makes the revelation immune to disconfirmation — heads the prophet wins, tails the science doesn't count.

## Counter-Objection
The hosts' position is not ad hoc if the revelation never *claimed* a uniform scientific basis in the first place: verse 4 ties it to countering "evils and designs... of conspiring men" and the framing is "the word of wisdom," not a medical treatise. A standard given "according to their understanding" is *expected* to carry period-bound detail (tobacco poultices) alongside enduring principle (temperance), exactly as biblical revelation carries ancient cosmology without thereby teaching it as science (cf. [[Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology Not Modern Science]]). The genuinely awkward residue — coffee/tea abstinence with no health rationale — is owned, not hidden, and relocated to the faith/obedience register, which is internally coherent even if a skeptic finds it unpersuasive. The prophet-versus-text tension this raises is treated directly in [[The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom]].
