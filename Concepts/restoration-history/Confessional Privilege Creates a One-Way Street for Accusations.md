# Confessional Privilege Creates a One-Way Street for Accusations

## The Claim
Because LDS clergy swear not to reveal what is said in a confidential interview, accusations about what happened "in those confession rooms" are structurally *unanswerable*: the accuser can allege anything and stands to gain financially, while the bishop is bound to silence. The episode names [[John Dehlin]] as the exemplar — "he asked about my underwear" is "an excellent, unverifiable, unfalsifiable accusation" that can be "lobbed ad infinitum." The asymmetry of incentive (millions to gain on one side, a sworn oath on the other) makes confession-room accusations a one-way street.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Secrecy + incentive = unfalsifiable, profitable accusation (strong — mechanism)
"Since no one knows what actually happens in those confession rooms, he can always use that to his advantage." The accusation is "unverifiable, unfalsifiable," and it targets "local, neighborly, volunteer dads who are always inviting the parents to sit in on their youth interviews." The accuser cannot be contradicted because the only other witness is oath-bound [^1].

### 2. The incentive structure is explicitly lopsided (strong)
"These accusations are a one-way street. One party looks to gain millions of dollars by lobbing accusations, and the other side swore a holy oath not to say what goes on in there. That opens huge opportunity to pile on insinuation." The financial motive runs entirely toward fabrication or exaggeration [^2]. See [[The Satanic Panic Was Driven by Financial and Reputational Incentives]].

### 3. Wealth plus reach guarantees a steady supply of suits (strong)
"As long as the church is wealthy, and as long as it is involved in regular people's lives, it will be the target of lawsuits." The episode caricatures the climate: even bystanders ("the Monopoly guy") get sued from every direction; sponsored law-firm links dominate searches for "LDS Church and abuse," "hungry to sling mud … and hope to get some money back" [^3]. This is the same incentive engine documented in [[The Satanic Panic Was Driven by Financial and Reputational Incentives]].

### 4. The confidentiality is a feature for victims, exploited by bad actors (moderate)
Confessional privilege exists so that abusers will actually confess and can be urged to stop — "having hundreds of thousands of … volunteer grown men bishops … urging people to stop abusing is an objectively good thing" (see [[The Abuse Helpline Routes Bishops to Legal Compliance Not Cover-Up]]). The same confidentiality that enables that pastoral leverage is what a litigant can exploit, knowing the bishop cannot rebut [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
The strongest counter: confessional privilege can genuinely shield abusers, real victims are routinely disbelieved and the "she's just after money" frame is a classic tool for silencing them, and the fact that an accusation is *unfalsifiable* does not make it *false* — clergy abuse in closed settings does happen. Treating every confession-room allegation as a cash grab risks re-victimizing the abused. This is a serious objection. The rebuttal does not claim such accusations are always false; it claims the *combination* of guaranteed secrecy and large financial upside creates a structural hazard that makes uncorroborated confession-room claims weak *as evidence*, especially where (as throughout this series) the broader pattern is recovered-memory contamination and incentive-driven litigation. The point is evidentiary asymmetry, not blanket dismissal.

## Counter-objection: "then abolish the privilege"
Removing clergy confidentiality, the episode argues, would push real abusers "deeper into the shadows" and forfeit the leverage volunteer bishops have to urge them to stop (see [[The Reporting Catch-22 and the Johnson Case]]). Whether the protective value of the privilege outweighs its exploitability is the genuine policy debate the series concedes is "a debate to be had."
