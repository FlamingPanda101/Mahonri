# Mistakes Versus Leading Astray

## The Point
[[Jeremy Runnells]] mocks the "ridiculous and inconsistent 187-year track record" of Latter-day Saint prophets and asks why anyone would "follow the prophet." Fife answers that the criticism smuggles in a false premise — *prophetic infallibility* — that the Church has never taught. Once that premise is dropped, the real distinction is between *making mistakes* (which prophets do) and *leading the Church astray* (which is the actual promise). His parallel: a critic could just as easily ridicule the "187-year track record" of the scientific method.

## Evidence
1. **The Church explicitly teaches fallibility.** Fife stacks quotations: [[Dieter F. Uchtdorf]] ("there have been times when members or leaders... have simply made mistakes"); D&C 3:6-9 where God reproves Joseph; and [[George Q. Cannon]] flatly calling Brigham Young "fallible... He was not infallible." The Book of Mormon title page concedes "if there are faults they are the mistakes of men."[^1]
2. **The priesthood ban as line-upon-line.** Two months after the 1978 revelation, [[Bruce R. McConkie]] said: "Forget everything that I have said... that is contrary to the present revelation... We spoke with a limited understanding and without the light and knowledge that now has come."[^2]
3. **The science parallel.** Fife rewrites Runnells's paragraph in the voice of a science skeptic, "Mr. Crunnells," ridiculing the scientific consensus for its discarded theories (Pluto-as-planet, geocentrism, spontaneous generation, phrenology, Aristotelian physics). The point: a track record of correction is a feature of an honest truth-seeking enterprise, not a disqualifier.[^3]
4. **Woodruff and the "parent" analogy.** Against the much-quoted Wilford Woodruff line that the Lord "will never permit" the President to lead the Church astray, Fife relays BYU professor Anthony Sweat's illustration: parents of seven children make countless mistakes, yet the children would all say their parents never led the family astray.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "God calls flawed instruments who make mistakes to do His work." [^1]

> "So, the critic asks, 'Follow a prophet, even though he may be wrong sometimes?' My answer? Yes. That's exactly what I'm saying. The risk is worth it, just like it's worth the risk of continuing the scientific method." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The note operationalizes the broader Latter-day Saint teaching that God works through fallible servants (see [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]] and [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]]). Fife adds the vehicle metaphor: when a critic finds a defect "in the vehicle," the demand to abandon the Church is like demanding he "run it off a cliff" with his family inside — when the vehicle still "reliably point[s] the saints to the true hero, Jesus Christ."[^4]
