# The Book of Daniel Was Written in the Second Century BCE

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] reports (per the session summary) the scholarly consensus that the [[Daniel|Book of Daniel]] is the **youngest book in the Hebrew Bible**, written in the **160s BCE** during the Maccabean Revolt against [[Antiochus IV Epiphanes]] — not in the Babylonian exile (6th century BCE) where it is *set*. Daniel was deliberately written to **look** as though it dated to the exile centuries earlier, but its detailed apocalyptic "prophecies" were aimed at giving hope to **2nd-century-BCE Jews** living under Seleucid persecution. The predictions are therefore *vaticinium ex eventu* — "prophecy after the fact" (see [[Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment]]).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Near-consensus dating.** McClellan notes (per the summary) that "the vast majority of scholars agree Daniel is the youngest book in the Hebrew Bible." The standard grounds: Daniel's "prophecies" of successive empires track history accurately down to the reign of Antiochus IV and then **become inaccurate precisely at the point of his death** — the signature of prophecy written *during* the events it describes and failing only where it ventures a genuine prediction.
2. **Linguistic and canonical placement.** Daniel contains late Hebrew and Aramaic and stands in the **Ketuvim (Writings)** of the Tanakh, not among the Prophets — consistent with a book that entered the tradition late (cf. the late textual horizon in [[The Oldest Biblical Texts Are Archaic Poems Embedded in Later Narrative]]).
3. **It fits the broader pattern of crisis-driven development.** The same Seleucid persecution that produced Daniel is, on McClellan's account, the engine behind the Hellenistic-era emergence of resurrection and afterlife judgment — [[Daniel 12|Daniel 12]]'s resurrection language is exhibit A. See [[Divine Punishment in the Afterlife Emerged as Theodicy Under Imperial Oppression]]. Daniel is not an isolated late book but part of a wave of apocalyptic literature responding to imperial oppression.
4. **The function is pastoral, not predictive.** Pseudonymous attribution to an ancient sage and the back-dated frame gave the book authority; its purpose was to assure persecuted Jews that the empire crushing them was already written into a divinely ordained sequence nearing its end.

**Steelman of the traditional view.** Conservatives defend a 6th-century, exilic Daniel: they argue the late Aramaic and Hebrew features are not decisive, that the historical "errors" critics cite (e.g. Belshazzar, Darius the Mede) admit harmonizations, and that ruling out *genuine* predictive prophecy by methodological fiat begs the question against the supernatural. On this view the precision of Daniel's forecasts is evidence *for* inspiration, not for late composition. McClellan's reply (per the summary) is the standard critical one: the evidence — linguistic profile, canonical placement, and especially the prophecy that is exact up to Antiochus and wrong about his end — is best explained by 2nd-century composition; appealing to genuine prediction to rescue the early date is precisely the dogma-over-data move the rest of his argument resists (see [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
This cuts hard against apologetics that cite Daniel's fulfilled "prophecies" (the succession of empires, the seventy weeks) as proof of supernatural foreknowledge. If the book was composed in the 160s BCE, those "predictions" are descriptions of the past dressed as prophecy, and the one stretch that is genuinely forward-looking (Antiochus's death) misfires. An apologetic that leans on Daniel's predictive accuracy must first win the dating argument on linguistic and historical grounds, not assume the exilic date.

## LDS Relevance
The point is double-edged and worth stating carefully. (a) Methodologically it reinforces the Restoration-friendly principle that texts are dated by evidence and shaped by their communities' crises — the same critical apparatus that underwrites the Deuteronomistic-reform reading the vault uses for the Book of Mormon. (b) But it also models a *vaticinium ex eventu* objection that critics turn against **Joseph Smith's** prophecies, where the worry is detail added after the outcome (see [[Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment]]); Latter-day Saints who accept the critical case on Daniel should hold their own prophetic-fulfillment claims to the same contemporaneous-attestation standard. McClellan, non-confessional, draws no LDS conclusion; the relevance is that the *method* he applies to Daniel is one the tradition must apply evenhandedly.
