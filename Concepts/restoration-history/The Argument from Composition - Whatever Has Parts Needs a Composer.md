# The Argument from Composition - Whatever Has Parts Needs a Composer

## The Point
The starting point of Feser's Neo-Platonic proof is that everything in our experience is *composite* — made of parts. [^1] A composite is in one sense less fundamental than its parts, because it cannot exist unless its parts exist and are combined in the right way: a chair scattered into legs, frame, and seat is no chair at all. [^1] Crucially, a composite cannot be the cause of its own parts being unified, for that would be "lifting itself up by its own metaphysical bootstraps" — an explanatory vicious circle. [^2] Therefore every composite thing requires a cause *outside itself* that holds its parts together. [^2]

## Evidence
- A whole "cannot exist unless the parts exist and are combined in the right way"; this dependence is not merely temporal but *atemporal* — true at every moment the thing exists, even if it had existed forever. [^1]
- The composite cannot cause its own composition, on pain of an "explanatory vicious circle, insofar as the existence of the whole would depend on the existence and arrangement of the parts, and the existence and arrangement of the parts would depend on the existence of the whole." [^2]
- The relevant causal series is *hierarchical*, not linear: the parts' causal power is derivative (like a stick's power to push deriving from the hand), so the dependence runs here-and-now, not backward in time. [^3]
- The principle is fully general: it holds for ordinary physical parts (legs, screws), for non-objects (temperature), and even for *metaphysical* parts like Aristotelian form/matter or Thomist essence/existence, "applying whatever the parts are of which a thing is composed." [^4]
- Feser rebuts the alternatives: a *further part* uniting the parts only pushes the problem back a step; calling the unity a "brute fact" is offering *no* explanation rather than a rival one; and appeals to laws of nature merely yield another composite (the things plus the law) whose composition again needs explaining. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "a composite depends on its parts not merely (and indeed not necessarily always) in a temporal sense, but more fundamentally (and always) in an atemporal sense." [^1]

> "The chair would be lifting itself up by its own metaphysical bootstraps, as it were." [^2]

> "the principle that whatever is composite has a cause is completely general, applying whatever the parts are of which a thing is composed." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this is a powerful argument: it shows that the existence of ordinary composite things here and now cannot be self-explanatory and points beyond the material order to a sustaining cause — undercutting the claim that the universe is a brute, uncaused given. Latter-day Saints can welcome that the cosmos depends on God as its ongoing source and sustainer. The divergence appears at the *terminus* (see [[The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One]] and [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]): Feser concludes the first cause must be *absolutely without parts*, whereas Restoration theology confesses a God "of body, parts, and passions" (D&C 130:22), embodied and within the framework of co-eternal realities — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]. The composition argument also presupposes [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation from nothing]] as its backdrop, which LDS theology rejects in favor of organization from pre-existing matter and intelligence.
