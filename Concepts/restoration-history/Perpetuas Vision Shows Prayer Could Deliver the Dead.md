# Perpetuas Vision Shows Prayer Could Deliver the Dead

## The Point
[[Vibia Perpetua]], martyred at Carthage in AD 203, recorded in her prison diary (*The Passion of Perpetua and Felicity*) a vision in which her **prayers delivered her dead brother Dinocrates** from suffering. The account is a vivid early case of *visionary redemption* — efficacious work performed by the living on behalf of the dead, here by a woman, for a child who died unbaptized of disease.

## Evidence
1. **The conviction to intercede.** Perpetua: "And I knew at once that I was worthy and that I ought to intercede on his behalf. And I began to pray for a long time for him and to cry out to God" [^1].
2. **The first vision — suffering.** She sees Dinocrates, who died at seven of a facial skin eruption, emerging from "a dark place where others were also suffering," hot and thirsty, his wound still festering, unable to reach a high pool of "waters of life" [^2].
3. **The second vision — relief.** After praying day and night: "I saw that place I had seen before, and Dinocrates was clean, well dressed, and looked refreshed. Where he had been wounded, I saw a scar." He drinks and begins to play "as children do" [^3].
4. **Interpretation.** Perpetua "realizes that her brother was freed from pain and had received a kind of salvific deliverance because of her prayers and petitions to God" [^3].
5. **Context of martyr authority.** This intercessory power is tied to the special status of the soon-to-be martyr; the vision "gives Perpetua an abiding resolve … to act on behalf of another, even unto martyrdom" [^3].

## Best Quotes
> "And I knew at once that I was worthy and that I ought to intercede on his behalf. And I began to pray for a long time for him and to cry out to God." [^1]

> "I saw that place I had seen before, and Dinocrates was clean, well dressed, and looked refreshed. Where he had been wounded, I saw a scar." [^3]

## LDS Connection
A second- to third-century, first-person Christian testimony that the dead are *not beyond help* — that prayer by the living changes the postmortem condition of the dead. This aligns with the LDS conviction (D&C 138) that the dead exist in a state where the labors of the living and the gospel can still reach them. The case of Dinocrates (an unbaptized child) also intersects with early debates over infant salvation (see [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]]).
