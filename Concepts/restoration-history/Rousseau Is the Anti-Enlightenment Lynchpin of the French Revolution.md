# Rousseau Is the Anti-Enlightenment Lynchpin of the French Revolution

## The Claim

Where the Constitution descends from the Enlightenment ([[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]]–[[John Locke|Locke]]–[[Montesquieu]]), the **French Revolution** descends from [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Rousseau]], whom [[Brian Thomassen]] calls "the theoretical lynchpin behind the French Revolution" and "an anti-Enlightenment figure" who "rejects it whole cloth" for something "far more akin to romanticism." The two revolutions thus have opposite intellectual parentage — and that is the deep reason the Restoration could occur in America but not France. See [[America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration]].[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Romanticism replaces the Enlightenment
The Enlightenment (English/French) ends and is replaced by **Romanticism** (German-rooted: *Sturm und Drang*, Goethe's *Werther*, then Blake/Shelley/Byron). Per [[Isaiah Berlin]], Romanticism is "a longing for the unbounded and the indefinable… an effort to return to the long-forgotten sources of life" — a **rejection of rationality** and propositional truth for the intuitive. Rousseau belongs to this anti-rational stream, not the Enlightenment.[^2]

### 2. Rousseau's core moves are anti-Enlightenment
- **Reason rejected for intuition** — "the herd of the textbook authors" are lost; truth is found by returning to the roots.
- **Property as the root of corruption** (against Locke) — "The first man who… enclosed a piece of ground… and found people simple enough to believe him was the real founder of civil society"; property is "the source of inequity and corruption."
- **The noble savage** — man stripped of civilization's artifice; "our minds have been corrupted in proportion as the arts and sciences have improved."
- **Natural rights supplanted by the general will** — sovereignty resides in the general will, which is community-specific.[^3]

### 3. "Forced to be free" and the noble lie
The general will needs a "great soul of the legislator… who can guide the common herd," who "of itself the people always wills the good, but… by no means always sees it." So the legislator may use "emotion, manipulation, awe, rhetoric, religion… the noble lie" for the majority's good — and "one can be forced to be free." [[Maximilien Robespierre|Robespierre]] (who carried Rousseau in his vest pocket) drew the conclusion: "Subdue by terror the enemies of liberty and you will be right."[^4]

### 4. The line Rousseau → Hegel → Marx, and the terror
The general-will logic ("you don't know what's good for you; I know what's good for you") is "the foundation of Marx" — false consciousness — traceable "from Rousseau through Hegel to Marx." Its political fruit is the Reign of Terror and, finally, **[[Napoleon]]** ("What is a throne? … In political terms, I am the throne," 1813), echoing Louis XIV's "I am the state" — a full circle back to absolutism, "with a lot of dead bodies in between." Contrast [[George Washington]]'s relinquishing of power.[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** The reading of Rousseau as a font of illiberal/utopian politics, and the Rousseau→Robespierre and Rousseau→Hegel→Marx genealogies, are a recognizable (often conservative) interpretive tradition — defensible but contested. Many scholars read Rousseau's "forced to be free" and general will more charitably (as democratic self-legislation, not totalitarian license), and historians dispute any single-thinker cause of the Terror. Thomassen also uses this explicitly to fault [[Steven Pinker]]'s *Enlightenment Now* for "fail[ing] to fully account for" Rousseau's anti-Enlightenment character. The claim is strongest as *intellectual genealogy* and weakest if taken as a mono-causal explanation of the Revolution's violence.
