# The Nauvoo Legion Was Disarmed and Outside Carthage's Jurisdiction

## The Claim
A natural question about the Carthage martyrdom is: why didn't the **Nauvoo Legion** — the Latter-day Saint militia loyal to [[Joseph Smith]] — protect the jail? [[Shannon Tracy]] gives three reasons. (1) **Jurisdiction:** the Legion was the militia of Nauvoo; Carthage lay outside its authority, so marching an armed force there would have been invading "foreign territory." (2) **Disarmament:** Governor [[Thomas Ford]], escorting Joseph, had the Legion's government-issued arms collected and the Legion disbanded. (3) **Joseph's own order:** he had directed the Legion to stand down and stay in reserve, foreseeing that an armed defense would trigger a "civil war" in Carthage costing far more lives. Tracy ties this to Joseph's vision that his death — but only his death — would satisfy the mob and spare the Saints.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The jurisdiction point is historically grounded.** The Nauvoo Legion was a chartered city militia; deploying it into Hancock County's seat against the state's own forces would itself have been an act of insurrection — exactly the pretext the anti-Mormons sought. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong — coheres with the documented political situation around the Nauvoo Charter.)[^1]
2. **Disarmament by the governor is documented.** Ford did collect the Legion's state-issued weapons; an unarmed, disbanded militia could not have mounted an organized rescue even had it wished to. (Evidence rating: moderate — Tracy's summary tracks the standard history.)[^1]
3. **Joseph's stand-down order frames it as deliberate sacrifice.** The episode connects the non-defense to Joseph's "lamb to the slaughter" resolve and his stated belief that his blood would buy the Saints temporary safety. See [[Joseph Smith Predicts His Own Martyrdom]]. (Evidence rating: moderate as motive; the prophetic framing is faith-claim, the tactical logic is plausible.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is partly an apologetic tidying of an awkward fact — that the prophet's own people did not save him. A critic can note the explanations are also self-serving: "he ordered them to stand down" converts a failure to protect into a noble choice, and the providential gloss ("his blood would satisfy them") is unfalsifiable. One could also argue the Legion's inaction reflected the genuine military hopelessness of the Saints' position more than a serene sacrificial plan. The fair, narrow residue is the structural fact: the Legion was a *city* militia, it had been disarmed by state authority, and Carthage was outside Nauvoo — so there was no lawful, armed body positioned to defend the jail, independent of how one reads Joseph's intentions.
