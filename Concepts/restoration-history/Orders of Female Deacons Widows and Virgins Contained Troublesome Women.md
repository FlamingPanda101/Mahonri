# Orders of Female Deacons Widows and Virgins Contained Troublesome Women

## The Point
As women's older leadership was absorbed by the male clergy, [[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] shows that **new** female orders — deacons, widows, virgins — emerged in the 2nd–4th centuries. But these orders functioned largely to bring "potentially troublesome single women … under the patriarchal authority of the bishop." Female deacons were confined to tasks of propriety; widows and virgins were mostly symbols of virtue, restricted by long lists of prohibitions.

## Evidence

### 1. Female deacons limited to women's modesty needs
The *Teachings of the Apostles* (Didascalia, early 3rd c., Syria) places female deacons in the clerical order but restricts them to functions preserving female modesty: chaperoning women who approach the bishop, seating women at the women's entrances, visiting women's homes where male deacons could not go, and **anointing women's bodies at baptism** (since baptism was performed naked) before the bishop performed the rite "with sobriety." They are forbidden to baptize or teach, "only to pray and hear the teachers." Female deacons are "honored … as the type of the Holy Spirit, doing or saying nothing apart from the [male] deacon." [^1]

### 2. Widows: honor without action
A few 2nd-c. Roman inscriptions attest an order of widows in the West, but the office was largely "a position of honor, more than one of action or leadership." The *Apostolic Tradition* (attributed to [[Hippolytus of Rome]]) says widows are *not* ordained by laying on of hands "because a widow does not offer the Eucharist nor does she have a 'sacred ministry'"; her "only duty is to pray." [^2]

### 3. The long list of accusations against widows
In the East, *Teachings of the Apostles* assigns widows only prayer, yet levels a "strikingly long list of accusations" — gossip, greed, embezzlement, usury, complaining. Widows are told to be meek, stay home, and are "specifically prohibited from answering religious questions, baptizing … praying over or laying hands upon anyone." Laughton's key inference: **this very condemnation proves** that women were in fact teaching, ministering, healing, baptizing, and managing donations — the text exists "to marginalize competitors for leadership." [^3]

### 4. Asceticism tightens the requirements
The 4th-c. *Apostolic Constitutions* (Syria) now requires female deacons to be virgins or once-married widows, forbids ordaining virgins, and bars any deaconess from blessing at the altar — her only roles are "to watch the doors and help the priests in the baptism of women 'for the sake of decency.'" [^4]

## Best Quotes
> these orders "brought potentially troublesome single women — especially widows and virgins — under the patriarchal authority of the bishop." [^1]

> the condemnation "suggests that even in the third century, women in some churches were actively engaged in teaching the gospel, ministering, visiting the sick … baptizing" — i.e., the prohibitions document the practice they forbid. [^3]

## LDS Connection
For [[Latter-day Saints]], this is a textbook case of using church-order documents *against the grain*: the prohibitions are evidence of robust female ministry being suppressed, supporting the chapter's thesis that women's authentic authority was real and was institutionally constrained — not that it never existed. [^3]
