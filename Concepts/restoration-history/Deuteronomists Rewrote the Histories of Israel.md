# Deuteronomists Rewrote the Histories of Israel

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] argues that a scribal-and-lawyerly party — the **Deuteronomists** — gained influence in Jerusalem from c. 750 BC, peaked under the boy-king [[Josiah]] (the reform of 623 BC), purged the temple of its older symbols, and then "told the history as they wanted it told." On this reading the books of Samuel and Kings are a slanted record: the kings who agreed with the reformers are "good kings," those who kept the old ways are "bad kings." The old believers (Isaiah's circle) had "no good word at all" for them and called them "the apostate generation."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A long campaign, not a single event (rated: Barker's reconstruction; mainstream that a Deuteronomistic layer exists).** The party "really come into written history in about 750 BC," constantly campaigning to make Jerusalem's religion "more in accordance with what they think religion should be like," removing and (between reforming kings) others restoring the temple's traditional symbols — a "to-ing and fro-ing" in Judah between "simple religion almost like Protestants" and "the old ways like the old Catholics."[^1]
2. **Editorial slander of the angel-priests (rated: Barker; philological).** Those who wrote the accounts "were so horrified by what they were describing… that it was their style not to use the actual words," so when describing the temple's angel-priests they used a near-homonym that "actually meant a male prostitute." The Deuteronomists "didn't like the idea of angels at all," dropped the title "Lord of hosts" for "Lord," and removed the lady of the temple, the angel-priests, the teaching that God could be seen and become incarnate, and the women weaving the priests' garments.[^2][^3]
3. **History rewritten by the victors (rated: analogical argument).** "If you go to any communist state the first thing they do is rewrite history… when the regime changes the history books change." Barker grants the old (pre-reform) telling "was a bit selective" too — the historian must "pick your way between the two" — and says the Dead Sea Scrolls and other ancient texts now let us "pick up what the old believers actually thought."[^4]
4. **The histories use Deuteronomy's vocabulary (rated: mainstream).** The companion video on the Documentary Hypothesis makes the same point from the source-critical side: whoever edited Joshua–Kings "seems to have been a big fan of Deuteronomy and he uses the vocabulary of Deuteronomy and the ideas of Deuteronomy" to edit the histories. See [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins]].

**Steelman of the critic.** Mainstream scholarship affirms a Deuteronomistic editorial layer in the Former Prophets (Noth's Deuteronomistic History) and a Josianic-era reform — but most scholars do *not* accept Barker's fuller reconstruction (a suppressed first-temple cult of the divine mother, angel-priests, and incarnation) as established history; it is an influential **minority thesis**. A conservative reading also resists the claim that the reform *corrupted* rather than *purified* Israelite worship. The honest frame is that the editorial layer is consensus while Barker's content-claims about what was purged are contested.

## Best Quotes
> "Josiah's people or their heirs, they were the ones who wrote the books of Samuel and Kings… and they told the history as they wanted it told."[^1]

> "The people that the old believers called the apostate generation are the people who compiled many of the books of the Old Testament, particularly the history books. So we've only got the history as they wanted it told."[^4]
