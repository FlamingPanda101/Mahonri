# Polygamy Was a Minority Practice That Declined Steadily

## The Claim
The popular image of a wholly polygamous nineteenth-century Utah is false. Using [[Kathryn Daynes]]'s demographic study of Manti, [[Britney Chapman Nash]] shows that even at its peak, fewer than half of community members belonged to a plural family, and the share fell sharply every decade afterward; most polygamous men had only two wives.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The data set.** No single national number exists (the records are too vast and incomplete), so the best evidence is localized: [[Kathryn Daynes]]'s *More Wives Than One* treats Manti, Utah as "a representative population of general trends," tracking it across roughly 40 years.[^2] *Rating: strong — a peer-reviewed quantitative study; Nash is candid that it is a local proxy, not a global census.*

2. **Peak, then steady decline.** The 1860 federal census shows "43.1% of Manti residents belonged to a plural family either as a husband, wife or child" — "almost half... at its height." It then fell to 36% (1870), 25.1% (1880), and 7.1% (1900); the 1890 census burned. The peak was early — the practice was announced in 1852, peaked in Manti about 1855–57, and "after 1860 it was a steady decline."[^3] *Rating: strong — concrete census percentages tied to a published study.*

3. **Most polygamists had only two wives.** "Most men that married polygamously just had two wives" — the image of "all men had a dozen or more wives... wasn't the case." Counting men, women, and children together, the percentage of *women* in plural families would run somewhat higher than the headline figure (since one man marries several women).[^4] *Rating: moderate–strong — the two-wives norm is well attested; the gender-adjustment is a correct methodological caveat Nash raises herself.*

4. **Yet it touched many lives cumulatively.** Over the whole period, "tens of thousands" were part of a plural family at some point, and even monogamous families likely had polygamous relatives — so it was "normal... part of the fabric of society" without ever being the majority arrangement at any one time.[^5] *Rating: moderate — a fair both-and framing reconciling "minority practice" with "culturally pervasive."*

5. **A second community confirms the peak-then-monogamy-minority pattern.** A separate [[Keystone]] video cites a demographic snapshot of *three different wards in Salt Lake City* at polygamy's height: in 1860, 44% of women were married polygamously, 36% monogamously, and 20% were unmarried — so even at the peak "there were still plenty of men and women who chose monogamy." Notably, polygamy was *least* common in the ward composed of 95% immigrants.[^6] *Rating: moderate–strong — an independent locality corroborating the Manti figures and the "not everyone practiced it" point.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic notes that 43% at peak is not a trivial fringe, that a single community (Manti) may not represent leadership circles or other settlements where rates differed, and that focusing on the *declining* curve can understate how central and mandatory-feeling polygamy was during the 1850s "Reformation" zeal. The disciplined conclusion: the statistics decisively refute the "everyone was polygamous" myth and document a real, steady decline, while conceding that at its height plural marriage was a substantial minority — not a rare exception — and that one town's numbers generalize only cautiously.
