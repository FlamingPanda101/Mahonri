# Satanic Panic Is a Recurrence of Salem-Style Hysteria

## The Claim
The 1980s "satanic panic" — a wave of accusations of satanic ritual abuse (SRA) — was not a discovery of real underground devil-worship but a recurrence of the same mass hysteria that produced the 1692 Salem witch trials. The pattern is: a few unverifiable accusers, a frightened public that abandons "innocent until proven guilty," and unprovable "spectral" evidence that ruins lives. Decades later the same panic reached the Latter-day Saints in Utah.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Salem as the template (strong; well-documented history)
In 1692 two girls, aged 9 and 11, shouted accusations of devil worship at townsfolk. Hysteria "strangled the rationale of the colonists," and out of a town of 500, **19 were hanged and one pressed to death — 4% of the population** — on spectral evidence no one could prove [^1]. The structural ingredients (young accusers, performative "buzzwords," suspended due process) recur in every later panic.

### 2. The 1980s reproduced the same structure (strong)
Three centuries later, "the same specter of fear arose over the mountains of Salt Lake City." SRA accusations were leveled at lay clergy and high-ranking church officials alike; "people have been imprisoned, families shattered, and lives ruined all over again" [^2]. The episode poses the test question: is it real this time, "or is this just the echoes of a satanic panic still reverberating through what locals call Zion?" [^3].

### 3. A primed cultural powder keg (moderate; cultural-history claim)
The episode lists the 1980s conditions: horror cinema brought home by VCRs (*The Exorcist*, *Rosemary's Baby*, *The Omen*), Dungeons & Dragons and MTV scares, milk-carton missing children (Etan Patz, 1979), and the October 1979 Fall River murders alleged to be a satanic ritual. "America was a powder keg ready to ignite" [^4][^5]. The D&D suicide/violence fear is rated by the episode as "completely untrue" — face-to-face teen gamers actually had *lower* rates [^6].

### Steelman of the opposing view
A defender of the SRA claims would argue Salem and the 1980s are disanalogous: modern cases involved trained professionals, medical examinations, and hundreds of children, not two girls. The rebuttal (see [[Suggestive Interviewing Induces False Allegations]] and [[McMartin Preschool Trial Produced No Convictions]]) is that the professionals' methods *manufactured* the testimony, and that volume of accusers is exactly what mass hysteria predicts.
