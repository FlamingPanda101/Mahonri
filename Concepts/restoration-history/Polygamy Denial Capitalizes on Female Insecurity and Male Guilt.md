# Polygamy Denial Capitalizes on Female Insecurity and Male Guilt

## The Claim
[[Selene Anderson]] argues that the Latter-day Saint polygamy-denial movement (e.g. Michelle Stone's "132 Problems") has gained traction not because the historical evidence favors it but because it **exploits an emotional substrate** — "women's insecurity and men's guilt surrounding this topic." Because the Church has been "reticent" to discuss plural marriage, it has "allowed fundamentalists to rule the conversation," leaving members to treat fundamentalist polygamy as the template.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The denial movement's energy is emotional, not evidentiary (rated: interpretive/strong as cultural diagnosis).** Anderson, approaching as a filmmaker rather than apologist, says the discomfort "comes from, well, we don't talk about it," so the topic is governed by emotional reasoning and "cognitive distortions" (all-or-nothing, black-or-white thinking) rather than the documentary record.[^1] *This is a diagnosis of the discourse, not a refutation of any specific historical claim — for those see [[Polygamy Denialism]] and [[The Implications Cascade of Polygamy Denial]].*

2. **Victim-framing of plural wives is itself misogynistic (rated: strong as argument).** The standard denial trope casts the women as brainwashed — "they just had to say that." Anderson rejects the dilemma's hidden premise: "are women agents and people or are they subservient to men? You can't have both." Treating the wives' own testimony as not-credible *because* they affirm plural marriage strips them of agency.[^2] See [[Plural Marriage Read Through Hypergamy and the Asymmetry of Reproduction]].

3. **Silence cedes the ground (rated: moderate).** "We need to stop engaging on this topic timidly... speak loud and boldly in defense of the Church." The remedy is engagement, not avoidance — overlaps [[Understanding Replaces Fear - the Knowledge Cure for the Polygamy Stigma]].[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The denial movement's defenders would say the "insecurity and guilt" framing is a way to *psychologize away* a genuine moral objection rather than answer it — that plural marriage really did involve coercive dynamics (teenage brides, Joseph's approach to already-married women), and that calling discomfort a "cognitive distortion" risks gaslighting people whose unease is morally appropriate. Anderson's own "listen to the women" principle cuts both ways: some plural wives recorded real anguish, not just empowerment. The strongest version of her point is narrow — that *blanket* dismissal of the wives' affirmations is misogynistic — not that all discomfort is irrational.

## Counter-Objection
A critic may answer: "I am not denying the women's agency; I am denying that an institution which pressured them is justified merely because some consented." Anderson's reply is that consent is exactly the hinge — where the existing wives and the new wife genuinely chose (the chain-of-consent model, [[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent]]), the practice can be just; the denial movement's error is refusing to credit that consent at all.

## Best Quotes
> "The polygamy denial movement has taken off so strong because they've been able to capitalize on women's insecurity and men's guilt surrounding this topic."[^1]

> "Are women agents and people or are they subservient to men? ... you can't have both, so which is it going to be? And I choose to believe in women and their capacity as rational beings."[^2]

> "We need to stop engaging on this topic timidly and can speak loud and boldly about this in defense of the Church."[^3]
