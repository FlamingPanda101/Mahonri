# Inspiration Presupposes Revelation in the Christian Theological Tradition

## The Claim

In both the Western and Eastern Christian theological traditions, **revelation is prior and fundamental; inspiration is secondary and derivative** — "you do not have inspiration outside of revelation under either model." [[Brian Thomassen]] presents this as background (not his own final view) so that the distinction between *revelation* and *inspiration* — relevant to the inspired-Constitution debate — is framed by how the broader tradition has actually understood the two terms.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Western model — [[Thomas Aquinas|Aquinas]] via Aristotle's four causes
In the *Summa Theologica*, Aquinas distinguishes *revelatio* (revelation) from *divina inspiratio* (inspiration) and makes revelation **primary** — the initial divine act to a particular agent. Mapping the production of *new truth* (scripture) onto Aristotle's four causes:
- **Formal cause** = revelation (the structure/boundaries from which the truth is received);
- **Efficient cause** = inspiration (the individual transmission — the man is "the chisel");
- **Material cause** = the parchment, pen, quill;
- **Final cause** = the salvation of man.
So inspiration is the *efficient* component playing off the *formally* revealed truth.[^2]

### 2. The Eastern model — the *via negativa* / apophatic tradition
The Eastern tradition (apophatic theology, hesychasm; tracing to [[Dionysius the Areopagite|Pseudo-Dionysius]]) is more **experiential** than propositional. Because the divine so far exceeds man, truth "can never [be] fully encapsulate[d]," even when manifested — so it is approached by negation ("it is not this, it is not that," the *via negativa*). Here revelation is **participation with the divine** and has a sanctifying component; inspiration again presupposes the prior, experiential revelation.[^3]

### 3. Both models put revelation first
"In both instances, revelation is going to be taken as prior or fundamental, and inspiration is going to be something that plays off of the revealed." Thomassen offers these as *options for the listener* — "which are not my view" — before giving his own *theopneustos*/utility reading. See [[The Constitution Is Inspired in the Sense of Useful Not Divinely Embodied]].[^4]

### 4. Why it matters for the Constitution debate
The conversation began from a live question: does God communicate things "meant for the collective but not the church" — e.g., inspiring a legislator or the Constitutional Convention? Thomassen also distinguishes **canon (binding)** from a mere **true claim (non-binding)**: e.g., [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] and Joseph F. Smith's vision ([[D&C 138|D&C 138]]) were true before being canonized (1976/1981). Hence "the Constitution is inspired" may be true but **non-binding** unless the canon says so. This pairs with [[The Constitution Is Not Said to Be Inspired in the Canon - Only Established]] and the property-distinctions in [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]].[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** Thomassen explicitly presents the Aquinas and Eastern models as *not* his own view and as schematic survey, conceding "we could say that they're wrong, but that's how it has been understood." The Aristotelian-cause mapping of Aquinas is a fair gloss but compresses a large scholastic literature; the East/West contrast (propositional vs. experiential) is a familiar generalization that specialists would nuance. The load-bearing point — that in the classical tradition *inspiration* is downstream of *revelation* — is sound and frames why a deflationary LDS reading of "inspired" is even available.

## Best Quotes

> "Revelation is the formal cause… inspiration is the efficient cause… the material cause is the parchment, the pen… the final cause is the salvation of man."[^2]

> "In both instances, revelation is going to be taken as prior or fundamental, and inspiration is going to be something that plays off of the revealed. So you do not have inspiration outside of revelation under either model."[^4]
