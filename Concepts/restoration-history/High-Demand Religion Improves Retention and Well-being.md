# High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"High-demand religion" is typically used pejoratively — a label implying the LDS Church is controlling, burdensome, or cult-like, extracting more than it gives. The objection treats high demand as a cost to be escaped.

## The Rebuttal

1. **High demand correlates with better retention (rated: moderate).** Demanding traditions "are more likely to actually keep their membership than... the lower demand religions because there's something there for people to grab onto."[^1] See [[Why the LDS Membership Lacks a Large Nominal Middle]].

2. **The education analogy (rated: weak/illustrative).** Dyer compares it to a high-demand education program: "low-demand education programs — yeah, maybe I got a little bit out of it; but when they're a little more high-demand — oh, that means I'm really going to derive the benefit from that."[^2] *Steelman:* the analogy is suggestive, not evidentiary; demand and benefit can diverge (a demanding program can also burn people out), and the framing risks rationalizing any cost as a hidden benefit.

3. **Dose-response well-being (rated: moderate, longitudinal).** "The higher demand... the religion, the more you're going to be deriving benefits of that religion"; greater earlier engagement predicts later well-being in panel data. See [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]] and [[Religiosity Benefits Mental Health Even After Leaving]].[^3]

4. **It produces investment and community (rated: moderate).** High demand "gets people very invested in wanting to be part of that community" — the host reframes the pejorative as a benefit.[^4] *Steelman:* the very stickiness that aids well-being for the engaged can intensify pain and identity-loss for those who exit, which connects to why LDS disaffiliates more often shed the label entirely.

## Best Quotes

> "High-demand religions are more likely to actually keep their membership than... the lower demand religions because there's something there for people to grab onto."[^1]

> "You think of a high-demand education program... low-demand education programs — yeah, maybe I got a little bit out of it. But when they're a little more high-demand — oh, that means I'm really going to derive the benefit from that."[^2]

> "The higher demand, if you will, the religion, the more you're going to be deriving benefits of that religion."[^3]
