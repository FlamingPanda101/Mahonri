# The Mission Builds the Missionary Even Without Converts

#type/claim

## The Claim
The value of a Latter-day Saint mission does not depend on its baptismal output. Even a missionary who converts no one is profoundly formed by the experience — so the program functions as a costly-signaling rite of self-formation, not merely a recruitment engine. The hosts derive this from [[Russell Moore]]'s own concession: "even if you don't baptize anybody, if you don't convert anybody, the benefits of sending your young men out there to do that are immense."[^1] This distinguishes the argument from the generic "strict church grows" retention thesis (see [[High-Demand Religion Retains More Members]]): the claim here is about *individual transformation*, with convert numbers held to zero.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "Nonverts" finding (Evidence: cited secondhand from Stephen Bullivant's *The Nonverts*).** Moore relays that an interviewer found LDS missionaries who never saw a single convert, "and what's amazing is that they didn't think their missions were a failure at all" — two years "trudging around rainy England ... is nothing if not character building," and the odds of them "returning home even more deeply committed to being upstanding lifelong Mormons will certainly have risen appreciably."[^2] *Steelman strength: strong as reportage, but it is one author's qualitative read, not a controlled outcome study.*

2. **Charles Callis, 1911 (Evidence: a century-old apostolic aphorism).** The hosts quote [[Charles Callis]], mission president in 1911: "Trudging around in the rain and the storm and talking to people might not be an efficient way to do missionary work, but it's the greatest way to build missionaries."[^3] This frames inefficiency-of-conversion as a *feature* — the labor itself is the formative product.

3. **Self-esteem study via the "Clint/Clank" X thread (Evidence: asserted, uncited on-air).** "More than seven in ten individuals who served as Latter-day Saint missionar[ies] say their time as a missionary increased their self-esteem, with most of them saying that the mission increased their self-esteem greatly," attributed to being pushed out of their comfort zone — limited family contact, working with strangers, adjusting to new cultures.[^4] *Steelman caution: a real percentage is quoted but the study is not named, and self-reported retrospective self-esteem is subject to effort-justification bias — the very mechanism a critic would flag (see below).*

4. **The "ultimate 75 Hard" framing (Evidence: rhetorical analogy).** Britney's comparison: the mission is a maximal voluntary-hardship challenge whose participants "swear by it," like the 75 Hard fitness regimen. "Anything worthwhile is high demand."[^5] The costly demand is reframed from pathology to the source of the value.

**Steelman of the opposing view (the high-control reading):** That missionaries who converted no one nonetheless rate the experience as a success is *exactly* what cognitive-dissonance and effort-justification theory predict: the more a person sacrifices for a group with no external payoff, the more they inflate its subjective worth to resolve the dissonance ("I gave two years — it *must* have been worth it"). On this reading, the self-esteem and commitment gains are not independent evidence that the program is good; they are the psychological signature of a sunk-cost / costly-signaling system that manufactures loyalty regardless of outcome. The same data are consistent with both "transformative spiritual formation" and "high-investment commitment-locking," and the episode does not supply a test that separates them — it points to the missionaries' self-assessments, which is the one source the dissonance objection predicts will be biased.
