# The Same Organization Means Functional Not Structural Continuity with the Primitive Church

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Grant Underwood]] reinterprets the sixth Article of Faith — "we believe in the same organization that existed in the primitive church, namely, apostles, prophets, pastors, teachers, evangelists, and so forth" — as a claim about **function**, not **structure**. The popular reading ("we're structurally the same as first-century Christianity… the same flowchart of officers and responsibilities") is, he argues, "an unsustainable claim," because "to the best of our historical excavation and reconstruction" the modern organizational chart "cannot really be found in the first century." [^1] The rescue is to ask "what did those terms identify *functionally* in the earliest years," affirm the article as a legitimate affirmation of faith, and let it "accommodate, as best we can reconstruct it, the historical realities of the first century." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Concedes the historical-structural data instead of fighting it.** Underwood grants that the first-century church did not have the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints' present organizational structure — the kind of point critics press as evidence against the Restoration. Reframing AoF 6 as functional continuity removes the burden of proving a literal structural match that the sources won't support. This is the same scholarly honesty about early-Christian diversity seen in [[Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree]].
2. **Parallels his "apostolicity" move in the book.** He reports doing the same with the Apostles' Creed's "one, holy, catholic, apostolic" — exploring what *apostolicity* "functionally" means rather than asserting a wooden institutional identity. The hermeneutic is consistent: read credal/scriptural affirmations for their functional intent, then map them onto reconstructable history.
3. **Coheres with the charitable apostasy posture.** Underwood pairs this with refusing to date or weaponize "the apostasy," quoting [[John Taylor]] ("if that's darkness, give me a little darkness") and [[Dallin H. Oaks]] on others keeping "the light alive." Treating continuity as functional lets the Restoration "celebrate the additional light" without claiming a one-to-one structural reproduction of the primitive church — the framework of [[A New Latter-day Saint Approach to Ancient Christians]], [[The Gathering of Truths from All Churches]], and [[Great Apostasy Is Not a Scriptural Term in LDS Standard Works]].

## Best Quotes
> "The current organizational flowchart cannot really be found in the first century. So what do we do with that article of faith? … I say, ah, we are the same in terms of function. What did those terms identify functionally in the earliest years?" [^1]

> "If you're moving beyond a kind of literalistic 'well, our organizational list of officers and responsibilities is exactly the same as it was in the first century' — that's really an unsustainable claim. But nobody says we have to make that claim. We have to understand that article of faith in a richer, broader way." [^2]

## Steelman the Critic
An evangelical critic will say this concedes the original LDS claim outright: the Restoration's traditional selling point was that Joseph Smith *restored* the very offices and structure of the apostolic church, lost in the Great Apostasy. Retreating to "functional continuity" makes Article of Faith 6 nearly unfalsifiable — almost any church with leaders, teachers, and missionaries performs those *functions*, so the article no longer distinguishes the LDS Church from the Christianity it claims to have superseded. A traditionalist Latter-day Saint may likewise resist, holding that priesthood keys and the specific apostolic office *were* uniquely restored, not merely their functions. The functional reading is strongest as historiography (the first-century data genuinely will not yield the modern flowchart) and weakest as apologetic leverage, since it trades a falsifiable structural claim for a far softer one.
