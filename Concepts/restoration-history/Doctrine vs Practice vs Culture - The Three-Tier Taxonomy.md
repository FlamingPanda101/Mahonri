# Doctrine vs Practice vs Culture - The Three-Tier Taxonomy

## The Claim
To make sense of change in the Church over time, [[Jacob Hansen]] argues one must distinguish **three concepts**: (1) **doctrine** — the official truth claims of the Church; (2) **practice / policy** — how those doctrines are *applied*, which varies with context; and (3) **culture** — the attitudes of members about doctrines and practices, the most changeable of all. Most apparent "doctrinal changes" are really changes of practice or culture mislabeled as doctrine. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Doctrine defined institutionally.** "By doctrine, I mean the official truth claims made by the authorities of the church" — specifically what the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve "claim to be true authoritatively as a group." A statement is not doctrine merely because one leader (even Brigham Young) said it. *Rating: strong; matches the Church's own "Approaching Mormon Doctrine" definition — cf. [[A Historian's Definition of Doctrine vs the Believer's]].* [^1]
2. **Practice/policy is context-dependent by design.** Because "principles are general truths," how they get applied "is going to vary." Hansen quotes [[Wilford Woodruff]] and the handbook principle that "procedures, programs... even some patterns of organization are subject to change... but the principles never change." *Rating: strong; this is a documented Church teaching.* [^2]
3. **Culture is the most volatile tier.** Culture is "merely the attitude of members regarding doctrines and practices" and shifts fastest; conflating member culture or a leader's speculation with doctrine produces the illusion of constant doctrinal flux. *Rating: strong — cf. [[Conflating Anecdote and Culture With Doctrine Misrepresents the Church]].* [^3]
4. **The diagnostic question.** "Is this a core spiritual truth or is this the way that we apply a core spiritual truth? That is the way you can tell the difference between a policy and a doctrine." *Rating: moderate-strong; a usable heuristic, though edge cases (leaders calling a policy "doctrine") complicate it.* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic charges the taxonomy is **self-serving and after-the-fact**: leaders have repeatedly called something "doctrine" (the priesthood ban, polygamy-for-exaltation) and then, once it changed, retroactively demoted it to "policy" or "culture." If the categories are assigned *after* knowing what changed, the scheme can absorb any change and is therefore unfalsifiable — the church "feels unchanging while actually changing." See [[Religions Persist by Feeling Unchanging While Actually Changing]].

### Counter-objection
Hansen concedes leaders have sometimes labeled practices "doctrine," but argues the **collective prophetic witness** breaks the circularity: a claim's status is fixed by its attestation *across dispensations*, not by present convenience. So the demotions are *principled* (the ban had no scriptural/cross-dispensational basis) rather than ad hoc. The taxonomy is only as strong as that cross-temporal canon — its honest load-bearing assumption. See [[The Collective Prophetic Witness]] and [[Change Is Not the Same as Contradiction]].

## Best Quotes
> "To understand changes in the church over time, it is critical to understand three different concepts: doctrine, practice (also known as policy), and culture. Practice or policy is how we apply the doctrines and it will change based on the context. And culture in the church is even more changeable, as this is merely the attitude of members regarding doctrines and practices." [^1]

> "The question to ask is, is this a core spiritual truth or is this the way that we apply a core spiritual truth? That is the way you can tell the difference between a policy and a doctrine." [^3]
