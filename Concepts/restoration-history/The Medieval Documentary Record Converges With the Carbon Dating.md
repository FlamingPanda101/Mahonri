# The Medieval Documentary Record Converges With the Carbon Dating

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection to Authenticity)
The strongest *historical* case against the Shroud is that the documentary record and the radiocarbon date point to the **same century**. Historian Nicola Sarzo's new find — a treatise by Nicole Oresme (c. 1370–1382) citing "a shroud of the Lord Jesus Christ" in a church in Champagne as a textbook example of clerical fraud — pushes the earliest known reference back from Bishop Pierre d'Arcis's 1389 memo and reframes the cloth, from its first clear appearance, as something educated clergy already regarded as man-made. History (cloth surfaces ~1350s, mocked 1370s, denounced 1389) and science (1988 carbon date of 1260–1390) independently converge.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The new earlier reference (Oresme) (medium)
Sarzo uncovered Oresme — bishop, mathematician, leading 14th-century intellectual — using the Champagne shroud as a *teaching example* of how "many clergymen thus deceive others in order to elicit offerings for their churches." This both pre-dates d'Arcis by a decade or two and shows the fraud charge was not a lone bishop's grudge but common knowledge among the learned.[^1]

### 2. The d'Arcis memo and papal ruling (medium)
The prior earliest record, Bishop Pierre d'Arcis's 1389–90 memo to the Pope, called the cloth a fraud, claimed his predecessor had found the forging artist, and reports that Clement VII allowed it to be shown only as a "figure or representation" of the true shroud — a devotional icon, not the genuine relic.[^2]

### 3. The convergence itself (strong, as stated)
The historical surfacing of the cloth lands "right in the same window as the carbon dating results." Two independent lines — dusty medieval documents and radiocarbon physics — pointing to one century is, Bertuzzi grants, "about as close to a bullseye as you can get" for the skeptic.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View (Defense of Authenticity)
Jeremiah Johnson presses back. (1) Oresme's Champagne line is a *generic* slam on relic scams; reading it as a precise reference to the Lirey shroud is "mere conjecture," not evidence. (2) Both Oresme and d'Arcis assume the image was *painted* — which the 1978 STURP findings decisively refute (no pigment) — so their reliability about the cloth's origin is undermined: they were wrong about the most basic fact. (3) The d'Arcis memo survives as two mashed-together drafts, and its publisher (Ulysse Chevalier) asserted without evidence that it reached the Pope; d'Arcis himself admits much of his accusation rests on hearsay. (4) Absence of clear earlier records is not proof of medieval *origin*; the cloth could have existed and been "kept under wraps."

## Counter-Objection
The convergence argument's force does not depend on the *painted-forgery* detail being correct: even granting STURP, the point is that *two independent dating methods* land in the same century, which is hard to attribute to coincidence regardless of how the image was made. Johnson's critiques (generic reference, shaky memo, hearsay) legitimately *blunt* the documentary case — downgrading it from "the medievals proved it fake" to "the medievals close to the cloth thought it fake" — but they do not move the cloth's first clear appearance out of the 14th century. The honest net result, which Bertuzzi adopts, is that the historical record neither proves authenticity nor explains the image, but does weigh against a first-century provenance.

## LDS Relevance
None specific; the Shroud debate is general-Christian. It is included as a worked example of weighing *converging independent evidence* (history + science) against a single anomaly (the image) — a methodological pattern relevant across apologetics.
