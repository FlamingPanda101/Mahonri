# NDEs and Religious Conditioning

## The Point
A natural objection: aren't these experiences just projections of the subjects' prior religious beliefs? Moody concedes religion shapes the experience *to some extent* — chiefly the *identity* a person assigns to the [[NDE - The Being of Light|being of light]] — but argues the *content* does not track religious background [^1]. Strikingly, no one described a heaven or hell resembling the conventional picture; several said the experience contradicted what their religious training had led them to expect; and people with no religious background at all reported the same content [^2].

## Evidence
1. **Identity varies, description doesn't.** "Though the description of the being of light is invariable, the identity ascribed to it varies, apparently as a function of the religious background of the individual" [^1].
2. **No conventional heaven or hell.** "Through all of my research... I have not heard a single reference to a heaven or a hell anything like the customary picture to which we are exposed in this society" [^1].
3. **Experience contradicts expectation.** One woman: "I had always heard that when you die, you see both heaven and hell, but I didn't see either one." Another expected "pearly gates" but instead found herself "hovering around my own physical body... I was just baffled" [^1].
4. **The non-religious report the same thing.** "Quite a few" reports came from people with "no religious beliefs or training at all," and their descriptions "do not seem to differ in content" from the devout [^2].
5. **After-effects on belief.** Some who had rejected religious doctrine "acquired religious feelings with new depth after the experience"; others said they had read scripture such as the Bible but "never really understood" it until their NDE [^2].

## Best Quotes
> "the description of the being of light is invariable, the identity ascribed to it varies, apparently as a function of the religious background of the individual." [^1]

> "I had always been taught in my religious upbringing that the minute you died you would be right at these beautiful gates, pearly gates. But there I was hovering around my own physical body, and that was it! I was just baffled." [^1]

## LDS Connection
That subjects encounter a real personage of light whom they then *name* according to their tradition fits the LDS conviction that the light of Christ is genuinely given to all people, however incompletely they understand or label it (Moroni 7:16-19; D&C 88:7-13). The repeated finding that the afterlife does not match the binary "pearly gates / hellfire" picture aligns with the Restoration's rejection of a simple two-destination scheme in favor of a progressive spirit world and degrees of glory (D&C 76; D&C 138) — see [[Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection]].
