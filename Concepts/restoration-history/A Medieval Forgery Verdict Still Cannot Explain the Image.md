# A Medieval Forgery Verdict Still Cannot Explain the Image

#type/claim

## The Claim
Bertuzzi's synthesizing point: even if one grants the skeptic *both* the 14th-century carbon date *and* the medieval documentary record, that verdict explains *when* the cloth appeared, not *how the image was made*. A "forgery in the Middle Ages" is not an explanation until someone specifies a medieval mechanism that reproduces the image's full feature set — and no one has. So the most defensible overall stance is **agnosticism**: each side is left with a hard, unanswered question.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Carbon dating dates the cloth, not the image (strong)
Radiocarbon tells you when the *linen* was made; it is silent about how the discoloration formed. A medieval *date* is therefore compatible with the image still being unexplained — the skeptic needs the *mechanism*, not just the date.[^1]

### 2. The medieval accusers were demonstrably wrong about the method (medium)
Both Oresme and Bishop d'Arcis assumed the image was *painted*. The 1978 STURP team found no pigment, dye, or brushstrokes. So the very sources used to establish "medieval fraud" were mistaken about *how* the fraud was supposedly done, which undercuts treating their verdict as an explanation of the image.[^2]

### 3. The full feature set is unreproduced (strong)
Dr. John Jackson cataloged 17 unique features of the Shroud; to this day no one has reproduced all of them. "If this was just a con job with paint, then why can't modern science recreate it?"[^2] See [[The Shroud Image Has No Known Forgery Mechanism]].

### 4. The remaining anomalies cut both ways (framing)
Whichever side you take, a burden remains. A forgery view must still explain the negative/3D image, the distress-pattern blood, and pollen/dirt samples matching ancient Israel. An authenticity view must still explain the carbon date and the historical silence before the 14th century.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic replies that "we can't yet reproduce it" is an argument from ignorance, and that the rational default for an object with a medieval date *and* medieval fraud accusations is "medieval artifact, mechanism not yet identified" — not "unexplained, therefore possibly miraculous." Convergent dating plus contemporaneous fraud reports is a strong cumulative case; demanding a fully specified reproduction before accepting forgery sets an unreasonably high bar that no ancient artifact's manufacture could meet.

## Counter-Objection
Bertuzzi grants the force of this and *does not* infer the miraculous — he infers **agnosticism**. The point is symmetry of burdens: the skeptic's "medieval artifact, mechanism TBD" is itself a promissory note, and it must still account for the negative image, the 3D encoding, the trauma-pattern blood, and the Israel-matching pollen *together*. Until a single demonstrated medieval technique does that, "forgery" labels the *date* but does not *explain* the object — which is exactly why a careful Christian apologist can decline to claim authenticity while also declining to treat the case as closed.

## LDS Relevance
None specific to the Restoration. The note's transferable value is methodological: it models intellectual honesty in apologetics — refusing to overclaim a favorable artifact, distinguishing "dated" from "explained," and landing on calibrated agnosticism when independent lines of evidence pull in opposite directions. Compare the vault's posture toward other contested physical evidence in [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]].
