# The School of Isaiah Preserved First-Temple Tradition in Exile

## The Claim
[[Margaret Barker]] argues that the people "most responsible for preserving the older ways were Isaiah and his disciples and then their descendants" — a "school of Isaiah" (not a formal institution but a continuing way of teaching) that kept the first-temple prophecies alive when the Deuteronomists had purged them. This circle preserved "all the prophecies that Christians came to use for Christmas… Good Friday and Easter," and the later legend has them going into exile east of the Jordan.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A school, not an institution (rated: Barker's reconstruction).** "I call them the school of Isaiah, but they weren't a formal school — just a group of people who perpetuated a certain way of teaching… and I think preserved old books." Barker concedes "that's something controversial," but holds they "certainly preserved what would have been in the books," including the prophecies later used at Christmas and Easter.[^1]
2. **Exile east of the Jordan (rated: dependent on later texts).** Non-canonical texts suggest the circle "were living somewhere east of the River Jordan… as a group of prophets in exile… who had brought with them their books and traditions from the city of Jerusalem which they regarded now as utterly wicked." The *Ascension of Isaiah* names Isaiah with Micah, Joel, Habakkuk, and others leaving "the wicked city" to live in poverty on a mountain.[^2][^3]
3. **The preface (Isaiah 1) frames the whole scroll (rated: textual).** Isaiah 1 — which Barker (with mainstream scholarship) treats as a late editorial preface — sums up the book's complaint: "sons have I reared… but they have rebelled against me," and "how the faithful city has become a harlot," i.e. second-temple Jerusalem of the reformers. See [[Deuteronomists Rewrote the Histories of Israel]].
4. **Late copies do not mean late texts (rated: Barker's methodological point).** Barker warns against the "sleight of hand" of dating the non-canonical texts late merely because the earliest copies are 4th-century AD — "until the Dead Sea Scrolls were found the earliest complete copy of the Hebrew Bible was more than 1000 AD." Old traditions can survive in young manuscripts.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** Mainstream scholarship recognizes redactional growth in Isaiah and an "Isaiah tradition," but the detailed "school of Isaiah… in exile east of the Jordan preserving first-temple secrets" is built substantially on late, legendary texts (the *Ascension of Isaiah*) and is Barker's reconstruction, not consensus. A critic notes that inferring pre-exilic *content* from such texts risks circularity. The existence of an Isaian editorial tradition is agreed; its identification with a suppressed first-temple cult is contested.

## Best Quotes
> "The people who were most responsible for preserving the older ways were Isaiah and his disciples and then their descendants… I call them the school of Isaiah."[^1]

> "They existed there as a group of prophets in exile… who had brought with them their books and traditions from the city of Jerusalem which they regarded now as utterly wicked."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The school of Isaiah is the bridge between the purged first temple and the Book of Mormon: if Isaiah's circle carried the old prophecies into exile, then [[Lehi]]'s family — who quote Isaiah more than any other prophet and revere him as a "visionary man" — can be read as an offshoot of that prophetic stream. This grounds the Deutero-Isaiah argument that follows in [[Second Isaiah Was an Oral Tradition of a Prophetic School]].
