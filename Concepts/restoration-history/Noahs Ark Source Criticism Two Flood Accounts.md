# Noah's Ark Source Criticism — Two Interwoven Flood Accounts

## The Claim

The flood narrative in Genesis 6–9 is composed of two originally separate and independent flood accounts — the **Priestly (P)** account and the **non-Priestly (J/Yahwist)** account — that have been woven together by a later redactor. This is not a fringe theory; it is "one of the most long-standing, widely agreed upon, best-supported source critical arguments that there is in the Hebrew Bible. There's pretty much no serious scholar who rejects the separate existence of a Priestly account in the Pentateuch." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Animal Count Contradiction

The clearest signal of two sources is the irreconcilable animal count:
- **P (Genesis 6:19–20):** Two of EVERY land animal and EVERY bird — no clean/unclean distinction
- **J (Genesis 7:2–3):** Seven pairs of clean land animals, one pair of unclean land animals, seven pairs of ALL birds — clean/unclean distinction present

The bird clause makes harmonization impossible, as both accounts cover ALL birds with different numbers. See [[Bible Contradiction - Noahs Ark Animal Count]].

### 2. The Flood Duration

P and J give different chronologies for the flood:
- **P:** The flood waters prevail for 150 days; total time in the ark is approximately one year
- **J:** Rain falls for 40 days and 40 nights; total time is shorter

These conflicting chronologies are woven together in the final text, creating the impression of a longer flood that incorporates both 40-day and 150-day periods — but in the original separate sources, each duration was complete and self-contained.

### 3. Divine Name Usage

The two sources use different names for God:
- **P** consistently uses *Elohim* (God)
- **J** consistently uses *YHWH* (the LORD)

This systematic distinction is one of the foundational observations of source criticism and holds across the entire Pentateuch, not just the flood narrative.

### 4. The Cause of the Flood

- **J (Genesis 6:5–7):** YHWH sees human wickedness and "grieves in his heart" — an anthropopathic, emotional God who regrets creating humanity
- **P (Genesis 6:11–13):** The earth is "corrupt" and "filled with violence" — a more abstract, cosmic statement of disorder, presented without divine emotional response

### 5. Terminology for Destruction

- **P** uses *mabbul* (flood) and describes the flood as a cosmic un-creation — the waters above and below return, reversing Genesis 1
- **J** uses the common verb for rain (*matar*) and describes a more conventional great rainstorm

### 6. Birds in the Resolution

- **J:** Noah sends out a raven once, then a dove three times (the familiar children's-story version)
- **P:** No bird-sending episode in the surviving P material — the flood simply ends when the waters recede

### 7. Literary Structure — Redactional Seams

The redactor preserved both accounts rather than choosing one, alternating blocks of P and J material. Passages like [[Genesis 7.8-9|Genesis 7:8–9]] are visible "seams" where the editor tried to bridge the two traditions: it acknowledges the clean/unclean categories (from J) but says they entered "two and two" (from P).

## The Scholarly Foundation — Key Texts

McClellan recommends the following works, all of which affirm the two-source flood hypothesis and provide the evidence:[^2]

- **David Carr**, *Reading the Fractures of Genesis* (foundational on method and theory) and *The Formation of Genesis 1–11* (newer synthesis) plus Kohlhammer commentary on Genesis 1–11
- **David Petersen**, Genesis commentary (Old Testament Library series)
- **Jan Christian Gertz**, Genesis 1–11 commentary (Historical Commentary on the Old Testament series)
- **Ron Hendel**, Genesis 1–11 commentary (Anchor Yale Bible, 2024)
- **Claus Westermann**, classic Genesis 1–11 commentary
- **Bill Arnold**, Genesis commentary (New Cambridge Bible Commentary series)
- **E.A. Speiser**, Genesis commentary (Anchor Bible, 1964) — a mid-20th-century standard reference

"All of these are going to point out, look, we've got two sources here that have been woven together." [^2]

## Genesis 7:8–9 — The Redactional Seam

[[Genesis 7.8-9|Genesis 7:8–9]] is a particularly clear example of the redactor's work. It acknowledges the clean/unclean distinction (from J) but says the animals entered "two and two" (from P). The verse attempts to hold both traditions together and in doing so reveals their original independence — it is a seam visible in the stitching.
