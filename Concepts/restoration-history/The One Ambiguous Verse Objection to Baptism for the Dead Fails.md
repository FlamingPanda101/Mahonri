# The One Ambiguous Verse Objection to Baptism for the Dead Fails

#type/claim

## The Objection Being Answered

A Catholic YouTuber ("Catholic Truth," host Brian) argues that Latter-day Saints "don't understand
the Bible," and offers baptism for the dead as Exhibit A: it is found in "the only verse in the whole
Bible," [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]], which is "ambiguous," "a random thing Paul
mentions in passing," and that "most scholars don't even understand" — "and yet Mormons have
constructed an entire doctrine around this."[^1] [[Jonah Barnes]], [[Cardon Ellis]], and Luke Hansen
rebut the objection's *structure* on five fronts.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. There is a *second* New Testament verse — rate: strong (text)
The "only one verse" premise is false: [[1 Peter 4.6|1 Peter 4:6]] — "for this cause was the gospel
preached also to them that are dead" — is a second proof-text. Combined with the Great Commission to
"baptize all nations" ([[Matthew 28.19|Matthew 28:19]]), preaching the gospel to the dead implies
baptizing them: "if you believe in the gospel, and that includes 'go ye and baptize'... then you'd be
baptizing the dead." So the doctrine is not hanging on a single ambiguous line.[^2]

### 2. "Only one verse" proves nothing — rate: strong (logical)
Many true things appear in exactly one verse (the panel's examples: snakes not biting the faithful;
the woman taken in adultery; the naked young man fleeing in Mark). Frequency is not the test of
canonicity or truth. Crucially this *tu quoque* lands hard on the *Catholic*: the objector's own
distinctives rest on equally thin textual footing — clerical **celibacy** leans on a single Pauline
preference (1 Cor 7), and **infant baptism** has *no* explicit verse — yet Catholics defend them. You
cannot say "one verse is too few" for an opponent's doctrine while building your own on one (or
zero).[^3]

### 3. Latter-day Saints are not *sola scriptura* — rate: strong (framing)
The objector "argues like an Evangelical" — as if doctrine must be derived from the closed canon
alone. But neither Latter-day Saints **nor Catholics** are *sola scriptura*. Latter-day Saints have
**continuing revelation**: a prophet can receive clarification on an admittedly ambiguous passage
(the rite is detailed in [[D&C 128]]/[[D&C 138]]). So the doctrine is not "constructed on" 1 Cor 15:29
as a foundation — that verse simply *also* happens to attest something revelation independently
confirmed. (The Catholic has a parallel mechanism: the pope speaking *ex cathedra*.) See
[[Revelatory Authority Must Match Interpretive Authority]].[^4]

### 4. The "scholars don't agree, so dismiss it" move is the Sanhedrin's move — rate: moderate (analogy)
Arguing "credentialed scholars can't agree what it means, therefore the radical's claim is invalid" is
precisely how the Pharisees and Sadducees dismissed Jesus ("our scribes don't recognize this man").
Appeal to scholarly non-consensus is not a refutation. Even a top Catholic apologist (Jimmy Akin) and
Joe Heschmeyer concede the **Trinity** itself could not be derived by one person reading the Bible and
"took a lot of people a lot of time to put together" — so the standard the objector applies to baptism
for the dead would gut his own central doctrine.[^5]

### 5. Paul cites the rite to prove a *point*, not in idle passing — rate: moderate (exegesis)
Far from "random," Paul deploys baptism for the dead as evidence in an argument *for the
resurrection*: "else why are they then baptized for the dead?" The rite is load-bearing in his logic,
which presupposes it was a known, real practice — consistent with the scholarship in
[[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]] and the early-Christian
attestations in [[Marcionites Practiced Proxy Baptism for the Unbaptized Dead]] and [[Work for the
Dead Is Attested in Early Christian Material Culture]].[^6]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **The strongest Catholic point is real:** 1 Cor 15:29 *is* genuinely contested, and Paul's "they"
  (third person) may distance himself from the practice. A careful critic would press exegesis here
  rather than the weak "only one verse" frame — and the panel's reply leans more on *parity*
  (you-do-it-too) than on settling the verse's meaning.
- **The infant-baptism/celibacy parity is a *tu quoque*** — it shows the objection is inconsistent,
  not that baptism for the dead is *true*. (The note is filed as a rebuttal of the objection's
  structure, which is exactly what it accomplishes.)
- **The 1 Pet 4:6 + Matt 28:19 inference is a chain**, not a single explicit command to baptize the
  dead; a Protestant reads 1 Pet 4:6 as Christ's one-time descent, not an ongoing rite.

**What survives:** the objection *as posed* — "only one ambiguous verse, therefore Mormons misread the
Bible" — fails decisively: there are two verses, single-verse doctrines are normal (including the
objector's own), and neither church is *sola scriptura*, so "constructed an entire doctrine around one
verse" misdescribes a revelation-grounded teaching.
