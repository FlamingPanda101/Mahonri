# Prophetic Stewardship Is Over the Institution and Ordinances Not Over All Truth

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]]'s constructive proposal for resolving the prophet-vs-scripture tension: prophets hold **stewardship over the institution of the church and guardianship of the ordinances** — and within that domain their authority is decisive ("no questions asked, 100%"). But on **non-revelatory matters outside institutional policy** — science, history, the details of family life, civic and political questions — consulting prophets "is like asking a lawyer for medical advice." Where two prophets contradict each other, the member is *invited* to "step in and grapple" and reach a personal conclusion, because each person also has a stewardship — over their own soul and family.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The brethren disclaim general expertise (strong)
Hatch stacks self-limiting statements: **M. Russell Ballard** — "I'm a general authority, but that doesn't make me an authority in general... I seek others... with degrees and expertise"; **Bruce R. McConkie** — general authorities "may or may not be authorities in... doctrinal knowledge or the receipt of the promptings of the Spirit"; **Boyd K. Packer** — leaders who didn't know the gospel's fundamentals before their call "will scarcely have time to learn them along the way."[^1]

### 2. The two domains (strong)
Hatch's rule: anything pertaining to *how the church is run* (callings, the structure of meetings, how baptisms and ordinances are performed) falls under prophetic stewardship — "100%." Matters *outside* institutional policy and the ordinances are not the prophet's domain of expertise.[^2]

### 3. The member's own stewardship (medium)
Greg Matsen and Hatch converge: when something "applies to what I am supposed to believe... or applies to my family," the member must "internalize" it and "take responsibility" — a stewardship over one's own soul, family, and callings that cannot be outsourced. George Q. Cannon is cited: "do not... put your trust in man, though he be a bishop, an apostle, or a president."[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic from the "follow the prophet" side objects that this hands the individual a veto: by labeling any uncomfortable counsel "non-revelatory" or "outside institutional policy," a member can dismiss prophetic guidance on family, morality, or civic duty at will — exactly the cafeteria approach the "all things, at all times" statements were meant to foreclose. Who decides what is "institutional" vs. "personal"? The boundary is itself contestable, and drawing it in the member's favor effectively makes the individual, not the prophet, the final authority.

## Counter-Objection
Hatch concedes a friend's worry that he "put[s] the prophets in too small of a box," but argues the boundary is principled, not arbitrary: it tracks the *brethren's own* repeated disclaimers of general expertise and the *nature of the matter* (does it concern church governance and ordinances, or a question of fact/history/medicine/family judgment?). The model does not license dismissing the prophet on church order — there it is absolute. And it *expands* rather than abdicates responsibility: the member must "go through everything... understand it, believe it, in order to execute it," which is the opposite of cafeteria convenience. The alternative — treating every prophetic utterance on every subject as binding — is the very overreach that prophets like Oaks and George Albert Smith themselves repudiated.

## Best Quotes
> "I'm a general authority, but that doesn't make me an authority in general." (M. Russell Ballard)[^1]

> "Consulting prophets on non-revelatory matters or on issues that fall outside of institutional policies and functions is like asking a lawyer for medical advice."[^2]
