# Divine and Human Natures Are Mutually Exclusive

## The Claim

Under classical (creedal/"perfect being") theism, the divine nature and the human nature have *contradictory* defining properties, so they cannot coexist in one person — just as "square" and "triangle" cannot. The hypostatic union therefore asserts a contradiction. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Knowledge.** To be divine is to be **omniscient** (all knowledge in one's mind); to be human is to have **limited** knowledge. Contradictory. [^2]
2. **Mutability.** To be divine is to be **immutable** (unchanging); to be human is to **change and grow** constantly. Contradictory. [^3]
3. **Origin.** To be divine is to be **uncreated** (eternally existent, not from nothing); to be human is to **come into being**, created out of nothing by God. One person cannot be both created and uncreated. The dodge "his human nature was created, his divine nature wasn't" answers a question not asked: *was the person created?* — which forces a single yes/no. [^4]
4. **Passibility.** To be divine (classically) is to be **impassible** (unaffected by anything outside oneself, without passions); to be human is to suffer. Contradictory — bearing directly on "who suffered on the cross." [^5]
5. **The square/triangle principle.** Natures are bundles of properties; you cannot instantiate blatantly contradictory properties (three sides *and* four sides) in one thing. A square-triangle, a married bachelor, a human-frog: all impossible for the same reason. (Evidence: the logic is valid *given* the classical definitions; the whole dispute is whether those definitions are correct — see steelman.) [^1]
6. **Steelman.** The orthodox reply denies the contradiction by relocating the predicates: the *person* is divine-and-human, but each property is held *according to* the nature that bears it (omniscient qua God, limited qua man), so no single nature has contradictory properties and no contradiction arises at the level of the person either, because the natures are not blended (Chalcedon: "without confusion, without change"). Some also reject the classical definitions themselves (open/relational theists deny strict immutability and impassibility), which would dissolve several of the contradictions independently of LDS theology. (Evidence: serious; the argument's force is proportional to one's commitment to classical attribute-definitions, which even many Trinitarians qualify.) The LDS counter is its own kenotic Christology (see [[Kenotic Christology - Jesus Emptied Himself and Did Not Know the Hour]]): rather than two natures, divinity *is* humanity fully realized, differing only in degree. [^6]

## Best Quotes

> "In the divine nature, you have all knowledge in your mind. In human nature, to be human is to have a limited knowledge. Those are incompatible." [^2]

> "The nature of a square is to have four equal sides. That's why you can't have a square triangle. ... their natures can't exist in the same thing." [^1]

> "My question is, was the person created? ... You have to make a choice. The person either was or wasn't." [^4]
