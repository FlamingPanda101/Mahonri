# Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity

#type/claim

## The Claim
The hosts of [[Ward Radio]] argue that when a blank-slate American pictures "a religious person," the image that now comes to mind is a Latter-day Saint — and that this is not mere stereotype but is *empirically validated* by the 2024 Pew Research Religious Landscape Study (37,000 respondents).[^1] Reading from a viral X thread by a user named Clint summarizing the data, they note that Latter-day Saints rank as the Christian group **most likely** across a long list of devotional and lifestyle metrics: to say they have become more religious over their lives, to pray daily, to volunteer, to call the Bible extremely/very important (out-ranking evangelicals), to attend services weekly in person, to read scripture monthly outside of services, to pray before meals, to belong to a congregation, to share the religion of their spouse, to have children under 18 at home, to pray or read scripture with their children, and to think about the meaning and purpose of life at least weekly.[^2][^3] The thesis: the LDS Church has become the *archetypal* religion of America the way a high-school quarterback is the archetypal jock — the default, most-recognizable instance of the category.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The metrics are ordinary practice, not fringe zealotry (rated: strong rhetorically; the data is real but secondhand on-air).** A host stresses that "none of this is particularly religious zealotry" — the markers are things like *having a young family*, *sharing a spouse's faith*, and *reading scripture with one's kids monthly*, a threshold he mocks as trivially low ("twelve times a year you crack a book").[^3] The argument's force is that LDS members top the list not by extremism but by sustaining a baseline of ordinary devotion that other American Christians have let lapse. This dovetails with the vault's [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]] — high demand correlates with higher, not lower, ordinary participation.

2. **"Think about the meaning and purpose of life weekly" is framed as the core fruit (rated: moderate, interpretive).** A host singles out this metric as the most important, arguing that the "calming influence of the eternal perspective" the Church supplies is what keeps a person from becoming "a consumerist cog in a machine" — an extended riff against corporate consumerism (Ford telling you your car is old, advertisers telling you your family will love you if you buy the product).[^5] The empirical claim (LDS top this metric) is enlisted to support a values claim (the Restoration produces reflective, non-consumerist lives).

3. **The data is asserted, not independently verified by the hosts (rated: caveat).** The entire numeric case is read off a screenshot of one X user's ("Clint's") summary of the Pew study; the hosts admit they do not know who he is and never open the underlying report on-air.[^1] Pew's 2024 Religious Landscape Study is a real instrument, and its findings on LDS daily prayer, weekly attendance, and scripture engagement are broadly consistent with prior Pew work — but the precise superlatives ("the *most* likely of any Christian group" on each line) are relayed thirdhand and would need to be checked against the published cross-tabs before being cited as fact. The authority of the *source* is C for exactly this reason.

4. **Steelman of the skeptic (rated: strong — survey artifacts cut against the claim).** A critic can grant every number and still resist the "archetype" conclusion. First, self-report devotional surveys reward groups whose culture *scripts* the right answers; an in-group with strong norms around prayer and attendance will over-report relative to looser traditions, so the gap partly measures social desirability and norm-clarity, not raw piety (cf. the vault's [[Definitely Believe Wording Skews Survey Results]] and [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]]). Second, "archetype of American religion" is a cultural-salience claim, and the *reason* Latter-day Saints are salient — the *Book of Mormon* musical, the horror film *Heretic* — is that they are a recognizable minority easy to caricature, not that they are the modal believer (Pew still counts them at roughly 1–2% of U.S. adults). Being the most-mocked instance and being the most-devout instance are distinct facts the argument fuses. The hosts partly grant this, treating the mockery itself as the price of being the poster child (see [[The Mockery of the Most Religious Is a Costly Signal of Faith]]).

## Best Quotes
> "If you're just... blank slate thinking of what's a church or what's really religious or whatever, you think, 'Oh, well, it's those Mormons. It's the Church of Jesus Christ.'"[^4]

> "And you know what's crazy is none of this is particularly religious zealotry... most likely to have children under 18 currently living at home — meaning just to have a productive young family."[^3]

> "Members of the Church of Jesus Christ are the most likely to just take a moment weekly to look inward and center themselves. Not Hindus, not our Muslim brothers and sisters."[^6]
