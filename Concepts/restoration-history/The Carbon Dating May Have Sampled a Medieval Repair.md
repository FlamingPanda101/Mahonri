# The Carbon Dating May Have Sampled a Medieval Repair

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Rebuttal of the Carbon Date)
To defend authenticity against the decisive 1988 radiocarbon result (1260–1390 AD), the most-discussed move is that the **sample was unrepresentative**: all three labs tested a *single corner* of the cloth, and that corner may have contained medieval *repair threads* rewoven into the linen after fire damage — so the labs may have dated a blend skewed toward the newer mending, not the original cloth. A separate, newer technique (wide-angle X-ray scattering, WAXS) suggests a far older, possibly first-century date.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Only one corner was tested (medium)
Radiocarbon dating is *destructive* — you cut off and destroy a piece — so in 1988 they tested just one corner rather than the whole cloth. If that corner is atypical, the date is atypical.[^1]

### 2. Known repairs could contaminate the sample (medium)
Researchers such as Joe Marino argue the tested corner may have been a blend of newer and older fibers, since medieval repairs are known to have been sewn into the cloth after fire damage; the labs would then have gotten an *averaged* age pulled toward the repair threads.[^1]

### 3. A newer method points older (medium, needs validation)
Wide-angle X-ray scattering analyzes how X-rays scatter through the linen's molecular structure; one WAXS study suggested the cloth could date to the first century. Bertuzzi stresses this needs much more validation before it can overrule the radiocarbon result.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic's strong reply: the "invisible reweave" hypothesis (Benford–Marino) has been widely criticized — the textile experts who examined the sample reported homogeneous linen with no patch, the three labs' results were statistically consistent with each other (not the scatter a blended sample would produce), and a seamless undetectable medieval reweave is itself an extraordinary, unevidenced claim. WAXS dating is sensitive to temperature/humidity history (the cloth survived fires) and is not an established, calibrated dating method. So the carbon date stands as the best evidence, and the contamination story is special pleading.

## Counter-Objection
The defender answers that the burden runs both ways: the original raw data were never fully released for independent reanalysis for years, and later statistical re-examinations have argued the three labs' sub-samples were *not* as homogeneous as claimed, consistent with a gradient across the corner. Granting that WAXS is immature, the legitimate conclusion is *not* "the carbon date is overturned" but "a single-corner destructive test is a thin reed for a unique object, and re-testing with multiple sites and non-destructive methods is warranted." Bertuzzi himself does not claim the date is *refuted* — only that its reliability is genuinely open, which is enough to keep authenticity on the table.

## LDS Relevance
None specific. The note is retained as a case study in how a lone, seemingly decisive scientific datum can rest on contestable *sampling* assumptions — a transferable lesson for evaluating "case-closed" empirical claims in any apologetic context.
