# The Son Bears Yah but Never El Elyon

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] observes a precise naming asymmetry in scripture: the Son claims the divine name **Yah** for himself, but **never** the names **El** or **El Elyon** ("Most High God") — those are reserved to the Father. The Son is not the Most High; he gives honor and glory to the Most High and is called into relationship by him. This grounds a real distinction of persons (and a primacy of the Father) without diminishing the Son's divinity. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Yah is shared, El Elyon is not (rating: moderate; lexical observation)
"The name Yahweh is given to the son. Now what's interesting is I never see the term Yahweh given to the Holy Ghost... He's never called Yahweh. In the same sense, however, the name El or Elohim is never used for the son... he never claims the divine name El Elyon, which means the Most High God." [^1]

> "Those are unique to the father... we see a distinction because the son is not the most high god. He gives honor and glory to the most high god and he is called to be in relationship by the most high god, but the most high god is the father." [^2]

### 2. Father has primacy as the source of divinity (rating: strong; Ostler's system)
The Father "has a primacy as the source of divinity... He's the one who has made Jesus divine. He's the one who exalted Jesus as Acts says." But honor and glory "don't mean you're a different kind of being." See [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]]. [^3]

### 3. Two thrones / right-hand enthronement (rating: moderate; textual)
The naming asymmetry matches the throne imagery: Daniel's "two thrones" (Most High + a son given a throne) and Psalm 110's Son at the Father's right hand. See [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7:13-14]], [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110:1]]. [^4]

### 4. Aseity belongs to the Father (rating: moderate; philosophical)
Ostler links this to [[Richard Swinburne]]'s point that only the Father has *aseity* — "the only one who is fundamental." But Ostler resists the conclusion that this makes the Son non-divine, because for LDS thought each divine person is uncreated and fully divine; the asymmetry is one of *source and honor*, not of ontological kind. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A Trinitarian or biblical-unitarian could press the asymmetry in opposite directions. The unitarian says: if only the Father is El Elyon and the only one with aseity, then strictly only the Father is God and the Son is a derivative being — Ostler's own data undercut Christ's full deity. The Nicene Trinitarian says: the asymmetry is merely the eternal *relations of origin* (the Son is begotten, not made), which preserves one *being* while distinguishing persons. Ostler answers the first by affirming the Son's full divinity-in-relationship, and the second by denying that "begotten not made" coheres with divine simplicity. See [[Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail]].
