# "Two Churches Within a Church" on Adam-God

## The Claim
The host ("Carden") frames the modern LDS dispute over Adam-God as a symptom of **"two churches within a church"** — two incompatible apologetic camps, each claiming "academic and spiritual consensus" while in fact there is "no consensus." One camp **minimizes** Adam-God to stay acceptable to Protestants in interfaith settings; the other **maximizes** it into an infinite regression of gods to stay acceptable to academics. His complaint: a layperson "can't get information on it and know actually what's up" unless they join one of the partisan camps, and "I feel it's somewhere here in the middle" [^1].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The "no ice on the rocks" maximalist camp (the framing's first half).** One camp "secretly believe[s]... Adam-God theory actually had some good parts to it and there's an infinite regression of gods," using it as the "no ice on the rocks bottle of Mormonism" that "kind of backdoor tells us what grandpa Jesus was up to." For interfaith optics they'll say "we don't believe it," but privately treat it as the deep doctrine [^2].

2. **The Protestant-facing minimizer camp (the second half).** The other camp is the "oh no, dear God, what did we do for 50 years" group whose FAIR-style articles reinterpret the Lorenzo Snow couplet ("as God once was, man may become") as merely "referencing the condescension of God" — God once *mortal* during Christ's earthly ministry — rather than an eternal regression. This camp is "desperate to be accepted by Protestants" [^3].

3. **Both audiences actually reject the church anyway (the host's sting).** "Both of them forget the Protestants hate us... and guess what, so do the academics." Academia "hates the Book of Mormon, doesn't believe in transoceanic... pre-Columbian" content and treats it as "data over dogma fabrication of the 19th century." So each camp courts a constituency that will never accept the church, which the host reads as evidence neither posture is truth-tracking [^4].

4. **Blyth's reply: doctrinal pluralism is by design (the resolution offered).** [[Christopher Blythe|Chris Blyth]] partly accepts the diagnosis but denies it is a scandal: "I don't believe we're all supposed to be aligned on every issue." The church's doctrine is "taught by a prophet... receiving revelation on what needs to be taught at that time," so private insights into mysteries — even true ones — are "still not the church's doctrine" and may be a thing one is obligated "to keep their mouths shut" about (he cites Lorenzo Snow being told to stay silent about godhood until it was restored to the church). Blyth does insist on infinite regression personally ("I'm actually worried about Latter-day Saints who aren't holding to that idea"), while separating it from Adam-God: "outside of Adam-God, I don't think those are as connected" [^5]. Blyth's deeper move is to **dissolve** the dilemma by directing the seeker to personal revelation, "looking beyond the mark," and basics ("are we... serving our fellow man") rather than to either camp's consensus [^6].

### Steelman of the critic
The host's own framing concedes the critic's strongest point: if competent, faithful Latter-day Saints "can't get information... and know actually what's up" about whether Adam is God, the church lacks a determinate answer on a question the host himself calls one "we should be united" on. Blyth's rejoinder — "Brigham Young seems to have disagreed with you in 1857" that this is a unity issue — is offered as a quip but underscores the instability: the historical prophet and the modern apologists do not agree on whether Adam-God is even the kind of thing the church must settle [^7].

## Best Quotes
> "There's like two churches within a church here. There's the old school people that kind of secretly believe... Adam-God theory actually had some good parts to it and there's an infinite regression of gods... And then there's the 'oh no, dear God, what did we do for 50 years' FAIR-Mormon article writing" camp. — "Carden" (host) [^2]

> "Both of them claim that they are the real version and have the academic and spiritual consensus. But in reality, I see no consensus... I just want to know what the truth is. And I feel it's somewhere here in the middle." — host [^1]

> "I don't believe we're all supposed to be aligned on every issue... even if I learned insights about anything that might be true, it's still not the church's doctrine." — Chris Blyth [^5]
