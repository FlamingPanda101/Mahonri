# The Scandinavian Trafficking Claim Is Sensationalized Folklore

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A Reddit meme claims early Latter-day Saint missionary work in Scandinavia "was really just a thinly veiled sex trafficking operation bent on funneling young women into the Utah plural marriage machine." [[Keystone]] argues this is a modernized version of 1800s anti-Mormon cartoons, refuted by the actual immigration statistics.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **No source, hostile origin.** The meme carries no source and comes from a user "clearly hostile to the church" — the first red flag for any historical claim.[^2] *Rating: moderate — an argument about evidentiary quality, not yet positive disproof.*

2. **The immigration statistics contradict trafficking.** This is the load-bearing point. Of over 10,000 Scandinavian converts who immigrated between 1853 and 1882, **74% did so in family groups**; only about **15% were marriageable women** and **12% marriageable men**, and many of these eligible individuals married each other. "These Scandinavian converts by and large embraced Mormonism in families." Stories of abduction "had their germ in occasional runaways and desertions," but "the statistics and the accounts of the converts themselves provide a convincing, not to say startling, corrective of folklore."[^3] *Rating: strong — a trafficking operation targeting young women is incompatible with a population that is three-quarters family migration and only ~15% single women.*

3. **It is recycled nineteenth-century propaganda.** The post "is really just a modernized version of the sensationalized 1800s anti-Mormon political cartoons." Per historian [[Richard Van Wagoner]], "the Mormon harem dominated by lascivious males with hyperactive libidos did not exist"; the "image of unlimited lust was largely the creation of gentile travelers to Salt Lake City more interested in titillating audiences back home than in accurately portraying plural marriage."[^4] *Rating: strong — situates the meme in a documented genre of hostile caricature; see [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem]].*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic could note that the statistics show *some* single women did emigrate and that "occasional runaways and desertions" existed, so the folklore was not invented from nothing; and that the power asymmetry of a proselytizing religion recruiting converts who then emigrate to a polygamous society raises real ethical questions even absent literal trafficking. The disciplined conclusion: the specific "sex trafficking operation" charge is false (the demographics refute it), while the broader discomfort about plural marriage's pressures is a separate, legitimate topic that the sensational meme actually obscures.

## Counter-objection
Keystone's own framing concedes the legitimate topic up front — its stated complaint is that piling sensational claims onto polygamy "dilutes" the genuinely worthwhile discussion. Granting that single converts emigrated and unhappy cases existed does nothing to rescue the trafficking claim, which asserts *organized targeting of women for sexual exploitation* — a claim the family-group majority and the documented consent process ([[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent]]) directly contradict.
