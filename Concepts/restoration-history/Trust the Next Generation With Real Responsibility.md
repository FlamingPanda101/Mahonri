# Trust the Next Generation With Real Responsibility

#type/claim

## The Claim
Young people disengage from a church less because they conclude *they* don't need it than because they conclude the church doesn't need *them*. A demanding faith that entrusts its youth with genuine responsibility — callings, leadership, teaching, lay ministry — therefore retains and forms them better than a low-expectation one. The claim is notable because the hosts adopt it directly from a critic, [[Russell Moore]], who wrote: "Too many of our young people are seeing a flawed version of Christianity, not because they've concluded that they don't need the church, but because they've concluded that the church doesn't need them."[^1] It pairs the empirical "lay-participation" mechanism with the scriptural "body with many members" image (see [[1 Corinthians 12.12-27|1 Corinthians 12:12-27]]).

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Moore's diagnosis, conceded by both sides (Evidence: shared premise).** "We need to ... not only train them to carry on the faith, but also trust them to do so. Our expectations for the next generation are not just high enough."[^1] Because the critic and the apologists agree on the mechanism, the disagreement collapses to *which* church actually practices it.

2. **Lay responsibility as the LDS instantiation (Evidence: structural / experiential).** A host recounts being made branch president as a teenager in Siberia after the leader left: "I was a kid ... and I'm like, oh my gosh, I got to take this seriously. I got to step up."[^2] The structural point: an all-lay church with home-teaching/ministering, youth-taught lessons, and rotating callings forces the "the church needs you" experience that Moore says evangelical youth lack — "they would die to have that kind of commitment and devotion."[^3]

3. **The "body, many members" turn (Evidence: scriptural tu quoque).** Moore grounds his case in Paul's image that "a critical aspect of membership is to use one's gifts to build up the church." The hosts ask why that excludes the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, and propose that the Church may simply "have a spiritual gift of developing programs that enable and empower their youth" — seminary, institute, family home evening — concluding, "it is not our excellence but your pride that is keeping your youth program down."[^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view (the high-control reading):** Handing a teenager an adult leadership burden ("I got to take this seriously") can be framed as either healthy trust or premature, high-pressure responsibilization that fuses adolescent identity to the institution before the person can evaluate it freely — a recognized feature of high-demand-group socialization. A critic would add that "the church needs you" can shade into "you owe the church," converting belonging into obligation and raising the psychological cost of ever leaving. The data the hosts would need — that entrusted youth flourish *and* remain free to exit without crisis — is asserted experientially, not demonstrated; the well-being side lives in [[The Fruits of the Church - Do Critics Have a Better Program]] and must carry that weight.

## Best Quotes

> "Too many of our young people ... have concluded that the church doesn't need them. ... We need to not only train them to carry on the faith, but also trust them to do so." — Russell Moore[^1]

> "I was off in the middle of Siberia and the branch president leaves and they're like, you're branch president now. ... I got to step up."[^2]

> "It is not our excellence but your pride that is keeping your youth program down. We welcome you into the body of Christ. You don't welcome us."[^4]
