# The Existential Reading - What Sustains Things in Being Here and Now

## The Point
The Aristotelian proof is not an argument about a first event in the distant past; it is an argument about what holds things in existence *at every present moment*. Feser repeatedly stresses that although the argument begins from observed change, it "moves on to the question of what explains the existence, at any moment, of the things that undergo changes." [^1] The regress in view is a regress of "the actualizers of the existence of things," and the first actualizer is first in the sense that "it can actualize the existence of other things without its own existence having to be actualized." [^1] This is why timing objections (simultaneity, relativity, a beginningless universe) miss the target: we could "ignore all that and just focus on the sheer existence of something at any instant." [^2]

## Evidence
1. **Existence, not just change, requires actualization.** The act/potency distinction "has application not only to the changes things undergo through time, but also to their very existence at any moment of time." Even the bare existence of water — "the fact that its atoms constitute water rather than discrete quantities of oxygen and hydrogen — involves the actualization of a potential and thus requires something to do the actualizing." [^2]
2. **A deeper efficient cause than the originating one.** For a radioactive Pb²¹⁰ atom there is the past generating cause, but "more to the present point, there is a deeper efficient cause in whatever it is that keeps the Pb²¹⁰ atom in existence here and now." That something exists *now* as this kind of thing "rather than not existing at all — itself presupposes the actualization of a potential," requiring "a cause in something already actual." [^3]
3. **Why this defeats the Newton/Einstein objections.** Newton's law "presupposes the existence of things like water molecules," so it "can hardly explain their existence." [^4] Even in an Einsteinian block universe with no real change, "the sheer existence of that universe as a whole — in a single, timeless moment, as it were — would involve the actualization of potential and thus an actualizer distinct from the world itself." [^5]
4. **Simultaneity is the wrong worry.** Even granting Hume/relativity quibbles about whether causes and effects are simultaneous, "questions about the duration of the events in which change occurs and about whether any such events are simultaneous are not really to the point." [^2] The existential dependency holds at each instant regardless.

## Best Quotes
> "Though the argument begins by asking what explains the changes we observe in the world around us, it moves on to the question of what explains the existence, at any moment, of the things that undergo changes." [^1]

> "There is a deeper efficient cause in whatever it is that keeps the Pb²¹⁰ atom in existence here and now." [^3]

> "Even its very existence — the fact that its atoms constitute water rather than discrete quantities of oxygen and hydrogen — involves the actualization of a potential and thus requires something to do the actualizing." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the strongest common ground in the whole chapter. The "here and now" reading lands squarely on a biblical truth the Restoration affirms wholeheartedly: "in him we live and move and have our being," and "in him all things hold together" — God as the *present sustaining cause*, not an absentee watchmaker ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). Against atheism, the argument that the cosmos cannot sustain *its own* existence moment-to-moment is real apologetic force an LDS thinker can borrow. The divergence is downstream: Feser concludes that the sustaining cause must be *purely actual* and therefore timeless, immutable, and immaterial ([[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]]). A Latter-day Saint can affirm a present, intimate, sustaining God while denying that "sustainer" entails "potency-free absolute" — especially since the existential argument, taken alone, shows only that the cosmos depends on something already actual, not that that something is the Aristotelian unmoved mover ([[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]). In fairness to Feser, the existential reading is genuinely deeper than crude "first domino" cosmological arguments and is hard for the naturalist to dodge; the LDS quarrel is not with sustaining causation but with the further metaphysical attributes packed onto the sustainer.
