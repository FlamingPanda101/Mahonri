## The Point
Over the first Christian centuries, a Sunday liturgy gradually developed as a worship service built around the Eucharist. Justin Martyr (mid-second century) described a relatively simple service of scripture readings, a sermon, and prayers before the blessing and sharing of bread and wine. When basilical churches were built in the fourth century, they solidified developments already evident in the third, producing a formal sequence still followed in liturgical churches today.

## Evidence
1. Justin Martyr (ca. AD 151–155): "On the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles [the Gospels] or the writings of the [Old Testament] prophets are read ... the president verbally instructs ... we all rise together and pray ... bread and wine and water are brought, and the president ... offers prayers and thanksgivings ... and the people assent, saying Amen."
2. To those absent, "a portion is sent by the deacons" — an early form of administering the sacrament to the homebound.
3. In the fourth-century basilica setting, the communal meal disappeared; larger congregations followed a formal sequence of prayers, hymns, scripture readings, and a sermon leading to the climactic blessing and partaking of bread and a cup of wine.
4. The unbaptized were dismissed before the Eucharist, which was kept private; the main weekly gathering was Sunday morning, though some places celebrated the Eucharist daily.
5. Generally there were no pews, benches, or chairs — the congregation stood throughout.

## Best Quotes
> "On the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles [the Gospels] or the writings of the [Old Testament] prophets are read, as long as time permits." [^1]

> "There is a distribution to each, and a participation of that over which thanks have been given, and to those who are absent a portion is sent by the deacons." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Justin's outline — gathering on Sunday, reading scripture, a sermon, prayers, blessing bread and wine, and sending portions to the absent by deacons — strikingly parallels the structure of the Latter-day Saint sacrament meeting and its provision for the homebound.
