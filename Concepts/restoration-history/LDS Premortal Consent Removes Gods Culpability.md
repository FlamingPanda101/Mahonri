# LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The creation dilemma charges that God is culpable for creating souls He foreknows will be damned, partly because they "didn't ask to be here" (non-consent). [[Hayden Carroll]]'s LDS solve (chapter eight of *The Creation Dilemma*) answers via three doctrines: (1) creation from pre-existing **co-eternal material/intelligences** rather than *ex nihilo*; (2) a **premortal life** in which spirits *consented* to enter mortality; and (3) the claim that God may be **bound by eternal law** to admit any intelligence that wills to come. Because the soul itself chose to enter "the creation cycle" knowing it might become a son of perdition, "that takes the moral framing off of God."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Consent answers the non-consent premise directly — rate: strong within the LDS frame.** The creedal dilemma's bite depends on the creature having no say in coming to exist (see [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]]). If a premortal self genuinely consented, the moral structure changes from "God imposed existence and its risks" to "the agent accepted existence and its risks" — much like an adult who knowingly accepts a dangerous undertaking.[^1] See [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]. **Steelman of the critic:** (a) Did the spirit consent with adequate information? Carroll says God did *not* tell them their specific outcome ("because I think if he did, then they would say I don't want to do this") — which undercuts informed consent and re-raises the dilemma at the premortal stage.[^2] (b) Consent does not obviously dissolve a *guardian's* duty: a lifeguard who lets a willing swimmer drown is still culpable, so "they chose to come" may not fully exonerate a God who could refuse. (c) The doctrine relocates rather than eliminates the puzzle: why is God *bound* to admit a spirit He foreknows will be lost?
2. **The "God is bound by eternal law" move — rate: distinctive but speculative (Carroll flags it as theory).** Carroll's "actual better answer" is that God is *obligated* to allow any intelligence that wills to enter the creation cycle — citing the precedent of Lucifer, whom God "let into the creation cycle" knowing he would become Satan.[^3] This grounds the solve in LDS finitist theology (God works within uncreated law, not above it). **Steelman:** this purchases a good God at the price of a *non-sovereign* God constrained by external law — exactly the cost the creedal critic warns against (cf. [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]], where denying premise #2 or #3 is the LDS escape). Carroll accepts this trade openly.
3. **It pairs with rejecting *ex nihilo* — rate: doctrinally integrated.** Consent only has a subject to attach to if intelligences pre-exist; thus this concept is inseparable from [[God Found Spirits He Did Not Create - Book of Abraham Solve]] and D&C 93's uncreated intelligence. Carroll notes open theism would *also* solve the dilemma, but he prefers the material-creation + consent route because he cannot reconcile open theism with fulfilled prophecy (see [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]]).

## Best Quotes
> "If somebody consents to come here and God says, 'Hey, you may end up a son of perdition.' And they say, 'I'm going to go anyway.' Then that takes the moral framing off of God." [^1]

> "Potentially, this is a theory, God is bound to allow somebody who wants to enter into the creation cycle to do so. For example, why did God let Lucifer into the creation cycle if he knew that he was going to become Satan?... We chose and God was required to let us come." [^3]

> "Without telling us obviously the outcome because I think if he did, then they would say I don't want to do this." [^2]
