# The Pace Memo Was Not an Investigation but a Persuasive Essay

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics treat the 1990 [[Glenn L. Pace]] memo as a damning *investigation* — "Detective Glenn Pace" uncovering satanic abuse across the wards. Ward Radio Part 3 rebuts this: the memo is not investigative at all but a persuasive essay arguing that recovered-memory therapy should be believed.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The vocabulary of investigation is simply absent (strong — textual)
"The word discover appears nowhere in the memo. Neither does the word investigate or investigation." Pace was not discovering anything; he was following up on reports already collected by therapists [^1].

### 2. Pace states a persuasive, not investigative, purpose (strong — self-description)
Pace says his aim "was to obtain an intellectual and spiritual conviction as to the seriousness of this problem within the church," and "spends most of the memo explaining why recovered memory therapy should be accepted and not dismissed as experimental." From the start he "admits that he is not interested in confirming the reports" [^2].

### 3. The proportions prove the genre (strong — structural)
Of 63 paragraphs, only 3 (paragraphs 2, 3, 19) discuss specifics; "90% of the memo speaks in general language," and "59 out of 63 paragraphs" treat "tentative theories of psychotherapy." Eleven of twelve pages recite from the Book of Ether and argue the patients should be believed [^3].

### 4. The logic is advocacy, not analysis (moderate — assessment)
Pace argues the witnesses' mental illness is *proof* of abuse and that their identical stories *confirm* each other — both inferences the episode shows run backwards (identical stories and shared diagnoses are equally signs of contamination). This is persuasion marshalled toward a conclusion, not neutral inquiry. See [[Identical Cross-Case Allegations Point to the Interviewer Not the Crime]] [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A critic could fairly say a pastoral leader is not required to write like a detective, and that an internal spiritual-welfare memo can still document a real problem even if its genre is hortatory. True — but the memo is precisely *cited by critics* as evidentiary proof of widespread abuse, and on that evidentiary question its own author disclaims investigation, collects no verifiable specifics, and devotes himself to defending a therapy method. As welfare counsel it may be sincere; as evidence of satanic crime it establishes nothing.

## Best Quotes
> "Any claim that the Pace Memo represents discovery is false. Nothing is discovered in the Pace Memo. The word discover appears nowhere in the memo. Neither does the word investigate or investigation." [^1]

> "His stated objective was to defend untested psychological theories. It is written as a persuasive essay, an apologetics piece." [^2]

> "Pace's memo sounds like a freshman college student's report in an entry-level psychology class. He's writing to convince the committee and himself that the claims shouldn't be dismissed as false." [^3]
