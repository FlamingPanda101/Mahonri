# Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event

## The Claim
The "Great Apostasy" is best understood not as a single dramatic rebellion or coup but as a **gradual, cumulative falling away** of authority, doctrine, and the gifts of the Spirit over the first several centuries — a slow drift in which "the righteous are removed and none perceives it." The necessity of a restoration depends on this apostasy: "if you don't understand the apostasy, you don't understand the need for the restoration."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Greek word supports both readings, but "falling away" fits the history (rating: moderate; lexical)
Per [[Todd Compton]], *apostasia* = *apo* ("away") + *histēmi* ("to stand"). It can denote an act of open rebellion/mutiny *or* a gradual standing-away. Day leans hard toward the gradual sense because the historical record shows incremental change rather than a single datable coup. [^1]

### 2. The New Testament itself warns of an internal, creeping decay (rating: strong; canonical)
Day marshals a chain of NT apostasy texts: [[2 Thessalonians 2.3|2 Thess 2:3]] ("a falling away first"), [[Acts 20.29-30|Acts 20:29-30]] ("grievous wolves"), [[1 Corinthians 11.18-19|1 Cor 11]] (divisions/heresies), [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians]], the Pastorals ("some shall depart from the faith"), and the Johannine epistles. The picture is internal perversion, not external desertion. [^2]

### 3. Hugh Nibley's "frog in warming water" framing (rating: moderate; LDS scholar)
Day cites Nibley: apostasy is "not a desertion of the cause but a perversion of it, a process by which the righteous are removed and none perceives it" — like frogs slowly warmed until cooked. The corruption is imperceptible from inside. [^3]

### 4. Old Testament prophetic backdrop (rating: weak-moderate; proof-texting)
[[Amos 8.11-12|Amos 8:11-12]] (a "famine... of hearing the words of the LORD") and [[Amos 3.7|Amos 3:7]] supply the prophetic theme of a coming drought of revelation that the gradual model fulfills. [^1]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The "open rebellion" reading is not baseless: Day grants the NT shows genuine open revolts — the Judaizers undoing Paul's work in Galatians, the secessionists of 1–3 John, Diotrephes expelling apostolic emissaries ([[3 John 1.9-10|3 John]]). A critic could also argue the whole apostasy thesis is unfalsifiable — any change counts as "drift." Day concedes he is "still formulating" his view and "reserves the right to change my mind tonight," which is intellectually honest but underscores the thesis's elasticity.

## Best Quotes

> "if you don't understand the apostasy, you don't understand the need for the restoration. Similarly, if you don't understand what the fall did, you don't understand the need for the atonement." [^4]

> "I'm leaning hard into falling away and less from an open rebellion... it's a gradual shift." [^1]

> "It's not a desertion of the cause, this apostasy, but a perversion of it, a process by which the righteous are removed and none perceives it." [^3]
