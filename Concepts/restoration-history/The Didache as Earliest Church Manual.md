# The Didache as Earliest Church Manual

## The Claim
The Didache (Greek *didachē*, "teaching/instruction") is plausibly the earliest Christian church manual — likely 1st century, "as old as the gospels" — functioning like a primitive [[D&C 27|Doctrine and Covenants section 20]] or [[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 4-5]]: it gives the order of the church, ritual prayers, and ethical instruction. Its contents both parallel and differ from later (and LDS) practice in revealing ways.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. It is a how-to-run-a-church document (rating: strong; textual)
Butler/Day: the Didache "contains prayer... it's like the first church manual," telling congregations how to baptize, pray, hold the Eucharist, and *elect* leaders (ch. 15) — comparable to D&C 20 or Moroni 4-5. See [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles]]. [^1]

### 2. The eucharistic prayers are strikingly un-creedal (rating: strong; primary text)
Day and Butler read the Didache's bread/wine prayers (via earlychristianwritings.com): the cup prayer thanks God "for the holy vine of David thy servant," the bread prayer for "life and knowledge... even as this broken bread was scattered over the hills and was gathered together and became one." Notably, **neither mentions the body and blood of Christ** — raising the question of how the earliest Eucharist was understood. Day says the literal body-and-blood interpretation is a *later* development. [^2]

### 3. Traveling prophets and the "three-day rule" (rating: moderate; striking detail)
The Didache assumes itinerant **prophets** circulating among churches, with a test: a guest preacher who overstays three days is a false prophet. This confirms that "in the first-century Christian communities you had people who were called prophets" — against later cessationism. [^3]

### 4. The "two ways" parallels 2 Nephi 9 (rating: moderate; Day's "Midrash")
Day notes the Didache's "way of life / way of death" doctrine parallels [[2 Nephi 9.39|2 Nephi 9]] (life vs. death) and the Dead Sea Scrolls' two-ways teaching (a connection Nibley flagged) — suggesting old Jewish material preserved in both. He flags this is speculative ("just Mike Day Midrash"). [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional reader notes the Didache's bread/wine prayers are *thanksgivings* (eucharistia) that need not exhaust early sacramental theology — Paul (1 Cor 11) and Ignatius already speak of the body and blood, so the Didache's silence is not denial. And the document, while early, was *not* canonized, so its divergences (elected leaders, traveling prophets) may reflect local practice the wider church corrected. Day grants "we do not know this was the first" eucharistic prayer, only that it is an old one, and that the body/blood question remains genuinely open in the earliest layer.
