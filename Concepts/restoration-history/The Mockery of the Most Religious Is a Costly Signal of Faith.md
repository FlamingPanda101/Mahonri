# The Mockery of the Most Religious Is a Costly Signal of Faith

#type/claim

## The Claim
Being the most religious religion is "a double-edged sword," the [[Ward Radio]] hosts argue: precisely because the Church of Jesus Christ has become the [[Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity|archetypal religion of America]], it is the one that satirists reach for when they want to mock *faith as such*.[^1] Their key move is that *The Book of Mormon* musical — which won seven Tony Awards — is "not about our faith in particular," but "making fun of faith in general," with Latter-day Saints merely conscripted as "the poster child."[^2] The mockery is therefore evidence of LDS religious seriousness, not of any peculiar LDS absurdity; it is the modern equivalent of the persecution Christ and Paul endured for visible devotion ("that's what Christ was too, that's what Paul was too").[^3] A second, sharper claim rides alongside it: the selectivity of the mockery exposes the mockers. The same liberal culture that would "scatter like roaches" at a *Muhammad: The Musical* feels free to lampoon Mormons — so the hosts call on critics to "drop the act" that they are pluralists and "just admit you're a tribalist."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "archetype" premise does the work (rated: moderate, conceptual).** The argument is a corollary of the empirical-archetype claim: if you want to caricature "religion," you pick the most recognizable specimen, just as the archetypal "jock" is a football quarterback. Satirists "look for the archetype" and "find us" because Latter-day Saints are the last group "where people actually attend," in contrast to mainline denominations the hosts depict as emptying out (an imagined "musical of a guy singing as everybody walks out the door" of the SBC).[^5] The visibility that makes the Church mockable is the same visibility that makes it the default religious exemplar. See [[Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity]].

2. **Selective mockery as a tribalism test (rated: strong as rhetoric).** The strongest version is not "we are persecuted" but "your mockery is inconsistent, and the inconsistency is the tell." A self-described free-speech purist among the hosts says he will not condemn comedy for being offensive, but insists the culture should stop pretending it is even-handed: "If you're down with mocking Mormons in a way you would never mock Jews, Muslims... let's just drop the act."[^4] This reframes the complaint from a thin-skinned grievance into a charge of bad faith against a culture that markets itself as tolerant — connecting to the costly-signaling logic the vault already develops for the Trinity in [[Trinity as Identity Marker and Costly Signal]].

3. **Christological framing of the mockery (rated: moderate; self-aware).** The hosts cast the willingness to be mocked as Christlike — "the second people don't like us, it's like Jesus energy" — while one of them immediately lampshades the move with the joke "we're so Christlike, bro," signaling awareness that claiming persecution can become self-flattering.[^3] The note preserves both the claim and the hosts' own irony about it; the substantive point survives the joke, but the self-deprecation is itself part of how Ward Radio guards against [[Equal Treatment Mistaken for Persecution]].

4. **Steelman of the critic (rated: strong — the argument over-reads the data).** A fair critic can grant the inconsistency charge and still deflate the conclusion. First, comedy targets the *culturally legible and safe*, and a small, geographically concentrated, historically American group is far more legible to a Broadway audience than Islam or Judaism — so the asymmetry may track *familiarity and physical safety* (no fatwa risk) rather than a principled anti-LDS animus, which weakens "they fear the others but mock us" into "they know us and don't fear reprisal." Second, the inference "mocked ∴ serious/Christlike" is too cheap: many silly things are mocked because they are silly, so mockery cannot by itself certify the mocked party's virtue without smuggling in the prior conclusion that the faith is admirable. Third, treating *The Book of Mormon* musical as evidence *for* the faith risks the same move the hosts criticize — reading external attention as vindication (cf. the vault's caution in [[Equal Treatment Mistaken for Persecution]]). The hosts partly concede the first point ("it's a package deal — we're the most religious and we can be the most mocked"), accepting the mockery as a cost of salience rather than proof of righteousness.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "When they wanted to make a musical making fun of [religion]... who do they think of? Who's the most caricature? ...That whole musical actually really really isn't about our faith in particular. It's about faith in general... but they chose us as the poster child for it."[^2]

> "If you're down with mocking Mormons in a way you would never mock Jews, Muslims... let's just drop the act. Just admit you're a tribalist."[^4]

> "It's a package deal. We're the most religious and we can be the most mocked. But that's what Christ was too. That's what Paul was too."[^3]
