## The Point
Baptism was the rite of initiation and entrance into the church — "the most foundational and important early Christian ritual." In the New Testament it was a submersion in water performed with little ceremony by an authorized person immediately upon a convert's acceptance of the gospel. Over the second through fourth centuries the rite developed an elaborate ceremony built on the New Testament's doctrinal foundation, heightening the symbolism of dying and rising with Christ.

## Evidence
1. NT baptism signified repentance, profession of faith in Christ, forgiveness and purification from sin, spiritual rebirth, dying to sin and rising to new life, preparation to receive the Holy Spirit, reception of salvation, and formally joining the church.
2. From the third century, baptism usually required months or years of formal teaching beforehand; the night before Easter became the favored occasion, heightening the burial-and-rising symbolism ([[Romans 6.3-4|Romans 6:3–4]]; Ephesians 2:5–6; Colossians 2:12–13).
3. The general fourth-century ritual: a prayer inviting the Holy Spirit upon the waters; the candidate disrobed; vocally renounced Satan; received an anointing with oil; entered the font; confessed faith in Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (Matthew 28:19) and was immersed after each confession; then received laying on of hands with another anointing, was clothed in a white robe, and joined the congregation for first Eucharist at sunrise on Easter.
4. The white robe alluded to "all of you who were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ" ([[Galatians 3.27|Galatians 3:27]]); the Greek verb *enduō* ("to clothe / put on") is the root of the English *endow* and *endowment*.
5. In Syriac tradition, the robe developed into a "Robe of Glory" that Adam and Eve wore in Eden but lost — reclaimed at baptism as a terrestrial anticipation of eschatological Paradise.

## Best Quotes
> "As the rite of initiation and entrance into the church, it was the most foundational and important early Christian ritual." [^1]

> "The Greek verb for 'to clothe or to put on' is enduō, from which we get the English endow and endowment." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison flags the *enduō* / endowment etymology and the white-robe "Robe of Glory" tradition as intriguing conceptual vocabulary, while cautioning Saints (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]) not to over-read one-to-one parallels. [^2]
