# Anti-Mormon Pseudo-History Pioneered the Woke History Method

## The Claim
[[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] advance a cultural thesis: the cynical, motive-impugning, source-selective "woke history" they associate with figures like [[Howard Zinn]] was *first* practiced on Joseph Smith. [[Fawn Brodie]]'s 1946 psychobiography is "the original CES Letter," "the original pseudo-history," "the original thin veneer of academia." Latter-day Saints were the canaries: the method that later tore down the Founders was rehearsed on their prophet, and "you didn't come to our defense back then."[^1]

> **Caution:** this is the hosts' contested cultural-political argument. The note steelmans the academic reply.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Brodie as patient zero (rated: the panel's thesis)
"Joseph Smith was the first guy ... the first one who got this kind of stupid Howard Zinnian fawnian psychoanalysis, purple-haired history, retrospective psychosis." The structure — pick a figure who can't answer, impute hidden motives, foreground only the damaging data — is what the hosts see recurring from Brodie to Zinn.[^1]

### 2. The Zinn/Columbus exhibit (rated: host testimony)
Ellis recounts his own primary-source "deep dive" on Christopher Columbus and concludes Zinn's treatment is "intentional ideological propaganda," which "caused me to lose all faith in the academic establishment." The generalization: history departments skew heavily left, so "your history is being written by people who hate you," who "want to make you a villain."[^1]

### 3. The affidavit analogy (rated: illustrative)
The hosts compare hostile 1830s testimony about Joseph (the basis of *Mormonism Unveiled*) to "the comment section of the 1830s," and imagine future historians psychoanalyzing them from cherry-picked YouTube comments — a vivid claim that hostile sources make terrible evidence, then and now.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A serious reply: (a) "woke history" is a polemical, ideologically loaded category, and lumping a credentialed (if contested) historian like Zinn together with Brodie obscures real differences in method and reception; (b) the "academy hates you" framing is a sweeping motive-attribution — exactly the move the hosts elsewhere condemn as psychohistory — and ironically commits the same sin; (c) plenty of *non*-hostile, rigorous scholarship on Joseph Smith exists (Bushman's *Rough Stone Rolling*, Turner's biography), so "history is written by people who hate you" is overdrawn; and (d) documented bad sourcing in *particular* works (Brodie's uncited Fanny Alger claim; the 1998 Jefferson headline) is the real, narrow grievance — and it stands on the evidence without the grand cultural thesis. The defensible core: hostile, motive-driven, source-selective history is a real failure mode, and some anti-Mormon literature exemplifies it.
