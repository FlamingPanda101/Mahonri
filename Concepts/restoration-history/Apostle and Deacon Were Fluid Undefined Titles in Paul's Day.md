# Apostle and Deacon Were Fluid Undefined Titles in Paul's Day

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] stresses that in Paul's time the titles **apostle**, **elder**, and **deacon** — today fixed priesthood offices restricted to men — "were new, still largely undefined, and much more fluid than they are today." Paul applies *apostolos* and *diakonos* broadly, to many people including women. Reading later, rigid office-definitions back into the first century is an anachronism.

## Evidence

### 1. NT leadership evidence is "fragmentary and vague"
Jesus called the Twelve and later the Seventy; James led at Jerusalem with a council of elders; Peter was the "rock." But "the primary calling and office of Christian leaders during the first century was to 'go into all the world, and preach the gospel'" — the community "had not yet defined many of its leadership positions beyond the apostolic calling." [^1]

### 2. *Apostolos* = "one who is sent forth"
The Greek term was used from the 5th c. BC for a messenger, diplomat, or **commissioned agent** authorized to act on another's behalf. Christians adopted it for those commissioned by Jesus Christ. Paul applies "apostle" not only to the Twelve but to Apollos, Barnabas, Titus, Epaphroditus, Silvanus, Timothy — and Junia. For Paul an apostle is generally one who had "encountered the risen Christ" and received a commission to preach. [^2]

### 3. *Diakonos* = honored servant/emissary, not menial helper
From the 5th c. BC *diakonos* meant "messenger," "mediator," or "servant" — and scholars argue it was "a position of responsibility and honor in higher circles." Of 31 KJV NT occurrences it is rendered "minister" 20×, "servant" 8×, "deacon" only 3× — "a demonstration of how translators' personal biases might inform their choice of words." The feminine *diakonissa* ("deaconess") does not appear before the 4th century, so Phoebe's title is the same masculine office-word Paul uses of himself. [^3]

### 4. *Presbyteros* = "old man" / elder; *episkopos* = "overseer"
"Elder" (presbyteros) was borrowed from Jewish synagogue governance (councils of elders, as at Jerusalem). "Bishop" (episkopos, "overseer/guardian") was a title held by Greek civic officials and by the leaders of Greco-Roman voluntary associations, which the house churches imitated. In much of the NT *episkopos* and *presbyteros* are used **interchangeably** (Acts 20:17; Titus 1:5–7; 1 Peter 5:1). [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "in Paul's time, these titles and the organizational structures that would come to define priesthood offices … were new, still largely undefined, and much more fluid than they are today." [^1]

> "we can see Paul using terms like apostle, elder, and deacon broadly and in various ways throughout his writings and in reference to both men and women." [^1]

## LDS Connection
For [[Latter-day Saints]] this guards against assuming the modern, fixed office-structure was simply lifted intact from the apostolic church. Laughton notes Paul himself "was not an official part of an organized quorum of Apostles, as Latter-day Saint readers might expect in light of Church organization today." The fluidity is itself the historical datum. [^2]
