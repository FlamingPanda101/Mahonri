# Steadying the Ark Names Overreach Beyond Ones Stewardship

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] unpack the phrase "putteth forth his hand to steady the ark of God" in [[D&C 85]]:8 as Latter-day Saint shorthand for "a person stepping outside their stewardship to interfere in a matter in which they really don't have any authority to do so." The image is drawn from the Old Testament episode of Uzzah, struck dead for grabbing the Ark of the Covenant (2 Samuel 6). Applied to the Missouri context, it warns Bishop [[Edward Partridge]] (and, more generally, anyone) against overriding the counsel of those the Lord has placed in leadership.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Uzzah episode supplies the idiom (rating: strong; biblical).** When the oxen shook the Ark, "Uzzah put forth his hand... and took hold of it... and God smote him there for his error, and he died by the Ark of God" (2 Samuel 6:6-7). The text gives no insight into Uzzah's motive, which is the point: even a *sincere* attempt to help, outside one's authority, is the error named.[^1]
2. **The phrase targets working outside channels (rating: moderate; interpretive).** "We can be sincere in our concern or our desire to help, but we also have to show a little trust and faith in the people the Lord has placed in positions of leadership." Other revelations "emphasize this idea that we work through channels of authority."[^1]
3. **It fits the Missouri rebellion (rating: strong; historical).** The rebuke lands because Church members in Missouri "keep pushing back against the counsel... from the leaders in Kirtland, particularly Joseph Smith." Verse 8 thus doubles as a specific warning to Partridge and "a general warning of people that... take it upon themselves to... lead the Church."[^2]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic worries this reading weaponizes a proof-text against legitimate dissent: "steadying the ark" can become a thought-terminating label for anyone who questions a leader's decision, even when the leader is genuinely in error. If sincere correction is always "overreach," the principle insulates leadership from accountability and discourages the kind of conscientious objection that healthy institutions need. Uzzah, after all, was trying to keep the sacred Ark from falling in the mud.

## Counter-Objection
The principle, as the hosts frame it, is about *jurisdiction*, not the suppression of all critique: it concerns acting "outside their stewardship... in a matter in which they really don't have any authority," not raising concerns *within* one's stewardship or through proper channels. The hosts elsewhere explicitly affirm honoring those who leave, dealing "gently" with the struggling, and acknowledging that leaders "can make mistakes" — so the note is not a blanket gag on disagreement but a check on self-appointed override of delegated authority (the same self-appointment that drives the "one mighty and strong" misuse — see [[The One Mighty and Strong Was Edward Partridge Not a Future Reformer]]). Uzzah's good intentions are precisely why the warning is sobering: sincerity does not confer jurisdiction.

## Best Quotes
> "The phrase 'steady the ark' has become shorthand for a person stepping outside their stewardship to interfere in a matter in which they really don't have any authority to do so."[^1]

> "We can be sincere in our concern or our desire to help, but we also have to show a little trust and faith in the people the Lord has placed in positions of leadership."[^1]
