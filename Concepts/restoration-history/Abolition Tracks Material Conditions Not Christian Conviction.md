# Abolition Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Conviction

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: abolition correlates with material conditions, self-interest, and technology — not primarily with Christian conviction. He grants the UK case was "profoundly Christian" but argues France, Haiti, and America fit a materialist model.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Haiti — the only successful slave revolt.** The Haitian revolution overthrew slavery and colonial rule by force. "I do not think Haitians were okay with slavery, then read the Bible and decided to rise up." Disutility plus opportunity, not doctrine, drove it. See [[Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming]].[^2] *(Strong — the most decisive self-emancipation was not a churchly project.)*

2. **France — abolish, then reinstate.** Revolutionary France abolished slavery (a "profoundly secularizing influence"), then [[Napoleon]] reinstated it on economic grounds. "When they abolished it, that was Christian influence; when they reinstated it, no, that was nothing to do with Christianity?" The whiplash exposes selective crediting.[^3] *(Strong reversibility argument — the same culture went both ways within a short span.)*

3. **Technology and economics.** Abolition "coincidentally came along with the Enlightenment" and with farming technology that made slavery less economically necessary; photography (the "poor Peter"/scourged-back image) shocked the public. "It's not as simple as some people started going to church."[^4] *(Moderate-to-strong — economic and media factors are real, though Scrivener notes technology itself flourished within Christendom.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener.** (a) Where rights language appears in French/Haitian abolition, it came *from* the UK abolitionist society — Christian genealogy again.[^5] (b) Why did abolition succeed in Christendom and not in the most technologically advanced society (China)? (c) The UK case is unambiguously Christian ([[William Wilberforce]], Quakers). See [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]].[^6] *(Strong — Scrivener concedes mixed motives and material factors explicitly: "five steps forward, four steps back, bloody mixed motives, technology playing a part." The dispute is over which factor is *decisive*, not whether each is present.)* O'Connor's bottom line: "Whether it was right or wrong is separate from whether it was indebted to Christianity — and that's the very question."[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "I do not think Haitians were living in slavery, kind of okay with it, and then started thinking about Christianity and read the Bible and decided to rise up."[^2]

> "When they abolished it, that was probably due to Christian influence. But when they reinstated it — no, no, that was nothing to do with Christianity?"[^3]

> "Napoleon said slavery is good for the economy, and so back it comes."[^3]
