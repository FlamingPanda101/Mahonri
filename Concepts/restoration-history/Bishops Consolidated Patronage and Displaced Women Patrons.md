# Bishops Consolidated Patronage and Displaced Women Patrons

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] identifies a specific economic mechanism by which women lost church influence: bishops **restructured the Roman patronage system around their own office**. Where wealthy women had once been independent patrons of house churches and charity, [[Cyprian of Carthage]] (3rd c.) made the bishopric "the primary benefactor of the community" — a "consolidation of charity" that, by the 4th century, replaced the personal-patronage networks through which women had exercised power.

## Evidence

### 1. Women's patronage was a real avenue of leadership
In Greco-Roman society "a person of higher social status … could function as mediator and dispenser of favor regardless of sex." Women patrons funded missionary work, hosted house churches, and supported other Christians — Phoebe (Paul's *prostatis*), Lydia, Tabitha, Marcella. [^1]

### 2. Cyprian routes all giving through the bishop
Cyprian "used his significant resources and personal social connections to establish the episcopacy … as the primary benefactor of the community." He personally oversaw disbursement of church funds to the poor and to clergy, and "urged wealthy Christians to make all charitable donations to the Church, which he would then distribute." He may have been "the first to insist that it is superior for an individual to donate to the Church rather than directly to the poor." [^2]

### 3. The rhetoric of heavenly honor
Cyprian invoked patronage language to attract donors: "how much more illustrious and great is the glory of having God and Christ as the spectator of the gift?" Wealthy donors are recast as patrons earning honor *before God* by giving *through the bishop*. [^3]

### 4. The net effect: women patrons replaced by bishops
"By encouraging wealthy Christians to give their donations to the Church and then redistributing those donations … the influence of the community's wealthy donors was weakened and the power and prestige of patronage was consolidated in the office of bishop." By the 4th century "women who had acted as patrons and leaders in the early house churches were now replaced by the bishops." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "bishops began to reorder the patronage system around their own office." [^2]

> "Women who had acted as patrons and leaders in the early house churches were now replaced by the bishops who served as both leader and patron of their congregations." [^4]

## LDS Connection
For [[Latter-day Saints]], this is a concrete, materially-grounded account of *how* female leadership eroded — not by revelation but by the centralization of money and honor in a single male office. It complements [[Tertullian Reframed Church Office as Roman Legal Privilege|the legal-rights reframing]] as the second prong of women's displacement, and frames the modern emphasis on bishops' welfare role with historical perspective.
