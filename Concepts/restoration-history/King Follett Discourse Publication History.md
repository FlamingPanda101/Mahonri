# King Follett Discourse Publication History

The [[King Follett Discourse]] has one of the most complicated and contested
publication histories of any sermon in Mormonism. The text was compiled from
multiple accounts, nearly suppressed from the official church history in 1912,
and has been repeatedly edited to soften or remove its most radical elements.

## Early Publication

Four contemporaneous scribes recorded the sermon:
- **[[Thomas Bullock]]**: personal shorthand
- **[[William Clayton]]**: longhand (Smith's personal secretary)
- **[[Willard Richards]]**: minutes-style notes
- **[[Wilford Woodruff]]**: diary record[^1]

A polished version drawing primarily on Bullock and Clayton appeared in the
*Times and Seasons* newspaper in August 1844, shortly after Smith's death.[^2]

About a decade later, [[Jonathan Grimshaw]], an employee in the Church Historian's
Office, produced a standardized copy based on all four accounts — with "some
repetition and some awkward insertions" — that became part of the serialized
*History of Joseph Smith*.[^3]

## The 1912 Suppression

In the early 20th century, [[BH Roberts]] — the most prominent LDS theologian and
historian of the Progressive Era — was "fascinated with Joseph Smith" and considered
the King Follett Discourse "the culmination of everything Joseph Smith had been
teaching."[^4]

Roberts published a version in church newspapers in 1909 and prepared it for inclusion
in the *History of the Church* series when it was published in 1912.[^5]

**But then it vanished.** While Roberts was away on a mission, something happened
to the manuscript:

> The first presidency comprised of [[Joseph F. Smith]] and [[Charles Penrose]] were
> not fans of the King Follett Discourse, at least as it was prepared by BH Roberts.[^6]

Two specific objections drove the suppression:

1. **Infant exaltation:** Joseph F. Smith had lost 13 children and "could not fathom
   not being able to watch them grow in the eternities."[^7]
2. **Spirit birth interpretation:** Charles Penrose fundamentally disagreed with
   Roberts's interpretation that God *organized* intelligences into spirits. Penrose
   believed spirits themselves were co-eternal with God.[^8]

**Pages 302 to 317** of the *History of the Church* — containing the discourse —
were physically removed during typesetting.[^9]

## Roberts Fights Back

When people told Roberts the discourse wasn't in the published history, he was
"so furious that he demands the church print 10,000 copies of just those pages
including the King Follett Discourse so everyone could read them."[^10]

The pages were finally re-added when the *History of the Church* was republished
in 1952.[^10]

## Later Editing

- [[Joseph Fielding Smith]] (son of Joseph F. Smith) included the discourse in
  his influential *Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith*, but "the portions like
  the infant exaltation were removed and the discussions of spirit birth were
  correlated into the mainstream LDS teaching."[^11]
- [[Bruce R. McConkie]] (Joseph Fielding Smith's son-in-law) frequently cited it
  as the best LDS sermon ever preached, even arguing it should be added to the
  LDS canon — though he disagreed with Roberts's footnotes on infant exaltation
  and spirit birth.[^12]
