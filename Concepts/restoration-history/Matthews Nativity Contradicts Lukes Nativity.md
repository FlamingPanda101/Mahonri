# Matthew's Nativity Contradicts Luke's Nativity

## The Claim

The nativity accounts in Matthew and Luke irreconcilably contradict each other in multiple key details. They cannot both be historically accurate, which supports the conclusion that the nativity stories are theological/literary constructions, not independent historical reports.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Different home cities for Joseph and Mary

**Matthew:** Joseph and Mary are living in Bethlehem. They have "nothing to do with Nazareth" until returning from their refugee stint in Egypt. When they settle in Nazareth, the text describes it as settling in a new place — "which is what you say when someone is moving somewhere new, not what you say when someone is returning home."[^2]

**Luke:** Joseph and Mary live in Nazareth. They travel to Bethlehem only because of the census. After fulfilling temple requirements, they "returned to Galilee, to their own town of Nazareth" ([[Luke 2.39|Luke 2:39]]).[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (textual). The internal logic of each narrative points to different home cities.

### 2. The flight to Egypt vs. peaceful return

**Matthew:** After Jesus's birth, Herod attempts to murder him (the slaughter of the innocents). The family flees to Egypt and only returns after Herod's death. They avoid Judea because Archelaus now rules there, so they settle in Nazareth for the first time.[^4]

**Luke:** After Jesus's birth, they peacefully fulfill temple requirements (presentation, circumcision) and then "mosey on back to Nazareth." Luke "does not seem to know that they were literally chased out of the country by the king." The slaughter of innocents is entirely absent from Luke.[^5]

McClellan calls the omission "profound authorial neglect" — Luke finds it important to record temple details but omits that the king tried to murder the infant and the family had to flee to another country.[^6]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (textual). The two accounts describe fundamentally different sequences of events.

### 3. Chronological impossibility

In Matthew, Herod asks when the star appeared and kills all boys "two years and under" — suggesting Jesus could be up to two years old at the time of the flight to Egypt, and the family was still in Bethlehem.[^7]

In Luke, the family fulfills temple requirements shortly after birth, returns to Nazareth, and "visited Jerusalem at the Passover every year." This sequence "does not allow for them to flee to Egypt until Herod dies" — it "does not allow for them to be persona non grata in Jerusalem" and "suggests they were free to come back once a year."[^8]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (chronological). The timelines are mutually exclusive.

### 4. Different mechanisms for the Bethlehem birth

**Matthew:** Jesus is born in Bethlehem because his family lives there. No census is mentioned. The star, magi, and Herod's plot drive the narrative.

**Luke:** Jesus is born in Bethlehem because of a worldwide census under [[Quirinius]] that required travel to an ancestral hometown — a mechanism that is "bafflingly asinine, just ahistorical" for reasons detailed in [[Census of Quirinius Is Historically Unworkable]].[^9]

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (historical plausibility). The census mechanism is independently problematic; the fact that Matthew and Luke use entirely different mechanisms to get Jesus to Bethlehem further suggests neither is historical.

## Best Quote

> "Matthew is telling the story in their own unique way to have Jesus fulfilling these prophecies. And the data don't support the conclusion that Jesus actually fulfilled these prophecies. The data — I would argue — don't even indicate he was born in Bethlehem. I think most likely he was probably born in Nazareth." — [[Dan McClellan]][^10]
