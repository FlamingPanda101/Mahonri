# Book of Enoch - Western Rediscovery 17th-19th Centuries
#type/claim

## The Claim
After dropping out of the Western and Orthodox canons by the 4th century, 1 Enoch was effectively lost to European Christendom — known only through patristic citations and travelers' rumors — for roughly 1,700 years, until it was *rediscovered* between the 17th and 19th centuries. The pivotal event was the Scottish explorer [[James Bruce (explorer)|James Bruce]] returning from Ethiopia in 1773 with three Ge'ez (Ethiopic) manuscripts, which launched the chain of European translations and critical editions that reintroduced the book to Western scholarship. Dan McClellan's framing in the source is partly cautionary: the rediscovery rests on *late, divergent* Ethiopic manuscripts, so the popular cheap editions descend from imperfect 19th-century work and should not be mistaken for a pristine ancient text.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Bruce's 1773 manuscripts were the catalyst.** Bruce brought back three copies of 1 Enoch in Ge'ez; McClellan notes these "three copies disagreed quite a bit with each other," were "pretty late manuscripts," and "not very trustworthy" — yet they were the spark for the entire Western recovery.
2. **The chain of editions (roughly).** Before Bruce, European knowledge was fragmentary — patristic references (Tertullian, Origen) and secondhand traveler reports. After Bruce came: a German translation (1801); the first English translation by Richard Laurence (1821); the first European printing of the Ethiopic text (1838, 105 chapters), later extended to 108; the first critical edition (1851); and [[R.H. Charles]]'s influential translation (1906).
3. **R.H. Charles's translation dominates popular editions — and that is a problem.** McClellan observes that "most people today who go to a bookstore or go online to try and find a version of the Book of Enoch are going to find R.H. Charles's translation," and warns most cheap editions "are from the 19th century or are garbage." He recommends instead the modern Hermeneia translation by [[George Nickelsburg]] and [[James VanderKam]], which incorporates the Qumran Aramaic fragments unknown to the 19th-century editors.
4. **Why it matters for apologetics.** The rediscovery story explains why 1 Enoch felt "hidden" and fuels modern "lost/suppressed scripture" excitement — but the scholarly point cuts the other way: the text was never truly secret (it survived openly in Ethiopia), and the versions driving popular hype are textually inferior (the conspiracy framing is rebutted in [[Book of Enoch - Conspiracy Theories of Hidden Knowledge Rebutted]]).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** An enthusiast for 1 Enoch will stress that "rediscovery" understates its significance: the 1947+ Dead Sea Scrolls then confirmed an *Aramaic* Enoch circulating before the New Testament, vindicating its antiquity and its influence on Jude and early Christology, so the 17th–19th-century recovery returned to the church a genuinely ancient and formative text, not a curiosity. One could argue the long Western absence was itself an accident of canon politics rather than evidence against the book's value. The vault's balancing point is that antiquity-of-the-tradition (real, per Qumran) must not be conflated with reliability-of-the-rediscovered-manuscripts (late and divergent, per Bruce's copies).
