# The 1852 Act Was Patterned on Northern Gradual-Emancipation Law

## The Claim
[[Daniel C. Peterson]] argues that the Utah *Act in Relation to Service* (1852) was not modeled on the Southern slave codes but on the **gradual-emancipation statutes of the free North and Midwest** — Indiana, Illinois, and the post–Northwest-Ordinance states — and on the apprenticeship machinery the British Empire used when it abolished slavery in 1833. [[Brigham Young]] insisted it not be titled an act "in relation to slavery"; its built-in legal mechanics were designed so that bondage would *expire* within a generation rather than perpetuate itself. The claim is narrower and more technical than "it was servitude not slavery": it is a claim about the statute's **legal lineage and emancipatory engineering**.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The free-state template (rated: strong; documentary).** Peterson: the act "was patterned on the gradual emancipation laws of the northeast and the midwest rather than on the slave codes of the south," and "closely approximated laws in Indiana and Illinois after the passage of the Northwest Ordinance." Such laws were "clearly set up with an intent to end slavery gradually, peaceably."[^1]

2. **Section 3 — children born free (rated: strong; the decisive mechanism).** The statute provided that "enslavers don't have a permanent hereditary claim to a slave's descendants. Children born to an enslaved mother are born free." Peterson calls this "the really clear gradual emancipation aspect" — "the second generation would not be slaves or servants at all."[^2] A multigenerational chattel system cannot survive a born-free rule for the next generation.

3. **No express protection of slavery — unlike New Mexico (rated: strong).** "The law didn't include an absolute prohibition of slavery, but it didn't include any express approval or protection of slavery as the New Mexico law did a decade later." It "tried to shift chattel slavery into a system of contracted labor."[^3] The contrast with the New Mexico statute (which *did* protect slavery) isolates the Utah act's emancipatory intent.

4. **The Somerset precedent for the prior neutrality (rated: moderate-strong).** Before 1852 the territory simply did not recognize slavery, echoing Lord Mansfield's 1772 *Somerset* ruling that slavery, being unnatural, "had to be approved, authorized by positive law" and so could not exist absent enabling legislation. The Saints "didn't ban it. They didn't authorize it. It wouldn't exist."[^4]

5. **The British 1833 apprenticeship parallel (rated: moderate).** When Parliament passed the Slavery Abolition Act (1833), it set up a transitional **apprenticeship** system rather than moving people instantly "from slavery to total freedom"; even many Quaker abolitionist newspapers opposed *immediate* abolition. Utah's territorial legislature attempted the same managed transition.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A legal regime that still binds a person to a master, recognizes their labor as transferable, and arrives only after five years of de facto toleration is a real deprivation of liberty, however much gentler than Mississippi — "gradual" emancipation is still emancipation *deferred*, and the enslaved person in 1852 remains unfree. Critics also note that the same legislature that limited Black servitude entrenched a developing priesthood-and-race teaching (see [[The Priesthood Ban Was 1850s False Doctrine From Protestant Race Theory]]). Peterson concedes the residual moral problem; his thesis is comparative and genealogical — *which model the law copied and which direction it pointed* — not that the statute was harmless. Even granting the free-state lineage, "had it been adopted nationally it would have prevented the Civil War" is a separate, speculative counterfactual the legal-lineage claim does not require.
