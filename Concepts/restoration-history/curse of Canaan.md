# curse of Canaan

## Overview
The "curse of Canaan" is the episode in Genesis 9:20-27 where, after Noah becomes drunk and is shamed in his tent by his son Ham, Noah awakes and pronounces a curse — not on Ham, but on Ham's son **Canaan**: "Cursed be Canaan; a servant of servants shall he be unto his brethren" (Gen. 9:25). The text names Canaan, the ancestor of the Canaanite peoples whom Israel later displaced, and says nothing about skin color or Africa. The natural reading ties the curse to the Canaanites' later subjugation, framing it as an etiology of national servitude rather than a racial mark.

## Apologetic relevance
For centuries this passage was misread as the "curse of Ham" and stretched into a racial doctrine, supplying a biblical pretext for African slavery and, in 19th-century America, for priesthood-and-race restrictions. In LDS, Protestant, and Catholic debate it functions as a case study in how a misread proof-text was weaponized: apologists distinguish what Genesis actually says (Canaan, not Ham; servitude, not skin color) from the slaveholders' gloss, arguing the racial reading is an externally imposed tradition the text never supports. For Latter-day Saints specifically it is central to rereading the Book of Abraham's lineage scheme without the priesthood-ban lens, since the old ban prooftexts leaned on conflating Ham, Canaan, and a "curse" of black skin.
