# Why NDE Reports Are Not Lies or Embellishments

## The Point
In his "Questions" chapter Moody answers the two commonest charges against the reports — that subjects are *lying* and that they have *embellished* their stories over time — and rejects both [^1]. Against lying, he points to the visible sincerity of the witnesses, the absurdity of a decades-long multi-state conspiracy, and the sheer similarity of independent accounts [^2]. Against embellishment, he notes that fresh accounts (some taken in the hospital, some read from contemporaneous notes) match decades-old ones, and that the real tendency runs the *other* way — toward **suppression**, people trying to forget [^3].

## Evidence
1. **Sincerity that cannot be faked.** Moody has watched "mature, emotionally stable adults... break down and weep" recounting events from up to three decades earlier, detecting "sincerity, warmth, and feeling" that make fabrication "utterly untenable" to him [^2].
2. **The conspiracy is absurd.** The fabrication hypothesis must explain why so many strangers "just happen to have come up with the same lie" over eight years; the idea that "a nice elderly lady from eastern North Carolina, a medical student from New Jersey, a Georgia veterinarian, and many others... banded together" in a hoax is not "a very likely possibility" [^2].
3. **Fresh accounts match old ones.** Interviews done "while they were still in the hospital recovering" are "of the same type" as those recounted decades later; some subjects read from notes "written down... shortly after they happened," again no different [^4].
4. **Often the first telling.** Moody was frequently "only the first or second person" told, and only "with great reluctance" — leaving "little or no opportunity for embellishment," yet these accounts match the rest [^4].
5. **Suppression, not embellishment.** The opposite mechanism dominates: one woman said "I tried to suppress it because I knew people weren't going to believe me anyway"; a Vietnam cardiac-arrest survivor said "I get choked up by trying to tell about it even now.... I have tried to forget it" [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "How is it that many people just happen to have come up with the same lie to tell me over a period of eight years?" [^2]

> "I feel that there is more to it, but I can't remember it all. I tried to suppress it because I knew people weren't going to believe me anyway." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint epistemology gives real weight to sincere personal testimony of spiritual experience (Moroni 10:4-5; D&C 8:2-3), and the multiplicity of independent, mutually corroborating witnesses is the scriptural standard for establishing truth — "in the mouth of two or three witnesses shall every word be established" (2 Cor. 13:1; cf. the Three and Eight Witnesses of the Book of Mormon). The pattern of reluctant witnesses suppressing sacred experience for fear of ridicule echoes the LDS counsel not to cast pearls before swine and the lived reality of guarding sacred things.
