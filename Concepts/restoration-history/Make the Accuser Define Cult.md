# Make the Accuser Define Cult

## The Point
The panel notices that [[Walter Martin]]'s *Kingdom of the Cults* chapter "doesn't at all indicate his justification for explaining why he believes Latter-day Saints to be a cult" — it just lists complaints. Their prescription: whenever someone calls the Church a cult, **ask them what they mean by the word**. Because the term is so elastic, the accuser's own definition forks into two losing branches — either by their definition the Church *isn't* a cult, or their definition is so broad it *also* catches Jesus's church, Moses and the Israelites, the military, sports teams, MLMs, and "the Dallas Cowboys fan base." Either way the label does no work, because it hinges on *control*, and the Church "give[s] you choice."[^1][^2][^3]

## Evidence
1. **The accusation is asserted, never argued.** "This whole chapter on Latter-day Saints, he doesn't at all indicate his justification for explaining why he believes Latter-day Saints to be a cult. All he does is… here's all these things I have complaints about… Okay, but how is it a cult?"[^1] *Rating: strong as a critique of Martin specifically; the burden is on the accuser.*
2. **The define-it fork.** "After you ask them what they mean by a cult… their answer will always be one of two different things. Either by their definition, our church is not a cult, so great. Or… our church is considered a cult, but so would also Jesus Christ's church and Moses and the Israelites."[^3] *Rating: strong; a clean dilemma that puts the definitional burden where it belongs.*
3. **The BITE model and its self-citation problem.** The most common definition is the **BITE model** (Behavior, Information, Thought, Emotional control), but "then the military, sports teams, MLMs are all cults." A host adds that the model's author allegedly "cites himself as the source on multiple of the studies," doing "eight studies and then cit[ing] his own studies."[^2] *Rating: the over-inclusion point is strong; the self-citation charge is hearsay flagged in-stream ("if you're watching this, please chime in").*
4. **Everything hinges on control.** "It all hinges on control. You have to be controlling, but the church doesn't control. They give you choice."[^2] *Rating: strong; reframes the debate around the actual diagnostic criterion — and the Church's emphasis on agency is the counter-evidence.*

## Best Quotes
> "He doesn't at all indicate his justification for explaining why he believes Latter-day Saints to be a cult. All he does is… here's all these things I have complaints about… Okay, but how is it a cult?" [^1]

> "Either by their definition, our church is not a cult, so great. Or… our church is considered a cult, but so would also Jesus Christ's church and Moses and the Israelites." [^3]

> "It's so vague that… the Dallas Cowboys fan base would fit as a cult… we got to be more pinpoint on cults." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Forcing a definition is the front-line move against the "cult" smear, and it dovetails with the rest of the vault's treatment: the term is a [[Cult - A Contaminated Pejorative Term|contaminated pejorative]] whose popular sense (small, controlled, charismatic-led) simply doesn't fit a 17-million-member, agency-emphasizing, publicly-audited church ([[Mormonism Is Not a Cult]]), while academic "family resemblance" definitions either exonerate it or over-generate ([[Family Resemblance Definition of Cults]]). And even where the label might be argued, leading with it is [[Calling Someone a Cultist Is Counterproductive|strategically self-defeating]].
