# Tabernacle Infallibility Misreads Brigham Young (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] claims [[Brigham Young]] taught that statements made in the tabernacle (which Heschmeyer glosses as "general conference") are "protected from error" — i.e., that teaching in the tabernacle is *ex cathedra* and infallible. He uses this to set up a debunking syllogism: find something taught infallibly, show it false, conclude the religion is false.[^1] The panel argues this is a misreading of the actual sermon.

## The Rebuttal

1. **The passage is about communal correction, not papal-style infallibility (rated: strong, textual).** [[Luke Irwin]] reads the [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young|Journal of Discourses]] passage in full. Brigham Young's point is that error becomes *manifest and correctable* at headquarters because the body of Saints can correct one another: "Here is the place for you to teach great mysteries to your brethren because here are those who can correct you." The "fault the elders of Israel do not fall into in this tabernacle, although they may in private homes" — i.e., when gathered as a corrected body they avoid error, not that any individual at the pulpit is incapable of being wrong.[^2]

2. **It is collective, not the prophet alone (rated: strong, textual).** [[Jacob Hansen]]: the passage applies "to like all the church, it's not just like me, the prophet. It's us as members of the church when we're together in a body of saints." Heschmeyer "turns it into 'Whenever I stand at the pulpit at General Conference, that's completely infallible.'"[^3]

3. **The Journal of Discourses was never canonized (rated: strong, factual).** Irwin notes Heschmeyer elsewhere in his material claims the Journal of Discourses *was* canonized — it was not — and that he "should know better" having done an hour-long video on Adam-God two years earlier.[^4] See [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]].

4. **The debunking syllogism requires a premise LDS reject (rated: strong, logical).** Hansen: critics want "something said infallibly" so they can run "Look, it isn't true, therefore religion false." But "the really great thing about being a Latter-day Saint is that we don't have that" — LDS believe in *reliability* (the prophet won't lead the Church ultimately astray), not personal infallibility. *Steelman:* a critic could argue this makes the position unfalsifiable — any embarrassing teaching can be reclassified as fallible opinion; the LDS reply is that this is exactly the honest epistemic position (see [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]]), and that it is *Catholicism* that holds the explicit infallibility standard it then has to defend.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Brigham's just saying the center of the church is the center of righteousness... when we're together in a body of saints we're protected from error. And then when we go out, we can fall into those errors. And Joe's trying to turn it into 'Whenever I stand at the pulpit at General Conference, that's completely infallible.'"[^3]

> "Here is a way to debunk a religion: you get something that is said infallibly and then you say, 'Look, it isn't true, therefore religion false.' ... The really great thing about being a Latter-day Saint is that we don't have that."[^5]

> "He actually misstates that the Journal of Discourses was canonized at one point. He should know better. He did a whole hour-long video on this two years ago."[^4]
