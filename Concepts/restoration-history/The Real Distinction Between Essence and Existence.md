# The Real Distinction Between Essence and Existence

## The Point
The engine of the Thomistic proof is the claim that in every ordinary thing — a stone, a tree, a dog, a human being — there is a **real distinction** between *what it is* (its essence) and *that it is* (its existence). [^1] This is not merely a verbal or conceptual distinction like that between "bachelor" and "unmarried man," where the two terms pick out one and the same reality; it is a distinction "that reflects objective, mind-independent reality itself and not merely the way we think about it." [^2] You know what a human being is — for Aquinas, a rational animal — and you separately know *that* human beings exist; these are two different things you know. [^1]

## Evidence
Feser gives three converging arguments for the distinction's reality:
1. **You can know an essence without knowing whether it is instantiated.** Given full descriptions of lions, pterodactyls, and unicorns, you still cannot tell from the essences alone which one exists, which is extinct, and which never existed. "If a thing exists, then its existence too is obviously part of its objective reality. So, if the essence and existence of a thing were not distinct features of reality, then knowing the former should suffice for knowing the latter, yet it doesn't." [^3] Judging falsely that lions don't exist is *not* to misconceive what a lion is — unlike judging that a lion is not a cat — which shows existence is no part of the essence. [^4]
2. **Contingency.** The things of experience exist but could have failed to exist. "Now, if the existence of a contingent thing was not really distinct from its essence, then it would have existence just by virtue of its essence... and would therefore not be contingent at all but necessary." Since they are contingent, essence and existence are really distinct. [^5]
3. **Uniqueness** (developed in [[Why There Can Be Only One Subsistent Existence Itself]]): anything whose essence just *is* existence could only be one of a kind, yet ordinary things come in pluralities; so in each of them essence and existence differ. [^6]

A real distinction need not be a *separable* one. The radius and circumference of a circle are really distinct (the latter is 2πr times the former) yet inseparable; likewise triangularity and trilaterality. [^7] So essence and existence are genuinely distinct aspects of a thing without either floating free of the other. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "So, we can distinguish between a thing's essence and its existence, between what it is and the fact that it is." [^1]

> "There are several reasons why the distinction between essence and existence must be a real distinction, a distinction that reflects objective, mind-independent reality itself and not merely the way we think about it." [^2]

> "Now, if the existence of a contingent thing was not really distinct from its essence, then it would have existence just by virtue of its essence. It would exist by its very nature, and would therefore not be contingent at all but necessary." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism this distinction is a powerful tool: it shows that the existence of contingent things is not self-explanatory but must be accounted for by something whose existence is not borrowed — a serious bar to any "the universe just is" stopping point (compare the regress reply in [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]]). A Latter-day Saint can fully grant that stones, trees, and bodies do not contain the reason for their own being. The divergence lies downstream, in what Feser infers must be at the bottom: a single being whose essence *is* existence (see [[Subsistent Existence Itself - Ipsum Esse Subsistens]]) and hence is metaphysically simple. The Restoration holds that intelligence and the "elements" are eternal and uncreated (D&C 93:29) — so not everything that is contingent in *arrangement* is contingent in *being*; matter and intelligence have no beginning and need no imparter of bare existence (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]). The argument's force against atheism is real; its terminus in a wholly simple Being Itself is the LDS point of departure.
