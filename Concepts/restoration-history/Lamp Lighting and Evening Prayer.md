## The Point
A household ritual associated with prayer was the lighting of lamps at the close of day. In an ancient world of profound darkness, the small clay oil lamp was among the most important household goods, and lighting it at evening — a ritual shared across Jewish, pagan, and Christian homes — invited prayer. For Christians, the returning light symbolized Christ, "the light of the world," and his coming.

## Evidence
1. Ephrem the Syrian wrote, "The eye in darkness hungers for light; therefore, after the sun sets, then there is the lamp."
2. Some sources indicate lamp lighting was the special duty of women.
3. Cyprian (ca. AD 200–258): "At the sunsetting and at the decline of day, of necessity we must pray again. When we pray and ask that light may return to us again, we pray for the coming of Christ, who will give us the grace of everlasting light."
4. From the third century, ceramic workshops produced lamps decorated with Christian symbols; from the fourth century these included the Chi-Rho christogram (the first two letters of *christos*).
5. The popularity of such lamps suggests Christians remembered Jesus as "the light of the world" who promised that whoever follows him "will never walk in darkness but will have the light of life" ([[John 8.12|John 8:12]]).
6. Communal lamp-lighting was central at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem, where (per Egeria's pilgrimage diary) a perpetually burning lamp in the tomb kindled all the candles of the assembly — a tradition continuing today as the Ceremony of the Holy Fire.

## Best Quotes
> "When we pray and ask that light may return to us again, we pray for the coming of Christ, who will give us the grace of everlasting light." [^1]

> "The flame, passed from the place of Christ's Resurrection to person after person throughout the congregation, gradually illuminating the whole church, created a beautifully symbolic moment ... the ritual mirrored the spreading of faith and testimony throughout the world, one transformed life at a time." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The imagery of one flame spreading person-to-person to illuminate the whole congregation — "the spreading of faith and testimony throughout the world, one transformed life at a time" — parallels Latter-day Saint understanding of testimony and missionary light. [^2]
