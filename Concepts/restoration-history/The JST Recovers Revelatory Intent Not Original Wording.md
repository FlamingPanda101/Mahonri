# The JST Recovers Revelatory Intent Not Original Wording

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Ether's Elephant]] argues that the [[Joseph Smith]] Translation aims at **the divine intent behind a passage**, not at its earliest *wording*. "The question is not what did the original text say? It is what was the intent of the passages from God. What did or does he want us to learn from the scriptures?"[^1] Joseph "seemed to grasp later in his life that the importance of scriptures is less in the words and more in the spirit,"[^2] and was "famously frustrated with the limitations of the English language" for conveying spiritual truth.[^3] On this view, written scripture is downstream of revelation, not identical to it — "what the scriptures contain are only hints of what existed in the prophet's mind,"[^4] and the JST is an attempt to carry that original *spirit* across rather than to reconstruct lost manuscripts.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Language is symbol, not the reality it points to — rate: moderate (philosophical)
"Fundamentally, language is just symbols that point to abstractions of reality. Language is not reality itself, but it attempts to point to reality."[^5] If even the autographs were a lossy symbolic encoding of a prophet's experience, then recovering the exact ancient *words* would not recover the *thing* God revealed — only the spirit behind them does. This is the same intuition as [[A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible]], applied to JST methodology.

### 2. Scripture is downstream of revelation — rate: moderate
The video invites the Protestant emphasis on scripture as "God-breathed" ([[2 Timothy 3.16-17]]) and then presses past the text: "If scripture is God-breathed… how do we get back to the breath of God? How do we get back to the spirit of God?"[^6] The JST is framed as that move upstream — from the surviving words back toward the originating divine intent. Compare [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]].

### 3. It explains why the JST adds and expands rather than merely correcting — rate: strong (internal)
If the aim were the autographs, expansions like the [[Book of Moses]] (text "that never appeared anywhere in your Bible") would be illegitimate. But if the aim is to surface the intent God wants taught, then revealed expansion is exactly the right tool — consistent with [[JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition]] and [[Moses 1 Is the Lost Preface and Hidden Key to the Bible]]. "Some of the translation does indeed give the translation of an account that was written on parchment but lost to history," yet even this "does not mean it's trying to recreate word-for-word ancient documents."[^7]

**Steelman of the critic:** An "intent, not wording" standard is close to unfalsifiable, and that is its liability. Because *intent* is invisible and unmeasurable, the model can absorb any discrepancy after the fact — there is no textual datum that could ever count against the JST, which is precisely what makes it unpersuasive to an outsider who wants a check. A sharper critic ([[Dan McClellan]]-type) adds: appealing to a prophet's private "mind" of which the text is only a "hint" places the actual content beyond audit entirely, so the claim "this expansion recovers God's intent" reduces to "trust the prophet." That may be coherent within a revelatory framework, but as *evidence* it begs the question — it presupposes Joseph's inspiration to license the reading that is supposed to demonstrate it.
