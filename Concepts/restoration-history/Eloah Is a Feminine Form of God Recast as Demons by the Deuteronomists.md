# Eloah Is a Feminine Form of God Recast as Demons by the Deuteronomists

## The Claim

[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that [[Deuteronomy 32.17|Deuteronomy 32:17]] preserves — and then *attacks* — the divine Mother. The verse (KJV: "They sacrificed unto **devils**, not to **God**… to gods whom they knew not") contains two telling words. First, the word for "God" here is ***Eloah*** — which Butler reads as a **feminine form** ("goddess"), the *o*-vowel being a late Masoretic insertion over an underlying *ʾLH* ≈ *Allah*/"goddess." Second, the word for "devils" is ***shedim***, which in unpointed Hebrew is **consonantally identical to *shadayim*** ("breasts" / the root of **Shaddai**). So the Deuteronomist is saying: *you think you're sacrificing to a goddess (Shaddai), but it's not a goddess — it's demons.* It is, in Butler's framing, "the OG witch hunt": the reformers branding the breasted Mother a demon. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Eloah* as a feminine-for-God — rate: weak-moderate (etymology contested; supporting his program)
"Not to God" uses *Eloah*. Butler: "the *o* sound is inserted by the Masoretes… really the word is actually *ʾLH*, which literally just means **goddess** — for all of the gymnastics of philologists trying to prove the contrary, this is a feminine for God." He had earlier noted that in Job, *Shaddai* stands in parallel with *Eloah*, "a feminine-looking name of God." So *Eloah* is read as another surviving feminine divine term. [^1]

### 2. *Shedim* = *shadayim* in consonantal Hebrew — rate: moderate (the consonantal collision is real; the inference is the claim)
"What's the word for demons? *Shedim* — sheen, doet, yod… on the back, *mem*. With unpointed Hebrew, *shed* (demon) and *shad* (breast) are the **same** — you can't tell them apart." So "sacrificed to *shedim*, not to *Eloah*" reads as a polemic *equating the goddess of fertility (Shaddai) with a demon* — "they're trying to say, no, that's a demon." This dovetails with [[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]] and the vault's [[Shedim Are Foreign Deities Not Demons]]. [^2]

### 3. Fits the broader Deuteronomist erasure pattern — rate: moderate (consistent with the documented reform)
The same reformers who "hate the sacred tree and destroy it and call her evil" (Isaiah's "call evil good and good evil"), who praise Hezekiah and Josiah for cutting down the Asherah, here "call **Shaddai**, who gave the patriarchs fertility… a **demon**." So Deuteronomy 32:17 is one more stroke of the program that wrote the Mother out of the Hebrew Bible — preserved more purely, Butler argues, in the pre-exilic Book of Mormon. See [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]] and [[The Judean Pillar Figurines Were the Smashed Mother in the First Temple]]. [^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The philology is the weak point and the objections are substantial. (a) ***Eloah* is standardly masculine**: it is simply the singular of *Elohim*, used ~57 times (mostly in Job) as an ordinary word for the one God; the "feminine for God = goddess" reading is a minority etymological claim that mainstream Hebraists reject, and Butler concedes scholars do "gymnastics… to prove the contrary" — i.e., the consensus is against him. (b) **The *shed*/*shad* collision is a coincidence of consonants, not evidence of identity**: Hebrew has many homographs in unpointed text, and *shedim* (a loanword cognate with Akkadian *šēdu*, protective/lesser spirits) has a clear, separate derivation from *shad* "breast" — the vault's own [[Shedim Are Foreign Deities Not Demons]] identifies *shedim* as **foreign deities**, which already explains Deuteronomy 32:17 (the polemic denies the *legitimacy* of the gods the nations sacrifice to) **without** needing a suppressed breast-goddess. (c) The reading is **driven by the conclusion**: having decided Shaddai = the Mother, Butler finds her under any consonant cluster resembling *shd*, which risks confirmation bias. (d) Deuteronomy 32:17's plain sense — sacrificing to *demons/no-gods* rather than to Yahweh — is a standard idol-polemic ("negation rhetoric"), not a coded attack on a fertility goddess. The defensible residue is narrow: it is *true* that the Deuteronomist literature is hostile to Asherah and that unpointed Hebrew creates real ambiguities a polemicist could exploit, so the *possibility* of a buried Shaddai-as-demon pun is not absurd — but the specific *Eloah* = goddess and *shedim* = *shadayim* identifications are contested etymologies that the mainstream, and even the vault's existing *shedim* note, read otherwise.
