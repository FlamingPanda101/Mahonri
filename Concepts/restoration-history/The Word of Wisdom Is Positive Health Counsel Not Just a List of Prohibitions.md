# The Word of Wisdom Is Positive Health Counsel Not Just a List of Prohibitions

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues the [[Word of Wisdom]] ([[D&C 89]]) is widely misread as merely "a set of prohibitions" — a list of things you cannot do — when it is more fundamentally **positive counsel on how to live a healthy life**. Recovering the historical context (especially [[Emma Smith]]'s health crisis) restores the revelation's affirmative thrust and, Bradley says, "gives power to the actual revelation itself."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The text leads with promises and prescriptions, not only bans (strong, textual).** Alongside the prohibitions (no tobacco, wine, strong/hot drinks), D&C 89 commends "all… herbs in their season," "vegetables," and "plant foods" as the dietary primary, with meat eaten "sparingly" and "only under certain conditions."[^1]
2. **It is framed as wisdom, not commandment (moderate, textual).** The revelation explicitly comes "not by commandment or constraint, but by revelation and the word of wisdom" — i.e., divine *counsel* for flourishing rather than a bare legal code (cf. [[The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom]] for how this "floor vs. living code" tension plays out historically).[^1]
3. **Context supplies the affirmative payoff (moderate, biographical).** Seen against Emma's postpartum struggle, the promises of marrow, strength, and stamina read as targeted blessings rather than abstractions — "if you understand the history, it even gives it more punch."[^2] See [[Emma's Difficult Childbirth Is the Immediate Context of the Word of Wisdom]].
4. **More than a physical law (interpretive).** Bradley adds it is "more than just a physical law" — the wisdom framing carries spiritual "treasures of knowledge," tying bodily health to the revelation's promise of wisdom.[^2]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
In *practice* the modern Word of Wisdom functions overwhelmingly as the four-fold prohibition (alcohol, tobacco, coffee, tea) that gates temple recommends — the positive dietary counsel (sparing meat, abundant grains/vegetables) is rarely enforced or even emphasized. A critic could say Bradley's "positive counsel" framing, while textually fair, describes the 1833 revelation more than the lived 21st-century standard, where the prohibitions are the operative core.

## Counter-Objection
The gap between text and current practice is real but does not refute the claim; it sharpens it. Bradley's point is precisely that the *revelation* is positive counsel even if the *administration* has narrowed to a few markers — and recovering the affirmative, contextual reading is meant to re-energize devotional engagement (the "punch" he describes) rather than to restate Church policy. The functional-marker value noted elsewhere ([[Word of Wisdom]]) and the positive-counsel reading are complementary, not rivals.
