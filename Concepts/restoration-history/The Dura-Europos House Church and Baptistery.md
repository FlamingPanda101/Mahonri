## The Point
The frontier town of Dura Europos, on the banks of the Euphrates in eastern Syria, preserves the oldest and most impressive archaeologically attested *domus ecclesiae* and the earliest known Christian baptistery (built around AD 240). A courtyard home was renovated for Christian worship, giving scholars an unusually clear picture of third-century liturgical life and baptismal practice.

## Evidence
1. A room in the west corner was converted into a baptistery furnished with a canopy-covered font and adorned with wall frescoes depicting biblical scenes.
2. The baptistery frescoes included Jesus healing the paralytic, walking on water, women at a tomb, the Samaritan woman (or Mary) at a well, the Good Shepherd with a flock, Adam and Eve in paradise, David slaying Goliath, and a procession of women.
3. A medium-sized space along the southwest wall served as a teaching room for newly initiated Christians (neophytes); a combined room and triclinium on the southeast formed an assembly hall (with a raised platform) for about seventy-five people.
4. The Good Shepherd painting lay directly above the font: to be baptized was to be rescued by the Good Shepherd and brought into the fold (Luke 15:4–6; John 10:11), with the rescued sheep aligned vertically toward the shepherd and horizontally toward the flock — suggesting the vertical (covenant with Christ) and horizontal (covenant with the community) bonds formed in baptism.
5. Even with a large body of water nearby, the community chose to build a private baptistery, indicating that the rite was felt to call for privacy.
6. Liturgical activities reconstructable from the building: catechism in the southwest meeting space, anointing with oil and immersion in the baptistery, and weekly assembly of baptized members for scripture reading, sermons, and the Eucharist.

## Best Quotes
> "The oldest and most impressive of these structures is at a frontier town in eastern Syria called Dura Europos. There, during the 240s AD, a courtyard home was renovated by converting a room in the west corner into a baptistery that was furnished with a canopy-covered font and adorned with wall frescoes depicting biblical scenes." [^1]

> "At Dura Europos, the font lay directly beneath a painting of a shepherd carrying a sheep on his shoulders toward a flock grazing and drinking at a river: to be baptized was to be rescued by the Good Shepherd ... and brought into the church fold, where one found 'living water' and nourishment to body and soul." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison notes the vertical and horizontal alignment of the Good Shepherd fresco "suggesting the vertical and horizontal relationships formed in baptism — the creation of a covenant bond with Christ and with the family of Christ's followers (compare [[2 Nephi 31|2 Nephi 31:10–20]]; Mosiah 18:8–10)." [^3]
