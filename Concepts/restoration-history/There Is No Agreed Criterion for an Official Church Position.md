# There Is No Agreed Criterion for an Official Church Position

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] argues that Latter-day Saints lack a single, agreed test for what counts as an **"official position" or binding doctrine**. Multiple LDS scholars have each proposed criteria — and they conflict, depend on malleable terms, and have never been authoritatively adopted. The honest conclusion is that the question "what is the church's official position on X?" has no crisp, mechanical answer; it requires judgment.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Competing scholarly checklists (strong)
Hatch surveys several:
- **[[Robert Millet]]** (2003, repeated 2007/2013): the four standard works + official declarations/proclamations; general conference and official gatherings; the general handbook and approved curriculum; and current prophetic teaching.[^1]
- **Three BYU religious-education professors** (2016): repeated scripture; the *united voice* of the current brethren; current and continuously taught teachings of general authorities/officers in their official capacity; recent church publications.[^2]
- **Michael Goodman**: true doctrine is eternal/unchanging, taught by the *united voice* of the brethren, and *salvific*.[^2]
- **Scott Woodward**: does it accord with repeated scripture, is it unitedly proclaimed by the apostles, is it confirmed by the Holy Spirit?[^2]

### 2. The criteria were challenged in print (medium)
When Millet published his four, the journal's next issue carried Lloyd Ericson asking "why these four?" — a five-year back-and-forth followed. The criteria are not consensus; they are contested proposals.[^1]

### 3. The terms are slippery (strong)
"United voice," "continuously taught," "official capacity," and "confirmed by the Holy Spirit" are all malleable. Hatch notes that confirmation by the Spirit is, by the brethren's own admission (Holland, Oaks), "very difficult" — thousands have left and even founded rival churches claiming the Spirit. So the tests defer to judgment they do not eliminate.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic (or a confident member) replies that the diversity of *academic formulations* doesn't mean there's no practical answer: in lived reality, what the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve *currently and unitedly* teach, especially when canonized or issued as an official proclamation, *is* the operative standard, and ordinary members navigate it without difficulty. Hatch is manufacturing a problem out of scholars' hair-splitting; the church's actual teaching authority is clear enough.

## Counter-Objection
Hatch's reply is that the hard cases are precisely where the "practical answer" breaks down — caffeine counsel reversed (see [[The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom]]), Adam-God taught from the pulpit by a prophet yet later disavowed, the Benson "14 Fundamentals" walked back. In each, sincere members applying the "current united voice" rule would have reached *different* conclusions at different times. That a tidy rule works for clear cases is no help where the disputes actually live; there the absence of an agreed criterion is exactly the point, and pretending otherwise is what produces the [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught|infallibility trap]].

## Best Quotes
> "He didn't state why it's these four in the criteria and why not other things. And they had a back and forth over the next five years in print."[^1]

> "By that many people asking that and that many answers being given, we know it's very, very difficult."[^3]
