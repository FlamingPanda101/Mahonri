# Newspaper Pressure Forced the Codification That Ended Adam-God

## The Claim
Historian [[Benjamin Park]] supplies the **specific mechanism** by which Adam-God was abandoned — not a single repudiating decree, but a decades-long process driven by external polemical pressure that pushed the church to systematize an alternative Godhead theology. After [[Brigham Young]]'s 1877 death the teaching faded; in the 1890s **RLDS missionaries** and a wave of **anti-Mormon newspaper series** weaponized it, forcing leaders to take a public stance; [[Joseph F. Smith]] distanced the church (1902), and [[James Talmage]]'s 1915 *Jesus the Christ* plus the 1916 "The Father and the Son" statement **codified** a Godhead that formally moved past Adam-God. The doctrine did not so much get voted down as get *crowded out* by a new, standardized theology built under fire [^1].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Post-1877 the apostles grew comfortable opposing it (strong — internal shift).** Once Young died, "more apostles became quite comfortable in speaking out against the idea." [[Wilford Woodruff]] (a believer who had recorded Young teaching it) and George Q. Cannon documented opposition, including in Quorum of the Twelve meetings; [[Joseph F. Smith]], nephew of [[Joseph Smith]], "expressed his discomfort or his hesitation." By the 1880s "they weren't really talking about it as much" [^2].

2. **1890s RLDS missionaries and newspapers made it a liability (strong — the external trigger).** In the 1890s [[Joseph Smith III|RLDS]] missionaries promulgated Adam-God as proof the Utah church "had broken away from... Joseph Smith's true gospel." With polygamy receding as the headline attack after the 1890 Manifesto, "things like Adam God doctrine took the forefront in opposition to Latter-day Saints" in newspapers — Park notes papers in 1897, 1898, and 1899 ran "entire series" on it, "which forced church leaders to finally take a stance" [^3].

3. **Joseph F. Smith's 1902 distancing (strong — first official retreat).** Answering a correspondent, Joseph F. Smith wrote that the doctrine "was never submitted to the councils of the priesthood, nor the church for approval... was never formally or otherwise accepted by the church... It is therefore in no sense binding." Park reads this as Joseph F. Smith **acknowledging Young taught it** — "even calling it doctrine in the sense that Brigham Young was teaching it" — while denying it was binding. The church's *Improvement Era* (1902) added that the church "has never formally adopted any theory concerning the subject treated by Brigham Young as to Adam" [^4].

4. **Talmage's *Jesus the Christ* (1915) supplied the replacement system (strong — the codification).** Park credits [[James Talmage]] — university professor, then president, then apostle — with the work that "formalized, standardized, systematized LDS theology of the Godhead," explicitly placing "Jesus Christ... over Adam in the eternal seniority." The following 1916 First Presidency statement "The Father and the Son" "codified many of the ideas that James Talmage had first taught." This is the constructive half: Adam-God ended because a rival, fully worked-out Godhead theology took its place [^5].

### Steelman of the critic
The critic's leverage is that this is a story of **reactive, externally-forced change** to "topics as crucial as the godhead" — exactly Park's stated lesson that "not everything in LDS theology can be taken for granted." If the determinative pressure was Protestant newspaper polemic and RLDS competition rather than fresh revelation, a skeptic can argue the church's Godhead was settled by *public relations and apologetic convenience*, not prophetic disclosure. Park's own framing partly concedes the point: later denials that Young "ever taught it" (Joseph Fielding Smith; Spencer W. Kimball in 1976) overcorrected into a falsehood, since Joseph F. Smith himself had conceded Young taught it. The apologetic reply lives in [[A Historian's Definition of Doctrine vs the Believer's]] and [[The Church Kept Most of Adam-God Rejecting Only Specific Tenets]]: codifying a clearer Godhead and dropping a few specific tenets is "continuity through change," not capitulation.

## Best Quotes
> "In 1897, 1898, and 1899 [newspapers] would often have entire series talking about the Adam-God doctrine, which forced church leaders to finally take a stance." — Benjamin Park [^3]

> "The doctrine was never submitted to the councils of the priesthood, nor the church for approval or ratification... It is therefore in no sense binding upon the church." — Joseph F. Smith (1902), quoted [^4]

> "In 1915, James Talmage published *Jesus the Christ*, which... formalized, standardized, systematized LDS theology of the Godhead... saying that Jesus Christ is over Adam in the eternal seniority." — Benjamin Park [^5]
