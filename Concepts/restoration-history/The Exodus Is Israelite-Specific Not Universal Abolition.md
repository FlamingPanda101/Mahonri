# The Exodus Is Israelite-Specific Not Universal Abolition

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: the Exodus is not an anti-slavery charter but the liberation of one *chosen* people. The freed Hebrews "immediately start taking slaves of their own from the nations around them," so reading Exodus as universal abolition is a *misreading*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The covenant, not anti-slavery, drives the Exodus.** "God remembered the covenant he made with Abraham" — the liberation is because the Israelites are God's chosen people, "not because God is anti-slavery. If God was anti-slavery, he would have put an end to it full stop there and then."[^2] *(Strong — the text frames deliverance covenantally, and the same God immediately sanctions foreign slavery.)*

2. **[[Leviticus 25.44-46|Leviticus 25:44-46]] sanctions chattel slavery of foreigners.** O'Connor reads it directly: "your male and female slaves may come from the nations around you... they will become your property for life... you can bequeath them to your children as inheritable property, but you must not rule over your fellow Israelites ruthlessly." This is *proactive permission*, not mere regulation of a pre-existing evil.[^3] *(Strong — distinguishes the Israelite/foreigner double standard; ties to [[Hebrew Bible Endorses Chattel Slavery of Foreigners]].)*

3. **The "regulation vs permission" distinction.** Scrivener defends [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21]] slave laws as God "regulating something he hates" (like divorce in [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew 19]]). O'Connor counters that Leviticus is not regulation of an inherited situation but *instructions on how to go acquire* slaves — "you may go and get them from the nations around you."[^4] *(Sharp — the divorce analogy fits *regulation*; it strains against *active acquisition commands*.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener — fulfillment in Christ.** Scrivener: those who read Exodus as pointing toward universal liberation are not misreading but reading it *through Christ* — "there's a fulfillment in Christ in that he comes as Jubilee" ([[Leviticus 25.10|Lev 25]], the year of release). Israel had to be a delimited nation with particular rules so that "the seed of Abraham" could be born; Christ then "dies the slave's death to rise up and liberate us all." See [[The Old Testament Was a Hammer for Abolitionists]].[^5] *(Genuinely the Christian-canonical reading; its strength is narrative coherence, its vulnerability — per O'Connor — is that the plain Israelite-specific sense is what the text *describes*.)*

## Best Quotes

> "The reason the Jews were freed from slavery in Egypt is not because God is anti-slavery — it's because the Jews were his chosen people."[^2]

> "It's not just regulating something he hates — it's like, you may go and get slaves from the foreign lands."[^4]

> "Who is actually misreading the story of the Exodus? Is it the abolitionist?"[^6]
