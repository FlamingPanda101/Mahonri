# The Babylonian Exile Produced Ezra-Nehemiah's Ethnic-Boundary Program

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] explains (per the session summary) that the **Babylonian exile** (after Judah fell to Babylon in 586 BCE) was the next great editorial and identity-shaping crucible after the Assyrian destruction of the north. With the people displaced and the temple gone, the surviving Judahite leadership faced an existential question: how does a small, conquered, scattered community keep from dissolving into the surrounding populations? The answer, codified in the post-exilic program associated with **Ezra and Nehemiah**, was a sharp tightening of the boundary around Judahite identity:

- **Strict ethnic boundaries.** Membership in the restored community was drawn tightly around descent and ritual purity, marking the returnees off from the surrounding peoples (including those who had remained in the land).
- **Anti-idol polemics.** The exilic and post-exilic literature hardens the rhetoric against divine images and foreign gods — the "you heard a voice but saw no image" aniconism that the vault documents as a *later* development, not the pristine original norm (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]).
- **Forced divorces of non-Judahite wives.** Most strikingly, the program demanded that Judahite men who had married women from outside the community **put those wives (and their children) away** — dissolving existing marriages to protect the purity and continuity of Judahite lineage.

On McClellan's reading this is not the timeless will of God dropped from heaven but a **community under threat engineering its own survival**: identity-protective legislation produced by a specific historical emergency.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The biblical record is explicit about the divorces.** The narratives of Ezra and Nehemiah themselves describe the mass dissolution of marriages to "foreign" women as a corporate act of the restored community — this is not a critical reconstruction smuggled in over the text but the program the post-exilic books openly narrate and endorse. McClellan is reading the boundary-policing off the surface of the literature.
2. **It fits the established pattern of crisis-driven composition.** The same method the vault applies to the Assyrian aftermath — conquered communities rewriting and re-bounding their traditions to survive — applies again here (see [[Deuteronomists Rewrote the Histories of Israel]]). The exile is the hinge: the Deuteronomistic History reaches its final shape in or after it, and the post-exilic leadership reads the catastrophe as vindication of strict covenant boundaries.
3. **The anti-idol turn is independently datable as late.** The hardened aniconism that accompanies the program is, on McClellan's broader argument, a later polemical development rather than the original Israelite norm — the archaeological and earlier-stratum record shows divine images in use in Israel and Judah (see [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]]). This dates the *intensity* of the boundary-drawing to the exilic/post-exilic moment.

**Steelman of the traditional view.** A traditional reader can hold that the post-exilic reforms were a *faithful* recovery of covenant fidelity rather than mere ethnic self-protection: intermarriage had historically pulled Israel toward idolatry, so the boundary-drawing was a principled guard on worship, not xenophobia, and the community was genuinely obeying perceived covenant obligations. McClellan's reply (per the summary) is not that the actors were insincere but that the *content* of the program — who counts, which marriages are dissolved, how hard the line against images is drawn — is visibly the product of a particular historical crisis, and reading it as the unconditioned voice of God flattens the human, defensive logic the texts themselves display.

## Apologetic Engagement
This pressures any apologetic that treats the post-exilic boundary legislation as straightforward divine command. The forced divorces in particular are an acute moral datum: a flat "this is simply what God required" reading must answer for the dissolution of real marriages and the displacement of wives and children. The honest frame is the one McClellan presses across the session — these are ancient people responding to imperial catastrophe by reshaping their identity, and the resulting rules carry the fingerprints of that emergency rather than a timeless moral absolute.

## LDS Relevance
The point is congenial to the Restoration's account of scripture as humanly shaped by communities with agendas and pressures. If even the post-exilic "purifying" reforms are historically conditioned identity-protection — drawing and redrawing the boundary of the covenant people under duress — then the claim that important material was reframed, hardened, or edited by later interested communities is historically ordinary, the same mechanism the vault invokes for the Deuteronomistic reform and the Book of Mormon's preserved pre-exilic theology (see [[Deuteronomists Rewrote the Histories of Israel]], [[The Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament Are Not the Same Book]]). McClellan, non-confessional, draws no LDS conclusion; the relevance is methodological.
