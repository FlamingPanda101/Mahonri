# Institutional Corruption Is Not Merely a Function of an Institution's Age

## The Claim

The student Nick offers a causal theory of religious corruption to [[Hayden Carroll]]: the "institutions of old are so corrupt" precisely *because* "they've been around for so long," so the relatively young Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has simply not had "enough time for the evil to infiltrate." [^1] On this model, corruption is a **near-deterministic function of an institution's age** — given enough centuries, *any* organization staffed by humans (including the Restoration) will rot. Carroll's response is two-layered. First, he **grants the critical premise as a conditional**: "from a critical view, you're exactly right. If we're going to assume that this is not God's church, I think there's a very good likelihood that it will happen." [^1] Then he denies the *unconditional* version by appealing to a defeater the age-model ignores — divine headship — developed in [[A God-Headed Church Is Guaranteed Against Corruption by Divine Foreknowledge]]: a church *actually headed by God* "will not become corrupt because he heads it," so age cannot be the lone variable. [^2]

The deeper apologetic point is that the age-theory is **double-edged**. It is an honest, non-special-pleading admission that the LDS Church's *current* good standing might be partly an artifact of youth rather than of divine protection — which is why Carroll concedes it so readily. But as a *general law* it proves too much and explains too little: age neither guarantees corruption (some old institutions reform) nor is required for it (young movements corrupt quickly), so "time elapsed" is at best a weak correlate, not the cause. The real question is *whether anything actively prevents* the drift — and that turns on the truth-claim about who heads the church, not the calendar.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The age-corruption correlation is real and the student names it honestly.** Long-lived institutions do accumulate the conditions for abuse: entrenched hierarchy, unaccountable wealth, reputational incentives to conceal scandal, and "so long" a runway for bad actors to embed. Nick's Vatican example (an archive "hoarded" for centuries) and his clergy-abuse concern are textbook cases where *duration* plausibly compounded harm. [^1][^3] The steelman: the burden is on the believer to show the Restoration has *some* mechanism the old institutions lacked, rather than merely being too new to have failed yet.
2. **The concession is candid, not evasive.** Carroll does not pretend the young Church is immune for natural reasons; he agrees that *absent* divine headship, corruption is the expected outcome — "you have to protect your home." [^2] This honesty is itself the argument's strength: it isolates the single load-bearing claim (God heads this church) and stakes everything on it, rather than special-pleading that LDS humans are constitutionally less corruptible than Catholic or Protestant humans. Cf. the parity logic in [[Adherent Hypocrisy Does Not Disprove the Religions Historical Claim]].
3. **Age is a confound, not a cause.** The believer's reply is that "time" is doing no real explanatory work: corruption is driven by *unchecked* power, and what matters is whether a corrective force (revelation, repentance, divine replacement of unfaithful leaders) operates — an old institution with that corrective could stay sound, and a young one without it could rot fast. So pointing to the Restoration's youth, by itself, neither indicts nor exonerates it; it merely defers the test. This is why Carroll redirects from the calendar to the headship question. [^2]

**Steelman of the critic.** (a) "God heads it" is *unfalsifiable insulation*: every actual institution claims divine sanction, so allowing one church to exempt itself from the age-law by fiat is special pleading the critic cannot check from outside. (b) The honest reading of Carroll's own concession is that, on *natural* evidence alone, the prudent prior is that the LDS Church will corrupt like the rest — the youth defense buys time, not vindication, and the critic can simply wait. (c) The model needn't be deterministic to bite: even as a *probabilistic* tendency ("older → more corrupt, on average"), it makes the Restoration's clean record exactly what we'd expect from a young institution and therefore *weak* evidence of divine protection — the believer needs an independent reason to think the protective mechanism is real, which pushes the whole dispute back onto the Book of Mormon and living-prophet claims.
