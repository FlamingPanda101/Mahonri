# The Nephilim Were Apostate Sethite Priesthood-Holders Not Supernatural Giants

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] argues — against the popular [[Michael Heiser]] "supernatural fallen angels" reading — that the "sons of God" and the **Nephilim/Watchers** of [[Genesis 6.1-4|Genesis 6:1-4]] were **mortal Sethite covenant men**: priesthood-holders ("overseers," Greek *episkopos* = bishop; translated "watchers" in some languages) who guarded a holy mountain, then **broke their covenants** by descending to the "daughters of Cain." They were not angels who "betrayed their first estate," not beings who "live on top of beanstalks" — they were once-righteous men who fell. Barnes builds this from Joseph Smith's [[Book of Moses|Moses 6-7]] (where "sons of God" is a *title* for the righteous baptized) read alongside the *Cave of Treasures*, *Jubilees*, and *1 Enoch*. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Sons of God" is a covenant *title*, not a species — rate: moderate (rests on Moses 6 as scripture)
In [[Book of Moses|Moses 6:68]] ("thou art… a son of God; and thus may all become my sons") and [[Book of Moses|Moses 7:1]], "sons of God" is the name given to those who "believed and became the sons of God," contrasted with "the daughters of men" who were sinful. Even Satan claims it falsely ([[Book of Moses|Moses 5:13]]: "I am also a son of God"). So Genesis 6's "sons of God saw the daughters of men" describes **covenant men intermarrying with the apostate line**, not divine beings mating with humans. This coheres with the vault's [[Bene Elohim Are Sons Not a Different Species]]. [^1]

### 2. "Watcher" = "overseer" = "bishop" — rate: moderate (etymology + apocryphal narrative)
The men on the holy mountain are called "overseers" — *episkopos* (*epi* "over" + *skopos* "to see"), the root of *bishop*; in some traditions rendered "watcher." When they go to the cliffs to watch the daughters of Cain "every evening" and "got just locked in," they earn the ironic title *watchers*. So when 1 Enoch names "the Watchers," Barnes reads it as **priesthood leaders who went voyeur**, not a class of angels. (Ellis's gloss: "the bishops of the day… betraying their covenants… the modern watchers" who "made it to bishop or stake president before they read the CES letter.") [^2]

### 3. The covenant, the trap, and the leader Azazel — rate: weak-moderate (composite of apocrypha)
Per the *Ethiopic Book of Enoch* and *Cave of Treasures*: the Sethites fear being "told on," so their leader **Azazel** makes them swear a **blood oath** ("no one's going to snitch"); they descend, cannot climb back ("the rocks are too steep"), and are trapped among the daughters of Cain. Barnes stitches several sources into a single narrative of *trapped apostates*, conceding "this is my theory that I piece together." [^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The supernatural reading is the majority scholarly *and* ancient view, and it is strong. (a) **1 Enoch itself plainly describes heavenly beings**: the Watchers "descend from heaven," are bound by archangels, and are judged as *angelic* rebels — the text's own frame is celestial, not a metaphor for backsliding bishops (this is the reading in [[Book of Enoch - The Watchers and Genesis 6 Expansion]]). (b) The **"Sethite hypothesis" is a later, post-biblical harmonization** (Julius Africanus, Augustine) developed precisely to *avoid* the embarrassment of angels procreating; critically, Second Temple Judaism and the New Testament ([[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]], [[2 Peter 3.5-6|2 Peter]]) read Genesis 6 as *angelic* transgression, so the mortal reading runs against the earliest interpretive tradition. (c) Barnes **openly disclaims expertise** ("I'm not a Hebraist… this is my theory"), and the host even tells him "you're arguing like an atheist" when he declares the giants are a Bible corruption — a sign the reconstruction is idiosyncratic. (d) Reading *bene elohim* as merely "righteous men" is contested even within the divine-council framework the vault elsewhere endorses, where they are *lesser divine beings* in Yahweh's council, not humans. The fair takeaway: Barnes's Sethite-priesthood reading is a **creative LDS synthesis** (legitimately grounded in Moses 6-7's "sons of God = covenant title"), but it is a minority reconstruction that cuts against both 1 Enoch's plain sense and the oldest Jewish-Christian interpretation, and Barnes presents it as such.
