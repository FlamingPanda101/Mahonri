# NDE - New Views of Death

## The Point
The most consistent aftereffect of the near-death experience is the **loss of the fear of death**.[^1] This is not a death wish — none of these people seeks death, all believe they have tasks to finish, and all reject suicide as a way back to what they glimpsed.[^2] Rather, death has simply ceased to terrify them, because survival of bodily death is no longer an abstract hope but "a fact of his experience."[^3] Tellingly, survivors abandon both the "annihilation" model of death (sleep, forgetting) and the cartoon **reward-punishment** model, replacing them with images of death as a *transition* — a homecoming, a graduation, an escape from prison — and a view of the hereafter centered on continued growth rather than unilateral judgment.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **No longer afraid.** "I am thoroughly convinced that there is life after death, without a shadow of a doubt, and I am not afraid to die."[^5] Even with a gun to his temple: "if they really kill me, I know I'll still live somewhere."[^5]
2. **Childhood terror healed.** A man who as a boy "used to wake up at night crying" in dread of death now says, "since this experience, I don't fear death. Those feelings vanished. I don't feel bad at funerals anymore. I kind of rejoice at them."[^6]
3. **Knowledge, not a death wish.** "The reason why I'm not afraid to die... is that I know where I'm going when I leave here, because I've been there before."[^7]
4. **Death as transition, not annihilation.** Survivors choose analogies of "a transition from one state to another," a "homecoming," "awakening," "graduating," and "escape from jail": "You just graduate from one thing to another—like from grammar school to high school to college."[^8]
5. **No cartoon heaven or hell.** "Not one person has painted the mythological picture... No one has described the cartoonist's heaven of pearly gates, golden streets, and winged, harp-playing angels, nor a hell of flames and demons with pitchforks."[^9]
6. **Cooperative development, not unilateral judgment.** The reward-punishment model is "abandoned and disavowed," replaced by "a vision which features not unilateral judgement, but rather cooperative development towards the ultimate end of self-realization," in which the soul's growth "does not stop upon death."[^10]

## Best Quotes
> "After you've once had the experience that I had, you know in your heart that there's no such thing as death. You just graduate from one thing to another—like from grammar school to high school to college." [^8]

> "Development of the soul, especially in the spiritual faculties of love and knowledge, does not stop upon death. Rather, it continues on the other side, perhaps eternally." [^10]

## LDS Connection
The conquest of the fear of death is the very promise of the Restoration's **plan of salvation**: because the spirit lives on and the body will rise, death is "swallowed up in the joy of Christ." Survivors' instinctive image of an intermediate, conscious state right after death matches Alma's revelation that "the spirits of all men, as soon as they are departed from this mortal body... are taken home to that God who gave them life" ([[Alma 40.11|Alma 40:11]]). Their rejection of a simple two-bin heaven/hell in favor of *graduated, continuing development* harmonizes remarkably with the LDS doctrine of **degrees of glory** and **eternal progression**, against the binary "pearly gates or pitchforks" picture they explicitly disown. Even their cautious "through a glass, darkly" humility about the hereafter ([[1 Corinthians 13.12|1 Corinthians 13:12]]) — Moody's own closing phrase — fits the LDS view that much remains to be revealed.
