# Polygamy Doctrine Did Not Change Only the Exaltation-Requires-Polygamy Idea Did

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that, contrary to "the church reversed its doctrine on polygamy," the **doctrine** of polygamy never changed: the collective prophetic witness has always held that **monogamy is the norm** while **plural marriage can be legitimately authorized at times** for special purposes (the teaching of [[Jacob 2]]). What changed was the *practice* (ended by the 1890 Manifesto) and, at most, one *disputed* and never-uniform doctrine — that exaltation *requires* polygamy — which was itself out of harmony with [[Jacob 2]]. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Jacob 2 sets the standing rule.** Plural marriage is the exception God may command "to raise up seed," with monogamy ("Adam and Eve") as the norm — "we are the only Christian faith that even attempts to coherently account for polygamy clearly existing in the collective prophetic witness." *Rating: strong as the scriptural anchor — see [[Jacob 2]].* [^1]
2. **The exaltation-requires-polygamy teaching was disputed even in its heyday.** Though "commonly taught" between roughly 1850 and 1890, "[[Wilford Woodruff]], Joseph F. Smith and many other apostles... are on record saying that Joseph never taught polygamy as a requirement for exaltation" and that "a man could be exalted in a single marriage." The historical record shows "conflicting messages." *Rating: moderate-strong; the requirement was never the unanimous, cross-dispensational consensus — cf. [[Belief in Polygamy Was Required for the Temple Only in 1856]].* [^2]
3. **A reductio against the requirement.** "Is Adam, the literal archetype of man, not exalted because he was only married to Eve?" The requirement contradicts the very prophetic archetype, marking it as the weak, correctable element. *Rating: strong internal argument.* [^3]
4. **Each plural sealing is still man-and-woman.** Quoting [[Greg Matson]]: plural marriage "is always a marriage between one man and one woman"; a man "will have several marriages with one woman each" — so it never redefined marriage as anything other than between a man and a woman, unlike same-sex marriage. *Rating: moderate; a definitional point used to block the [[Patrick Mason]] "we haven't always taught man-woman" claim.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
[[Patrick Mason]] and others reply that "it doesn't take a PhD in history to know that we've not always consistently taught that marriage between a man and a woman is the only marriage ordained of God" — pointing out that 19th-century leaders gave "fiery sermons" that polygamy could *never* end, right before it ended, and taught it as essential to exaltation. On this reading the Church's marriage teaching demonstrably *has* shifted, undercutting the claim that present marriage doctrine is immutable.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: the sermons were about the *practice* (which the model always allowed to change with context) and the exaltation-requirement was the *disputed, single-era* teaching — both correctable without contradicting the collective witness, which consistently holds *monogamy-norm-with-authorized-exceptions*. None of it amounts to the Church ever sanctioning a non-man-woman marriage. Hence polygamy's history is **not** a precedent for redefining marriage. See [[Gay Marriage Is Not Analogous to the Priesthood Ban or Polygamy]] and [[Change Is Not the Same as Contradiction]].

## Best Quotes
> "The church has not changed its doctrine on polygamy. The church fully believes what Jacob 2 teaches, specifically that monogamy is the norm, but polygamy at times can be legitimately authorized." [^1]

> "While the practice of polygamy changed, the only doctrine around polygamy that changed was likely the disputed doctrine that polygamy was a requirement for exaltation. And again, this was a change back into alignment with the collective witness and the original teachings found in places like Jacob 2." [^3]
