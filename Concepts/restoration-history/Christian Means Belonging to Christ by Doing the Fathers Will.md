# Christian Means Belonging to Christ by Doing the Fathers Will

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] proposes replacing the **creedal** test for "Christian" (assent to the Nicene Trinity) with a **fruits-and-obedience** test drawn from Christ himself. The etymology of *Christianos* is "follower of / belonging to Christ" (the *Christ-* root = "anointed one"; the suffix = "belonging to"). The membership question should therefore be decided by whether a person *belongs to Christ* — and Jesus' own criterion for that, in the Sermon on the Mount, is doing the will of the Father, not passing a doctrinal exam [^1].

This is distinct from the vault's other two "are Mormons Christian?" plays: [[Define Christian First and the Concession Follows]] (get the *opponent* to supply a minimal definition) and [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]] (concede "non-creedal" while affirming Christ-centeredness). Here the *definition itself* is grounded in Matthew 7 and offered as the standard "Christ necessarily sets."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Jesus' own membership test is "doeth the will of my Father" — rate: moderate-to-strong (dominical text)
[[Matthew 7.21|Matthew 7:21-23]]: "Not every one that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven; but he that doeth the will of my Father which is in heaven." Those who prophesied and cast out devils "in thy name" but worked iniquity hear "I never knew you." Carroll reads this as Christ defining who *belongs to him* — and the criterion is *doing the Father's will*, with the preceding [[Matthew 7.16-20|"by their fruits ye shall know them"]] as the diagnostic. The doctrinally-orthodox-but-disobedient are excluded; the imperfect-but-following are included (with grace covering the imperfection) [^1][^2].

### 2. The creedal standard is historically contingent, not dominical — rate: moderate
Carroll argues the Nicene/Athanasian definition is a *post*-New-Testament construction whose own framers conceded difficulty: the councils were marked by "heated contentions," and dissenters "were just cast out as heretics" — so "who's to say the ones who won out were holding the truth?" [[Jeffrey R. Holland]]'s clip supplies the documentary backing (Harper's Bible Dictionary: the conciliar Trinity "is not to be found in the New Testament"). If the creeds are not in the New Testament, they cannot be the *biblical* definition of "Christian." See [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]] [^3].

### 3. The "oneness" Christ wanted is unity, not one substance — rate: moderate
On the positive side Carroll affirms the Godhead from [[John 17.20-23|John 17]]: Jesus prays believers "may be one... *as* thou Father art in me" — the same oneness extended to disciples, hence a oneness of purpose/indwelling, not of substance. So the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are "one in purpose... in love, gentleness, kindness, justice, grace, mercy," which is the oneness "that matters." See [[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality]] [^4].

### 4. Opponents already concede the fruits — rate: moderate (turnabout)
Carroll notes Catholic and Protestant apologists routinely grant Latter-day Saints are "the greatest people ever... kind... service-oriented" — i.e., they *pass* the Matthew 7 fruits test — yet damn them solely for rejecting the Trinity. On the proposed definition that is backwards: the very people Christ's stated criterion would recognize are excluded by a criterion Christ never stated [^5].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The creedal Christian answers that "Christian" is not a label each person redefines from a favorite proof-text but a *historic* term whose content is the rule of faith — and that Matthew 7 is about *eschatological judgment* (who enters the kingdom), not about the *semantic boundary* of the word "Christian," so Carroll equivocates between "saved/known by Christ" and "counts as Christian for purposes of theological classification." Moreover, "doing the will of the Father" is not creed-free: John 6:40 makes the Father's will *believing on the Son* rightly, and 1 John ties fellowship to a true confession of who the Son is — so a gravely defective Christology (a Son who is the same *kind* as creatures, not *homoousios*) arguably *fails* the will-of-the-Father test, not passes it. On this view the fruits are necessary but not sufficient: orthodoxy of worship (worshiping the right God) is part of the Father's will, and that is exactly the point in dispute (see [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]], [[Worshiping the Right Jesus - Created vs Uncreated]]). The Latter-day Saint reply is that the creeds smuggle a fourth-century metaphysic in as the silent baseline, that Holland's dictionaries show it is *not* the New Testament's own category, and that Jesus, asked point-blank, gave a fruits-and-obedience test (Matthew 7) rather than a substance test — so the burden is on the creedalist to show why an extra-biblical definition should override the dominical one.

## Best Quotes
> "Not everyone that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven, but he that doeth the will of my Father which is in heaven... that is how I want to measure whether or not someone is a Christian. Are they trying to follow Christ?" [^1]

> "I'm going to propose a different metric or rubric by which we judge whether or not people are Christian other than whether or not they ascribe to the third- and fourth-century creeds." [^3]

> "They admit that their life is lived in a Christian manner... but then because they don't believe in the Trinity as laid out in the Nicene Creed, that's why they're going to hell. I would say that's not what Jesus taught." [^5]
