# Neurological Explanations of NDEs Rebutted

## The Point
Because two striking NDE features — the panoramic life "review" and the out-of-body experience — have rough neurological look-alikes (temporal-lobe seizures with vivid flashbacks; "autoscopic," self-seeing hallucinations), some propose the NDE is just a brain malfunction [^1]. Moody examines both parallels closely and shows the **differences outweigh the similarities** [^2][^3]. Decisively, he notes that the proposed explainers are *themselves unexplained*: there is no accepted theory of autoscopic hallucinations either, so reducing the OBE to autoscopy merely "substitute[s] a bafflement for an enigma" [^4]. He also reports that, despite routinely asking, he found only **one** case of any residual neurological deficit after an NDE [^5] — the encounters do not behave like brain injuries.

## Evidence
1. **Life-review vs. seizure flashbacks.** A temporal-lobe seizure patient saw vivid, three-dimensional flashbacks — superficially like the NDE review. But the seizure images came in *no order*, were *trivial* not significant, were "not... presented to him for judgmental or educational purposes," and could *not* be recalled afterward — whereas NDE subjects report their review in life-order, as meaningful, and remember their lives *better* afterward [^2].
2. **OBE vs. autoscopic ("self-seeing") hallucination.** In autoscopy the double is perceived as *alive* (sometimes more alive than the subject), often talks and gives instructions, and is usually seen only from the chest up — while in the OBE the body is seen as a *lifeless shell*, fully and silently [^3].
3. Ironically, autoscopic doubles (wispy, transparent, colorless, passing through doors) resemble what Moody calls the *spiritual body* far more than the physical body the dying person actually sees — so the analogy cuts the wrong way [^3].
4. **The fatal flaw:** "the existence of autoscopic hallucinations would not give us an explanation for the occurrence of out-of-body experiences. The simple reason is that there is no explanation for autoscopic hallucinations, either" — many conflicting theories, none accepted [^4].
5. Moody "often asked whether there were any residual deficits"; across all his cases he found only one mild partial paralysis — "the only example I have found of neurological damage following a near-death encounter" [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "So, to try to explain all out-of-body experiences as autoscopic hallucinations would only be to substitute a bafflement for an enigma." [^4]

> "Unlike those seen in near-death experiences, the memory images did not come in the order of his life, nor were they seen all at once, in a unifying vision. They were not highlights or significant events in his life; he stresses their triviality." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Reducing the life-review and the spirit's exit to misfiring neurons assumes the mind is wholly a product of the brain. Moody's careful contrasts — orderly, meaningful, memory-enhancing review; a body seen as a lifeless shell — resist that reduction. This comports with the Restoration teaching that the spirit is an entity with its own faculties and moral history (Abraham 3:25 on probation and memory of choices; Alma 40 on the spirit's continued life), not an artifact of cortical activity.
