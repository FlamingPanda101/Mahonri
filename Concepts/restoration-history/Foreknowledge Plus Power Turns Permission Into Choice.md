# Foreknowledge Plus Power Turns Permission Into Choice

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Defenders say God merely **permits** damnation rather than willing it. [[Hayden Carroll]] rebuts: "permission only has moral force when someone lacks the power to prevent an outcome." God lacks no such limitation — "not constrained by ignorance, weakness or necessity. He could simply refrain from creating, and in that context permission collapses into choice. The button was still pressed."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The bridge-engineer parallel** — rate: strong. "If an engineer designs and builds a bridge knowing in advance that it will collapse and kill those who cross it, he cannot later say 'I merely permitted the disaster.' Foreknowledge combined with power transforms permission into responsibility." *(moral-parallel)* [^1]
2. **God has no excusing condition** — the three classic excuses for "mere permission" (ignorance, weakness, necessity) are all unavailable to an omniscient, omnipotent, a se God. *(elimination)* [^2]
3. **Reduces to the button** — since God could have refrained from creating at all, the permit/will distinction dissolves: "the button was still pressed." See [[The Creation Dilemma]]. *(synthesis)* [^1]

**Steelman of the permission defense:** the will/permit distinction does real work even for an omnipotent God *if* preventing the evil would require eliminating a greater good (e.g., libertarian freedom) — God permits the misuse of freedom because overriding it would destroy the good of freedom itself (the *principle of double effect*: the bad is foreseen but not intended, and the good outweighs). The bridge analogy arguably fails because the engineer gains nothing from the collapse, whereas God permits damnation only as the side-effect of a genuinely good freedom-endowing creation. Hayden's counter (again): this routes back to creation — God could refrain from *creating the bridge-crossers at all*, so the double-effect good must be weighed against simply not making the person, and a foreknown-damned individual's existence does not pass that test.
