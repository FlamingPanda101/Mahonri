# Christianity Is a Restoration of First-Temple Religion Not an Invention

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler]] argues for a "softened" version of the thesis that does not depend on dating any apocryphon: **first-temple Israelite religion had multiple descendants** — rabbinic Judaism, Christianity, the gnostics, the Samaritans, the Mandaeans — and early Christianity is best understood not as a bizarre 1st-century *aberration* from Judaism but as one heir *restoring* the older religion. Jesus and John came "to restore the ancient Israelite religion, not to just change it." The most economical explanation for why a wave of texts insists early Christians were the *legitimate* heirs of the first temple is that there was something real there to inherit.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. First temple → many descendants, not one straight line — rate: moderate (historical model)
Butler's frame: the old "first temple → second temple → rabbinic Judaism, and then those weird Christians misunderstood everything" picture treats Christianity as "a hundred-year offshoot before they finally got their own club car." Against this, "first temple had multiple descendants" — biblical Jews, *other* kinds of Jews, Christians, gnostics, Samaritans, Mandaeans — each carrying part of the older religion.[^1] This aligns with the vault's [[Christianity Began as a New Religious Movement]] and [[Christianity and Judaism Were Meant to Be One Religion]].

### 2. The gospel writers are intimately Jewish yet furious at the *scribes* — rate: moderate (textual observation)
The synoptics and John know first-century Palestine and its institutions intimately (so the authors are Hebrews) yet are "very upset with the Jews" — which raises the question "who are they?" The answer Butler favors: Hebrews who do not identify with the scribal party. Jesus' harshest condemnations fall on "the people writing the canon… because they've perverted the canon, they've rewritten the history."[^2] This is the New Testament face of [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes]].

### 3. Which is more probable — restoration or *ex nihilo*? — rate: moderate (abductive)
Butler's central question: "is it more likely that Christianity came from somewhere, or is it Christianity *ex nihilo*?" The claim that "the most important thing in the history of things was only thought up in 30 AD" — or borrowed wholesale from the Hellenistic city-state god — is less probable than that the early Christians "were carrying on, restoring, or responding to" things genuinely in their own people's history. Even at the "softest level," a wave of post-Book-of-Mormon documents claiming first-temple descent is "astonishing vindication for Joseph Smith."[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The model is attractive but the inference is loose. (a) **"Came from somewhere" ≠ "restored a pre-Christian Christianity."** Mainstream scholarship already holds Christianity emerged *from* Second-Temple Judaism and its scriptures — that is uncontroversial and does not entail a *suppressed first-temple Christianity* with atoning Messiah, Heavenly Mother, and proxy ordinances. Butler's "softened" version smuggles the strong conclusion back in via "in agreement with the Book of Mormon on multiple points." (b) **Multiple descendants is standard, and cuts against uniqueness.** That first-temple religion fed many later movements is a reason to see Christianity as *one* contingent development among several, not as the *true* restoration — the model is neutral between "Christianity recovered the original" and "Christianity is just another branch." (c) **Hostility to "the Jews" has simpler explanations.** Intra-Jewish sectarian polemic (Qumran vs. the Jerusalem priesthood, Pharisees vs. Sadducees) is well attested without positing a lost first-temple Christianity; the gospels' anti-scribal rhetoric fits that milieu. (d) **Direction of the apocrypha.** That later Christians *claimed* first-temple legitimacy shows their self-understanding, not that the claim is historically true; appropriating prestigious ancestry is a common move. The disciplined takeaway: the "restoration, not invention" framing is a coherent and even fruitful lens, and the abductive "ex nihilo is improbable" point has some force; but it establishes at most that Christianity has real Israelite roots — a mainstream claim — not the specific LDS reconstruction of *what* was restored, which still requires the contested textual case.
