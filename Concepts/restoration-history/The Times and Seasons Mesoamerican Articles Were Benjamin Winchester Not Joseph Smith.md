#type/claim

# The Times and Seasons Mesoamerican Articles Were Benjamin Winchester Not Joseph Smith

## The Claim
[[Jonathan Neville]] argues that the 1842 *Times and Seasons* articles linking the Book of Mormon to the Central American ruins of Stephens and Catherwood — long cited as evidence that Joseph Smith endorsed a Mesoamerican setting — were *not written by Joseph*. Joseph was only the nominal editor; the articles, Neville contends, were authored by the excommunicated early member [[Benjamin Winchester]], who had been promoting the Central-American connection in his own newspaper as a marketing strategy.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Editor" was a nominal title.** Each issue said the paper was "edited, printed and published by Joseph Smith," yet "nobody thinks Joseph Smith was running the printing press." Neville argues the same logic applies to "edited": Joseph was the *publisher* in charge, not the working editor — and Neville cites instances where Joseph "didn't know it was in the paper until after he read it."[^1] *(Moderate — the nominal-masthead point is reasonable; that it extends to *every* article is an inference.)*
2. **The Nephi/Moroni error.** When Joseph Smith—History was first printed under Joseph's editorship, the messenger was named **Nephi**, not Moroni ([[Joseph Smith History 1.34|JS—H 1:34]]) — an error left uncorrected for ~20 years, showing Joseph "hadn't reviewed that obviously." Wilford Woodruff's journal (summer 1842) records that Joseph was so busy "he hardly has time to sign the stuff we prepare for him."[^2] *(Moderate-to-strong — the misprint and Woodruff's note are documentary, and do show limited editorial attention.)*
3. **Winchester is the better candidate.** Matt Roper's stylometry placed the ~900 words of original article text *outside* the writings of Joseph, John Taylor, and Wilford Woodruff. Neville, pursuing a distinctive phrase, kept landing on Winchester, who had published the same Central-American material in his *Gospel Reflector* the year before and was contributing anonymously to Nauvoo papers via William Smith. Neville faults Roper for a "black box" stylometry (undisclosed database).[^3] *(Weak-to-moderate — the positive attribution to Winchester is Neville's own reconstruction and is disputed; the negative result (not the three named men) is the firmer part.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This attribution is contested by other Latter-day Saint scholars and rests on inference. (a) Joseph Smith's editorship was not purely nominal: he signed editorials, took public responsibility for the paper's content, and the Wentworth Letter (his own, same period) *does* engage the Central-American material — so attributing the Times-and-Seasons articles to Winchester requires explaining why Joseph corrected Orson Pratt's hemispheric claims in Wentworth but not these. (b) Winchester had been fired and left Nauvoo before/around when the articles ran, complicating a clean authorship story; Matt Roper and Neal Rappleye have argued the editorial circle (John Taylor, W.W. Phelps, Woodruff) is the more economical source. (c) Stylometry on ~900 words is statistically thin, and Neville's own positive identification is, by his admission, a reconstruction he could not get a specialist to co-author. The honest, narrower residue: the articles' authorship is *uncertain*, and the confident claim that "Joseph Smith endorsed Mesoamerica" overreads a masthead — but so does the confident claim that Winchester definitely wrote them.

## Best Quotes
> "Nobody thinks Joseph Smith was running the printing press, even though [the masthead] said he printed it... so why would anybody think he's editing it either? He's just the nominal editor."[^1]

> "When it was published under Joseph Smith as editor it said Nephi [not Moroni]... and Wilford Woodruff wrote that Joseph is so busy he hardly has time to sign the stuff that we prepare for him."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The attribution matters because the Times-and-Seasons articles are the strongest documentary prop for a Mesoamerican setting endorsed by Joseph; if Winchester wrote them, that prop falls and the field is left to [[Oliver Cowdery's Letter VII States Cumorah Is in New York as Fact|Cowdery's Letter VII]]. It also illustrates Neville's broader [[Clarity Charity and Understanding and the Faith Model|"faith model"]]: what looks like a "fact" (Joseph endorsed Mesoamerica) is, he argues, an *assumption* about who held the pen.
