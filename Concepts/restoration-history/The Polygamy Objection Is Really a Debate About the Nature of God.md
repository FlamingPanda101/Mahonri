# The Polygamy Objection Is Really a Debate About the Nature of God

## The Claim
[[Selene Anderson]] reframes the deepest polygamy objection as a **dispute about the nature of God**, not about 19th-century sociology. She quotes denial-leader Michelle Stone's own admission that the question is "a debate on the nature of God" — Stone holding that "God could never command this." Anderson inverts it: she believes "God would never command something that's not for our benefit and for our good," trusting that "his ways are higher than our ways."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The real premise is theological, not historical (rated: strong as analysis).** Once both sides agree on the documentary record (that Joseph taught and practiced plural marriage — see [[Whether Joseph Practiced Polygamy Has No Bearing on Eternal Polygamy]]), the remaining disagreement is whether a good God *could* command it. That is a claim about God's character, which historical data cannot settle.[^1]

2. **"Fair" is not the highest value (rated: interpretive).** Anderson argues the objection "it's not fair that one man has multiple women" assumes fairness is the supreme good. Citing Terryl Givens (Wayfare conference) — "forgiveness is inherently an expression of moral asymmetry" — and the Atonement itself, she contends the universe is built on a "perpetual imbalance" that variety necessitates; demanding strict sameness is "a false idol."[^2] She marshals the parable of the laborers ("Is it not lawful for me to do what I will with mine own?") and Jesus on John the Beloved ("If I will that he tarry... what is that to thee?").[^3]

3. **Trust as the resolving move (rated: faith claim, by her own framing).** "I believe that our Heavenly Father knows what will make us happier... better than we do." The willingness "to allow things you don't want to be true to be true" is, she says, what "will set you free."[^4] Overlaps the knowledge-cure theme of [[Understanding Replaces Fear - the Knowledge Cure for the Polygamy Stigma]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's reply is that "trust God's higher ways" is exactly the move that can launder *any* commanded harm — it is unfalsifiable and can be deployed to defend atrocities as easily as plural marriage. Michelle Stone's instinct ("a good God could not command this") is a legitimate use of moral reasoning as a check on revelation; Anderson's framework risks subordinating conscience to authority. And recasting a concrete welfare question (did this practice hurt women?) as an abstract debate about divine prerogative can dodge the empirical harm. The strongest version of Anderson's claim is epistemic humility — that our moral intuitions are *fallible* guides to what a maximally good God would permit — not that they should be silenced.

## Counter-Objection
"If moral intuition can't judge a command, why trust that *this* God is good in the first place?" Anderson's answer is that the trust is grounded elsewhere — in her conviction that "his church is the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints" and in the Atonement as evidence of a God who makes asymmetry *just* through agency and grace; given that prior, deference on a hard command is reasonable, not blind.

## Best Quotes
> "Michelle Stone said it herself. It's a debate on the nature of God... her being like, God could never command this. And I say instead, I believe that God would never command something that's not for our benefit and for our good. And his ways are higher than our ways."[^1]

> "Forgiveness is inherently an expression of moral asymmetry... I'm asking you to consent willingly to a perpetual imbalance in the universe." (Terryl Givens, quoted)[^2]

> "The willingness to allow things you don't want to be true to be true... is what will set you free."[^4]
