# Chicago Statement on Inerrancy Redefines Literal

## The Claim

The **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** (International Council on Biblical Inerrancy, October 1978) is a 25-article creed, each "we affirm X and we deny Y," produced "to affirm afresh the doctrine of the inerrancy of scripture." [^1][^2] [[Dan McClellan]] argues its **Article XV** quietly redefines "literal" into incoherence: it affirms reading the Bible "according to its literal or normal sense," defined as "the grammatical-historical sense," then adds that this "will take account of all figures of speech and literary forms." [^3] So "literal here also includes the intent to use things figuratively," which "is not what literal generally is referring to" and "is open to interpretation again." [^4][^5]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The definition is self-undermining.** If "literal" already encompasses figurative language, the term carries no discriminating content: "you've just said it's only literal except when it's not, and that doesn't seem helpful." [^5] The threshold between literal and figurative is reintroduced, and someone must adjudicate it. See [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]]. **(Strong — the article's own wording invites the critique.)**
2. **It exhumes Origen only to re-condemn him.** Article XV denies "the legitimacy of any approach... which the literal sense does not support," which McClellan reads as "dredging Origen's body back up to once again announce it anathema." [^6] See [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]]. **(Rhetorical but apt.)**
3. **It forbids science from correcting scripture.** Another article: "We affirm that Genesis 1–11 is factual... We deny... that scientific hypotheses about earth history or the origin of humanity may be invoked to overthrow what scripture teaches about creation" — i.e., "the Bible overrules science." [^7][^8] And it insists "the scriptures can never disagree with each other." [^9] **(Strong — directly quoted.)**
4. **It starts from dogma and reasons backward.** "None of these things are based on any kind of evidence or pure logic or reason. We're starting from the dogmas and then... constructing the rationalization." [^10] See [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]] and [[Literalism Is Ideological Not Textual]]. **(Strong as internal critique.)**
5. **Steelman.** Inerrantists argue Article XV is the *opposite* of wooden literalism — it explicitly protects genre sensitivity, so an inerrantist can read poetry as poetry and apocalyptic as apocalyptic while still affirming truthfulness. McClellan's reply is that this concession is exactly the problem: once genre judgments are licensed, the inerrantist can relabel any inconvenient text "figurative," so the doctrine becomes unfalsifiable and the real authority is the interpreter's tradition. See [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]]. **(Moderate-to-strong — the disagreement is whether genre-sensitive inerrancy is principled or ad hoc.)**

## Best Quotes

> "We affirm the necessity of interpreting the Bible according to its literal or normal sense. The literal sense is the grammatical-historical sense... [and] will take account of all figures of speech and literary forms found in the text." [^3]

> "You've just said it's only literal except when it's not, and that doesn't seem helpful to me." [^5]

> "We deny... that scientific hypotheses about earth history or the origin of humanity may be invoked to overthrow what scripture teaches about creation. So basically just saying the Bible overrules science." [^8]
