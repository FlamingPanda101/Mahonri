# The Apostasy Was the Church Driven Into the Wilderness Not Annihilated

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] argue that [[D&C 86]] reframes the Great Apostasy in a way "different than our typical cultural narrative." The common LDS story — "all the apostles were killed and the priesthood was taken from the earth and the Church was gone" — is, they say, an oversimplification developed in the 20th century. D&C 86 instead has Christ himself, interpreting the Parable of the Wheat and Tares ([[Matthew 13.24-30|Matthew 13:24-30]]), say the tares "drive the church into the wilderness" — an allusion to [[Revelation 12]]. The Restoration is therefore not the re-creation of a vanished church but the church *coming out of the wilderness*; "in some important sense, the Church never actually fully left the earth."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Christ himself testifies to the apostasy (rating: strong; scriptural).** D&C 86, alongside the First Vision and D&C 1, is "where the Savior directly himself explains how the apostasy took place." The field is the world, the apostles are the sowers, and "after they have fallen asleep" the enemy sows tares that "choke the wheat, and drive the church into the wilderness."[^1]
2. **"Driven into the wilderness" = [[Revelation 12]] (rating: strong; intertextual).** In Revelation 12 the church is a woman who "fled into the wilderness" from a seven-headed dragon. D&C 86 deliberately invokes this, and the Lord repeatedly describes the Restoration as the reverse motion — the church called "forth out of the wilderness" (D&C 5:14; 33:5-6) and brought "out of obscurity and out of darkness" (D&C 1:30).[^2]
3. **The Nauvoo Temple stonework depicts the Revelation 12 woman (rating: moderate; material culture).** The temple's sun-, star-, and moon-stones — which puzzle observers because the stars sit above the sun — match Revelation 12's woman "clothed with the sun," a "crown of twelve stars," the "moon under her feet." The architects said the facade represents "the Church, the bride, the Lamb's wife of Revelation 12" — announcing she has come out of the wilderness.[^2]
4. **"Restored church" is not Joseph Smith's idiom (rating: moderate; lexical-historical).** The phrase "restored church" is "not in the Doctrine and Covenants"; Griffiths attributes it to Elder Talmage. The Lord's own language is *coming out of the wilderness*, which implies continuity (the teachings of Christ and sincere followers "remained") even though "the keys of the kingdom... were definitely taken to heaven."[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
Two opposite critiques converge. (a) A **traditional Christian** says this concession undercuts the LDS apostasy claim itself: if the church "never fully left the earth" and faithful Christians persisted with Christ's teachings, why posit a *total* apostasy requiring a restoration at all? The "wilderness" softening looks like a retreat toward ordinary Christian continuity. (b) A **traditional Latter-day Saint** worries it dilutes the stark "the Church was gone" narrative that motivates the necessity of the Restoration and the exclusivity claims of D&C 1:30.

## Counter-Objection
The hosts hold both halves together by distinguishing *keys/authority* from *teachings/people*: "The keys of the kingdom, they were definitely taken to heaven. The Apostles were killed, the keys were gone, but the teachings remained." So the apostasy is real and decisive *with respect to priesthood authority and apostolic governance* (which is what the Restoration restores), while the wilderness imagery captures that God "never abandoned his people" and continued to inspire reformers (Joseph F. Smith praised "Calvin, Luther, Melanchthon" as "inspired... to accomplish what they did"). Against the traditional-Christian critique, the distinctive LDS claim survives intact (lost keys, needed restoration); against the traditional-LDS worry, the refinement is presented as *more* historically honest, not less faithful — it stops "discount[ing] all the good that was done by Christians during those centuries." See [[The Priesthood Lineage Continued Hidden Through the Apostasy]].

## Best Quotes
> "We usually simplify the apostasy by saying all the apostles were killed and the priesthood was taken from the earth and the Church was gone. But this is leaving little hints that no, it wasn't."[^1]

> "He doesn't say the Church was gone in the apostasy... Instead, he says the Church was driven into the wilderness, and the Restoration is this process of bringing the Church back out of the wilderness."[^3]
