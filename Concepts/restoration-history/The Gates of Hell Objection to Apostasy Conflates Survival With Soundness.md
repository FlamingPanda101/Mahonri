# The Gates of Hell Objection to Apostasy Conflates Survival With Soundness

## The Claim
Marvelous Wonder targets the stock objection that [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18]] ("the gates of hell shall not prevail against it") refutes any LDS claim of a universal apostasy: if an institution descended from first-century Christianity still exists, then the gates of hell never prevailed, so no restoration was needed. The channel isolates **two distinct errors** in this reasoning. First, the bare *survival* of an institution proves only that it survives, not that it remained free of error — "the fact that the institution still exists only means that it still exists," and a body can persist while missing the decisive thing, "the presence and guidance of living apostles." Second, the objection assumes "hell" in the verse means the Christian afterlife of torment, when the underlying word is *hades / sheol*, "the general afterlife where the spirits of all the dead go." On both counts the verse does not say what the objector needs it to say.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Survival ≠ soundness (rating: strong; logical).** The inference "the institution exists, therefore it is not in error" is a non sequitur, and the video pins it on "apostolic succession apologists" who "use this faulty reasoning." Continuity of an organization is compatible with that organization losing apostolic authority — the very thing the Restoration claims was lost.[^1]
2. **"Hell" = the realm of the dead, not eternal torment (rating: strong; lexical).** The KJV "gates of hell" renders Greek *hades* / Hebrew *sheol*, the undifferentiated abode of the dead — the same place Christ "descended" to between crucifixion and resurrection (1 Peter 3:19-20; 4:6; the Apostles' Creed). So the verse is not about a fiery hell besieging the church at all.[^2] This is the lexical backbone shared with [[The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church]].
3. **The "best answer" is restorational (rating: moderate; internal).** Having relocated "hell" to the spirit world, the channel argues the verse's real fulfillment is the opening of that realm to the dead — which only the Restoration's temple ordinances accomplish (developed in [[Temple Work for the Dead Fulfills the Gates of Hell Promise]]).[^2]

## Opposing Side (Steelmanned)
The Catholic/Protestant indefectibility reading, voiced by Redeemed Zoomer and pressed by [[Joe Heschmeyer]] in [[The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church]], grants that the majority of the institutional church can fall into heresy at any given time *but* holds that the Holy Spirit guarantees it will never *finally* be overcome. On this view the promise is precisely that there will always be a visible, authoritative church to return to — so a total apostasy requiring restoration is exactly what the verse forecloses. Heschmeyer adds that if "hell" means "the power of death," then a 1,700-year disappearance of the institutional church *caused by* the death of the apostles is the power of death prevailing — turning the lexical point against the LDS reading.

## Counter-Objection
The steelman assumes "the church" the verse protects is the *institution*. Marvelous Wonder's first point denies this: an institution can outlive its apostolic authority, so its survival does not show the gates failed to prevail over the thing that mattered (apostolic governance). And the indefectibility reading still owes an account of the verse's afterlife vocabulary — if "hell" is *hades*, the promise reads most naturally as Christ bursting the gates of death (resurrection) and, on the LDS extension, unlocking the spirit world, not as a guarantee of unbroken institutional succession. The dispute thus reduces to whether "the church" in Matthew 16 denotes the institution or the body of believers (cf. [[Great Apostasy and Need for Restoration]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Does the existence of an institution mean that the institution isn't in error?... Of course it doesn't. The fact that the institution still exists only means that it still exists. One major puzzle piece missing from it is the presence and guidance of living apostles."[^1]

> "The word translated as hell here is Hades in Greek or Sheol in Hebrew — both terms representing the general afterlife where the spirits of all the dead go."[^2]
