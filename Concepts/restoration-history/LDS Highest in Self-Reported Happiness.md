# LDS Highest in Self-Reported Happiness

#type/claim

## The Claim
On Pew data, Latter-day Saints report the **highest percentage saying they are "very happy" with their lives (42%)** — tied with Hindus and Jews — and tie evangelicals for first on feeling "a deep sense of spiritual peace and well-being." Dyer frames this with deliberate humility rather than triumphalism.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Spiritual peace, tied #1 (Evidence: strong, Pew).** On "How often do you feel a deep sense of spiritual peace and well-being?" Latter-day Saints and evangelicals "were tied for first ... from any other religions."[^2]

2. **"Very happy with life," highest at 42% (Evidence: strong, Pew).** Among options very/somewhat/not-very happy, 42% of Latter-day Saints chose "very happy" — "the highest percentage ... though tied with Hindus and Jewish."[^3]

3. **Honest framing of the non-fit majority (Evidence: methodological integrity).** Dyer pre-empts the "doesn't match my experience" objection: "We would expect 58% of people to say ... that doesn't match me." Group trends "should not invalidate somebody's difficult experience."[^4] This candor strengthens credibility.

4. **Built-in humility via theology (Evidence: framing).** Citing Nephi's "two churches" ([[1 Nephi 14.10|1 Nephi 14:10]]) and President Oaks's reading that the "Church of the Lamb of God" includes people of all faiths who follow God, the host insists "we don't have a corner on happiness ... we were tied with Jews and Hindus."[^5] Dyer adds his work is "intended to be utilized for any religion."[^6]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** Self-reported happiness is a notoriously soft metric, vulnerable to social-desirability bias — communities that valorize gratitude, testimony, and "I know the church is true" may *report* higher happiness without higher underlying well-being. A 42% "very happy" rate means a 58% majority is *not* very happy, and the headline is a *tie*, not a clear lead. Cross-cultural happiness comparisons are confounded by response-style differences. And being tied with Hindus and Jews (very different theologies) suggests the driver may be community/practice broadly, not LDS doctrine specifically.

## Best Quotes

> "We and the evangelicals were tied for first ... on [deep sense of spiritual peace and well-being]."[^2]

> "The percentage of those who say that they're very happy with their life is 42% ... the highest percentage, though tied with Hindus and Jewish."[^3]

> "We don't have a corner on happiness or religiousness or closeness to God necessarily."[^5]
