# Both Sides Overcorrected in the LDS-Evangelical Divide

## The Claim

The mutual hostility between Latter-day Saints and creedal Christians is, in part, a story of **two-sided overcorrection**: each tradition, under pressure, "circled the wagons" and saw too little good in the other. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] (LDS) and the interviewing Evangelical pastor model the de-escalation — agreeing that both groups have reacted in un-Christlike ways, and that the goal now is to be both *distinct* and able to "sit at a table together" as fellow believers in Christ. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Latter-day Saints have at times circled the wagons too — rate: strong (a candid concession)

Muhlestein concedes that "certainly... many members of our church — and I'm sure there are still some today, and even some leaders — based on the persecution and the different things" have said in effect "we believe this, and what those other people believe is just so wrong," and "in trying to set ourselves in distinction from other beliefs... we haven't always been as willing to see the good in others." Both groups "have had some pretty strong reactions that aren't always as Christlike as we" should be. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a forthright self-critique from the LDS side, rare in apologetic exchanges.

### 2. The "lunch table" irony — rate: moderate (illuminating image)

Muhlestein names the tension: from the First Vision on, the church "push[ed] away from the lunch table" — "No, we are our own thing" — and now, years later, it can feel like "Hey, can we have a seat at the lunch table?" "Wait — you guys excused yourself a long time ago." He owns the awkwardness: "we want to be both distinct and part of the group at the same time." See [[Same Reference Different Beliefs - You Worship the Same Jesus]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a self-aware framing of the in-group/out-group dynamic, not a historical proof.

### 3. The hardest sting is being called "not Christian" — rate: strong

For Latter-day Saints, "the thing that is most difficult... is to be considered not Christian," when "in our view we are as Christian as Christian can be." The seat at the table Muhlestein wants is one where "Catholics, Orthodox Christians, Protestants, and Latter-day Saints — we all believe in Christ," however differently construed. The pastor agrees evangelicals should not say "you believe in a different Jesus and move on" without first understanding what Latter-day Saints testify and *hearing their experience*. See [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]] and [[Define Christian First and the Concession Follows]]. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — both interlocutors converge on the same irenic conclusion.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A creedal Christian can answer that "overcorrection on both sides" risks a false symmetry: the historic creeds did not *start* the breach — the Restoration did, by declaring the creeds an abomination and claiming a total apostasy, which is a categorically stronger claim than "you have some doctrine wrong." On this view, calling Latter-day Saints "not Christian" is not bigotry but a sincere boundary judgment about the nature of God and Christ. Muhlestein partly grants the asymmetry (the church "excused itself from the table"), but argues the *fruits* test and shared devotion to Christ warrant table-fellowship despite real, non-trivial doctrinal disagreement — the point is mutual understanding and charity, not papering over the differences. The pastor's own contribution (share testimonies, do not "look for the smoking gun to disqualify your faith") frames this as a discipline of listening, not a doctrinal concession.

## Best Quotes

> "In trying to set ourselves in distinction from other beliefs... we haven't always been as willing to see the good in others." [^1]

> "From the First Vision on, [we] push away from the lunch table... only to, what it feels like all these years later, be like 'Hey, can we have a seat at the lunch table?' It's like, wait — you guys excused yourselves a long time ago." [^2]

> "Rather than just looking for the smoking gun as to why I can disqualify your faith, listen to the story of what the individual has experienced." [^3]
