## The Point
One of the earliest-attested practices of Christian worship is the blessing and partaking of bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus Christ's death and Resurrection. The earliest communities did this within a common meal shared by the house-church congregation; later it was separated from the meal and became the climactic high point of a formal worship service. It carried rich cultural meaning, since in the biblical world sharing a meal implied "a sharing of one's life."

## Evidence
1. The earliest communities participated in the rite "in the context of a common meal shared by the house-church congregation" (Acts 2:42, 46; 1 Corinthians 11:17–34; Jude 1:12).
2. NT names for the ritual: the Lord's Supper (1 Corinthians 11:20), communion (1 Corinthians 10:16), the breaking of bread (Acts 2:42, 46); later, Eucharist (Greek *eucharistéō*, "giving thanks"), *mystērion* ("mystery/rite"), or Latin *sacramentum* (root of English "sacrament").
3. The communal-meal setting preserved the Last Supper's original context, where Jesus instituted the sacrament and said "Do this in remembrance of me" (1 Corinthians 11:23–26; Luke 22:19–20), and recalled the Passover.
4. Jesus's inclusive table fellowship and feeding miracles (Mark 6:30–44; 8:1–10; John 6) anticipated the joyful messianic feast (Isaiah 25:6–10; Matthew 26:29).
5. At the Church of the Multiplication (Tabgha), a fifth-century mosaic of loaves and fishes shows only four loaves — perhaps because the fifth, the real one, would be placed on the altar above the mosaic, "the living church with its living worshipers" completing the scene.

## Best Quotes
> "One of the earliest-attested practices of Christian worship is the blessing and partaking of bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus Christ's death and Resurrection." [^1]

> "Ritual can link the past and present: it can connect the living worship community to people and events of the past. It can bridge the distance between earth and heaven." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison's framing of the Eucharist as a ritual that connects worshippers to Christ and to past events, bridging earth and heaven, parallels the Latter-day Saint understanding of the sacrament as a weekly covenant renewal of remembrance.
