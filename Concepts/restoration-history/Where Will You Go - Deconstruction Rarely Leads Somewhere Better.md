# Where Will You Go - Deconstruction Rarely Leads Somewhere Better

## The Point
Deconstruction is usually framed as liberation — leaving a flawed belief system to think freely. [[Jacob Hansen]] presses the unasked question: *where will you go?* (the implicit echo of Peter's "to whom shall we go?" in [[John 6.68]]). His warning, drawn from [[Jordan Peterson]] reading [[Friedrich Nietzsche]], is that deconstruction has two very different outcomes. You can "jump from one belief system to another" — abandon one boat for a better one. But there is a darker road: a *meta-critique* in which you conclude that "protective structures themselves are not to be trusted." That is not arriving at a better boat; it is choosing the open water. And the open water, in Hansen's allegory, is not freedom but drift, exposure to every storm, and eventual drowning. The point is that abandoning faith is not automatically an upgrade; the swimmer who mocks the boats "doesn't understand the ocean," and the honest question is whether deconstruction leaves you anywhere better than where you began.

## Evidence
- Hansen quotes Peterson channeling Nietzsche directly: "imagine that you live within a belief system and then something arises to challenge the belief system. Not only does the belief system collapse but something worse happens: your belief in belief systems collapses, and that's the road [to nihilism]." [^1]
- Peterson lays out the fork: "one alternative is jump to another protective structure, fine. Another alternative is protective structures themselves are not to be trusted — bang, you're in chaos. How the hell are you going to get out of that? That's the pathway to nihilism." [^2]
- In the allegory, abandoning faith = jumping into the water: "those who abandon faith are those who are basically saying that the water is not so bad and that they enjoy swimming in the calm water. But these people don't understand the ocean — they don't understand its power, beauty, and danger." [^3]
- Even the swimmers know better when storms come: "sometimes you will see a swimmer clinging to a little bit of driftwood... when a storm comes, because even the swimmer deep down doesn't want to be tossed around and drowned." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Not only does the belief system collapse but something worse happens: your belief in belief systems collapses, and that's the road [to nihilism]... protective structures themselves are not to be trusted — bang, you're in chaos." — [[Jordan Peterson]], paraphrasing [[Friedrich Nietzsche]] [^1]

> "Those who abandon faith are those who are basically saying that the water is not so bad and that they enjoy swimming in the calm water. But these people don't understand the ocean." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The title question deliberately recalls [[John 6.68]]: when many disciples abandoned Jesus over a hard teaching, Peter answered, "Lord, to whom shall we go? thou hast the words of eternal life." The LDS frame treats deconstruction not as a destination but as a redirection question — *toward what?* Empirically, leaving rarely means landing in confident secularism; see [[Most Who Leave the LDS Church Do Not Become Atheists]]. And the deepest danger Hansen names — that one stops trusting *any* framework of meaning — is the nihilism diagnosed in [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]]. The constructive LDS answer is reconstruction: not clinging blindly to the first boat, but building a more seaworthy one, which Hansen develops in [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]].
