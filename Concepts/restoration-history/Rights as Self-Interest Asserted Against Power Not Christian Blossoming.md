# Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: the history of rights is not a "blossoming of religious tradition" but people "asserting their own self-interest" against power. Rights are won by leverage, not bestowed by doctrine.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Magna Carta as the archetype.** "There's this incompetent king the barons are upset with... they show up with their swords and say, you have to abide by these rules and be below the law. And the king, with 500 swords in his face, goes okay, fine. That's where you get the birth of rights — not a bunch of people praying and going, I think God would want us to have these rights."[^1] *(Strong, vivid — Magna Carta was indeed baronial self-protection, not a theological treatise.)*

2. **The Reformation and American religious liberty as conflict-driven.** Protestants asserted freedom of worship because "their own interests weren't being served by the church"; American church-state protections protected churches *from each other* and from the state — "an assertion of self-interest," not a flourishing of doctrine. See [[Freedom of Speech and Religion Emerged From Conflict Not Doctrine]].[^2] *(Plausible — captures the realpolitik of toleration.)*

3. **Haitian revolution: oppressed people seize opportunity.** "The only successful slave revolt... is Haiti. I do not think Haitians were okay with slavery and then read the Bible and decided to rise up." See [[Abolition Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Conviction]].[^3] *(Strong example of self-interest over doctrine.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener — rights talk has a Christian genealogy.** Scrivener replies that when reformers, barons, and revolutionaries *say "rights,"* "they are showing us the intellectual genealogy, and it runs right through the heart of Christendom" — back to [[Rights Talk Traces to Medieval Canon Lawyers|11th-12th century canon lawyers]] who invented the idea that "the poor person has a *right* to sustenance."[^4] He distinguishes the *conceptual vocabulary* (Christian) from the *occasion* of its assertion (self-interest). *(Genuinely strong — O'Connor's examples explain *why people pushed* but not *why the conceptual category of "rights" existed to push with*. Scrivener concedes the messiness — "five steps forward, four steps back, bloody mixed motives, technology playing a part.")*

## Best Quotes

> "It's not a bunch of people sat around praying and going, you know what, I think God would want us to have these rights."[^1]

> "I think those rights are an assertion of self-interest. They're not a flourishing of religious tradition."[^5]

> "When they say rights, they are showing us the intellectual genealogy, and it runs right through the heart of Christendom." — Scrivener[^4]
