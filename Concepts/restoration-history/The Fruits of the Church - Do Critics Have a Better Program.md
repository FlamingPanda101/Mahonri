# The Fruits of the Church - Do Critics Have a Better Program

## The Point
Critics often grant nothing to the Church and claim that "any good in Mormonism is available outside of Mormonism." Fife answers with a "by their fruits" argument grounded in social-science data: religious practice generally — and active Latter-day Saint practice in particular — correlates with markedly better mental health, physical health, longevity, marital stability, charity, and well-being. His recurring challenge to critics: if you want me to leave, where is the *program* that delivers comparable results? "Where is it? How do I join? Does it work?"

## Evidence
- **Religion and health broadly.** Fife marshals VanderWeele (Harvard: regular service attendance linked to ~30% lower mortality over 16 years, a five-fold lower suicide rate, ~30% lower incidence of depression), Koenig (Duke), Andrew Sims (former president of the UK Royal College of Psychiatrists, who lists ~19 outcomes religion improves), Arthur Brooks, Rodney Stark, and the Heritage Foundation's fifteen benefits of religious practice [^1].
- **Latter-day Saints specifically.** Pew's 2014 Religious Landscape Survey ranks Latter-day Saints highest in religiosity (≈69% high religious commitment vs. 30% of the U.S. public); active LDS men in Utah live ~8 years longer and active LDS women ~6 years longer than their less-active and non-LDS counterparts; LDS divorce rates are the lowest or among the lowest across studies from 1985-2024; LDS report among the highest life satisfaction and "spiritual peace" of any group surveyed [^2].
- **The suicide/antidepressant myth.** Critics cite Utah's above-average antidepressant use and suicide rate as proof the Church harms members. Fife counters that Utah County (the most heavily LDS county) ranks *lowest* in Utah for antidepressant use, that Idaho's large LDS population is below average, and that elevation, gun access, rurality, the happiness/suicide paradox, and out-of-state treatment/"troubled-teen" facilities better explain the numbers — citing suicide-prevention coordinator Dr. Michael Staley that there is "no data" tying Utah youth suicide to LDS–LGBT friction [^3]. (Treated in full at [[Utah Suicide Rate Implicates the Church]].)

## Best Quotes
> "Do the critics have a better program than the Church to support well-being? Where is it? How do I join? Does it work?" [^1]

> "When comparisons are made, Latter-day Saints are typically found to have better mental health than those of other religions or no religion." — Dyer, Judd, Gale & Finlinson (2023 review of 46 studies), quoted by Fife [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the "fruits" wing of Fife's apologetic, complementing the empirical-flourishing notes ([[A Science of Human Flourishing]], [[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]], [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]]) and directly rebutting the [[Utah Suicide Rate Implicates the Church]] objection. The argument is Matthew 7 in social-science form: the [[Latter-day Saints|Latter-day Saint]] "program" demonstrably produces good fruit.
