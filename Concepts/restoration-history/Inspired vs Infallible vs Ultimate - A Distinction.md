# Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate — A Distinction

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A pivotal definitional move by Hayden: a text can be **inspired, trustworthy, and authoritative** *without* being **infallible** (incapable of error) or **ultimate** (the apex authority by which all others are judged). Conflating these is the affirmative's core mistake.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Three separable properties — rate: strong, clarifying
- **Inspired / reliable / authoritative** — Hayden grants all of these for whatever God deems scripture.[^2]
- **Infallible** — "incapable of error in what it teaches," an "extraordinary claim" requiring "extraordinary justification," especially for a text existing "only in copies, translations, and additions."[^3]
- **Ultimate** — "not merely one authority amongst others... the authority by which all other authorities are evaluated, corrected, or overruled," sitting atop the hierarchy with "the final say when conflicts arise."[^4]

**Assessment:** the taxonomy is analytically clean and is the scaffolding of Hayden's whole case — most of Kelly's evidence (Jesus quoting scripture, fathers citing it, LDS apostles praising it) establishes only *inspired/authoritative*, leaving the upper two rungs unproven.

### 2. The moderator's own definitions reinforce it — rate: framing
Moderator Chris pre-defined the terms: *infallible* = "not capable of error"; *ultimate authority* = "all other authorities are inferior and must comply with, submit to, and be judged by this standard."[^5] Hayden simply holds Kelly to these agreed definitions. **Steelman of Kelly:** Protestant theology *does* distinguish these and affirms all three deliberately (inerrancy + material/formal sufficiency); Kelly's failure is execution, not that the categories are confused in principle. A sharper affirmative could argue θεόπνευστος entails inerrancy and Christ's "scripture cannot be broken" (John 10:35) entails infallibility.

### 3. Why the distinction matters to the LDS position — rate: framing
By conceding inspiration/authority while denying infallibility/ultimacy, Hayden models the LDS stance: the Bible is true scripture and is loved, but it is *one* authority alongside living prophets and additional scripture — not a self-sufficient, error-free supreme court. The distinction is the doorway to an *open* canon.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "A text can be inspired, trustworthy, and even authoritative without being infallible or ultimate."[^1]

> "To say that a text is infallible is not merely to say that it is generally reliable or divinely influenced. It is to say that it is incapable of error in what it teaches. That's an extraordinary claim."[^3]

> "An ultimate authority is not merely one authority amongst others. It is the authority by which all other authorities are evaluated, corrected, or overruled."[^4]
