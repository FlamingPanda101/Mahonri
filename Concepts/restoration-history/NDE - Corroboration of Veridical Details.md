# NDE - Corroboration of Veridical Details

## The Point
Moody asks whether NDEs can be checked **independently** of the experiencer's own report — and finds that, in several cases, they can.[^1] People out of their bodies describe events in the physical world (resuscitation procedures, conversations, who was present, what they wore) that later prove accurate, sometimes baffling the very doctors who knew the patient to be "dead."[^2] Moody is methodologically cautious — he does not claim this "proves" survival — but the veridical, later-confirmed details are exactly the kind of evidence that tells against pure hallucination.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Doctors baffled by accurate resuscitation accounts.** "Several doctors have told me... that they are utterly baffled about how patients with no medical knowledge could describe in such detail and so correctly the procedure used in resuscitation attempts, even though these events took place while the doctors knew the patients involved to be 'dead.'"[^2]
2. **A sister's words, overheard from another room.** A dying girl, Kathy, left her body and went "into another room in the hospital where she found her older sister crying and saying, 'Oh, Kathy, please don't die.'" The sister "was quite baffled when, later, Kathy told her exactly where she had been and what she had been saying."[^4]
3. **The whole sequence retold to a disbelieving doctor.** "I told him the whole story, from the time I stopped breathing until the time I was kind of coming around. He was really shocked to know that I knew everything that had happened."[^5]
4. **Confirmed external details.** A patient told his father "who had dragged my body out of the building, and even what color clothes that person had on... and even about all the conversation that had been going on," and the father confirmed "these things were true" — though "my body was physically out this whole time, and there was no way I could have seen or heard these things without being outside of my body."[^6]
5. **"Jack" names the unseen bed.** Before surgery, the being of light had shown Jack the recovery-room bed he would occupy; afterward he correctly told the astonished anesthesiologist, "I'm in that first bed on the right just as you come in from the hall."[^7]
6. **Moody's honest caveat.** He notes corroborating events are often attested only by the dying person and a few intimates, he has promised anonymity, and even dramatic after-the-fact stories would not "constitute proof."[^8]

## LDS Connection
Corroborated, veridical perceptions during clinical death support the proposition at the heart of the plan of salvation: that **the spirit can act and perceive apart from the mortal body**, and therefore survives its death. This coheres with the Restoration's teaching of a conscious spirit that, at death, departs the body intact in identity and awareness (Alma 40:11; cf. [[Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection]]). Latter-day Saints can welcome such evidence while sharing Moody's epistemic humility: like NDE survivors who relate their experience but "don't proselytize," the LDS position is that spiritual realities are finally confirmed by witness and the Spirit, not coerced by proof. See [[NDE - Reluctance to Tell Others]] and [[NDE - The Being of Light]].
