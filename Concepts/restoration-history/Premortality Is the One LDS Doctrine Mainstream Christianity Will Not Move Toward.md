# Premortality Is the One LDS Doctrine Mainstream Christianity Will Not Move Toward

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] observes an "irrefutable convergence of Christianity on so many of these themes" once considered uniquely Mormon — universalism, the possible embodiment of God, the passibility of God — but singles out **premortal existence** as the lone exception: "It's only with the premortality that I haven't seen any movement. And I've never been able to understand why that is so deeply problematic for Christians, that even the most progressive and open-minded just won't go there." He frames the broader convergence with [[David Paulsen]]'s essay title *Are Christians Mormon?*, which he says he is "in some ways" trying to expand into a book.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The convergence is broad but premortality-shaped-hole — rate: an honest asymmetry.** Givens lists the movements he *has* seen: "I see this with universalism, with the possible embodiment of God... with the passibility of God. It's only with the premortality that I haven't seen any movement." This sharpens the vault's general convergence claim ([[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]]) by naming its limit.[^2]
2. **It is not for lack of precedent — rate: accurate.** Premortality is not novel to Joseph Smith: [[Origen]] taught a form of it (see [[Origen on the Preexistence and Fall of Souls]]), and the vault documents scattered modern echoes ([[Pre-Existence in Mainstream Christian Thought]], including [[David Bentley Hart]]). Yet the *living* mainstream, Givens notes, resists it as it does not resist embodiment or passibility.[^2]
3. **The anecdote of the Methodist dean — rate: illustrative of the wider pattern.** Givens recounts celebrating Moses 7's weeping God at an interfaith conference; a "dean of theology" claimed "Methodists were there ages before Joseph Smith," to which Givens replied, "are you familiar with your creeds?" (the Methodist creed has God "without body, parts, or passions"). The dean conceded "we don't really pay attention to those creeds" — Givens's evidence that mainstream practice has quietly moved "beyond those creedal limitations" on *passibility and embodiment*, even where premortality stays frozen.[^3]
4. **A theory of the resistance — rate: speculative.** Givens does not fully explain *why* premortality is uniquely off-limits; he confesses he has "never been able to understand" it. The implicit candidate is that premortality most directly threatens [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference|creation ex nihilo]] and the Creator/creature boundary — to have existed before this life is to be, in some sense, uncreated, which the other convergent doctrines do not require.[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing (mainstream Christian) side:** The resistance is principled, not arbitrary. Premortality collides with creatio ex nihilo and the doctrine that each soul is a fresh divine creation (traducianism/creationism — see the vault's [[Origins of the Soul - Traducianism vs Creationism vs Preexistence]]); it imports a quasi-Platonic pre-fall of souls (the very Origenist teaching the church *condemned*); and it strains the goodness of embodiment (if souls pre-exist, the body looks like a demotion). Convergence on passibility or divine embodiment can be absorbed as *recoveries* of biblical emphasis, whereas premortality requires surrendering the Creator/creature asymmetry that anchors classical theism — so the "no movement" is the system protecting its foundation, not mere prejudice.

**Counter-objection (Givens's rejoinder):** That the resistance clusters exactly where ex nihilo is at stake is precisely Givens's point — it shows the real load-bearing dogma is not biblical revelation but the philosophical commitment to ex-nihilo sovereignty (see [[Sovereignty Was Privileged Over Love and Creation Ex Nihilo Drove It]]). The church already houses premortality in its own history (Origen) and Scripture is at least open to it; the selective freeze betrays that the boundary is being defended *as* a boundary, not because the texts forbid it.
