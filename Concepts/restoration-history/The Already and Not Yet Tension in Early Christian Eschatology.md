# The Already and Not Yet Tension in Early Christian Eschatology

## The Point

[[Nicholas J. Frederick]] frames the entire problem of early Christian eschatology as a tension between the **"already"** and the **"not yet."** [^1] Jehovah had already descended and established his kingdom — Jesus had come, died, and risen — but the kingdom "was somehow not yet fully realized." [^1] Paul's apostolic task was to "situate the new world in which the Christians lived in a way that made sense of the lived experience of a political and social world in which nothing really seemed to have changed." [^1] Frederick notes this is "normal for scholars of the New Testament," citing G. K. Beale's D-day / V-day analogy: the first coming of Christ was D-day (the decisive defeat of the enemy), and the second coming is V-day (the enemy's final surrender). [^2]

## Evidence

1. **Paul lived the tension personally.** Describing the order of the resurrection to the Thessalonians, Paul hoped to be among "we who are alive, who are left" who "will be caught up in the clouds" — apparently expecting to witness the return himself (see [[1 Thessalonians 4.16-18|1 Thessalonians 4:15-17]]). [^3]
2. **Paul both affirmed and deferred the End.** He told the Thessalonians the Second Coming was certain but two things had to happen first: a "rebellion" (Greek *apostasia*) and the revelation of "the man of sin" (2 Thessalonians 2:3) — yet even these were "already at work" (2 Thessalonians 2:7), the already/not-yet in a single passage. [^3]
3. **The spirit secures the "already."** For Paul the temporary physical absence of Jesus was not cause for concern: God had given his Spirit to believers, who already "have a part in God's new kingdom" (see Ephesians 1:13-14), one in which "there is neither Jew nor Greek... bond nor free... male nor female" (Galatians 3:28). [^3]
4. **The resolution differed by century.** The unresolved tension drove the three later millennial positions (premillennialism, postmillennialism, amillennialism), each "reacting in some way to the anxiety of Jesus's prolonged absence." [^4] Augustine's eventual amillennial settlement is described as "the 'already' finally outlasting the 'not yet.'" [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Many early Christians found themselves caught up in this tension between already and not yet." [^1]

> "Christians live between D-day and V-day. D-day was the first coming of Christ, when the opponent was defeated decisively; V-day is the final coming of Christ, when the adversary will finally and completely surrender." — G. K. Beale, quoted by Frederick [^2]

## LDS Connection

Frederick notes that 2 Thessalonians 2:2-7 — the apostasia passage at the heart of this tension — has "a long interpretive history in our own faith tradition," where it grounds the Latter-day Saint doctrine of a Great Apostasy preceding the Restoration. [^6]
