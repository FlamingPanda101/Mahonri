# Most Early Christians Believed in an Intermediate State Between Death and Resurrection

## The Point
[[D. Jill Kirby]] establishes that "most ancient Christians believed that humans, with the exception of the martyrs, passed to some sort of 'intermediate' experience between their death and their resurrection." Because scripture on this state was "extremely limited," early Christians "used reason to extend the biblical insights of faith" — producing details that sometimes differ from modern revelation but that share a common testimony of continued spiritual progression governed by God's justice-through-mercy.

## Evidence
1. **The unresolved questions.** Where did Christ preach (1 Peter 3:18–20)? What expiation is possible after death ([[Matthew 12.32|Matthew 12:32]])? Martyrs went straight to heaven ([[Revelation 6.9-11|Rev 6:9]]) — "but what about the righteous who were not killed for their testimony?" ([[Luke 16.22|Luke 16:22]], the bosom of Abraham) [^1].
2. **Reason supplementing scripture.** Early Christians "did not invent their responses … They studied scripture, they pondered vigorously in private and in community, and they prayed," grounding answers in hermeneutics and logic [^2].
3. **Martyrs as the exception.** Only martyrs (and "exceptionally holy people") were "prepared to face the consuming fire of God at the moment of their death"; the rest passed through an intermediate experience [^3].
4. **Justice through mercy.** Kirby's "Between Death and Resurrection" box: in death as in life "God's justice is worked out through his mercy as we continue our spiritual labors and progression after our demise, until we are finally prepared to meet God" [^4].
5. **The condition.** "The state of souls after death follows from their orientation while they lived" — "this life is the time … to prepare to meet God" ([[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:32]]) — "even if that preparation is not completed before death" [^4].
6. **Why revelation is limited.** "Our lack of revelation regarding the afterlife reflects the wisdom that wills us to be 'anxiously engaged' in this life rather than experts in the next" (D&C 58:27–28) [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "Most ancient Christians believed that humans, with the exception of the martyrs, passed to some sort of 'intermediate' experience between their death and their resurrection." [^3]

> "In death as in life, God's justice is worked out through his mercy as we continue our spiritual labors and progression after our demise, until we are finally prepared to meet God." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Kirby draws the connections explicitly, citing D&C 138 throughout: Christ is the central figure whose Atonement is "the key to spiritual progression" (D&C 138:35); progression continues after death (D&C 138:25–34, 50–52); the state follows from mortal orientation ([[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:32]]; D&C 138:20–22). The widespread early-Christian belief in a *postmortem intermediate state of continuing progression* is a strong historical antecedent to the LDS **spirit world** and dissolves the binary heaven/hell assumption (see [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]]).
