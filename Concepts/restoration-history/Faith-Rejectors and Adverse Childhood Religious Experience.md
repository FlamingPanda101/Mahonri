# Faith-Rejectors and Adverse Childhood Religious Experience

## The Claim
The roughly 10% of leavers who fully reject religion — rating it harmful and saying they "don't believe anymore" — are distinguished not only by stronger liberal political leanings but by a markedly higher rate of adverse childhood experiences, including abuse occurring within a religious setting.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Profile of the faith-rejectors (rated: strong).** They are "the lowest you can be in terms of rating spiritual and religion as important," think religion is harmful and has no place in society, and the primary stated reason for leaving is "because they don't believe anymore" — about 10% of the sample, "like zero on thinking religion is a good thing."[^1]

2. **Liberal-leaning but with a nuance on extremity (rated: moderate).** Faith-rejectors skew "far more politically liberal." Dyer adds nuance to the U-shaped curve: while the *very* conservative leave more than the merely conservative, those with liberal leanings are more likely to leave overall than the very conservative. See [[Political Identity Now Supersedes Religious Identity]].[^2]

3. **Adverse childhood experiences, including religious abuse (rated: strong).** Using measures of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) — "all kinds of things such as abuse" — the team could examine abuse "within a religious sphere," finding faith-rejectors "more likely to have grown up and experienced some abuse within a religious sphere."[^3] *Steelman:* Dyer explicitly refuses to caricature: "I don't want to paint people who are in that category as all having experienced that" — it is an elevated probability, not a universal cause, and the warmly framed analysis should not be read as dismissing the legitimacy of abuse-driven departure.[^4]

4. **Symmetry with positive-experience findings (rated: strong).** The mirror image — positive childhood religious experience strongly predicting adult retention — appears in recent Pew findings and reinforces the protective-versus-harmful framing. See [[Positive Childhood Religious Experience Predicts Adult Retention]].[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "They are the lowest you can be in terms of rating spiritual and religion as important... one of the primary reasons they say that they left is because they don't believe anymore."[^1]

> "These are individuals who are more likely to have grown up and experienced some abuse within a religious sphere... you can fully understand why a person might be coming from that position."[^3]

> "I don't want to paint people who are in that category as all having experienced that, but they were just more likely to have had that kind of experience."[^4]
