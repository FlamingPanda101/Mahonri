# Why There Can Be Only One Subsistent Existence Itself

## The Point
Feser argues that if there is anything whose essence and existence are *not* distinct — anything that "just is existence itself" — then "there cannot in principle be more than one such thing." [^1] This uniqueness claim does double duty: it is one of the three arguments for the real distinction in ordinary things (since ordinary kinds have many members, they cannot be Existence Itself), and it later grounds the identification of Subsistent Existence Itself with the one God who is the cause of everything else. [^2]

## Evidence
Suppose two things, A and B, each just *is* existence itself. For them to be two rather than one, something must differentiate them. There are only two ways things are ever differentiated: [^3]
1. **As two species of one genus** (e.g., rational animal vs. some other animal). But then A would be "existence itself PLUS" some specific difference — and so would *not* be existence itself, but existence-itself-plus-something. [^4]
2. **As two members of one species**, which are individuated "by virtue of being associated with different parcels of matter." [^5] But then A would be "existence itself PLUS this particular bit of matter" — again not existence itself, but existence-plus-matter. [^6]

In general, any differentiator added to "existence itself" makes that instance *not* be merely existence itself after all. [^7] Feser also blocks a "middle" escape — that existence might be one *part* of a thing's essence alongside others. On analysis, if existence is the part on which the whole thing depends, then existence turns out to be the true essence, collapsing back into "essence just is existence itself." [^8] Hence the things of our experience, which come in pluralities, cannot be Existence Itself, and so in each of them essence and existence are really distinct. [^9] Whatever is Subsistent Existence Itself is therefore one of a kind — and, since everything else depends on it, "the cause of everything other than itself." [^10]

## Best Quotes
> "If there is something whose essence and existence are not really distinct ... then there cannot in principle be more than one such thing." [^1]

> "If A has such a 'specific difference', then it will not be that which just is existence itself; rather, it will be that which just is existence itself PLUS that specific difference." [^4]

> "There really is no sense to be made of there being more than one of something which just is existence itself." [^7]

## LDS Connection
As an argument *against atheism*, the uniqueness reasoning shows that the ground of all contingent being cannot be just one more item in the inventory of the world — a real check on materialist "brute fact" cosmologies. The sharp LDS divergence is the conclusion that there can be only **one** being at the deepest level. The Restoration affirms a plurality of divine persons and a real distinction of persons in the Godhead, and reads scripture's *elohim* language as permitting genuine plurality of divine beings (see [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]] and [[Personages vs Subsistent Relations - What Person Means]]). Feser's uniqueness proof works only on the prior assumption that the first principle is *Ipsum Esse* — pure, undifferentiated existence with no positive nature to be shared or instanced. An LDS metaphysic that takes the divine to be a fully actual *personal being* (not abstract Existence Itself) is not bound by the "existence-plus-a-difference" dilemma, because divine persons are differentiated as persons, not as instances of bare esse. The argument is formidable on its own premises; those premises — divine simplicity and *Ipsum Esse* — are exactly what the Restoration declines (see [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]] and [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]).
