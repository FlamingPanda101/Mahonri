# Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The most common assumption about plural marriage is that men "were primarily motivated to enter polygamy for sexual gratification" — more wives meaning more sex — so men were uniformly "all in on the polygamy train."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The records repeatedly show reluctant men
Historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] reports being "still surprised every time I found records of men just dragging their feet, not wanting to participate in polygamy, which was very real." Sexual motivation "may have been true for some," but it is one story among many, not the rule.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (primary-source diaries and Nash's career-long research; she flags her own prior assumption being overturned).

### 2. Polygamy was a burden, not a perk
"There was a lot of responsibility that came with polygamy." The ideal required a man already "set financially" to provide a home for each wife, to be "looked to as the head of the household" resolving disputes, and to "satisfy all of those different emotional and spiritual needs from both their wives and children." "Simple lust would not make a marriage succeed."[^3] See [[Sister-Wife Solidarity Was a Lived Support System]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the responsibilities are well documented; the structural point is sound).

### 3. The cost-benefit argument
"If sexual gratification was the goal, there are a lot easier ways to achieve that goal than to get married and have a whole additional family to support financially and emotionally." A surviving member's letter to a non-member aunt makes exactly this point: "there are much easier ways to have affairs than to... make a lifetime commitment."[^4] This is the polygamy-specific form of the argument that the practice was costly, not indulgent — compare [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem]] and [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (a strong abductive argument; it shows the sex motive is a poor *general* explanation, not that no man was so motivated).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can grant that responsibility tempered lust yet insist the system was still *structured* to give men sexual and reproductive access to multiple women that women did not get to men, that some men (and some leaders) clearly did desire additional young wives, and that "it was hard work" does not refute "it was also about sex." The honest reply concedes sexual motivation existed in particular cases (Nash says so) while maintaining that the *theology-was-invented-for-sex* thesis fails: it cannot explain the foot-dragging, the financial and emotional burden, or the easier alternatives, and it flattens a documented diversity of motives into a single caricature.

## Best Quotes
> "How much I carried that assumption even after years of researching the topic, and was still surprised every time I found records of men just dragging their feet, not wanting to participate in polygamy."[^2]

> "To invent a theology just to entitle a man to more sexual partners just doesn't fully make sense to me, at least."[^4]
