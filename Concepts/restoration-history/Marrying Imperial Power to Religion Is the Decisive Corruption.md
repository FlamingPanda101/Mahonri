# Marrying Imperial Power to Religion Is the Decisive Corruption

## The Claim
The fully-formed Great Apostasy is located not primarily in any single doctrinal error (e.g. the wording at [[Council of Nicaea|Nicaea]]) but in the **fusion of imperial political power with Christianity**: "once you've married imperial power to religion, now all manner of corruption can take place." Day attributes this emphasis to Joseph Fielding Smith — that by the post-Nicene councils the apostasy is "fully there," and the reason "isn't necessarily on the doctrine... but that we've married imperial power with religion."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Joseph Fielding Smith's dating of the completed apostasy (rating: moderate; LDS authority)
Day recalls (attributing it to Joseph Fielding Smith rather than Bruce R. McConkie) the view that "by the time we're at Nicaea the apostasy is fully there" — and crucially that its completion is marked by the church-state marriage, not merely by creedal metaphysics. [^1]

### 2. Philip Jenkins, *Jesus Wars* — leaders worse than the Sopranos (rating: moderate; cited historian)
Day is reading [[Philip Jenkins]]'s *Jesus Wars* and quotes its claim that once imperial power married Christianity, "the leaders of Christianity make the Sopranos look weak" — i.e. they began "killing people because you believe the wrong thing." The point: post-Constantinian doctrinal disputes were settled with state violence. [^2]

### 3. The contrast that reframes modern polemics (rating: moderate; rhetorical)
Day's takeaway: "when somebody on the internet says you're not Christian, you're worshiping a different Jesus, that is light stuff compared to what these guys did" — modern boundary-policing is a pale echo of an era when heresy was lethal. [^2]

### 4. A recurring pattern of suppressing dissent (rating: moderate; historical generalization)
The empowered church "disregard[s] who knows how many books and create[s] their own canon," and over the centuries keeps meeting dissenting Christian groups — "whether it's the Gnostics or the Bogomils or the Cathars" — with "you disagree, we're going to kill you." Persecuted plurality, not unity, is the through-line. See [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]]. [^3]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional Christian (or secular historian) can grant that Christendom committed real violence while denying it constitutes an *apostasy*: Constantine's patronage also preserved scripture, funded councils that clarified orthodoxy, and ended persecution *of* Christians. Coercion and corruption by individual rulers and bishops, on this view, are sins within a church that nonetheless retained valid doctrine and sacraments — the "gates of hell" not prevailing. Jenkins himself narrates the violence as intra-Christian power politics, not proof the faith itself was lost. Day's reply is the restorationist one: a church that needs the sword to enforce belief has already lost the Spirit that should bind it.
