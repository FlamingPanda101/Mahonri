# Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues that the divine name **Shaddai** (KJV "the Almighty") is best understood as the "**breasted God**" — a *feminine* divinity — and therefore preserves the **Mother in Heaven** inside the Hebrew Bible. The argument runs through (a) the Hebrew word *shad* = "breast," (b) the fertility blessing Shaddai gives in [[Genesis 49.25|Genesis 49:25]] ("blessings of the **breasts** and of the **womb**"), read as a deliberate pun, and (c) [[Mark Smith]]'s identification of Shaddai with **Asherah**, who carries the very same Ugaritic title "breasts and womb."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Shad* means "breast"; *Shaddai* looks like "the Breasted One" (rating: moderate; etymology contested)
Dictionaries list several uncertain meanings for *Shaddai* — "destroyer" (*shadad*, to inflict divine punishment), "God of the mountains" (Akkadian *shaddu* = mountain), "God of the fields" (*sade* = field). Butler notes Hebrew *shin*/*sin* share a letter, and that ***shad* = breast** (Akkadian *shaddu* also means breast). A *yod* suffix functions like a gentilic/"the one of," so *Shaddai* "looks like it means **breasted** — the one of the breasts." [^1] [[Frank Moore Cross]] held that *breast* is actually the **primary** meaning of the root — Babylonians looked at mountains and said "those look like breasts," not the reverse. [^2]

### 2. The blessing of Shaddai is a fertility/mother blessing — and a pun (rating: moderate)
In Genesis 49:25 Shaddai blesses Joseph "with... blessings of the **breasts** (*Shaddayim*) and of the **womb** (*Rechem*)." Butler: reading Shaddai as "the destroyer" here is absurd — "You shall be blessed by the destroyer... blessings of the breasts and of the womb" makes no sense — whereas it is a perfect **pun**: *Shaddai* blesses with the blessings of *Shaddayim*. The maternal, fertility content fits a mother figure. [^3] (Hebrew has a dual number ending *-ayim* for natural pairs — *yadayim* "two hands," *raglayim* "two legs" — so *Shaddayim* = "two breasts.") [^4]

### 3. Mark Smith equates Shaddai with Asherah via a shared title (rating: moderate; cites peer-reviewed scholar)
Ugaritologist **[[Mark Smith]]**, in *The Early History of God*, argues that pre-monarchical Israel had "a mother and a father and a son," and that Shaddai points to the **mother**: at Ugarit one of **Asherah's** titles is precisely "**breasts and womb**," the same blessing Shaddai gives. Smith concludes (without leaning on the etymology) that "Shaddai seems to be Asherah, because Shaddai is given the same title as Asherah at Ugarit." [^5] This dovetails with [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]].

### 4. Coheres with a feminine-looking partner-name in Job (rating: weak-moderate; supporting)
Butler observes that in Job, *Shaddai* (used ~30 of its 48 OT occurrences there) stands in repeated parallelism with **Eloah**, "a feminine-looking name of God" — another thread suggesting feminine divine vocabulary survived in the text. [^6] The Mother-recovery program here complements [[Wisdom Personified as the Mother in Heaven]].

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Most lexicographers do **not** accept "breasted God" as the meaning of *Shaddai*; leading proposals remain "(God of the) mountain/steppe" (from *šadû/šadday*) or "the Almighty/overpowerer." The Genesis 49:25 *Shaddai*/*shadayim* link is a **paronomasia** (sound-play) that the ancient writer may have enjoyed without believing it was the word's etymology — Butler himself grants the writer "was not an etymologist." And the Asherah identification is Smith's reconstruction of *pre-canonical* religion, not a claim about the deity the final editors worshipped; classical theists read *El Shaddai* throughout as a masculine epithet of the one God ([[Exodus 6.3|Exodus 6:3]]). Butler's reply: the *combined* weight — *shad* = breast, the breasts-and-womb blessing, the Eloah parallel, and Smith's Asherah title — makes the feminine reading the better explanation of the data.

### 6. Independent corroboration from Margaret Barker (rating: strengthens; non-LDS scholar, same claim)
In a separate on-camera interview, [[Margaret Barker]] makes the **same core argument independently**: *shaddai* derives from *shad* (breast), so *El Shaddai* is most naturally "**the God with breasts**," and the KJV "God Almighty" of [[Exodus 6.3|Exodus 6:3]] is "not an accurate translation" but part of a program to "bury the lady." Barker ties this to the physical evidence she works from — the thousands of breasted [[The Judean Pillar Figurines Were the Smashed Mother in the First Temple|Judean pillar figurines]] ("we found hundreds of them"). [^7] That a non-LDS biblical scholar reaches the breasted-God reading from a different evidentiary base (archaeology + her temple-theology reconstruction) adds weight beyond Butler's etymological case. See [[The Judean Pillar Figurines Were the Smashed Mother in the First Temple]] and [[Deborah and Asenath Preserve the Lady of the Bush]].
