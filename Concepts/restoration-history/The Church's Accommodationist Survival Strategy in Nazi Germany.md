# The Church's Accommodationist Survival Strategy in Nazi Germany

## The Claim

The official Latter-day Saint response to the Nazi regime — from [[Heber J. Grant]] down through mission presidents like [[Roy Welker]] — was **accommodationism**: recognizing and obeying the regime's laws and coexisting with it, while *neither* endorsing *nor* opposing it. [[Stephen Smoot]] argues from the dossier that this was a deliberate **survival strategy** to protect the Church and its members under a totalitarian regime — not cowardice and not secret sympathy — and that, whatever its discomforts, it demonstrably worked: the Church was never banned, unlike the Jehovah's Witnesses.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The three options framework.** A religious minority in 1933 had three paths: (a) **open resistance** (Jehovah's Witnesses → concentration camps); (b) **enthusiastic conformity** (Ludwig Müller's pro-Nazi "Reich Church," which "will cost you your soul as a Christian"); or (c) **accommodation** — submit to the regime's laws, carve out a neutral space, neither endorse nor oppose. Church leaders chose the third.[^2]

2. **Grounded in LDS scripture.** Leaders justified the posture from the [[12th Article of Faith]] ("we believe in being subject to kings, presidents, rulers, and magistrates, in obeying, honoring, and sustaining the law") and from [[Doctrine and Covenants 134]] (Oliver Cowdery's 1835 statement on government). When Nazi officials asked local leaders their view of the government, the 12th Article of Faith was the "go-to response."[^3]

3. **It was a response to explicit threat, not cowardice.** The dossier shows the Gestapo summoning Welker on 30 April 1936 and warning that "in the event of anti-state propaganda activities, the strictest state police measures would be carried out against the sect." Days later Welker ordered missionaries to avoid all politics — and sent the Gestapo a copy. Smoot argues the old "feckless cowards" reading collapses against documented coercion: "maybe it's because they have been overtly threatened by the Gestapo."[^4]

4. **Accommodation did not mean endorsement.** The First Presidency and general authorities openly condemned the *ideology*: John A. Widtsoe (1936) called any ideology denying free agency incompatible with the faith; Levi Edgar Young called the persecution of Jews "deplorable"; the October 1939 First Presidency statement condemned "war and all of its foul brood"; and a 1943 general-conference statement named Nazism and communism as incompatible with the gospel, warning of divine punishment. The Nazis themselves noticed the Church never gave an official endorsement. See [[The Nazis Deemed Latter-day Saint Teachings Ideologically Incompatible with National Socialism]].[^5]

5. **Why the Church was never banned.** Smoot gives two reasons: the accommodationist posture kept a target off the Church's back, and the Church was small (~8,000 members in 1933, ~13,000 by 1939). A memorandum for the Ribbentrop bureau says the regime was "not concerned with the smaller sects like the Mormons" and would target larger groups first — with the ominous implication ("you Mormons are next," per a Gestapo interrogator) that the small size only deferred the danger.[^6]

6. **Honest limits / steelman (load-bearing).** Smoot does not whitewash. He concedes some leaders' overtures were "very cringey" and "uncomfortable" to read post-1945 (e.g., Alfred C. Rees's 1939 *Völkischer Beobachter* article drawing rosy parallels between the Saints and the Nazis). He grants that individual Saints made genuinely wrong choices (the pro-Nazi branch president [[Arthur Zander]]), and that the policy is fairly criticized by some. His twofold answer: historically the strategy "worked"; theologically, as a believer, he holds the Lord guided the Saints through an impossible situation "whatever their imperfections." He also warns against "Monday morning quarterbacking" from 80 years of safety, citing [[Otto Berndt]]'s charitable post-war verdict on fellow members ("like [[Saul of Tarsus]] in persecuting the Christians").[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "We will recognize the regime, we will submit ourselves to the laws... we will coexist with the regime, but we will not officially either endorse or oppose the regime." [^2]

> "Maybe it's because they have been overtly threatened by the Gestapo that if they do discuss politics and do any perceived anti-state propaganda activities, basically they're going to be thrown in concentration camps." [^4]

> "Even if the church takes officially an accommodationist stance towards totalitarian regimes like the Nazi party, it is officially condemning the ideology of those regimes." [^5]
