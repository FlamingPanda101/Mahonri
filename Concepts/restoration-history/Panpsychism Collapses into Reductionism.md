# Panpsychism Collapses into Reductionism

## The Point
Panpsychism — the view that consciousness is inherent in all matter — is the most tempting "third option" against the cosmic conscious argument's premise 1 (mind is *either* personal *or* natural). [[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]] argues it is no escape: it is "incomplete," because "if consciousness is in all material things we still need an explanation of consciousness and why we are conscious."[^1] Saying everything material is conscious does not explain why *our unified* consciousness exists, so panpsychism still leaves only two real options — reduced to smaller parts, or grounded in a personal source.[^2]

## Evidence
Jones presses two distinct problems:
1. **The combination problem (William James).** If "every atom has its own consciousness and these micro levels of consciousness combine to form our consciousness, that would still be a reductionist view and suffer from the same problems of how conscious experience can be divided into smaller parts."[^3] So most panpsychism is just reductionism with conscious bricks instead of physical ones — and thus falls to the same objection as materialism (cf. [[Consciousness Is Fundamental and Irreducible]]).
2. **Panpsychism is realism, not idealism (Charles Hartshorne).** Jones quotes Hartshorne at length: panpsychism "is not a doctrine of the unreality of the spatio-temporal world ... or its reduction to mere ideas in the human or divine mind"; its constituents "are just as real as human minds," merely "of an extremely low subhuman order."[^4] Because panpsychism is "panpsychical realism" — affirming a mind-independent nature and even a pluralism of low-grade psyches — it sits on the *naturalistic* side of the ledger. Jones concludes panpsychism "would simply be another type of reductionism and would be most similar to a naturalistic origin of consciousness."[^5] Either horn returns the dilemma to Premise 1, so the argument's path to a personal source is preserved.

## Best Quotes
> "It is not enough to simply say that everything material is conscious; we still need to explain why." [^2]

> "Panpsychism would simply be another type of reductionism and would be most similar to a naturalistic origin of consciousness." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Jones distances his idealism from panpsychism's *realism of low-grade psyches* — but Latter-day Saint thought is, interestingly, friendlier to a cosmos suffused with mind than classical theism is. Scripture speaks of light/intelligence pervading and "giveth life to all things" (D&C 88:11–13), and the elements themselves being addressed and responsive. The crucial LDS difference matches Jones's own point against panpsychism: the existence of pervasive intelligence still requires that *persons* be grounded in something personal, not merely in a heap of combining micro-minds. Honest note: an LDS reader can grant Jones's combination-problem critique while remaining more open than he is to a world genuinely "full of intelligences" — provided personhood is not reduced to their summation.
