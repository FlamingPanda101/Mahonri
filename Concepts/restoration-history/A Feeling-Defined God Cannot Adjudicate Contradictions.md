#type/claim

# A Feeling-Defined God Cannot Adjudicate Contradictions

## The Claim
When the pluralist student **Alexa** defines God experientially — "whatever people define that feeling as, it's like that's God," and getting "a good feeling" from serving others *is* getting closer to God — [[Hayden Carroll]] presses the structural problem: **a feeling cannot adjudicate between contradictory claims about what is good or true.** "Just because someone feels like they're getting closer to God doesn't validate the truthfulness of their religion." [^1] If two people's sincere feelings of "good" contradict, the feeling itself offers no way to say which is right — "if people contradict in their feelings of truth, how do you know which one is right?" [^2] Carroll stress-tests the criterion with hard cases (the Satanist who feels close to "God," the person who feels justified in an abortion, ultimately Hitler) to show that *feeling-closeness* is consistent with doing the opposite of good. This is the **epistemic** counterpart to the **historical** wedge in [[Contradictory Truth Claims Cannot All Be True - The Resurrection Test]], and it is distinct from the intra-LDS [[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]] (which concerns personal revelation overriding prophets), here applied to *inter-religious* arbitration.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman of the argument)
1. **Feeling is the wrong kind of thing to settle a truth-claim.** Carroll's core distinction: the *phenomenology* of drawing near to God is real and even, he grants, "a divine principle," but it cannot certify that the *propositions* of the religion producing it are true. [^3] Muslims feel they draw near to God; Carroll judges (on doctrinal grounds) they "are getting farther away" — the feeling cannot break the tie. [^1] *Rating: strong; a felt state is not truth-apt, so it cannot validate a truth-claim without an independent standard.*
2. **The contradiction is internal to the criterion.** Alexa supplies the premise herself: religions "all contradict," which is *why* she follows none. [^4] But if feeling-good-about-it is the test, and people's feelings about what is good contradict, the test cannot do the very job (adjudicating) for which it was offered. Carroll: "if people contradict in their feelings of truth, how do you know which one is right?" [^2] *Rating: strong; this is an internal-coherence point, not an external imposition.*
3. **The hard cases expose the gap.** Carroll escalates: "what about a Satanist who thinks... God is Satan and they're getting closer, but they're doing immoral things?" [^5] And: "what if somebody thinks their God allows them to have an abortion — is that bringing them closer to God?" [^6] If feeling-closeness defines goodness, these count as "getting closer to God," which Alexa resists — revealing she covertly relies on a standard beyond feeling. *Rating: strong as a reductio; Alexa's reluctance to bite the bullet concedes the point.*
4. **It terminates in the grounding problem.** Pushed to the limit, the feelings criterion cannot distinguish "Mother Teresa's actions from Hitler['s], because they're just doing what they think is right" — "Didn't Hitler think he was doing the right thing?" [^7] Absent an objective standard, sincere moral feeling licenses atrocity, which is the full argument of [[Relativism Kills Itself - The Hitler Argument Against Self-Created Reality]] and [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]. *Rating: strong; the move from "feeling defines good" to "no ground to condemn Hitler" is the standard and decisive reductio.*

### Counter-pressure (the feelings side)
- **Alexa's narrowing reply.** She protects the criterion by restricting its scope: bringing up Satanism is "a little bit removed," because all *theistic* feeling aims at "a greater good — that's kind of the idea of God." [^8] On her refined view the relevant feeling is not any feeling but the felt pull toward the Good, which already filters out the Satanist. *Steelman: a perceptual account of moral/spiritual experience (the feeling as awareness-of value) is not obviously circular — it parallels trusting sense perception.* Carroll's rejoinder is that this just relocates the question to "what is God? what is good?" — which feeling still cannot answer when the goods contradict. [^9]
- **Not a denial of spiritual experience.** The note is *not* the claim that religious feeling is worthless; the LDS tradition prizes the witness of the Spirit. The point is narrower: feeling is a *witness to be integrated*, not a self-certifying trump that can settle contradictions on its own — cf. [[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]], where genuine revelation is expected to *align with* reason, prophets, and outcomes rather than override them.
