# Profiting From War Charge Confuses Passive Holdings With Endorsement

## The Point
On the Tucker Carlson show, filmmaker Nathan Apffel charged that because Ensign Peak Advisors holds stock in defense contractors, "the LDS church profits from war."[^1] The hosts grant the bare fact — if the Church owns Lockheed Martin and its stock rises during conflict, the fund's value rises — but show the *inference* is the problem. Owning a sliver of a defense contractor inside a broad-market portfolio is **passive ownership of a public company**, not endorsement of, profiteering from, or advocacy for war. The charge equivocates between "is incidentally exposed to" and "is in the business of and seeks to expand."

## Evidence
1. **The holding is tiny relative to the portfolio.** Ensign Peak holds roughly $25 million in Lockheed Martin out of a ~$3.5 billion portfolio; "25 million sounds like a lot... out of 3.5 billion, that's very little."[^2] A defense contractor (Raytheon) reportedly sits around the 92nd-largest holding — "way down" the list.[^3] A position this small cannot plausibly signal an institutional strategy to profit from conflict.

2. **Defense stocks ride the index, not a war-seeking thesis.** S&P 500-tracking and mutual-fund-style investing means a fund "rise[s] and fall[s] with the markets, with the general growth of the economy."[^4] Holding the major government contractors is the default consequence of owning the broad market — the same mechanism by which BlackRock and ordinary retirement funds hold them — not a bet placed *because* one wants war.

3. **The Church already screens out what it disapproves of.** Ensign Peak intentionally *excludes* alcohol, tobacco, gambling, pornography, and media conglomerates (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Netflix, Starbucks, even Bath & Body Works).[^5] A source present at the fund's founding under [[Gordon B. Hinckley]] described liquidating donated stock from a company that "advertised in Playboy."[^6] The hosts note the irony: "instead of getting credit for not profiting off of tobacco companies, they're being criticized because they invest in some companies that part of their business is military."[^7]

4. **The "incentive to promote war" version has zero evidence.** [[Tucker Carlson]] escalated to "they're probably lobbying to declare war on people because they know it'll make them money." The hosts: "There is zero evidence the church does that. That's actually contrary evidence... We preach the gospel of love and peace everywhere that we go."[^8] The malice has to be *assumed*, then read back into a $25M index position.

5. **A factual statement is not a moral indictment.** The hosts are candid: "it is a factual statement if the church has invested in Lockheed Martin, and their stocks go up because war is happening, the church is directly profiting off of war."[^9] But conceding the accounting fact is not conceding the *framing* — that the Church therefore "ha[s] an incentive to promulgate more wars."[^10] The leap from passive gain to active malice is exactly where the argument fails.

## Best Quotes
> "In the pie chart of their investments, actually 25 million sounds like a lot. Lockheed Martin, out of 3.5 billion, that's very little." [^2]

> "Instead of getting credit for not profiting off of tobacco companies, they're being criticized because they invest in some companies that part of their business is military weapon." [^7]

> "The way it's being framed [is] they have an incentive to promulgate more wars and conflict in the world so that they get more money." [^10]

## LDS Connection
Ensign Peak's screening criteria reflect a stewardship ethic — the [[parable of the talents]] applied to consecrated funds while avoiding direct participation in what the Church views as sin (pornography, addictive substances). Tithing and investment reserves are administered by a council under an unpaid lay ministry (cf. Doctrine and Covenants 119–120), aimed at building the kingdom, not enriching officers — the same structural point that defuses [[The SEC Settlement Does Not Prove the Church Hid Its Wealth|the "hidden wealth" charge]]. Holding the broad market is morally distinct from manufacturing or advocating for the products one incidentally owns.
