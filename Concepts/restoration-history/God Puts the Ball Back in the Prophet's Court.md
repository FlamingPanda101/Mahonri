# God Puts the Ball Back in the Prophet's Court

#type/claim

## The Claim
When a prophet brings God a problem, God often **declines to dictate the solution** and instead invites the prophet to propose one — then sustains it. The paradigm case is the brother of Jared in **Ether 2–3**: facing barges with no light, he asks God what to do, and God answers with a question — "what will ye that I should prepare for you that you may have light?" God puts "the ball back in the prophet's court," asking the prophet to choose the play rather than running it for him.[^1] This shows that prophetic agency-in-revelation is *by design*: God wants the prophet to deliberate and decide, which is precisely the condition under which a prophet can also get something wrong.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **God reframes the request as a question (rated: strong, scriptural).** "Instead of solving the problem, instead of telling the offensive coordinator which play to run, God puts the ball back in the prophet's court." Paraphrasing [[Ether 2.23-25]]: "in this case, I'm not going to tell you what to do. You tell me what play you want to run and I'll support it."[^1] The brother of Jared then proposes the sixteen molten stones and God touches them ([[Ether 3.14]] cluster) — the prophet's initiative, ratified by God.

2. **Connects delegation to fallibility (rated: core inference).** This is the engine of [[God as Head Coach and the Prophet as Offensive Coordinator]]: if God *invites* the prophet's own judgment, then mistakes are the predictable shadow of a real invitation, not a malfunction. A channel that only ever transmitted dictation could never err — but it would also never let the prophet "learn and improve."[^2]

3. **Consistent with the participatory model of revelation (rated: corroborating).** The pattern — God supplying the framework and the human supplying effortful initiative — matches the Latter-day Saint "participatory" reading of how revelation and translation work (see [[Learning by Experience over Book Learning]] and [[David Whitmer Tight Control Account vs the Participatory Translation Model]]), against a purely passive "upload" model.

4. **Steelman — the critic's reply.** A skeptic notes the text is selective: elsewhere God *does* give exact specifications (the ark's cubits, the tabernacle pattern), so "God invites your input" cannot be the general rule — it is read into the cases where a prophet succeeded and quietly dropped where a prophet failed. Worse, the principle is unfalsifiable: a correct prophetic initiative shows "God trusts the prophet's agency," while an incorrect one shows "God lets prophets learn by erring," so no outcome can count against the office. The believer's reply is that the claim is modest — it establishes only that *agency-in-revelation is one genuine mode* (Ether 2–3 is an explicit proof-text, not an inference), which is all that is needed to explain why a true prophet can still make mistakes. It does not assert this is the only mode, and it leaves the testing of prophets to an independent fruits standard.

## Best Quotes

> "But instead of solving the problem, instead of telling the offensive coordinator which play to run, God puts the ball back in the prophet's court."[^1]

> "In other words, in this case, I'm not going to tell you what to do. You tell me what play you want to run and I'll support it. And that's what happens."[^1]
