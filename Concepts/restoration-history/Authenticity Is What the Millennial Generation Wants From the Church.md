# Authenticity Is What the Millennial Generation Wants From the Church

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] and the *Stick of Joseph* hosts argue that a major driver of Latter-day Saint disaffiliation is a *generational shift in top virtues*: where older generations prize **loyalty**, millennials and younger prize **authenticity** — and a church culture of polished, scripted, "read-to-me" presentation reads to them as inauthentic, pushing them toward more candid spaces (e.g. unscripted podcasts like Joe Rogan). The hosts deliberately leave their own interview unedited "because you like it more natural."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The virtue shift.** "Millennial or above, their top virtue [is] loyalty; millennial below, their top virtue [is] authenticity. And so the reason why the best shows are like Joe Rogan podcasts is because it's just authentic."[^1] *(Weak-to-moderate — a plausible cultural generalization, but asserted without data; functions as a working hypothesis, not a finding.)*
2. **Scripted worship feels hollow.** The hosts contrast General Conference ("conferences read to me... it's read to me, it's not an authentic thing") with the same leaders speaking extemporaneously at stake conferences ("completely different... people leave uplifted"). The same complaint extends to sacrament meeting, where "we're just so polished."[^2] *(Moderate — a widely shared subjective impression; the claim that this *causes* departure is interpretive.)*
3. **Honesty about struggle builds spirit.** Day and the host note that meetings where men openly admitted struggling (e.g. pornography recovery groups) produced lasting spiritual feeling — "it changes the spirit that is in that room" — whereas a culture demanding the appearance of having it all together suppresses that.[^3] *(Moderate — an experiential argument; persuasive anecdotally.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A skeptic can grant the diagnosis while doubting the prescription. (a) "Authenticity" is itself a marketable aesthetic; an unedited podcast is not automatically *truthful*, and the appetite for raw candor can reward confident error over careful, "polished" accuracy. (b) The data on disaffiliation point less to *style* than to substance and belonging — surveys in this vault attribute leaving largely to drift, social/political factors, and online communities (see [[Most Who Leave Drifted Away Rather Than Rejected Faith]] and [[The Online Echo Chamber Accelerates Disaffiliation]]), not chiefly to scripted meetings. (c) Liturgical structure exists partly to remove ego and the cult of personality — a virtue the hosts themselves praise elsewhere when they note that all-music meetings "take the ego out." The defenders' fair, narrower point is that *avoidable* inauthenticity — performance for its own sake, refusal to acknowledge that scripture and prophets are fallible — needlessly alienates younger members, and is correctable without abandoning order.

## Best Quotes
> "One of the reasons why this generation is being lost, millennial generation [and] below, is because... millennials and below, their top virtue [is] authenticity. And so the reason why the best shows are like Joe Rogan podcasts is because it's just authentic — it's just people talking."[^1]

> "I think that's one of the biggest reasons why the millennial generation is leaving: if we had a little bit more authenticity, if we had conversations like this... then I really believe the millennial generation and below wouldn't be so leaving in droves the way that they are."[^2]

## LDS Connection
The claim is the *motive* the hosts give for the channel's whole format — unedited, candid, willing to "get fact-checked." It dovetails with [[Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free]]: the hosts argue that demanding inerrancy of scripture (like evangelicals do of the Bible) is part of the same inauthenticity that drives people out, and that owning fallibility is more faith-building, not less.
