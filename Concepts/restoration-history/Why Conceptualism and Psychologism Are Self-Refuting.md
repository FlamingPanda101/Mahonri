# Why Conceptualism and Psychologism Are Self-Refuting

## The Point
If nominalism fails (only names, no universals), the remaining anti-realist option is **conceptualism**: universals and propositions are real but are *wholly constructed by the human mind*, with no existence independent of it.[^1] Feser argues this middle way faces "insuperable difficulties." Because the same concepts and propositions are entertained by different people across different times — and must be, for communication to occur — they cannot be private mental constructs.[^2] The related doctrine of **psychologism** (reducing logic and mathematics to laws of human psychology) is shown to be flatly self-refuting: any attempt to state it must rely on the very objective universals and logical standards it denies.[^3]

## Evidence
**Against conceptualism — objectivity of concepts and knowledge.** When you and I entertain the concept of triangularity, or the Pythagorean theorem, we entertain *one and the same* concept referring to one and the same universal — not your private theorem and my private theorem. The same universals were grasped by people long dead and will be grasped by people not yet born; a new intelligent species could grasp them too. So they have a foundation outside any individual human mind.[^4] Feser presses the communication point: if the universals you mean were sheer constructs of *your* mind, they would be inaccessible to anyone else, your idea of snow entirely different from mine, and we could never mean the same thing — which is absurd, since we plainly do communicate, and must even to *disagree about conceptualism itself*.[^2]

**Against psychologism (Frege's worry).** Psychologism says logic and math describe only how our minds work, not objective reality; add cultural/biological relativism and all our concepts become contingent and revisable.[^5] But to *defend* such an account one must appeal to universals (selection pressures, genetic mutations, social trends), to mathematical and scientific principles, and to standards of logic — "the very things the view in question tells us have no objective validity." So the view undermines itself.[^3] Feser then closes the Kantian escape route: if the relativist retreats to saying it is a *necessary* fact about our minds that we think this way, he is now claiming objective knowledge of the nature of our minds — precisely the kind of mind-independent knowledge his theory forbids. Either way he is caught: deny objective validity and he cannot defend his position; affirm it and he contradicts his own view.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Suppose that, as conceptualism implies, the universals and propositions you entertain were sheer constructs of your mind. Then it would be impossible for you and anyone else ever to communicate." [^2]

> "If he insists, as his theory must lead him to, that our concepts, standards of logic, and so forth have no objective validity, then he cannot so much as defend his own position; if he claims that they do have validity... then he's just contradicted his own view in the very act of defending it." [^7]

## LDS Connection
This is a strong ally against the relativizing, "logic is just how our brains evolved" move common in atheistic naturalism: Feser shows it is literally incoherent, since stating it presupposes the objective standards it denies (see also [[The Laws of Logic Are Immaterial and Point to a Mind]] and [[The One Over Many Argument for Universals]]). Latter-day Saint theology is comfortable affirming objective, mind-independent truth — "truth is knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) is realist, not conceptualist, in spirit. Representing Feser fairly: his conclusion is that objective truth requires grounding in an intellect, and he will argue for one necessarily existing divine intellect; the Restoration agrees truth is anchored in a divine Mind while parting company on whether that Mind *authors* eternal truth or *honors* it as co-eternal ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]).
