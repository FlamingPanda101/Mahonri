# Swimmers vs Ship-Builders - The Ethics of Deconstruction

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] turns from the *consequences* of deconstruction to its *ethics*, drawing a sharp moral contrast between two kinds of people on the ocean. **Ship-builders** labor to construct a seaworthy craft, plot a course, and "invite others to join us in the voyage." **Swimmers** offer no boat of their own; they float in the water "laughing at the people in the boats," and — worse — "take great pleasure in trying to sink them." Hansen's charge is that critique is cheap and construction is costly: "it's much easier to point out the leaks in other people's boats; it's much harder to build a seaworthy craft." He is careful to grant that some "prying loose of rotten planks or remodeling of the ship is necessary and good" — honest reform has its place. But there is a categorical difference between repairing or improving a vessel and being a "wrecker" whose only product is wreckage. The question he leaves with the viewer is personal: "are they ship builders or ship destroyers?" — and, "which of these roles have I tried to play?"

## Evidence
- The two groups: builders call "join our crew," while "there's another group, a group in the water swimming around laughing at the people in the boats." [^1]
- Destruction as the swimmers' aim: "the swimmers don't just mock the boats, they take great pleasure in trying to sink them... better to sink the ships so we can all drift around together." [^2]
- Critique is easy, building is hard: "it's much easier to point out the leaks in other people's boats; it's much harder to build a seaworthy craft that can endure the onslaught of storms." [^3]
- Even rival builders who sink others' ships are no better: "just because you sink their ship does not mean they will join your crew. So who are you... are they ship builders or ship destroyers?" [^4]
- Hansen quotes the Charles Benvegar poem of the wrecking crew — "I can easily wreck in a day or two what builders have taken years to do" — and turns it inward: "am I a builder who works with care... or am I a wrecker who walks to town, content with the labor of tearing down?" [^5]
- The qualifier: "surely some occasional prying loose of rotten planks or remodeling of the ship is necessary and good, but why focus on destroying the boats of others?" [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "It's one thing to be trying to build a boat and calling others to join you in a worthwhile journey in a more secure and seaworthy craft; it's another thing entirely to be a swimmer — someone who offers nothing but a life adrift in the meaningless cruel void and who takes perverse pleasure in pulling others into the water." [^4]

> "I can easily wreck in a day or two what builders have taken years to do... am I a builder who works with care... or am I a wrecker who walks to town, content with the labor of tearing down?" — Charles Benvegar, quoted by Hansen [^5]

## LDS Connection
This reframes the deconstruction debate as a stewardship question, resonant with the gospel ethic of building Zion and edifying rather than tearing down (Ephesians 4 on edifying the body; the charge to be "anxiously engaged in a good cause"). Hansen's distinction between healthy reform ("remodeling the ship," "prying loose rotten planks") and pure demolition gives believers a constructive posture toward their own faith: honest examination and improvement are welcome, but the aim is a more seaworthy vessel, not wreckage. It also offers a charitable but pointed reading of online faith-crisis discourse — the difference between a critic who is helping repair a boat and one who only "pulls others into the frigid waters." See the consequence-side argument in [[Where Will You Go - Deconstruction Rarely Leads Somewhere Better]] and the positive vision in [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]].
