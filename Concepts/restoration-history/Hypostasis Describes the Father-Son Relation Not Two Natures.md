# Hypostasis Describes the Father-Son Relation Not Two Natures

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Classical Christology speaks of the "hypostatic union" — the union of the divine and human *natures* in the one *person* (hypostasis) of Christ. The objection Ostler addresses is that this is a *biblical* concept. He concedes the *word* *hypostasis* is biblical (it appears in [[Hebrews 1.3|Hebrews 1:3]]) but argues it is used there for the **relation of the Father and the Son**, not for two natures united in one person — so the technical "hypostatic union" rips the term from its biblical context. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Hebrews 1:3 is the *only* NT use of *hypostasis* in this sense.** "The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being (*charaktēr tēs hypostaseōs autou*)." Ostler: it is "talking about the oneness or the relation between the Father and the Son. How he shares God's glory, not how he's one person." *Rating: a fair lexical point* — Heb 1:3 does describe the Son's relation to *the Father's* hypostasis, not an internal union of natures. [^3][^4]

2. **The technical Chalcedonian sense is later.** The "hypostatic union" (one person, two natures) is the language of Chalcedon (451; Ostler dates "the creed of Chalcedon in 421," a slip — Chalcedon was 451), not of Hebrews. *Rating: correct that the technical usage is a later development*; the historical point stands even with the date error. [^5]

3. **Hebrews frames an *enthronement*, not a two-nature union.** "He sat down at the right hand of the majesty" → quoting Psalm 2 ("you are my son, today I have begotten you") and Psalm 110 (the most-cited text in early Christian writing). The Son is *adopted/enthroned* and given all glory by inheritance. *Rating: strong intertextual reading* (cf. [[Hebrews 1.1-2|Hebrews 1:1-2]], [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110:1]], [[Psalm 2.7|Psalm 2:7]]). [^6][^7]

4. **Steelman of the classical reading.** The doctrine of the hypostatic union does not *claim* the word "hypostasis" is used technically in Hebrews; it is a *systematic* term coined to protect what the NT teaches narratively (one Christ who is both God and man — John 1:14, Phil 2:6-8, Col 2:9). That a council in 451 fixed precise vocabulary no more makes the doctrine "unbiblical" than later words like "monotheism" or "incarnation." And Heb 1:3's "exact representation of his hypostasis" + "upholds all things by his word of power" is itself a *high-deity* description of the Son. *Rating: a strong rebuttal* — the argument targets a *word*, not the underlying claim; the doctrine never rested on Heb 1:3's lexeme. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "It's the only time the hypostasis appears anywhere in the Bible... when we're using the term hypostatic, it actually is a biblical term." [^3]

> "This isn't talking about the union of one nature in a person to another nature in the same person. It's talking about the oneness or the relation between the Father and the Son." [^4]

> "When they use this term and talk about the hypostatic union, they're ripping the term out of its context and using it in a way that it was never meant to be used." [^9]
