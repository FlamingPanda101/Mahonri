# Positive Childhood Religious Experience Predicts Adult Retention

## The Claim
Children who have positive religious experiences are far more likely to remain in their religion as adults; those with negative experiences are far more likely to leave. This underscores a pastoral imperative to make children's religious experiences loving and safe.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Recent Pew findings (rated: strong, as reported).** The host recalls Pew data that "those who had positive childhood religious experiences were far, far, far more likely to stay in that religion when they grew up versus those who had negative experiences as a child," which Dyer affirms.[^1]

2. **The mirror of the faith-rejector profile (rated: strong).** This is the positive image of the adverse-experience finding among faith-rejectors. See [[Faith-Rejectors and Adverse Childhood Religious Experience]].[^2] *Steelman:* the relationship is partly "common sense" and likely bidirectional — children already inclined toward faith may experience church more positively — so causal claims should be tempered.

3. **The Primary application (rated: moderate, normative).** Dyer cites the LDS Children's handbook: the number-one purpose of Primary is to "help the kids feel the love of the Lord" — "if nothing else happened in church that day, help them feel the love of the Lord." Early affective associations with a church building persist into adulthood.[^3]

4. **Not deterministic (rated: moderate).** Dyer stresses childhood experience is influential but not destiny: "we're not trapped in those past experiences, but they certainly do have an impact on how we perceive things, including our church."[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Those who had positive childhood religious experiences were far, far, far more likely to stay in that religion when they grew up versus those who had negative experiences as a child."[^1]

> "The number one purpose of Primary: help the kids feel the love of the Lord. If nothing else happened in church that day, help them feel the love of the Lord."[^3]

> "What happens when we're kids [is not] our destiny by any means... but they certainly do have an impact on how we perceive things, including our church."[^4]
