# Growth Past the Second Generation Validates a Movement

## The Claim
[[Christopher Blythe]] proposes a rough sociological test for the competing succession claims: "the ability of a movement to grow past its first or second generation says a lot about it." Since the 1844 succession crisis "at the end of the day is an argument," sustained multi-generational growth is offered as a real-world indicator of which factions had something durable — and only two of the six, the Brighamites and the Bickertonites, still grow. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "line to get in" analogy (rated: moderate, illustrative).** Blythe likes "basic analyses": just as the classic rejoinder to "if America/capitalism is so bad, why is there a big line to get into America and not into the USSR?", a faith that keeps attracting and retaining members past its founding generation has demonstrated something the others have not. He stresses he is "not saying how wealthy you are determines your spirituality." [^1]

2. **Only two factions are growing (rated: moderate, observational).** Blythe judges the Brighamite church and the **Bickertonites** to be the live ones. He has visited Bickertonite churches, says "you can feel the Spirit there," and reports (via Josh Gay) lots of converts — many immigrants and disaffected Latter-day Saints, plus high-profile conversions (Mitiga; Jacob Isbell). By contrast the **Community of Christ** has had to "sell off assets just to be solvent," which he "can't... [see] as growth." See [[Where Mormons Rank on the Church-Sect Continuum]]. [^2]

3. **The dwindling tail (rated: strong, descriptive).** The Cutlerites are down to about six elderly members; the Strangites to roughly 60–70; the Hedrickite Church of Christ (Temple Lot) holds "maybe slightly less members than the Bickertonites." Blythe notes many of these churches simply "reshuffle membership over the years," and that even the largest non-LDS body is contracting ("the big beast is going way down"). [^3]

**Steelman / limits.** The test is admittedly crude: numerical growth can reflect immigration, birth rates, or social fabric rather than doctrinal truth, and Blythe concedes uncertainty about whether the Bickertonites are still growing "as of just a while ago." A critic could note the same metric would once have flattered Islam or the Shakers at their peak. The panel's use of it is therefore corroborative, not probative — a secondary confirmation of the keys-based case for the Brighamite line (see [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]]), not a freestanding proof.

## Best Quotes
> "The ability of a movement to grow past its first or second generation says a lot about it." [^1]

> "I think there's really only two sects that are still even growing. Aren't the rest just drastically dwindling?" [^2]

> "Lots of these churches, they just kind of reshuffle membership over the years... The big beast is going way down." [^3]
