# The Thinking Has Been Done Was Repudiated by George Albert Smith

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The slogan **"When the prophet speaks, the thinking has been done"** is frequently cited — by critics as proof of LDS mind-control, and by some members as a maxim of obedience. [[Trevan Hatch]] points out that the line was *not* a prophetic teaching at all: it appeared in a 1940s *Improvement Era* editorial, and the sitting president of the church, **George Albert Smith**, *repudiated* it in writing as a misrepresentation of the church's ideal.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Provenance: an editor, not a prophet (strong)
The phrase originated when an *Improvement Era* editor inserted the line "When our leaders speak, the thinking has been done" into the magazine in the 1940s. It was editorial content, never a revelation or official teaching.[^1]

### 2. The prophet's explicit disavowal (decisive)
George Albert Smith — then President of the Church — disliked it and wrote a letter to the editor stating that even to imply members of the church should not do their own thinking "is grossly to misrepresent the position of the church, the ideal of the church."[^1] So the highest authority of the day rejected the very slogan later weaponized in his name.

### 3. It coheres with the broader pattern (medium)
This fits the chorus of prophetic statements *encouraging* members to think and verify — George Q. Cannon ("the people who have embraced this gospel have had to think for themselves"), Ballard ("I seek others... with degrees and expertise"), Oaks (a single leader's statement is "not meant to be binding"). The "thinking has been done" maxim is the outlier the leaders themselves corrected. See [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies that the *disavowal* is less culturally operative than the *slogan*: regardless of George Albert Smith's letter, "the thinking has been done" continues to circulate as a meme and to shape an obedience culture, especially in the Intermountain West — so quoting the repudiation is a debater's move that doesn't reflect lived reality, where the phrase still functions as intended.

## Counter-Rebuttal
That the phrase persists *as a meme* is precisely Hatch's complaint, and it strengthens, not weakens, the apologetic point: the slogan never had the authority its users assume, and a prophet on record rejected it. The correct response to a persistent misquotation is to surface the repudiation — which Hatch keeps "handy" for exactly this purpose — not to concede the slogan's authority because some still repeat it. The persistence is a cultural problem to correct, not evidence that the church *teaches* thoughtless obedience; cf. [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught]].

## Best Quotes
> "George Albert Smith... said that even to imply that members of the church should not do their own thinking is grossly to misrepresent the position of the church, the ideal of the church."[^1]
