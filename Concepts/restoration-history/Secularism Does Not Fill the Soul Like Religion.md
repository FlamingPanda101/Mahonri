# Secularism Does Not Fill the Soul Like Religion

#type/claim

## The Claim
Pure secularism, "from a data standpoint ... is related to, on average, lower mental health" and "simply doesn't fill the soul like religion does." Part of any religious resurgence may be people "realizing that religion does have a pull that you just don't get anywhere else."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The data linkage (Evidence: moderate; restates the religion-health correlation).** "The alternative, which is pure secularism ... is related to ... on average lower mental health and those kinds of things."[^1] This is the inverse statement of [[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]].

2. **The "looking for something else" pattern (Evidence: anecdotal + plausible).** Earlier in the interview, the host and Dyer note that people who leave religion often redirect the same meaning-seeking and service drive into intense political activism — "we're still searching for that meaning ... and political parties can provide a lot of that."[^2] Secular substitutes are sought precisely because the religious function is unmet.

3. **Service/meaning as the unmet need (Evidence: theoretical).** Religion uniquely combines transcendence, community, and structured service; Dyer argues these are what "fill the soul," and their absence is what secularism leaves open.[^3]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** "Doesn't fill the soul" is a value claim smuggled in beside a contested empirical one. The secular-happiness gap shrinks or vanishes in highly secular, high-welfare societies (Scandinavia), suggesting the religion-wellbeing link is partly a function of social support and *cultural fit* — being secular in a religious society is isolating, but secularism itself is not intrinsically soul-starving. Selection and confounding (see [[Religiosity Correlates With Better Mental and Physical Health]]) apply equally here. Many secular people report rich meaning via family, work, philosophy, and community; the claim that this "you just don't get anywhere else" overstates the evidence. Notably, the source's own humility ([[1 Nephi 14.10|1 Nephi 14:10]], Oaks) concedes meaning is available across many faiths — a small step from conceding it outside religion entirely.
