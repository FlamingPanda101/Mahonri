# Time Is Not the Hero - Decay Outruns Assembly

## The Point
Origin-of-life storytelling leans on deep time: given "hundreds of millions of years," what looks impossible becomes "virtually inevitable" (so the Veritasium cartoon claims).[^1] Meyer rebuts the assumption directly. Quoting biologist George Wald's mid-century line that "time is the hero of the plot," he answers: "Well, it's not." The processes of chemical *decay* happen at much faster rates than the processes of chemical *assembly*, so if you let things run, life-relevant building blocks "decay faster than anything will get built." Time therefore works *against* the materialist scenario, not for it.[^2] This is the inverse of the usual intuition that more time gives chance more chances.

## Evidence
1. **The cartoon's appeal to time.** The animation argues that across "hundreds of millions of years," the formation of a replicator that "might seem impossible to us becomes virtually inevitable."[^1]
2. **Wald's slogan, rejected.** George Wald, a scientist interested in prebiotic chemistry in the 1950s, said "time is the hero of the plot"; Meyer's reply is blunt: "Well, it's not."[^3]
3. **Decay beats assembly.** "The processes of chemical decay happen at much faster rates than the processes of chemical assembly... if you let things run, they're going to decay faster than anything will get built. And so time actually works against you."[^2]
4. **A forthcoming peer-reviewed treatment.** Meyer notes a "recent paper that's even more damning to this idea about time being the hero of the plot," part of a volume he and organic chemist James Tour have coming out with Cambridge University Press.[^4]
5. **Even granting a favorable environment, chance still fails.** "Even without the James Tour consideration of the fact that things are degrading faster than they're building," the available time can search only a tiny fraction of the possible amino-acid combinations.[^5] See [[The Bike-Lock Analogy - Why Chance Cannot Search Sequence Space]].

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology affirms a designed and ordered cosmos and a God who *organizes* eternal matter through real, lawful processes. This argument simply removes a popular escape hatch — "given enough time, anything happens" — by appeal to chemistry, and it does so without any claim about the age of the earth (the timescales are granted). For an LDS reader it functions as a natural-theology resource pointing to an organizing intelligence rather than to creation *ex nihilo*: if undirected chemistry decays faster than it builds, the order we see is better explained by a directing mind. The inference lands on a generic designer, leaving the scientific details open. See [[The Blobs Cartoon and Why Life Will Not Polymerize in Water]].
