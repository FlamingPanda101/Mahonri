# The One Mighty and Strong Was Edward Partridge Not a Future Reformer

## The Claim
[[Scott Woodward|Woodward]] and [[Casey Paul Griffiths|Griffiths]] call the "one mighty and strong" of [[D&C 85]] "probably the most misinterpreted prophecy in the whole Doctrine and Covenants." It has been "the subject of a ton of speculation," with "dozens of splinter groups" and self-appointed reformers claiming to be the promised figure who will "set in order the house of God." The hosts argue, following the **1905 First Presidency statement**, that the prophecy is a *conditional warning* about a specific man — Bishop [[Edward Partridge]] — who was neglecting his stewardship over the inheritances of the Saints in Missouri. Because Partridge repented, the need for a replacement "passed away, and the whole incident of the prophecy closed."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The 1905 First Presidency reading fixes the context (rating: strong; official).** The statement (Joseph F. Smith, John R. Winder, Anthon Lund) establishes that the whole letter concerns "the affairs of the Church in Missouri" and the consecration inheritances, and identifies the "man who was called and appointed of God" to divide the inheritances as Edward Partridge, who was "at that time out of order... putting forth his hand to steady the ark."[^1]
2. **The text predicts the man's death if he fails (rating: strong; textual).** [[D&C 85]]:8 says the steadying-the-ark man "shall fall by the shaft of death, like as a tree that is smitten by the vivid shaft of lightning." Nobody claims this happened to Partridge — confirming the prophecy was *conditional* and was averted by his repentance, not fulfilled by a later reformer.[^2]
3. **Partridge's later record refutes the "he failed" premise (rating: strong; historical).** Far from failing, Partridge became "one of the most honored names in the Church": in the Missouri persecutions he was tarred and feathered before a mob that wanted to kill him and "disarmed them by his Christlike nature." To accept the prophecy as still-future is to assert, against the record, that Partridge failed and was struck down.[^2]
4. **It is a textbook case of prooftexting (rating: moderate; methodological).** The hosts label the misuse "prooftexting" — latching onto a phrase "without looking what the entire context is" — and note the 1905 statement was issued partly because Second-Manifesto-era polygamy holdouts were breaking off and invoking the verse.[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A dissenter (or a break-off movement) replies that prophecy can have *multiple* or *dual* fulfillments — the hosts themselves grant "scripture... can have multiple fulfillments" — so even if Partridge was the *immediate* referent, a latter-day "one mighty and strong" who sets the church in order need not be excluded. The grand, cinematic language ("scepter of power," "clothed with light," "a fountain of truth") seems to outstrip a mere bishop's reinstatement and invites a larger, future application.

## Counter-Objection
The dual-fulfillment escape is blocked by the death clause: a genuine future "one mighty and strong" would presuppose that the *original* appointee (Partridge) "fell by the shaft of death," which is false. Once the conditional's antecedent fails (Partridge did *not* fall away to his destruction; he repented), the consequent (a replacement being sent) never triggers. The elevated language fits a bishop being warned and (potentially) replaced by "one having the Spirit and power of that high office," per the 1905 reading; it does not license a free-floating prophecy detachable from its Missouri context. The pattern of misuse also reveals motive: the verse is typically deployed by those who already wish to "question the leadership of the Church" — which D&C 85:8's warning about "steadying the ark" directly rebukes (see [[Steadying the Ark Names Overreach Beyond Ones Stewardship]]).

## Best Quotes
> "This is a prophecy that has become so misinterpreted, probably the most misinterpreted prophecy in the whole Doctrine and Covenants... dozens of splinter groups from the Church have used this passage to justify their attempt to reform the Church or to start their own church."[^1]

> "If you accept the prophecy of one mighty and strong, you're saying that Edward Partridge failed. Not just that he failed, but that he was struck down — which the historical record just doesn't bear up."[^2]
