# Loss of Apostolic Authority Caused the Christological Factions

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues the mechanism of the Great Apostasy was the **loss of a central, living authority**: while the apostles lived, they adjudicated doctrinal questions; when they died, there was no body to settle disputes, so the church fractured into the "great christological debates" of the first centuries — and Paul's epistles are best read as *pre-emptive damage control* against false teaching already creeping in.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The apostles functioned as a central adjudicating body — rate: moderate (scriptural-historical)
Hansen points to Acts: "Peter is like, we don't eat certain things because they're unclean, and then he changes that… the apostles acted as this centralized governing body. When they go away, you then have these theological questions but you don't have a central authority to adjudicate them, and so what arises is factionalism."[^1] The Jerusalem council (Acts 15) is the model of living authority resolving disputes.

### 2. Paul's letters are anti-apostasy damage control — rate: moderate (canonical)
"You have the good news [the four gospels] and then the bad news… essentially all of the letters of Paul, which our Christian brothers and sisters love, is him saying, no, stop, you idiots, not that way — writing letters to everybody trying to pull them back from apostate doctrines." Hansen's rhetorical question: "why the letters of Paul at all? What was he trying to accomplish? He's trying to fight false teachings coming into the church."[^2] Cf. [[Galatians 1-6 Marvel Ye Are So Soon Removed Early Apostasy]].

### 3. Scripture names a coming apostasy — rate: strong (proof-text)
Hansen cites the New Testament's own predictions: [[2 Thessalonians 2.3|2 Thessalonians 2]] ("let no man deceive you… there will be a falling away" — the Greek *apostasia*) and 2 Peter's warning that "there's already false teachers among us." The text forecasts the very fragmentation he describes.[^2] See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The Catholic/Orthodox reply is direct. (a) **Authority did not vanish — it was transmitted.** Apostolic succession (bishops, councils, the Petrine office) is precisely the claim that the adjudicating authority *continued*; the ecumenical councils (Nicaea, Constantinople, Chalcedon) are presented as that authority *working*, resolving the christological disputes definitively, not as evidence of its absence. The LDS argument assumes succession failed, which is the point in dispute. (b) **Controversy is not apostasy.** The existence of heresies (Arianism, Docetism) and Paul's corrective letters show a *living* church policing its boundaries — exactly what a divinely guided institution would do — not proof the church fell; the New Testament warnings about false teachers are warnings *to be heeded*, and the church claims it heeded them. (c) **The "falling away" texts are contested.** 2 Thessalonians 2:3's *apostasia* is read by many as an eschatological event preceding the Second Coming, not a description of a total 2nd-century collapse; building a universal apostasy on it overreads the passage. (d) **Selective use of Acts.** Peter's vision (Acts 10) shows the apostles *receiving ongoing revelation*, which the Catholic says continued through their successors. The disciplined version: the observation that the apostolic generation exercised a unique adjudicating authority, and that doctrinal factionalism intensified afterward, is historically fair; but whether that authority *lapsed* (LDS) or was *handed on* (Catholic/Orthodox) is the crux, and the factions plus Paul's letters are equally compatible with a church that survived its disputes — so this datum motivates the apostasy thesis without proving it. The harder-edged version that the keys actually departed is treated in [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]].
