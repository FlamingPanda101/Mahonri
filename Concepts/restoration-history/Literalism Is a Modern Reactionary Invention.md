# Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention

## The Claim

Hard biblical literalism is not the ancient default but a recent, defensive reaction to modernity. "Their perspective, this literalist perspective is super modern. It is less than a hundred years old really in the way that they're practicing it... and up until that point, Christianity didn't look like that." [^1][^2] It was forged in the 19th–20th centuries as a "rhetorical line in the sand" against the historical-critical method, geology, evolution, and the discovery of deep time. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The trigger was the Enlightenment and 19th-century science.** Rationalism, historical criticism, [[Thomas Jefferson]] excising New Testament miracles, the age of the earth, dinosaurs, and "the nature of the species" ([[Charles Darwin|Darwin]]) "challenged traditional interpretations of the Bible. And the response was to lean into literalism." [^4][^5][^6] Literalism is thus *reactive*: "it's reacting against stuff more than it's actually going out and seeking stuff." [^7] **(Strong — matches the standard genealogy of fundamentalism.)**
2. **The doctrines were asserted, not discovered.** Six 24-hour creation days, the global flood, a historical Adam and Eve, and the [[Genesis 10|table-of-nations]] genealogies were declared "100% true. Not because we had been studying it and we arrived at this conclusion, but because we needed to assert this." [^8] See [[Literalism Is Ideological Not Textual]]. **(Strong.)**
3. **It is younger than the science it opposes.** "It's so funny because they are so mad about all of this modern science, and what's weird is that their perspective is super modern." [^1] McClellan dates the practiced form to roughly 100–150 years. [^9] **(Strong — *The Fundamentals* date to 1910–1917; the Chicago Statement to 1978.)**
4. **Steelman.** A defender would say that affirming the truth of Genesis is not novel — the *substance* of belief in creation, Adam, and the flood is ancient, even if the polemical *packaging* (inerrancy, univocality) is modern. McClellan's distinction answers this: pre-modern Christianity affirmed scripture's truth while denying it "rested on the surface" (Origen, Augustine), whereas modern literalism collapses truth into the surface and forbids the older interpretive latitude. See [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]] and [[The Fourfold Sense of Scripture]]. **(Fair — the rebuttal turns on *how* the texts were read, not whether they were believed.)**

## Best Quotes

> "This literalist perspective is super modern. It is less than a hundred years old really in the way that they're practicing it." [^1]

> "The rise of modern science, the scientific method, critical theory, all these kinds of things challenge traditional interpretations of the Bible. And the response was to lean into literalism." [^6]

> "In at least the case of things like inerrancy today, it's reactive. It's reacting against stuff more than it's actually going out and seeking stuff." [^7]
