# An Innocent Cleared by Every Investigator Was Still Driven to Death

## The Claim
The human cost of the Marion Smith / ISAT panic is concentrated in "Bill," [[Marion Smith]]'s son-in-law. Every independent check that touched his case exonerated him — two police searches, multiple investigators, a Johns Hopkins / [[R. Jan Stout]] psychiatric evaluation, and finally Smith's own children refusing to testify — and *still* he lost his children, his marriage, repeated lawsuits, and his life. His case is the cleanest demonstration that recovered-memory accusations, once launched, can destroy a demonstrably innocent person regardless of the evidence. (The episode withholds his surname; he is "Bill.")

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Cleared by every independent authority (strong)
When "Bill" was accused, the relatives who had grown up with him "unanimously agreed that it was … 'definitely not possible.'" He was examined by "an accredited and distinguished psychiatrist," Dr. [[R. Jan Stout]], who "concluded that Bill was very likely not a perpetrator." Police "searched Bill's home for the phantom videotapes" and found nothing; "another Salt Lake County investigator also looked into Bill and concluded that he was innocent. The police concluded he was innocent." A Johns Hopkins psychiatrist found his mental state "appropriate" and that he was "not much risk to the community" [^1].

### 2. The case died because there was nothing to try (strong)
"Perhaps most devastating for Smith was when her own children stood up to their mother and refused to put the granddaughters on the stand. Without the primary witnesses testifying, the case was dead on arrival." The collapse was internal — the accusers would not produce the children — not an exoneration the church engineered [^2]. See [[No Evidence the LDS Church Suppressed the Investigations]].

### 3. Exoneration did not stop the destruction (strong — the core point)
"Regardless, Bill had his children taken away. His wife divorced him. He was sued many times and was dragged in and out of court-ordered psychiatric treatment." In 1993 another of Smith's daughters "suddenly recovered memories of abuse and sued Bill for 2.5 million dollars"; he "had no money left to give," and his bishop was helping with his house payments. After Smith published "a second book about Bill's unproven abuse," "Bill committed suicide" [^3]. The recovered-memory mechanism (see [[Recovered Memory Therapy Manufactures Abuse Memories]]) supplied each fresh suit.

### 4. The family's reaction reveals the dynamic, not Bill's guilt (moderate; quoted clinically)
Smith reportedly wrote that Bill's death "was a cause of … shock and rejoicing for our family … 'everyone agreed suicide was the best action he ever chose.'" The episode reads this not as evidence Bill was guilty but as evidence of how completely the accusers had committed to the narrative — a closed system that rejoiced at an innocent man's death rather than revise its belief [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
The strongest counter: a polygraph "failure" is mentioned, abusers are often cleared by relatives who cannot believe it, suicide is not proof of innocence, and an investigator concluding "not much risk" is not the same as "did not do it." Fairly stated. But polygraphs are not admissible precisely because they are unreliable; the *converging* judgment of police (twice), a separate county investigator, two psychiatric evaluations including Johns Hopkins, and the total absence of the alleged tapes is far weightier than a single failed lie-detector test; and the case ended because the accusers *withheld* the witnesses, not because Bill was proven guilty and escaped. On the evidence presented, the asymmetry points overwhelmingly toward innocence.

## Counter-objection: "where there's smoke there's fire"
The volume of suits and accusations is offered as if it were corroboration. But repetition is not evidence (see [[Accusations Cannot Transmute Into Evidence]]); each new suit here was generated by a fresh "recovered memory" within the same family, not by new external facts. Multiplying allegations from a single contaminated source does not raise their joint probability.
