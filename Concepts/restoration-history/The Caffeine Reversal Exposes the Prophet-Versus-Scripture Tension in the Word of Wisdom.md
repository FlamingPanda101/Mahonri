# The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] uses the [[Word of Wisdom]]'s history with caffeine as a clean worked example of how the "follow the prophet" and "follow the scripture" criteria for doctrine *cut against each other*. For most of the 20th century members were counseled (by prophetic voices) to avoid caffeine; that counsel was later effectively reversed, with a church PR rationale that *appealed to the text* of D&C 89 over the accumulated prophetic teaching. Meanwhile the *text itself*, read historically, doesn't say what current practice assumes either — so neither "the prophets said" nor "the revelation says" yields the status quo cleanly.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Decades of prophetic counsel against caffeine (strong)
Hatch searched the corpus and found members were counseled to avoid caffeine **over 80 times between 1908 and 2002** — a strongly *repeated*, *continuously taught* prophetic emphasis by the very criteria scholars use to mark official doctrine.[^1]

### 2. The reversal and its scripture-based rationale (strong)
Caffeinated drinks are now sold at church-owned BYU. When this drew press, a church PR statement noted that **"caffeine is not mentioned in Doctrine and Covenants 89"** — i.e., it sided with the *text of the revelation* over decades of prophetic teaching, the inverse of "the living prophet takes precedence."[^2]

### 3. The text itself undercuts current practice (strong)
Read in its 1830s context (per scholars like Steven Harper), D&C 89 was "not by commandment," permits "mild barley drink" (low-alcohol beer) and *restricts* meat. Joseph Smith bought and drank beer from Frederick Moessner's store and took wine to Carthage. So a strict scripture-only reading would *permit* beer and *limit* meat — also not the modern standard. Both criteria, applied consistently, diverge from current practice.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The standard reply is that the Word of Wisdom is a *living health code* progressively defined by prophetic interpretation: the canonical text is a floor, and prophets extend and adjust its application (adding tea/coffee, declining to bind caffeine) as a matter of ongoing revelation and pastoral prudence. The "caffeine reversal" is thus not a contradiction but exactly how a living church is supposed to administer a standard — and "mild barley drink" is an antiquarian detail superseded by later prophetic counsel against alcohol.

## Counter-Objection
Hatch's point survives the "living code" reply because it concerns *which authority is decisive*, not whether change is allowed. If prophetic interpretation is the living, decisive voice, then the PR appeal to "it's not in D&C 89" *abandons* that principle and lets the 1830s text overrule 80+ years of prophetic counsel — the scripture-supreme strand winning. You cannot consistently say both "the living prophet extends the revelation and takes precedence" (to justify banning tea/coffee) and "it's not in the revelation, so it's open" (to justify selling caffeine). The example shows the church *itself* switching between the two competing criteria depending on the desired outcome (see [[The Living Prophet Versus the Standard Works Are Two Competing LDS Authority Traditions]]).
