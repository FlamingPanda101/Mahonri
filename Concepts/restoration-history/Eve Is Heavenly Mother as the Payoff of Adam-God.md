# Eve Is Heavenly Mother — the Redemptive Payoff of Adam-God

## The Claim
Rather than treat Adam-God as a pure embarrassment to be quarantined, [[Christopher Blythe|Chris Blyth]] argues it carries a genuine theological **benefit**: it "teaches us who Heavenly Mother is." On the Adam-God scheme, **Eve is Heavenly Mother**, so the Eden narrative becomes the story of Heavenly Mother "choosing to bring forth the offspring that she's helped." The doctrine also "gives us a story, a narrative behind mother Eve" and, per the host's extrapolation, explains "what grandpa God was up to" — why an already-exalted, resurrected being would condescend to fall [^1].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **It supplies a Heavenly Mother narrative the church otherwise lacks (strong — the distinctive payoff).** Blyth: "it teaches us who heavenly mother is... it gives us a story, a narrative behind mother Eve." Asked directly "who does it say Mother Eve is," he answers: "Mother Eve is heavenly mother. That is Adam-God." The fall of Eve then reads as Heavenly Mother's deliberate act of bringing forth her children [^1].

2. **It dignifies Adam and Eve rather than degrading them (moderate — theological motive).** Blyth frames Joseph's whole project as "redeeming" Adam and Eve, who in the traditional Bible are "as good as heathens... dirty, filthy, horrible creatures that people blame." Much "misogyny... comes from Eve being the mother of all sin," and Joseph (echoing Jacob in the Book of Mormon: people "talk about our first parents with contempt... don't do that") reverses it. Adam-God is the maximal form of that dignification — the first parents are literally our Heavenly Parents [^2].

3. **It coheres with the exalted-couple mechanics Brigham taught (supporting — internal logic).** The host states the "logical conclusion": granddaddy God "condescended to become Adam," left "his already perfectly resurrected state in order to fall," and the woman he fell with "would have been heavenly mother." Blyth affirms the mechanism: exalted beings have spirit children, "and in order to give them mortal bodies, they came to earth... whatever made their bodies corrupted again," enabling the human race [^3].

### Steelman of the critic
The "payoff" cuts both ways. The same move that makes Eve Heavenly Mother is exactly the tenet the church now **rejects** (Adam-and-Eve are *not* our Heavenly Parents — see [[The Church Kept Most of Adam-God Rejecting Only Specific Tenets]]). A critic can note that presenting Adam-God as the *solution* to the Heavenly Mother question implies the official position is theologically poorer for lacking it — an awkward result for a doctrine McConkie called damning. Blyth himself hedges: even if true, "it's still not the church's doctrine," and a Latter-day Saint's job may be "to keep their mouths shut" about such mysteries [^4].
