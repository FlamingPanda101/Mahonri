# The Enoch Rediscovery Timeline Postdates Joseph Smith

## The Claim
The force of the Enoch parallels (see [[Cirillo Catalogs Substantial Enoch Parallels Beyond Dispute]]) depends on **availability**: could [[Joseph Smith]] have read the ancient Enoch texts whose content his Book of Enoch ([[Moses 6|Moses 6]]–[[Moses 7|7]], 1830–1832) anticipates? The video lays out a rediscovery/translation timeline showing that the texts carrying the matching details were either untranslated, undeciphered, or undiscovered until **decades after** Joseph published — and in the case of the Dead Sea *Book of the Giants*, more than a century after.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **1773 — Bruce's Ethiopic manuscripts, ignored (rate: moderate).** James Bruce brought Ethiopian manuscripts containing Enoch texts back to Europe in 1773, but "they were largely ignored at the time."[^2]

2. **1821 — Laurence's 1 Enoch, obscure (rate: moderate).** Archbishop Richard Laurence published the first English translation of 1 Enoch (the "Ethiopian version") in 1821, but it "didn't really make a big splash" — a limited-circulation London scholarly text unlikely to be in rural upstate New York. (Conceded: this version *did* technically predate Joseph; the apologetic weight is that the *matching* details are concentrated in the texts below, not in 1 Enoch.) See [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]].[^3]

3. **1832 — Joseph publishes (rate: fixed point).** Joseph's Book of Enoch material was published around 1832, "in the thick of it."[^4]

4. **2 Enoch — not translated until 1896 (rate: strong, chronology).** The Slavonic 2 Enoch, which supplies the "lad" descriptor and Enoch's journey/calling details, "wasn't translated until 1896" — well after Joseph's death, ruling it out as a source.[^5]

5. **3 Enoch — discovered 1832 but not published until 1892 (rate: strong, chronology).** The Greek 3 Enoch was discovered the same year Joseph published "but ... not deciphered and published until 1892," so "even if someone had found 3 Enoch back then, they couldn't have read it."[^6]

6. **Dead Sea Scrolls / Book of the Giants — from 1946 (rate: strong, chronology).** The Hebrew and Aramaic fragments, including the *Book of the Giants* (source of the **Mahijah/Mahawai** name), emerged starting 1946 — over a century after 1832. See [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch]].[^7]

> The structure is a "reverse anachronism": the matching content sits in texts that became readable only *after* Joseph, so borrowing is chronologically blocked for those specific parallels. Even the skeptic [[Harold Bloom]] found Joseph's access to such ancient concepts remarkable.[^8]

## Steelman of the Critic
The timeline argument is weaker than it looks if the matching details can be sourced to texts that *were* available. (a) 1 Enoch (Laurence 1821) existed, and a determined reader, or someone relaying its ideas, is not impossible — the "limited circulation" claim is a probability judgment, not a proof of inaccessibility. (b) Some "unique" parallels (weeping God, personified earth, secret combinations) have analogues in materials Joseph could access, including the Bible and contemporary religious culture. (c) An AI-narrated summary may compress or mis-state publication dates; the dates should be checked against primary bibliography before leaning hard on them. (d) The decisive items are the ones genuinely confined to post-1832 texts — above all the *Book of the Giants* Mahawai name — so the argument is strongest when narrowed to those, not to the whole parallel list.

## Best Quotes

> "The second Book of Enoch, the Slavonic version, that wasn't translated until 1896 ... so that rules that out as a source."[^5]

> "Third Enoch, the Greek version, discovered in 1832 — same year as Joseph Smith's publication — but not deciphered and published until 1892. It's like a puzzle within a puzzle."[^6]
