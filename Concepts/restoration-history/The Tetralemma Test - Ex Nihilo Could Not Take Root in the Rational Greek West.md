# The Tetralemma Test - Ex Nihilo Could Not Take Root in the Rational Greek West

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Brian Thomassen]] offers an indirect, historical proof that creation *ex nihilo* is irrational: he points to **Buddhism**. The Buddhist *tetralemma* (a four-cornered logic that affirms propositions like "X both is and is not" and "X neither is nor is-not") directly violates the Greek **law of the excluded middle** — for any proposition, either it or its negation must be true. Because the Greek mind, as the inventor of logic, was *bound* by that law, genuinely irrational claims could find no foothold there. This, Thomassen argues, is *why* Buddhism — despite ferocious missionary success across Asia — never penetrated the Greek West: "it hit the Greek mind and [the] wall with its Greek rationality and it couldn't." It only succeeded where the indigenous systems (e.g. Taoism, Confucianism) "were not as robust metaphysically."[^1][^2]

The payoff is by analogy: *ex nihilo* is *equally* irrational by the Parmenidean standard (it asserts the negative causation that "nothing comes from nothing" forbids — see [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]]). So it, too, could never have arisen from or been accepted by Greek philosophy. The only place an irrationality like this *did* take hold was *inside* the Christian space — which is exactly why a "baseline absurdity" ended up sitting at the core of creedal theology.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The excluded middle is non-negotiable for the Greeks
Thomassen sets the Aristotelian logical square (universal/particular affirmatives and negatives — all four of whose terms *exist*) against the Buddhist tetralemma. In the tetralemma, the third and fourth corners — "A and not-A" and "neither A nor not-A" — are, from a Greek perspective, "a fundamental violation" of the excluded middle: "any proposition, either it or its negation is true." Box three ("he is tall *and* he is not tall") and box four ("he is *not* tall, but he is also *not not* tall") are simply "an absurdity."[^1]

### 2. Logic gated which ideas could spread
Thomassen's historical-religious point: when "Buddhists come into that space, the Buddhist position is absurd... and so there was no grounding for it to take root, because on a baseline level, it's recognized as irrational." The same axiom that rejected the tetralemma is what would have rejected *ex nihilo* — "that which is irrational has no space within the Greek mind."[^2]

### 3. Buddhism succeeded only where metaphysics was thinner
The contrast case proves the mechanism. Buddhism spread explosively east (China, Korea, Japan, Southeast Asia) but stalled at the Greek frontier. Where it did take hold, "the indigenous belief systems... whether it's Taoism or Confucianism... were not as robust metaphysically, and that's why Buddhism was able to take hold." Two products of the axial age met and "they cannot occupy the same space. One of them is going to have to win" — and against Greek rationality, the irrational loses.[^4]

### 4. The Trinity is the parallel absurdity that *did* slip through
Thomassen treats the Trinity as the same kind of move: not Greek philosophy, but Greek *vocabulary* dressing up "the most literal thing possible" read off John's Passover discourse ("they're one... it's just the most literal thing possible dressed up [to sound like] Greek philosophy"). Like *ex nihilo*, it took hold *within* Christianity, not within the Greek philosophical world it borrowed words from. (For the related but distinct vault claim that the Trinity's key term was an imperial imposition, see [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].)[^5][^6]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A defender can press two points. First, the tetralemma is not obviously irrational: modern paraconsistent and many-valued logics (and arguably quantum superposition) formalize "both/neither" states coherently, so the "excluded middle" is a contingent feature of *classical* Greek logic, not a law of reality. Second, and more importantly, the analogy proves too much. Buddhism *did* fail to penetrate the West, but Christianity — *including ex nihilo and the Trinity* — *succeeded* spectacularly in that very same Greek-speaking world, and was defended for centuries by formidably rigorous Greek-trained minds (the Cappadocians, Augustine, the whole scholastic tradition). If *ex nihilo* were a flat violation of the excluded middle, it could not have commanded the assent of the most logically sophisticated culture in antiquity. So either it is *not* irrational, or "the Greek mind rejects the irrational" is false.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **The analogy is about origin, not survival.** Thomassen's claim is that an irrationality cannot *arise from* Greek philosophy or be accepted *as philosophy* — which is consistent with its later spreading as *revelation that rejects philosophy*. That is precisely the documented trajectory: second-century Christians first mimicked philosophical argument, then turned and said "philosophy itself is bad... our God can do anything, whereas your God is bound by reason." It conquered the West by *overruling* Greek rationality, not by satisfying it.[^7]
2. **Sophisticated defense ≠ rational premise.** That brilliant minds later systematized the doctrine shows ingenuity in *building on* a fixed premise, not that the premise is sound — the same can be said of any internally elaborated but ill-founded system (cf. the vault's treatment of tightly-reasoned theologies resting on a single bad starting move).
3. **Paraconsistent logic is anachronistic.** Whether 20th-century logics can model contradiction is irrelevant to whether *classical* Greek thought — the actual historical gatekeeper — could absorb it. By that standard, which is the one that governed antiquity, both the tetralemma and *ex nihilo* are out of bounds.
