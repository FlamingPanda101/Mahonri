# Early Saints Tied Women's Emancipation to the Restoration

## The Claim
[[Selene Anderson]] argues that 19th-century Latter-day Saints did not experience plural marriage as misogyny but explicitly **linked the advancement of women's rights to the Restoration** — believing the "curse of Eve" was being lifted "line upon line" in the dispensation of the fullness of times. The historical record of Utah women's public life, she says, contradicts the modern narrative of Latter-day Saint patriarchy-as-oppression.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Utah women voted first (rated: strong, historical — verify specifics).** Utah women were "the first in the United States to vote, 50 years before" the 19th Amendment; married women owned property; and they had unusually liberal divorce (see [[Polygamous Utah Had the Nations Easiest Divorce Especially for Women]]). Anderson credits the Better Days 2020 Foundation's research for surfacing this.[^2]

2. **George Q. Cannon's 1884 essay (rated: strong as a primary quotation; read on-air).** Anderson reads at length from [[George Q. Cannon]] in the *Juvenile Instructor* (1884): under plural marriage a woman "becomes in some respects the head of her family... compelled to assume responsibility... her full powers" are called forth, until "the penalty that was laid upon women in the beginning, that thy desire shall be to thy husband, and he shall rule over thee, will be repealed, and she will stand side by side with man." Plural marriage is cast as the *mechanism* of emancipation, not its enemy.[^3]

3. **A culture of female public achievement (rated: moderate, illustrative).** The *Woman's Exponent* (run by Emmeline B. Wells) advocated both women's rights and polygamy; women were "called and set apart" to earn medical degrees; the Deseret Hospital board was entirely women. The non-member visitor Mrs. (Elizabeth) Kane was "shocked" that these women had "all these different sorts of careers."[^4] Anchored to [[Eliza R. Snow]] and her circle as the engine of the "intellectual movement among" the sisters.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A historian could note this is a *selective* reading: Utah women's suffrage (1870) was also politically instrumental — it let the Mormon majority entrench its voting power, and Congress *revoked* it in 1887 (Edmunds–Tucker) partly to attack polygamy. Cannon's lofty rhetoric coexists with real constraints on the same women, and citing the most accomplished figures (Wells, Snow, women doctors) survivorship-biases away the ordinary plural wife's harder experience. The honest claim is that early Utah was *more* progressive on women's public roles than the surrounding culture — not that plural marriage was straightforwardly liberating for every woman in it.

## Counter-Objection
"Reading Eve's curse as 'repealed by polygamy' is post-hoc theology, not evidence." Anderson's reply is that the theology is *primary-source* — Cannon and his contemporaries said it themselves in 1884; the modern misogyny-narrative is the anachronism, projecting fundamentalist polygamy back onto a Utah culture that, on its own testimony, understood the practice as emancipatory.

## Best Quotes
> "Women in Utah were the first in the United States to vote, 50 years before that amendment. ... I was born and raised in Utah and no one has ever informed me of this."[^2]

> "The penalty that was laid upon women in the beginning, that thy desire shall be to thy husband, and he shall rule over thee, will be repealed, and she will stand side by side with man." (George Q. Cannon, 1884)[^3]

> "These women going off being called and set apart to go get medical degrees and become doctors... the Deseret Hospital, the board of directors was entirely women."[^4]
