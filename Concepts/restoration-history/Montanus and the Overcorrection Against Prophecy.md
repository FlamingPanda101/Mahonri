# Montanus and the Overcorrection Against Prophecy

## The Claim
The 2nd-century [[Montanus|Montanist]] crisis (c. 170 AD) marks the point where the institutional church *overcorrected* against living prophecy. Montanus was acknowledging a real loss — that the gifts of the Spirit and prophecy had waned — but the church responded not by seeking revelation again, but by declaring that **revelation and prophecy belong to the apostolic past**. This same logic later confronts Joseph Smith.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Montanus diagnosed a real absence (rating: moderate; historical)
Montanus, with the prophetesses Priscilla and Maximilla, had ecstatic visions and drew crowds wanting prophecy — at a time when "people are saying prophecy is in the past." He was naming the felt loss of spiritual gifts. [^1]

### 2. The wrong response cements the loss (rating: moderate; Day's thesis)
"Instead of responding and saying 'you're right, we don't have any revelation, maybe we should think about this,' the response is 'well, there is no revelation.' So the response actually takes us further away." Day calls this the "beginnings of God has kind of stopped speaking." [^2]

### 3. Direct line to Joseph Smith's minister (rating: moderate; LDS application)
When the boy Joseph reported that God spoke to him, the minister's rebuttal was that "all revelation has ceased with the apostles." Day: "There are bishops having that kind of conversation at about 170 AD." The cessationist reflex is ancient. [^3]

### 4. Brown's pendulum framework (rating: moderate; cited scholar)
[[Raymond Brown]] (*The Churches the Apostles Left Behind*): every church swings between **central control** and **charisma** (gifts of the Spirit). Too much charisma yields chaos (everyone a prophet, "Hiram Page scooting around on the floor"); too much control "strangles off people's experience of the Spirit." Montanism pushed the pendulum hard toward central control. Butler argues the modern LDS church balances the two via the priesthood-line / personal-line model. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The cessationist case (then and now) is that the apostolic sign-gifts were authenticating credentials for *foundational* revelation (Eph 2:20, the church "built upon the foundation of the apostles and prophets"), which closed once the foundation and the canon were complete — so Montanus's "new prophecy" was rightly rejected as adding to a finished deposit, and his movement's excesses (datable false predictions, fanaticism) vindicated the church's caution. Day's reply: rejecting *Montanus* did not require denying *all* future revelation, and that broader denial is the overcorrection that constitutes part of the apostasy.

## Best Quotes

> "He's acknowledging something everybody is saying is happening, but the response... is 'well, there is no revelation.' So the response actually takes us further away." [^2]

> "Any spiritual organization has a pendulum... between central control at one end and charisma — the gifts of the Spirit — at the other." [^4]
