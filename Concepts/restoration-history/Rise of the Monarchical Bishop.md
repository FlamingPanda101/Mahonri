# Rise of the Monarchical Bishop

## The Claim
The office of the single ruling bishop over a city (the "monarchical episcopate") was not an original apostolic institution but a development that solidified in the 2nd century, hardened in the 3rd, and fused with imperial political power in the 4th. The Greek *episkopos* ("overseer") describes a function (watching over the flock) that crystallized into an office as Christianity emerged from synagogue structures.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Etymology: overseer, not monarch (rating: strong; lexical)
*Episkopos* = *epi* ("over/upon," as in *epidermis*) + *skopos* ("watcher"). Day's LDS analogy: a ward bishop sits "up on the stand," watching over the congregation, prompted by the Spirit. The word originally names a pastoral function. [^1]

### 2. A 1st-century local leadership, often elected (rating: moderate; historical + Didache)
In the 1st century the leader was local and frequently *elected* ("we really like Jackson, he's going to be our bishop"), per Didache ch. 15. There was "an absence of a centralized authority" but real local authority. See [[The Didache as Earliest Church Manual]]. [^2]

### 3. The trajectory: local → monarchical → imperial (rating: moderate; periodization)
Day's timeline: 1st-century local leaders → 2nd-century **monarchical bishops** (one main bishop per city) → 3rd-century well-established sees → 4th-century bishops as instruments of empire/political power, with larger cities (Rome, Antioch, Alexandria, Constantinople, Jerusalem) commanding more powerful sees. [^3]

### 4. Possible late/pseudonymous warrant (rating: moderate; critical scholarship)
The Pastorals (which describe the *episkopos*) may be a 2nd-century composition not by Paul, per the scholarship Day cites — consistent with the office being a later institutional crystallization rather than a dominical appointment. See [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles]]. [^1]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
[[Ignatius of Antioch]] (c. 107 AD) already presupposes the single bishop as the center of church unity — "do nothing without the bishop" — which is early enough that defenders argue the monarchical episcopate is apostolic in origin, simply attested as it matured. The Pastorals' *episkopos* and the appointment of elders in Acts/Titus are read as the seed of the institution. Day's reply: even Ignatius argues *for* obeying bishops (a sign it was being contested), and the office's fusion with imperial power is plainly a later, corrupting development.

## Best Quotes

> "By the second century we have what's called monarchical bishops, meaning that in a city there would be a main bishop there." [^3]

> "An episkopos... they are the ones that are overseers." [^1]

> "They were elected, like you would elect, 'Hey, we really like Jackson, he's going to be our bishop.'" [^2]
