# The Living Prophet Versus the Standard Works Are Two Competing LDS Authority Traditions

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] documents that Latter-day Saint leaders have articulated **two opposing traditions** on what is supreme when prophet and scripture conflict. One exalts the *living prophet* over the canon; the other subordinates prophets *to* the canon. Both are quoted from prophets and apostles — so the tension is internal to LDS authority discourse, not a critic's invention.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The living-prophet-supreme strand (strong)
- **Brigham Young** (relayed by Wilford Woodruff, 1897): when compared with the living oracles, "those books [the scriptures] are nothing to me; I would rather have the living oracles."[^1]
- **Ezra Taft Benson** (1980): "Beware of those who pit the dead prophets against the living prophets, for the living prophets always take precedence."[^1]
- **Orson F. Whitney** (1916, repeated 1976): the Saints do not do things because they are "printed in a book... No book presides over this church and no book lies at its foundation."[^1]

### 2. The scripture-supreme strand (strong)
- **Harold B. Lee** and **Joseph Fielding Smith**: if a prophet "writes something which is out of harmony with the revelations, then every member of the church is duty bound to reject it."[^2]
- **Bruce R. McConkie** (*Mormon Doctrine*): "prophets do not rank with the standard works."[^2]
- **[[Dallin H. Oaks]]** (Oct. 2019): "Not every statement made by a church leader, past or present, necessarily constitutes doctrine. A single statement... often represents a personal, though well-considered, opinion, but is not meant to be binding."[^2]

### 3. The historical test case (medium)
Hatch notes Joseph F. Smith's handling of Brigham Young's Adam-God teaching: within ~15 years of Young's death, Joseph F. Smith said Young "was speaking as a man," and the test he applied was *canonical process* — Young's view was never "brought to the councils, presented to the council of the church, and voted on" and accepted into the canon.[^3] (Cross-ref [[Was Adam-God Official Binding Teaching]].)

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A defender of clean prophetic supremacy argues the two strands are *reconcilable*, not contradictory: the living prophet never contradicts *canonized* revelation but interprets and applies it for the present, so "living oracles take precedence" governs *application* while "reject what contradicts the revelations" governs the rare case of a leader's private error. There is no real conflict — only different emphases for different situations.

## Counter-Objection
Hatch grants the harmonization is *available* but argues it does not dissolve the practical problem: the two strands hand ordinary members *opposite default postures* when a tension arises — "follow the living prophet, full stop" vs. "test the teaching against scripture and reject it if it fails." Which default a member adopts changes the outcome in exactly the disputed cases (caffeine, Adam-God, the 14 Fundamentals), and the tradition supplies prophetic quotations for *both* defaults. So the conflict is real at the level of *decision procedure*, even if a clever synthesis exists in the abstract. This is why Hatch ultimately proposes a *stewardship* boundary (see [[Prophetic Stewardship Is Over the Institution and Ordinances Not Over All Truth]]).
