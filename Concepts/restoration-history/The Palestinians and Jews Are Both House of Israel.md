# The Palestinians and Jews Are Both House of Israel

## The Claim

On [[Ward Radio]], historian [[Don Bradley]] argues that the modern Israeli–Palestinian conflict is, in part, a conflict *within* the House of Israel — because the **Palestinians descend largely from the Samaritans**, who themselves descend from the northern (ten-tribe) Israelites left in the land after the Assyrian conquest. The chain: when Assyria conquered the northern kingdom (~722 BC), some Israelites who remained intermarried with imported Assyrian settlers to become the **Samaritans**; the Samaritans were a populous people for centuries (estimated ~2 million by the 700s AD), known throughout Jesus' lifetime and after; most were then **forcibly converted to Islam** in the centuries following the rise of Islam, leaving only a few hundred self-identifying Samaritans today. The descendants of those converted Samaritans, living in the region of Samaria (part of today's Palestinian territory), are the **Palestinians**. Therefore "when the Jews and the Palestinians are fighting, they're actually both descendants of the House of Israel."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Samaritans' origin and persistence is mainstream history — rate: strong
That the Samaritans arose from the mixing of remnant northern Israelites with peoples resettled by Assyria (cf. 2 Kings 17), that they remained a substantial community into the early Islamic period, and that they were drastically reduced (today numbering only several hundred) is well-attested. Bradley presents this as the uncontroversial backbone of the argument.[^1]

### 2. Regional continuity of population — rate: moderate
The inference that today's Palestinians include descendants of the long-resident Samaritan/Israelite population of Samaria is a reasonable demographic continuity claim: populations of a region carry forward ancestry across conquests and conversions. It coheres with the panel's broader point that diaspora Israel repeatedly blended into host populations ([[Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent]]).[^1]

### 3. It reframes the "lost" tribes as partly *found* — rate: moderate (apologetic)
The claim supports the episode's thesis that the ten tribes are not wholly vanished: some are visibly identifiable (the Samaritan-descended Palestinians), which feeds the LDS doctrine of a literal, traceable House of Israel being gathered — see [[The Gathering of the Lost Ten Tribes]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic notes the genealogy is broad-brush. (a) **Palestinian ancestry is mixed and contested**: the modern Palestinian population descends from a long succession of peoples in the Levant — Canaanite, Israelite, Samaritan, Greek, Roman, Arab and others — so "descendants of the Samaritans" captures one strand among many, not a clean lineage; the same is true of modern Jews. (b) **Population genetics complicates "House of Israel" as a biological category** — both Jewish and Palestinian populations show deep shared Levantine ancestry, which arguably *undercuts* tidy tribe-by-tribe descent more than it confirms it. (c) **The doctrinal payoff doesn't require the biology**: even if exact descent is unprovable, the LDS gathering is covenantal/adoptive ([[Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent]]), so the point can be made theologically without the demographic claim. A Latter-day Saint reply: precisely because the lineage is messy and shared, Bradley's framing is irenic rather than triumphalist — its force is moral (two peoples at war are kin) and it illustrates that "lost" Israel did not evaporate but assimilated into known populations, which is all the gathering doctrine needs.
