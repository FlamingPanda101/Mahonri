# Information from a Mind - The DNA Code in Debate

## The Point
Meyer's foundational premise, which he developed during his Cambridge PhD and laid out in *Signature in the Cell*, is that "information comes from an intelligent source." Tracing any information back to its origin — "a hieroglyphic inscription, a paragraph in a book, a headline in a newspaper, or a section of code in a software program" — we always arrive at "a mind, not a material process."[^1] The digital code along the spine of the DNA molecule, together with the cell's information-processing machinery, is therefore "decisive evidence of intelligent design."[^2] Atkins denies the premise outright — "that's absolutely wrong" — insisting information can grow without an agent and that the order/information distinction is a confusion.[^3] The exchange turns on whether *macroevolution* requires *new* information and whether any known mechanism can supply it.

## Evidence
1. **The premise stated.** "What we know from our experience is that information always arises from an intelligent source."[^4] The discovery of "the digital code stored along the spine of the DNA molecule" raised the question: "Where did it come from?"[^1]
2. **Atkins' denial.** "That's absolutely wrong, and that's where I want to engage you... there are numerous examples of structures emerging without an agent actually generating the structure."[^3]
3. **New information vs. modified information.** Atkins says DNA "changes by accident... by mistakes in copying," some of which give an advantage and propagate.[^5] Meyer and Haville press the distinction: "I'm not talking about changes, I'm talking about novel new information being introduced." The question is "not how you modify existing information, it's how you get the information in the first place to get life going."[^6]
4. **The software analogy.** "If you want to give your computer program a new function, what do you have to give it? You have to give it digital code. To get life going you need digital information in the DNA molecule."[^7]
5. **Even Dawkins concedes the gap.** Meyer notes Dawkins acknowledged "at the very end of *Expelled*" that "no one knows from an evolutionary point of view how life first began," because the field cannot account for the *origin* of the information needed to get life going.[^8]
6. **The falsifiability test.** Meyer offers a concession of his own: if scientists produced a genuine explanation for the origin of life's information, "we would get on board with it" and not "close [our] doors" — answering Atkins' charge of a "hidebound prejudice."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "Whenever we trace information back to its source we always come to a mind, not a material process." [^1]

> "I'm not talking about changes, I'm talking about novel new information being introduced." [^6]

> "That's absolutely wrong... there are numerous examples of structures emerging without an agent." (Atkins) [^3]

## LDS Connection
The inference from coded information to a mind is a congenial natural-theology resource for Latter-day Saints, who affirm a designed, ordered cosmos and a Creator. The fit is broad rather than specific: the argument lands on a generic "intelligent source," not the distinctively LDS God who *organizes* eternal, uncreated matter and intelligences rather than creating from nothing. An LDS reader can also affirm Meyer's intellectual posture — that the inference is held provisionally and would yield to a genuine naturalistic account — as consonant with a faith that prizes both study and revelation (D&C 88:118).
