# Didache

The **Didache** (Greek for "teaching"; full title *The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles*) is a short early-Christian church manual, usually dated to the **late 1st or early 2nd century**, making it among the oldest non-canonical Christian writings. It opens with the "Two Ways" (the way of life and the way of death) moral catechism, then gives practical instructions on **baptism** (preferring running water, allowing pouring three times when immersion is impossible), fasting, the **Eucharist** (with thanksgiving prayers over cup and bread), and **church order** — how to test itinerant prophets and apostles, and the appointment of bishops and deacons. Lost for centuries, it was rediscovered in the *Codex Hierosolymitanus* (1873, published 1883). It is prized as a window onto how the earliest post-apostolic congregations actually *worshiped and governed* themselves.

## Relevance
The Didache supplies primary-source texture for the vault's **apostasy** and **early-Christianity** arguments. Because it shows liturgy and church structure in flux so early, it is evidence that practice and offices *developed* over time rather than springing fully formed — material the vault uses when arguing that later doctrines/structures are post-apostolic accretions. It also figures concretely in [[Calling Scripture the Word of God Is a Late Patristic Development]], where [[Trent Horn]] cites the Didache among the earliest fathers who use "the word of God" to mean the spoken Christian *message* (not a written corpus), supporting the claim that "Scripture = the word of God" is a *later* patristic development. Its early baptism and Eucharist instructions are likewise useful comparanda for ordinance-restoration discussions.
