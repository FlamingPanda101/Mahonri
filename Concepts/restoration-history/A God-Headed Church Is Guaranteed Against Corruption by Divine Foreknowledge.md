# A God-Headed Church Is Guaranteed Against Corruption by Divine Foreknowledge

## The Claim

Pressed by the student to admit that the LDS Church will eventually corrupt like every other institution, [[Hayden Carroll]] grants the **distinction between individual and organizational corruption** (an abusive bishop is an unavoidable "human flaw in the system," not "an organizational widespread acceptance of malfeasance" [^1]) and then offers the load-bearing exception: a church that is *genuinely headed by God* cannot become corrupt at the institutional level "because he heads it... unless Jesus can become corrupt." [^2] The student lands the strongest available counter: **"it is God's church but it is run by men all the way to the top."** [^2] Carroll's solution is a **foreknowledge-and-calling argument**: "Can God call someone who will be faithful? If he knows the future, he can call someone who's going to do that." [^2] Because an omniscient God knows in advance which men will remain faithful, He can staff His church — top to bottom — with leaders He foreknows will not apostatize, so the "run by men" objection does not reach a *divinely* governed institution. Carroll frames this as internally "solvable with our theology" while openly conceding that "from a critical lens, you're exactly right" *if* the divine-headship premise is dropped (the conditional developed in [[Institutional Corruption Is Not Merely a Function of an Institution's Age]]). [^2]

This is a narrower and more mechanism-specific claim than the usual "gates of hell shall not prevail" indefectibility argument (see [[The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church]]): it is not exegesis of a proof-text but a *theodicy of institutional incorruptibility* grounded in divine foreknowledge plus the power of calling. It also coexists with LDS teaching that prophets are **fallible** ([[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]]) — the claim is not that called leaders are sinless or err-free, but that God will not permit the *institution as a whole* to be captured by sanctioned evil, because He selects and (if needed) replaces its heads.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The individual/organizational distinction is principled, not ad hoc.** Carroll readily concedes that "because we have humans, we are probably going to have... bishops who abuse that power" and will "call that corruption, sure" — what he denies is "an organizational widespread like acceptance of malfeasance like what's going on in the Vatican." [^1] The category line (rogue individuals vs. institutionally sanctioned wrongdoing) is exactly the line that the age-of-corruption objection blurs, and it is the line a believer must hold to say a church can be sound while its members sin.
2. **Foreknowledge plus calling is a coherent mechanism.** Granting classical omniscience, the argument is valid: if God knows future free choices, He can issue callings only to those He foresees will remain faithful, and can remove or forestall those who would not — so the inference "men run it, therefore it will corrupt" is blocked, because the *selection* of those men is not left to chance or to human politics. It parallels the LDS conviction that the Lord "will not suffer" His prophet to lead the church astray. This is what Carroll means by "it's solvable with our theology." [^2]
3. **It concedes the critical case honestly and isolates one premise.** Carroll does not claim the mechanism is *demonstrable* from the outside; he repeatedly grants that "from a critical lens, you're exactly right" and that absent divine headship corruption is likely. [^2] The argument's strength is its modesty: everything rides on the single, separately-argued claim that this *is* God's church (which Carroll elsewhere stakes on the Book of Mormon and living prophets, cf. [[You Can Only Follow Jesus by Joining the Church He Commands]]), not on any implausible claim that LDS leaders are humanly incorruptible.

**Steelman of the critic.** (a) **Unfalsifiable insulation.** "God foreknows and calls only the faithful" cannot be checked empirically and conveniently re-describes *any* future scandal as either an "individual flaw" (quarantined) or a sign the church was never God's — so the thesis predicts nothing and risks being unfalsifiable special pleading. (b) **The foreknowledge–freedom tension.** If God reliably ensures only never-falling men reach the top, in what sense are their callings, or their faithfulness, *freely* chosen? The mechanism may purchase incorruptibility at the cost of the libertarian free will LDS theology elsewhere prizes — or, if the leaders remain free, then God's foreknowledge does not *guarantee* anything, only predicts it, and a free leader could still fall. (c) **The track-record problem.** LDS history already contains leadership-level reversals a critic will read as institutional error (e.g., the priesthood-and-temple ban and its 1978 reversal); the believer must classify these as non-corrupting "human flaws" or developmental, which looks like moving the line to wherever the theory needs it. (d) **Parity.** Catholics make the *same* "Christ heads the church, the gates of hell will not prevail" move; if the LDS apologist rejects Catholic indefectibility (per the Restoration's own apostasy claim), he cannot consistently treat divine headship as an automatic anti-corruption guarantee — which suggests headship alone is not sufficient, and human response still matters.
