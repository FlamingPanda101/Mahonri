# First Wives Could Be Coerced by the Law of Sarah

## The Claim
Asked whether women were "forced or coerced into plural marriages," historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] gives a per-story answer — and then names the group most exposed to coercion: not prospective plural wives but **first wives**. [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]]'s "law of Sarah" encouraged a wife to consent to her husband taking a plural wife, but provided that if she refused "without a good reason," the husband "was excused" and could marry anyway — putting a first wife in "a very uncomfortable position."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Coercion was real but varied.** Some women "did feel forced or coerced," through "social or family pressure," and "this wasn't just women — men also felt that pressure," especially those holding ecclesiastical position who were "asked to participate... to show an example." There was also genuine "theological pressure" for those who "wanted to be all in."[^2] *Rating: strong — Nash affirms coercion existed while resisting the blanket "all women were forced" claim; see [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]] for the male side.*

2. **Women fiercely rejected the "duped sheep" stereotype.** The period caricature that Latter-day Saint women were "unthinking beings who were duped" "infuriated them." Nash reads May Talmage's 1883 *Woman's Exponent* letter ("An Indignant Mormon Mother"): "I entered into plural marriage of my own free will because I knew it was right... where is the man or set of men who could compel them?"[^3] *Rating: strong — a verbatim primary source in the women's own publication.*

3. **The law of Sarah's coercive edge.** D&C 132 "gave an exception that if a woman refused her husband to have a plural wife without a good reason... the man was excused from the law of Sarah and could marry anyway" — and "who judges whether or not a reason is good enough?" Nash's own polygamous first-wife ancestor, interviewed in the 1930s, "said she gave her consent but she was human and resented it."[^4] *Rating: strong — the text of D&C 132 is explicit; the ancestor testimony is firsthand.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A defender of the practice can stress that, in practice, "the most successful marriages were those made in unison," that many husbands "heeded the counsel" of a reluctant wife, and that Utah's liberal divorce gave unhappy first wives an exit. But this does not blunt the structural point a critic presses: a revelation that lets a husband proceed *over his wife's objection* whenever an authority deems her reason insufficient encodes coercion into the system itself, regardless of how kindly individuals behaved. The candid conclusion — which Nash herself reaches — is that first-wife coercion was a genuine, scripture-backed feature ("heartbreaking then... now"), not merely an abuse by bad actors.

## Best Quotes
> "If a woman refused her husband to have a plural wife without a good reason... the man was excused from the law of Sarah and could marry anyway. So it put first wives in a very uncomfortable position."[^4]
