# Plato's Myth of Er and the NDE

## The Point
Moody argues that [[Plato]] (428-348 BC), in the *Phaedo*, *Gorgias*, and especially Book X of *The Republic*, describes death in terms "precisely like" modern near-death reports [^1]. For Plato death is the separation of the incorporeal soul from the body; the freed soul is less limited, thinks more clearly, meets departed spirits and guardian guides, and faces a "judgment" in which a divine being displays all its deeds — and in the **Myth of Er** a soldier returns from the funeral pyre to report the realms beyond [^2]. Plato also anticipates the NDE's two great obstacles: the unreliability of the bodily senses and the inadequacy of human language [^3].

## Evidence
1. **Death as separation of soul from body.** Plato defines death exactly so, with the soul subject to "many fewer limitations"; notably, "time is not an element" of the realms beyond — what we call time is the "moving, unreal reflection of eternity" [^1].
2. **Meeting spirits and guides; the crossing.** The separated soul may "meet and converse with the departed spirits of others" and be guided by guardian spirits; some expect a boat to carry them across water to "the other shore" [^1].
3. **Death as awakening, not sleep.** In *Phaedo* the body is "the prison of the soul"; Plato inverts the old view — *birth* is the sleeping and forgetting, and death an awakening and remembering, the soul thinking and reasoning more clearly than before [^4].
4. **The judgment / life review.** Soon after death the soul faces a "judgment" in which "a divine being displays before the soul all the things — both good and bad — which it has done," paralleling the NDE [[NDE - The Life Review|life review]] [^4].
5. **The Myth of Er (Republic X).** Er, a slain soldier, revives on the funeral pyre and reports: his soul left his body, joined other spirits, reached "openings" leading to the afterlife where souls were judged by beings who saw "at a glance" all they had done; Er was sent back to tell the living what the other world was like [^5].
6. **Plato's two caveats.** He labels the details "probabilities, at best": the imprisoned soul is fooled by the physical senses, and human language "conceals rather than reveals" — truth can be indicated only "by analogy, through myth, and in other indirect ways" [^6].

## Best Quotes
> "the moving, unreal reflection of eternity." [^1]

> Er "was not judged. Instead, the beings told him that he must go back to inform men in the physical world concerning what the other world was like." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Plato's pre-mortal, more-conscious soul that "comes into the physical body from a higher and more divine realm" and *forgets* at birth strikingly anticipates the LDS doctrines of premortal existence and the veil of forgetfulness (Abr. 3; Alma 40). His commissioned messenger Er — sent back specifically to testify of the next world — parallels the Restoration's pattern of visionaries returning to bear witness. His judgment scene, where a divine being lays bare every deed, fits the LDS teaching of a perfect bright recollection of all our guilt (Alma 11:43; 2 Ne. 9:14).
