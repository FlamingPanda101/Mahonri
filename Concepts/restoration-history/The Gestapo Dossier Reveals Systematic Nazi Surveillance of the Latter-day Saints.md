# The Gestapo Dossier Reveals Systematic Nazi Surveillance of the Latter-day Saints

## The Claim

A roughly 500-page archival dossier in the German Federal Archives (Bundesarchiv) in Berlin — produced by the Sicherheitsdienst (SD), the Nazi party/SS intelligence service tied to the Gestapo — documents that the Nazi state placed the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints under **systematic, coordinated, Reich-wide surveillance** between 1933 and 1939. [[Stephen Smoot]], who translated and analyzed the material for the [[BH Roberts]] Foundation, argues this "significantly alters our... contextualized understanding" of LDS history in Nazi Germany, because the watching was not incidental but a deliberate intelligence operation against a religious minority.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Scale and provenance.** The dossier comprises about 500 pages — memoranda, notes, letters, reports, confiscated material, and photographs — held in the Bundesarchiv and (to Smoot's mild disappointment as a researcher hoping for a Berlin trip) already digitized online. It originates with the SD, the intelligence arm roping together the SS and Gestapo, and spans 1933 to roughly November 1939, stopping shortly after the invasion of Poland.[^2]

2. **It was effectively unstudied.** Smoot found that only one prior author had so much as cited the material — in a single offhand footnote noting "a dossier on the Mormons" in the Berlin archives — and no one had worked through its content in published scholarship, which is what prompted his investigation.[^3]

3. **The surveillance was systematic, not a one-off.** "The surveillance is throughout Germany... it is systematic. It is coordinated. It is in multiple cities. It is under multiple jurisdictions... throughout the Reich is where the saints are being surveilled." Smoot catalogs the reports in both the published and online versions.[^4]

4. **Agents inside meetings — including Grant's.** SD/Gestapo members attended LDS services and reported on them clinically ("then they said their prayers and their prayers were weird"). When [[Heber J. Grant]] toured Germany in 1937, the Gestapo planted informants in his audiences — including a "Fräulein" in Berlin taking notes on his remarks. See [[Heber J. Grant Before the Swastika Flag Is a Decontextualized Cheap Shot]].[^5]
