# LDS Multi-Translation Policy Shift

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] reports that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints "recently implemented a new Bible translation policy" endorsing **multiple translations**: the KJV remains the official English Bible, but the church now recommends others "for different reading levels," including the **NRSVue** and the **NIV**.[^1] McClellan, a former scripture translation supervisor for the church, says he "had some influence on that decision."[^2] He frames the shift as the church catching English speakers up to a reality non-English speakers have lived with for generations. See [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A trajectory from no policy to KJV-default to pluralism — rate: strong
McClellan traces the history: early Latter-day Saints had "no kind of de jure translation that was correct" — you "could use whatever translation you like";[^3] the KJV became the *de facto* default and then a near-dogmatic identity ("we're King James Version people") when critical-text translations appeared; and now the church is "moving away from that and saying, hey, try other translations."[^4] This internal evolution illustrates the episode's thesis that even a single tradition's "Bible" is a contingent, revisable choice. **Steelman of a confessional reader:** the policy can be read not as conceding relativism but as a pragmatic accommodation for comprehension while preserving doctrinal anchoring in the KJV — i.e., the church still has *a* preferred Bible. McClellan's point is narrower: the move admits no translation is uniquely correct.

### 2. The driver was non-English translation work — rate: strong
"A large part of it was the fact that we were trying to provide translations into other languages."[^2] McClellan notes the naive assumption "don't we just have the King James Version in our language?" misunderstands what a translation is; outside English (and a few English-adjacent creoles/pidgins) Bibles are translated from sources, not from the KJV — the Almeida (Portuguese), Reina-Valera (Spanish), Russian versions, etc., "differ."[^5] When a General Conference talk leaned on an English KJV reading, "the other languages, their Bible say something different," forcing the church to grapple with translational fuzziness.[^6] See [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]].

### 3. The NIV "plug my nose" anecdote — rate: moderate
McClellan recounts that in translation testing the NIV scored best on "language register and style," "even above the KJV," and he "had to plug my nose" reporting it.[^7] This is a candid admission that translation evaluation involves measurable criteria (register, readability) even where he personally dislikes the result — a useful counterweight to pure subjectivism (see [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]]). Rate moderate as it is a personal recollection.

## Best Quotes
> "My tradition, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, recently implemented a new Bible translation policy where they're now endorsing multiple translations. The KJV is still the official one, but they're now saying, hey, go wild."[^1]

> "We've moved from the Latter-day Saints started off without any kind of de jure translation that was correct... and now we're moving away from that and saying, hey, try other translations."[^4]

> "The King James Version is an English translation of the Bible... 'I don't think you know what a Bible translation is.'"[^5]
