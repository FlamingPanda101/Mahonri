# Drop the Word of Wisdom Cliches

## The Claim
Before defending sacramental water, the [[Salty Saints - Why Water Is Better Than Wine in the Sacrament|Salty Saints]] panel issues a methodological caution: stop teaching the familiar Word-of-Wisdom cliches used to explain why Jesus and the ancients drank wine. "None of this is true and it doesn't help" — these "copes" set young members up to feel deceived when they later learn better. The gospel "is strong enough on its own."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The specific cliches disowned (strong, enumerated).** The panel names them: that Jesus only drank unfermented grape juice (so he wasn't "sinning"); that ancient wine was lower alcohol than modern wine; that they drank wine because water was unsafe ("they could have found clean enough water"); and that drunkenness mattered less anciently. "These are all the cliches… frankly untrue. These are lies."[^1]
2. **The pastoral harm (interpretive).** The danger is generational: "when your kids grow up and learn [the falsity] of these statements," trust erodes. Teaching shaky apologetics is worse than teaching none, because it manufactures a future faith crisis.[^1]
3. **Confidence in the underlying doctrine (moderate).** "The gospel is strong enough on its own. You can stand on it… It's not moving. You don't need copes like this." The honest move is to drop the bad arguments without fearing for the doctrine.[^1]
4. **Consistency with honest method elsewhere (interpretive).** This mirrors the broader faithful-apologetics posture of conceding weak arguments and over-claims (cf. [[Drop the Word of Wisdom Cliches|this caution]]'s kinship with how other faithful channels correct their own side), strengthening credibility by self-correction.

## Apologetic Engagement
A defender of the traditional talking points might argue some contain a kernel of truth (ancient wine *was* often diluted; water *could* be unsafe in some settings), so calling them flatly "lies" overcorrects. The fair reply is the panel's own: the cliches are deployed as *load-bearing apologetic claims* ("so Jesus wasn't sinning"), and in that role they are false or unsupported — Jesus drank real, fermented wine, and the Word of Wisdom's force does not depend on retconning antiquity. Whatever partial truths exist do not rescue the *use* to which the cliches are put.

## LDS Relevance
The concept is a piece of *apologetic hygiene* for Latter-day Saints: it protects rising-generation faith by refusing to build it on debunkable folklore, and it clears the ground for the episode's actual case (water as a rich symbol; the sacrament as sustenance) which does not need the cliches. It also models the self-correcting honesty that makes faithful commentary trustworthy.

## Best Quotes
> "Don't tell your kids that wine back then was lower alcohol content than modern wine… none of this is true and it doesn't help."[^1]

> "The gospel is strong enough on its own… You don't need copes like this, which is just frankly untrue. These are lies."[^1]
