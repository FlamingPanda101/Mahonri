# Adoptionism and Docetism — The Two Christological Extremes

## The Point
Before the great councils, second-century Christians wrestled with whether Jesus was human, divine, or both. Two extreme answers were declared heresies: **adoptionism** (Jesus was a mere righteous human "adopted" as God's Son) and **docetism** (Jesus only *appeared* human but was wholly divine). Between them, mainstream Christians affirmed — paradoxically — that Jesus was *both* fully human and fully divine, but **not** half-and-half like a Greek demigod. This framing sets up the whole Nicaea-to-Chalcedon trajectory.

## Evidence
1. The starting question is Peter's confession (Matthew 16:16) and Paul's hymn (Philippians 2:6–7): Jesus was "in the form of God" yet "made in the likeness of men" — "understood as somehow both human and divine."[^1]
2. **Psilanthropism / adoptionism:** some held Jesus "was a mere human being (*psilanthropism*; from Greek *psilōs*, 'merely,' and *anthrōpos*, 'human') who was so righteous that God adopted him to be his son (*adoptionism*) at baptism or the Resurrection."[^2]
3. **Docetism:** others held Jesus "was so divine and incorruptible that he would not or could not take upon himself a corruptible mortal human body … He appeared to be human when in fact he was only divine (*docetism*; from Greek *dokeō*, 'to seem' or 'to appear')."[^3]
4. "Both adoptionism and docetism were declared heresies."[^3]
5. The mainstream affirmed Jesus "fully both human and God" — explicitly *not* "half-human and half-god like the demigods of Greek and Roman mythology."[^4]
6. The soteriological stakes: per [[Gregory of Nazianzus]], "For that which [Christ] has not assumed, he has not saved" — full humanity must be assumed for humanity to be fully redeemed.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> Some held Jesus "was a mere human being … who was so righteous that God adopted him to be his son … Others insisted that Jesus was so divine … that he … appeared to be human when in fact he was only divine. … Both adoptionism and docetism were declared heresies." [^2]

> "For that which [Christ] has not assumed, he has not saved." — [[Gregory of Nazianzus]] [^5]

## LDS Connection
The author warns Latter-day Saints against *casually* describing Christ as "half-human and half-God," which Trinitarian Christians would hear as a Hercules-style demigod and which could "denigrate the power of the Atonement" — "If Jesus is only half human, then he only redeemed half of what it means to be human."[^6] LDS and Nicene Christians can jointly affirm Jesus "is fully human and fully divine."
