# Pace Interviewed Self-Selected Therapy Patients Not a Cross-Section

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A "sloppy read" of the Pace memo makes it sound as if Bishop Pace randomly sampled ordinary Latter-day Saints and discovered they had all been abused by Satanists — implying the abuse was pervasive across the membership. Ward Radio Part 3 rebuts this: his 60 interviewees were a pre-filtered, self-selected therapy population, not a cross-section.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The names came pre-selected from therapy intake (strong)
Pace "already had the names of self-selected and self-reported victims," supplied by LDS Social Services, where patrons "had been given counseling by therapists … and had reported recovered memory of abuse." He began immediately because "he already had a list of names." Pace "indicates that he has not seen anyone who was not already seeking help" [^1].

### 2. Explicitly not representative (strong — the core point)
"The 60 people Pace interviewed were not chosen at random. They were not a cross-section of membership at large. They were not representative of the Latter-day Saints. They were 60 people who used LDS Social Services for therapy and reported recovered memories of horrific abuse" [^2].

### 3. A near-universal clinical profile (strong — selection artifact)
"All 60 of the interviewees … had psychological problems. Most of them had been diagnosed as having multiple personality disorder or some other form of dissociative disorder." A sample drawn from dissociative-disorder therapy will, unsurprisingly, report dissociative-disorder content [^3].

### 4. The institution made SRA reports statistically inevitable (moderate — base-rate context)
LDS Social Services was "gigantic" — 63 offices by 1990, serving members and non-members alike across many service lines. "It would be highly suspicious if somehow LDS Social Services did not receive reports of SRA along with every other therapist and counseling service in America at that time." Receiving such reports is therefore expected background noise, not evidence of a real abuse ring. See [[The Satanic Panic Reached the LDS Church in Utah]] [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A defender of the memo could argue that abuse victims are *necessarily* self-selected — people disclose abuse precisely by seeking help — so a therapy population is exactly where real victims would appear, and dismissing them as "not a cross-section" risks discounting genuine suffering. The rebuttal: the objection is not to listening to patients but to the inference that 60 dissociative-disorder therapy clients reporting recovered satanic memories demonstrates *church-wide* satanic crime. The sample can establish that these individuals were suffering; it cannot establish the prevalence the memo's readers attribute to it.
