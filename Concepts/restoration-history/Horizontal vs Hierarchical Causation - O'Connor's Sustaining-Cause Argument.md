# Horizontal vs Hierarchical Causation - O'Connor's Sustaining-Cause Argument

## The Claim (skeptical)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] — surveying the strongest case *for* God — argues that the first-cause argument is widely misunderstood and, properly stated, is "probably the most powerful argument for the existence of God." The mistake is to picture causation **horizontally**, as a chain running *backward in time* (dominoes, ancestors), where the argument looks weak because the series might simply regress forever with no first member. The powerful version is **hierarchical**: a *present-tense* chain in which each member's causal power is *borrowed* from the layer beneath it and is lost the instant that layer is removed. Such a chain cannot regress infinitely — an infinite series of *borrowers* with no lender would have "literally no causal power in this instant right now" — so it must terminate in a **sustaining cause** that imparts power without borrowing it, holding the whole chain in being *now*.[^1] Notably, O'Connor presents this as a genuinely strong argument an honest agnostic should respect, while insisting it establishes only "some kind of fundamental sustaining principle," not yet "capital-G God."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The glass-of-water illustration.** "Why is this water here? Because it's being held up by the glass… the glass by my hand, my hand by my arm… the chair by the ground." Remove any link and "everything further up the chain than that would instantly lose its causal power… Instantly." So "there is no actual causal power except for whatever is at the basis of this giving the whole chain causal power."[^1] *(A vivid restatement of the per-se / essentially-ordered series; see [[Why a Hierarchical Series Cannot Regress to Infinity]].)*

2. **The horizontal contrast, stated precisely.** With dominoes or generations "you don't require the first causes to remain in existence." "My grandfather caused my father… then my grandfather died… my father still has the causal power to bring me about." You can "take that earlier cause… throw it in the bin," and the chain keeps going. Hierarchical causation is the opposite: "the shoulder needs to stay there causing the arm in order for the arm to cause the hand."[^1] *(O'Connor correctly isolates the inherent-vs-derived power distinction that does all the work — matching Feser's instrumental/primary point.)*

3. **It is sustaining, not originating — and so it refutes deism.** The cause is "not like a billion years ago set something in motion, but right now sustaining it in existence," "in the same way that… the law of gravity sustains all of the objects in this room." A God who merely "knocked over the first domino and then went away and the dominoes keep on falling over and he's long dead" is therefore incoherent: "he's holding up the microphone above my head right now in this instant. And if he were to disappear, the whole thing would just collapse." Hence "either there's no God at all, or there's a God who's still here right now holding it all in place."[^2] *(O'Connor's anti-deism conclusion converges with the vault's [[Hell - Watchmaker Wind Up and Go Rebutted]].)*

4. **Explicit Aristotelian-Thomist pedigree.** He credits [[Thomas Aquinas]]' *Summa Theologica* (the first three of the Five Ways are "kinds of causal arguments") and the argument from motion/change: change is potential becoming actual, "potential can only be actualized by something which is already actual," terminating in [[Aristotle]]'s "unactualized actualizer… something which is pure act." From pure actuality he derives immateriality ("if something is material, you could potentially divide it… potential qualities"), then timelessness and spacelessness — "a spaceless, timeless creative first cause of the universe."[^3] *(An accurate, if compressed, sketch of the Thomistic derivation in [[From First Actualizer to Pure Actuality]] and [[Divine Attributes Follow From Pure Actuality]].)*

5. **Honest about its limits — a cumulative case.** "This alone isn't strong enough to establish the existence of capital-G God… there's no singular argument for the existence of God. What you have is a kind of cumulative case." Getting to a *good* or *loving* or *personal* God "requires more."[^4] *(Intellectually candid; he concedes exactly what apologists also grant — that the cosmological argument yields a sustaining first cause, not the full divine portrait.)*

## Apologetic Engagement

O'Connor is here handing the apologist a gift: an agnostic Oxford philosopher affirming that the hierarchical first-cause argument is "probably the most powerful argument for the existence of God" and that it *refutes deism*. The vault's cluster is the natural home for this, and the convergence is strong.

- **The core logic is already mapped.** [[Why a Hierarchical Series Cannot Regress to Infinity]] (Feser) makes O'Connor's exact point with the paintbrush/mirror illustrations — "an infinitely long paintbrush handle could not move itself" — and stresses, as O'Connor does *not* quite make explicit, that the impossibility is about *derived vs. underived* power, not mere simultaneity or temporal firstness. The apologetic engagement is to *complete* O'Connor's argument with Feser's precision.
- **The sustaining-cause picture is the vault's own.** [[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]] (Turek) — "God is as necessary to the universe as a band is to music; once the band stops playing, the music is over" — is the same here-and-now dependency O'Connor describes, and [[The Existential Reading - What Sustains Things in Being Here and Now]] frames it as the strongest reading of the cosmological argument.
- **Anti-deism convergence.** O'Connor independently reaches the conclusion of [[Hell - Watchmaker Wind Up and Go Rebutted]]: the watchmaker who "winds it up and walks away" cannot be the God the argument delivers, because the materials and constants themselves require continuous sustaining.
- **The honest gap O'Connor names is the right one.** His concession — this establishes a sustaining principle, not a good/personal God — is precisely where the *cumulative* case continues (moral argument, fine-tuning, revelation). The Restoration adds a distinctive twist the Thomistic derivation forecloses: a Latter-day Saint can affirm a primary, underived *sustaining* cause while contesting the further inference that this cause must be a potency-free, bodiless, absolutely simple being (see [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]]) — locating underived realities plurally (God plus co-eternal intelligence, matter, and law). So the LDS engagement, like Feser's, *accepts* O'Connor's regress argument and contests only the identification of the terminus.

**Steelman the foil.** O'Connor's strongest residual move is the one he flags himself: even granting an immaterial, timeless sustaining cause, "there are lots of debates as to the nature of what that thing could be" — perhaps "the law of gravity" or some impersonal principle. The apologetic burden is exactly to show *why the sustaining ground must be a mind* rather than a brute law (the gap between "fundamental principle" and "God"), which is where the cosmological argument hands off to design and moral arguments.

## Best Quotes

> "There's another way that you can think about causation… the hierarchical causation in this time slice right now, this glass being held up right now has some kind of foundational cause which is giving life to this entire causal chain without which the whole thing would fall apart. So it's a sustaining cause."[^1]

> "He's holding up the microphone above my head right now in this instant. And if he were to disappear, the whole thing would just collapse… So you either kind of have to say there's no God at all, or there's a God who's still here right now holding it all in place as we speak."[^2]

> "This alone isn't strong enough to establish the existence of capital-G God… there's no singular argument for the existence of God. What you have is a kind of cumulative case."[^4]
