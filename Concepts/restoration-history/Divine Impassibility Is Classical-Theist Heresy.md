# Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy

## The Claim
Classical theism holds that God is **impassible** — he "does not have passions," cannot suffer, cannot be moved out of his state of endless bliss by creatures. When [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]] said "of course God feels things... the Bible says he's grieved," he (per Hansen) *denied* impassibility — a creedal commitment — and the cheering audience didn't realize they had just rejected classical theism. This is the LDS point: the Bible's emotive, responsive God *contradicts* classical theism.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Impassibility is a defining creedal attribute
In classical theism God is impassible: to suffer is to lack, to change from bliss to sorrow is to change — and God lacks nothing and does not change. "The creeds literally say that God is impassible." So affirming that God genuinely feels grief is, on classical-theist terms, heresy. *(Strong as a description of classical theism; impassibility is indeed standard in the tradition — e.g. the Westminster Confession's "without... passions.")* [^1]

### 2. Avery (allegedly) conceded it instantly
Asked whether we can cause God to suffer, Avery immediately affirmed God feels things "because the Bible says he's grieved" — without recognizing the concession. Hansen's reaction: "You guys all just rejected classical theism — and that's our point." *(Moderate: reported through an adversarial recap; Avery's own framing is unavailable. Many Protestants do hold a "qualified passibility," so this may be his considered view, not a slip.)* [^2]

### 3. The Bible plainly presents an affected God — which is the LDS view
Scripture repeatedly shows God grieved, moved, responsive. Latter-day Saints embrace this; their argument is precisely that the biblical God is *not* the impassible God of the philosophers. The "heresy" only bites those committed to classical theism. *(Strong as an internal-consistency argument.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The classical theist replies that biblical grief-language is **anthropopathism** — accommodated, figurative speech for an unchanging God's constant relation to a changing world (as fire "softens wax and hardens clay" without itself changing). On this view Avery, if he affirmed literal passibility, departed from his *own* tradition, but a careful classical theist (Aquinas, the Reformed scholastics) would say impassibility *protects* God's perfection and that the LDS "passible God" is a finite, dependent deity — more like the gods of the nations than the Creator. Whether anthropopathism is exegesis or evasion is the live question; the LDS side treats wholesale figurative reading of God's emotions as special pleading (see [[Special Pleading and God Logic]]).

## Best Quotes
> "To say that the all-powerful God can in some way be... hurt by mere human beings or taken out of his state of endless bliss... is absolute heresy. That's why the creeds literally say that God is impassible." [^1]

> "He's like, 'Yeah, of course God feels things'... and I'm going — you guys all just rejected classical theism, and that's our point." [^2]

> "We reject classical theism because you're right — the Bible very clearly teaches that. But they just didn't even understand that they were literally committing heresy." [^3]
