# Gay Marriage Is Not Analogous to the Priesthood Ban or Polygamy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
"The Church changed on Black people and the priesthood, and on polygamy, so it's only a matter of time until it changes its definition of marriage to accept gay marriage." [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Jacob Hansen]] gives two layers. First, the bare "if X and Y changed, then Z will change" inference is a **non-sequitur**. Second, the stronger version — *premise 1: the Church will ultimately change doctrine to align with social norms; premise 2: gay marriage is now a social norm; therefore the Church will accept it* — must be **rejected at premise 1**, because if true it would mean the Church "is not ultimately driven by God, but rather by the social norms of man," and so "is not what it claims to be." The reliable guide to God's will is the collective prophetic witness, not the social trend. [^2]

Then he marshals a point-by-point **disanalogy**: [^3]

1. **Precedent.** The priesthood ban had at least theoretical precedent (not all groups always held the priesthood) and Black men were ordained *before* the ban; gay marriage has "no precedence in theory or practice." Plural marriage was still always man-and-woman. *Rating: strong.*
2. **Category.** The ban was a *policy*; the sinful nature of homosexual behavior is a claim about *moral truth*. "And that's a meaningful difference." *Rating: moderate-strong.*
3. **Scriptural basis.** The ban "had no basis in scripture," while "homosexual behavior is literally condemned in essentially every dispensation." *Rating: contested — depends on exegesis of the relevant texts — but the cross-dispensational pattern is the load-bearing claim.*
4. **Origin and antiquity.** The ban "originated in the 15th and 16th century in Protestantism"; the teaching on gender and marriage "go[es] back to creation itself" and is "essential in the plan of salvation." *Rating: strong within the LDS frame.*
5. **Scale of implication.** Reversing the ban implied only that *one era's* prophets erred — "something we already believe." Accepting gay marriage would imply "our entire understanding of the plan of salvation has been wrong all along," rendering "the temple, exaltation, eternal increase... incoherent" — a "theological nuclear bomb." *Rating: strong as an internal-coherence argument — cf. [[Rejecting Polygamy Unravels Sealing and the Plan of Salvation]].*
6. **Permanence.** The ban "was always known to be temporary"; "there is no indication that the ban on homosexual behavior is just a temporary thing." *Rating: moderate.*

## Steelman (the opposing view)
The strongest opposing case: (a) leaders gave absolute "this will *never* change" sermons about polygamy *immediately before* the Manifesto, so confident permanence-claims have a poor track record (Hansen concedes he doesn't lean on "repeatedly saying this can never change"); (b) [[Patrick Mason]]'s historical point that the Church "has not always consistently taught" man-woman-only marriage; (c) the same "it's just culture / it realigned with the witness" rhetoric used to explain the ban's reversal could, a critic argues, later be deployed to explain a marriage reversal. The disanalogy holds only if the *cross-dispensational* condemnation of homosexual behavior is genuinely uniform and the man-woman pattern genuinely universal.

## Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: the polygamy precedent cuts the *other* way — what changed there was practice and a *disputed single-era* doctrine, never the man-woman definition (see [[Polygamy Doctrine Did Not Change Only the Exaltation-Requires-Polygamy Idea Did]]). And the ban's reversal was a *return* to the collective witness, whereas accepting gay marriage would *contradict* it across every dispensation (see [[Change Is Not the Same as Contradiction]]). He is careful to add this "is not an attack on gay people," affirming support for equal civil/legal unions while opposing the *redefinition of marriage* itself.
