# 1 Corinthians 15 Christ Hands the Kingdom Back to the Father

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] reads [[1 Corinthians 15.24-28|1 Corinthians 15:24-28]] as Paul's reflection on the Daniel 7 enthronement: Christ reigns until he has subdued all enemies (the last being death), then *hands the kingdom back to God the Father*, and "the Son himself will be made subject to him who put everything under him, so that God may be all in all." Paul explicitly excepts the Father from the "everything" subjected to Christ. This shows a Father who is supreme and a Son who serves and returns the kingdom. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **Paul expressly brackets the Father out.** "When it says that everything has been put under his feet it is clear that it does not include God himself who put everything under Christ." *Rating: strong* — the text itself draws the exception (v.27). [^3]

2. **The Son is *made subject* at the end.** "Then the Son himself will be made subject to him who put everything under him." *Rating: a genuine subordination text* that has always been hard for strict co-equality; it grounds Ostler's "relational subordination." [^2]

3. **It closes the Daniel 7 loop.** Daniel: kingdom *conferred on* the Son of Man; 1 Cor 15: the Son, having destroyed all enemies, *hands it back*. "God may be all in all" = everything brought into alignment/harmony with God. *Rating: a clean intertextual reading* (see [[Daniel 7 Son of Man Is Enthroned by the Ancient of Days]]). [^4][^5]

4. **Steelman of the Trinitarian.** The subjection here is *economic and functional* — the incarnate, mediatorial Son completing his redemptive mission and presenting the kingdom — not an *ontological* demotion; "God all in all" is the Triune God's consummated reign, and the *Son's* own kingdom is eternal (Luke 1:33, Daniel 7's "everlasting dominion"). Even Ostler concedes the subordination is "one of honor, obedience, and glory, and not one where he has somehow less deity." *Rating: a strong reply* — the passage proves *distinction* and *functional order*, which Trinitarians affirm, not a lesser-god Christology. [^6]
