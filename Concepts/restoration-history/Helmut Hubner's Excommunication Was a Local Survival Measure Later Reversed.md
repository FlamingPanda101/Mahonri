# Helmut Hübner's Excommunication Was a Local Survival Measure Later Reversed

## The Claim

The charge that "the Mormon church excommunicated an anti-Nazi dissident during World War II" — leveled over the posthumous excommunication of resistance martyr [[Helmut Hübner]] — is, [[Stephen Smoot]] argues, a decontextualized "cheap shot." The excommunication was a **local** act under direct Gestapo pressure, motivated (for the leader who reluctantly consented) by the need to protect the Hamburg Saints; the Church's First Presidency had no knowledge of it, and **reversed it in 1948**, restoring Hübener's blessings.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two very different local motives.** Hübener's branch president [[Arthur Zander]] was an outspoken Nazi who wanted him excommunicated. But his district president [[Otto Berndt]] — himself a Gestapo target who opposed the excommunication — consented for an entirely different reason. See [[The Church's Accommodationist Survival Strategy in Nazi Germany]].[^2]

2. **Coerced under interrogation.** Because Hübener used a church-owned typewriter for his leaflets, the Gestapo suspected church leaders were collaborators and interrogated Berndt for days. He was told that excommunicating the boy "will be a signal that... he was acting on his own and other local church members or leaders weren't involved." Recalling the Gestapo's 1936 threat of "the strictest police state measures," Berndt relented to shield the congregation — not from sympathy with the regime.[^3]

3. **Salt Lake had no idea.** After 1939 the Church in Germany was cut off from American oversight; communication was severed. Smoot stresses this was "clearly not a case of church leaders in Salt Lake City... because they were so ardently pro-Nazi" excommunicating Hübener — "they had no idea what was happening until after the war."[^4]

4. **The 1948 reversal.** When contact was reestablished and Max Zimmer was tasked to assess the German situation, on his recommendation the First Presidency overturned the excommunication, posthumously restored Hübener's blessings, and he later received temple ordinances — the opposite of an institution endorsing his punishment.[^5]

5. **The contrast case.** [[Paul Herbert Schick]], who also defied the regime (refusing the Nazi salute), was *not* excommunicated — undercutting the claim that the Church systematically purged anti-Nazis. The Hübener excommunication turned on the specific Gestapo pressure his use of church property created, plus a Nazi branch president.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "They had no idea what was happening until after the war. It was his local church leadership that was doing this." [^4]

> "If you will excommunicate this boy, that will be a signal that... he was acting on his own and other local church members or leaders weren't involved." [^3]
