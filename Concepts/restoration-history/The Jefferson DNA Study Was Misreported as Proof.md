# The Jefferson DNA Study Was Misreported as Proof

## The Claim
The Ward Radio panel uses the [[Thomas Jefferson]]–[[Sally Hemings]] case as a controlled example of how a rumor hardens into "fact." A 1998 *Nature* headline ("Jefferson fathered slave's last child") was taken as DNA proof of paternity — but the lead researcher [[Eugene Foster]] himself published, within weeks, that the data prove only that **a Jefferson** (not Thomas specifically) fathered Eston Hemings, and the better historical explanation is Thomas's brother [[Randolph Jefferson]]. The case is offered as a transferable lesson for evaluating hostile claims about Joseph Smith.[^1]

> **Caution:** Jefferson–Hemings paternity remains debated; the Monticello Foundation and many historians affirm Thomas's paternity. This note reports the panel's argument and Foster's own published clarifications, and steelmans the mainstream view.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Y-chromosome can't single out Thomas (rated: strong, the study's own logic)
The Jefferson Y-chromosome is shared across the male line, so a match to Hemings's descendants implicates *any* Jefferson male — including Randolph and his sons — not Thomas uniquely.[^1]

### 2. Foster repudiated the headline (rated: strong, primary)
Foster wrote that "the title assigned to our study was misleading. It presented only the simplest explanation of our molecular findings," and that the findings "do not prove Thomas Jefferson was the father of Sally Hemings's children." He added he "repeatedly said for the record that this study could not prove anything conclusively." (January 1999 / January 2000 statements.)[^1]

### 3. Randolph is the better-fit candidate (rated: moderate)
Historical records place Randolph socializing in the Monticello slave quarters and "being very friendly with Sally Hemings," and present around the conception window while Thomas was in Paris — making Randolph the "inference to the best explanation."[^1]

### 4. The institutional contradiction (rated: moderate)
The Monticello website's Hemings section opens, "years after his wife's death, Thomas Jefferson fathered at least six of Sally Hemings's children," yet its cited source — Foster's own assessment report — says the study establishes only that "Hemings's father was a Jefferson." The panel highlights the gap between the asserted claim and its own footnote.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The mainstream reply (which the note preserves): the DNA is *one* strand of a **convergent** case — the Monticello Foundation and the Thomas Jefferson Heritage scholarship also weigh Jefferson's documented presence at Monticello nine months before each of Hemings's births, Madison Hemings's own 1873 testimony naming Thomas as his father, and the pattern of the children's conceptions — and on that totality most historians judge Thomas the most probable father, treating the Randolph hypothesis as a minority position. So "Foster said it doesn't prove it" is true of the genetics *in isolation* but does not settle the historical question, and the panel arguably commits the inverse error (treating "not proven by DNA alone" as "disproven"). The note's defensible core is narrow and documented: the 1998 *headline* overstated what the genetics showed, and Foster said so.

## Best Quotes
> "The title assigned to our study was misleading. It presented only the simplest explanation of our molecular findings." — Eugene Foster [^1]

> "This study by itself does not establish that Hemings's father was Thomas Jefferson, only that Hemings's father was a Jefferson." — Foster assessment, quoted [^1]
