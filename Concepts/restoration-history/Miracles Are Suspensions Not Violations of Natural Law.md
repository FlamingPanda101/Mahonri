# Miracles Are Suspensions Not Violations of Natural Law

## The Point
Against Hume's definition of a miracle as a "violation of a law of nature," Feser argues that on Aristotelian-Thomistic metaphysics laws of nature are descriptions of the *natures* of things, and so are metaphysically necessary and cannot be violated. [^1] A miracle is therefore not a *breach* of natural law but a **suspension** of it — God, as the cause who conserves and concurs in everything, either adds something supernatural to the order of things or withholds his sustaining concurrence so a normal effect does not follow. [^2] Properly understood, miracles are "not contrary to nature [but] rather ... beyond nature." [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Laws are the laws of natures.** "The laws of nature are the laws of natures" — descriptions of the dispositions a thing has by virtue of its essence. [^4] A disposition can be present yet fail to manifest (blocked, or its triggers absent), so non-manifestation is not a "violation." [^5] And if the laws were different, the *natures* would be different and the things would be different: anything that turned to chocolate mousse at 32°F "just wouldn't be water." Hence laws are metaphysically necessary, and violations are impossible. [^6]
2. **A miracle is supernatural, not preternatural.** A miracle "elevates the nature of a thing to a power that cannot be accounted for by this nature," so it can only have a *divine* cause — not the merely preternatural feats of angels, who are themselves created and conserved by God. [^3]
3. **God cannot "intervene" or "interfere."** Since God causes everything, he is not one cause alongside others who occasionally steps in. Quoting Herbert McCabe: "To interfere you have to be an alternative to, or alongside, what you are interfering with. If God is the cause of everything, there is nothing that he is alongside." [^7] The world is not "an airplane on autopilot, with God interfering from time to time to perform a course correction." [^8]
4. **Addition or subtraction.** Better analogies: God is a musician who can improvise beyond the written score (the Resurrection, the loaves and fishes — additions), or refrain from playing certain notes (Daniel's friends not burned in the furnace — a subtraction). [^9] Following Oderberg, a miracle is not God leaving a law on the books while declining to enforce it (a breach), but temporarily *revoking* the law so that what would otherwise count as a violation no longer does (a suspension). [^10] The subtraction miracle is simply God "refraining from acting as a conserving and concurring cause." [^11]
5. **The bridge to revealed theology.** Only God can work a miracle, so miracles function as a divine "seal of approval" authenticating a prophet's message — "the bridge between natural theology and revealed theology," giving faith a rational basis. [^12]

## Best Quotes
> "To interfere you have to be an alternative to, or alongside, what you are interfering with. If God is the cause of everything, there is nothing that he is alongside." [^7]

> "The world is not like an airplane on autopilot, with God interfering from time to time to perform a course correction." [^8]

> "It is not a case of God violently interfering with what he has brought about, but rather a case of his not bringing certain things about in the first place." [^11]

## LDS Connection
On the possibility and reality of miracles, Latter-day Saints and Feser are firm allies against Humean skepticism: God exists, so miracles are possible ([[If God Exists, Miracles Are Possible]]), and the Resurrection in particular is real and pivotal ([[The Resurrection as the Linchpin of Christianity]]). Feser's reframing — miracle as *suspension* rather than *violation*, with the world more like music than a machine — is a genuinely useful apologetic tool a Latter-day Saint can adopt almost wholesale, and his point that miracles authenticate prophetic revelation resonates deeply with a restorationist who believes God still seals his word with signs. The qualifier is metaphysical rather than doctrinal: Feser's whole account runs on continuous conservation and concurrence from a purely actual, simple God ([[Divine Conservation and Concurrence]]), so a Latter-day Saint who locates God within a framework of co-eternal matter and law will describe the *mechanism* of miracles differently — less "the one cause who is alongside nothing" and more an embodied God acting with and upon eternal realities ([[God as the Present Sustaining Cause - The Band Plays On]]). To be fair to Feser, his critique of the crude "violation" model is decisive against a common atheist objection, and the suspension/subtraction distinction handles awkward cases (like the fiery furnace) better than most theistic accounts.
