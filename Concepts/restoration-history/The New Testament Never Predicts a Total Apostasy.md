# The New Testament Never Predicts a Total Apostasy

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]]'s third reason for not being Mormon is that he does not believe the church Christ founded ever fell into a **total** Great Apostasy. He stresses how much Catholics and Latter-day Saints agree: both reject *sola scriptura*, both hold that Christ established one authoritative church with ordained priesthood and divinely given teaching authority. The disagreement is over what happened next. Catholics believe "the gates of hell never prevailed against the church"; the New Testament and the Fathers warn of *some* people falling away or *some* false prophets, but — Horn argues — "none of them ever talks about a total apostasy," none says "the entire church would fall away or that every bishop would be replaced with a false prophet." He cites Matthew 28:20 ("I am with you always, until the end of the age") and Ephesians 3:20–21 ("to him be glory in the church... to all generations"), and treats alleged "man-made ordinances" as legitimate development of doctrine, the same reply he gives Protestants.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, scriptural)** Matthew 28:20 and Ephesians 3:20–21 are genuine indefectibility prooftexts; on their face they promise Christ's abiding presence with the church across generations.[^1] See [[Matthew 28.20|Matthew 28:20]] and [[Ephesians 3.20-21|Ephesians 3:20–21]].
2. **(Moderate)** The "partial not total" observation is accurate as a reading of the warning passages (Acts 20:29–30; 2 Thessalonians 2; 1 Timothy 4): they predict apostasy *of some*, not annihilation of the church.[^1]
3. **(Moderate, shared ground)** Horn's development-of-doctrine framing is consistent: he applies the same standard to Catholic practices that Protestants call "man-made," strengthening the internal coherence of his position.[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Latter-day Saint reply reframes "apostasy" away from "every last believer apostatized." (1) The apostasy was a **gradual falling away**, not a single event, and what was lost was *priesthood authority and keys*, not the existence of Christians ([[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]], [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]]). (2) The core loss is **the divine family and temple theology** — the Mother in Heaven, deification, the first-temple pattern — corrupted under Hellenization, which can happen even while bishops and Bibles persist ([[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]], [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple]]). (3) Indefectibility texts are read as conditional or as fulfilled in the *restored* church across dispensations, and the very *closing of the canon* was used to declare prophecy ended — itself a symptom of apostasy ([[Closing the Canon Was Used to Declare Prophecy Ended]]). (4) The convergence of mainstream Christianity *back toward* LDS doctrines (theosis, embodied God, divine council) is offered as evidence that something had been lost and is being recovered ([[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]], [[Seven Restoration Doctrines the Broader Church Is Drifting Toward]]).

## Counter-objection
Horn's argument is strong against a *caricature* of the apostasy (total disappearance of Christianity) but weaker against the actual LDS claim (loss of priesthood keys and temple/family theology). His indefectibility texts can be met two ways: a Latter-day Saint may read "I am with you always" as a promise to the apostolic mission that is renewed when authority is restored, and may note that Catholics themselves concede massive *de facto* corruption (the Avignon papacy, three simultaneous claimants, sedevacantist-shaped crises) without conceding *de jure* defection — so "the gates of hell never prevailed" is already doing interpretive heavy lifting on the Catholic side. Conversely, Horn lands a real hit: if the church only needed *authority* restored, the LDS must explain why Christ's promise of abiding presence and the Spirit's guidance (John 16:13) did not preserve at least the *keys*. The dispute ultimately turns on whether "the church" is defined by an unbroken visible-institutional succession (Horn) or by the presence of priesthood authority and the fulness of temple ordinances (LDS) — see [[The Same Organization Means Functional Not Structural Continuity with the Primitive Church]].

## Apologetic Engagement
Because Horn shares so much LDS ground (anti-*sola-scriptura*, priesthood, authoritative church), this is the most *intramural* of his three reasons and the one where the LDS reply is best developed in the vault. The effective engagement concedes that no text predicts the extinction of all believers, then relocates the apostasy to authority and temple theology, and presses the canon-closure and divine-family losses. Distinguish this from the cosmological "Reason 1" ([[Mormonism Rejects Classical Theism So the God Objection Bites]]) and the historicity "Reason 2" ([[The Book of Mormon Has No Map and No Confirmed Geography]]).

## LDS Relevance
Apostasy/Restoration is the hinge of the whole debate: if there was no loss, there was nothing to restore. Horn's careful "partial not total" point is worth conceding so the argument can move to its real subject — the loss of priesthood keys and the divine-family/temple pattern — handled by the apostasy and Restoration-convergence clusters.
