# Subordination Within the New Testament

## The Claim
"The entire New Testament is a subordinationist text from start to finish." The Son receives authority from the Father, prays to the Father, calls the Father "my God," does not know what the Father knows, and is finally "subjected" to the Father — a consistent pattern incompatible with the Son being numerically the same *being* as the Father.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Son is ultimately subjected to the Father
[[1 Corinthians 15.24-28|1 Corinthians 15:24-28]]: the Son hands the kingdom back to the Father and "the Son also himself [will] be subject" to him. The text explicitly *excepts* the Father from what is subjected — two parties, not one being. *(Strong: a flagship subordinationist text the early church wrestled with.)* [^1]

### 2. A relational chain of headship
[[1 Corinthians 11.3|1 Corinthians 11:3]]: "the head of Christ is God." The Father stands as Christ's head in the same chain as Christ over man — relational order. And Paul repeatedly calls the Father "the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ" ([[Ephesians 1.3|Eph 1:3]]; cf. [[2 Corinthians 1.3|2 Cor 1:3]]; 1 Peter 1:3). *(Strong as a cumulative pattern.)* [^2]

### 3. The Son lacks knowledge the Father has; submits his will
[[Mark 13.32|Mark 13:32]]: the Son does not know the day or hour. [[Luke 22.42|Luke 22:42]]: "not my will, but thine." A single being cannot be ignorant of what it knows or submit its will to itself. The early (ante-Nicene) fathers read such texts as subordinationism; the hypostatic union was later constructed to neutralize them. See [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]]. *(Strong; the historical claim about ante-Nicene subordinationism is broadly supported.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Pro-Nicene Trinitarians (and most modern exegetes) read these texts through (a) the **economy of salvation**: the Son's subjection and "the Father is greater" pertain to his *incarnate, sent* role, not his eternal essence; and (b) the **two natures**: ignorance ([[Mark 13.32|Mark 13:32]]) and a distinct will ([[Luke 22.42|Luke 22:42]]) belong to the assumed human nature, while the divine Son remains co-equal. They add that [[1 Corinthians 15.24-28|1 Cor 15:28]]'s subjection is the *Son's* glad ordering of redeemed creation back to the Father, not evidence of inferior being — and that John's high Christology ([[John 1.1|John 1:1]], [[John 20.28|John 20:28]]) and the worship of Jesus require full deity. "Eternal functional subordination" lets some Trinitarians even grant *relational* order without conceding inequality of being — which is, notably, close to the LDS model (see [[Ontological Equality With Relational Subordination]]); the residual dispute is whether the shared "what" is one *being* or one *Godhead*.

## Best Quotes
> "The entire New Testament is a subordinationist text from start to finish... the earliest church fathers — you're going to find a very clear pattern. They're all subordinationist." [^3]

> "How does the same being submit its will to itself? How does it receive authority from itself?... what being was Jesus praying to?" [^4]

> "The head of every man is Christ, the head of the wife is her husband, and the head of Christ is... God. Not even the Father — God." [^2]
