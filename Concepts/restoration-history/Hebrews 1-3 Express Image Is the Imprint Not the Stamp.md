# Hebrews 1-3 Express Image Is the Imprint Not the Stamp

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Hebrews 1.3|Hebrews 1:3]] — the Son is "the express image of his person (*hypostasis*)" — proves (a) the creedal word *hypostasis* is biblical and (b) the Son is the same being as the Father.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The word *hypostasis* is biblical; its *creedal definition* is not
[[Go With Jordan]] notes *hypostasis* appears in Hebrews 1:3. The panel grants it — but argues the 1st-century sense (the Greek word for "person," the impress/substance of a thing) is not the 4th-century technical "subsistence within one being." "When Hebrews was written... that wasn't the definition of that Greek word at that time. It was altered and changed to fit later." So the verse's vocabulary does not import the creed. *(Moderate-strong; the semantic shift of *hypostasis* across centuries is real and well documented.)* [^1]

### 2. The imprint is not the thing that made the imprint
"Express image" evokes a **wax seal** stamped by a signet: the imprint is the *exact* image of the stamp — but it is a *second* thing, not the stamp itself. "Is the thing that's imprinted the same as the thing that made the imprint? No." The Son being the express image of the Father therefore distinguishes two things, not identifies one being. *(Strong: the imagery itself implies duality.)* [^2]

### 3. "Right hand of the Majesty" is a king-and-right-hand-man image
The verse seats the Son "on the right hand of the Majesty on high." A king's right-hand man is not the king; the right-hand position presupposes two parties. Read in context with Hebrews 1:1-2 (God "hath spoken... by his Son"), it is relational and agential, not consubstantial. See [[Subordination Within the New Testament]], [[Divine Agency and the Divine-Name Bearer]]. *(Strong as a contextual reading.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The Trinitarian replies that Hebrews 1:3 is one of the NT's **highest** Christological texts and cuts the *other* way: the Son is the "radiance (*apaugasma*) of God's glory" and the "exact imprint (*charaktēr*) of his very being," upholding the universe by his word — language of shared divine being, not mere delegated agency. The wax-seal point can be turned around: an imprint shares the *exact form* of the seal, which is precisely "same essence, distinct person" — the Nicene claim. And by 4th-century usage *hypostasis* came to do useful work *because* texts like this needed a term for the distinct-yet-divine Son. So the verse, far from excluding consubstantiality, is among its strongest supports; the dispute again reduces to whether the shared "what" is one *being* or one *Godhead* (see [[Three Whos and One What Framework]]).
