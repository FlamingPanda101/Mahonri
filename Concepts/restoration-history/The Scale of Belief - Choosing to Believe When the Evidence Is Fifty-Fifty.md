# The Scale of Belief - Choosing to Believe When the Evidence Is Fifty-Fifty

## The Point
[[Austin Fife]] closes his point-by-point reply to the [[CES Letter]] with the positive case and a confession about how proof actually works. After "countless hours" reading critics and apologists alike — "the good, bad, and ugly of previous church policies" — he discovered that the Church "can never be proven true or false": for every piece of positive evidence "negatives lurked around the corner," and for every unflattering fact "a logical counter-explanation presented itself." His scale of belief settled "about 50/50."[^1] At that point neutrality was not an option: "Whether I believed or did not believe, either was a leap of faith. In due time, I decided that I would believe."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The evidence is "not conclusive, but it is compelling."** Fife's epistemic thesis is honest on both sides: "While I cannot prove that the Church is definitively true beyond a reasonable doubt, there is great space for belief. To pretend otherwise is disingenuous."[^3] This mirrors the [[Belief Must Precede Knowledge]] framework — argument maintains "a climate in which belief may flourish" rather than compelling it.

2. **Both verdicts are a leap.** Because the scale rests near even, *disbelief* is no safer or more rational a default than belief — each requires commitment beyond the evidence. Fife reframes the choice as an active decision rather than a passive failure to be convinced.[^2] This is the autobiographical complement to [[Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake]], where the scale is weighted deliberately before testing it against critics.

3. **Trading "I know" for "I believe" was liberating, not diminishing.** "Giving up on the 'I know' statements I was accustomed to in exchange for 'I believe' declarations was incredibly liberating. The gospel that I knew so well became new and exhilarating to me."[^4] The shift lowered the bar from certainty to honest faith without emptying the conviction.

4. **The Book of Mormon is what tips the positive side.** Fife says that when he tried to leave, "the Book of Mormon stumped me. It stumps the critics, too" — no skeptic "after desperate attempts for 200 years, has ever given a model that explains the Book of Mormon in a convincing way."[^5] The believing model (an angel and gold plates) is, on his reading, less improbable than the critics' reconstructions of a "manipulative, horny con man" who accidentally "built the most Christ-centered church and people in the latter days."[^6]

5. **Outside the Church he had *more* unanswered questions, not fewer.** The decisive practical point: "I had way more unanswered questions outside the Church than I do inside."[^7] Belief was the option that left the fewest loose ends.

## Best Quotes
> "I could never prove that the Church of Jesus Christ is true or false. Negatives lurked around the corner, no matter how much positive evidence I found... Eventually, my scale of belief vs. disbelief felt about 50/50." [^1]

> "Whether I believed or did not believe, either was a leap of faith. In due time, I decided that I would believe." [^2]

> "The evidence for the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints' truth claims is not conclusive, but it is compelling... there is great space for belief. To pretend otherwise is disingenuous." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Fife's "scale" is a restoration epistemology of preponderance, not proof — the converging witnesses of scripture, fruits, and the Spirit weighed together rather than any single unbreakable demonstration. It enacts [[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]]: belief is an experiment freely chosen and nurtured, more valuable to God than sign-compelled "sure knowledge," and the seed's swelling is positive evidence weighed before the verdict. By granting that disbelief is also a leap, Fife answers the CES Letter not by claiming certainty but by relocating the decision onto honest ground — the same posture as [[Death Is Not the Worst Thing - Reframing the Problem of Evil]] and [[Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion]], where the weight of the whole picture, not a single proof, carries belief.
