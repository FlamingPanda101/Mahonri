# Movements Must Institutionalize to Survive Their Charismatic Founder

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Johnny Harris]] frames the "corporationifying" of the Church — the J. Reuben Clark moment, correlation, a "more standardized, literal, controlled theology" — as a sinister drift toward control, contrasting it with an early era of "creative theological interpretation."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The pattern is sociologically normal, per Max Weber
[[Keystone]] invokes [[Max Weber]]: "in order for movements to survive and grow long after the death of their initial charismatic leader, at some point they've got to institutionalize in some way." Charismatic founders "rebel against societal norms... and spark a revolution," but "over time that fire has to be translated into structure, rules and traditions" — "things need to get a little more boring or routine," and "what was once revolutionary becomes orthodox."[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — this is Weber's well-established "routinization of charisma," standard in the sociology of religion.

### 2. It is observed everywhere, not unique to the Church
Keystone names parallels: "We see this in the early Christian church after the death of their charismatic leader Jesus Christ. We see it with George Washington and the birth of the United States... It's all over the place."[^3] The same dynamic produced the institutional Catholic Church and the constitutional American republic. Compare [[Christianity Began as a New Religious Movement]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the cross-case generality is exactly what makes the Weberian account explanatory rather than ad hoc.

### 3. So institutionalization is survival, not corruption
"That process is actually really fascinating and really normal." Reading the Church's standardization as proof of a control agenda mistakes a universal life-cycle feature of successful movements for a unique moral failing.[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the descriptive point is solid; whether *any particular* change is good remains a separate question.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can grant Weber and still press the point: "it's normal" does not mean "it's benign." Routinization can genuinely concentrate authority, suppress the founding charisma's openness, and be experienced as control by those inside it — describing a process is not endorsing every use of it. Harris can also argue that the *content* of LDS correlation (centralized messaging, narrowed theological latitude) is a stronger form of institutional control than other movements underwent. The honest reply: Weber defuses the *insinuation* that institutionalization is evidence of bad faith or a uniquely Mormon pathology, while leaving open the legitimate, case-by-case question of whether specific centralizing choices were wise or excessive.
