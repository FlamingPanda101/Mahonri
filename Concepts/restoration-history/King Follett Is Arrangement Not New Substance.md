# King Follett Is Arrangement Not New Substance

#type/claim

## The Claim

A common objection treats the [[King Follett Discourse]] as the moment Joseph Smith invented his most radical doctrines out of thin air, late in life. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] argues instead that the sermon was "revolutionary not for the substance of the ideas but the arrangement of them" — almost everything in it had been revealed more than a decade earlier.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Textual antecedents (strong, internally verifiable).** Griffiths roots the sermon's substance in [[D&C 93|D&C 93]] (intelligence uncreated, man "in the beginning with God," elements eternal) and the [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]] (translated mostly 1835, with some 1842 work). King Follett is "Joseph Smith taking all the puzzle pieces that he's been assembling over his prophetic career and putting it together into this one big package."[^2] The evidence here is strong because the priority of D&C 93 (1833) and Abraham (1835) over the 1844 sermon is documentable.
2. **Capstone trajectory (moderate).** Griffiths traces a chain: angels and humans are the same kind of being → [[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130]], "when the Savior shall appear we shall see that he is a man like ourselves" → King Follett, where God too is of that kind. The discourse is the "Capstone of his prophetic career."[^3] This is an interpretive synthesis, so moderate rather than decisive.
3. **Steelman of the opposing view.** A critic can grant the antecedents yet still insist King Follett crosses a genuinely *new* line: D&C 93 says intelligence is uncreated, but it never explicitly says *God the Father was once a mortal man.* The leap from "man co-eternal with God" to "God was once as we are now" is a substantive addition, not mere rearrangement — which is precisely why Griffiths himself classes the latter as esoteric (see [[God Was Once a Man Is Esoteric Not Core Doctrine]]). So "arrangement not substance" is fairer for exaltation than for the once-mortal-God claim.

## Best Quotes

> "King Follett and the King Follett sermon were kind of revolutionary not for the substance of the ideas but the arrangement of them. Almost everything that Joseph Smith is teaching in this sermon is found in revelations given more than a decade earlier."[^4]

> "It's basically Joseph Smith taking all the puzzle pieces that he's been assembling over his prophetic career and putting it together into this one big package where he hits some big ideas."[^5]
