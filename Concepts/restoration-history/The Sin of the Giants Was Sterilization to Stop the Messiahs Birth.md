# The Sin of the Giants Was Sterilization to Stop the Messiahs Birth

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]] propose a synthetic thesis: the antediluvian "sin of the giants" that provoked the flood was a deliberate campaign of **sterilization, abortion, and child-sacrifice** aimed at cutting off human reproduction — and thereby preventing the **incarnation of the Messiah** (Jehovah being born "of the seed of the woman"). Weaving together the Books of Enoch, the **Book of Jasher**, the *Book of the Giants*, the **Genesis Apocryphon**, **3 Enoch**, **Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer**, and the **Mandaean** tradition, they argue God flooded the earth to "press reset" because, with births ceasing, the plan of salvation itself was collapsing. Barnes repeatedly flags it as "my riffing… a theory I put on the framework of Joseph Smith's stuff."[^1][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Multiple apocrypha describe antediluvian anti-natalism — rate: the texts do say this, harmonized by Barnes
Jasher 2:19-21 has wives made to "drink a draught that would render them barren… that their beautiful appearance might not fade"; Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer has the antediluvians resolve "we will restrain ourselves from multiplying"; the fragmentary *Book of the Giants* (Dead Sea Scrolls) hints at a couple who "did not become pregnant" and at rituals harming children. *Moderate: these are real motifs in the named texts, but they are scattered across disparate, late, and (for Jasher) pseudepigraphal sources that Barnes stitches into one narrative; no single ancient text states the unified "sterilize to stop the Messiah" thesis.*[^1]

### 2. Joseph Smith's "tent-keepers" and "wild man" fit the picture — rate: depends on Webster-1828 glosses
[[Moses 6.37-38|Moses 6:37-38]] has guards ("tent-keepers," read as protection against rampant theft/graverobbing — the "slippery" curse) and calls [[Enoch]] "a wild man," which Barnes parses via Webster's 1828 as *fertile/reproducing* — i.e., a natural birth. With [[Moses 6.31|Moses 6:31]]'s "but a lad," he infers Enoch was a rarity born after ~65 years of no births. *Weak-to-moderate: the readings are ingenious but lean on selecting one dictionary sense ("wild" = fertile) over the obvious one ("wild" = untamed/prophet-of-the-wilderness, which is Nibley's own reading); the panel admits it is interpretive.*[^2]

### 3. Enoch and Noah as anomalous wonder-children — rate: real type-scene, contested application
The Genesis Apocryphon's birth of Noah (Lamech doubting the child's paternity) and 1 Enoch 106-107 present miraculous, questioned births — which Barnes reads as shock at *any* birth in a sterile age. The vault's [[Book of Enoch - Birth of Noah as Wonder Child]] documents the type-scene academically. *Moderate as to the texts; the "because births had ceased" motive is Barnes's overlay, not the texts' stated reason (which is fear of Watcher paternity).*[^3]

### 4. The thesis "explains" Matthew 24:37 and Genesis 6 — rate: clever rereading, not required
[[Matthew 24.37-39|Matthew 24:37-39]] ("as the days of Noe… eating and drinking, marrying") is reread as consuming offspring/sterilizing draughts; "the end of all flesh is before me" (Genesis 6:13) is reparsed as God *witnessing* flesh ending (subject = "the end of all flesh"); 3 Enoch 44:8 ("how could I deliver my great right hand by their hands?") is read as: with babies aborted, the Son cannot be born. *Weak as proof — these are possible but non-obvious construals chosen because they fit the thesis; standard readings (general violence/corruption, God decreeing the end) require none of it.*[^4]

### 5. War and famine preceded the flood — rate: partial canonical hook
The panel says God sent a war and then a famine/drought before the flood; [[Genesis 5.29|Genesis 5:29]] ("the ground which the Lord hath cursed") is read as that famine, with Joseph Smith's Moses material and Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer supplying "no rain." *Moderate: there is a real interpretive crux at Genesis 5:29, and a graduated-chastisement reading is defensible, though "war then famine then flood" is mostly extra-canonical.*[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic raises several objections. (a) **Harmonization across incompatible sources.** Jasher (a medieval pseudepigraph, not the lost biblical Jasher), the Mandaean *Ladam* (a late Gnostic-adjacent corpus), Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer (8th-9th c. AD), and the Dead Sea Scrolls *Book of the Giants* come from wildly different milieus and centuries; assembling them into one antediluvian "abortion conspiracy" is a modern construction, not an ancient datum — and the *Book of the Giants*'s text is so fragmentary ("only every other word survives," as the hosts say) that the lurid reconstruction is largely interpolation. (b) **Genesis itself gives a different reason.** Genesis 6:5, 11-13 names *violence* (ḥamas) and pervasive evil of the heart, not anti-natalism, as the cause of the flood; the "sterilization to stop the Messiah" motive appears in none of the canonical flood texts. (c) **Lexical cherry-picking.** Resting "wild man = fertile birth" on one Webster sense, against Nibley's wilderness-prophet reading, is special pleading; likewise reparsing "the end of all flesh is before me." (d) **Motivated presentism.** The episode is explicitly aimed at contemporary abortion/"feminism," which is a reason to be cautious that the ancient texts are being read through a modern polemic ("I'm just reading the texts" is asserted but the selection and synthesis are not neutral). The defensible residue: Second-Temple and later Jewish traditions *did* greatly expand the terse Genesis 6 account (Watchers, cosmetic/abortifacient "arts," child-harm), and the LDS pre-existence framework gives a coherent theological reason to prize fertility and oppose abortion ([[LDS Abortion Position Follows From Pre-Existence Not Cultural Caving]]) — but that is a thematic, not evidential, link, and the specific "the giants sterilized humanity to abort the Messiah, so God flooded the earth" storyline is a creative apologetic synthesis the sources do not, individually or together, actually assert.

## LDS Connection
A maximalist instance of the vault's "Joseph restored lost antediluvian context" pattern (cf. [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]], [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch]], [[Book of Enoch - Birth of Noah as Wonder Child]]) fused to a pro-natalist reading of the gospel and the LDS abortion ethic ([[LDS Abortion Position Follows From Pre-Existence Not Cultural Caving]]). It is best filed as **enthusiast synthesis, not demonstration**: individual textual motifs are genuine, but the unifying "sterilization-to-stop-the-Messiah → flood" thesis is Barnes's own framework, conceded as such, and Genesis names violence rather than anti-natalism as the flood's cause.
