# Tertullian Reframed Church Office as Roman Legal Privilege

## The Point
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] (drawing on [[Karen Jo Torjesen]]) identifies [[Tertullian]] (c. AD 200) as one of the first to explain the church "in terms of Roman law and government." Instead of defining clergy by a *commission to preach*, Tertullian defined the offices by the **rights and privileges** they held — the right to baptize, teach, restore the penitent, and offer the Eucharist. Casting office as a bundle of public legal *rights* (Roman *ius*) had a "lasting detrimental effect on women's status," because Roman women had no public rights.

## Evidence

### 1. Clergy as the senatorial class
Tertullian "understood the proper order of the Church to reflect Roman society, with different classes and ranks." The clergy (bishops, elders, deacons) "represented the senatorial or elite ruling class of Rome, while the laity … represented the plebeian or lower classes." [^1]

### 2. Office defined by rights, not by mission
He defined the clerical offices "not by special commissions to preach the gospel but rather by the special rights and privileges these offices held." Torjesen notes Tertullian "uses the same word for 'right' (*ius*) to describe clerical privileges as was commonly used in the Roman legal system to describe a citizen's rights." [^2]

### 3. The household-manager model had favored women
Torjesen: "so long as church leadership continued to model itself on the familiar role of household manager, there was no cultural barrier to women assuming leadership roles." Women heads of household led house churches naturally. Redefining office as *public legal right* destroyed that basis, since "respectable women … could not appear with their heads unveiled or speak in a public setting without shaming themselves." [^3]

### 4. Tertullian also polices the virgins of Carthage
Consistent with this, Tertullian denounced the *Acts of Paul and Thecla* as forgery — reasoning Paul "would [not] give a female the power to teach and baptize" — and demanded the unveiled virgins of his church cover their heads, "a reminder … that they were still women and still in a subordinate position to the clergy." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Tertullian defined the clerical offices of the Church not by special commissions to preach the gospel but rather by the special rights and privileges these offices held." [^2]

> "so long as church leadership continued to model itself on the familiar role of household manager, there was no cultural barrier to women assuming leadership roles." — Karen Jo Torjesen [^3]

## LDS Connection
This pinpoints a *mechanism* of apostasy that a [[Latter-day Saints|Latter-day Saint]] reader can name precisely: the substitution of a Roman legal-rights model of office for the original gospel-commission model. Laughton presents the loss as cultural drift, leaving room for the restoration to recover the missionary/household pattern (cf. home-centered worship and women's expanding roles). [^3]
