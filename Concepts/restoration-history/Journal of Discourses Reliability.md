# Journal of Discourses Reliability

## Overview

The question of how far the *Journal of Discourses* — the 26-volume Victorian-era compilation of Latter-day Saint sermons (chiefly [[Brigham Young]]'s) — can be trusted as a verbatim record of what was actually said. The short answer urged by [[Daniel Peterson]] on Ward Radio: **not very far**, because the transcripts were filtered through a stenographer with a discernible editorial slant.

## The Claim

The sermons were taken down in Pitman shorthand by **George D. Watt** and others, then transcribed and edited before printing. [[LeGene Kuth]]'s research found that Watt **systematically made Brigham Young sound more authoritarian** than the underlying shorthand warranted — sharpening tone, hardening commands, and amplifying the "rough edges" that critics later quote as the real Brigham. The published text is therefore a mediated artifact, not a recording.

Two cautions follow:

1. **Source-critical caution.** Quoting the *Journal of Discourses* as if it were a transcript overstates its evidentiary weight; the editorial layer must be accounted for. See [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]].
2. **Status caution (the other direction).** Although it is unreliable as verbatim text, the collection was historically [[Journal of Discourses Was Treated as Scripture|treated by many members as near-scripture]] — a status it cannot bear given its provenance.

This feeds the broader defense of [[Brigham Young's Character]] (a sincere, loyal man whose record was distorted by editing, cherry-picking, and [[Presentism and Historical Context|presentism]]).
