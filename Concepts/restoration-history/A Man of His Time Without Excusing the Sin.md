# A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Two opposite errors are addressed. (1) Critics condemn [[Brigham Young]] outright for racist statements, asking "Why would God choose somebody that would [say/do] that?" (2) Defenders deploy "he was a man of his time" as a thought-terminating cliché that "just shuts their brain off" and seems to *justify* the sin. Peterson wants a third path: acknowledge the genuine wrong, refuse both the anachronistic condemnation and the cheap excuse.[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **He really did say wrong things (rated: strong; concession).** Peterson does not whitewash: "Brigham did say some things that make my skin crawl a little bit. ... I wish he hadn't said them." He does not claim these were "revealed doctrine."[^2] This is the steelman built into the argument — the defense begins by granting the offense.

2. **The Lincoln test (rated: strong, rhetorically powerful).** At the FAIR conference Peterson read a quotation that Black people should not hold leadership over whites, let the audience assume it was Brigham, then revealed it was [[Abraham Lincoln]]. The point: such views "were in the air," shared even by abolitionists, who could oppose slavery yet still believe Black people unequal.[^3] So singling out Brigham for a near-universal 19th-century assumption is selective.

3. **No one fully escapes their culture (rated: moderate-strong).** "Nobody ever escapes the time and culture in which he or she was raised. Not completely." A prophet who *fully* transcended his era "would be impossible to deal with... on a different planet."[^4] The benefit of studying history is realizing that we, too, will look foolish or wrong to the future ("a fish doesn't know it's swimming in water"); Peterson names abortion tolerance as a candidate for future condemnation of our era.[^5]

4. **The line is real but not everywhere (rated: the key qualifier).** Pressed on where empathy becomes excuse-making, Peterson concedes "there are obviously things like mass murder that are wrong in any era." Presentism is wrong *only* where "the cultural fashions have changed so much" that judging a past person as if they had our advantages is unfair — not as a blanket amnesty.[^6] This is what distinguishes this concept from naive relativism. See [[Presentism in Historical Judgment]].

5. **Culture vs. conduct (rated: moderate, biographical).** Peterson illustrates with his own mother, raised in St. George, who occasionally used an offensive term but "never used it in a harmful way" and "never... unkind to anyone... over any racial issue." He regrets having raised her publicly, because listeners then demanded he "condemn" her — refusing to, "I'll not condemn my mother. She was a really good woman." The distinction: inherited *language/assumptions* differ from how one actually *treats* people.[^7]

6. **"Think yourself into the mindset" — the Wilberforce frame (rated: strong; the clearest statement).** At FAIR, Peterson grounds the same point in Eric Metaxas's *Amazing Grace*: for 5,000 years "slavery was as accepted as birth and marriage and death ... the idea of human civilization without slavery was unimaginable," so that even Wilberforce and the abolitionists dared at first only to fight the slave *trade*, not slavery itself. Reading the past requires "think[ing] ourselves into this mindset" rather than projecting 21st-century moral clarity backward. Peterson still concludes plainly: "obviously I don't believe in slavery. ... Humans don't have a right to own other human beings."[^8] This is the disciplined form of the "man of his time" point — historical empathy that refuses to become endorsement.

## Best Quotes

> "That isn't Brigham Young. That's Abraham Lincoln. Because it was in the air. ... don't judge Brigham Young harshly for believing what Abraham Lincoln and abolitionists thought at the same time."[^3]

> "Nobody ever escapes the time and culture in which he or she was raised. Not completely. That person would be impossible to deal with."[^4]

> "There are obviously things like mass murder that are wrong in any era. ... But there are other things where... it is really wrong to look at a person from that previous generation and judge them as if they've had my advantages."[^6]
