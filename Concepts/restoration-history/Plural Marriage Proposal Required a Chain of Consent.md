# Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent

## The Claim
The popular image of plural marriage as a "free-for-all" — men "collecting women like Pokémon" — is false. [[Keystone]] shows that a plural proposal had to pass through a **chain of consent**: the existing wife, then the church president, then the prospective wife's parents, then the woman herself. A "no" at any link normally ended the matter.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Step one: the existing wife's consent.** [[Orson Pratt]] (1853): "Before any man takes the least step towards getting another wife, it is his duty to consult the feelings of the wife which he already has and obtain her consent." Keystone is candid that this standard "didn't always pan out this way" — if the first wife lacked "good reason" (a subjective term), the husband could proceed — but the expectation was real.[^2] *Rating: moderate–strong — a documented norm with an honestly noted gap between ideal and practice; cf. [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]]'s "law of Sarah."*

2. **Step two: the church president's approval.** Plural marriage was a priesthood ordinance, so per [[Orson Pratt]] "no man in Utah who already has a wife has any right to make any propositions of marriage to another lady until he has consulted the president over the whole church." [[Brigham Young]] "did sometimes say no" — e.g. he denied [[Peter Shirts]], telling him to "continue to lead the life of a hermit" until he was willing to live where a family could thrive.[^3] *Rating: strong — the Peter Shirts denial is a concrete counterexample to the free-for-all image.*

3. **Steps three and four: parents, then the woman.** For couples Brigham Young did not personally know, a bishop or stake president sent a letter of recommendation. If approved, the man needed the prospective fiancée's *parents'* consent — "if their consent cannot be obtained, this also ends the matter" (Pratt) — and only then could approach the woman, whose "no" was likewise final.[^4] *Rating: strong — a documented multi-veto process.*

4. **Courtship was often minimal by design.** Per [[Britney Chapman Nash]], "many women who became plural wives experienced little to no courtship, which appealed to Victorian sensitivities about the appropriate behavior of married men." One woman recalled, "married men didn't do any courting... they just came and asked us, and if we wanted them, we agreed." Researcher [[Kathryn Daynes]] acknowledged "most stories confirm this kind of restraint."[^5] *Rating: moderate–strong — well-attested, and reframes "no courtship" as decorum rather than coercion.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic would press the documented gap: if a first wife's objection could be overruled when she lacked "good reason," and if "good reason" was judged by men, then her "consent" was not a true veto; and a young woman facing a proposal endorsed by her parents and her prophet bore real social and theological pressure even with a formal right to refuse. The multi-step process could function as layered authority pressure as much as protection.

## Counter-objection
The objection is partly conceded — Keystone explicitly flags that the first-wife standard "didn't always pan out" and that pressure existed (see [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]] for the men's-side pressure too). But the existence of *real vetoes that were actually exercised* — Brigham Young refusing petitions, parents and women ending matters — refutes the specific "free-for-all / Pokémon-collecting" caricature the note targets. Pressure short of force is a different and lesser claim than the lustful-free-for-all image, and the consent chain is documented in the practitioners' own procedural sources.
