# Roman Primacy Was a Later Claim Not First-Century

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Catholic claim that the bishop of Rome was the supreme head of the church from the beginning — Peter handing primacy to a chain of Roman successors — is the objection examined here. Day and Butler argue that universal Roman primacy is a *later* development: the explicit "the bishop of Rome is in charge" claim does not appear in the first couple of centuries and is never universally accepted (the East never agrees).

## The Rebuttal

### 1. No first-century primacy claims (rating: moderate; Day's reading of the literature)
Day: from his (admittedly non-exhaustive) reading, "they're not making claims like [only-listen-to-Rome] in the first couple hundred years." Early writers say the apostles *set up* bishops, and "I knew John, listen to me" — proximity arguments, not Roman-supremacy arguments. See [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles]]. [^1]

### 2. Succession-to-Rome appears late 2nd century (rating: moderate; Butler)
Butler dates the first surviving claim that "Peter chose Linus to succeed him as bishop of Rome" to "late in the second century" — and notes it "does not say [Linus was head] of the church, or that Peter was the head of the church." [[Clement of Rome|Clement]] (96 AD) and [[Ignatius of Antioch|Ignatius]] speak of apostles appointing bishops but not of Roman supremacy. [^2]

### 3. Primacy is claimed only c. 400 by Leo I (rating: moderate; Day, hedged)
Day attributes the strong "the bishop in Rome is the *papa*, the one in charge" claim to roughly Leo I (~400), and immediately notes it "is going to be countermanded by the bishop of Constantinople," launching a ~600-year contest. Constantine had set up Constantinople as "Rome of the East." [^3]

### 4. The East never concedes (rating: strong; ongoing schism)
Roman primacy is "arguably... never established" because the Eastern Orthodox reject it permanently. Butler adds that even some conservative Lutherans claim to *be* the original Catholic Church and treat Rome as in error. Primacy is contested, not foundational. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Catholic case rests on [[Matthew 14.28-31|Matthew 16:18-19]] ("thou art Peter, and upon this rock... the keys"), Peter's prominence in Acts, his martyrdom in Rome, and an early succession-list tradition ([[Irenaeus of Lyons|Irenaeus]], c. 180) tracing Roman bishops back to Peter and Paul. On this reading the *doctrine* of primacy is implicit from the start and merely articulated more explicitly over time as challenges arose — development, not invention. Day grants the charted succession "is a good argument," but holds the explicit supremacy claims are demonstrably later and never universally received.

## Best Quotes

> "From my reading, no, they're not making claims like that in the first couple hundred years." [^1]

> "I think it's late in the second century that we have the first surviving writing that says Peter chose Linus to succeed him as bishop of Rome... but that does not say as head of the church." [^2]

> "Arguably it's never established, because with the Orthodox they don't agree with that." [^4]
