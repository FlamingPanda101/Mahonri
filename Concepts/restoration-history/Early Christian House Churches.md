## The Point
Before Christians had the resources or the numbers to build dedicated structures, the earliest followers of Jesus gathered for worship in the homes and workspaces of local believers. These "house churches" (the "household[s] of faith") of the first through early third centuries hosted the basic activities of earliest Christian worship — reading, praying, eating, singing, and conversing — for small groups of roughly ten to fifty believers. Because Christians had not yet developed a distinct material culture, no clearly identifiable archaeological remains of an early house church survive; scholars reconstruct them from contemporary domestic and industrial structures.

## Evidence
1. The Acts of the Apostles claims the first such settings were the homes and "upper rooms" of members scattered throughout Jerusalem, where small groups could meet outside the purview of public authorities (Acts 1:12–26; 2:1–4, 43–47).
2. As the message spread, disciples continued meeting in converts' homes and workspaces in major cities of the eastern Mediterranean — Syria, Asia Minor, Greece, and Italy.
3. A prominent setting was the home of a wealthy convert, accessed through a doorway (fauces) into an enclosed atrium with a central rainwater pool (impluvium), surrounded by living rooms (cubicula), an open-air garden (hortus), and a richly decorated dining room (triclinium) with reclining couches.
4. Where no wealthy patron existed, believers met in smaller living rooms of multistory apartment buildings (insulae) above taverns and workshops, in industrial workshops, in taverns/hotels with gardens, in residential spaces above markets, or even in barns and stables of villa estates (used by believing slaves).
5. Comparative archaeological sites include Pompeii, Herculaneum, and Ostia (Italy); Corinth (Greece); and Ephesus (Asia Minor).
6. The domestic setting fostered a remarkable degree of social equalization among men, women, children, and slaves of the group (see [[Galatians 3.28]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Long before Christians had resources to build their own structures or had congregation sizes that required large meeting places, the earliest followers of Jesus naturally gathered for their community activities in the houses of local believers." [^1]

> "A wide variety of physical spaces from the Greco-Roman world could be used as house churches, as they simply needed to accommodate localized groups of between ten to fifty believers who assembled to read, pray, eat, sing, and converse (the basic activities of earliest Christian worship)." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Matthew Grey draws an explicit parallel: in the early days of the Restoration, Latter-day Saint assembly spaces for study, sermons, and sacraments included open-air venues (riversides, wooded groves) and repurposed private structures (homes and workspaces), before the community moved to dedicated chapels, assembly halls, and temples. Understanding how various physical settings housed the communal activities of Jesus's earliest followers can help Latter-day Saints appreciate the religious experience of their predecessors. [^3]
