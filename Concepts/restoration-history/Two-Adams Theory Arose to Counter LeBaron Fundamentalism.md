# The "Two-Adams" Reading Arose to Counter LeBaron Fundamentalism

## The Claim
[[Christopher Blythe|Chris Blyth]] offers a historical explanation for why the church cracked down on Adam-God in the mid-twentieth century and why the **"two Adams" / "Adam is only a title"** reading was developed: it was a **reaction to fundamentalist polygamist proselytizing**, not a disinterested doctrinal correction. Adam-God "becomes a major component to fundamentalist thought" and got carried "in a missionary way" by the **Church of the Firstborn of the Fullness of Times**, founded in 1955 by the LeBaron brothers (Joel, Ross, and Floren). [[Spencer W. Kimball]] — who grew up around the LeBarons in the Mexican colonies — and [[Bruce R. McConkie]] reacted to this threat, and "it's right in this era that two atoms comes out to try to explain Brigham Young's teachings" [^1].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The fundamentalist church actively recruited (strong — explains the urgency).** Unlike earlier fundamentalist splinters that kept a "separate secret priesthood" while still claiming the LDS Church was the church, the Church of the Firstborn "actually does missionary work" — you "might go walk out to your car after church and find a leaflet from missionaries" of the rival church. A "huge chunk of missionaries in France get converted... and sent home early"; reining this in "is one of the first things President Hinckley had to do as a general authority" [^1]. Adam-God was a live recruiting tool, so the church had institutional reason to redefine it.

2. **Kimball had personal exposure to the LeBarons (moderate — biographical).** Kimball "grew up around LeBarons" in the colonies (the post-Manifesto plural-marriage refuge), and as apostle/prophet he had repeated, intimidating interactions with them — LeBarons "showing up at his home," and a hotel-room encounter where one of a former friend's sons appeared in "the old school garment... designed to threaten him." So Kimball's well-known rejection of Adam-God is, on this reading, partly defensive against a specific schism [^2].

3. **McConkie's *How to Start a Cult* targeted the LeBarons (corroborating).** One of McConkie's earliest writings, "How to Start a Cult," is "a discussion of the LeBarons" — direct evidence that the apostle most associated with denouncing Adam-God was preoccupied with this fundamentalist movement [^3].

4. **A schism "to the right," historically novel (frames the stakes).** Blyth calls this "the first time we've had an exit from the church to the right and not to the left" — modern-day polygamy-affirmers gaining steam — which Whitfield likens to today's polygamy *deniers*. Each decade has "their sort of major schism heresy the church deals with," and Adam-God was the doctrinal battlefield of the 1950s–60s one [^4].
