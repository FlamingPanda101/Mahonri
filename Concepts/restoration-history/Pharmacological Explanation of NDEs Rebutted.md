# Pharmacological Explanation of NDEs Rebutted

## The Point
A common debunking move is to attribute the near-death experience (NDE) to the drugs given to a patient during a medical crisis, since certain anesthetics and hallucinogens (LSD, ketamine, nitrous oxide) can produce dissociative, dreamlike states [^1]. Moody concedes the surface plausibility but argues the explanation fails on the data: drug-induced "death" visions are vague, mutually inconsistent, and markedly different from real NDEs, and — decisively — in many cases **no drug was administered at all**, often long before any medical attention was reached [^2]. The enormous variety of drugs given (aspirin, antibiotics, adrenalin, gaseous anesthetics — most with no central-nervous-system effect) and the fact that drugged and undrugged patients report the *same* experiences sink the hypothesis [^2].

## Evidence
1. Moody deliberately selected the anesthetic case (a nitrous-oxide dental experience) that *most* resembles real NDEs — yet it is "extremely vague," its light is impersonal, it carries no ineffable peace, and the subject denies leaving her body, attributing the rotation to the dentist's chair rather than her own motion [^1].
2. The most significant counter-fact: "in many cases no drug had been administered prior to the experience nor, in some cases, were drugs given even after the near-death event"; subjects often insist the experience occurred "before any kind of medication was given... long before they obtained any sort of medical attention" [^2].
3. The drugs actually used "range from substances such as aspirin through antibiotics and the hormone adrenalin to local and gaseous anesthetics. Most of these drugs are not associated with central nervous system or psychic effects" [^2].
4. "There are no differences as groups between the experiences related by those who were given no drugs at all and the experiences related by those who were under medications" [^2].
5. One woman "died" twice years apart; she had *no* experience the first time (when anesthetized) and a complex one the second time (under no drugs) — the opposite of what the drug theory predicts [^2].
6. Moody adds that even granting drugs alter consciousness, the assumption that drug-states are therefore "unreal" is not universally held; across history hallucinogens (peyote/mescaline cults) have been treated as *pathways* into other realities — so a drug parallel need not mean the NDE is illusory [^3].

## Best Quotes
> "The most significant one is simply that in many cases no drug had been administered prior to the experience nor, in some cases, were drugs given even after the near-death event." [^2]

> "It also should be noted that there are no differences as groups between the experiences related by those who were given no drugs at all and the experiences related by those who were under medications of various types." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The reductive drug hypothesis presupposes that the mind is nothing but brain chemistry. Moody's data resist that materialism: experiences arise where no chemical cause exists. This is congenial to the Restored understanding that the spirit is a real entity distinct from the body and survives its dissolution — the "inner man" is not merely a pharmacological epiphenomenon (Alma 40; Alma 42:13; [[D&C 93|D&C 93]] on the spirit as intelligence in its own right).
