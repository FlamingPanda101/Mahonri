# Closing the Canon Was Used to Declare Prophecy Ended

## The Point
[[Thomas A. Wayment]] shows that **regulating prophecy** went hand in hand with closing the canon. The same logic deployed against Jews — that the Holy Spirit had withdrawn and prophecy had ceased (early Christian supersessionism, e.g. [[Justin Martyr]]) — was also turned against fellow Christians who rejected "controlled or regulated revelation." Cessationism is thus not a neutral observation but a tool of boundary-drawing and authority.

## Evidence

### 1. Justin Martyr: prophecy ended among the Jews
In *Dialogue with Trypho* (late 2nd c.), Justin argues the gifts of the Spirit came to "rest upon" Christ and thus "to come to an end with him, so that there would be no more prophets among your people as of old." This is an early statement of **supersessionism** — that God ended his covenant with the Jews and removed the prophetic voice — contrasted in the chapter with Paul's own view (Romans 11:26). [^1]

### 2. The same argument turned against Christians
"The regulation of prophecy was used against Jews and also other Christians … who broke away from the Western Church because those Christians rejected the Western view of controlled or regulated revelation." [^2]

### 3. Hippolytus: scripture as the measure of inspiration
[[Hippolytus of Rome]] (early 3rd c.) sought to "normalize Christian worship practices" by subordinating inspiration to scripture: "scripture and inspiration needed to be received in measure and ought to be subject to one another." His test-case is the failed prophet of Pontus, who predicted the Day of Judgment within a year, led the brethren to ruin their lands and possessions, and was "shamed as a deceiver" when nothing happened — "but the Scriptures were shown as true." [^3]

### 4. The closing of the canon had revelatory implications
Wayment frames the whole chapter around the point that "the closing of the canon had implications for what could be revealed." A closed canon is, in effect, a declaration that revelation has a terminus. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "there would be no more prophets among your people as of old … for after him there has not been a prophet among you." — Justin Martyr, *Dialogue with Trypho* 87 [^1]

> "If it does not happen just as I said, do not believe the Scriptures anymore but do whatever each of you wishes." — the false prophet of Pontus, in Hippolytus, *Commentary on Daniel* 4.19 [^3]

## LDS Connection
For [[Latter-day Saints]], this exposes cessationism as a historically contingent move, not a self-evident truth — the same "prophecy has ceased" reflex that the boy Joseph Smith met from a minister (cf. [[Montanus and the Overcorrection Against Prophecy]]). Wayment notes Jewish sources describe prophecy continuing (Josephus records a Jewish prophet foretelling the temple's AD 70 destruction), undercutting Justin's claim. [^1]
