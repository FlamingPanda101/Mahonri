# The Polygamy Q&A Page Concedes Major Historical Facts the Church Long Denied

## The Claim

Reading the new Gospel Topics Q&A page on polygamy as a *historical document* rather than as something to attack, historian [[Benjamin Park]] argues its most significant feature is the **list of facts it now openly concedes** — facts the Church institutionally downplayed or denied for much of the 20th century. The page documents real institutional evolution: it admits Joseph's 30+ wives, raises the participation figure to **20–30%** (against [[Gordon B. Hinckley|Hinckley]]'s old 2–5%), grants that leaders taught polygamy was **necessary for exaltation**, and acknowledges **post-Manifesto polygamy** continued with leadership approval. Park's frame: the page reveals "where the LDS institution currently stands" on what it is willing to say out loud.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. 30+ plural wives, including teenagers — rate: strong (page concession)
The page admits [[Joseph Smith]] had over 30 plural wives. Park notes this was already conceded in the 2014-era Gospel Topics essays, but its restatement matters: the page is built around preventing a faith crisis *when* members learn this, rather than denying it. (The teenage-wife dimension — e.g. [[Helen Mar Kimball]] — is the part most likely to shock.)[^2]

### 2. 20–30% participation vs. Hinckley's 2–5% — rate: strong (a reversed number)
On the Utah period, the page states **20–30% of Latter-day Saints** were involved in plural marriage in some way. Park calls this "a long way from Gordon B. Hinckley in the 1990s, who insisted that only two to five percent" practiced it — a number Park considers misleading. Conceding that polygamy *shaped* the 19th-century Mormon experience, rather than being a fringe practice, is a substantive correction (cf. [[Polygamy Was a Minority Practice That Declined Steadily]]).[^3]

### 3. Polygamy taught as necessary for exaltation — rate: strong (page concession)
The page admits many LDS leaders taught that polygamy was **necessary for exaltation** — a point the Church "had long denied for many years." Park flags that this admission carries an unacknowledged cost: it concedes a form of **prophetic fallibility** (leaders taught as doctrine something later abandoned), without the page following that implication through (cf. [[Belief in Polygamy Was Required for the Temple Only in 1856]]).[^4]

### 4. Post-Manifesto (post-1890) polygamy with leadership approval — rate: strong (page concession)
The page acknowledges that polygamy continued after [[Wilford Woodruff]]'s 1890 Manifesto, including new sealings performed with leadership approval — something the Church denied for a long time. Park's one critique is the word "only" in the page's "**only** 200 polygamous sealings took place after 1890": that *only*, he says, "is doing a lot of work."[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic might say these are not real concessions but managed disclosures. (a) Most of these facts were already public in the Gospel Topics essays a decade earlier or in scholarship; the page restates rather than reveals, so calling them "newly conceded" overstates the novelty. (b) Each concession is paired with a faith-preserving frame (the page's stated goal is preventing faith crises), so the candor is *instrumental* — disclosed to inoculate, not merely to inform. (c) Park himself flags residual minimizing ("only 200 sealings"; Hinckley's discredited 2–5%), which shows the page still shapes the numbers rhetorically. So the fair conclusion is that the page marks **genuine institutional movement toward candor on the hardest facts**, while remaining a *correlated, faith-protective* document rather than a neutral historical account — which is exactly why Park reads it as a snapshot of "where the institution currently stands."
