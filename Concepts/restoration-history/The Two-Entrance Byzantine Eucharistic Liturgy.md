## The Point
The common Constantinian and Byzantine worship service had two parts or "entrances." The First Entrance ("liturgy of the word") was open to catechumens and baptized alike, centering on scripture reading and the sermon. The Second Entrance ("liturgy of the faithful" or "Entrance of the Mysteries") dismissed the uninitiated, closed and veiled the doors, and brought baptized members into the Eucharistic mysteries — culminating in receiving the consecrated emblems through the parted veil at the chancel screen.

## Evidence
1. First Entrance: a procession of bishop, priests, and deacons in liturgical vestments bearing the Gospels (the "Word of God"), followed by the congregation, all moving from the narthex into the nave with singing, incense, prayers, and offerings. The Gospels were placed on the altar and read; then the bishop delivered the sermon from his cathedra.
2. Second Entrance: the uninitiated catechumens were dismissed, the doors closed or veiled, and the "liturgy of the faithful" began with a procession of the Eucharistic bread and wine placed on the veiled sanctuary altar.
3. Initiated members cited a creedal profession of faith and participated in the anaphora (Eucharistic prayer), which included the trisagion ("Holy, Holy, Holy is the Lord God Almighty"; Isaiah 6:1–4; Revelation 4:2–8), liturgically merging the earthly church with the heavenly temple.
4. After the bishop consecrated the bread and wine, the congregation formed a line at the chancel screen, where the bishop offered the emblems through the parted veil.
5. Cyril of Jerusalem instructed celebrants to receive the Eucharist with the right hand while holding the left hand underneath "in the shape of a throne" to symbolically receive the royal body of Christ.
6. A final prayer of thanksgiving and a priestly blessing (based on Numbers 6:22–27) preceded the dismissal and exit-procession.

## Best Quotes
> "For the Second Entrance, or Entrance of the Mysteries, the uninitiated catechumens were dismissed, the doors of the church were closed or veiled, and the 'liturgy of the faithful' would commence." [^1]

> "The initiated members of the congregation ... would then cite a creedal profession of faith and participate in the anaphora — a Eucharistic prayer led by priests in the sanctuary that included the trisagion ... which liturgically represented the merging of the earthly church with the heavenly temple." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Grey and Ellison both note the formalized sequence, solemn processions, communal prayers, and a "climactic end only for the baptized representing communion with deity" as resemblances worth pondering — while cautioning that close examination often dissolves apparent one-to-one parallels to Latter-day Saint temple worship. [^3]
