# Mystagogy - Teaching Sacraments Only After Baptism

## The Point
In the fourth-century Jerusalem rite Egeria describes, baptismal candidates were deliberately *not* taught "the mystery of baptism" until after they had actually experienced the ordinance. Only then, over the eight days following Easter, did the bishop unfold "the deep mysteries of baptism" — a practice of reserving the deepest sacramental teaching for the initiated.

## Evidence
1. **Mysteries withheld until after the ordinance.** "Baptismal candidates are not, however, taught about the mystery of baptism until after they have experienced that ordinance on Easter Sunday. After that, the bishop spends eight days teaching them the deep mysteries of baptism." [^1]
2. **The "awe-inspiring rites."** Cyril's elevated language foreshadows what one scholar (Edward Yarnold) called "the awe-inspiring rites" of initiation the candidates were preparing to receive. [^2]
3. **Sequence of initiation.** Candidates emerged from "baptism by immersion and anointing with oil, preparatory to receiving the Eucharist," partaking of the Eucharist "for the first time in the company of the believers, and then hearing another set of sermons revealing the mysteries of this holy ordinance." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Baptismal candidates are not … taught about the mystery of baptism until after they have experienced that ordinance on Easter Sunday. After that, the bishop spends eight days teaching them the deep mysteries of baptism." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The principle of withholding the deepest meaning of an ordinance until after one has received it — sacred knowledge reserved for the initiated, unfolded only after participation — is a recognizable structural parallel to Latter-day Saint temple practice, and an instance of "holy envy" the chapter invites.
