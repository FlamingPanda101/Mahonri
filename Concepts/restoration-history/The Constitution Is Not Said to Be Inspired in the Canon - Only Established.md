# The Constitution Is Not Said to Be Inspired in the Canon — Only "Established"

## The Claim

[[Brian Thomassen]] restricts the "was the Constitution inspired?" question to **canon** — what is binding on a member of the church — and observes that **the word "inspired" appears nowhere in the canon** with respect to the Constitution. The canonical predicate is "**established**" (and "suffered to be established"), not "inspired." Anyone asserting "the Constitution is inspired" is *inserting* a word the text does not contain. ("If the word's not in there, you're inserting it. Why are you inserting? … I'm going to go with the text that I am bound to.")[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The relevant canon says "established," not "inspired"
- [[D&C 101]]:80 — "I have **established** the Constitution of this land, by the hands of wise men whom I raised up." (Note: "Constitution" capitalized.)
- [[D&C 98]]:5–6 — "the law of the land which is **constitutional**… belongs to all mankind." (Note: "constitutional" *lowercase* — possibly the base meaning, an organizing/fundamental-law principle, not necessarily the U.S. Constitution.)
- [[D&C 101]]:77 — "the laws and constitution of the people, which I have **suffered to be established**."
None of these uses "inspired."[^2][^3]

### 2. "Established" ≠ "inspired"
Webster's 1828 glosses *established* as "to affirm, to confirm, or to appoint." The further phrase "**suffered to be established**" reads naturally as "allowed / permitted to be established" (cf. the divine-right-of-kings debates and King Benjamin's "suffered to be king" in Mosiah). The predicate the text actually supplies is permission/appointment, which Thomassen develops into a *utility* reading rather than divine dictation. See [[The Constitution Is Inspired in the Sense of Useful Not Divinely Embodied]].[^4]

### 3. The same "insert the word" pattern occurs with the Bible
There is likewise no verse calling the Bible "inspired" except [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]], and even there the Greek (*theopneustos*) does not mean "inspired." So "the Constitution is inspired" repeats a habit of reading a later category back into a text that lacks it. The disciplined move is to argue *from* the canonized wording. This dovetails with the canon/inspiration distinctions in [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]].[^5]

> **Steelman / honest assessment:** Thomassen repeatedly grants that this is a *positioning*, not a refutation — "people can disagree with my position; I'm simply saying the word is not in the text." Critics could fairly reply that (a) restricting to the literal word "inspired" is a strong lexical constraint, since "established by the hands of wise men whom I raised up unto this very purpose" plausibly *conveys* inspiration without the term; and (b) general-authority teaching (which he deliberately brackets) has long affirmed an inspired Constitution. The argument's force is methodological discipline (argue from binding canon), not a demonstration that the Constitution is *un*inspired.

## Best Quotes

> "If somebody says the Constitution is inspired, does that word appear, say, in the Doctrine and Covenants? … And it does not. There's no verse that says the Constitution is inspired."[^6]

> "I'm simply saying the word is not in the text. And if the word's not in there, you're inserting it… I'm going to go with the text that I am bound to as a member of the church. That's my sandbox."[^1]
