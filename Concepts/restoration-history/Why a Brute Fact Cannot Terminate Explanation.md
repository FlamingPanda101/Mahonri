# Why a Brute Fact Cannot Terminate Explanation

## The Point
A standard escape from the cosmological argument is to halt the regress at a "brute fact" — most fundamental laws of nature that simply have no explanation. Feser argues this is incoherent. [^1] Some philosophers (he cites J. L. Mackie and Bertrand Russell) hold that we can explain phenomena by laws, those by deeper laws, until we reach a most-fundamental level that is just an unintelligible brute fact. [^2] But an explanatory regress terminating in a brute fact is, on inspection, "no more coherent than the notion of an effect being produced by an instrument that is not the instrument of anything." [^3] Feser also defends PSR against the famous "conjunction of all contingent truths" objection by denying that propositions are extra objects needing explanation. [^4]

## Evidence
1. **The shelf-and-book analogy.** Suppose a book stays aloft because it rests on a shelf, but the shelf's staying aloft is a brute fact with no explanation. Have we explained the book's position? No: by hypothesis the shelf has *no* tendency to stay up, so it has none to *impart* to the book — "the 'explanation' the shelf provides in such a case would be completely illusory." Relocating the brute tendency to the book itself only "relocates" the illusion. [^5]
2. **The nomological regress.** The same holds for laws: if law A is a brute fact, then "if there is neither anything about A itself that can explain A's own operation nor anything beyond A that can explain it, then A has nothing to impart to B or C" — so the appearance of having explained the less-fundamental laws is illusory. [^3]
3. **The "big conjunctive proposition" objection rebutted.** Critics argue the conjunction of all contingent truths is itself contingent, yet can be explained neither by a necessary truth (which entails only necessary truths) nor by a contingent one (which would be part of the conjunction, making it self-explaining) — so PSR fails. [^6] Feser replies that, for the Scholastic realist, propositions are abstract objects existing only in minds; they are not a "third kind of object" over and above concrete things and the minds entertaining them. Once we have explained the concrete states of affairs and the minds, "we have explained everything that needs to be explained." [^7]
4. **Explanation need not be entailment.** The objection also assumes an explanans must logically entail its explanandum. But, quoting Alexander Pruss, "Scientific causal explanations, in general, simply do not give conditions that entail the explanandum" — e.g., the sun's gravity explains the planets' orbits without strictly entailing them. "What PSR requires is that an explanans make an explanandum intelligible," not that it entail it. [^8]
5. **Quantum objections fail a fortiori.** Even if quantum phenomena lacked a *cause*, "they certainly have an explanation, since they presuppose and are made intelligible by the laws of quantum mechanics." [^9] The burden, Feser concludes, is on the critic to show how PSR can coherently be rejected. [^10]

## Best Quotes
> "The appearance of having 'explained' C and B is completely illusory if A is a brute fact, because if there is neither anything about A itself that can explain A's own operation nor anything beyond A that can explain it, then A has nothing to impart to B or C." [^3]

> "What PSR requires is that an explanans make an explanandum intelligible, and there is no reason to think that that requires logical entailment." [^8]

## LDS Connection
This defense is a strong shared weapon against atheism. The "it's just a brute fact" move — whether applied to the universe's existence, the fine-tuned constants, or the fundamental laws — is exactly the maneuver Latter-day Saint apologists also reject when debating materialists (compare [[Nothing Comes from Nothing - The Krauss Equivocation]]). Feser's point that a brute fact can *impart* no intelligibility downstream is forceful and faith-friendly. The honest divergence is downstream again: Feser's anti-brute-fact reasoning, combined with divine simplicity, drives to a single self-explanatory *actus purus*. The Restoration agrees reality is intelligible and not grounded in sheer brute facts, but locates the self-existent, non-brute layer in eternal realities — uncreated intelligence, matter, and law (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]) — rather than in a metaphysically simple being. Note one fair tension for the LDS side: eternal law and uncreated matter must themselves be intelligible rather than brute, a demand Feser would press; LDS thinkers answer that these are necessary, self-existent features of reality, not unexplained surds (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]).
