# Monotheism Is a 17th-Century Invention

## The Claim

"Monotheism" as a category — one being as the sole ground of all reality, denying any other divine beings — is a modern (17th-century) concept anachronistically read back into the Bible. The Bible instead teaches a divine plurality existing in unity (the Godhead). [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The term is modern.** Per [[Joshua Sijuwade]] (quoting the scholarly consensus), "monotheism is something which came later in the 17th century"; biblical religion predates and does not fit the concept. (Evidence: historically the *word* "monotheism" was coined by Henry More in the 1660s — accurate; the stronger claim that the *concept* is wholly absent from the Bible is contested.) [^1]
2. **Biblical oneness is qualitative.** What the Bible asserts is Yahweh's unique supremacy (qualitative), not numerical solitude — see [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]]. [^2]
3. **Practical upshot.** "If you're out preaching monotheism, you are not preaching what the Bible teaches. The Bible teaches a divine plurality that exists in a unity. This is what we call the Godhead." [^3]
4. **Steelman.** Critics grant the *word* is modern but deny that settles the *concept*: ideas can predate their labels (Israel could be functionally monotheistic before anyone coined "monotheism," just as "gravity" existed before Newton named it). They argue Deutero-Isaiah, the Shema's later reception, and Second Temple texts show a real movement toward exclusive monotheism, and that "the term is recent" is a semantic point that doesn't disprove the underlying belief. (Evidence: a strong logical objection — etymological recency ≠ conceptual absence; the LDS argument over-leans on the dating of the word.) The LDS reply: the burden is on the monotheist to show the *concept* (sole-existence) in texts that elsewhere presuppose a divine council, and the "boast of supremacy" idiom (see [[None Beside Me Is an ANE Boast of Supremacy]]) better explains the apparent exclusivity. [^4]
