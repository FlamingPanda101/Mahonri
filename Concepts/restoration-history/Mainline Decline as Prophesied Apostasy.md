# Mainline Decline as Prophesied Apostasy

#type/claim

## The Claim
The panel reads the declining clergy-belief numbers — especially mainline Protestants' soft affirmation of the bodily resurrection — through a Latter-day Saint lens: this is the prophesied "**awful situation**" of a corrupted Christianity, the rise of "therapeutic deism," and a fulfillment of warnings against a counterfeit gospel. [[Jonah Barnes]] frames it as God's verdict that "their professors of religion are all corrupt" — a problem with the pastors, not the people.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Galatians 1:8 — "another gospel" (the keystone proof-text).** Clergy who do not preach the bodily resurrection are said to "preach a separate gospel," directly invoking [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]]: "though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel... let him be accursed."[^2] *Rating: the panel's strongest scriptural hook; a recurring Ward Radio motif. The steelman is that Paul's anathema targeted Judaizing law-keeping, not modern liberal christology.*

2. **Ether/Mormon — "awake to our awful situation."** The host alludes to the Book of Mormon warning (cf. [[Ether 3.14|Ether]]; Mormon's lament) that people will "someday awaken to our awful situation," applied here to being "surrounded by Christians who don't believe that Christ rose from the grave."[^3] *Rating: homiletic application, not exegesis of a specific verse.*

3. **The First Vision allusion.** "Their professors of religion are all corrupt... He didn't say the people are all bad. It's about the pastors" — echoes the Joseph Smith–History account where the Lord says the creeds were an abomination and "those professors were all corrupt."[^1] The panel is careful to aim the critique at clergy, not laity. *Rating: faithful to the LDS source text's wording.*

4. **"Therapeutic deism" as the diagnosis (cultural).** [[Jonah Barnes]] invokes his meme of "therapeutic deism with a side of anti-Mormonism from the mainline Jesus place" — a drive-thru, feel-good religion stripped of the costly resurrection claim.[^4] This borrows the sociological category "Moralistic Therapeutic Deism" (Smith & Denton) as evidence of hollowing-out. *Rating: apt cultural framing; the borrowed term lends it credibility.*

5. **The martyr contrast (rhetorical).** "We were staked and burnt and eaten by the lions because we said he rose from the grave. And now it's like, 'Eh, well, a fourth, a fourth.'"[^5] The early-church willingness to die for the bodily resurrection is set against modern clergy ambivalence. *Rating: powerful rhetoric; not a data point.*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A mainline theologian would reject the "apostasy" frame: mainline Protestantism's doctrinal latitude is a *feature* (liberty of conscience on non-creedal matters), not decay; many "soft" clergy still center the gospel ethically and pastorally. Moreover, reading a 25% non-affirmation rate as fulfilling ancient prophecy is post-hoc — the same data could be explained by ordinary secularization, survey artifacts (see [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]]), or theological reinterpretation rather than supernatural prophecy-fulfillment. Even the skeptical host on the panel cautions that the decline is "not so surprising" given Protestant diversity, and need not be read apocalyptically.[^6]

**Context.** This concept is the episode's LDS payoff: the Great Apostasy thesis (mainstream Christianity drifted from original truth) is here re-evidenced not by ancient sources but by *contemporary survey data on living clergy* — a rhetorically novel move that turns demography into apologetics.
