# Christology - Enochic Background
#type/claim

## The Claim
Early Christian understandings of who Jesus is did not emerge in a vacuum: they drew on a developed Second Temple "Son of Man" tradition crystallized in the Book of Parables (1 Enoch 37-71). That tradition already supplied a heavenly, pre-existent, worshipped mediator who sits on a throne of glory and judges the wicked. When the New Testament presents Jesus as the Son of Man, it is reaching into this Enochic conceptual pool, so the Book of Enoch forms part of the essential background for early Christology.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The Enochic Son of Man is proto-christological.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], 1 Enoch is "so significant to the discussion of early Christology because of the Son of Man character." The figure is named before creation, is concealed with God until the eschaton, receives worship alongside God, and executes final judgment — features the New Testament then attaches to Jesus. Developed in [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]].
- **A shared Danielic source, personalized.** Both 1 Enoch and the Gospels build on Daniel 7:13-14's "one like a son of man." Enoch personalizes the collective Danielic figure into an individual heavenly mediator; the Gospels do the same with Jesus, which is why Jesus's preferred self-designation carries Enochic resonances of authority, hiddenness, and eschatological judgment.
- **Transmission was plausible.** 1 Enoch was attested at Qumran (eleven manuscripts) and quoted as scripture by Jude, so its categories were "in the water" of first-century Judaism and available to shape how Jesus's earliest followers conceptualized him.
- **Steelman / caveats.** Skeptics of direct dependence note (1) the dating of the Parables (1 Enoch 37-71) is debated — they are the one section *absent* from the Qumran Enoch fragments, so a late-1st-century-CE date would make them contemporaneous with, not prior to, the Gospels; and (2) Jesus's "Son of Man" usage may draw straight from Daniel without Enochic mediation, and sometimes simply means "human one" (Ezekiel's idiom). The cautious claim is that Enoch evidences a *milieu* in which such a heavenly Son of Man was conceivable; the strong claim of literary borrowing is more contested. This is consistent with treating Enoch as conceptually formative without being historical (see [[Book of Enoch - Not Historical but Conceptually Influential]]).
