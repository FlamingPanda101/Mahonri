# Three Millennial Positions: Premillennialism, Postmillennialism, Amillennialism

## The Point

The single mention of "a thousand years" in [[Revelation 20.2-3|Revelation 20]] — the only place in the New Testament a millennial reign appears — "would come to dominate future eschatological discussions among Christians." [^1] (*Millennium* = Latin *mille*, "thousand," + *annus*, "year.") Over the following two thousand years, [[Nicholas J. Frederick]] explains, three positions developed, each "reacting in some way to the anxiety of Jesus's prolonged absence." [^1]

## Evidence

1. **Premillennialism** — "likely... the earliest of the three." It teaches that Jesus's Second Coming will occur *prior to* the Millennium, the thousand years of peace. "Simply put, this view holds that things get worse before they get better." [^1]
2. **Postmillennialism (progressive millennialism)** — "a more recent development," teaching that Jesus will *not* return until the *end* of the millennial thousand years. Held by "more liberal-leaning Christians who see the current state of the world as generally positive and continually improving because of the preaching of the word of God and the increasing success of social reform movements." [^2] (Credit for systematizing it is "often given to Daniel Whitby (1638-1726), an English Unitarian minister.") [^3]
3. **Amillennialism** — the view that "the thousand-year period mentioned in the book of Revelation should be interpreted symbolically rather than literally." [^2] This became the dominant Western position thanks largely to [[Augustine]]. See [[Augustine Recast the Millennium as the Present Age of the Church]].
4. **American resurgence of postmillennialism.** Frederick notes postmillennialism "saw a resurgence in the United States following the events of the American Revolution and the Second Great Awakening," but Joseph Smith and contemporaries such as William Miller "argued for a premillennial view of history." [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Over the next two thousand years, three different views on the millennium would develop, all of them reacting in some way to the anxiety of Jesus's prolonged absence." [^1]

> "Premillennialism teaches that Jesus will return to earth prior to the Millennium, and postmillennialism teaches that Jesus will return after the Millennium." [^5]

## LDS Connection

"This viewpoint [premillennialism] is held by many Christians in the United States, including members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints." [^1] Frederick cites Jon R. Stone: most Latter-day Saints "would identify comfortably with premillennialism, the millennial position held by many Americans." [^6]
