# Divine Agency and the Divine-Name Bearer

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
When Jesus speaks and acts as God — receiving worship, bearing the divine name, doing what the Father does ([[John 5.19-23|John 5:19-23]]) — this proves he *is* the one being Yahweh.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The kingship / agency motif: a representative bears the king's name
Throughout the OT and NT, a king sends a representative who **bears the royal name** and "will literally speak as if they're God" — "I, the king, declare the following." A first-century reader would instantly recognize this *shaliach*/agent motif. So Jesus acting and speaking as God need not mean he is the same being as the Father; he is the sent, name-bearing representative. *(Strong: the agency/*shaliach* tradition is well attested in Second Temple scholarship.)* [^1]

### 2. [[John 5.19-23|John 5:19-23]] is agency, not identity
"The Son can do nothing of himself but what he seeth the Father do" — the Father *shows* and *commits* judgment to the Son and *sends* him. That is delegated authority (agency) plus total unity of will, not numerical identity. *(Strong.)* [^2]

### 3. The cross-exam trap and its resolution
Avery's most effective move: "Can the one sent in Yahweh's name claim to be Yahweh?" Framed about a *king's messenger*, the honest answer is "no — a messenger isn't the king." But the parallel fails: with the **Godhead/court**, a member *can* say "I am the court," because "Yahweh" can name the one *what*. Hansen says he kept insisting "but we're talking about Yahweh" while Avery kept it about a generic king — so the audience perceived a contradiction that wasn't one. *(Moderate: a self-serving reconstruction of a moment Hansen concedes he lost rhetorically, but the who/what distinction is a coherent escape.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
The agency reading has real limits: agents in Jewish tradition are *not worshipped*, yet Jesus receives worship ([[John 20.28|John 20:28]], Phil 2:10) and is given the *unshared* divine name "LORD" (Phil 2:9-11 applying Isa 45:23) — Richard Bauckham's "divine identity" Christology argues the NT includes Jesus *within* the unique identity of Yahweh, not merely as his agent, because he shares the throne, creation, and the Name in ways no creaturely *shaliach* does. So the divine-name-bearer motif, the Trinitarian says, is evidence the Son belongs to the one being, not that he stands outside it. The LDS rejoinder is that "sharing the throne / Name" is exactly what a divinely authorized, fully-divine member of the Godhead would do, and that worship of the agent who bears the Name is the *point* of the motif (cf. [[Divine Name Theology]]).
