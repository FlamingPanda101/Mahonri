# Anti-Mormon Tropes Trace Back to a Few 1830s Forged Affidavits

## The Point
Reading [[Walter Martin]]'s *The Kingdom of the Cults* on air, the panel hits the claim that **E. D. Howe** was "a contemporary of the Smiths who did tremendous research." They show that Howe's *Mormonism Unveiled* (1834) — the first anti-Mormon book and the headwater for a huge share of later "Joseph Smith the money-digging fraud" material — rests on affidavits gathered by **Dr. Philastus Hurlbut** ("doctor" was his *given name*, not a credential). Hurlbut was an excommunicated dissenter who, per the *Saints* history and the Wikipedia entry they read aloud, **likely wrote the statements himself and had illiterate Palmyra neighbors sign them**, was found in court to have made death threats against Joseph Smith (barring him from continuing), and then **sold the collection to Howe**. Citing this as "tremendous research" is the core problem: a recycled trope can be traced back to a single forged, hostile source.[^1][^2][^3]

## Evidence
1. **The provenance is the rebuttal.** "His tremendous work was literally getting illiterate neighbors of Joseph Smith to sign affidavits he wrote… that he ended up in court in the 1840s." Hurlbut was so libelous "that a court of law… actually found him guilty," so "he couldn't publish his book, so he sold them to E. D. Howe, and E. D. Howe published them as *Mormonism Unveiled*."[^3] *Rating: strong; checkable against the historical record and matches [[Hurlbut Affidavits Need Their Hostile Context]].*
2. **The chain is recursive.** A chat-prompted point: "every anti-Mormon source can be traced… back to this document where it's, 'I quoted someone who also quoted this document, but we quoted it in two different documents so we could use our own.'" Two hostile witnesses sign affidavits "simultaneously using each other as witnesses," and it enters the record as fact.[^4] *Rating: moderate-to-strong; illustrates citation laundering, the mechanism behind [[The Internet Rewards the Short Lie Over the Long Truth]].*
3. **Self-correction on the details (a tier-C caveat).** The panel first conflated Hurlbut with **John C. Bennett** (Nauvoo, joined 1840, spiritual wifery / illicit abortions) before correcting on air: Bennett's anti-Mormon affidavit campaign is a *separate, later* episode; the *Mormonism Unveiled* chain is Hurlbut → Howe, Kirtland/Ohio, 1834. Latter-day Lion in the chat further tied Howe to the [[Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin|Spalding manuscript theory]] (which Hurlbut's own examination undercut).[^5][^6] *Rating: the underlying source-chain claim is sound; the live mix-up is why this is panel commentary, not scholarship.*

## Best Quotes
> "His tremendous work was literally getting illiterate neighbors of Joseph Smith to sign affidavits he wrote… they could not understand, to disparage him so badly that he ended up in court in the 1840s." [^3]

> "Every anti-Mormon source can be traced… back to this document where it's, 'I quoted someone who also quoted this document'… so we could use our own." [^4]

> "Imagine a Nazi apologist in 1939 publishing excerpts from *Mein Kampf* as legitimate history of the Jews, and then saying I'm forced to because there's no positive history of Jewish people." [^7]

## LDS Connection
This is the historiographic backbone of Latter-day Saint apologetics on Joseph Smith's character: the harsh "money-digger / vicious habits / destitute of moral character" portrait that critics still quote (and that Martin reprints as evidence) descends from a commissioned, hostile, and partly fabricated affidavit campaign. Knowing the provenance lets a believer answer the trope at the root rather than rebutting each downstream repetition — the same discipline as [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem]] and [[Read the Primary Sources Before Judging a Person]].
