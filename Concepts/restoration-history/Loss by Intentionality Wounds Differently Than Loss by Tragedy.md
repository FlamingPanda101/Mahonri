# Loss by Intentionality Wounds Differently Than Loss by Tragedy

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] draws a distinction the "love makes a family" framework collapses: **not all parental loss is the same**. A child who loses a parent to *tragedy* (death, and historically war or childbirth) and a child who loses a parent to *intentionality* (no-fault divorce, surrogacy, donor conception, single-parenthood-by-choice) both carry a wound — but the wounds differ in kind and in long-term effect. "There's actually a difference in how children go about being motherless and fatherless and the impact that it has on them long term."[^1]

The mechanism she and [[John Anderson (media)|John Anderson]] develop has three parts, built largely on Anderson's own case (his mother died of cancer when he was three; he was raised by his unremarried father):
1. **The absent parent's motive.** A child who loses a parent to death knows "she would be there if she could"; a child who loses a parent to divorce may carry the knowledge that the parent "is out there but doesn't care enough to be involved." "I lost my mother, but she loved me and she didn't want to go" versus "I lost my mother because she ran off with her boss."[^2]
2. **Mourned-with versus isolated.** When Anderson looked back on his mother, "everybody in [his] world" — father, sister, grandparents, aunts and uncles — "joined [him] in [his] grief" and "validated [his] loss," remembering her fondly. A child of divorce is often "the only one that looks back fondly on the home that they used to have," and when they voice it they are told "you wouldn't want your parents to be unhappy" or "your mother has found her true self now." "Children who lose their parent to intentionality… still have a burden. They're just very often isolated and trying to process it completely solo."[^3]
3. **The historical inversion.** Modern medicine and the end of mass-casualty wars mean children now lose parents to tragedy far less often — but we have *replaced* that with intentional loss: "what we do have is children losing their mother or father due to intentionality, because some adult wanted it that way." "We used to label mother loss as tragedy. Now we are creating mother loss in the name of progress."[^4]

Faust attaches an empirical claim (cited secondhand): "children… who lose a parent to death tend to fare better than children who lose their parent through divorce."[^5] She also concedes the relevant nuance on high-conflict marriage (see Supporting Evidence).

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The phenomenology is well-attested in adoptee/donor-conceived testimony (rate: real, qualitative).** The distinction between "lost but loved" and "given up / chosen against" tracks a recurring theme in first-person accounts from adoptees and donor-conceived adults, who frequently report a distinct grief tied to *intentional* relinquishment. This is genuine experiential evidence even though it is not a controlled outcome study.[^2][^3]
2. **The high-conflict concession strengthens credibility (rate: strong, primary).** Faust does not overclaim: she grants that "children do experience some measure of relief if the parents divorce" in a *high-conflict* marriage — while insisting those children still do not fare better than children of *low-conflict intact* marriages, and that the real solution is to build a low-conflict marriage through counseling and accountability. Conceding the strongest counter-case is a mark of a serious argument.[^6]
3. **The "fare better after death than divorce" claim is plausible but thinly sourced here (rate: weak as stated).** Faust attributes it to "a couple studies" without naming them on-air. There is a real research literature on differential outcomes by *type* of parental absence (bereavement vs. divorce), and the direction she reports is defensible, but in this source it is an uncited summary and should be flagged as such. *Rate: directionally supportable, but not established by anything cited in the episode.*[^5]

### Steelman of the progressive view

A fair progressive response: (a) **The comparison may be confounded.** Children of divorce differ from bereaved children in many ways besides the *reason* for loss — ongoing inter-parental conflict, custody instability, economic disruption, a living parent who may model the very behavior the child resents. If divorce outcomes are worse, the cause may be conflict and instability, not "intentionality" per se, which would mean low-conflict divorce or amicable co-parenting need not wound as Faust claims. (b) **"Intentionality" is doing moral, not empirical, work.** Grouping a mother fleeing an abusive husband, a couple using a sperm donor, and a deadbeat parent under one label ("some adult wanted it that way") flattens morally distinct situations; a donor-conceived child raised by two loving parents is not obviously in the same category as a child abandoned for an affair. (c) **The bereavement comparison can cut the other way.** A child of an amicable divorce retains an *available, living* parent who can still show up, apologize, and build a relationship — an option the bereaved child does not have; some research on resilience emphasizes ongoing contact. (d) **Avoiding divorce can trap children in harm.** Faust's "build a low-conflict marriage" prescription assumes both parents *can* and *will* do that work; where one will not, keeping the marriage "for the children" can expose them to exactly the chronic conflict her own argument concedes is damaging. Faust's reply is that the *category* of intentional, isolating loss is real even if individual cases vary, and that the policy danger is normalizing the deliberate manufacture of parent-loss as if it were costless.

## LDS Relevance

The distinction reinforces the LDS emphasis — codified in *The Family: A Proclamation to the World* — on **marital permanence and fidelity** as a structure owed to children, not merely an adult preference. The Proclamation warns that family disintegration brings "the calamities foretold by ancient and modern prophets," and Faust's "isolated in grief" mechanism gives a concrete, child-level face to that warning: the harm is not only statistical but relational and often unwitnessed. This complements [[The Law of Chastity as a Family-Stability Engine]], which frames covenant-keeping ("get married, be faithful, have children, in that order") as the upstream cause of family stability. The Restoration's strong stance against casual divorce and its sacralizing of family bonds through sealing are, on this reading, protective of precisely the child who would otherwise mourn alone. (An LDS reader will also note that the Church's narrow exceptions and its pastoral care for single-parent and blended families keep the *principle* from hardening into condemnation of those who suffered loss they did not choose.)
