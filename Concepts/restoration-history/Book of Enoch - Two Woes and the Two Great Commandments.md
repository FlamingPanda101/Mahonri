# Book of Enoch - Two Woes and the Two Great Commandments
#type/claim

## The Claim
The Epistle of Enoch (the woe-oracle section of 1 Enoch) pronounces curses on the wicked that cluster around two core failings — wrongs against God and wrongs against neighbor (oppressing the poor, perverting justice, idolatry/false worship). This pairing anticipates the structure Jesus gives the Law in the "two great commandments": love God and love your neighbor. The Enochic woes thus show that the two-fold summary of righteousness was already in the Second Temple ethical air, part of the conceptual background the New Testament inherits.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Woes built on social and cultic sin.** 1 Enoch's woe-oracles repeatedly condemn the rich and powerful who exploit the righteous and pervert justice, alongside those who commit idolatry and blaspheme — a two-track indictment covering the vertical (toward God) and horizontal (toward neighbor) axes of obligation. Per [[Dan McClellan]], this maps naturally onto the two great commandments (Matthew 22:37-40; cf. Deuteronomy 6:5 and Leviticus 19:18).
- **A continuity argument.** As with the larger Enochic background of early Christianity, the value here is showing the New Testament's moral framing did not appear from nowhere: the love-God/love-neighbor distillation has antecedents in the apocalyptic woe tradition, reinforcing [[Christology - Enochic Background]]'s theme that 1 Enoch shaped the conceptual world of Jesus's earliest audience.
- **Steelman / caveats.** The two-fold structure (duties to God / duties to neighbor) is broadly shared across Israelite covenant tradition — it is arguably already implicit in the Decalogue's two tablets — so one need not posit specific literary dependence of the Gospels on 1 Enoch to explain the parallel. The careful claim is that 1 Enoch attests the pairing in Second Temple Judaism, making it part of the milieu, rather than proving Jesus drew the two great commandments from Enoch in particular.
