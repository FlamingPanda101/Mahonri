# Comparing Models to Find the Most Coherent Jesus

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s distinctive epistemic posture in this conversation: he tells people "I am a Christian fundamentally before I am a Latter-day Saint."[^1] He treats Protestantism, Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and the Latter-day Saints as four competing *models* of the Jesus of the New Testament, and says the reason he constantly compares Latter-day Saints to the other groups is that he is "literally trying to evaluate which one of these options makes the most sense."[^2] The capstone: if he were convinced the Trinity were actually true, "I would convert."[^3] His claim is that this comparative, falsifiable method is the honest one — and that being a Christian means "genuinely trying to follow the Jesus of the New Testament," a description he extends to Protestants, Catholics, and Orthodox alike.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **Christian identity is prior to LDS identity.** By ranking "Christian first, Latter-day Saint second," Hansen reframes the whole "are Mormons Christian?" question: the operative definition of *Christian* is "someone genuinely trying to follow the Jesus of the New Testament," which is a discipleship criterion, not a creedal one (cf. [[Identity of Jesus Is Not a Theology Quiz]], [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]]). *Rating: rhetorically disarming* — it claims the broad tent before the metaphysics is argued.

2. **The comparison is the method, not an attack.** When Ruslan notes Hansen always measures Latter-day Saints against other Christian groups, Hansen owns it as the point: the models are genuine rivals and he is comparison-shopping for coherence with the New Testament. This also licenses his tu-quoque that Catholics and Orthodox "believe different things about [Jesus]" than each other (the filioque) — see [[Catholic Doctrinal Development Is the Same Double Standard]]. *Rating: a real move toward intellectual symmetry,* though Ruslan replies that the creedal traditions share a unified ontology Latter-day Saints do not.

3. **Stated falsifiability — "I would convert."** Hansen offers a concrete defeater for his own position: demonstrate the Trinity and he switches. *Rating: a strong sincerity signal* and an attempt to model the openness he asks critics to extend to Latter-day Saints. It also previews why he treats the GodLogic Trinity debate as central — for him the Trinity, not Joseph Smith, is the hinge.

4. **It pairs with reliability-not-infallibility.** The comparative method dovetails with how Hansen handles prophets: he weighs the whole tradition as a collective, reliable witness rather than demanding any single infallible authority (see [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]], [[The Collective Witness Model of Knowing]]). The same instinct — weigh the body of evidence, do not absolutize one data point — runs through both.

**Steelman of the critic.** Ruslan's pushback is twofold. First, the "I'd convert if the Trinity were true" offer is *still subjective* — Hansen concedes being "convinced" is itself a judgment call, so the bar can float. Second, the comparison flattens a real asymmetry: the Protestant/Catholic/Orthodox traditions, for all their infighting, converge on an ontologically transcendent, uncreated God and an uncreated Christ, whereas the LDS model alone denies the creator/creation gulf — so treating all four as equivalent "models" smuggles in the conclusion that the difference is one of degree, not kind. The critic can also note that "trying to follow the Jesus of the New Testament" is broad enough to include groups all sides would call heterodox, so it under-defines *Christian*.

## Best Quotes
> "I tell people that I am a Christian fundamentally before I am a Latter-day Saint." [^1]

> "Why do I constantly compare Latter-day Saints to other Christian groups? It's because I'm literally trying to evaluate which one of these options makes the most sense." [^2]

> "If I were to be convinced that the Trinity was actually true, I would convert." [^3]

> "When I... say what I mean by a Christian, I mean someone who's genuinely trying to follow the Jesus of the New Testament... I look at these traditions and say that they're Christian in that sense." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This is the meta-frame for Hansen's whole apologetic: rather than starting from "Joseph Smith was a prophet," he starts from "which model of the New Testament Jesus is most coherent?" and argues Latter-day Saints belong in that comparison as Christians. It reframes the disqualification debate ([[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]]) as a question of comparative coherence and explains why he makes the Trinity — not the gold plates — the decisive battleground.
