#type/claim

# Christian Metaphorical Reading Is Unfalsifiable Retreat

## The Claim (skeptical)
[[Dinesh D'Souza]] reframes three centuries of Jewish–Christian disputation as a clash of reading strategies: "the Jews are the biblical literalists, and the Christians are the metaphorical readers." His showcase: Jesus predicted the Temple's destruction (fulfilled when Rome razed it in 70 AD, ~40 years later) — yet when challenged, Jesus "doesn't say, 'wait 40 years'"; he says, "you're misreading me completely. When I said the temple, I was actually speaking of the temple of my body."[^1]

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]'s objection cuts at the *timing* of the interpretation: "He doesn't actually say that" — at least not in the moment of challenge. The metaphorical reading ("temple of my body") is **supplied after the fact** to absorb an awkward case. O'Connor's worry generalizes the pattern: a reading that can switch between literal and metaphorical *whenever the literal reading embarrasses it* is unconstrained — it can rescue any text from any objection, which means it predicts nothing and can be falsified by nothing. When O'Connor finally asks D'Souza point-blank, "Would you say then the flight to Egypt is a metaphor?", the demand is to *fix the reading in advance* rather than choose it to suit the verdict.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Post-hoc reading selection.** In D'Souza's own example the literal prediction *did* come true (the Temple fell), yet the move on offer is to relocate the referent to Jesus's body. The interpretation is chosen by which reading survives scrutiny, not fixed by the text — exactly the "you can broadcast on it anything you want" hazard Sam Harris raises in [[Metaphorical Truth and Narrative Reading]] ("tennis without the net").[^1]
2. **Heads-I-win structure.** A literal fulfillment (Temple razed) is claimed as a hit; a literal *embarrassment* is reread as metaphor (body). Both outcomes confirm the faith. A claim compatible with every result has no evidential content — the core unfalsifiability complaint.[^2]
3. **It evades the historical question.** O'Connor's repeated, unanswered "is the flight to Egypt a metaphor or not?" shows the retreat in action: rather than say whether [[Matthew 2.13-15|the Egypt episode]] is *history* (and so can contradict [[Luke 2.39|Luke]]) or *metaphor* (and so makes no historical claim), the metaphorical reading lets the apologist decline to commit — which is precisely what [[Prophecy-Driven Invention Undermines Gospel Trustworthiness|the invention objection]] needs answered.[^2]
4. **"Literalist vs metaphorical" misdescribes the dispute.** O'Connor is not demanding wooden literalism; he is demanding *consistency* — a reading fixed independently of the desired conclusion. Framing the disagreement as literal-vs-metaphor lets D'Souza cast objections as naïve, when the real charge is *ad hoc* reading. Compare [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]]: in practice the "metaphorical" reading is governed by what the tradition needs to be true.

## Apologetic Engagement
The apologist has principled replies, but they cost something:

- **The double-referent is textual, not invented.** The "temple of my body" reading is not D'Souza's *ad hoc* save — it is *in the text*: John 2:19–21 has Jesus say "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up," and the narrator gloss "But he spake of the temple of his body." So the metaphor has first-century warrant; it is not retrofitted by modern apologists. (That said, O'Connor's narrower point stands: the gloss is the *narrator's*, and Jesus does not offer it to his challengers in the moment — so it does not rescue the *exchange* D'Souza describes.)

- **Genre fixes the reading, if fixed in advance.** The non-arbitrary version of D'Souza's case is that *genre* — apocalyptic, parable, mundane history — determines literal vs figurative *before* any objection arises, not after. [[Metaphorical Truth and Narrative Reading|Narrative reading]] and [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context|accommodation]] are legitimate *if* the interpretive key is principled and applied consistently. The apologist's burden, which D'Souza does not discharge here, is exactly O'Connor's demand: *commit* to whether the Egypt narrative is history or symbol, and abide the consequences either way.

- **Latter-day Saint angle — let the literal stand and own it.** A Restoration reader can decline the retreat altogether: read [[Matthew 2.13-15|Matthew]] as making a genuine historical claim, grant it may sit in tension with [[Luke 2.39|Luke]], and locate inspiration in fallible human authorship rather than inerrant harmony (see [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context]]). This *answers* the unfalsifiability charge by refusing the literal/metaphor switch — paying with strict inerrancy rather than with intellectual consistency. The lesson O'Connor's objection teaches the apologist is real: a reading worth defending must be the reading you would have given *before* you knew which one the objection threatened.
