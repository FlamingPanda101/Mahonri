# The Purely Actual Actualizer - Aristotle's Unmoved Mover

## The Point
The hierarchical series of actualizers of a thing's *existence* must terminate in a cause that can actualize without itself being actualized — and Feser argues this terminus cannot merely *happen* to be uncaused; it must be devoid of all potentiality. "It just is actual, always and already actual ... it doesn't merely have actuality ... but it just is pure actuality itself." [^1] This is "an uncaused cause, or to use Aristotle's famous expression, an Unmoved Mover. More precisely, we might call it an unactualized actualizer." [^1] From the single fact that change occurs, Feser concludes there exists a **purely actual actualizer** that sustains everything in being at every moment.

## Evidence
- Why no potentiality at all: "being devoid of potentiality, there is nothing in it that could have needed any actualizing." It "could not in principle have had or needed" a cause. [^1]
- Stage 2 derives the divine attributes from pure actuality alone:
  - **Immutable** — having no potentials, "it cannot go from potentiality to actuality." [^2]
  - **Eternal / timeless** — "since existing within time entails changeability, an immutable cause must also be eternal in the sense of existing outside of time altogether." [^2]
  - **Immaterial, incorporeal** — matter entails changeability and temporality. [^2]
  - **Perfect** — a privation is an unrealized potential; pure act has none. [^3]
  - **One** — two of a kind need a differentiating feature; pure act has no privation/feature to differ by, so there can be only one. [^4]
  - **Omnipotent** — it is "the source of all the actualizing power anything else has." [^5]
  - **Fully good, intelligent, omniscient** — via the principle of proportionate causality, the forms of all things preexist in it "in something like the way thoughts exist in us." [^6]
- The result is reached "by beginning with ordinary individual objects" — a coffee cup — not from any premise about "the universe as a whole." [^7] Formal premises 12–14 conclude "there is a purely actual actualizer." [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "It doesn't merely have actuality, the way the things it actualizes do, but ... it just is pure actuality itself." [^1]

> "Since the cause of things is pure actuality and therefore devoid of potentiality, it cannot go from potentiality to actuality and is thus immutable or unchanging." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the **decisive fork** between Feser's classical theism and the Restoration. As an argument *against atheism*, the move is strong: it establishes a necessary, non-derivative sustainer of all existence here and now, which no materialist cosmology supplies. But the derived attribute-package — pure actuality entailing **immutability, timeless eternity, strict immateriality/incorporeality, and absolute simplicity** — is precisely what Latter-day Saint theology rejects of God the Father, who is embodied, acts within time, and grows in glory. The critique [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] argues this Aristotelian terminus was retrofitted onto the living, relational God of scripture; [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture]] presses that the "unmoved mover" is a philosopher's posit, not the biblical portrait; and [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]] develops the broader incompatibility. The LDS reply is not to deny a first sustaining cause but to deny that "purely actual" (zero potentiality) is required — a God who can act, relate, and respond has *actualized* capacities without being frozen into Aristotle's static perfection. Be fair to Feser: his inference is rigorous *given* the premise that any potentiality demands an external actualizer; the Restoration contests whether the ultimate being must be utterly potential-free.
