# Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls

## The Claim

Citing [[Yuval Noah Harari]], [[Glenn Scrivener]] argues that equality was "generated from a belief in God and souls" — and that "if we no longer believe in God and souls, then what do we do with this idea of equality?" The worry: secular modernity keeps the *conclusion* (equality) while discarding the *premises* (image of God, immortal souls) that supported it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The borrowed-capital argument.** This is the "Choose Your Miracle" chapter of Scrivener's book: the idea that equality "just came out of the ether" in the secular West is itself a quasi-miraculous claim; it must be grounded in something, and the historical something is the Christian story.[^2] *(A familiar apologetic — moral realism needs a metaphysical anchor; secular humanism inherits Christian conclusions without the foundation.)*

2. **[[Tom Holland]]'s self-aware version.** Holland reportedly reasons: "I do believe in the human-rights thing, and I now see the dependence upon the Christian thing, and I might as well be hung for a sheep as for a lamb — so I'm going to dive into the Christian thing."[^1] *(Illustrates the thesis functioning as a *conversion* argument: accept the fruit, accept the root.)*

3. **Steelman of O'Connor — the grounding question is separable.** O'Connor's key move: he is **not** offering a rival grounding for Western values. "The reason I'm here is not to provide a better grounding for Western values... I'm offering an internal critique." Whether equality is *true* or *well-grounded* is a different question from whether Christianity *historically caused* it.[^3] As an [[Ethical Emotivism as O'Connor's Metaethic|emotivist]], he cheerfully accepts that his moral statements lack objective grounding — "if you say that leaves you in a hole, I might just say, yeah, it totally does."[^4] *(Sharp — Scrivener's Harari point is a metaethical argument; O'Connor concedes the metaethics is "tangential" to the historical thesis under examination, defusing it.)*

4. **Shared intuitions, different foundations.** Brierley and Scrivener note all three share the moral intuitions despite radically different metaethics — see [[Shared Intuitions Despite Different Metaethics]].[^5] *(This reframes the dispute: the live question is historical causation, not present-day grounding.)*
