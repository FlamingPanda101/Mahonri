# The Real Faith Crisis Is Being Forced to Choose Between Faith and Science

## The Point

James P. Porter — a physiologist and former dean of BYU's College of Life Sciences who
candidly admits his *own* lingering discomfort with universal common ancestry — argues
that it is **not** the teaching of evolution that causes students to lose their faith.
The damage is done by *requiring* them to choose between faith and science. The cure is
not to suppress the science but to give students space to "grapple with these questions
'by study and also by faith'" (D&C 109:7) and to accept God's timetable for resolving
them.[^1] On this view a faithful university is the *best* place to teach evolution,
because there the subject can be "bathed in the light and color of the restored gospel"
rather than weaponized against belief.[^2]

## Evidence

- **The false dichotomy is the threat.** Porter watched the McLean v. Arkansas
  "creation-science" litigation (1981–82) and read both the science (fossil record,
  radiometric dating, the age of the earth) and Church writings. The hardest reading was
  Joseph Fielding Smith's *Man: His Origin and Destiny*, whose ultimatum — "you cannot
  believe in this theory of the origin of man, and at the same time accept the plan of
  salvation... You must choose the one and reject the other" — Porter says "pains me
  today because it forced the choosing between faith and science that I believe is not
  necessary."[^3]

- **Uncertainty is normal in *both* domains.** As dean, Porter's convocation talks
  taught that faith and science each contain uncertainty, and that this is acceptable.
  Faith, from Latin *fides* ("to trust"), is by Alma's own definition "not to have a
  perfect knowledge" (Alma 32:21). Science, too, lives with uncertainty — the standard
  practice of accepting a 5% chance of error when rejecting a null hypothesis. Patience,
  not abandonment, is the right response to incomplete understanding.[^4]

- **A faithful framing for the classroom.** When Porter taught introductory biology he
  opened the evolution unit with the Church's own 1992 BYU packet *Evolution and the
  Origin of Man*, stressed that science tests only physical evidence (not "things of the
  Spirit"), shared the adage "the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence," and
  noted that temple-recommend-holding members hold every level of acceptance "from zero
  to total" before teaching the science itself.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "I do not believe that it is the teaching of evolution that causes students to lose their faith. I believe it is the requiring them to choose between faith and science that can lead to a crisis." [^1]

> "We need to give them space and encouragement to grapple with these questions 'by study and also by faith' and teach them to accept God's timetable in finding their answers." [^1]

> "There is no better place than BYU for the teaching of evolution because our professors can, and do, bathe this subject 'in the light and color of the restored gospel.'" [^2]

## LDS Connection

Porter models faithful scholarship from a position of admitted personal uncertainty: a
believing scientist need not have all the answers to defend honest inquiry. The
restored-gospel framing — study *and* faith together (D&C 88:118; 109:7), with the
promise that at Christ's return he "will tell us all things" about the Creation
(D&C 101:32–33) — dissolves the supposed forced choice. True science and the gospel are
not rivals to be ranked but partial views awaiting fuller light. Compare
[[Faith Is Blind vs Faith Is Evidence-Based]] and the recognition that origin questions
and operational science answer different questions ([[Operation Science vs Origin Science]]).
