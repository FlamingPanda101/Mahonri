# Rights Talk Traces to Medieval Canon Lawyers

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]] argues the *concept* of subjective rights was developed by 11th-12th century canon lawyers, who turned the powerful person's *obligation* to the poor into the poor person's *right* to sustenance: "the poor person has a right to sustenance. They are entitled."[^1] This discourse matured through the high medieval period into [[John Locke|Locke's]] natural-law language of "the rights of man."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **From obligation to entitlement.** The historical innovation: reframing charity (a duty of the strong) as a claim (a right of the weak). "That starts to be a discourse developed through the high medieval period to the point where somebody like John Locke is speaking in absolute terms about the rights of man... getting all this language from medieval theologians, 100%."[^1] *(Strong — this tracks Brian Tierney's influential scholarship on the medieval origins of natural-rights theory.)*

2. **Rights as shield, not sword.** Scrivener distinguishes the original conception — rights as protection of the weak (their inviolable worth cannot be traded for the communal good) — from the modern "rights as a sword." The shield conception is the Christian one.[^2] *(Plausible and illuminating; a real shift in rights discourse.)*

3. **Steelman of O'Connor — selective emphasis.** O'Connor counters that Scrivener "places too much stock" in the Gregorian Reforms as an origin of rights talk: those reforms were mostly about clerical celibacy and reasserting *papal* power against secular investiture — "quite against the democratic principles" of the West.[^3] And the same "protect the weak from the strong" idea appears in [[Hammurabi]]'s prologue, predating Christianity entirely. See [[Western Values Predate or Arise Independently of Christianity]] and [[Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming]]. *(Fair — the canon-lawyer thesis is contested, and "rights" as protection-of-the-weak has pre-Christian analogues; but Scrivener's specific claim is about the *subjective-right* concept, which is more defensible than the bare value.)*

4. **The deeper question: why call these "Christian intuitions"?** O'Connor asks directly why "the strong not oppressing the weak" and "inviolable dignity" are Christian rather than common. Scrivener: "Yeah, that's a Christian thing."[^4] *(This is where the disagreement bottoms out — Scrivener treats them as Christian by genealogy; O'Connor treats them as common human intuitions with multiple sources.)*

## Best Quotes

> "In the 11th and 12th centuries you start to get the idea that the poor person has a right to sustenance. They are entitled."[^1]

> "They are getting all this language from medieval theologians. 100%."[^1]
