# The Heart Cannot Embrace What the Mind Cannot Believe

## The Claim
Pivoting from epistemology to *why he personally remains* a Latter-day Saint, [[Hayden Carroll]] states a maxim that orders the two: **"the heart cannot embrace what the mind cannot believe."** Spiritual conviction does not bypass the intellect; it presupposes that the mind has first found the claim credible. So before he rests anything on the witness of the Spirit, he says, "I've put at least a few hours of thought into these things" — and only then offers the [[The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument|Book of Mormon as the keystone]] of his religion.[^1] The maxim functions as the hinge between his negative case (the [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon|three problems with Sola Scriptura]]) and his positive evidence, and as a direct answer to the charge that Latter-day Saint belief is mere [[Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of Revelation|"feelings alone"]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
1. **It guards against fideism.** The principle denies that a "burning in the bosom" can certify a proposition the mind has independent reason to reject. Spiritual confirmation, on this view, is the lens that focuses evidence the intellect has already gathered — not a substitute for it. This is the same fallibilist posture as Carroll's five-medium [[Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of Revelation|triangulation]], where reason "can detect self-defeating ideas" and the Spirit binds the witnesses together rather than overriding them.[^1]
2. **It explains the order of his talk.** Carroll spends the bulk of the talk on argument — grace, the incoherence he sees in Sola Scriptura, and the case that God (not a book) is the ultimate authority — and reaches the Spirit-borne keystone testimony only at the end. The maxim is the stated reason for that sequence: the mind is engaged first so the heart has something it *can* embrace.[^1]
3. **It coheres with "embrace all truth."** Because the mind must be satisfied, the principle licenses bringing every faculty and source to bear — senses, reason, conscience, observed fruits, and authority — rather than walling off belief from scrutiny. It pairs naturally with [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]: a God who still speaks invites investigation rather than fearing it.[^1]

**Steelman of the critic.** A skeptic can grant the slogan and still resist the conclusion. (a) The maxim is necessary, not sufficient: that a mind *can* believe a claim (finds it not-impossible) does not make the claim true, and the move from "the dictation feat is hard to explain naturally" to "therefore revelation" is exactly the inference critics contest. (b) Order-of-operations cuts both ways — an opponent could say the heart's prior commitment is what makes the mind willing to "believe," i.e., motivated reasoning dressed as due diligence. (c) Plenty of mutually exclusive faiths could recite the same maxim, so it does not by itself adjudicate between them. Carroll's reply would be that this only returns the question to the evidence and to whether the converging witnesses (reason included) actually favor his conclusion — which is the work the rest of the talk is trying to do.

## Best Quotes
> "I first want to say that I believe that the heart cannot embrace what the mind cannot believe. I hope it's obvious that I've put at least a few hours of thought into these things." [^1]
