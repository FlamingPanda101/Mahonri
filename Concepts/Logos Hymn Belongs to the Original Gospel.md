# The Logos Hymn (Prologue) Belongs to the Original Gospel

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The prologue / Logos hymn ([[John 1.1|John 1:1]]–18) is **secondary** — a pre-existing Hellenistic-Jewish or Christian hymn (the kind of *logos* theology found in [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]]) tacked on later, because it is more poetic than the rest of John and the word *logos* for Jesus barely recurs after chapter 1.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The stylistic difference is real but not decisive
The prologue is more poetic and "elevated" in style, and *logos* as a title for Jesus appears essentially only there — the observations that prompted the "added hymn" theory. [[Hugo Mendez]] grants the data honestly before answering it. *(This is the steelmanned objection: distinctive style + non-distributed vocabulary.)* [^1]

### 2. The prologue is a hermeneutical key to the whole gospel
The introduction is "really critical for how the gospel develops all sorts of themes and ideas" — often treated as a hermeneutical key. Méndez cites [[Adele Reinhartz]]: without the hymn, "it doesn't quite feel like the Gospel of John would be as complete as it is." *(Strong; structural/literary integration outweighs surface style.)* [^2]

### 3. The prologue's theology saturates the rest of John even without the word
Though Jesus is called *logos* (and "God") only at the start, "you would be hard-pressed to read all kinds of verses in John without the knowledge that Jesus is divine and... the word of God." And the *logoi* (words) Jesus speaks are a critical running theme. *(Strong; thematic continuity rebuts the "isolated vocabulary" point.)* [^3]

### 4. It supports, not undercuts, single authorship
Keeping the prologue with the book is of a piece with Méndez's broader case that John is the work of one author with a unified vision — see [[Single Authorship of John Against Redaction Layers]]. *(Connective; coherence with his larger thesis.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The hymn-source hypothesis has serious support: the prologue's rhythm and parallelism look hymnic, several lines may pre-date the gospel, and *logos*-Christology really is absent from the narrative body. A pre-Johannine hymn adapted by the evangelist remains a respectable reading; Méndez concedes "the fun of scholarship is the debate" and that this is where he stands, not a settled fact.
