# Deuteronomy Was Patterned After the Vassal Treaty of Esarhaddon

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the book of [[Deuteronomy]] was composed using the **Vassal Treaty of [[Esarhaddon]]** (the Assyrian *adê*, early 7th century BCE) as its main literary template. Critical scholars "for a long time now have been convinced" that Deuteronomy borrows the treaty's structure and even its language — the same sequence of preamble, divine witnesses, exclusive-loyalty stipulations, and blessing-and-curse sanctions. On this reading, in the analogy "Esarhaddon is God, and the Israelites are whatever vassal nation Esarhaddon has just whooped up on."[^1][^2] Because the treaty postdates the book's purported Mosaic setting by 500–700 years, the dependence undercuts a literal Mosaic authorship and fits the standard dating of Deuteronomy to [[Josiah]]'s late-7th-century reform.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"You shall love" the suzerain.** Section 24 of the treaty commands the vassal to "love" crown prince [[Ashurbanipal]] "like yourselves." This covenant-love idiom is exactly the Shema's "you shall love the LORD your God with all your heart, might, and strength" ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deut 6:4]]) and the "as yourself" of the second great commandment.[^3][^4]
2. **No other lord / no other gods.** "You shall not seek any other king or any other lord against him" (§17) and "do not place any other king or any other lord over you" (§25) parallel "you shall have no other gods before me" ([[Deuteronomy 5.7|Deut 5:7]], Exodus 20). McClellan glosses the suzerain's posture as "I am the captain now… you will not go looking for other captains."[^5]
3. **Heaven and earth as witnesses.** The treaty calls on "all the gods of heaven and earth" as witnesses; Deuteronomy calls "heaven and earth to witness against you… I have set before you life and death, blessings and curses" ([[Deuteronomy 30.19|Deut 30:19]]).[^6]
4. **The curses match — including cannibalism.** Deuteronomy 28's siege curse that you will "eat the fruit of your womb, the flesh of your own sons and daughters" ([[Deuteronomy 28.53|Deut 28:53]]) and the curse to "destroy your name and your seed" (Deut 29) correspond to the treaty's §47: "in your hunger eat the flesh of your sons… may your ghost have nobody to take care of the pouring of libations to him" — matching down to the loss of an afterlife.[^7][^8]
5. **It was not a one-off copy.** The *adê* was "drafted anew every time for every individual," but with highly consistent elements — so the parallel is to a known, repeated genre, not a single coincidental text.[^9]
6. **Mainstream, not fringe.** "Most critical scholars are in pretty widespread agreement that Deuteronomy is picking up a lot of inspiration from… the vassal treaty of Esarhaddon" — the same Documentary-Hypothesis milieu as the [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins]].[^10]

## LDS Relevance
This is the critical-scholarship sibling of the LDS Deuteronomistic-reform argument: both agree Deuteronomy is a 7th-century product of [[Josiah]]'s circle rather than a Mosaic original (see [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]], [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]]). LDS apologists welcome the late, redactional, reform-driven dating because it grounds the claim that a *pre-reform* First Temple theology was suppressed — the theology the Book of Mormon's Lehi is said to preserve (see [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]]). The friction point is the *content*: McClellan's thesis says even the covenant frame is borrowed Assyrian imperial machinery (see [[The Covenant Idea Is Borrowed From Assyrian Vassalage]]), which presses on any reading that treats the Sinai/temple covenant as a self-standing divine institution rather than a culturally-mediated form — a tension the existing [[Ancient Covenant Pattern and the Blood of the Covenant]] already concedes by rooting the covenant template in ANE (incl. Assyrian) treaty practice.
