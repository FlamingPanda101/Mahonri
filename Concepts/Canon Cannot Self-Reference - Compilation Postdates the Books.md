# Canon Cannot Self-Reference — Compilation Postdates the Books

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Protestants sometimes cite a verse inside the Bible to establish the Bible's canon or authority. [[Hayden Carroll]] argues this is logically impossible: **the compilation of books happened after the books were written, so no individual book can refer to the collection that did not yet exist.** "Show me a scripture in the New Testament that identifies the New Testament canon... it doesn't exist because the book can't refer to a compilation that does not yet exist."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The temporal-priority argument is sound — rate: strong
This is straightforwardly correct as a matter of history and logic. The 27-book NT and the OT canon are *post-compositional* groupings ratified over centuries (the 27-book NT list first appears in Athanasius' 367 CE Festal Letter; the OT canon's scope was still disputed into the rabbinic period). A first-century author could not have referenced a list finalized centuries later. Therefore any verse cited *as* a self-designation of the canon is necessarily being read anachronistically. See [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]]. **Steelman of the Protestant:** the self-attestation claim is usually subtler — not that a verse *lists* the canon, but that each inspired book bears intrinsic marks of divinity that the believing community *recognizes* (Michael Kruger's "self-authentication"). On this view the canon is discovered, not legislated, so the absence of a self-referential verse is expected and harmless. Carroll does not engage this recognition model directly; his argument defeats the cruder "the Bible names its own canon" claim but not the sophisticated one.

### 2. Applies to "do not add to the book" prooftexts — rate: strong
Carroll anticipates the standard prooftexts — [[Revelation 22.18-19|Revelation 22:18-19]] ("add nothing"), [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]] (the faith "once delivered"), Deuteronomy 4:2 — and notes each refers to *its own immediate document or message*, not to the later 66-book compilation; books were still being written after Jude.[^2] This is the consensus scholarly reading (Revelation's warning is a standard ancient colophon protecting *that scroll*). See existing [[Revelation 22 - Add or Take Away Rebutted]].

### 3. The pastoral application: don't fear unfamiliar prooftexts — rate: moderate
Carroll's takeaway is psychological: when a Protestant quotes a verse you've never heard, "it does not mean what they want it to mean... Don't be scared."[^3] **Caution:** this is confidence-coaching that risks pre-deciding the outcome before hearing the argument — the opposite of the careful soundness analysis the course elsewhere teaches. A disciplined apologist should evaluate each prooftext on its merits rather than assume in advance that "it doesn't exist." The general principle (canon postdates books) is valid; the blanket assurance that *no* verse could ever support the opponent is overreach.

## Best Quotes
> "The compilation of books happened after the writing of the books. So the book can't refer to a compilation that does not yet exist."[^1]

> "Show me a scripture in the New Testament that identifies the New Testament canon... it doesn't exist."[^4]
