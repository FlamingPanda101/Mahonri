# Listening to Practitioners' Own Voices Dissolves the Need to Defend

## The Claim
The most common way a Latter-day Saint engages plural marriage is from "30,000 feet" — as an abstract principle to be defended, attacked, or explained through statistics and a historian's interpretation. Historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] argues that this distance is exactly the problem. When you instead read the *first-person words* of the women and men who actually practiced polygamy — their own journals and their own defenses of their own choices — the felt need to defend the principle dissolves, because the practitioners already shouldered that role themselves. The argument is methodological: proximity to primary voices, not the accumulation of data, is what brings peace.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "abstract concept" is what gets demonized.** Nash: "it's easy for us to demonize or view an abstract concept... and make up our own minds how they must have felt about what they were experiencing. But it's completely different once you read their own words, their own defenses of their choices."[^1] The error is filling a silence with our own projected emotions.
2. **The practitioners were not naïve about the controversy.** They were "very aware" of the controversy in their day and often wrote precisely *because* they wanted later readers to understand — so their defenses are not accidental but deliberate testimony.[^2]
3. **Personal documents carry less filtering.** Public sermons and First Presidency statements are valuable, but "it doesn't get more personal than" a private journal: "that's where you get the raw emotion... you connect with them on a human emotional level."[^3] These were "real people struggling with something or choosing something difficult in the name of religious faith."
4. **Layers of filtering separate us from them.** Even a careful historian inserts a filter, and "the questions historians ask today and the questions historians ask in the future will change." What does *not* change are the voices of the practitioners themselves — which is why Nash made giving them voice the organizing principle of her book over data or interpretation.[^4]
5. **The empirical result.** Nash repeatedly heard from readers: "I've struggled with polygamy for so long, but somehow by the end of your book, I was okay." She attributes this specifically to "hearing from the people themselves, not having it be filtered through someone else."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "It's easy for us to demonize or view an abstract concept and make up our own minds how they must have felt... But it's completely different once you read their own words, their own defenses of their choices. It brought me peace. I no longer felt like I needed to defend a principle I didn't understand. They had already taken on that role."[^1]

> "What won't change are the voices of Latter-day Saints, and... they needed to be heard and given a voice."[^4]
