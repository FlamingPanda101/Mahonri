# The Conflicting Commandments Are an Artifact of the Temples Double-Creation Reconciliation

#type/claim

## The Claim

The familiar puzzle — that God gave Adam and Eve two *contradictory* commandments (multiply and replenish the earth; do not eat the fruit), seemingly "setting them up to fail" because they cannot reproduce without first transgressing — is not actually a feature of Genesis. [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] argues the conflict only appears when the two creation accounts are *conflated*: "multiply and replenish" belongs to Genesis 1 (where humanity at large, not a couple, is created and there is no forbidden tree); Genesis 2 has the garden and the tree but no command to multiply. The juxtaposition comes from the *temple*, where Joseph Smith — solving the "double creation" problem by pouring the contents of Genesis 2–3 into the *structure* of Genesis 1 — placed the two commandments side by side. Solving the double-creation problem thereby *created* the conflicting-commandments problem.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **The two commandments live in different accounts.** "The commandment to multiply and replenish is in Genesis 1... where everything gets created and everything is told to multiply and replenish. There's no hint of this in Genesis 2." Therefore "the conflicting commandments idea comes from conflating the two accounts together and having two commandments given in the garden."[^2]
- **Genesis 1 has no couple and no tree.** "Genesis 1, it's not couples that are created. There's no trees to not eat from. They're just told to multiply and replenish. There's no conflict there at all. It's Genesis 2 where we get those things."[^3]
- **The juxtaposition is Joseph Smith's temple reconciliation.** "In the temple, he solves the double creation issue by taking the contents of Genesis 2 through 3 and putting them into the structure of Genesis 1. And so in the temple, we get those two commandments juxtaposed against each other. But that's not actually how we have it in Genesis, Moses or Abraham."[^4]
- **One solution generates the next problem.** "In solving one problem to create this ritual narrative with ancient history as a structure for covenant-making in the temple, he solves the double creation problem but creates the conflicting commandments problem."[^5]
- **It also dissolves the "God can't command the impossible" worry.** The objection assumes they were commanded *in the garden* to multiply; but "they're not commanded in the garden to multiply and replenish" (that is Genesis 1, "except in the temple"). Combined with the point that Adam and Eve were [[The Fall Was a Change of Degree or Scope Not an Absolute Reversal|physically capable of reproducing]], the apparent violation of "the Lord giveth no commandments... save he shall prepare a way" (1 Nephi 3:7) evaporates.[^6]
- **Don't read the temple as the pristine original.** The deeper lesson is methodological: "a lot of people want to see Moses and Abraham as and the temple as correcting Genesis except when read closely that doesn't work very well." The temple is "adapting it for inspired purpose with other revelation on top of it," not "restoring the ultimate original of Genesis."[^7]

## LDS Connection

This is a precise, faith-promoting dissolution of a stock objection that even believing members raise. It depends on the same source-critical fact catalogued in [[Two Independent Creation Accounts in Genesis]] and [[Genesis Weaves Two Creation Accounts From Edited Sources]] — that Genesis 1 and Genesis 2 are distinct accounts — and it extends the principle that [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy|the temple adapts and accommodates rather than transcribes the original]]. Far from undermining the temple, it locates the "contradiction" in our flattening of distinct texts, and frees the endowment to be what Spackman calls "a hybrid anthology multifunction thing" structured "for covenant-making" rather than a documentary of Eden.
