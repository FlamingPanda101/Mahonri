# The Rock of Matthew 16 Is Peters Confession Not a Papal Office

## The Claim

[[Gavin Ortlund]] confronts the Catholic flagship proof-text, [[Matthew 16.18|Matthew 16:18]] ("thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church... the keys of the kingdom"), and argues it does not establish an ongoing Petrine *office*. Three observations carry the case:

1. **Binding and loosing is not unique to Peter.** The authority granted to Peter here is "reiterated to all the disciples two chapters later" (Matt 18:18), consistent with the NT pattern that authority to govern the church is given "to all the apostles conjointly." [^1]
2. **Peter answers representatively.** Jesus asks "who do *you* (plural in the Greek) say that I am?"; Peter replies on behalf of the Twelve ([[Matthew 16.16|Matt 16:16]]). So it is "legitimate to at least ask" whether what is given to Peter is ultimately to be shared. [[Augustine]] held exactly this: "What was given to Peter was given to the whole church... Peter... represented the person of the church." [^2]
3. **The "rock" is polyvalent — and best read as Peter-in-his-confession.** Patristic exegesis offers three referents for the rock: Christ, Peter, or Peter's confession; "these options are not necessarily mutually exclusive," and many fathers combine them. Augustine's mature view (from his *Retractions*) fuses them: the rock is Christ, so Peter — *in confessing* Christ — is the rock. "Thou art Peter, and not thou art the rock, was said to him. But the rock was Christ." Peter is the rock "not as the first holder of an ongoing ecclesiastical office, but as representing the apostolic confession of Christ." [^3]

Ortlund extends the point to the other Petrine texts: Jesus restoring Peter in Luke 22 ("strengthen your brothers") and John 21 ("feed my sheep") restores him to *ordinary apostolic function* after his denials; reading Vatican I supremacy into "feed my sheep" is "a post hoc maneuver." He parodies the inference with "Pauline supremacy" wrung from 2 Corinthians 11:28 ("care for all the churches") — anxiety for the churches "is not the same thing as a jurisdictional authority." [^4]

He buttresses this with the claim that the fathers did *not* read these texts papally: per the Orthodox scholar David Bradshaw, patristic exegesis of John 21 finds no "office or responsibility different from that of the other apostles," and (citing the scholar he names "Sychynski") patristic exegesis of Matthew 16 "had nothing to do with the existence of an ongoing Petrine ministry." [^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Catholic)

The Catholic reading is genuinely formidable and must be stated at full strength:

1. **The verse is addressed to Peter alone, by name, with a new name.** Jesus singles out Simon, renames him *Petros* ("rock"), and immediately builds the church "upon this rock" — the most natural antecedent of "this rock" is the man just renamed. The Aramaic substrate (*Kepha*... *Kepha*) erases the Greek *Petros/petra* gender distinction Protestants lean on, so "you are Rock, and on this rock" reads as one identification.
2. **The keys are singular to Peter.** While binding/loosing recurs in Matt 18, the *keys of the kingdom* (Isaiah 22:22 royal-steward imagery) are given to Peter alone in Matt 16 — a distinct, personal, dynastic-steward grant that the chapter-18 parallel does not repeat.
3. **Peter's narrative primacy.** He is always named first in apostolic lists, speaks for the group, is the rock of Luke 22:32 ("strengthen your brothers"), and is uniquely commissioned to "feed my sheep" (John 21) — a concentration of singular commissions hard to explain on a purely egalitarian reading.
4. **"Representative" need not exclude "first office-holder."** That Peter acts for the church (Augustine) and that he heads its ongoing government are not contradictory; a steward represents the household *and* holds an office. Development theory then accounts for the explicit articulation arriving later (see [[Catholic Doctrinal Development Is the Same Double Standard]] for the contested status of that move).

The standing tension is interpretive weight, not the text's existence: Ortlund concedes "lots of different views" exist among the fathers and does not claim the confession-reading is the *only* patristic option — only that it is well-attested, non-eccentric, and fits the wider NT where Christ is repeatedly "the rock on which the church is built." The Catholic rejoinder is that none of those parallels *cancels* a personal grant to Peter in this verse.

## LDS Relevance

Latter-day Saints share the conclusion — Matthew 16 does *not* found a perpetual Roman papacy — but reach it on opposite premises. Where Ortlund reads the rock as Peter's *confession* (so no transferable office was ever instituted), the LDS tradition (e.g. [[Bruce R. McConkie]], James E. Talmage) typically reads the rock as **revelation** — the principle by which Peter knew Christ ("flesh and blood hath not revealed it unto thee") — and affirms that real keys *were* conferred on Peter and the Twelve, only to be *lost* in the Great Apostasy and later *restored* through Joseph Smith. So the three positions diverge sharply: Catholics keep the office and the succession; Ortlund denies the office was ever instituted; the LDS affirm the office/keys existed but departed and were restored (see [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]] and [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]]). Ortlund's representative-confession exegesis, if accepted, cuts equally against the LDS "keys were conferred and lost" reading as against Rome — the keys in Matt 16 are, on his view, the church's, not a single office-holder's.
