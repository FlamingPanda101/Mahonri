# Putting Dogma Over Data

## The Claim
"Dogma over data" is [[Dan McClellan]]'s signature diagnosis of the apologetic method (and the name-root of his *Data Over Dogma* project). Applied to the Pharaoh case, the claim is that the apologist does not derive conclusions from the text but **imposes pre-held dogmas onto the text and subordinates the data to them.** The wax/clay analogy exists because the conclusion (God never overrides agency; the Bible is univocal) is fixed in advance, so contrary data must be explained away with invented frameworks. "It's putting dogma over data."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Presupposition drives the reading.** The analogy "was necessary when you come to this text presupposing the univocality of the Bible and presupposing that the Bible would never represent God robbing a human of their agency." The presupposition, not the verse, generates the interpretation.[^2]
2. **Fabrication to fit.** "You have to make stuff up. You have to fabricate and jin up scenarios and frameworks and principles that you can then overlay upon the narrative to make it all feel like it makes sense under the interpretive lenses that we have brought to the text."[^3]
3. **Truthiness over evidence.** The argument persuades because "it feels truthy to them because of the very similitude of the argument," not because it is grounded in the text. "The argument is not based on the text."[^4]
4. **Subordination of text to dogma.** "The text has to be subordinated to those interpretive lenses, those unifying frameworks and those dogmas."[^5]

**Steelman.** Every interpreter brings a framework; "no interpretation is presuppositionless" is itself a scholarly commonplace (hermeneutics, the role of theory in archaeology). A confessional reader could fairly retort that McClellan, too, operates within a framework — methodological naturalism and the historical-critical consensus — so the charge of "dogma over data" can be aimed back at any school. **But** McClellan's narrower, defensible point is comparative: the wax/clay framework is *ad hoc*, invented for this single problem, with no independent textual warrant, whereas source/historical criticism is a general method tested across many texts. The asymmetry is in whether the framework is imposed *to rescue a fixed conclusion* or applied *as a falsifiable method*. (Cross-reference [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship]] and [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]].)
