# Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Whichever historic model of the Trinity the apologist adopts — the **Latin** (Augustinian/Thomistic, built on divine simplicity) or the **Eastern** (Cappadocian, person-forward) — [[Blake Ostler]] argues both face "insuperable logical problems," while LDS social trinitarianism avoids them. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Latin Trinity + simplicity is incoherent (rating: strong; philosophical)
"Any view that adopts divine simplicity is not going to be able to make any sense of the Trinity where there are truly distinct beings... they can't do it." Under simplicity there can be no really-distinct properties, so [[Thomas Aquinas]] had to reduce the persons to *subsistent relations* (the Son = "filiation," the Father = "paternity"). But "a relationship requires two things to be in relation," and simplicity forbids two distinct things — so the move fails. See [[Personages vs Subsistent Relations - What Person Means]]. [^2]

> "I believe that the Latin trinity is simply incoherent. Any view that adopts divine simplicity is not going to be able to make any sense of the trinity where there are truly distinct divine beings." [^2]

### 2. The Eastern Trinity has only one uncreated being (rating: strong; philosophical)
The Eastern Church "uses the term person in a much more univocal way with what we mean by person." But "because there's only one uncreated being, only one uncreated person, we now have to ask: in what sense is Christ the same kind of divine being as the father?" Some Easterns go further — only the Father "has a *say*" (aseity), "is the only one who's fundamental" — which entails "you are the only god," and "the son can't be God." See [[The Son Bears Yah but Never El Elyon]]. [^3]

### 3. Swinburne's aseity point yields an ontological gap (rating: moderate; philosophical)
Per [[Richard Swinburne]], to have *aseity* is "to exist in and of himself without dependence on another." If only the Father has it and the Son is derivative, "now we have an ontological distinction, because a dependence relation is different than a self-sufficient" one — "they can't be the same being." And a radically omnipotent being cannot be "dependent on another being for your very existence." [^4]

### 4. LDS social trinitarianism is more coherent (rating: moderate; comparative)
For Latter-day Saints "each of the divine persons is uncreated" and "fully divine in the same sense," so the father-son relationship is "much more literal and logical-contradiction-free." There is "a form of social trinitarianism that Latter-day Saints can and ought accept." See [[Personages vs Subsistent Relations - What Person Means]]. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Latin defender argues simplicity does *not* collapse the persons because the relations are *subsistent* (the relating just *is* the person), so "two things to relate" misreads the doctrine; the Eastern defender argues monarchy-of-the-Father preserves one God without making the Son a creature, since the Son's divinity is *eternally derived* (begotten, not made) and thus equally uncreated-in-kind. Both deny aseity entails sole deity. Ostler's rejoinder: "subsistent relation" is unintelligible without distinct relata, and "derived but equally uncreated" is exactly the tension Arius exploited — the Son is either dependent (and so lesser) or independent (and so a second God). The LDS model bites the bullet by affirming multiple uncreated persons united by love. See [[The Trinity Cannot Count to Three]].
