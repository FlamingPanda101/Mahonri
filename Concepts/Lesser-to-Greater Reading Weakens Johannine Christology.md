# The "Lesser-to-Greater" Reading Weakens John's Christology

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Jesus' use of Psalm 82 ([[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]) is a **qal va-homer** ("lesser-to-greater") argument: if scripture could call mere human judges "gods" without blasphemy, then how much more appropriate for Jesus — sanctified and sent by the Father — to call himself the Son of God.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The apologist is invoking *qal va-homer* (framing)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** names the technique: the apologist is "appealing to the rabbinic principle of *qal va-homer*, which is Hebrew for light and heavy and is a reference to a specific halakhic and logical principle of whatever applies to a lesser premise also applies to a greater or more significant premise." [^1] On the apologist's version, Psalm 82's humans "who have no divinity at all within them" are the lesser case justifying Jesus' greater claim. [^2]

### 2. The argument actually *lessens* Jesus' divinity (strong, internal coherence)
McClellan judges this "not a very convincing argument for a lot of exegetes because the comparison would rather lessen Jesus's divinity and would undermine the author of the Gospel of John's own christology." [^3] The problem is the *kind* difference: it amounts to saying "those regular old wicked humans over there who had no divinity whatsoever but just because of a technicality could be referred to as gods — well, that's the same thing as me saying I'm the son of God." [^4] "A lot of scholars have said this doesn't really fit the way John tries to represent Jesus's divinity." [^5]

### 3. A better reading exists (cross-ref)
Because the *qal va-homer* reading is weak, McClellan prefers the earlier rabbinic **Sinai-divinization** reading of Psalm 82 (see [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]]), which *strengthens* John's logos christology rather than diluting it: the word of God is what divinizes, and Jesus is that word.

### 4. Steelman of the *qal va-homer* reading
Many mainstream commentators (e.g. in the a fortiori tradition of reading John 10:34-36) *do* take the passage as a genuine lesser-to-greater argument and see no problem: the point is forensic (Jesus is acquitted of *blasphemy*, not ranked among the judges). On that view the comparison concerns the *legitimacy of the title*, not Jesus' ontological status, so no diminishment follows. McClellan's reply is that John's overall high christology makes the bare forensic reading feel like a downgrade, which is why "it's not a very popular reading" among those sensitive to John's christological aims. [^6]
