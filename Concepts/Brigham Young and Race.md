# Brigham Young and Race

## Overview

This is a hub note for the apologetic treatment of **Brigham Young's record on race** — the priesthood-and-temple ban and his 1852 Utah legislation on slavery. The defense (Daniel Peterson, Ward Radio) holds that the record is *complex and easily distorted by presentism and cherry-picking*: the origins of the priesthood ban are genuinely unclear; Young's 1852 "An Act in Relation to Service" is read by apologists as a *transitional* measure toward freedom rather than a pro-slavery endorsement, paired with claims of relatively kind treatment of enslaved and Black members. The recurring comparison is to Abraham Lincoln, whose own recorded views on race were, by modern standards, objectionable — the point being that 19th-century figures cannot be measured solely against 21st-century norms.

This note sits under [[Brigham Young's Character]] and is the racial sub-case of that larger rehabilitation.

## The Steelman

The critic's strongest form: the priesthood ban was taught and defended as *doctrine* with explicit racial rationales for over a century, and an appeal to presentism cannot neutralize a teaching the church itself later disavowed (the 2013 "Race and the Priesthood" essay repudiates the old justifications). Reading the 1852 slavery act as "transitional" is also contestable — it legally recognized servitude in the territory. The apologetic reply concedes error in the *justifications* while resisting the inference that Young was therefore a hateful man or a false prophet.
