# Humanism Is Itself a Faith Position
## The Point
The secular humanist typically casts the contrast with religion as *reason vs. faith*: religion rests on faith, humanism on evidence. The borrowed-capital argument turns this on its head. The humanist's foundational commitment — that human beings have a special, universal dignity — cannot be derived from observation of nature; it is accepted by faith. So humanism is "no less a faith position" than Christianity, and a faith position with Christian parentage at that. (This is the same insight, framed historically, that PragerU frames philosophically in [[The Value of Human Life Is a Faith Claim Not a Rational One]].)

## Evidence
1. **Stated as a faith position to an atheist's face.** In dialogue with the atheist philosopher [[Daniel Dennett]] — who calls himself a humanist — Holland says the foundation of humanism, "the notion that humans have a distinct status that they are somehow special," "is a faith position, and it is no less a faith position than that of a Protestant or a Catholic, and it is recognizably bred of the marrow of Christian civilization."[^1]

2. **Dependent on a Christian-shaped society.** "You would not be a humanist were it not for the fact [you live] in a society shaped by Christian assumptions."[^2] The point is not that Dennett is secretly religious but that his moral starting point was supplied by a culture Christianity built.

3. **Reason cannot manufacture the premise.** Holland grants the humanist's special status of humans is "a quite strange one" precisely because nothing in Greek or Roman thought — or in bare biology — yields it (see [[Universal Human Dignity Comes from Genesis Not the Greeks]]). If the dignity premise is not a conclusion of reason, it functions as an article of faith. Without some such grounded premise, the consistent secular endpoint is value-skepticism; cf. [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]] and [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]].

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint epistemology already treats first principles as faith-grounded ("by faith all things are fulfilled," Ether 12:3) rather than pretending morality floats on neutral reason. The advantage claimed is not that the Saint *also* has a faith premise, but that the faith premise is anchored in a real, knowable God who is "the standard" of goodness — so human worth has a true ground, not borrowed sentiment. This reframes the debate from "faith vs. reason" to "*which* faith premise can actually bear the moral weight placed on it." See [[Secular Humanism Lives on Borrowed Christian Capital]].
