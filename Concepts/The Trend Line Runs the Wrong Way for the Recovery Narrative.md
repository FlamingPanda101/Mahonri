# The Trend Line Runs the Wrong Way for the Recovery Narrative

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] presses a *directional* argument that goes beyond the static claim that early Christians were subordinationist (see [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]]). The creedal defense is that Nicaea *recovered* or *made precise* the original apostolic teaching. But if the Nicene position were the original apostolic teaching, "then the sources closest to the apostolic era should reflect it most clearly. And that's the opposite of what we find."[^1] [[Justin Martyr]], writing within a generation of the apostles, describes a subordinate "second God"; [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] — a disciple of [[Polycarp]], who was a disciple of [[John the Evangelist|the Apostle John]] — describes the Son and Spirit as the Father's "two hands." So "the theology closest to the apostles is the most clearly subordinationist. The trend line goes the wrong way for any recovery narrative."[^2] A genuine recovery would show the earliest sources most Nicene and later drift away; instead the earliest sources are *least* Nicene and the resemblance *increases* toward the 4th century — the signature of construction, not retrieval.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The chain of transmission is unusually short and traceable (rated: strong on the data).** The Polycarp link gives a two-step human chain from John to Irenaeus, making Irenaeus's "two hands" Christology hard to dismiss as a late distortion of an originally Nicene faith. [[Origen]] — "arguably the most prolific and influential theologian of the 3rd century" whose framework shaped the patristic era — subordinated the Son in ways later classified as semi-Arian, and was posthumously condemned as a heretic by the 5th Ecumenical Council in 553. "The man whose thinking shaped early Christianity didn't actually pass the orthodoxy test that his intellectual heirs established."[^3] Arnold notes [[R.P.C. Hanson]] concluded the 4th-c. church "wasn't retrieving an established apostolic doctrine. It was searching for one," and [[Jaroslav Pelikan]] organized his five-volume history around *development*, not preservation.[^4]

2. **It absorbs the "proto-Nicene voices existed" objection (rated: strong).** Arnold grants there were early thinkers using language later Nicene theology found agreeable — but "the very existence of proto-Nicene voices doesn't establish that the Nicene position was the *original universal consensus*. It just establishes that the question was open." An open question resolved by imperial council "is exactly what I've been describing"; the diversity itself is the evidence, since a clear ancient consensus would have produced no Arian controversy to resolve.[^5] This complements [[The Diversity Test - What a Community Tolerates Reveals Its God]] but inverts its valence: here the *historical* spread, not a present community's tolerance, is the datum.

3. **Steelman: progressive precision (rated: fair — the disagreement is about direction).** The creedal reply is that "the church fathers were moving toward greater precision, and Nicaea represents the culmination of that process," so early imprecision is expected. Arnold's counter: "Greater precision would be a description, but not a justification" — it says nothing about whether the mechanism carried binding authority, and it does not rescue the *directional* problem, because a development-toward-precision and a recovery-of-the-original predict opposite gradients in the earliest sources. He cites [[Rowan Williams]] (*Arius: Heresy and Tradition*): Nicaea "represented a decision rather than any discovery."[^6] *Rating: the trajectory argument is strong against "recovery"; it concedes that "development" is a coherent alternative description — which is precisely the [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction|development-vs-contradiction]] fault line.*
