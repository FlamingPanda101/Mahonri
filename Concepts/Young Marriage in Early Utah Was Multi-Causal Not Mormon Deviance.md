# Young Marriage in Early Utah Was Multi-Causal Not Mormon Deviance

#type/claim

## The Claim
Harris notes that some early-Utah polygamous brides were very young — implying multiple wives "13 years old" for [[Brigham Young]] and that young marriage was a distinctively Mormon symptom. Keystone offers context: only **one** of Brigham's wives (Elizabeth Fairchild) was thirteen, and she "was never part of Brigham Young's household"; and the broader pattern of young marriage in 1850s Utah is the product of **converging non-Mormon factors** — presentism, the absent concept of "teenagerhood," frontier conditions, and the mathematics of polygamy — not a unique Mormon deviance. Keystone is explicit: "I'm not trying to persuade you to be okay with it. I'm just giving you some additional context to explain... why the data says what it says."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Brigham/Elizabeth Fairchild correction (specific fact).** Harris "kind of implies that there were multiple" 13-year-old wives; "technically, one of them was." Elizabeth Fairchild was never in Brigham's household, they had no children, and she divorced him in 1855 after about ten or eleven years.[^2] *Rating: strong — narrow, checkable biographical correction.*

2. **Presentism / shifting age of consent (context).** Applying today's standards to the past is presentism: in the mid-1800s the age of consent was roughly 10–12 (as low as 7 in Delaware), "shocking, but that's what it was." Keystone adds he knows of no LDS women marrying as young as 12-or-under.[^3] See [[Presentism in Historical Judgment]] and [[Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr]]. *Rating: moderate — the legal datum is real; presentism is a legitimate but limited principle.*

3. **"Teenagerhood" did not yet exist (conceptual shift).** The life-stage between childhood and adulthood is a modern invention; adulthood was keyed to *physical development and capacity for work*, not age — a 13-year-old who could "do the work of a mother and bear children" was considered marriageable. Our categories of childhood/adolescence "developed a lot over the last 150 years."[^4] *Rating: moderate — well-supported social history; does not by itself settle the ethics.*

4. **Frontier geography drove the age down (independent of religion).** Frontier women, Latter-day Saint or not, "were more likely to get married at younger ages to older men — that's just the circumstances of the frontier."[^5] *Rating: moderate — a real demographic pattern not unique to Mormons.*

5. **Polygamy mathematically lowers marriage age (mechanism).** In a community where men take additional wives, women in each age cohort are "spoken for," pushing the search to "the younger generation where women are just becoming marriageable." So young marriage is partly a *structural consequence* of polygamy plus frontier life plus the 1800s.[^6] *Rating: moderate — internally coherent demographic argument.*

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman of Harris)
The context does not dissolve the moral problem, and Keystone concedes as much. Even if "teenager" is anachronistic and frontier statistics are real, the practice paired underdeveloped adolescents with much older men under intense religious authority — a dynamic the vault treats critically in [[Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr]] (Reel's rebuttal: "wrong... no matter what century"). A critic can also argue that explaining *why* the data looks as it does shades into normalizing it, and that "only one was thirteen" still concedes a thirteen-year-old sealing. The defensible kernel is factual and demographic; it is offered as context, not exoneration, and Keystone explicitly declines to argue the reader into approving of it.
