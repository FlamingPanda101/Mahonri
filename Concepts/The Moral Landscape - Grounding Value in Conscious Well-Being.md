# The Moral Landscape — Grounding Value in Conscious Well-Being

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that morality needs no religious or metaphysical grounding: bracket "morality, right and wrong, good and evil" and what remains undeniable is a **navigation problem** in the space of possible conscious experience, which runs from needless hellish suffering to flourishing. Value is the actual or potential change in that experience.[^1]

## Argument

1. **Start deeper than morality — start with consciousness.** Whatever consciousness ultimately is (neurochemistry, simulation, eternal souls), "something seems to be happening," and these experiences "can be really really bad or really really good." This is the bedrock all of us share.[^2]

2. **Ethics = navigation.** "We have a space of possible experience in which we can navigate," and morality/ethics are simply "the terms we use for how we think about our behavior affecting one another's experience." Alone on a desert island morality lapses, but well-being does not.[^3]

3. **Moral realism without certainty.** "It's possible not to know what you're missing" — to live less happily than one could and lack the wisdom to change. The landscape has higher and lower regions; there can be right and wrong *directions* even where multiple irreconcilable "peaks" of equivalent well-being exist (a limited moral relativism *within* realism).[^4]

4. **The "cash value" of any value claim** is "the actual or potential change in consciousness for some conscious system somewhere." This generalizes beyond humans to any system that can suffer or thrive — even a hypothetical conscious machine.[^1]

5. **Peterson's challenge.** Harris's axiom (the bad life is universally distinguishable from the good life) sits uneasily with his concession of irreconcilable equivalent peaks; and locating value in conscious states still needs an a priori frame to *select among facts*. See [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]]. *Steelman of Harris: the frame is the felt structure of experience itself — "put your hand on a hot stove" gives an unarguable reason without moral philosophy.*[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Forget about morality, forget about right and wrong and good and evil. What is undeniable is that what we have here is a navigation problem." [^1]

> "The lights are on... and being on they reveal a spectrum of experience." [^2]

> "It's possible to be living in a way where you are less happy than you could be and not to know why... if anything matters that matters." [^4]
