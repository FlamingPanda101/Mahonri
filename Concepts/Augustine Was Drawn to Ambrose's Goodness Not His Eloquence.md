# Augustine Was Drawn to Ambrose's Goodness Not His Eloquence

## The Point
[[Augustine]], a professional teacher of rhetoric, first attended [[Ambrose of Milan]]'s sermons (AD 384) merely to assess his oratorical skill, "bored and contemptuous of the subject-matter." What ultimately moved Augustine toward Christianity was not Ambrose's eloquence but his *person* — his fatherly kindness — illustrating that the two things at work in early Christian preaching were "the preacher's language and the preacher's goodness."

## Evidence
1. **Augustine the rhetoric critic.** "I used enthusiastically to listen to him preaching to the people, not with the intention which I ought to have had, but as if testing out his oratorical skill … I hung on his diction in rapt attention, but remained bored and contemptuous of the subject-matter. My pleasure was in the charm of his language." [^1]
2. **Won by the man, not the message (at first).** "That 'man of God' received me like a father … I began to like him, at first indeed not as a teacher of the truth, … but as a human being who was kind to me." [^2]
3. **Heal's diagnosis.** "This episode from Augustine's Confessions suggests that at least two things were at work in the early Christian sermon: the preacher's language and the preacher's goodness." The late-ancient world was "a great age of rhetoric," and learning to read, interpret, and persuade was "the equivalent of elite graduate school training in our day" — but "rhetoric can be used for good or ill, which is why the Christian preacher sought for holiness as well as eloquence." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "I hung on his diction in rapt attention, but remained bored and contemptuous of the subject-matter. My pleasure was in the charm of his language." (Augustine, *Confessions*) [^1]

> "I began to like him, at first indeed not as a teacher of the truth … but as a human being who was kind to me." (Augustine, *Confessions*) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Heal uses Augustine's experience to elevate the *character* of the minister above rhetorical polish — that more seekers are won "through the goodness and kindness of the preacher than by his profound eloquence alone." This aligns with the Latter-day Saint emphasis on Christlike ministering and the Spirit over polished oratory (cf. D&C 50:13–22).
