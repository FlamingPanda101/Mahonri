# No Free Will Does Not Entail No Purpose - Carroll's Reply

#type/claim

## The Claim

The standard LDS-apologetic chain runs *atheism → no free will → no purpose →
nihilism*. The interesting move in this conversation is that the two atheist
students, **Sid and Raven, break the chain at exactly one link**: they **grant
determinism** — conceding they have no [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality|libertarian free will]]
and accepting the "we are dominoes" picture — yet deny that purposelessness
follows. Their counter-claim: *purpose is function and impact on the world*, and
a causally determined thing still has a function. A falling domino has a
necessary role; "if you as the domino didn't fall, then the next one wouldn't
fall either."[^1] So you can lack free will and still have purpose; therefore the
no-free-will premise does **not** make atheism "unreasonable" on the purpose
front.[^2]

[[Hayden Carroll]]'s reply is to attack the redefinition. He argues **purpose
necessitates agency** — to *have* a purpose is to *choose* an aim, "to use your
agency... to do something."[^3] A domino is not pursuing a purpose; it is merely
being caused, and "there's not an actual objective purpose" in randomness, only
"direction."[^4] He adds an observer condition: "If there's no conscious being to
observe the purpose, is there actually a purpose to it?"[^5] — which loops back to
the [[Consciousness and First Cause Problem for Atheism|consciousness problem]],
since the consistent atheist also denies a real conscious self. Carroll's
positive contrast is goal-directed and teleological: "I'm trying to become like
God... that's a goal,"[^6] a purpose grounded in a chosen end, not a caused
trajectory.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

The students' position is the strongest part of this exchange and deserves a
clean statement — this is the determinist steelman the rest of the cluster
mostly skips.

1. **Function is purpose; free will is not required for function (Raven).**
   "I don't think you have to know your purpose to have a purpose... I define
   purpose as impact and... function in the world."[^7] On this view "purpose" is
   a third-person, causal-role notion: a heart's purpose is to pump blood
   whether or not the heart "chose" it. Carroll actually *concedes* this on
   Raven's own definition — "if that's how you're defining purpose, you don't
   need free will to have a function in the world."[^8] The dispute is therefore
   **semantic at its root**: it turns on whether "purpose" means *chosen aim* or
   *causal function*.

2. **Determinism is granted, not resisted (Sid).** Sid repeatedly accepts the
   hard-determinist frame — "as far as we can know we have a free will, but our
   free will can be explained by external factors,"[^9] and agrees the domino
   "can't not fall."[^10] This is an honest, [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism|Harris-style]]
   compatibility move: behavior is real and caused, and calling the caused
   behavior "choice" is just the language we have. The steelman: Carroll's
   nihilism conclusion smuggles in *libertarian* assumptions about what "purpose"
   must be; a naturalist who never wanted libertarian purpose loses nothing.

3. **Optimistic, self-authored meaning without illusions (Raven).** Raven
   self-describes as "an optimistic nihilist": no inherent purpose, but "it's up
   to you to choose," and a goal "doesn't define your purpose."[^11] The
   steelman here is that meaning can be a stance one takes toward a life, not a
   metaphysical property the universe must supply — so the absence of cosmic
   purpose is not the absence of a livable, motivating purpose.

4. **Carroll's strongest pushback — the observer condition.** Against (1)–(3)
   Carroll presses that a purpose with no conscious subject to whom it matters,
   and no agent who selected it, is not *purpose* but *direction*: "purpose
   cannot exist in a random world."[^12] He offers a deliberate steelman of the
   students before answering — "maybe I'll try to steelman you... each domino is
   fulfilling a purpose. But the issue is it's randomness causing the dominoes to
   fall, so there's not an actual objective purpose."[^4] The unresolved crux:
   Sid/Raven want a *deflationary, functional* purpose; Carroll insists only a
   *chosen, observed, teleological* purpose is the real thing — and that
   atheism, lacking agency and a real observer, cannot supply it. The
   conversation ends in mutual respect without either side conceding the
   definition.

## Best Quotes

> "I don't think the argument that no free will implies that there's no purpose
> [works]... and that makes atheism unreasonable." — Raven[^2]

> "Purpose is a functionality. Your impact on the world, the things you
> change." — Sid[^13]

> "I would say purpose necessitates free will, because you have to be making
> choices... to use your agency, as we would say, to do something. But if you're
> just a domino..." — [[Hayden Carroll]][^3]

> "If there's no conscious being to observe the purpose, is there actually a
> purpose to it?" — [[Hayden Carroll]][^5]
