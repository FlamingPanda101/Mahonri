# Joe Heschmeyers Quran Parallel Proves Too Much Against the Resurrection

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Joe Heschmeyer]]'s strategy against the "argument from translation" is a **parity argument**: Muslims make a structurally identical "no one could have produced this book" argument for the Quran, yet the Quran and the Book of Mormon reach contradictory conclusions; an argument form that, if valid, would prove two incompatible religions true must therefore be a *bad form*.[^1] [[Jacob Hansen]] (with [[Luke Hansen]]) replies that this **proves too much**: the very same *reductio / ad absurdum* form — "imagine commissioning unschooled men to produce this impossibly complex work" — is a staple of mainstream Christian apologetics for the Gospels and the Resurrection. If Heschmeyer's meta-rule disqualifies the Book of Mormon argument merely because a parallel argument exists for a rival faith, it equally disqualifies the Christian's own argument for the Resurrection. The correct move is not to retire the form but to do what Heschmeyer skipped: **compare the weight of evidence**, not the shape of the rhetoric.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The form is already in Christian use.** Hansen quotes apologist Glenn Scriber's *The Air We Breathe*, which stages exactly this argument: "Okay, Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John... we need you to write the most influential work in literature... sinless but fully alive... miracles... that could therefore be contradicted by the generation to which you're writing... believable both near and far, now and later. Got it? Now get to work."[^2] *(Strong — this is a verbatim, widely-circulated example of the Christian ad-absurdum for the Gospels; Hansen's parallel is exact.)*
2. **A celebrity endorsement of the same form.** Scriber appeals to [[Jordan Peterson]], who "finds it so difficult to believe that human beings invented this unbelievably preposterous story" — "in his words, an impossible task."[^2] If the form's conclusion ("only God could produce this") is respectable for the Gospels, it cannot be ruled out of court for the Book of Mormon by fiat. *(Moderate — Peterson is cited-commentary tier, not a historian; the point is rhetorical parity, not Peterson's authority.)*
3. **The parity actually cuts the other way once evidence is weighed.** Heschmeyer's own argument, applied consistently, would "negate similar arguments for the gospel and resurrection." So either both ad-absurdum arguments stand or fall on *evidence*, in which case the Book of Mormon's dictation (≈270,000 words, single narrative draft, ~65 days, eyewitnesses, surviving manuscripts) outweighs the Quran's (non-narrative, piecemeal over ~23 years, murky transmission) — see [[The Quran Parallels the Doctrine and Covenants Not the Book of Mormon]] and [[Muhammad and Joseph Smith Are Apples to Oranges]].[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Heschmeyer's underlying logic is genuinely forceful and not a mere debating trick. (a) When an argument *schema* delivers contradictory outputs depending only on which book you plug in, that *is* a red flag that the schema is tracking something other than truth — the parity objection is a recognized tool against over-strong design/improbability arguments. (b) Hansen's reply does not rescue the *form*; it concedes the form alone "doesn't prove anything" and relocates the entire burden onto a separate evidential comparison — which means Heschmeyer was right that the bare "no one could write this" intuition is not probative. (c) A critic can press that the Gospels/Resurrection parallel is double-edged: if that argument is *also* weak (many would say it is), then showing the Book of Mormon argument shares its form is not a vindication. Hansen's honest concession is exactly this — the translation feat "doesn't prove anything" on its own; it is one strand among the witnesses, Hebraisms, and prophecy that together lower the plausibility of a naturalistic account. The live disagreement is therefore not "is the form valid?" but "whose underlying evidence is heavier?" — the question Hansen says Heschmeyer never engaged.

## Best Quotes
> "The problem is that Joe's argument here proves too much. If two arguments can be disproven because they sound similar to each other, yet they reach contradictory conclusions, then that would negate similar arguments for the gospel and resurrection."[^1]

> "Joe compared the type of arguments the Muslims and Latter-day Saints use. What he should have been doing is comparing the weight of evidence on each side. The devil — or perhaps God — is in the details."[^2]

> "This is why Jordan Peterson finds it so difficult to believe that human beings invented this unbelievably preposterous story. It is, in his words, an impossible task." — quoting Glenn Scriber, *The Air We Breathe*[^2]
