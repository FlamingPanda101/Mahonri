# Divine Investiture of Authority Is an Anachronistic LDS Harmonization

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection (defended in the clip by [[Jacob Hansen]]) is the LDS doctrine of **divine investiture of authority**: "a sovereign can endow a representative with the authority to speak on behalf of the sovereign and maybe even *as* the sovereign." [^1] The analogy offered is the ancient king who "would send someone out who would bear his name and speak as though they were the king" — and, Hansen claims, such an agent *could* claim to be the king. [^2] [[Dan McClellan]] grants the framework's existence in LDS theology but argues it cannot do the historical work required.

## The Rebuttal

1. **It originates as an internal LDS harmonization, not as ancient data.** McClellan traces the doctrine to attempts "to harmonize the non-Trinitarianism of Mormonism with the apparent Trinitarianism of the Book of Mormon," where Jesus speaks as the Father. He names [[James Talmage]] as primarily responsible for its articulation (e.g., the 1916 "The Father and the Son"). *Rating: a well-grounded genealogy of the doctrine; its motivation is confessional harmonization.* [^3]

2. **It "leans on authority rather than logic and data."** McClellan says the framework "doesn't really work incredibly well... is making a lot of assumptions" and rests on ecclesiastical authority. *Rating: a methodological critique; persuasive against using the doctrine as a neutral historical category, less so as an internal theological claim.* [^4] *Steelman of the LDS side:* divine investiture is not offered as ancient Near Eastern history but as a revealed theological model; on its own terms it coherently distinguishes the persons while explaining shared divine speech.

3. **No ancient West Asian source licenses an agent claiming to *be* the king.** This is the decisive empirical point McClellan concedes to "God Logic": "nowhere in any of the other broader ancient West Asian literature is a messenger allowed to say I am the king in light of the divine investiture of authority." On this specific exchange, "Jacob loses the battle." *Rating: strong — this is McClellan endorsing the opposing debater's narrow factual win.* [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The divine investiture of authority... originates in attempts to try to harmonize the non-Trinitarianism of Mormonism with the apparent Trinitarianism of the Book of Mormon." [^3]

> "It is leaning on a lot of authority rather than logic and data. And that's why it's not really going to go well for Jacob." [^4]

> "Nowhere in any of the other broader ancient West Asian literature is a messenger allowed to say I am the king in light of the divine investiture of authority. So Jacob loses the battle in that regard." [^5]
