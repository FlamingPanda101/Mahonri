# Canonization Is a Community Process Not a Unilateral Prophetic Act

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Stephen Smoot|Steven Smoot]] and the Raplees argue that adding scripture to the LDS canon is a *communal* act, not a unilateral declaration by the prophet. The prophet "cannot just get up there and just unilaterally say this is now scripture"; canonical material is compiled by many hands over time and then ratified by a **sustaining vote** of the assembled Church in general conference. This both explains why the Pearl of Great Price bears the fingerprints of so many editors and why even an apostle's proposal (McConkie's) could be rejected — the process has real checkpoints.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Canonization requires a sustaining vote, by precedent.** The 1880 canonization happened when George Q. Cannon presented Orson Pratt's edition to general conference: "All in favor, please manifest it... Do you accept it as scripture? ... And that's how you get new scripture." The 1976 additions were likewise read by N. Eldon Tanner as a "resolution for sustaining vote" — "It is proposed that we sustain and approve this action and adopt these revelations as part of the standard works" [^1][^2].

2. **It is a multi-generational chain of compilers, not one author.** Smoot: "These books don't just like poof out of the ether... It's people that put these books together" — "Joseph Smith produces it, [[Franklin D. Richards]] gathers it, the saints bring it to Utah, [[Orson Pratt]] standardizes it... then [[James Talmage]] and others realize there needs to be adjustments." The compilation itself is communal and incremental [^3].

3. **The prophet cannot act alone.** "It's not like the prophet cannot just get up there and just unilaterally say this is now scripture. Like we go through... when we sustain new canonical material. It's a community gets together." Neil adds the aspiration "to be able to participate in the process of canonization... it's brought before the church and sustained" [^4].

4. **The rejection of McConkie's proposal proves the process bites.** "We just talked about an instance where an apostle proposed scripture and it was ultimately rejected." It was still "a very prayerful and revelatory" matter — McConkie "took it very seriously, but ultimately had to consult with the Lord" — showing the process is genuinely deliberative, not rubber-stamping rank [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "It's not like the prophet cannot just get up there and just unilaterally say this is now scripture... It's a community gets together." [^4]

> "These books don't just like poof out of the ether out of thin air into our hands. It's people that put these books together." [^3]

> "We just talked about an instance where an apostle proposed scripture and it was ultimately rejected." [^5]

## LDS Connection
This grounds [[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug]]: change is acceptable precisely because it passes through priesthood-key authority *and* communal ratification. It is the concrete LDS instance of the broader Catholic-style claim in [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]], and it qualifies the worry in [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority]] by locating a check (the sustaining vote) on the prophet's unilateral power. The rejected [[McConkies Unpublished Pearl of Great Price]] is its proof case.
