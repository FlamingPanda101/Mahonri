# The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Protestants often treat the Bible as a single, internally harmonious book that "speaks with one voice" — a property apologists call **univocality**. [[Hayden Carroll]] argues this is false: the Bible is a **library** of many books by many anonymous authors across centuries, "not meant to be" read as one univocal voice; they "don't speak of each other."[^1] The univocality assumption is what lets Protestants "build" the Trinity by stitching verses from different books together as if a single divine author intended the synthesis.[^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "library" framing is mainstream — rate: strong
That the Bible is an anthology composed over roughly a millennium by diverse authors in diverse genres and theologies is the consensus of critical scholarship (the very name *ta biblia*, "the books," is plural). Treating it as univocal is a *theological* commitment, not a literary given. This dovetails with the vault's existing academic-biblical-studies notes. **Steelman of the Protestant:** univocality is not a claim about *human* authorial agreement but about a *single divine author* (the Holy Spirit) speaking through many hands — so apparent diversity is harmonized at the level of divine intent (the "analogy of scripture," Westminster 1.9: "the infallible rule... is the Scripture itself"). On this view, cross-book synthesis is legitimate because one mind stands behind all the books. Carroll's reply is that this divine-univocality is itself an *unjustified presupposition* — nobody has shown God intended the books to be read as one. See [[The Three Unjustified Protestant Canon Presuppositions]].

### 2. The Trinity-construction application — rate: moderate
Carroll's payoff: when asked "where does the Bible teach the Trinity?" Protestants assemble verses from many books and say "add these together — God wanted us to have it all together."[^3] He counters that this *only works if you presuppose univocality plus single divine authorship*, and that the resulting doctrine is a synthesis no single biblical author taught. See [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]] and the existing [[Trinity Was Constructed Not Revealed]], [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]]. **Caution:** the LDS canon faces the inverse situation — Carroll praises the Book of Mormon as *genuinely* univocal "because Mormon did it" (a single editor),[^4] which concedes that univocality is a real interpretive virtue when an editorial hand exists. This is a fair point but also shows the argument is about *whether* a unifying author exists, not whether univocality per se is illegitimate.

### 3. Library reading explains Old/New Testament "ping-pong" — rate: moderate
Carroll notes Protestants defending the Trinity "jump from Old Testament to New Testament and back," which only coheres if the corpus is univocal.[^5] An honest reader should note this cuts both ways: *Latter-day Saints also* read Christ into the Old Testament (e.g., Jehovah = pre-incarnate Christ), which itself assumes a degree of canonical unity. The library point is strongest as a caution against *unconscious* harmonization, not as a blanket ban on inter-textual reading.

## Best Quotes
> "The Bible is a compilation of a bunch of books. It's a library... they don't speak of each other."[^1]

> "Do you guys know what univocality is? ... it's the Bible speaking with one voice... The Bible doesn't get that luxury."[^6]

> "Most likely they're going to go to a bunch of different books and say if you add all these together, this is the Trinity. And it's like, where did God say that?"[^3]
