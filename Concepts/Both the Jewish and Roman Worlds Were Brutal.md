# Both the Jewish and Roman Worlds Were Brutal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (Scrivener): Christianity entered a uniquely brutal Greco-Roman world (infanticide, gladiators, pederasty) and civilized it — implying the brutality was external to the biblical tradition.

## The Rebuttal

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: the Jewish world Christianity *also* entered — the world of the Old Testament — was equally brutal. So the "Christianity civilizes brutality" story applies symmetrically and cannot single out Rome.

1. **The OT origin story is brutal.** "The Jewish exodus from Egypt and then the immediate enslavement, the immediate genocide of Canaanite tribes... is really really bad and brutal."[^1] *(Strong — the conquest narratives are violent by any modern standard.)*

2. **The symmetry argument.** If Christianity "flourishes" out of a brutal *Greek/Roman* past, one can equally say enlightenment values "flourished" from a brutal Greek tradition — so brutality-then-flourishing is not distinctively Christian. "The brutality of the Roman world Christianity entered is mirrored by the brutality of the Jewish world into which Christianity entered."[^2] *(Effective — neutralizes Scrivener's framing of Rome as uniquely dark.)*

3. **Steelman of Scrivener — the Jews were already counter-cultural.** Scrivener replies it was "the Jews who, within Roman society, were appalled by the brutality of Rome" — infanticide, and pederasty (which they called *phthoria*, "child destruction," not *paiderastia*, "child love").[^3] So the Jewish tradition was *already* the moral critic, not merely another brutal culture. *(Strong historical point — Second Temple Judaism did distinctively oppose exposure and pederasty.)*

4. **Scrivener's "lump in the throat" move.** The brutalities of Joshua "put a lump in both our throats" but "wouldn't have put a lump in Marcus Aurelius's throat... or Muhammad's... or Sun Tzu's." O'Connor counters: "They might put a lump in Aristotle's throat." Scrivener: only if the Canaanites weren't, for Aristotle, "natural slaves" — a just cause of war.[^4] *(See [[The Recoil from OT Violence Is Itself Christian]]. This pivots the debate from "who was brutal" to "whose moral recoil is doing the work.")*

## Best Quotes

> "The brutality of the Roman world that Christianity entered into is mirrored by the brutality of the Jewish world into which Christianity entered, on the Christian story."[^2]

> "It was the Jews who, within Roman society, were appalled by the brutality of Rome — by infanticide, by pederasty, which the Jews called phthoria... child destruction."[^3]
