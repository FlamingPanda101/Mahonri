# Inerrantist Certainty Is a Feeling Not a Finding

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that when someone insists "the Bible has no contradictions," the load-bearing thing being defended is **not a conclusion about the text but an emotional state** — the *feeling* of certainty. "Their certainty about the Bible not having any contradictions is not based on logic or reason. It's based on feelings. And really certainty itself is a feeling. Conviction is a feeling."[^1] The descriptive question (does the text contradict itself?) is, on his telling, settled — "the Bible is full of contradictions; that's not up for serious debate"[^2] — so the persistence of the inerrancy claim is best explained psychologically: it is a commitment one *has* and *wants to keep*, dressed up post hoc as "ironclad reasoning and logic and evidence."[^3]

This is a claim about the **epistemic status and motivation** of inerrancy, distinct from (though it cooperates with) McClellan's claims about harmonization *method*. Where [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]] targets the *direction of reasoning* (commitment precedes evidence) and [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]] targets the *standard* (unfalsifiable "not impossible"), this note targets the *affective core*: certainty is a feeling, and the inerrantist mistakes that feeling for a finding.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The predictive experiment.** McClellan publicly forecast the behavior of inerrancy defenders before engaging them: they would "respond with some weak apologetics" read online or in a book, fail to answer "an expert criticism of the argument," and then "either appeal to authority or argue by assertion or run like a squirrel." He reports that "every last one of them has done precisely what I said."[^4] A claim that lets you predict your interlocutor's moves in advance is doing real diagnostic work: the responses track an emotional commitment (defend the dogma) rather than a live engagement with evidence.
2. **The behavioral tell.** When it is exposed that defenders "don't really know the arguments well enough to be able to assess their strength or their weakness, they freak out and they just don't respond or they just keep repeating the same assertions."[^5] Repetition-under-refutation and emotional escalation are signatures of [[Motivated Reasoning and the Tides of Bias|motivated reasoning]] — reasoning *toward* a foregone conclusion — not of inquiry.
3. **Lowering the bar to feel validated.** Because defenders "really want it to be true," they "lower the evidentiary and the logical bar" so it is "much easier to get over," the goal being "whatever makes them feel validated and justified in their dogma, in their credence, in their belief."[^6] This connects the affective claim to the methodological one: the feeling is *why* the bar gets buried (cf. [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]]).
4. **Certainty as phenomenology, not proof.** McClellan's general premise — "certainty itself is a feeling; conviction is a feeling"[^1] — has independent support. Robert Burton's *On Being Certain* (2008) argues the "feeling of knowing" is a brain-generated sensation that can fire independently of, and even contrary to, the evidence; the felt conviction is not itself evidence that the belief is true. McClellan's point is the applied corollary: a strong feeling of inerrancy is not a measurement of the text.

**Steelman.** The strongest reply is that this is partly an **ad hominem about motive**, and motives are hard to read. (a) *Everyone* reasons from prior commitments — McClellan's own confidence that "the Bible is full of contradictions" is also held with conviction, and conviction-is-a-feeling cuts both ways; the premise does not by itself favor either side. (b) Plenty of credentialed scholars affirm inerrancy and publish in peer-reviewed venues, so "they only argue from feelings" is false as a blanket generalization and is selecting on a sample of weak Twitter respondents. (c) A belief can be both emotionally held and true. The defensible core of McClellan's claim is therefore narrower than the rhetoric: *for the population he actually engaged*, the defenses are behaviorally indistinguishable from dogma-protection (predictable failure modes, repetition, no competent counter), and the felt certainty is being treated as if it settled a question that is properly settled by textual evidence. The legitimacy of the point rests on the **first-order data** — the actual contradictions documented elsewhere (see [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]]) — not on the mind-reading.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan's anti-inerrancy point is unexpectedly **congenial to the Latter-day Saint position on scripture**. The eighth Article of Faith reads: "We believe the Bible to be the word of God *as far as it is translated correctly*" — an explicit qualification that the LDS tradition does *not* hold to biblical inerrancy in the evangelical/[[Chicago Statement on Inerrancy Redefines Literal|Chicago Statement]] sense. Where the inerrantist must defend the proposition that the received text contains no errors or contradictions, a Latter-day Saint can grant the descriptive observation — that the Bible contains contradictions, transmission errors, and competing voices — without any threat to the faith, because the doctrinal commitment was never to a flawless text in the first place. Several consequences follow:
- The "feeling of certainty" McClellan diagnoses is, in LDS framing, *not required*. The tradition's epistemology of scripture is built around continuing revelation and the testimony of the Spirit rather than the inerrancy of a closed canon, so conceding textual contradictions does not trigger the defensive, dogma-protecting reflex he describes.
- This is double-edged. The LDS view escapes the *Protestant* inerrancy trap, but McClellan's underlying critique — that **felt conviction is not a finding** — applies with equal force to any tradition (LDS included) that treats a spiritual feeling of certainty as if it were probative of a historical or textual claim. The honest LDS appropriation keeps McClellan's epistemic warning while cashing in the doctrinal relief Article of Faith 8 provides.
- Practically, this makes McClellan a useful (if non-confessional) ally against the specific evangelical demand that one must affirm biblical inerrancy to be a faithful Christian — a demand the LDS tradition already declines.

## Best Quotes
> "Their certainty about the Bible not having any contradictions is not based on logic or reason. It's based on feelings. And really certainty itself is a feeling. Conviction is a feeling." [^1]

> "I think that you're going to respond with some weak apologetics… and then I'm going to respond with an expert criticism of the argument and then you're not going to be able to respond competently. You're going to either appeal to authority or you're just going to argue by assertion or you're going to run like a squirrel." [^4]

> "It's really just about whatever makes them feel validated and justified in their dogma, in their credence, in their belief. And they imagine that they have this ironclad reasoning and logic and evidence." [^3]
