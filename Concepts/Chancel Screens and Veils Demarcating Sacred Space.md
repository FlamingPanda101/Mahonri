## The Point
In monumental churches, a veiled chancel screen (templon) set the sanctuary (apse) apart from the congregation in the nave, hiding sacred activities such as the consecration of the Eucharist. The veiled screen came to represent the gate of heaven joining earthly and heavenly realms. The exact timing varied regionally, but over time veils, hanging curtains, and cloth coverings were increasingly used to highlight and guard the sacred nature of church liturgy.

## Evidence
1. In some regions (Syria, Palestine, Jordan, Egypt), veiled chancel screens appeared with the earliest basilical churches; in Constantinople, veiled screens were added only in later centuries.
2. Fourth-century liturgical texts describe blessings pronounced over veils in eastern churches (Liturgy of Saint James 2; Liturgy of the Blessed Apostles 11); sixth-century Jordanian church mosaics frequently depict hanging curtains over doors, shrines, and apses.
3. Curtains and veils served multiple functions: in baptisteries (to protect the modesty of initiates anointed and baptized in the nude), at church doors (to shield the Eucharist from the uninitiated), between columns (to demarcate congregational spaces), above the chancel screen (to separate nave from sanctuary), and on the altar (to cover the Eucharistic bread and wine).
4. The veiled chancel screen was viewed as representing the gate of heaven, connecting earthly (nave) and heavenly (sanctuary) realms — analogous to the veil of the Jerusalem temple.
5. During the Eucharist, the bishop offered the sacramental emblems "through the parted veil" at the chancel screen — the meeting point between earth and heaven.

## Best Quotes
> "Over time, veils, hanging curtains, and other cloth coverings become increasingly used as a way to highlight and guard the sacred nature of the liturgies being performed within churches." [^1]

> "The bishop — standing on the opposite side of the barrier — the meeting place between earth (the nave) and heaven (the sanctuary) — would offer the sacramental emblems through the parted veil, allowing initiated worshippers to experience fellowship with the divine presence and heavenly realm." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Grey explicitly warns Latter-day Saints against "parallelomania" but affirms that the demarcation of sacred space through "doors, veils, and chancel screens that facilitated ritual movement within the building" offers "profound conceptual vocabulary" for the temple experience. The veil at the meeting point of earth and heaven is among the most suggestive of these. [^3]
