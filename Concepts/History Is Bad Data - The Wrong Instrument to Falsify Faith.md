# History Is Bad Data - The Wrong Instrument to Falsify Faith

## The Claim

Cardon Ellis (Ether's Elephant), arguing "as a scientist," contends that the historical record is **structurally bad data** — and therefore the *wrong instrument* with which to falsify the claims of the Restoration. The surviving record is "both highly biased — because what survives is not random — and highly discrete," with gaps that widen the further back one goes. Borrowing the statistician's maxim "every model is wrong, some are useful," he holds that history warrants *even more* skepticism than statistics, so "whatever you hear, have a healthy level of skepticism." The companion claim (Luke Hansen, via Popper) concedes faith should be falsifiable in principle, but insists *church history* is not the tool that can do the falsifying.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Survivorship bias.** What survives from the past is not a random sample; it is filtered by who kept records, what was destroyed, and whose perspective was preserved. Conclusions drawn from a biased survival set are unreliable — calibrated skepticism is the appropriate default toward almost any historical claim.

2. **Discreteness and gaps.** The record is sparse and discontinuous, with widening gaps further back, so any reconstruction is a *model* fitted to fragments — and "every model is wrong, some are useful." This makes confident negative verdicts ("this *proves* X about Joseph Smith") epistemically overconfident. The disciplined complement is the source-critical method of [[Source Criticism in Mormon History]].

3. **Wrong instrument, not unfalsifiable faith.** Hansen grants (via Popper) that a thesis rigged to be unfalsifiable "can't tell you anything about reality," and that a testimony is *not* unfalsifiable — if Jesus told a member the church was false, they should follow Jesus. The crux is the *instrument*: "I don't think you can use church history as a way to falsify the claims of the restoration." Critics' "what if we *proved* X" hypotheticals are answered, "maybe just present your argument instead of us pretending you have a good one."

4. **Testimony was not built on history to begin with.** Faith ideally rests on a spiritual witness, into which church history "only kind of fits" as one area — so a historical shock attacks a load the testimony was never carrying. See [[Inverted Testimony - Why Faith Built Top-Down Collapses]] and the [[The Gold Pill Parable - Bulverism Against the Messenger|gold-pill parable]] (rejecting the cure because of the messenger is bulverism).

## Steelman / Counter

The critic's strongest reply: "history is bad data" proves too much — it is selectively deployed to neutralize *unfavorable* history while favorable historical claims (the Restoration's own founding events) are asserted confidently, an isolated-skepticism double standard. If the record is too noisy to falsify, it is equally too noisy to *confirm*. The apologetic rejoinder distinguishes *calibrated* skepticism (proportioning confidence to evidence quality) from *total* skepticism, and locates the real warrant for faith in spiritual experience rather than in the historical record either way — see [[Living With Uncertainty Is a Faith Skill]].
