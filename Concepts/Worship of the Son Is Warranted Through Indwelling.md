# Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Objection: "If Jesus is just a messenger, like the angel of Yahweh, then he's not worthy of worship. Only the Father is. Are you saying we shouldn't worship Jesus?" And, more pointedly: why worship the Son at all rather than just the Father, "who's ultimately the greatest"? [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Never worship an angel — but the Son is not an angel (rating: strong; textual)
"We should never worship an angel" (cf. the rebuking angel of [[Revelation 22.8-9|Revelation 22]]). The Son is categorically different: "the son is given worship throughout the New Testament." See [[The Angel in Revelation Who Refuses Worship]]. [^2]

### 2. Mutual indwelling makes the persons inseparable in action (rating: strong; Johannine)
"Because the son is in the father and the father is in the son, and the son only does what the father has commissioned him to do... they can't act without one another. Jesus cannot act without the father, but the father is acting through the son." Worship of the Son therefore reaches the Father. See [[Co-inherence as Mixing Light to Form the Godhead]]. [^3]

> "the worship that I give goes to the son because the father has chosen to exalt him and deliver the kingdom to him and given him the glory that would merit worship." [^3]

### 3. Worship flows from exaltation by the Father (rating: strong; textual)
The Father "has chosen to exalt him... and given him the glory that would merit worship" ([[Philippians 2.9-11|Philippians 2]]). "We worship the father, son, and holy ghost as one god." [^3]

### 4. The one thing humans never give the divine persons: worship-as-deference-to-source (rating: moderate; subtle)
Ostler frames the only real asymmetry as one of *honor and gratitude*: divinized humans will possess "every property of divinity that they have," but will always honor the divine persons for issuing "the invitation to be in the relationship." The Son likewise is eternally grateful to the Father (John, the Synoptics). "There is a sense of subordinationism, but that doesn't mean that he's not fully divine." See [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]]. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian presses a dilemma: if worship is *latria* (worship due to God alone), then worshiping the Son entails the Son is God-by-nature, not an exalted-and-indwelt agent — otherwise it is idolatry, the very thing the Revelation angel forbids. "Worship reaches the Father through the Son" sounds like venerating a creature as a conduit. Ostler answers that the Son is *not* a creature (he is uncreated and fully divine), so worshiping him is not idolatry; the indwelling/exaltation framing explains *why* worship is fitting without requiring numerical identity of persons or one simple substance. The remaining dispute is over the meaning of "one God." See [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]].

## Best Quotes

> "Should never worship an angel. Here's the distinction. The son is given worship throughout the New Testament. And here's why." [^2]

> "Jesus cannot act without the father, but the father is acting through the son." [^3]

> "There is a sense of subordinationism, but that doesn't mean that he's not fully divine. He has all the properties to be fully divine." [^4]
