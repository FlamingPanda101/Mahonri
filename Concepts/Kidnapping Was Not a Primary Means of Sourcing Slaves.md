# Kidnapping Was Not a Primary Means of Sourcing Slaves

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that even if [[Exodus 21.16|Exodus 21:16]] *were* a real restriction on kidnapping, it still could not "question, condemn, or prohibit" slavery, because for a kidnapping ban to inhibit the slave trade, **kidnapping would have had to be the only or at least a major means of sourcing enslaved people** — and in ancient West Asia it was not. Surveying the Hebrew Bible and the reconstructed economy of "the central and northern hill countries and the southern Negev," he finds "no indication that kidnapping was any kind of primary means of sourcing enslaved folks." A ban on a marginal supply channel leaves the institution untouched.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The actual supply channels are named in the text (rated: strong; primary text).** [[Leviticus 25.44-46|Leviticus 25:44-46]] explicitly endorses sourcing chattel slaves by **purchase** — from non-Israelite families, from other Israelites in the land, and from surrounding nations — "or through conquest." These, not kidnapping, were "the main means of sourcing enslaved folks in ancient Israel."[^1] See [[Hebrew Bible Endorses Chattel Slavery of Foreigners]].
2. **A ban on a non-major channel cannot abolish the practice (rated: strong; argued).** Because kidnapping was not a load-bearing source of slaves, prohibiting it "in absolutely no way, shape or form whatsoever" limits or inhibits "the practice of buying, selling and owning other human beings."[^1]
3. **Distinct from the in-group-scope argument (orientation).** This is a *second, independent* defeater layered on top of the scope point: even granting the law applied broadly, it would still fail to touch slavery for economic reasons. See [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]] for the separate argument that the law protects only freeborn Israelite men.

## LDS Relevance
Sharpens how Latter-day Saints read the Mosaic slave laws as an [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible|accommodated]], time-bound legal order rather than a timeless moral charter: the Torah regulates an existing slave economy (purchase, conquest) rather than dismantling it, which fits a developmental view of revelation meeting a people "in their context" more than a claim that Sinai already encoded abolition.

## Best Quotes
> "For this to be a law that questions, condemns, or prohibits slavery, kidnapping would have to be the only or at least a major means of sourcing enslaved people. But... we don't see any indication that kidnapping was any kind of primary means of sourcing enslaved folks."[^1]

> "We can look in Leviticus 25:44-46 for the quite explicit endorsement of chattel slavery through the purchasing of non-Israelite enslaved folks from their families or from other Israelites... or from other nations around you or through conquest."[^1]
