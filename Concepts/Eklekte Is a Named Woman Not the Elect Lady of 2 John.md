#type/claim

# Eklekte Is a Named Woman Not the Elect Lady of 2 John

## The Claim
[[Lincoln H. Blumell]], a BYU papyrologist, argues that the addressee of [[2 John 1.1|2 John]] — for ~1,800 years rendered "the elect lady" (*eklektē kyria*) and treated as a metaphor for a church — was actually a real woman whose name was *Eklekte* (Latin *Electa*). On this reading 2 John becomes the only New Testament letter whose principal addressee is a named woman, a datum hiding "in plain sight" because two reduplicated Greek letters dropped out of the text. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The earliest commentator already said so.** The earliest extant Christian commentator on the recipient, [[Clement of Alexandria]] (2nd c.), states the letter was written to a woman *Eklekte nomine* — "Eclecta by name." Clement survives only via a 6th-c. Latin epitome (Cassiodorus) and has been dismissed by modern scholars, but Blumell argues he was simply right. [^2]
2. **"Simple" Greek names are undifferentiated from the adjective.** Greek has compound names and *simple* names that are identical in form to an ordinary noun or adjective — e.g. *Stephanos* means both "Stephen" and "crown," disambiguated only by context. *Eklektē* is the feminine of the adjective "chosen/elect," but it is *also* an attested woman's name: Blumell gathers Greek and Latin inscriptions of Roman-period women named *Eklekte / Electa*. Modern critical editions (Nestle-Aland) impose accents, capitalization, and word spacing absent from ancient manuscripts — so by *not* capitalizing they pre-decided it was an adjective. [^3]
3. **The grammar of a Roman letter-opening demands a name.** Thousands of surviving Roman-period letters from Egypt open identically: sender, then immediately the recipient's name, then a definite article, then the honorific *kyrios/kyria* ("lord/lady"). [[3 John]] opens this exact way — "the elder to Gaius." In 2 John, *kyria* (which occurs only at 2 John 1 and 5 in the whole NT) appears in letters only as a *substantive title*, never as a common noun. Read as "elect lady" the address is grammatically anomalous; read as "to **the** lady Eklekte" it matches the standard formula. [^4]
4. **A dropped definite article (haplography) explains the corruption.** The correct text requires *eklektē **tē** kyria* ("to the lady Eklekte"). The name ends in *-tē* and the article is *tē*, so a scribe copying unspaced text easily skipped one of the reduplicated syllables — exactly the kind of error Blumell routinely emends in documentary papyri. He notes that were this an ordinary papyrus letter, every papyrologist would emend the article back without hesitation; only because it is canonical scripture does the obvious correction become "touchy." [^5]
5. **Manuscript evidence is actually present.** The fuller reading with the article shows up in some manuscripts — and they form a *family*, meaning the variant was deliberately copied, not an isolated accident. [^6]
6. **Verse 13 is not a counter-example — it confirms the thesis.** Critics object that v. 13 ("the children of thy elect sister") would force the sister to share the name. But in v. 13 the word stands *without* a definite article, so grammatically it can only be the adjective "elect," whereas v. 1 (after the emendation) is a proper name with the article. The two verses differ precisely as name vs. adjective. Blumell reads v. 13 as a deliberate pun — "your sister who is also *elect / choice*, true to your name" — the same kind of name-pun the NT plays on Peter (*petros/petra*) and on Onesimus. [^7]

## Why It Was Missed
The metaphor "elect lady = the church" became so standard that no one re-questioned the Greek — and Blumell flags a recurring tell: defaulting to metaphor is often "an excuse" when readers don't actually know what a text says (cf. [[Christian Metaphorical Reading Is Unfalsifiable Retreat]]). A famous 1550 printing by Robert Estienne (Stephanus) had capitalized *Eklektē* as a name, but it was dismissed because, without restoring the article *tē*, the Greek still didn't parse — so capitalizing alone never solved the problem. The reading also coheres with the letter's content: v. 10's command not to receive false teachers "into the house" fits *Eklekte* as a [[Patrons and Patronesses of Early House Churches|patroness whose home hosted a congregation]], like Lydia (Acts 16) or Nympha (Col 4:15). This sits alongside other "rediscovered woman" cases — [[Junia Is a Woman Called Prominent Among the Apostles|Junia]] in Romans 16, and Nympha mis-rendered as the man "Nymphas." [^8]
