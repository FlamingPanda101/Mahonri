# Trinity Metaphors Are All Heresies

#type/claim

## The Claim
Closing the episode, [[Dan Beecher]] asks [[Dan McClellan]] for his "favorite Trinity metaphor" — the shamrock, the three states of water (solid/liquid/gas), the three-headed dog Cerberus, Rice Krispies' "Snap, Crackle, and Pop." McClellan's punchline is that the popular analogies people use to *explain* the Trinity in fact illustrate **condemned heresies**: the water-states analogy "is the heresy of modalism."[^1] The deeper point is that every intuitive picture of the Trinity collapses into a position the councils anathematized (modalism, tritheism, partialism), which is itself evidence that the orthodox formula is not intuitively modelable — only verbally specifiable and institutionally enforced.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The water-states analogy is indeed modalism — rate: strong
Modalism (a.k.a. Sabellianism/modalistic monarchianism) holds that Father, Son, and Spirit are not three distinct persons but three *modes* or manifestations of one God — exactly what "the same water appearing as ice, liquid, and steam" depicts. It was condemned in the 3rd century. McClellan's identification is correct.[^1] *Rating: strong; standard heresiology.*

### 2. The pattern generalizes across the common analogies — rate: strong
Catechetical tradition itself flags these failures: the shamrock/three-leaf and "three parts of an egg" analogies tend toward **partialism** (each person is only a *part* of God); "three forms of water" tends toward **modalism**; "three men named Dan" or Cerberus's three heads tend toward **tritheism** (three gods). The well-known "St. Patrick's Bad Analogies" catechetical video makes precisely this point. *Rating: strong; the claim that the metaphors map onto named heresies is broadly accepted even by orthodox teachers.* **Steelman:** a Trinitarian concedes all analogies fail and treats this as *expected*, not damning — the Trinity is *sui generis*, so any creaturely picture must distort it; the failure of analogies confirms the doctrine's transcendence (cf. [[Chalcedon Hypostatic Union as Mystery Defense]]).

### 3. McClellan's inference (the analogies' failure is evidence against the doctrine) — rate: persuasive but contestable
The implicit argument: a coherent concept should admit *some* faithful model; if every model is heretical, the concept may be incoherent rather than merely transcendent (linking to [[Trinity Is Fundamentally Contradictory]]). *Rating: persuasive as a probe; the orthodox reply that incomprehensibility ≠ incoherence is a live response.*
