# Hypostatic Union Creates Two Jesuses

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The hypostatic union: Jesus is one person with *two natures*, fully human and fully divine. The Trinitarian uses it to reconcile the "limited" sayings (ignorance, suffering, a God above him) with the "divine" ones — attributing each to the relevant nature. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **In practice it manufactures two persons.** To deploy the doctrine, the apologist mentally constructs "divine Jesus" and "human Jesus" and assigns data to whichever solves the problem: ignorance and death go to human Jesus, omniscience and miracles to divine Jesus. But two distinct subjects of attribution just *are* two persons — the doctrine smuggles in the very duality it claims to avoid. (Evidence: this is a coherence/practice objection; orthodoxy formally denies two persons via the *communicatio idiomatum*, but Hansen's point is that the explanatory *use* treats them as two.) [^2]
2. **The death dilemma.** "Did *divine* Jesus die on the cross?" If yes, a divine being died and suffered — but classical theism says God is immutable and **impassible** (cannot suffer or be affected). If no, then only "human Jesus" died — but then a mere man, not God, atoned, undercutting the infinite-worth rationale for the atonement. Either horn damages the orthodox account. (Evidence: a genuine and classic tension; "who died on the cross?" is a real problem for strict impassibility.) [^3]
3. **Two non-exclusive natures are fine — but these aren't.** One person *can* hold two compatible natures (e.g., "father" and "man"). The hypostatic union fails because divinity and humanity, as classical theism defines them, are *mutually exclusive* (see [[Divine and Human Natures Are Mutually Exclusive]]) — like square and triangle. (Evidence: depends on the contested definitions of the natures; strong if classical definitions are granted.) [^4]
4. **Steelman.** The orthodox reply: the union is *one hypostasis (person) in two natures* (Chalcedon), and predicates attach to the *person* via the nature appropriate to each — so "the Son died" is true *according to his humanity* without a second person or a change in the divine nature. The "two Jesuses" charge mistakes a distinction of *natures* for a division of *persons*, the very Nestorian error Chalcedon condemned. (Evidence: this is the official answer and it is internally articulated.) The LDS rejoinder: the move only names the mystery — a *single* subject that is at once omniscient-and-ignorant, impassible-and-suffering, uncreated-and-created is asserted, not explained, and "according to his X nature" is the special-pleading hinge. [^1]

## Best Quotes

> "What you do is you actually create two people. You create divine Jesus and you create human Jesus. But you say, 'No, no, no, it's one person with two natures.'" [^2]

> "Did divine Jesus die on the cross? ... if you say human, then he couldn't have atoned for our sins, but if you say divine, then ... how could something divine die? Or suffer?" [^3]

> "It feels like essential modalism. It's just — it gets away with it." [^1]
