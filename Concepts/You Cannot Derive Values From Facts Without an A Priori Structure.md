# You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]]'s central objection to [[Sam Harris]]'s *Moral Landscape*: you cannot read values straight off the facts, because there are too many facts to interpret without a prior value structure — and that structure is partly biological, partly socially constructed, and never fully inspected.[^1]

## Argument

1. **The facts underdetermine values.** "I don't believe that you can derive a value structure from your experience of the observable facts; there's too many facts, you need a structure to interpret them." The structure may have been shaped by facts over evolutionary time, "but it's not derived from the world of facts over the timeframe that you inhabit."[^1]

2. **Where is value located? The antique / Elvis's guitar.** Take an antique apart and you will not find its value in the material substrate; the value is "a social agreement about its position in a hierarchy." Elvis's guitar is worth more than an identical guitar solely because of "the dominance hierarchy of values that's been socially constructed around the guitar" — value "is located in interpersonal space."[^2]

3. **Harris's counter: these are still facts.** Facts about subjective and intersubjective states are real facts — "we can make objective claims about the subjective experience of people like ourselves." The guitar's value "is a measure in the change this provokes in your experience," reflected in brains and behavior; even JFK's dying thought is a fact we can make true/false claims about without accessing it.[^3]

4. **Peterson: that imports values into "fact" at a cost.** "We can stretch the domain of what constitutes a fact so that the domain of fact starts to incorporate the domain of values, but we do that with some... damage to the domain of fact." And the multiplicity-of-interpretation objection Harris levels at scripture applies "precisely the same" to extracting value from facts. *Steelman: both projects face a selection problem; neither escapes the interpretive frame.*[^4]

5. **Harris: no grounding problem — it's a pseudo-problem.** Like "what is the meaning of life?", the demand for an absolute grounding presupposes a slot that needn't be filled; we navigate by acknowledging the felt spectrum of experience. Peterson holds we *do* apply an a priori frame that "narrows our perception to almost nothing," and that this is "absolutely overwhelming" in the evidence. See [[The Moral Landscape - Grounding Value in Conscious Well-Being]].[^5]
