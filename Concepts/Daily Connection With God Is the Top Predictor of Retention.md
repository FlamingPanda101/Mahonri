# Daily Connection With God Is the Top Predictor of Retention

## The Claim
Across longitudinal data tracking teens into their early twenties, the single strongest factor keeping people connected to the Church is a felt, regular, daily connection with God.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Longitudinal design captures pre-departure traits (rated: strong, methodological).** Most datasets observe people only at one time point — after they have already disaffiliated — so their measured traits may be *consequences* of leaving. Dyer's panel data observe characteristics *before* disconnection, giving a cleaner read on causes. "Before they disconnected from their religion, what were they like... what we found is that the number one thing that kept people connected with the church was daily connection with God."[^1]

2. **Face-validity and coherence (rated: moderate).** A felt daily relationship with God plausibly anchors identity more than institutional habit alone — "if they felt on a regular basis that they've been connected with God, then that helps to keep them in, which makes a lot of sense."[^2] *Steelman:* "felt connection with God" is partly endogenous to faith itself, so it may be as much a marker of belief as an independent cause of staying — the arrow can run both ways.

3. **Contrasts with the leading predictor of leaving (rated: strong).** The same data flag political extremity as a top driver of disaffiliation, framing retention as a contest between a Godward pull and a politicizing pull. See [[Political Identity Now Supersedes Religious Identity]].[^3]

## Best Quotes

> "With these data we can actually see... before they disconnected from their religion, what were they like and what were their characteristics."[^1]
