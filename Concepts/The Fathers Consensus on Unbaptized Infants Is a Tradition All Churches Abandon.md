# The Fathers Consensus on Unbaptized Infants Is a Tradition All Churches Abandon

#type/claim

## The Claim
**[[Gavin Ortlund]]** turns the Catholic appeal-to-the-fathers against itself with a **tu quoque on tradition**. Catholics press baptismal regeneration as the unanimous teaching of the fathers; Ortlund answers that there is an even *more* long-standing patristic consensus that *all* modern churches — Catholic included — now reject: from **[[Augustine]]** down to the Reformation, the "common doctrine" of every Western theologian was that infants who die *unbaptized* are denied the **beatific vision** (limbo and related schemes were attempts to soften, not overturn, this). If the fathers' near-universal agreement can be a *mistake* there, then "all the church fathers taught baptismal regeneration" cannot function as a slam-dunk either — especially once one asks *what they meant* by baptism and regeneration.[^1] **[[Trent Horn]]** concedes the force ("a big giant red flag"), grants the historical consensus from Augustine to the Reformation, and replies that this is exactly why a **magisterium** is needed to distinguish genuine sacred tradition from mere widespread patristic opinion — illustrating with the **usury** parallel.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The actual consensus and its breadth (Evidence: historical, conceded by both).** Both men, and the International Theological Commission's own 2007 study, agree that from Augustine to the Reformation it was the *common doctrine* — held by "every theologian," though never infallibly defined — that unbaptized infants do not receive the beatific vision; what happens to them was disputed, but their *exclusion* from heaven's full vision was the default.[^2] *Strength: not a Protestant caricature — Horn affirms the summary and the ITC corroborates it.*

2. **The selectivity charge (Evidence: structural).** Modern Catholics (and everyone else) now *hope for the salvation* of unbaptized infants, departing from that long tradition. So the move "you can't deny baptismal regeneration because the fathers were unanimous" is selectively applied — the same churches abandon an *equally* unanimous tradition when conscience and reflection demand it.[^1] *Strength: a clean parity argument — it does not refute baptismal regeneration, but it disarms the "consensus = certainty" lever.*

3. **The "what did they mean?" qualifier (Evidence: linguistic).** Ortlund argues the fathers' "regeneration in baptism" language carries the *same sign/signified slippage* as circumcision, so the consensus is less monolithic than advertised: he reads **[[Justin Martyr]]** and **[[Cyril of Jerusalem]]** as treating the baptizand as *already made new before* the water — Justin's "regenerated" said of one already renewed, Cyril citing Cornelius (born again at faith) while urging baptism's necessity. As soon as one asks "what is meant by baptism, by regeneration," the unanimity "starts to get a lot more opaque."[^3] *Strength: undercuts the premise (uniform meaning) the consensus argument rides on.*

4. **The fruits-of-tradition test (Evidence: scriptural analogy).** Ortlund frames his stance not as radical individualism but as recognizing that "errors come into the tradition" — the same discernment Jesus authorizes for prophets in [[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]] applies, in principle, to inherited doctrines.[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing view (Horn):** The parallel actually *vindicates* the Catholic system rather than embarrassing it. Horn grants the infant-limbo consensus and the **usury** case — before the 11th–12th centuries *every* father condemned lending at *any* interest as the sin of usury, yet the Church's understanding of *what constitutes* usury legitimately **developed**. The lesson is not "consensus is worthless" but that patristic agreement, while a serious witness, is *not ipso facto* part of the deposit of faith: the writings of the fathers are a *witness to* sacred tradition, not identical with it, and a living **magisterium** is required to discern which is which — just as a magisterium is needed to settle what Scripture means. On this reading the unbaptized-infants consensus was *theological opinion under-determined by definition* (the ITC could revisit it precisely because it was never dogmatically defined), whereas baptismal regeneration is woven through the liturgy, the rule of faith, and conciliar teaching. So Ortlund's red flag is real but double-edged: it shows the *need for* an authoritative interpreter, which is the very thing the Protestant lacks. The argument therefore lowers the evidential value of bare consensus for *both* sides without, by itself, settling whether baptism regenerates.

## LDS Relevance
This exchange is fertile ground for the Restoration's signature claim: that the post-apostolic church *did* accumulate doctrinal error which only restoration (not a magisterium) could correct. The unbaptized-infant consensus — and the Augustinian original-sin engine behind it — is, for Latter-day Saints, a textbook case of an *apostate* development, decisively reversed by revelation: little children are whole, "not capable of committing sin," and need no baptism (Moroni 8:8–22; D&C 137:10). Where Horn answers the consensus-error problem with a *magisterium* and Ortlund answers it with *Scripture-as-norm*, the LDS answer is *continuing revelation through living prophets* — the same epistemic gap, resolved a third way. The case also exposes a tension Latter-day Saints exploit: Catholics already concede that a "unanimous consensus of the fathers" can be wrong, which weakens the broader Catholic appeal to patristic unanimity against the Restoration. See [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]] and [[Infant Baptism Preceded Its Theological Rationale]].
