# Presuppositionalism Cannot Refute a Coherent Rival Worldview Like Mormonism

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] turns the presuppositional method back on **Austin** ([[Austin (Apologia Church)]]) with a self-described concession from the school's own founder: "[[Greg Bahnsen]] was the one who said you cannot use presuppositional apologetics on Mormons, because we have our own consistent, coherent worldview."[^1] The argument: presuppositionalism works by showing a rival worldview is *internally incoherent* (atheism cannot ground logic, induction, morality — see [[Bahnsen's Method - Arbitrariness, Presuppositions, Inconsistency]]). But the [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic|internal critique]] only bites on a worldview that *contradicts itself*. The LDS worldview, Carroll says, supplies its own coherent account of God, revelation, and authority (a living prophet, continuing scripture, a self-consistent metaphysic), so the transcendental argument finds no internal seam to pry — leaving the Reformed apologist with nothing but to **assert** his own standard, which is exactly what Carroll spends the conversation pressing as unjustified.[^2] He affirms the method's power where it belongs ("presuppositional apologetics destroy atheists and I'm all for that")[^1] precisely to sharpen the point that it stalls against a coherent theistic rival.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The method's reach is limited to incoherent worldviews — rate: strong
This is a correct statement of how the transcendental argument is supposed to function. Van Til and Bahnsen aim to show the unbeliever's worldview *cannot account for* the preconditions of intelligibility — i.e., it is self-refuting. That move requires an *internal* contradiction in the target. A worldview that is internally consistent (whatever its truth) denies the method its lever; the apologist must then argue *externally* (evidence, history, exegesis) — the very mode presuppositionalism was built to transcend. Carroll's structural claim is sound and mirrors the LDS use of the method documented in [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic]] and [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]].

### 2. The LDS worldview answers the "by what standard" challenge with named presuppositions — rate: moderate
Carroll's leverage is that the Restoration *openly* names its starting points — a living God who speaks, prophets, an open canon — rather than smuggling in an unargued infallible book. When Austin demands the LDS justify their standard, Carroll's reply is symmetry: the Mormon grounds authority in the living God who reveals (of which scripture is a manifestation), and challenges Austin to do better for the closed 66-book canon, which Austin concedes he will not justify ("that's not my job to justify").[^3] See [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture]]. **Caution:** "internally coherent" is not "true," and Carroll knows it — the claim establishes a *stalemate at the level of method*, not LDS vindication. Defeating the presuppositional disarm leaves the positive LDS case still to be made on other grounds.

### 3. Provenance of the Bahnsen attribution — rate: weak / unverified
The specific quotation — that Bahnsen *named Mormons* as exempt because of worldview coherence — is Carroll's **recollection**, not a sourced citation, and a committed Calvinist like Bahnsen would never have granted that LDS theology is *true*. The defensible core (which Bahnsen did hold) is that the transcendental argument targets worldviews that fail to supply the preconditions of intelligibility; whether he ever conceded Mormonism *passes* that test is unverified. The [[Greg Bahnsen]] note records a *different* Carroll paraphrase to the same effect ("destroys every worldview except" those with a prophet, revelation, and book — "Mormons and the Muslims"), suggesting Carroll is recounting a remembered theme, not a located text. Treat the attribution as a paraphrase.

**Steelman of the presuppositionalist.** A Bahnsenian has two strong replies. **First**, coherence is necessary but not sufficient: the transcendental argument claims that *only* the Christian (Trinitarian, Creator-creature) worldview can account for logic, universals, and induction — so a Mormon worldview with a *finite, embodied, contingent-within-a-chain* God allegedly *cannot* ground necessary truths and abstract universals, making it (on the Reformed analysis) covertly incoherent even if it *feels* internally consistent. On that view Carroll has not shown LDS coherence; he has shown the seam is metaphysical rather than verbal. **Second**, the "you won't justify your standard" jab cuts both ways: the presuppositionalist *intends* the appeal to God's self-attesting revelation to be the rational stopping point (every worldview terminates in an ultimate authority that cannot be justified by something more ultimate without ceasing to be ultimate — see [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture]]). By that logic, "I don't need to justify that God has spoken" is not a *failure* of the method but its *thesis*; the Mormon faces the identical regress for *his* ultimate authority. The honest scorecard: Carroll lands a real *tu quoque* — the presuppositionalist cannot demand a justification he himself refuses to give — but the deeper Reformed claim (that *only* Trinitarian theism grounds intelligibility) is left contested, not refuted, by the bare assertion that Mormonism is "coherent."
