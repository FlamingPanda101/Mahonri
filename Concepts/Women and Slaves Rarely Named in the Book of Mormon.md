# Women and Slaves Rarely Named in the Book of Mormon

## The Claim
The Book of Mormon **names women only rarely** (about six examples), apparently following Nephi's own reticence — he never names his sisters or wife. This naming convention is itself an ancient feature, and it makes the named **servant-woman Abish** ([[Alma 19.16|Alma 19:16]]) doubly remarkable: both her gender and her servile status would normally leave her unnamed. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Scarcity of named women.** Only ~6 named women appear. Nephi "never names his sisters or his wife"; he does mention/quote his mother [[Sariah Attested as an Ancient Female Name|Sariah]], and quotes Isaiah. Later writers seem to follow his convention. Strength: **moderate-high** (a real textual pattern). [^1]
2. **Slaves/servants rarely named.** In ancient texts, slaves "rarely get names." Abish is both a woman *and* a servant, yet she *is* named — and given a name-revealing introduction (see [[Abish Means My Father Is a Man - Vision of Her Father]]). Strength: **moderate-high**. [^2]
3. **Convention as authenticity marker.** That the text reflects an ancient *reticence* about naming women (rather than a modern egalitarian impulse) is itself a subtle consistency with an ancient milieu. Strength: **moderate** (suggestive, not decisive). [^1]

**Steelman:** A critic argues that under-naming women is also exactly what a 19th-century male author steeped in the Bible would do unreflectively, so it is weak evidence of antiquity; and that with so few named women, "six examples" is too small a sample to build a "convention" on. The point is best treated as *consistency* with an ancient setting and as the backdrop that makes Abish's naming conspicuous — not as standalone proof.

## Best Quotes
> "It's rare and painfully rare that women are mentioned by name in the Book of Mormon. We only have six examples." — [[Matthew Bowen]] [^1]

> "Abish is both a woman and a slave ... and slaves don't get names, rarely ... but she's both and she gets named." — host [^2]
