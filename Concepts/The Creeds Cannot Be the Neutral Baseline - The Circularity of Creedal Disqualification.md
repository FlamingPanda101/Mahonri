# The Creeds Cannot Be the Neutral Baseline - The Circularity of Creedal Disqualification

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the *decisive* point is not whether creedal theology is true but what role it is allowed to play. Critics deploy the [[Council of Nicaea|Nicene]] and Chalcedonian creeds as a "seemingly neutral, ancient, and apostolic baseline" by which all other Christian claims are measured: the LDS Church doesn't fit the creeds, therefore Latter-day Saints are disqualified. But if those creeds "were established by a contested political process three centuries after Christ, using philosophical language that the apostles never employed, and in ways that required imperial coercion to enforce, then they can't actually function as that neutral baseline. They're just a claim like any other — a historically situated, philosophically shaped, and politically conditioned claim."[^1] Applying that claim as an objective standard to rule out other claims is **circular reasoning**: "you're defining orthodoxy by the councils and then rejecting anything outside the councils as unorthodox without ever explaining where the councils got the authority to make that definition binding."[^2]

This is distinct from the genetic "imperial coercion" point (see [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]]): Arnold concedes a politically messy process *could* still reach a true conclusion. The circularity charge is narrower and survives that concession — it attacks the creeds' use as a *standard*, not their content.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The baseline-vs-claim distinction is the load-bearing move (rated: strong, conceptual).** Arnold explicitly separates two questions: "the historical circumstances of how the creeds were produced don't by themselves settle whether the theology is correct. What the circumstances *do* settle is a different and more exact question" — whether the creeds are the neutral measuring stick.[^3] Demoting the creeds from "the apostolic baseline" to "one historically-situated claim among others" removes their privileged adjudicating status without having to prove them false. This is the inverse of the concession in [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]], where the LDS side grants ~95% of Nicaea and relocates the fight to *consubstantiality*.

2. **The chain-of-transmission rebuttal is itself broken at Nicaea (rated: moderate-to-strong).** The standard counter is that the creeds inherit apostolic authority because Christ → apostles → bishops → councils. Arnold answers that even granting the chain "in principle," Nicaea was convened and presided over by an unbaptized emperor with no place in apostolic succession, and the mechanism that overrode the bishops (imperial pressure producing signatures under duress, e.g. [[Eusebius of Caesarea]]'s letter) is "the chain being bypassed." Claiming the output carries apostolic authority "still demands you to assume what the argument is supposed to prove."[^4] He cites the Donatist controversy and the Council of Arles (314) — which ruled that ordination validity does not depend on the ordainer's moral state — as making the succession "institutional rather than genuinely apostolic." See [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]].

3. **The same circularity recurs in the "spirit guided it anyway" defense (rated: strong).** Arnold notes the deepest version is circular twice over: the question on the table is whether the council fathers had binding authority; "you can't demonstrate that it wasn't lost by appealing to the council's decisions, because the authority of those council decisions is exactly what's in question."[^5] **Steelman of the creedal side:** a Trinitarian historian would reply that this proves too much — *any* doctrinal authority (including LDS prophetic authority) faces a regress, and the church's continuous reception of Nicaea over 1,700 years is itself evidence of the Spirit's guidance, not mere question-begging. Arnold's rejoinder is that the LDS case "points outside itself" to datable external confirmations (Nahom, Dead Sea Scrolls, chiasmus), whereas creedal authority is a closed loop "with nothing external to break it open."[^6] *Rating: the circularity charge is logically clean; whether the LDS alternative escapes its own circularity is the contested follow-up.*

## Best Quotes
> "They can't actually function as that neutral baseline. They're just a claim like any other. A historically situated, philosophically shaped, and politically conditioned claim."[^1]

> "You're defining orthodoxy by the councils and then rejecting anything outside the councils as unorthodox without ever explaining where the councils got the authority to make that definition binding."[^2]

> "The next time someone tells you that you can't be a Christian because you don't conform to the Nicene Creed, the question to ask is a simple one. Where did the Nicene Creed come from? And who gave its authors the authority to define Christianity for all time?"[^7]
