# Theism Makes Science Possible

## The Point
Turek's headline claim for the Science chapter is not the usual one. The point is "not that science supports theism but that theism supports science. In other words, theism makes doing science possible."[^1] We could not do science reliably if atheism were true, because science depends on immaterial realities — orderly natural laws, the laws of logic and mathematics, the reliability of reason, and objective moral honesty — that a purely material universe cannot supply.[^2] Modern science was in fact born among believers who expected nature to be lawful precisely because they believed in a Law Giver.[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The founders expected order because they believed in a Law Giver.** "Modern science got its start by Christians who, as astronomer Johannes Kepler put it, were studying how nature worked in order to understand 'God's thoughts after Him.'" Bacon, Galileo, Newton, and Kepler could rely on the regularity and consistency of natural law because they believed a Law Giver stood behind it — and that regularity is necessary to do science at all. "If nature behaved in an erratic, unpredictable fashion, then life and science would be impossible."[^3]
2. **Atheism undercuts the very tools it uses.** As Turek argues across the book, materialism "scuttles free will and destroys our confidence in everything we think." He quotes atheist Thomas Nagel: "Evolutionary naturalism provides an account of our capacities that undermines their reliability, and in doing so undermines itself."[^2]
3. **Every "CRIME" is a precondition of science.** Causality, Reason, Information/Intentionality, and Morality/Evil are each immaterial realities "either necessary for science or [that] impact science in a significant way." Scientists cannot gather or interpret data without the laws of logic and reason; "they can't produce honesty in a test tube — they have to be honest themselves."[^4]
4. **The war is science vs. atheism, not science vs. religion.** "I'm not saying atheists can't do science. Obviously they can. What I am saying is that they are unwittingly stealing tools from God in order to do it."[^5] See [[The Stolen Concept - Atheists Borrow from God to Argue Against Him]].

## Best Quotes
> "While there is certainly evidence from science to support theism, the most important point for this chapter is not that science supports theism but that theism supports science. In other words, theism makes doing science possible. We wouldn't be able to do science reliably if atheism were true." [^1]

> "Thus, the war is not between science and religion, but between science and atheism." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology offers an unusually direct welcome to this thesis. The Restoration teaches that a divine, intelligent light literally orders and sustains the cosmos: it is "the light of truth... which is in all things, which giveth life to all things, which is the law by which all things are governed" ([[D&C 88|D&C 88:6–13]]), and "the glory of God is intelligence, or, in other words, light and truth" ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:36]]). An orderly, law-governed creation is exactly what one should expect if reality is pervaded by such intelligence, so the LDS reader affirms Turek's "theism grounds the lawfulness science assumes." One honest qualification: Turek's grounding is classical (an ex-nihilo Creator imposing law on matter), whereas LDS thought casts law, intelligence, and matter as eternal realities God organizes and works within ([[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30|D&C 130:20–21; Alma 30:44]]). The expectation of an intelligible, lawful cosmos is shared; the metaphysics underneath differs.
