# Virgin Birth Is a Later Development in the Jesus Tradition

## The Claim

The virgin birth of Jesus is not an original, early element of Christian belief but a "posthumous secondary accretion to the Jesus tradition" — a later development added as concerns about Jesus's divine origins intensified in the decades after his death. It appears only in the later gospels ([[Matthew the Evangelist|Matthew]] and [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]], with Luke's nativity account possibly being even later than the rest of Luke), and is absent from the earliest Christian writings.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Absent from Paul and Mark — the earliest sources

[[Dan McClellan|McClellan]]: "Paul and Mark say nothing about Jesus's origins except that Paul says, you know, he was born of a woman, but great. Happened to most of us. Really sets him apart there. So there's nothing significant about his origins until we get to the later gospels."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Primary (textual). [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]]'s letters (~50s CE) and [[John Mark|Mark]] (~70 CE) are the earliest Christian writings; neither mentions a virgin birth. Paul's sole reference ([[Galatians 4.4|Galatians 4:4]]) says Jesus was "born of a woman" — language that would be strikingly understated if Paul knew a virgin birth tradition.

### 2. Progressive "pushing back" of divinity

McClellan argues the gospels show a pattern: "they begin to push the divinity of Jesus's origins further and further back in time." [[John Mark|Mark]] has no birth story — [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] becomes divine at his baptism. [[Matthew the Evangelist|Matthew]] and [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] add virgin birth narratives. [[John the Evangelist|John]] pushes divinity to the pre-existent Logos. This trajectory suggests the virgin birth developed as christological reflection deepened.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (critical inference). The developmental pattern across the gospels is well-recognized in NT scholarship, though the exact sequence and dating are debated.

### 3. The nativity account in Luke may itself be a secondary addition

McClellan suggests the nativity account in Luke 1-2 "is probably even later than the rest of Luke. I think it's a secondary edition to the Gospel of Luke as well."[^4] This would make the virgin birth an accretion even within Luke itself.

> **Evidence rating:** Secondary (scholarly hypothesis). Some scholars argue Luke's nativity is a later addition based on stylistic and structural differences from the rest of Luke-Acts. Not universally held, but a recognized position.

### 4. Cultural sensitivities about sexuality likely drove the development

McClellan suggests the virgin birth "probably developed as sensitivities about the nature of sexual desire and sexual intercourse is leading people to want to distance the divine Messiah from a natural birth." He connects this to the later development of the immaculate conception doctrine as "the further teasing out of those concerns."[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Tertiary (sociological inference). This is a plausible historical explanation for the development, consistent with known Greco-Roman attitudes toward divine figures and purity, but speculative.

## Best Quote

> "This notion that there's a virgin birth is widely agreed to be a posthumous secondary accretion to the Jesus tradition where you're starting to get sensitivities to the origins of Jesus." — [[Dan McClellan]][^6]

## Counter-arguments / Related

- Apologists argue Matthew was simply recording history as it happened, not developing a tradition. But see [[Matthews Nativity Contradicts Lukes Nativity]] for reasons the two accounts cannot both be historically accurate.
- The Septuagint's _parthenos_ rendering may have influenced Matthew (see [[Isaiah 7.14 Virgin Birth Prophecy Is Mistranslated]]) but McClellan doubts it was the sole motivation: "I don't think it is solely a result of the translation of alma as parthenos."[^7]
