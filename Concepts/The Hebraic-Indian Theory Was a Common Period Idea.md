# The Hebraic-Indian Theory Was a Common Period Idea

## The Claim
The idea that Native Americans descend from the ten lost tribes of Israel — the "Hebraic-Indian theory" — was not invented by [[Ethan Smith]]. It was a widespread, even waning, idea by the 1820s, advanced by multiple contemporary authors. This cuts two ways: it makes *View of the Hebrews* unremarkable (so any "parallel" could derive from the broader milieu, not the book), but it also means that *if* the [[Book of Mormon]] drew on the milieu, it did so in a strikingly atypical form.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Multiple named contemporaries advanced the theory (rated: strong, documented).** Ethan Smith himself cites figures like Elias Boudinot, James Adair, a Jewish-American writer named Noah (Mordecai Manuel Noah), and Josiah Priest — all circulating the Hebraic-Indian theory, which "goes at least back to early colonial America, even earlier."[^2] Ethan Smith "is not just a one-off here. He's not the only guy arguing for this."
2. **The theory was contested and waning, not a given (rated: strong, primary).** Smoot tracked down contemporary reviews of *View of the Hebrews*: "It's also not like a universal given. ... There are people who are like, I don't know if I buy this theory." Its zenith was the late 18th century; "by the 1820s when he publishes this, it's waning in its influence."[^3]
3. **Diffuse milieu undermines source-specificity (rated: moderate, logical).** If the idea is "all over the place," then a shared concept in the Book of Mormon "doesn't really have a lot of explanatory power" for dependence on *View of the Hebrews* specifically — it could come from anywhere.[^4] *Steelman:* a critic can reply that *View of the Hebrews* is still the most accessible, most-distributed, geographically-nearest instance of the theory to Joseph Smith, so it remains the best single candidate even if the idea was common. Smoot's counter is that the Book of Mormon's version is "different in like every respect" (see [[Book of Mormon Lacks the Hebrew Markers View of the Hebrews Uses]]).

## Best Quotes

> "These are ideas that are not totally out of left field during the period. ... You have guys like Elias Boudinot, James Adair, Noah ... Josiah Priest ... circulating this idea of what's called the Hebraic Indian theory."[^2]

> "It's also not like a universal given. Like not everybody's buying this theory. ... It's very much a live discussion happening, but really at its zenith it's like in late 18th century. By the 1820s ... it's waning in its influence."[^3]

> "Once you've watered it down that much ... it's just this generic concept that's all over the place ... this doesn't really have a lot of explanatory power for the Book of Mormon at all."[^4]
