# The Pearl of Great Price Is the Most Unstable Volume of LDS Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Stephen Smoot|Steven Smoot]] opens by asserting there is "no volume of scripture as unstable or in flux as the Pearl of Great Price." Rather than poofing into existence as a single revealed unit, it was assembled eclectically from scattered Joseph Smith material, then rearranged, expanded, contracted, and re-canonized across more than a century and a half. Smoot treats this instability not as an embarrassment but as the clearest available case study in what an *open canon* actually means for Latter-day Saints — and as the antidote to being "blindsided" by critics who point out that old editions contained content now removed.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It was born as a distribution pamphlet, not a revealed book.** Apostle [[Franklin D. Richards]] compiled the first edition in **1851** in Liverpool so British Saints could access Joseph Smith writings otherwise buried in newspapers (*Times and Seasons*, *Millennial Star*). Smoot calls compiling it "the 19th century version of let's post it online" — better circulation, **not** a claim to be a new standard work. It was *not canonized* at this stage [^1][^2].

2. **The contents and arrangement have repeatedly changed.** The 1851 edition opened with "extracts from the Prophecy of Enoch" (now Moses 7), included Doctrine and Covenants material, and ended with the poem "O Say, What Is Truth?" [[Orson Pratt]] (1878) rearranged the Book of Moses into its modern shape and conformed the text to the RLDS-published JST. [[James Talmage]] (1902) deleted the D&C duplications, dropped the poem, added chapters/verses, and reworded the fourth Article of Faith. "Everything that's currently in the Pearl of Great Price is there [in 1851], but... your table of contents is going to look different" [^3][^4][^5].

3. **Even canonized content moved out again.** In 1976 two visions (Joseph Smith's celestial-kingdom vision; Joseph F. Smith's redemption-of-the-dead vision) were canonized *into* the Pearl of Great Price, then in 1979/1981 **moved to the Doctrine and Covenants** as sections 137 and 138 — "they had a very short life in the Pearl of Great Price" [^6][^7].

4. **The instability extends to the text and even the facsimiles.** Smoot notes copyist errors and printer alterations across editions ("even the facsimiles have undergone some modification"), and frames the existence of two Book of Moses manuscripts (OT1, OT2) and the separate "Visions of Moses" (Moses 1) as a "complicated textual history" requiring a study edition to track variants [^8][^9].

## Best Quotes
> "There's no volume of scripture as unstable or in flux as the Pearl of Great Price." [^1]

> "Understanding the history of the Pearl of Great Price is really key to understanding what an open canon means for Latter-day Saints and if we even could have new scripture in the future." [^1]

> "How did we get the red-headed stepchild of the canon... it's eclectic. You've got a couple things of ancient scripture... then things from church history... and the Articles of Faith." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the foundational, descriptive backbone for the episode's two normative claims — that this is healthy ([[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug]]) and legitimate ([[Canonization Is a Community Process Not a Unilateral Prophetic Act]]). It also supplies the counter-frame to the Protestant charge that an authoritative-but-mutable canon is no canon at all ([[The LDS Tradition Emptied the Canon Rather Than Expanded It]]).
