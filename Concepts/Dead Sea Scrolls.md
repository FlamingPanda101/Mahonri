# Dead Sea Scrolls

The **Dead Sea Scrolls** are a collection of roughly 900–1,000 manuscripts (mostly fragmentary) discovered between 1947 and 1956 in eleven caves near **Qumran** on the northwest shore of the Dead Sea, generally associated with a Jewish sectarian community of the late Second Temple period. They date from approximately the 3rd century BCE to the 1st century CE, making them about a thousand years older than the previously oldest complete Hebrew Bible manuscripts (the medieval Masoretic codices). The corpus includes the oldest known copies of nearly every Hebrew Bible book (the Great Isaiah Scroll being the most famous), plus sectarian rule texts, *pesharim* commentaries, and previously lost or rare works such as 1 Enoch and Jubilees. They are first-rank primary evidence for **textual criticism** because they attest the *fluidity* of the biblical text before standardization, preserving variant readings and showing that the Septuagint sometimes reflects a genuine Hebrew *Vorlage* rather than later corruption.

## Relevance
The scrolls anchor several of the vault's arguments. (1) They give material, pre-Christian attestation for books and readings the vault relies on — e.g. the eleven Qumran copies of **1 Enoch** in Aramaic, which prove the text's antiquity and original language. (2) Their witness to pre-exilic / Second Temple theology (divine council, multiple *elohim*, plural divine traditions) supports the "the later text was flattened" line used against strict metaphysical monotheism. (3) For **Book of Mormon Isaiah** debates, the Great Isaiah Scroll matters: it shows an Isaiah text close to the Masoretic Text yet with variants, which both sides invoke when arguing whether the Book of Mormon's Isaiah variants are plausible ancient readings or KJV-dependent. In short, the scrolls are the go-to evidence that the biblical text had a living, variant-rich history rather than a single frozen original.
