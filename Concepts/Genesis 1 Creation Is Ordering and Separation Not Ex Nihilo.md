# Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation, Not Ex Nihilo

## The Point

Read in its ancient context, Genesis 1 does not describe creation out of nothing but the *organization* of pre-existing material, and its governing logic is *separation*. The Hebrew *bara* ("create") means something closer to "organize" than to creation from nothing; *bereshit* is best read not as an absolute "in the beginning" but as a temporal clause — "when God began to create sky and land, the land being empty and desolate" — which presumes something already there. [^1] The structuring verb of the chapter is *hibdil*, "to separate": light from darkness, upper from lower waters, day from night, and creatures "according to type." Creation is "fundamentally a process of dividing in order to put things into their proper places." [^2] This reading fits Latter-day Saint theology better than creation ex nihilo.

## Evidence

- *Bara* "means something like 'organize' rather than active creation out of nothing"; the original Genesis 1 "presumes that there is already something there when God begins his creative activity: 'empty and desolate' land." [^1]
- *Bereshit* lacks the definite article; the NJPS renders Gen. 1:1–2a "When God began to create heaven and earth—the earth being unformed and void," turning the verse into a statement about the *state* of things at creation, not an absolute beginning. Joseph Smith's exegesis of *bara* in the King Follett discourse is "defensible from the Hebrew." [^1]
- *Hibdil* ("to separate") is the same verb used in Leviticus 10:10 for priests putting "difference between holy and unholy" — linking creation to priestly, temple-oriented division and classification. [^2]
- Genesis 1 is ascribed to a priestly school concerned with temple, order, and classification of clean/unclean. [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "Creation in Genesis is not framed around the idea of creation out of nothing but is based on the organization of material that is already extant." [^1]

> "Creation in Genesis 1 is fundamentally a process of dividing in order to put things into their proper places." [^2]

## LDS Connection

The "organize" reading aligns with Joseph Smith's King Follett teaching and with the broader Restoration rejection of [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation ex nihilo]] in favor of God organizing pre-existing matter. It shows how attention to the Hebrew and the ancient context recovers a creation theology more native both to ancient Israel and to Latter-day Saint doctrine.
