# The Father's Presence Was the Decisive Variable in Polygamous Family Wellbeing

## The Claim
Plural-marriage families ranged across a broad spectrum from genuinely functional and happy to tragic and chaotic. Historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] argues, from the practitioners' own records, that the single factor most strongly predicting which end of that spectrum a family landed on was the *presence and involvement of the father* — both emotionally and economically. This reframes the common assumption that the wives uniformly "pulled the short end of the stick," and it locates the suffering in absentee fathering rather than in plurality as such.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Both extremes are documented; Nash refuses to flatten them.** Asked for especially functional families and for "just chaos," she answers "both — absolutely," and reports "really tragic stories about dysfunctional families... for all different reasons."[^1] The steelman is built in: she does not claim plural families were uniformly happy.
2. **The father's presence emerges as the recurring variable.** Studying the stories "showed me the important role of the father in family... particularly in polygamous families. It really affected a wife's experience and children's experiences if the father was more present."[^1]
3. **The pain of the absentee husband, in their own words.** Wives whose husbands they saw only "a couple times a year" recorded that "my husband came and he felt more like a visitor than a spouse." Such women "had to develop a sense of independence that didn't necessarily come naturally" in a period when women depended heavily on men to function in society.[^2]
4. **Involvement smoothed the hardship.** "If a man was willing to step in more and provide more economically, that helped to smooth things." Where it was absent, some women "had to entirely support themselves in dysfunctional families."[^3]
5. **The candid application to Brigham Young.** Nash and the host apply the principle even to the Church's president: [[Brigham Young]] "wasn't necessarily as involved as maybe his families would have hoped" — understandable given so many families and his office, but an honest concession that high status did not guarantee a present father (compare [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem]]).[^4]
6. **The aggregate verdict still leans positive.** Despite the dysfunction at one tail, "in general women tended to say they were content with their decision to marry plurally."[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "It really affected a wife's experience and children's experiences if the father was more present... they tended to experience more pain if they never saw their husband."[^2]

> "My husband came and he felt more like a visitor than a spouse."[^2]

> "We just kind of think the women were the ones who pulled the short end of the stick... We don't think often about the challenges that men went through."[^1]
