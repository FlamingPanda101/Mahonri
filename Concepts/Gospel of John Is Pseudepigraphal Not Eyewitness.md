# The Gospel of John Is Pseudepigraphal, Not an Eyewitness Account

## The Claim
The Gospel of John is "fundamentally a pseudepigraphal work" — written by an author who **presented himself as something he was not**, an eyewitness to the life of Jesus ("the disciple whom Jesus loved"). It is neither a literal eyewitness memoir nor the work of a tradition-gathering community.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The text self-presents as an eyewitness from the start.** [[John 1.14|John 1:14]] — "the word became flesh and dwelt among us... and we have seen his glory" — positions the author as one who saw Jesus. *(Strong as a reading of the text; this self-claim is what the pseudepigraphy thesis interprets.)* [^1]
2. **The Beloved Disciple is woven through the book and credited as author.** He leans on Jesus at the Last Supper, is first at the empty tomb, stands at the cross, and is named the writer in [[John 21.24|John 21:24]] ("the disciple... who has written these things"). *(Strong; the authorial claim is explicit in the text.)* [^2]
3. **That eyewitness claim is historically implausible for the named candidate.** The most natural candidate, John son of Zebedee, is presented in Acts as illiterate and as a Galilean fisherman — see [[Beloved Disciple Was Not John the Son of Zebedee]]. If the named eyewitness cannot be the author, the eyewitness framing is a literary device. *(Strong; combines internal and external evidence.)* [^3]
4. **It fits a wider Johannine pattern of pseudepigraphy.** The Gospel "spawned all kinds of pseudepigraphal literature in its wake," including (Méndez argues) 1–3 John and perhaps Revelation — the Gospel is the head of a pseudepigraphal stream. *(Moderate; depends on the companion theses.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Richard Bauckham and others argue seriously that John preserves genuine eyewitness testimony (e.g. *Jesus and the Eyewitnesses*), and the explicit first-person "we have seen" could be a truthful claim by a real disciple other than John son of Zebedee. "Pseudepigraphal" is a judgment about intent the text never admits; a sympathetic reader can take the eyewitness framing at face value or as honest community memory.

## Best Quotes
> "What I argue is that this is fundamentally a pseudepigraphal work... a work by an author who presented himself as something he was not, as an eyewitness to the life of Jesus." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^4]

> "The author seems to present himself as someone who has seen Jesus in his lifetime. He says that the word became flesh and dwelt among us... and we have seen his glory." [^1]

> "At the very end of the book in chapter 21 verse 24 you hear the text say that this disciple, this disciple whom Jesus loved, is the one who has written this book." [^2]
