# The Laws of Nature Are a Brute Fact

## The Point
**Objection:** Granting that science cannot explain the fundamental laws of nature and that philosophy can address such questions, the atheist (with Bertrand Russell and J. L. Mackie) may still say there simply *is* no explanation of why the fundamental laws are as they are — they are an inexplicable "brute fact," and "that's all that can be said."[^1] **Reply:** Feser gives three answers. The move violates the **principle of sufficient reason**, which he has independently defended as true; it is purely **ad hoc**, adopted only to dodge the proofs; and it is **self-undermining**, because if the deepest law has no explanation, then no higher law it grounds can really explain anything either.[^2]

## Evidence
- Since the PSR is true, "there must be an explanation of the fundamental laws of nature, and this objection is just a nonstarter."[^3]
- The position has "no motivation at all" except to avoid arguments like Feser's — "an ironic result given that atheists often like to accuse theists of tailoring their philosophical premises to fit a desired conclusion."[^4] The only thing that could motivate it independently — a successful Humean or quantum objection to the PSR — has already failed.[^4]
- Lloyd Gerson's retort applies: it is no good to answer a given explanation by muttering "maybe things ultimately just have no explanation." The reply is, "I just gave you an explanation. If you don't want to accept it, then you have to show me specifically what is wrong with it, not pretend that it doesn't exist!"[^4]
- A regress of laws is a hierarchical (essentially ordered) causal series and "must terminate in something that is self-explanatory." To explain P by law A, A by law B, then call B a brute fact, is "like placing a book on a shelf, and the shelf on two brackets, but then letting go of the brackets in midair and expecting the book and shelf to stay aloft."[^5]
- Only what is "purely actual, without parts, has existence as its very essence, and is thus absolutely necessary" can terminate the series, because only such a thing is self-explanatory.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "They are just there, as an inexplicable 'brute fact', and that's all that can be said." [^1]

> "To 'explain' some phenomenon P in terms of a law of nature A ... but then to say that law of nature B itself has no explanation but is just a 'brute fact', is like placing a book on a shelf, and the shelf on two brackets, but then letting go of the brackets in midair and expecting the book and shelf to stay aloft." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Against the atheist who halts inquiry at "the universe just is," Feser is a strong ally: the demand for an ultimate, self-explanatory ground is one Latter-day Saints share, since LDS cosmology also refuses an unexplained surd at the bottom of reality. The classical-theist *content* of Feser's terminus, however, is where the Restoration diverges. Feser's self-explanatory ground is the purely actual, absolutely simple being whose essence is existence (see [[The Purely Actual Actualizer - Aristotle's Unmoved Mover]] and [[The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One]]). LDS theology holds that intelligence and matter are themselves uncreated and eternal — they are not "brute facts" in the atheist's sense of *unintelligible*, but they are genuinely *underived*, co-eternal with God rather than produced by Him out of nothing (compare [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). So a Latter-day Saint can press Feser's anti-brute-fact logic against atheism while noting that Feser's own "absolutely necessary, simple" terminus is a philosophical commitment the Restoration replaces with eternally existing realities ordered by eternal law. Be fair to Feser: his target here is the atheist's refusal to explain *at all*, and on that refusal he and the Saint stand together.
