# Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change

## The Claim
When the critic demands that "skeptical scholars" must *prove* the societies behind the Bible underwent cultural change, [[Dan McClellan]] inverts the burden of proof. Change is not the extraordinary claim; **stasis is.** "All human societies change. It is the notion that these societies did not change over the course of a thousand years that would require an argument."[^1] The apologetic move defaults to presupposing no social or cultural change and then says, "unless and until you can prove my presupposition false, we should default to my presupposition" — even though the presupposition "goes against literally all the data."[^2]

McClellan is explicit that he uses "cultural evolution" not in a narrow technical sense but "in the broader sense of change in response to environment."[^3] The claim that the societies producing the Hebrew Bible over ~1,000 years did *not* change is, he says, "absolutely laughable," because all human societies have "no other possible choice but to" change.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The default runs the wrong way.** Demanding proof of change "otherwise we just default to presupposing that there was no social or cultural change" reverses the actual probabilities; the burden belongs on whoever asserts a millennium of stasis.[^2]
2. **No argument for stasis can even be generated.** "No argument can be generated for its plausibility because all human societies change." The apologist does not offer "an argument that it's most likely or even... that it's plausible" — only the bare presupposition.[^5]
3. **The presupposition survives only by audience capture.** The content creator "knows that they can get away with it because they know the overwhelming majority of their audience comprises folks who have already accepted the very same presupposition. And so no case needs to be made because we're all on the same page dogmatically."[^6] (This is the rhetorical, not evidential, ground of the claim — cf. [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]].)
4. **Connects to the dating argument.** The burden-shift is what licenses [[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah]]: if change is the default, the apologist cannot assume a stable, perennial anti-Asherah norm; they must produce a datable pre-Josiah text that condemns Asherah, and none exists.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A conservative could grant that *societies* change while denying that *revealed truth* changes — i.e. Israelite religion may have drifted, but the canonical norm (monotheism, anti-idolatry) was revealed early and the drift is the apostasy. On this view "change" and "the original norm" are not the same variable, so McClellan's appeal to universal social change does not by itself locate the norm late. **But** McClellan's reply is methodological: the claim that a *specific* norm (anti-Asherah exclusivity) was present-and-revealed early is itself a positive historical claim requiring datable evidence, and the apologist supplies none — they smuggle the stability of the *norm* in under the cover of treating the *text* as inerrant and univocal (see [[Putting Dogma Over Data]] and [[Synchronic Change in the Conception of Deity]]).

## LDS Relevance
The same burden-shift undergirds the Latter-day Saint use of the Deuteronomistic-reform thesis: if Israelite religion demonstrably *changed* — and the burden is on whoever claims it did not — then a pre-reform "First Temple" theology (divine council, embodied/visible deity, a divine feminine) is the historically expected baseline rather than a Mormon novelty. McClellan himself draws no LDS conclusion (he is non-confessional), but his refusal to let apologists presuppose a static, always-monotheistic Israel is the same evidential ground LDS apologists rely on when they argue the Book of Mormon preserves what the reform later edited out (see [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]]).
