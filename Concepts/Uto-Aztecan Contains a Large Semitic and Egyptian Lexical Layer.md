# Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer

## The Claim
[[Brian Stubbs]] argues that the **Uto-Aztecan** language family — one of the largest in the Americas (30–40 languages, 11 branches, spanning the U.S. Southwest to El Salvador, including the Nahua/Aztec languages) — contains a massive, systematic substrate of **Hebrew, Aramaic, and Egyptian** vocabulary. He counts roughly **1,657 cognate sets** of Semitic/Egyptian origin, about **60%** of the ~2,700 cognates in his comparative corpus, split into roughly **700 Semitic and 500 Egyptian** items plus a divergent dialect set.[^1][^2] Because mainstream historical linguistics treats a 10% lexical overlap as sufficient to posit a language relationship (modern English retains only ~15% of Old English, the rest being French/Latin loans), Stubbs argues this volume far exceeds the threshold and is best explained by Semitic-and-Egyptian-speaking migrants — i.e., [[Book of Mormon]] peoples — as a founding component of Proto-Uto-Aztecan.[^3] This is offered as positive linguistic evidence for the Book of Mormon in ancient America.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The corpus is large and patterned, not a grab-bag (rated: moderate, contested).** Sample cognates: Semitic *baraq* "lightning" → Uto-Aztecan *berok* (in 6–8 languages); Hebrew *bat* "daughter" → *bat-ta*; Aramaic *bakura* "livestock" → *buku*; Egyptian *sbq* "calf of the leg" → *sipica*.[^4][^5] Stubbs stresses it is "a numbers game": isolated lookalikes occur by chance, but hundreds obeying the *same* shifts cannot. See [[Sound Correspondences Distinguish Descent from Coincidence in Language]].
2. **Stubbs has genuine standing in the field (rated: strong on credentials, narrow on thesis).** His *Uto-Aztecan: A Comparative Vocabulary* is mainstream-praised (Kenneth Hill: "a monumental contribution"). He holds an M.A. in linguistics and completed PhD comprehensives in Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic.[^6] *Steelman:* credentials in a field do not transfer to a fringe sub-claim, and Stubbs himself **concedes the Semitic/Egyptian thesis is outside the consensus** of the three-to-five active Uto-Aztecanists.[^7]
3. **The two strata share one sound system, fitting a bilingual source (rated: moderate, interpretive).** The Hebrew and Egyptian cognates undergo the *same* correspondences (e.g. *b* → *p*, pharyngeals rounding vowels), which Stubbs reads as one group that spoke both — matching [[Mormon 9.32-34]]'s claim the Nephites kept records in reformed Egyptian and would have used Hebrew.[^8] See [[Mormon 9.32-34]].
4. **Outside reviewers gave favorable notice (rated: weak-to-moderate, anecdotal).** [[David H. Kelley]] (non-LDS Mayanist) called a draft "the most interesting and significant piece of research I have seen in years"; [[John S. Robertson]], Dirk Elzinga, Royal Skousen, Eric Elliott, and Roger Westcott are cited as positive.[^9][^10] *Steelman:* these are mostly private appraisals or LDS-institutional reviews, not a shift in the peer-reviewed consensus; Stubbs notes two linguists tried "to take it down," and of ten Semitic scholars he polled, five called it strong, three could not assess the Egyptian, and two did not answer.[^11] The strongest skeptical reply is structural: with a 22-consonant Semitic inventory and thousands of Uto-Aztecan words, a sufficiently permissive set of allowed sound-shifts and meaning-shifts can mine spurious matches at scale (the methodological worry Stubbs's own "Brit-ish" example concedes), so the **discipline's non-adoption** — not just the raw count — carries the weight.
