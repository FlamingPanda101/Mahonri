# Kimball Questioned the Priesthood-Ban Prooftexts in His Margins Decades Before the Revelation

## The Claim
[[Spencer W. Kimball]]'s personal scriptures contain handwritten margin notes in which he questioned, with question marks, the scriptural texts then used to justify withholding the priesthood from Black members — written *long before* he became the prophet who received the 1978 revelation ([[Official Declaration 2]]). Beside the [[Pearl of Great Price]] passages cited to support the ban he wrote "Question mark," and beside the word "Negro" he again marked a question mark — reading, the hosts paraphrase, as "Is that really what that's supposed to be?" The annotations let one "peer into the mind of Spencer W. Kimball" wrestling with the status quo in real time, years before the revelation came.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The notes predate his presidency by decades (rated: strong, documentary).** Collector Reid dates this set to the scriptures Kimball's wife Camilla gave him in **1938**, used until **1955** — meaning the marginalia were made when Kimball was a newly called apostle (called ~1943), roughly **23–40 years before** the 1978 revelation. "This is way before... that might have been before he was a prophet... He was given this set about five years before he's called to be an apostle."[^2]

2. **The annotated texts are the ban's own prooftexts (rated: strong, specific).** The question marks fall on "any of the scriptures in the [[Pearl of Great Price]] that were used to justify Blacks not getting the priesthood" — i.e., precisely the passages (in the Abraham/Moses material) that 19th- and 20th-century leaders read as grounding the restriction. The "Negro?" note registers doubt about the very category being used. See [[Reading Abraham 1 Without the Priesthood-Ban Lens]].

3. **The hosts read it as revelation-in-preparation, not heterodoxy (rated: moderate, devotional).** "He's questioning the status quo in real time. What's cool is that's how revelation works." And: "It's like God's preparing him." The marginalia are offered as evidence that the 1978 revelation grew out of a long personal wrestle, harmonizing with the broader LDS frame that [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets|God works through fallible prophets]] who grow into their callings. *Steelman:* a critic could object that a private question mark from the 1940s is thin evidence of doctrinal doubt — a question mark is ambiguous, and an apostle could mark uncertainty about an *interpretation* without rejecting the ban itself; the collector is reading intent into a glyph. The LDS reply: the note is not load-bearing as proof of policy, only as a window into a devotional and intellectual openness that later bore fruit.

4. **It complements, not contradicts, the "ban was the development" thesis (rated: contextual).** This claim concerns Kimball's *interior questioning* of the ban's prooftexts; it sits alongside but is distinct from the historical argument that [[The Priesthood Ban Was the Development Not Its Reversal|the ban itself was a later development]] absorbed from surrounding Protestantism. Together they frame 1978 as the un-development of an absorbed error, foreshadowed in a future prophet's own margins.

## Best Quotes

> "Any of the scriptures in the Pearl of Great Price that were used to justify Blacks not getting the priesthood? Question mark. ... He's trying to figure it out."[^1]

> "Negro question mark? ... He goes, 'Is that it? Is that really what that's supposed to be?' ... So he's questioning the status quo in real time. What's cool is that that's how revelation works."[^1]

> "It's like God's preparing him, you know what I mean? ... I got goosebumps."[^1]
