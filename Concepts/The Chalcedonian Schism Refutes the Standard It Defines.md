# The Chalcedonian Schism Refutes the Standard It Defines

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] turns the Chalcedonian Definition (451) — "fully God and fully human in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation," the statement most creedal Christians treat as definitive on Christ's nature — into evidence *against* its own use as a universal test of Christian legitimacy. Chalcedon "produced an immediate permanent schism that has never been resolved across 1,500 years." The Coptic Church of Egypt, the Ethiopian Orthodox Church, the Armenian Apostolic Church, and the Syriac Orthodox Church all rejected it and were cut off from Rome and Constantinople.[^1] These are not peripheral: the Coptic Church traces to the apostolic era and the Ethiopian has been continuously present since the 4th century — together "tens of millions of Christians who have maintained unbroken apostolic traditions" while describing Christ's nature in non-Chalcedonian terms. The argument is explicitly **structural, not Christological**: "If the Chalcedonian definition functions as the universal definition of Christian legitimacy, then these three communities — which actually *predate* Chalcedon, trace their origins to the apostolic era, and have never accepted its formula — are not Christian communities by that very standard. The conclusion reveals the basic inadequacy of the standard, not the churches."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The disqualified communities predate the standard (rated: strong).** Arnold's leverage is temporal priority: a definition cannot be the timeless apostolic baseline if applying it excludes churches *older than the definition itself* that trace to the apostolic age. "A definition of Christianity that rules out some of the oldest continuously practicing Christian communities in the world is not really describing the original faith. It's describing a fourth and fifth century settlement that a significant portion of ancient Christianity never accepted and still doesn't."[^3] This is the same demotion-from-baseline move as [[The Creeds Cannot Be the Neutral Baseline - The Circularity of Creedal Disqualification]], applied to Chalcedon rather than Nicaea, and it parallels how the [[The Filioque Was a Later Addition That Helped Cause the Great Schism|filioque]] later fractured even the Chalcedonian churches.

2. **20th-c. ecumenical dialogue suggests the split was a terminology dispute (rated: moderate-to-strong).** Joint statements between Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, and Oriental Orthodox theologians concluded the 451 split "may have been rooted in a philosophical and linguistic misunderstanding rather than a genuine theological disagreement." If so, "the council that settled Christ's nature definitively produced a 1,500-year fracture between some of Christianity's oldest communities over a terminology dispute."[^4] That a *terminological* misunderstanding could permanently sunder apostolic-era churches underscores how much the creedal project depends on contested philosophical vocabulary — connecting to the broader Hellenization thesis. Arnold stresses "that's the record, not my record, but the actual record that creedal Christianity's own historians have produced," citing [[Lewis Ayres]] for the period.

3. **Steelman: the explicit non-claim (rated: fair — Arnold pre-empts the obvious rebuttal).** Arnold is careful: "I'm not arguing that these churches are theologically correct in their non-Chalcedonian Christology. That's a separate question entirely. The argument here is structural."[^5] **The creedal reply** would be that the Oriental Orthodox are *miaphysite*, not heretical Nestorians, and that post-1990 Christological agreements show the churches were "always" essentially orthodox — i.e., Chalcedon's substance held and only words divided them. But that reply *concedes* Arnold's point: if the formula's words could exclude orthodox apostolic churches for 1,500 years, the words cannot be the neutral instrument of legitimacy critics treat them as. *Rating: the structural argument stands regardless of who is Christologically correct; the steelman strengthens rather than rebuts it.* See [[Ephesus and Chalcedon Defined the Two Natures of Christ]] for the doctrinal content the vault treats elsewhere.
