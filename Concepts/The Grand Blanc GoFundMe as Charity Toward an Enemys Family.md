# The Grand Blanc GoFundMe as Charity Toward an Enemy's Family

## The Claim
After a gunman attacked a Latter-day Saint meetinghouse near Grand Blanc, Michigan, a group of Latter-day Saint media figures in the [[Ward Radio]] text thread launched a GoFundMe for the shooter's *own* family — knowing the son has a chronic illness — and Latter-day Saint donors raised, in [[Jacob Hansen]]'s telling, "well over $200,000." [^1] Hansen presents this not as a public-relations stunt but as the lived content of the faith: "at the heart of our religion is loving other people and following the person who loved us first." [^2] The episode functions as an argument-by-fruits — a concrete, datable demonstration that the love commanded in [[John 13.34|John 13:34]]–[[John 13.35|John 13:35]] ("a new commandment... that ye love one another, as I have loved you") is being enacted toward the family of an enemy, which is precisely the kind of charity the "are Mormons even Christian?" rhetoric claims the tradition lacks.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The recipients are the enemy's family, not the victims.** The fund was raised for the family of the man who killed worshippers — "they deserve help like any other innocent person does," organizer Dave Butler told Fox News, noting "I don't believe those kids are guilty." [^3] *Rating: strong;* directing charity toward the perpetrator's dependents, rather than only the bereaved, is the costly, counterintuitive form of love that distinguishes enemy-love from ordinary in-group generosity.

2. **The donors, not the personalities, are the story.** Hansen insists "the real heroes here aren't us... it's these individual donors that were giving, you know, $10, $20 here and there." [^1] He reads donation messages: a widow's relative whose great-aunt's helper was killed writes, "as a fellow veteran, I know he dealt with the horrors of war and PTSD... No hate, just love and prayers" [^4]; another simply quotes the hymn *Where Can I Turn for Peace*. [^5] *Rating: strong;* the grassroots, small-dollar, message-laden character makes the charity hard to dismiss as orchestrated optics.

3. **It models "as I have loved you," not earned forgiveness.** Hansen ties the act to the *manner* of Christ's love: "he says as I have loved you. The thing is, we don't deserve, we don't earn Christ's forgiveness and love. He gives it freely." [^6] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it grounds the gesture theologically in unmerited grace ([[John 13.34|John 13:34]]) rather than mere humanitarian sentiment, connecting fruit to doctrine.

4. **The breadth of support undercuts the sectarian charge.** Hansen notes "a lot of ex-Mormons even... have come in and supported this. There's been people that aren't of our faith... that have jumped in and helped." [^7] *Rating: moderate;* the cross-boundary participation weakens any framing of the effort as tribal self-promotion and strengthens the read of it as recognizable Christian charity (cf. [[John 13.35|John 13:35]], "by this shall all men know").

**Steelman of the skeptic.** A critic might say a viral fundraiser proves only that a large, affluent, well-networked community can move money quickly, and that charity does not validate theology — fruit and doctrine are separable (see [[Fruit Versus Theology - Are They Separable]]). The claim does not need fruit to *prove* doctrine true; it needs fruit only to *rebut* the specific accusation that Latter-day Saints are sub-Christian in love. As an answer to "you're not Christians," enemy-directed charity is exactly on point — and it lands against the backdrop that the same week's anti-Mormon animus could indict no comparable Mormon *conduct* at all (see [[Extreme Anti-Mormonism Is Reactionary Hatred for Belief Alone]]).
