# The Word of God Was Never Confined to the Written Word

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Sola scriptura holds that the written Scripture is the sole infallible rule of faith. [[Trent Horn]]'s positive conclusion is that, because our confidence that the *written* word of God is the word of God rests on the *unwritten* word of God (sacred tradition), "God's revelation was never confined to the written word of God" — and therefore sola scriptura is false.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The catechetical analogy: the single Word needs human sentences — rate: moderate
Citing the Catechism (the one Word God speaks is Christ), Horn notes humans cannot grasp this single perfect self-utterance directly, "so we need God's word broken down into human constructs like sentences for us to better understand this divine reality."[^2] Scripture is that breaking-down — genuinely the word of God in written form — but "our knowledge of these sentences in scripture being the word of God does not come from scripture alone."[^2] This restates the epistemic dilemma of [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]] as a positive theology of revelation.

### 2. From "gradual recognition" to "tradition" — rate: moderate
Because identifying Scripture as the word of God was, on Horn's patristic survey, a *gradual recognition* (see [[Calling Scripture the Word of God Is a Late Patristic Development]]), Horn infers it "represented a gradual recognition of a sacred tradition about God's revelation."[^3] Parallel to the canon: "just as we know which human writings are scripture through the sacred tradition of the canon… we know that scripture is the word of God through sacred tradition as well."[^3] **Steelman of the Protestant:** "recognition develops over time" does not entail "an infallible tradition is the source"; doctrinal articulation can mature while the *ground* remains Scripture's own divine character. Horn's argument needs the further premise that an *infallible* result (Scripture is infallibly the word of God) requires an *infallible* recognizer — which a Protestant can deny by accepting only providential, fallible recognition.

### 3. The Vatican II triad as the resolution — rate: framing
Horn closes with [[Dei Verbum]]: the magisterium "is not above the word of God, but serves it"; and "sacred tradition, sacred scripture, and the teaching authority of the church… are so linked and joined together that one cannot stand without the others."[^4] The reliance on the *unwritten* word for confidence in the *written* word is the wedge that defeats "Scripture alone." See [[Scripture as Word of God Is Itself a Doctrine of Sacred Tradition]] and [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]] for the same structural move from a non-Catholic (LDS) angle.

### 4. The honest limit — rate: candor
Horn explicitly hedges: his patristic results are "tentative," and his positive case is an *inference to the best explanation*, not a demonstration. The strength of the conclusion therefore tracks the strength of the earlier exegetical and lexical sections rather than standing on its own.[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Our reliance on the unwritten word of God for our confidence in the written word of God means that God's revelation was never confined to the written word of God. And so sola scriptura is false."[^1]

> "Just as we know which human writings are scripture through the sacred tradition of the canon of scripture which represents an infallible rule of faith beyond scripture, we know that scripture is the word of God through sacred tradition as well."[^3]

> "This teaching office or magisterium is not above the word of God, but serves it, teaching only what has been handed on." — Dei Verbum, as quoted[^4]

> "Sacred tradition, sacred scripture, and the teaching authority of the church… are so linked and joined together that one cannot stand without the others." — Dei Verbum, as quoted[^4]
