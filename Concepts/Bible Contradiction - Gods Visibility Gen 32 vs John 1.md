# Bible Contradiction — God's Visibility (Gen 32:30 vs John 1:18)

## The Contradiction

The Bible contains two irreconcilable statements about whether God can be seen:

- **Genesis 32:30:** "I have seen God face to face, and my life is preserved" — Jacob claims a direct, face-to-face encounter with God and survives.
- **John 1:18:** "No man hath seen God at any time" — an absolute, unqualified negation of any human seeing God, ever.

A Christian apologist (the content creator being responded to in this video) presents this as a contradiction that disproves the Bible.[^1] Dan McClellan agrees it IS a contradiction — but argues this is because the biblical texts genuinely reflect different, evolving conceptions of God's nature, not because someone misread a passage.

## Supporting Evidence — Why This IS a Genuine Contradiction

### 1. The Hebrew Bible Presents a Corporeal, Visible God

The earliest Hebrew Bible texts depict God as anthropomorphic, corporeal, male-presenting, and visible. God "walks around and enjoys a succulent Chinese meal with Abraham and with others" — Genesis 18 depicts Yahweh eating with Abraham in physical, embodied form.[^2] This is not an isolated passage. Multiple texts present God with a body that can be seen, touched, and interacted with physically. See [[Anthropomorphic God in the Hebrew Bible]].

### 2. The "Face" Texts Mean What They Say

In Genesis 32:30, Jacob declares he has seen God "face to face" (*panim el-panim*) — the same idiom applied to Moses in Exodus 33:11 and Deuteronomy 34:10. In the Hebrew Bible's own idiom, "face" (*panim*) is where all the glory radiates from, and to see God's face IS to encounter the full presence of God. McClellan states: "you do have people who look directly at God's face. That is the full essence of God."[^3]

### 3. The Hebrew Bible Contains Internal Tensions on Visibility

Exodus 33:20-23 says no one can see God's face and live, yet the same book says Moses "spoke face to face with God like one speaks to their friend" (Exodus 33:11). Jacob sees God's face and survives. Other figures "fear they're going to die because they've seen God and they don't or are amazed that they have seen God and they have not died."[^4] The tension exists within the Hebrew Bible itself, not merely between OT and NT.

### 4. John 1:18 Has No "Limited Essence" Carve-Out

"Absolutely nothing in the New Testament suggests that people saw God, but just not the full send God. That's not anywhere in the New Testament. In the New Testament, you cannot see God. Period. Doesn't matter how much of God's glory is there, you cannot see God. Period."[^5]

### 5. The Historical Reason: Greek Philosophy Changed the Concept of God

The contradiction arose because the conception of God's nature changed over time. Early Israel understood God as anthropomorphic and visible; later, under Greek philosophical influence, God became immaterial, invisible, and "without body, parts, or passions." The NT authors inherit this later conception. "You go from God is a human-shaped male-presenting corporeal entity who walks around... to, it's dangerous to look at God because God's face is so gloried... to, okay, God cannot be seen... And then Greek philosophy shows up and says, 'No, God is invisible. To see God would violate what God is supposed to be.'"[^6] See [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]].

## The Scholarly Consensus — Overwhelming Agreement

McClellan emphasizes: "the scholars who study this and write about this are in widespread agreement that God had a body in the Hebrew Bible that was visible and corporeal and could be touched. And then that changed because the authoritative knowledge changed."[^7] He cites a seven-book scholarly bibliography spanning from 1964 through recent publications — all university-press or academic-press works representing the consensus view that God's corporeality in the Hebrew Bible is a settled scholarly finding.

## The Two Apologetic Objections (and Why They Fail)

### Objection A: Limited Essence Theophany
"They saw God, but in a limited form — not God's full essence. Like seeing sunlight without staring at the sun."[^8]

**Rebuttal:** This is a "separate third thing" rationalization not found anywhere in the biblical text. The Hebrew Bible contains people looking directly at God's face — which IS the full essence, the source of glory. The "limited essence" concept is imposed onto the text by interpreters who presuppose the Bible cannot contradict itself. See [[Limited Essence Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]].

### Objection B: Pre-Incarnate Christ Appearances
"Jacob and Moses actually saw the pre-incarnate Christ / the Word — not God the Father."

**Rebuttal:** Like the limited-essence argument, this invents a third entity (Christ as buffer) not supported by the texts. It is "also built on a foundation of the presupposition of univocality... it's begging the question because the conclusion is built into the premise."[^9] See [[Pre-Incarnate Christ Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]].

## Best Quotes

> "The notion that there were appearances of God where the glory was ratcheted down and they didn't see the full essence, which is a concept that you cannot find anywhere in the Hebrew Bible, is that separate third thing. That's the rationalization that you don't find anywhere in the Bible because you do have people who look directly at God's face. That is the full essence of God." [^3]

> "If your worldview requires you reject the notion that it's a contradiction, you're going to be intuitively pushed into accepting inferior data and rather silly argumentation, which is fallacious because it's begging the question — circular reasoning. And you're going to reject the notion that this is a contradiction. That's not because there is any reason or logic or evidence that supports that notion. It's because you cannot accept that it is a contradiction." [^10]
