# Secular Societies Score Better on Well-Being Metrics

## The Point
[[Sam Harris]] argues that the data refute the claim that religion is a necessary guarantor of social health. On "almost every measure of societal health, the least religious countries are better off than the most religious." The most atheistic nations on earth — Denmark, Sweden, Norway, the Netherlands — consistently outperform religious nations, and even the anomalously pious United States grows more secular as it grows more prosperous and stable.[^1] At minimum, he concludes, this "proves, conclusively, that a high level of unbelief need not lead to the fall of civilization."[^2]

## Evidence
Harris cites cross-national comparisons on life expectancy, infant mortality, crime, literacy, GDP, child welfare, economic and gender equality, health care, education, environmental protection, lack of corruption, political stability, and charity to poorer nations — on all of which the least religious countries rate better.[^1] He leans on the independent researcher Gregory Paul's "Successful Societies Scale" and "Popular Religiosity Versus Secularism Scale," which he reads as linking religious conviction to *societal insecurity*: religiosity is "strongly coupled to perceptions of societal insecurity," the poor are more religious than the rich both within and between nations, and U.S. religiosity correlates with high economic inequality (and even with racism).[^3] He concedes that "the mere correlation between societal dysfunction and religious belief does not tell us what the connection is between them," but insists the data abolish religion's claim to be the indispensable foundation of a healthy society.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "On almost every measure of societal health, the least religious countries are better off than the most religious." [^1]

> "These data should abolish the ever-present claim that religion is the most important guarantor of societal health. They also prove, conclusively, that a high level of unbelief need not lead to the fall of civilization." [^2]

> "Religiosity is strongly coupled to perceptions of societal insecurity." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's own most careful sentence is the concession that "correlation… does not tell us what the connection is," and the apologetic reply lives precisely there. (1) **Direction of causation is wide open.** Gregory Paul's own data are equally consistent with the reverse arrow Harris himself half-grants: where prosperity, security, and a strong welfare state already meet material needs, the *demand* for the consolations religion historically supplied falls — so secularity may be a luxury good of stability, not its cause. Scandinavia did not become safe and literate *because* it abandoned faith; it was overwhelmingly Lutheran while building the very institutions (literacy, hospitals, the idea of the equal dignity of every soul) it now coasts on. (2) **Aggregate national indices wash out the individual-level signal.** When the comparison is run between *individuals* rather than between rich and poor *nations*, religiously committed people in those same societies tend to report higher well-being, give more to charity, volunteer more, and (as the vault notes) show higher marital stability and "spiritual peace" — see [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]] and [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]]. The cross-national picture and the individual picture come apart, and Harris reports only the one that favors his thesis. (3) **The goods are not interchangeable.** Even granting every metric, well-ordered societies do not obviously supply what religion claims to — a ground for the *equal dignity* of persons, a community of belonging, a meaning that survives suffering and death; see [[Secularism Does Not Fill the Soul Like Religion]]. (4) **The metaethical hole remains.** This is the deepest point: every metric on Harris's list (welfare, equality, "child welfare," charity) is *already a moral value*. To call a society with higher equality "better off" presupposes the obligatory worth of persons that his naturalism still cannot ground — see [[The Moral Landscape - Grounding Value in Conscious Well-Being]], and on the theistic side [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] and [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]. Harris's data can show religion is not *necessary* for a stable polity; they cannot show that secular naturalism can justify the very goods by which it scores the win.
