# Impotence Rhetoric Is Idol Polemic Not Ontological Denial

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that when [[Psalm 115.4-7|Psalm 115:4–7]] describes the idols as having mouths that cannot speak, eyes that cannot see, ears that cannot hear, and so on, the cumulative list of incapacities "implies non-existence" — a mute, blind, deaf, immobile being is a being that is not real.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The impotence list describes the *idol*, not the deity (rating: strong)
Psalm 115 explicitly says "their idols are silver and gold, the work of human hands." The catalogue of sensory failures is predicated of the manufactured object, which by definition cannot speak or see — not of the deity the object indexes. It is the same idol polemic as [[2 Kings 19.18|2 Kings 19:18]] and 2 Chronicles 32. [^1] See [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]].

> "Psalm 115 is not saying well deeon and Marduk and molok and Bale are out there they just have all these handicaps this is a rhetorical way of saying what 2 Kings 19 said... these are the gods that are not Gods they're the work of men's hands" [^2]

### 2. "It's a rhetorical way of saying it's not real" — about the idol (rating: strong)
McClellan agrees the rhetorical *force* is "this thing isn't real," but the referent is the cult statue, not the deity. The point is "denying real deity to the idol not to the god that the idol indexes." [^3]

> "to say that an entity is mute blind deaf unfeeling immobile and Silent... is a rhetorical way of saying it's not real" [^1]

### 3. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's strongest point is that the impotence rhetoric *flows directly into* the narrative contrast where Yahweh "can show up and act" while the rivals do nothing — most vividly the Baal prophets at Carmel, who get "no voice and no one answered" (1 Kings 18), echoing Psalm 115's deaf/mute idols. This makes it look like the rivals simply are not capable of agency, i.e., do not exist. [^4] McClellan answers this with the territorial reading: Baal's silence at Carmel is not non-existence but the wrong storm deity being out of his purview. See [[Elijah Contest Is About Territorial Storm-Deity Purview]].
