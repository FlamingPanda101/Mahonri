# Free Will Defense of Hell — Rebutted

## The Defense Being Rebutted

Hell is the result of God **honoring human free choice**. Those in hell are
there by their own preference — as [[C.S. Lewis]] said, "The doors of hell
are locked from the inside." The purpose of hell is not to punish but to
**respect the autonomous choices** humans freely made in mortality. [^1]

## Rebuttal Part 1: Creation Ex Nihilo Negates Free Will

If God creates souls **from nothing** with perfect foreknowledge of what each
soul will do and continuously **sustains their attributes** in existence, then
those souls are not meaningfully free.

### The Video Game Character Analogy

A custom-built character in a video game:
- You provide power to the console (sustaining the universe)
- You select the character's attributes, properties, powers
- You adjust the sliders for quickness, strength, intelligence
- Your sustaining energy allows the character to navigate the game

Is this character **free in any meaningful sense**? If it "chooses" to reject
God, why didn't God create it with different attributes? Why not "turn up the
sliders" for devotion, righteousness, or faith in Christ? [^2]

God allegedly chooses to create individuals with divine foreknowledge that
they won't do what's required to avoid damnation — and sets the attributes
where they are. Either way, the free will of the individual is either
**non-existent or highly limited**. It would have been better to simply create
a soul destined for heaven rather than create one destined for hell. [^2]

## Rebuttal Part 2: The Parental Analogy

Even granting free will, the defense fails on a deeper moral level.

Suppose your young child decides "of his own free will" that he's sick of
being in a parent-child relationship and would like to enter the **Eternal
Conscious Torment Machine** — a device that will hijack his psychological
state and put him in permanent, unending psychological torment from which he
can never exit.

**Would it be moral for you, as a parent, to honor that choice?**

The answer is clearly no — especially considering:
- The child is acting on **limited knowledge and experience**
- The child may be misbehaved, but misbehavior doesn't warrant **eternal**
  consequences
- A finite misbehavior cannot justify allowing eternal horrifying effects
- Parents are sometimes justified in letting children face natural consequences
  to learn — but what goal does *eternal conscious torment* serve for a finite
  misbehavior? [^3]

"If free will is invoked, it seems that God is still not justified in allowing
us in our limited state of knowledge and experience to make choices that result
in such horrible and permanent consequences." [^3]

## The Deeper Point

The free will defense changes the framing from "God punishes" to "God honors
choice" — but this is a distinction without a moral difference when God **set
up the system** knowing exactly who would choose what, **created those people's
attributes**, and could have created them differently with different
propensities. The defender must explain why God created the soul at all if he
knew it was destined for eternal torment. [^2]
