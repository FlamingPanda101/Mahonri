# Are Latter-day Saints Christians

## The Claim
The dialogue reaches a notable convergence on terminology. [[Jacob Hansen]] concedes: "I actually think you're correct in saying we're not *credal* Christians. We don't hold to the same creedal notions about God... in that aspect, I'm fine with making that distinction." [^1] [[Gavin Ortlund]] agrees the LDS view is "pretty widely deviant of historic Christianity" and that, given the "gargantuan differences" over what God and reality are, being classed as a departure "should not be a surprising thing." [^2][^3] Hansen affirms the LDS "consider ourselves Christian" depending on definition, centering Christ as "the only means by which salvation can be attained." [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (and the remaining dispute)

1. **Agreement on the descriptive label "non-credal."** Both accept that LDS theology rejects the Nicene/classical creeds. *Rating: a clean, mutually-affirmed distinction* — it defuses the merely-verbal fight over the word "Christian."

2. **Shared Christ-centered self-description.** Hansen opens by quoting a church leader: "we baptize them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Ghost... leading them back to the presence of the Father through the ministry, atonement, and grace of his Son." [^5] *Rating: genuine common vocabulary* — both affirm Christ as the sole means of salvation, the resurrection, and the New Testament.

3. **The substantive disagreement persists under the shared words.** Ortlund's whole point is that affirming "Jesus is Lord" while meaning "fully divine in nature but not consubstantial, and the same *kind* of being as humans" is a different God (see [[Worshiping the Right Jesus - Created vs Uncreated]], [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]]). *Rating: this is why the label question is downstream* — the real issue is the content of "God," not the word "Christian."

4. **Steelman of Hansen's fairness complaint.** Hansen objects that questioning LDS *salvation* (not just the label) over "a theological understanding of a doctrine that many people struggle to understand themselves... seems a bit harsh." [^6] *Rating: emotionally and rhetorically strong;* Ortlund's reply is the duty-of-candor framing (see [[Trinity Is a First-Rank Salvation Issue]]) plus the claim that the disagreement is about identity, not technicality.
