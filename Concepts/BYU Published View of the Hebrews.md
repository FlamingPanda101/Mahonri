# BYU Published View of the Hebrews

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
**Objection:** *View of the Hebrews* is the "Mormon Necronomicon" — a dangerous book full of "dark secrets" that "disprove" the religion, which the LDS Church is conspiring to suppress and hide from members. (Jerald and Sandra Tanner popularized the "Necronomicon" framing.)[^1]

**Rebuttal:** The opposite is true. **BYU published its own edition of *View of the Hebrews*** (1990s), the Religious Studies Center hosts it free online, the Church History Library holds original copies, and Scripture Central archived it. You cannot simultaneously suppress a book and reprint it for everyone to read. The institutional behavior demonstrates how non-threatening the book actually is.[^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **BYU reprinted the supposedly forbidden book (rated: strong, documented).** "Ladies and gentlemen, BYU published its own edition of View of the Hebrews. That's how much not of a thing this is."[^2] The point of the edition: "just go read the book yourself. It's not a deep dark secret."
2. **The book is freely and widely available (rated: strong, documented).** It is "on the Religious Studies Center website for free," in the Church History Library, and in the Scripture Central archive — explicitly because "we're not worried about people reading this."[^3] A genuine cover-up would not promote access.
3. **Reception history shifted only because of Brodie, not because of danger (rated: moderate, historiographical).** LDS engagement moved from citing the book *positively* to *refuting plagiarism claims* only after [[Fawn Brodie]] made it an issue — addressed by the *Encyclopedia of Mormonism*, the *Ensign*, and Hugh Nibley.[^4] *Steelman:* a critic can argue the BYU edition is itself an apologetic maneuver — controlling the narrative by framing the reprint — and that "available now" doesn't refute earlier institutional reticence. Smoot's reply: the charge was never institutional suppression but textual dependence; making the full text universally accessible and inviting readers to judge for themselves is the strongest possible move *against* a side hiding something (see [[Joseph Smith Cited View of the Hebrews as Evidence]]).
