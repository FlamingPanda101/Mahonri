# 2 Maccabees 7:28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Being Rebutted)

Wes Huff asserted that "Within Jewish tradition, explicitly creation from nothing is clearly expressed in 2 Maccabees 7:28. 'Look at the heavens and the earth and see everything that is in them and recognize that God did not make them out of things that existed.'" He added that the Greek text "clarifies that God did not create out of pre-existing materials, contrasting sharply with Greek philosophical teaching."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

The scholarly consensus — sustained for over 50 years across multiple generations of scholars, including those who DEFEND creation ex nihilo — is that 2 Maccabees 7:28 does NOT teach creation out of nothing. The passage states that God did not make the world "out of things that existed" (Greek: οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων / ouk ex ontōn), which in the context of Hellenistic Jewish and Greek philosophical thought refers to creation from unformed, shapeless matter — not from absolute nothingness. The phrase "not out of things that existed" was a standard idiom for describing the ordering of pre-existent material substrate occupying what Greek philosophy called "non-being."

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Gerhard May (1970s German / 1994 English): Flat Rejection

Gerhard May's foundational study *Creatio Ex Nihilo: The Doctrine of 'Creation out of Nothing' in Early Christian Thought* (German original mid-1970s, English translation 1994) includes an extended discussion of 2 Maccabees 7:28 and "flatly rejects the notion that it teaches creation out of nothing."[^2]

May is the standard scholarly reference on the development of the doctrine, and his rejection of 2 Maccabees 7:28 as a proof-text carries substantial weight.

### 2. Jonathan Goldstein (1984): Even a Defender Concedes

Jonathan Goldstein, who WANTED to defend creation ex nihilo, acknowledged in his Anchor Bible commentary on 2 Maccabees that the passage does not teach it:

> "Origen took 2 Maccabees 7:22-29 as the first unequivocal statement in scripture of the doctrine of creation ex nihilo. In my commentary to the passage, however, I prove that Origen was mistaken. Though the passage displays profound philosophical knowledge, it does not assert creation ex nihilo."[^3]

This is critically important: Goldstein is a DEFENDER of creation ex nihilo who would have preferred to find it here — but the text wouldn't cooperate.

### 3. David Winston (1984): Goldstein's Argument Doesn't Work

David Winston, in the same year and same journal (*Journal of Jewish Studies*), responded to Goldstein:

> "Winston provided a response that same year... pointing out that Goldstein's argument was not accurate and also agreeing that 2 Maccabees 7:28 does not indicate creation ex nihilo."[^4]

Goldstein later responded and had to concede: "You got me on a bunch of things there," acknowledging Winston had him on many points, though he tried to salvage portions of his case.[^5]

### 4. James Hubler (1995 Dissertation, University of Pennsylvania)

James Hubler's dissertation *Creatio, Matter, Creation, and the Body in Classical and Christian Philosophy through Aquinas* includes a lengthy discussion of 2 Maccabees 7:28, likewise rejecting it as a creation ex nihilo text.[^6]

### 5. Markus Bockmuehl (2012): The Consensus Articulated

Markus Bockmuehl, Professor at Oxford University, published in 2012 a study of creatio ex nihilo in Palestinian Judaism and early Christianity. He articulated the scholarly consensus clearly:

> "It is certainly true that frequently cited Septuagintal and New Testament passages which assert God's creation of what is seen from what is not seen, or things which are out of things which are not, should not be short-circuited into statements about creatio ex nihilo. Exegetes today are in widespread agreement on this point."[^7]

On 2 Maccabees 7:28 specifically:

> "2 Maccabees 7:28, for example, affirms not that God made the heavens and the earth out of nothing, merely that he made them not out of existing things. The writer applies this principle to human conception in the womb, which is clearly a case of God making human beings out of what is not a human being."[^7]

The contextual application to human conception is decisive: a baby in the womb is NOT made "out of nothing" — it is made out of biological materials that are "not a human being." The same principle applied to cosmic creation means God made the world out of materials that were "not a world" — unformed matter, not nothingness.

> "If God makes out of non-being the things that are, this need not be ex nihilo but merely his making out of shapelessness the things which have shape."[^8]

### 6. Gary Anderson (2018): The Softest Possible Defense

Gary Anderson, co-editor of *Creation Ex Nihilo: Origins, Development, Contemporary Challenges* (2018), takes the softest possible pro-ex-nihilo position — and STILL can't claim the text teaches it:

> "Let me begin with the Bible. The two most commented upon texts are Genesis 1:1 and 2 Maccabees 7:28. For many modern scholars, 2 Maccabees appears to be the better candidate of the two, for it seems to contain an explicit denial of the pre-existence of matter. 'Look at the heavens and the earth and see everything that is in them and recognize that God did not make them out of things that existed.' But as scholars have shown, the assertion that God did not make the world out of things that existed could have merely implied that he fashioned the world from unformed matter. For we have contemporary Greek evidence for the use of an almost identical idiom to describe the engendering of children by their parents."[^9]

Anderson's conclusion is the weakest possible endorsement:

> "This does not mean that the author of 2 Maccabees understood the term this way. At the same time, that possibility cannot be ruled out. As a result, this text fails as a decisive proof text for the doctrine. The most we can say is that 2 Maccabees is patient of the doctrine of creatio ex nihilo."[^9]

McClellan's gloss: "That's somebody who's coming to defend creation ex nihilo and taking as soft an approach as possible, but still saying it's not clear in this text."[^9]

### 7. The Greek Philosophical Context: "Non-Being" ≠ Nothing

The key phrase in 2 Maccabees 7:28 is οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων (*ouk ex ontōn*) — "not out of things that exist." In contemporary Greek philosophical usage, "non-being" (τὸ μὴ ὄν / *to mē on*) did not mean absolute nothingness. It referred to unformed, shapeless matter — the material substrate before it received form and function.

Aristotle described creation as bringing things "from non-being into being" (*Physics* 1.8, 191b) and destruction as taking them "from being to non-being" — yet [[Aristotle]] categorically rejected creation ex nihilo. For Aristotle, "non-being" was the privation of form in pre-existing matter, not the absence of matter itself.[^10]

McClellan explains:

> "This actually fits quite well into the existing Greek philosophical ideas about the creation of the world out of a material substrate that did not have form and function and was considered to occupy what is called non-being. And in fact Aristotle talks about the creation of things as a process of bringing them from non-being into being and then the destruction of things as a process of taking them from being to non-being. So this is par for the course when it comes to creation out of pre-existing material."[^10]

### 8. The Context in 2 Maccabees 7 Supports the Material-Substrate Reading

The broader passage (2 Maccabees 7:22–29) is a mother's speech to her son during the Maccabean martyrdoms. She says:

> "I do not know how you came into being in my womb. It was not I who gave you life and breath, nor I who set in order the elements within each of you. Therefore the Creator of the world, who shaped the beginning of humankind and devised the origin of all things, will in his mercy give life and breath back to you again." (2 Macc 7:22–23, NRSV)

The mother's argument is: God formed you in my womb from materials that were "not you" — and God formed the world from materials that were "not the world." The analogy presupposes pre-existing biological material (the mother's body), just as the cosmic application presupposes pre-existing material substrate.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

The strongest case for reading 2 Maccabees 7:28 as creation ex nihilo: The text says God made the world "not out of things that existed" (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων). The most natural reading of "things that exist" is "anything that exists" — i.e., God didn't use any pre-existing material at all. The mother's speech contrasts God's creative power with human limitations: she didn't make her son's body (God did), and God made the whole cosmos. The rhetorical force of the passage is to emphasize God's absolute creative sovereignty, which is most naturally understood as creation without pre-existing materials. Origen, reading the text in the 3rd century, understood it this way — and he was a native Greek speaker closer to the text's composition than modern scholars.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **The womb analogy works against ex nihilo.** The mother says God formed her son in her womb — a process that clearly uses pre-existing biological material. If the cosmic creation parallels this (as the text's structure suggests), it too uses pre-existing substrate.[^7]
2. **"Not out of things that existed" was standard philosophical idiom for ordering pre-existent matter.** [[Aristotle]] and other Greeks used "from non-being" language without meaning creation ex nihilo.[^10] The author of 2 Maccabees was philosophically literate and operating within this idiom.
3. **Origen's 3rd-century reading is anachronistic.** [[Origen]] read the text AFTER Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus had invented the doctrine of creation ex nihilo (late 2nd century) and retroactively imposed it on earlier texts. Origen was seeing the text through the lens they created.[^11]
4. **The consensus spans defenders and critics alike.** When scholars who WANT to find creation ex nihilo in 2 Maccabees (Goldstein, Anderson) nonetheless concede it's not clearly there, that's a powerful indicator.[^3][^9]
5. **The text's own language parsed carefully.** "Not out of things that existed" (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων) is NOT the same as "out of nothing" (ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων / ex ouk ontōn). The latter formulation — which WOULD explicitly state creation ex nihilo — only appears later, in Theophilus of Antioch and Irenaeus.[^12]
