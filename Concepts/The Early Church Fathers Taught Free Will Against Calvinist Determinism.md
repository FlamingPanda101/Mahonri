# The Early Church Fathers Taught Free Will Against Calvinist Determinism

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that the earliest post-apostolic writers — those "closest to the apostles" in time — uniformly taught libertarian free will, and that Calvinist determinism is a *later* development absent from the primitive church. He quotes [[Justin Martyr]] ("unless the human race have the power of avoiding evil and choosing good by free choice, they are not accountable for their actions"), [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (Matt 23:37 "set forth the ancient law of human liberty... God made man a free agent from the beginning... not by compulsion of God. For there is no coercion with God"), and [[Clement of Alexandria]] ("revolt is in our own power just as obedience is in our own power. Wherefore voluntary actions are judged"). [^1] [[James White]], who teaches church history, answers that Justin was a Greek philosopher who "continued wearing the Greek pallium his entire life" and "was not influenced almost at all by the Apostle Paul," so his soteriology carries no weight against Paul's Romans 9. [^2] White counters that [[Clement of Rome]] ("First Clement") "talks about the elect, talks about predestination," and that the Epistle to Diognetus teaches imputed righteousness — while granting these were "not the primary subject of almost any of the early writers." [^3]

## Evidence
- **Hansen's chain of citations is the affirmative's patristic core.** He claims "all the early church fathers if you go study them... it isn't until much later that they start to get towards these ideas," presenting free will as the consensus of the sub-apostolic age. [^1]
- **White's two-pronged rebuttal: discredit Justin, redirect to exegesis.** (1) Justin's Hellenism makes him a poor witness to Pauline theology; (2) the only authority that matters is "God's divine word," not "polls of Christians" or church fathers. [^2] White twice insists the resolution turns on exegeting Romans 9, not counting patristic noses.
- **The Matthew 23:37 sub-dispute.** Hansen reads Irenaeus reading "how often would I have gathered... and ye would not" as proof of resistible grace and human liberty. White replies that Irenaeus misread it: Matt 23:37 is "a judgment passage against the Jewish leadership," distinguishing the *leaders* (who obstruct) from the *people* (the object of Jesus' ministry) — so it says nothing about irresistible grace. [^4]
- **White's competing patristic witnesses.** He offers Clement of Rome on election/predestination and Diognetus on imputed righteousness, conceding these themes were not central to the fathers — which cuts both ways, since it concedes the fathers did not foreground Calvinist soteriology. [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Unless the human race have the power of avoiding evil and choosing good by free choice, they are not accountable for their actions." — Hansen, quoting Justin Martyr [^1]

> "God made man a free agent from the beginning... not by compulsion of God. For there is no coercion with God." — Hansen, quoting Irenaeus [^1]

> "Justin Martyr... was not deeply influenced by Pauline soteriology and theology. I read Romans chapter 3 and I believe Romans chapter 3, and therefore my theology is significantly more influenced by Paul than Justin Martyr." — White [^2]

## LDS Connection
The patristic appeal is a load-bearing plank for the Restoration's broader thesis that primitive Christianity differed from the later creedal synthesis (cf. [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]]). On free will specifically, the early fathers' insistence that "there is no coercion with God" coheres with the LDS doctrine of inviolable agency rooted in the **uncreated, co-eternal intelligence** ([[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]; [[Coerced Love Is Not Love - Agency Against Irresistible Grace]]) — and Irenaeus' reading of the Fall as childlike growth rather than total corruption ([[Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth Not Corruption]]) directly opposes the total-depravity premise of TULIP. The honest scorecard: White is right that quoting fathers is not exegeting Paul, and that a few writers (Clement of Rome) used election language — so the argument proves the *direction* of the early consensus, not a knock-down. But White's move to discredit Justin as "too Greek" cuts against the creedal project too, since the later Trinitarian and impassibility doctrines the Reformed tradition inherits are *far* more indebted to Greek philosophy than Justin's free-will teaching was. The LDS apologist can press that asymmetry: if Hellenism disqualifies Justin on agency, it disqualifies much of Nicaea on metaphysics.
