# Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Harmonizing apologists present their reconciliations as the *conclusion* of honest inquiry into the text — "we looked, and there is no contradiction." The implied posture is neutral investigation that happens to vindicate inerrancy.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The real audience is the harmonizer, not the critic (rated: moderate–strong; psychological claim)
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the move is not aimed at convincing him, peer review, or "people capable of abstract or critical thought." It is aimed inward — at the believer who "really, really, really" needs the dogma to remain *not impossible*. The reconciliation functions as **reassurance**, not demonstration.

### 2. Commitment precedes the evidence (rated: strong, as a structural point)
The tell is the direction of reasoning: the conclusion (no contradictions) is fixed in advance by dogma, and reconciliations are generated afterward to protect it. This is **motivated reasoning**, and it is why the standard is set at "not impossible" rather than "best supported" — only an unfalsifiable bar can guarantee the predetermined result (see [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]]).

### 3. It opts out of the relevant epistemic community (rated: moderate)
By targeting self-reassurance rather than peer review, the project removes itself from the corrective mechanisms — critique, replication, falsification — that distinguish inquiry from apologetics. The conclusion is therefore insulated rather than tested.

### Steelman of the opposing view
This is the weakest-to-attack and most contestable of McClellan's points, because it is partly an **ad hominem about motive**, and motives are hard to read and vary across individuals. A fair defender notes: (a) plenty of credentialed scholars affirm inerrancy and engage peer review (e.g., evangelical biblical scholarship has its own journals and standards); (b) *everyone* reasons from prior commitments, including critics who presuppose naturalistic composite authorship; and (c) a harmonization can be both motivated and correct. The charitable reconstruction of McClellan's point is therefore not "harmonizers are dishonest" but "when the operative standard is *not impossible*, the practice is structurally indistinguishable from dogma-protection regardless of sincerity" — which keeps the critique tied to method rather than to hearts.

### Scholarly context
The observation parallels the broader distinction between **confessional** and **critical** biblical study: the former begins from doctrinal commitments (inerrancy, canon) it treats as non-negotiable; the latter brackets them. McClellan's claim is that the harmonizing definition of "contradiction" smuggles the confessional commitment into what is presented as a neutral test.
