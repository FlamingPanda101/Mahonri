# Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Horses are the single most-cited Book of Mormon anachronism: New World horses went
extinct millennia before 600 BC, and domestic horses only return with the Spanish
(post-1500 AD), so any horse in the Book of Mormon must be a 19th-century mistake.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Native horse bones dated to Book of Mormon times

A paper in the *Texas Journal of Science* reported horse bones from central Mexico.
The collagen was too deteriorated to date the bones directly, but carbon material in
the same stratigraphic layers dated to **1600 BC, 1500–400 BC, 500–400 BC, and ~AD
70–225** — squarely within the Book of Mormon window. (Notably, horses are not
mentioned in the Book of Mormon after the first century AD.)[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (real published data, but indirect dating, single study, low-tier journal).

### 2. These are native, not Spanish, horses

Bone analysis identified the species as horses native to the Americas, not imported
Spanish horses — ruling out the standard explanation that intrusive Spanish bones
sank into deeper layers. The site is excavated by paleontologists (it is rich in
animal remains, not a contested archaeological zone), reducing the disturbance
worry.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (species ID addresses the main alternative explanation).

### 3. The stratigraphy was clean

The researchers reported very clean stratigraphy with no evidence of disruption, and
the carbon dates show a smooth progression (older below, younger above) rather than
the inversions a disturbed site produces. Lead author [[Wade Miller]], a retired LDS
geologist, confirmed to Rappleye directly there was no evidence of disturbance.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (clean dates are corroborating, but author's affiliation is a flagged caveat).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The strongest objection is honest and Rappleye grants it: this is **one data point**
against an entrenched paradigm, the *bones themselves* were not dated, the journal is
low-tier, and a genuine horse culture should leave abundant material culture (saddles,
horseshoes, horses in Maya carvings) that is entirely absent pre-Columbus. He
therefore refuses to call horses "resolved" — only "trending," with additional
unpublished pre-Columbian (post-Book of Mormon) radiocarbon dates from independent
labs hinting that the full story is more complicated than the extinction paradigm
allows.

## Best Quotes

> "Horses I would put in the trending category... there was a paper published in the Texas Journal of Science... the carbon datings for layers that had horse bones in them came out to 1600 BC... between like 70 and 225 AD. That's our timeline."[^2]

> "They're not Spanish horses. They're native horses. And it's really hard for horse bones from an earlier layer to somehow end up in a higher layer."[^3]

> "I'm not ready to say we've resolved horses... but there's some really interesting data here... that makes me say I'm not ready to close the door on the possibility there were horses either."[^4]
