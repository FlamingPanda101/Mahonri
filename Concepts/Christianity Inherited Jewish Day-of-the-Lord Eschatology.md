# Christianity Inherited Jewish Day-of-the-Lord Eschatology

## The Point

Early Christian eschatology did not begin with Jesus's resurrection; it grew directly out of the eschatological hopes already prominent in Second Temple Judaism. [[Nicholas J. Frederick]] stresses that most of the first generation of converts came out of Judaism — Jesus was a Jew who ministered primarily to Jews — and they "brought with them their own Jewish preconceptions into their understanding and interpretation of the Christian message." [^1] The Hebrew prophets had taught that Jehovah had "a grand destiny in mind for the house of Israel," a hope that intensified after the fall of the northern kingdom (722 BC) and the Babylonian conquest of Judah (586 BC). Survivors "envisioned a final, decisive 'day of the Lord,' when Jehovah would return, conquer the wicked, and vindicate Israel."

## Evidence

1. **Postexilic prophets supplied the imagery later Christians reused.** [[Zechariah 8.4-5|Zechariah]] envisioned old men and women sitting peacefully in Jerusalem's streets with children at play; [[Isaiah 65.17-18|Isaiah]] predicted "new heavens and a new earth"; [[Ezekiel 47.8|Ezekiel]] prophesied of healing waters flowing from a new temple; and [[Joel 2.30-31|Joel]] foretold "blood, and fire, and pillars of smoke" with the sun darkened and the moon turned to blood. [^1]
2. **The hope was a matter of *when*, not *if*.** "For many Jews at the time of Jesus, wearied from centuries of persecution and yearning for their redemption, divine vindication was a matter of when rather than if. And many hoped that when would be soon." [^1]
3. **The reconciliation problem was acute.** For Jews who believed in Jesus, the question became how to reconcile their inherited picture of the end with the fact that "Rome [was] still in charge," Israel's enemies went unpunished, and "the earth seem[ed] to continue as if nothing important had actually occurred." [^2]

## Best Quotes

> "Eschatological ideas were already prominent within the Judaism of Jesus's time, mostly in the writings of the Hebrew prophets." [^1]

> "Yes, Jehovah had already descended and established his kingdom on the earth, but that kingdom was somehow not yet fully realized, and many early Christians found themselves caught up in this tension between already and not yet." [^2]
