# Eternal Light-Darkness Dualism Gives Evil a Positive Ontology

#type/claim

## The Claim
When [[Jacob Hansen]] refines the LDS eternal substrate as not "mere chaos" but "**light and darkness, chaos and order**" — "the nature of inherent reality itself," within which minds (including God) "choose the light" — [[Christian Mario]] presses this as **eternal cosmological dualism**, the strongest of his charges. If light and darkness (good and evil) are two co-eternal, self-existent principles "that reality is grounded on," then: (1) "that's not creation, that's dualism," and dualism "cannot explain unity," since "if light and darkness are equally ultimate, there's no reason one should triumph over the other"; (2) evil is thereby given "a positive existence... its own being," an *ontology* of evil; and (3) a God who must *separate* light from darkness "is not omnipotent" — "a god who shares eternity with darkness." Mario's sharpest sub-argument is from divine perfection: "**desire implies lack**. A being who desires order lacks the very perfection he's supposed to embody... in Orthodox theology, God does not desire order. His being is order."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (Orthodox / classical-theist side).** (1) *Dualism cannot ground unity or victory.* If good and evil are equiprimordial and mutually defining, then "each depends on the other to define itself" — "you can't know light without darkness" — so neither is self-explanatory and "the moment they're defined by [each other] they stop being ultimate." Worse, nothing in the metaphysics guarantees the light wins; the assurance of God's final triumph becomes groundless.[^2] (2) *Evil-as-being is incoherent.* Mario presses Hansen to admit evil has "a real eternal being of its own," then runs the privation argument's mirror: if evil has positive being it "either causes itself or receives being from something else," reintroducing the very dependence the LDS system tried to escape. (Classical theism, by contrast, treats evil as *privatio boni* — a lack, not a substance.) (3) *Desire implies lack.* The crown of the argument targets Hansen's claim that "inherent in the nature of God... is a desire for creating order." Mario: to desire order is to picture "a God who finds himself in disorder and wishes to fix it," so "order exists as an ideal outside of him which he tries to achieve" — "a law above the lawgiver," an "ideal above the divine." A God who *lacks* and *strives* is subordinated to the standard he strives toward, hence not the ground of order but its servant. This dovetails with classical *impassibility* (God is not acted upon; cf. [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]]).

**Steelman (LDS reply — Hansen).** Hansen partly *embraces* the framework and offers an agency-based reading. (1) *Light/darkness are undifferentiated until separated.* The two are not two warring lumps but "undifferentiated," and "we're the ones who separate the light from the darkness." The "great work of creation is actually the distinction of things into good and evil," "the great differentiation" — the separating of sheep from goats, gathering "the children of the light." Evil "doesn't really have any power until it's divided." So Hansen denies the standoff: separation, driven by agency, is exactly how light comes to triumph (he cites 2 Nephi 2; cf. [[2 Nephi 2.11]] on opposition in all things). (2) *Constrained God is a feature, not a bug.* "I reject that God is omnipotent in a radical sense" — a God who works within eternal conditions is precisely the LDS theodicy that dissolves the problem of evil where classical theism cannot (see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]], [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]], and the organizer model in [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer]]). (3) *Impassibility is the heresy.* The "desire implies lack" move presupposes the impassible *actus purus* God; Latter-day Saints reject that as a Hellenized distortion of the relational, feeling God of scripture (see [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]]). A God who *wills* order out of love is not deficient but personal. (4) *Intellectual candor.* Hansen repeatedly flags that this is "still formulating" in his own mind, that some of it is "not part of official LDS thought" but "my own philosophical takes," and says he is "open to" evil having ontology without staking his position on it — a steelman should not over-commit him to a settled doctrine of eternal evil. The exchange ends with Mario judging the view collapses "into absurdity" and Hansen unpersuaded; the crux is whether opposition (light/darkness) is *equiprimordial dualism* (Mario) or a *separable, agency-resolved* polarity within a fundamentally good order (Hansen).

## Best Quotes
> "If light and darkness are both eternal you've divided being into two uncreated substances that eternally oppose each other. And also you've now kind of given evil an ontology too. It has a positive existence in LDS." — Christian Mario [^3]
