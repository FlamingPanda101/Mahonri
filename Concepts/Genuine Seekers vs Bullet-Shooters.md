# Genuine Seekers vs Bullet-Shooters

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] teaches a triage rule: **a person who is genuinely seeking is not shooting bullets** — they have honest questions and deserve patient answers ("dodge bullets" / explain history and context). A person who is *attacking* is not really seeking, so you "disarm" them by challenging their presuppositions instead of feeding them rebuttals.[^1] You can usually tell which is which "in about 10 or 15 seconds."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The distinction is pastorally sound — rate: strong
Carroll's rule tracks a real difference between *inquiry* and *polemic*. A sincere family member "genuinely struggling" gets careful, contextual answers (Book of Abraham, polygamy, etc.); a hostile street critic at the Easter pageant gets the presuppositional disarm.[^3] An attendee crisply restates it: "if they're attacking, they're not actually seeking, so you go and disarm them; if they have a genuine question, properly explain."[^4] This is good conversational ethics and aligns with [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]].

### 2. Worked example: hostile ex-Mormon content — rate: moderate, uncharitable edge
Carroll names ex-LDS YouTubers (Bill Reel, Radio Free Mormon, Alyssa Grenfell, "Nemo the Mormon") as "bullet-shooters" who "don't care about the answers... gish gallop and throw mud... and make a lot of money doing it," and says "all the big ex-Mormon YouTubers are atheists," so the move is to "show how incoherent atheism is."[^5] **Steelman of the critics:** painting *all* prominent ex-LDS voices as bad-faith atheist grifters is itself a presuppositional, motive-impugning move — the very "I'm right so you're wrong" pattern the course warns against (here: "they disagree, therefore they're insincere"). Some critics raise genuine historical/documentary issues that deserve the "dodge bullets" treatment Carroll reserves for friends. The triage rule is sound; the blanket categorization of named individuals is the weakest, least charitable part of the section.

### 3. "Genuine" is hard to read and self-serving if misused — rate: caution
Because the rule lets the apologist *decide* whether an interlocutor is sincere, it can become a way to avoid hard questions by relabeling a tough questioner as a "bullet-shooter." Carroll's own counsel elsewhere — don't add your own presuppositions, don't be a jerk, allow the Spirit to work[^6] — is the necessary corrective. See [[Spirit Over Argument - The Limits of Apologetics]]. Used honestly (read intent, then respond appropriately), the rule is wise; used defensively, it becomes an exit hatch.

## Best Quotes
> "If the person is genuine, they're not shooting bullets."[^7]

> "If they're attacking, they're not actually seeking, and so you go and disarm them. They have a genuine question — important — so then properly explain."[^4]

> "They don't care about the answers. They're just going to gish gallop and throw mud at the wall until something sticks."[^5]
