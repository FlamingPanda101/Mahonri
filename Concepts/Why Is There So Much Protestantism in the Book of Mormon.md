# Why Is There So Much Protestantism in the Book of Mormon

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Aaron Shafovaloff]] presses an intertextuality challenge: "Why is there so much Protestantism in the Book of Mormon? Why weren't ancient Native Americans discussing controversies that looked very peculiar to 19th-century folk Protestant sensibilities?" He points evangelicals to Mormonism Research Ministry's *BookofMormon.com* color-coded 1830 text, where (he argues) Joseph Smith "is essentially taking at times almost like a copy-paste from the New Testament" — First Nephi quoting Revelation or John — even when "translating" texts set ~600 BC, before those New Testament books existed.[^1] The "big deal," he says, is that "it really looks like Smith was appropriating in some direct fashion the King James Old and New Testament."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The anachronism is textual, not archaeological (rated: strong as a *prima facie* problem).** Unlike debates over horses or steel, this objection is internal to the text: KJV New Testament phrasing and 19th-century Protestant theological concerns appear in a record claiming pre-exilic Israelite origin. Shafovaloff invites readers to "scroll down to influences" on the site and see that "Joseph Smith had his favorites" among biblical books.[^1] See [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Variants Are Not Copied from the KJV]] for the parallel (and contested) Isaiah case.

2. **Shafovaloff fairly catalogs the LDS explanations (rated: strong, intellectually honest).** Rather than strawmanning, he surveys three in-house LDS responses:
   - **Royal Skousen's early-modern-English thesis** — the Book of Mormon's English reflects a pre-Joseph-Smith translation in the early modern (Reformation-era) period, dictated by Joseph through the seer stone as an "already translated work."[^2]
   - **Spirit-world pre-translation** — "maybe this translation was done in the spirit world already."[^3]
   - **[[Blake Ostler]]'s creative-participant / expansion theory** — Joseph Smith is given "creative license as a creative participant... to add his own stories, his own thoughts," which (an LDS scholar arguing) explains "why we have so much 19th-century Protestant theology and thinking and sensibilities... woven into the text."[^3]

3. **Skousen clarification (rated: a fair-play correction).** When Shafovaloff initially framed Skousen as holding Joseph "was rendering this from some other text," [[Jacob Hansen]] interjected that this misrepresents Skousen: Skousen holds the *finally rendered English* reflects an earlier English, not that Joseph possessed a physical source text. Shafovaloff accepted the correction and re-stated his own claim (a supernaturally supplied already-translated work) and apologized "if I've misrepresented Skousen."[^4] The exchange models careful sourcing on a contested point.

4. **Steelman of Hansen's reply — dynamic translation (rated: strong, the standard LDS answer).** Hansen concedes the lexicon freely: "I have no doubt that Joseph Smith was using the lexicon that was familiar to himself when he rendered the text." He denies a "tight word-for-word translation" and affirms a "functional dynamic translation" — so KJV phrasing is the *target language Joseph reached for*, not proof of fabrication.[^5] On this view the presence of familiar Protestant English is exactly what a 19th-century *revelatory rendering* (not a transcript) would look like. See [[Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism]] and [[Read the Book of Mormon as a Translated Ancient Text]].

5. **Hansen redirects to the deeper issue (rated: moderate — changes the battlefield).** Hansen argues the intertextuality dispute is downstream of the canon question: "ultimately we have to confront this idea of a closed canon and that revelation has ceased. Because if revelation has not ceased, then you're in a very different kind of set of circumstances."[^6] If God can give binding revelation through a living prophet, a dynamically rendered text using KJV idiom is unremarkable; the objection only bites if one *presupposes* a closed canon. See [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]] and [[The LDS Tradition Emptied the Canon Rather Than Expanded It]].

6. **Unresolved crux (rated: genuine standoff).** The critic says dynamic-translation is unfalsifiable cover for anachronism; the apologist says the critic assumes a transcript model the LDS never held. Whether KJV intertextuality is *evidence of 19th-century authorship* or *an artifact of revelatory translation* depends on priors about whether revelation is ongoing.

## Best Quotes

> "It really looks like Smith was appropriating in some direct fashion the King James Old and New Testament, even when he's translating texts that preceded the New Testament."[^1]
