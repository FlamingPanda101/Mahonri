# Hebraic-Indian theory

The **Hebraic-Indian theory** was the widely circulated early-modern and early-19th-century idea that the Native peoples of the Americas descend from ancient Israelites — often specifically the "ten lost tribes." It was a popular ethnographic and missionary speculation rather than scripture or scholarship, advanced in print by a string of writers across the 1700s and early 1800s. It was already waning as a serious hypothesis by the 1820s.

## Relevance

The theory is the period backdrop against which the vault discusses *View of the Hebrews* and the claim that it influenced the Book of Mormon. [[Ethan Smith]] wrote *View of the Hebrews* (see [[View of the Hebrews Is Not Scripture or a Novel]]) as one expression of this broader theory, which was also pushed by contemporaries such as Elias Boudinot, James Adair, Mordecai Manuel Noah, and [[Josiah Priest]]. The vault's point — developed in [[The Hebraic-Indian Theory Was a Common Period Idea]] — is that because the idea was a *common period commonplace*, the existence of similar claims in *View of the Hebrews* and the Book of Mormon does not establish dependence: both drew on the air of the times.
