# We Make Men Without Chests - The Great Irony

## The Point
The first chapter ends on Lewis's most quoted line — a bitter paradox: **"We make men without chests and expect of them virtue and enterprise. We laugh at honour and are shocked to find traitors in our midst. We castrate and bid the geldings be fruitful."**[^1] A culture that teaches its young that all values are merely subjective feelings systematically destroys the trained sentiments (the "chest") that *produce* courage, loyalty, and honour — and then is baffled when those qualities are absent. You cannot debunk value at the root and harvest virtue at the branch.

## Evidence
Ward gives the irony concrete historical teeth. In a letter Lewis tied the Green Book's subjectivism to military collapse: "Gaius and Titius were, as you will not be surprised to learn, Australians. Singapore knows what comes of Green Books now" — a pointed (if "perhaps not entirely fair") link between the textbook's teaching and the perceived cowardice of troops at the Fall of Singapore in 1942.[^2] This explains Lewis's later claim that abstract reasoning cannot hold a soldier to his post: "In battle it is not syllogisms that will keep the reluctant nerves and muscles to their post in the third hour of the bombardment. The crudest sentimentalism... about a flag or a country or a regiment will be of more use."[^3] Lewis "knew whereof he spoke," having been under bombardment himself in the First World War.[^3] The mechanism of the irony is the educators' own stated aim — "to fortify the minds of young people against emotion"[^4] — combined with the fact that the "'debunking' side of their work... will really tell": the corrosive lesson, not the nominal subject, is what actually forms (or deforms) the pupil.[^5]

## LDS Connection
Lewis's paradox — demanding fruit from a tree we have deliberately killed at the root — is a sharp tool for the Latter-day Saint cultural critique. Scripture warns that godliness reduced to mere form is powerless: men "having a form of godliness, but denying the power thereof" (2 Timothy 3:5), arriving precisely among those "without natural affection" (2 Timothy 3:3) — geldings bidden to be fruitful. The Restoration's answer is to rebuild the chest by cultivating righteous *desire* and charity ([[Some Things Merit Our Response - Ordinate Affections]]); "a good tree cannot bring forth evil fruit" (3 Nephi 14:18), but neither can a tree uprooted by subjectivism bring forth good. The irony is also the practical sting of the [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]] case: a metaethic that dissolves value into feeling ([[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]-style subjectivism) cannot then summon the very virtues — honour, courage, fidelity — that only a believed-in objective good can sustain.
