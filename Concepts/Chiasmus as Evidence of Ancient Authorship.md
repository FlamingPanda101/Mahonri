# Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship

## The Claim
The hosts treat chiasmus — an ancient Semitic ABBA / ABCBA literary form — as evidence that the Book of Mormon is a genuine ancient text rather than a 19th-century composition. The reasoning: ordered, nested inverted parallelism is a hallmark of Hebrew literature; if the Book of Mormon contains rich, intentional chiasms (like the claimed [[Concentric Chiasmus in Alma 24|concentric chiasm in Alma 24]]), this fits authors "who claim to be Jewish having left the old world coming for the new" — and would be improbable for [[Joseph Smith]] to fabricate unaware [^1][^2].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Genre fit.** [[Cardon Ellis]] notes that for people claiming Israelite descent, finding a Jewish literary form "you would almost expect," including ones "you didn't catch right away that we find later" — framing late-discovered chiasms as confirmation rather than special pleading [^1]. *Rating: this expectation cuts both ways — see steelman.*
2. **Ordered language ≠ guessing.** Cardon argues "you're not going to have ordered language like that if you're just guessing," and Jonah escalates to the [[Infinite Monkey Theorem Against Random Chiasmus|infinite-monkey theorem]] to argue structured text cannot arise by chance [^2][^3].
3. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) **Confirmation problem:** if both early-found *and* late-found chiasms count as evidence, the hypothesis is unfalsifiable — exactly the worry Cardon voices ("are we just seeing them anywhere?"). (b) **Availability in 1829:** chiasmus/inverted parallelism was discussed in pre-1830 biblical scholarship (e.g., Robert Lowth's work on Hebrew poetry, John Jebb, Thomas Boys), and the KJV Joseph dictated from is saturated with parallelism, giving an unconscious template. (c) **Base rate:** short ABA patterns occur readily in any repetitive rhetoric; the field's own standard (statistical significance, density, boundary control — the [[John W. Welch|Welch]] criteria) is precisely what this episode skips. (d) The hosts themselves frame the segment as speculative and admit avoiding scholarly vetting [^4].
4. **The monkeys cut against, not for.** The infinite-monkey rebuttal shows random typing won't produce Shakespeare; it does not show that a literate 19th-century author immersed in KJV cadence couldn't produce parallelism — a different and easier task than blind randomness [^3].
