# Inclination Is Not Action — The Predisposition Does Not Compel the Act

## The Point
There is a decisive moral difference between *having an inclination* and *acting on it*. A person may not choose to *desire* something, yet still choose whether to *do* it — and moral responsibility attaches to the second, not the first. This distinction quietly dismantles the genetic-determinism argument from within: pressed on whether "a person doesn't choose to be sexually attracted to children can they choose whether or not to actually have sex with children," the critic answers "yes and no... some people you either can make a choice to do something or you can't." [^1] But "yes" for *any* case concedes the point: where the act is chosen, the unchosen inclination has not eliminated agency — it has only set the stage on which agency operates. The determinist case needs *every* act to be compelled; admitting even one chosen act breaks the universal claim.

## Evidence
- **The concession.** Asked directly whether someone attracted to children "can... choose whether or not to actually have sex with children," the answer is "um yes and no... it is yes or no because some people you either can make a choice to do something or you can't — you were determined by it." [^1]
- **The structure of the admission.** By splitting cases into "can choose" and "was determined," the critic grants that *desire* and *deed* come apart: for the "can choose" cases, the unchosen attraction did not necessitate the act. [^1]
- **It coexists with constraint.** The point is not that no one is ever compelled (severe pathology may diminish capacity) but that unchosen inclination does not *by itself* establish compulsion — most agents with an inclination still face a genuine fork. [^1]

## Best Quotes
> "[Can they] choose whether or not to actually have sex with children? Um, yes and no... some people you either can make a choice to do something or you can't." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint moral psychology is built precisely on the inclination/action gap. The "natural man" arrives with unchosen appetites, yet is called to "yield to the enticings of the Holy Spirit, and putteth off the natural man" (Mosiah 3:19) — temptation is not transgression. Lehi frames mortal life as a standing choice between "liberty... or captivity," with agents "free to choose" (2 Nephi 2:27; cf. [[2 Nephi 2.8]]), which assumes that what one is *inclined* toward and what one *does* are distinct. This is why judgment is "according to their works" and "the desires of their hearts" once acted upon ([[Alma 41.3-7]]), and why agency — the uncreated intelligence that "act[s] for itself" ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:29–30]]; [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]) — is the locus of responsibility rather than the raw inclination. The distinction also exposes the limit of [[Sam Harris]]'s psychopathy argument ([[Evil as a Natural Phenomenon - Harris on Psychopathy]]): even granting that some brains *lack* the machinery for moral feeling, the very fact that we *excuse* the genuinely incapable while *holding* the capable shows we already track this line — and that for the ordinary agent, predisposition is the terrain of moral struggle, not its abolition (see [[The Genetic-Determinism Argument Against Moral Responsibility]]).
