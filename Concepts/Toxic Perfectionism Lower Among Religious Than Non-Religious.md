# Toxic Perfectionism Lower Among Religious Than Non-Religious

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A BYU therapist asserted offhand that Latter-day Saint students are "really high in perfectionism ... because of the gospel," reflecting a widespread assumption that LDS high standards ("be ye therefore perfect," [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]]) breed pathological perfectionism.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Live data fact-check (Evidence: strong, longitudinal panel).** Dyer "ran the numbers right there" on data tracking the same youth from age 12. Result: "Latter-day Saints and religious people in general were far lower in toxic perfectionism than those of no religion."[^2]

2. **The numbers (Evidence: strong).** ~12% of Latter-day Saints scored high in toxic perfectionism — which Dyer calls "too high" — but the comparable figure was **over 20% for atheists/agnostics.**[^3] The religious group is roughly half as affected.

3. **Toxic perfectionism is the natural-man baseline, not church-induced (Evidence: interpretive).** "That toxic perfectionism is a natural human tendency.... part of even the natural man." Surrounded only by church members at BYU, observers wrongly attribute a universal human tendency to the Church.[^4]

4. **Defines the construct correctly (Evidence: conceptual rigor).** Toxic perfectionism "isn't about having high standards" (healthy) "or even about not meeting those standards" (everyone falls short) — it is about *self-worth collapsing* on failure. See [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]].[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A still-elevated 12% (Dyer himself calls it "too high") means the lived experience of perfectionistic LDS members is real and not erased by a favorable average. The comparison to atheists/agnostics may be confounded — distressed, perfectionistic people may *leave* religion, inflating the non-religious rate (reverse causation / selection). "Religious people in general" lumps LDS with all faiths, so the LDS-specific effect is less isolated than the headline suggests. And the original therapist's claim was about *BYU students* specifically, a higher-pressure subpopulation than "religious people in general."

## Best Quotes

> "Latter-day Saints and religious people in general were far lower in toxic perfectionism than those of no religion."[^2]

> "About 12% of Latter-day Saints were high in toxic perfectionism ... that number was over 20% for atheists agnostics."[^3]

> "That toxic perfectionism is a natural human tendency ... part of, if you will, even the natural man."[^4]
