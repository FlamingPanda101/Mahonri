# The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] analyzes the moral "ought" as a **directional / hypothetical** statement — "a statement about a direction toward an end or goal" — and then argues that because *all* sane creatures share one end (escaping their own suffering) "as a matter of objective fact," moral claims acquire an objective basis. "There are actual right and wrong answers to moral questions because there are right and wrong answers as to what brings about more or less suffering." [^1][^2][^3] This is the constructive payoff that combines [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]] with [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]] — a [[Sam Harris]]-style derivation of objective morality from well-being.

## Argument
1. **The ought is directional, not free-floating.** "The ought is a statement about a direction toward an end or goal; otherwise the term would lose all meaning." Example: "if I want to get to San Diego from Los Angeles, I ought to go south, and if I want to get to San Francisco, I ought to go north." *(conceptual analysis of "ought")* [^1]
2. **So every ought presupposes a desired outcome.** The ought "is the word that we use to point to a behavior we must take if we desire a particular outcome." It is, in effect, a hypothetical imperative: *if* you want end E, you ought to do A. *(premise)* [^1]
3. **There is a universally shared end.** "All sane people everywhere desire to move away from their own suffering" — an "outcome that all sentient sane creatures shared universally as a matter of objective fact." *(empirical premise, from [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]])* [^2]
4. **Therefore moral oughts have an objective basis.** "Under the assumption that the person I'm dealing with is sane and desires their own well-being, I could say that they ought not do things that would cause their suffering, or that they ought to do things… that would likely lead them to their well-being." *(conclusion)* [^2]
5. **Right and wrong track suffering and well-being.** "There are actual right and wrong answers to moral questions because there are right and wrong answers as to what brings about more or less suffering." The universal desire to avoid suffering thus "acts as the objective basis for moral statements." *(summary)* [^3]
6. **Open problem, deferred.** Hansen immediately raises the obvious objection — "isn't well-being subjective?" — and postpones it to the next installment. *(acknowledged gap)* [^3]

**Steelman of the critic:** Three standard objections converge here. (1) *Hypothetical oughts aren't moral oughts.* "If you want to reach San Diego, go south" is mere instrumental advice; deriving morality from it yields only what *you* should do to get what *you* already want — not a binding duty toward *others*. (2) *The agent-relativity gap.* Even if everyone seeks their *own* well-being, that grounds at most an egoistic "ought" per person; it does not show I ought to promote *your* well-being, which is what morality demands. Hansen's example tellingly stays in the second person ("they ought not do things that would cause *their* suffering"). (3) *The subjectivity of well-being* — which Hansen himself flags as unanswered. Until "well-being" is given an objective, interpersonal measure, calling the resulting morality "objective" is premature. (Compare the rival LDS grounding in [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]], which locates the standard in relational flourishing rather than individual suffering-avoidance.)
