# Material vs Formal Sufficiency — The Home Depot Analogy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] concedes Scripture is **materially sufficient** (everything needed for doctrine is *contained* in it, explicitly or implicitly) but denies it is **formally sufficient** (clear and self-interpreting enough to be assembled correctly without a living teaching authority). His analogy: "Scripture has everything I need to do theology in the same way Home Depot has everything I need to build a house — but if I tried to build a house out of everything at Home Depot, you'd die in a fire trap, because I don't know how to put it all together."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The material/formal distinction is the real crux — rate: strong
Horn isolates the load-bearing claim: the debate is not whether the content is *there* but whether Scripture *alone* can be rightly assembled. He supports the distinction with the Protestant scholar [[Timothy Ward]]: "in general the fathers assert the material sufficiency of scripture but deny its formal sufficiency."[^2] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers argues Horn commits a "bait and switch" — Rogers defends *both* material *and* formal sufficiency, proving formal sufficiency from perspicuity texts (light, "have you never read?"). See [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture]].

### 2. Horn's own definition wobbles in cross-ex — rate: a point for Rogers
Under Rogers' questioning, Horn restricts material sufficiency to *what is necessary for salvation*, and even that only "if you have a universal living magisterium"; he denies every doctrine of the faith is in Scripture (e.g., that Nicaea was a legitimate ecumenical council).[^3] Rogers seizes this: "What is material sufficiency according to Rome? We don't know. Nobody knows" — calling Horn's position a "moving target" against the Reformed clarity claim.[^4] **Assessment:** Rogers scores a rhetorical point on Catholic ambiguity, but it does not rescue *formal* sufficiency, which Horn still attacks via the hard-cases (see [[Perspicuity Fails the Hard-Cases Test]]).

### 3. The 2 Timothy 3:17 grammar move — rate: moderate
Horn argues in [[2 Timothy 3.16-17]] it is the *man of God* who is called "complete/equipped for every good work" (ἄρτιος), while Scripture is called *profitable* (ὠφέλιμος), not *sufficient*; he parallels 2 Tim 2:21 ("ready for every good work" by avoiding bad influences) and James (patience "perfects" you).[^5] **Steelman of Rogers:** the instrumental clause "*that* the man of God may be complete" makes Scripture the means *by which* completeness comes — so the equipping is *through* Scripture, which Rogers reads as sufficiency.

## Best Quotes
> "Scripture has everything it needs for me to do theology in the same way Home Depot has everything I need to build a house. But if I tried to build a house out of everything at Home Depot, you'd probably die in a fire trap very quickly because I don't know what I'm doing when I try to put all of it together."[^1]

> [Timothy Ward, quoted by Horn] "In general the fathers assert the material sufficiency of scripture but deny its formal sufficiency."[^2]

> [Rogers] "What is material sufficiency according to Rome? We don't know. Nobody knows."[^4]

> [Horn, 2 Tim 3:17] "You know what is called sufficient? Not scripture. The man of God is called sufficient. Scripture is called ōphelimos — helpful, useful, profitable."[^5]
