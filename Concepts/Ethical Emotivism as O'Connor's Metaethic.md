# Ethical Emotivism as O'Connor's Metaethic

## The Claim

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] identifies as an **ethical emotivist**: moral statements are not truth-apt propositions but expressions of emotion. "Murder is wrong" is like saying "murder" with "an angry emoji and an exclamation mark next to it."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **No objective moral facts.** "I don't think there is some truth in the universe — qualities called goodness and badness, should and shouldn't — that are real and actually exist the way this table exists."[^2] *(Clear statement of anti-realist metaethics; emotivism [Ayer, Stevenson] is a coherent if minority position.)*

2. **Distinct from subjectivism.** Emotivism is *not* "murder is wrong = I don't like murder" (which would be a truth-apt psychological claim). It is a pure *expression* of distaste, with no truth-value.[^3] *(Accurate — O'Connor correctly distinguishes emotivism from subjectivism; this is a precise philosophical point.)*

3. **Owns the discomfort.** "A lot of people find that troubling... emotivism is not going to console you. If you say that leaves you in a hole, I might just say, yeah, it totally does."[^4] *(Intellectually honest — he doesn't claim emotivism grounds or vindicates Western values.)*

4. **Why he says it's "tangential."** Crucially, O'Connor insists his metaethics is beside the point of the historical debate: "Western values historically have *not* been emotivist." He is offering an *internal critique* of Scrivener's thesis, not a rival grounding. See [[Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls]].[^5] *(Strategically important — it blocks Scrivener's "you have no grounding for your values" counter, since O'Connor isn't claiming to ground them.)*

5. **Steelman of the opposing view.** This is the target of the standard [[Moral Argument Against Atheism|moral argument]]: if morality is mere emotion, the abolitionist's claim that slavery is "really" wrong reduces to a feeling. Scrivener probes: "As an emotivist, what would you have said in the House of Commons for abolition?" O'Connor: he might not have been an emotivist in 1800 (the position wasn't invented), and might have appealed to objective principles "like Lincoln" — separating *what he could say then* from *whether Christianity caused* abolition.[^6] *(The exchange shows the metaethics and the historical thesis are genuinely separable, which is O'Connor's point.)*

## Best Quotes

> "If you say murder is wrong, it's like saying the word 'murder' and then putting an angry emoji and an exclamation mark next to it. It doesn't have truth value."[^1]

> "I don't think objective morality exists."[^2]

> "If you say doesn't that leave you in a hole, I might just say — yeah, it totally does."[^4]
