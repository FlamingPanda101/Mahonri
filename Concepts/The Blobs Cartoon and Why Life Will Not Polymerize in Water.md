# The Blobs Cartoon and Why Life Will Not Polymerize in Water

## The Point
Reacting to a viral Veritasium animation that depicts life emerging from simple "blobs" combining by chance in a prebiotic pond, Meyer argues the cartoon "tells a wonderful story" that "is not plausible scientifically at almost every point along the way."[^1] The most basic problem is chemical: the long, information-bearing biomolecules (proteins and DNA) "will not polymerize in water." Linking their subunits into chains is energetically unfavorable in an aqueous environment — so the classic "warm little pond" or "prebiotic soup" runs the chemistry in the wrong direction.[^2] The famous Miller-Urey experiment does not rescue the story: it yielded only two or three amino acids in a vessel full of other chemicals that immediately interfere.

## Evidence
1. **Polymerization fails in water.** The "polymers, the long information-bearing biomolecules, such as proteins and DNA, will not polymerize in water"; the reaction "is run energetically... going the wrong way," even in a hydrothermal vent — a point organic chemist James Tour has made repeatedly.[^2]
2. **Miller-Urey's meager, contaminated yield.** The 1952–53 Miller-Urey spark-discharge apparatus simulated lightning striking simple gases and produced "two or three of the protein-forming amino acids," but inside "a vessel with lots and lots of other chemical subunits."[^3]
3. **Interfering cross-reactions and "melanoidin."** Those amino acids "right away" react with the other products in "interfering cross reactions," creating a sludge called melanoidin — "the curse of the prebiotic chemist."[^4]
4. **The atmosphere was wrong.** The early atmosphere "was not reducing as those experiments presupposed," so it was "energetically unfavorable to the formation of the amino acid subunits" in the first place.[^5]
5. **The simulations were unrealistic.** The experiments that "lent plausibility" to the story "were done under unrealistic conditions that did not match those on the early Earth," and their products would be subject to cross-reactions and would not polymerize into long chains.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The polymers, the long information-bearing biomolecules, such as proteins and DNA, will not polymerize in water." [^2]

> "Those other chemicals are subject to what are called interfering cross reactions... creating a sludge called melanoidin, which is the curse of the prebiotic chemist." [^4]

> "This cartoon tells a wonderful story... it's not plausible scientifically at almost every point along the way." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The argument targets the adequacy of an *unguided* chemical pathway to life, not the age of the earth or the fact of natural processes — territory on which Latter-day Saint thought takes no fixed position. What the chemistry supplies is a natural-theology resource congenial to the LDS picture of a God who *organizes* eternal, uncreated matter through ordered means rather than creating *ex nihilo*: if the raw chemistry will not climb toward life on its own, that points toward an organizing intelligence directing the process. The inference lands on a generic designing mind, which an LDS reader can affirm while leaving the biological details open. See [[DNA as Digital Code - The Information Enigma]].
