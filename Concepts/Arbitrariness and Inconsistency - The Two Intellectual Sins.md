# Arbitrariness and Inconsistency - The Two Intellectual Sins

## The Point
[[Greg Bahnsen]]'s foundational diagnostic move in presuppositional apologetics is that there are only "two great intellectual sins that men commit no matter what field of study you're in": **arbitrariness** and **inconsistency**.[^1] No one is permitted to be arbitrary if they are "trying to present a rational basis for what you believe," and no one is rational who contradicts themselves.[^1] These are not Christian rules imposed from outside but the conditions of rationality itself, which "any intellectually honest person will have to concede."[^2] Because apologetic disputes presuppose that both parties are "seeking to be rational as they seek the objective truth," the unbeliever cannot exempt himself from these standards — and Bahnsen estimates that "probably 80% of your work in apologetics is taken care of if you'll just be on the lookout for people saying anything they want to say."[^3]

## Evidence
Bahnsen distinguishes the two sins precisely. **Arbitrariness** is asserting something without grounds — "just to believe anything you wish" — which "is not a sign of rationality."[^2] **Inconsistency** is self-contradiction, which is irrational regardless of the field.[^1] He grants that "people of any worldview can engage in being arbitrary," so the charge is not partisan; the point is that *to argue at all* is to renounce the right to be arbitrary, since "to make a rational case they will have to provide good reasons for their assertions or else they are offering a mere opinion."[^4] He grounds the standard in the purpose of education itself: people are "supposed to have principles by which they test theories" and a "manner of reasoning" that lets them "draw a conclusion without just... saying well I feel this way."[^5] [[Jacob Hansen]] notes that the video's sequel will take up the second, deeper problem — that "most people's worldviews are also profoundly incoherent" — explicitly pairing arbitrariness (this video) with inconsistency.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "One of the two great intellectual sins that men commit no matter what field of study you're in is arbitrariness. The other one is inconsistency." [^1]

> "Subjectively it seems to me probably 80% of your work in apologetics is taken care of if you'll just be on the lookout for people saying anything they want to say." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration treats reason and revealed truth as allies, not rivals, so Bahnsen's demand for non-arbitrary, non-contradictory grounding is congenial to Latter-day Saint apologetics. Scripture defines truth as "knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) — a mind-independent reality one may not simply construct at will — and declares that "the glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36), which dignifies disciplined reasoning. The LDS nuance is that the deepest standard against which arbitrariness is measured is not merely the abstract laws of logic but **eternal law that God himself honors** ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]): morality is non-arbitrary because it is anchored in an eternal order, not because a bare divine will (or a human one) decrees it. This both affirms Bahnsen's transcendental instinct and refines its theological ground (compare [[Knowing Morality vs Grounding Morality - Epistemology and Ontology]]).
