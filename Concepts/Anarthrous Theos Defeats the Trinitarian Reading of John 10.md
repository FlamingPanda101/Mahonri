# Anarthrous *Theos* Defeats the Trinitarian Reading of John 10

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
In [[John 10.30|John 10:30]] Jesus is claiming to be the very God of Israel (the Trinity), and his quotation of Psalm 82 is just a clever rabbinic "lesser-to-greater" defense of that claim.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The self-defeating dilemma (decisive)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** argues the Trinitarian reading makes Jesus' own argument incoherent. The apologist skipped the middle of the exchange: in [[John 10.33|John 10:33]] the crowd says, "We're stoning you because you being human make yourself divine." [^1] If the accusation were "you make yourself the very God of Israel," then Jesus' reply — pointing to Psalm 82's honorifically-divine humans/beings — would be "an entirely irrelevant straw man." [^2] So the very fact that Jesus' Psalm-82 reply is *responsive* proves the charge was *not* "you claim to be Yahweh."

### 2. The grammar fixes the charge (strong)
The decisive datum is the Greek of [[John 10.33|John 10:33]]: "It is an anarthrous occurrence of the Greek word *theos*." [^3] Without the article, the sense is "you, being human, make yourself **a god / divine**," not "you make yourself **[the] God**." McClellan: usual translations wrongly render it "God capital G God the very God of Israel," but the anarthrous form supports the qualitative reading "divine." [^3] See also [[John 10-33 - Making Yourself God (Missing Article)]].

### 3. The logic, laid out (strong)
"If they can be called Gods, divine, not the very God of Israel, then what's wrong with me saying I am a god or divine? So this argument is self-defeating because it points out that Jesus cannot be claiming to be the very God of Israel." [^4] The only readings that keep Jesus' argument *valid* are: (a) Jesus claims to be *a god / divine*, met by the Psalm-82 honorific-humans reading, or (b) Jesus appeals to the Sinai divinization reading (see [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]]) — in both cases "we must interpret the accusation as Jesus claiming to be either a god or divine, which is what is going on in John 10." [^5]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A Trinitarian could grant the anarthrous *theos* yet argue that John elsewhere (1:1, 20:28) ascribes full deity, so 10:33 is the crowd's *understatement* of a claim John affirms more strongly elsewhere. McClellan's reply is that one must read 10:30–39 as a coherent *unit*: within this pericope Jesus' defense only works on the qualitative reading, and importing the conclusion from other verses is the very "interpretive lens" imposition he objects to. The high-christology texts are themselves better explained by image christology — see [[Image Christology in the Gospel of John]].
