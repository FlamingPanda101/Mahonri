# Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical

## The Claim
Creation *ex nihilo* — God creating everything out of absolutely nothing — is a post-biblical doctrine (likely traceable to [[Augustine]] and the apologists' Hellenizing project), not the teaching of the Hebrew Bible. Joseph Smith's teaching that intelligence/matter is uncreated and co-eternal is, on this view, closer to the original biblical picture.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Scholarly dating (rating: moderate; Butler's appeal to consensus)
Butler: "the large majority of biblical scholars would say that the idea of out-of-nothing creation... did not exist pre-Christianity. That is not biblical." (This reflects the work of scholars such as Gerhard May, *Creatio ex Nihilo*, who date the formed doctrine to the 2nd century.) [^1]

### 2. It is bound up with the Hellenized God (rating: moderate; systematic)
Day suspects *ex nihilo* "is coming from Augustine's understanding" — part of the same Greek-philosophical apparatus that produced the immaterial unmoved-mover God. See [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]]. [^2]

### 3. It generates the problem of evil (rating: moderate; theological)
"If you believe that God created everything out of nothing... that's the reason why creedal Christians have the problem of evil — all evil, it has to be God; there's no way around it." Joseph Smith "solves it" by denying that humans/matter were created from nothing, removing God as the sole author of evil. [^3]

### 4. Restoration coherence (rating: moderate; LDS systematics)
Butler argues that, taking the whole biblical canon, the uncreated-matter view "turns out to be more of a biblical view than the rest of the Christian world." It coheres with the divine-family theology (intelligences/spirits not manufactured *ex nihilo*). See [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]]. [^3]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Defenders cite [[Hebrews 11.3|Hebrews 11:3]] ("the things which are seen were not made of things which do appear"), [[Romans 4.17|Rom 4:17]] (God "calleth those things which be not as though they were"), and [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]] read as absolute beginning, arguing *ex nihilo* is the natural inference from divine omnipotence and aseity even if the *technical formula* is post-biblical. The doctrine guards God's sovereignty and uniqueness — if matter is co-eternal, God is not the absolute source of all. Butler grants the formula is "a development," but holds the unformed-chaos language of Genesis (*tohu wabohu*; the deep already present) better fits creation-from-disorder than from absolute nothing.
