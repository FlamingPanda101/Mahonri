# Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians

## The Point
The whole debate hangs on what "Christian" means. [[Jacob Hansen]] concedes the descriptive label "we will not consider ourselves *creedal* Christians" while insisting Latter-day Saints *are* Christians, since their religion centers on "the testimony of the apostles and prophets concerning Jesus Christ" — that He "died, was buried, rose again the third day, and ascended to heaven."[^1] He even grants the Apostles' Creed wholesale and ~95% of the Nicene, locating the sole break at the *consubstantiality* of the Son with the Father.[^2] [[Allie Beth Stuckey]]'s reply is that this verbal agreement masks a substantive divide: the dispute is not the word "Christian" but whether the LDS gospel and God are the saving ones (see [[Priesthood Authority Is the Necessary But Not Sufficient Dividing Line]], [[Three Persons Share One Nature Not One Being]]).

## Evidence
- Hansen's self-definition tracks the move documented in [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]]: accept "non-creedal," keep "Christian," and relocate the fight to the *content* of "God."
- He frames the LDS-Catholic parallel strongly — both hold "an ecclesiastical structure and priesthood that governs the ordinances" — to argue the LDS exclusivity claim is no stranger than Rome's "no salvation outside the Church."[^3]
- Stuckey's snort/laugh at the "I'm a Christian, just not a creedal Christian" framing signals the evangelical intuition that the qualifier gives the game away — the Nicene content *is* the historic definition of Christian orthodoxy.

## Best Quotes
> "We would consider ourselves Christians, but we will not consider ourselves creedal Christians." [^1]

> "The Apostles' Creed actually is one that we would embrace... and even the Nicene Creed, to be honest, 95% of it we would believe wholeheartedly. The area that we take issue with is the consubstantiality of Jesus Christ and the Father." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the load-bearing move of LDS ecumenical apologetics: concede the *historic-creedal* label to keep the *biblical-Christ* label, then argue that creedal metaphysics (one being, simplicity, *ex nihilo*) is the post-biblical accretion — not the worship of Christ. Stuckey's counter is the duty-of-candor position: if "God" differs at the level of being and kind, then affirming "Jesus is Lord" is affirming [[Different Jesus Both Uncharitable and Precisely True|a different Jesus]].
