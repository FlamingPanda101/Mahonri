# Pilgrimage Commandment Was to See God's Face

## The Claim

The thrice-yearly pilgrimage commandment — most clearly stated in [[Deuteronomy 16.16|Deuteronomy 16:16]] and paralleled in [[Exodus 34.24|Exodus 34:24]] and [[Isaiah 1.12|Isaiah 1:12]] — originally commanded Israelite males to **see the face of God** three times a year at the chosen sanctuary, not merely to "appear before" him. [[Dan McClellan]] argues the standard English rendering ("appear before the LORD") is a knowing mistranslation that follows the [[Masoretes|Masoretic]] vowel pointing rather than the underlying consonantal text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The literal Hebrew word order signals "see."** McClellan renders Deuteronomy 16:16 literally: "three times in a year will see all of your males the face of Adonai your God." The verbal root **רָאָה (ra'ah), "to see,"** is the head verb, and "the face of God" reads as its direct object.[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — this is a straightforward observation of the consonantal text and verb root, uncontroversial among Hebraists.)*

2. **The active vs. passive turns entirely on the vowels.** In the finite forms the same consonants can be read active (Qal, "you will see") or passive (Niphal, "be seen / appear). The Masoretes supplied vowels making it passive. McClellan argues the surrounding particles and prepositions favor the active.[^3] *(Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong for the finite verses; the consonants are genuinely ambiguous here, which is why McClellan concedes the finite cases "could go either way" and rests the decisive argument on Isaiah — see [[Infinitive Construct in Isaiah Proves Active Reading]].)*

3. **The direct-object marker points to "see," not "appear."** McClellan notes the construction uses the direct-object particle plus "my face" (*panai*), not the preposition for "before" (which would be *liphnei*). "You don't appear *to* my face; you appear *before* me." The object marker fits a transitive "see the face," not the intransitive "appear."[^4] *(Evidence rating: strong — this syntactic detail is the crux of the argument and is hard to reconcile with the passive reading.)*

4. **The offering clause coheres with a visit, not a vision-passive.** The same verse adds "they shall not appear before the LORD empty-handed," fusing the command with obligatory offerings (see [[Seeing Gods Face as Costly Signaling and Offering]]). The pilgrimage was a concrete temple visit to behold the divine image.[^5] *(Evidence rating: contextual.)*

**Steelman of the traditional reading:** The Masoretic vocalization is itself an ancient witness (the Tiberian Masoretes preserved a reading tradition centuries old), and the passive Niphal "appear / present oneself" is grammatically possible for the consonants in the finite verses. A defender can argue the tradents vocalized passively because that *was* the inherited meaning, reflecting the theological conviction (from [[Exodus 33.20|Exodus 33:20]]) that one cannot see God's face. The strongest counter is that this conviction is precisely the motive McClellan posits for the alteration, and the Isaiah infinitive construct cannot be explained away the same way.

## Best Quotes

> "I think it's most clearly given as a command in Deuteronomy 16:16... but it's always mistranslated. It's always knowingly mistranslated."[^1]

> "Three times in a year will see all of your males the face of Adonai your God... the consonants want you to understand this in the active. So the verbal root ra'ah means to see... in the active it means you see the face of God, but if you twist it into the passive, it's be seen to the face of God, which is translated as appear before God."[^2]

> "It's a direct object marker and then my face. You don't appear to my face. You appear before me. It should be liphnei, but it's at panai."[^4]
