#type/claim

# The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon

## The Claim

Critics dismiss the Book of Mormon witnesses as "all believers," but [[Josh Gailey]] argues this forgets the **empty stone box** in the New York hill — a physical, locatable object that functions for the Book of Mormon exactly as the **empty tomb** functions for the resurrection.[^1] The same three criteria apologists use to argue the tomb was empty — **proximity, enemies, unlikely testimonies** (Gailey's "PET" rubric, drawn from [[Gary Habermas]] and used by William Lane Craig) — apply with equal or greater force to the stone box.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Proximity — the movement begins at ground zero
For the resurrection, the Jesus movement starts in Jerusalem, within walking distance of Joseph of Arimathea's tomb, where doubters could go look.[^3] Likewise the Restoration begins within a ~10-mile radius (Manchester, Palmyra, Fayette) of the hill — "walking distance." Anyone wanting to know whether Joseph really had an artifact could go see whether there was a hole and a stone box in the hill, "and many did."[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the geographic parallel is exact; its evidential weight rides on the box actually having been seen — see point 3).

### 2. Enemies — the opponents' own actions admit the box
In Matthew 28 the enemies' explanation ("the disciples stole the body") concedes the tomb was empty.[^5] The Book of Mormon analogue: hostile neighbors and former treasure-hunting associates did not laugh Joseph off — they went to the hill, became convinced something had been taken, and dug, tore up the cooper's shop, and tried to steal the "gold Bible."[^6] Their action is an enemy admission of the hole and the box. Developed in [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (behavior of hostile parties is harder to dismiss than friendly testimony).

### 3. Unlikely testimonies — and a richer documentary base than the resurrection
The resurrection's unlikely witnesses are the women (whose legal testimony Josephus and the Talmud disparaged), making their primacy a mark of authenticity.[^7] The Book of Mormon's unlikely witnesses include lifelong skeptics like [[Isaac Hale]] and the hostile [[Lorenzo Saunders]], plus a court-bound [[Josiah Stowell]]. Gailey stresses the *quantity*: only a handful of resurrection documents exist, versus ~200 sources from the three witnesses, 60+ from the eight, and 21 people handling the plates — early, contemporaneous, often handwritten.[^8] He likens the printer's manuscript in [[Oliver Cowdery]]'s hand to "having a document in the handwriting of Peter listing the apostles who saw the risen Jesus."[^9]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the documentary volume is real; the *comparison* to the resurrection is the speakers' bold framing).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic can grant every structural parallel and still resist the conclusion. The empty tomb evidences a *missing body*; the empty stone box evidences, at most, a *hole in a hill* — which farming, money-digging, or any prior excavation could produce, with no demonstrable link to gold plates. The "enemies' actions" prove only that neighbors *believed* Joseph had something (credulous rural treasure-lore was rampant), not that he did. And cataloguing 200 sources counts re-tellings by interested parties, not 200 independent observers. The reply is that the box's location and details were known to *hostile* sources who had no faith-motive to confirm them, and that the count includes genuinely unsympathetic witnesses (see [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]]) — but the believer should concede the empty-tomb / empty-box parallel is an argument by analogy whose force tracks how independent and hostile the corroborating sources really are.

## Best Quotes

> "Those who claim that there are no credible witnesses... are completely forgetting the stone box, which is essentially the empty tomb of the resurrection for the Book of Mormon."[^1]

> "If somebody wanted to know if Joseph really did have an artifact, they could go to the hill and see whether or not there was a stone box in the hill for themselves. And many did."[^4]

> "If Christians had a document... in the handwriting of one of the apostles... listing the names of all the apostles who affirmed seeing Jesus — that would be revolutionary. Well, that's what we have."[^9]

## LDS Connection

The argument is offered as shared ground with Christians who already accept the resurrection on historical grounds: if that method is sound, the *same* method applied to the plates and the stone box yields golden plates as the best explanation. Latter-day Saints affirm the resurrection so strongly they hold that Christ visited the Americas, so a "cross-continental independent attestation of the risen Christ" is, for them, the natural payoff rather than a competitor to the gospel. The honest form of the claim depends on the converging, partly hostile data marshalled in [[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]] and [[Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box]].
