# The Compassion Revolution of the Cross

## The Claim

[[Glenn Scrivener]] argues Western compassion originates in the "transvaluation of all values" effected by the cross: in a steep Roman hierarchy where the slave's death was the extreme penalty, "the God of heaven" shows up *on* that cross — "the highest figure descending to the deepest depth to have the greatest effect on the most number of people for the longest duration." This burst "a compassion revolution."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Roman default morality was reciprocal, not compassionate.** Citing [[Theresa Morgan]], Scrivener notes the common Roman aphorism: "every day do something to help your friends and harm your enemies" — the second half of which moderns recoil from. In a "world of scarcity" and "in-group amity / out-group enmity," this "perfectly describes nature."[^2] *(Strong — accurately characterizes pre-Christian elite ethics; the cross's elevation of the lowest is a real inversion.)*

2. **Concrete institutional fruits.** Scrivener lists charity, hospitals, orphan care, and the abolition of gladiatorial games, [[Surviving Traces of Human Sacrifice in the Hebrew Bible|infanticide]], and pederasty as downstream of this revolution.[^3] *(Strong on institutionalization — see [[Institutionalization Is the Unique Christian Contribution]] — but O'Connor distinguishes the *value* from its *institutional form*.)*

3. **Christians ended infant exposure.** Christians are documented as the antidote to exposure (abandoning infants — largely girls and the disabled — to die); even O'Connor grants "we have records of Christians being the ones who were anti this."[^4] *(Strong and conceded by both.)*

4. **Steelman of the opposing view:** O'Connor warns against overstatement in both directions — just as the church should not be called "the greatest roadblock" to progress, neither should it be credited with "the entire development of the Western tradition." And "compassion" is so "ambiguous a term" that it crops up everywhere, so the bare value cannot be uniquely Christian.[^5] He also argues the Jewish world Christianity entered (Canaanite conquest) was *as* brutal as the Roman one — see [[Both the Jewish and Roman Worlds Were Brutal]]. *(Fair — Scrivener largely concedes by retreating to the *institutionalization* claim.)*

## Best Quotes

> "Here is the highest figure descending to the deepest depth to have the greatest effect on the most number of people for the longest duration. Here is an unimprovable story of compassion."[^1]

> "Here is the sacrifice of the fittest for we the weakest, so that we the weakest might survive and thrive and pass on a compassion revolution."[^1]

> "A very common aphorism back in Roman times was: every day do something to help your friends and harm your enemies."[^2]
