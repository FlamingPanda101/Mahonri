# The Diversity Test - What a Community Tolerates Reveals Its God

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Aaron Shafovaloff]] argues that the *deepest* difference between the two faiths is "what our respective communities are willing to tolerate diversity over." Run a thought experiment: ask 100 Latter-day Saints whether Heavenly Father was perhaps a sinful mortal prior to being exalted, and you get wide divergence; ask 100 evangelical seven-year-olds the same question and you get a unanimous, reflexive "no." The *spread of permitted answers*, not any single answer, exposes what a community actually worships.[^1] It reframes the "same God?" question away from proof-texting and toward the sociological shape of each tradition's belief about deity.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The asymmetry is empirically grounded, not hypothetical (rated: moderate, primary-source anecdote).** Shafovaloff cites his own 2014 documentary *God Never Sinned*: when he asked Latter-day Saints whether the Father was once a sinful mortal, "about two-thirds were in the scope of yes or maybe, and about one-third" said God never was a sinful mortal.[^2] The evangelical side, by contrast, is "rock-solid... zero-divergent, zero-diversity": "God was always God, He was never a sinful mortal... there's no heavenly grandfather, there's no higher than the most high."[^3] If a pastor preached an infinite regress of gods, "it's not as though we would just sort of have a polite discussion about internal diversity of views" — it would be excommunicable, not negotiable. See [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]].

2. **It targets *worship*, not mere opinion (rated: strong, conceptual).** The force of the argument is that the doctrine in question is "worshipful, emotionally invested, existential" — the very identity of the God being adored. A community can tolerate diverse views on adiaphora; it cannot tolerate diverse views on *who its God fundamentally is* without revealing that the question is, for it, open. That openness is itself the datum. This connects to the evangelical insistence on the *most high* (Isaiah 46:9; see [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]] for the contested reading).

3. **Steelman of Hansen's rebuttal (rated: strong — the asymmetry cuts both ways).** [[Jacob Hansen]] replies that "a diversity of opinions exists in every tradition": [[Justin Martyr]] was a subordinationist, [[Tertullian]] and [[Clement of Alexandria]] held views the later church rejected, and Protestantism today tolerates denominations that ordain women and perform same-sex marriages while others "stone gay people."[^4] So mere internal diversity cannot by itself discredit a movement — otherwise historic Christianity, riven by christological controversy before Nicaea, would fail its own test. Hansen further insists the infinite regress is *not* taught by LDS scripture and that he rejects it, so the "dominant" view Shafovaloff surveys is tradition/speculation, not binding doctrine. See [[LDS Father Was Always Fully Divine (No Eternal Regression)]] and [[Modern LDS Tensions on King Follett Discourse]].

4. **Shafovaloff's counter-narrowing (rated: moderate).** He concedes the general point but narrows the claim: he cannot find Christians who are "diverse on the issue of whether God was perhaps a sinful mortal or whether he was once not God." Protestants differ on ethics and secondary doctrines, not on the aseity and eternal Godhood of the *most high*. He grants that Hansen "rejects what his prophets and apostles have taught" on the Father having a Father — but insists that for equipping purposes he must engage the "dominant historical position," not Hansen's minority retrieval.[^5] The unresolved crux: is the relevant comparison *any* internal diversity (Hansen) or diversity *about the identity of God specifically* (Shafovaloff)?

## Best Quotes
> "I think one of the biggest differences here is what our respective communities are willing to tolerate diversity over."[^1]

> "In the historic Christian community, there's a rock-solid worshipful, emotionally invested, existential, beautiful, unifying, zero-divergent, zero-diversity belief that God was always God, He was never a sinful mortal, He never had to become a God."[^3]

> "A diversity of opinions exists in every tradition. Justin Martyr was a subordinationist... the majority of the early church fathers and the New Testament teach a subordinationist model." — Jacob Hansen[^4]
