# Fruits Means Outcomes Not Right Belief in the Trinity

## The Point
When Jesus said "by their fruits ye shall know them" ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]]), did he mean *the observable outcomes of a lived lifestyle* or *correct doctrinal belief in the Trinity*? The hosts note that many evangelical critics take "fruit" to mean right belief in "the triune formulation of God," so that good lived outcomes do not count. Against this, they argue the plain sense of the passage — a tree is known by what it actually produces — points to measurable outcomes like stronger families, charity, and closeness to Christ.

## Evidence
1. **The two readings stated plainly.** The hosts paraphrase the critic's view: "fruits don't mean the outcomes that come from living a certain lifestyle ... it is just by right belief in the triune formulation of God."[^1] They reject this as making the metaphor of a fruit-bearing tree meaningless.

2. **The arboreal logic of the text.** "A tree, when it grows, it produces fruits. If they're good fruits, then it should be a good tree."[^2] On this reading the test is necessarily *output*-based: a good tree "cannot bring forth evil fruit" (Matthew 7:18), so observable good — "people experience an increased proximity to Jesus Christ and love in their life and happiness and stronger family ties" — is itself the fruit.[^3]

3. **It is the empirical wing of an old debate.** This is the popular-media counterpart to the more careful exchange in [[Fruit Versus Theology - Are They Separable]], where Gavin Ortlund argues fruit and theology are *organically connected* (confession is part of the fruit), not cleanly separable. The Latter Daily Saints hosts press only the narrower, evidential half: a corrupt tree should not be producing the best family-structure numbers in the country.

4. **Why the hosts say critics resist it.** They frame the insistence on belief-only fruit as "poisoning the well" and "mists of darkness" (1 Nephi 12:17) — rhetoric meant to keep honest seekers from looking at the actual outcomes.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "A lot of people from the Evangelical community will take a look at that and be like, 'Yeah, fruits don't mean the outcomes that come from living a certain lifestyle. No, it is just by right belief in the triune formulation of God.'" [^1]

> "A tree, when it grows, it produces fruits. If they're good fruits, then it should be a good tree." [^2]

> "If good things happen — like people experience an increased proximity to Jesus Christ and love in their life and happiness and stronger family ties — you might think that, 'Hey, maybe that's a fruit.'" [^3]

## LDS Connection
The argument applies the Savior's own diagnostic ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:16-20]], echoed in Moroni 7) and feeds directly into the data-driven case of [[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]] and [[Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion]]. The deeper question of whether *good fruit can be separated from doctrinal truth at all* is handled in [[Fruit Versus Theology - Are They Separable]] — the two notes are complementary, not duplicative: this one is about what "fruit" *means*, that one about whether fruit *settles* theology.
