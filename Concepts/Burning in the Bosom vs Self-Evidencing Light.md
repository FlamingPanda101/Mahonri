# Burning in the Bosom vs Self-Evidencing Light

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] argues [[Anthony Rogers]]' self-authentication amounts to a Mormon-style **"burning in the bosom"** — a subjective inner conviction that "this is scripture." Horn notes people report the same experience reading the Quran, the Book of Mormon, or Christian Science in jail; so the experience cannot identify the true canon. Rogers replies the objection conflates the *objective* self-authenticating authority of the word with the *subjective* Spirit-wrought reception of it.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Horn's parity argument — rate: strong
"I've heard people in jail read the Quran and it does that to them, or the Book of Mormon ... lots of people make that claim."[^1] If the felt conviction is the test, rival scriptures pass it equally — a serious problem given Rogers' own jail-cell conversion testimony. **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers grants the *subjective* experience is not the ground of authority; he invokes [[Luke 24.32]] (the Emmaus disciples' burning hearts) to argue the burning explains *why a person accepts* the objectively self-authenticating word, not *what makes* it authoritative.[^2]

### 2. The objective/subjective distinction — rate: moderate, and the cleanest exchange in the debate
Rogers: "Objectively, I'm saying this word is from God. The sun is shining whether anybody recognizes it or not. But it's because God gives people eyes that they can see the sun."[^2] This is a coherent Reformed account (objective Word + internal testimony of the Spirit). **Steelman of Horn:** the distinction is theologically tidy but *operationally* useless for the canon question — it still gives no public, shareable criterion by which a sincere Muslim's "objective Quran + Allah gives eyes" claim can be adjudicated against the Christian's. The dispute reduces to *which* book objectively shines, which returns to [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]].

### 3. The self-contained-God disambiguator — rate: Rogers' best counter
Rogers breaks the parity in his closing: the Quran's god swears by created things and Mormonism's god is an exalted man, so neither is self-contained and neither book can self-attest — whereas only the aseity-possessing triune God can speak self-referentially.[^3] **Assessment:** this is a *theological* (not experiential) disanalogy that genuinely distinguishes the cases — but it presupposes Christian theism, so it persuades insiders more than it adjudicates between traditions for a neutral inquirer.

## Best Quotes
> [Horn] "He's essentially appealing to what would be a burning in the bosom ... I've heard people who are in jail read the Quran and it does that to them or the Book of Mormon or Christian Science."[^1]

> [Rogers] "That's not the same thing as the self-authenticating nature of scripture. This refers to *why* somebody will accept that word which is self-authenticating. It's because of the operation of the spirit in the heart."[^2]

> [Rogers] "The light is shining. This is God's light. And those whose hearts he operates on experience it to the saving of their soul. And yes, it's called a burning in their heart. That's not Mormonism."[^2]

> [Rogers] "I was in a jail cell when this word of God came into it and pierced my soul to believe unto its salvation. I didn't need a pope. I didn't need a magisterium."[^4]
