# White to Pure Edit Tracks the Priesthood Ban Not Revelation

## The Claim

**Objection (the Christian critic):** The change of "white and delightsome" to
"pure and delightsome" in 2 Nephi 30:6 was not driven by revelation but by social
pressure — it followed shortly after "the lifting of the priesthood ban," when
racially charged language in scripture had become embarrassing. The timing
implies a human, public-relations motive inconsistent with a divinely fixed
text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The timing correlation

The edit's proximity to the change in the church's racial policy is the heart of
the argument: a revealed text being revised to fit shifting attitudes about race
looks like a document accommodating its audience.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Suggestive. Correlation in timing is real, but the
> word "white" had appeared (and was edited) in earlier editions as well, so the
> causal story is not airtight.

## Steelman of the Opposing View (the LDS reply)

Saints Unscripted answers that "white" and "pure" are near-synonyms in idioms of
ceremonial/moral purity, so the substitution clarifies rather than changes
meaning — and that the deeper question (can revealed text be edited?) is resolved
by a dynamic model of revelation. The strongest LDS point is that the original
"white" never carried the modern racial sense the critic imports. See
[[White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning]] and
[[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin]].

## Best Quotes

> "This change doesn't seem motivated by revelation. It seems motivated by the
> lifting of the priesthood ban just a few years earlier." — the critic[^1]
