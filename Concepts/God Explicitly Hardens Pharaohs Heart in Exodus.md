# God Explicitly Hardens Pharaoh's Heart in Exodus

## The Claim
McClellan's central exegetical claim: the book of Exodus "repeatedly and explicitly describes God hardening Pharaoh's heart." The hardening is not an automatic effect of glory on a badly-curated heart but a **selective, time-specific act that God performs at particular moments for particular reasons.** "It's something that God selectively does to individuals, whether Pharaoh or Pharaoh and his officials or the Egyptian people."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **[[Exodus 4.21|Exodus 4:21]]** — Before any plague: "I will harden his heart so that he will not let the people go." God announces the hardening in advance as his own plan.[^2] (Strong — pre-emptive, first-person divine declaration.)
2. **[[Exodus 7.3-5|Exodus 7:3-5]]** — "But I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and I will multiply my signs and wonders... The Egyptians shall know that I am the Lord."[^3] (Strong — ties hardening directly to God's purpose.)
3. **[[Exodus 9.12|Exodus 9:12]]** — "But the Lord hardened the heart of Pharaoh, and he would not listen to them, just as the Lord had spoken to Moses." Narrated fulfillment of the announced plan.[^4] (Strong — narrator, not Pharaoh, names the cause.)
4. **[[Exodus 10.1-2|Exodus 10:1-2]]** — "Go to Pharaoh, for I have hardened his heart and the heart of his officials." God claims responsibility outright.[^5]
5. **[[Exodus 14.4|Exodus 14:4]]** / **[[Exodus 14.17-18|Exodus 14:17-18]]** — At the Red Sea: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart and he will pursue them so that I will gain glory for myself," extended even to "the hearts of the Egyptians."[^6] (Strong — shows the hardening is repeated and broadened, not a one-off.)
6. **Selective, not automatic.** "The hardening of these hearts is not something that is just constantly ongoing because these people have poorly curated the soil of their hearts. It's something that God selectively does."[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing reading.** Defenders note the Hebrew uses *three different verbs* (חזק *ḥazaq* "strengthen/make firm," כבד *kāḇēḏ* "make heavy," קשׁה *qāshâ* "make hard/stubborn") and that early plagues sometimes have Pharaoh hardening *himself* before God does — suggesting a narrative arc where God ratifies Pharaoh's own resolve. This is a real literary observation. **But** it does not rescue the wax/clay analogy: even granting the arc, the text still has *God* as the explicit causal agent at the climactic moments and states *God's* purposes, which is all McClellan needs.

## Best Quotes
> "It's something that God selectively does to individuals, whether Pharaoh or Pharaoh and his officials or the Egyptian people. It's something that God is selectively doing for the expressed explicit purpose of bringing about God's own rhetorical goals." [^1]

> "The Lord is the one who did this. If the Lord had not stepped in to do this, Pharaoh would have let them go." [^7]

> "It's not just naturally happening to Pharaoh at any given point. It's something that God causes to happen at specific points. And God is explaining that it is for specific reasons." [^8]
