# The Compass Without a Calibrator

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Hayden's closing analogy crystallizing the **authorization** argument: declaring the Bible "infallible and ultimate" without a divine designation "is like pointing to a compass and declaring it infallible without ever establishing who calibrated it or when."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Usefulness ≠ authorization or infallibility — rate: strong
"The compass may still be useful. It may even point in the right direction most of the time. But usefulness is not the same thing as authorization or infallibility."[^2] **Assessment:** the analogy is the memorable distillation of the whole negative case — it concedes the Bible's practical reliability (a working compass) while denying the two disputed properties absent a calibrator (a divine authorizer). It pairs with the maxim Hayden repeats: "Saying something is true doesn't make it true."[^3]

### 2. The expectation-of-evidence argument — rate: moderate
"If God intended the modern Protestant canon to function as the infallible and ultimate authority for all Christians, we would reasonably expect God to tell us that clearly, publicly, and unambiguously. Today, that evidence was not produced."[^4] **Steelman of Kelly:** an argument from silence is defeasible — God may calibrate the "compass" *providentially* through history and the church's reception rather than by a dramatic announcement; absence of a recorded proclamation is not proof of absence of authorization. Hayden's reply (running throughout) is that a *fallible* process cannot certify an *infallible* result. See [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]].

### 3. "Sincerity is not the same as authorization" — rate: strong closer
Hayden ends by granting Kelly's sincerity and the Bible's inspiration/authority while maintaining the specific claims were never grounded: "I respect his sincerity, but sincerity is not the same as authorization."[^5] **Assessment:** a disciplined concession-and-deny structure that keeps the negative within its narrow burden.

## Best Quotes
> "Calling something the ultimate authority without divine designation is like pointing to a compass and declaring it as infallible without ever establishing who calibrated it or when."[^1]

> "The compass may still be useful... But usefulness is not the same thing as authorization or infallibility."[^2]

> "I respect his sincerity, but sincerity is not the same as authorization."[^5]
