# The Threefold Pattern of the Christian Sermon

## The Point
Heal extracts from Jesus's sermon at the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:16–22) a threefold structure that "appears to have been influential in shaping the sermon in the Christian tradition": (1) a liturgical setting, (2) a reading of scripture preceding and grounding the sermon, and (3) the application of that scripture to the present moment of the congregation.

## Evidence
1. **Liturgical setting.** Jesus's "gracious words" are spoken "as part of a formal and structured religious worship occurring in a building dedicated to the worship of and teaching about God." Though Jesus preached in many settings, "here we see the usual practice of preaching in the context of a worship service, a practice that became normative within both early Judaism and Christianity." [^1]
2. **Scripture precedes the sermon.** "The sermon is preceded by and based on a reading of scripture." Whether the Isaiah reading was part of a fixed cycle or chosen by Jesus is unclear, but "the pattern of reading the scriptures as part of the weekly worship service quickly became established." [^1]
3. **Application to the present.** "Jesus applies the scripture to his setting, in this case by proclaiming the fulfillment of a prophecy in the moment of the sermon." The scriptures thereby "cease to be embers from the past and instead become the living fire of prophecy speaking directly within the presence of the congregation." [^1]
4. **Persistence of the pattern.** "The early Christian homily (another name for a sermon) continued to follow this threefold pattern seen in the preaching of Jesus," embodying (quoting Philip Rousseau) "an urgent engagement on the pastor's side and a release of spiritual energy among the people he addressed." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "The scriptures, in the course of the sermon, cease to be embers from the past and instead become the living fire of prophecy speaking directly within the presence of the congregation." [^1]

> "The sermon fans this fire of prophecy, spreading the message of Christ among the congregation to instruct and inspire them in their discipleship." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The "application to the present" element — scripture made into "living fire" that speaks directly to the gathered congregation — closely parallels the LDS practice of "likening the scriptures unto us" (1 Nephi 19:23), which the chapter's surrounding material repeatedly echoes.
