# The Angel of Yahweh Bears the Divine Name

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that in the Hebrew Bible a distinct divine being — the **Angel of Yahweh** — *bears the name of Yahweh* and so can appear and speak *as* Yahweh. In [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20-21]] God sends an angel "since his name, my name, is in him," and in [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]] it is "the angel of the LORD" who appears in the burning bush, even though the text then has God speak the "I AM." This name-bearing pattern parallels how the NT gives Jesus the divine Name. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **Exodus 23:20-21 explicitly puts the Name *in* the angel.** "Do not rebel against him... since his name, my name, is in him." The angel has authority to *represent* Yahweh and even to forgive (or not forgive) rebellion. *Rating: strong textual basis* for a name-bearing agent. [^3]

2. **Exodus 3:2 introduces the bush theophany as "the angel of the LORD."** Most readers leap to the "I AM" (Yahweh) of 3:14, but "go up to 3:2" — it is an *angel* in the flames. *Rating: an accurate observation* about a real exegetical seam in the text. [^4][^2]

3. **The "I AM" (*ehyeh asher ehyeh*) is dynamic, not Thomistic.** Ostler argues it means "I will be / I will become what I will become" — a verb of forward movement — not the static *actus purus* / unmoved-mover of classical theism. *Rating: a legitimate philological dispute*; the imperfect *ehyeh* does carry future/dynamic force, against the "self-existent Being" gloss. [^5]

4. **Steelman of the opposing reading.** (a) The "angel/Yahweh" alternation may be a redactional seam — Ostler himself entertains that the angel is an *interpolation* (though he rejects it as the best view), and the classical/Trinitarian reads it as the *pre-incarnate Christ* (the Angel of the LORD Christophany), which is *also* a "name-bearing divine person" reading but lands on consubstantiality. (b) "His name is in him" can mean the angel acts with delegated *authority* without implying a separate god. *Rating: fair* — the data supports a distinct name-bearing agent, but whether that agent is a *separate* deity or the Son within one Godhead is underdetermined; Ostler grants the text is "fairly sophisticated" and he is "not certain how best to resolve" the tension. [^6][^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Since his name my name is in him. Now the angel has power to represent Yahweh." [^3]

> "There the angel of the Lord appeared to him in flames of fire from within the bush. He's like, 'What? It's an angel?'" [^4]

> "I am means I will be I will become what I will become. It's actually more of a verb about moving forward... It's not the kind of thing they're talking about at all." [^5]

> "It's the angel of Yahweh who bears the name of Yahweh... for all intents and purposes, he is Yahweh to Moses in that moment because he's acting as Yahweh's name bearer." [^8]
