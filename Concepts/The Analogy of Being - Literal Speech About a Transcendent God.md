# The Analogy of Being - Literal Speech About a Transcendent God

## The Point
Before deriving any divine attribute, Feser must answer a prior problem: if God is purely actual, simple, immaterial, immutable, and timeless — radically unlike anything we experience — how can the words we use of creatures ("exists," "powerful," "good," "knows") apply to Him at all? [^1] His answer is the *analogy of being*. Terms can be used univocally (same sense), equivocally (unrelated senses), or **analogically** (related but not identical senses). [^2] God-talk is neither univocal (that would make God just another being like us) nor equivocal (that would empty the words of content and collapse into agnosticism), but analogical — and crucially, *analogical predication is still literal*, not metaphorical. [^3] This is the linguistic engine that lets classical theism make positive, true claims about a transcendent God.

## Evidence
1. **Three uses of terms.** "Fido is a dog" / "Rover is a dog" is univocal; "a bat in the attic" / "swung the bat" is equivocal; "this wine is good" / "George is a good man" is analogical — "not exactly the same … but the two uses are not utterly different and unrelated either." [^2]
2. **"Being" itself is analogical.** Feser grounds this in act and potency: potentiality is real (so not equivocally "being") yet really distinct from actuality (so not univocally "being"); "the only sense remaining is an analogical sense." [^4] Being "does not name a genus," so substance, attribute, essence-distinct-from-existence, and God (whose essence *is* existence) are not species of a common "being." [^5]
3. **Why not pure apophaticism.** If we could speak of God only univocally, we could say only what He is *not* — "a purely apophatic or negative theology." But then "how can we so much as say that God exists … or that he causes things"? [^6] Negative theology is "part of the story … but … cannot be the whole story, or it would undermine the very arguments that led us to affirm that there is a God." [^6]
4. **Why not equivocation.** If "God has power" used "power" in a wholly different sense, "no one has ever explained exactly what it means" — we are left with "agnosticism, or … even atheism." [^7]
5. **Nonunivocal ≠ nonliteral.** The analogy of attribution and of proper proportionality "are literal uses of language, not metaphorical uses. 'Nonunivocal' does not entail 'nonliteral.'" [^8] Feser notes physics likewise stretches language (wave-particles, curved space) and is best read as analogical. [^9]

## Best Quotes
> "The analogical use of terms is an intermediate kind of usage." [^2]

> "Confining ourselves to univocal and equivocal terms would make it impossible to assign any positive content to what we say about God. We would be left with agnosticism, or … even atheism." [^7]

> "The analogy of attribution and the analogy of proper proportionality are literal uses of language, not metaphorical uses. 'Nonunivocal' does not entail 'nonliteral.'" [^8]

## LDS Connection
The analogy of being is the indispensable hinge of classical theism: it is what allows Feser to call a simple, timeless, immaterial God "powerful," "good," and "loving" while denying that these mean in God what they mean in us. It is also, quietly, where much of the Restoration's disagreement is decided in advance. Latter-day Saints can grant Feser's logical point — that univocal-only or equivocal-only theology fails — yet observe that the *need* for thoroughgoing analogy is generated by the prior commitment to a radically transcendent, simple God. If God the Father is instead an exalted, embodied person who shares a kind with His children (D&C 130:22), then much God-language can be far more *univocal* than classical theism allows: God's love, knowledge, and personhood are the same *kind* of thing as ours, raised to perfection, not merely analogous to them. The Restoration thus reads the analogy of being as a downstream necessity of the Hellenized God-concept — once you accept divine simplicity ([[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]), you *must* speak only analogically, and apophatic negation ([[Apophatic Theology Describes God by Negation]]) becomes central. To be fair to Feser, the doctrine is not a dodge: it is a careful, ancient theory of predication, and his insistence that analogy is *literal* (not metaphor) blocks the easy charge that classical theists are "just speaking poetically." The genuine fork is upstream — over whether the first cause is the simple, purely actual One that requires analogy, or the personal Father whose attributes can be named more directly (see [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]]).
