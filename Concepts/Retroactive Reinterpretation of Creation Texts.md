# Retroactive Reinterpretation of Creation Texts

#type/claim

## The Claim

Once Tatian, Theophilus of Antioch, and Irenaeus originated the doctrine of creation ex nihilo in the late 2nd century CE, they — and subsequent Christian tradition — retroactively reinterpreted earlier Jewish and Christian texts (2 Maccabees, the Shepherd of Hermas, Romans, Hebrews, Philo) through the new doctrinal lens. Texts that originally reflected the conventional Greco-Roman view of creation from pre-existent material substrate were reread as affirming creation from nothing. This retroactive reinterpretation became the normative interpretive framework, which is why modern apologists like Wes Huff read these texts and "see" creation ex nihilo — because the lens created by Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus is the one they inherited, not because the texts originally taught it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Mechanism: Originators Reinterpret Predecessors

> "They turn around and they look at all the things that came before — from the Shepherd of Hermas, from 2 Maccabees, from Romans, from Hebrews, from Philo, from these other texts — and they reinterpret what was endorsing the conventional wisdom about that pre-existing material substrate occupying the realm of non-being as assertions of creation out of nothing."[^2]

The originators of creation ex nihilo did not claim to be inventing something new — they claimed to be clarifying what was always there. But this claim is itself part of the reinterpretation: they read the new doctrine back into the old texts, producing a chain of "witnesses" that seemed to support it.

### 2. Origen as Case Study

Jonathan Goldstein demonstrated this dynamic with Origen (3rd century CE):

> "Origen took 2 Maccabees 7:22-29 as the first unequivocal statement in scripture of the doctrine of creation ex nihilo. In my commentary to the passage, however, I prove that Origen was mistaken. Though the passage displays profound philosophical knowledge, it does not assert creation ex nihilo."[^3]

Origen, writing a century after the doctrine was formulated, read 2 Maccabees through the lens of creation ex nihilo and "found" it there. But the text itself, in its own historical and philosophical context, teaches creation from pre-existent unformed matter — the standard view of Hellenistic Judaism.[^3]

### 3. Linguistic Shift: "Not Out of Existing Things" → "Out of Nothing"

The linguistic evidence tracks the reinterpretation:

- **2 Maccabees 7:28 (c. 100 BCE):** οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων (*ouk ex ontōn*) — "not out of things that exist." Means: not out of already-formed things; God formed the world from unformed matter.
- **Theophilus of Antioch (c. 169 CE):** ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων (*ex ouk ontōn*) — "out of things that are not." Means: out of absolute nothingness. Deliberately reverses the philosophical axiom.

The shift from negative particle + "existing things" to "things that are not" as the creative source is subtle in Greek but theologically momentous. Later readers, familiar only with the ex nihilo framework, read "not out of existing things" as equivalent to "out of nothing" — but the two formulations served different philosophical functions in their original contexts.

### 4. The "Constitution" Analogy

McClellan provides a modern analogy for how a concept's meaning shifts and then the new meaning is retrojected:

> "It's like the word 'constitution.' When the US Constitution was being composed, the word 'constitution' didn't mean a single document, a textual thing. It referred to the entire body of the form of government and the social contract and traditional approaches to natural law and things like that. It was only in the process of ratifying and then defending the constitution that 'constitution' came to be a word that referred specifically to the written manifestation of that body of concepts and ideas."[^4]

> "And now we think a constitution is a written text. But when they were writing the US Constitution, they did not think of 'constitution' as a written text. Things changed, and then that changed interpretive lens became the dominant interpretive lens."[^4]

The parallel: the late-2nd-century fathers changed what "creation" meant (from ordering pre-existent matter to generating matter from nothing), and that new meaning became so dominant that later readers cannot see the texts as meaning anything else — even though the original authors meant something different.

### 5. The Normative Lens Problem

> "And because Wes is not familiar with the scholarship, because Wes is not interested in questioning the dominant, the normative apologetic interpretive lenses, he cannot see that."[^5]

The key methodological point: the problem is not just that Huff is wrong about specific texts, but that he cannot SEE why he might be wrong because the interpretive lens through which he reads is the very framework the originators of the doctrine imposed. He reads 2 Maccabees 7:28 and "sees" creation ex nihilo because that's what the tradition trained him to see — not because the text teaches it.

### 6. The Scope of the Reinterpretation

McClellan identifies multiple texts that were retroactively reread:

- **2 Maccabees 7:28** — from "not out of existing things" to "out of nothing"[^2]
- **The Shepherd of Hermas** — from "out of non-being" (formless matter) to "out of nothing"[^6]
- **Romans 4:17** — from "calls into existence things that do not exist" (covenantal promise context) to metaphysical creation ex nihilo[^2]
- **Hebrews 11:3** — from "visible made from invisible" to creation from nothing[^2]
- **Philo of Alexandria** — from Middle Platonic ordering of pre-existent matter to proto-ex-nihilo[^2]
- **Genesis 1:1-2** — the *tohu wa-bohu* (formless and void) was original to the text, but later readers reinterpreted it as the initial state AFTER creation-from-nothing rather than the pre-existing chaos

### 7. This Is How Doctrinal Development Works

McClellan's point is not that the doctrine is "false" because it developed — it's that the development is historically traceable, and denying the development in favor of claiming the doctrine was always there produces bad readings of ancient texts. The doctrine was an authentic theological innovation responding to genuine philosophical challenges, and it became the normative Christian position. But the historical record shows it was NOT present in the texts the apologists claim it was.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

A defender of Huff's position might argue: This "retroactive reinterpretation" framework presupposes that the later doctrinal formulation is a distortion rather than a clarification. If God genuinely inspired Scripture, and the doctrine of creation ex nihilo is true, then the "seeds" were genuinely present in the earlier texts — even if the original authors didn't fully understand the implications of what they wrote. The later fathers were drawing out the sensus plenior (fuller sense) of Scripture under the Spirit's guidance, not imposing an alien framework. The fact that 2 Maccabees says God made the world "not out of things that exist" while the wider Greco-Roman world uniformly taught creation from pre-existent matter suggests the author WAS saying something distinctive — even if the precise philosophical formulation came later.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **Sensus plenior is a theological claim, not a historical one.** It may be true theologically that the "fuller sense" was always there — but historically, the texts meant what their authors and original audiences understood them to mean. And the original audiences of 2 Maccabees, Philo, Romans, and Hebrews lived in a world where "creation" meant ordering pre-existent matter.
2. **The linguistic evidence is clear.** "Not out of things that exist" (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων) and "out of things that are not" (ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων) are different formulations with different philosophical functions. Collapsing them into the same doctrine erases the actual historical development.
3. **The Wisdom of Solomon 11:17 is the smoking gun.** Written roughly contemporaneously with 2 Maccabees, it explicitly says God created the world "out of formless matter" (ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης). This is the standard Hellenistic Jewish view. It would be extraordinary for 2 Maccabees to teach creation ex nihilo while Wisdom of Solomon — from the same milieu — explicitly teaches creation from formless matter.
4. **The development is traceable and fits the historical pressures.** The doctrine emerges exactly when and where we would expect given the Gnostic and philosophical debates of the late 2nd century. It doesn't "pop into existence" randomly — it emerges for specific, identifiable reasons that make sense of why earlier texts didn't teach it.[^7]
