# Moody Coined Near-Death Experience and Ended the Lazarus Syndrome

## The Point
The very term **"near-death experience" did not exist before *Life After Life* (1975)** — Moody coined it [^1]. Before then, physicians filed the phenomenon under "the Lazarus Syndrome," a label implying it was the result of medical pathology, and patients themselves often had no name for it, fearing they were "mentally ill or had had hallucinations from drugs or a lack of oxygen to the brain" [^1]. By listening to survivors of cardiac arrest and naming what they reported, Moody converted a stigmatized, pathologized anomaly into a nameable, study-able category — producing what Morse calls a collective "aha" that something "very spiritual happens to us when we die" [^1].

## Evidence
1. **The term was new.** "Prior to the publication of *Life After Life*, the term *near-death experience* did not even exist" [^1].
2. **The old pathologizing label.** "Physicians called it 'the Lazarus Syndrome,' implying that it was the result of medical pathology" [^1].
3. **Patients had no category and feared ridicule.** Patients "often" thought "they were mentally ill or had had hallucinations from drugs or a lack of oxygen to the brain," and "people were afraid to talk about them for fear of being ridiculed," frequently doubting "the validity of what had happened to them" [^1].
4. **It is common, not rare.** George Gallup "estimated that 5 percent of the general population has had a near-death experience" [^1].
5. **Technology created the witnesses.** By the early 1970s "successful resuscitation from cardiac arrest was commonplace"; before modern intensive care "it was rare to survive clinical death," so Moody was "the first to recognize that these same patients could contribute to our understanding of the last few minutes of life" [^2].
6. **Naming reduced stigma.** "Today, no one has to feel ridiculed or mentally ill simply because they have been fortunate enough to have had a near-death experience" [^3].

## Best Quotes
> "Prior to the publication of *Life After Life*, the term *near-death experience* did not even exist. Physicians called it 'the Lazarus Syndrome,' implying that it was the result of medical pathology." [^1]

> "Once Dr. Moody took the time to actually listen to people who had survived heart attacks and so learned about their near-death experiences, our society had a sort of collective 'aha' and began to realize that something very spiritual happens to us when we die." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Moody's act of *listening to first-hand witnesses of the boundary of death and taking their testimony seriously* parallels the LDS posture toward revealed accounts of the spirit world — e.g., Joseph F. Smith's vision of the dead (D&C 138) and Alma's teaching that the spirit lives on consciously after the body dies (Alma 40:11–14). The naming and de-stigmatizing of NDE testimony made a spiritual reality discussable in a secular age, much as restored doctrine reopened serious discourse about conscious survival of death.
