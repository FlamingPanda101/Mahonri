# Sovereignty Was Privileged Over Love and Creation Ex Nihilo Drove It

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] argues that classical Christianity made **sovereignty**, not **love**, the supreme divine attribute — and that this was "not a coincidence" but emerged "simultaneously with the development of [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE|creation ex nihilo]]." Given a choice between "a God who can fashion like a carpenter a beautiful piece of furniture" (working from pre-existing material) and "a God who could just say 'light be,'" the church chose the commanding sovereign — because ex-nihilo creation maximizes God's power and *distance*. Love "is about intimacy and connection and interrelatedness"; "sovereignty is about distance." Givens grants God *is* sovereign but denies God "thinks that's his most important attribute."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The two-gods framing — rate: vivid, rhetorically central.** Givens opens and closes the interview on it: "Given a choice of two gods, by which one will you be more struck? A god who can fashion like a carpenter, or a god who could just say 'light be'?" The carpenter-God (organizing pre-existent matter, the [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1]]/Timaeus picture) is the one Latter-day Saints retain; ex nihilo selects the other. See [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]].[^1]
2. **Motive history of ex nihilo — rate: moderate (contested intellectual history).** Givens reads the doctrine's victory as "the reaction against Gnosticism" *plus* a preference for sovereignty over love. He dates its ascendancy: "creation ex nihilo... didn't become orthodoxy until the third going into the fourth century; until that time you had divided opinion, but it was quite common to believe that God worked out of pre-existing materials." This matches the vault's [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE|late-2nd-century-origin]] thesis.[^3]
3. **Sovereignty = distance, illustrated — rate: evocative.** Givens: "[[Anselm of Canterbury|Kierkegaard]]" — actually he cites **Kierkegaard** — "says there is an infinite qualitative difference between God and us. I don't see what purposes that serves other than a kind of abject humility and difference, which has nothing to do with paternal love." He adds the medieval datum that clasped hands as the posture of prayer were "borrowed from the public display of obeisance to a sovereign."[^2][^4]
4. **The privileging is theological, not merely historical — rate: a value claim.** A God whose "paramount quality" is love is intimate; a God whose paramount quality is sovereignty is remote, "the king keep[ing] his distance from the courtiers." Givens makes the LDS countermove: God's "capacity to love infinitely" is what God himself treats as supreme. This links to the critique that glory-centered theology is self-regarding — see [[Hell for Gods Glory Is Narcissism Not Love]].[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing (classical) side:** Ex nihilo was not chosen to inflate power but to secure two non-negotiables: God's *aseity* (he depends on nothing, including no co-eternal matter) and his *transcendence* (he is not one item among the furniture of the world). On this view sovereignty and love are not rivals but identical in the simple God — his love *is* sovereign and his sovereignty *is* love, and Kierkegaard's "infinite qualitative difference" guards grace (only an utterly transcendent God can stoop in the Incarnation without it being self-promotion). A craftsman-God shaping pre-existent stuff is, the classical theist says, a finite demiurge, not the Creator — and a God constrained by uncreated matter is *less* free to love, not more. See the vault's natural-theology treatments under [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]].

**Counter-objection (Givens's rejoinder):** Aseity can be preserved without ex nihilo (God remains the uncreated organizer and source of all order), and "transcendence" purchased at the price of "abject... difference" buys nothing a *father* needs. The tell, for Givens, is the historical *timing*: sovereignty-language and ex-nihilo crystallize together, which is what you'd expect if the doctrine's function was to elevate distance over intimacy rather than merely to refute Gnostic dualism.

## Best Quotes
> "Given a choice of two gods, by which one will you be more struck? A god who can fashion like a carpenter a beautiful piece of furniture, or a god who could just say 'light be'? It's not a coincidence that the privileging of the quality of sovereignty over love emerges simultaneously with the development of creation ex nihilo theology."[^1]

> "If the paramount quality of God is love, then that's about intimacy and connection and interrelatedness. Sovereignty is about distance."[^2]

> "Now look, I believe God is sovereign of the universe, but I don't believe he thinks that's his most important attribute. I think he believes that his capacity to love infinitely is."[^2]

> "It's only in the Middle Ages that the clasped hands become the symbol of petitionary prayer, because it's borrowed from the public display of obeisance to a sovereign."[^4]
