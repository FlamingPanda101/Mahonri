# Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]] locates the origin of **sola scriptura** in a power struggle, not a fresh exegetical discovery. Because the medieval fourfold sense (see [[The Fourfold Sense of Scripture]]) left meaning "kind of up in the air," the church became the "ultimate authority" over interpretation, which "endows the institution with an awful lot of authority, an awful lot of power." [^1] The Reformers pushed back by insisting "the sense needs to be located in the actual text itself," collapsing the four senses toward the text. "Sola scriptura comes out of the Reformation period and the rhetoric against the church's authority." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The motive was authority, not literalism per se.** The complaint of the reformers was "the church has too much authority over what the scriptures have to say," and sola scriptura was an "attempt to kind of wrestle that back." [^1][^2] The doctrine is "the idea that the scripture only is the authority... the scripture alone is the sole and sufficient authority." [^3] See [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]]. **(Strong as historical framing.)**
2. **It was mostly about faith and salvation, not cosmology.** "In this period a lot of folks are like related to faith and salvation, not necessarily related to the origins of the earth or... the genetic origins of humanity." [^4] When pressed, the reformers' sola scriptura was "really mostly about matters pertaining to faith and salvation," not a charter for young-earth science. **(Strong — corrects a common assumption.)**
3. **Luther and Calvin were not proto-literalists.** [[Dan Beecher]] expected literalism to "start... in finding its footholds" with [[Martin Luther|Luther]] and [[John Calvin|Calvin]], "and it turns out not — they still had plenty of allowance for metaphor and poetry and figurative speech... and a nonliteral view of cosmology." [^5][^6] The reformers moved the literal/figurative threshold only modestly. See [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]]. **(Strong — Luther and Calvin both read Genesis cosmology accommodationally.)**
4. **Steelman.** A Protestant would object that sola scriptura is not a mere power grab but a recovery of the proper norm (Scripture as the *norma normans*), and that locating meaning in the grammatical-historical sense is exegetically sound, not reactionary. McClellan's reply is that even on its own terms the doctrine relocated, rather than removed, the interpretive-authority problem: someone still decides which sense is "the" scriptural one. See [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]] and the existing [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]]. **(Moderate — the historical claim is strong; the theological evaluation is contestable.)**

## Best Quotes
