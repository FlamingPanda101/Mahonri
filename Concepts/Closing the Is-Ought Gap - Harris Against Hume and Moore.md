# Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore

## The Point
Harris attacks the most famous barrier to his project: David Hume's claim that no description of how the world *is* can establish how we *ought* to behave, and G. E. Moore's "naturalistic fallacy" / "open question argument" (it always seems sensible to ask, of any natural property proposed as the good, "but is *that* good?").[^1] Harris argues this "verbal trap is easily avoided when we focus on human well-being."[^2] If we define "good" as "that which supports well-being," then "the regress initiated by Moore's 'open question argument' really does stop": while it is sensible to ask whether a given pleasure is good, "it makes no sense at all to ask whether maximizing well-being is 'good.'"[^2]

## Evidence
Harris reframes "ought" so that the gap never opens. The notion that oughts can be justified "only in terms of other 'oughts,' never in terms of facts" is, he says, "an artificial and needlessly confusing way to think about moral choice," even "a dismal product of Abrahamic religion" that now constrains atheists.[^3] His deflation: "to say that we ought to treat children with kindness seems identical to saying that everyone will tend to be better off if we do."[^3] He presses three further points. First, the **divide is illusory in three senses**: well-being claims reduce to facts about brains; the very idea of objective knowledge "has values built into it"; and beliefs about facts and values "seem to arise from similar processes at the level of the brain."[^4] Second, the **bootstrapping / tu quoque**: you cannot prove that we *should* value evidence or logic without already using them — "what evidence could prove that we should value evidence?" — yet no one thinks this makes science unscientific, so why demand more of morality?[^5] Third, he notes Moore explicitly and sides against him, denying he is positing "the Platonic Form of the Good" or that "certain actions are intrinsically wrong."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "While I think this verbal trap is easily avoided when we focus on human well-being, most scientists and public intellectuals appear to have fallen into it." [^2]

> "It makes no sense at all to ask whether maximizing well-being is 'good.'" [^2]

> "To say that we ought to treat children with kindness seems identical to saying that everyone will tend to be better off if we do." [^3]

> "What evidence could prove that we should value evidence? What logic could demonstrate the importance of logic?" [^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris does not so much *close* the is–ought gap as *define* it shut — and the definition is the disputed premise. **(1) Stipulation, not derivation.** Saying "let 'good' mean 'what supports well-being'" makes "maximizing well-being is good" trivially true *by fiat*; Moore's question simply migrates to the definition: "but is *well-being* what we ought to maximize?" — which is perfectly coherent. The gap reopens one level up (see [[You Cannot Get From Facts to Values Without an Interpretive Structure]]). **(2) "Ought" = "better off" loses the obligation.** Harris's reduction ("we ought to be kind" = "everyone will tend to be better off") converts a *categorical* moral duty into a *hypothetical/instrumental* one — what to do *if* you want a certain outcome. That is exactly the move analyzed (and questioned) in [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]: instrumental advice toward an end you happen to share is not yet a binding duty toward *others*, which is what morality means (see [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]). **(3) The bootstrapping point actually helps the theist.** Harris is right that evidence and logic must be valued *prior* to any proof — but that is the apologist's argument that rational and moral norms are *given*, not generated by physics, and so point beyond the material order to a rational ground of being ([[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]]). On naturalism the unprovable starting norms are brute and arbitrary; on theism they are the lawful structure of a created, intelligible, value-laden world ([[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]], [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]). Hume's gap is not a piece of "Abrahamic" confusion to be waved away — it is the standing reminder that values are not the kind of thing brute facts secrete, which is exactly why morality needs a ground that mere facts cannot supply.
