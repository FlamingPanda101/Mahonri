# Freedom of Speech and Religion Emerged From Conflict Not Doctrine

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]: "We definitely cannot ground in the Bible the idea of freedom of speech. You can be put to death for believing or worshiping the wrong thing." Freedom of speech and religion emerged from sectarian *conflict* and self-interest, not from biblical doctrine.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Bible is the wrong place to look.** In Scripture, "you can be put to death for worshiping the wrong thing"; O'Connor notes when God delivers monotheism to Israel, worshiping other gods is "immediately punishable by death."[^1] *(Strong textually — the OT is not a charter of religious liberty.)*

2. **Two competing developmental stories.** O'Connor frames the choice: either religious liberty is a *flourishing* of an idea inherent in Christianity (which he finds "extremely strange reading the Bible"), or it is a principle asserted *against* the Christian tradition as the church's authority waned — which fits the actual chronology (Reformation disputes → American church-state conflicts → secularization). See [[Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming]].[^2] *(Sharp dichotomy; the "against the tradition" story matches the timeline well.)*

3. **Locke excluded atheists.** [[John Locke]], the great theorist of toleration, thought atheists "couldn't be trusted" and couldn't make binding agreements — "so much for freedom of religion."[^3] *(Strong — undercuts the claim that the Christian theorist produced *modern* religious liberty.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener — the Christian backstory is undeniable.** Scrivener: free speech "was absolutely hammered out in Protestant-Catholic disputes," with John Milton and dissenters from the Church of England — "you cannot answer why America has a First Amendment without consulting Christian history." He grants the path runs through Christians "acting for Christian purposes," even if the modern result (freedom *from* religion, secularism) is "some considerable distance from the Christian origins."[^4] *(Strong — the historical actors were overwhelmingly Christian, and the secular outcome arguably grew out of intra-Christian conflict. The disagreement is whether "grew out of conflict among Christians" counts as "grounded in Christianity.")*

5. **Secularism as a post-Christian product.** Scrivener's later move: "the secular is definitely a Christian-y kind of thing... separation of church and state developed into freedom of religion, now taken to mean freedom *from* religion." Only "the only society on the planet that's ever thought of itself in religionless terms" could produce this.[^5] *(This is the unfalsifiability flashpoint — even secularism is folded back in. See [[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]].)*
