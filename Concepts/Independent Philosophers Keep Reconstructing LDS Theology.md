# Independent Philosophers Keep Reconstructing LDS Theology

## The Point
[[Alex Arnold]] (Moroni's Standard) argues that secular and non-LDS philosophers, "following their disciplines" with "no interest in defending the church," keep arriving independently at the distinctive positions of the Restoration. The frame is the agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]], who set out to test [[Sam Harris]]'s claim that Mormonism is the least plausible Christianity and concluded the reverse — that LDS theology, *because of what it removes from classical Christianity*, is "among the more philosophically defensible options on the table."[^1] Green's summary is the thesis in miniature: "If I didn't become a Mormon, but I did somehow convert to some form of Christianity, I'd probably just reinvent about 70% of Mormonism and then technically not be a Mormon."[^2] The argument is explicitly **comparative, not evidential**: the convergence "does not prove the LDS Church is true," but it "dismantles the Harris framing" that the restored doctrines are "obviously invented additions" — because positions reached "from multiple independent directions" are "most likely aren't the novelties of a con man" but "the conclusions of careful inquiry."[^3]

## Evidence
- **The roster of independent arrivals.** Arnold names, as non-LDS thinkers who reconstructed LDS-shaped positions from their own fields: [[Emerson Green]] (agnostic philosophy of religion); [[Dale Tuggy]] (the social Trinity — relational, not metaphysical, oneness — is the most defensible Trinitarianism, and the LDS Christ is "more philosophically coherent and more consistent with the New Testament"); [[David Ray Griffin]] (finite theism is "the only intellectually honest answer to the problem of evil," argued in *God, Power, and Evil*, 1976); [[Philip Goff]] (moved from atheism toward a God who "works within mathematical law rather than above it" on fine-tuning grounds); and [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] (the LDS framework "offers a more defensible answer to the problem of evil than classical Christianity").[^3][^4][^5][^6]
- **The removals, not additions, are the convergence.** The pattern only works because the Restoration *subtracts* the post-apostolic philosophical imports and lands on what predates them: creation *ex nihilo* (a second-century invention — see [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]] and [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]]); the Nicene Trinity (see [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]]); and eternal conscious torment. What is left is "a framework that returned to the pre-creedal, pre-edited theological baseline."[^7]
- **It is the same pattern biblical scholarship already shows.** Arnold ties the philosophers' convergence to the textual one — [[Michael Heiser]] on the divine council, [[Emanuel Tov]] and [[Frank Moore Cross]] on the theologically motivated editing of Deuteronomy 32, [[Bart Ehrman]] and [[Richard Bauckham]] on the diversity of early Christology. The vault's [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]] documents the parallel claim from the scholarly side.[^8]
- **The author's own road.** Arnold reaches the same place "through ancient history and archaeology" rather than philosophy: the God of "the oldest sources looked nothing like the God of the councils." He presents his story as a fourth independent vector converging with the philosophers, the biblical scholars, and the cosmologists.[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "He said that LDS theology, specifically because of what it removes from classical Christianity, is actually among the more philosophically defensible options on the table. He's not converting, but he followed the argument to where it led." [^1]

> "If I didn't become a Mormon, but I did somehow convert to some form of Christianity, I'd probably just reinvent about 70% of Mormonism and then technically not be a Mormon." [^2]

> "They're following philosophy, biblical theology, and physics and just arriving at structurally similar conclusions... What this convergence establishes is not that the church is true, but it establishes that the doctrinal framework that the restoration occupies is where serious independent inquiry keeps arriving." [^10]

## LDS Connection
This is a meta-argument *about* the distinctive doctrines rather than a defense of any one of them. Its restraint is its strength: Arnold concedes outright that "lots of people could converge on similar conclusions about God's nature without concluding that Joseph Smith was a prophet," and that "whether Joseph Smith reached them by revelation or by some other means is a separate question."[^11] The work the convergence does is narrowly to defeat the *a priori* dismissal — the assumption that the Restoration is self-evidently a 19th-century invention "plus some weird extra things." Each convergence has its own vault note: finite theism in [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]], the Godhead in [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]] and [[Why the Trinity vs Godhead Debate Actually Matters]], embodied/eternal-matter cosmology in [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]] and [[Modern Physics Is Consistent With Eternal Matter Not Ex Nihilo]], and pre-existence in [[Pre-Existence in Mainstream Christian Thought]]. The same "convergence, not proof" epistemic standard the vault applies to Book of Mormon historicity ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]) governs here.

**Steelman the critic.** (1) *Selection bias* — Arnold names the philosophers who land near LDS positions; the far larger number who do not (and remain atheists, or classical theists) is not weighed. (2) *Convergence underdetermines the source* — even granting the positions, "careful inquiry" is itself a rival explanation to revelation for how Joseph Smith reached them, which Arnold concedes. (3) *Partial overlap* — Green's "70%" and O'Connor's concession are about *God's nature*, not the Book of Mormon, priesthood, or Joseph Smith's prophethood, so the convergence is narrower than "reconstructing Mormonism."
