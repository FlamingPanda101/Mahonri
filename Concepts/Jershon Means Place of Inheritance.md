# Jershon Means "Place of Inheritance"

## The Claim
The place-name **Jershon** (better pronounced *Yarshon*) derives from Hebrew *yarash* ("to inherit, to possess") + the place-forming appellative *-on*, "place of inheritance." It appears precisely where the Nephites give the land to Lamanite converts **"for an inheritance"** ([[Alma 27.22-26|Alma 27]]), and the wordplay recurs at the Zoramite resettlement (Alma 35). [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The etymology.** Hebrew *yarash* = "to inherit/possess." Though *Jershon* is **not** attested in the biblical corpus, it analyzes cleanly as a standard Hebrew place-name. The *-on* ending is a known appellative emphasizing the quality of the root. Strength: **high** (transparent Hebrew morphology). [^1]
2. **The narrative pun.** [[Alma 27.22-26|Alma 27]] repeatedly stresses that this land Jershon is given "for an **inheritance**" to the converted Lamanites. The English "inheritance" tracks the Hebrew root invisible to an English reader — so this is *not* reducible to KJV vocabulary. Strength: **high**. [^2]
3. **Recurrence.** The same wordplay reappears in Alma 35 when Zoramite converts are settled with the Lamanites in Jershon. Repetition argues for design. Strength: **moderate-high**. [^3]
4. **Scholarly assessment.** [[John W. Welch]] (with Bob Smith) found this case "unavoidable" — a "slam dunk." Strength: **high** (endorsed by a careful scholar). [^4]

**Steelman:** A critic notes Jershon is *unattested*, so it's a reconstruction, not a found name; and that "inheritance" is a common biblical idea, so any land-grant story would use it. The strongest counter is that the pun rides on the Hebrew root *yarash* — a non-obvious, non-English link — and recurs in two separate resettlement contexts, which is hard to produce by accident from an English-only composition.
