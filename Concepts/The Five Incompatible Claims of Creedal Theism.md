# The Five Incompatible Claims of Creedal Theism

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Hayden Carroll]] formalizes the [[The Creation Dilemma]] as an inconsistent pentad. Classical creedal Christianity (Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant) affirms all five: **(1)** God is omniscient and knows the entire future with certainty; **(2)** God is omnipotent and perfectly free — nothing forces him to create; **(3)** God creates all persons from nothing (ex nihilo); **(4)** some people will end in hell / eternal conscious torment; **(5)** God nevertheless wills the good of every person. "These five claims cannot all be true at the same time, which requires rendering the debate resolution false."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Each claim is mainstream, not fringe** — rate: accurate. Hayden stresses these are "historic and affirmed across Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant traditions," so the contradiction is internal to creedal theism, not a strawman. *(doctrinal survey)* [^1]
2. **Exhaustive exit-options** — to dissolve the contradiction, exactly one must be revised: limit foreknowledge (deny 1), limit freedom (deny 2), reject creation ex nihilo (deny 3 — the LDS route, [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]]), deny eternal torment (deny 4), or redefine "good" (deny 5). "There is no sixth option." *(disjunctive-syllogism)* [^2]
3. **Cross-exam lock-in** — in cross-examination Hayden gets Gavin to affirm each premise individually (God knows the absolute future; God is all-powerful with nothing equal/superior; God freely creates or refrains; some go to hell eternally; hell is worse than heaven; God wills the good of all), then asks how (3)+(5) cohere — driving Gavin to "mystery." See [[Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good]]. *(elenctic method)* [^3]

**Steelman of the affirmative:** a classical theist can deny the pentad is *strictly* inconsistent by (a) deploying the *antecedent/consequent will* distinction (claim 5 means God antecedently wills all good, compatible with consequently permitting freely-chosen damnation), or (b) the *analogical predication* of "good" (claim 5's "good" is not univocal with human good — Thomism). Hayden treats both as covert revisions of claim 5 that "empty" the word *good*. The strongest classical reply is Molinism (middle knowledge: perhaps no feasible world has free creatures and no damned), which Hayden does not directly address.

## Best Quotes
> "These five claims cannot all be true at the same time, which requires rendering the debate resolution false. Something has to give."[^1]

> "Either foreknowledge must be limited, freedom must be limited, creation from nothing must be rejected, eternal torment must be denied, or good must be redefined. And there is no sixth option."[^2]
