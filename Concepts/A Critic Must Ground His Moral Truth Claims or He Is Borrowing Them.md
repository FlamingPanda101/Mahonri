# A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them

## The Point
When a critic attacks a religion for being *morally* wrong — patriarchal, unjust, "behind" on social issues — he is not merely reporting a preference; he is making an objective moral truth claim. That move requires a standard by which the accusation is *true* independent of anyone's opinion. [[Jacob Hansen]]'s strategy with the critic Bill Reel is to grant the accusations a hearing and instead audit their footing: on a naturalist worldview that denies human "rights" as "a myth," there is no objective moral law to ground the charge. The indignation is therefore borrowed capital — the critic helps himself to an objective morality his own framework cannot supply, the classic "stealing from God" move (cf. [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]). The debate is thus relocated from *is Mormonism wrong?* to *do you have any standard by which "wrong" means anything at all?*

## Evidence
Reel opens with a thick moral claim: rejecting the collective good because "we live in a patriarchy where certain groups of people have privilege" and use it to "place that trauma on other human beings."[^1] That is an objective accusation of injustice. Yet pressed on what undergirds it, Reel denies the very category that would do the grounding: "people don't have rights, that's a myth, that's as Harari would say."[^2] Hansen names the tension — a critic who can call privilege unjust but cannot say *why* anything is unjust has, in effect, smuggled in a standard he officially rejects.

Hansen makes the structure explicit by distinguishing *choosing* a morality from *grounding* one: he summarizes Reel's worry as "I'm subjectively choosing what is a good morality, and that only with God are we actually able to ensure that we have a morality that we know is good."[^3] The whole interview is an extended demonstration that without a transcendent anchor the critic's moral vocabulary — "trauma," "privilege," "unhealthy," "harm" — keeps presupposing an objective measure that the naturalist ontology has already denied him.

## Best Quotes
> "people don't have rights, that's a myth, that's as Harari would say that people have rights." [^2]

> "you're trying to pinpoint this idea that I'm subjectively choosing what is a good morality, and that only with God are we actually able to ensure that we have a morality that we know is good." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the apologetic payoff of the moral argument turned defensively: objective moral judgments require a transcendent ground, so a critic leveling them against the restored gospel is implicitly conceding the kind of moral realism that points toward God (see [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]; [[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]]). The Latter-day Saint version sharpens rather than blunts the point — morality is rooted in *eternal law* that God himself honors, so "wrong" is wrong against a fixed measure, not divine whim. To accuse the Church of injustice is to appeal to exactly the objective standard the secular critic's worldview cannot underwrite, which is itself evidence that the standard is real and external — the "borrowing" diagnosis (see [[Borrowing Objective Morality to Condemn Evil]]; [[Stealing the Category of Evil - Sitting in Gods Lap to Slap His Face]]).
