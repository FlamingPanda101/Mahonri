# The Codex Sinaiticus Marginal Note Postdates Jerome

#type/claim

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

[[John D. Meade]] (with [[Wes Huff]]) points to a **marginal note** on the page of **Codex Sinaiticus** (4th c.) containing [[Isaiah 9.6|Isaiah 9:6]]: the main column copies the Septuagint ("messenger of great counsel"), but the margin supplies a fairly literal rendering of the Hebrew ("Wonderful counselor, strong God, the mighty one, ruler of peace... father of the coming age"). Meade presents this like a study-Bible footnote and argues it shows that, *already in the 4th century* — when Christians supposedly cared only about the Septuagint — readers of Sinaiticus could see the Hebrew. The note is offered as evidence that concern for the Hebrew **predated Jerome**, making Jerome "the last link in a chain" rather than an innovator.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (McClellan's Rebuttal)

### 1. The first text-critical question was never asked: who wrote it, and when? (rating: strong; methodological)

When a text-critic sees a marginal note, "the very first question they ask is who added it and when." Meade never showed the main text *with the note visible* — and once you do, the note is plainly in a **different hand and different ink** from the main scribe. That alone forbids treating it as contemporary with the 4th-century transcription.[^2]

> "When someone with text critical training sees a manuscript that has a marginal note, the very first question they ask is who added it and when. That question was not asked about this marginal note." [^2]

### 2. The note is a later *correction*, by identified correctors (rating: strong; primary scholarship)

Ken Penner's commentary on LXX-Isaiah attributes the elements to distinct correctors: corrector **CA** added the Greek of the marginal note (matching the Hebrew), **CB** added the cross-signs (the little pluses), and corrector **D** added the red dots. These are later hands coming in to correct the manuscript — not the original production.[^3]

### 3. Scholarly consensus dates the C-correctors to the 5th–7th centuries — after Jerome (rating: strong; the decisive point)

The dating is secure and old:
- **Scrivener (1864)** — "the greater part of the changes... belong to C of about the 7th century."
- ***Scribes and Correctors of Codex Sinaiticus* (1938)** — the C-correctors "assigned by some to the fifth, by others to the 7th century."
- **Jongkind, *Scribal Habits of Codex Sinaiticus* (2013)** — the various C-correctors placed "around the 7th century."
- **Malik (2015)** — Milne & Skeat allowed "between the fifth and 7th century"; Skeat later dated CA specifically to the **6th century**, and Myshrall's paleography concurred.

So the note is dated **fifth-to-seventh century** (most likely late-5th-to-early-7th) — i.e. *after* Jerome's pivot from the priority of the Greek to the priority of the Hebrew (the *Hebraica Veritas*). The marginal note "did not exist prior to Jerome's argument."[^4]

> "We have a pretty secure scholarly consensus that the corrector CA... added that marginal note between the fifth and the 7th centuries CE... In other words, after Jerome had already affected that pivot." [^4]

### 4. Therefore it cannot be evidence for the thesis it was adduced to support (rating: strong)

If the note postdates Jerome by a century or more, it cannot show pre-Jerome Christian concern for the Hebrew. Meade's appeal "falls to pieces because it did not exist prior to Jerome in the 4th century when Codex Sinaiticus was first transcribed." Presenting a fifth-to-seventh-century correction as something "already there for Christians to consult" in the 4th century is, McClellan argues, an oversight no trained text-critic should make — and plausibly an intentional one.[^5]

### 5. The fallback (Eusebius) does not rescue it

McClellan pre-empts the move to [[Eusebius of Caesarea|Eusebius]]: yes, Eusebius noted that the Hebrew of Isaiah 9:6 works better for Christians — but everyone has always known that, and it was a *flagged exception* to his otherwise pro-Septuagint position, not evidence against the consensus that Jerome began something new. Rufinus, Augustine and others "argued fervently with [Jerome] to abandon this silly notion that the Hebrew was superior to the Greek."[^6]

## LDS Relevance

The four titles of Isaiah 9:6 ("Mighty God," "Everlasting Father") are load-bearing in Trinitarian apologetics, and apologists often reinforce them with appeals to manuscript antiquity. Showing that the headline manuscript "proof" of early Christian concern for the Hebrew here is actually a post-Jerome correction blunts an argument frequently deployed against the LDS reading of these as **throne-names** rather than ontological identifications — and models the text-critical discipline (date your evidence before you use it) that Latter-day Saint apologetics can apply to such claims.

## Best Quotes

> "That marginal note did not exist prior to Jerome's argument for the Hebraica Veritas. And so... that argument falls to pieces because it did not exist prior to Jerome in the 4th century when Codex Sinaiticus was first transcribed." [^5]

> "They seem to want the viewer to understand that this marginal note is contemporary with the transcription of Codex Sinaiticus... I have a hard time believing that that was not intentional." [^7]
