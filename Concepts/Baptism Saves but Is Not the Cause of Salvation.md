# Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation

#type/claim

## The Claim
**[[Gavin Ortlund]]** argues that Scripture can rightly say "baptism saves" (1 Peter 3:21) *without* baptism being the **efficient cause** of regeneration. The biblical language is **metonymy**: baptism names and stands in for the larger reality it visibly represents — faith, union with Christ, forgiveness — the way "the blood of Jesus saves" names a whole saving event (Christ's death as a person, not the literal fluid), or the way a coronation is "when the monarch begins to reign" without *causing* the reign. So baptism genuinely conveys grace and is more than a symbol; it is just not the moment one passes from spiritual death to spiritual life.[^1] **[[Trent Horn]]** rejects this: baptism is the *primary cause* of regeneration (faith being a necessary associated condition, since forced baptism is not salvific), and the metonymy reading needs an independent argument before it can be imported.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The blood-of-Jesus parallel (Evidence: linguistic, strong by analogy).** When Scripture says we are saved "by the blood of Jesus," no one means the fluid as distinct from his flesh and death; it is the whole person and event named by a part. Ortlund proposes "baptism saves" works identically — baptism named as representative of the events leading up to it.[^3] *Strength: a recognized figure of speech (synecdoche/metonymy) that all parties already grant for "blood."* Horn's rebuttal is precisely that the parallel is not automatic — "I would need another powerful argument that it's not just baptism," because for "blood" we have independent reason to know it is not only the fluid.[^2]

2. **The coronation analogy (Evidence: illustrative).** A coronation is "not technically the cause of when they become the monarch but it's the kind of formal visible public expression of that and can be spoken of as such." So salvific language attaches to the public rite without making it the cause.[^1] *Strength: shows ordinary speech routinely calls a public ceremony by the name of the reality it ratifies.*

3. **The circumcision parallel (Evidence: scriptural, structural).** Sign and thing-signified slide into each other in the OT: a Jew could say "be circumcised, coming to the living God" — meaning *become a Jew*, of which circumcision is the sign, salvation part of the package — without circumcision *causing* the relationship. [[Romans 2.29|Romans 2:29]] ("circumcision is a matter of the heart, by the Spirit") teaches what the physical rite signifies. Ortlund applies the same sign/signified logic to baptism.[^4] Horn answers that baptism is deliberately *superior* to circumcision and really does effect what it signifies ([[Colossians 2.11-12|Colossians 2:11-12]]), so the parallel breaks.[^5]

4. **The "cause" is multivalent (Evidence: conceptual).** Both men agree the whole dispute hinges on the word *cause*, which "can be parsed in so many ways." Ortlund is willing to call baptism *a* cause in a representative sense and even say "baptism saved Cornelius"; Horn calls it a *primary* cause. The disagreement is over *which kind* of causation 1 Peter 3:21 asserts — and the verse itself frames baptism as "an appeal to God," not the mechanical act in the water.[^1] *Strength: reframes an apparent flat contradiction as a dispute about causal categories.*

**Steelman of the opposing view (Horn / baptismal regeneration):** The natural reading of 1 Peter 3:21 ("baptism now saves you") and the relentless patristic association of baptism with the *remission* of sins favor baptism as the actual instrument of regeneration, not a mere label for something already complete. Metonymy is a real figure, but invoking it here looks ad hoc — a way to retain the biblical word "saves" while denying its plain referent. For "the blood of Jesus" we have independent doctrinal reason to read a part-for-whole; for baptism the burden is to show the same, and pointing to circumcision actually cuts the other way, since the New Covenant sign is repeatedly presented as accomplishing what the Old Covenant sign only foreshadowed (Col 2:11-12; Titus 3:5; Acts 22:16). On Horn's view the position avoids two errors at once: the "mere symbol" reductionism *and* a magical hyper-*ex opere operato* in which grace flows automatically apart from faith — Catholic theology requires the recipient to yield in faith for the baptismal grace to bear fruit. The metonymy thesis, however ingenious (Horn calls it exactly that — "ingenious... a lot of explanatory power"), therefore does not by itself defeat the regenerationist reading; it relocates the whole question onto the meaning of *cause*.

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint position sits closer to Horn than to Ortlund on the *efficacy* of the ordinance, but reframes it: baptism by proper priesthood authority is *required* for the remission of sins (Acts 2:38; Moroni 8:25–26; 3 Nephi 11), not a metonym for a prior, faith-only regeneration. Where Ortlund's metonymy move dissolves baptism's causal role, the Restoration *intensifies* it — extending saving baptism even to the dead through proxy (1 Corinthians 15:29; D&C 128), which presupposes that the ordinance itself does real saving work that mere prior faith could not supply. Ortlund's argument, if accepted, would undercut *both* baptismal regeneration *and* baptism for the dead in one stroke; Horn's regenerationism shares the LDS premise that the rite is causally necessary while denying the LDS claims about authority and postmortem application. See [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]] and [[Marcionites Practiced Proxy Baptism for the Unbaptized Dead]].
