# The Surviving Book of Mormon Artifacts Are Documentary Not Sacred

## The Point

Griffiths's constructive move is to redirect the artifact question away from the missing relics toward what *does* survive: a set of **documentary artifacts** that "maybe make it less of a slam dunk, but still allow us to create a functional narrative."[^1] These are not the sword or the plates but paper-and-stone items from the translation process — and crucially, they can be *tested against the descriptions* the participants gave. The evidentiary value is not "here is a holy object" but "here is a physical thing whose features match the account."

## Evidence

- **The original manuscript (~28%).** Placed in a Nauvoo cornerstone to preserve it, water seeped in and destroyed all but about 28%; what remains "for the most part" matches the descriptions Joseph Smith and others gave of the process.[^2]
- **The printer's manuscript.** The earliest *full* copy, made by [[Oliver Cowdery]] and Hyrum Smith as a backup "just in case anything went wrong during the printing process" — it offers "a lot of clues as to the structure of the Book of Mormon and its translation."[^3]
- **No punctuation — a confirmatory tell.** Both manuscripts have *no* punctuation. Griffiths: if Joseph were simply composing, "it seems like he'd put in a few periods, semicolons"; but an Egyptian/Semitic source document would have none — fitting a [[Read the Book of Mormon as a Translated Ancient Text|translated ancient text]] rather than a 19th-century composition.[^4]
- **A seer stone with traceable provenance.** A stone "matches the description... given by several participants" ([[Emma Smith]], [[David Whitmer]]) and was photographed for the Joseph Smith Papers; see [[Provenance Is the Standard for Authenticating Book of Mormon Artifacts]] and [[Seer Stones and Divining Rods as Revelatory Means]].[^5]
- **The "characters document."** A paper headed "characters" (misspelled) in the hand of [[John Whitmer]], one of the eight witnesses and Church historian — likely characters he copied directly from the plates; the Church purchased it in 2024. Griffiths notes a scholarly caveat that the characters might belong to a later Book of Abraham project, but judges the "most likely scenario" is Book of Mormon plate characters.[^6]

These survivals are why the LDS case is built on convergence rather than a single decisive relic ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]).

## Best Quotes

> "What we do have are a number of artifacts that maybe make it less of a slam dunk, but still allow us to create a functional narrative surrounding it."[^1]

> "Both the printer's manuscript and the original manuscript have no punctuation... If the Book of Mormon is originally an Egyptian document and it's in a Semitic language, they don't have any."[^4]

> "It just seems like... the most likely scenario is that these are characters that he copied down as one of the eight witnesses of the Book of Mormon."[^6]

## LDS Connection

Griffiths models how a faith-friendly historian handles evidence honestly: he concedes the relics are gone, declines to overstate the documents ("less of a slam dunk"), and even surfaces the competing scholarly read of the characters document. The apologetic weight rests on whether the surviving artifacts *match the witnesses' descriptions* ([[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]]) — and they largely do, which is the kind of correlation the missing sacred objects ([[The Sacred Book of Mormon Artifacts Were Withdrawn Not Lost]]) cannot supply.
