# The Arian Used Sola Scriptura to Reject Nicaea

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]]'s "role" argument holds that Scripture is consistently used *over* other authorities — including testing the councils, as William Lane Craig does when he "brings even the statements of ecumenical councils before the bar of scripture."[^1] [[Trent Horn]]'s reply is a historical reductio: **the Arian heretic used exactly that move to reject the Council of Nicaea.** The fourth-century Arian bishop Maximinus appealed to **2 Timothy 3:16** and argued that Nicaea's non-biblical word *homoousios* ("same substance") "was not binding on Christians," because "those words which are not found in the scriptures are under no circumstance accepted by us, especially since the Lord warns us... in vain they worship me, teaching human commandments."[^2] So "scripture alone tests the councils" is not a Protestant safeguard against heresy — it was a *heretic's weapon against orthodoxy*.

The decisive datum is *how the orthodox answered*: not with more proof-texts, but by appeal to the **council's authority**. [[Augustine]] said *homoousios* must be accepted because it was declared "at the Council of Nicaea by the Catholic fathers with the authority of truth and the truth of authority." [[Ambrose of Milan]]: "neither death nor the sword can detach me from the rule of the Nicene Council." Theodoret: "I test my teaching by applying to it like a rule and measure the faith laid down by the holy and blessed fathers at Nicaea."[^3]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The same method yields opposite verdicts — rate: strong
Maximinus and Athanasius both claimed Scripture; the word that settled the question (*homoousios*) is admittedly *not in Scripture*. If the bare text were the only infallible rule, the Arian's objection is unanswerable on its own terms — the orthodox had to invoke a *conciliar* authority that could bind beyond the explicit words of Scripture. This is the live, concrete instance of the abstract point in [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]] and complements [[Homoousios Was Coined to Refute Arius]].

### 2. "Bringing councils before the bar of scripture" proves too much — rate: strong
Horn's edge: William Lane Craig denies Christ has a human will, and "no earnest Christian wants to be considered a heretic, but we Protestants recognize scripture alone as our ultimate rule" — *which is the very sentence Maximinus wrote.* "Without these infallible rules, doctrine is decided by debate" — and Horn quips he need not out-argue Craig "because an ecumenical council says he's wrong," appealing to the church as "the pillar and foundation of truth" ([[1 Timothy 3.15|1 Tim 3:15]]).[^4]

### 3. Steelman of Ortlund's reply — rate: contested
Ortlund's available counters: (a) the orthodox *also* argued *homoousios* is the *necessary entailment* of biblical teaching (the Son is fully God, not a creature), so they were not abandoning Scripture but drawing "good and necessary consequence" from it; the Arian's exegesis was simply *wrong*, and being outvoted by a council is not what made it wrong. (b) Councils having *real authority* to articulate biblical truth is fully consistent with sola scriptura, which denies only their *infallibility*, not their authority (see the umpire distinction in [[The 80 Percent Rule - Sola Scriptura Is Caricatured Into the Excluded Middle]]). (c) Ortlund notes that his own patristic witness (Augustine) elsewhere subordinates *plenary councils* to Scripture, so Augustine's praise of Nicaea need not imply *infallibility*. Horn's rejoinder: if Nicaea can be *resisted* whenever a sincere reader's exegesis differs, then it cannot actually *settle* anything — which is why the fathers grounded its bindingness in its *authority*, not merely its agreement with their reading.

## LDS relevance
For Latter-day Saints the episode is **a tu quoque, not an endorsement of Nicaea**. It shows the Protestant "scripture alone" method cannot, by itself, exclude Arianism — vindicating the LDS complaint that creedal precision required an *extra-biblical* authority the New Testament never authorized (see [[Trinitarianism Is Anachronistic to the Biblical Period]] and [[Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity]]). But the LDS exit differs from Horn's: rather than a *magisterium* binding the conscience to *homoousios*, the Restoration appeals to *continuing revelation* and reads the Father–Son texts literally, so it neither needs Nicaea's non-biblical vocabulary nor concedes the council's infallibility — the LDS would say *both* Maximinus and Augustine were arguing about a metaphysical puzzle Scripture never posed.
