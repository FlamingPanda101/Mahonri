# Doctrine Is Defined by the Unified Voice of the Highest Authorities

## The Claim
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] argues Mormonism is hard to critique because, lacking a catechism, it has no fixed doctrine — so he goes to "the highest sources of authority," which he treats as "random statements made by church leaders throughout the history of the church." He claims you cannot pin down what Mormons believe; it is "like trying to nail Jello to a wall."[^1] The panel responds with a precise definition of LDS doctrine.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Doctrine ≠ any leader's statement (rated: strong, definitional).** [[Jacob Hansen]] defines doctrine as "a specific truth claim that is endorsed by the highest authorities of the church *in total*" — e.g., if the Quorum of the Twelve and First Presidency (all 15) affirm a position as the official position, that is doctrine. Doctrine is not policy, not implementation, and not the speculative theory of an individual leader.[^2] This collapses Heschmeyer's move of treating an isolated [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young|Journal of Discourses]] sermon as top-tier authority.

2. **The "secret book" fallacy (rated: strong, rhetorical).** Critics behave as if they have located "the secret book of the real answers," when the Church's officially controlled website, manuals, and published materials are openly available. Hansen: "If you go to our website... you got pages and books and manuals and all sorts of just mountains of stuff that we believe... you're just angry because it isn't that exciting. It isn't as weird as you thought."[^3]

3. **The green-book parallel (rated: moderate, illustrative).** [[Luke Irwin]] recounts that the source Heschmeyer references on-camera but refuses to show the cover of turned out to be a self-published compilation, "Photo reprints of hard-to-get Mormon documents" — a 1970s anti-Mormon scrapbook of underlined old Adam-God and Journal of Discourses material. Quote-mining a hostile compilation is "pulling an Apologia Studios trick," not consulting authoritative doctrine.[^4]

4. **Only one doctrine has actually changed (rated: contested but specific).** Hansen claims that on the strict definition — official truth claims taught by the highest leaders in unison — the *only* item that has reversed is the teaching about Black people and the priesthood ban. *Steelman:* a critic will note this concedes a real, significant reversal on a matter of human dignity, and that the "deep doctrine" category is convenient for quarantining embarrassing teachings; the LDS reply (see [[The Priesthood Ban Was the Development Not Its Reversal]]) is that even this was the *absorption* of error, not original doctrine.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Doctrine isn't policy. It isn't how things are implemented. It's a specific truth claim that is endorsed by the highest authorities of the church in total."[^2]

> "If you go to our website, which is endorsed and kind of controlled by the official representatives of our church who live right now and say what our doctrine is, you can find... mountains of stuff that we believe. You're just angry because it isn't that exciting."[^3]

> "Name something in the church doctrinally — white shirts and colored shirts and whether or not you can be bishop?"[^5]
