# Abraham as a Backstory to Genesis

## The Claim
The [[Book of Abraham]] is best understood as a *backstory* to the Abraham narrative in Genesis. Where Genesis presents Abraham third-person and "flat" — God appears "out of the blue" and promises land, posterity, and blessing without Abraham ever stating what he wants — [[Abraham 1.1-4|Abraham 1]] supplies a first-person account in which Abraham has already articulated his motives, making him "a very relatable human being."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The literary contrast is real and textual (rated: strong).** [[Don Bradley]] notes that in Genesis, Abraham "doesn't actually state his motives. We don't see his inner conflict about when he's commanded to sacrifice his son Isaac." It is "a basic third-person narrative versus a first-person account."[^2] This is an accurate description: Genesis 12 opens with the divine call to Abram with no prior characterization. *Steelman:* a critic (e.g. [[Dan McClellan]]-style historical-critical reading) would say the Book of Abraham is a 19th-century expansion *retrojecting* relatable motives, not recovering a lost original — the very relatability is the tell of a modern author. Bradley's frame does not refute this; it presupposes Joseph Smith's prophetic role (see [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]).

2. **The opening verses do supply explicit motives (rated: strong, textual).** Abraham says he "saw that it was needful for me to obtain another place of residence" (a home), sought "greater happiness and peace," "the blessings of the fathers and the right whereunto I should be ordained to administer" (priesthood), to "possess greater knowledge," and blessings for his posterity (family).[^1] These map onto universal human desires — home, peace, family, knowledge, spiritual power.

3. **The backstory deepens the Binding of Isaac (rated: moderate, interpretive).** Because Abraham himself was nearly sacrificed and God intervened, "maybe he trusts that the Lord will intervene again for his son Isaac." The attempted-sacrifice backstory makes Abraham's later obedience "much more poignant."[^3] See [[The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ]].

## Best Quotes

> "In Genesis... you have God coming to Abraham with no backstory about who Abraham is or what he's all about or what he wants. ... But you open up the Book of Abraham... and that is exactly what you have."[^2]

> "He says, 'I, Abraham, saw that it was needful for me to obtain another place of residence.' He wants a home. Ah, we can all understand that, right?"[^1]

> "Abraham, in some ways, from a literary vantage point, he's kind of a flat character in the sense that he doesn't actually state his motives."[^2]
