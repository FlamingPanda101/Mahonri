# Conditional Access Tu Quoque Against the LDS God

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]] spends much of his cross-examination turning the tables on [[Hayden Carroll]]: if a truly benevolent God's access cannot be "structurally restricted," then the LDS God — who is "bound when ye do what I say" ([[D&C 82.10|D&C 82:10]]), offers exaltation through covenant/worthiness, and gates the highest glory ([[D&C 76]]) — fails Hayden's *own* benevolence standard. "Can a being be omnibenevolent if access to him is structurally or hierarchically restricted?"[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Internal-consistency probe** — rate: moderate as debate tactic, weak for the resolution. Gavin extracts that LDS doctrine has God "bound" by obedience and offers tiered exaltation, then argues that is the same "conditional access" Gavin condemns. *(internal-critique)* [^2]
2. **Hayden's worthiness answer** — Hayden denies access is hierarchically restricted and counters that LDS theology is arguably *more* loving (universal postmortem opportunity vs. historic Catholic limbo for unbaptized infants; St. Joseph/Mary/Adam/OT prophets saved without recorded sacraments). *(counter)* [^3]

**Why it does not rescue the resolution (the key meta-point):** Hayden repeatedly flags this entire line as a *category error* for the debate. The resolution is about the **triune (creedal) God**, not the LDS God. "Mormonism could be totally false and that would mean nothing either way for the debate resolution." Even if Gavin scores points against LDS theology, that is *ad hominem / tu quoque* — it does nothing to show the *triune* God escapes [[The Creation Dilemma]]. See [[The Debate Is About Coherence Not the LDS God]]. A tu quoque can be rhetorically effective but is formally irrelevant to whether the affirmative has met its burden.
