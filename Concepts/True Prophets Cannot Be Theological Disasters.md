# True Prophets Cannot Be Theological Disasters

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Aaron Shafovaloff]] offers a "principled point" he says crystallizes the whole evangelical–LDS divide over authority: **a genuine prophet of God does not "fundamentally, publicly and persistently misrepresent God."** Evangelicals (and, he claims, the Catholic and Eastern Orthodox traditions) answer *no* to the question "can a true prophet be a theological disaster?"; the LDS apologetic space answers *yes* — it has "more of an escape hatch" and "more leeway." He drives the point with [[Blake Ostler]]'s own admission that [[Brigham Young]]'s theology "was a disaster," and frames the LDS move as having "had to create a category for true prophets that can be wrong prophets, good prophets that can be disasters."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "high view of prophets" premise (rated: strong within its tradition).** Shafovaloff: "evangelicals have a very high view of prophets." On this view a true prophet leading "hundreds of thousands or millions of people, even temporarily astray, by claiming something is the very revelation and voice of God" is a contradiction in terms — it would make God's appointed spokesman an instrument of false worship.[^1] The standard is not mere personal sin (everyone sins) but *doctrinal misrepresentation of God Himself*. See [[Brigham Young Failed Prophet Argument]].

2. **The hostile-witness corroboration (rated: strong, primary source).** The cited concession comes from a *believing* LDS philosopher, [[Blake Ostler]]: that Brigham Young's theology "was a disaster." Shafovaloff weaponizes an in-house judgment: if the tradition's own thinkers grant that a sustained prophetic teaching was a theological catastrophe, the evangelical premise (true prophets don't do that) implies Brigham was not, in those teachings, speaking for God.[^2] See [[Modern LDS Tensions on King Follett Discourse]].

3. **Shafovaloff's diagnosis of the LDS adaptation (rated: moderate, interpretive).** He argues the "true-but-wrong-prophet" category was *manufactured* to absorb the church's own history — i.e., it is post hoc damage control rather than a doctrine derived from scripture. This pairs with his [[The Diversity Test - What a Community Tolerates Reveals Its God|diversity-tolerance argument]]: the same elasticity that tolerates diverse views of God also tolerates "disaster" prophets.

4. **Steelman of Hansen's biblical counter (rated: strong — turns the test on the Bible).** [[Jacob Hansen]] replies that the Old Testament's own prophets fail the strict test: Jonah prophesies Nineveh's destruction "in forty days" and it does *not* happen (Jonah 3); prophecies are routinely *contingent* on human free will, so "it's impossible to have a prophecy and free will that is not ultimately contingent." More pointedly, Hansen argues the same prophets, taken as inerrant, have God commanding genocide — "hack up five-year-old girls with swords" (the Amalekites) and stoning for homosexual acts. If true prophets cannot misrepresent God, the evangelical must either own those commands as God's literal will or admit prophets can be wrong — the very category Shafovaloff says only Latter-day Saints need.[^3] See [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]], [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]], and [[Rejecting Biblical Inerrancy Solves the Moral Contradiction Problem]].

5. **Shafovaloff's posture rather than answer (rated: contested).** Pressed on the Amalekite slaughter, Shafovaloff declines to affirm or deny that God literally commanded it; he adopts a *posture* — "pause and reflect and have a special sense of reverence... taking the total of the context into account... looking at what other Christians have said." He insists he does "not have the option" of "a blanket rejection of whole swaths of the Old Testament" or of saying "prophets can be disasters."[^4] Hansen presses that this either embraces "an abhorrent moral system" or quietly does the same selective-rejection work he criticizes in Latter-day Saints — locating the real disagreement at biblical inerrancy (see [[Inspired vs Infallible vs Ultimate - A Distinction]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Do we fundamentally and principally believe that true prophets can be theological disasters? ... In the evangelical tradition, even in the Catholic, Eastern Orthodox tradition, the answer is no. In the Latter-day Saint apologetic space, the answer is yes."[^1]

> "There's a very prominent LDS philosopher today named Blake Ostler... he says that the theology of Brigham Young was a disaster."[^2]

> "Is Jeremiah a false prophet? Is Jonah a false prophet? Because they made false prophecies in the Bible itself. Are prophecies ever contingent?" — Jacob Hansen[^3]
