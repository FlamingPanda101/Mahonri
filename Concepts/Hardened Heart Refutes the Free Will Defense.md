# Hardened Heart Refutes the Free Will Defense

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A foundational premise of the wax/clay apologetic — and of much popular theodicy — is that **God never interferes with human agency.** The free-will defense holds that God so respects human choice that he will not override it; hence Pharaoh's hardening *must* be self-caused. McClellan targets this premise directly: "the notion that God would not interfere with human agency is flatly precluded by the story of the Exodus."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The text shows God overriding agency.** "Exodus 4–14 very clearly represent God meddling in human agency so that God's own purposes can be achieved." The hardening texts are a sustained counterexample to the no-interference premise.[^2] (Strong — multiple chapters, explicit divine first-person purpose statements.)
2. **God compels resistance.** "When Pharaoh didn't want to push back, God compelled Pharaoh to push back" — the opposite of leaving Pharaoh's will untouched.[^3] See [[Hardening Pharaohs Heart Was to Display Gods Power]].
3. **The framework is presupposed, not derived.** The no-interference axiom is "the interpretive lenses and the unifying frameworks and the dogmas that are being imposed upon the texts." The text is "subordinated to those interpretive lenses."[^4] (See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]].)
4. **Steelman of the free-will defender.** A philosopher of religion could distinguish *libertarian* free will (the defense's usual home) from this narrative and argue (a) Exodus is using anthropomorphic, polemical rhetoric not metaphysics; (b) Pharaoh freely set his disposition first, so God's hardening is a *consequence* of, not a violation of, his agency (cf. Romans 1's "God gave them up"); or (c) hardening confirms rather than creates Pharaoh's resolve. These are serious moves. **But** they relocate the debate to *how* God overrides/uses the will, conceding that he does so — and none recovers the wax/clay claim that God takes *no* action at all.

## Best Quotes
> "So the notion that God would not interfere with human agency is flatly precluded by the story of the Exodus. Exodus 4–14 very clearly represent God meddling in human agency so that God's own purposes can be achieved." [^1]

> "The argument is based on the interpretive lenses and the unifying frameworks and the dogmas that are being imposed upon the texts because the text has to be subordinated to those interpretive lenses." [^4]
