# Trinity Was a Good-Faith Effort Not Dishonesty

## The Claim
Contra apologists who call Trinitarian reasoning "super dishonest," [[Chandler Henry|Chandler]] argues the Trinity was a **sincere, good-faith** attempt to make sense of difficult scripture under brutal historical constraints — not deception. The framing matters: charity, not accusation, is the accurate and persuasive posture.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Fathers faced a genuine, hard problem
By ~500 BC the Greco-Roman intelligentsia had reached a kind of monotheism (the Aristotelian prime mover / uncaused cause). Then came scriptures of a man declaring himself God, praying to his Father, with a Spirit descending as a dove — apparently *three* who are God, against a monotheistic backdrop, while pagans were polytheists. "How do we make sense of all this?" That is a real puzzle, not a con. *(Strong as a historical framing of the problem the Fathers actually faced.)* [^1]

### 2. Heresies forced councils that asked good questions
Modalism, the Marcionites, Montanists, Patripassianists, subordinationists — each error forced an ecumenical council, and these groups "actually brought up good questions" (e.g. if all are God, why is the Father treated as superior?). The Trinity emerged from genuine debate, even if it ended "a little uncoherent." *(Moderate-strong; fair to the conciliar history, though the panel still judges the result incoherent.)* [^2]

### 3. They preserved the church under persecution, pre-printing-press
"When you're trying to preserve the church in an ancient world with no internet, with no fast communication, and people literally killing each other as heretics," the Fathers' effort deserves patience — the *same* patience LDS ask for Joseph Smith's imperfect early record-keeping (a manuscript saved from a burning building amid mob violence). *(Opinion-weighted but a coherent fairness argument; ties apologetic charity to a tu quoque about Restoration history.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Two pushbacks. (1) From the *other* LDS panelists and super-chats: maybe it *was* just stupid/wrong, and excessive charity concedes too much (Grant Rounds: "Not dishonest — stupid"). (2) From Trinitarians: "good-faith but incoherent" is still condescending — they would say the development was not damage-control but the Spirit-guided church faithfully articulating what scripture entails, and that the result *is* coherent (the ousia/hypostasis distinction is a feature, not a bug). Both sides thus reject the middle position from opposite directions; Chandler's value-add is modeling apologetic charity (cf. the stream's "intervention on being more Christ-like in interfaith dialogue" framing) regardless of who is right on the metaphysics.

## Best Quotes
> "I wouldn't say super dishonest... It's actually not dishonest. It's a sincere effort to try and fit a square peg in a circular hole." [^1]

> "They did kind of force themselves into a trinity that... is unfortunately a little uncoherent... but when you're trying to preserve the church in an ancient world... I wouldn't be so quick to say it's something as like dishonesty." [^2]

> "I actually demand this same level of patience for us. When people want to criticize Joseph Smith... early Christianity kind of suffered from the same problem." [^3]
