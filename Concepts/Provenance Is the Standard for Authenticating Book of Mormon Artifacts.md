# Provenance Is the Standard for Authenticating Book of Mormon Artifacts

## The Point

Because the surviving Book of Mormon items are ordinary-looking objects (a stone, sheets of paper), Griffiths argues their authenticity turns on **two converging tests**: (1) does the object *match the descriptions* given by participants, and (2) does it have a clean *provenance* — a traceable chain of custody back to the translation. This is a historian's evidentiary standard rather than an appeal to the miraculous, and it is what lets him say a given object is probably genuine without claiming certainty.

## Evidence

- **A short chain of custody is a strength.** The seer stone passed through only four owners: [[Joseph Smith]] gave it to [[Oliver Cowdery]] when they finished translating; Oliver kept it until his death in 1850; it went to his wife Elizabeth Whitmer Cowdery; then to her brother-in-law Phineas Young (Brigham's brother); and it has been in Church custody since. Griffiths: "only having four owners is a relatively short chain of custody for something that's about 150 years old."[^1]
- **Description-matching as the second leg.** The stone "matches the description that [[Emma Smith]] and [[David Whitmer]] and several others gave of a seer stone that Joseph Smith used" — so provenance and description corroborate each other.[^2] This is the same logic applied to the manuscripts (see [[The Surviving Book of Mormon Artifacts Are Documentary Not Sacred]] and [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]]).
- **Honest hedging where provenance is thinner.** Griffiths says "we're not 100% sure that it is the seer stone," only that it matches the description and traces back to Joseph — modeling calibrated rather than triumphalist claims.[^3]
- **The characters document's documented line.** The paper in [[John Whitmer]]'s hand stayed in the Whitmer family until the late 19th century, was sold to the RLDS Church (later Community of Christ), and was purchased by the LDS Church in 2024 — "that one has a clear provenance."[^4] Provenance, however, settles *who handled the paper*, not *what the marks are*: Griffiths still weighs whether the characters are Book of Mormon plate characters or belong to a later Book of Abraham project.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "It has a clear provenance that can be traced back to Joseph Smith... only having four owners is a relatively short chain of custody for something that's about 150 years old."[^1]

> "We're not 100% sure that it is the seer stone, but it matches the description of the seer stone that's given by several participants."[^3]

> "That one has a clear provenance. Our church did purchase it in 2024 and now it's in our collection."[^4]

## LDS Connection

This provenance standard explains why the Church invests in projects like the Joseph Smith Papers (which published photographs of the seer stone) and acquisitions like the 2024 characters-document purchase: the apologetic value of these artifacts depends on a documented, checkable chain back to the translation, paired with the participants' descriptions. It is the same care that lets a faith-friendly historian distinguish a genuinely traceable object from the missing sacred relics ([[The Sacred Book of Mormon Artifacts Were Withdrawn Not Lost]]).
