# Two Rival Canon Traditions Neither Settled Before Trent

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]]'s synthesizing thesis: in the early and medieval church there were **two parallel canon traditions**, neither of which had established dominance by the 16th century. One follows [[Jerome]] (and the Eastern fathers), favoring the **shorter, Hebrew-aligned** Old Testament; the other follows [[Augustine]] and the 4th-century **North African councils** (Hippo, Carthage), favoring the **wider deuterocanonical** canon. The Reformation debate is therefore *not* "the church's settled Bible vs. Protestant subtraction," but a choice **between two genuine early traditions** — and Protestants picked the one with the strongest claim to apostolic antiquity. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the Catholic reply
Rome can grant "two streams" and still win. (a) The relevant question is which stream the church's **Spirit-guided magisterium** ratified — and at Hippo, Carthage, Florence, and finally Trent it consistently ratified the *wider* canon; that conciliar trajectory, not a tally of individual scholars, is what binds. (b) Augustine's stream was the **liturgical mainstream** of the Latin West for a thousand years, while Jerome's was a learned minority report. (c) "Two traditions, you choose" is exactly the *private-judgment* move the Reformation is faulted for: absent an authoritative church to adjudicate, the individual just selects the tradition he prefers and calls it apostolic.

### Ortlund's reply
- **The two-tradition map is the honest description.** "What you have in the West is two different traditions: the tradition represented by Jerome... and the tradition represented by Augustine and these... fourth-century councils." This tension "succeeds into the medieval era." Naming it defeats the *unanimity* claim outright. [^1]
- **Neither was dominant by 1500.** "Neither one had established dominance by the 16th century." The shorter canon persisted even among Catholic cardinals (Cajetan, Ximenes) appealing to Jerome right up to Trent (see [[The Deuterocanon Was Disputed Until Trent Not Removed]]). [^2]
- **The tie-breaker is antiquity, not preference.** Ortlund's answer to the "you just chose" charge: the choice is principled because the shorter canon "has important early testimonies in its favor going back to the 2nd century" — [[Melito of Sardis]], [[Origen]], the Bryennios list — and "is most likely to be the earliest, closest to the apostles." The debate "becomes which of these traditions is correct," and Protestants argue from apostolic origins (see [[The Earliest Canon Lists Are the Shorter Canon - Melito and Origen]]). [^3]
- **Symmetry on the wrinkles.** Even the wider stream was internally inconsistent — Hippo/Carthage's Septuagint **1 Esdras** was dropped by Trent — so "it's simply false that Trent reiterated exactly the earlier tradition." Both traditions had internal variation; that cannot count only against the shorter one. [^4]

## LDS Relevance
The "two rival traditions, neither settled" picture is among the most *LDS-friendly* claims a Protestant makes, because it concedes that there was **no single, unbroken, authoritative biblical canon** in the first fifteen centuries — precisely the historical premise the restoration narrative needs against both Catholic and Protestant appeals to a sealed Bible. Where the LDS view parts company is the resolution: Ortlund breaks the tie by reaching *backward* to the apostolic-era shorter list and then *closing* the canon there; the LDS tradition takes the same unsettledness as license to keep the canon *open* under living revelation, neither adopting Jerome's list nor Augustine's as final. So this argument is a strong ally against the "the church always had this exact Bible" claim, and a foil for any view — Catholic, Protestant, or otherwise — that ends with a permanently closed canon.

## Best Quotes
> "What you have in the west is two different traditions: the tradition represented by Jerome... and the tradition represented by Augustine and these... significant fourth-century councils, especially North African councils, that favor the deuterocanonical books." [^1]

> "The Protestants are adhering to one early tradition following Jerome and corresponding closely to many Eastern fathers, while the Roman Catholic Church is following another early tradition following Augustine... and then the debate becomes which of these traditions is correct. But neither one had established dominance by the 16th century." [^2]

> "Not only does the Protestant canon have historical support, it has that historical support that is most likely to be the earliest, closest to the apostles." [^3]
