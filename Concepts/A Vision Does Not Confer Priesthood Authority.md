# A Vision Does Not Confer Priesthood Authority

#type/claim

## The Claim
On the Latter-day Saint view, supernatural experience and priesthood authority are categorically distinct: receiving a theophany or an angelic visitation — even repeated ones — does **not** by itself authorize a person to baptize or administer ordinances. [[George Q. Cannon]] makes this the heart of his discourse, and the hosts argue Joseph Smith's own conduct proves he understood it: despite the First Vision and the visits of Moroni, Joseph "never attempted... to officiate in any of the ordinances of the house of God... until he received the everlasting priesthood" — conferred explicitly by [[John the Baptist]] (Aaronic) and by [[Peter]], James, and John (Melchizedek). [^1][^2] The hosts press this into an argument for Joseph's **sincerity**: a fraud inventing a church would have started baptizing immediately; Joseph's restraint ("temperate and sober-minded") shows a man waiting on what he believed was real authority. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Cannon's explicit thesis.** "The prevalent idea in the world has been that if a man should be so favored as to receive the ministration or visitations of angels... he would be completely invested with the power necessary to preach the gospel... But the Prophet Joseph not only received the ministration of angels," yet did not act "before he was really ordained of God to administer the ordinance." [^1] *Rating: strong as primary-source LDS theology* — it cleanly separates charisma/revelation from delegated authority.

2. **The textual hook in Joseph Smith History.** Oliver and Joseph went to pray about baptism *because they encountered it while translating*, and only then did the messenger ([[John the Baptist]]) confer the priesthood of Aaron ([[Joseph Smith History 1.68-69|JS-History 1:68-69]]). [^4] The order is: study → inquiry → conferral — not vision → self-authorization.

3. **The Book of Mormon already models the restraint.** [[Mosiah 21.27-28|Mosiah 21]]: King Limhi and many wished to be baptized, "but there was none in the land that had authority from God. And Ammon declined doing this thing, considering himself an unworthy servant." [^5] The hosts note "unworthy" may mean *unauthorized*, not merely sinful — the same priesthood logic embedded in the narrative (and Ammon later baptizes in Alma 19, after the order is right). *Rating: a strong internal parallel.*

4. **The contrast case is the argument.** [[Matthew 10.1|Matthew 10:1]] shows Jesus *giving* the Twelve "power against unclean spirits," which the hosts read as deliberate conferral of authority — distinguishing gifts of the Spirit available to any believer from being "set apart" with delegated authority. [^6] See [[Priesthood Authority Is the Necessary But Not Sufficient Dividing Line]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic can read Joseph's "restraint" less charitably: the angelic-conferral accounts (the John the Baptist / Peter-James-John ordinations) were *recorded years later* and are absent from the earliest narratives, so the tidy "he waited for authority" sequence may be a retrospective construction rather than contemporaneous fact — in which case the sincerity argument leans on the very documents whose dating is contested. A measured LDS reply: even granting the later documentation, the *behavioral* datum stands — there is no evidence Joseph was baptizing converts in 1827-1828 on the strength of his visions, and the public movement only organizes after the claimed restorations; the restraint is visible in what he did *not* do, independent of when the conferral stories were penned. The deeper point — that visions ≠ authority — is also doctrinally load-bearing for the broader LDS apostasy/restoration model (see [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]]) and the sincerity case in [[Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud]].

## Best Quotes
> "Even if you have an angelic visitor and you have a theophany, that doesn't necessitate that you have the authority to act in God's name." [^3]

> "It is a remarkable fact... that notwithstanding the prophet Joseph had all these manifestations, and was... a prophet and seer and revelator, he never attempted... to officiate in any of the ordinances of the house of God or in the gospel of salvation until he received the everlasting priesthood." [^2]

> "Ammon declined doing this thing, considering himself an unworthy servant." [^5]
