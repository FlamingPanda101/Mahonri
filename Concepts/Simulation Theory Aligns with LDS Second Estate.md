# Simulation Theory Aligns with LDS Second Estate

## The Claim

Some atheists embrace simulation theory — the hypothesis that our reality is a
computer simulation created by a more advanced civilization — as a
naturalistic alternative to theism. [[David Butler]] argues this is ironic
because LDS theology already contains a remarkably similar concept: mortal life
is a "second estate," a temporary probationary state away from "base reality"
(God's presence), from which we return. Atheists who find simulation theory
plausible are unknowingly affirming a core LDS doctrine.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Atheists Embrace Simulation Theory (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Cultural)

Butler notes that "there's that simulation theory that goes out and atheists
like that and they think that it's an argument against the idea of God."[^1]
Figures like [[Elon Musk]] and philosopher [[Nick Bostrom]] (author of the
simulation argument) have popularized the idea that we might be living in a
simulation — and many secular-minded people find this intellectually
respectable while dismissing theism.

### 2. LDS Theology Already Describes a "Simulation" (Evidence Rating: Primary — Doctrinal)

Butler draws the parallel:

> "We actually believe in the simulation. There was a time given to men of
> probation. Yes. And we're put in right now. We are not in — we are in a state
> that is away from base [reality]. Like we've left base reality and come here
> in our second estate — and then we are going to return back to base reality.
> Reality is eternal life with God."[^1]

The LDS plan of salvation maps directly onto simulation theory's structure:
- **Base Reality:** The pre-mortal existence with God (First Estate).
- **The Simulation:** Mortal life — a temporary, embodied probationary state
  where we are separated from direct knowledge of base reality (the "veil").
- **Return to Base Reality:** Resurrection, judgment, and return to God's
  presence (or other kingdoms).
- **Purpose of the Simulation:** Testing, learning, growth — "a time... of
  probation" to see "if they will do all things whatsoever the Lord their God
  shall command them" (Abraham 3:25).

### 3. The Irony (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

The point is not that simulation theory *proves* LDS theology true, but that
atheists who entertain simulation theory have already conceded the conceptual
structure of theism:

- A higher intelligence (the simulator/programmer) created this reality.
- Our current experience is not ultimate reality.
- There is a purpose to the simulation.
- We are subjects within a designed system.

If an atheist accepts that an advanced alien civilization could create a
simulation we inhabit, they've accepted every conceptual building block of
theism except the identity of the creator. The LDS claim is simply that the
"programmer" is God.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Simulation Theory Is Naturalistic; LDS Theology Is Supernatural
**Steelman:** The simulation hypothesis posits a natural, physical computer
running our universe — not a supernatural God. The simulator is just a more
technologically advanced physical being. This is fundamentally different from
theism, which posits a transcendent, uncreated God. Conflating the two is a
category error.

**Response:** The distinction between "natural" and "supernatural" collapses
when you posit beings with enough power to create entire universes. From our
perspective inside the simulation, the simulator is functionally God — they
created our reality, know everything that happens in it, and could intervene at
will. LDS theology actually bridges this gap: God is an exalted, embodied being
who operates through eternal law — He is not "supernatural" in the sense of
being pure spirit outside space and time. The God of LDS theology is more like
an advanced being who has mastered eternal law than a disembodied Platonic
form.

### Objection 2: The Parallel Is Superficial
**Steelman:** Many religious traditions have concepts of an "illusory" world and
a "higher" reality — Plato's cave, Hindu Maya, Gnosticism. Finding a loose
parallel between simulation theory and LDS doctrine is not distinctive and
doesn't constitute evidence. You could say the same about Buddhism or the
Matrix.

**Response:** Butler's point is narrower: it's aimed at atheists who find
simulation theory intellectually respectable but dismiss theism as
superstitious. If you grant the conceptual possibility of a created,
non-ultimate reality with a purpose and a creator-intelligence, you've conceded
the rational framework for theism. The remaining dispute is about who the
creator is, not whether the concept of a creator is rational.
