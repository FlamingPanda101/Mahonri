# Homoousios Was Coined to Refute Arius (Not Found in Scripture)

## The Point
The defining word of the Nicene Creed — *homoousios* ("consubstantial," same essence) — is **not in scripture**. It was deliberately adopted at the [[Council of Nicaea]] (325) for one purpose: to block [[Arius]]'s interpretation of the biblical term "begotten." The dispute between Arius and [[Alexander of Alexandria]] turned on whether Christ was *created* or *begotten*, and the council had to reach for a non-scriptural Greek philosophical term to settle it. This is direct evidence that the Trinity's core vocabulary is a later technical construct layered onto the Bible, not biblical language itself.

## Evidence
1. The debate began in Alexandria. Both [[Arius]] (a presbyter) and bishop [[Alexander of Alexandria]] agreed Christ is divine but disagreed how he relates to the Father.[^1]
2. **Arius:** the Son "was not before he was begotten … He is not eternal or coeternal … with the Father." For Arius, "begotten" meant a time when God "made" Christ — "there was a time when Christ was not."[^2]
3. **Alexander:** scripture says Christ was "begotten," not "created"; a begotten son inherits the father's nature, so Christ is "just as much God as God the Father."[^3]
4. Constantine convened the council; 200–300 bishops gathered from Spain to Persia, beginning May 20, 325 — "the first great ecumenical council."[^4]
5. **The clarification:** "to beget" and "to make" differ — a potter "makes" a pot but "begets" a son, and the son is far more like the potter than any pot. So Nicaea glossed "only-begotten" as "out of the essence [*ousia*] of the Father" and "consubstantial [*homoousios*] with the Father."[^5]
6. **Not scriptural:** "*homoousios* (from the Greek *homo*, 'same,' and *ousia*, 'substance' or 'essence') … is not used in scripture. The term was adopted to refute Arius's interpretation."[^6]
7. The creed condemned Arian phrases: "there was a time when [the Son] was not," "before he was begotten he was not," "he came to be out of what was not."[^7]
8. *Homoousios* does NOT mean Father and Son are one *person* (that would be [[Modalism Monarchianism and Sabellianism|modalism]]); Chalcedon (451) later clarified Christ is also *homoousios* with us humans, proving it cannot mean "same person."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> The Greek word *homoousios* … "which is often translated as 'consubstantial' … is not used in scripture. The term was adopted to refute Arius's interpretation of scripture." [^6]

> A potter "makes" a pot but "begets" a son; and that son of the potter would be much more like the potter than any pot could ever be. [^5]

## LDS Connection
The non-scriptural origin of *homoousios* is a central LDS apologetic point: the Trinity's load-bearing term had to be *invented* to resolve a 4th-century philosophical dispute. The author also notes (the LDS-friendly twist) that Chalcedon's clarification — Christ is *homoousios* with *humanity* too — opens the door to reading "same essence" as shared by all humans, the LDS theosis intuition.
