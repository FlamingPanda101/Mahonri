# Eve Completes Adam's Broken Chiasmus in Moses 5
#type/claim

## The Claim
In Moses 5:10-11 (LDS Pearl of Great Price), Adam's exclamation of praise after the Fall reads like a chiasmus that is *missing one line* — the second occurrence of the word "transgression" that the symmetry requires. [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]] proposes Adam leaves the figure incomplete because he cannot bear to "relive that transgression." In the very next verse Eve answers him, putting "transgression" at the front — "Were it not for our transgression we never should have had seed... and known good and evil, and the joy of our redemption" — thereby *completing* the chiastic figure and voicing the fortunate-fall theology. The literary "broken-then-completed" structure is read as evidence of intentional, ancient-style composition embedded in Joseph Smith's revelation.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The deliberate gap.** Per the [[Moses 5.10-11]] reading, Adam blesses God "because of my transgression," producing a figure that looks like a chiasm short one element — the missing second "transgression." The omission is read as characterization (Adam flinching from the word), not as a flaw.
- **Eve supplies the center.** Eve restores "transgression" to the front of her statement, completing the inverted parallelism and articulating why the Fall was necessary (seed, knowledge of good and evil, the joy of redemption). It is fitting, Barnes notes, that Eve — who took the fruit and "knew the fall was good" — is the one who voices the fortunate-fall logic.
- **Chiasmus framing.** The argument sits within the broader LDS use of chiasmus as a marker of ancient authorship (Jack Welch's 1970s discovery in the Book of Mormon, with [[Alma 36.1|Alma 36]] as the flagship); Barnes stresses not every chiasm must be large, just as not all poetry rhymes. See [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]]. It pairs with the apocrypha argument in [[The Life of Adam and Eve Fills the Gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses 5]].
- **Steelman / caveats.** The source is self-described non-scholarly, comedic LDS apologetics, and the host disclaims peer review. Critics will object that a "chiasm missing exactly the line my reading needs" is unfalsifiable — one can always declare an absent element the meaningful gap — and that two short verses are slim grounds for intentional chiastic design rather than ordinary Hebraic parallelism (or coincidence in an English text). The claim is best held as a suggestive literary observation about Moses 5's two-verse Adam/Eve exchange, not a demonstrated proof of ancient authorship.
