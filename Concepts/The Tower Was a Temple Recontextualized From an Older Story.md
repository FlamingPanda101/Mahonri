# The Tower Was a Temple Recontextualized From an Older Story

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Mike Day]] argues that the Genesis "Tower of Babel" is not primarily an etiology of why humans speak different languages, but a **temple** — and that the story is an older tradition *recontextualized* by later biblical editors as anti-Babylonian polemic. Two load-bearing observations carry the case: (1) the very name **Babel** is a Hebrew "dad joke" — *balal*, "to mix/confuse" — that subverts Babylon's own self-understanding as "the house of God," and (2) the Book of Mormon text (Ether, Omni, Helaman) says only **"the tower,"** never "the Tower of Babel," so reading Babel into the Jaredite record is the modern reader's importation. The payoff: a "tower to get to heaven" is *temple* code, and the episode functions as a **type of false religion/ideology** — a counterfeit ascent — paralleling both the Jaredites and Lehi, who each flee corrupted sacred space. This is distinct from the vault's [[The Stick of Joseph - Clues Reveal the Unknown Editor of Genesis|Genesis-editor]] and [[Babylon Taunt Formula Shows Negation Is a Boast of Superiority|Babylon-taunt]] material: here the load-bearing move is *tower = temple* plus *recontextualized older source*, not redactional seams or negation rhetoric.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Babel" is a polemical pun on *balal*.** The biblical writers, who "don't like" Babylon, turn Babylon's *Bab-ilu* ("gate/house of God," where heaven and earth meet) into "the house of mixing/confusion." Day notes Babylon carries its own creation myth (Marduk overcoming chaos as the son of a great god), and the Bible's authors are subverting its directives — "ner-ner, you're not the legit ones." "The whole thing is a dad joke." [^1]
2. **A tower "to get to heaven" is temple language.** A ziggurat is precisely "where heaven and earth meet"; a structure built so "they might get to heaven" (the wording quoted from Helaman 6:28) is code for a temple — a place of ascent to God. So the Tower narrative is a temple narrative, read against the [[Lehi's Dream Encodes the Three Temple Rooms by Hebrew Name-Puns|temple-ascent ideology]] the channel develops elsewhere. [^2]
3. **The Book of Mormon never says "Tower of Babel."** Ether 1:3 speaks of "the great tower"; Omni 1:22 of "the tower" at the confounding of the language; Helaman 6:28 of "that same being who put it into the hearts of the people to build a tower sufficiently high that they might get to heaven." The phrase "Tower of Babel" appears only in the (1920/1981) chapter *headings*, not the text — the same pattern Day flags for "Nephite coinage" and "hill Cumorah." So a critic's objection that "the Tower of Babel is fiction, therefore the brother of Jared cannot be historical" misfires: the text is not committed to Babel-the-event. [^3]
4. **Recontextualization is the editors' normal method.** Because pre-exilic scribes "don't have word [processors]," they build on top of older scrolls rather than starting fresh — so an old tower-story could be "repackaged by later editors" to fit a theological agenda (Day's parallel: scribes who rewrote Ugaritic hymns to Baal as Psalms to Yahweh). The technical term is *recontextualization*. This is the same editorial behavior diagnosed in [[The Stick of Joseph - Clues Reveal the Unknown Editor of Genesis]]. [^4]
5. **The tower as a type of false religion, lived not merely told.** If the tower is a counterfeit temple — a man-made ascent — it becomes a perennial type: Day and the hosts apply it to false ideologies that promise heaven-on-earth (the explicit example: Marxism's "tower to heaven" and its body count), invoking [[Jordan Peterson]]'s reading that the confounding of language describes people who "say the same words" while the words "mean different things." On this reading the Jaredites and Lehi tell *the same story* — fleeing the wickedness of corrupted sacred space to live the religion of Yahweh as they see it. [^5]

**Steelman of the skeptic.** This is admittedly speculative — Day repeatedly says "I don't know," "this is a crazy theory I've come up with right now." A critic will note: (a) the mainstream reading of Genesis 11 *is* an etiology of language-dispersion, and "tower = temple" is an overlay, not the plain sense; (b) that the Book of Mormon avoids the proper name "Babel" does not make the Jaredite tower a different structure — Omni and Ether clearly set it at "the confounding of the language," i.e., the same event; (c) appeals to a lost "older story" are unfalsifiable; and (d) the Marxism/ideology application is homiletic, not exegetical. **But** the argument's modest core is defensible: the *balal* pun and the ziggurat-as-meeting-of-heaven-and-earth are standard ancient-Near-East scholarship, and the chapter-heading-vs-text distinction is textually correct — so "the Book of Mormon is not hostage to the historicity of 'the Tower of Babel'" stands even if the fuller temple-typology remains a devotional reading.

## Best Quotes

> "It doesn't say Tower of Babel in the Book of Mormon — it says the tower... What if there was a tower story that got recontextualized or repackaged by later editors?" [^3]
