# Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The harmonizing apologist holds that as long as one can *conjure up* a scenario — however implausible or improbable — under which two passages are consistent, the alleged contradiction is dissolved. The scenario need not be in evidence; it need only be not impossible.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Conjuring unevidenced scenarios inverts the burden of proof (rated: strong)
[[Dan McClellan]] characterizes this move as the freedom "to conjure up any and all possible scenarios that are not in evidence." Normal historical and textual reasoning asks *what the evidence supports*; this method instead asks *what cannot be ruled out*. That shifts the burden entirely onto the critic to prove a negative (that no reconciliation is possible), which for interpretive questions is essentially unmeetable.

### 2. "Lowering the bar and burying it underground" (rated: strong)
McClellan's image is that the method does not merely set a low evidentiary standard — it removes the standard from view entirely. A test that everything passes tests nothing. Because the harmonizer can always invent another "Rationalization C" (cf. the structure McClellan describes elsewhere, [[Rationalization C - The Third Thing Harmonization]]), no quantity of textual tension can ever count against inerrancy. The hypothesis becomes **unfalsifiable**.

### 3. Unfalsifiability disqualifies it as inquiry (rated: strong)
A claim immune to all possible counter-evidence is not a finding about the text; it is a commitment held prior to the text. This is why McClellan says the harmonizer is not trying to convince peer review or critical thinkers — see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]].

### Steelman of the opposing view
The harmonizer could reply that *all* historical reconstruction involves inference beyond direct evidence, and that demanding positive proof for every harmonizing detail would also paralyze ordinary historiography (we routinely posit unattested travel, lost sources, etc.). Fairly stated, the issue is not inference per se but **calibration**: legitimate reconstruction proportions confidence to evidence and remains revisable, whereas the criticized method assigns maximal confidence (the contradiction is *dissolved*) on the strength of bare possibility, and is not revisable in principle. The defect is the asymmetry and the immunity to revision, not the use of inference.

### Scholarly / methodological context
This mirrors a standard critique in philosophy of science (Popper on falsifiability) and in source criticism: explanations that can absorb any data lose explanatory value. The two-source reading of the flood account, for instance, makes a **risky, evidence-anchored** prediction (doublets, vocabulary shifts, the divine-name alternation) that the text either does or does not display — and it does. See [[Genesis 7.2-3|Genesis 7:2-3]] and [[Genesis 7.8-9|Genesis 7:8-9]].
