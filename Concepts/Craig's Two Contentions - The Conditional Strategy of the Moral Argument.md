# Craig's Two Contentions - The Conditional Strategy of the Moral Argument

## The Point
Craig structures the entire debate around two **conditional** claims, and deliberately does *not* argue that God exists. **(1)** "If God exists, then we have a sound foundation for objective moral values and duties." **(2)** "If God does not exist, then we do not have a sound foundation for objective moral values and duties."[^1] Because the claims are conditionals, Harris's atheism — even if true — leaves them untouched: "Maybe Dr. Harris is right, that atheism is true. That wouldn't affect the truth of my two contentions. All that would follow is that objective moral values and duties would then, contrary to Dr. Harris, not exist."[^1] The strategy quietly weaponizes the shared premise: Craig and Harris *agree* objective morality exists, so if only theism can ground it, atheism is in trouble — a tacit *modus tollens* toward God's existence without ever defending it directly.

## Evidence
Contention (1) has two sub-points. Objective moral *values* are grounded in God's nature: as "the greatest conceivable being," God "is not merely perfectly good. He is the locus and paradigm of moral value," whose "holy and loving nature provides the absolute standard against which all actions are measured."[^2] Objective moral *duties* are then "constituted by God's commands," which are "not arbitrary" but "must be consistent with his holy and loving nature."[^3]

Contention (2) is the load-bearing half, and Craig insists it is where "the real debate" lies.[^4] On atheism, humans are "accidental byproducts of nature" with no more objective worth than "insect well-being or rat well-being," and morality is mere "herd morality" — a biological adaptation (Craig quotes Michael Ruse: "ethics is illusory… an aid to survival and reproduction").[^5] The conditional framing lets Craig set aside every objection to God's *existence* (problem of evil, hell, biblical atrocities) as strictly "irrelevant" to two if-then statements — the move analyzed in [[Ontology vs Semantics and Universality vs Objectivity - Craig's Two Distinctions]]. This is the live-debate form of the standard [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]] and the syllogism in [[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]].

## Best Quotes
> "First, if God exists, then we have a sound foundation for objective moral values and duties. And second, if God does not exist, then we do not have a sound foundation for objective moral values and duties." [^1]

> "I shall not be arguing tonight that God exists… Maybe Dr. Harris is right, that atheism is true. That wouldn't affect the truth of my two contentions." [^1]

> "What I'm offering is a sound foundation for the objective moral values and duties that we both hold dear." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The conditional strategy is fully usable by Latter-day Saint apologists, since it is neutral about the *content* of theology and turns only on whether a transcendent ground is needed. The Restoration sharpens contention (1)'s second clause, however. Craig grounds duties in divine *commands* flowing from God's nature; LDS theology grounds the moral order in **eternal law** that God himself honors and administers rather than authors from nothing ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]), which sits on the "third horn" of Euthyphro ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]) and avoids pure divine-command voluntarism even more decisively than Craig's reply. The tacit *modus tollens* (objective morality exists; only God grounds it; therefore God) is the same logic the vault records as turning evil into evidence *for* God ([[Borrowing Objective Morality to Condemn Evil]]) — a believer can run Craig's two contentions and then add the LDS premise that the standard is not God's bare will but the eternal nature of goodness that God perfectly embodies.
