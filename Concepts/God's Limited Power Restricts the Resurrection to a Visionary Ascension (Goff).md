# God's Limited Power Restricts the Resurrection to a Visionary Ascension (Goff)

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Philip Goff]] argues that if God's power is *limited*, then the resurrection cannot be the bodily, public, miracle-working event of the traditional reading — because such a God would face exactly the objections the limited-God hypothesis was built to escape. "If God can raise people from the dead, well, okay, we get back to why doesn't God do that more often"; a physically risen Jesus with "supernatural abilities... could have revealed himself to millions, put the truth of Christianity beyond dispute," reviving both the problem of evil and the problem of hiddenness.[^1] Goff's solution is to **identify the resurrection with the ascension**: Easter morning is the moment Jesus "returns to the father," and thereafter Jesus is experienced in *visions* — "a bit more like... Paul's experience on the road to Damascus" — overwhelming, involuntary, individual and group apparitions that prove "something real and radically new is taking place," even though it is "not a matter of seeing and touching a body."[^2] The view is thus *downstream of his theodicy*: the shape of the resurrection is set by what a constrained God is permitted to do.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The hiddenness/PoE constraint is the engine.** Goff is explicit that the motive is not antecedent interest in revising Christology: "it's to do with God's limitations... It would get us back to the problem of evil. It would get us to the problem of hiddenness."[^3] A God who selectively raises bodies but lets millions die young is back in the dock; a God experienced in inward visions is not. This is the resurrection-side application of the limited-God case in [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]].
- **Still a *physical* resurrection, not a spiritual one.** Goff insists he is *not* the easy target of a purely spiritual resurrection. Drawing on Paul's seed-and-wheat image and N.T. Wright's own term **"transphysicality,"** he claims "this is a physical resurrection but radically different... Maybe I'm taking it to its logical extreme."[^4] Jesus prepped his followers with the end-time-resurrection concept as "the best tool they had... to get onto this" transformed, physically-alive-but-radically-different state.[^5] He cites the Catholic philosopher Karen Kilby that his view is "not heretical by Catholic standings because it's physical."[^6]
- **The Eastern Orthodox theosis frame.** The visions are made sense of by the Orthodox idea that "the death and resurrection of God transformed the relationship between God and humanity," deepening the experiences of God available to us — God and physical reality "coming together through Jesus."[^7] See [[Acts 17.28-29]] ("in him we live and move and have our being") and [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4]].
- **The scholarly cover Goff claims (and partly retracts).** Goff: "there are a fair number of scholars who think the first Christians identified the resurrection" with the ascension, quoting Dale Allison (p. 224) that "for early Christians, Jesus' resurrection was his ascension."[^8] Craig corrects him live: Allison *reports* this as a common view but does not endorse it, and Goff concedes "I slightly misspoke... he's inclined to the view" of a close chronological gap, not strict identity.[^9] Rate: **a real but blunted appeal to authority** — the identity thesis has less scholarly backing than Goff first implied.
- **Steelman both sides.** *For Goff:* the move is internally coherent — a limited God forces a non-interventionist resurrection, and the visionary reading fits the involuntary, overwhelming character of the earliest reports (cf. the grief/vision hypotheses in [[Minimal Facts Fails - Grief Experiences Explain the Resurrection Appearances]]) while still affirming *physical* transformation. *For Craig:* it is the burden of N.T. Wright's *Resurrection of the Son of God* (Gary Habermass's "800-page word study") that **anastasis** never meant "being taken up into heaven out of this world" for pagans who denied it or Jews who believed it; first-century Jews "had a different category" for visionary experiences and "would not describe that with the language of resurrection from the dead." So the view is "simply unJewish" and "at odds with the historical evidence."[^10] Craig adds a *self-referential incoherence* charge: Goff says God is "too different" from physical creatures for us to share his life unless the incarnate logos is taken up into God physically — but "how could Christ's physical human nature be united with God if ours cannot?"[^11] Goff's reply: Jesus, "assuming Jesus is God incarnate, is part of one being with God," so his union creates a precedent and "closeness" that ours lacks.[^12]

## LDS Relevance
This is a non-LDS analytic philosopher letting the *constraints on God* dictate his Christology — and the structural parallel to Restoration thought is sharp, even though the conclusions diverge. Latter-day Saints share Goff's starting premise that a God who "works within an eternal law that he didn't author" is more defensible against evil than the omnipotent God of the creeds (the convergence catalogued in [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]] and [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]]), and they share his insistence on a genuinely *physical*, embodied resurrection over a "purely spiritual" one. But the LDS payoff runs *opposite* to Goff's on the empty tomb: because the LDS God is embodied and acts within the created order, a literal, bodily, "seeing-and-touching" resurrection (Luke 24:39; the angelic promise that "this same Jesus... shall so come in like manner," [[Acts 1.11]]) is exactly what a constrained-but-real God *does* accomplish — it does not reintroduce a hiddenness problem, because LDS theology already locates God's apparent hiddenness in the necessity of a probationary veil rather than in a deficit of power (see [[Schellenberg's Hiddenness Argument and the Christian Reply]] and [[Divine Hiddenness Is a Gift That Sanctifies]]). Goff and the Restoration thus agree that *omnipotence is the problem* and that the resurrection is *physical*; they split on whether a finite God's resurrection is a private vision (Goff) or a public, embodied fact (LDS). Goff inadvertently shows how far the "limited-God + physical resurrection" combination can be pushed — and where the LDS embodied-God model gets off his train.

## Best Quotes
> "If God can raise people from the dead, well, okay, we get back to why doesn't God do that more often... if Jesus raised in a physical body with certain supernatural abilities, it seems to me God Jesus could have revealed himself to millions, put the truth of Christianity beyond dispute." [^1]

> "The moment of the resurrection on Easter morning is the moment Jesus returns to the father, and Jesus is thereafter experienced in visions. A little bit like... Paul's experience on the road to Damascus." [^2]

> "This is a physical resurrection but radically different. Maybe I'm taking it to its logical extreme." [^4]

> (Craig) "The word resurrection — anastasis — never referred to the assumption of a person bodily into heaven... whether it was among pagans who denied it or Jews who believed in it... Philip's view is simply at odds with the historical evidence." [^10]
