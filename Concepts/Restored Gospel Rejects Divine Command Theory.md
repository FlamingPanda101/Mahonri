# Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] opens his ethics installment by naming what he calls "one of the unique features of the restored gospel": its rejection of **divine command theory** — the view "that God determines what is right and wrong," such that "an immoral act such as animal abuse could be made moral if God commanded it." On the LDS account, "even God himself is subject to Eternal law," and "it is his Conformity to the celestial order of being that makes him God." Morality is therefore *prior to and independent of* God's will; God is God because he perfectly conforms to it, not because he authors it. [^1] This is the same horn-selection that drives [[The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory]].

## Argument
1. **Divine command theory makes morality arbitrary.** If "God's nature is ultimately the source and standard of all moral judgments" by sheer fiat, then any act — even animal abuse — "could be made moral if God commanded it." The content of morality would float free of anything we could recognize as good. *(reductio)* [^1]
2. **The restored gospel grounds morality in eternal law, not divine will.** Against this, the LDS view holds that "even God himself is subject to Eternal law." There is a "celestial order of being" that is not a product of God's choice. *(theological premise)* [^1]
3. **God's divinity consists in conformity to that order.** "It is his Conformity to the celestial order of being that makes him God" — divinity is an *achievement of alignment* with an objective standard, not the power to set the standard. *(definitional)* [^1]
4. **Theosis is participation in the same order.** "We are invited to enter into that Celestial order of being and join him in that order of being in what we call godhood." The same law that constitutes God's divinity is open to us. *(soteriological)* [^1] Compare [[Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis]].

**Steelman of the divine-command defender:** A sophisticated theist replies that this is a false dichotomy. On the "God's nature" version of divine command theory (Alston, Adams), God neither obeys an external law nor commands arbitrarily — God *is* the Good, the standard itself, so there is no independent "eternal law" over him to which he conforms. Hansen's view, the critic adds, seems to demote God to one more being inside a prior metaphysical framework, raising the question of where that framework's authority comes from. The LDS reply (developed elsewhere) is that morality is not an external rulebook but the intrinsic structure of love and flourishing among eternally co-existent persons — so "subject to eternal law" does not mean "ruled by an alien legislator." See [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]] and [[Uncreated Intelligences Make Free Love Possible]].
