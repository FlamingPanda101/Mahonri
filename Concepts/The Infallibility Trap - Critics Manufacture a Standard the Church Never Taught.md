# The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught

## The Point
[[Austin Fife]] argues that the single most common move in critical arguments is a hidden false premise: that Church leaders **are or should be infallible**. The trap is rhetorical. If a critic can first get a member to accept leader infallibility, then produce one documented error, the case is over before it begins — "well, that is 'game over.'"[^1] Fife says recognizing this manufactured premise is what answered "nearly all" of his gospel questions: once he accepted that "men and women called by God are still subject to human error and mistaken judgment," the supposed knockdown evidence lost its force.[^2] The Church never claimed the standard the critic is wielding — "They are not infallible, nor have we ever taught that they are."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The premise is smuggled in by the critic, not the Church.** Fife found it "curious" that ex-members would assert infallibility *for* Church leaders, until he saw the function: it is a setup. Accept the premise, and any single mistake becomes disqualifying. Reject it, and the whole genre of "gotcha" history collapses to "a few human imperfections, even among prophets of God."[^1]

2. **Scripture itself models God calling flawed people.** "All of the evidence from the scriptures suggests that God is perfectly comfortable calling imperfect people to His work."[^2] The standard the LDS actually hold is reliability, not infallibility — see [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]] and [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]].

3. **Anachronistic judgment is the companion error.** Fife pairs the trap with presentism: "Judging past leaders based on today's standards is immature and unwise."[^3] He is willing to concede hard cases on these terms — "Was the African ban potentially a racist policy? Could be. And I hate that that happened. Are the prophets and apostles still called of God? Yes."[^4]

4. **It is a pastoral problem, not only a logical one.** Some former members "sincerely believed that church leaders should be infallible" while active; Fife treats the discovery that "adults are imperfect" as a painful but necessary maturation, and hopes "God calls the weak and simple" can become faith-promoting rather than faith-destroying.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "If a critic of the Church can get me to accept the false premise that Church leaders are infallible and then show me an instance where they erred, well, that is 'game over.'" [^1]

> "Once I accepted that men and women called by God are still subject to human error and mistaken judgment, I answered nearly all of my gospel questions." [^2]

> "They are not infallible, nor have we ever taught that they are. Judging past leaders based on today's standards is immature and unwise." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The restoration's prophetic model assumes fallibility from the start: the Book of Mormon title page warns that "if there are faults they are the mistakes of men," and Doctrine and Covenants 1:24-28 frames revelation as given to "weak" servants in their "language" so they can grow. Fife's "infallibility trap" is the practical apologetic of that doctrine — it dissolves the critic's strongest lever by refusing a premise the Church never held, much as [[Prophets Point to Christ Through Their Failings]] turns prophetic weakness into a witness rather than a scandal.
