# The "Johannine Community" Did Not Exist

## The Claim
The 20th-century scholarly construct of a "Johannine community" — a single community (or chain of communities) that produced and transmitted the Gospel and letters of John — is unnecessary and probably false. The similarities among these texts are better explained by **later authors copying and imitating an earlier text** than by a shared tradition-bearing community. [[Hugo Mendez]]'s 2020 article, "Did the Johannine Community Exist?", in effect answers "no."

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The community idea is an inference, not a datum.** The notion arose to explain why the Gospel and letters share distinctive language ("word," "life," "abiding," "doing the truth") yet differ in subtle ways — scholars posited multiple authors who must have been socially connected. *(Scholar's account of the hypothesis's origin; moderate evidence — it is a reconstruction by its own admission.)* [^1]
2. **A simpler mechanism fits the data: literary copying (pseudepigraphy).** Méndez argues each author "really is copying from other authors" — imitating an earlier text's style to look like the same author, the well-attested ancient phenomenon of pseudepigraphy. *(Strong as an explanatory parsimony argument; the mechanism is independently documented.)* [^2]
3. **Clear ancient analogues exist.** Paul wrote letters, and others wrote letters in Paul's name — some canonical, some not — so authors writing in the name/style of a predecessor is a known, broad pattern, not an ad hoc move. *(Strong; comparative.)* [^3]
4. **The authors may have been geographically dispersed.** If the texts are imitative rather than communal, their authors "might have been located in actually very different communities, geographies, settings" — removing the need for a single locale. *(Moderate; follows from the model rather than proving it.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The community hypothesis is not arbitrary: shared "social dialect" really does mark religious groups (Méndez's own example — people who say they're "going to mass" are probably not Southern Baptists), and the texts' tight verbal/thematic overlap is real. A common community is a natural inference, and it also honored the mid-century assumption that gospel traditions were transmitted orally/communally before being written. The disagreement is about the *best* explanation of agreed data, not about the data.

## Best Quotes
