# The Conditioners Step Outside the Tao and Remake Human Nature

## The Point
The final stage of Lewis's prognosis is the arrival of the **Conditioners**: a managing class that has stepped entirely outside the Tao and now treats the moral law itself as something to be manufactured rather than obeyed.[^1] "They know how to produce conscience and decide what kind of conscience they will produce. They themselves are outside, above."[^1] Having abandoned every given value, they have nothing left to guide them but raw impulse — and so the values they instil in their subjects are grounded "in nothing but the Conditioners' arbitrary [will]."[^2] Ward emphasizes that this is "the wrong kind of objectivity": moral value *is* objective, but it must never be **objectified**, reduced to a mere thing one stands over and manipulates.[^1]

## Evidence
Ward describes the abolition of man as a **two-stage process**: "first, the Conditioners abolish their own humanity by embracing subjectivism; second, they abolish the humanity of everyone else by conditioning them in their own image and likeness."[^3] Lewis is careful, Ward notes, to insist the Conditioners are not necessarily wicked: "I am not supposing them to be bad men. They are, rather, not men (in the old sense) at all" — they are "men who have sacrificed their own share in... humanity in order to devote themselves to the task of deciding what 'Humanity' shall henceforth mean."[^4] Gilbert Meilaender, quoted by Ward, draws out why this is self-destructive: "though we can refuse to accept the maxims of the Tao as binding upon us, in so doing we turn against our nature and deprive ourselves of... our humanity... It is logically possible to use our freedom in this way, but it is... ontologically disastrous."[^4] Once outside the Tao, the Conditioners find every motive collapses into a *petitio* — there is no longer any "ought," only "sic volo, sic jubeo" ("as I will, so I command"), the naked **will to power**.[^5] Ward links this to Ratzinger's "dictatorship of relativism" and to Juvenal's haunting question, "*Sed quis custodiet ipsos custodes?*" — Who will control the controllers?[^5] He also observes that B.F. Skinner, in *Beyond Freedom and Dignity*, far from refuting Lewis, simply **restated and welcomed** the very abolition Lewis warned against: "What is being abolished is autonomous man... To man qua man we readily say good riddance."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "It is not that they are bad men. They are not men at all... Nor are their subjects necessarily unhappy men. They are not men at all: they are artefacts." (Lewis) [^7]

> "Everything except the *sic volo, sic jubeo* has been explained away." (Lewis) [^5]

## LDS Connection
The Conditioners — who "decide what kind of conscience they will produce" while standing "outside, above" the moral law — are a precise image of the counterfeit plan rejected in the premortal council, in which souls would be remade and saved by compulsion, their agency overridden for the conditioner's "own reasons" (Moses 4:1-4). Latter-day Saint theology insists that even God does **not** stand "outside, above" eternal law as an arbitrary will; He is the embodiment of that law, not its inventor (cf. [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; Alma 42). Conscience is not a product to be engineered but the **Light of Christ** given to every person (Moroni 7:16-19; D&C 88:11-13). And the Conditioners' "two-stage" self-destruction — losing their own humanity in order to dominate others — illustrates 2 Nephi 2's teaching that agency turned against eternal law is fundamentally self-defeating. Where [[Science Can Determine Human Values]] and Skinner would gladly "de-homunculize" man, Lewis and the Restoration both insist the inner person is real, eternal, and not the Conditioners' to abolish.
