# Prophetic Failed-Prophecy Test Is Self-Refuting for Cessationists (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] applies the Deuteronomy 18 test to Joseph Smith: a true prophet's predictions come true; Joseph's temple prophecies (e.g., building a temple in his lifetime) did not; therefore he fails "the normal way we test whether a prophet is a true or false prophet."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The objector's own church rejects the test (rated: strong, internal critique).** [[Luke Irwin]]: Catholic Answers — Heschmeyer's employer — and [[Trent Horn]] (his colleague) teach that "Jesus represents the fullness of God's divine revelation, and so there will be no more prophets giving public revelation after him." If Catholicism is *cessationist* about prophets, then "you do not use the test of prophet to determine if there's a prophet. You don't believe in them anymore." Heschmeyer cannot pose as neutrally applying Deuteronomy 18 to a category he has already declared closed.[^2] See [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic]].

2. **Scripture itself has conditional/unfulfilled prophecy (rated: strong, biblical).** Heschmeyer concedes the steelman: [[Jonah 3.4|Jonah]] proclaims "in forty days Nineveh will be destroyed," and it is not — because there is "an implicit conditionality to some of the promises of God." [[Jacob Hansen]] adds that this objection "argues like an atheist": skeptics use the very same move against Jesus' own "this generation shall not pass" prophecies. To accept conditional/unfulfilled prophecy for Jonah and Jesus but not for Joseph Smith is a double standard.[^3] See [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]] and [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]].

3. **A factual correction undercuts Heschmeyer's reliability (rated: moderate, ad rem).** Irwin notes Heschmeyer repeatedly claims Joseph Smith died "in an attempt to break out of jail in a gunfight," which "just didn't happen" — and urges him to read [[Richard Bauckham|Richard Bushman]].[^4] *Steelman:* the strongest critical version concedes conditionality exists but argues some of Joseph's prophecies were *unconditional and specific* (e.g., the Missouri temple "in this generation," D&C 84), so the Jonah/conditionality escape may not cover every case. The LDS reply is twofold: (a) the conditionality and human-fallibility principles plausibly do cover them, and (b) the *objector* has no standing to wield the test at all, having abolished living prophecy.

## Best Quotes

> "It says the Bible says Jesus represents the fullness of God's divine revelation... so you don't use Deuteronomy to test if there are prophets anymore. By the way, that's from Trent Horn, your esteemed colleague."[^2]

> "This is arguing like an atheist. Every atheist is like, 'Remember when Jesus said in this generation you guys will...' — the Bible literally has that exact same language."[^3]

> "There's an implicit conditionality to some of the promises of God." — Joe Heschmeyer[^3]
