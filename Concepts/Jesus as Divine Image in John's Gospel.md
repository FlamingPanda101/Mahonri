# Jesus as Divine Image in John's Gospel
#type/claim

## The Claim
When John's Gospel says "he that hath seen me hath seen the Father" (John 14:9), it is not asserting that Jesus *is* the Father, nor that one literally perceives God's own self in Jesus. It operates on the ancient Near Eastern logic of the **divine image**: Jesus *bears* the Father's presence the way a cult image or idol was held to bear the presence of its deity. To look at the image is to look at the god — but you are not seeing the god's own essence directly. On this reading John 14:9 is fully compatible with John 1:18's "no one has seen God," because the two verses describe mediated presence, not direct vision.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The image logic stated.** Per [[Dan McClellan]], "to look at Jesus is to look at God... It's not because Jesus is the Father. It's because Jesus bears the Father's presence just like a divine image... You're not actually seeing God's own self." The grammar of "seeing God" in John is the grammar of representation, not identity.
- **Ancient background.** In the surrounding cultures a divine image localized and made present an otherwise unseen deity; worship directed at the image was directed *through* it to the god. John reframes Jesus as that image-bearer — the true locale of the Father's presence — which is why seeing him counts as seeing the Father.
- **It dissolves a supposed contradiction.** This reading is the constructive flip-side of [[Pre-Incarnate Christ Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]]: the New Testament's own "see God through Jesus" framework is image-mediation, a *different* mechanism from the direct, unmediated Old Testament theophanies (Jacob and Moses seeing God "face to face"). Recognizing the two mechanisms removes the need to harmonize them by force.
- **Steelman.** A traditional Trinitarian will object that John's high Christology (the Logos who "was God," John 1:1) means Jesus shares the divine essence, so "seeing the Father in him" is more than image-representation. The image reading need not deny John's exalted Christology; it claims only that the *visibility* language works by presence-bearing, which is what lets John affirm both Jesus's divinity and the Father's invisibility without contradiction.
