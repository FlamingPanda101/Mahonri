# If You Want Strict Monotheism Be a Muslim

## The Claim

If one genuinely wants *strict* monotheism — one being as the sole ground of reality with no other divine beings — the consistent destination is Islam, not biblical Christianity. The Bible's divine plurality-in-unity sits between strict monotheism and crude polytheism. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Sijuwade's Islam argument.** [[Joshua Sijuwade]] (whom Avery has debated) argues Islam presents itself as restoring Abrahamic monotheism but in fact *reverses* Judaism's ontology: from "multiple divine beings under one supreme God" to "no other divine beings at all." Thus Islam, not the Bible, embodies strict monotheism. (Evidence: a clever turnabout; it accurately notes that *tawhid* is more numerically strict than the Hebrew Bible's divine-council picture.) [^1]
2. **The rhetorical jab.** "If you want strict monotheism, where you have one being that's the ground of all reality, go be a Muslim. 'Cause that isn't what the Bible teaches." Hansen plans to put this to Avery directly. [^2]
3. **Function in the argument.** It reframes the burden: the Trinitarian who attacks the LDS Godhead as "polytheistic" is, by demanding strict oneness, actually closer to the Islamic position than to the biblical divine-council data. (Evidence: rhetorically effective; depends on the [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum|divine-spectrum]] thesis being granted.) [^3]
4. **Steelman.** The Trinitarian responds that this is a false trichotomy: classical Trinitarianism is *itself* a third option — one being, three persons — precisely designed to affirm robust monotheism *and* the deity of Father, Son, and Spirit, without either Islamic unitarianism or polytheism. The Christian doesn't have to "become a Muslim" to be a monotheist; he denies that strict numerical oneness requires unipersonality. (Evidence: this is the entire point of the doctrine the conversation is contesting; the jab presupposes the Trinity has already failed the coherence test.) The LDS reply: the Trinity's "one being, three persons" is the very formula the [[The Who or What Test for the Word God|who/what test]] and the [[Three Persons Sharing a Nature Are Still Three Beings|counting argument]] claim is incoherent, so it is not available as a stable third option. [^1]
