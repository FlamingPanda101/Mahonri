# Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30

## The Claim

The physical constants and initial conditions of the universe are so precisely
calibrated for life that design is overwhelmingly more probable than chance.
Remarkably, the Book of Mormon prophet [[Alma the Younger]] articulated a
recognizably modern teleological argument around 74 BC — centuries before
[[Thomas Aquinas]] and nearly two millennia before modern cosmology discovered
the fine-tuning problem. This constitutes evidence both for the existence of
God and for the ancient authenticity of the Book of Mormon.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Fine-Tuning of Physical Constants (Evidence Rating: Primary)

Modern physics has identified approximately 20-30 physical constants and
initial conditions whose values fall within extraordinarily narrow,
life-permitting ranges. If any were slightly different, life of any kind would
be impossible. [[Hayden Carroll]] argues that the odds of these constants
aligning by chance are "astronomically slim" — so slim that design is more
reasonable than chance.[^1]

Key examples (not exhaustively listed in the video but implicit in the
discussion):
- **Gravitational constant:** Slightly stronger, and stars burn too hot and
  fast for life; slightly weaker, and stars never ignite.
- **Strong nuclear force:** Slightly different, and either no atoms heavier
  than hydrogen form, or all hydrogen is consumed.
- **Cosmological constant (dark energy):** Fine-tuned to 1 part in 10^120 — if
  different by a tiny fraction, the universe either recollapses instantly or
  expands too fast for galaxies to form.
- **Earth's tilt and orbit:** The "Goldilocks zone" and axial tilt are
  precisely right for stable climate and seasons.[^2]

### 2. Alma 30:44 — An Ancient Teleological Argument (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

Alma's rebuttal to the atheist Korihor in [[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]] invokes
the cosmic order as evidence of a "Supreme Creator":

> "All things denote there is a God; yea, even the earth, and all things that
> are upon the face of it, yea, and its motion, yea, and also all the planets
> which move in their regular form do witness that there is a Supreme
> Creator."[^3]

Key observations:
- **"Its motion"** — the text references the earth's movement (orbital
  mechanics), not a stationary earth.
- **"Regular form"** — the planets follow law-like, mathematically regular
  orbits. This is precisely what modern fine-tuning arguments emphasize: the
  planets don't move chaotically but in stable, life-permitting patterns.
- **Temporal Priority:** [[David Butler]] notes that "Alma is the original
  teleological arguer way before St. Aquinas or any of the other early church
  fathers who used it."[^4] If the Book of Mormon is an ancient text, this is a
  striking anticipation of arguments that would not be formalized for over a
  millennium.

### 3. The Constants as "Irrevocably Decreed in Heaven" (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

Butler connects the physical constants to [[D&C 130.20-21]]:

> "There is a law, irrevocably decreed in heaven before the foundations of this
> world, upon which all blessings are predicated — And when we obtain any
> blessing from God, it is by obedience to that law upon which it is
> predicated."[^5]

The parallel: physical constants are "laws irrevocably decreed" that govern the
universe's operation. God did not create these laws arbitrarily; they are
eternal, and God himself operates in harmony with them. This distinguishes LDS
theology from voluntarist theologies where God could have made any laws He
wanted.[^5]

### 4. Joseph Smith Could Not Have Known About Fine-Tuning (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Historical Inference)

> "First of all, Joseph Smith, if he wrote the Book of Mormon, he didn't know
> about the fine-tuning argument. The fine-tuning argument was not — as far as
> the initial conditions and the constants — didn't exist, right? Because we
> didn't understand what was out there the way that we do."[^6]

This is a key apologetic move: if Joseph Smith fabricated the Book of Mormon in
1829, how did Alma 30:44 anticipate a cosmological argument that would not be
developed in modern form until the late 20th century? The pre-Joseph Smith
design arguments (Aquinas, Paley) were based on biological complexity, not
cosmological fine-tuning. The physical constants were not discovered until the
20th century.

Carroll concedes that "there were pre-Joseph Smith theologians who were using
the teleological argument" for biological design, but "the physical constants,
the scientific ones, the theologians didn't know. They've actually been
verified or confirmed or validated in time."[^6]

### 5. The "Constants Don't Change" Rebuttal to Infinite Time (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Logical)

Some atheists respond: "Given infinite time, eventually the constants will
align." Carroll identifies this as an oxymoron:

> "A constant does not change. That's the — so it's not — entropy doesn't
> change. Entropy is a law of the universe that they would accept."[^7]

A constant, by definition, does not fluctuate over time. If the gravitational
constant were truly a *constant*, it cannot "eventually align" — it either is
or isn't. The multiverse is an attempt to escape this by positing *different*
constants in *different* universes, but this merely relocates the problem. See
[[Multiverse as Ad-Hoc Escape from Fine-Tuning]].

### 6. LDS Doctrine of Eternal Matter Aligns with Conservation Laws (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

Butler notes that atheists often appeal to the law of conservation of
mass-energy ("matter was neither created nor destroyed") — and LDS theology
already affirms this:

> "We actually do believe that and we believe in God."[^7]

Because LDS theology teaches that matter is eternal (not created *ex nihilo*),
it does not face the problem of explaining how something came from nothing — a
point of alignment with scientific conservation laws that *ex nihilo* theisms
struggle with.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: The Anthropic Principle Explains Fine-Tuning Without Design
**Steelman:** Of course we observe a life-permitting universe — if it weren't,
we wouldn't be here to observe it. The anthropic principle (weak form) states
that our observations are necessarily conditioned by the fact that we exist as
observers. We shouldn't be surprised that the universe appears fine-tuned; any
universe with observers must have life-permitting parameters.

**Response:** The weak anthropic principle does not *explain* why the constants
have the values they do; it merely notes that *if* they were different, we
wouldn't be here to notice. This is a selection effect, not a causal
explanation. As Carroll argues with the laundry analogy (see [[Laundry Dryer
Analogy for Design]]), improbability still demands an explanation — "there is a
chance" is not the same as "this is the more reasonable explanation." The
strong anthropic principle (the universe *must* have these properties) is
teleological and arguably theistic.

### Objection 2: Alma 30:44 Is Vague and Not a Modern Fine-Tuning Argument
**Steelman:** "The earth, its motion, and the planets in their regular form" is
a generic design argument that any pre-scientific person could formulate by
looking at the sky. It does not reference physical constants, quantum
mechanics, or specific fine-tuning parameters. Reading 20th-century cosmology
into a 19th-century (or ancient) text is anachronistic.

**Response:** Carroll's claim is more modest: "Joseph Smith... didn't know
about the fine-tuning argument" as a *scientific* argument with specific
constants.[^6] The point is not that Alma 30:44 *teaches* modern physics, but
that it anticipates the *structure* of the argument — cosmic order as evidence
of a creator — and that Joseph Smith, if he fabricated the text, happened to
include an argument whose full scientific force would not be appreciated for
over a century. This is an argument from congruence, not identity.

### Objection 3: Multiverse Theories Remove the Need for Fine-Tuning
**Steelman:** If there are a vast (possibly infinite) number of universes with
varying physical constants, then it is not surprising that at least one (ours)
has life-permitting values. We're just the lucky universe.

**Response:** See [[Multiverse as Ad-Hoc Escape from Fine-Tuning]] for a full
rebuttal. In brief: the multiverse is itself a theoretical postulate with no
empirical evidence, and it merely pushes the fine-tuning problem back one
level — what fine-tuned the multiverse-generating mechanism?
