# The New Testament Canon Is a Reaction to Marcion

## The Claim
The orthodox New Testament canon exists largely as a **reaction to [[Marcion]]**'s rival canon. There was no fixed, complete Bible for the first ~400 years; the earliest surviving list of the 27 NT books is [[Athanasius]]'s 367 Easter letter, and the canon was assembled by debated criteria, not handed down whole.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Marcion forced the question (rating: strong; standard church history)
Marcion (~144 AD) rejected the Old Testament and its God, keeping only an edited Luke and ten Pauline letters. "It's because of his proposal of what is canon that we even have a canon... the Christian canon is a reaction to Marcion." [^1]

### 2. Three criteria for inclusion (rating: strong; per Athanasius/Day)
Day lists the working rules: (a) **orthodox** — it must not teach anything heretical; (b) **apostolic** — connected to an apostle (Mark via Peter, Luke via Paul); (c) **catholicity** — commonly used across the churches. A book failing any one was excluded. [^2]

### 3. Athanasius's 367 list (rating: strong; documentary)
In a letter fixing the date of Easter, [[Athanasius]] proposed exactly the 27 books now in the New Testament — "the earliest one that I can find that says that's what is going to be in the canon." [^3]

### 4. The canon was genuinely contested at the edges (rating: moderate; historical)
Revelation was "on the chopping block"; the Didache and the Shepherd of Hermas "almost made it." This shows the boundary was decided, not self-evident. See [[The Didache as Earliest Church Manual]] and [[Heresy Was Decided by Votes Not Demonstrable Truth]]. [^2]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Evangelicals argue the church *recognized* (did not *create*) the canon: the apostolic books carried inherent, self-authenticating authority (cf. [[2 Timothy 3.14-17|2 Tim 3:16]]; Peter already treating Paul's letters as "scripture," 2 Pet 3:16), and Marcion merely accelerated a recognition already underway, since collections of gospels and Paulines circulated before him. The closing-the-canon warnings (e.g. [[Revelation 22.18-19|Rev 22:18-19]]) are invoked against *adding* revelation. Day's counter: Rev 22:18-19 applies to that book, not the Bible (which didn't yet exist), and the four-century delay plus debated criteria show the canon was a human-conciliar product reacting to crises like Marcion.
