# Religiosity Benefits Mental Health Even After Leaving

## The Claim
Whatever level of religiosity a person maintains tends to associate with better mental health — so even someone who leaves the institutional Church can retain mental-health benefits by continuing private practices like prayer, scripture study, and connection with God.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Dose-response across time (rated: moderate, longitudinal).** "The more religious you are earlier tends to relate to increases in well-being across time" — a relationship established by tracking people longitudinally rather than at a single snapshot.[^1]

2. **Benefit decoupled from membership (rated: moderate).** "Whatever level of religiosity you have is typically associated with better mental health. So if you leave the religion... but you're still doing your prayer, scripture study, you're still connecting with God, all that, that might be a wind blowing in a different direction to benefit you."[^2] *Steelman:* "religiosity" measured post-exit may itself select for more stable individuals; the wind metaphor is rhetorical rather than a precise effect size, and the claim does not quantify how much leaving costs versus how much continued private practice recovers.

3. **Coheres with the retention predictor (rated: moderate).** It dovetails with daily connection to God being the top retention factor — the same private spirituality that keeps people in also benefits those who drift out. See [[Daily Connection With God Is the Top Predictor of Retention]].[^1]

## Best Quotes

> "Whatever level of religiosity you have is typically associated with better mental health."[^2]

> "If you're still doing your prayer, scripture study, you're still connecting with God... that might be a wind blowing in a different direction to benefit you in that way."[^2]

> "The more religious you are earlier tends to relate to increases in well-being across time."[^1]
