# John 1:1c — Anarthrous *Theos* and a Subordinate, Participatory Divinity

## The Claim
In [[John 1.1|John 1:1]]c — "and the Word was God" — *theos* is **anarthrous** (no definite article). Read against [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]]'s usage, this marks the Word (Logos) as **divine but not the ultimate God of the universe**: a being with a *subordinate, participatory* divinity grounded in its oneness with God. John's Christology is high but **not yet Nicene/Trinitarian**.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The grammar: anarthrous, pre-verbal *theos*.** John 1:1 reads *kai theos en ho logos* — "the Word was God" with **no article** on *theos* (the "anarthrous" feature). Clause 1:1b, by contrast, has the article — "the Word was with [the] God." The contrast is doing theological work. *(Strong; the Greek datum is uncontested.)* [^1]
2. **Philo's distinction is the template.** [[Philo of Alexandria]] distinguishes the transcendent ultimate God (with article, *ho theos*) from the *logos* — God's externalized mind/reason/thought — which is "with" God yet abstractly distinct, and for which Philo does **not** use the article. Méndez argues John builds "on the exact same foundation." *(Strong; a direct Hellenistic-Jewish parallel from the same era.)* [^2]
3. **Mediator theology: God acts through intermediaries.** John is "swimming in the same streams of Hellenistic Jewish philosophy" in which the ultimate God interacts with creation through intermediary beings, the highest being his own abstracted mind/word. The Logos is divine "because of its oneness with God." *(Strong; situates the verse in its philosophical milieu.)* [^3]
4. **High but pre-Nicene: the "cake vs. ingredients" image.** Méndez tells students Nicene theology is "the cake" while John supplies "the ingredients" (eggs, flour, sugar) — necessary for, but not identical to, the later synthesis of Nicaea and Constantinople. *(Moderate; an analogy clarifying the developmental claim.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Grammarians like [[Daniel Wallace]] argue the anarthrous pre-verbal predicate *theos* is **qualitative** — affirming the Word fully shares the divine nature (not "a god," not "the Father") — which is precisely what later Trinitarian orthodoxy claims. On this reading 1:1c does not subordinate the Word; it distinguishes person from person while affirming one nature. The Philonic parallel shows the *grammar*'s logic but does not by itself prove John shared Philo's subordinationism rather than later orthodoxy's reading.

## Best Quotes
> "What you have at the very end of that verse is 'and the word was God' without a definite article in Greek. That's what we mean by anarthrous." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^1]

> "For Philo... [there is] the transcendent ultimate God of Judaism, which he uses a definite article for... and then the logos, the word of God... [for which] he doesn't use a definite article. I think that John is building on the exact same foundation." [^2]

> "This word is not, I think in the gospel of John's conception, the ultimate god of the universe, but this is a divine being... who has, I think in John's conception, a subordinate participatory divinity. It's divine because of its oneness with God." [^3]

> "Nicene theology is the cake. The ingredients are eggs and flour and sugar... you absolutely need them for cake. John's theology wasn't quite that of Nicaea and first Constantinople." [^4]
