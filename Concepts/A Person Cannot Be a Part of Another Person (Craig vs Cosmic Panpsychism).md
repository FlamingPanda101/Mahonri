# A Person Cannot Be a Part of Another Person (Craig vs Cosmic Panpsychism)

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[William Lane Craig]] presses a personhood-mereology objection against [[Philip Goff]]'s cosmic panpsychism (the universe as a single conscious agent of which we are parts). Quoting Goff — "the universe is a conscious subject. All other things from planets to human beings to fundamental particles are conscious subjects that exist as proper parts of the universe subject" — Craig replies that "it is almost axiomatic in the philosophy of mind that one person cannot be a part of another person. When two persons get together, they are never a third person." Therefore "since we are persons, the universe cannot... be a person of which we are parts." And if that agentive universe is identified with the pantheistic God, the result "spells the end of God's being a person."[^1] This is a *distinct* argument from the combination problem: that one asks how micro-experiences sum into a unified mind; this one asks whether **personhood** can stand in a part/whole relation at all.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The mereological intuition.** Craig's load-bearing premise is that personhood does not aggregate: persons in combination form a group, a couple, a crowd — never a single super-person who has them as constituent persons. If that holds, Goff's "universe-subject with person-parts" is incoherent at the level of *persons*, independent of whether phenomenal qualities can combine. Rate: **the sharpest single objection in Craig's philosophy-of-mind set**, because it targets the structure Goff actually committed to in print.
- **Bundled with the category-mistake and causal-closure charges.** Craig frames Goff's view as non-reductive physicalism in which mental states are properties of physical objects, then argues (i) it is a "category mistake" to ascribe mental properties (sad, jubilant) to a physical object like a brain, which has only mass, density, location; (ii) it is "unscientific" because biology shows consciousness needs complex neural substrate (vertebrates yes; "ants, slugs, oysters, algae, bacteria, mushrooms" no; chairs and pens emphatically no); and (iii) causal closure of the physical makes mental states epiphenomenal and rules out free agency — so "the universe is not a free agent after all," and a pantheistic God identified with it "is not a free moral agent."[^2]
- **Goff's replies.** (1) *Russellian monism*: physics "doesn't really tell us what matter is... just gives us this abstract mathematical structure"; as Hawking put it, physics doesn't say what "breathes fire into the equations," so we cannot rule out that the matter in a brain (or cosmos) is constituted by consciousness.[^3] (2) The conscious part/whole claim is **not** axiomatic — "that is false; there is a big debate with two sides on this," and Goff cites his own co-written paper with Luke Roelofs on subject-summing.[^4] (3) On freedom, Goff is "open to strong emergent forms of panpsychism" with "new conscious unities, new minds that emerge at the human level that have their own causal powers" and libertarian free will — so "you needn't even have part-wholes."[^5] (4) Crucially, Goff *retreats from the cosmic-mind view itself* for the purposes of his Christianity, preferring a looser **panentheism** (a Venn-diagram overlap of God and world) and stressing the conscious-universe thesis "is not the deal breaker for me... God being limited in power" is.[^6]
- **Steelman both sides.** *For Craig:* even granting Russellian monism about *matter*, the objection is about *persons*, not micro-qualities — and the intuition that "two persons never make a third person" is robust; Goff's emergentist escape arguably concedes the point (a new emergent mind with its own powers looks more like substance dualism, which Craig notes "borders on" William Hasker's emergent-substance dualism). *For Goff:* the "axiom" is genuinely contested in the technical literature (subject-summing is live), and since Goff does not *need* the cosmic-person thesis for his limited-God Christianity, the objection, even if sound, does not touch the position he actually defends.

## LDS Relevance
The vault already meets panpsychism's *combination* problem in [[Panpsychism Collapses into Reductionism]]; Craig's part/whole argument is the complementary *personhood* objection, and it lands close to a genuine Latter-day Saint sensibility. Restoration cosmology is friendlier than classical theism to a universe "full of intelligences" — light/intelligence that "giveth life to all things" and fills the immensity of space (D&C 88:11–13) — yet it firmly preserves Craig's conclusion that **persons are not parts of a larger person**: human intelligences are uncreated, individual, agentive selves, never proper parts dissolved into a cosmic subject (see [[A Cosmic Mind Grounds the Universe (Theistic Idealism)]]). This is why the LDS reader can affirm a mind-suffused cosmos *and* Craig's anti-pantheist point at once — the same "grant the critique, keep the openness" balance the vault strikes against panpsychism elsewhere. Where the LDS view parts company with Craig is upstream: Goff is reaching for limitation in God because the omnipotent God cannot answer evil, and *that* instinct (a God constrained by what eternally is) the Restoration shares — see [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]]. The cosmic-person *mechanism* fails, but the *constrained-God* motive behind it is the very ground LDS theology occupies.

## Best Quotes
> "It is almost axiomatic in the philosophy of mind that one person cannot be a part of another person. When two persons get together, they are never a third person... since we are persons, the universe cannot therefore be a person of which we are parts." [^1]

> (Craig) "It's just a category mistake to say that the brain has mental properties. The brain is not happy. The brain is not sad... These are properties of a self or soul and not of a physical object." [^2]

> (Goff) "Bill said something that was just false... that it is axiomatic that you can't have conscious part-wholes — that is false. There is a big debate with two sides on this." [^4]
