# The Septuagint of Isaiah 9-6 Is Expansive Not Truncated

#type/claim

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

[[John D. Meade]] reads the **Septuagint** of [[Isaiah 9.6|Isaiah 9:6]] as: "...his name will be called **messenger of great counsel**" (*megalēs boulēs angelos*) — and then deliberately stops: "I'll just stop reading there because there's no other name that he'll be called by." He characterizes this as a "weird" interpretive rendering. The effect (whether intended or not) is to make the Greek look as if it simply *ignored* the rest of the Hebrew titles ("Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace"), and far more divergent from the traditional Hebrew than it really is.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (McClellan's Rebuttal)

### 1. The Greek did not drop the rest — it read it as a *clause* (rating: strong; primary, the central point)

The ancient translators did not treat the final words (*avi'ad sar shalom*) as a name at all; they rendered them as a **statement**. Meade omits this. Citing a scholarly discussion of LXX-Isaiah (the red boxes McClellan shows), the Greek runs **two extra lines beyond** "messenger of great counsel," producing the clause: "for I will bring peace to the rulers, peace and health to him." The Septuagint here is therefore **expansive** — "doubling up" on that part of the Hebrew — not deficient.[^2]

> "the Septuagint is actually expansive here... the Greek translation seems to be doubling up on that part of the Hebrew." [^2]

### 2. Omitting the expansive half misrepresents *which* part is "weird" (rating: strong)

Meade calls the surviving "messenger of great counsel" phrase the weird rendering. But the genuinely unusual move — turning a string of names into a full sentence with *added* content — is exactly the part he cut. "We've omitted the actual weird part of the rendering... unless we were trying to give people the impression that the only rendering was this reference to the messenger of great counsel," i.e. that the Greek was far more divergent from the Hebrew than it is.[^3]

### 3. The real driver: the LXX evades a *human* being called "God" (rating: strong; literary-historical)

The rendering is not arbitrary. The translators "are just trying to make sense of the fact that this passage seems to be naming a human God. And they don't like that" — lacking a theology that makes Jesus the God of Israel, they take the standard escape hatch: read a Hebrew word for "God/gods" as "angel/messenger." So *el gibbor* ("Mighty God") becomes part of "messenger of great counsel." This is the same LXX maneuver documented at [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]].[^4]

### 4. Evangelicals apply this LXX move with a double standard (rating: strong; comparative)

Where the same "angelizing" rendering suits them, evangelicals *prefer* the LXX over the Hebrew:
- **[[Psalm 8.5|Psalm 8:5]]** — the LSB (Huff's preferred translation) reads "a little lower than the **angels**," but the Hebrew says *elohim*, "gods."
- **[[Psalm 29.1-2|Psalm 29:1]]** — the LSB reads "O ye **mighty**" / "sons of the mighty," punting to the KJV, but the Hebrew *bene elim* means "sons of **gods**."

The old justification — that *el / elim / elohim* derive from a root meaning "to be mighty" — was "definitively put to rest" by **Marvin Pope in 1955** and rejected by scholars for 70+ years. So the LXX rendering is dismissed as "weird" where it threatens theology and *adopted over the Hebrew* where it serves it.[^5] See [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]].

## LDS Relevance

The Isaiah 9:6 throne-names are a staple proof-text for the creedal "Jesus is the Most High God" reading. McClellan's point cuts both ways usefully for Latter-day Saint exegesis: the verse's Hebrew really does appear to call a human figure *el gibbor* ("Mighty God"), and it is the *Septuagint* — not the Hebrew — that flinches from that, by demoting "God" to "messenger." That undercuts the apologetic habit of leaning on Greek tradition to police what the Hebrew titles can mean, and it exposes a selective, theology-driven use of the LXX.
