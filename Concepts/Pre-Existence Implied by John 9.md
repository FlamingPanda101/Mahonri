# Pre-Existence Implied by John 9

## The Claim

The disciples' question to Jesus about the man born blind — "Master, who did
sin, this man, or his parents, that he was born blind?" ([[John 9.1-3|John 9:2]]) — only makes sense if the disciples presupposed some form of pre-mortal
existence in which the man could have sinned before birth. Jesus's response
("Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents") does not correct the premise
of the question, suggesting He found it intelligible even if He rejected its
specific application. This, combined with [[Jeremiah 1.5|Jeremiah 1:5]] and
other texts, constitutes biblical evidence for pre-existence — a doctrine
formally preserved only in LDS theology among major Christian traditions.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Logic of the Disciples' Question (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

[[Hayden Carroll]] and [[David Butler]] walk through the logic:[^1]

1. The man was "blind from his birth" (John 9:1) — a congenital condition.
2. The disciples ask, "Who did sin, THIS MAN, or his parents, that he was born
   blind?" (emphasis added).
3. The question includes the man himself as a potential sinner whose sin caused
   his own congenital blindness.
4. For the man's own sin to have caused his congenital blindness, the sin must
   have occurred BEFORE he was born.
5. Sin requires existence, agency, and moral capacity.
6. Therefore, the question presupposes the man existed (and could sin) before
   birth.

> "If he didn't exist before he was born, how could he have sinned?"[^2]

### 2. Jesus Did Not Correct the Premise (Evidence Rating: Primary — Argument from Silence)

> "He didn't correct them. He didn't say, 'Why are you asking that? That's a
> dumb question. How could he?'"[^3]

Jesus's response addresses only whether sin was the *cause* of the blindness
("Neither... but that the works of God should be made manifest"), not whether
the question itself was coherent. If the disciples held a heretical belief
about pre-existence, Jesus had an opportunity to correct it — and didn't.

This is an argument from silence, but a suggestive one: Jesus often corrected
misunderstandings (e.g., "Ye do err, not knowing the scriptures" — Matthew
22:29). His silence on the pre-existence premise, combined with the fact that
He engages the question on its own terms, suggests the premise was not
problematic.

### 3. Ancient Jewish Context (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Historical)

Carroll notes:

> "There actually is — as far as I understand — there are ancient Jewish texts
> that show that potentially the early Christians and early Jews believed in a
> pre-existence, that the spirits were drawn from somewhere to come into
> [bodies]."[^4]

This aligns with scholarship on Second Temple Judaism. Some Jewish traditions
(particularly those influenced by Hellenistic thought, such as Philo of
Alexandria and the Wisdom of Solomon) contain concepts of pre-existent souls.
The Dead Sea Scrolls also contain hints of pre-mortal existence. While
mainstream rabbinic Judaism rejected pre-existence, it was a live option in the
first-century context.

### 4. Complementary Scriptures (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

[[Jeremiah 1.5|Jeremiah 1:5]]: "Before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee;
and before thou camest forth out of the womb I sanctified thee, and I ordained
thee a prophet unto the nations." Carroll notes "several other verses" that
indicate pre-mortal existence.[^4]

These texts, read together, form a cumulative case that pre-existence was a
concept familiar to biblical authors — not a later LDS innovation.

### 5. Creedal Christianity Has No Answer (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Rhetorical)

Carroll describes bringing this argument to other Christians:

> "I bring it up and they just say, 'Well, we don't — we don't know.' Yeah.
> There's no answer for it. Because there's no doctrinal solve and it's not
> explicit, right? It's definitely an implicit implication."[^4]

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: The Disciples Were Asking a Foolish Question
**Steelman:** The disciples, as first-century Jews, may have held folk beliefs
about prenatal sin (perhaps the fetus could sin in the womb, or the soul could
sin in a previous life via metempsychosis). Jesus doesn't endorse their
theology merely by entertaining their question — He redirects it to a higher
purpose ("that the works of God should be made manifest"). Reading pre-existence
into this is eisegesis.

**Response:** Carroll anticipates this: "How can you sin in the womb?"[^2] The
fetal-sin interpretation requires the fetus to have moral agency and the
capacity for sin — which is theologically incoherent in any tradition. And
metempsychosis (reincarnation) was a Greek concept, not a Jewish one. The more
parsimonious reading is that the disciples believed the man existed before
birth and could have sinned in that prior state.

### Objection 2: This Is a Standard Rabbinic Discussion, Not Pre-Existence
**Steelman:** The disciples' question reflects a standard rabbinic debate about
whether suffering is caused by one's own sin or ancestral sin (cf. Exodus 20:5,
"visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children"). The question "who
sinned" could mean: (a) Did the man's parents sin, causing his blindness as
punishment? (b) Was the man's blindness a punishment for sins he would commit
later in life (anticipatory punishment)? (c) Was it a punishment for sins in
the womb? None of these require pre-existence. The "this man" clause simply
completes the either/or question structure.

**Response:** Interpretation (b) — anticipatory punishment for future sins — is
theologically problematic and not attested in Jewish sources. Interpretation
(c) — fetal sin — is what Carroll dismisses as incoherent. The remaining
options are ancestral sin or pre-existence, and the question explicitly
includes the man himself. While the rabbinic-debate framing is legitimate, the
question's structure (did *he* sin or did his parents) is most naturally read
as: was this the result of his own action (in a prior state) or his parents'?

### Objection 3: Jesus's Silence Doesn't Prove Endorsement
**Steelman:** Arguments from silence are weak. Jesus may have had many reasons
not to correct the disciples' theology at that moment — pastoral sensitivity,
timing, or a desire to focus on the miracle. We can't assume silence =
endorsement.

**Response:** Agreed — this is a cumulative argument, not a proof. The weight
lies in the combination: (a) the question presupposes pre-existence, (b) Jesus
engages rather than corrects, (c) other scriptures (Jeremiah 1:5) independently
suggest pre-existence, (d) some Second Temple Jewish texts attest to the
concept, and (e) LDS theology provides a coherent framework that makes sense of
all these data points. Any one piece is suggestive; together they form a
cumulative case.
