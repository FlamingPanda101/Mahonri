# Osiris Is a Type Not a Person — The Blessed Dead Become Osiris

#type/claim

## The Claim
The standard "copycat" charge — *Jesus is just a recycled Osiris* — fails because it misreads what "Osiris" denotes. In Egyptian funerary religion Osiris functions as a **type/title** that the deceased *becomes*, not a single character a savior-myth was cloned from. A dead person buried with Book-of-Breathings text is "Bob Osiris," "Dave Osiris" — "everybody is put into that type," and in doing so "you are taking on the name of God," exactly as the king "takes on the name of Jehovah in the temple drama." [^1] On the lion-couch the figure "shouldn't surprise us": Osiris "seems to be somebody whom you become," and the scene "is never about Osiris — it's always about somebody," the *blessed dead*. [^2] So the hosts inverts the slogan: "It's not that Jesus is *just* Osiris — it's, oh my goodness, Jesus *is* Osiris" — Christ being the one who dies, is resurrected, and thereby opens the type to all, his aspect **Wepwawet** ("opener of the way") leading the dead safely past judgment so they can "become Osiris ourselves." [^3] On this reading Horus (the king/priest) is the role critics mistake for Christ, when in fact "Osiris is Christ" and Horus is "more like Peter." [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **Egyptological grounding is real.** That the deceased is identified *as* "the Osiris N." (Osiris + personal name) in funerary texts, and that Osiris is "the blessed dead" one assimilates to, is mainstream Egyptology — co-host [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] states it in those terms and invokes Plutarch and the Pyramid-Text antiquity of Osiris. *Rating: the descriptive premise (Osiris as a become-able status) is solid.*
2. **It dissolves the copycat objection on its own terms.** If "Osiris" is a *type* rather than a unique biography, then surface resemblances between Jesus and "Osiris" are not evidence of literary borrowing but of a shared death-and-exaltation *pattern* — the very thing a typological theology predicts. The argument neatly distinguishes Horus (king/priest role) from Osiris (the one who dies and rises), defusing the popular "father-god/son-god = God/Jesus" mapping. *Rating: a coherent reframe.*
3. **It coheres with LDS temple theology of the divine name.** "Taking on the name of God" by passing through the type mirrors the endowment's covenant of putting on Christ (see [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]]) so that "when you stand at the veil… he sees Christ." The parallel is internal-to-the-system but consistent. *Rating: theologically congruent.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Three pressures. (a) **Parallelomania**: "becoming Osiris" and "putting on Christ" share *vocabulary* (name, type, ascent) without sharing *content*; the deification logic of Egyptian funerary cult and of LDS exaltation arise in unrelated socio-religious contexts, and a motivated reader can map almost any death-rebirth schema onto the endowment — exactly the caution LDS scholars themselves raise (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). (b) **The reframe cuts both ways**: granting that "Osiris" is a type rather than a person does *not* establish that Christ is its true referent; a secular historian reads the same data as humans projecting a royal exaltation ideology, with the LDS "Jesus *is* Osiris" as the believing overlay, not a finding. (c) **Selectivity**: the hosts foreground the flattering parallels (name-taking, opener-of-the-way) and pass over disanalogies (dismemberment, the missing phallus, Isis's role, the polytheistic pantheon). A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is narrowly *defensive* — it only needs to show the copycat charge misfires — and the convergence of name-theology, substitutionary death, and a guide "past the monster" is striking enough to count as a "form of the truth," not proof of derivation.
