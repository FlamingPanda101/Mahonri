# Humans Are the Same Species as God (Acts 17 Offspring)

## The Claim
Scripture teaches humans are the **same species/kind** as God. In [[Acts 17.28-29|Acts 17:28-29]] Paul calls us God's "offspring" (Greek *genos*, "kind/kindred"), and [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:4]] makes believers "partakers of the divine nature." Humans hold the divine nature *in potentiality* (an acorn); God holds it *in actuality* (the oak tree). This shared nature is why we can become "one" with God.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. *Genos* = kind/species, applied to humans and God
Acts 17:28-29 quotes the poet Aratus: "we are also his offspring (*genos*)." Paul builds an argument *from* the shared kind: because we are God's offspring, we shouldn't think the "Godhead" (*to theion*) is like gold or stone. The same word denotes our kinship of *kind* with God. *(Moderate-strong: *genos* does carry kind/kindred sense; the inference to "same species in the LDS sense" is a theological extension Paul's pagan-poet citation doesn't independently require.)* [^1]

### 2. The acorn/oak: same nature, different degree of actualization
We share God's nature but hold it in potential, as an acorn holds oak-hood; God has it fully actualized. This grounds theosis without erasing the Creator/creature distinction *of degree*. See [[Acorn and Oak Analogy for Theosis]]. *(Moderate; an analogy, persuasive within LDS metaphysics.)* [^2]

### 3. Shared nature explains "becoming one with God"
Because we are the same kind, we can be "one" with God exactly as the persons of the Godhead are one — by unity, not by being absorbed into one being ([[2 Peter 1.4|2 Pet 1:4]]; cf. [[John 17.20-23|John 17]]). See [[Oneness Means Unity Not Numerical Identity of Being]]. *(Strong as an internal-coherence point.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Mainstream Christian exegesis reads "offspring" in Acts 17 as **creaturely dependence**, not consubstantial kinship: Paul is doing missionary contextualization, quoting a Stoic poet to argue that idols (gold/stone) cannot be the Creator on whom all creatures depend — the point is God's *otherness* from his artifacts, not humans' sameness with God. And [[2 Peter 1.4|2 Peter 1:4]]'s "partakers of the divine nature" is classically read as *participation by grace* (energies/relationship, per Eastern Orthodoxy) — sharing in God's life, not in his uncreated essence. On this view the LDS reading erases the Creator/creature line that the very passages presuppose, making God a senior member of our species rather than the ground of being. The LDS rejoinder leans on *genos*'s plain semantic range and on the broader Restoration ontology (see [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]]).

> "Because we share the same nature with God, we can be one with God the same way they are one with each other." [^2]
