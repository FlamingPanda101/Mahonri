# The Abolition of Man - From Modest Reception to Prophetic Classic

## The Point
Lewis judged *The Abolition of Man* a near-total failure — it "has been almost totally ignored by the public," he wrote in 1955, and called it "a v. poor seller."[^1] Ward argues that this self-assessment was simply wrong, and is "even further from the mark" about the book's later standing.[^2] By sales and early reviews the book did well from the start, and it has since become "a genuine and seminal classic," widely regarded as among Lewis's most penetrating and prophetic works — answering the question of whether the argument is merely dated wartime polemic or a perennial diagnosis.[^3]

## Evidence
**Reception was strong, not poor.** The first 1943 print run "sold out almost instantly," with three reprints in ten months and a revised second edition in 1946; by the normal measure for academic philosophy "Abolition was a solid seller right from the start."[^4] The lectures were unusually well attended (audiences of 520, 432, and 391, far above the surrounding years).[^5] Early reviewers praised "the fineness of Mr. Lewis's thought," and the *New York Herald Tribune* called it "an all-too-plausible picture of man's destiny after the concept of absolute values has gone out the window."[^6]

**Its later standing is that of a prophetic classic.** Ward catalogues a "large and diverse readership — philosophers, educators, literary critics... jurists, atheists, agnostics, people of faith."[^7] John Lucas calls it "a powerful argument, reminiscent of Plato, Pascal and Dostoievski"; Alan Jacobs ranks it "the most profound of Lewis's cultural critiques"; the future Pope Benedict XVI acknowledged "the keen accuracy" of its moral diagnosis; and atheist philosopher John Gray calls it "prescient," "prophetic," and "as relevant now... if not more so."[^8] Both *National Review* and *The Intercollegiate Review* placed it among the ten best nonfiction books of the twentieth century.[^9] It has detractors too — Ayn Rand scrawled "The abysmal bastard!" in her margins, and Gregory Bassham called it "one of the most jaw-dropping straw-man attacks in literary history."[^10]

**Why it endures.** Ward links the book's gravity to Lewis's own losses in the Great War: Lewis turned twenty the month the 1918 Armistice was signed and had "cheated death by a hair's breadth," and he treats "death for a good cause" as "the crucial test of objective value."[^11] The book is thus "a mental product of the 'war to end all wars'" yet aimed at "the unchanging moral and spiritual conflict that faces people of every culture in every place in every generation."[^12]

## Best Quotes
> "The Abolition of Man has gone on to establish a reputation as a genuine and seminal classic." [^3]

> Atheist philosopher John Gray calls it "prescient," "prophetic" and "as relevant now" as when it first came out, "if not more so." [^8]

## LDS Connection
The book's growing reputation rests on a thesis Latter-day Saints can affirm wholeheartedly: that there is an **objective, eternal moral law** whose abandonment endangers humanity itself. That so varied a "chorus" — Catholic, atheist, Protestant, jurist — keeps returning to Lewis's case illustrates the LDS conviction that the moral light of conscience (the **Light of Christ** that "giveth light to every man," Moroni 7:16-19; D&C 88; see [[D&C 88]]) is genuinely universal and recognizable across traditions. The book's perennial relevance is precisely what one would expect if the moral law is eternal rather than culturally invented. It remains a sober ally against naturalist attempts to relocate the foundation of value in human feeling or measurable well-being, such as [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]].
