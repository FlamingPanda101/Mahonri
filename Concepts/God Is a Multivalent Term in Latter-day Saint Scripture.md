# God Is a Multivalent Term in Latter-day Saint Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim
Matt Fradd's "contradicting revelations" charge is that the Book of Mormon teaches **one** God ("Is there more than one god?" — "No," Alma 11) while later revelation has **gods** (plural) organizing the heavens and the earth and promises the righteous will be "gods... with all power" — "a huge change in foundational beliefs." Luke Hansen replies that this is only an internal critique if it uses *LDS* definitions, and that in Latter-day Saint theology the word **God — even capital-G God — is multivalent**: it can denote (a) God the Father, the Most High; (b) the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit together (the Godhead); or (c) the person of Jesus Christ alone. The supposed contradiction dissolves once the term's range is recognized — and the proof is that the shift happens **inside a single conversation**:
- In [[Alma 11.38-39|Alma 11]], Amulek answers "No" to "more than one God" while distinguishing the Father from the Son; a few verses later he calls "Christ the Son, and God the Father, and the Holy Spirit... one Eternal God." Same speaker, same dialogue: first the *person of the Father*, then the *shared divine nature of the Godhead*. [^1]
- The "gods" of [[Abraham 4.1|Abraham 4]] are identified one chapter earlier (Abraham 3) as **subordinate** beings — Jesus and the noble premortal spirits who "are not only lesser gods than the Father, but also serve under his command." This is the ancient Israelite **divine council**, echoed in "Let us make man in our image." [^2] See [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]] and [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]].

So Abraham 4 does not contradict Alma 11; "at most it gives us another way this multivalent term can be used." For the related point that *development* over time is not contradiction, see [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]]; for the reading test, [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Fradd)
1. **An internal critique can be legitimate.** Fradd's stated aim is an *internal* contradiction; if LDS scripture really did affirm a strict numerical "one God" in the same sense it later affirms many, that would be a genuine problem. The burden Hansen accepts — show the senses differ — is the right one. *Rating: the objection is well-formed; it stands or falls on the exegesis.*
2. **The two senses are textually anchored, not ad hoc.** The shift from "the Father is one God" to "the Godhead is one God" occurs *within Alma 11 itself*, and the subordination of the Abraham 4 "gods" is stated *in Abraham 3*. The multivalence is read off the immediate context, not imposed to escape the charge. *Rating: strong; same-passage evidence is hard to dismiss as special pleading.*
3. **It coheres with the wider biblical pattern.** The divine-council backdrop ("Let us make man") gives the plural-"gods" reading independent footing in the Hebrew Bible. *Rating: converges with mainstream ANE scholarship.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic replies that "multivalence" can rescue almost any apparent contradiction — if *God* can mean three different things, no statement about God can ever be falsified — and that the *load-bearing* worry is not semantics but **ontology**: Alma 11's "one God" reads naturally as numerical monotheism, while the King Follett / plurality-of-gods trajectory affirms *many ontologically distinct* gods and human deification, which classical monotheism denies. Calling the later gods "subordinate" still concedes additional beings properly called *gods*, which is exactly what Isaiah 44:6 (on the mainstream reading) rules out. The Latter-day Saint reply is that "one God" in Alma is relational/covenantal and that the subordinate-council reading is the native Israelite one (see [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]]); the dispute is therefore about which monotheism the Bible actually teaches, not about an internal Book of Mormon contradiction.

## Best Quotes
> "In Latter-day Saint theology, the word God, even capital G God, is a multivalent term. It means different things in different contexts." [^1]

> "When Alma says there is only one god, he's referring to the person of the Father... but later in the same conversation, he refers to the whole Godhead as one eternal God." [^1]

> "The gods in Abraham 4 are Jesus and the other noble spirits that we are explicitly told are not only lesser gods than the Father, but also serve under his command. This does not contradict Alma." [^2]
