# The Genealogy Contradiction — Matthew Names Jacob, Luke Names Heli as Joseph's Father

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] uses the conflicting genealogies of Jesus as a **concrete battering ram** against biblical inerrancy in conversation with **Austin** ([[Austin (Apologia Church)]]), a presuppositional apologist who has just asserted the Bible is infallible. Matthew's genealogy says "**Jacob** begat Joseph the husband of Mary" ([[Matthew 1.16|Matt 1:16]]); Luke's says Joseph "was the son of **Heli**" ([[Luke 3.23|Luke 3:23]]). Joseph cannot have two different biological fathers, so on the plainest reading the two texts contradict. Carroll's strategic point is methodological: against a presuppositionalist who *asserts* infallibility rather than *argues* for it, a single demonstrable error breaks the whole edifice — "if there's a contradiction in the Bible, your entire worldview falls apart."[^1][^2] He challenges Austin to read Luke 3 aloud on the spot and then to solve it *from the text alone*, predicting (correctly, in the clip) that Austin can only promise to "research it later."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The harmonizations require extra-textual assumptions — rate: strong
Carroll concedes the standard solves but presses that they are not *in* the text: "you cannot solve this with the text alone — you have to make some pretty big assumptions."[^4] The dominant harmonization is that **Luke traces Mary's line** (Heli is Joseph's father-in-law, Mary's father), so the two lists are different lineages. But neither Gospel says this; Luke explicitly says Joseph "was *the son of* Heli," not "son-in-law." A second harmonization invokes **levirate marriage** (one father biological, one legal). Both are reconstructions imported to *save* inerrancy, not readings the text yields — the same move flagged in [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]. This is the load-bearing argumentative point and is correctly stated.

### 2. The contradiction lands hardest against presuppositionalism specifically — rate: strong
The force is not that the contradiction is unanswerable in the abstract (apologists have answered it for centuries) but that it is **fatal to a method that grounds everything on the canon's unargued infallibility**. If knowledge itself presupposes an inerrant Bible (the Van Til–[[Greg Bahnsen|Bahnsen]] move), then one uncorrected error collapses the foundation rather than merely costing a footnote. Against an evidential apologist the genealogy is a puzzle; against a presuppositionalist who "won't justify the standard," it is a [[Presuppositionalism Cannot Refute a Coherent Rival Worldview Like Mormonism|disarming]] counter-strike. See [[The Word of God Fallacy - The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven]].

### 3. The performative point — an apologist who cannot answer in real time — rate: moderate
Carroll's secondary aim is rhetorical: to show that a man asserting the Bible's infallibility cannot, when asked, locate or resolve a textbook discrepancy in his own scriptures, and is reduced to "I'll have to do some research."[^3] **Caution:** this proves unpreparedness, not that the text is unharmonizable; an unanswered question in a street conversation is not a demonstrated error. Carroll partly grants this by walking through the harmonizations himself, which concedes that *answers exist* — his real complaint is that they are unprovable from the text and that the presuppositionalist had no business asserting infallibility he cannot defend.

**Steelman of the inerrantist.** The Matthew/Luke divergence is one of the most-studied harmonization cases in Christian history, and the Mary's-line reading is ancient (defended in different forms since the early Fathers), not an ad hoc modern patch. Grammatically, Greek genealogies routinely telescope and can encode legal vs. biological descent; "son of" (*huios*) is elastic. An inerrantist will say the burden runs the other way: the existence of a coherent, long-attested reconstruction means the texts are *not* demonstrably contradictory, only underdetermined — and underdetermination is not error. On that view Carroll has shown the harmonization is extra-textual (true) but not that it is *false* (which he never establishes). The honest scorecard: Carroll wins the methodological point against bare presuppositional assertion, but "the Bible contradicts itself" is stronger than the evidence licenses — the most one can say is that inerrancy here is *rescued by assumption rather than read off the page*.

## Best Quotes
> "You're saying the Bible's infallible and I'm showing you a contradiction... who's Joseph's father? In Matthew his father's name is Jacob. Now go to Luke 3 — and if it doesn't say Jacob, you've got a huge problem here."[^2]

> "You cannot solve this with the text alone. You have to make some pretty big assumptions. And the biggest assumption people make is that one of them is actually Mary's father — so it's Joseph's father-in-law."[^4]

> "If there's a contradiction in the Bible, your entire worldview falls apart. By the way, that's what happened to Daniel."[^1]
