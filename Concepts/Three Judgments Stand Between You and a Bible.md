# Three Judgments Stand Between You and a Bible

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that before anyone can even point to "what you think is the Bible," they must make **three judgments**, none of which has an objectively correct answer:[^1]

1. **Canon** — "How many books are in your Bible? Which are those books?"[^2]
2. **Manuscripts** — which manuscript traditions you "use to cobble together your canon" (Masoretic, Septuagint, Dead Sea Scrolls, Textus Receptus, critical text, etc.).[^3]
3. **Translation** — which of the many translations you will prioritize.[^4]

"All you can say is this is what we prefer," leaving you "two steps removed from being able to say *this is the Bible*."[^5] See the dedicated notes [[The Canon Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]], [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]], and [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Each judgment is genuinely undecidable on neutral grounds — rate: strong
For each axis McClellan stresses "there's no way to say this is objectively the right answer."[^5] The canon has no agreed master list; the manuscripts have no agreed base text; the translations have no agreed best rendering. The three compound: even a "correct" canon read in the "wrong" manuscript or translation yields a different Bible. **Steelman:** a confessional reader can argue the three judgments are not equally weighted — providence (or the church's guidance) constrains the range so the variations are theologically trivial. McClellan's reply is that the variation is *not* trivial (e.g., whole Old Testaments differ for Greek Orthodox readers using the Septuagint; see [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]]).

### 2. The graded awareness point — rate: moderate
McClellan observes that most US evangelicals are most aware of and have the most say in the *translation* question, less aware of the *canon* question (usually "done and dusted" by their tradition), and least aware of the *manuscript* question — "even though all three of them are critical to what ends up giving them their Bible."[^6] This asymmetry of awareness is itself evidence that the "Bible" people hold is the product of unexamined inherited choices. See [[Putting Dogma Over Data]].

### 3. The choices can be participated in or merely inherited — rate: strong
"Most people, most traditions, they're not involved in these decisions. A lot of people don't even know that they're happening."[^7] You cannot stand at a pulpit and freely switch canons "and be long for that pulpit."[^7] The judgments are usually enforced by institutions, not chosen by the reader — which underscores that "your" Bible is a social artifact.

## Best Quotes
> "There are three different judgments that you have to make before you can even get to what you think is the Bible."[^1]

> "You are now two steps removed from being able to say this is the Bible... All you can say is this is what we prefer."[^5]

> "All three of them are critical to what ends up giving them their Bible."[^6]
