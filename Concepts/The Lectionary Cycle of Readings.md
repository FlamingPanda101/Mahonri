# The Lectionary Cycle of Readings

## The Point
The lectionary was a fixed collection of scriptural readings assigned to Sundays and holy days within the liturgy. It likely began as continuous reading of scripture in worship but developed into a structured annual cycle keyed to the festival calendar, ensuring readings were "appropriate to the day."

## Evidence
1. **Definition.** "The lectionary was a collection of scriptural readings that formed part of the liturgy, or worship service, for Sundays and holy days. Readings were taken from the Old and New Testaments, especially the Psalms and the Gospels." [^1]
2. **Development from continuous to cyclical reading.** "The lectionary may have begun as a continuous reading of the scriptures in worship services, but it soon developed into a cycle of readings appropriate for Sunday services and holy days." [^1]
3. **A concrete example.** "In the early Syriac lectionary lists, the story of the binding of Isaac (see Genesis 22) and the story of Joseph were read as Old Testament readings for Easter" — both typologically tied to Christ's death and resurrection. [^1]
4. **Egeria's witness.** Egeria noticed that the readings and preaching for festivals such as Holy Week or Epiphany were "appropriate to the day," a remark Heal identifies as a reference to the lectionary cycle. [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "The lectionary may have begun as a continuous reading of the scriptures in worship services, but it soon developed into a cycle of readings appropriate for Sunday services and holy days." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The lectionary illustrates the deep antiquity of organizing communal scripture study around an annual calendar — a structural analogue to the modern Latter-day Saint "Come, Follow Me" curriculum, and another point of recognizable continuity rather than corruption.
