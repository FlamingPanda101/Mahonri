# The Weirdest Animal That Ever Existed

## The Point
When the atheist says "you're just an animal," the most powerful reply is not to deny it but to grant it and follow it to its breaking point. [[Jacob Hansen]] concedes the label and answers: "I am the weirdest freaking animal that has ever existed." The slogan "we're just animals" is meant to *deflate* human significance by lumping us in with everything else made of star-dust. But the empirical record runs the other way: of all the creatures that have ever lived, exactly one builds rockets, paints Sistine Chapels, and reflects on its own origins. A category ("animal") that contains both the saber-toothed cat and the species that flew to the moon is doing no explanatory work — the interesting fact is the discontinuity the label is being used to hide.

## Evidence
Hansen's argument is comparative and historical. Across "the eons of our planet" there have been creatures broadly like today's animals — saber-toothed cats comparable to house cats, ancient creatures "somewhat comparable" to giraffes.[^1] But there has never been "a creature that flew to the moon," never one that "created the Sistine Chapel," nothing "even remotely like human beings... even even close."[^2] The vast majority of animals across natural history simply "did animal stuff": they ate, lived, reproduced, and died.[^3] Granting the materialist's own framing, then, does not yield "just another animal"; it yields a single, staggering outlier. That is why Hansen's response to "you're just an animal" is to embrace the premise and let the conclusion indict it: "I am the weirdest freaking animal that has ever existed."[^4]

The move is structurally a *reductio with a concession*: accept "human = animal," and the reductive claim collapses under the weight of the data it was supposed to explain away. The same shape appears when materialism is turned on reasoning itself (see [[Self-Defeating Statements Refute Materialism]]) and when a flat naturalism is shown to undercut the very rationality science depends on (see [[Atheism Undercuts the Rationality Science Requires]]). The discontinuities Hansen points at — abstract thought, art, science, the capacity to "think about these type of things"[^1] — are precisely what a mind-is-only-matter account struggles to deliver (see [[The Mind Is Not the Brain]]).

## Best Quotes
> "an atheist would say we're equivalent, we're all Stardust... but animals don't think about these type of things... never in a million years could an animal do this." [^1]

> "has there ever been a creature that flew to the moon, has there ever been a creature that created the Sistine Chapel, has there ever been anything even remotely like human beings — like even even close?" [^2]

> "the atheists would look at you and say you're just an animal, and I'd look at them and say I am the weirdest freaking animal that has ever existed." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration gives a name to the discontinuity Hansen merely points at: human beings are not the top end of an animal continuum but the literal offspring of God, made in His image. Genesis sets man and woman apart as created "in our image, after our likeness" ([[Genesis 1.26-27]]), and the Psalmist marvels that God is "mindful" of so exalted a creature ([[Psalm 8.4]]). For Latter-day Saints this is not metaphor but lineage and destiny: God's whole work is "to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man" ([[Moses 1.39]]), and man shares the divine nature in kind (see [[God and Man Are the Same Species]]). On this view the "weirdness" of the human animal is exactly what we should expect — we are weird because we are not *merely* animals; we carry the image and potential of God, which is the opposite of being "just animals."
