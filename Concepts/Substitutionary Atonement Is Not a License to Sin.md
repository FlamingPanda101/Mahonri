# Substitutionary Atonement Is Not a License to Sin

#type/claim

## The Claim
The stream's most substantive non-dilemma exchange is with a sincere, self-described "middle-way" Muslim caller from India ("Abishek"), who says his real obstacle to Christianity is not the Islamic dilemma but the **fairness of the atonement**: how can a loving, forgiving, all-powerful God require an innocent man (Jesus) to die for sins others committed?[^1] [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]] answers that the doctrine, rightly stated, neither makes Jesus a victim nor licenses sin.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Jesus is not a helpless victim — he lays his life down voluntarily.** From [[John 10.17-18|John 10:17–18]]: "No one takes [my life] from me, but I lay it down of my own accord. I have authority to lay it down, and authority to take it up again." Avery uses this to defuse the "unfairness" intuition: the divine Son "willfully comes and takes on flesh... to give his life for humanity because he loves us. He's not some helpless child."[^2]
2. **Grace precedes our worthiness.** From [[Romans 5.8|Romans 5]]: "while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." God's wrath stands over sin; Christ "took on that wrath for us and saved us from it" for those who believe.[^3]
3. **"Isn't it a free ticket to sin?"** The caller raises the classic objection. Avery answers from [[Romans 6.3-4|Romans 6]] — the very next chapter — which asks "Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means." Baptism unites the believer to Christ's death: "we've died to sin... raised up to new life." So a Christian who sins *struggles* with sin and repents, versus an unbeliever who *walks in* and *practices* sin; the believer is covered by grace, the unbeliever "has to take on their sin their own self."[^4]
4. **A free-will theodicy for the origin of sin.** Pressed on why God created a world he foreknew would fall, Avery gives a standard free-will / soul-making answer: God did not create sin; love requires the genuine freedom to choose or reject God, so "moral freedom" — "one of the greatest values we can imagine" — necessitates the possibility of sin. Sin "came through Adam" ([[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12]]), not from God.[^5] *(This is the free-will defense familiar from Plantinga; a Muslim or atheist interlocutor can press the further "why permit foreseen evil?" and the problem of divine foreknowledge vs. libertarian freedom, which Avery only gestures at.)*
5. **Steelman of the Muslim objection.** The caller's worry is a serious one shared across traditions: penal substitution can look like *transferred guilt* (punishing the innocent to acquit the guilty), and Islam's alternative — God forgives directly, by mercy, without a cross — is *prima facie* simpler and is itself rooted in the Qur'an's denial of the crucifixion (see [[Islam Denies the Deity and Divine Sonship of Christ]]). Avery's reply does not so much refute the moral objection as *reframe* it: substitution is self-giving love, not imposed punishment, because the substitute is God himself. Whether that reframing answers the fairness worry is exactly the live disagreement.
