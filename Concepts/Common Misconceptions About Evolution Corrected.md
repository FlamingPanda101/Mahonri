# Common Misconceptions About Evolution Corrected

## The Point
[[Tyler A. Kummer]] and [[Jamie L. Jensen]], as biology educators, catalog the recurring misunderstandings that "tend to impede student acceptance" of evolution and "lead to a faulty sense of conflict between science and the restored gospel."[^1] Their claim is that the *perceived* conflict often rests on a caricature; correcting the science removes the roadblock. Four misconceptions in particular do most of the damage.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **"Humans came from monkeys" (ladder thinking).** The iconic chimp-to-man parade is "not what evolutionary biologists believe." Chimps are *not* the ancestor of humans; rather, "some extinct species existed that is an ancestor of both humans and chimps," whose population split onto two distinct paths. "Chimps are not the ancestor of humans; they are a relative that descended from a common ancestor."[^3] Ladder thinking wrongly implies evolution had a goal (producing humans) with other species "jumping off the path" — but evolutionary forces act on *all* populations.[^4]
2. **"Microevolution is real but macroevolution isn't."** People accept small changes (drug-resistant bacteria, dog breeds) but balk at large-scale speciation. Yet "macroevolution is simply the large-scale result of many microevolutionary events. It is just a difference in scale" — the *same* mechanisms (selection, drift, gene flow, mutation) operating over far longer time. Dinosaurs-to-birds is just many squirrel-on-two-sides-of-the-Grand-Canyon events stacked across deep time.[^5]
3. **"Why don't we see new species forming now?"** Speciation "usually take[s] very long periods of time"; the honest answer is "you just have not lived long enough (nor will you) to see one" — though rare rapid cases exist.[^6]
4. **Individuals evolve (the Lamarck error).** Cutting off Dobermans' tails does *not* yield tailless puppies; staring at screens does not make the next generation near-sighted. Evolution changes the *genetic frequency of a population over time*; only heritable changes in sex cells pass on. "Populations evolve, not individuals." (The authors note the "Pokémon" confusion: that game's "evolution" is really metamorphosis.)[^7]
5. **"There hasn't been enough time for randomness to produce a human."** This conflates two things. Mutation is random, "but the number of mutations that are viable is *not* random." Natural and sexual selection are *non-random* filters that constrain which random changes persist, dramatically raising the odds of reaching adapted forms. "Mutations are random, but the number of mutations that are viable is not random."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Chimps are not the ancestor of humans; they are a relative that descended from a common ancestor." [^3]

> "Macroevolution is simply the large-scale result of many microevolutionary events. It is just a difference in scale." [^5]

> "Mutations are random, but the number of mutations that are viable is not random." [^8]

## LDS Connection
Most faith-and-evolution anxiety attaches to caricatures, not to the science. The "humans came from monkeys" image is the very thing many fear contradicts being created "in the image of God" — yet evolutionary biology never claims it; common ancestry with apes is a different and more modest claim, fully compatible with spiritual sonship. The "just randomness / too improbable" worry (a staple of anti-evolution apologetics) misfires because selection is a *non-random*, ordering process — which a Latter-day Saint can readily see as the lawful means through which a Creator "at the helm of the creative process" works. Removing these errors is itself pastoral: the data show that better understanding of evolution *lowers* perceived conflict (see [[Accepting Evolution with Joy Is Possible]]). The authors close with D&C 88:118 — "seek learning, even by study and also by faith." See the positive definition in [[What Evolution Is and Is Not - Descent with Modification]] and the supporting data in [[The Evidence for Common Descent]].
