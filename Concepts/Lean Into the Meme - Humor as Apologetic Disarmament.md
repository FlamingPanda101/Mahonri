# Lean Into the Meme - Humor as Apologetic Disarmament

#type/claim

## The Claim
For the low-stakes schoolyard challenge — the question fired off "between periods," where the asker is not sincere but probing — the [[Ward Radio]] hosts argue the *optimal* response is neither fight nor flight but **humor that owns the caricature**. When a peer needles "I heard you get your own planet," the recommended reply is not an excursus on apotheosis but "Yeah, and my planet's gonna have rings — it's gonna be lit. Let's go to class."[^1] [[Jonah Barnes]] grounds the move in a decision tree: a hostile drive-by question is rarely a request for information; it is the question *"are you someone I can ask? My pastor said you'd run away scared — is that true?"*[^2] A joke answers *that* question — it signals **"I'm not offended, I'm comfortable, you can come to me"** — without taking the bait of a debate there's no time to win. The hosts read the tactic as deliberately *disarming*: the critic's pastor has primed them to expect a flustered retreat, and lighthearted ownership ("best cult ever, bro") defeats the expectation by refusing the premise that the question is dangerous.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The joke decodes the *real* question being asked — rate: strong, perceptive
The load-bearing insight is that a between-periods jab is a *social* probe, not an intellectual one: "they're not asking whether you get planets, they're asking, *are you someone I can ask?*"[^2] On that reading, a sincere 60-second answer over-responds and a defensive answer mis-responds; humor is calibrated to the actual move. Jonah ties this to a Stoic register — never apologize for your motives when attacked (he invokes Epictetus) — recasting the joke as *internal* equanimity expressed outward rather than evasion.[^4] *Rating: a genuine contribution* — it reframes "do you get a planet?" from a doctrine question into a confidence test, which is exactly the diagnostic the vault already makes for sincere youth questions in [[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]]; this note extends it to the *insincere* case.

### 2. Self-deprecation as a long-game signal — rate: strong, with a real-world illustration
The deeper claim is that comic ownership *plays the long game*: it advertises an unashamed, approachable believer, so that "some fiery atheist Calvinist might come back years later and say, you've always been nice to me — what is it you believe?"[^5] Cardon's corporate anecdote concretizes it: presenting to assembly-line workers, he made a mock "number of wives" chart (0,0,0,…,1,1,1) and a projected "mac-and-cheese consumption" graph for his six kids — the data slide's actual payload was *"I'm a Latter-day Saint, I'm okay with it, and you can ask me about it."*[^6] *Rating: a credible account of how levity functions socially* — it lowers the threat level of the topic so that the *bleachers* (third-party listeners) feel safe to ask later, which the hosts elsewhere call the most valuable audience.

### 3. Humor is the *triage* answer, not the universal one — rate: balanced
The hosts bound the tactic carefully: the decision tree routes a jab to humor, but a *sincere* questioner gets a 60-second answer, a five-minute answer, or "let's go talk to the missionaries" — humor is the response to insincerity and time-pressure, not a substitute for substance.[^7] **Steelman of the critic:** comic deflection can shade into the very dismissiveness the method claims to avoid — a constant "best cult ever, bro" could read as *never* engaging, leaving a quietly sincere asker (or a watching bleacher) with the impression that the believer *only* has jokes. The hosts concede the boundary themselves — every comic answer is told to end with "and if you want to know more, let's talk."[^7] The note preserves the tension: the joke earns the right to the conversation; it is not the conversation. This is the same humility-not-defeat posture the vault gives missionaries in [[Missionaries Are Tour Guides Not Question Answerers]], and the comic register is the inverse face of the vault's [[The Mockery of the Most Religious Is a Costly Signal of Faith|costly-signal]] point — there the believer *absorbs* mockery; here he *pre-empts* it by mocking the caricature himself.

## Best Quotes
> "They're asking, 'Will you engage with me? My pastor said you guys will run away scared. Is that true?' And what's your response? 'Bro, yeah — and my planet's gonna have rings. It's gonna be lit.'"[^2]

> "If you can communicate to them, 'I'm an open book' … that, to all you teachers and teenagers out there, is gold."[^6]

> "You're signaling: I am open and comfortable with my faith. Ask me whatever. I'm not ashamed. It's not weird. Yes, let's talk about it. I'm proud of it."[^1]

## LDS Connection
The frame is explicitly youth ministry: the segment answers questions written down by a real teachers quorum being "peppered" at school, and the counsel is aimed at 15-year-olds, parents, and leaders rather than debaters. Humor-as-disarmament is the insincere-question complement to the vault's confidence thesis: where [[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]] says a *sincere* youth is gauging whether you'll cower, this note says an *insincere* peer is gauging the same thing, and a joke that owns the caricature is the fastest proof you won't. Both protect the *bleachers* — the silent listeners who decide, from your reaction, whether the Church is a thing you can ask about.
