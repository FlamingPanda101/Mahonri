# Early Christianity Was a Preaching Religion

## The Point
Kristian S. Heal argues that preaching "punctuated the religious experience of early Christians" at every stage of life, and that the surviving corpus of sermons — several thousand of them — is "the surest guide to what the earliest Christians were taught and believed." Faith itself, on this view, "comes from hearing it proclaimed" (Romans 10:17).

## Evidence
1. **Preaching at every life-stage.** Early Christians heard sermons at conversion (missionary sermons), in preparation for baptism (catechetical sermons), weekly in worship (Sunday/weekday sermons), and at death (funerary sermons). These sermons "retold, explained, and expounded the stories and teachings of Jesus." [^1]
2. **The scale of the surviving corpus.** "Several thousand sermons survive from early Christianity, written in Armenian, Coptic, Greek, Latin, and Syriac." Within this collection lies "the surest guide to what the earliest Christians were taught and believed." [^1]
3. **Scriptural roots of preaching.** John preached in the wilderness (Mark 1:4); Jesus preached "the gospel of the kingdom of God" (Mark 1:14) and in synagogues (Luke 4:16–22); after the ascension the apostles continued to "teach and preach Jesus Christ" (Acts 5:42); even scattered ordinary believers "went about preaching the word" (Acts 8:4). The Epistle to the Hebrews is thought by many to have begun as a sermon (Hebrews 13:22). [^2]
4. **Scholarly summary.** Heal quotes Hughes Oliphant Old: "Christianity from its earliest beginnings was a preaching religion. At the center of its worship was the reading and preaching of scripture." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Preaching punctuated the religious experience of early Christians." [^1]

> "Christianity from its earliest beginnings was a preaching religion. At the center of its worship was the reading and preaching of scripture." (Hughes Oliphant Old, quoted) [^2]

## LDS Connection
Heal's box "Why It Matters" borrows the logic of Romans 10:17 to make preaching "foundational not only to the spreading of the Christian message but to the ongoing project of teaching, inspiring, guiding, chastening, and caring for the souls of those who turn to Christ" — a description that maps closely onto Latter-day Saint ministering and Sunday worship.
