# Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
That [[Brigham Young]] was "pro-slavery" and a hater of Black people — the natural inference from his racist statements and the 1852 legislation.

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **He freed Green Flake of debt (rated: strong, documentary).** Brigham bought Green Flake from his owner and set him up with a farm in south Utah Valley. When Green said he was struggling to pay off his wife's former owner for her freedom, Brigham told him: "Stop paying them. You don't owe them anything. ... You owe them no debt." Peterson: "That's striking to me for a man who's supposedly dedicated to slavery."[^1] (Sourced to [[Amy Tanner Thiriot]]'s *Slavery in Zion* and/or *This Abominable Slavery*.)

2. **The coat-helping scene (rated: strong, eyewitness-derived).** Abolitionist visitor Elizabeth Kane — herself holding "traditional racist attitudes" — was scandalized to round a corner and find Brigham ("the Pope of the Mormons") personally helping Isaac James, a Black man with crippling joint pain, put his coat on. Her complaint was that James didn't show "the proper gratitude that someone of his station should show" — inadvertently documenting Brigham's casual disregard for the racial hierarchy she expected.[^2]

3. **The wagon-bed accommodation (rated: moderate, eyewitness-derived).** On the same trip south, Brigham's party emptied a wagon and fitted it with a cushioned bed so Isaac James (suffering from arthritis/rheumatism) could ride more comfortably — again upsetting Kane that "white men would do that for a Black man."[^3]

4. **Defending a mistreated Black man's dignity (rated: moderate; Peterson hedges the citation).** Peterson recalls a quotation in which a Black man — apparently mentally unbalanced — was treated badly, and Brigham objected that the man "is a descendant of Adam like everybody else, and he should be treated as such."[^4]

5. **Billy Johnson vision (rated: faith claim, not historical proof).** A Ghanaian pioneer reported Brigham appearing to him in vision after Joseph's death-era racism — used by Peterson to argue Brigham "grew beyond" his earthly limitations. See [[Billy Johnson]] and [[Brigham Young as Spiritual Man Not Just Colonizer]]. *Steelman:* a posthumous vision report cannot establish Brigham's mortal attitudes; it functions theologically, not historically.

**Steelman of the critic overall:** Kind treatment of *individuals* coexists with structural racism — Brigham still articulated the priesthood-and-race teaching and held that whites should lead. Peterson grants exactly this: "Was he a racist by my standards in the early 21st century? Yes — but so was Abraham Lincoln. ... But did he treat individual Black people poorly? No, apparently not."[^5] The claim is narrow: *personal opposition to slavery and personal decency*, not absolution from the racial views of his era.

## Best Quotes

> "And Brigham says to him, Stop paying them. You don't owe them anything. Just stop. ... You owe them no debt. And that's striking to me for a man who's supposedly dedicated to slavery."[^1]

> "There I saw Brigham Young... the Pope of the Mormons, helping Isaac James put his coat on. ... It bothered her... that this is just not appropriate for a white man... Well, Brigham Young obviously didn't think there was anything improper about it at all."[^2]

> "Was he a racist by my standards in the early 21st century? Yes, but so was Abraham Lincoln. ... But did he treat individual Black people poorly? No, apparently not."[^5]
