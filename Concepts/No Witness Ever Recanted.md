# No Witness Ever Recanted

## The Claim

To the extent the historical record reaches, every witness reaffirmed his testimony up to his deathbed; none ever recanted, even after leaving the church, falling out with [[Joseph Smith]], facing mobs, or being deliberately plied with alcohol.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Lifelong, decades-long persistence
[[David Whitmer]] bore his testimony for sixty years (1828–1888), continually mocked, losing opportunities, even held at gunpoint by mobs — yet never backed away.[^2] Peterson argues this *strengthens* him as a witness.
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (length and consistency of the public record).

### 2. Public re-affirmation under challenge
When John Murphy of Polo County claimed Whitmer had denied his testimony, Whitmer published a statement — "I have never at any time denied my testimony" — co-signed by the town's leading banker, prominent attorney, former mayor, and sitting sheriff attesting he was of sound mind and good character; it ran in the Richmond newspaper, which still survives.[^3] See [[John Murphy]].
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a documented, witnessed, contemporaneous public statement).

### 3. Reaffirmation even when recanting would have helped
[[Oliver Cowdery]], out of the church and seeking office, reaffirmed rather than denied when his connection surfaced, repeatedly torpedoing his own career.[^4] [[Martin Harris]] returned to the church in his final years and bore testimony to anyone who would listen, with a signature formula — "as clearly as you see that [object], I saw the angel."[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (behavior contrary to self-interest).

### 4. Holds even under intoxication and mob pressure
Critics tried to get Martin Harris "a little tipsy" and interrogate him; he still did not back down. [[Hiram Page]] kept insisting on his testimony even while being beaten, until the mob's leader gave up.[^6]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the intoxication and beating episodes rest on traditional accounts but are multiply reported).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The standard objection Peterson himself raises: the witnesses kept up the testimony to protect their reputations, not because it was true.[^7] He answers that this fails because they were demonstrably *not* afraid of public opinion — they accepted beatings, lost farms, and forfeited careers — so the reputational-protection motive evaporates. A residual skeptical point: "no recantation on record" is partly an argument from silence, since a private deathbed doubt could simply not have been documented.

## Best Quotes

> "It's significant that none of them ever backs away from the testimony that he bears... right up to their deathbeds. They're always bearing their testimony."[^1]

> "If he didn't understand me then, let him understand me now. I have never at any time denied my testimony."[^3]

> "There's no evidence that these guys were afraid of public opinion because they took a beating for sticking with their testimony."[^7]
