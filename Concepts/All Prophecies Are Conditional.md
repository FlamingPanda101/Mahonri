# All Prophecies Are Conditional

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Jacob Hansen]] (as the critic) presses: God makes prophecies about the future — including, in the [[1 Nephi 13.40|Book of Mormon]], the coming of [[Joseph Smith]]. If the future is outside God's control (open theism), how can he prophesy accurately without exhaustive foreknowledge? Doesn't accurate prophecy require a future "set in stone"? [^1] See [[Foreknowledge and Free Will Are Logically Incompatible]].

## The Rebuttal
[[Blake Ostler]] gives a *three-category* account of how an open-theist God prophesies. Category one is conditionality:
1. **Jeremiah states the rule explicitly** — rate: strong, direct prooftext. [[Jeremiah 18.7-10|Jeremiah 18:7-10]]: "Sometimes I threaten to uproot and tear down and destroy a nation... but if that nation turns from its evil, I also repent of the evil which I threatened to do." Ostler: "What is drawn from this is, well, God's prophecies are all conditional." *(scriptural)* [^2]
2. **Jonah is the paradigm case** — rate: classic illustration. God says "I will destroy Nineveh" ([[Jonah 3.4|Jonah 3:4]]); the people repent; God relents; Jonah is angry that God "didn't do what he said he was going to do." The condition was implicit — "if the people repented, he didn't intend to do that." *(narrative)* [^3]
3. **Ostler's own scholarship** — rate: locates the argument in published work. He cites his paper "*Hermeneutical Assumptions and Open Theism*" in the journal *Element*, where he walks through how to handle such texts. *(self-citation)* [^4]
4. **Honest about the implicit-condition move** — rate: intellectually candid. He concedes that reading in unstated conditions can be ad hoc: "I'm not a real fan of implicit texts... reading into it something that saves it from itself just because I needed to save it." He accepts the cost because he does not hold scripture infallible. *(methodological)* [^5] See [[Failed Prophecies Show Scripture Is Not Infallible]].

(The other two categories — prophecies God *intends to bring about himself*, and *causally determined* outcomes — are treated under [[God Fulfills Prophecy as a Master Chess Player]].)

**Steelman of the critic:** A defender of exhaustive foreknowledge argues that "all prophecies are conditional" cannot be sustained: messianic and apocalyptic prophecies (Christ's birth at [[Micah 5.2|Bethlehem]], the resurrection, the precise [[1 Nephi 13.40|Book of Mormon predictions]]) are stated unconditionally and depend on countless *free* human choices (Judas's betrayal, Pilate's verdict). Reading an implicit "unless people freely choose otherwise" into every such text is, by Ostler's own admission, a salvage move. The conditional model fits warning-oracles like Jonah well but strains on detailed predictive prophecy — which is exactly why Ostler supplements it with the "God brings it about himself" category and ultimately bites the bullet that some prophecies simply *failed*.
