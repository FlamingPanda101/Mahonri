# The Early Church Did Not Operate Under Sola Scriptura

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]]'s third argument: if sola scriptura was not true during the writing of the NT, it had to "become normative at a later time" — but the early Christians never lived under such a rule. [[Ignatius of Antioch]] (AD 107) tells believers to "follow the bishop as Jesus Christ follows the Father"; [[Clement of Rome]] speaks of apostolic *successors*; the Apostles' Creed names "the holy Catholic Church" but not Scripture; [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] refers heretics to "that tradition which originates from the apostles ... preserved by the succession of presbyters." Per [[Jaroslav Pelikan]], "in the ante-Nicene church there was no notion of sola scriptura." Therefore sola scriptura "never started at all" and is a man-made tradition.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The patristic argument from silence-plus-positive-witness — rate: strong
Horn does not merely note silence; he cites positive appeals to bishop, succession, and church (Ignatius, Clement, Irenaeus 3.4.1; the Creed) and a top-tier historian (Pelikan).[^1] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers replies (a) even if true, "let God be true though every man a liar" — patristic practice cannot overturn a doctrine grounded in Scripture; and (b) he actually thinks the fathers *did* hold sola scriptura, citing Nemesius and Clement of Alexandria on self-authentication. Horn's rejoinder: those same fathers used the *church's* authority (Clement against the Quartodecimans; Augustine against the Donatists) — see point 3.

### 2. The Irenaeus "if the apostles had left us nothing in writing" text — rate: contested, both sides score
Rogers argues Irenaeus' famous passage is a *conditional* ("*if* it had been the case that God had not left us writings") and so does not establish a co-equal tradition.[^2] Horn counters with [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] *Against Heresies* 3.3.2–3: "it is the duty of every church to agree ... with the church of Rome which is the most preeminent" — odd for a sola-scriptura advocate.[^3] **Assessment:** Rogers correctly reads the conditional; Horn correctly shows Irenaeus *also* grounds truth in apostolic-succession churches. Irenaeus held both Scripture *and* tradition against heretics who had neither — which actually fits Horn's "material sufficiency + ecclesial authority" model better than strict sola scriptura.

### 3. Augustine, Ambrose, Vincent of Lérins — rate: moderate, the cross-ex battleground
Horn cites Augustine ("I would not believe the gospel unless moved by the authority of the Catholic Church"; the Donatist controversy "placed beyond dispute by the decree of a plenary council"), Ambrose ("you cannot flee the rule of faith given at Nicaea"), and **Vincent of Lérins** ("by the decree and authority of a council the rule of the church's faith may be settled").[^4] **Steelman of Rogers:** Rogers grants the church has *ministerial* (not *magisterial*/infallible) authority, and that councils were "always conducted" from Scripture; he cites Augustine's "let us treat scripture like God speaking" as evidence of self-authentication. The real disagreement is *ministerial vs magisterial* authority, not whether the fathers valued the church.

## Best Quotes
> "Ignatius of Antioch in AD 107 never cites the New Testament as scripture. But he tells his audience to follow the bishop as Jesus Christ follows the father."[^1]

> "According to then-renowned Lutheran scholar Jaroslav Pelikan, in the ante-Nicene church there was no notion of sola scriptura."[^1]

> "How should it be if the apostles themselves had not left us writings? Would it not be necessary to follow the course of the tradition which they handed down?" [Irenaeus, quoted by Horn][^5]

> "[Irenaeus] against heresies 3.3.3 ... it is the duty of every church to agree not with scripture but with the church of Rome which is the most preeminent and ancient of the churches."[^3]
