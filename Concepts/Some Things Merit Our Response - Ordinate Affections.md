# Some Things Merit Our Response - Ordinate Affections

## The Point
Against the Green Book's claim that "sublime" merely vents a feeling, Lewis insists that **some objects and acts genuinely merit certain responses**. The waterfall really was sublime; Coleridge's emotion was *appropriate* to it. On this older view, emotions are not brute facts beyond appraisal: they can be reasonable or unreasonable, fitting or unfitting, **just or unjust**. This is the classical and Augustinian doctrine of *ordinate affections* (*ordo amoris*) — that there is a right ordering of love and feeling, a way our hearts *ought* to be tuned to the worth of things. Lewis therefore defends "just sentiments" and "stable sentiments" even as he attacks mere "sentimentalism."[^1]

## Evidence
Ward quotes Steven Reagles glossing Lewis: the Cora Linn waterfall "'merited' the designation 'sublime' not because the viewer had 'sublime' feelings but because the waterfall was in itself 'sublime,' an awesome embodiment of cascading liquid crystal."[^2] The category Lewis cares about is *fittingness*: an emotion answers to a reality outside the self. Hence Ward's careful distinction — Lewis "finds fault with 'sentimentalism' (emotions that are excessive or ungrounded)" but "is keen to defend 'just sentiments' and 'stable sentiments.'"[^1] Gaius and Titius commit "an elementary mistake in logic": fearing corrupted emotion, they reject emotion as such. Lewis answers with one of his favorite tags, *abusus non tollit usum* — "abuse does not abolish use": that feelings *can* be corrupted into irrational emotionalism "is no justification for regarding emotion as altogether and necessarily irrational."[^1] Lewis is equally careful not to overcorrect into pure objectivism: invoking Shelley's "internal adjustment," Ward notes Lewis affirms we are conscious *subjects* who "accommodate our sensibility in the light of what we experience" — yet the value we are adjusting *to* is really there.[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "The Cora Linn waterfall was an external object of nature that 'merited' the designation 'sublime' not because the viewer had 'sublime' feelings but because the waterfall was in itself 'sublime.'" [^2]

> "Whereas Lewis finds fault with 'sentimentalism'... he is keen to defend 'just sentiments' and 'stable sentiments.'" [^1]

> "*Abusus non tollit usum* ('abuse does not abolish use')." [^1]

## LDS Connection
That affections can be *ordinate* — rightly proportioned to the real worth of things — is deeply congenial to Restoration thought, which makes the schooling of *desire* central to discipleship. Disciples are commanded to "let virtue garnish thy thoughts unceasingly" (D&C 121:45) and judged in part by their desires (Alma 41:3; D&C 137:9), precisely because the heart can be aligned or misaligned with what truly merits love. The Light of Christ furnishes the standard by which an affection counts as *just*: it is "the Spirit of Christ... given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16). This is the live answer to well-being subjectivism: where [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]] grounds value in felt states of the brain ([[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]]), Lewis and the Restoration alike hold that the feeling is answerable to a worth that precedes and measures it.
