# The James the Less Conflation Inflates the Martyr Count

## The Claim
The single genuinely well-attested early-Christian martyrdom commonly slotted into the "the apostles all died for their faith" list belongs to **[[James the brother of Jesus]]** — but he was *not* one of the Twelve. Later Christians merged him with **James the Less (James son of Alphaeus)**, who *was* one of the Twelve, and thereby transferred the one solid martyrdom onto an apostle who has no early martyrdom tradition of his own. [[Dan McClellan]]: "Later Christians conflated James the Less, the son of Alphaeus, with James the brother of Jesus. James the brother of Jesus has probably the best historical argument for martyrdom... but James the Less as James the son of Alphaeus doesn't have any martyrdom traditions until starting in the fourth and fifth centuries CE." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The brother of Jesus has the strongest early evidence — and is independent of the Twelve.** James the brother of Jesus is a major figure in the early Jerusalem church, and his execution is reported by [[Josephus]] (*Antiquities* 20) — a 1st-century, non-Christian source. That is precisely the kind of early, externally-attested datum the apologetic *wants*, but it attaches to Jesus's brother, not to an apostle on the list of Twelve. [^1] *Rating: strong; the Josephus notice is widely regarded as the best-attested martyrdom in the apostolic generation.*

2. **James son of Alphaeus has no martyrdom tradition until the 4th–5th centuries.** Taken on his own terms, the apostle James the Less attracts death-traditions only centuries later — the same late, apocryphal pattern as the other apostles (see [[Apostolic Martyrdom Traditions Are Late Apocryphal Legend]]). The famous "thrown from the temple" story is borrowed from accounts of the *brother*, not independently attested for the son of Alphaeus. [^1] *Rating: strong; without the conflation the apostle James the Less is evidentially empty.*

3. **The conflation does evidential double duty.** By identifying the two Jameses, the tradition (a) supplies the otherwise-bare apostle with a dramatic martyrdom and (b) lets an apologist count the brother's well-evidenced death toward the *apostolic* tally — inflating "how many of the Twelve were martyred." Disentangling them removes one of the few solid data points from the apostle column. *Rating: strong; a clean illustration of why source-disambiguation matters before tallying martyrs, per [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]].*

## LDS Relevance
The point is a **methodological caution that LDS apologists should welcome and apply consistently**. The Restoration tradition routinely insists on disentangling later accretions from early sources and on "diving into the primary sources before repeating the talking point." Here that same discipline trims the popular witness-of-the-Resurrection tally: an apologist who appeals to the apostolic martyr-count (cf. [[The Resurrection as the Linchpin of Christianity]]) cannot quietly fold the *brother of Jesus* into the Twelve. The honest version of the argument keeps the well-evidenced case (the Josephus notice about James the brother of Jesus) labeled accurately and does not let it prop up apostles for whom the evidence is late and apocryphal — the same standard the vault applies when it deflates late, single-sourced hostile claims about Joseph Smith.
