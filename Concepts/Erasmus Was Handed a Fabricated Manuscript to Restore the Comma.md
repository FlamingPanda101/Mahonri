# Erasmus Was Handed a Fabricated Manuscript to Restore the Comma

#type/claim

## The Claim

The [[1 John 5-7 - Johannine Comma Interpolation|Comma Johanneum]] entered the printed Greek New Testament not through manuscript evidence but through institutional pressure on one scholar. When [[Erasmus]] published his Greek text in 1516, he omitted [[1 John 5.7|1 John 5:7]] because he could find it in **no Greek manuscript**. Churchmen objected that he had dropped "the greatest verse on the whole Trinity." Erasmus replied that he could not find it in any Greek copy — and (on the panel's telling) was then handed a single Greek manuscript "found in England" that conveniently contained exactly the wording needed. Suspecting it had been **made up for the occasion**, but under threat of excommunication, he nonetheless folded the clause into later editions of his *Textus Receptus*, from which it passed into the King James Bible "to this day." The "banner verse that testifies of the Trinity" is therefore "an interpolative fraud." [^1]

The panel reads the manuscript evidence directly off the three great uncials: in **Codex Vaticanus** (image 1441), **Codex Sinaiticus**, and **Codex Alexandrinus**, the text runs from the end of v. 6 (*alḗtheia*, "truth") straight into v. 8 — *pneuma* (spirit), *hydōr* (water), *haima* (blood) — with no heavenly-witnesses clause between. "There is no verse seven in any of the original Greek manuscripts because that verse did not exist." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

**Where the panel is on solid ground (and the steelman of the traditional reply).** The core textual facts are not in dispute and are conceded across the scholarly spectrum, including by conservative evangelical text critics: the Comma is absent from every Greek manuscript before roughly the 14th–16th centuries, from the Old Latin, and from the early Fathers' running citations of 1 John. Erasmus *did* omit it from his first (1516) and second (1519) editions and *did* add it in his third (1522). A traditional, careful defender of the King James / *Textus Receptus* would grant all of this and retreat to a narrower position — that the *doctrine* of the Trinity never rested on this verse alone (it is read out of the Gospel of John's prologue, the baptismal formula of Matthew 28:19, the Pauline benediction of 2 Corinthians 13:14, and the broader sweep of the canon), and that a single corrupt clause therefore says nothing about whether the doctrine itself is biblical. On that move, see [[Trinity Is an Interpretation Not Explicit in Scripture]] and the panel's own rebuttal at [^3].

**Where the panel embellishes (intellectual honesty).** The dramatic "they fabricated a manuscript to win a wager" story is the *legendary* form of the episode and should be flagged as such. The manuscript in question — **Codex Montfortianus** (Gregory-Aland 61), now at Trinity College Dublin — is real and does contain the Comma, but in awkward Greek that looks back-translated from the Latin Vulgate. The notion that Erasmus made a formal *promise/wager* to include the verse "if a single Greek manuscript could be produced," and that one was then manufactured to corner him, comes from a later anecdote (traceable to a 1790s remark) and is regarded by most specialists as embroidery rather than documented fact. What *is* documented is that Erasmus thought the manuscript suspect and inserted the clause reluctantly. The argument therefore survives without the wager legend: the Comma is a verifiable late interpolation regardless of the exact arm-twisting that put it into print. This is the honest core the existing vault note already establishes — see [[1 John 5-7 - Johannine Comma Interpolation]].

**Why it matters to the LDS case.** If the *one* near-explicit Trinity proof text is a demonstrable scribal addition, then the doctrine must be *constructed* from inference and tradition rather than *found* on the page — which is precisely the Latter-day Saint claim about the post-apostolic development of the creeds. Cf. [[Trinity Was Constructed Not Revealed]], [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].
