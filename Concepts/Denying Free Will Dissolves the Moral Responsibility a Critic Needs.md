# Denying Free Will Dissolves the Moral Responsibility a Critic Needs

## The Point
A critic who blames a religion for moral failings needs agents who are *responsible* for their choices — otherwise "blame" is incoherent. But if, as the naturalist holds, "everything is cause and effect" and there is no real free will, then no one "makes choices," and "moral responsibility" loses its subject. [[Jacob Hansen]] turns this on the critic [[Bill Reel]]: Reel affirms hard determinism — agency is "extremely limited or non-existent," child predators are "determined" by genetics — yet wants to hold the Church and its prophets *culpable*. The two cannot stand together. This is the metaethical mirror of [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]: the same determinism that the naturalist invokes to excuse the wrongdoer also dissolves the standing to condemn anyone, including the critic's target.

## Evidence
Hansen first secures the premise. "Science says that everything is cause and effect," and pressed on it Reel agrees he does not believe in free will: "no I don't… we don't have agency."[^1] He universalizes it: "if you were born in their circumstances… you'd be racist," and "if I was born into a family where I had the predisposition to be a serial killer… I might turn into a serial killer with no choice of my own."[^2] He extends the same determinism to child predators, relaying a geneticist's claim that "child Predators aren't choosing to do that, they are predisposed genetically," so that agency "is nothing like what Mormonism taught you."[^3] Reel even concedes the linguistic awkwardness: "we all act as though we have agency even if some of us understand that that agency is extremely limited or non-existent."[^4]

Hansen then springs the lock: if "you don't even make the choice," then "how can we have a coherent discussion about moral responsibility if you're not morally responsible for any of your choices because you ultimately don't make choices?"[^5] The critic's first premise — "people are only morally responsible for the choices that they make" — combined with "we don't make choices" yields *no one is morally responsible for anything*, which guts the moral indictment of the Church the critic is trying to deliver.

## Best Quotes
> "you don't believe that human beings have free will? — Free Will no I don't. — so we don't have agency? — yeah I don't." [^1]

> "we all act as though we have agency even if some of us understand that that agency is extremely limited or non-existent." [^4]

> "so how can we have a coherent discussion about moral responsibility if you're not morally responsible for any of your choices because you ultimately don't make choices?" [^5]

## LDS Connection
Moral agency is foundational to Latter-day Saint theology — humans are agents able to "act and not be acted upon," and accountability presupposes genuine choice. The hard determinism the critic imports is therefore not a neutral scientific result but a worldview commitment that, taken seriously, abolishes praise, blame, repentance, and reform alike. Note the asymmetry Hansen exposes: the determinist excuses the wrongdoer ("no choice of my own") yet still reaches for blame against the Church — a sign he is borrowing a robust notion of responsibility his own system has denied (see [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]). Compare Sam Harris's attempt to keep moral responsibility while denying free will ([[Moral Responsibility Without Free Will - Harris's Reconciliation]]); the LDS view instead retains real agency as the precondition for any moral talk at all (see [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]).
