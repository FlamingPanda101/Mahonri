# The Judas Argument — Better Never to Have Been Born

## The Claim
Scripture itself concedes the key premise of the problem of hell. In [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]], Jesus says of Judas, "it had been good for that man if he had not been born." If a person's fate can be *worse than non-existence* — and Jesus says it can — then a free creator with perfect foreknowledge could have refrained from creating that person and thereby spared them. Since God created Judas anyway, knowing the outcome was worse than never having lived, the case is "not a mystery; that's a contradiction" [^1].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The verse supplies the comparison the argument needs — rate: strong (the comparison), contested (the inference).** The button argument depends on non-existence being preferable to a damned existence; Jesus' lament grants exactly that ordering for Judas [^1]. The Greek **καλὸν ἦν αὐτῷ εἰ οὐκ ἐγεννήθη** ("it were good for him if he had not been born") is a comparative judgment of a fate against non-being. See [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] and its prior use in [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]]. **Steelman of the theist:** (a) the saying may be Semitic *hyperbole* — a woe-oracle expressing the gravity of betrayal, not a literal metaphysical ranking of being vs. non-being; (b) "the Son of man goeth as it is written of him" frames Judas's role within prophecy, and many readers (and the LDS "son of perdition" category) treat Judas as an exceptional case, not a template for the ordinary damned; (c) even granting the ranking, a libertarian-free-will theist says Judas *made* his fate, so the lament indicts Judas's choice, not God's creative act.
2. **It internalizes the critique — rate: strong rhetorically.** The argument's power is that it is *Jesus*, not a skeptic, asserting a fate worse than non-existence; a Christian cannot dismiss the premise as anti-Christian without dismissing Christ. This is why the verse is a recurring vault prooftext (cf. [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] "Used in").
3. **Drives straight to the disjunction.** Granting the premise forces one of the five exits in [[Five Divine Attributes Cannot All Be True]] — most naturally, denying *ex nihilo* creation (the LDS route) so that God did not unilaterally bring Judas into being.

## Best Quotes
> "Jesus himself, talking about Judas, the man who betrayed him, said it would have been better for that man if he had never been born." [^1]

> "If God knew before creating Judas that his life would end somewhere worse than never having lived at all, then why create him? God didn't have to. Nothing forced his hand... That's not a mystery. That's a contradiction." [^2]
