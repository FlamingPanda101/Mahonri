# Not Even the Devil Can Raise the Dead - The Aquinas Falsifiability Defense

#type/claim

## The Claim
This is [[Trent Horn]]'s direct rebuttal to the charge that "it's demonic" is unfalsifiable (the objection developed in [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable]]). Horn argues the demonic explanation **can be falsified**, because demonic power has hard limits. Following [[Thomas Aquinas]], he holds that the devil is a *creature*, and only God is *Creator* and "the author of life and death"; therefore the devil cannot raise the dead. If he could, he could subvert God's plan and usurp God's unique authority over life, which God will not allow. This yields a usable criterion: a **demonic explanation is ruled out for the Resurrection** (laws of nature genuinely suspended, life restored) but remains live for events the devil *could* in principle stage.[^1][^2]

Horn pairs this with a second Thomistic distinction: **a wonder is not the same as a miracle.** "Just because something is a wonder doesn't mean that it's a miracle." Some events merely make you scratch your head ("how did you do that?") without breaching nature. The demonic hypothesis therefore must be "used with care" — not scattered "willy-nilly" — but *when the evidence overall points that direction, it is fair to point it out,* exactly as Jesus reasoned about Beelzebul in [[Matthew 12.24-26]].[^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
**Steelman of Horn.** The defense is genuinely more sophisticated than the "get out of jail free card" caricature, and it answers the unfalsifiability charge on its own terms:
1. **It supplies a defeater condition.** "If X then not-demonic" where X = raising the dead. That is a real falsification rule: produce a verified resurrection and the demonic hypothesis dies. So the explanation is not immune to evidence in principle — which is precisely what "unfalsifiable" denies.[^2]
2. **It is internally principled, not ad hoc.** The limit on demonic power is derived from classical theism (creature/Creator, God as sole author of life), not invented to win the argument. The same metaphysics that licenses *some* demonic explanations *forbids* others.
3. **It has scriptural warrant for evaluability.** Jesus does not treat "demonic" as unexaminable; he tests its coherence ("a house divided"). Horn's move is that demonic claims are *adjudicable by their fit with the evidence*, the opposite of a conversation-stopper.[^4]
4. **The "by-its-own-design" hook.** Horn's deepest point: what makes Mormonism a candidate at all is that it advertises a founding *otherworldly encounter* as a virtue. Once a supernatural origin is claimed, asking *which* supernatural source is not gratuitous — it is the question the claim itself raises.[^5]

**Where Aquinas's limit is contestable (cross-examination).** [[Hayden Carroll]] never disputes the metaphysics directly, but his standing reply applies: even granting the devil cannot raise the dead, this only narrows the *cases* — it does nothing to show that *any particular* unexplained event (the Book of Mormon's production) is in fact demonic rather than natural. The Aquinas limit tells you what the devil *can't* do; it gives no positive evidence that he *did* anything here. So the criterion, while real, is nearly inert for the disputed case: the Book of Mormon is a *text*, not a corpse restored to life, so it sits squarely in the zone where "the devil could in principle do it" — and therefore equally in the zone where a natural author could. Carroll's "it feels more like a magic trick than a miracle" lands exactly here (see [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]]). Honest tension: Horn has rebutted "unfalsifiable in principle," but has *not* converted that into "evidenced in fact" for the one case the debate is about.

## Best Quotes
> "There are some things not even the devil can do. Just because something is a wonder doesn't mean that it's a miracle."[^3]

> "Not even the devil — being a creature, and God is creator — the devil cannot bring someone back to life. He can't raise the dead, because God is solely the author of life and death... God would not allow him to subvert his plans."[^2]

> "If the evidence overall points in that direction, I do think that it's fair to point that out... but it should be employed very carefully among competing hypotheses."[^4]
