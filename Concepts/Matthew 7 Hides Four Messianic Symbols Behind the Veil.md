# Matthew 7 Hides Four Messianic Symbols Behind the Veil

#type/claim

## The Claim
Reading Matthew 7 as the **holy of holies** of the [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Sermon-as-ordinance]], [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that the odd "good gift" sayings (Matt 7:9-10) encode **four Messianic objects associated with the space behind the veil**. After the triple petition "ask, seek, knock" (7:7-8) — read as the request for *admission* to the kingdom — Jesus asks: "what man... if his son ask **bread**, will he give him a **stone**? Or if he ask a **fish**, will he give him a **serpent**?" [^1] Butler's observation: on the *Matthew 6* side of the veil you had **bread** (the [[Epiousios Is Yahweh Bread Not Daily Bread|Yahweh-bread feast]]); on the *Matthew 7* side there is a **stone** — and behind the veil historically sat **the foundation rock** (the boulder of the *debir*) and, before [[Hezekiah]]'s reforms, **the brass serpent** (the *nehushtan*). [^2] So the four paired items — bread/stone, fish/serpent — are read as **four Messianic signs**, and the saying becomes ritual: "you knock the three knocks, they get you in, then some things are going to be presented to you, and you better give [the right one] — if you're asked a fish, you better get a fish." [^3] Butler explicitly flags this as **provocative but unresolved** ("I don't have a full answer here... I think there's a thing here that I can't quite see; I don't think it's nonsense, I just don't understand it"), and notes the number **four** itself recurs as a teaser for the next session. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **A boulder and a brass serpent really were behind the veil.** Both data are biblical: the *debir* held the Ark atop the foundation stone (developed in [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Part I]]), and 2 Kings 18:4 records that Hezekiah destroyed the bronze serpent Moses had made (the *nehushtan*), which had stood as a cult object — implying it had a sanctioned place beforehand. So "stone" and "serpent" are not arbitrary: they name objects with a genuine holy-of-holies pedigree. *Rating: the two negative items map onto real temple furniture; that is the strongest leg.*

2. **The Nephite valuation of the serpent is unambiguously positive and repeated.** The brazen serpent is invoked as a *type of Christ* in the Book of Mormon (and John 3:14), and the hosts note the Nephites treat it with reverence "not just once... it's repeated." That a behind-the-veil serpent could be a *Messianic* sign rather than a merely cursed thing is therefore internally supported. See [[The Two Serpents of Eden and the Divine Name Switch]]. *Rating: the positive-serpent reading has canonical backing.*

3. **The ask/seek/knock → admission reading fits the ordinance arc.** Treating the triple petition as a request for entry, immediately followed by objects "presented" to the entrant, tracks a recognizable rite-of-passage structure (petition → presentation → recognition) and dovetails with the room-three "seek the kingdom" climax. *Rating: coherent within the frame.*

4. **Intellectual honesty is itself a marker.** Butler repeatedly brackets this as a half-formed intuition rather than a settled claim, which (paradoxically) raises its credibility as *observation* rather than motivated construction: he reports a pattern he cannot fully cash out ("there's a thing here I can't quite see"). *Rating: the candor distinguishes this from over-confident parallel-hunting, though it also means the claim is admittedly incomplete.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This is the most speculative move in the episode, and the parent note's **parallelomania** worry bites hardest. Mainstream exegesis reads Matthew 7:9-10 as a plain *a fortiori* argument about a good Father answering prayer — bread and fish are simply staple foods, stones and serpents simply useless/dangerous look-alikes (a flat round loaf resembles a stone; an eel-like fish resembles a snake); the pairing is rhetorical, not an inventory of veil-objects. Reading "stone" as the foundation rock and "serpent" as the *nehushtan* requires the reader to already have the holy of holies in view and to ignore that bread and fish (the *positive* items) have no special behind-the-veil status. Butler's own "I can't quite see it" concedes the pattern does not close. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is offered tentatively and as *the four items being Messianic symbols*, which is defensible independent of the furniture-mapping (bread, fish, stone-as-rock, serpent-as-type-of-Christ all carry Christological freight elsewhere) — the right posture is to hold it as a flagged anomaly worth watching, not a load-bearing proof.

## Best Quotes
> "What man is there of you who, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Well, that's weird — because on the other side of the veil, in Matthew 6, you had bread, and on the Matthew 7 side there is a stone." [^1]

> "Prior to Hezekiah's reforms there was also a serpent inside the veil — the serpent on the nehushtan. So at one time there was a boulder and a brass serpent behind the veil. It's really provocative that the verses refer to that." [^2]
