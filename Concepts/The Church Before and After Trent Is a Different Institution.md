# The Church Before and After Trent Is a Different Institution
#type/claim

## The Claim
Michael Horton proposes — and [[Jordan Cooper]] and [[Gavin Ortlund]] develop — that "the church before the Council of Trent and after the Council of Trent is a different institution." Before Trent (1545–63), the doctrines that later defined Roman Catholicism against Protestantism — its theory of justification, the rejection of sola scriptura, and the exact extent of the canon — "were not settled, not binding." Trent *closed* questions that had been genuinely open, hardening latitude into dogma and anathema. Therefore "neither the Protestant Reformers nor the post-Tridentine Roman Catholic Church is exactly in continuity with the previous centuries"; the Reformers were not breaking with a fixed ancient consensus, because no such fixed consensus yet existed on these points.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Justification had real latitude.** [[Jaroslav Pelikan]] is cited for the greater variation on justification permissible before Trent. Medieval theologians held a spread of views (Augustinian, Thomist, nominalist) that Trent's Decree on Justification later narrowed and partly condemned.
2. **Sola scriptura was not yet forbidden.** The panel claims [[Thomas Aquinas]] could affirm the material sufficiency of Scripture "in exactly those words" — a position Trent's elevation of unwritten traditions later excluded. If the church's own Doctor held a view Trent condemned, the post-Trent church is not simply identical to the pre-Trent church.
3. **The canon was open.** [[Cardinal Cajetan]] — "one of Luther's strongest opponents" — questioned the canonicity of the deuterocanon and even a few New Testament books; there was "no definitive declaration of the canon" before Trent's 1546 decree. (Cross-links to [[Two Rival Canon Traditions Neither Settled Before Trent]], [[The Deuterocanon Was Disputed Until Trent Not Removed]], and [[The Council of Trent Canon Vote Was a Tiny Minority]].)
4. **The Augsburg Confession's repeated catholicity claim.** Because these matters were undefined, the 1530 Augsburg Confession could insist again and again "we teach nothing against the Catholic tradition" — a claim only intelligible if the boundaries had not yet been drawn at Trent.

**Steelman of the Catholic view.** A Catholic responds that Trent *defined* (made explicit and binding) what the church had always *held* implicitly — development and clarification under pressure of heresy, not innovation, exactly as Nicaea defined *homoousios* without inventing Christ's divinity. Pre-Trent "latitude" reflects undefined edges, not contradiction of the faith; Aquinas's high view of Scripture coexisted with tradition and the magisterium and was never *sola* in the Reformation's exclusive sense. On this view the institution is continuous; Trent is the same church speaking more precisely. The Protestant claim and the Catholic claim again turn on a single hinge: whether Trent's definitions are *development* of the deposit or *addition* to it.
