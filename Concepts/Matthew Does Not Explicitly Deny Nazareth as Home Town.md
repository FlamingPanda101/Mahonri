# Matthew Does Not Explicitly Deny Nazareth as Home Town (Apologetic Objection)

## The Objection

Apologists object that Matthew "never says that Joseph and Mary were from Bethlehem and not Nazareth. He never claims that. They just happen to be there then." Since Matthew doesn't explicitly say they _weren't_ from Nazareth, the apparent contradiction with Luke (where they clearly live in Nazareth) can be harmonized.[^1]

## Why It Fails

### 1. The narrative logic makes sense only if Bethlehem is home

McClellan: "The narrative states pretty clearly that they are coming back out of Egypt and they're like, 'Oh, we can't go back to Bethlehem. I guess we got to go to Galilee.' And the text says that they settle in Galilee in Nazareth, which is what you say when someone is moving somewhere new, not what you say when someone is returning home."[^2]

### 2. If Nazareth were home, the narrative would be different

"If this is where they were originally from, the narrative would be different. This is not the way you describe someone returning home." The natural reading of Matthew's account is that they had never lived in Nazareth before — it's a settlement, not a homecoming.[^3]

### 3. Geography reinforces this

Beecher observes that Nazareth is "so much farther away" from Bethlehem — you wouldn't describe that journey as "settling" if you were just returning to where you already lived. "When you're going that far out of your way, you'd mention that that was home."[^4]

## Best Quote

> "The text says that they settle in Galilee in Nazareth, which is what you say when someone is moving somewhere new, not what you say when someone is returning home. If this is where they were originally from, the narrative would be different." — [[Dan McClellan]][^5]
