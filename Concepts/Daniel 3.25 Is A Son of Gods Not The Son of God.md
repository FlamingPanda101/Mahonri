# Daniel 3.25 Is A Son of Gods Not The Son of God

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the KJV's "the form of the fourth is like **the Son of God**" at
[[Daniel 3.25|Daniel 3:25]] is an over-reading. The Aramaic *bar elahin* is (1) **indefinite** —
"a son," not "the Son" — and (2) **plural** — *elahin* is "quite explicitly plural," gods. The
accurate rendering is "a son of gods."[^1] He uses the case to show that non-English Latter-day
Saints already read Bibles that diverge from the KJV at theologically loaded points, and have done
so for generations.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The grammar — rate: strong
"*Bar elahin* is one, indefinite, and two, *elohim* is quite explicitly plural. It is a son of
gods."[^1] Two independent grammatical features (the absence of a definite article and a plural
noun) both cut against the Christological "the Son of God" reading retrojected by the KJV. Compare
the LDS-friendly point that [[God Is a Multivalent Term in Latter-day Saint Scripture|"god/gods" is
a multivalent term]].

### 2. The Portuguese-translation episode — rate: strong (first-hand)
Supervising the church's 2015 Portuguese Bible, McClellan faced ecclesiastical reviewers who wanted
to change the public-domain base text's "a son of gods" to match the KJV — worried that "we got
paintings hanging in chapels all over the world with Jesus in the fiery furnace." His reply: "I'm
just here to tell you what the text says… it's up to you." They kept "a son of gods."[^2] A
concrete instance of the translation pluralism documented in [[LDS Multi-Translation Policy Shift]].

### 3. Non-English members already live with KJV-divergent Bibles — rate: strong
McClellan's broader point: "there are more members of the church who are not first-language speakers
of English than that are," and their Spanish (2009) and Portuguese (2015) Bibles "differ from the
King James version in a lot of ways."[^3] So the anxiety that new English translations will create
"dissonance" is misplaced — most of the church already navigates this. **Steelman of a confessional
reader:** later canonical usage (or the Greek of Daniel) might warrant a singular-divine reading
theologically; McClellan's claim is about the Aramaic *as written*, which the reviewers themselves
ultimately conceded.

## Best Quotes
