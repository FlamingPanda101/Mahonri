# Understanding Replaces Fear - the Knowledge Cure for the Polygamy Stigma

## The Claim
The discomfort many Latter-day Saints feel about plural marriage is, on historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]]'s diagnosis, largely a *fear of the unknown*: when members lack concrete information, they fill the vacuum with worst-case scenarios, rumor, and the sensational stories they have absorbed. The remedy is not better apologetic slogans but knowledge — putting a human face on the practice so it is "no longer a vague" threat. She frames this with [[Marie Curie]]'s maxim that nothing in life is to be feared, only understood. The claim's discipline is that the cure is *accurate* understanding, including the negatives — not a sanitized one.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The mechanism of the fear.** "When we don't know about something we often will fill it in with worst-case scenarios or the speculative stories or the sensational stories that we've heard." A "more realistic view... can help a lot of not carrying the baggage of polygamy."[^1]
2. **Putting a face on it disarms it.** "Sometimes when we put a face on something so it's no longer a vague... it's not fear of the [unknown]." Naming and particularizing the people removes the abstract dread.[^2]
3. **The Curie frame.** Nash closes on [[Marie Curie]]: "Nothing in life is to be feared. It is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more so that we may fear less." Acquiring knowledge "can calm that fear that we often feel when approaching the topic of polygamy."[^3]
4. **A prescriptive challenge.** She urges anyone "afraid of the topic or doesn't want to learn about it to fight that impulse and learn something about it from the people, and see if that changes you at all."[^4]
5. **Reported efficacy — with the honest caveat.** The knowledge cure is not a defense of a "rose-colored" history: Nash's stated priority was a "balanced and relatively normal view," correcting *both* the sensationalized-negative stories *and* the "overly good stories that aren't really representative," because both distort.[^5] The peace comes from truth, which is why this pairs with [[Their Covenants Are Not Mine to Defend]].
