# Genesis 49 Names Three Distinct Gods in Jacobs Blessing

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] argues that the blessing of Joseph in **Genesis 49:22–26** preserves, in a single continuous Hebrew passage, the **divine family** of earliest Israel by naming **three distinct divine beings**: the **Bull of Jacob** (*Abir Yaakov* = Yahweh), **El** ("the God of your father"), and **Shaddai** ("the Almighty"). Because they appear back-to-back, each acting and giving a different blessing, the text is more naturally read as *three gods* than as three titles for one God — the reading only collapses once later verse-breaks and the italicized inserted word "God" are imposed. This is offered as direct biblical evidence (not just Ugaritic parallel) for the Father–Mother–Son family.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Three names, three actors, one breath (rating: moderate; exegetical/Butler)
Butler reads vv. 24–25 as a chain: the arms of Joseph's hands "were made strong **by the hands of the Bull of Jacob**... **by El** of your father, who shall help thee... and **by Shaddai**, who shall bless thee." "There's the Bull of Jacob, there's El, and then there is Shaddai... they're three different things." On a continuous-text reading "it's way more explicit that it's talking about three different divine beings as opposed to different names for the same." [^1]

### 2. The "Bull of Jacob" is Yahweh (rating: moderate)
The KJV "**mighty God** of Jacob" renders *Abir Yaakov*, where *Abir* — italicized "God" is an insertion — probably means **bull**: "the literal translation is the bull of Jacob." Paired with "the shepherd, the stone of Israel," this is Jacob's/Israel's national God, i.e. Yahweh. Developed in [[The Bull of Jacob Shows Yahweh Was Depicted as a Bull]]. [^2]

### 3. El and Shaddai are *not* the same as the Bull (rating: moderate)
Verse 25 then invokes "the God of your father" — Hebrew ***El*** — "who shall help thee," and "the **Almighty**" — Hebrew ***Shaddai*** — "who shall bless thee with... blessings of the breasts and of the womb." Butler stresses that the fertility blessing fits a **mother** figure and is a pun on *Shaddayim* (breasts); see [[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]]. So the passage lines up El (father) + Shaddai (breasted/mother) + the Bull (the virile son's image) — the divine family. [^3]

### 4. Punctuation and inserted words drive the monotheizing reading (rating: strong as a methodological point)
"Grammar and verse breaks definitely control how we understand scripture... They inserted the word *God* in the Bible." The oldest Hebrew is unvocalized, unpunctuated consonants; English chapter/verse divisions and supplied words ("even," "but," "*God*") are what let a reader treat three named beings as one God under different epithets. Remove them and the plurality is on the surface. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The standard reading takes *Abir Yaakov*, *El*, and *Shaddai* as **three honorific titles of the one patriarchal God** stacked in poetic parallelism — exactly the kind of piling-up of divine epithets that Hebrew blessing-poetry favors (cf. "the God of your father... the Almighty" as apposition). On this view Genesis 49, in its final canonical form, is monotheistic praise, and the El/Shaddai vocabulary is the same deity the patriarchs knew under those archaic names ([[Exodus 6.3|Exodus 6:3]]). Butler concedes the text *can* be read that way but argues the most natural sense of three differently-named beings performing three different acts — before editorial smoothing — is three gods, consistent with the Ugaritic family and with [[The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh]].
