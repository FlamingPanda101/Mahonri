# The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims

## The Claim (Objection)
The single largest evidential weakness across the catalog of Joseph Smith's "fulfilled prophecies" is that most are not contemporaneous records. They are reminiscences set down years or decades after both the prophecy and its supposed fulfillment — often in the *History of the Church*, in conference talks, in late affidavits, or in diaries written long after the events. A prediction recorded only after its outcome is known carries almost no evidential weight, because the recorder already knows how the story ends.[^1]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **The source himself raises it as the master caveat.** Larry "the Murf," a believing Latter-day Saint, opens the whole list by conceding "the vast majority of them are written years after the supposed prophecy happened, later reminiscences, many of them coming from the history of the church." (Evidence rating: strong — this is the proponent's own admission, not a hostile critic's.)[^1]
2. **Concrete dating gaps recur.** Philo Dibble's account of the [[Falling of the Stars Prophecy]] comes from a diary "written in 1892," roughly six decades after the 1833 event.[^2] The Wilford Woodruff "fill the Rocky Mountains" prophecy is dated to an 1834 meeting but "first heard" claim appears in an 1898 conference report.[^3] The "chimneys to mark the desolation" Jackson County prophecy is absent from contemporary documents and surfaces only in compilations written after the Civil War.[^4] (Evidence rating: strong, multiple independent instances.)
3. **The believing escape hatch is named honestly.** The host allows that "maybe something was said from Joseph Smith and then when it was later fulfilled they're then writing it down perhaps making it more specific, more miraculous." This concedes the kernel-plus-embellishment model rather than wholesale fabrication. (Evidence rating: moderate — plausible but unfalsifiable.)[^1]
4. **Steelman of the believing view.** A late record is not automatically false. Oral transmission in a tight community can preserve a genuine saying; the absence of an early *written* record in a frontier, semi-literate movement is expected and is not proof of invention. The host explicitly refuses to "automatically dismiss them and throw them out."[^5] The honest scholarly position is that lateness lowers evidential value sharply without proving falsity.

## Best Quotes
> "The vast majority of them are written years after the supposed prophecy happened, later reminiscences, many of them coming from the history of the church."[^1]

> "Maybe something was said from Joseph Smith and then when it was later fulfilled they're then writing it down perhaps making it more specific, more miraculous. So that's why I do approach these with some caution."[^1]

> "I don't think just because some of these are late recollections automatically dismiss them and throw them out. But I think we also do need to be cautious and use critical thinking as well."[^5]
