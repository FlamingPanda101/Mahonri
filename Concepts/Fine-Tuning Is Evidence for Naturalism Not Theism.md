# Fine-Tuning Is Evidence for Naturalism Not Theism

#type/claim

## The Claim (skeptical)
This is [[Sean Carroll]]'s most striking inversion, and the reason he says the [[Chance, Necessity, or Design - The Fine-Tuning Trilemma|fine-tuning argument]] "is the best argument that people have for the existence of God. But it's a terrible argument."[^1] His first objection: what fine-tuning *actually* establishes is narrow. It means "the constants of nature... take on values that allow for the existence of physical configurations of matter that behave like living creatures" — things that "metabolize... reproduce... process information... while obeying the laws of physics."[^1] But that, Carroll says, "is exactly what you would expect if God didn't exist."[^1]

The reasoning is a likelihood asymmetry. **Under naturalism**, the only way to get life *at all* is through physical, information-processing matter, so naturalism *requires* the constants to fall in the life-permitting window: "if you want to have life in a naturalistic cosmology, you better have some complex information processing systems, and the constants of nature better allow for those."[^2] **Under theism**, no such physical scaffolding is needed — "God can make anything alive because God is God"; indeed many theists "believe that there is life after death that doesn't involve physical stuff" and so needs no fine-tuning at all.[^2] Therefore the observation that life depends on delicately-set physical constants "is evidence that should increase your credence in naturalism, not theism."[^2] Carroll grants the easy half — "the likelihood that the parameters of nature would allow for life would be large under theism... I think that's okay" — but insists theism over-predicts: it is compatible with far too many non-physical ways of making life for the *physical* fine-tuning to count in its favor.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Fine-tuning is conditional on a physical route to life.** The fine-tuned quantities (vacuum energy, gravitational strength, etc.) matter only because life here is built from atoms, stars, and chemistry. A worldview on which life *must* be physical predicts exactly this dependence; a worldview on which life *need not* be physical does not require it.[^1][^2]
2. **Theism is too flexible to be confirmed by physical constants.** Because an omnipotent God could instantiate minds in countless non-physical ways (souls, afterlife, miracle), the specifically *physical* and *law-bound* character of our life is not what theism uniquely predicts. Carroll says theists "really underell God's ability to do things."[^2]
3. **It still plays by scientific rules — which is why it's "the best."** Carroll respects fine-tuning precisely because "it plays by the rules of scientific investigation": treat the universe as "a data point," propose a theory, and "judge my theories using Bayesian reasoning... calculate a likelihood."[^4] The objection is internal to that method: run the likelihoods honestly and the physical-life datum favors naturalism.
4. **Bridges to the prediction objection.** This argument is the front half of a one-two punch with [[Theism Doesn't Predict the Universe We Observe]]: even where theism scores (life-permitting constants are "large under theism"), the *form* of the evidence (brute physical lawful life) already leans naturalist before the big-universe data are even added.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the sharpest challenge in the video and deserves a real answer, not a dismissal.

- **The likelihood comparison is doing all the work — and it smuggles in a contested theism.** Carroll's inversion depends on the premise that theism is *indifferent* between physical and non-physical creation. The vault's positive case ([[Fine-Tuning - Why the Universe Is Best Explained by a Mind]], [[Anthropic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer]]) targets a different likelihood: P(a rationally ordered, mathematically elegant, *intelligible* cosmos | mind) vs. (| no mind). Even if a *bare* "God could do anything" hypothesis over-predicts, a *specified* theism — God intends embodied moral agents who learn within a stable, law-governed world — predicts exactly a physical, fine-tuned universe. Carroll himself concedes "you can accommodate and expect that exactly this kind of universe is what God would have made"; the LDS form makes that prediction non-arbitrary.
- **LDS theology blunts the "God needs no physics" premise directly.** Restoration doctrine holds that embodiment is *the point*: spirits seek physical bodies, "spirit and element inseparably connected receive a fulness of joy," and even God is embodied. On this view a *physical*, fine-tuned cosmos is not an awkward extra God could have skipped — it is precisely the kind of world a God committed to embodiment and eternal progression would set up. So the very datum Carroll says favors naturalism (life *must* be physical here) is *expected* under LDS theism, collapsing his asymmetry. Cf. [[Philip Goff]] and the "God who works within mathematical law" theme.
- **"Best argument but terrible" concedes the structure.** Carroll grants the argument is scientifically respectable and that life-permitting constants are "large under theism." The disagreement is not whether fine-tuning is real or evidential, but which prior to use for "what would God make?" That is the post-hoc dispute handled in [[Theism Doesn't Predict the Universe We Observe]] — and it is answerable by specifying the theism in advance rather than retrofitting it.
- **Watch the God-of-the-gaps mirror.** Carroll's first objection is *not* a gaps argument (it grants the physics); see [[The God of the Gaps Charge Against Design]] for why the design inference here is an inference to the best explanation, not an appeal to ignorance — the same standard cuts both ways.
