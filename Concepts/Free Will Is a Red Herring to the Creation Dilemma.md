# Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A standard defense of divine omnibenevolence appeals to human free will: God is not responsible for damnation because the damned *freely* reject him. [[Hayden Carroll]] declares this "wholly irrelevant to this debate — it's a red herring." He grants free will entirely: "My argument has nothing to do with free will. I don't have a problem with someone choosing hell."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The dilemma is upstream of freedom** — rate: strong. Because the morally decisive act is *creation* (see [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]]), conceding maximal libertarian freedom changes nothing: the question is why God created a person whose free damnation he foreknew, when he could have refrained. *(scope-clarification)* [^2]
2. **Strategic concession** — by granting free will, Hayden immunizes his argument against the affirmative's most-rehearsed move and forces the debate onto creation. Gavin's opening had pre-loaded a free-will/agency response (the "barometer" and *Adjustment Bureau* illustration); Hayden notes none of it touches the dilemma. *(debate-tactics)* [^3]
3. **Even sinless free creatures are possible** — in cross-exam Hayden gets Gavin (as a Catholic) to admit God *can* create sinless free-willed persons (Mary, in Catholic doctrine; translated figures like Enoch, Elijah, Moses). If God can create free persons who don't go to hell, "then why create anybody who's going to go to hell?" See [[God Could Have Created Only the Saved]]. *(internal-critique)* [^4]

**Steelman of the free-will defender:** the defense is *not* meant to answer "why create this person" but "is God the author of their sin" — and to that narrower question free will is decisive. The defender can then add that the *value of a world of free agents* (some of whom may fall) justifies creation, shifting the burden to the greater-good debate (see [[The Greater Good Defense Instrumentalizes Persons]]). So calling free will a "red herring" is fair only against the *naive* version; the sophisticated version routes through greater-good, which Hayden separately rebuts.
