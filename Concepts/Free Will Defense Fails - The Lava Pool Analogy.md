# Free Will Defense Fails - The Lava Pool Analogy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The standard creedal defense of the creation dilemma is the **free-will defense**: God creates the damned because love requires giving genuine free will, and "existing and going to hell is better than not existing at all." People *choose* hell; God merely respects the choice.[^1] Carroll and the panel attack this with the **lava-pool / wood-chipper analogy**: if your adult friend wants to use his free will to jump into a lava pool (or walk into a wood chipper), would you "love him enough to let him," or would you do everything in your power — even physically tackling him — to stop him?[^2]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Permission is not love when you can prevent catastrophe — rate: strong intuition.** The analogy shows that we do *not* treat non-interference with self-destruction as loving; we treat intervention as loving. Since God *can* stop the self-destruction but "chooses not to when he can," the free-will defense does not make God good — "it actually makes it worse."[^2] **Steelman of the objector:** there is a morally relevant disanalogy — tackling your friend at the lava pool prevents a *one-time, irreversible physical death*, whereas coercing someone's eternal *moral* orientation would destroy the very thing (genuine love/relationship) that can only be freely given. A forced "yes" to God is no relationship at all; God's restraint preserves the possibility of authentic love. This is the heart of the libertarian free-will theodicy and the panel does not fully dismantle it.
2. **The defense quietly downgrades God from judge to manufacturer — rate: pointed.** Carroll reframes: with the free-will defense, "we shift from being a God who's adjudicating our decisions while using our free will to being a God that's just like creating things for destruction."[^3] He illustrates with the difference between UN peacekeepers (who use force reluctantly to enforce peace) and a person who "takes joy in destroying things and torturing animals" — both use guns, but motive and vibe differ.[^4] The point: foreknowingly creating for a foreknown bad end looks less like respecting agency and more like manufacturing doom.
3. **Connects to the upstream-choice argument — rate: coherent.** The free-will defense answers "who is to blame for the sin?" but the dilemma asks "why create at all?" — so even a successful free-will defense leaves the *creation* decision untouched (see [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]], [[Hell - Free Will Defense Rebutted]]). Carroll also notes that on a strict *ex nihilo* view the creature is "not free by definition," so the defense may not even get off the ground — though he generously grants libertarian freedom for the sake of argument.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Your friend wants to use his free will to go jump in a lava pool. Are you going to let your friend do that or are you going to do everything in your power, even if physically, to restrain your friend?... The free will defense doesn't make God good cuz God can stop them, but he chooses not to when he can." [^2]

> "We shift from being a God who's adjudicating our decisions while using our free will to being a God that's just like creating things for destruction." [^3]

> "How is it loving to not try to stop them when you can?... It doesn't solve the problem. It actually makes it worse." [^2]
