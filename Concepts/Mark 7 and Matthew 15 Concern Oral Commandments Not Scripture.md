# Mark 7 and Matthew 15 Concern Oral Commandments, Not Scripture

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A Protestant may answer Horn's lexical challenge by pointing to **Mark 7 and Matthew 15**, where Jesus rebukes the Pharisees for nullifying "the word of God" with their traditions — apparently equating Scripture with the word of God.[^1] [[Trent Horn]] argues these passages refer to God's *orally delivered* commandments, not to the written text.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Neither chapter contains the Greek word for Scripture (graphē) — rate: strong
Horn notes that Mark 7 and Matthew 15 "do not contain the Greek word for scripture, graphē."[^1] The vocabulary of "Scripture" is simply absent; the dispute is about *commandment*, not *text*. This is a clean lexical observation that undercuts the proof-text at the level of word choice.

### 2. The "word" the Pharisees voided was the spoken Decalogue — rate: strong
What did the Pharisees contradict? The commandment to honor father and mother — i.e. **the orally spoken words God revealed to Moses in [[Exodus 20.12|Exodus 20]]** (the Ten Commandments) and Moses' reiteration in Deuteronomy.[^1] Jesus' charge is that the corban practice "made void the command God spoke to Moses to honor one's parents and the command Moses spoke in Deuteronomy to not curse one's parents."[^2] The referent is the *delivered commandment*, consistent with the prophecy/speech sense of "word of God." See [[Word of God Means Prophecy Preaching or Christ Not Scripture]].

### 3. The Matthew 15:6 textual variant — rate: moderate, corroborating
Horn adds that [[Matthew 15.6|Matthew 15:6]] has a textual variant: some manuscripts read that the Pharisees make null "the law" or "command of God," not "the word of God."[^2] This variant, if original or even early, shows the tradition identifying the referent as Yahweh's *orally delivered command*, not the written words describing it in Exodus/Deuteronomy.[^2] **Steelman of the Protestant:** the harder reading "word of God" is well attested and may be original; and the commandment is, after all, recorded in Scripture, so "word of God = the commandment Scripture preserves" still implicitly honors the text. Horn's reply is that even granting "word of God," the *referent* is the spoken command, not the document — so the verse does not establish "the written text is the word of God." The variant is corroborating, not load-bearing.

### 4. Net effect on the Protestant case — rate: framing
These are among the strongest proof-texts for "Scripture = word of God," and on Horn's reading they reduce to God's spoken commandment. Combined with [[Romans 3.2 Says Oracles Logia Not Words Logos]] and [[John 10.35 Cannot Be Broken Means Cannot Be Nullified]], the apologetic that Scripture self-attests as "the word of God" loses its textual footing.

## Best Quotes

> "Matthew 15:6 even contains a textual variant with some manuscripts recording Jesus saying, the Pharisees make null the law or command of God, not the word of God."[^2]
