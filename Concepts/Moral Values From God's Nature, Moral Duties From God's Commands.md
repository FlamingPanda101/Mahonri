# Moral Values From God's Nature, Moral Duties From God's Commands

## The Point
Craig's first premise — *if God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist* — depends on a two-part grounding that is the distinctive engine of his moral argument. Moral **values** (what is *good* or *bad*) are grounded in God's *nature*: "God's nature provides an objective reference point for moral values. It's the standard against which all actions and decisions are measured."[^1] Moral **duties** (what is *right* or *wrong*, what we are *obligated* to do) are grounded in God's *commands*: "God has expressed his moral nature to us as commands. These provide the basis for moral duties."[^2] Remove God and you remove both the standard and the lawgiver, leaving "no objective reference point" — only one person's viewpoint, "no more valid than anyone else's."[^3]

## Evidence
The values half is argued by analogy to physical reference points: "Without some objective reference point, we have no way of saying that something is really up or down."[^4] Absent God, morality becomes subjective "like a preference for strawberry ice cream; the preference is in the subject, not the object, so it doesn't apply to other people."[^3]

The duties half is illustrated from God's character flowing into command: "God's essential attribute of love is expressed in his command to love your neighbor as yourself," and that command "provides a foundation upon which we can affirm the objective goodness of generosity, self-sacrifice, and equality, and we can condemn as objectively evil greed, abuse, and discrimination."[^2] Without that lawgiver, the question "Who or what lays such duties upon us?" has only one honest atheist answer: "No one."[^5] On naturalism humans are "just accidents of nature, highly evolved animals," and "animals have no moral obligations to one another. When a cat kills a mouse, it hasn't done anything morally wrong."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "God's nature provides an objective reference point for moral values. It's the standard against which all actions and decisions are measured." [^1]

> "God has expressed his moral nature to us as commands. These provide the basis for moral duties." [^2]

> "But if atheism is true there is no ultimate standard, so there can be no moral obligations or duties. Who or what lays such duties upon us? No one." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The values/duties structure is fully usable by Latter-day Saint apologists and complements the broader cluster ([[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]]; [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]). The LDS nuance lands on the *duties* half: Craig grounds obligation in God's commands, which can sound like divine-command voluntarism, but Restoration theology reads the commands as the expression and administration of *eternal law* that God himself honors rather than invents from nothing ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]). So "duties from God's commands" is best understood as God authoritatively revealing and enforcing an eternal moral order, not making things obligatory by bare fiat — which is exactly the third-horn reading of the values half ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]). The point that morality has two parts — value *and* obligation — also matches the LDS emphasis that the good life is both becoming good (Christlike nature) and keeping covenants/commandments (cf. [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]).
