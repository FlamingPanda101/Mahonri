# Restoration of Blessings Enacts I Remember His Sins No More

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] tells his own story of leaving the Church as an **ex-Mormon atheist** — convinced he "had the goods on Joseph Smith" — and returning, to argue that the Church's administrative practice of **restoration of blessings** is a literal enactment of the Savior's promise, "to him that repenteth, I, the Lord, will remember his sins no more." [^1] He describes calling the bishop in tears, certain his hostile resignation letter (in which he had threatened to sue the Church and "borne my anti-testimony on all different levels") would bar his return, and being told: **"Son, if the Church couldn't forgive, it couldn't be the Lord's Church."** [^2] The mechanics carry the theology: after rebaptism but before restoration of blessings, the member's record shows the *original* temple ordinance dates but marks them inactive; once blessings are restored, the record shows the **original baptismal date "as if you had never left,"** keeping no record that the person ever departed, so a future ward "would never know." [^3] Bradley reads this confidentiality as the institution applying Christ's forgiveness — "your ward remembers it no more, just like the Savior remembers it no more" — and pairs it with the cultural critique that Latter-day Saints have wrongly **"villainized"** those who leave (the [[Religious Demand Versus Korihor Priestcraft Objection|Korihor]] stereotype), against which [[Dieter F. Uchtdorf|President Uchtdorf]]'s "Come, Join With Us" urged a pastoral approach. [^4] The frame loops back to the temple: returning to the Provo Temple, Bradley *for the first time* understood the endowment — via [[Ether 3.14|Ether 3]] — as his own being "redeemed from the fall" and re-admitted into Christ's presence. [^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The record-keeping practice is real and checkable.** That a restoration of blessings restores the original baptismal date and that the Church does not retain a "left the Church" mark on the ward record are matters of administrative policy, described here by someone who went through it and obtained permission to speak about it. The institutional behavior, not just the sentiment, embodies "remember his sins no more." *Rating: concrete, first-person, falsifiable.*

2. **The hostile-witness value carries unusual weight.** Bradley was not a sympathetic doubter but a *combatant* — an ex-Mormon-community activist who "thought it was impossible" to return. A former adversary testifying that the return process was the opposite of punitive is harder to discount than an insider's reassurance, and it is the same reconversion narrative that anchors [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment|his endowment-in-the-1830-text discovery]]. *Rating: strong rhetorically.*

3. **It self-corrects an in-group failing.** Bradley names and repudiates the LDS-cultural tendency to project the Korihor "horns and a tail" model onto those who leave, citing [[Dieter F. Uchtdorf|Uchtdorf]]'s conference correction. Owning the community's pastoral failures (rather than denying them) strengthens the claim's honesty and connects to the vault's empirical work on *why* people actually leave ([[The Four Cited Reasons People Leave Are Crowned by Culture]], [[Most Who Leave the LDS Church Do Not Become Atheists]]). *Rating: substantive, not merely defensive.*

4. **The doctrine cited is canonical.** "I, the Lord, will remember [his sins] no more" is scriptural (D&C 58:42-43; cf. Isa. 43:25, Jer. 31:34), so reading the administrative practice as its enactment is grounded in the standard works, not invented sentiment. *Rating: doctrinally anchored.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic grants the warmth of Bradley's experience and still raises two objections. (a) **Survivorship / anecdote**: one returnee's gracious reception does not establish a pattern; others report being met with suspicion, interrogation about their doubts, or social cost, and a single moving story cannot bear the weight of "this is how the Church treats those who leave." (b) **Records confidentiality cuts both ways**: erasing the departure from the record can also be read as institutional image-management — making apostasy *invisible* rather than *forgiven* — and the requirement of a year's wait plus First-Presidency-level approval for restoration is hardly a no-questions-asked embrace. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is narrowly that the *practice expresses* the doctrine of divine forgetting, not that every ward lives up to it — Bradley explicitly concedes the **cultural villainizing** as a real fault needing correction — so the anecdote illustrates the *ideal the policy encodes* (original baptismal date restored, no black mark retained), while the cultural critique honestly grants the gap between that ideal and members' behavior.

## Best Quotes
> "I called the bishop in tears and I said, 'They're never going to let me back into the Church.' And he said, 'Son, if the Church couldn't forgive, it couldn't be the Lord's Church.'" [^2]

> "Once you have a restoration of blessings... it shows your original baptismal date as if you had never left... They want the fact that you were ever out erased from the record — that your ward remembers it no more. Right? Just like the Savior remembers it no more." [^3]

> "We have a strong tendency to — I'll use the word — villainize people who leave the faith... I had this thing projected onto me, that I had horns and a tail, that I was out to destroy souls." [^4]
