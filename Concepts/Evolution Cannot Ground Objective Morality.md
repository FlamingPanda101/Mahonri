# Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality

## The Point
Turek answers the most common naturalist reply — that evolution, cooperation, or a "social contract" gives us objective morality so we can survive. His verdict: the argument "doesn't survive." Biology can *describe* what does survive; it cannot *prescribe* what ought to survive, and it has no authority to obligate anyone.[^1] Even Richard Dawkins, he notes, admits "it's pretty hard to get objective morality without religion" and calls himself "a passionate anti-Darwinian when it comes to the way we should organize our lives and our morality."[^2]

## Evidence
Turek lays out a sequence of replies.[^1] (1) Explaining morality by biology is a **category mistake** — "you can't explain an immaterial moral law by a material biological process," just as justice has no chemical composition. Moral laws are *prescriptive* and come from authoritative agents; biological processes are merely descriptive. (2) Survival cannot be made a moral *right*: "Biology describes what does survive, not what ought to survive." If survival were the standard, "should a person rape to propagate his DNA?" — which is exactly how Hitler used evolutionary theory.[^3] (3) Physical survival isn't the highest virtue — self-sacrifice is. (4) Since evolution is *change*, evolved morals must change, so they cannot be objective and unchanging. (5) A "social contract" can't ground objective morality, because judging a *contract-breaker* wrong again requires a standard "beyond any social contract, like we did in order to condemn the Nazi social contract." (6) The "we wouldn't survive without cooperation" claim is "a pragmatic issue, not a moral issue" — and isn't even true, since criminals and dictators like Stalin often prosper.[^4]

Crucially, Turek enlists Sam Harris *against* the evolutionary grounding: "Evolution could never have foreseen the wisdom or necessity of creating stable democracies, mitigating climate change... or of doing much else that is now crucial to our happiness."[^5] Turek's gloss: "evolution describes a survival-of-the-fittest outcome. It doesn't prescribe a moral outcome" — so Dawkins and Harris, when they reject Darwinian ethics, "are stealing a moral law from God."[^6] He concludes that reason is only "a tool by which we discover what the moral law is," not the source of why it exists.[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "You can't explain an immaterial moral law by a material biological process. Justice is not made of molecules... How could a mutating genetic code have the moral authority to tell you how you ought to behave?" [^1]

> "Biology describes what does survive, not what ought to survive. Why should humans survive as opposed to anything else? And which humans, we or the Nazis?" [^3]

> "evolution describes a survival-of-the-fittest outcome. It doesn't prescribe a moral outcome. That's why Richard Dawkins and Sam Harris... are anti-Darwinian when it comes to morality. They just don't realize that they are stealing a moral law from God." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Turek's central engine here is the is–ought gap, the same insight Michael Ward draws from Lewis: no purely factual premise (what *does* survive) can yield a normative conclusion (what *ought* to be done) ([[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]; [[You Cannot Derive an Ought From an Is]]). Sam Harris's well-being project is the naturalist foil — it tries to close that gap by stipulating "well-being" as the standard ([[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]; [[Closing the Is-Ought Gap - Harris Against Hume and Moore]]; [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]]) — but Turek's point is that the stipulation is itself an unargued borrowing. Latter-day Saint theology grounds the "ought" not in mutable genes but in eternal moral law that God honors and administers ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]), and locates human moral worth in our being made in God's image ([[Genesis 1 Image of God as Seedbed of Rights]]) rather than in reproductive fitness — exactly the fixed, non-biological ground Turek says the evolutionary account lacks.
