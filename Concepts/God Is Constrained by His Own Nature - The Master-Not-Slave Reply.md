# God Is Constrained by His Own Nature - The Master-Not-Slave Reply

#type/claim

## The Claim
In the [[Thoughtful Faith - Mormonism vs Protestantism Who Has the Right View of God|nature-of-God debate]], [[Jacob Hansen]] turns the Protestant's own metaphysics into a concession that *even the classical-theist God is constrained*. The wedge is an admission [[Daniel Ortner]] volunteers: "the Bible says God cannot lie," and "Can God change his nature? No." Hansen pounces: "Then he has a constraint. Because he can't change his nature." He generalizes the principle: **"A truly omnipotent being has the power to be anything they want to be"** — including the power to alter its own nature — so a God who *cannot* change his nature is, by that very fact, not omnipotent "in a radical sense." Ortner refuses the inference, drawing a distinction Hansen rejects as merely verbal: God "is not a slave to his nature... God is the master of his nature. He's the one who's acting on his nature. Everything God does is free, but it's in accordance with his nature." The disputed question: is being unable to act against one's own nature a *limitation on power*, or is it simply what it means to be a perfectly stable agent?[^1][^2]

The move matters because it neutralizes a standard Protestant objection to LDS theology — that the LDS God is "limited" (working within eternal law, organizing rather than creating ex nihilo, unable to override agency). Hansen's reply: *your* God is limited too. Both sides locate a fixed point the deity cannot transgress; they differ only on *where* the wall sits and whether to call it "constraint."

## Supporting Evidence
**Steelman (LDS side — Hansen).** (1) *Symmetry / tu quoque.* The Protestant repeatedly faults the Restoration for a God bounded by eternal law and recalcitrant materials (see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). But once Ortner grants that God "cannot lie" and "cannot change his nature," he has conceded the genus the LDS occupies — a God with a fixed, non-negotiable nature he cannot transcend. The only remaining debate is over *which* constraints obtain, not *whether* God is constrained, which defuses "your God is too small" as a one-sided weapon. (2) *Omnipotence over one's own essence.* Hansen presses the strongest form of omnipotence as a *reductio*: if "all-powerful" means the unrestricted power to actualize any logically statable state of affairs, then it must include the power to *redefine oneself* — and since no one (Ortner included) thinks God can make himself a liar or evil, *nobody* actually holds radical omnipotence. Better to admit, with [[B.H. Roberts's Definition of Divine Omnipotence|B.H. Roberts]], that divine omnipotence is power exercised *within* a fixed nature and order, which is exactly the LDS picture. (3) *Dialectical payoff.* This frees the LDS to own its "constrained" God without embarrassment and to deploy that constraint where it does real work — the problem of evil and the necessity of the Atonement (see [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]] and [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]]).

**Steelman (Protestant / classical-theist side — Ortner).** (1) *Inability-to-self-destruct is not impotence.* Ortner's master/slave distinction tracks a real one in classical theism: God acts *from* his nature freely, and the impossibility of acting *against* it is not an external chain but the absence of any defect or potential-for-corruption. "God cannot lie" (Titus 1:2; Heb 6:18) is traditionally read as a *perfection*, not a deficiency — a being who *could* make itself evil would be *less* perfect, not more powerful. Omnipotence is standardly defined over the *logically possible* and the *consonant with God's nature*; "change your own essence" may be incoherent (a thing cannot be the very ground of its own alteration) rather than a power God lacks. (2) *Internal vs. external constraint.* Ortner's whole strategy is to grant the *internal* constraint of God's character while denying any *external* one — and that is precisely the line that separates classical theism from LDS finitism: the creedal God is bounded *only* by himself, the LDS God additionally by co-eternal matter, intelligences, and law he did not author. So Hansen's "symmetry" is incomplete; "self-bounded" and "bounded by another" are not the same metaphysical situation (cf. the realm/fundamental-reality dispute, [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]]). (3) *Master language.* Calling God "the master of his nature" preserves divine aseity and sovereignty: God is not subject to his nature as to a higher authority; his nature simply *is* him (divine simplicity — see [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]]), so "constraint by his nature" collapses into "being himself," which is no limitation at all.

The exchange stalls on a definitional fork that recurs across the whole debate: whether "omnipotence" is unrestricted will-to-power (Hansen's stress test) or maximal power *coherent with a perfect nature* (Ortner's classical reading). On the first, every theist's God is constrained; on the second, only the LDS God is constrained by *something other than himself*.
