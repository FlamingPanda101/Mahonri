## The Point
Sacred music, psalms, and hymns — long part of Israel's worship — were naturally part of Jesus's life and his followers' worship from the start. Several early hymns appear embedded in the New Testament. Outside the canon, the earliest surviving Christian hymns (Phos hilaron, the Oxyrhynchus hymn, and the works of Ephrem and Ambrose) reveal how ancient Christians organized their daily lives around remembrance of Christ and expressed faith through artistic forms.

## Evidence
1. Jesus's life "began and ended with hymns": Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:47), the angels' "Glory to God in the highest" (Luke 2:14), the hymn sung at the Last Supper (Mark 14:26), and Jesus crying out Psalm 22 on the cross (Mark 15:34).
2. NT references to hymn singing: 1 Corinthians 14:26; Ephesians 5:19; Colossians 3:16.
3. Around AD 112, the Roman governor Pliny the Younger reported that Christians "sing responsively a hymn to Christ as God" (Letters 10.96–97) — describing antiphonal singing.
4. *Phos hilaron* ("Joyous Light"), sung at evening lamp lighting, was already regarded as very ancient when it appears in a fourth-century text (possibly dating to the second or third century); it is still sung in Eastern Orthodox evening services.
5. The Oxyrhynchus hymn (papyrus, late third century) is the earliest Christian hymn with musical notation; musicologists have reconstructed its melody.
6. Ephrem the Syrian (ca. AD 306–373) left over four hundred hymns, many for women's choirs; through hymns he was "able to transcend frozen formulas." Ambrose of Milan spurred the development of Latin hymn singing in the West (e.g., *Aeterne rerum conditor*).

## Best Quotes
> "Augustine (AD 354–430): 'Do you know what a hymn is? It is a song in praise of God. If you praise God and do not sing, you do not utter a hymn; if you sing and do not praise God, you do not utter a hymn.'" [^1]

> "When Christians meet to worship they 'sing responsively a hymn to Christ as God.'" [^2]

## LDS Connection
Ellison observes that "many [hymns] may have been written by ordinary believers unknown to us, giving us a glimpse at the faith of laypersons who made their own contributions to church life" — echoing the participatory, congregational hymn culture valued by Latter-day Saints. [^2]
