# A Primitive Limit in God's Nature vs an External Eternal Constraint (Goff vs LDS)

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Philip Goff]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] agree that God is limited; they disagree about **where the limitation comes from** — and Goff argues his answer is the simpler one. Goff: "There's just one necessary being... There's no base reality outside of God. So again, maybe a bit simpler. There's just God, but it's a primitive fact about God that God can do some things and can't do others." When Hansen presses that nothing exists outside God to constrain him, Goff refuses to locate the limit in anything external: "I'm not thinking of these limitations as outside of God... It's just a primitive fact about God that God can do some things and can't do other things. That's just the bottom line." His parsimony argument is explicit: "We've all got to have primitive facts. You have base reality as the primitive fact... I just have *God can do some things and can't do others.*"[^1] Hansen's counter is that a limit *internal* to God's nature is worse, not simpler: it makes God's nature "deeply flawed" — "for some reason within God... he could do it another way, but his nature restricts him" — whereas the Latter-day Saint locates the constraint in an **eternal reality God works within**, which (Hansen argues) is the more coherent and Christ-consistent placement.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
- **Both sides concede an unexplained brute fact — the dispute is its *address*.** Goff's repeated move is that *everyone* terminates explanation somewhere (he cites Wittgenstein, "explanation has to end somewhere"); the only question is whether the stopping-point is "God can do some things and can't do others" (one item, inside God) or "there is an eternal base reality" (a second necessary item, outside God). Goff claims one terminus is more economical than two. **Rate: a genuine parsimony point** — Occam's razor does favor one necessary being over God-plus-substrate, *if* the two stories explain the data equally well.
- **Hansen's reply 1 — an internal limit impugns God's perfection.** If God is "the base of nature itself, of reality itself," Hansen asks, "how could there be a limitation in his nature?" A God who *could* have made a gentler world but whose own essence inexplicably forbids it looks arbitrarily defective; locating the constraint in *eternal law that even God is subject to* keeps God maximally good while still finite. This is the standard LDS placement — see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]] and [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]].
- **Hansen's reply 2 — Goff's parsimony is bought with explanatory loss.** The LDS picture's *extra* posit (an uncreated substrate of intelligences / spirit-matter) is doing work Goff's bare primitive cannot: it explains *why* love must be free and uncoerced, *why* opposition is necessary, and *why* the constraints are what they are, rather than leaving "God simply can't" as an unanalyzable surd. On this reading the LDS model trades one brute fact for slightly more posited furniture but buys back intelligibility (cf. [[Why a Brute Fact Cannot Terminate Explanation]] and [[Uncreated Intelligences Make Free Love Possible]]).
- **Steelman both sides.** *For Goff:* a primitive fact about God's nature is no more mysterious than the classical theist's "God exists necessarily" — and it avoids the LDS burden of explaining how a *second* self-existent reality coexists with God without either limiting the other (the very dilemma pressed in [[Two Self-Existent Realities Cannot Coexist]]). One necessary being with built-in limits really is the leaner ontology. *For Hansen:* parsimony in *number of entities* is not the only theoretical virtue; a model that renders divine limitation *unintelligible* (Goff's "it just is a fact") scores worse on explanatory depth than one that grounds the limitation in the nature of love, agency, and eternal law. Hansen is willing to pay one extra posit to stop God's nature from looking like a defect.

## LDS Relevance
This is the cleanest articulation in the vault of the **fault line *within* the finite-God family**: Goff and the Restoration are allies against classical theism (both deny an unlimited God who could have prevented all suffering) but rivals over the *metaphysics of the limit*. The Restoration's answer — God is supremely good and supremely powerful *within* an eternal order he did not author (uncreated intelligences, eternal law, spirit-matter; see [[The Uncreated Element Is Only the Most Fundamental Substrate]]) — is precisely the "base reality outside of God" Goff rejects as an unnecessary extra. The apologetic payoff cuts both ways: against the *classical* theist, the LDS can borrow Goff's own concession that limiting God is unavoidable to solve evil ([[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]]); against *Goff*, the LDS argues that putting the limit *inside* God's nature makes the Father look flawed and impersonal, whereas eternal law preserves a perfectly good *person* (the disagreement that surfaces next as [[Goff - The Father Is Not a Mind, God Is the Only Non-Conscious Thing]] and [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]]). The Latter-day Saint accepts the label Goff offers as a defeater — "there is a reality more fundamental than God" — and treats it not as a demotion but as the ground that lets God be both finite *and* unflawed.
