# Interracial Marriage in Joseph Smith's Family

## The Claim
Interracial marriage was "not some distant, far-off hypothetical issue" to [[Joseph Smith]] — it was present in his own biological family and among his in-laws. [[Don Bradley]] argues this personal proximity shaped Joseph's "lens" on the curse/race questions raised by [[Abraham 1.21-27|Abraham 1]] and may help explain his later anti-racist actions.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A first cousin married a Native American woman during the Book of Mormon translation (rated: moderate, genealogical).** Joseph's cousin on the Mack side, Stephen Mack Jr. (son of [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s brother Stephen Mack), "marries a Native American woman while Joseph is translating the Book of Mormon" — "at the same time, over in Illinois."[^1] Bradley candidly admits he hasn't studied the local controversy level of this particular case: "I haven't looked closely at that one."[^2] *Steelman:* this is the weaker of the two examples — a cousin's marriage is geographically and relationally distant, and Bradley's own uncertainty about its reception limits how much "proximity" it establishes.

2. **Emma Smith's aunt married a Black man — the stronger case (rated: strong, documented).** [[Emma Smith]]'s father Isaac Hale had a sister, Antha Hale, who "married a Black man" named Joseph Wallace; "they were married... and had a family," "several children together," then called "mulatto."[^3] This made Joseph Smith an uncle-by-marriage to a Black man and gave Joseph and Emma's children cousins "who were a mix of Black and white."[^3]

3. **The 1789 Connecticut wedding cost the minister his pulpit (rated: strong, documented).** The marriage was so controversial that the couple traveled to find a "radical preacher... actually willing to perform the marriage" — Elam Potter — who "got kicked out of being the pastor of his congregation" for officiating.[^4] Dated to 1789, "right after the Constitution was passed," "nearly seven decades before the Emancipation Proclamation," with Southern slavery "going very strongly" — yet in Connecticut (the North). Emma's father Isaac Hale "was probably at this very controversial wedding."[^5]

4. **Interpretive payoff (rated: moderate, speculative).** Bradley: "You can't say that God made these people get married, but it definitely affects his lens... It might help to explain some of his actions subsequent to this."[^6] The host (Stephen Jones), who is biracial, reacts with strong personal resonance.

## Best Quotes

> "Emma Smith's aunt married a Black man. ... Married and had a family. They had several children together. Who at the time were called mulatto."[^3]

> "Because he performed that marriage, he got kicked out of being the pastor of his congregation."[^4]

> "Interracial marriage is not some distant, far-off hypothetical issue to him. It's very much present in his own family. And so Joseph has to think about these issues."[^6]
