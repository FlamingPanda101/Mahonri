# Their Covenants Are Not Mine to Defend

## The Claim
Many Latter-day Saints carry plural marriage as a personal burden — feeling obligated to justify, on their own conscience, "a practice that will get you kicked out of the church today" but "was commanded by God 150 years ago." Historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] offers a reframe that resolves the tension without either rejecting the history or pretending it away: the 19th-century Saints made *their own* sacred covenants and their own choices; a member today is responsible to make and keep *her own* covenants, not theirs. The burden of defending their decisions is not hers to bear — it was theirs, and they bore it. This is offered as pastoral counsel for finding peace, not as a dodge of the historical questions.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Naming the tension honestly.** Nash states the hard form of the problem rather than softening it: members feel "uncomfortable or even ashamed" that what "will get you kicked out of the church today, we teach was commanded by God 150 years ago."[^1] The concept earns its resolution by first conceding the genuine difficulty.
2. **The failed first move: disowning it.** Growing up in St. Louis she initially reacted by saying "Mormons don't practice polygamy anymore" and rejecting it "as not affiliated with us, not a part of me" — which she came to see as a form of embarrassment and shame, not a real answer.[^2]
3. **The mature move: it was their choice to defend, not mine.** Reading how the practitioners defended their own choices "helped me feel free to... not defend them anymore — it was their choice, it was what they determined to do."[^3] The defense was discharged by the people who actually made the covenant.
4. **The covenant boundary.** "It's not my burden to bear" led her back to the questions that *are* hers: "Can I accept the church as it is today? Can I make and keep my own sacred covenants today? I don't have to make and keep their sacred covenants... but can I keep mine?"[^4]
5. **No whitewash required.** Crucially, this is paired with refusing to "remain in the realm of speculation and rumor" — Nash insists on "getting the bare facts," holding the genuine negatives alongside the positives.[^5] The freedom comes from a truthful view, not a rosy one (compare [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]]).

## Best Quotes
> "It's not my burden to bear, and led me to... can I accept the church as it is today? Can I make and keep my own sacred covenants today? I don't have to make and keep their sacred covenants, but can I keep mine?"[^4]

> "Looking back at their words and seeing how they defended their own choices helped me feel free to not defend them anymore — like it was their choice."[^3]

> "It was freeing to no longer feel responsible for them or [need to] protect them from their reality."[^4]
