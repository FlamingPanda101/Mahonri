# Debt Slaves Were Not Paid Wages

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Joshua HS asserted that Old Testament debt slaves "were even paid" earnings, to the point that a slave could enrich himself and buy his way out — framing slavery as a paid economic arrangement.[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **Flat textual denial (rated: strong; primary text).** "There is no part of the Bible that suggests that debt slaves were paid."[^2]

2. **The release-gift is not wages (rated: strong; primary text).** The closest thing is [[Deuteronomy 15.12-15|Deuteronomy 15]]'s instruction to furnish a newly freed debt slave with resources so he isn't immediately destitute — "but that is not payment for their service." McClellan notes this provision even "contradicts what we read elsewhere."[^2]

3. **The self-redemption passage is misapplied (rated: strong; primary text).** The Leviticus text Joshua leaned on is about an Israelite enslaved to a foreigner being redeemed by family or by his own prospering — not about earning wages from the master.[^3]

4. **The logic is incoherent (rated: strong; argued).** "Why would you enslave someone so that you can earn back the debt that you are owed and then pay them that money anyway? That's not how any of this worked."[^4]

5. **Steelman.** A defender could argue the release-gift functioned economically like deferred compensation. McClellan's reply: a one-time exit grant given precisely *because* the freed person owns nothing is the opposite of wages earned during service.[^2]

## Best Quotes

> "There is no part of the Bible that suggests that debt slaves were paid. The closest you get is Deuteronomy's insistence... But that is not payment for their service."[^2]

> "So again, absolutely nothing indicated they were paid."[^3]
