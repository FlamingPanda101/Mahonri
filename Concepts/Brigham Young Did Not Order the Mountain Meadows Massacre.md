# Brigham Young Did Not Order the Mountain Meadows Massacre

## The Point
The accusation is that [[Brigham Young]] ordered the 1857 Mountain Meadows Massacre, in which a militia of southern Utah Latter-day Saints killed the emigrants of the Fancher party — "because nothing in the territory would have happened without his decree." Dr. [[Daniel C. Peterson]] rebuts both the specific claim and the premise it rests on.

## Evidence
- **The "nothing happened without his decree" premise is false.** Peterson directly denies it ("Oh, it did") — the whole autocrat assumption collapses on the physical facts of frontier Utah (see [[Brigham Young Was Not the Autocrat of Utah]]). A huge territory with horseback as the fastest communication made central micromanagement impossible.[^1]
- **His actual order was to leave the train alone — and it arrived too late.** "The classic case is when he sends orders down to Cedar City to leave the Fancher train alone... don't do anything to them, and it gets there too late." Even though the express rider "goes as fast as he possibly can," he "can't get there in time."[^2] Brigham's documented instruction was the *opposite* of an order to kill.
- **The "evil but not stupid" argument.** Peterson's signature move: "Let's assume for a moment that Brigham Young really is evil. Nobody's ever accused Brigham Young of being stupid. And the Mountain Meadows Massacre was stupid. It came at the worst possible time. It was a disaster and has been ever since. Brigham would not have authorized that."[^3] The massacre was strategically catastrophic for the Church — exactly what a shrewd leader would never order.
- **He was horrified by it.** Far from authorizing the atrocity, "he was horrified by it."[^1]

## Best Quotes
> "Now so this idea that I've heard people say, well, the Mountain Meadows Massacre must have been ordered by Brigham Young because nothing in the territory would have happened without his decree. Oh, it did — and the massacre did too. He was horrified by it." [^1]

> "The classic case is when he sends orders down to Cedar City to leave the Fancher train alone... and it gets there too late. Even though the man on horseback goes as fast as he possibly can, can't get there in time." [^2]

> "Let's assume for a moment that Brigham Young really is evil. Nobody's ever accused Brigham Young of being stupid. And the Mountain Meadows Massacre was stupid." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This protects Brigham Young from the gravest charge leveled against him while modeling careful historical reasoning: distinguishing what a leader *ordered* from what subordinates did, and weighing motive against strategic plausibility. Peterson notes the channel intends a full dedicated episode on Mountain Meadows; here the point is narrow — the surviving documentary trail shows Brigham trying to *prevent* the massacre, and the "he controlled everything" premise is independently false.
