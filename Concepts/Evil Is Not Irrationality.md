# Evil Is Not Irrationality

## The Point

The modern habit of labelling mass murderers and tyrannical regimes "mad" or
"irrational" is a category error. Evil is not a malfunction of reason; it is a
moral failure. Calling it "madness" is wishful thinking born of the false
assumption that reason and goodness go together.

## Argument

1. For almost three centuries the dominant view has been that "evil doesn't make
   sense" — that it is irrational — which is why dictators are routinely called
   "madmen" and their regimes products of "madmen" [^1].
2. Examples of this reflex: Stalin called irrational, Pol Pot a madman, Mao's
   Cultural Revolution (50–75 million killed in peacetime) called "madness," the
   Iranian regime's threats against Israel dismissed as irrational [^1].
3. But this rests on the false association of reason with good. If reason is
   morally neutral (see [[Reason Is Morally Neutral Like a Tool]]), then evil
   regimes can pursue their ends quite rationally [^2].
4. Therefore the correct label is not "irrational" or "mad" but "evil": "the
   next time you read of some terrible crime or some terrible regime, please
   don't dismiss it as irrational or mad. Call it for what it is. Evil." [^3].
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) One might respond that some atrocities *do*
   involve genuine delusion or psychopathology, and that "irrational" can mean
   "contrary to humane values" rather than "logically incoherent." Prager's
   point still stands against the stronger claim that evil is *necessarily*
   irrational — much evil is coldly instrumentally rational.

## Best Quotes

> "Evil, we have been told for almost three centuries, doesn't make sense. It's
> irrational."[^1]

> "Please don't dismiss it as irrational or mad. Call it for what it is. Evil."[^3]
