# Scientism - The Metal Detector Fallacy

## The Point
**Objection:** Science's unparalleled predictive power and technological success (the argument of Alex Rosenberg and others) show that "what science reveals to us is probably all that is real," so theology and metaphysics give no genuine knowledge.[^1] **Reply:** This is a non sequitur, "about as bad" as arguing that because metal detectors find coins better than any other method, coins are probably all that exists.[^2] A method tuned to one slice of reality — for physics, the aspects susceptible of mathematical modeling — tells us nothing about whether *other* aspects exist; and the master-claim of scientism is itself a *philosophical* claim that no experiment could establish, so it is self-refuting.[^3]

## Evidence
- A method's success at its proper task never proves there is nothing it is blind to. Metal detectors might succeed "on every single occasion" and still not make it even probable that the world contains nothing but metal; physics is exactly parallel.[^2]
- Rosenberg's charge that critics inconsistently trust their cardiologist but reject scientism begs the question — it assumes science detects the *whole* of reality, "which is precisely what is at issue."[^4]
- The demand that metaphysics list its "predictive successes" is like demanding the metal-detecting successes of gardening or cooking, then discarding spades and spatulas. That a tool is useful for prediction shows only that the world has predictable aspects, not that "those aspects exhaust nature."[^5]
- The fallback — "science tells us everything *worth* knowing" — is "like avoiding classes you know you won't do well in and then appealing to your high grade point average as evidence of your superior intelligence," and the claim that only science-answerable questions matter is itself an unscientific, philosophical claim.[^6]
- The retreat to "naturalized philosophy or mere conceptual analysis" is a version of **Hume's Fork** (all knowledge is either relations of ideas or matters of fact), which is "notoriously self-refuting" because it is neither.[^7] Logic, mathematics, and the demonstrations of natural theology all "fall between the tines of Hume's Fork" — necessary and objective, yet neither empirical science nor mere conceptual analysis.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Metal detectors have had far greater success in finding coins and other metallic objects in more places than any other method has. Therefore, what metal detectors reveal to us ... is probably all that is real." [^2]

> "That a method is especially useful for certain purposes simply does not entail that there are no other purposes worth pursuing nor other methods more suitable to those other purposes." [^5]

> "The advocate of scientism already has oceans of knowledge for which he cannot account ... and thus has no business questioning the epistemological credentials of natural theology. He is like a thief caught red-handed with the loot, who demands that the police who have apprehended him produce the pink slip for their cruiser." [^9]

## LDS Connection
Feser's reply is a powerful ally against the secularist who treats laboratory science as the only road to truth. The Restoration is emphatically anti-scientism: it commands the Saints to "seek learning, even by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118) and teaches that truth is known "by the power of the Holy Ghost" through multiple convergent channels, not one method (Moroni 10:5). So Feser's exposure of a worldview that cannot satisfy its own standard lands cleanly for a Latter-day Saint, and parallels the Turek-sourced [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting]] and [[Philosophy Is the Foundation of Science]]. One classical-theist assumption to flag fairly: Feser grounds the reply in the **principle of sufficient reason** that *everything* has an explanation (see [[The Principle of Sufficient Reason]]), and he treats the necessary being's existence as strictly demonstrable. LDS thought welcomes intelligibility and a rational order, but it locates ultimate reality in eternal, uncreated intelligences and matter rather than a purely actual simple being — so the agreement is firmest on epistemology (truth is bigger than science) and the divergence appears only later, over the *nature* of the first principle the argument reaches.
