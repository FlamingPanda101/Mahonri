# Social and Subordinate Trinitarianism Is the Early Church View

## The Claim
The guest self-identifies as a **social Trinitarian** / **subordinate Trinitarian** and argues this is not a fringe LDS invention but (a) the model of the early church and Eastern Orthodoxy, and (b) the position of the best LDS philosophers. "I identify as a Trinitarian, as a social Trinitarian, or as a subordinate Trinitarian, which Blake Ostler and David Paulsen would also say Mormons are Trinitarian." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Definition of social Trinitarianism (the core)
Asked what it means, the guest answers: "that the three persons of the Trinity have separate centers of consciousness and are separate persons and separate beings ... and have separate entities." [^2] This is functionally the LDS Godhead — three distinct beings united in will/purpose. **Rating: an accurate statement of social Trinitarianism**, which does hold distinct centers of consciousness; whether "separate beings" is compatible with *any* Trinitarianism is the contested point.

### 2. The "you already believe the Godhead" exchange (the tension)
The co-host presses the obvious objection: "That sounds like you believe in the Mormon Godhead, but you don't want to be kicked out of the Trinitarian club. So you just say, 'I'm a social Trinitarian.'" [^3] And: "isn't social Trinitarianism inherently not the Godhead? ... isn't it our heresy?" [^4] The guest's reply is to point to **Paulsen and Ostler** and to scold the term "Godhead" as unsophisticated: "When someone says Godhead, there's a lack of nuance ... it often times shows they haven't done the homework." [^5] **Rating: this is the weakest exchange** — the guest does not actually resolve whether "social Trinitarian" and "LDS Godhead" differ; he reframes rather than answers.

### 3. The authority appeal
"Before you say this is some fringe belief, the best two philosophers we've had in the last 80 years are social Trinitarians in Latter-Dayism" — i.e. **Blake Ostler** and **David Paulsen** — "or, you know, Eastern Orthodox." [^6] **Rating: appeal to authority**; Ostler and Paulsen are indeed serious LDS philosophers who frame the Godhead in social-Trinitarian terms, but Eastern Orthodoxy is *not* social Trinitarian in the LDS sense (it affirms one divine essence).

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
Critics (and even some sympathetic to the co-host's worry) argue that "social Trinitarian with separate beings" simply *is* tritheism dressed in Trinitarian vocabulary. Classical Trinitarianism insists on one *being* (ousia) in three persons (hypostases); positing "separate beings" abandons the numerical unity that defines the doctrine. On this view the guest is doing exactly what the co-host accuses — relabeling the Godhead to stay in the "Trinitarian club." Conversely, a hardline LDS apologist would say the guest concedes too much by *wanting* the Trinitarian label at all, since the restoration's whole point is that the Godhead is *not* the Trinity. The genuine scholarly debate (Ostler, Paulsen vs. classical theists) is over whether perichoretic unity of will can do the work creeds assign to unity of essence.
