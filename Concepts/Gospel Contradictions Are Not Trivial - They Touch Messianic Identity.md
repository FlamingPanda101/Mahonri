#type/claim

# Gospel Contradictions Are Not Trivial - They Touch Messianic Identity

## The Claim (skeptical)
When [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] presses the flat question — one nativity has the holy family flee to Egypt, another has them present Jesus at the Jerusalem Temple, "Are they both correct or is one of them wrong?" — [[Dinesh D'Souza]] dismisses it: "Who the heck cares?" He calls the worry "not only trivial, but [an indication of] a kind of unwillingness to try to get what the text is trying to communicate."[^1]

O'Connor's reply distinguishes two versions of the objection and rescues the serious one. The *bare* discrepancy (which town first?) might look pedantic. But the discrepancy matters because of *why* it exists: if an evangelist invented the flight to Egypt to fulfill [[Hosea 11.1|Hosea 11:1]], then the contradiction is a symptom of [[Prophecy-Driven Invention Undermines Gospel Trustworthiness|theology overwriting history]] — and that bears directly on whether Jesus can be shown to be the Jewish Messiah. "I do [care], and so do probably people who are listening to this debate." Even D'Souza, pressed, concedes: "The point you're now raising is not trivial."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The triviality charge is a dodge, not an answer.** "Who cares?" does not adjudicate *whether* one account is false; it changes the subject from truth to edification. O'Connor's rejoinder — that he, and the audience, do care — exposes that the dismissal presupposes the very metaphorical reading under dispute (see [[Christian Metaphorical Reading Is Unfalsifiable Retreat]]).[^1]
2. **Stakes ride on the contradiction.** The discrepancy is not decorative: it is the visible seam where a fulfillment-narrative ([[Matthew 2.13-15|Matthew's]] Egypt episode) collides with a non-fulfillment narrative ([[Luke 2.39|Luke's]] peaceful return). If the seam exists *because* one author bent events to a prophecy, the contradiction is evidence about the **reliability and messianic argument** of the gospels — the opposite of trivial.[^2]
3. **The opponent concedes the point.** D'Souza's own retreat from "trivial" to "not trivial" once the prophecy-motive is made explicit is the cleanest confirmation: the worry was never pedantic; it was about whether the text "twist[s] history" to make a theological case.[^2]
4. **"Not impossible to harmonize" is the wrong bar.** A harmonization that merely shows the accounts *could* be squared does not show they *are* both historical (cf. [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]] and [[Luke Could Have Omitted the Flight to Egypt]]). Dismissing the question as trivial skips this evidentiary step entirely.

## Apologetic Engagement
The apologist can grant O'Connor's *meta*-point — contradictions touching messianic identity are not trivial — while resisting his conclusion:

- **Distinguish "discrepancy" from "contradiction."** Classic harmonizations (the family stays in Bethlehem up to ~2 years, flees to Egypt, *then* settles in Nazareth) attempt to order the events rather than dismiss them. The honest apologetic move is not "who cares?" but "here is a sequence in which both stand" — though, as O'Connor and [[Matthews Nativity Contradicts Lukes Nativity|the critical case]] insist, such harmonizations must clear the [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction|"not merely possible"]] bar, and [[Luke 2.39|Luke 2:39]]'s "they returned to Nazareth" resists an inserted multi-year exile.

- **Inerrancy is not the only orthodoxy.** A Latter-day Saint (and many non-fundamentalist Christians) can simply *grant* that two human authors recorded the infancy differently — [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context|"God's word in human words"]] — without conceding that Jesus is therefore not the Messiah. The messianic claim rests on the resurrection and the whole arc of fulfillment, not on the infancy harmonization. So even a *real* contradiction here is not the faith-defeater O'Connor's framing implies; it defeats *strict inerrancy*, a narrower target.

- **D'Souza's instinct, charitably restated.** What D'Souza gropes toward with "what the text is trying to communicate" is a legitimate point about genre and authorial intent — but he deploys it as a *conversation-stopper* rather than an argument, which is why O'Connor wins the exchange. The apologetic version must *show* the communicative purpose, not invoke it to wave away the historical question. Where the purpose is genuinely typological (the Egypt/Exodus pattern), the contradiction-charge softens into an interpretation-charge; where it is plainly mundane history, the apologist owes a harmonization that meets the evidentiary bar.
