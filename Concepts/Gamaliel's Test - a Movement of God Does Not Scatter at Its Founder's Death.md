# Gamaliel's Test - a Movement of God Does Not Scatter at Its Founder's Death

## The Claim

[[Joe Heschmeyer]]'s positive argument from [[Acts 5.34-39|Acts 5]]. The Pharisee Gamaliel (Paul's teacher) lays down a diagnostic before the Sanhedrin: movements of merely human origin disperse once their leader is killed — he names **Theudas** (~400 followers, "slain... and all who followed him were dispersed and came to nothing") and **Judas the Galilean** ("he also perished, and all who followed him were scattered") — "but if it is of God, you will not be able to overthrow them." Heschmeyer applies the test to the question of apostasy: the decisive moment for the LDS thesis is the **death of the apostles**, supposedly the trigger for the church's collapse. Yet the apostolic church *did not scatter* — "you don't have the 12 apostles forming their separate churches." By contrast, the founders Hansen's framework would parallel *did* fracture immediately upon death: **Joseph Smith's death produced the 1844 succession crisis**, and Muhammad's produced "a Sunni-Shia split." So by Gamaliel's own criterion, the early church bears the mark of being *of God* — which is the opposite of a movement abandoned to apostasy. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The test is scriptural, not invented (rating: strong; it is the text's own logic)
The non-scattering-at-death criterion is stated *in Acts 5* by a sympathetic outsider, so Heschmeyer is not imposing an external standard but applying one the LDS side also accepts as canonical. [^1]

### 2. The historical contrast is real and pointed (rating: moderate; the comparison)
The apostolic generation did *not* split into twelve rival apostolic churches at the apostles' deaths, whereas the 1844 Latter-day Saint succession crisis (Brigham Young vs. Sidney Rigdon vs. James Strang et al.) and the Sunni-Shia division are textbook founder's-death fractures. The asymmetry is the argument's force. [^1]

### 3. It pairs with the "by their fruits" frame (rating: moderate; turns Hansen's own test)
Hansen closes by urging "by their fruits ye shall know them." Heschmeyer's Gamaliel argument is the same shape pointed the other way: the *fruit* of the early church was not dispersal but explosive growth ([[Rodney Stark]]'s ~33 million by 350; Bart Ehrman's *Triumph of Christianity*), the signature of a movement God did not overthrow. See [[Daniel 2's Stone Kingdom Dates Indefectibility to the Roman Empire]]. [^2]

### 4. It feeds the "remarkable omission" point (rating: moderate; cumulative)
If the church *had* collapsed shortly after the apostles, that cataclysm would be both prophesied and *noticed*; instead there is silence and continuity, consistent with a movement that endured. See [[No Old-Covenant-Style Prophecy Foretells the Restoration]]. [^2]

### 5. Steelman of the LDS reply ([[Jacob Hansen]])
Hansen has two lines (developed in the debate and the vault). **(a) Wrong unit of analysis.** The apostasy is *not* the body of believers scattering — Hansen grants the body of believers and the Spirit persisted — but the loss of *institutional authority/keys*; so "they didn't disperse" answers a claim he is not making. Heschmeyer is comparing the survival of *Christianity broadly* with the LDS claim about *priesthood authority*. **(b) The succession crisis was resolved, not fatal.** Latter-day Saints argue the 1844 crisis was settled precisely *through* the divinely-instituted mechanism of the Quorum of the Twelve holding the keys — so it demonstrates an *orderly* transfer, not a Theudas-style collapse. See [[Succession Resolved Through the Keys of the Twelve]]. And God may withhold prophets when people *kill* them (Hansen's Matthew 23:37 argument), so the apostles' deaths at the hands of persecutors fit a pattern of justified withdrawal, not failure.

### Steelman of Heschmeyer's counter to those replies
On (a): Gamaliel's test is about whether the *movement Jesus founded* can be "overthrown," and the LDS claim is precisely that its essential institutional life *was* overthrown for 1,700 years — so survival-of-the-broad-movement is on point, not beside it. On (b): invoking the Twelve-with-keys to explain why the *Mormon* succession held only highlights that the *apostolic* church had an even stronger continuity and needed no such rescue; and "God withholds prophets when they're killed" proves too much, since prophets and apostles were martyred *throughout* salvation history (Heschmeyer: "there was no 400 years of silence" — Anna the prophetess, John the Baptist) without authority being withdrawn.

## Best Quotes

> "If on the death of Jesus and the apostles we scatter, then it wasn't of God. When Joseph Smith dies, there's a succession crisis in 1844. When Muhammad dies, there's a Sunni-Shia split... You don't have the 12 apostles forming their separate churches." — Heschmeyer [^1]

> "For if this plan or this undertaking is of men, it will fail. But if it is of God, you will not be able to overthrow them." — Gamaliel, quoted by Heschmeyer ([[Acts 5.34-39|Acts 5:38-39]]) [^1]

> "That doesn't look like a movement that's failed." — Heschmeyer, on the early church's growth [^2]
