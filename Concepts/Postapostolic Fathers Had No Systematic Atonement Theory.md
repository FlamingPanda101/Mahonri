# Postapostolic Fathers Had No Systematic Atonement Theory

## The Point
Cecilia M. Peek states plainly that the earliest Christian writers after the Apostles "had no recognized Atonement theories, no established definitions of grace and salvation, and no fully developed vocabulary or analogies to explain what grace and salvation were or how they worked." [^1] The thinkers "to whom we look for so much of the thought and language we now depend on to make sense of the Atonement" were themselves "just beginning to develop an understanding," relying on Old Testament ideas and heavily on Paul's letters. The most sustained engagement waited until Augustine (354-430). [^1] This is a frank patristic admission of **doctrinal development** — the precise pattern Latter-day Saints appeal to in describing an apostasy and the gradual, contested clarification of Christian belief.

## Evidence
1. **No systematic soteriology in the first generation.** Peek: "One cannot speak of a systematic investigation of grace and salvation in the earliest generation of postapostolic writers." [^1]
2. **1 Clement (c. AD 95-96) ties grace to repentance, not a theory.** Pseudonymously attributed to [[Clement of Rome]], it speaks of "the grace of repentance" won by Christ's blood and the "yoke of his grace." Clement's emphasis on repentance as the necessary *response* to grace "allows for the possibility that such a debate" (grace vs. works) "was already happening in nascent Christianity." [^2]
3. **Justin Martyr (mid-2nd c.) reads grace backward into the Old Testament.** Using *charizetai* (same root as *charis*), Justin says Christ "bestowed favor on Abraham" by calling him to leave his land — a figure for sin — so Christians too may "inherit the Holy Land" of eternal life. [^3]
4. **The four atonement "models" are later scholarly classifications**, not first-century doctrines: Christ the Illuminator, Restorer, Victor, and Victim — each grown from the careful reading of scripture, and ancient authors freely **bridge more than one**. [^4] See [[Four Ancient Atonement Models]].

## Best Quotes
> "Ancient Christian writers had no recognized Atonement theories, no established definitions of grace and salvation, and no fully developed vocabulary or analogies to explain what grace and salvation were or how they worked." [^1]

> "Let us fix our eyes on the blood of Christ and understand how precious it is to his Father, because, being poured out for our salvation, it won for the whole world the grace of repentance." — 1 Clement 7:4 [^2]

## LDS Connection
A Latter-day Saint apologetic uses this candid scholarly picture two ways: (1) it confirms that core soteriological vocabulary was *developed* over centuries rather than handed down whole, undercutting claims of a fixed, complete early creed; and (2) it shows the grace/works tension was live from the start (1 Clement), normalizing the LDS insistence that grace *requires* a response of repentance and obedience. Compare [[Canon Began as a Rule of Faith Not a List of Books]] and [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]].
