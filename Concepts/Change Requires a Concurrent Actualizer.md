# Change Requires a Concurrent Actualizer

## The Point
Once change is understood as the actualization of a potential, Feser argues that the **principle of causality** follows by analysis, not mere generalization: "no potential can be actualized unless something already actual actualizes it." [^1] A merely potential coldness "can hardly do anything, precisely because it is merely potential. Only what is actual can do anything." [^2] So "any change requires a changer" — and crucially the changer must be **concurrent**, acting at the very moment the potential is being actualized, not merely at some earlier point in time. [^2]

## Evidence
- The principle is not an inductive guess. It "follows from what change is: the actualization of a potential." Potential coldness "cannot make itself actual. Only something already actual can do that." [^2]
- The argument is reinforced against David **Hume**: imagining a stone appearing in empty space is only conceiving of it "without at the same time conceiving of its cause," which "is completely unremarkable" — like conceiving a trilateral without thinking "triangle." It does not follow that it could exist *uncaused*. [^3]
- Elizabeth **Anscombe**'s point: to show a thing "popped into existence" rather than was "merely transported," Hume must specify causes for each scenario — so spelling out the case "ends up bringing back in the notion of a cause." [^4]
- The principle "is as well supported by experience as any claim could be"; W. Norris Clarke notes that were it false, "an elephant, or a hotel could appear suddenly on your front lawn out of nowhere," and "it should be the easiest thing in the world for them to be popping up all the time." [^5]
- Formal premises 4–5: "No potential can be actualized unless something already actual actualizes it (the principle of causality)"; "So, any change is caused by something already actual." [^1]

## Best Quotes
> "Now potential coldness can hardly do anything, precisely because it is merely potential. Only what is actual can do anything." [^2]

> "If the principle were false, 'then nothing at all would be required to produce anything at all: an elephant, or a hotel could appear suddenly on your front lawn out of nowhere.'" [^5]

## LDS Connection
This principle is **metaphysically neutral and a genuine ally**. The claim that a potential must be actualized by something already actual — and the demolition of the Humean "uncaused popping into existence" fantasy — is exactly the ground Latter-day Saint apologists stand on against materialist accounts of a self-caused universe (compare [[Causality Applies Beyond Space and Time]] and [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]]). Note that Feser's principle is *narrower* than "everything has a cause": it says only what goes from potential to actual needs a cause. That very precision is useful to the LDS apologist who must explain why an eternal God needs no cause. Nothing here conflicts with the Restoration; the concurrent-actualizer point becomes contestable only when Feser later uses it to argue the terminus must be a *purely actual* being with no potentiality at all (see [[The Purely Actual Actualizer - Aristotle's Unmoved Mover]]).
