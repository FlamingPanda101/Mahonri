# Jewish Non-Recognition of Jesus Is a Textual Problem, Not Only a Spiritual One

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] reframes a familiar question. The "standard answer" — the Jews "were looking for a conquering king and Jesus came as a servant" — is "partially true," but it "sidesteps something that deserves a direct answer."[^1] Jesus claimed ([[Luke 24.44|Luke 24:44]]) that "everything written about him in the law of Moses, the prophets, and the Psalms had to be fulfilled" — "the entire Hebrew scriptural tradition pointed to him, not a handful of ambiguous passages... all of it."[^1] So: "If the scriptures pointed clearly to Jesus, and if the most scripturally educated people in the world were immersed in those scriptures their entire lives, why didn't they see it?"[^2] Spiritual stubbornness "doesn't fully answer that. Spiritually stubborn people still read words on a page. If the words clearly pointed to Jesus, the establishment would have had to actively argue against what the text said, not simply fail to notice it."[^2] "The more historically interesting answer is that by the time Jesus arrived, the texts the establishment was reading had been through a process of editing and exclusion that removed the very tradition that he was most explicitly drawing from."[^2] This sets up the [[The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah|Deuteronomistic editing]] argument and the [[The Communities That Preserved Older Texts Recognized Jesus First|communities pattern]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The premise is Jesus' own.** [[Luke 24.44|Luke 24:44]] (and 24:27, "beginning at Moses and all the prophets") does present the *whole* Hebrew Bible as testifying of Christ, so the puzzle Arnold poses is internal to the Christian claim, not imposed. *Rating: a fair framing of the data.* [^1]
2. **The "still read the words" point is logically clean.** If a text *plainly* taught X, expert readers would have to *argue against* X, not merely miss it — so persistent, learned non-recognition is at least *consistent with* the text not plainly teaching X (in their edition). *Rating: a genuinely sharp observation; it shifts the burden interestingly.* [^2]
3. **It is offered as a *supplement*, not a replacement.** Arnold grants the spiritual/servant-vs-king answer is "partially true," seeking a fuller account rather than denying human responsibility. *Rating: appropriately measured; avoids overclaiming.* [^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **The dichotomy is false** — recognition of a Messiah is not like reading a flat propositional sentence; it requires *interpretation* of typology, poetry, and prophecy, so devout experts could read the very same (unedited) words and reasonably reach different conclusions, especially about a *crucified* Messiah (a categorical scandal, 1 Cor 1:23). (b) **The New Testament itself blames hardness of heart and veiled understanding** (2 Cor 3:14-15; John 12:39-40 citing Isaiah), i.e., a *spiritual* explanation, without positing textual deletion. (c) **Plenty recognized him from the standard scriptures** — the apostles, Paul (a Pharisee trained in the standardized canon), and the Beroeans "searched the scriptures" and believed; so the edited canon was evidently *sufficient* for recognition, undercutting the claim that the texts no longer pointed to him. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument is comparative and cumulative, not absolute — it does not deny spiritual factors or that *some* recognized Jesus, but asks why *the institutional establishment* disproportionately did not, and answers that the *marginalization* of the divine-mediator tradition made the specific figure Jesus claimed to be (a pre-existent heavenly Son of Man) harder to see for those reading only the standardized corpus; that Paul and others recognized him is consistent with the pattern that recognition correlated with exposure to the *fuller* tradition (see [[The Communities That Preserved Older Texts Recognized Jesus First]]).
