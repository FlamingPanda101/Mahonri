# Accepting Evolution with Joy Is Possible

## The Point
Evolutionary biologist [[T. Heath Ogden]] argues that evolution can be received "with joy" rather than fear, and that doing so requires no abandonment of core Latter-day Saint belief.[^1] He opens with a careful distinction: asked "Do you believe in evolution?" he answers "No — I *accept* evolution based on the body of evidence that supports it," reserving "believe" for things like God or a spouse's love. No one asks "do you believe in gravity?"; like gravity and plate tectonics, evolution simply *happens* and is well understood.[^2] His thesis: "Accepting evolution does not require one to renounce core Latter-day Saint beliefs; reconciliation is possible," and "we do not help ourselves or others by hiding from evolutionary knowledge."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **Truth from every source.** Ogden grounds joyful acceptance in the LDS commitment to truth "no matter where it originates" (9th Article of Faith; John Taylor; the inscription on [[James E. Talmage]]'s headstone: "Within the gospel of Jesus Christ there is room and place for every truth thus far learned by man, or yet to be made known").[^4]
2. **The Church has no official position.** Ogden surveys the BYU "Evolution and the Origin of Man" packet and shows a *transition* from the marginally anti-evolution 1909 statement to neutral language by 1925/1931. The New Era plainly states "the Church has no official position on the theory of evolution." The scriptures "tell *why* man was created, but they do not tell *how*."[^5]
3. **Knowledge can come through a fossil.** Ogden refuses to "place a restriction on God regarding the manner by which he reveals" — God "may do so through a prophet, but he may also certainly accomplish it through a fossil, a study of comparative morphology... or an evolutionary analysis of DNA."[^6]
4. **Conflict is usually a *human* problem.** Quoting chemist [[Henry Eyring]]: "There is no conflict in the mind of God, but often there is conflict in the minds of men." Eyring's counsel to a troubled student: "In this Church, you only have to believe the truth. Find out what the truth is!"[^7]
5. **Understanding *reduces* perceived conflict.** Ogden cites his own education research: an increase in students' knowledge of evolutionary theory leads to a *decrease* in their perceived conflict between religion and science.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "I do not believe in evolution. ... I accept evolution based on the body of evidence that supports it." [^2]

> "Accepting evolution does not require one to renounce core Latter-day Saint beliefs; reconciliation is possible. We do not help ourselves or others by hiding from evolutionary knowledge." [^3]

> "There are few ways in which good people do more harm to those who take them seriously than to defend the gospel with arguments that won't hold water." (Henry Eyring) [^9]

## LDS Connection
Ogden models the affective shift the whole reconciliation literature aims for: from *fear* to *joy*. His believe/accept distinction protects faith — "believe" stays anchored to God and revelation, while "accept" tracks evidence — so the two never compete for the same ground. Crucially, he leans on the Church's actual neutrality ("no official position"; scriptures say *why*, not *how*) to show that accepting human evolution does not contradict any binding doctrine. The First Presidency's own 1910 words frame the posture: "Our religion is not hostile to real science. That which is demonstrated, we accept with joy." And D&C 88:78–79 commissions the Saint to "learn... of things both in heaven and in the earth."
