# Augustine Recast the Millennium as the Present Age of the Church

## The Point

[[Augustine]] (AD 354-430) produced the eschatological settlement that dominated Western Christianity for the next thousand years: **amillennialism**. In *The City of God* he "convincingly argued that the battle between Jesus and Satan lay not in the future but in the past" — it had been fought on a spiritual plane, and "Jesus had already triumphed." [^1] The thousand years of [[Revelation 20.2-3|Revelation 20]] did not describe a future earthly reign but "referred to the period between Jesus's first and second comings and represented the period of the Church." [^2] With this, [[Nicholas J. Frederick]] writes, "the 'already' finally outlast[ed] the 'not yet.'" [^2]

## Evidence

1. **History was no longer climactic.** For Augustine "the earth's history was not in any way moving toward a climactic moment where Jesus would victoriously appear and vanquish the wicked." The "city of God" and "city of the world" would both continue; Satan would eventually be stripped of power, but not through a dramatic Parousia. [^1]
2. **It was a coping mechanism for a collapsing world.** Augustine's reworking "allowed Christians to come to terms with a world that was crumbling around them." Alaric I sacked Rome in AD 410, but "by locating the true work of God in heaven, Christians found a way to cope... they had survived Roman persecution and now they had outlived Rome." [^1]
3. **Christ still returns — but briefly.** Augustine taught Jesus will physically return "at some future point," but his reign "will not last for one thousand years" and "will be short because the kingdom of God is already present on the earth in the form of the Church." The Second Coming inaugurates judgment and the new heaven and new earth. [^2]
4. **He inherited the method from Alexandria.** Augustine "was heavily influenced by Clement of Alexandria and Origen and adopted their allegorical methodology" (see [[Clement and Origen Allegorized Away the Physical Second Coming]]). [^2] Frederick stresses Augustine "did not invent" amillennialism — "strains of it already appear" before him, and Charles Hill argues a "non-chiliastic eschatology was present in the Church to rival chiliasm from beginning to end." [^3]
5. **It became the medieval default.** "Thanks in large part to Augustine, the next thousand years of Christian history would find the Christian church largely leaning in an amillennial direction" — the predominant Endtime view "in both the Roman Catholic and Orthodox branches of Christianity throughout the Middle Ages." [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Augustine's monumental work, The City of God, convincingly argued that the battle between Jesus and Satan lay not in the future but in the past. It had been fought on a spiritual plane, and Jesus had already triumphed." [^1]

> "The urgency for Jesus's Second Coming and the premillennial hope of the first- and second-century church was largely abandoned by the time the fourth century ended, the 'already' finally outlasting the 'not yet.'" [^2]

## LDS Connection

Frederick's framing implicitly contrasts the Augustinian/amillennial settlement with the restored Latter-day Saint emphasis on a literal, physical, premillennial return — aligning modern LDS eschatology with the *earlier* (Papias, Justin, Irenaeus) literalist stream that Augustine displaced rather than with the medieval mainstream. See [[Early Christian Premillennialism Was the Earliest Millennial View]]. [^4]
