# Origen on the Preexistence and Fall of Souls

## The Point
[[Origen]] of Alexandria (c. 185–254) was "one of the first Christians to argue for, and develop, a systematic theology of the preexistence of souls" in *On First Principles*. Unlike [[Irenaeus of Lyons]], who grounded human nature in Genesis, Origen located it in souls created by God before time, who *fell* by misusing free will — a "cooling" away from the divine fire. He built this from a careful reading of scripture (Jeremiah 1:5; Romans 9; Luke 1) combined with [[Plato]]'s philosophy, chiefly to vindicate God's justice in judging people before birth. His scheme was later condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople (553).

## Evidence
1. Origen's driving problem was divine justice: how can God love Jacob and hate Esau, or fill John the Baptist with the Spirit, while still in the womb "before they had time to act"? Without prior existence, "God fills some with the Holy Spirit neither by judgement nor according to merits."[^1]
2. He found scriptural footing in Jeremiah 1:5 ("Before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee") — though he is the only Father the author has found reading it as premortal existence rather than foreknowledge.[^2]
3. Souls are rational beings with "free will and volition," "according to their proper nature … bodiless," yet — crucially — **created by God**, not eternal beings of God's own nature. "Uncreated" and "eternal" were terms "reserved for the Godhead: the Father, Christ, and the Holy Spirit."[^3]
4. The "fall" happens outside Eden: all souls except Christ's "cool" away from the divine fire by their choices, producing a hierarchy of souls (the devil being "coldest"). Yet a cooled soul "has not lost the power of restoring itself."[^4]
5. Christ's soul uniquely "chose to love righteousness … unchangeably," with no "possibility of sin."[^5]
6. Origen read Genesis as *two* creations: souls (Genesis 1:26–27, "invisible, incorporeal, incorruptible") then the physical body (Genesis 2:7).[^6]
7. Mortality is a chance for fallen souls to cultivate "seeds" of goodness and be "renewed according to the image and likeness of God," returning to union with God (he called this final restoration *apokatastasis*).[^7]
8. He drew on [[Plato]]: the soul's goal is "to become as far as possible like God" — though Origen insists Genesis 1:26 taught this before Plato.[^8]

## Best Quotes
> Without prior existence, "it would seem that God fills some with the Holy Spirit neither by judgement nor according to merits, and sanctifies [them] undeservedly." [^1]

> "Uncreated" and "eternal" were terms reserved for the Godhead: the Father, Christ, and the Holy Spirit. [^3]

## LDS Connection
LDS readers "will resonate with some aspects" — God created premortal intelligences who had agency, and that agency had mortal implications. But the LDS premortal doctrine comes from Restoration scripture unavailable to Origen (Moses 3:5; Abraham 3:22–26; D&C 93:29; 138:53–56), whereas Origen's came from Bible + Platonism. Key divergences: Origen treats the body as a corrupting "tomb" to be discarded (only the soul is resurrected), which LDS theology rejects; and his idea that mortal inequalities reflect *premortal sin* differs from the LDS view.
