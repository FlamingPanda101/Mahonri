# Yahweh Is the Father Who Gave the Son the Divine Name by Adoption

## The Claim
Against the proposal that "Yahweh is multi-personal," [[Blake Ostler]] argues Yahweh is **a single person — the Father** — who has honored the Son by giving him the exalted name Yahweh as his own, through ancient adoption. Jesus' "I am" and divine-name claims are the speech of the name-bearing **agent** of the Father, not evidence that Yahweh is three persons or that the Son *is* the Father. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Adoption conferred name, inheritance, and authority (rating: strong; ANE)
In the ancient world a benefactor would honor a beloved person by *adopting* him into his family, which meant the adoptee "inherited everything." Christ "inherits from the father a dozen times in the New Testament" — and "how do you inherit if you're the very same one-and-the-same person being?" [^1]

> "Yahweh is a single person, the father who's honored his son by giving him the exalted name Yahweh as his own. So this functions in an ancient context of adoption." [^1]

### 2. Philippians 2 = sharing the title as divine gift (rating: strong; textual)
Ostler grounds the name-transfer in [[Philippians 2.9-11|Philippians 2]]: the Father "highly exalted him and gave him a name above every name." This is "very clearly sharing a title as part of a cultural understanding of what is entailed in divine adoption." [^2]

### 3. John identifies the OT Yahweh-in-vision as the Son (rating: moderate; textual)
John reads [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]] (Isaiah's throne vision of Yahweh) as a vision of **Christ** (John 12:41). For Ostler this shows the Son is "the one that interacts and is the God of the Old Testament who actually interacts" — as the Father's commissioned agent. "The father has made him his agent." [^3]

### 4. "I am" in John 8 is spoken by the name-bearer (rating: moderate; exegetical)
In the very pericope of [[John 8.58|John 8:58]], Jesus uses the divine name "as one that he has because he's one with the father." He is "the agent of Yahweh who had his name," not claiming to be the Father. [^4]

### 5. Even Trinitarians deny Jesus is the Father (rating: strong; ad hominem-to-position)
Ostler exposes the "illicit move": "Yahweh is the Father; Jesus is Yahweh; therefore Jesus is the Father" — a conclusion "not even a traditional Christian could accept," since Trinitarians hold the persons are distinct. See [[The Father and Son Distinct - Even Trinitarians Deny Jesus Is the Father]]. [^5]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian rejoinder is that the New Testament does more than transfer a *name*: it applies *exclusive-worship* texts (Phil. 2 quotes Isa. 45:23) and creator-prerogatives to Jesus, which on Richard Bauckham's "divine identity" reading place Jesus *within* the one divine identity rather than alongside it as an exalted agent — and an agent does not receive worship (cf. the rebuking angel). Ostler answers that worship of the Son is licensed not by ontology but by the Father's exaltation and the persons' mutual indwelling. See [[Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling]].

## Best Quotes

> "Yahweh is the father, and Yahweh gives the name and honor and exaltation to the son. This is in Philippians 2." [^2]

> "when Christ is using the divine name for himself, he's not claiming to be the father... 'Oh well, Yahweh is the father. Jesus is Yahweh. Therefore, Jesus is the father.' But not even a traditional Christian could accept that." [^5]

> "I am the agent of Yahweh who had his name... but I'm actually the being that you're talking of. I was the one that was seen in vision there. I'm the one through whom the creation was done." [^4]
