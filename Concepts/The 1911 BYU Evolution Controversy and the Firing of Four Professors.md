# The 1911 BYU Evolution Controversy and the Firing of Four Professors

#type/claim

## The Claim

In 1911 — before BYU was even accredited — the Church removed four professors ([[Ralph Chamberlain]], [[William Chamberlain]], [[Joseph Peterson]], and [[Henry Peterson]]) for teaching organic ([[Charles Darwin|Darwinian]]) evolution and arguing it could be reconciled with the LDS gospel. Historian [[Benjamin Park]] presents this not as a simple "science vs. religion" clash but as a **personnel and authority crisis** born of the post-polygamy assimilation era: a generation of elite-credentialed LDS academics ran up against church leaders who feared assimilation had gone too far and drew a red line where evolution was perceived to undercut scriptural truths about the Fall, the Atonement, and the Resurrection. The episode is the **first** instance of BYU administrators moving to root out secular learning deemed threatening to doctrine, and Park argues it rhymes directly with recent Provo disputes. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The trigger was perceived assimilation overreach, not science as such.** The crisis came amid the Reed Smoot-era drive to Americanize the Church. LDS academics earned PhDs at top universities and brought that learning to Provo; President [[George Brimhall]] recruited the four professors and gave them freedom to teach. President [[Joseph F. Smith]] simultaneously drove Americanization *and* defended orthodoxy, fearing Darwinian evolution undercut foundational scripture. Park flags the irony that most 19th-century Christian teachers accepted Darwin; the Mormon backlash belonged to early-20th-century fundamentalist retrenchment. [^2] [^3]

2. **The 1909 statement did not settle it; institutional retrenchment escalated.** A 1908 Church Education policy barred non-member sources in Bible instruction. The watered-down 1909 "Origin of Man" statement (see [[The 1909 Origin of Man Read in Its Historical Context]]) left room for "evolutionary experimentation" so long as it did not cut into core doctrine — but stake presidents kept complaining BYU was "going haywire." [^4]

3. **The Cummings report (Jan 21, 1911) framed evolution as a threat to scripture.** Superintendent [[Horace Cummings]]' investigation found teachers treating "the theory of evolution... as a demonstrated law," its application to "the fall, the atonement, and the resurrection" the "most important and damaging to the faith," and the Bible "treated as a collection of myths, folklore, dramas." Most alarming to leaders: students were "wholesale" embracing evolution and doubting the Bible — the red line. [^5]

4. **A five-apostle committee issued an ultimatum.** The committee (five apostles plus Cummings and Brimhall) held full-day interrogations of the four professors, who defended evolution as reconcilable with LDS teaching, then delivered the choice: **stop teaching evolution or lose your jobs.** [^6]

5. **Student support could not stop it.** The Chamberlains published defenses in the BYU student paper; 95 of 114 students signed a petition warning the firings would be "a death blow to our college work." The *Deseret News* refused it; the *Salt Lake Tribune* ran it; journalists called it a crisis for higher education. The Church did not back down. **[[Henry Peterson]]** was fired first; **[[Joseph Peterson]]** and **[[Ralph Chamberlain]]** resigned; **[[William Chamberlain]]** was spared the ultimatum but soon left. [^7]

6. **The Church quietly signaled overreach — and the dissidents' view eventually prevailed.** Within a year or two [[James Talmage]], and later [[John Widtsoe]], were called to the Quorum of the Twelve, partly to mediate the bad press. A century on, BYU runs a world-leading evolutionary-biology program (see [[Evolutionary Biology at BYU Is an Academic Success Story]]), and BYU-educated Latter-day Saints accept evolution at higher rates than white evangelicals — so, Park concludes, "the Chamberlains and the Petersons won." [^8] [^9]

## Best Quotes

> "They eventually discovered that four professors were unapologetically teaching organic evolution and arguing that it could be reconciled with the LDS gospel. It resulted in their firings and resignations." [^1]

> "The theory of evolution is treated as a demonstrated law, and their application of it to gospel truths gives rise to many curious and conflicting explanations and interpretations of scripture. Its relation to the fall, the atonement, and the resurrection are perhaps the most important and damaging to the faith of the students." — Horace Cummings report, 1911 [^5]

> "In the end, the Chamberlains and the Petersons won, even if of course there are plenty of LDS members, including some leaders, who continue to doubt scientific theories like Darwin's evolution." [^9]
