# The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism

## The Point
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that the Nicene Trinity — "one God in three persons, each fully God and none greater than the others, yet somehow distinguishable" — "was developed over three centuries of theological conflict using Greek philosophical terms that don't appear anywhere in the New Testament," chiefly *homoousios* ("same substance"), "the term Nicaea settled on... not anywhere in scripture."[^1] His convergence claim: the analytic philosopher of religion [[Dale Tuggy]] (SUNY Fredonia, **not LDS**) "has argued in peer-reviewed philosophy of religion that the social Trinity — meaning divine unity understood as relational rather than metaphysical — is the most defensible Trinitarian position available." And "whether he knows it or not, what he's describing as the best option is structurally what the LDS Church has taught since 1830."[^2]

## Evidence
- **The John 17 same-word argument.** Arnold's sharpest move: in John 17 Jesus prays that his disciples "may be one as we are one." If that oneness "means ontologically identical substance, then every Christian is supposed to literally merge into each other and into God. Nobody reads it that way." The plain reading is "unity of purpose, will, and love" — but then *that* is also what "one" must mean when Jesus describes his relationship to the Father. "The Trinity formulation requires you to apply two different definitions of the same word to two verses in the same prayer," because the alternative — that Jesus describes his relationship to the Father "as relational rather than metaphysical" — "is actually the LDS position."[^3] This is the vault's [[John 17 Oneness Is Unity Not Shared Being]] argument, here put in [[Dale Tuggy]]'s mouth.
- **Tuggy on the coherence problem.** Arnold reports that Tuggy "has spent [his] career demonstrating that the Nicene formulation can't be made logically coherent without first importing assumptions that then generate new contradictions."[^4] The social-Trinity alternative escapes the contradiction precisely by making oneness relational, not numerical.
- **The LDS Godhead stated.** Against the Trinity, "we believe in the Godhead instead, meaning God the Father and Jesus Christ are distinct, separate, embodied beings. The Holy Ghost is a distinct personage of spirit. And they are three beings unified in purpose, will, and love, but not three persons of one substance."[^5] Arnold's claim is that "when you describe that to a trained philosopher, the typical Christian paradox disappears."
- **The "different Jesus" objection runs backward.** Arnold grants the LDS Jesus differs from "the creedal Jesus" but presses that the New Testament Jesus "prays to the Father as a distinct person and says the Father is greater than him," "asks why God has forsaken him," "receives authority," and is "exalted" (Philippians 2; Acts 2:36, *epoiesen* = "made"). He cites [[Bart Ehrman]] and the conservative evangelical [[Richard Bauckham]] that "the diversity of early Christology, including adoptionist and exaltation views that predate Nicene theology, is the actual mainstream position in academic New Testament scholarship," so "the creedal Jesus was not recovered from the New Testament — he was constructed at councils."[^6] Tuggy "again, not LDS, has argued that the LDS description of Christ is actually more philosophically coherent and more consistent with the New Testament text."[^7]

## LDS Connection
This is one of Arnold's cleanest convergences: a credentialed non-LDS philosopher arrives, in peer-reviewed work, at a relational account of divine oneness that is "structurally what the LDS Church has taught since 1830." It directly supports the vault's case in [[Why the Trinity vs Godhead Debate Actually Matters]], [[Three Persons Share One Nature Not One Being]], and [[Ontological Equality With Relational Subordination]]: the LDS Godhead is not a deficient Trinity but the position the Nicene formula's own internal pressures push philosophers back toward. It is one strand of the larger thesis in [[Independent Philosophers Keep Reconstructing LDS Theology]].

**Steelman the critic.** (1) *Social Trinity ≠ LDS Godhead* — most social trinitarians (e.g., Tuggy's targets, and Tuggy's own unitarianism) still affirm divine *immateriality* and reject three *embodied* beings, so "structurally what the LDS Church teaches" overstates the overlap; the convergence is on relational oneness, not on corporeality or a plurality of fully divine beings. (2) *Tuggy is a biblical unitarian*, not a defender of any Trinity — describing him as endorsing "the most defensible Trinitarianism" compresses a more complicated position (he argues the social Trinity is the best Trinitarian option *if one must be a trinitarian*, while himself rejecting the Trinity). (3) The John 17 "same word, two senses" point has standard trinitarian replies (analogical predication; the *as* signals likeness, not identity of kind) that the episode does not engage.
