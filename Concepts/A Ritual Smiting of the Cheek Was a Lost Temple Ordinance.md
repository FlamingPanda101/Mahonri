# A Ritual Smiting of the Cheek Was a Lost Temple Ordinance

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] and [[Mike Day]] argue that the **smiting of the cheek** in [[Matthew 5.39|Matthew 5:39]] ("whosoever shall smite thee on thy right cheek, turn to him the other also") is not merely a moral teaching against revenge but the trace of a **ritual action** belonging to an ancient temple initiation — a rite in which the candidate was struck (the Book of Mormon suggests *struck hard, repeatedly*) before being raised to a new, enthroned status. [^1] This rite, they hold, is one of the "lost ordinances" softened to at most a symbolic "mock slap" in the modern endowment but recoverable from scripture. [^2] It is a specific, named extension of [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the broader Sermon-as-ordinance thesis]]: where that note treats the Sermon's *structure*, this note isolates one *embodied gesture* within it and gathers the cross-textual evidence that it was real, violent, and sacred.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **Two Book of Mormon passages mark cheek-smiting as a desecration of "that which was sacred."** [[Helaman 4.12|Helaman 4:12]] lists, among the apostates' sins, "smiting their humble brethren upon the cheek, making a mock of that which was sacred." Butler's pivot is that you can only *mock* something sacred — so the smiting that was mocked must itself have been a sacred act, not random violence. In [[Alma 14.14|Alma 14:14-25]] the chief judge and the elite of Ammonihah smite the bound Alma and Amulek on the cheek, returning "again and again" until — counting the episode — they have done so **seven times**, and only on the seventh do the prisoners stand (i.e., they had been struck down). Ammonihah is elsewhere a city that "once knew the mysteries" and has weaponized the liturgy ([[Alma 12]]), making a *reversed ordinance* the natural reading. *Rating: the cleanest internal evidence; the "seven" count and the "that which was sacred" gloss are real textual data, not invented.* [^3][^4]

2. **The number seven matches Egyptian initiation (via Nibley).** Butler notes that [[Hugh Nibley]], reading the Joseph Smith papyri (the Book of Breathings), describes **seven ritual blows** in Egyptian rites by which one passes from the grave to Eden / the afterlife. The seven cheek-smitings of Ammonihah then look like the dark inversion of a seven-fold initiatory beating. *Rating: a striking convergence, but Nibley is a contested authority and "the blows exist in other ancient rituals too" is asserted, not documented in the episode.* [^5]

3. **The Babylonian Akitu festival ritually slapped the king before enthronement.** Hayden Paul adds the **Akitu** New Year festival: the king, before being anointed and enthroned, was slapped hard enough to draw tears and a confession of loyalty to the god — humbled, *then* exalted. This supplies an independent Near-Eastern pattern of "beaten down before enthronement," which Butler maps onto Christ's passion and onto Nephi. *Rating: the Akitu humiliation of the king is genuinely attested in Assyriology; whether it is genetically related to an Israelite temple rite is the speculative leap.* [^6]

4. **Psalm 22 and Isaiah supply the lament half of the pattern.** [[Hayden Paul]] (writing his dissertation on the Psalms) reads **Psalm 22** — heavily quoted by the Gospel passion narratives — as the surrounded-and-beaten initiate: "I am a worm" (v.6), "they laugh me to scorn" (v.7), "the bulls of Bashan have beset me" (v.12, which [[Michael Heiser]] reads as demonic powers of darkness), pierced hands and feet (v.16). **Isaiah** likewise "gave my back to the smiters" (Isaiah 50:6). John 18 (Christ smitten before the high priest) is offered as a possible mocked ordinance. *Rating: the passion-Psalm 22 link is mainstream; reading it as a temple *ordinance* (not just suffering) is the distinctive LDS overlay.* [^7][^8]

5. **It explains why Nephi stresses he was "large of stature."** The companion claim — that Nephi's size ([[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]]) is flagged precisely because the initiation was *physically survivable only by a big youth* — gives the thesis an internal payoff: a detail critics read as incidental becomes load-bearing. Developed in [[Nephis Large Stature Explains He Could Survive the Temple Initiation]]. *Rating: elegant if granted, circular if not.* [^9]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The governing objection is **parallelomania plus unfalsifiable retrofitting** (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]): a symbol-dense, elastic endowment lets a believer who learns the rite first read almost any text of suffering — Psalm 22, Isaiah's servant, Christ's passion, the Akitu slap, an Egyptian funerary spell — as the *same* lost ordinance after the fact. Each anchor has an ordinary reading that needs no temple rite: Matthew 5:39 is a Near-Eastern honor-insult (a backhanded slap) and a teaching on non-retaliation, not a liturgical stage direction; Helaman 4:12 and Alma 14 describe *persecutory cruelty* — mocking what the *victims* hold sacred (their persons, their God, their preaching) without implying a smiting-ordinance; the Ammonihah "seven" is a narrative count of repeated abuse, and Hebrew/Book-of-Mormon sevens are ubiquitous. Nibley's "seven blows" is a single, much-criticized reading of the papyri; the Akitu humiliation is royal, annual, and Babylonian, not an Israelite temple initiation; and the participants themselves concede this is "bonus material" they could not yet write into the book and have "no archaeological evidence of a temple somewhere where people were actually doing this" — "principally literary evidence." Granting every parallel establishes only that a modern reader *can* perceive a rite, not that Israelite or Nephite worshippers *practiced* one. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim is offered as the best *unifying* explanation of features the ordinary readings leave inert and scattered — the "that which was sacred" gloss in Helaman, the unexplained sevenfold count in Alma, the passion's saturation in Psalm 22, and the cross-cultural "humbled-then-enthroned" shape — and the right discipline is to keep the parallel *functional* and provisional (per [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]] and [[Ten Abandoned Temple Practices Show Continuity Through Change]]), not to declare a real pattern empty merely because it is symbol-dense.

## Best Quotes
> "The Book of Mormon gives us reason to think that... this was a ritual gesture and it was something closer to a beating." [^1]

> "Smiting their humble brethren upon the cheek, making a mock of that which was sacred. So they could only mock it if... the smiting was sacred." [^3]

> "There's this ritual that took place in Babylon called the Akitu festival... the king, before he's anointed and enthroned, has to go through a ritual slapping... you got to slap him so much that he elicits tears and confesses his loyalty to God, and when he does, now he's ready to be enthroned — he's been humbled." [^6]

> "Nibley talks about how Egyptian initiation ordinances include seven ritual blows... the seven blows of death. And what we have here [Alma 14]... seven times they are smitten." [^5]
