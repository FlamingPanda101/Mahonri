# Hardening Pharaoh's Heart Was to Display God's Power

## The Claim
McClellan argues the text supplies its own, consistent answer to "why did God harden Pharaoh's heart": **so that God could display power and glory.** The purpose is exclusively rhetorical/theological — to make God's might manifest to the Egyptians, to Israel, and to the world "so that you may know that I am the Lord." In plain terms: "It was clearly so that God would have the opportunity to flex on Pharaoh, on Moses, on the Egyptians, on the rest of the world... God wanted the opportunity to show off and so needed Pharaoh to be pushing back."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **To multiply signs and wonders.** [[Exodus 7.3-5|Exodus 7:3-5]] and [[Exodus 11.9-10|Exodus 11:9]]: God hardens "in order that my wonders may be multiplied in the land of Egypt."[^2] (Strong — explicit purpose clause.)
2. **"To show you my power."** [[Exodus 9.12|Exodus 9:14-16]]: "Indeed, by now I could have stretched out my hand and struck you... But this is why I have let you live: to show you my power and to make my name resound through all the earth." God states he *deliberately prolonged* the conflict.[^3] (Very strong — God says he could have ended it and chose not to.)
3. **To "gain glory for myself."** [[Exodus 14.4|Exodus 14:4]] / [[Exodus 14.17-18|Exodus 14:17-18]]: "so that I will gain glory for myself over Pharaoh and all his army... and the Egyptians shall know that I am the Lord."[^4]
4. **To make fools of the Egyptians and seed the memory.** [[Exodus 10.1-2|Exodus 10:1-2]]: "that you may tell your children and grandchildren how I have made fools of the Egyptians."[^5] (Links to [[Exodus as Post Hoc Memorial Narrative]].)

**Steelman.** A theologian could grant the "display of power/glory" motive yet deny it is mere vanity: in an Ancient Near Eastern polemical context, the contest demonstrates YHWH's supremacy over Egypt's gods and Pharaoh's claimed divinity, vindicating the oppressed and grounding Israel's covenant identity — arguably a *just* self-revelation, not narcissism. **But** this is the same "for God's glory" justification McClellan critiques elsewhere (see [[Hell - For Gods Glory Defense Rebutted]]): it still concedes God overrode Pharaoh's agency, and "glory obtained through wrath and vengeance" raises the moral problem rather than dissolving it.

## Best Quotes
> "Here, God is quite explicitly saying, I'm responsible for this, and I did it so that I could show off. So that you could have these stories to tell. So that you could know that Adonai is not to be messed with." [^6]

> "By now I could have stretched out my hand and struck you and your people with pestilence, and you would have been cut off from the earth. But this is why I have let you live: to show you my power and to make my name resound through all the earth." [^3]

> "Why is Pharaoh's heart being hardened? Because God wants the time to show off." [^7]

> "It was because God wanted the opportunity to show off and so needed Pharaoh to be pushing back. And when Pharaoh didn't want to push back, God compelled Pharaoh to push back." [^8]
