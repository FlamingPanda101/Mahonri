# Harris Describes Morality But Cannot Ground Concern for Others

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] grants [[Sam Harris]] a real victory: Harris correctly identifies *what moral language describes*. When we call something good or bad, we are describing a conscious creature's movement toward well-being or toward suffering — Hansen affirms, "I actually do believe that what moral language describes is good, is the nature of well-being, is the nature of something's relationship to suffering."[^1] But describing what good *is* does not tell you why you are obligated to pursue anyone's good but your own. Because well-being is experienced privately, Harris's account collapses into self-interest: the good "for you is your well-being," and that leaves the door open to raising your own well-being at others' expense.[^2]

## Evidence
Hansen builds the gap with a concrete case. Watching your children prosper may bring you well-being — but so might enslaving your neighbor. "Enslaving my neighbor might bring me well-being, might make me happier... might be better for someone else — so how do you deal with that contradiction?"[^2] Harris's first axiom (well-being is the only intelligible value) does not rank *whose* well-being counts, and on naturalism each conscious creature only ever directly experiences its own. So the system describes value correctly while leaving the crucial moral question — why prefer the well-being of others over your own — without an answer. This is the grounding/obligation problem already pressed against Harris in [[Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures]] and [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]: a theory that only ranks states as better or worse describes *value*, not *obligation*.

## Best Quotes
> "Yes, that is what the moral language describes, but you're right — his morality falls short, because in that objective world he's talking about well-being in the abstract... the good for you is your well-being." [^1]

> "Watching your children prosper might bring you well-being, but you know what else? Enslaving my neighbor might bring me well-being... might be better for someone else — so how do you deal with that contradiction?" [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the clearest "gets it right and misses" moment: Hansen agrees with Harris against divine-command voluntarism — morality really does track flourishing and suffering, not arbitrary divine fiat (see [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]). What the Restoration adds is the missing obligation. In the restored gospel your well-being is not an isolated private fact; it is bound to others' under the law of love, so that to harm your neighbor is to harm yourself. Where Harris can describe well-being but cannot generate a duty toward the neighbor, the gospel grounds that duty in eternal law and in the intrinsic worth of every soul. Represented fairly, Harris is right about what good *is*; he is missing why that good lays a claim on us.
