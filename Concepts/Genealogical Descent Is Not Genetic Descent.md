# Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The slogan "DNA can't just disappear" is reductive. Specific genetic markers really can become undetectable in a population — not because the people vanished, but because **genealogical descent and genetic descent are not the same thing**. You have real flesh-and-blood ancestors who contributed *zero* DNA to you, and whole lineages (especially Y-chromosome and mitochondrial) can drop out through a single break in unbroken father-to-son or mother-to-daughter transmission.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Markers are obscured, not annihilated

[[Ugo Perego]] and others document that over time a marker can become "diminishingly difficult to detect" or vanish *from the available data* without the DNA having literally disappeared — through genetic drift, the founder effect, and bottlenecks. The honest formulation is detectability, not nonexistence.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (correct framing of what "lost" means in population genetics).

### 2. Uniparental lineages break easily

The Y chromosome passes father → son and mitochondrial DNA passes mother → daughter. [[Stephen Smoot]]'s worked example: if Laman has one son and four daughters, and that son in turn has only daughters, Laman's Y chromosome is simply *gone* from his descendants — even though Laman has many living descendants. A single all-daughter (or all-son) generation erases a uniparental marker permanently.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a basic, correct property of uniparental inheritance).

### 3. You carry none of most ancestors' DNA

Because each child gets a *randomized* 50% from each parent (not a clean 25% per grandparent), genetic contribution dilutes and varies more every generation back. Within ~10 generations you have on the order of 10,000 genealogical ancestors; go further and it is millions. Many of these real ancestors "contribute nothing to your DNA" — they lived, died, had children, and left no genetic trace in modern populations. So a historical Lehite could be a genuine ancestor of millions of Native Americans while leaving no detectable Lehite DNA.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the genealogical/genetic-ancestor distinction is mainstream population genetics).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can object that this is asymmetric: while *uniparental* (Y / mtDNA) markers break easily, **autosomal** DNA is inherited from all ancestral lines, so a founding population usually leaves *some* genome-wide trace even when its Y and mtDNA lineages are lost — "your brother might carry it, or your cousin might." The reply, granted in the source, is that go back far enough and there genuinely are ancestors who contributed to *no one's* modern genome; and for a small founding group whose non-adapted genes are selected against and diluted into a much larger [[The Philistine DNA Study Shows Genetic Signals Vanish into Host Populations|host population]], even the autosomal contribution can fall below detectability. The point is not that erasure is guaranteed, but that "DNA can't disappear" is false as stated.

## Best Quotes

> "Let's imagine Laman had one son and four daughters... his Y chromosome goes to his son, Laman Jr. And then Laman Jr. has four daughters and no sons. Where's Laman's Y chromosome now? It's gone."[^3]

> "You have ancestors who are your real flesh and blood living ancestors... who contribute nothing to your DNA."[^4]

> "You go back far enough and you have people who just did not contribute genetic material to modern populations, but they still lived. They still died. They had kids."[^4]
