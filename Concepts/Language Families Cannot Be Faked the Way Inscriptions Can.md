# Language Families Cannot Be Faked the Way Inscriptions Can

## The Claim
[[Brian Stubbs]] makes an epistemic argument for *why* linguistic evidence, if real, outranks the artifacts critics usually demand. When skeptics ask "where is the reformed Egyptian? where are the inscriptions?", they are asking for the *most forgeable* class of evidence — a single unearthed inscription can always be dismissed as a hoax. A **living language family**, by contrast, "cannot be fabricated": thousands of present-day speakers of the related languages, and the inherited [[Sound Correspondences Distinguish Descent from Coincidence in Language|sound-correspondence]] structure tying them from ancient to modern times, are "beyond fabrication." Either the [[Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer|1,657 cognate sets]] are really there or they are not — and you cannot manufacture 1,500 speakers of a language family.[^1][^2] So, the argument runs, the absence of an indisputable inscription is the *wrong* place to look; the language data is the stronger evidence precisely because it is unfakeable.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Inscriptions are the soft target; languages are the hard one (rated: moderate, conceptual).** "Written records unearthed in the Americas are often labeled hoaxes, but language ties, when apparent, show specific ties from ancient to modern times. And the thousands of speakers of the related languages are beyond fabrication."[^1] This reframes a perennial critic demand ("show me the plates / the script") as a category error. Compare [[Reformed Egyptian Characters Were Small]].
2. **It is a binary, not a judgment call about a relic (rated: moderate, but premise-dependent).** "You can't create 1,500 speakers of related languages for a language family." The data "holds up or it's not — it's not something you can just fabricate."[^2] Structurally this resembles the [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence|witness argument]]: it would require "a massive conspiracy" rather than a single forged object.
3. **The honest limit (rated: the decisive caveat).** *Steelman:* the argument only bites *if* the cognate sets are genuine descent rather than the analyst's artifact. The unfakeability of Uto-Aztecan's existence is not in dispute — what is disputed is whether its vocabulary actually *derives from* Semitic and Egyptian, which is exactly the claim mainstream Uto-Aztecanists have not accepted (see [[Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer]]). So "you can't fake a language family" is true but proves less than it sounds: the contested step is the *interpretation* of real data, not the fabrication of fake data. The argument shifts the burden well only after the descent relationship is independently granted — which is the very point at issue.
