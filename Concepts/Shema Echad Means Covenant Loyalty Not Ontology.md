# Shema Echad Means Covenant Loyalty Not Ontology

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

"Hear, O Israel, the LORD our God, the LORD is one" ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deuteronomy 6:4]]) is pressed as a metaphysical statement of numerical/ontological oneness — proof that God is one being. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The Hebrews weren't doing ontology.** Ancient Hebrews (c. 3000–600 BC) had no category of *ontology* (the metaphysics of being) — that was a later Greek preoccupation. Reading the Shema as a claim about God's *being* imports an alien thought-world. (Evidence: broadly supported by scholarship on the non-philosophical character of early Hebrew religion; the framing is somewhat sweeping but directionally mainstream.) [^2]
2. **`echad` can mean "alone," not just numerical "one."** The actual phrase is *Yahweh eloheinu, Yahweh echad*. *Echad* admits a reading "Yahweh alone." On the covenant-loyalty reading, the Shema is a *pledge* — "Yahweh is our God, Yahweh alone" — answering "to whom do you give allegiance?" not "how many beings is God?" (Evidence: the lexical range of *echad* and the covenantal genre of Deuteronomy support this; it is a recognized scholarly reading, paralleled by the [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Song of Songs]] use of the cognate.) [^3]
3. **Genre is catechism/pledge.** Deuteronomy's concern is covenant fidelity amid the gods of the nations ("don't go after the Canaanite gods"), so a loyalty-oath reading fits the book's whole rhetoric. The Shema is "a statement of covenantal loyalty," not of ontology. [^4]
4. **Steelman.** The traditional monotheist replies that even if the *primary* force is loyalty, Deuteronomy elsewhere makes apparently absolute claims ("the LORD he is God; there is none else beside him," Deut 4:35,39), and later Judaism and Jesus himself ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|cited in Mark 12]]) treat the Shema as foundational to *the* one God — so loyalty and a robust (even if not Greek-philosophical) monotheism are not mutually exclusive. (Evidence: a fair corrective; "monolatry shading into monotheism" is itself a major scholarly position, and Deut 4 is harder for the pure-loyalty reading.) The LDS reply handles the "none else" texts as ANE boasts of supremacy (see [[None Beside Me Is an ANE Boast of Supremacy]]) and maintains that *echad* + genre still won't yield *one being in three persons*. [^1]

## Best Quotes

> "The thought categories that existed in the ancient world for a Hebrew ... they were not thinking about ontology. That is a concept that was literally outside of their world." [^2]

> "This word ehad can be used to mean either one as in like a numerical one or alone. ... 'Yahweh is our God, Yahweh alone.' ... It's a statement of covenantal loyalty." [^3]
