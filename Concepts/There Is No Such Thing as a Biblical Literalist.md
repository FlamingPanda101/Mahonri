# There Is No Such Thing as a Biblical Literalist

## The Claim

[[Dan McClellan]]'s "hot take" is blunt: **there is no such thing as a biblical literalist.** Biblical literalism is "a fiction that is used to try to curate the authority of the Bible and to assert authority over what the Bible actually says." [^1][^2] No reader actually applies the plain sense of every verse, because doing so consistently would force the reader to affirm contradictions, scientific falsehoods, and the existence of rival gods. What is marketed as "literalism" is really a selective, identity-driven posture.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Nobody reads everything literally — everyone concedes genres.** Even hard inerrantists grant that the Bible contains poetry, metaphor, allegory, and figures of speech (the Chicago Statement itself says so). [^3] So the real question is never "literal or not" but *where to draw the threshold* between literal and figurative — and that line is supplied by the reader, not the text. See [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]]. **(Strong — this is conceded by the literalists' own documents.)**
2. **The "who decides?" problem.** McClellan: "Here's why there's no such thing as a literalist. Who gets to decide when it is or isn't? Well, obviously I do. And by me, I mean whoever is talking." [^4] The decision about which contradictory verse counts as the "really literal" one is made by the interpreter, exposing the authority as the tradition, not the page. See [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]]. **(Strong.)**
3. **Steelman of the literalist.** A sophisticated inerrantist would say literalism means reading each text "according to its grammatical-historical sense," respecting authorial intent and genre — not wooden flatness. McClellan's reply: that is exactly Chicago Article XV's move, and it smuggles "the intent to use things figuratively" into the word *literal*, which "is not what literal generally is referring to" and "is open to interpretation again." [^5][^6] So the redefinition rescues the label only by emptying it of content. See [[Chicago Statement on Inerrancy Redefines Literal]]. **(Moderate-to-strong — turns on whether "literal" can coherently include the figurative.)**
4. **Textual vs. ideological literalism.** "Textual literalism results in acknowledging contradiction... scientific falsehoods... historical events that don't line up with reality." [^7] Because real literalists refuse those outcomes, their literalism must be *ideological* — driven by dogma, not by the text. See [[Literalism Is Ideological Not Textual]]. **(Strong as an internal critique.)**
