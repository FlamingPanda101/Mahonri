# We Call God Good Because He Saves Us From Suffering

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] accepts the [[Sam Harris]] premise — moral language describes a thing's relationship to suffering and well-being — and then turns it into a test for the word "good" itself. Would we call God good if He did nothing but cause endless, eternal misery? "If God literally just made me suffer and made everyone suffer forever, why would we ever use the word good to describe Him?"[^1] We call God good precisely because He *saves us from* suffering and gives us the capacity for real joy. Goodness, on this account, is not raw power; it is what moves conscious beings away from suffering toward well-being.

## Evidence
Hansen runs the same test on the Cross. Someone being suffocated and tortured to death is plainly bad — yet Christians call the crucifixion *good*. Why? Only because of what it accomplishes: "it wouldn't have been a good thing if it was just that he died on the cross and that was it and no one was saved." Worse, if Christ's death meant we are all going to die on crosses and suffer eternally the same fate, "I wouldn't call that a good thing."[^2] The same event is good or bad entirely according to its relationship to suffering and salvation. This confirms Harris's descriptive insight while exposing what it lacks: the framework needs an ultimate context — real deliverance from suffering — to make the words "good" and "God" coherent at all.

## Best Quotes
> "If God literally just made me suffer and made me suffer forever and made everyone suffer forever, why would we ever use the word good to describe God? We use the word good because God can provide — He can save me from suffering." [^1]

> "When we look at the instance of the cross I say that's a good thing — but it's only a good thing because it wouldn't have been a good thing if it was just that he died on the cross and no one was saved." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is where the Restoration supplies what naturalism cannot. Hansen sides with Harris *against* divine-command voluntarism — God is not good merely because He is powerful or because whatever He commands is good by fiat (see [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]] and [[Goodness and Power Are Not the Same Thing]]). God is good because, within a universe of eternal law, He actually delivers conscious beings from suffering into joy. The plan of salvation is the cosmic guarantee that the Harris-style description of goodness has a real referent: suffering is not the last word, the Atonement reorders the worst possible misery toward well-being, and so "good" is not an empty honorific but a true description of what God does for His children.
