# The Life of Adam and Eve Fills the Gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses 5
#type/claim

## The Claim
The apocryphal *Life of Adam and Eve* (and related Adam literature) parallels Joseph Smith's Moses 5 so closely that the two texts fill each other's narrative "holes." [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]] maps Moses 5's sequence — cast out, offer sacrifice, an angel/voice declares the gospel, [a gap], Adam celebrates the resurrection, Eve draws the wise conclusion — onto the *Life of Adam and Eve*, which supplies the missing element (the resurrection being preached) while Moses 5 supplies what the apocryphon lacks (Adam's celebration). Although the surviving manuscripts are late (~11th century), Barnes argues the traditions "agree with Joseph Smith in impossible ways," which he takes as evidence that Joseph's revelation restored genuinely ancient material he could not have known.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Complementary gap-filling.** The core move is that the two narratives are interlocking: where Moses 5 jumps, the Adam apocrypha continues, and vice versa, producing a fuller combined story than either alone. This mutual completion (rather than mere overlap) is presented as the striking datum.
- **"Impossible" agreement.** Because the *Life of Adam and Eve* was not available to Joseph Smith in 1830, points of convergence are argued to be inexplicable by ordinary 19th-century access — the same logic LDS apologetics applies to other ancient parallels. The argument is the apocryphal companion to the chiasmus reading in [[Eve Completes Adam's Broken Chiasmus in Moses 5]].
- **Genre awareness.** Barnes treats the apocrypha as late-attested but tradition-bearing — valuable for the old material it may preserve, not as scripture — which connects to how pseudepigraphal Adam literature is weighed (see [[Pseudepigrapha and Authority]]).
- **Steelman / caveats.** Critics will note the source is openly non-scholarly and comedic, and that "agree in impossible ways" overstates the case: Adam-and-Eve expansions are a broad, widely diffused tradition, so generic motifs (sacrifice, an angelic message, a fortunate fall) can appear independently without literary dependence. Late manuscripts also raise the possibility of medieval development rather than primitive tradition, and parallels can be cherry-picked while differences are downplayed. The defensible claim is that Moses 5 shares notable structural and thematic features with the Adam apocrypha; the strong claim that this proves restored ancient content is contested and rests on the harder-to-establish "Joseph could not have known" premise.
