# Debate: Is Eternal Hell Compatible with a Perfectly Good God — and Does the LDS Model Dissolve the Problem?

## The Question
Classical Christianity has historically taught **eternal conscious torment** (ECT): the unredeemed suffer forever, consciously, with no exit. The problem of hell asks whether that doctrine can survive the very attributes it presupposes — that God is omnipotent, omniscient, perfectly just, and perfectly loving — once you add the further classical premise that God creates each soul *ex nihilo* and sustains it at every instant ([[Problem of Hell and Creation]]). The dispute is not usually "does hell exist?" but a three-way contest over **what hell is and whom it implicates**: (a) the traditional defender holds ECT is just and that a loving God honors the damned's own libertarian choice; (b) the critic argues infinite punishment for finite sin is disproportionate and that creation-from-nothing makes God the author of the outcome, so ECT *undermines* God's goodness; (c) the Latter-day Saint argues the dilemma is an artifact of creedal metaphysics — deny *ex nihilo*, add a premortal life and postmortem evangelism, replace binary heaven/hell with graded glory, and the problem largely dissolves, leaving only a tiny, self-chosen outer darkness. The live question is therefore which framework the attributes actually fit — and whether the LDS "dissolution" is a genuine solve or a relocation of the puzzle ([[The Soteriological Problem of Evil - The Inconsistent Triad]]).

## Case for TRADITIONAL ETERNAL CONSCIOUS TORMENT (the defense)
- **Sin against an infinite God merits infinite punishment.** [[Jonathan Edwards]] and [[William Wainwright]] hold that every sin, being committed against a being of infinite worth, warrants unending punishment — so the disproportion the critic alleges is illusory once the *object* of the offense is weighed (the view rebutted in [[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]]; tier: this is the strongest *positive* defense, but it is stated by the rebuttal note, not steelmanned in its own note).
- **None of us is innocent, so any salvation is mercy.** [[Wes Huff]]'s line — "I deserve hell 100% of the time" — frames damnation as the *default just desert* and heaven as unearned grace, so God wrongs no one ([[Hell - Appeal to Sinfulness Rebutted]]; tier: coherent within total-depravity premises, conceded by the LDS side to be internally consistent given *ex nihilo*).
- **The Fall, not God, disqualifies humanity.** Human incapacity for salvation traces to Adam's free choice ([[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12]]), not to God's design, so responsibility sits with the creature ([[Hell - Appeal to the Fall Rebutted]]; tier: the defense's cleanest deflection of authorship, though the rebuttal presses why God keeps *creating* fallen souls).
- **Libertarian free will: hell is chosen, not imposed.** The strongest defense, which the critic's own source calls "the most persuasive": God respects agency, and "forced love is not love." [[C.S. Lewis]]'s formula — *"the doors of hell are locked from the inside"* — recasts hell as a self-chosen state, not external cruelty ([[Hell - Free Will Defense Rebutted]], [[C.S. Lewis]]; tier: strong, and structurally the same move the LDS side itself uses for outer darkness — see [[Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends]]).
- **Free will is the price of love.** [[Frank Turek]]'s general theodicy: to abolish moral evil God must abolish freedom, but freedom is the precondition of love — "God didn't want robots" ([[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy]]; tier: the best-developed positive case, though it is built for the problem of *evil* broadly, not ECT specifically).
- **Omnibenevolence ≠ universalism ≠ coercion.** A Catholic defender ([[Gavin Ortlund]] is the Protestant interlocutor; the Catholic is "Gavin the debater") fences the definitions: God can will the good of all ([[1 Timothy 2.4|1 Tim 2:4]], [[Scripture Explicitly Affirms Universal Salvific Will]]) without saving all, because acceptance is free ([[Omnibenevolence Does Not Equal Universalism or Coercion]]; tier: a *correct and strong* conceptual distinction — but see "Where it turns," since the critic grants it and relocates the problem upstream of freedom).
- **God's glory / greater-good warrants.** Harsher Calvinist and Thomist defenses: [[John Calvin]] has God ordain damnation "for his own glory," [[Thomas Aquinas]] has the saved behold the sufferings of the damned (rebutted in [[Hell - For Gods Glory Defense Rebutted]], [[Hell as a Greater Good Treats People as Tools]]; tier: weak by the defense's *own* friends — even most ECT proponents disown the "for God's glory" framing as the rebuttals note).

## Case for THE PROBLEM OF HELL (the critic)
- **Finite sin cannot merit infinite punishment.** [[David Bentley Hart]]: retribution as "unending suffering imposed as recompense for the actions of a finite intellect and will must be by any sound definition disproportionate, unjust, and at the last nothing more than an expression of sheer pointless cruelty" ([[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]]; tier: strong — Hart is an A-tier theologian; the "infinite-object" reply is its only serious counter).
- **Creation *ex nihilo* makes God the author of the outcome.** If God conjures each soul from nothing with its exact attributes and sustains it, he owns the atheist's unbelief and the thief's theft — so damning them is to punish his own design ([[Problem of Hell and Creation]]). The "watchmaker who winds it up and walks away" defense fails because God also makes the gears and the physical constants ([[Hell - Watchmaker Wind Up and Go Rebutted]]; [[Jonathan Kvanvig]]: the analogy is "defective to the core").
- **The button thought experiment.** Press a button and a fully real person exists whose life you foresee ends in eternal torment; you lose nothing by walking away. *No good agent presses it* — and God, with foreknowledge and the freedom not to create, is the button-presser ([[The Button Thought Experiment for the Problem of Hell]]; tier: strong as a pre-theoretical moral premise; the theist's escape is to deny the situations are analogous).
- **Scripture itself concedes a fate worse than non-existence — the Judas argument.** In [[Matthew 26.24|Matthew 26:24]] Jesus says of Judas "it had been good for that man if he had not been born." If *Christ* grants that some fate is worse than never existing, a free creator could have spared that person by not creating them ([[The Judas Argument - Better Never to Have Been Born]]; tier: strong rhetorically because the premise comes from Jesus, contested on the inference — the steelman reads it as Semitic woe-hyperbole, and a libertarian says Judas *made* his fate).
- **The free-will defense is undercut at creation.** Even granting Lewis's "locked from the inside," the critic ([[Hayden Carroll]]) calls free will "a red herring": the dilemma bites *before* freedom operates — why create *this* person at all, foreknowing the loss, when refraining costs God nothing? ([[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]], [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]]; tier: this is the load-bearing reframing — it neutralizes the defense's single best card by conceding it).
- **Greater-good defenses instrumentalize persons.** If some are created to fail so others may succeed, those lives are mere tools — a Kantian wrong we condemn everywhere else ([[Hell as a Greater Good Treats People as Tools]]); and glory won "through wrath and vengeance" is vice, not glory ([[Hell for Gods Glory Is Narcissism Not Love]]).
- **"God's goodness is not our goodness" is what makes the cruelty sayable.** [[Terrell Givens|Givens]]'s "most scathing critique": once the tradition severs continuity between God's moral nature and ours (Augustine's "his justice is not our justice"), "good" can mean anything when applied to God — so damning infants can be re-narrated as done "lovingly," and a "whole history of abuses became possible." With [[Tom Holland|Tom Holland]] ("moral chaos results when love is cut loose from its theological foundations") and Wiesel ("if God's moral universe isn't our moral universe, then what's the use of any of this?"), Givens demands a *univocal core*: divine goodness cannot include acts we would unhesitatingly call monstrous in a human — which directly disarms the "for God's glory" and greater-good defenses above. See [[Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide]] (Givens, B; channel tier C). *(The Thomist reply: "good" is predicated *analogically*, so "his justice is not ours" can mean *transcends*, not *contradicts* — which Givens calls the very slide that empties the word.)*
- **The sovereignty-over-love package is a datable contingency, not the biblical given.** Givens argues classical Christianity made *sovereignty*, not love, the supreme attribute, and that this crystallized "simultaneously with the development of creation ex nihilo" — because ex-nihilo creation maximizes God's power and *distance* ("love is about intimacy… sovereignty is about distance"). If sound, this is the genealogy *behind* the creation dilemma: the very premise (sole ex-nihilo authorship + a glory-first God) the critic needs to indict is itself a late, motivated construction, not a fixed datum. See [[Sovereignty Was Privileged Over Love and Creation Ex Nihilo Drove It]] (Givens, B; channel tier C). *(The classical reply: ex nihilo secures aseity and transcendence, and in the simple God sovereignty *is* love — not a rival to it.)*
- **Marcion was right that pure love can't be reconciled with a literally-read OT.** Givens's qualified rehabilitation of [[Marcion]]: an unqualifiedly loving God sits uneasily with a literal Old Testament of "retribution… vengeance… judgment," and the Marcionite church's two-century survival shows the appeal was real. Marcion's *cure* (discard the OT) was wrong, and so is [[Tertullian]]'s ("God loves within reasonable limits" — but "there's nothing reasonable about a parent's unqualified love"). See [[Marcion Was Right That a God of Pure Love Cannot Be Reconciled With a Literal Old Testament]] (Givens, B; channel tier C) — note the LDS *third* option below: locate the cruelty in transmission, not in God.
- **The doctrine itself is a late composite, not a fixed biblical given.** "Hell" flattens four distinct words — Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, Tartarus — into one ([[English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell]], [[Gehenna Was the Valley of Hinnom Not a Burning Garbage Dump]]); the ECT package assembled "well after the Bible," partly as theodicy under imperial oppression ([[Hell - Development of the Concept]], [[Divine Punishment in the Afterlife Emerged as Theodicy Under Imperial Oppression]]; tier: academic-biblical, useful for loosening "ECT = *the* biblical teaching," neutral between the remaining positions).
- **The unevangelized seal the injustice — the soteriological triad.** If salvation comes "only through Christ" yet millions never heard, a loving God seems to condemn [[Stephen T. Davis]]'s "Oohku" for an accident of birth ([[The Soteriological Problem of Evil - The Inconsistent Triad]]; [[Thomas V. Morris]]'s "scandal"; tier: strong, and the hinge the LDS fourth premise is built to answer).

## Case for THE LDS DISSOLUTION (reframing the dilemma)
- **Deny *ex nihilo*: God organizes co-eternal intelligences, he does not conjure doomed beings.** The structurally decisive move. If spirits are uncreated and self-existent, God's act is *parenting/organizing*, not authorship-from-nothing, so "the button was never God's alone to press" ([[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]], [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]], [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]; tier: decisive *within* LDS premises — the steelman concedes the price is divine aseity, and that a residual foreknowledge problem can return).
- **Replace binary heaven/hell with degrees of glory.** Salvation is a spectrum toward one's "fullness of potential," each kingdom "incomprehensibly happy"; rejecting truth "stunts growth," it does not damn ([[LDS Afterlife - Degrees of Glory]], [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]], [[Degrees of Glory vs Heaven]], [[Three Degrees of Glory in the Bible]]; tier: strong on the problem-of-hell front — it dissolves "would God damn a sincere modalist?"; [[Gavin Ortlund]] counters that the Gospels' "lurid categories" still entail a binary threshold).
- **Postmortem evangelism supplies the missing fourth premise.** [[David Paulsen]] dissolves the soteriological triad by adding: those who die without a chance to accept Christ get that chance after death, via spirit-prison preaching and vicarious ordinances ([[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]], source [[Paulsen - Joseph Smith and the Problem of Evil]]; tier: B — a credentialed BYU philosopher; this is the cleanest answer to Oohku in the vault).
- **"Hell" is mostly temporary, remedial, and escapable.** LDS usage distinguishes (a) the temporary **spirit prison** where the unrepentant suffer until they pay or accept Christ from (b) permanent **outer darkness**. [[Cardon Ellis]]: "eternal damnation" means *God's* punishment (God is eternal), not endless duration — "I can choose to undamn them" once they have progressed or paid ([[Eternal Damnation Is Gods Not Endless]]; tier: strong within D&C 19's gloss, but a *minority* reading of *aiōnios* — Matthew 25:46 — that creedal exegetes contest).
- **LDS does affirm a real hell — the caricature is wrong both ways.** [[Hayden Carroll]] corrects the "Mormons deny hell" myth from [[D&C 76]] and D&C 18: the unrepentant *will* suffer ([[Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell - DC 76 and DC 18]]). The honest summary is not "Mormons do/don't believe in hell" but "a hell that is mostly temporary, remedial, and escapable, with a small permanent category" — nearer Eastern-Orthodox/purgatorial models than either flat universalism or Calvinist double-predestination.
- **Outer darkness is tiny and self-chosen.** Reserved for "sons of perdition" who "know the light, they hate light"; the panel rejects annihilationism and keeps it chosen — "people who will be there infinitely are the ones who want to be there infinitely" ([[Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends]]; tier: this is the LDS version of Lewis's "doors locked from the inside," shrinking ECT's population to near-zero — but see "Where it turns," it does *not* fully cover perdition).
- **Premortal agency + consent shift the moral framing off God.** Spirits *consented* to enter mortality knowing the risk ([[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]], [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]), and God may be *bound by eternal law* to admit any intelligence that wills to come ([[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]; tier: distinctive but speculative — Carroll flags the "eternal law" point as theory, and the steelman notes consent was given *without* knowing the specific outcome, which dents informed consent and re-raises the dilemma at the premortal stage).
- **Patristic precedent exists for near-universal restoration.** [[Origen]]'s *apokatastasis* taught an eventual restoration of all (even, he speculated, the demons), paired with the preexistence of souls — a striking early-Christian parallel to graded, near-universal redemption ([[Origen's Apokatastasis Taught Universal Restoration]]; tier: historical, shows the LDS shape is not unprecedented, though Origen was later condemned).
- **The God who generates the dilemma is the wrong God: he weeps, and his glory is downstream of love.** [[Terrell Givens|Givens]] contrasts the *theocentric* Anselmian God who "created us for his own glory because he loves his glory" (so even the atonement is about "rectifying God's… dishonor") with the *anthropocentric* weeping God of [[Moses 7|Moses 7]], whose "work and… glory" is "your immortality and eternal life" — glory a "collateral benefit," not the "primary intent." This pulls the rug from the "for God's glory" defense at its root: if God's own highest objective is human joy, a glory-first ECT mis-describes God's character. See [[The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric]] (Givens, B; channel tier C). *(The Reformed reply: willing his glory and willing our good are the *same* act in the highest good — "glorify God and enjoy him forever.")*
- **A self-limiting God relaxes the foreknowledge premise the dilemma rides on.** Givens reinterprets the "omni" attributes around an embodied, agency-respecting God: omniscience need not include the future ("if free will is real, then the future doesn't exist yet"), and omnipotence is *self-limiting* — "if he's weeping then things aren't unfolding in the way that he would like… so he limits himself and allows agency." Since the creation dilemma in its sharpest form presupposes *absolute foreknowledge* of each soul's loss, an open-future, self-limiting God blunts the "foreknowing, costless decision to create *this* person" premise. See [[A Self-Limiting God - Reinterpreting Omniscience Omnipotence and Omnipresence]] (Givens, B; channel tier C; this is open theism's core — cf. [[Open Theism as a Dilemma Solve]]). *(The classical reply: an open-future God is a temporal learner, not the God who declares "the end from the beginning"; and this trades omniscience for relevance.)*
- **The OT's cruelty is a transmission artifact, not God's character.** The Marcion problem (above) only forces discarding the OT *if* it must be read literally; the Restoration's [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]] thesis lets the Latter-day Saint keep both Testaments *and* a God who is "only love," locating the "retribution… vengeance" in fallen redaction rather than in God — an option unavailable to Marcion and to the creedal tradition. See [[Marcion Was Right That a God of Pure Love Cannot Be Reconciled With a Literal Old Testament]]; this is the dissolution-side counterpart to the critic's use of the same note.

## Where it turns
The debate does not turn on whether hell exists, nor on whether finite sin "feels" disproportionate to infinite punishment — the proportionality intuition ([[Hell - Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment]]) is conceded by almost everyone except the strict infinite-object defender. It turns on three pivots:
1. **Does the action move upstream to creation, or stay downstream at the creature's choice?** This is *the* crux. The traditional defense's whole weight rests on libertarian free will + Lewis's "locked from the inside." The critic's decisive counter is to *grant* free will entirely and relocate the wrong to the prior act: God's free, foreknowing, costless decision to create *this* person ([[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]], [[The First Morally Decisive Moment Is Creation Not Rebellion]], [[The Button Thought Experiment for the Problem of Hell]]). If that move succeeds, every free-will and greater-good defense is sidestepped rather than refuted — and the only way to block it is to deny that God is the sole, ex-nihilo author of the outcome. The Givens material presses the same crux from two new angles: genealogically, the load-bearing premise (sole ex-nihilo authorship + a glory-first God) is itself a *datable contingency* that crystallized with ex nihilo to privilege sovereignty over love ([[Sovereignty Was Privileged Over Love and Creation Ex Nihilo Drove It]]); and morally, the defenses that *redescribe* the outcome as loving ("for God's glory," greater-good) depend on severing God's goodness from ours ([[Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide]]) — so if "good" must keep a univocal core, those defenses are not available in the first place. A self-limiting, open-future God ([[A Self-Limiting God - Reinterpreting Omniscience Omnipotence and Omnipresence]]) further weakens the *foreknowing* half of the critic's own premise — cutting against the strongest form of the dilemma, which is why it sits on the dissolution side.
2. **Does denying *ex nihilo* actually dissolve the problem, or relocate it?** The LDS solve is powerful precisely because it severs premise (1)'s pivot at the root: if intelligences are co-eternal and *consented*, God neither authored them nor imposed existence ([[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]], [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]). But the steelman bites back twice: (a) consent was given *without* foreknowledge of the specific outcome ("if he did, then they would say I don't want to do this"), which undercuts *informed* consent and re-raises the button at the premortal council; and (b) for the **sons of perdition**, outer darkness "does seem pretty primally final," so the consent + eternal-law machinery — not the "temporary hell" gloss — must carry the full weight for the very cases where the dilemma is sharpest ([[Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends]]).
3. **Is the punishment infinite in *duration*, or only "God's"?** The LDS deflation of "finite sin / infinite punishment" depends on reading "eternal" as *whose* it is, not *how long* ([[Eternal Damnation Is Gods Not Endless]]). This is genuinely defensible from D&C 19 but is a minority exegesis of *aiōnios*; the creedal critic and the academic-biblical voice ([[English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell]]) agree the *traditional* hell is durationally everlasting — so the LDS model wins this point only by *changing the doctrine under dispute*, which is fair as a constructive proposal but does not refute ECT on ECT's own terms.

## Most authoritative view
The strongest, most defensible synthesis the vault supports: **the problem of hell is decisive against the *traditional ex-nihilo + eternal-conscious-torment* package, and the LDS reframing dissolves it — but by changing the metaphysics, not by winning the argument on creedal ground, and at real cost.** Three load-bearing data carry this. First, the critic's single best move is conceptual, not rhetorical: *granting* libertarian free will and relocating the wrong to creation ([[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]]) sidesteps Lewis, Turek, and the greater-good theodicies at once, and the only reply that touches it is to deny sole ex-nihilo authorship. Second, that denial is exactly what the Restoration supplies — co-eternal intelligences, premortal consent, postmortem evangelism, graded glory, a near-empty outer darkness ([[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]], [[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]], [[LDS Progression Model vs Binary Heaven and Hell]]) — and the most authoritative single statement is [[David Paulsen]]'s B-tier philosophical case that *ex nihilo* is "the trouble-making postulate." Third, intellectual honesty requires naming what the solve costs and does not cover: it trades divine aseity/sovereignty for a constrained, finitist God; its "eternal = God's" reading is a minority exegesis; its consent defense runs on *un*-informed consent; and the sons of perdition remain a genuine remainder where God "let Lucifer into the creation cycle" foreknowing the result. Net: against the *classical creedal* God, the critic is right that ECT and omnibenevolence cannot both stand — but the dilemma is a function of creedal metaphysics (ex nihilo + absolute foreknowledge + binary fate), and a model that drops those premises is not the target of the argument. "Eternal hell is incompatible with a good God" is true of the doctrine most Christians have held; "the problem of hell refutes theism as such" is not — the LDS framework is a live, coherent counterexample, purchased at a metaphysical price the creedal theist will not pay.
