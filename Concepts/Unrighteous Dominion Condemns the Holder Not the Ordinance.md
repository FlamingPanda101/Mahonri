# Unrighteous Dominion Condemns the Holder Not the Ordinance

#type/claim

## The Claim
When a priesthood holder "covers his sins" or exercises "unrighteous dominion," [[D&C 121.39|D&C 121:37-39]] says the heavens withdraw and "amen to the priesthood or the authority of that man." The hosts ask the sharp question: if such a man then performs an ordinance in that state, is the ordinance *void* for the person receiving it? Their conclusion — flagged as their reasoning, not a citation — is that the sin "brings condemnation upon the priesthood holder" but "does not affect the person that's receiving the ordinance." [^1] The ordinance still flows because validity rests on the *chain of authority and keys* presided over by the President of the Church, not on the personal worthiness of the individual administrator: even a "broken vessel... drinking condemnation to my own soul" sits within a line of authority that holds the keys. [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The power/authority/keys distinction is load-bearing.** The hosts (and [[George Q. Cannon]]) separate three things people "go along" conflating: *power* (which depends on righteousness), *authority* (delegated, within bounds), and *keys* (the right to direct). Blessings are pronounced "by the authority of the Melchizedek priesthood," not "by the power" — because the power can withdraw while the authority/keys structure remains. [^3] *Rating: a genuine and important LDS systematic distinction.*

2. **Authority is bounded by stewardship.** Cannon warns men "not to overstep the bounds of the authority conferred upon them." The hosts illustrate with family: a sealed father has authority within his own family but none over an adult child's parenting — abuses "all come from men wanting to have more authority than what God has given them." [^4] *Rating: a clean practical gloss on D&C 121's "unrighteous dominion."*

3. **This is the LDS answer to a Donatist-style worry.** Whether a sinful minister's ordinances "count" is precisely the ancient **Donatist** controversy. The LDS resolution here parallels the catholic answer (the rite's validity is not hostage to the minister's holiness) while locating that guarantee in *keys* rather than in *ex opere operato* sacramental mechanics. See [[Donatism and the LDS Model of Priesthood Validity]]. *Rating: a coherent structural answer*, explicitly hedged by the hosts ("I'm winging this").

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic notes the hosts themselves are unsure ("hit us in the comments if you have a source") — and the principle sits in tension with the harder readings of D&C 121: if "amen to the priesthood of that man" is taken strictly, his *authority* really is gone, so on what basis does the ordinance still convey? Saying "the line of authority above him still holds keys" risks proving too much (it could validate almost any act under a valid hierarchy) or too little (it quietly concedes the individual's authority lapsed, which is the catholic *ex opere operato* move under a different name). A measured LDS reply: the official position (as the hosts half-recall from church leaders) is exactly that personal unworthiness brings condemnation on the holder without nullifying a properly-authorized ordinance for a worthy recipient — the keys/authorization, not the holder's spiritual state in the moment, are what the ordinance runs on. This keeps the system from collapsing into either Donatism (every sinful priest's acts void) or pure mechanism, and it dovetails with the broader keys theology in [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]] and [[Priesthood Authority Is the Necessary But Not Sufficient Dividing Line]].
