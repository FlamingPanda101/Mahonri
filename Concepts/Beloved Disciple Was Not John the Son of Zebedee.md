# The Beloved Disciple Was Not John the Son of Zebedee

## The Claim
The "disciple whom Jesus loved," to whom the Gospel attributes its authorship, is **not** John the son of Zebedee, the Galilean fisherman-apostle. The traditional attribution does not fit the profile the New Testament gives of John.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **John son of Zebedee is presented as illiterate.** [[Acts 4.13|Acts 4:13]] calls Peter and John *agrammatoi* — "unlettered/illiterate" — men untrained to write. A Gospel as theologically and philosophically sophisticated as John is an implausible product of such an author. *(Strong; explicit NT testimony plus the literary character of John.)* [^1]
2. **The Beloved Disciple has Jerusalem high-priestly access; John does not.** In John 18 "the other disciple" is a friend of the high priest with special access to his home. The John of the Synoptics is a Galilean fisherman on the Sea of Galilee, living nowhere near Jerusalem, let alone among its elite. *(Strong; direct character mismatch.)* [^2]
3. **The traditional fit is only partial.** John son of Zebedee being one of Jesus' three closest disciples (Mark/Matthew/Luke) is the *one* point that recommends him — but "that's kind of almost as far as it goes," and the other attributes "don't fit quite neatly." *(Moderate; concedes the kernel of the tradition while showing it underdetermines the identification.)* [^3]
4. **20th-century scholarship reached the same verdict.** By the 20th century scholars judged the attribution to John son of Zebedee not the fit they wanted and looked for another author — the consensus context for Méndez's stronger pseudepigraphy thesis. *(Moderate; appeal to scholarly trend.)* [^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Patristic tradition (e.g. Irenaeus citing Polycarp) ties the Gospel to John, an early and not-easily-dismissed line of testimony; literacy in Acts 4 may be rhetorical or refer to lack of *formal scribal* training rather than total illiteracy decades later; and a Galilean with priestly connections is not impossible. The identification remains the historic default for good reasons even if Méndez finds it inadequate.

## Best Quotes
> "John is presented to us in Acts chapter 4 as someone who was illiterate, someone who didn't know how to write, someone who was never trained to write a gospel." — [[Hugo Mendez]] [^1]

> "This disciple... is supposed to be a friend of the high priest who has special access to the high priest's home. The John we know from Matthew, Mark, and Luke is a simple fisherman living in Galilee." [^2]

> "By the 20th century scholars looked at the traditional attribution of the gospel to John the son of Zebedee and thought that this isn't quite the fit we want for authorship. We need to be looking for another author." [^4]
