# The Angel in Revelation Who Refuses Worship

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Trinitarian inference: a figure who speaks the divine titles ("I am the first and the last," "I am God") must *be* God; therefore Jesus' divine-title sayings prove his deity in the consubstantial sense. [[Blake Ostler]] uses Revelation to break that inference. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. An angel speaks the divine titles, then forbids worship (rating: strong; textual)
In Revelation an angel appears, presents himself "as if he's God," and when John "falls down to worship him, the angel is absolutely beside himself." See [[Revelation 22.8-9|Revelation 22:8-9]] (cf. Rev. 19:10). [^1]

> "the angel looks at him and says, 'Idiot, what are you doing? I'm not God. Get up. I'm your fellow servant like you.'" [^2]

### 2. Bearing the divine name ≠ being God (rating: strong; logical)
The episode shows "very expressly... a messenger who bears the divine name appears as God" yet is not God. So Jesus' use of divine titles cannot, by itself, establish that he is the one God; it is consistent with the [[Messenger Shaliach Tradition Explains Bearing the Divine Name|shaliach pattern]]. [^3]

### 3. But the Son *is* worshiped — so he is not merely an angel (rating: strong; Ostler's own position)
Ostler is careful: "we should never worship an angel." The difference is that the New Testament *does* give worship to the Son, because the Father "has chosen to exalt him and deliver the kingdom to him and given him the glory that would merit worship," and because Father and Son mutually indwell. The angel disanalogy guards against reducing Christ to a mere messenger. See [[Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling]]. [^4]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
The Trinitarian can agree the *angel* is not God yet argue the comparison cuts the other way: precisely because Scripture so emphatically forbids worship of even exalted angels, the *worship the Son receives* (and accepts) signals he is on the Creator side of the line, not an honored agent. Ostler concedes the worship but relocates its ground in exaltation-by-the-Father and perichoresis rather than in numerical identity — which leaves the dispute on the meaning of "one God," handled at [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]].

## Best Quotes

> "It's like the angel in Revelation when he appears and says, 'I am the first and the last,' and he presents himself as if he's God. But when John falls down to worship him, the angel is absolutely beside himself." [^1]

> "we see this very expressly in revelation where we have this notion of a messenger who bears the divine name appears as God" [^3]

> "Should never worship an angel. Here's the distinction. The son is given worship throughout the New Testament. And here's why..." [^4]
