# Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization

#type/claim

## The Claim

The standard Latter-day Saint account treats creation *ex nihilo* as a symptom of the apostasy — the "Hellenization" of Christianity, Greek philosophy creeping into the faith. [[Brian Thomassen]] argues this is **backwards**. *Ex nihilo* is not a Greek idea at all; it is *antithetical* to the Greek model. Greek thought, from [[Parmenides]] onward, is founded on *ex nihilo nihil fit* ("nothing comes from nothing"), and it allowed creation exactly two forms — **organization** (form imposed on pre-existing material) or **emanation** (something proceeding out of a prior being) — *neither* of which is creation from nothing. Therefore the doctrine cannot have come from the Greeks. "The Greeks are not the bad guys here." It was manufactured *inside* the Christian space in the second century (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]), as a deliberate break from the Greek axiom, in order to make God maximally superior to and free from his creation.[^1][^2]

This sharpens the broader LDS case: *ex nihilo* is an absurdity sitting at the root of creedal theology, and the Restoration simply returns to what every ancient culture already assumed — that there was always a *something*.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Greeks invented logic, and logic forbids "nothing from nothing"
Thomassen grounds the argument in [[Parmenides]], "the creator of logic": the very first principle of every later logical system is *ouden ex oudenos* — nothing can come from nothing, so "there must be a something from the beginning. Otherwise, nothing else can come." Negative causation is impossible. This Parmenidean premise then "informs everything else" in Greek thought, which is precisely why *ex nihilo* is foreign to it.[^1]

### 2. Greek creation has exactly two options — and ex nihilo is not one
Working forward from Parmenides, Thomassen lays out a derivation argument: anything that exists is either *necessary* (uncaused) or *derived* from something prior, and an infinite regress of derived beings is impossible, so derivation must terminate in something uncaused. Derivation itself takes only two forms:
- **Organization** — e.g. building a ship, which requires pre-existing wood that is then ordered.
- **Emanation** — e.g. a web proceeding from a spider; the product comes *out of* a prior being.

"Those are the only two models that are possible within a logical [framework]... from Parmenides all the way forward." And "neither of them is *ex nihilo*."[^2][^3]

### 3. The "out of nothing" idiom was never metaphysical (Xenophon)
The strongest opposing proof-text, [[2 Maccabees 7.28|2 Maccabees 7:28]], is defused by a Greek parallel that *predates* it. [[Xenophon]] (5th c. BC, a student of Socrates read as widely as Plato and Aristotle) wrote that parents "bring forth their children out of nothing" — using the *exact same phrasing*. No Greek reader took this as an ontological claim; it just means "a thing is now here that wasn't there before," like a baby or a table. "It is not a philosophical statement about being." So the same idiom in 2 Maccabees carries no *ex nihilo* freight either. (The vault documents the same conclusion via Gary Anderson's parallel and the scholarly consensus in [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]]; Thomassen's contribution is naming Xenophon as the specific, pre-Maccabean source.)[^4][^5]

### 4. No ancient culture had "nothing" — so ex nihilo is an outlier needing a special origin
Every theogony — Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, Hittite, Egyptian, Greek — begins from a primordial *something* that gets organized; "chaos is never created." Hesiod's *Theogony* opens "at the first chaos came to be," with chaos preceding the gods (see [[Hesiod]]). Hebrew lacks any word for absolute absence (*ʾein*, *ʾefes*, *tohu* all denote unavailability, insignificance, or formlessness — never a void). Because *ex nihilo* matches *nothing* in the ancient world, it cannot be inherited from that world; it has to have been *invented* later and for a specific reason.[^6][^7]

### 5. The actual source is a Christian over-correction, not Greek influence
The doctrine arose in the second century from figures clustered around Antioch — the Gnostic [[Basilides]], then [[Tatian]] and [[Theophilus of Antioch]] — who argued God must be *superior* to and unconstrained by his creation, so any co-eternal material (which would require a "design" and thereby limit God) had to be denied. Thomassen's summary: "it's not coming out of Greek philosophy. In fact it's isolated from Greek philosophy... we now need to explain Genesis 1 and we're just going to say the most literal thing." (The late date and anti-Gnostic motive are covered in [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]; what this note adds is that the move was a *departure from*, not an *importation of*, Greek rationality — and that the same "most literal thing" instinct later produces the Trinity.)[^8][^9]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

The traditional reply: *ex nihilo* is not Greek *philosophy*, granted — but it is the natural product of **biblical revelation correcting** Greek philosophy. Scripture asserts a real beginning and an absolutely sovereign Creator; a God who merely shuffles pre-existing matter is diminished, sharing the stage with an eternal rival principle. The Greek axiom "nothing comes from nothing" is exactly the pagan limitation that revelation overturns. So the second-century fathers were not *inventing* an absurdity; they were drawing out the implication of monotheism — that God is the source of *everything*, matter included — using Greek vocabulary to articulate a non-Greek truth. That the formulation post-dates the New Testament no more makes it false than the word "Trinity" post-dating it does.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **The biblical text it claims to "draw out" says the opposite.** Genesis 1 opens with pre-existing *tohu wa-bohu* and *tehom* that God does not create but orders (see [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]), and *bara* means "organize," not generate from nothing (see [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]]). There is no scriptural "implication" to draw out — the implication runs the other way.
2. **"Correcting" the excluded-middle is just adopting an irrationality.** To "overturn" *ex nihilo nihil fit* is to assert negative causation, which the Greeks rightly classed as impossible. Calling this a higher revelation rather than a logical violation is the very move that, Thomassen argues, lands at "a baseline absurdity" under creedal theology (see [[The Tetralemma Test - Ex Nihilo Could Not Take Root in the Rational Greek West]]).
3. **The motive is documented and apologetic, not exegetical.** The originators argue *against* the prevailing view to maximize God's freedom and superiority over matter — a polemical need, not a reading of a text (see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]).
4. **It still concedes the historical point.** Admitting the doctrine had to be *articulated* in the second century, against the consensus, grants exactly what the claim asserts: it was not there before, and it did not come from the Greeks.
