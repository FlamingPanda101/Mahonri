# Who Joins New Religious Movements

## The Stereotype
NRM recruits are "weak, vulnerable, gullible individuals being taken
advantage of by cunning cult leaders."

## The Data

### Demographics
- **Overwhelmingly middle-class**, educated, white.
- **University students** — bright, engaged, capable young people.
- Disproportionately from **secular, non-religious backgrounds**.
- **Secular Jews** joined at rates vastly out of proportion to their
  population percentage — but always from very secular homes where
  there was "very little practice, very little real sense of how they
  would pursue a spiritual aspect to their lives."

### The Contradiction
Dawson: "In what way is this privileged class of young people weak and
vulnerable and gullible?" The stereotype doesn't match the data.

## Why They Actually Join

### Existential Needs, Not Pathology
The consistent finding across decades of research: people join NRMs
because something is **missing**, not because something is **wrong**
with them.

Key drivers:
1. **Need for certainty** — in a world of "too many choices, too many
   options, too much greyness"
2. **Identity** — finding who you are and where you belong
3. **Meaning** — "people's needs for deep and serious meaning in their
   lives, for value, for some kind of significance to their existence"
4. **Moral clarity** — wanting to be aligned with what's right

Dawson: "If someone's engaging in something you think is outlandish, the
answer is rarely that they are outlandish or rarely that they are
psychologically damaged. The answer is almost always: here's a person
with some real need that's not getting met."

### Normal Psychological Processes
People don't join through brainwashing — they join through:
- Curiosity
- Inquisitiveness
- Doing their own research
- Slowly finding ideas cogent and appealing

Dawson: "People often come to what appear to be extreme positions through
very normal processes."

### The "Extreme = Clear" Dynamic
In a world of moral murkiness, more extreme perspectives offer more
clarity. This is a **feature** that attracts people, not evidence of
manipulation. "Regrettably, in a world with a great deal of murkiness,
the more extreme perspectives are the ones that have the clarity."

## The Departure Pattern
- 90% leave voluntarily within 2 years.
- Many don't reject the ideas — they just say "I can go back to being a
  normal person. I might keep meditating, but I'm going back to school."
- The need was temporary and satisfied — once their identity issues were
  worked through and they matured, the intensive involvement was no
  longer necessary.

## Apologetic Application
- LDS converts fit the NRM profile: educated, seeking meaning, from
  diverse backgrounds.
- Joining an unusual religion is a **normal** human response to
  existential needs — not a sign of weakness or pathology.
- The "brainwashed" slur collapses against the demographic reality of
  who actually joins and the 90% voluntary departure rate.
