# The Coming of Elias Before the Second Coming Was an Early Christian Doctrine

#type/claim

## The Claim

Multiple early Church Fathers expected a literal return of Elijah ("Elias") as a forerunner before the Second Coming of Christ, fulfilling [[Malachi 4.5-6|Malachi 4:5]]. This expectation has since "died out entirely" from mainstream Christian dogma — Catholic Answers, for example, treats Malachi's prophecy as already fulfilled in John the Baptist and admits no future forerunner-prophet before Christ's return. [[Joseph Smith]] restored the doctrine: [[Angel Moroni]] quoted Malachi 4:5 to him at his first appearance, and [[D&C 110]] records Elijah's literal return in the Kirtland Temple. The panel argues this is the least defensible doctrine for a fabricator to invent, because it had already been "stripped out of other faiths" and is plainly scriptural — Joseph would have had to retrieve it from early patristic sources not taught by anyone around him.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### "Elias" is a forerunner *role*, not only a person
Luke 1:17 says John the Baptist would go before Christ "in the spirit and power of Elias" — making *Elias* a heraldic title that can be borne by more than one forerunner. This dissolves the apparent contradiction in [[Matthew 17.10-13|Matthew 17:11-13]], where Jesus both affirms "Elias truly shall come first and restore all things" (future) and identifies the Elias who "is come already" as John the Baptist. One Elias (John) preceded the First Coming; another precedes the Second.[^2]

### Hippolytus of Rome (c. 200 AD)
> "The Saviour is to be manifested again at the end of the world as judge. It is a matter of course that His forerunners must appear first, as He says by Malachi and the angel... These then shall come and proclaim the manifestation of Christ that is to be from heaven."[^3]

[[Hippolytus of Rome]] expects *forerunners* (plural) performing signs to call men to repentance before Christ's return as judge — citing the same Malachi prophecy.

### Justin Martyr (to Trypho)
> "Shall we not suppose that the word of God has proclaimed that Elijah shall be the precursor of the great and terrible day, that is, of His second advent?"[^4]

[[Justin Martyr]] explicitly assigns Elijah's coming to the *second* advent, not the first.

### A broad patristic chain
The panel (quoting [[Barry Bickmore]]) reports that Victorinus, [[Methodius of Olympus]], [[Cyprian of Carthage]], Lactantius, [[Jerome]], [[Augustine]], and Theophylact "all expressed the belief that Elijah would come to restore all things before the second coming of the Lord" — i.e., it was widespread across the first centuries, not an isolated opinion.[^5]

### The undesigned Passover coincidence
[[D&C 110]] places Elijah's appearance on 3 April 1836 — Passover — the very feast at which Jews keep an open chair and cup for Elijah's return. The panel treats Joseph's placement of the event on the traditional Jewish day of Elijah's coming as a coincidence a fabricating "1830s farmer" is unlikely to have engineered.[^6]

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

Mainstream (esp. Catholic) Christianity does not deny Malachi 4:5; it reads the prophecy as *already fulfilled*. Jesus' own words in [[Matthew 17.10-13|Matthew 17:12-13]] ("Elias is come already... the disciples understood that he spake unto them of John the Baptist") are taken to close the matter: John the Baptist *was* the promised Elijah, so no further literal Elijah is expected before the Second Coming. On this reading the early Fathers' future-Elijah expectation was speculative eschatology — never dogmatically defined, and reasonably set aside — not a lost essential of the faith. The Catholic claim that "the deposit of faith has been given" and is complete entails no need for a future forerunner-prophet.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **Matthew 17 itself distinguishes two comings.** In the same breath that Jesus identifies John as the Elias who "is come already," He uses the *future* tense — "Elias truly shall come first and restore all things." If John exhausted the prophecy, the future clause is redundant. Luke 1:17's "in the spirit and power of Elias" supports reading Elias as a repeatable forerunner-role, leaving a future Elias open.
2. **The Fathers were not idiosyncratic.** Hippolytus, Justin, and a chain through Augustine and Jerome held the future-forerunner view across centuries and regions — the burden is on the modern position to explain why a once-broad expectation vanished.
3. **The reinterpretation is convenient, not exegetical.** As the panel notes, the modern move is not to delete the verses but to say "that's not what those verses mean" — a retreat to a fulfilled reading precisely where the older literal reading would require continuing prophecy, which a closed-canon, closed-revelation system cannot accommodate. This fits the [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event|gradual-apostasy]] pattern: a doctrine quietly reinterpreted out of existence.
4. **A fabricator would not restore an abandoned doctrine.** The apologetic force is that reviving an *unfashionable*, already-discarded belief — and pinning it to the correct Jewish feast — is the opposite of what a con artist optimizing for plausibility would do.
