# Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer

## The Point
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that the restored gospel's rejection of [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation ex nihilo]] opens **three** legitimate ways for Latter-day Saints to understand God's relation to the universe — and each handles the problem of evil differently. They are: (1) **finitism** — "God after creation," a being who grew up mastering a pre-existing universe; (2) **process / panentheism** — "God with the universe," who has only *persuasive* (non-coercive) power over self-creative intelligences; and (3) the **organizer** view — "God before the universe," who organizes the eternal order and has been God from all eternity. Ostler holds all three are "open options for Mormons," though he defends the third. The taxonomy is itself an apologetic asset: LDS theology can host multiple coherent theodicies where classical theism is locked into one all-determining God. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **Finitism ("God after creation").** God "grew up in an already existing universe to be God" by mastering its laws — a "super-scientist... who figured out at some point that being loving was far superior." *Virtue:* it "simply dissolves the problem of evil" — God permits evil because he is "overwhelmed," lacking the power to stop it. *Costs Ostler raises:* it "neuters God," strains the axioms of science (how does this body travel faster than light, escape a black hole?), and guts prayer — "why would you pray to a being who can't do any better than you?" And if you re-inflate God's power to rescue prayer, you "just recreate the problem of evil." [^2][^3]
2. **Process / panentheism ("God with the universe").** Built on Joseph Smith's *intelligences* and on pan-psychism (everything is conscious to some degree; we are organized conglomerations of intelligences). God "does not have coercive power, he has only persuasive power"; he "lures" reality toward his will, and "it may be that the universe doesn't follow." Developed by Alfred North Whitehead and Charles Hartshorne; among Latter-day Saints it tracks Orson Pratt and B.H. Roberts. *Virtues:* dissolves evil (God can't coercively stop it), fits biological evolution and quantum physics, and gives prayer real meaning (we *join* God to persuade reality). *Fatal cost:* "could God guarantee victory over evil on this view? It can't be guaranteed" — free intelligences might never come around, contrary to scripture. Ostler held this view "for about 15 to 20 years." [^4][^5]
3. **Organizer ("God before the universe").** "God actually does what the scriptures say he does — he organizes the order in the universe," and "has been God from all eternity." This view "has more in common with classic Christianity, Judaism, and Islam than the other two." Natural law is explained by *divine concurrence*: a thing's power to be organized depends on **both** God's organizing power **and** the thing's own eternal "natural propensities" (an Aristotelian view) — two hydrogen and one oxygen make water because that potential is "inherent in them from all eternity," yet "there's no water without God's organizing power." This is the platform for Ostler's [[The Agape Theodicy - Evil as the Arena for Learning Divine Love|agape theodicy]]. [^6][^7]
4. **A shared root.** All three "reject creatio ex nihilo." The first two make the universe *recalcitrant* (evil arises because the world resists God); the third makes the world a *lovingly ordered* arena (evil arises from agency and chaos within a good design). [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "God is just another really really smart being in the universe who exists within the universe itself and has kind of figured out the game... there isn't a lot of difference between God and just a really really smart alien." [^2]

> "God does not have coercive power, he has only persuasive power, and he works on things over time to bring them to be what he wants them to be — he lures them... it may be that the universe doesn't follow God's persuasive power." [^4]

> "The order in the universe is due to God's organizing activity. God organized the universe and God has been God from all eternity." [^6]

## LDS Connection
This taxonomy *is* the structure of Latter-day Saint philosophy of religion on God and creation. All three options presuppose the bedrock LDS commitments: matter and [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency|intelligences]] are co-eternal and uncreated, and God is therefore an *organizer*, not an ex-nihilo creator (see [[Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism - The Hidden Determinism of Classical Theism]]). Each model preserves genuine agency and a [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|constrained God]], which is exactly why all three can answer the problem of evil where classical theism cannot — the shared LDS premise that God did not author the will is what [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil|solves the problem of evil]]. Ostler models intellectual humility the vault values: he presents finitism (cf. John Stuart Mill's finite God), process theology, and his own organizer view as live options, insisting it is "okay" for Latter-day Saints to weigh "three separate possible options within Mormon thought" rather than demand a single settled metaphysic. His own preference — the organizer God — is the one most continuous with scripture and the one on which his agape theodicy is built.
