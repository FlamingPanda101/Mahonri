# The Who or What Test for the Word God

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The Trinitarian says "God is three persons but one God" and presses oneness texts ("the Lord our God is one Lord," "God is one") as proof that the Father, Son, and Spirit are numerically **one being**. The whole Trinity case, on Hansen's reading, hinges on the single ambiguous word *God*. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The diagnostic move.** Every time *God* appears, ask: are you using it to name a **who** (a person) or a **what** (a nature/essence)? In the standard Trinity diagram the three *persons* — Father, Son, Spirit — are explicitly the *whos*. The "God" in the center therefore cannot itself be a who (the whos are already accounted for); it must be a *what*. (Evidence: strong — this just reads the diagram's own logic. The diagram literally labels the corners "persons" and the center "God.") [^2]
2. **The forced clarification wins the debate.** When the Trinitarian says "God is one," ask whether that "one" is one *who* or one *what*. If "a who," ask *which* one of the three — and any answer is modalism or denies one person's deity. If "a what," the LDS side simply agrees: "there is only one Godhead." Either way the same-being conclusion never follows. [^3]
3. **Steelman.** The sophisticated Trinitarian (and Hansen grants "the theologians who actually know the difference between a who and a what") will say *God* names neither a Boethian person nor a bare nature but a *concrete particular* — the one divine substance that just *is* tri-personal, where "person" means a subsistent relation, not a separate center of consciousness. On this view the who/what dichotomy is a false binary: the *ousia* is one concrete thing subsisting in three *hypostases*. The LDS reply is that this only relocates the equivocation: if the one *ousia* is a concrete particular with a single will and mind, you have modalism; if it has three wills/minds, you have three beings; "subsistent relation" is a label for the mystery, not a solution. (Evidence: this is the live disagreement; the LDS side is making a coherence objection, not a proof.) [^4]
4. **Linguistic grounding.** Natures (essence, being, substance) answer *what* something is — the collection of its properties — and are abstract, like the number seven; a *person* is the concrete instantiation. "I'm Jacob" (who) vs. "I'm a human" (what). Confusing the two is what lets the word *God* slide between "the divine nature" (shared by three) and "a single divine individual." [^5]

5. **Ostler's independent corroboration.** [[Blake Ostler]] poses the identical test — "When you say God, are you talking about a who or a what?" — and adds the sharper version: "Is the *Trinity* a who or a what?" His answer is that the Trinity "is not a who; the Trinity is a thing or a *what*," so calling it "God" without specifying which of the three persons is meant is "a category mistake." He uses *Godhead* for the *what* and *God the Father / Son / Holy Ghost* for the three *whos* (personages). [^6][^7] See [[Personages vs Subsistent Relations - What Person Means]].

## Best Quotes

> "If you're dealing with the Trinity, it all comes down to the word God and what they mean by that term." [^1]

> "Is the Trinity a who or is the Trinity a what? ... the trinity is not a who. The trinity is a thing or a what." — Blake Ostler [^7]

> "You can ask this question, is God a what or a who? ... Considering the who's are the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, what does this mean God is? He can't be a who. So, he must be a what." [^2]

> "All you got to do is get that clarification of when they're talking about God as a who or as a what. ... As soon as he clarifies that, it's over." [^3]
