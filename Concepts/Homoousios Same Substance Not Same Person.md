# Homoousios Same Substance Not Same Person

## The Claim
Day offers an irenic LDS reading: Latter-day Saints can affirm that the Father and Son are *homoousios* — of the **same substance/stuff** — while denying that they are the same *person*. The Arian-vs-orthodox fight turned on a single iota (*homoousios*, "same substance," vs. *homoiousios*, "like substance"), and Day argues both sides were partly talking past each other.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The iota distinction (rating: strong; historical-linguistic)
At Nicaea the contested terms were *homoousios* (same *ousia*/being) vs. *homoiousios* (like being) — differing by the inserted iota. The council settled on "same substance, three persons (*hypostases*)." Day explains the vocabulary clearly. [^1]

### 2. LDS scripture supports a "same stuff" reading (rating: moderate; restoration canon)
Day points to [[D&C 88|D&C 88:11-13]] (the "light... which is in all things... the law by which all things are governed," proceeding from God's presence) and [[D&C 93|D&C 93]] as describing Father and Son made "of the same stuff" — so "in that sense we are Trinitarian; we do believe they're *homoousios*." Indeed humans are "made of the same stuff" too. [^2]

### 3. The real disagreement is personhood, not substance (rating: moderate; Day's thesis)
"I believe that the Father and the Son are *homoousios* — of the same substance — but I don't believe they are the same person." Day suspects the creedal "three persons, one substance" may be closer to LDS belief than usually assumed, *if* one drops the further Trinitarian claim that the Father is unembodied. [^3]

### 4. Butler's sharpening (steelman of the Trinitarian side) (rating: moderate)
Butler corrects Day's irenicism: the creed does *not* mean "we're both made of meat" (generic shared nature) but "it's the same meat" — numerically one identical essence. By contrast Day means the Father, the Son, and humans are all "star stuff" but *different blobs* of it. So LDS "same substance" is actually closer to *homoiousios* (similar) than the creed's *homoousios* (numerically identical) — a real, not merely verbal, difference. Butler also notes the Father is embodied in some Vatican/Michelangelo art, complicating the "Father is unembodied" assumption. [^4]

### 5. Steelman noted within the source
Day himself flags that when you press Trinitarians on how three persons share one numerically identical substance while the Son is embodied and the Father is not, "a lot of them are going to say it's a mystery" — which loops back to [[Nicene and Athanasian Creeds Apply Labels Not Explanations]]. The irenic "we both believe *homoousios*" move only works if "substance" is read generically, which the creed resists.

## Best Quotes

> "I believe that the Father and the Son are *homoousios*. They are of the same substance, but I don't believe that they are the same person." [^3]

> "What they mean is it's the same — not like we're both made of meat. It's the same meat." [^4]

> "We're made of the same stuff, Father and Son. That's in you. You are made of God the Father's stuff." [^2]
