# The Stolen Concept - Atheists Borrow from God to Argue Against Him

## The Point
Turek's central thesis is that the New Atheists "are using aspects of reality to argue against God that wouldn't exist if atheism were true."[^1] When atheists appeal to science, reason, morality, and the reality of evil to disprove God, they are tacitly helping themselves to features of reality that only a theistic universe can supply. In effect, "they are stealing from God in order to argue against Him."[^1] Theism can ground these realities; atheistic materialism, Turek argues, cannot — so the atheist's strongest weapons turn out to be on loan from the very worldview he is attacking.[^2]

## Evidence
Turek's framing comes largely from letting atheists speak for themselves. Richard Dawkins concedes "it is pretty hard to defend absolutist morals on grounds other than religious ones,"[^3] yet still wants to call cheating, child-rearing in religion, or the God of the Old Testament genuinely *wrong* — which presupposes the objective standard his materialism denies. In the Foreword, Ravi Zacharias sharpens the same move into a chain: to call something *evil* you must assume *good*; to assume good you must assume a *moral law*; a moral law requires a *moral lawgiver* — "but this moral lawgiver is precisely who atheists are trying to disprove."[^4] Zacharias likens it to "stepping on a bridge to cross a chasm, all the while believing there is no bridge": the atheist reaches an "unbridgeable chasm, given their starting point," and silently builds a bridge from borrowed theistic capital.[^5] Turek notes that the reality of evil cuts against the atheist twice: "From where do they even get the category of evil? And second, how do they break its stranglehold?"[^6] The aspects of reality are so woven into common sense that "many atheists seem to take them for granted. But they simply can't exist if atheism is true."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "atheists are using aspects of reality to argue against God that wouldn't exist if atheism were true... In effect, they are stealing from God in order to argue against Him." [^1]

> "These aspects of reality are so much part of our common sense that many atheists seem to take them for granted. But they simply can't exist if atheism is true. Theism can explain them, but atheism cannot." [^7]

> "It is to the more serious 'borrowing' of this sort that Frank Turek points—and rightly brands it 'stealing.'" [^8]

## LDS Connection
The "stolen concept" strategy is a natural ally for Latter-day Saint apologetics: the Restoration likewise insists that morality is real and binding, that the cosmos is rational and intelligible, that intelligence and agency are genuine, and that these point beyond blind matter to God. Alma's teleological reasoning that "all things denote there is a God" (Alma 30:44) runs along the same grain as Turek's argument that reality presupposes its Author (see [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]]). The structural critique of atheism's incoherence is shared ground (see [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]], [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]]).

One honest qualification: Turek's argument is fundamentally about *grounding* — that these realities require a transcendent Source. Latter-day Saint theology agrees there is such a Source but locates moral law, intelligence, and matter as *co-eternal* realities that God organizes and works within rather than creates from nothing (Abraham 3:18–22; D&C 93:29). So the LDS reader affirms Turek's "no God, no objective morality" instinct while parting company with the classical-theist assumption (developed in his Causality chapter) that grounding requires *creation ex nihilo*. The borrowing critique stands; the metaphysics underneath it differs. See [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]].
