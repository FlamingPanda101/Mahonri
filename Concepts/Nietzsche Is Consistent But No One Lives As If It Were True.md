# Nietzsche Is Consistent But No One Lives As If It Were True

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] credits [[Friedrich Nietzsche]] as the most *serious* atheist precisely because he is rationally consistent: if there is no God, then morality is arbitrary, "it's all just power, and it is what it is." Nietzsche doesn't dodge the conclusion. But the very feature that makes him consistent exposes the problem — "no one like lives that way." And, Hansen argues, that gap is itself evidence against the belief: "people don't believe how they act." If you cannot live as though your worldview were true, that is reason to doubt you really believe it.[^1]

## Evidence
Hansen treats livability as a test of sincerity, not just comfort. Nietzsche's honesty earns intellectual respect: "at least he has a consistent argument... it is consistent, like rationally, even if you don't live that way."[^1] Yet the universal failure to actually live by arbitrary-power nihilism — to treat all values as mere assertions of will — suggests the position is held only verbally. People keep loving, keeping promises, condemning cruelty, and demanding justice, all of which presuppose values that the Nietzschean framework says are illusions. The behavior betrays a belief the mouth denies. This is the same diagnosis developed in [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]]: consistent atheism terminates in a place no one can inhabit.

## Best Quotes
> "The most serious atheism is like Friedrich Nietzsche — he just recognizes this and says yeah it's all arbitrary, it's all just power, and it is what it is. And the thing is, no one like lives that way." [^1]

> "At least he has a consistent argument... it is consistent rationally, even if you don't live that way — which you can say, well, there's an inconsistency: do you really believe it if you don't? People don't believe how they act." [^1]

## LDS Connection
There is charity here for the honest doubter: Hansen praises Nietzsche's refusal to pretend. The restored gospel answers the unlivability not by demanding people suppress their moral instincts but by vindicating them — those instincts are real perceptions of a real moral order, because every person is a co-eternal intelligence and morality is grounded in eternal law and the law of love, not in arbitrary power. Where Nietzsche says our deepest commitments are masks over the will to power, the Restoration says they are the soul recognizing truth. The fact that no one can live as a consistent nihilist is, for the believer, a quiet witness that we were made for something the naturalist cannot supply.
