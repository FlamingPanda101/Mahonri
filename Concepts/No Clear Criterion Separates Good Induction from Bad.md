# No Clear Criterion Separates Good Induction from Bad

## The Point
One of the best ways to feel the contrast between deduction and induction, [[Philosophy Tube]] argues, is to notice that **deduction is "complete"** — "it is clear what makes a good deduction. Namely that the premises entail the conclusion" — **whereas "it isn't really clear what makes a good induction."** [^1] We can recognize particular inductions as good or bad (the cannonball inference is good; "all swans ever observed are white, therefore all swans are white" is bad), but we lack a clean general rule that sorts them — the classic **problem of induction**. [^1][^2]

## Argument
1. Deduction has a clear success condition: a good deduction is one whose premises *entail* the conclusion. The standard is fully specified. [^1]
2. Induction lacks such a clear condition: "it isn't really clear what makes a good induction." There is no equally crisp rule stating when premises render a conclusion well-supported. [^1]
3. We nonetheless have reliable intuitions about *cases*: the cannonball argument (observed to fall 99 times → will fall the 100th) "is clearly good induction." [^1][^3]
4. And we recognize clear failures: "all swans ever observed are white, therefore all swans are white is clearly a bad induction." [^1] (Historically, black swans existed in Australia — observation of a finite sample never guarantees a universal generalization.)
5. Steelman of the worry: cases (3) and (4) have the *same logical form* — generalizing from observed instances to all instances — so whatever distinguishes the good one from the bad one is not the form of the argument itself. Absent a stated criterion, the line between reliable and unreliable induction remains unarticulated. [^1]
6. This does not impugn induction's usefulness (it remains "a fantastic guide to truth," see [[Deduction Is Top-Down and Induction Is Bottom-Up Reasoning]]); it marks a genuine open problem in epistemology about *justifying* inductive inference, relevant to any account of how we know empirical truths. See [[Epistemology Is the Account You Give for How You Know]].

## Best Quotes
> "One of the best ways to appreciate the contrast between deduction and induction is to recognize that deduction is complete. It is clear what makes a good deduction. Namely that the premises entail the conclusion." [^1]

> "But it isn't really clear what makes a good induction. The Cannonball argument is clearly good induction, but all swans ever observed are white. Therefore all swans are white is clearly a bad induction." [^1]
