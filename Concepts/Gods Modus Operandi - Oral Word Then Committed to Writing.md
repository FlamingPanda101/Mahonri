# God's Modus Operandi — Oral Word Then Committed to Writing

## The Claim
[[Anthony Rogers]] argues God has a consistent, unbroken **modus operandi**: He first puts His words in the mouths of prophets who preach *orally*, then commands them to **commit the word to writing** so it can serve "in perpetuity" as the objective standard for distant generations. Therefore the existence of an oral phase does not refute sola scriptura; the written deposit is the *terminus* of God's established pattern, and the apostles followed suit — always preaching *from* the scriptures and then writing.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The OT pattern: speak, then write for future generations — rate: strong (as pattern), inferential (as proof of consolidation)
Rogers stacks texts: "Write this in the book as a memorial" (Exodus 17); "take this book of the law and place it beside the ark ... as a witness" (Deut 31); "go write it on a tablet ... that it may serve in the time to come as a witness forever" (Isaiah 30:8); "this will be written for the generations to come" (Psalm 102:18); Habakkuk 2:2 ("make it plain ... that he may run who reads it").[^1] The pattern is real and well-evidenced. **Steelman of Horn:** the pattern shows *writing preserves* the word; it does *not* show revelation was ever *restricted* to the written word, nor that public revelation would cease. Horn's standing demand — "cite a passage saying all future revelation will be confined to the written word alone" — is left unmet. See [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]].

### 2. The apostles preached only from the scriptures — rate: moderate, over-generalized
Rogers cites Acts 8 (Philip "beginning from this scripture"), Acts 17 (Paul "reasoned from the scriptures" three Sabbaths), Acts 18 (Apollos "demonstrating from the scriptures"), Acts 26 (Paul to Agrippa: "nothing but what Moses and the prophets said").[^2] **Steelman of Horn (strong here):** [[2 Thessalonians 2.15]] and 2 Thess 2:5–6 show Paul taught things orally (e.g., what restrains the man of lawlessness) that he *never wrote down* and that scholars cannot reconstruct — directly contradicting "the apostles only preached what was in scripture." Rogers' generalization over-reads narrative summaries of synagogue evangelism as a universal rule.

### 3. The apostles wrote by the Spirit's impulse — rate: moderate
Rogers infers the apostles must have written under the Spirit because they were not "rogue individuals"; Paul says "what I am writing is the Lord's commandment" (1 Cor 14:37), and Jesus commanded John to "write" in Revelation.[^3] **Steelman of Horn:** Horn's qualifier was precise — "during his *earthly ministry* Jesus never told anyone to write anything down." Rogers' reply that Jesus "by analogy" commanded the others to write is an *inference*, which Rogers candidly classes as "a logical inference, which is part of sola scriptura." Horn presses that no actual command exists, so the consolidation thesis is assumed, not proven.

## Best Quotes
> "God's modus operandi in scripture is to put forth his word, put it into the mouths of his prophets who preached it and then command them to commit it to writing."[^4]

> "This is God's modus operandi, his established pattern of communicating his truth to future generations ... It is a consistent pattern, an unbroken pattern. There's no indication that this changed or turned around."[^1]

> "Toward the end of the book of Acts ... Paul said to King Agrippa, 'I stand to this day testifying ... stating nothing but what Moses and the prophets said was going to take place.'"[^2]
