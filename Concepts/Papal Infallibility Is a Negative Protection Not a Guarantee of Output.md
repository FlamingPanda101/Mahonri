# Papal Infallibility Is a Negative Protection Not a Guarantee of Output

#type/claim

## The Claim
Late in the dialogue [[Hayden Carroll]] presses a sharply-framed LDS objection: **no human source can be infallible**, including the biblical text, because fallibility is the default for anything that "touches the human person, goes to the human mind." His argument is from free will — *unless God controls a person like a puppet, overriding their will, anything they produce is capable of error.* He runs the same lever he uses on Protestants who claim an infallible Bible written by free human authors: "If you believe God literally possessed Paul and it was God writing, maybe you could have an argument — but nobody wants to say that, because we have free will." To Carroll, calling the magisterium (or scripture) infallible is therefore a "category" mistake and is itself *unfalsifiable*: "the dogmas — you can't falsify them."[^1][^2]

[[Trent Horn]]'s reply reframes what *infallibility* means. It is **a negative protection, not a positive guarantee of output** — it prevents formally teaching error; it does not supply the right answer on demand, and so it requires no override of the will. His analogy:

> Suppose the Pope were infallible at calculus, and is given a 100-question exam. What is the *fewest* number he could get right? The answer is **zero** — because infallibility only protects from *asserting* error. The Pope could look at every problem, say "I don't have the right answer for this," and simply *decline to answer*. He will not put down a wrong answer; he is *not* guaranteed to put down a right one.[^3][^4]

The theological payoff: infallibility "explains why we'd have liked some theological formulas in certain past parts of church history" that the Church never positively supplied — "the Holy Spirit doesn't necessarily positively provide them." It only **prevents** the Pope or bishops from *formally and officially teaching in error*. Crucially, this dissolves Carroll's puppet objection: God need not turn anyone "into a little puppet or marionette." The protected person still **freely chooses** the truth they affirm; God merely ensures they "will not fall into some kind of error" when teaching definitively. And — Horn adds elsewhere — to say the Church "*can* teach infallibly" does not mean all its teachings are infallible.[^5][^6]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
**Steelman of Horn.** The negative-protection model is a real and load-bearing distinction in Catholic theology, and it neutralizes the specific objection Carroll raised:
1. **It targets the exact premise of the puppet argument.** Carroll's objection assumes infallibility = *God forcing correct output*, which would indeed require overriding the will. Horn's reply is that this is a misdescription: a *negative* charism constrains only the failure mode (asserting falsehood), leaving the positive act free. The calculus case makes vivid that protection-from-error and compulsion-to-truth are logically distinct — you can have the former with zero of the latter (score: 0/100).[^3]
2. **It explains the historical data better.** If infallibility were a positive guarantee, the Church would have promptly produced every needed formula; instead doctrine develops slowly and many questions stay open for centuries. The negative model *predicts* exactly that pattern — silence and delay are permitted; only definitive error is barred. Carroll's framework has no comparable explanation for why a "puppet-controlled" magisterium would be so often silent.[^5]
3. **It rescues free will, which Carroll prizes.** Horn grants Carroll's own value — human freedom — and shows infallibility need not violate it. He even extends it to rational defects: "God can correct rational defects... so people don't make those errors," while Carroll insists this only works "if the person is willing and able to see it." The remaining disagreement is about whether grace can perfect the intellect without coercion, not about puppetry.[^7]
4. **Carroll's own concession.** Carroll, hearing the analogy, says: "I can see that, and I can appreciate that actually... that's a good analogy. By the way, your analogies are on point." He does not refute the *content*; he retreats to a different objection — that the *rule itself* ("who established that the charism works this way?") is baseless and circular, which relocates the dispute to **Church authority**, not to infallibility's coherence.[^8]

**Where Carroll keeps the pressure on.** Carroll's fallback is the move he makes throughout the conversation: even a *coherent* model is not an *evidenced* one. "I understand the concept as you've explained it... but it's baseless. There's no evidence for that, and it's circular — who said that? Who established that rule?" The negative-protection account is internally consistent, but its warrant traces back to the Church's claim of authority to define the charism — the very authority the [[A Living Magisterium Settles What Sola Scriptura Cannot|magisterium]] debate is about. So Horn has shown the doctrine is *not incoherent* and *not puppetry*, but (Carroll argues) has not yet shown it is *true* without presupposing the Church's authority — which both men agree ultimately bottoms out in the great-apostasy question.[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "Suppose the Pope is infallible at calculus, and he's given a calculus exam with 100 questions. What is the fewest number he will get correct? ... It's zero. Because the infallibility just protects from error."[^3]

> "The Holy Spirit doesn't necessarily positively provide them. It just prevents the Pope or the bishops from formally and officially teaching in error... God doesn't have to turn them into a little puppet or marionette."[^5]

> "The default position is that humans are fallible, therefore they cannot create infallible things — and to say otherwise is a theological claim that you have to at least demonstrate." — Hayden Carroll[^2]

> "When I say Catholicism can teach infallibly, that doesn't mean all its teachings are infallible."[^6]
