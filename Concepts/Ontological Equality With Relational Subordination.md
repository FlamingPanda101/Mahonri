# Ontological Equality With Relational Subordination

## The Claim
The Son's subordination to the Father is **relational**, not **ontological**. By *species/kind* (ontology) the Father and Son are equal — both fully divine, the same "rank." But *relationally*, the Son is dependent on the Father because he is the Son. The two distinctions resolve the texts where Jesus is "fully God" yet calls the Father greater.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The two senses of "equal" and "lesser"
Three human beings are ontologically equal (same species) yet, if one is the father of the other two, the sons are relationally dependent on him. Likewise the divine persons share the divine nature (equal in kind) while the Father holds relational primacy. This dissolves the apparent contradiction between "Jesus is fully God" and "the Father is greater." *(Strong: a clean and standard distinction; it is essentially the structure of "eternal relations of origin" in Trinitarian theology too.)* [^1]

### 2. The court has a chief justice
On the [[The Court Analogy for the Godhead|court]] model, all justices are equal *as* members of the highest body, but there is a chief justice — internal order without inequality of being. Maps directly onto relational subordination within ontological unity. *(Moderate-strong.)* [^2]

### 3. Trinitarians admit the same structure
Even Trinitarians say the Father, Son, and Spirit share one fully divine nature yet stand in relational order — so "ontological equality with relational subordination" is common ground; the only residual dispute is one *being* vs one *Godhead*. [[Cardon Ellis|Cardon]] cites Elder Holland: the Father and Son are one "in almost every way imaginable, except they're separate beings." *(Strong as a convergence argument.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
Trinitarians warn this slides toward **Arianism** if "relational subordination" implies the Son is *derived* or lesser in *being*. The orthodox version ("eternal relations of origin": the Son eternally begotten, not made) insists the order is purely relational *within one identical essence* — precisely to avoid ranking degrees of divinity. From that vantage the LDS model, by making the persons separate *beings*, has *more* than relational subordination — it has numerically distinct gods, which the Father-greater texts do not require and which monotheism forbids. The LDS reply: "separate beings united in one Godhead" is not tritheism (see [[Latter-day Saints Are Not Tritheists]]), and the biblical Father is consistently *a* God to the Son ([[Ephesians 1.3|Eph 1:3]]), which an "identical essence" reads only by qualification.

## Best Quotes
> "There is a relational subordination that exists within an ontological unity... we are all of the same species, so we're all equal in that sense, but we would be different if one of us was the father of the other two." [^1]

> "Elder Holland talks about how we believe that the Father and the Son are one in almost every way imaginable, except for they're separate beings — just like me and Jacob are separate beings." [^3]

> "Relationally, the father is above the son... because he is the father." [^4]
