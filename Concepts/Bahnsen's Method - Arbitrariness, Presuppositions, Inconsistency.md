# Bahnsen's Method - Arbitrariness, Presuppositions, Inconsistency

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] adopts the presuppositional method of [[Greg Bahnsen]] (with [[Cornelius Van Til]]) as a three-step procedure for engaging any worldview: (1) look for **arbitrariness** — opinions asserted with no basis, testable by asking "by what standard?"; (2) surface the unargued **philosophical presuppositions** a person has "just taken for granted"; and (3) expose the **internal inconsistencies** ("dialectical tensions") in those presuppositions.[^1][^2] The aim is not to refute every argument piecemeal but to show that the foundation a person reasons *from* is incoherent. This is the engine that produces [[The Sin of an Inconsistent Worldview]] and shares the structure of [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]].

## Evidence
1. **The three steps, in Bahnsen's words.** "Step one, look for arbitrariness — opinions that don't have any basis. Step two, look for underlying philosophical presuppositions that have not been argued, that have just been taken for granted. Step three, show the incoherence of that philosophical bias."[^2]
2. **Start with presuppositions.** "The key to engaging with others intellectually was to start by understanding what philosophical presuppositions form the foundation of a person's worldview and then to examine those presuppositions for inconsistencies."[^1]
3. **Worked example — Epicurean empiricism self-refutes.** Bahnsen takes Epicurus as "the patron saint of 20th-century America." The premise "all knowledge is perceptual in nature" is itself "not one that is known on the basis of perception" — so "here you have a philosophy that refutes itself."[^3] Compare [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting]].
4. **A second internal tension.** Epicurus said reality is made of imperceptibly small atoms, yet "could not possibly have perceived imperceptibly small bits of matter" — committing himself, on his own empiricist rule, to something he could never have perceived.[^4]
5. **The summary restatement.** Hansen closes by re-listing the three: test arbitrariness by asking "by what standard," look for the presuppositions "people bring to the table," then "look for inconsistencies in a person's worldview and its corresponding presuppositions."[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Step one, look for arbitrariness... Step two, look for underlying philosophical presuppositions that have not been argued... Step three, show the incoherence of that philosophical bias." — Greg Bahnsen [^2]

> "The premise that all knowledge is perceptual in nature is not one that is known on the basis of perception... here you have a philosophy that refutes itself." — Greg Bahnsen [^3]

## LDS Connection
A Latter-day Saint apologist can wield Bahnsen's method without importing Van Til's Reformed presuppositionalism wholesale: the transferable core is the demand that *every* worldview justify its own starting points rather than smuggle them in. Restoration epistemology is comfortable here because it openly names its presuppositions — that truth is knowable, that the Spirit witnesses, that intelligence is eternal — rather than pretending to start from a neutral, unargued empiricism. The self-refutation of "all knowledge is perceptual" parallels the LDS rejection of [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting|scientism]]: both expose a worldview's standard failing its own test. Hansen's own caution is that this tool is so powerful it should be used in public debate, not pastoral ministry (see [[Tear Down Worldviews in Public Debate Not Private Ministry]]).
