# No Old-Covenant-Style Prophecy Foretells the Restoration

## The Claim

Asked the closing question — *what would it take to believe God restored a corrected church through a latter-day prophet?* — [[Joe Heschmeyer]] (Catholic) and [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] (Protestant) converge on a single argument from the *internal logic of biblical prophecy*: **God always foretells His major redemptive-historical disruptions in advance, in concrete detail — yet Scripture contains no equivalent forward-pointing prophecy of a great apostasy and Restoration.** Therefore the very absence of such a prophecy is evidence against the LDS Restoration narrative.[^1]

Two complementary versions:
- **Heschmeyer — the "God would have told us" argument.** If Jesus intended the story *not* to reach its fullness in Him, and for the church to require later correction, "He would have told us... He wouldn't have promised the Spirit of truth to lead us into all truth... He wouldn't have promised that the gates of hell wouldn't prevail." Every great cataclysm of salvation history is explicitly and accurately prophesied — the **Babylonian captivity** (Nebuchadnezzar, the 70 years) and the **destruction of the temple** — "in a detailed way that even a non-believer can read." "There is no equivalent thing pointing forward to Joseph Smith." Heschmeyer notes that the Joseph Smith Translation, heavily quoting Genesis, *has to add* a prophecy of Joseph Smith "because there are none in the Bible."[^2]
- **Shafovaloff — the "divine mic drop" / closed-new-covenant argument.** Reading redemptive history as a series of *stacked covenants* anticipating the Messiah (the offspring promise of [[Genesis 3.14-19|Genesis 3:15]], Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic), Shafovaloff argues the Old Testament *prophesies forward* to a final prophet-priest-king. When Jesus arrives He "ratifies a fixed new covenant" and finishes the atoning work — "kind of like a giant divine mic drop." Crucially, while the Old Testament *anticipated* a new covenant and "a major deposit of revelation" to match it, "the New Testament... doesn't anticipate a *better* new covenant. It doesn't anticipate a *newer* new covenant. And therefore it's not fitting for us to expect a newer New Testament."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The premise about prophetic foretelling is well-grounded (rate: strong, biblical pattern).** Scripture does pre-announce its hinge events: Jeremiah names the 70-year exile; Daniel and the Gospels foretell the temple's fall; the prophets chain toward a coming Messiah. The challenge is genuinely felt — the analogy "you said Nebuchadnezzar would come for 70 years, and he did" sets a real evidential bar. An apostasy-and-restoration of the magnitude LDS claim (a centuries-long loss of the true church) is arguably a larger redemptive-historical event than the exile, so its prophetic silence is conspicuous.[^2]
2. **The "fullness in Christ" texts cut against a needed later corrective (rate: strong for the traditions' frame).** John 16:13 (the Spirit "will guide you into all truth"), Matthew 16:18 ("the gates of hell shall not prevail"), and the new-covenant finality language (Hebrews) are marshaled to say the church was promised *preservation*, not predicted *collapse-and-restart*. See [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]] for the LDS counter-reading of these same texts.[^1]
3. **The JST-prophecy point is a sharp internal jab (rate: rhetorically strong, evidentially modest).** That Joseph Smith's revision of Genesis *inserts* a prophecy naming Joseph (JST Genesis 50) is, for Heschmeyer, a tell: if the canonical text already foretold the Restoration, no insertion would be needed. (An LDS reply: the insertion is presented as *restoring* a "plain and precious" prophecy lost from the text, not inventing one — which is exactly the move the apostasy thesis predicts.)[^2]

### Steelman of the LDS reply ([[Jacob Hansen]])

Hansen largely *defers* this thread to the next night's dedicated apostasy debate, but the vault supplies the LDS rejoinders the argument must answer:
- **The premise can be turned around.** Old Testament precedent *does* include apostasy-and-restoration on a national scale — Israel's repeated falling-away and the loss/recovery of the Law under Josiah — so a later apostasy is a *coherent biblical category*, not an alien one. See [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category]].
- **"Plain and precious things" were lost.** On the LDS view the relevant prophecies *were* in the record and were removed/obscured during the very apostasy in question, so demanding to find them in the *surviving* canon begs the question. See [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]].
- **The Spirit was never wholly withdrawn.** Hansen explicitly denies a total absence of the Spirit during the apostasy — "I don't believe the Spirit was completely devoid from the earth for all time" — so the "Spirit of truth" promises are not straightforwardly violated; what was lost was *priesthood authority and the institutional fullness*, not all grace or truth. See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]] and [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]].
- **Convergence as indirect vindication.** Hansen and the broader LDS apologetic argue mainstream Christianity is *drifting back* toward restored doctrines (embodied God, theosis, premortality), which they read as belated evidence the Restoration recovered something real. See [[Seven Restoration Doctrines the Broader Church Is Drifting Toward]] and [[LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity]].

### Steelman of the Christian rebuttal to those replies

The "the prophecies were removed" move is double-edged: it makes the thesis *unfalsifiable* (any missing evidence is explained as deletion), which is itself an epistemic cost. And conceding the Spirit and a "body of believers" persisted throughout sits awkwardly with the stronger Restoration rhetoric (the creeds as "abominations," a *complete* apostasy) that the Christians press Hansen on — a tension Heschmeyer flags directly ("I'm trying to harmonize a *complete* apostasy with the Holy Spirit actively guiding somebody into the fullness of truth for that 1,700-year period").[^4]
