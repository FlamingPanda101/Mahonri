# Skepticism Must Be Applied Consistently to Atheism Too

## The Point
**Claim ([[Robert Gurr]]):** Healthy skepticism is the "Goldilocks zone" between gullibility (believing on too little evidence) and denial (rejecting despite strong evidence) — and fairness demands "a balanced, consistent standard." Echoing Steve Benson's challenge to [[Sandra Tanner]] ("why not apply the same rigorous skepticism to the Bible that you applied to the Book of Mormon?"), Gurr presses the same on ex-Mormon critics: they apply *maximal* skepticism to the Restoration (demanding zero ambiguity, no prophetic fallibility, no historical gaps) but *minimal* skepticism to their own worldview, accepting "abiogenesis despite no mechanism," "consciousness from matter despite no theory," and "the natural origin of man despite massive explanatory gaps." Ex-Mormons "skip the middle," sliding from gullibility about feelings straight into denial about God.[^1]

**Rebuttal ([[Bill Reel]]):** Reel refuses the frame: "you're putting me into a position that I actually don't hold." He calls himself an "atheist mystic" — open to "a creative energy of the universe that is all-present, all-powerful, all-knowing," and aware of "research that talks about consciousness being fundamental to the universe" — so the anti-atheism gambit "probably won't work well with me. It would work much better with a nice clean atheist." His positive epistemology: pick "the conclusion that requires the least amount of conjecture and allowances," and on that test religious narratives across the board read as "myth stories."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The consistency principle is sound but the target slips (rated: moderate).** Demanding a single evidential standard is fair (the vault agrees critics shouldn't special-plead). But Gurr aimed it at "atheism," and Reel isn't a hard atheist, so the abiogenesis/consciousness volley partly misses. The transferable point survives: whatever one's worldview, large explanatory gaps shouldn't get a skepticism discount — see [[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]].
2. **The mirror-asymmetry argument (rated: strong, transferable).** Gurr's deep move parallels the vault's standing reply to the unfalsifiability charge: naturalism, too, re-describes contrary data as "we'll explain it eventually" — "heads naturalism wins, tails give it time" (see [[Heads Faith Wins, Tails Reason Loses - The Unfalsifiability Charge]]). Reel's least-conjecture razor is itself contestable, since "fewest allowances" is doing heavy lifting and can be wielded by either side (see [[The Least-Conjecture Test and the Goalpost-Moving Pattern]]).
3. **The morality sub-exchange (rated: contested, and Gurr's stronger ground).** Pressed for his moral foundation, Reel grounds ethics in lived intuition and harm-avoidance ("being 47 years old, my intuition... can gauge pretty well what is right"). Gurr presses the classic grounding objection (lions kill, orangutans coerce — "where is your moral foundation?"), the substance of [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]. *Steelman of Reel:* he turns it back — religion's "constantly shifting" rules (polygamy, the priesthood ban) give *no* hardline moral truth either, so importing a religious narrative doesn't solve grounding.

## Best Quotes
> "Why did you not apply the same rigorous skepticism to atheism that you applied to Joseph Smith and the restoration?" [^1]

> "On one side of skepticism is gullibility... on the other side is denial... in the middle is a healthy skepticism. It's proportioning belief to the strength of the evidence." [^1]

> "You're putting me into a position that I actually don't hold... I'm an atheist mystic... this mode of forming an argument probably won't work well with me. It would work much better with a nice clean atheist." — Reel [^2]

## LDS Connection
Gurr's opening is a worldview-level apologetic: before defending the Book of Mormon, establish that a *consistent* skeptic must weigh theism's evidence (creation, fine-tuning, consciousness, moral realism) on the same scale as the Restoration's — and that selective hyper-skepticism toward Joseph Smith, paired with credulity toward naturalist promissory notes, is itself a failure of method. The vault houses both halves: the pro-theism leverage ([[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]], [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]) and the caution that "least conjecture" can be turned against the believer just as easily ([[The Least-Conjecture Test and the Goalpost-Moving Pattern]]). Reel's "mystic" dodge is a reminder that the gambit only bites against a self-identified hard atheist.
