# Social Trinitarianism Better Fits the Distinct Persons of Scripture

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] argues that **social trinitarianism** — the view that Father, Son, and Spirit are "distinct centers of consciousness" and therefore "distinct persons" — both better matches what "most people intuitively believe" and better fits the New Testament, while the classical one-substance Trinity "create[s] more problems than [it] solve[s]." He cites [[Jurgen Moltmann]] as "the only theologian of real standing" he knows who "plainly espouses" it. Givens reads the Trinity historically as a *finesse*: a way for originally-Jewish believers to "affirm... Jewish monotheism" ([[Deuteronomy 6.4|the Shema]]) while acknowledging "they have introduced another character into the Godhead." On the polytheism charge he is content to say "we believe in a plenitude of gods, but we worship one."[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Moltmann's argument, paraphrased — rate: faithful to social trinitarianism.** "He says, well, clearly they have to be distinct centers of consciousness, and if they're distinct centers of consciousness, then they're distinct persons." Givens adds Moltmann's worry "that maybe too much was compromised" in how the Trinity answered the monotheism problem. See [[Jurgen Moltmann]].[^2]
2. **The intuitive default — rate: an observational claim.** Givens is "surprised that social trinitarianism hasn't caught on more... because it's what most people intuitively believe. Even when they say trinitarianism, they're kind of imagining" distinct persons. His example: teaching Catholics in South America, "they always assume that... God the Father is a distinct entity from Jesus Christ."[^4]
3. **The Trinity as historical damage-control — rate: a reconstruction.** Early critics ([[Celsus]], plus Romans "Porphyry" and "Lucian") charged that Christians "can't figure out if you're worshiping one God or two." Being Jewish, believers "are not going to challenge monotheism, the Shema," so "the Trinity kind of emerges as a way to finesse that problem." The vault develops the canon-pressure version at [[Canonization Forced the Harmonization That Produced the Trinity]].[^3]
4. **The unsolved problems — rate: real internal tensions.** Givens lists the costs of strict one-substance unity: (a) "where's Jesus Christ's resurrected body now? How does that get reabsorbed into the Trinity?" (see [[Embodied Risen Christ Strains the Trinity]]); (b) how can the Son be passible while the Father is not — "that's even a heresy, patripassianism" — "if they're one nature"; (c) "paradox becomes an explanation for so much... he's unknowable... which is discouraging." Cf. [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity]].[^5]
5. **The beauty he concedes — rate: a genuine steelman from within.** Givens grants "one of the most beautiful aspects of trinitarian thought is that we see love modeled in the Trinity," the persons so "self-sacrificing on behalf of the others" they "become difficult to differentiate," and that [[John 17.20-23|Christ's intercessory prayer]] ("just as I am one with the Father, I want all of you to... be one") presents the Trinity as a *model to emulate*. His worry is the opposite move: that perfect intra-trinitarian sufficiency lets theologians say "God doesn't need us. We're extraneous" (he names Anders Nygren).[^6]

**Steelman of the opposing (Nicene) side:** Classical trinitarians charge that "three distinct centers of consciousness" is not orthodox trinitarianism at all but **tritheism** wearing its name — precisely the error Nicaea's *homoousios* and the later "one being, three persons" grammar were built to exclude. The unity of God is not a "finesse" of the Shema but its fulfillment: one *being*, not three gods cooperating. The resurrected body and Father/Son asymmetry are handled by the two-natures Christology of Chalcedon (the Son is impassible *as God*, passible *as man*), so Givens's "patripassianism" point misfires — the tradition agrees the *Father* does not suffer, which is exactly why the natures are distinguished. "A plenitude of gods" simply concedes the polytheism the critics alleged.

**Counter-objection (Givens's / LDS rejoinder):** Calling distinct persons "tritheism" only bites if one assumes numerical-substance monotheism is the biblical given — but the New Testament's *differentiated* language (the Son prays to, is sent by, and is distinct in will from the Father) reads more naturally as distinct persons unified in love and purpose than as one mind in three relations (see [[Differentiated Worship Vocabulary in the New Testament Refutes the Trinity]]). And appealing to Chalcedon's two natures to absorb every tension is, Givens implies, the very "paradox-as-explanation" that renders God "unknowable." Worshiping one God while affirming others exist is monolatry, the actual posture of much of the Hebrew Bible — not the pagan pantheon the polytheism charge imagines.
