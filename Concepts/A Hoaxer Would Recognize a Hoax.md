# A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax

## The Claim
A novel sincerity argument from the [[Kinderhook plates]] episode, credited on the show to "our friend Tanner": if [[Joseph Smith]] were himself a con man who had fabricated and planted his own set of plates, he would have been *immediately* suspicious of someone else's plates "discovered" by a convenient dream and dug up before witnesses — the exact playbook he would supposedly have run himself. "Game recognizes game." Yet Joseph took the Kinderhook plates seriously and treated them as plausibly authentic — which is strange on the fraud hypothesis and natural on the sincerity hypothesis.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The con-man model predicts instant suspicion (rated: strong as an internal-tension argument).** "If he's a hoaxer himself, if he's a con man... who made a fake set of plates and conned people with them, then you would think he would be immediately suspicious the moment someone else brings it." The forgers were "taking something out of his own playbook" — a fraud would see it coming.[^1] *That Joseph did not is evidence against his being a fellow practitioner.*

2. **He engaged sincerely, using tools he believed reliable (rated: moderate, behavioral).** Joseph "takes them seriously," reaching for his Hebrew lexicon and Egyptian alphabet to try to understand the plates — the conduct of a believer in genuine ancient records, not a fellow forger humoring a rival's con.[^2] (How that comparison worked: [[Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation]].)

3. **It converges with the no-revelation point (rated: strong, combined).** The same episode shows Joseph received no revelation and produced no sequel. The panel's two-fold reading: the *absence* of revelation argues his revelatory gift was genuine (it didn't fire on a fake), and his *sincere interest* argues he was not a con artist (a con artist would have smelled the con). Both vectors point at sincerity.[^3]

4. **Distinct modus operandi (rated: strong, comparative).** Forgers (Kinderhook; the Michigan relics of Soper and Savage) *parade* fakes to maximize witnesses for money or fame. Joseph's pattern is the inverse — he did not stage public digs of the gold plates and did not parade the Kinderhook plates for gain. Different operating procedure, different kind of actor. This complements [[No Contemporary Accusation That Joseph Smith Forged Plates]].

**Steelman of the critic.** The argument proves less than it appears. (a) A *pious* fraud (the [[Refusing the Pious Fraud Category|Dan Vogel model]]) need not be a cynical grifter scanning for rivals' tricks; a sincere self-deceiver could earnestly hope another genuine record had surfaced. (b) Confirmation bias cuts the other way: a man invested in an ancient-American-records narrative is *primed to believe* corroborating finds, so credulity — not the impossibility of fraud — could explain his interest. (c) The inference "a forger would spot a forgery" assumes a uniform forger psychology the evidence doesn't establish. The honest scope of the argument is therefore modest: it is one more datum favoring sincerity over the cynical-con model, not a disproof of every fraud theory. See [[Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud]].
