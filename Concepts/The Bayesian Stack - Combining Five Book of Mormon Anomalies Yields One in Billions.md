#type/claim

# The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions

## The Claim

[[Matt Vallejo]]'s central move is that no single anomaly of the [[Book of Mormon]] settles anything — but their *conjunction* does. He isolates five hard-to-explain features (low formal education, ~265,000-word length, ~4,000-words/day dictation speed, distinct authorial voices, and structural chiasmus), assigns each an individual probability against 1820s publishing data, and then combines them. Crucially, he refuses the naive step of simply multiplying all five (which would treat them as fully independent and yield an even more "preposterous" number); instead he applies a **conditional-probability chain rule** that builds in 20–30% mutual dependency between the categories, to be deliberately conservative. The result is the headline figure: a ~30% dependency gives **1 in 15.4 billion**, a ~20% dependency gives **1 in 23 billion**, for [[Joseph Smith]] producing the book naturalistically.[^1][^2]

The rhetorical force is that this is offered as a *floor*, not a ceiling: Vallejo argues that adding more anomalies, or relaxing the generous dependency assumption, only makes the number worse, and he repeatedly invites critics to redo the calculation with their own inputs — "do your own, bro" — predicting any honest methodology lands somewhere similarly extreme.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The conjunction insight is methodologically sound.** Vallejo correctly notes that evaluating one feature in isolation ("low education ⟹ ~7% chance") is "too simplistic"; a fabrication theory must account for *all* the features holding simultaneously in one artifact. Stacking jointly-improbable, partly-independent conditions is exactly how cumulative-case and design-detection arguments work.[^4] *Rating: the structure of the argument is legitimate; this is the same logic as [[The Combined Improbability of the Naturalistic Witness Feat]], applied to the text's literary features rather than the witness phenomena.*

2. **He chose the conservative direction at every fork.** Rather than multiply for the biggest number, he assumed dependency to shrink it; he used generous upper bounds on each variable (e.g., a 3-in-41,000 chiasmus rate instead of the empirical 1-in-41,000). He also argues the features may actually *work against* each other naturalistically — low education makes a long book *less* likely, and writing fast vs. writing long are "two different skills" that trade off — which, if true, would lower the joint probability further, not inflate it.[^5][^6] *Rating: a real strength — the self-imposed conservatism blunts the "you rigged the inputs" objection.*

3. **The component probabilities are each independently defended.** Length and education come from the [[The 1820s Imprint Baseline Places the Book of Mormon at the Statistical Extreme|41,000-imprint baseline]]; speed from the [[Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier|NaNoWriMo/standard-deviation comparison]]; voices from the Roper–Fields–Schaalje stylometry study (~1 in 1,000); chiasmus from its near-absence in 1820s imprints (~0.7%). See [[Stylometry Shows Multiple Authors in the Book of Mormon]] and [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]].[^7]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the argument's most vulnerable point, and the strongest critic replies are serious:

- **Garbage-in on the priors.** A chain rule is only as good as its inputs, and several are eyeballed. The ~1-in-1,000 "voices" figure and the 20–30% dependency coefficients are asserted, not derived; small changes in any prior swing the final number by orders of magnitude. Vallejo concedes he is "not a mathematical genius" and a "tourist in stats," which cuts both ways.
- **The dependencies are almost certainly stronger than 30%.** Education, length, speed, voices, and chiasmus are not five clean dice rolls. They plausibly flow from a *single* latent cause — deep immersion in the King James Bible and oral-sermon culture — so conditioning on "this person produced a long KJV-styled religious text" may absorb most of the apparent improbability. Treating partly-redundant features as near-independent inflates the result; this is the same "piling up improbabilities manufactures awe" objection [[Kolby Reddish]] raises against [[The Combined Improbability of the Naturalistic Witness Feat]].
- **Reference-class and selection problems.** The five features were *selected because* they are unusual (a Texas-sharpshooter risk), and the 1820s-imprint reference class may be the wrong denominator — Joseph was not a random 1820s author but a charismatic religious founder steeped in scripture, a tiny, non-representative sub-population. See [[The Book of Mormon Is Measured Against the Wrong Period]].
- **A small probability is not an explanation.** Even granting the number, "1 in 23 billion" only shows naturalistic authorship is *improbable on these assumptions*; it does not by itself establish the divine alternative, which carries its own priors. Critics such as [[Trent Horn]] note that an unexplained *method* is weaker evidence than a suspended law of nature. See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].

Vallejo's own framing partly absorbs these: he presents the figure as showing belief "is not crazy" rather than as a proof, and rests his actual conviction on spiritual experience.

## Best Quotes

> "Most people will say, let's just multiply all the probabilities together... If you simply multiplied all those probabilities together, you're assuming all these categories are independent... Let's just say they're somewhat dependent."[^8]

> "That probability with a 30% dependency is 1 in 15.4 billion. That's the chance Joseph has of naturalistically writing the Book of Mormon... if you say let's assume a little bit less of a dependency... then the probability goes to 1 in 23 billion."[^2]

> "I invite everybody... apply the same style of thinking... you're likely to find a different end number. But I will tell you this, whatever methodology you get to and you get a different number, it's going to wreck your mind."[^3]
