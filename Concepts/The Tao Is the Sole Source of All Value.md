# The Tao Is the Sole Source of All Value

## The Point
The central claim of Lewis's second chapter, "The Way," is that the *Tao* — the traditional doctrine of objective value — is the one and only fountainhead of every value judgment we make. There is no standpoint *outside* it from which it could be criticized or grounded in something more basic, because any criticism must itself appeal to some value, and all values are inside the *Tao*.[^1] Ward draws the parallel to Lewis's argument about reason in *Miracles*: "We have to assume value before we can critique value, just as we have to assume reason before we can fault reason. Only by operating from within the worlds of value and reason can we make judgements about those worlds that are not self-defeating."[^2]

## Evidence
Lewis frames this with the deliberately drastic image of the *experimentum crucis*: even "the true doctrine might be a doctrine which if we accept we die. No one who speaks from within the Tao could reject it on that account."[^3] Ward glosses that for those committed to obeying the *Tao*, the prospect of destruction "is no reason to reject it," because "we owe absolute allegiance to the true doctrine, even if it destroys us."[^4] The *Tao* is therefore not one option among competing systems but the precondition of there being a moral question at all. As Micah Watson puts it (quoted by Ward), "The Way" is "an extended meditation on the truth of the first principle of practical reason… to do good and avoid evil. There is no response to be made to someone who asks why we should favor good rather than evil, no more fundamental ground to appeal to."[^5] Hence Lewis's warning against the "direct frontal attack" on values: "If you persist in that kind of trial you will destroy all values, and so destroy the bases of your own criticism as well as the thing criticized. You must not hold a pistol to the head of the Tao."[^6]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint theology is unusually hospitable to Lewis's claim that moral value is ultimate and underived — more so than classical theism. In the restored gospel, the moral order is not something God invented by fiat but **self-existent eternal law** that even God honors and is "constrained" by ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; cf. Alma 42:13, 22-25, where if justice were destroyed "God would cease to be God"). This is the LDS rejection of [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory|divine command theory]]: good is not good merely because God wills it. Lewis's "no ground more basic than the Tao" thus parallels the LDS "no ground more basic than eternal law." It is also the decisive reply to naturalist grounding projects like [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]: Harris must still answer "but *is* well-being what we ought to maximize?" — and any answer presupposes a prior value he cannot derive from facts (see [[You Cannot Derive an Ought From an Is]]). Value is received, not engineered.
