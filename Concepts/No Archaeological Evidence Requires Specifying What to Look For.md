# No Archaeological Evidence Requires Specifying What to Look For

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

"There is zero archaeological evidence for the Book of Mormon" is, like the DNA
version, not a usable test until the objector says *what* would count. Usually the
demand reduces to wanting an artifact that explicitly labels itself "Nephite" — a
standard met by virtually no ancient civilization.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The demand is for an inscription no civilization provides

What critics implicitly want is a stela saying "Welcome to Zarahemla, 100 BC" or "I am
the king of the Lamanites." Almost no ancient people left self-labeling artifacts of
that kind; most archaeology is potsherds, and Mormon never describes Nephite pots — so
we do not even know what a "Nephite potsherd" looks like. See [[Mormon Is an
Ethnocentric Redactor Focused on the Witness of Christ]].[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (an accurate point about how archaeology actually works).

### 2. We don't know where they lived

We cannot point to "Nephite" artifacts largely because we don't know where the
Nephites lived; without a fixed location, the New World data cannot be correlated to
the text. (Compounded by [[Only Two Percent of the Americas Has Been Excavated]].)[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the localization problem is genuine).

### 3. Correlating text to material is a method, and Old World data converges

Properly correlating a written record with the archaeology of a region is a scholarly
method, not a hunt for a single proof-artifact. Where Rappleye applies it to the Old
World setting (7th-century BC Jerusalem; the Arabian journey), much "lines up really
well" — most strikingly **Nahom**. See [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward
Turn]].[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (method is sound; Old World convergence is the strongest case).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can fairly insist the bar is not "a Zarahemla sign" but simply *some*
distinctive material correlate (Old World crops, writing systems, metallurgy, draft
animals) clustering where the text places a literate, agricultural, metal-using,
horse-and-chariot society — and that this broader signature is genuinely absent in the
proposed New World heartland. The reply: the redactor's ethnocentric focus, the small
assimilated population, and the localization problem jointly predict a faint New World
footprint, while the *Old World* leg (Nahom, the river, Bountiful) supplies the positive
correlation the method can actually deliver.

## Best Quotes

> "What it comes down to often is we don't have an inscription that says welcome to Zarahemla, 100 BC."[^2]

> "What does a Nephite potsherd look like? I don't know. Mormon never describes their pots."[^2]

> "When I study how scholars integrate written source material with the archaeological material and I attempt to do the same thing with the Book of Mormon, there's a whole lot of stuff that lines up really well."[^4]
