# Philo Did Not Endorse Creation Ex Nihilo

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Being Rebutted)

Wes Huff claimed that "creation ex nihilo [is expressed] in the Hellenistic Jewish thinker Philo of Alexandria," presenting Philo as a pre-Christian Jewish witness to the doctrine of creation from nothing.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

The scholarly consensus — sustained across decades and multiple specialists — is that Philo of Alexandria did NOT endorse creation ex nihilo. Philo held to creation from pre-existent matter, consistent with the Middle Platonic and Stoic philosophical frameworks he operated within. He came as close to creation ex nihilo as his philosophical milieu permitted, but ultimately could not cross the line because the conventional wisdom of his day held that nothing can come from nothing. Philo taught that God created the world by ordering, shaping, and imparting form to a pre-existent, lifeless, passive material substrate.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. David Winston (1975): Philo Taught Creation from Pre-Existent Matter

David Winston, a leading Philo scholar, provided a definitive summary in *Religious Syncretism in Antiquity* (1975) and his article on Philo's theory of cosmogony:

> "Philo taught that God created the world from a pre-existent matter. Indeed, he thought that Moses had anticipated Plato in this matter by teaching in Genesis that there was water, darkness, and chaos before the world came into existence."[^2]

Winston quotes Philo directly (*De Opificio Mundi*):

> "Moses writes: Philo knew that at the very least one principle of being must be active, the other passive, and that the active cause is the universal mind, absolutely pure and unmixed, beyond virtue, beyond knowledge, and beyond the good itself and the beautiful itself. The passive principle, on the other hand, is in itself lifeless and motionless, but when moved, shaped, and quickened by reason, is transformed into the most perfect master work, this world."[^2]

This is explicit dualism: active cause (God / Reason) + passive principle (pre-existent matter) = creation. Not creation from nothing.

### 2. Winston Clarifies Philo's "Non-Existent" Language

Philo frequently describes God as "calling the non-existent into existence" — language that superficially sounds like creation ex nihilo. Winston clarifies that in Philo's Middle Platonic framework, "non-existent" refers to formless, disordered matter occupying the realm of non-being — not absolute nothingness:

> "God called the non-existent into existence and produced order from disorder, qualities from things devoid of quality, similarities from dissimilars, identities from the totally different, fellowship and harmony from the disassociated and discordant, equality from inequality, and light from darkness. For he and his beneficent powers ever make it their business to remake the faulted aspects of the lesser being and adapt it for the better."[^2]

Note the pattern: God transforms EXISTING things (disorder → order, things devoid of quality → things with quality, etc.). The "non-existent" is chaotic, formless, passive matter — a standard Platonic category.

### 3. Gerhard May (1994): Philo Rejected Creatio Ex Nihilo

Gerhard May devoted an entire section of his foundational study to Philo's rejection of creation ex nihilo:

> "May talks about Philo and Philo's rejection of creatio ex nihilo."[^3]

May positions Philo as standing firmly within the pre-ex-nihilo consensus: Philo wanted to elevate God's creative sovereignty as high as possible, but the philosophical constraint of *ex nihilo nihil fit* ("nothing comes from nothing") — shared by Platonists, Aristotelians, and Stoics — prevented him from reaching the doctrine.

### 4. James Hubler (1995): Philo Excludes Matter from What God Created

James Hubler's University of Pennsylvania dissertation found a key textual indicator:

> "The best textual evidence for uncreated matter comes from *Quis Rerum Divinarum Heres Sit* where Philo excludes matter from the things God praises at the consummation of creation. God praises all the things which he has created, but Philo does not include matter."[^4]

If matter were created by God, Philo would logically include it among the things God praises at creation's completion. He does not — because matter is uncreated, eternal, and passive.

Hubler further notes:

> "Despite his many borrowings from the Stoics, Philo has retained the dualism of Plato and like Plato makes eternal and intractable matter the source of difficulty and necessity in the world. Difficulty and evil does not flow from the source of good and reason. It is not from God."[^4]

Philo's theodicy REQUIRES eternal matter: if evil and difficulty don't come from God, and God created everything from nothing, where does evil come from? Philo's answer: from the intractable pre-existent matter that resists perfect formation.

### 5. Philo's Philosophical Constraints: Middle Platonism

Philo was deeply committed to a Middle Platonic worldview that also incorporated Stoic elements. In this framework:

- **Platonic dualism:** The *Timaeus* teaches creation by a divine craftsman (Demiurge) who orders pre-existent chaotic matter by looking to eternal Forms. Creation is imposition of order, not generation from nothing.
- **Stoic physics:** The Stoics held that God and matter are co-eternal principles; God permeates and organizes matter, which is passive.
- **The axiom *ex nihilo nihil fit*:** "Nothing comes from nothing" was virtually universally accepted in Greco-Roman philosophy. Parmenides formulated it; Plato, Aristotle, Stoics, and Epicureans all affirmed it in different ways.

McClellan summarizes:

> "Because the conventional wisdom of the day within the Platonic and Middle Platonic worldview that he was frequently endorsing along with the Stoic didn't accept the notion that anything could come out of nothing, Philo can't quite get there. He has to endorse creation out of a pre-existing material substrate."[^5]

### 6. Philo's Reading of Genesis 1

Philo read Genesis 1 through a Platonic lens. The "water, darkness, and chaos" (*tohu wa-bohu*) of Genesis 1:2 were, for Philo, precisely the pre-existent matter that God then ordered. Winston notes Philo believed "Moses had anticipated Plato in this matter."[^2] In other words, Philo saw Genesis 1 as teaching Platonic creation-from-pre-existent-matter — not creation ex nihilo — and considered this a point of Mosaic priority over Greek philosophy.

## The Opposing View (Steelmanned)

The strongest case that Philo taught creation ex nihilo: Philo repeatedly uses language of God creating "out of non-being" (ἐκ μὴ ὄντων / *ek mē ontōn*) and "calling the non-existent into existence." In *De Somniis* 1.76, Philo writes that God "created all things, bringing them into being from not-being." This sounds remarkably like later Christian formulations. Philo also emphasizes God's absolute sovereignty and the radical dependence of all creation on God in ways that strain the Platonic dualism of co-eternal matter. Some scholars (e.g., Harry Wolfson) have argued Philo effectively arrived at creation ex nihilo even if he didn't use the precise later terminology.

## Why the Steelman Fails

1. **"Non-being" ≠ "nothing" in Philo's Middle Platonic framework.** As Winston demonstrates, "non-being" for Philo is the formless, quality-less, passive material substrate — a standard Platonic category, not absolute nothingness.[^2]
2. **Philo explicitly posits two principles: active (God) + passive (matter).** This is dualism, not creation ex nihilo. If God created matter as well as form, there would be only one principle.[^2]
3. **Philo excludes matter from the things God created and praises.** The textual evidence from *Quis Rerum Divinarum Heres Sit* is hard to explain if Philo believed matter was created.[^4]
4. **Philo's theodicy depends on uncreated matter.** Evil and difficulty come from the intractable, passive material principle — not from God. If God created everything from nothing, Philo's theodicy collapses.[^4]
5. **Philo's Genesis exegesis treats "darkness, water, chaos" as pre-existent matter.** He reads Genesis 1:2 as depicting the passive principle awaiting divine ordering — not as describing an initial creation of matter.[^2]
6. **The consensus is robust and long-standing.** Winston, May, Hubler, and the broader field of Philo studies agree Philo did not hold creation ex nihilo. The view that he did (e.g., Wolfson) is a minority position.
