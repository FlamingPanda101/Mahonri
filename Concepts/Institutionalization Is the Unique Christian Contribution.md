# Institutionalization Is the Unique Christian Contribution

## The Claim

When pressed that the *values* (compassion, equal treatment) predate Christianity, [[Glenn Scrivener]] retreats to a narrower, sturdier claim: Christianity's unique contribution was to **institutionalize** and **scale** these values — turning private virtue into hospitals, charities, and orphanages for the poor and sick *as such*. "Common grace did not institutionalize things like hospitals."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Pre-Christian medicine served utility, not the poor as poor.** Scrivener: you can have a [[Hippocrates|Hippocratic Oath]] and Galen's manuals, but "that doesn't institutionalize this thing that is not just for the slave to return him to economic fruitfulness, not just for soldiers to return them to military service, but for the poor and the sick as sick." Scaling it institutionally "is a unique Christian contribution."[^1] *(Strong — the historical claim that the hospital-for-the-indigent is a Christian institutional innovation is widely accepted, even by secular historians.)*

2. **O'Connor largely concedes the institutional claim.** "Hospitals in particular, absolutely Christian at least in their modern conceptions."[^2] *(Major concession — the dispute narrows here.)*

3. **But O'Connor argues this *shrinks* the thesis.** If the thesis becomes "compassion exists, and Christians worked out we should found hospitals to instantiate it," that is "a much more mild thesis... much more in line with what I'm saying," not the grand claim that Christianity is *responsible for the values* that drove the developments.[^3] *(Sharp — distinguishes value-origin from institutional-form, and the thesis's interesting version was about value-origin.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener:** He has a principled reason institutions, not just ideals, matter in Christianity specifically — incarnation, the body of Christ, and serving Christ *in* the poor/prisoner/stranger (Matthew 25) mean Christian ethics is intrinsically embodied and communal, not Platonic. This is why values got *embedded* rather than floating as ideals.[^4] *(Genuinely strong — it explains *why* Christianity institutionalizes where Stoicism or Hippocratic medicine did not. See [[The Christian Origin of Equality]].)*

## Best Quotes

> "Common grace did not institutionalize things like hospitals... that it is for the poor and the sick as sick — that scaling and becoming institutional is a unique Christian contribution."[^1]

> "If the form you're talking about is essentially institutionalized hospitals, then the thesis has suddenly shrunk from 'compassion is Christian' to 'compassion exists and Christians worked out we should found this thing called a hospital' — which is cool, but it's a much more mild thesis."[^3]
