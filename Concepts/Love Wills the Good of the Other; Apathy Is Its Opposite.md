# Love Wills the Good of the Other; Apathy Is Its Opposite

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] defines love in terms of his suffering/well-being framework: "love is to will the good of the other — the good of the other; to will someone's good is to will that they move away from suffering." [^1] This connects love directly to the moral "ought": precisely *because* a person loves you, they will tell you "that you ought not" put your hand on a hot stove. Love is therefore not sentiment but an orientation toward another's movement away from suffering — which is why "apathy is the opposite of love, because it does not care about the moral direction of others and therefore doesn't care about their ultimate well-being." [^2]

## Evidence
The definition does real argumentative work. First, it grounds *other-directed* moral language: the framework had established that I can tell a sane person they "ought not" harm *themselves* (see [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]); love is what extends concern from my own well-being to another's, so that I can meaningfully say *you* ought to avoid *your* suffering. Second, by making **apathy** (not hatred) the opposite of love, Hansen frames indifference to another's moral direction as a moral failure, not a neutral stance. Third, the definition sets up the central problem of the next section: if "the only well-being you experience is your own," why would a purely rational agent will the good of others at all? Hansen's resolution — that love "is connected to righteousness" and is "the force that connects all mankind together" — is completed only when God connects everyone's well-being (see [[Pure Rationality Makes Morality Selfish - Only God Connects All Well-Being]]). [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "That is what love is. Love is to will the good of the other — the good of the other; to will someone's good is to will that they move away from suffering." [^1]

> "Apathy therefore is the opposite of love, because it does not care about the moral direction of others and therefore doesn't care about their ultimate well-being." [^2]

> "It's why love is connected to righteousness. It is the force that connects all mankind together." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Hansen's "love is to will the good of the other" is the classic formula (Aristotle/Aquinas) here folded into a Latter-day Saint key: love is "connected to righteousness" and is "the force that connects all mankind together," culminating in the prayer that disciples "may be one" ([[John 17.20-23]]). This resonates with the broader claim that the law of love is the single ground of morality (see [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]) and that morality is not bare value but carries **obligation** ([[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]) — to love is to take on the other's good as something one *ought* to will.
