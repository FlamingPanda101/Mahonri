# Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible — The Anachronism of a Closed Canon

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that "scripture" in the first and second centuries CE is **not isometric** with what we now mean by "the Bible." Today the closed canon "undergirds our conceptualization of scripture," so the two terms feel coextensive — but in the earliest period *scripture* was "a far broader category than what we now know as the Bible."[^1] When Paul says "all scripture is God-breathed" ([[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16-17]]), no one is confused about what he means — but he does *not* mean a closed 66-book list, because no such bounded list yet existed. To assume he does is to "read back an anachronistic perspective due to our modern privilege" of bound volumes.[^2] The tell: "no one ever referred to any such delineated collection of texts as the Bible at any point prior to the 4th century CE."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The concept of scripture was unbounded in the first century — rate: strong
McClellan's positive reconstruction: "the concept of scripture was not yet bounded in the first century CE. It was not yet a concept of a closed canon, and what was considered scripture included a lot more texts in the first century CE than is recognized now."[^4] He insists this be argued *from* data rather than asserted: the burden is on anyone claiming an early closed list to produce evidence, "and there are no data that actually establish that as the most likely reconstruction."[^5] This is the mainstream critical position and it is the canon-specific instance of a method the vault documents elsewhere — not reading modern bounded categories into ancient sources (cf. [[Religion as Anachronistic Category]], [[Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category]]).

### 2. Christians and Jews cited now-excluded books *as* scripture — rate: strong
The decisive evidence that the boundary was open: writers inside the tradition quote texts we now exclude and call them scripture. [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]] quotes [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude|1 Enoch and calls it prophecy]] — "indisputably acknowledging that text as inspired scripture."[^6] [[Tertullian]] argues "fervently" that 1 Enoch is inspired scripture into the late 2nd century.[^6] What counted as *Jewish* scripture "differed from person to person, from time to time, from place to place," with no first-century assertion of a closed list matching today's Old Testament.[^7] This dovetails with the Protestant concession in [[The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha]] that patristic citation of apocryphal books was real and authoritative.

### 3. "The Christians had a Bible — the Old Testament" is the cleanest anachronism — rate: strong
Huff's "let's not forget the first-century Christians had a Bible, we call it the Old Testament" is, McClellan says, "entirely false" — not because there were no Jewish scriptures, but because the *closed and delineated list* matching the modern OT is "demonstrably false," resting on retrojecting a 4th-century-and-later boundary.[^7] The argument is symmetrical and disciplined: just as we shouldn't impose modern publishing convenience on antiquity, we shouldn't impose a modern *canon* on it either — "allow the data to operate on their own terms."[^2] **Steelman of Huff:** the genuine letters of Paul and the gospels really *were* widely recognized as inspired by the early 2nd century, so there was a stable *core*; McClellan grants this but answers that a recognized core is not a *closed boundary*, and "widely recognized" is exactly the messy, gradient situation his argument predicts — not the clean settled list the apologetic needs.

## LDS Relevance
If "scripture" was a broader, open category before the 4th century and the closed "Bible" is a later construct, then the Protestant assumption that scripture *just is* the 66-book canon is itself the anachronism — and the LDS position (additional scripture, an open canon) is closer to the earliest Christian usage than the closed-canon tradition is. The Restoration's claim that revelation and scripture were never meant to be sealed off matches a first century in which the boundary had not yet been drawn. The same data also caution the LDS reader: an *open* category is not a *formless* one, so the question of *who* authoritatively bounds scripture remains live (see [[The Church Bounds Scripture It Does Not Write It - A Consensus Requires Enforcement]]).
