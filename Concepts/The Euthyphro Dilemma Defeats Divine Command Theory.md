# The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Calvinist grounds morality in God's commands: a thing is good *because* God commands it, and God is therefore the objective moral standard. [^1] See [[Divine Command Theory Is Metaethics Not Ethics]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The dilemma itself** — rate: classic, decisive. [[Blake Ostler]]: "Does God command something because it's good, or is it good because God commands it?" If the former, "there's something independent from God which makes it good... so it's not divine command theory." If the latter, then "God could command that our entire moral duty consists in murdering children just for the fun of it." Either horn destroys the view. *(philosophical dilemma)* [^2]
2. **Scripture supplies the horrifying horn** — rate: rhetorically powerful, exegetically contestable. Ostler cites the *ḥērem* ("the ban") — God commanding Israel to "kill every living thing at [[Joshua 8.26|Ai]]," "little children, every moving thing like scorpions and beetles." If "good = commanded," this genocide was *good*, which exposes the arbitrariness as "totally capricious." *(scriptural)* [^3]
3. **Philosophical consensus** — rate: appeal to authority, but accurately reported. "There's a reason why the vast vast vast majority of philosophers simply reject it out of hand and view it as even a non-starter." *(sociological)* [^4]
4. **The escape via "God's nature" still loses for Calvinism** — rate: the key move. The standard reply ("good is grounded in God's *nature*, not arbitrary will") only works if God's nature is essentially *loving*. But the Calvinist God decrees eternal torment, so on the nature-horn his "goodness" includes damning innocents — which is "good" in name only. See [[Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good]], [[God's Wrath Is the Natural Consequence of Rejecting Love]]. *(theological)* [^2]

**Steelman of the divine-command reply:** The "third horn" (associated with William Alston and Robert Adams) denies that the dilemma is exhaustive: God neither consults an external standard *nor* commands arbitrarily, because God *is* the Good — the supreme exemplar that the term "good" refers to, the way the standard meter bar once fixed "meter." On this view asking "is God good because he meets the standard?" misfires, since God *is* the standard. Ostler's reply (implicit): even a standard-setting God must be *recognizably* loving, or "good" has been emptied of content — the same critique leveled at the [[Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good|appeal to mystery]].

## Best Quotes
> "The Euthyphro problem is this: does God command something because it's good, or is it good because God commands it?" [^2]

> "If it's good because God commands it, then God could command that our entire moral duty consists in murdering children just for the fun of it. And in fact, God did something like that when he commanded them to kill every living thing at Ai." [^3]

> "There's a reason why the vast vast vast majority of philosophers simply reject it out of hand and view it as even a non-starter." [^4]
