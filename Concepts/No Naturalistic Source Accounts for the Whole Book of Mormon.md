# No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon

## The Point
Critics claim Joseph Smith plagiarized or adapted some earlier book to produce the [[Book of Mormon]] — the usual candidates being the Spaulding Manuscript, *View of the Hebrews*, *The First Book of Napoleon*, *The Late War*, and the Bible. Fife's unifying rebuttal is that *every* proposed source has the same two fatal defects: (1) it accounts for only a small fraction of the 269,320-word text, and (2) there is no evidence Joseph ever owned, read, or referenced it during translation.[^1] If a critic answers "yes, Joseph used a non-divine source," then "they are burdened with developing a working theory for where it came from" — and none of the candidates supplies one.[^2]

## Evidence
The word-count arithmetic alone sinks each single-source theory:[^3]
- *View of the Hebrews* (~57,000 words) — "where did the other 200,000 words come from?"
- *The First Book of Napoleon* (~22,500 words) — leaves ~230,000 unexplained; and the CES Letter's "parallels" are stitched from phrases across ~25 scattered pages ("skip a page… skip four pages…").[^4]
- *The Late War* (~56,632 words) — leaves ~200,000 unexplained; the Johnsons' 2013 word-print study found 74 parallels in a 260,000-word book, "many of those correlations also correlate with the King James Version."[^5]
- The Bible — only 5%-15% of the Book of Mormon, "where did the other 85%-95% come from?"[^6]

The evidentiary test (repeated for every candidate): "Do we have an eyewitness of Joseph using the book? Did he even have a copy of it? Did anyone else he knew have a copy of it? Do we have anyone in letters or journal entries mentioning him referencing it?" — the answer is uniformly no.[^7]

**The Spaulding Manuscript** was the dominant theory from *Mormonism Unvailed* (1834) until the manuscript surfaced and was published in 1885, revealing it to be a "pseudo-pirate romance novel about Romans" with virtually nothing in common with the Book of Mormon. Critics then retreated to a hypothetical, never-found "second manuscript."[^8] (See [[Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin]].) *View of the Hebrews* was not proposed as a source until I. Woodbridge Riley in 1902 — 72 years after publication, and unnoticed even by its own author, Ethan Smith, who lived 19 more years.[^9] (See [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]].)

**The "Tactic Change" / moving target.** Fife highlights that the critics' theory of Joseph himself has reversed over time: from the early "ignorant fool" who needed Spaulding to write it for him, to Fawn Brodie's 1945 "religious genius" who composed it himself, to the recent moderate claim that the 1829 Joseph simply "had the skills, intelligence, experience, and resources" to dictate it in 90 days. "In a little over 100 years, critics went from Joseph being an ignorant fool to someone else writing the Book of Mormon to Joseph being a religious genius."[^10] The skill inventory required — college-level vocabulary, long-form dictation, consistent geography across 600 place-name references, distinct authorial voices, chiasmus — is contradicted by contemporaries: David Whitmer called him "a man of limited education [who] could hardly write legibly," and Emma said he "could neither write nor dictate a coherent and well-worded letter, let alone... a book like the Book of Mormon."[^11] (See [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].)

## Best Quotes
> "Could Joseph Smith have written or dictated the Book of Mormon by using another non-divine source? If critics answer 'yes,' then they are burdened with developing a working theory for where it came from."[^2]

> "In a little over 100 years, critics went from Joseph being an ignorant fool to someone else writing the Book of Mormon to Joseph being a religious genius."[^10]

> "Is it reasonable to think that Joseph Smith used all these listed sources (and much more) from memory to dictate the Book of Mormon in 65 days? Is there any proof that he used any of these sources?"[^7]

## LDS Connection
This is a burden-of-proof reversal in the Latter-day Saint's favor. Critics present each source theory as a self-sufficient explanation, but stacking them only multiplies the implausibility: Joseph would need to have memorized a private library and seamlessly recombined it off the top of his head with no notes, no witnessed copies, and no later slips of the tongue. The shifting portrait of Joseph (fool → genius) is itself an instance of the [[The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit|moving-goalposts and Texas Sharpshooter tactics]]: the data never changed, only the narrative built around it.
