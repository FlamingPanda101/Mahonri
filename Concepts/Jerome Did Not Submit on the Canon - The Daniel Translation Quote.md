# Jerome Did Not Submit on the Canon - The Daniel Translation Quote

#type/claim

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A widely-circulated counter to [[Jerome]]'s narrow canon (see [[Jerome Consistently Excluded the Deuterocanon]]) holds that **Jerome ultimately "submitted to the judgment of the churches"** and so accepted the deuterocanon as scripture. The line comes from *Against Rufinus*, where Jerome asks, "what sin have I committed in following the judgment of the churches?" [[Gavin Ortlund]] calls this reading "totally bogus" and argues the quote, in context, has *nothing to do with the Catholic-vs-Protestant canon question.* [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### Steelman of the objection
Taken at face value, the phrase looks decisive: Jerome appears to subordinate his private Hebrew-canon preference to ecclesial authority, exactly the Catholic principle that the *church*, not the individual scholar, determines the canon. If Jerome conceded that the churches' judgment trumps his own, then enlisting him as a "Protestant precedent" for a narrower canon would be special pleading — he himself deferred to the body that received the wider canon. This is "all over the internet," and both popular Catholic and some Protestant writers have repeated it.

### Ortlund's rebuttal
- **The dispute is about two Greek *translations of Daniel*, not the canon.** Jerome is comparing the Septuagint (LXX) Daniel with the version by Theodotion (a 2nd-century Jewish scholar). Both Greek Daniels contain the *same* additional material (Susanna, Bel and the Dragon) absent from the 12-chapter Hebrew Masoretic Daniel. So the "judgment of the churches" concerns *which Greek translation to use* — an issue "not in the ballpark" of which books are canonical. [^2]
- **Jerome's reason is the Hebrew, and the judgment is his own.** He prefers Theodotion's Daniel for its greater fidelity to the Hebrew; the very next section of his work is titled a *Vindication of the Hebrew text*. Far from going against himself, Jerome states his *own* opinion that the LXX Daniel "often differs from the truth and with proper judgment is repudiated." He even says he doesn't know *why* the churches went with Theodotion — there is "no council he's submitting to." [^3]
- **Later writings confirm no change of view.** Letter 107 — written *after* the 382 council Jerome supposedly submitted to — counsels a young mother on her daughter's reading and **still warns against the apocryphal books**; Letter 53 likewise omits the deuterocanon. [^4]
- **Scholarly backing.** Ed Gallagher frames any genuine "change" debate as only about whether Jerome's *earlier* canon was *wider* — "nobody is talking about him widening his view late in life." The submission-quote reading inverts this entirely. [^5]

## LDS Relevance
This rebuttal is an *intramural* Christian dispute over a primary source, and it cuts against the Catholic/Orthodox appeal to ecclesial authority over the canon — the same authority-of-the-church premise that the LDS tradition also resists (compare [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]]). For the open-canon view, the lesson is narrower: it shows how thin and contestable the patristic "evidence" for a fixed wider canon can be, since a single ambiguous clause about *Daniel manuscripts* was inflated into a doctrine of canon-submission. But note the asymmetry — Ortlund deploys this to defend a *closed shorter* canon, not the LDS claim that the canon stays open to new revelation.

## Best Quotes
> "What's in view here is which is the better of these two Greek translations, both of which have this additional material. This is not a Catholic versus Protestant issue at all... so hopefully we can just put to bed this 'submitting to the judgment of the churches' claim. As far as I can tell... this is just a totally bogus claim." [^2]

> "He states his own opinion that the Septuagint Daniel often differs from the truth and with proper judgment is repudiated. So he's not going against his own judgment in any way." [^3]
