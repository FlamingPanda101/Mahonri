# Second Temple History

## The Claim
The Second Temple period (~516 BCE – 70 CE) is the context for understanding the destruction of the Jerusalem temple — an event that shapes the theological framing of multiple New Testament texts, particularly the Gospel of Mark. The temple was destroyed in 70 CE by Roman forces under Titus during the First Jewish-Roman War, approximately the same time Mark's gospel was being written.[^1]

## Key Facts

1. **Construction:** The Second Temple was completed ~516 BCE after Cyrus the Great's decree allowed Judahite exiles to return from Babylon (~538 BCE) and rebuild. It stood for nearly 600 years before its destruction.[^2]

2. **Destruction (70 CE):** The Roman siege of Jerusalem culminated in the burning and demolition of the temple. [[Josephus]], an eyewitness, describes Roman soldiers pushing stones off the temple walls that cratered the stone sidewalks below.[^1]

3. **Mark's context:** Mark's gospel was written at or shortly after the temple's destruction. [[Dan McClellan]] argues this historical context is essential for understanding the [[Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor for Temple]] — from Mark's perspective, "the tree is cursed and has withered, the temple is gone, the leadership is gone."[^1]

4. **Cyrus the Great:** [[Cyrus the Great|Cyrus]]'s decree initiated the return and the Second Temple period. He is named in [[Isaiah 45.1-7|Isaiah 45:1]] as YHWH's "anointed one" (*mashiach*), nearly 200 years before his birth according to a traditional reading — a key piece of evidence for the [[Deutero-Isaiah Theory]].

## Best Quote

> "It's destroyed. The temple is gone. ... Mark is writing right around the time the temple has just been destroyed. So Mark is looking at recent events when he writes this narrative."[^1]

— [[Dan McClellan]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast
