#type/claim

# Prophecy-Driven Invention Undermines Gospel Trustworthiness

## The Claim (skeptical)
[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] advances an *inference to the best explanation* for a nativity discrepancy: an evangelist invented the flight to Egypt in order to portray Jesus as fulfilling Old Testament prophecy — and this invented motive should lower our trust in his historical claims generally. The evangelist, O'Connor notes, *tells us* his aim: the family went down to and came back from Egypt "that it might be fulfilled... Out of Egypt I called my son" ([[Hosea 11.1|Hosea 11:1]]). "It seems to me like what [he] is doing here is attempting to display Jesus as a fulfillment of the Old Testament prophecy. And in fact, he tells us that that's what he's doing." If the episode is fabricated to satisfy a prophecy, then where the text "appears that he's making historical claims, we should note his willingness to twist history in order to fulfill a theological" agenda.[^1]

Crucially, O'Connor distinguishes this from the [[Nazarene Prophecy Has No Known Hebrew Bible Source|"Nazarene"]] case: "unlike the Matthew prophecy, the prophecy actually exists, and we can interrogate it." Hosea 11:1 is a real, recoverable text — and when interrogated, "in Hosea God is talking here about the *nation of Israel*," not a future Messiah. So the fulfillment is a *reapplication* of a verse that, read in context, was never a messianic prediction.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The author states the motive.** Unlike inferred motives, the prophecy-fulfillment intent is explicit in the text ("that it might be fulfilled"). [[Matthew 2.13-15|Matthew 2:13–15]] frames the whole Egypt episode as existing *for the sake of* the Hosea quotation. O'Connor's move is simply to take that stated purpose seriously as a *historiographical* warning sign.[^1]
2. **The source prophecy doesn't say what it's used to say.** [[Hosea 11.1|Hosea 11:1]] is retrospective ("*When* Israel *was* a child... I... called my son out of Egypt") and corporate (Israel = the son). Using it as a Messianic prediction requires a typological move the original text does not license — exactly the kind of "twist" that makes invention a live hypothesis.[^1]
3. **It contradicts the other nativity.** [[Luke 2.39|Luke 2:39]] has the family return peacefully to Nazareth with no Egyptian sojourn (see [[Matthews Nativity Contradicts Lukes Nativity]]). One of the two cannot be straight history, and the prophecy-fulfillment framing supplies a *reason* one would be invented — strengthening the inference over a bare "they just differ." (Note: O'Connor attributes the flight to *Luke*; it is in fact Matthew that narrates it, with Luke giving the contradicting account.)
4. **It generalizes — the trustworthiness inference.** The damage is not local. If an evangelist "twist[s] history in order to fulfill" prophecy once, by his own admission, then his bare historical assertions elsewhere inherit a discount. This is the load-bearing step: from one motivated invention to a *general* deflation of gospel reliability.[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
Several replies are open to the apologist:

- **Typology is not deception.** The strongest answer is that [[Matthew 2.13-15|Matthew]] is doing *typology*, not history-faking, and his first-century readers knew it. Reading Israel's Exodus as a pattern recapitulated in the Messiah is a recognized Jewish interpretive convention; "fulfilled" (*plēroō*) often means "filled full / brought to its intended pattern," not "predicted in advance." On this reading the Hosea citation is a theological *interpretation* of a remembered event, not evidence the event was manufactured. O'Connor's inference equivocates between "the author reads this event prophetically" and "the author invented this event."

- **A stated theological aim does not entail a fabricated fact.** Every historian selects and frames for a purpose; [[Luke 2.39|Luke]]'s prologue openly states an apologetic intent (to give Theophilus "certainty"), yet O'Connor elsewhere grants Luke is interviewing sources. Purpose-driven narration is not the same as invention — the inference from "motivated" to "fictional" needs independent support, which is why the apologetic literature treats the [[Bethlehem Birth Is Likely an Invention to Fulfill Prophecy|Bethlehem-invention]] and [[Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment|retrofitting]] charges as *defeasible* rather than decisive.

- **The accommodationist need not defend inerrant history.** From a Restoration vantage, [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context|revelation comes "in human words"]] and through fallible scribes who may differ in detail; the gospel's saving claims do not stand or fall on harmonizing the infancy narratives. So even granting O'Connor's deflation of *this* detail, the trustworthiness discount on the *core* testimony (death and resurrection, attested across independent strands) is far weaker than a blanket "twist history" verdict implies. The honest concession is that O'Connor has located a real tension; the overreach is treating one typological framing as a solvent for the whole tradition.
