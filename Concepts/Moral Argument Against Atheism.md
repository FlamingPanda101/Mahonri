# Moral Argument Against Atheism

## The Claim

Atheism cannot ground objective morality. If there is no transcendent moral
standard — no divine lawgiver, no eternal moral law — then moral claims are
reducible to personal preference, social convention, or evolutionary
programming. There is no basis for saying that any action is *actually* right
or wrong — only that we happen to like or dislike it. This is not merely a
theoretical problem; it has practical consequences: under atheism, one cannot
coherently say that Hitler's actions were objectively wrong, or that Mother
Teresa's were objectively good. Leading atheists like [[Richard Dawkins]],
[[Alex Rosenberg]], and [[Alex O'Connor]] honestly concede this implication.
The moral argument thus presents a dilemma: either abandon atheism or abandon
the belief that anything is truly right or wrong.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Richard Dawkins: "No Evil, No Good" (Evidence Rating: Primary — Atheist Source)

> "The universe we observe has precisely the properties we should expect if
> there is at bottom no design, no purpose, no evil, no good, nothing but
> pitiless indifference." — [[Richard Dawkins]], *River Out of Eden*[^1]

[[Hayden Carroll]] presents this as a concession from the world's most famous
atheist: on the atheistic worldview, the universe is morally indifferent.
There is no objective "evil" or "good" — there are only physical facts. Dawkins
is being consistent: if matter is all there is, moral properties (which are not
physical) cannot exist.

### 2. Alex Rosenberg: No Moral Difference Between Mother Teresa and Hitler (Evidence Rating: Primary — Atheist Source)

> "There's no moral difference between the acts of Mother Teresa and those of
> Hitler. Nihilism rejects the distinction between acts that are morally right
> and ones that are morally wrong." — [[Alex Rosenberg]], *The Atheist's Guide
> to Reality*[^2]

Carroll notes: "He says nihilism — he's talking about atheism. He agrees that
atheism is nihilism essentially. Leads logically leads you to it."[^2]

Rosenberg, a professor of philosophy at Duke, is not a fringe figure. His
statement is a clear, unambiguous admission from a credentialed atheist
philosopher that atheism entails the collapse of moral distinctions.

### 3. Alex O'Connor: Emotivism (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Atheist Source)

> "Alex O'Connor says he's an emotivist. When he says that something is wrong,
> it's just him saying, 'I don't like that.' There's no eternal standard of
> morality or laws or anything."[^3]

[[Alex O'Connor]] (CosmicSkeptic), one of the most prominent young atheist
voices, identifies as an emotivist — the meta-ethical view that moral
statements are expressions of emotion, not truth-claims. "Murder is wrong" =
"Boo, murder!" — a feeling, not a fact.

Carroll's point: this is the consistent atheist position. If there's no God, no
eternal law, no transcendent moral reality, then moral language is just
sophisticated emoting. Most people (including most atheists) don't live this
way — they act as if some things are really wrong — but the logic of atheism
demands it.

### 4. The Campus Atheist Concedes the Point (Evidence Rating: Primary — Conversation)

Carroll engages a student:

> "But you couldn't justify it by saying it's actually right or wrong. Like for
> example, you'd say that Hitler and Mother Teresa — like their actions were
> not any more or less moral than each other."
>
> Student: "I think Hitler what he thought he was doing to himself was right in
> his moral capacity... I couldn't say that he was morally right or wrong
> because that's — I mean it's not for me to decide."
>
> Carroll: "Agree. But that's what atheism leads you to. So I think that's
> absurd because then how do we have societies?"[^4]

The student cannot bring himself to say Hitler was objectively wrong — because
on atheism, there's no basis for that judgment. Carroll then presses: if
governments decide right and wrong, what about when Hitler WAS the government?
The student has no answer.

### 5. Practical Incoherence (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Pragmatic)

> "How do we have societies?"[^4]

If morality is just preference or social convention, then moral progress is
impossible (there's no objective standard to progress toward), moral reform is
incoherent (the reformer is just expressing a different preference), and
justice is just the imposition of the majority's preferences on the minority.
The entire project of civilization — laws, human rights, justice — presupposes
objective moral truths that atheism cannot supply.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Objective Morality Without God (Secular Moral Realism)
**Steelman:** Many atheist philosophers (Erik Wielenberg, David Enoch, Russ
Shafer-Landau) defend moral realism without theism. They argue that moral
truths are abstract, necessary truths — like mathematical truths — that don't
require a lawgiver. "Torturing children for fun is wrong" is true in the same
way that "2+2=4" is true — independently of any mind, divine or human.

**Response:** Carroll doesn't engage secular moral realism in this video, but
the standard theistic response is: (a) Abstract objects don't stand in causal
relations — how can a non-physical, non-causal moral truth *obligate* physical
beings? (b) The existence of objective, non-natural moral truths in a purely
natural universe is deeply puzzling — it's a "queer" property (the
argument from queerness). (c) Even if moral truths exist as abstract objects,
they don't ground moral *accountability* — who or what holds you accountable to
an abstract truth? Theism provides a personal foundation for both the ontology
of morality AND its practical force.

### Objection 2: The Euthyphro Dilemma
**Steelman:** Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it
because it's good? If the former, morality is arbitrary (God could have
commanded cruelty). If the latter, morality is independent of God, and atheists
can access it directly. Either way, God is not necessary for morality.

**Response:** LDS theology has a distinctive answer: morality is grounded in
*eternal law* that is co-eternal with God. God does not invent morality; He
perfectly embodies and administers eternal moral law (see [[D&C
130.20-21|D&C 130:20-21]]). This is neither horn of Euthyphro: morality is
neither arbitrary divine command nor independent of God in a way that makes God
irrelevant — rather, God is the perfect moral agent who reveals and administers
eternal law. This LDS solution is arguably more philosophically robust than
both voluntarism (divine command theory) and secular moral realism.

### Objection 3: Atheists Can Live Moral Lives
**Steelman:** The fact that atheism can't *ground* moral objectivity doesn't
mean atheists can't *be* moral. Most atheists live ethical lives, often
indistinguishable from religious people. The moral argument attacks atheism's
meta-ethics, not its practical ethics. But that just means atheists are
philosophically inconsistent — not that atheism is false.

**Response:** Carroll would likely agree that many atheists are morally good
people — but that they're "borrowing" moral convictions from a theistic
worldview they've rejected. The argument is that their moral praxis is
inconsistent with their meta-ethics. Whether this is a problem for the atheist
depends on how much they care about consistency. But Carroll's point is that
atheism as a SYSTEM cannot deliver what most people (including most atheists)
want from morality: real right and wrong, not just preferences.
