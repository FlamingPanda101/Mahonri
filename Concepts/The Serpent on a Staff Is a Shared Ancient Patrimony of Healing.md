# The Serpent on a Staff Is a Shared Ancient Patrimony of Healing

#type/claim

## The Claim

The serpent-on-a-staff (Asclepius' rod, Hermes' caduceus) and Israel's [[Numbers 21.8-9|brazen serpent]] are *not* a case of Greek borrowing from Israel or vice versa, but **independent survivals of a common ancient inheritance** of serpent symbolism — power, healing, renewal, and boundary-crossing. [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]], surveying [[James Charlesworth]]'s *The Good and Evil Serpent*, argue this shared patrimony is why the serpent reads as a *healing* sign across cultures, and they note that Hebrew gematria assigns the *same* value (358) to *nachash* ("serpent") and *mashiach* ("Messiah"). [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **The healing-serpent symbol is everywhere, not uniquely Israelite.** At the *asclepion* of Kos, pilgrims practiced *incubation* (sleeping in a sacred place to receive a healing dream), and Asclepius is depicted with a serpent-entwined staff — still the universal sign for pharmacy/hospital. Charlesworth places Hermes with a serpent-bound staff too. Butler's framing: the Greeks "get it from… a common patrimony or ancient inheritance where both of these come from." [^3][^4]
- **The serpent is a natural symbol of resurrection and world-crossing.** The two reasons the hosts find most compelling for *why* a serpent can image Christ: (1) it **sheds its skin** — "it seems to leave behind a body but still live"; (2) it **crosses every world** — underground, up trees, across water — so it can image a being who descends to the dead and ascends to heaven. Hermes (a boundary-crosser) carries the serpent for the same reason. [^5][^6]
- **Gematria links the serpent to the Messiah.** Hebrew letters double as numerals; *nachash* sums to **358**, and *mashiach* (Messiah) also sums to **358**. The hosts cite "Vayner" (the *Roots of the Bible* tradition): "the serpent and Messiah have… the same value and essence. The serpent is the redeemer on the opposite side." They concede gematria is "a messy, loose thing," but argue the coincidence "is something to pay attention to." [^2][^7]
- **Charlesworth's "handmade midrash" is the method.** Rather than asserting one meaning from the pulpit, Charlesworth catalogues "25 or 30" things the serpent symbolized and invites readers to *travel, read, and interpret* — to "get out of our seat of 'I know everything.'" The hosts tie this posture to "look and live" with "a contrite spirit": seeing scripture "with new eyes." [^8][^9]
- **The motif even surfaces in the New Testament's "handle serpents" sayings.** A Greek Orthodox mural at Capernaum shows Christ enthroned in judgment holding a serpent — which a monk glossed simply as "power." Butler links this to Mark 16's "they shall take up serpents" and Paul's viper on Malta (Acts 28), reading them not as snake-handling endorsements but as *coded* statements that following Christ confers rightful *power* (cf. [[D&C 121.39|D&C 121:41-46]], power by persuasion and love). [^10][^11]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **"Common patrimony" is unfalsifiable and risks [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple|parallelomania]]:** protective/healing serpent symbolism is so widespread that shared images are weak evidence of any *specific* historical link; a serpent on a staff is an obvious icon for a creature whose venom both kills and (in antiquity) heals, needing no shared ancestry. (b) **Gematria proves nothing by itself:** with only 22 letters, many unrelated Hebrew words share sums; *nachash* = *mashiach* = 358 is the kind of coincidence cherry-picked *after* the thesis, and rabbinic gematria is a late, playful interpretive device, not evidence of original authorial intent. (c) **The Capernaum-mural → Mark 16 → "power" chain is admitted speculation** ("maybe I'm way off the reservation"), layering a modern devotional gloss onto texts (Mark's longer ending is itself text-critically secondary). A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is deliberately modest — *not* that Israel is the fountainhead, but that a healing-serpent symbol was a genuine, broad ancient inheritance, so the brazen-serpent-as-Christ type is culturally *legible* rather than bizarre; the gematria and mural are offered as suggestive "midrash," not proof.

## Best Quotes

> "I don't think we're saying that the Greeks get this symbol of the serpent around a staff from the Jews… I think we're saying they get it from a common patrimony or ancient inheritance where both of these come from." [^4]

> "The serpent sheds its skin. It seems to die and still live. It leaves behind a body yet it lives… [and] it's a creature that crosses worlds." [^5]

> "The serpent and Messiah have the same sum total of components. They have in essence the same value and essence. The serpent is the redeemer on the opposite side." — Vayner, quoted [^2]
