# The Four Cited Reasons People Leave Are Crowned by Culture

## The Point
From a self-described ~15,000-respondent disaffiliation study, [[Jeff Strong]] reports that **40% of once-active Latter-day Saints have stopped participating in the last ~25 years** and that those who leave cite **four primary reasons** — but his central thesis is that none of the four is the *root*: church *culture* "crashes over the top of those four... like jaws," amplifying each one.[^1][^2] The reasons name the wound; culture, he argues, is the water (or its absence) that decided whether the wound ever festered.

## Evidence

1. **The four cited reasons, with shares (rated: moderate; large but non-probability sample).** Strong lists: (a) *depleted / "the lifestyle isn't working,"* including feeling the culture is too controlling, mental-health strain, guilt/shame, and time demands — **~6%**; (b) *church history* — concerns about the Restoration narrative, prophetic fallibility, polygamy, the priesthood/temple racial restriction — **~42%**; (c) *social disagreement* with church positions on sexual identity, women's voice (most respondents said "not priesthood office, but a bigger voice"), and financial transparency; and (d) *church experience* — **~18%** — people "looking for the living water and bread of life... and just not finding him" in their ward.[^3][^4]

2. **The headline 40% triangulates with independent research (rated: strong).** Strong stresses his number is not the important one but notes the **Harvard CES study and the Pew Religious Landscape Study** both report a figure "a little higher" than 40%, so "two independent, world-class pieces of research plus mine all triangulate around approximately 40%."[^5] This convergence is the strongest part of the claim even though his own sample is non-probability. See [[Church Is Hemorrhaging Members at a Unique Rate]].

3. **The interpretive move: reasons ≠ root (rated: plausible, interpretive).** "There are these four ways, which are the reasons, and then culture crashes over the top of those four, like jaws, making them worse."[^2] This reframes the survey from a list of *causes* into a list of *occasions* whose severity is set by an unmeasured cultural variable — the load-bearing inference of his book *Torn*. See [[Culture Is Soil - Protection and Nourishment Must Be Balanced]].

4. **A parallel, more rigorous taxonomy exists (rated: corroborating).** [[Justin Dyer]]'s peer-grade analysis of [[Ryan Burge]]'s data sorts *leavers* into four **groups by destination/attitude** (faith keepers, faith-leaners, distant, faith-rejectors), where Strong sorts by **cited reason**. The two cut the same population differently and broadly agree that the loud, antagonistic, belief-driven exit is a minority. See [[The Four Groups of LDS Disaffiliates]] and [[Most Who Leave the LDS Church Do Not Become Atheists]].

5. **Honest limits (steelman).** Strong volunteers the disclaimers himself: the study "was not a probability sample, we're not claiming representation, we're not claiming cause and effect."[^6] More pointedly, a critic can note that ~42% citing *church history* is a large, doctrinally serious category that the "it's really culture" framing tends to demote — and the panel's later move to shrink it (see [[Church History Is Often the Soundbite Adopted After a Bad Cultural Experience]]) risks explaining away the single most-cited reason. Self-reported survey reasons are also weak evidence for motive: as one host put it, "ask a girl why she wants to break up with you — she'll never be honest." That cuts both ways: it can hide doctrinal reasons *or* relational ones.

## Best Quotes

> "There are these four ways, which are the reasons, and then culture crashes over the top of those four. Like jaws, you know, making them worse." [^2]

> "Our study suggests that 40% of once active Latter-day Saints have stopped participating in the last 25 years." [^1]

> "The Harvard CES study says a little higher number. The Pew Religious Landscape Study says a little higher number than 40%. So two independent, world-class pieces of research plus mine, all three triangulate around approximately 40%." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The pattern Strong describes — that a felt experience decides whether the "issues" ever take hold — matches the parable of the sower, where the same seed dies in stony ground for lack of root and is choked among thorns, but bears fruit in *good ground* ([[Matthew 13.3-9|Matthew 13:3-9]]). Alma's seed likewise grows only where the heart does not "cast it out by your unbelief" and gives it place ([[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]]). The Restoration's pastoral remedy is therefore not merely better arguments but nourishment: every member is asked to "succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 81.5|D&C 81:5]]), which is what makes the soil good enough that the four reasons rarely become four exits.
