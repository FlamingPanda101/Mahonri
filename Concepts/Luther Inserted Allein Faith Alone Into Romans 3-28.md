# Luther Inserted *Allein* (Faith Alone) Into Romans 3:28

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that *sola fide* is "not a biblical doctrine recovered from corruption" but "a 16th-century invention by a German monk who... added a word to the Bible to make his theology work."[^1] When [[Martin Luther]] translated the New Testament into German in 1522, he rendered [[Romans 3.28|Romans 3:28]] "a man is justified by faith **alone** (*allein*) apart from the works of the law" — and "the word *alone*... is not anywhere in the Greek. It's not in any manuscript."[^1] When Catholic scholars pointed this out, Luther defended the addition (in his 1530 *Open Letter on Translating*), arguing the German idiom required it for clarity — and, as Arnold puts it, "that Dr. Martin Luther would have it so. So the man added a word to the Bible, and then cited himself as the authority."[^2] Arnold pairs this with Luther's hostility to James (calling it "an epistle of Straw" and trying to relocate it) to argue Luther *both* added support for his doctrine *and* tried to demote the book that contradicted it.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Greek lacks any word for "alone."** [[Romans 3.28|Romans 3:28]] reads *dikaiousthai pistei... chōris ergōn nomou* ("justified by faith... apart from works of the law"); there is no *monon*/"only." Luther's *allein* is an interpretive addition, a matter of record. *Rating: factually correct and verifiable.* [^1]
2. **Luther defended it in writing.** The *Open Letter on Translating* (1530) explicitly defends *allein* on idiomatic grounds, and Luther's self-authorizing tone ("*sic volo, sic iubeo*" — so I will, so I command) is a primary source. *Rating: well documented; Arnold's "cited himself as the authority" is a fair gloss of the letter's posture.* [^2]
3. **The James hostility is corroborated independently.** Luther's "epistle of straw" remark and attempt to relocate James are attested across the vault — see [[Luther and the Epistle of Straw]] — so the "added *allein*, demoted James" pattern is not a one-source claim. *Rating: solid, cross-corroborated.* [^2]
4. **It puts *sola fide* in tension with *sola scriptura*.** If the doctrine's flagship prooftext required *adding* a word, then the "Bible alone" cannot be cleanly cited for "faith alone" — see [[Faith Alone Appears Once in Scripture and Is the Verse That Denies It]]. *Rating: a sharp internal-consistency point against the Protestant authority structure.*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The standard Protestant (and Lutheran) defense is substantive, not evasive: (a) **the insertion is interpretive, not corruption** — Luther argued, with grammatical precedent, that idiomatic German requires *allein* to convey an exclusive sense the Greek *chōris ergōn* already carries ("justified by faith, *not* by works" ⇒ "by faith *alone*"); German negation often needs the *allein*/*nur* to read naturally, so he was *translating sense*, not adding doctrine; (b) **earlier writers used the phrase** — Catholic and patristic authors (e.g., a line attributed to Origen, and others) had written *sola fide* in non-Lutheran senses, so Luther did not coin the words from nothing; (c) **Paul's argument is exclusive on its face** — "apart from works of the law" *functions* as "by faith alone," so the word adds emphasis, not new content; (d) Luther never claimed the *word* was in the Greek — he claimed the *meaning* was. A Latter-day Saint reply (and Arnold's): even granting the translation defense, the doctrine then rests on Luther's *interpretive* judgment about Paul's sense — which is exactly the point, since the *one* verse that uses the actual phrase "faith only" ([[James 2.24|James 2:24]]) *denies* it, and reading Paul whole ([[Romans 2.6-7|Rom 2:6-7]]; [[Ephesians 2.8-9|Eph 2:8-10]]) shows he excludes *the law of Moses as a means of earning righteousness*, not the works of a faithful life — see [[Paul Read Whole Rejects the Law of Moses Not Good Works]]. The translation may be defensible; the *doctrine built on the added word* is the disputed move.
