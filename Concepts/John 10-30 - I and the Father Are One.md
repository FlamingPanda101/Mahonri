# John 10:30 — "I and the Father Are One"

## The Charge
> "Jesus said 'I and the Father are one.' Checkmate. Trinitarians win. They are one
> being."

## The Verse (KJV)
> "I and my Father are one." (John 10:30)

## Rebuttal Points

### 1. Definitions: What Does "One" Mean?
- **Trinitarian definition**: One in essence/substance (*homoousios*) — three
  persons, one being.
- **LDS definition**: One in unity, purpose, mission, and desire — three separate
  beings perfectly united.
- The LDS Church ALSO teaches the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are **one** — so the
  verse doesn't distinguish between the two views.
- Braden: "It's how we define one. In a Trinitarian's mind, one is three persons,
  one essence. Whereas we would define one as perfectly one in unity, mission,
  desire, etc. Not one being."

### 2. Context: Jesus Prays for the Disciples to Be "One" Too
In John 17:20-23, Jesus prays that his disciples "may be one, as we are." If "one"
means one being/essence, Jesus is praying for all believers to merge into a single
being — which no Trinitarian believes.

This is the strongest internal contextual rebuttal:
> "That they all may be one; as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee, that they
> also may be one in us... that they may be one, even as we are one." (John 17:21-22)

### 3. Proof-Texting Error
Reading a single verse in isolation as though it's the author's complete theology
is exactly how most of these misconstructions happen. John wrote one continuous
epistle, not chapter-and-verse sound bites.

## Bottom Line
Jesus's "oneness" with the Father is a oneness of unity and purpose —
the same kind of oneness He desired for all His disciples.
