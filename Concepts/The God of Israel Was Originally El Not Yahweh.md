# The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues there were originally **two** gods of Israel, and the *first* was not Yahweh but **El**. The name *Israel* (*Yisra-EL*) embeds the theophoric element **El** — the Northwest Semitic patriarchal high deity — and most scholars hold El, not Yahweh, was the people's original patron. A deity named **Yahweh** is not attested anywhere in the region until the mid-9th century BCE; he became Israel's patron later and was **conflated with El** (probably around 1000 BCE) until one god was both El and Yahweh. This is the *historical-dating-and-merger* version of the El/Yahweh story — distinct from, and underneath, the static "divine family" picture in [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The name "Israel" is built on El, not Yahweh (rated: strong; onomastic).** "The original, based on the name Israel, is El, the Northwest Semitic patriarchal high deity. And so Israel probably borrowed that deity and their divine profile from the broader Northwest Semitic world."[^1] The earliest external reference to the people — the Merneptah Stele context (~1208 BCE) — carries no deity named Yahweh anywhere within centuries.[^2]

2. **Yahweh has no attestation until the 9th century BCE (rated: strong; epigraphic).** "We don't have any inscriptional reference to Yahweh at all until around the middle of the 9th century BCE" — the **Mesha/Moabite inscription**, the **black obelisk of Shalmaneser III**, the **Tel Dan stele**, and personal names with Yahwistic theophoric elements. By then a deity named Yahweh "showed up and became probably the patron deity of Israel."[^3]

3. **The two were conflated, not identical from the start (rated: moderate; reconstruction).** McClellan dates the merger to roughly 1000 BCE: Yahweh "was conflated with El and they became one and the same deity… and many centuries later became the only god that exists."[^4] This staged sequence — separate El, late Yahweh, conflation, then monolatry, then monotheism — is the spine of the critical reconstruction.

4. **Coheres with the name-revelation texts (rated: mainstream within the field).** The Priestly [[Exodus 6.3|Exodus 6:3]] has God appear to the patriarchs as *El Shaddai* but withhold the name *Yahweh* until Moses — exactly what a later-arriving Yahweh layered onto an older El religion would look like. See [[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses]].

5. **Steelman of the opposing view.** Traditional scholars read El and Yahweh as two **titles/epithets of the one God** from the beginning (Exod 6:3 then meaning "I was not *known* in the character the name signifies"), with the patriarchal *El* references being the same deity later called Yahweh. On this reading the epigraphic silence reflects accidents of survival, not theological history, and *Yisra-EL* is simply a generic "God" element. McClellan's reply is that the simplest account of an El-based name plus centuries of Yahweh-silence plus the explicit name-withholding texts is two originally distinct deities later merged.

## LDS Relevance
A genuine *plurality* of divine beings standing behind the canonical "God of Israel" — El the high father and Yahweh among the divine sons — matches the Latter-day Saint reading that the original biblical theology was not creedal *ex nihilo* monotheism but a divine council/family later collapsed into one absolute being. The El/Yahweh distinction is the historical hinge LDS apologists cite (with [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deut 32:8-9]]) for [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]]. (McClellan himself frames this descriptively as critical scholarship, not as an LDS argument.)
