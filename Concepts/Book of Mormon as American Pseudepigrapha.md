# Book of Mormon as American Pseudepigrapha

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Kyle Beshears]] declines to call the Book of Mormon inspired, canonical, or ancient scripture ("I've read it through three times and that's just not where I land"), labeling it instead **"American Pseudepigrapha"** — "a text that is falsely attributed... to certain authors." But he equally refuses the dismissive anti-Mormon posture: the label "does not mean it's useless." [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **Definition of the label.** Pseudepigrapha = falsely-attributed authorship; in this case to "the redactors of the Book of Mormon and ultimately Mormon himself." [^2] *Rating: a precise technical category* borrowed from biblical studies, deliberately less polemical than "fraud."

2. **Not useless, not to be desecrated.** He rejects the "knucklehead evangelical [who] recently lit it on fire to light a cigar"; the text is not "to be disrespected like that." [^3] *Rating: consistent with his center-holding method* (see [[Holding the Tension Between Anti-Mormonism and Syncretism]]).

3. **A snapshot of 19th-century American Protestantism.** "It captures a lot of the zeitgeist of American Protestantism in the early 19th century... a very unique snapshot into the interests [and] concerns of Americans who were Protestant." [^4] *Rating: the core scholarly thesis;* it is "answering questions that are being asked" in that period.

4. **Worked example: the fate of those who never heard the gospel.** A pressing 19th-century question — "why would God allow a people group to go... 1,400 years without ever having access to the gospel?" — is answered by the Book of Mormon's claim "that indigenous people on this continent are descended from Jews and... were introduced to the gospel," thereby "sustaining God's justice." [^5] *Rating: a strong illustration* of the "answering contemporary questions" thesis.

5. **It implies the Bible's insufficiency.** "The presence of the Book of Mormon suggests insufficiency of the Bible." He prefers "extended the canon" over "opened the canon," since "closed" canon means "silent," not that God no longer speaks. [^6] *Rating: a careful conceptual distinction* that reframes the standard sola-scriptura objection.

6. **"What it is" over "what it says."** "I'm more interested in what the Book of Mormon is rather than what the Book of Mormon says." [^7] *Rating: a candid statement of method;* he flags that host [[Steven Pynakker]] is fascinated by both the material/plates question and the literary/narrative one.

**Steelman of the LDS view.** A Latter-day Saint would invoke [[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]]'s promise of a personal spiritual witness — which Beshears himself calls "very different than anything... in any kind of religious text" — and argue that the "19th-century concerns" thesis is consistent with an ancient text *translated for* a 19th-century audience. Beshears engages this honestly elsewhere (see [[Joseph Smith Did Not Author the Book of Mormon]]), conceding the text's sophistication while still denying ancient origin.

## Best Quotes

> "It captures a lot of the zeitgeist of American Protestantism in the early 19th century... It's answering questions that are being asked." [^4]

> "I'm more interested in what the Book of Mormon is rather than what the Book of Mormon says, if that makes sense." [^7]
