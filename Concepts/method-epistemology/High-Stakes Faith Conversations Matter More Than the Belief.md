# High-Stakes Faith Conversations Matter More Than the Belief

## The Point
Jeff Strong, working with author Joseph Grenny (*Crucial Conversations*; The Other Side Academy), researched the conversations families have when a loved one is in faith transition — and found the *conversation itself* shapes outcomes more than the person's beliefs do. The data: 83% of people going through a faith transition reach out to someone to talk; 40% of those conversations "go badly"; talking to a *devout parent* makes it "twice as likely to go badly," and a *devout church leader* "four times as likely."[^1] The pivotal finding: "the conversation itself is more important than what the person actually believes and whether they stay or go." If it goes well and "people feel safe and supported in their wrestling, there's a good chance they'll stay and wrestle. And if they do not feel safe and supported, they will continue the wrestle, but somewhere else."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Don't talk "under the influence."** Strong's most-cited principle (from Grenny): never have a high-stakes faith conversation "under the influence" — where the influence is "fear, anxiety, anger, empty chairs at the dining table." The effective move is to "take a breather," calm down, and "get in terms with where you're really at" first.[^3]
2. **Work on yourself before fixing them.** The effective parent in Strong's contrast got honest about his own story — "I'm not a good father if my son comes home from a mission... I want the validation, I want people to admire my family" — and chose a better story: "the father that's wise and patient and supportive." The ineffective move is to "focus on fixing them."[^4] Strong's own failure with his son Kale ("we immediately drew the wrong conclusions... fear screws your brain up and then you do the wrong things") is the negative case.[^5]
3. **Connection, not correction.** "These conversations need to be about connection, not about correction." The accompanying disposition is greater trust in God: "God's got this. The spirit will work on people... and not everybody has the same path."[^6] One father's prayer "How can I turn my son to repentance?" was met, he felt, with a rebuke: "This isn't about you, and this isn't about repentance. This is him and his life."[^7]
4. **Culture conditions the failure.** The same father realized "for 40 years the culture had conditioned me to believe that the only success was that all my children serve missions, all my children marry in the temple, and the celestial dining table is full" — a cultural script that made him crave validation and drove the bad conversation.[^8] This ties the conversation problem back to [[Culture Can Crash Over Doctrine in a Faith Crisis|cultural conditioning]]. *Steelman:* one might object that prioritizing "the conversation" over "the truth" risks affirming someone into unbelief or treating doctrine as negotiable. Strong's claim is narrower and empirical — *safety predicts whether a wrestler stays to wrestle at all*; he is not saying beliefs are unimportant, but that a hostile conversation forecloses the very relationship in which truth could be worked out.

## LDS Connection
Strong's research reframes ministering to a doubting loved one as a *relational* task before an apologetic one — consistent with the gospel's insistence that persuasion comes "by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned" and never "by compulsion" (D&C 121:41–42), and that "perfect love casteth out fear" (1 John 4:18) — the very fear Strong identifies as the corrupting "influence." That a *devout* parent or leader is statistically the *most* likely to mishandle the conversation is a sobering caution: zeal without safety drives the wrestler away (cf. the elder brother of the prodigal, who could not celebrate a returning sibling, Luke 15:28–30; whereas the father runs to embrace). It complements the diagnostic counsel in [[Diagnose the Struggle - Moral Disagreement vs Intellectual Doubt]] — first understand the real struggle, then prioritize connection — and it explains a mechanism behind [[Most Who Leave Drifted Away Rather Than Rejected Faith|drifting rather than rejecting]]: people often leave the *community* (after unsafe conversations) before they ever leave the *doctrine*.
