# The Rider and the Elephant - Intuition Drives Reason

## Overview

[[Jonathan Haidt]]'s rider-and-elephant metaphor as it is deployed in the Thoughtful Faith epistemology series: the mind is a small **rider** (conscious verbal reasoning) sitting atop a large **elephant** (the ~99% of automatic, intuitive processing that actually drives behavior). The sharper point this note isolates — beyond the basic anatomy laid out in [[The Rider and the Elephant Model of the Mind]] — is the *order of operations*: **intuition comes first and reasoning comes second.**

## The Claim

The elephant makes a quick, pattern-matched judgment "within a second," and the rider's reasoning is recruited *afterward* to justify a conclusion the elephant already reached. Reason is therefore not the master that weighs evidence and then decides; it is more often the press secretary that explains a decision already made by intuition. This is Haidt's **social intuitionist** model: moral and worldview judgments are intuition-first, post-hoc-rationalized.

Two consequences follow:

1. **For persuasion** — reasons aimed only at the rider fail if the elephant is "digging in its heels"; you must [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First|speak to the elephant first]].
2. **For epistemic humility** — since we are not as in-control of our beliefs as we feel, calibrated humility about our own reasoning is warranted ([[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think]]).

The Thoughtful Faith host then makes a confessional move that is the host's, not Haidt's: identifying the elephant with the scriptural "spirit" / Hebrew heart. See [[The Elephant Is the Spirit - The Hebrew Heart]].
