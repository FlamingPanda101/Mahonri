# Define Christianity Before Contrasting It With Mormonism

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
**Objection format:** "In *Christianity*, God is X… while in Mormonism, God is Y" — the standard contrastive frame of "Are Mormons really Christian?" content, which sets a singular "Christianity" against "Mormonism" on the nature of God, salvation, scripture, etc.

**Rebuttal:** [[Cardon Ellis]] and the panel ([[Dalton Williams]], [[Devin Barnes]], [[Braden Gospel Guardian|Braden]]) press the critic to **define which Christianity**. "In Christianity, God is…" presupposes a unanimous Christian doctrine that does not exist: Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, Presbyterians, Pentecostals, Baptists disagree among themselves on the very points being used to exclude Latter-day Saints. Pushed, the implicit definition "gets whittled down until it's literally just their brand, in their sect, as taught by their pastor in their hometown" — "the reformed Baptist church of the baby Jesus of Nazareth from the southwest side." The demand for a definition exposes that the "Christianity" doing the excluding is a particular tradition, not a neutral standard.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Christianity" is not doctrinally monolithic — rate: strong (uncontested fact)
On nature of God (Trinity vs. social/relational models, divine simplicity debates), soteriology (Calvinist monergism vs. Arminian synergism vs. Catholic infused grace), authority (sola scriptura vs. scripture-plus-tradition/magisterium), and sacraments, mainstream Christian bodies differ sharply. A flat "in Christianity, X" elides real intra-Christian disagreement. *Strong: this is simply true of global Christianity.*[^1]

### 2. The exclusion criterion is usually the speaker's own sect — rate: strong rhetorical point
When asked to specify, the critic typically lands on distinctives not shared by Catholics/Orthodox/Pentecostals — i.e., the operative "Christianity" is the speaker's denomination. The panel dramatizes the reductio ("…from the southwest side"). *Strong as a debate move: it shifts the burden onto the objector to state and defend a non-arbitrary boundary.*[^1]

### 3. Several "uniquely Mormon" doctrines are in fact catholic — rate: strong (vault-documented)
The same episode shows that deification ([[All the Church Fathers Taught Apotheosis]]), a grace-centered reading of works ([[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]]), and the eternality of Christ despite the "brothers" language ([[Jesus and Satan Are Brothers Only If Nothing Is Created]]) are not Mormon novelties but have patristic/biblical pedigree — so the contrastive frame often misstates *both* sides. *Strong as cumulative support.*[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic has a serious reply: pluralism does not make *every* boundary arbitrary. (a) **There is an ecumenical core.** Most who press "are Mormons Christian?" appeal not to their local sect but to the **historic creeds** (Nicene/Chalcedonian) and the **ecumenical councils** as the shared definition of "Christian orthodoxy" across Catholic, Orthodox, and most Protestant bodies — and by *that* widely-held standard, the LDS rejection of the Trinity (one God in three persons, *homoousios*), creation *ex nihilo*, and the Creator-creature distinction is a genuine, non-arbitrary difference, not mere hometown sectarianism. So "define your Christianity" can be answered ("creedal/Nicene Christianity") without collapsing into "my pastor's church." (b) **"They disagree too" is a tu quoque.** That Calvinists and Arminians differ on predestination does not show that *all* differences are equal; intra-creedal disputes occur *within* a shared confession of the Triune God, whereas the LDS difference is *about* that confession — a difference in kind, not degree. (c) **Self-definition vs. external category.** Latter-day Saints are free to call themselves Christian (they worship and follow Christ), and the question may be better posed as "creedally orthodox?" than "Christian?"; the panel's reframing can dodge the substantive theological disagreement by relocating it into a semantics quarrel. The defensible core for the panel is real and useful: the *bare* contrastive slogan "in Christianity, God is X" is sloppy and often smuggles a sectarian standard, so demanding a definition is a fair and clarifying move — but the strongest critics *can* supply one (creedal/Nicene Christianity), at which point the conversation should proceed to the actual doctrinal merits (Trinity, ex nihilo, theosis) rather than treating the definitional point as a victory.
