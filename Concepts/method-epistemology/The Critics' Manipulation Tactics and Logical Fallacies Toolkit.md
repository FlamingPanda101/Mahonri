# The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit

## The Point
Austin Fife's central methodological claim is that the case against the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints rarely fails on the facts; it fails on the *interpretation and presentation* of the facts. "Ultimately, the manipulation happens in the presentation of data and history, not the data and history itself."[^1] After a decade engaging the [[CES Letter]] and Mormon Stories, Fife catalogs a recurring set of rhetorical tactics and logical fallacies that critics deploy, arguing that once a reader learns to name them, "I couldn't unsee it."[^2] The catalog is meant as a reusable diagnostic toolkit: hold any critical argument up against the list and ask which move is doing the persuading.

## Evidence
Fife's table (pp.9-14) names roughly two dozen tactics. The highest-leverage ones for evaluating Restoration arguments:[^3]

- **The Big List (Gish Gallop / Proof by Verbosity)** — overwhelming the reader with sheer mass so that even if one concern has an answer, "there are 100 more." Fife calls the CES Letter "the hydra of manipulation strategies."[^3]
- **False Premise / Begging the Question** — smuggling in an unsupported premise so the conclusion follows automatically; "even if the target does not agree with the conclusion, the believed false premise itself can generate doubt." Example: "The Church *hid* that Joseph used a seer stone, thus the Church is immoral."[^4]
- **Non-Sequitur** — a conclusion that does not follow from the premise ("Nelson was in a college fraternity; how is he God's prophet?").[^4]
- **Questionable Cause (correlation vs. causation)** — in Fife's experience "the primary type of data critics use," e.g. "Utah has a higher suicide rate, thus the Church makes people suicidal."[^4]
- **Argument from Ignorance** — "true because not yet proven false," e.g. the pre-1830s "ancient people never wrote on metal plates" claim, later overturned.[^5]
- **Shifting the Burden of Proof** — making a claim, then demanding the believer disprove it ("Brigham conspired to kill Joseph. Prove I'm wrong.").[^5] (See [[The Tu Quoque Deflection and the Burden of Proof]].)
- **Generalization / Hasty Generalization** — indicting the whole institution from one anecdote (a single ward's mishandling of abuse "proves" the Church is abusive).[^5]
- **Texas Sharpshooter** — taking loose parallels out of context and painting bullseyes around them after the fact; Fife's example is treating *View of the Hebrews* or *The Late War* as the "source" of the Book of Mormon.[^6] (See [[Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy in Canon Criteria]] and [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]].)
- **Half-Truth** — "still a lie at its core ... more destructive than a lie because a half-truth requires untangling the lie from the truth."[^7]
- **Presentism** — judging 1830s figures by 2020s standards (e.g. early leaders' partial Word of Wisdom observance).[^7]
- **Double Standard** — applying a test to the Restoration that the critic's own tradition would fail ("If the gold plates are real, where are they?" — the same logic dissolves the Ten Commandments tablets or the witnesses of Christ's resurrection).[^8]
- **Myth of Infallibility** — assuming prophets must be near-perfect, then displaying any fallibility as a defeater. "The formula for critics is easy: 1) assume church leaders are infallible, 2) show an example of fallibility."[^9]
- **Naturalist Assumptions** — ruling out the supernatural a priori ("Angels don't appear to men to give them golden plates"), which Fife calls "close-minded by definition."[^9]

Fife frames the deployment of these tactics as "doubt bombing," quoting former Latter-day Saint anthropologist Manuel Padro that the strategy is to "bombard you with so many doubt-provoking questions that they can cause your resolve to collapse."[^10]

## Best Quotes
> "Ultimately, the manipulation happens in the presentation of data and history, not the data and history itself."[^1]

> "Once I started seeing it, I couldn't unsee it."[^2]

> "A half-truth is still a lie at its core. It is more destructive than a lie because a half-truth requires untangling the lie from the truth."[^7]

> "[The tactic is] trying to coerce you into a situation where they can bombard you with so many doubt-provoking questions that they can cause your resolve to collapse and your identity to fall apart… If that sounds abusive, it's because that's what it is." — Manuel Padro[^10]

## LDS Connection
For a Latter-day Saint facing a faith crisis, this toolkit reframes the encounter: the goal is not to out-research a professional critic on archaeology, linguistics, and law overnight, but to identify *which rhetorical move* a given argument depends on. Naming the fallacy neutralizes its force and buys time to "learn more," which Fife found almost always dissolved the negative narrative. It also defuses the [[Argument from Silence Is Not Always a Fallacy|argument-from-silence]] and burden-shifting pressure by relocating the burden where it belongs — on the party making the positive claim.
