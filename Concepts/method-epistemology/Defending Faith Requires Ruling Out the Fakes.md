# Defending Faith Requires Ruling Out the Fakes

## The Claim

The governing thesis of the episode: a believer who wants to defend the Book of Mormon must **actively debunk the forged artifacts**, because "eliminating the bad gives you clarity of vision" on the genuine evidence. [[Josh Galey]] frames the whole exercise as *delighting in truth and denouncing deception* ([[2 Nephi 33]]), even when it means telling fellow believers their cherished artifact is fake.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Fakes discredit the real case — rate: moderate (apologetic strategy)
Galey wants the "door to close" on objects that are "clearly fake, clearly just made up," because so long as believers cite forgeries, critics can tar the *authentic* evidence by association. Ruling out the fakes is therefore not an attack on faith but a precondition for a credible positive case. The hosts note members are doubly exploited — by "woke scolds" who weaponize their humanity and by hucksters who "leverage your belief against you" to sell forgeries.[^2]

### 2. The believing heart checked by evidence — rate: moderate (epistemology)
[[Cardon Ellis]] articulates the method: let "the believing heart be checked by the evidence," then reassess belief in light of it — and where one's belief outruns current evidence, "continue to believe, waiting for future evidence," a posture he says is "almost always rewarded." This keeps faith and honest inquiry in tension rather than collapsing either.[^3]

### 3. The spirit of the critique — rate: n/a (tone)
Galey explicitly disclaims wanting to upset anyone, invoking [[2 Nephi 33]] ("I glory in plainness; I glory in truth; I glory in my Jesus"). Challenging a paradigm is framed as healthy when done in love — "let's look at that together" — modeling charitable debunking among co-believers.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

There is a real tension the slogan can paper over. (a) The same "wait for future evidence" charity that the hosts extend to the (genuine but unprovenanced) gold plates is what *defenders of the fakes* invoke for the Michigan Relics and Padilla stones — so "rule out the fakes" and "keep believing past the evidence" can pull in opposite directions, and the line between them ("this lacking object is real, that lacking object is fake") can look motivated rather than principled. (b) A skeptic notes that the asymmetry — demanding rigorous forensics for *others'* artifacts while accepting the central LDS artifacts on *witness testimony and spiritual confirmation* — is exactly the inconsistency the panel elsewhere criticizes in critics. (c) "Eliminating the bad clarifies the good" assumes there *is* a robust body of good Book-of-Mormon archaeology to clarify, which is itself contested. So the strongest form of the principle is epistemic hygiene that any honest inquirer should accept — do not cite known forgeries; let evidence discipline belief — while recognizing that *which* objects count as "ruled out" versus "awaiting evidence" must be decided by the same forensic standard for friendly and unfriendly artifacts alike, on pain of special pleading.
