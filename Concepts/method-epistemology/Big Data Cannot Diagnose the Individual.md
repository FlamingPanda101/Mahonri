# Big Data Cannot Diagnose the Individual

## The Claim
Macro-level religious data describe the overall "forest" of trends but cannot characterize any single "tree." Two symmetrical fallacies must be avoided: generalizing one person's experience to everyone, and imposing aggregate trends onto a specific person. Good practice uses the macro picture to *contextualize* individuals, not to judge them.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Forest and trees, both directions (rated: strong, methodological).** One can "miss the forest for the trees" (lost in minutiae) but also "miss the trees for the forest" (so focused on the macro you miss the individual). Big datasets describe the landscape; an individual saying "my experience isn't reflected here" is "not surprising at all."[^1]

2. **Two symmetrical fallacies (rated: strong).** The fallacy of "this is my experience, therefore everybody's," and the fallacy of "these are the macro trends, therefore this individual's experience." Both confuse levels of analysis.[^2]

3. **Application to ministry (rated: moderate, normative).** Dyer, a former bishop, says having the data "in my head" helps "contextualize what this person is experiencing," but "you always want to understand the person who's right in front of you... at every moment."[^3] *Steelman:* this principle can be misused defensively — invoking "every situation is individual" to deflect any aggregate finding one dislikes; Dyer's own argument depends on taking macro data seriously, so the caution is about *level-appropriate* inference, not data skepticism.

4. **Application to the suicide narrative (rated: strong).** The Utah-suicide claim is presented as a concrete case of "missing the forest for the trees" — looking only at Utah without its region. See [[Utah Suicide Rate Implicates the Church]].[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "You might miss the forest for the trees... However, you can also miss the trees for the forest — you're focusing so much on the macro that you miss the individual."[^1]

> "There's the fallacy of saying 'well, that's everybody's experience in the church.' There's also the fallacy of saying 'well, these are the macro trends, and then that's going to be an individual's experience.'"[^2]

> "Having all the data in my head is a great thing, but you always want to understand the person who's right in front of you."[^3]
