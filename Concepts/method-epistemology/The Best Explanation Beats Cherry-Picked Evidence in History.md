# The Best Explanation Beats Cherry-Picked Evidence in History

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] lays out the historian's core method as a corrective to both apologetic and critical cherry-picking: because the historical record is **messy**, you can almost always find *some* evidence for a proposition — even a false one — so the existence of a supporting source settles nothing. The right questions are (1) what does the **preponderance** of the evidence show, and (2) which **model** explains the *greatest amount* of the data in the *simplest* way. A good explanation is the one that accounts for the most facts with the least contrivance; a conspiracy theory typically fails because it explains the data in a needlessly complicated way and ignores glaring problems (above all, the absence of any defector). He adds a practical maxim: "much of history is just getting the events in the **right order**" — build a tight chronology and the cause-and-effect relationships "pop out… like watching dominoes fall."[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "evidence on both sides" experience — rate: strong, concrete
Bradley recounts researching whether [[Joseph F. Smith]] authorized post-1904 plural marriages: looking only at the evidence that he *didn't*, he was "completely convinced"; looking only at the evidence that he *did*, he was "totally convinced" the other way. The lesson is that one-sided evidence-gathering manufactures false certainty, which is why the *totality* and the best-fit model must govern.[^1]

### 2. Conspiracy theories are penalized for needless complexity — rate: strong as method
Applied to the [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]]-day deed to [[Hyrum Smith]] and [[Emma Smith]], Bradley prefers the simple explanation (William Clayton's clerical error — wrong name written, deed left incomplete, re-made to Emma) over a forgery conspiracy, which would be "needlessly complicated," fail to explain the data, and require that *no one* among scores of participants ever exposed it. This is the same scoring he applies to polygamy denial. See [[The Eyewitness Asymmetry Refutes the Polygamy Conspiracy Theory]].[^2]

### 3. Chronology as method — rate: strong, distinctive
"Look at things in their context… see the events in their order. When you do that, the relationships between events just pop out." Getting the sequence right is what lets the historian see motive and causation rather than impose them. Bradley elsewhere deploys exactly this to re-order [[Joseph Smith]]'s plural marriages (see [[Joseph Smith Married Pregnant Women Refuting the Sexual-Motive Thesis]]).[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can grant all of this as textbook method and still object that "inference to the best explanation" is only as good as the explainer's priors: what counts as "simplest" and which data are weighted is where bias re-enters, so the method does not by itself adjudicate contested cases — a believing historian and a skeptical one can each claim their model is the "best fit." There is also a risk of the method becoming unfalsifiable: if every awkward datum can be absorbed as an "anomaly the best model tolerates," the framework can protect a favored conclusion. The honest reply (which Bradley's own practice models) is that abduction is defeasible and public — competing models are laid side by side and scored on coverage and parsimony, and the believer must be willing to *change his mind* when the data demand it, as Bradley says he did on Joseph Smith's polygamy motive.

## Best Quotes
> "The historical record is messy and… we will find evidence pointing in different directions. So the question isn't just 'can I find evidence pointing to something?' Often you can, even if that thing never happened."[^1]

> "The best explanation is the model that explains the greatest amount of the data in the simplest way possible."[^2]

> "Much of history is just getting the events in the right order… then often the relationships between events just pop out. It's like watching dominoes fall."[^3]
