# Psychohistory Dresses Conjecture in the Veneer of Academia

## The Claim
The Ward Radio panel argues that [[Fawn Brodie]]'s *No Man Knows My History* (1945/46) is the prototype of a flawed genre — **psychohistory** (her own term, "psychobiography") — that reads Freudian motive into a historical figure and "presents conjecture as fact" while wearing "the thin veneer of academia." Joseph Smith, they say, was the *first* major figure subjected to this method; the same method later produced Brodie's Jefferson book, which real historians savaged.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Brodie's method by her own label (rated: strong, documented)
Brodie wrote five biographies, four using Freudian psychology; Wikipedia calls her a "psychobiographer." Her Joseph Smith portrait — "a fraudulent genius of improvisation" — has been described even by admirers as "a work that presented conjecture as fact."[^1] The panel's gloss: "that's a nice way of saying this chick don't know what she's talking about and she's BSing."[^1]

### 2. A hostile-witness historian (rated: strong, primary review)
[[Gary Wills]] — a left-of-center, Obama-voting, Pulitzer-winning historian, *not* a conservative or a Latter-day Saint — reviewed Brodie's Jefferson book and wrote: "Two vast things, each wondrous in itself, combined to make this book a prodigy — the author's industry and her ignorance. One can only be so intricately wrong by deep study and long effort enough to make Miss Brodie the fasting hermit and the very saint of ignorance." That a non-aligned expert demolished her method blunts the "you only hate her because she's anti-Mormon" reply.[^1]

### 3. The "veneer" pattern, generalized (rated: moderate, the panel's thesis)
The hosts argue psychology lends itself to this because it "itself has the thin veneer of academia" (invoking the replication crisis), and tie it to modern critics whose "expertise" is psychoanalysis (e.g. [[John Dehlin]]) — diagnosing motives from a distance "like the people that try and diagnose Donald Trump ... from Twitter."[^1] In Brodie's *Joseph Smith* book she likewise asserted "there is some evidence that Fanny Alger bore Joseph Smith a child" while citing none — conjecture dressed as documentation.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A serious reply: (a) Brodie was a genuinely industrious, archive-driven biographer and a former Latter-day Saint who knew the milieu — "popularizer of a flawed genre" should not collapse into "she invented everything," and her Joseph Smith biography remains historically significant (see [[Fawn Brodie Popularized the View of the Hebrews Theory]]); (b) psychobiography as such is not worthless — historians do legitimately infer motive and psychology from evidence, and the abuse of a method doesn't discredit every use; and (c) the "veneer of academia / replication crisis" move is itself a broad-brush dismissal that proves too much (it could be turned on any discipline). The panel's strongest, narrowest point is the documented one: Brodie repeatedly stated conjecture (Fanny Alger; Jefferson–Hemings) as fact without supporting citation, and a non-aligned expert (Wills) said so in print.
