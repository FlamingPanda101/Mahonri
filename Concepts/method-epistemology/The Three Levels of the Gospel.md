# The Three Levels of the Gospel

## The Claim
Relayed by [[Daniel C. Peterson]] from a talk by historian Stanley Kimball (descendant of [[Heber C. Kimball]]): faith maturity has three "levels." Level A is the simplified Sunday-school story (Saints always right, villains simply evil). Level B is its anti-Mormon inversion (everything good is secretly bad, everything taught is wrong). Level C is the mature synthesis: the Church's *claims are true*, even though the humans living them have often erred.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Level A — the "junior Sunday school version" (rated: described).** "The Saints are always right. We always did the right thing, and the bad guys are just bad... they just are evil." Kimball, as a historian, found this "not quite accurate," but judged it "fundamentally right."[^2]

2. **Level B — the anti-Mormon inversion (rated: described).** "Everybody you thought was good is bad. Everything the Saints did that you thought was good is evil. Everything we teach is wrong." Once someone internalizes Level B, "it's difficult sometimes to save them."[^3] This maps onto the [[Hero-Flattening as a Cultural Disease]] reflex.

3. **Level C — the mature synthesis (rated: the goal).** "You take on some of that negative stuff and you realize, yeah, it's not entirely false. But level A is not fundamentally wrong." The claims are true; the imperfect humans teaching and practicing them have not always gotten it right. "The Lord is working through imperfect vessels."[^4] This is the historiographic form of [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]].

4. **The pastoral tradeoff (rated: candid concession).** Kimball admitted that taking people *through* Level B risks losing them: "Well, some people are going to lose faith... I wouldn't want that on my conscience. So maybe level A is the best after all." Peterson reports this tension without fully resolving it.[^5] *Steelman of the critic:* if Level A is knowingly oversimplified yet preferred for retention, critics charge the institution with managing information rather than telling the truth. Peterson's counter is the 1 Nephi 8 reading below.

5. **Saints needed to repent too (rated: applied to Missouri).** Working on *Becoming Brigham*, Peterson concluded the Saints in Missouri sometimes had it coming: poor communication, claims like "this land is ours because the Lord gave it" without clarifying they meant to *buy* it. "There are things we did that were wrong." The white-hats/black-hats picture is "still a little bit that way, but not entirely."[^6]

6. **1 Nephi 8 framing (rated: scriptural).** In Lehi's vision of the tree of life, "Lehi is not the focus... It's the tree." If you want something perfect, "you're not going to find it in the Church" — only in Christ; the institution of fallible people will not be perfect.[^7]

## Best Quotes

> "There's level A. That's the junior Sunday school version... The Saints are always right... and the bad guys are just bad."[^2]

> "My ideal as a historian would be to get them to level C, which is where you take on some of that negative stuff and you realize, yeah, it's not entirely false. But level A is not fundamentally wrong."[^4]

> "If you want something perfect, you're not going to find it in the Church. ... collectively, the Church doing things isn't going to be perfect."[^7]
