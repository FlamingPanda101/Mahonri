# Culture Can Crash Over Doctrine in a Faith Crisis

## The Point
Jeff Strong's disaffiliation survey (~15,000 respondents) found four primary stated reasons people leave the LDS Church: lifestyle (~6%), church history (42%), disagreement with social issues or doctrine (33%), and negative church experience (~18%).[^1] But his central thesis is that the fourth reason is really a *cultural* undercurrent that "crashes over the top of the other three, making them worse."[^2] The evidence for this is that plenty of members hold serious concerns about church history or hold differing views on social issues *and remain active*; what distinguishes those who leave is a spiritually "unnourishing" church experience. As Strong puts it: if your church experience is great you can usually carry the other issues, "but if you've got those issues and you're having a spiritually unnourishing church experience, you're probably not going to stay."[^2] The upshot: a faith crisis is rarely caused by data alone — culture is the amplifier that turns a manageable concern into an exit.

## Evidence
1. **The four buckets, with church experience as the hidden multiplier.** Lifestyle is small (~6%) and often about depletion ("hollowed out," guilt and shame) rather than wanting to sin. History (42%) and social/doctrinal disagreement (33%) are the familiar categories. The fourth, "church experience" (~18%), is mostly *devout* people who feel the church "doesn't feel Christ-centered... it feels institutional," heavy on "prophets and temples and obedience, but less about the carpenter of Bethlehem."[^1] Strong calls these "waves," and says this wave magnifies the rest.[^2]
2. **The "stayers with concerns" test.** Strong asks the interviewer whether he knows people with significant church-history concerns who remain active and faithful (yes, "many"), and people with different views on social issues who remain active (yes). That doctrine and history "matter, for sure" yet do not by themselves force an exit is the empirical hinge of his argument: "there's something else."[^3]
3. **Leavers are sincere and invest years.** 85% of those who stepped away "spent more than 100 hours over several years," and 49% spent effectively unlimited hours over at least five years trying to work through the issues. The mindset is "I love this church... I've encountered these things that are really concerning to me, and my journey now is to do everything I can to resolve those and stay" — not "I wanted an excuse."[^4] This dismantles the "lazy / couldn't cut it" stereotype (cf. [[Most Who Leave Drifted Away Rather Than Rejected Faith]]).
4. **A bounded scope.** Strong deliberately brackets top-down policy and structural factors ("things that come from church leaders... we don't have as much control over that") to focus on the member-level culture every Latter-day Saint helps create through how they "understand, interpret, and apply" doctrine and policy — where there is "wide variability."[^5] *Steelman:* a critic could reply that this conveniently shields institutional decisions from scrutiny and that "culture" can absorb blame that belongs to doctrine or leadership; Strong's own data (history at 42%) shows doctrine/history are genuine drivers, not mere symptoms.

## Best Quotes
> "There's an undercurrent of a cultural component that's huge. And we got to deal with it." [^2]

> "If your church experience is great, you can probably deal with some of those other issues without too much trouble. But if you've got those issues and you're having a spiritually unnourishing church experience, you're probably not going to stay." [^2]

> "85% of those that stepped away spent more than 100 hours over several years, and 49% spent limitless hours over at least 5 years trying to work through the issues." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Strong's "culture amplifies doctrine" thesis sharpens the standard pastoral task of diagnosing a struggle (see [[Diagnose the Struggle - Moral Disagreement vs Intellectual Doubt]]): the presenting intellectual concern (history, polygamy, translation) may be real and yet not be the load-bearing cause — the relational, cultural experience of the ward often is. This fits the scriptural priority on charity and nourishment as the soil of faith: the word must be received into good ground and "nourish[ed]... with great care" (Alma 32:37), and a branch starved of nourishment "withereth away" (Alma 32:38). It also rhymes with the "fourth bucket" worry that members can drift toward an institutional religion of "prophets and temples and obedience" while neglecting the weightier matters Christ named (Matt. 23:23) — the living water and bread of life. The apologetic implication is humbling: defending the faith intellectually is necessary but insufficient if the community itself fails to nourish those who are wrestling.
