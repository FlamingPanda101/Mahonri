# Hero-Flattening as a Cultural Disease

## The Claim
[[Daniel C. Peterson]] argues that contemporary culture suffers from a "disease" of reflexive cynicism toward all heroes, institutions, and leaders — an insistence that every great figure, if you learn "the real truth," secretly has "feet of clay," is a hypocrite, or is "in it for the money." This impulse is what makes it psychologically *easy* to "flatten" [[Brigham Young]] (the source's title) and other historical greats.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The valet proverb (rated: strong as an illustration).** "No man is a hero to his valet" — but the rejoinder is "that's not because there aren't any heroes; that's because valets are valets." Someone whose only view of a great person is packing his luggage and preparing his aftershave will see only an ordinary human, missing the genuine greatness.[^2] *Steelman of the cynic:* proximity does reveal real flaws that distance hides, and hagiography is a real failure mode. Peterson concedes heroes have human traits — his point is that flaws don't cancel greatness.

2. **Aristides the Just (rated: strong; classical attestation via Plutarch).** In ancient Athens an annual ostracism let citizens banish someone without stating a reason. Aristides — nicknamed "the Just" — was banished when one illiterate voter (who didn't recognize him) said he was "sick of hearing him called Aristides the Just." Peterson reads this as envy: we don't want a living reminder that someone is better than we are.[^3] This shows the anti-hero impulse is ancient and recurrent, not merely modern.

3. **The Churchill/Hitler inversion (rated: moderate).** A recent revisionist claim that "the real villain in World War II is Winston Churchill" exemplifies the disease: it is Hitler and Mussolini who are the villains. Churchill had flaws, "but my feeling is he may have been raised up by the Lord to do what he did at the time he did."[^4] *Steelman:* historical revisionism sometimes legitimately corrects whitewashed records (Peterson elsewhere accepts this for the Saints in Missouri — see [[The Three Levels of the Gospel]]); the disease is not revision per se but the *flattening* reflex that erases all greatness.

4. **Democracy and envy as engines (rated: speculative but reasoned).** Peterson suggests democratic egalitarianism can "feed mediocrity" — "I'm just as good as the next guy, and he's no good, so I don't have to be any good" — and that cutting heroes down to our level is more comfortable than aspiring to rise to theirs.[^5] He links this to Mormon's lament that "miracles have ceased" and faith has failed in the land.

5. **Information glut as accelerant (rated: moderate).** The shift (~last 10 years) is partly explained by the internet making once-library-bound quotations universally available (the *CES Letter* era), so out-of-context material now circulates freely.[^6] This connects to [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]].

## Best Quotes

> "There's always something behind the scenes that... this leader has got feet of clay. He's really a hypocrite, or he's really in it for the money... It's, I think, a kind of disease that's afflicting our culture right now, where we don't like the idea of heroes."[^1]

> "He said, I'm so sick of hearing him called Aristides the Just. ... Throw him out of the city because everybody thinks he's a really just man, and I'm sick of it."[^3]

> "It's not good for society to have no heroes, no idealism, to be cynical all the time."[^7]
