# Presentism and Historical Context

## Overview

**Presentism** is the historiographical error of judging people of the past by the moral standards, knowledge, and sensibilities of the present, rather than reading them in the context of their own time. The note as used here is the version [[Daniel Peterson]] presses in defense of [[Brigham Young]] on Ward Radio: critics extract a 19th-century figure's words and conduct, strip the context, and grade them on a 21st-century rubric.

## The Claim

The argument runs:

1. **Context changes meaning and culpability.** A position that looks indefensible today (on race, on social hierarchy, on rhetoric) was, in its own decade, common across the surrounding culture. Peterson's comparison: **Abraham Lincoln** held views on race that no modern public figure could voice, yet we do not therefore dismiss Lincoln wholesale — we read him as a man of his time.
2. **Cherry-picking compounds presentism.** Quoting the harshest line, mediated through the editorially biased [[Journal of Discourses Reliability|Journal of Discourses]], and then judging it by present standards, stacks two distortions on top of each other.
3. **The honest limit.** Properly applied, contextualizing is *not* the same as excusing — see [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]]. The aim is accurate judgment, not exoneration; a wrong remains wrong even when it was widely shared.

The vault treats this as a recurring move applicable both to formal history ([[Presentism in Historical Judgment]]) and to popular/folk judgments of past belief ([[Presentism in Folk Belief Judgment]]).
