# The Diachronic Collective Witness - Read the Prophets Across All Time

## The Claim
When [[Steven Pynakker]] tries to trap [[Jacob Hansen]]'s [[The Collective Witness Model of Knowing|collective-witness epistemology]] — *wouldn't a 1940s Latter-day Saint have used "the collective witness" to defend the priesthood ban?* — Hansen sharpens the model into a **diachronic** rule: the collective witness is not a snapshot of what the prophets taught in any one era but the testimony of *all* the prophets read **across the whole sweep of time**, and its function is to flag **contradictions, not novelty**. A new revelation consistent with the whole is admissible; a teaching that contradicts the standing collective witness is treated as an *aberration* — including aberrations spoken by prophets themselves. This is what lets Hansen reject Brigham Young's "Adam-God" and the priesthood ban as departures *from* the collective witness rather than expressions *of* it. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The model is over all of time, not one time-slice.** "When you say the collective witness in a particular time period, see that misunderstands the model. The model says, what does it say over all of time?... if we were to take all the prophets and put them in a line... throughout all the way from Adam to Russell M. Nelson." [^1] *Rating: strong as a clarification;* it directly defeats Pynakker's "1940s Mormonism" framing by denying that any single generation's consensus *is* the collective witness.

2. **Individual prophets can be aberrations.** "There would be some of them that would say things that the rest of them would look at them and be like, wait, what? Like Brigham's out there like, Adam is God... So yes, in Brigham's time there were ideas that were stupid and wrong. But I don't look just at Brigham or at any one time in the church." [^2] *Rating: strong;* by building prophetic error *into* the model, Hansen makes it falsification-tolerant — a wrong teaching does not break the system, it is identified as the outlier against the mass.

3. **The test is contradiction, not innovation.** "You can reveal something new that's consistent with the collective witness, but when you contradict it, I'm... going to be way more skeptical." [^3] On the priesthood ban specifically, Hansen demands a *positive* revelation: "you have to make the case that God has actually somewhere given a revelation telling us that sub-Saharan African peoples should not have the priesthood. I have never been shown any such revelation and therefore I don't believe it." [^4] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it gives a real criterion (does X contradict the standing witness?), though it pushes the weight onto how "fundamental"/"contradictory" is judged — see the steelman.

4. **Applied prospectively to gay marriage.** The same rule governs a hypothetical future change: gender complementarity is woven through "all the rest of the collective witness" (he cites [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew 19:3-9]], "male and female... the two shall become one flesh"), so absent "a deep and powerful revelation from God... I would reject it because I have every grounds to on what has already been revealed." [^5] He reads a redefinition of gender as "social pressure making a decision," not revelation. *Rating: contested;* consistent application of his own model, but whether the priesthood ban and a gender redefinition are genuinely *dis*analogous (ban = aberration; complementarity = collective witness) is exactly what Pynakker disputes.

**Steelman of the critic.** Pynakker's challenge is sharper than Hansen's reply fully neutralizes: a 1940s believer *did* have a First Presidency statement calling the ban "doctrine... never questioned by any of the church leaders," embedded in a wider Christian consensus on the "curse of Ham," with apparent scriptural hooks (the Cain/Ham lineage tradition, the Book of Abraham on Egypt — see [[Priesthood Ban Origins]]). To that believer the ban looked like the collective witness, and complementarity-revision could look like an aberration to a future believer. The model therefore appears to rely on a *later* vantage to sort "aberration" from "witness," which risks circularity: the collective witness is whatever survives once we have already decided which prophets erred. Hansen's defense is that he demands an affirmative revelation for any exclusionary or contradictory teaching and finds none for the ban — but he must concede (and does, re: Brigham) that contemporaries can mistake an aberration for the witness, which is precisely the critic's point.
