# A Church-Sanctioned Apologetics Program to Inoculate the Youth

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]], [[Luke Hansen]], and [[Cardon Ellis]] propose that the Church should build a **deliberate, sanctioned apologetics-and-inoculation program** — even a dedicated stake "apologist" calling — to equip members (especially the youth) *before* they encounter hostile criticism online. The model floated on air is Brett McDonald's *LDS Truth Claims* lectures. The pitch follows from their "scale" model of belief: most people who leave "went to a church that was trying to give them spiritual experiences, not training them in apologetics," so the believing pan was never loaded with the *secular/evidential* ("blue marble") reasons the church rarely supplies. A formal program would supply them.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Inoculation theory predicts it works.** The medical metaphor is apt: exposing members to weakened forms of the strongest criticisms *first*, with the best scholarly responses, builds resistance — versus encountering a full-strength attack cold, online, framed by a hostile narrator. This is the institutional application of [[Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake]]. *Rating: strong — inoculation against persuasion is a well-supported finding in social psychology (McGuire), and faith-transition studies repeatedly cite "encountering it first from critics" as a precipitant.*
2. **The "blue marble" gap is real and pastoral.** Per [[The Scale Replaces the Shelf as the Model of Belief]], many members rely on spiritual experience alone (yellow marbles) and are "totally unequipped when it comes to apologetics," so a single well-aimed criticism can tip the scale. A program targeting the evidential gap addresses the actual failure mode. *Rating: accurate description of a common vulnerability.*
3. **Structural independence keeps it honest.** The proposal deliberately keeps the program *structurally separate* from official doctrine so it can be *updated as evidence changes* without implicating the Church's teaching authority — answering the obvious objection that apologetic arguments age. *Rating: a thoughtful design feature that anticipates the main risk.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Critics (including faithful ones) note real hazards: (a) *backfire* — teaching the controversies can introduce doubts the member would never have met, and a weak or dishonest "response" can accelerate rather than prevent loss; (b) *officializing apologetics* risks binding the Church's authority to arguments that later collapse, which is exactly why the institution has historically kept apologetics at arm's length (FAIR, the Maxwell Institute, *Saints*, the Gospel Topics essays are deliberately *not* a calling); (c) a "stake apologist" could harden a culture of *debating* over *ministering*. A measured reply: the proposal's own "structural separation" clause and its companion emphasis on *charitable* method (see [[Make Bible Bashing Great Again - Charitable Curious Debate]]) are designed precisely to mitigate (a)–(c); and the status quo already *is* an inoculation program, just an informal and uneven one. The honest core: the *need* (equip before exposure) is well-grounded; the *form* (a formal calling vs. better-resourced voluntary efforts) is the genuinely contestable part.

## LDS Relevance
This is the *institutional* payoff of the scale model: if belief is a scale you load with both spiritual and evidential reasons, the Church's job is to *supply the missing reasons* systematically rather than leaving members to find them mid-crisis. It is a proposal, not established practice — the vault catalogs it as an idea advanced on Ward Radio, weighed against the genuine risks above.
