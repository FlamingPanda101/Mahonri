# Religions Persist by Feeling Unchanging While Actually Changing

## The Claim
Drawing on religious-studies scholar [[Steven Taysom]], historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] argues that historically *persistent* religions — "particularly those focused on an unchanging God" — survive precisely because they "give their adherents... a sense that their faith is unchanging while simultaneously changing." Relevance demands adaptation; psychological security demands the felt constancy of God. Latter-day Saints, Stapley says, "do that really well." This dissolves the apparent paradox of a change-averse tradition that nonetheless revises its temple ceremonies.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Taysom thesis.** Persistence "is because they give their adherents... a sense that their faith is unchanging while simultaneously changing... You have to be relevant... all the while helping them feel like they're living an unchanging religion, that God is the same and you can rely on him."[^1] *Rating: strong as a descriptive sociological generalization; it names a credentialed scholar and matches observable patterns across traditions.*

2. **It predicts the personality split over change.** Because the tradition trades on felt stasis, "there are certain personality types... when there is change they kind of get bent out of shape," or after stable periods "assume it can never change." The framework explains *why* temple revisions provoke disproportionate distress in some members without implying the revisions are illegitimate.[^2] *Rating: moderate — a plausible corollary, illustrative rather than proven.*

3. **The historical reality check.** Stapley closes the loop with the brute fact: "the church is different than when you were a kid... and it's wildly different than when Joseph Smith organized the church in 1830" — yet members rightly call it "true and living." The change is real; so is the continuity of identity.[^3] This is the descriptive backbone under the normative claims of [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]] and [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]]. *Rating: strong — the datable changes (sacrament prayer, white clothing, penalties, sealing language) are independently documented.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can weaponize the thesis: if a religion *manufactures* a feeling of permanence while quietly changing, that is precisely the mechanism of an institution managing its members' perceptions — "unchanging while changing" can read as a polite description of obscuring revision behind reassuring rhetoric. The believer's reply is that the same dynamic describes *any* living tradition (including science, law, and language), that Latter-day Saint historians like Stapley are the ones *surfacing* the changes rather than hiding them, and that distinguishing a stable covenantal core from adaptable forms is an honest account, not a sleight of hand — though it does place the burden on the tradition to say *which* elements are the unchanging core.
