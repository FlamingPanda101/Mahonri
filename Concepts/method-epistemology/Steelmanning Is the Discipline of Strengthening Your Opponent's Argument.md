# Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument

## The Point
Moderator [[Bret Weinstein]] opens the dialogue by having [[Sam Harris]] and [[Jordan Peterson]] each *steelman* the other's position — restate it as strongly and fairly as possible — before any rebuttal. Peterson frames this as the basic discipline of any serious thinker.[^1]

## Argument

1. **Steelmanning defined and modeled.** Rather than attack a weak version of the opponent, each man reconstructs the other's strongest case. Harris reconstructs Peterson on the depth of stories; Peterson reconstructs Harris's twin-dangers framing (fundamentalism vs nihilism) and the *Moral Landscape*.[^2]

2. **Why it is rational hygiene.** "You want to take arguments that are against your perspective and you want to make them as strong as you possibly can so that you can fortify your arguments against them. You don't want to make them weak because that just makes you weak." *Steelman: a position only counts as tested if it survives its strongest opposition.*[^1]

3. **The scientist's motive.** "What scientists are trying to do... is to try to find out if there's something that we're thinking that's stupid." Generating opposition in one's own imagination and failing to undermine a view is *not* proof it is right; an external critic who finds a real mistake is welcomed as "progress towards a more solid theory of being."[^3]

4. **Difficulty acknowledged.** Weinstein notes that anyone who has tried to steelman a view they severely disagree with "knows just how hard this is" — which is why the exercise is itself evidence of good faith and the reason the audience should "give them some leeway."[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "You want to take arguments that are against your perspective and you want to make them as strong as you possibly can so that you can fortify your arguments against them; you don't want to make them weak because that just makes you weak." [^1]

> "What scientists are trying to do... is to try to find out if there's something that we're thinking that's stupid." [^3]

> "If someone comes along and says you've made a mistake in this fundamental proposition — yes, great, that means I can make progress towards a more solid theory of being." [^3]
