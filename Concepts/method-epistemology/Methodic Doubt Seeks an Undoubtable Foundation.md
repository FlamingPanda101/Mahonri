# Methodic Doubt Seeks an Undoubtable Foundation

## The Point
Before Descartes can affirm anything, he tries to demolish everything. **Methodic doubt** is the deliberate strategy of "finding absolute certainty by doubting everything that is possible to doubt." [^1] The goal is foundationalist: an "absolute undoubtable truth" on which to "build a system of knowledge on a solid foundation," so that whatever survives the doubt can be trusted completely. [^1]

## Argument
1. **Knowledge needs a secure foundation.** Descartes, a 17th-century philosopher, wanted to ground all knowledge on something that could not in principle be mistaken — so he set out to doubt every belief that admits even the faintest possibility of doubt. *(foundationalist premise)* [^1]
2. **The senses and the external world fall to doubt.** It is at least *possible* — however unlikely — that "we're in some sort of cosmic dream, or perhaps we're being deceived by a powerful demon." If that scenario cannot be ruled out, then "we can't know with absolute certainty that the world around us is actually there." So the external world is set aside as not absolutely certain. *(global skepticism about the external)* [^2]
3. **Even features of the self are doubtable.** You can doubt whether you are a good person, whether you are even human, whether you "actually have hands" — perhaps "you're actually an alien octopus creature dreaming all of this up." Almost every specific belief about yourself can be questioned. *(doubt turned inward)* [^3]
4. **The method leaves a remainder.** Methodic doubt is not skepticism for its own sake; it is a sieve. What it cannot wash away becomes the bedrock. That remainder is the cogito. *(setup for the foundation)* [^4] See [[The Cogito Is Self-Verifying Through the Act of Doubting]].

**Steelman of the critic:** A skeptic can grant the method but deny it ever reaches bedrock. If the criterion is "doubt anything possibly false," one may press it onto the cogito itself — the reliability of the very reasoning ("thinking things exist") could be the demon's deception, and the move from "thinking is occurring" to "*I* exist as a persisting thinking thing" smuggles in a substantial self. On this reading methodic doubt proves too much: rigorously applied it corrodes its own foundation, which is precisely what the narrator's promised "logical uncertainty principle" presses. See [[The Logical Uncertainty Principle Challenges the Cogito]].

## Best Quotes
> "The full argument relies on what's called methodic doubt — a strategy of finding absolute certainty by doubting everything that is possible to doubt." [^1]

> "Although unlikely, it's at least possible that we're in some sort of cosmic dream, or perhaps we're being deceived by a powerful demon, and so we can't know with absolute certainty that the world around us is actually there." [^2]
