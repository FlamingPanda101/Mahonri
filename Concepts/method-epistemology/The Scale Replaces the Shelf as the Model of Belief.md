# The Scale Replaces the Shelf as the Model of Belief

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues the popular "**shelf**" metaphor for managing doubt is a bad model and should be replaced with a **scale**. The shelf analogy (you stack unresolved issues on a shelf until it "breaks") is, he says, "a really stupid way of thinking about your faith — poor craftsmanship, who builds a shelf that breaks with two books on it?" [^1] The better picture: belief is a **balance scale**. On one pan go *reasons to doubt* (red marbles — "anti-Mormon apologetics," historical issues, of varying sizes/weights); on the other go *reasons to believe*, of **two distinct kinds**: yellow marbles = **spiritual experiences**, and blue marbles = **secular/evidential reasons** (apologetic evidences). [^2] Hansen profiles failure modes by which pan people load: the *new believer* (a few yellow, easily snatched away), the *cynic* (only red), the *naïve believer* (only yellow — "all I know is I've had good spiritual experiences"), the *spiritual giant / average bishop* (heavy red doubts outweighed by powerful yellow experiences), and the *evidence seeker* (blue only, "somewhat balanced"). The **ideal** combines all three colors — a believer who can answer questions *and* draws on spiritual witness, so "we should be able to intellectually justify our beliefs," not only feel them. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The scale captures *weighing*, which the shelf cannot.** A shelf models only accumulation-until-collapse; a balance models *comparison of competing weights*, which is how testimony actually works — a preponderance of converging witnesses, not a single breaking point. This is the model the vault already commends in [[Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake]]. *Rating: a genuinely better metaphor for evidential reasoning.* [^2]
2. **It distinguishes spiritual from evidential reasons without opposing them.** By color-coding yellow (spiritual) and blue (secular), Hansen lets both count on the *same* pan, resisting both pure fideism ([[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]]) and pure evidentialism. *Rating: a useful, ecumenical framing.* [^3]
3. **The "naïve believer" diagnosis is real and pastoral.** Many members are "totally unequipped when it comes to apologetics" and rely on yellow marbles alone, which leaves them exposed when doubts "jump on." *Rating: an accurate description of a common vulnerability.* [^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic notes the scale **smuggles in the same problem it diagnoses**: who decides a marble's *size*? If spiritual experiences (yellow) are allowed unlimited weight, the scale can always be tipped to belief regardless of the evidence — making it unfalsifiable, the very charge leveled at "doubt your doubts." The shelf metaphor's value, for ex-members, was precisely that it modeled a *threshold* where accumulated unresolved problems become rationally decisive; replacing it with an always-rebalanceable scale can look like motivated reasoning. A measured reply: Hansen does *not* exempt belief from testing — his companion claim is that the scale "must be exposed to an earthquake" of criticism *after* the positive case is built ([[Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake]]) — and the model's point is descriptive honesty: people *do* weigh spiritual and evidential reasons together, so a metaphor that represents weighing is more accurate than one that represents only collapse. The "who sizes the marbles" problem is real but applies to *any* evidential model, believing or skeptical.

## Best Quotes
> "The shelf breaks? That's a really stupid way of thinking about your faith. Poor craftsmanship — who builds a shelf that breaks with only two books on it?" [^1]

> "You have a scale, and on one hand reasons to doubt, on the other reasons to believe... the yellow are spiritual experiences, the blue are secular reasons to believe." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The scale operationalizes a Restoration-friendly epistemology of *converging witnesses* (Moroni's spiritual confirmation placed *alongside* rational evidence, not against it). It frames the practical task of discipleship as loading the believing pan with both spiritual and intellectual reasons — and sets up the institutional proposal in [[A Church-Sanctioned Apologetics Program to Inoculate the Youth]] to supply the "blue marbles" the church rarely teaches.
