# Contradictory Sources Must Be Weighed Not Merely Counted

## The Claim
The existence of a few hostile sources that *appear* to contradict the Book of Mormon witnesses is not, by itself, decisive. [[Keystone]] argues that responsible history does not stop at "contradictory sources exist"; it asks how *strong* each statement is — its provenance, dating, firsthand-ness, and internal coherence — and weighs that against the total body of what the witnesses said and did across their lives. On that weighing, the critical quotes "lose their bite."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Mere existence is not enough.** "Just the fact that contradictory sources like these exist isn't enough. They may provide a quick way for critics to shrug off the witnesses, but when we take the time to analyze the strength of these statements and compare them to all of the other statements from the witnesses... these critical quotes lose their bite."[^1] *Rating: strong as a principle of historiography — it is the standard way historians adjudicate conflicting testimony.*

2. **The witnesses' lifelong consistency is the counterweight.** "The witnesses knew what they saw and they testified of those things throughout the rest of their lives." A handful of late, secondhand, or garbled hostile reports is being set against decades of consistent affirmation, including after estrangement from the Church.[^2] See [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]]. *Rating: strong — the asymmetry in quantity and quality of evidence is the heart of the argument.*

3. **The applied cases demonstrate the method.** The same episode shows the procedure in action: the [[Anthony Metcalf Entrancement Quote Is a Late Doubled Embellishment|Metcalf quote is a doubled, misdated reminiscence]], the [[Critics Conflate Martin Harriss Pre- and Post-Translation Plate Experiences|Burnett and Clark quotes conflate two experiences]], and the [[The CES Letter Double-Counts a Single Copied-and-Pasted Source|"eye of faith" sources are one source double-counted]]. Weighing, not counting, is what exposes each.[^1] *Rating: moderate — the method is sound, though its application is performed by an apologist and a critic could weight the same sources differently.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can argue that "weigh, don't count" cuts both ways and can become a license to discount any inconvenient source as "weak," while crediting faith-promoting statements uncritically. The genuinely neutral version of the principle requires symmetry: the believer must apply the same provenance-and-dating scrutiny to the witnesses' *affirmations* (many of which are also late reminiscences) that he applies to the hostile reports. Keystone's case is strongest where it identifies objective defects (a misdated interview, a duplicated citation) rather than merely asserting that a source is weak.
