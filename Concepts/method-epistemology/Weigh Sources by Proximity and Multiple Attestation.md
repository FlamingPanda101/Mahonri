# Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation

## The Point
Asked how Latter-day Saint apologists "distinguish between legitimate historical criticism and weak or circular sources," the panel answers that **good history is no different from modern forensics or journalism**: you don't publish a claim as fact without **two independent, verifiable sources**, you rank evidence **primary → secondary → tertiary**, and you give weight to accounts **closest in time and substance** to the event (an actual participant over a later reporter who wasn't there). The first diagnostic question to any critic is simply *"Do you have a source for that?"* — no source means the claim is being parroted from somewhere else.[^1][^2][^3]

## Evidence
1. **The journalism/forensics analogy.** "Good history is really no different than modern forensics and modern criminal investigation and modern journalism. Most journalists worth their salt aren't going to say something unless they find two sources that were both verifiable before they publish something as having been true."[^1] *Rating: strong; multiple attestation is a standard historiographic criterion.*
2. **The source hierarchy and the Elvis test.** "There's a hierarchy of sources going from primary to secondary to tertiary." Would you prefer a journal written by a girl who was *in the audience* at an Elvis concert, or one written by a *New York Times* reporter who wasn't there? Historians "give preference to ones that were closest either in substance or in time to the event."[^3] *Rating: strong, with a self-aware caveat that memory degrades over time (the criminologist's lens).*
3. **Applied to the CES Letter.** When you actually read the cited sources, "the vast majority… are either horrendously misinterpreted or taken out of context," the source is "often… not qualified at all" (e.g., [[Jeremy Runnells|Runnells]]'s Book of Abraham citations come from people who "never studied Egyptology"), and "sometimes they don't even exist."[^2] *Rating: strong as a method; the specific audit is asserted, not shown here.*
4. **The hearsay double standard.** Critics will accept "someone said they saw him in the barn" (Fanny Alger) — "a guy that knew a guy that heard from a guy" — as damning, yet reject the firsthand witness accounts that "strengthen the argument for… the golden plates." The "do you have a source?" filter cuts both ways and exposes the asymmetry.[^4] *Rating: strong; a clean reciprocity point.*

## Best Quotes
> "Good history is no different than good modern investigation and modern journalism… you don't want to create a… narrative that somebody did something or said something unless you have two sources that can back it up." [^1]

> "Would you preference a journal written seven years after Elvis's concert by a girl that was in the audience, or… by a New York Times reporter that was not even there?" [^3]

> "The first one is: do you have a source for that? If they don't have a source for it, I immediately know that they're parroting an argument that they heard from somewhere else." [^1]

## LDS Connection
This is the methodological engine behind much LDS apologetics on church history: the strongest pro-Joseph evidence (the [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence|Book of Mormon witnesses]]) is firsthand and multiply attested, while the strongest anti-Joseph material often turns out to be late, hostile, single-sourced hearsay (see [[Anti-Mormon Tropes Trace Back to a Few 1830s Forged Affidavits]]). The same standard that vindicates the witnesses is the one that deflates the [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem|Hurlbut affidavits]] — apologists are asking critics to apply *one* consistent rule of evidence.
