# The Internet Rewards the Short Lie Over the Long Truth

## The Point
The [[CES Letter]] faults the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints for urging members to use "divinely appointed" resources when researching hard questions, framing it as the Church hiding the truth.[^1] Fife — who admits this advice bothered him too — argues the counsel is reasonable once you understand the *information environment*: online, "anything remotely controversial... is a mess of disinformation and propaganda," and the medium structurally favors the critic. "A lie online is clean and short. The truth has depth and takes time to understand."[^2] The manipulation, again, is not in the data but in how the medium rewards the side with the simpler story.

## Evidence
Fife marshals three observations about the asymmetry between misinformation and correction:[^2]
- **Speed asymmetry** — a 2018 MIT study found false news online travels roughly six times faster than the truth.
- **Effort asymmetry (Brandolini's law)** — "the amount of energy needed to refute [BS] is an order of magnitude bigger than that needed to produce it." A critic can manufacture a half-truth in one sentence; the believer needs paragraphs of context to untangle it.
- **Cognitive asymmetry** — "Short, quick, and easy-to-understand answers are the most appealing at first glance," so when confronted with unflattering information "it is tempting to accept it" rather than do the work.

Fife quotes Bruce and Marie Hafen (*Faith Is Not Blind*) on members rattled by a phrase or story taken out of context: "Their problem is not that they know too much about Church history, but that they don't know nearly enough. And they have been conditioned by the oversimplifications of social media to expect a short answer to any question."[^3] He pairs this with the classic observation, in Heisenberg's, Pope's, and Bacon's words, that shallow learning misleads while deep learning corrects — "A little learning is a dangerous thing; drink deep, or taste not the Pierian spring."[^4] Fife's own experience confirmed the pattern: "the negative narratives spun by critics almost always had a good answer. All I had to do was learn more."[^5]

He also flags the related **"accusation in the mirror"**: critics charge the Church with the very thing they do — e.g. complaining that leaders intrude by discussing the temple garment, when in reality "critics talk far more about my underwear than any church leader ever has."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "The internet is a great place to find out when Taco Bell closes. But ask Google which political party to vote for?... Is the Mormon Church a cult? Yikes."[^2]

> "A lie online is clean and short. The truth has depth and takes time to understand."[^2]

> "Their problem is not that they know too much about Church history, but that they don't know nearly enough." — Bruce and Marie Hafen[^3]

> "The information is not the problem. The real issue is in the interpretation."[^5]

## LDS Connection
This reframes the Church's counsel to seek reliable sources not as censorship but as a rational response to a hostile medium: the format of social media systematically advantages the [[The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit|half-truth, the Gish Gallop, and the burden-shift]] over the long, contextual answer that faith claims require. For the Latter-day Saint, the practical takeaway is patience — refusing to let the speed and tidiness of a critical soundbite stand in for its truth, and being willing to "drink deep" before concluding. It is the methodological complement to the substantive finding that, on inspection, [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon|the critics' strongest arguments do not survive]].
