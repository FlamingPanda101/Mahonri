# Decentralize - Crop-Dusting vs Gardening in Member Retention

## The Point
The panel's proposed remedy for disaffiliation contrasts **"crop-dusting" with "gardening."** Top-down, centrally-planned programs — handbooks, lesson manuals, correlated curricula, churchwide campaigns — are like crop-dusting a field: broadly useful but indiscriminate, unable to meet the needs of any particular plant. Retention, [[Jeff Strong]] argues, depends on **gardening**: local, individualized attention — ministering, a great bishop, real friends — that tends each plant by name. "What we need is more gardening."[^1][^2] Host [[Cardon Ellis]] pushes this to a structural conclusion: radically *decentralize* the institution.

## Evidence

1. **The crop-dusting metaphor (rated: strong as illustration).** "People who love the church... develop programs and ideas and direction and handbooks and lesson manuals... and they deploy that and it's awesome... But people are unique. Every plant in the garden is unique."[^1] Strong's lavender farm is the worked example: 1,600 plants on a single drip system all get "the same amount of water for the same amount of time," but he must still inspect each plant and "turn the water off" or "dial it up" individually — "I can't crop dust."[^3]

2. **Gardening = the relational fix members actually report (rated: moderate, anecdotal but convergent).** Asked "what's the fix?", Strong and the hosts repeatedly land on relationships: "awesome home teachers," "a phenomenal ministering brother or sister," "a really, really great bishop," and Elder Holland's maxim that "everyone needs a friend and a calling."[^4] This dovetails with the retention-side data that a felt connection — to God and to people — is what keeps members in. See [[Daily Connection With God Is the Top Predictor of Retention]] and [[Faithkeepers Would Return if a Friend Invited Them]].

3. **The decentralization proposal (rated: speculative, Cardon's view).** Cardon argues for liquidating much of the Church Office Building's PR apparatus and "returning that authority and money to the locals" — e.g., handing a stake president a "$50,000 billboard budget for Santa Clarita" so PR is designed by people who know the area, including buying local radio time for a youth show over a "centrally planned Easter takeover."[^5][^6] Strong does not fully endorse the structural claim but frames the same principle pastorally: cultivation "starts with you individually, your own heart, and then your personal relationships... your marriage, your family, your friends. And then maybe you get to your ward."[^7]

4. **Both, not either (rated: the synthesis the episode reaches).** Strong's repeated correction is that this is *not* an argument against programs: "We need that and we need gardening. We need both."[^2] Cardon concedes the limit too — "I can't recreate the Boy Scouts single-handedly," because "you can't do some things by yourself," which is *why* programs exist.[^8] The disagreement is one of *balance and locus*, not a wholesale rejection of central coordination.

5. **Honest limits / steelman.** Decentralization trades a known failure mode (one-size-fits-all neglect of individuals) for an unmentioned one: without correlation, local variability can mean *worse* care, doctrinal drift, and the loss of the very standardization that produced post-war growth — a tension Cardon himself names elsewhere in the episode (correlation "yielded a large amount of growth" before becoming "a burden").[^9] "Gardening" is also labor-intensive and depends on already-engaged members; it is a description of what good wards do, not a mechanism for producing them at scale. And the "fix" remains anecdotal — a felt remedy ("awesome home teachers") generalized into policy.

## Best Quotes

> "The idea that we can fix everything from the top is crop dusting. And what we need is more gardening." [^2]

> "For my plants, all 1,600 to be okay, I got to take care of all 1,600. I can't crop dust." [^3]

> "Decentralize our church, return that authority and money to the locals so that they have to get to know their locals better, and you cultivate that culture." [^6]

## LDS Connection
"Gardening" is the ministering ideal restored in 2018 — leaving the ninety and nine for the one ([[Luke 15.3-7|Luke 15:3-7]]) — and the charge to watch over the church "always" and be "with and strengthen them" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 20.53-55|D&C 20:53-55]]). The Good Samaritan's individual, hands-on care over the priest's correct-but-passing-by orthodoxy is the same contrast (see [[Orthopraxy over Orthodoxy]]). At the same time the Restoration affirms central order — "let all things be done in order, and by common consent" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 28.13|D&C 28:13]]) and a body of many members under one head ([[1 Corinthians 12.12-27|1 Corinthians 12]]) — which is why the episode's wiser landing point is *both* the crop-duster and the gardener, not one against the other.
