# High-Cost Religion Makes Leaving More Costly

## The Claim
The drop in well-being after leaving religion is not uniform across faiths. [[Sam Hardy]] and [[Justin Dyer]]'s 2025 longitudinal study found the shift toward negative functioning was "much more severe for those leaving The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints than for several other religions." Hardy explains this as characteristic of a **"high-cost religion"** — one that demands more effort to follow its standards. Precisely *because* the commitment is large and the identity central, exiting it produces a more dramatic emotional impact. This is the mirror image of the retention thesis: the same high demand that makes a faith "sticky" and beneficial for the engaged makes leaving it more painful.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Asymmetric exit cost in the data (rated: moderate).** Among the nine-year panel, the move toward negative functioning after leaving was steeper for those exiting the LDS Church than for those leaving other traditions studied.[^1] *Steelman:* this could partly reflect selection (LDS leavers may differ systematically from leavers of low-demand faiths), the tighter social embedding of LDS life (so exit means a larger relational rupture), or regional sampling; the article reports the pattern without controlling-variable detail.

2. **The "high-cost religion" mechanism (rated: moderate).** "If you leave a church that requires a lot of commitment and it's really important to you, there's more of a dramatic impact." The cost of leaving scales with the depth of prior investment.[^1]

3. **Same coin as the demand–benefit thesis (rated: moderate).** This is the symmetric counterpart of [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]] and explains the bimodal membership in [[Why the LDS Membership Lacks a Large Nominal Middle]]: a tradition with "something there to grab onto" both retains the committed and wounds the departing more deeply. The two findings reinforce rather than contradict each other.
