# Exposing a Public Deception Is Not an Ad Hominem

#type/claim

## The Claim

The most-pressed objection on the [[Ward Radio]] stream — voiced repeatedly by host [[Cardon Ellis]] playing devil's advocate — is that scrutinizing the *author's* motives rather than the letter's arguments is just an ad hominem attack. [[Michael Peterson]] and the panel answer that it is not, for two reasons. First, when one person carries out "a colossal public deception," reporting that deception is legitimate even if the report is perceived as personal: "we reject the ridiculous idea that a colossal public deception carried out by one person should not be exposed because the report of it might be perceived as a personal attack." Second, and more pointedly, the honesty of the origin claim is *not external* to the [[CES Letter]] — it is printed on its front page (and in Google's result snippet) as a factual assertion ("one Latter-day Saint's honest quest to get official answers"). Testing whether that marketed claim is true is therefore engaging the document's own content, not changing the subject.[^1]

The panel sharpens this into a thesis-level point: because the CES Letter's entire argument is that *the Church is untrustworthy and hides things*, the trustworthiness of the accuser is the decisive variable. [[Luke Hansen]] frames it: "in a way the letter is almost supporting evidence for his personal experience, not the other way around" — so impeaching the personal-experience narrative strikes at the letter's load-bearing premise, not a peripheral character trait. Hence their slogan: it is "not an issue, it is *the* issue."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **The deception is the marketed product.** The CES Letter's own front-page self-description ("honest quest to get official answers") is presented as a checkable factual claim; if [[Adversarial Church-Bashing Predates the CES Letter by Months|the author was already an adversarial critic for months]], the marketed origin is false on its own terms.[^3]
- **Trust is what actually moves people.** Peterson argues that studies and intuition agree people leave largely over *whether they can trust the institution* — so a manufactured trust-narrative is doing the persuasive work, making it the proper target of critique rather than an evasion of it.[^4]
- **It addresses content, not just character.** [[Luke Hansen]] anticipates the ad-hominem charge directly and answers that the wrapper-critique "is clearly addressing content within the letter itself," since the introduction and its sincerity claims *are* part of the text.[^2]
- **Planning destruction undercuts "sincere quest."** The panel cites a contemporaneous critic's reaction (quoted in Peterson's report) that crowdsourcing and arranging material "to be most destructive" is "not what you do when you're looking for truth" — evidence that bears on the truth of the stated purpose.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "We reject the ridiculous idea that a colossal public deception carried out by one person should not be exposed because the report of it might be perceived as a personal attack." [^1]

> "It's not just an issue, it is *the* issue... people lose their faith largely because of whether they feel they can trust the church. If the church is hiding things then it is untrustworthy — and what the things actually are isn't as important as whether they're being hidden." [^4]

## A Note on the Limit of This Argument

The distinction the panel draws is real but must be policed carefully: it is fair to test the *marketed origin claim* (a factual assertion the author himself published), yet it remains a genuine genetic fallacy to treat the letter's *individual historical objections* as refuted merely because their author is judged insincere (see [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]]). The strongest form of the panel's case is the narrow one — that a false sincerity-narrative voids the letter's *trust-based rhetorical strategy* — not the broad one that motive-impeachment settles the underlying history. A fully fair treatment still owes Runnells's substantive points a content-level answer, which the channel pursues elsewhere (e.g., [[Ward Radio - View of the Hebrews and the Book of Mormon]]).
