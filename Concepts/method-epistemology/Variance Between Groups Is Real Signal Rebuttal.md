# Variance Between Groups Is Real Signal Rebuttal

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Against the objection that the clergy survey is unreliable noise (see [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]]), [[Jonah Barnes]] rebuts: **the between-group variance is the signal.** Whatever wording problems exist apply roughly equally to every respondent, so the large, consistent differences *between* traditions cannot be dismissed as random error — they reveal genuinely different belief cultures.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Shared bias cancels in comparisons (strong/logical).** If every respondent reads "definitely" the same skeptical way, that bias depresses *all* groups together and washes out when you compare Evangelical to Catholic clergy. The fact that the gap survives means it is not produced by the wording.[^1] *Rating: strong — this is sound measurement logic.*

2. **The gaps are too large for error bands (strong).** Adam and Eve: ~23–24% (Catholic/mainline) vs. ~80–88% (Evangelical/Black Protestant); bodily resurrection: ~75% (mainline) vs. ~99% (Evangelical).[^2][^3] Plausible survey margins of error are 2–5 points; these gaps are 25–65 points. The third host independently estimates "grace" for wording at only 5–10 points — far short of the observed spread.[^4]

3. **Burge's own takeaway corroborates (moderate).** The cited analyst [[Ryan Burge]] concludes the data "does an excellent job of illustrating how distinct Evangelicalism is even inside American Christianity."[^5] The professional's read is precisely *between-group distinctiveness*, matching Barnes's point.

4. **A spectrum still appears within groups (concession that strengthens).** The rebuttal does not deny *some* noise — Barnes says "I'm not saying it's all noise" — only that the structured differences are real.[^6] This bounded claim is more defensible than a maximalist one.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The skeptic can still argue that *differential* wording sensitivity, not equal bias, drives the gap: e.g., mainline clergy may be trained to read "definitely" more literally/humbly than Evangelicals, so the same word means different things across cultures — in which case part of the "variance" is itself a measurement artifact rooted in differing epistemic norms. This is a real and subtle threat that the panel only partly addresses.

**Methodological context.** Barnes's argument is a folk version of a genuine principle: **fixed-effect comparisons are more robust than absolute levels.** Common-method variance and acquiescence bias tend to attenuate or shift levels uniformly; they less easily manufacture large, theologically coherent rank-orderings (Evangelical > Black Protestant > Catholic ≈ mainline) that line up with independently known doctrinal commitments.
