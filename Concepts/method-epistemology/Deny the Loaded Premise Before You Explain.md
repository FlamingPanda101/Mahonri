# Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain

#type/claim

## The Claim
Working through real questions Latter-day Saint teenagers get "brigaded" with at school, the [[Ward Radio]] hosts isolate a recurring failure mode and name it: **you can give a *true* answer and still lose, if your true answer silently accepts the hostile *framing* of the question.** Each schoolyard challenge — "Do Mormons wear *weird* underwear?", "Is there a *secret bunker*?", "Did Joseph Smith *marry* a 14-year-old?" — smuggles a pejorative premise inside it. The hosts run [[Ryland Hutchinson]] as a live foil: he answers earnestly and affirmatively ("we wear sacred clothing… yes, technically there is [a vault]… he might have been sealed to a 14-year-old"), and is corrected each time with the same verdict — **"you just validated the premise again."**[^1][^2] The prescribed two-step is: (1) *deny the loaded word first* — the comic shorthand is "No, you anti-Mormon numbskull, who told you that?" — then (2) *substitute the accurate description* (sealing ≠ marriage; sacred clothing ≠ "weird underwear"; a records vault ≠ a "secret bunker"). [[Cardon Ellis]] generalizes the move to the whole genre of othering pejoratives: calling exaltation "getting your own planet" is "no different a cruel pejorative than me telling Christians they worship a giant sky daddy or a spaghetti monster in the sky."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. "Yes, technically" hands the framing to the critic — rate: strong on rhetoric
The diagnostic is concrete and repeatable. Asked the bunker question, Ryland says he "wouldn't describe them as a bunker, but…", and Cardon pounces: "So you're not saying it's *not* a bunker?"[^2] The point is that a question like "do you wear *weird* underwear?" is not a request for information but a frame to be ratified or refused; an honest "yes, we have sacred clothing" answers a question that wasn't asked while conceding the adjective that was. The fix is to **answer the smuggled adjective explicitly** — "No. We don't wear *weird* underwear. We have religious clothing, like priests' vestments or a graduate's cap and gown" — and even to turn the loaded word back ("define *weird* — you planning on wearing a cap and gown to graduation? That's a weird hat").[^4] *This is sound debate technique: never negate by repeating the opponent's predicate.*

### 2. The "sealed, not married" distinction is the load-bearing case — rate: strong, but depends on the underlying history
On the most "potent" attack — Joseph Smith and the 14-year-old ([[Helen Mar Kimball]]) — the method does real work: Cardon insists "he was *sealed* to a 14-year-old girl. Do you know the difference?… I am sealed to a 14-year-old boy, my son," reframing from "marriage" (with its modern connotations) to the LDS category of *sealing*, which he is also under to "hundreds and hundreds of people."[^5] *Rating: the reframe is legitimate as far as it goes* — it correctly refuses the equation marriage = sealing — but the note flags that it leans on the contested historical claim that the Helen Mar union was a non-conjugal "eternity-only" sealing, a claim developed elsewhere in the vault (see [[Joseph Smith and Helen Mar Kimball Sealing]] and [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]]). The premise-denial buys time and changes the terms; it does not by itself settle the history.

### 3. Refusing the pejorative is symmetrical and self-aware — rate: balanced
Cardon's "sky daddy / spaghetti monster" parallel makes the principle reciprocal rather than special pleading: *every* tradition can have its sacred claims rendered ridiculous by a hostile relabeling, so the LDS objection to "you get your own planet" is the same objection a Christian would raise to "you worship a sky daddy."[^3] This connects the move to the vault's broader treatment of weaponized vocabulary — see [[Avoiding the Pejorative Magic]] (the word *magic*) and [[Cult and Evangelical Are Both Loaded Words]]. **Steelman of the critic:** premise-denial can curdle into *mere* denial — a brush-off that refuses the framing *and* the substance ("No, and who told you that?" with nothing behind it). The hosts guard against this only partially; their own gag answer ("numbskull") is explicitly *not* recommended as the whole reply, and they pair it with substantive reframes and an open door ("if you want to know more, let's talk"). The danger the note preserves: denying the premise is step one, not the argument — done alone it reads as the very "cowering"/evasion the youth are testing for (see [[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]]).

## Best Quotes
> "You just validated the premise again."[^2]

> "Was he *married* to a 14-year-old girl? I'd say he was *sealed* to a 14-year-old girl. Do you know the difference?"[^5]

> "Saying Mormons get their own planet is no different a cruel pejorative than me telling Christians that they worship a giant sky daddy or a spaghetti monster in the sky. Same attitude."[^3]

## LDS Connection
The pastoral target is the Latter-day Saint youth or parent, not the debate stage: the hosts built the segment from a real teachers-quorum activity where teens wrote down the questions peers "harass" them with. The method complements the vault's confidence-over-recall counsel in [[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]] — a teen who calmly *refuses the frame* ("we don't wear weird underwear; we have religious clothing") signals exactly the unflustered confidence that keeps a question from hardening into a crisis, whereas an anxious "well, yes, technically…" telegraphs that the loaded word landed. It is the private-ministry register: refuse the pejorative, restate the truth, leave the door open.
