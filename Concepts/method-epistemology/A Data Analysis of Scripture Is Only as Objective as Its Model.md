# A Data Analysis of Scripture Is Only as Objective as Its Model

#type/claim

## The Claim
Even when a study of scripture *looks* quantitative and objective, its outputs inherit the biases of whatever model produced them. [[The Bald Coder]] is unusually candid about this: his Book of Mormon scores were generated by a large language model (Gemini), and "large language models are trained on human-biased data," so "you should never blindly believe anything that a large language model tells you, no matter how convincing it sounds." The veneer of numerical objectivity does not transfer truth-value to the conclusion.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The objectivity is borrowed, then re-subjectivized.** The creator's pitch is that NLP "gives a little bit more objectivity to things that are usually subjective" (the sentiment-analysis analogy: one-star reviews objectively contain more negative words). But the move from word-level sentiment to a holistic "Christ-centeredness score" reintroduces a judgment call that the LLM makes opaquely on a prompt — so the apparent objectivity is recaptured by the model's training distribution.[^2] *(Rating: secondary/sound — accurately describes a real limitation of LLM-as-judge; the score is a model artifact, not a measured property of the text.)*
2. **The author disclaims proof outright.** "Is this absolute proof that the Book of Mormon is the word of God? Absolutely not. This analysis was done using large language models, and large language models are trained on human-biased data."[^1] He extends the disclaimer symmetrically to the Bible — neither book's truth can be settled this way.[^3] *(Rating: primary admission — the source itself caps its own epistemic weight.)*
3. **A general methodological lesson.** This is a clean case study for apologetic method: tools that produce numbers (stylometry, sentiment scores, LLM ratings) are only as reliable as their construction, and a believer or critic who waves around a favorable score is committing the same error in opposite directions. The honest use is *illustrative*, not *probative*.[^2] *(Rating: assertion/methodological — a reasonable inductive lesson.)*

**Steelman of the "data still helps" view.** One could argue that even biased, model-generated scores are *better than pure vibes*: if both books are scored by the *same* biased model, the bias partly cancels in the *comparison*, so relative rankings may carry some signal even if absolute numbers don't. That is a fair partial defense — but it assumes the model's bias is uniform across very different texts (ancient Hebrew narrative vs. 19th-century-English scripture), which is exactly what's unverified, and it still cannot bridge from "scores high on Christ-vocabulary" to "is true." The creator's own conclusion — that no technology can settle the question — is the safer resting point.

## LDS Relevance
This note is a *guardrail* for the apologetics library itself: the vault contains genuinely substantive quantitative arguments (e.g. [[Stylometry Shows Multiple Authors in the Book of Mormon]], wordprint studies in [[Doctrine and Covenants 132 Wordprint Analysis]]), and this principle marks the line between those peer-reviewed methods and an unvalidated LLM-scoring exercise. It protects the believer from overclaiming on the strength of impressive-looking numbers and models the intellectual honesty the creator himself displays.
