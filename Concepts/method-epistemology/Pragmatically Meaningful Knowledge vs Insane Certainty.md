# Pragmatically Meaningful Knowledge vs Insane Certainty

## The Point

[[Blake Ostler]] answers radical skepticism (the "brain in a vat" / *Matrix* problem) not by refuting it logically but by **rejecting the standard of certainty it presupposes**. He distinguishes *insane epistemological certainty* — the demand that we rule out every rival interpretation, including being a brain in a vat — from *pragmatically meaningful knowledge*, which is what we are entitled to when our faculties function properly. We trust our ordinary experience because we *must* to live; spiritual experience is owed the same default trust. [^1]

## Argument

1. **The skeptical argument is valid.** If we were brains in a vat fed identical neural inputs, our experience would be indistinguishable from reality; therefore we cannot, with *absolute* certainty, know we are not brains in a vat — or even that a "memory" of a spiritual experience wasn't implanted five minutes ago. Ostler concedes this is a genuinely valid argument. [^1]
2. **But no one actually believes it.** "No one in this room believes that he or she is a brain in a vat." Anyone who seriously did would be "properly referred to counseling." The skeptical hypothesis is, for us, *literally unbelievable* given "the form of totality of our experience." [^2]
3. **Two kinds of knowledge claim.** (a) *Insane epistemological certainty*: knowledge requires completely discounting all rival interpretations — a standard "only an insane person really worries about." (b) *Pragmatically meaningful knowledge*: based on all our background knowledge and the way we must live, "to trust our experience is the only thing that really makes sense." [^3]
4. **Application to spiritual experience.** We are "entitled to rely on our experience when we experience it in a properly functioning way." If we experience the burning *as coming from God*, that is, on this pragmatic standard, "very good reason to believe that it does in fact come from God." Demanding more is demanding the insane standard. This connects to the move that knowing the source "requires faith and trust and rests upon a choice." [^3]

A related epistemic caveat Ostler stresses: even granting all this, the experience can still be *doubted* (many who had it later lose their testimony), and you "could be wrong about what you think you know" even though you "can't be wrong about what you know" — knowledge as justified-true-belief is hard precisely because justification does not guarantee truth. [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Only an insane person really worries about whether they're a brain in a vat or stuck within the Matrix." [^3]

> "We're entitled to rely on our experience when we experience it in a properly functioning way. If we experience our experience as coming from God... that's very good reason to believe that it does in fact come from God." [^2]

> "How could you be wrong about what you know? You can't. But you could be wrong about what you *think* you know. That's the simple answer." [^4]
