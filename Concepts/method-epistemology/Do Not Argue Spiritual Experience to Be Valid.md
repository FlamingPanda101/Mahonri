# Do Not Argue Spiritual Experience to Be Valid

## The Point

[[Blake Ostler]] makes a self-referential epistemic point: he will *not* try to argue or give evidence that spiritual experiences are valid — because **the very act of arguing would betray that he thinks something more basic and trustworthy than the experience exists.** If spiritual experience is genuinely foundational, you cannot prop it up with a more-basic reason without contradicting yourself.[^1][^2]

## Argument

1. **Arguing for X presupposes something more basic than X.** Ostler: "If I were to attempt to argue ... to prove that to you, I would only show ... that I believe that in reality there's something more basic and trustworthy than spiritual experiences." To argue the foundation is to deny it is the foundation.[^1]
2. **This is the foundationalist structure applied honestly.** It mirrors [[Foundationalism Ends the Regress in Basic Beliefs]]: a genuinely basic belief is justified but "not the sort of thing that one has a reason for." Demanding a further reason for it is a category mistake.[^2] *(Steelman: this is a coherent, even rigorous, move — it is the same logic by which a foundationalist refuses to justify perceptual basic beliefs by appeal to something more basic.)*
3. **Reasons still have a subordinate role.** Ostler clarifies he is "not stating that I won't give reasons or that I won't do my best to reason with you" — only that at bottom the arguments are not what is "most trustworthy and basic."[^3]
4. **Spiritual experience is evidence only for the experiencer.** It is "a mistake to take spiritual experiences as evidence for anyone but the person who has the experience"; treating one's own experience as evidence binding on others "would be like ... idolatry."[^4] *(Cross-examination: this is candid but double-edged — it immunizes the belief from external disproof, but at the cost of granting it has no probative force for anyone else. A skeptic will say an unargued, private, self-certifying foundation is indistinguishable from one that is simply mistaken; Ostler's reply is that *every* epistemology terminates in something it cannot further justify.)*

## Best Quotes

> "I will not give some argument or evidence to try to persuade you ... that your spiritual experiences are valid ... If I were to argue in that way, I would show conclusively that I really don't believe what I'm about to tell you."[^1]

> "It is a mistake to take spiritual experiences as evidence for anyone but the person who has the experience."[^4]
