# Interpretation Is Not Empirically Verifiable

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the interpretation of ancient texts is **not subject to empirical verification**. Unlike a claim about the physical world, an interpretive claim cannot be settled by direct observation or measurement. As a consequence, almost nothing one might conclude about a text is *logically, humanly, or physically impossible* — including readings as exotic as "this is a secret code" or "God embedded a meaning the human author never intended." This is the engine that makes the "not impossible" standard worthless: if nothing is impossible, then "not impossible" rules out nothing.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Interpretation has no observational decision procedure (rated: strong)
There is no instrument that reads off "the meaning" of a verse the way a thermometer reads temperature. Meaning is reconstructed from context, grammar, genre, and historical setting — all of which underdetermine a unique answer in hard cases. McClellan's point is descriptive and largely uncontroversial in hermeneutics: interpretive claims are *argued for*, not *measured*.

### 2. The space of "possible" readings is therefore unbounded (rated: strong)
Because no reading can be empirically falsified in the strict sense, a sufficiently motivated reader can always generate a consistency-preserving construal — secret codes, authorial-unaware divine meaning, etc. McClellan reports both of these have been offered to him in real debates. This is precisely why "not impossible" cannot adjudicate contradictions (see [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]]).

### 3. But unverifiable does not mean unevaluable (rated: strong; the key nuance)
McClellan is *not* claiming all interpretations are equal. Empirical non-verifiability does not collapse into "anything goes." Interpretations are still ranked by **plausibility** — fit with the language, genre conventions, historical context, and the rest of the corpus. The right standard is therefore *best-supported reading*, not *only logically possible reading*. This distinction is what licenses confident judgments of contradiction (see [[The Horse in the Backyard Analogy]]).

### Steelman of the opposing view
A thoughtful inerrantist could press: if interpretation is unverifiable, then the *critic's* claim that two texts contradict is *also* unverifiable, so neither side can claim the high ground. This is the strongest counter. The reply is that contradiction-claims and harmonization-claims are not symmetric in their evidential demands: asserting a contradiction rests on the **plain, attested** sense of each text, while the harmonization rests on an **additional, unattested** scenario. By Occamite and burden-of-proof norms, the reading that posits fewer unevidenced entities is preferred — so the asymmetry McClellan exploits survives the objection. (This is the same logic developed in [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]].)

### Scholarly / linguistic context
The point connects to a familiar tension between **authorial intent**, **textual sense**, and **reader-response** in hermeneutics. Critical scholarship privileges the historically reconstructed sense the original audience could plausibly have accessed — which is why appeals to "secret codes" or meanings unknown to the author are treated as special pleading rather than exegesis.
