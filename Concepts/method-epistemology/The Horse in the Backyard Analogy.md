# The Horse in the Backyard Analogy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The harmonizer holds that because a reconciliation between two passages is *not impossible*, the critic cannot legitimately claim a contradiction. The unstated premise is that confidence requires the alternative to be impossible.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The analogy (rated: strong as an illustration)
[[Dan McClellan]] offers an everyday parallel: suppose a stranger on Twitter tells him there is a horse in his backyard right now. Is it *physically possible*? Sure. Is there actually a horse there? Of course not. The gap between "possible" and "actual" is exactly the gap the harmonizer ignores.

### 2. Justified confidence does not require impossibility (rated: strong)
We constantly and rightly reject claims that are *possible but unevidenced*. No one withholds the judgment "there is no horse in my yard" merely because a horse is not metaphysically impossible. The same standard, applied to scripture, lets one confidently say two passages contradict — "just as confidently as I can say there is not a horse in my backyard right now." The relevant question is never *could it be reconciled?* but *is the reconciliation supported?* (see [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]]).

### 3. Application to specific cases (rated: strong)
McClellan applies the analogy to a list of textbook contradictions — the two creation accounts, the two accounts of Joseph's sale, who killed Goliath, the death of Ahaziah, the death of Judas, the two nativities. In each, a harmonization is *possible* but unevidenced, so by the horse standard one may confidently call them contradictions. See [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]].

### Steelman of the opposing view
The disanalogy a defender would press: the horse claim has a *cheap, decisive* empirical check (go look in the yard), whereas a textual contradiction has no such check — interpretation is not empirically verifiable (see [[Interpretation Is Not Empirically Verifiable]]). So, the objection runs, the confidence transfers illegitimately from an observable case to an unobservable one. The strongest reply is that the analogy is not about *observation* but about *evidential proportioning*: in both cases we apportion belief to the balance of evidence rather than to bare possibility, and in both cases the unevidenced alternative loses. The horse case simply makes vivid a norm we already accept and then quietly suspend for cherished texts.

### Scholarly context
The analogy popularizes a basic Bayesian point: a hypothesis with negligible prior and no confirming evidence remains improbable regardless of being logically possible. Treating "logically possible" as "live" is the error.

## Best Quotes
> "If somebody on Twitter were to tell me that there's a horse in my backyard right now, is it physically possible that that is the case? Sure. Is there a horse in my backyard right now? Of course not." [^1]

> "Just as confidently as I can say there is not a horse in my backyard right now, I can say that these things contradict each other." [^2]
