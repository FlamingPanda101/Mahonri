# Stacked Improbable Harmonizations Multiply Down to a Sliver

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Inerrantists defend a pair of conflicting texts by chaining together several "not impossible" claims — *and the rope broke, and "acquired" means the priests bought it for him, and the field had two names...* — each presented as enough to preserve harmony. [[Dan McClellan]] rebuts the **method**: because the harmonization requires *all* of these independently-improbable claims to be true *simultaneously*, the probability of the whole is lower than the probability of the least likely link alone.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Conjunction shrinks probability (rated: strong, basic probability).** "The probability of all of them being true is less than the probability of the least likely one of them being true all by itself." Each added "not impossible" multiplies in, so "as you stack up these arguments... the probability... diminishes down to the tiniest little sliver of not impossible."[^1] This is just the conjunction rule: P(A∧B∧C) ≤ min(P(A),P(B),P(C)).

2. **Two interpretive moves, not one (rated: strong).** McClellan distinguishes (a) inventing *scenarios* not in evidence (the rope breaking; the body decomposing) from (b) asserting implausible *interpretations* (Daniel Wallace's middle-voice reading of "acquired" in [[Acts 1.18-19|Acts 1:18]] so that Judas isn't the buyer). A full harmonization needs several of *both*, and "they all have to be true."[^2]

3. **It renders the authors incompetent (rated: moderate-strong).** For the harmonization to work, *both* storytellers must be "wildly negligent and incompetent," each omitting the very details (the hanging; the fall; who really bought the field) "one would expect them to include." A reading that makes both authors buffoons is dispreferred to one that takes each at his word as telling a *different* story.[^3]

### Steelman of the opposing view
The harmonizer's instinct echoes a legitimate principle of charity: when evidence underdetermines, prefer a reading on which witnesses cohere, and real historians do reconstruct unattested-but-plausible bridging events. If each individual link were *probable* (not merely possible), the conjunction could still be reasonable. The strongest inerrantist version says: "these are independent, individually-likely details, and a fair reader stitches them." McClellan's reply is that the links are *not* individually likely — they are unattested and strained — so the charity principle does not license them (see [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]]), and the maneuver is deployed to defend a prior dogma rather than as a neutral tie-breaker (see [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
This generalizes beyond Judas (see [[The Death of Judas Contradiction]]) and Ahaziah: it is a tool for evaluating *any* multi-step harmonization. The takeaway for apologetics is that you cannot win a contradiction case by listing possibilities — you must show each bridging claim is *evidenced and probable*, because their conjunction is what has to be true, and conjunctions of weak claims are very weak.

## LDS Relevance
McClellan is non-confessional. For Latter-day Saints the principle is a double-edged but useful epistemic tool: it supports declining to defend biblical inerrancy (which the tradition already rejects — Article of Faith 8), and it equally disciplines Restoration apologetics — multi-step harmonizations of Book of Mormon or Joseph Smith data should meet the same "evidenced, not merely possible" bar. The honest method is symmetric.
