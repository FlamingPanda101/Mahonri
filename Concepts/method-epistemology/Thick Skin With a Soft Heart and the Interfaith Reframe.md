# Thick Skin With a Soft Heart and the Interfaith Reframe

## The Claim
[[Jared Halverson]] proposes a posture for engaging critics and people of other faiths: develop "thick skin while still maintaining a soft heart," and reframe believer/ex-believer (or member/anti-Mormon) confrontations as **interfaith dialogue**. The goal is to be a *judge* of truth rather than a *lawyer* for a side, and to "disagree without becoming disagreeable" — never leaving someone "with a bad taste in their mouth," because in a Bible bash "you lose even when you win."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Judge, not lawyer.** Because faith is "non-provable and non-disprovable," Halverson would rather raise Latter-day Saints who can be "good judges" — impartial, weighing evidence on both sides and considering the consequences of a verdict — than better "lawyers for the defense," who are merely trying to win. He grounds the contrast in the Taft/Roosevelt distinction ("a judge is trying to be impartial... a lawyer is trying to pick all of the truth that argues your side").[^2] *(Strong as a stated method; it deliberately lowers the adversarial temperature.)*
2. **The four-fold balance.** "It's easy to have thick skin and a hard heart" (I can't be hurt, but I'll hurt you) and "easy to have a soft heart and thin skin" (I won't hurt you, but you've hurt me); the difficult, Christ-modeled combination is thick skin *with* a soft heart — balancing "the sanctity of the sacraments and the worth of souls."[^1] *(Moderate — a memorable reframing of the two great commandments.)*
3. **Reframe as interfaith.** Instead of "I stayed in the church and you didn't, so you're an apostate" (which puts the other on the defensive), Halverson reframes: "I believe certain things, you believe different things — that sounds like interfaith dialogue, and I'm good at interfaith dialogue." He reads [[Matthew 5.25|Matthew 5:25]] ("agree with thine adversary quickly") as the Greek "*be agreeable* with thine adversary while thou art in the way," not "sign off on everything they say."[^3] *(Moderate — a practical de-escalation; the Greek gloss is a reasonable reading.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A skeptic can answer that "interfaith reframe" is a rhetorical move that quietly wins an argument the believer could not win head-on: by recasting an ex-member's reasoned departure as a mere change of "faith," it brackets the *truth question* the ex-member actually raised and relabels disagreement as benign pluralism. The ex-member may reasonably object that they did not adopt a different faith on equal footing but concluded the *original* claims were false — and that "let's just stay agreeable" can function to forestall scrutiny. The honest test, which Halverson himself names elsewhere, is whether one applies the same critical standards to one's own tradition's claims as to others'. His fair reply is that the reframe is about *tone and relationship*, not about conceding the substance: he explicitly contends "for the faith" (he tells the story of a bold scribal-vs-authoritative exchange — see [[Scribal Religion Versus Authoritative Religion]]) while refusing contention, which he calls "confrontation without contention" or "convicted civility."

## Best Quotes
> "How do I develop thick skin while still maintaining a soft heart? Because it's easy to have thick skin and a hard heart... and it's easy to have a soft heart and really thin skin... The balance of those two is really hard to come by."[^1]

> "Instead of believer / ex-believer, it's 'I believe in certain things, you believe in different things.' That sounds like interfaith dialogue to me — and I'm good at interfaith dialogue."[^3]

## LDS Connection
Halverson, whose academic specialty is anti-religious rhetoric, presents this as the precondition for any fruitful witness: the substance of the restoration's claim (see [[Scribal Religion Versus Authoritative Religion]]) only lands if the relationship survives the conversation. It pairs with [[The Marriage Analogy for Interfaith Dialogue]] and complements [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]] — charity toward persons, rigor toward claims.
