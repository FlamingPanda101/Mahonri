# Cultural Pressure to Conform Produces Pretense

## The Point
Jeff Strong's fourth cultural tension is "single-crop soil" — "soil that only grows one kind of plant." When a ward, family, or relationship pressures everyone to be the same kind of Latter-day Saint, the result is *pretense*: "pretending to be something you're not so that you can fit in," which "creates incredible internal dissonance."[^1] Crucially, some of the standards driving this are not real doctrine at all but cultural inventions — e.g., the unwritten rule that you must be able to say "I know," not merely "I believe": "It's not real in our theology or our doctrine, but it's very real in the culture."[^2] People who can't perform the expected mold receive repeated signals that "there's not space for me," which is "depleting and a bit suffocating," and "if people stay in that state perpetually, they generally won't stay in the church."[^3] The healthy alternative to conformity is not uniformity but *harmony* — "unity through difference."[^4]

## Evidence
1. **Culturally manufactured standards.** Strong distinguishes God-given standards (e.g., the law of chastity) from standards "we sometimes create culturally that aren't actually real." The "believe vs. know" gatekeeping is his prime example — real in culture, absent from doctrine.[^2] The bishopric-beards anecdote (a stake president rescinding a calling over facial hair) illustrates how a tradition can be enforced as if it were a standard.[^5]
2. **Gather, don't sort.** Strong's corrective principle: "Standards exist to elevate people, not sort them." Christ "talked about sorting a lot... but our role as members is to gather. God is the sorter," and "the sorting doesn't happen now, it happens later." In practice: "when somebody is different, love them, embrace them, bring them in. You don't prune a plant before it's even sprouted." Restoring balance means "more love and acceptance," explicitly "not to lower the standards."[^6]
3. **Harmony vs. the kazoo orchestra.** God made people different; the things that "draw us towards God are unique and different." Conformity is "everybody in the orchestra... playing a kazoo," whereas harmony uses many instruments. He grounds this in Nephi's teaching that God "speaks unto men... according to their understanding" (2 Ne. 31:3) — illustrated by a missionary who felt the Spirit through a Led Zeppelin lyric.[^4]
4. **Where it leads.** "If that gets too powerful in a family or relationship or ward, then the people that don't fit are going to really suffer. And at some point, they will start to pull back because they can't be who they really are."[^7] *Steelman:* a critic could warn that "harmony, not conformity" risks dissolving any shared standard into anything-goes individualism. Strong pre-empts this by twice refusing to lower standards — his target is *pretense and false uniformity*, not the commandments themselves; the unity he wants is Pauline (the body of Christ "needs all the members," 1 Cor. 12), not doctrinal relativism.

## LDS Connection
Strong's "gather, don't sort" maps directly onto the gathering of Israel: the members' commission is to gather, while final judgment — the separating of wheat from tares — is God's and is reserved for the harvest, not now (Matt. 13:28–30). His "harmony not conformity" is Paul's body-of-Christ teaching that the body is not one member but many, and that it errs when it wants everyone to be a hand or an eye (1 Cor. 12:14–21). Naming a "believe vs. know" gate as cultural rather than doctrinal is pastorally important: testimony in scripture grows by degrees — Alma frames faith as an experiment that only gradually yields knowledge (Alma 32:27–34), and the father of the possessed boy is received with "Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief" (Mark 9:24). A culture that demands performed certainty pressures the honestly-uncertain into pretense, which is one mechanism behind the PIMO phenomenon (see [[PIMO Clergy Physically In Mentally Out]]) — the opposite of the safe, supportive belonging that helps wrestlers stay (see [[High-Stakes Faith Conversations Matter More Than the Belief]]).
