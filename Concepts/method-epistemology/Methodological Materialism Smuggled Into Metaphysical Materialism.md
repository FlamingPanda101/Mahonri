# Methodological Materialism Smuggled Into Metaphysical Materialism

## The Claim (Distinction Drawn)
[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] — an agnostic, not an apologist — argues that science legitimately *assumes* materialism as a **method** ("to do science, we assume material causes"), but that our culture has illegitimately upgraded this working assumption into a **metaphysics**: the claim that "there really is *only* the material." That move is unwarranted, and it both "shuts a door" on inquiry and leaves many people "deeply unsatisfying[ly]" boxed in.

## The Argument

### 1. Two different claims wear the same word (strong, clarifying)
- *Methodological materialism*: a discipline brackets non-material causes to get traction — a rule of procedure, agnostic about what ultimately exists.
- *Metaphysical materialism*: the ontological thesis that the material is all there is.
The first does not entail the second; treating a *method* as a *discovery* is a category error. O'Connor: science "assumes materialism methodologically... but we've transformed that into a metaphysical assumption that there really is only the material."[^1]

### 2. The upgrade is self-undermining as inquiry (medium)
Converting the assumption into dogma "completely shuts a door of possible exploration" — it forecloses questions (about consciousness, value, the pre-cosmic) by definition rather than by evidence. O'Connor notes a cultural shift *back* toward openness precisely because the materialist promissory note ("we'll explain it eventually") has gone unpaid for a long time.[^2]

### 3. Physics itself counsels humility (medium)
O'Connor and [[Robert Greene]] point out that fundamental physics — "the smartest people on the planet" — confesses deep ignorance at the subatomic level (quantum weirdness, the pre-Big-Bang, fine-tuning of the constants). If the hardest science is this far from closure, confident metaphysical materialism is unearned. O'Connor adds a meta-caution: *attractive* worldviews (theistic *or* nihilistic) deserve extra suspicion because "we are more likely to accept them on less evidence."[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The materialist replies that methodological materialism is justified *abductively*, not arbitrarily: every phenomenon ever actually explained turned out to have a physical mechanism, so projecting materialism as the best metaphysical bet is an inductive inference, not a smuggle. "Materialism of the gaps" objections cut both ways — invoking the immaterial wherever science is currently silent is its own fallacy. The track record of physical explanation is the warrant.

## Counter-Rebuttal
O'Connor's reply (consistent with his agnosticism): an unbroken record of *physical* explanations is exactly what methodological materialism *guarantees by construction* — it only ever looks for physical causes, so it can only ever find them; that success cannot then be cited as evidence that *only* physical causes exist without circularity. The honest conclusion is not metaphysical materialism but suspended judgment: "I have no problem saying we can't explain it yet" — without pretending the method has settled the ontology.

## Apologetic Engagement
This is a gift from a hostile-leaning witness: the distinction is the backbone of the apologetic reply to scientism (see [[Scientism - The Metal Detector Fallacy]], [[Five Classes of Truth Science Cannot Establish]]). When a critic says "there's no scientific evidence for God/the soul," the move is to ask whether they are reporting a *result* of inquiry or merely the *rule* that excluded the answer in advance. The caveat: O'Connor's point clears space for the immaterial in general; it does not, by itself, argue *for* God or the Restoration — it disarms an overreaching defeater rather than building a positive case.

## LDS Relevance
The Restoration is famously *materialist about spirit* — "there is no such thing as immaterial matter; all spirit is matter, but... more fine or pure" ([[D&C 131|D&C 131:7-8]]). So the LDS quarrel is less with materialism per se than with the *reductive* metaphysics that denies mind, agency, and the divine. O'Connor's distinction lets the Latter-day Saint accept science's method wholeheartedly while rejecting the smuggled dogma that neural tissue is all a person is (cf. [[The Mind Is Not the Brain]]).
