# Five Classes of Truth Science Cannot Establish

## The Claim

[[Ether's Elephant]], quoting philosopher [[William Lane Craig]], argues that there are whole categories of truth that everyone rationally accepts but that science cannot in principle prove — so "science lives in the natural world yet there are truths that cannot be discovered, proven or even discussed scientifically." The point is not anti-science; it is anti-*scientism* (the thesis that science is the only source of knowledge). If science cannot ground these five, then the demand that God be provable "by science" is a category mistake.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Logical and mathematical truths.** "Science presupposes logic and math so that to try to prove them by science would be arguing in a circle." The tools science uses cannot be outputs of science.[^2] *Rating: strong; this is a standard and widely conceded point about the presuppositions of inquiry.*

2. **Metaphysical truths.** "There are other minds other than my own," "the external world is real," "the past was not created 5 minutes ago with an appearance of age" — none is empirically demonstrable, yet all are rational to hold.[^2] *Rating: strong; these are classic underdetermined beliefs that science assumes rather than proves.*

3. **Ethical truths.** "You can't show by science whether the Nazi scientists in the camps did anything evil as opposed to the scientists in western democracies." Statements of moral value are not read off the data.[^2] *Rating: strong on the is/ought point; a moral realist still needs a separate grounding, which is where the [[Moral Argument Against Atheism|moral argument]] enters.*

4. **Aesthetic judgments.** "The beautiful like the good cannot be scientifically proven." Value of beauty is not a measurable property.[^2] *Rating: moderate-to-strong; a naturalist can offer evolutionary debunking accounts of aesthetic response, but those explain the *response*, not the *value*.*

5. **Science itself.** "Most remarkably... science cannot be justified by the scientific method. Science is permeated with unprovable assumptions" (the uniformity of nature, the reliability of the senses and intellect).[^2] *Rating: strong; this is the self-referential core of the anti-scientism argument — see [[Scientism Is Self-Refuting]].*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A sophisticated naturalist need not embrace crude scientism. (a) The "five classes" may be defended *pragmatically* or *philosophically* rather than scientifically without conceding anything to theism — admitting non-scientific knowledge is not admitting *religious* knowledge. (b) Logic, math, and the uniformity of nature can be cast as *presuppositions* or *inference-to-best-explanation* posits, not as truths needing the same kind of grounding God would. (c) Even granting all five, nothing yet follows about God's existence; the argument only clears scientism out of the way, leaving the positive case for God still to be made (which the video concedes — it denies science can *prove* God). The honest takeaway is modest: science is not the sole arbiter of truth, so "prove God in a lab" is the wrong test — not that God has thereby been shown.

## Best Quotes

> "Logical and mathematical truths cannot be proven by science. Science presupposes logic and math so that to try to prove them by science would be arguing in a circle."[^2]

> "And finally, most remarkably would be science itself. Science cannot be justified by the scientific method. Science is permeated with unprovable assumptions."[^2]

## LDS Connection

The video uses Craig's list (shared, non-LDS-specific philosophy of science) to set up a positively Restoration conclusion: since science is the wrong instrument for the God question, the legitimate path is experiential — the [[The Scientific Method Applied to Faith Yields Personal Revelation|personal experiment that yields personal revelation]]. Restoration epistemology is explicitly anti-scientism, pairing "study" with "faith" (D&C 88:118) and knowledge "by the power of the Holy Ghost" ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4–5]]). The classes themselves are common ground with classical theism (cf. [[Scientism Cannot Describe or Explain All Reality]]).
