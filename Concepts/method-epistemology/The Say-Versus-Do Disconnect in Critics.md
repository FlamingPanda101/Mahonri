# The Say-Versus-Do Disconnect in Critics

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s central charge against high-output critics like [[John Dehlin]], RFM, and [[Alyssa Grenfell]] is a **coherence** charge, not a motive charge: "What you say speaks way quieter than what you actually do." A critic may *say* "I'm not anti-Mormon; the church does a lot of good," but if the near-totality of his *content* portrays the church as abusive, dishonest, dangerous, bigoted, and lying about claims it knows are false, then the stated self-description and the produced body of work are in flat contradiction. Hansen presses the dilemma both ways: if the church is as bad as the content says, the critic *should* want it destroyed and should say so; and if it does as much good as the critic privately concedes, the channel should *reflect that ratio*. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Output, not testimony, reveals the real verdict.** "Look what you say speaks way quieter than what you actually do, and the content that John produces, that RFM produces, that Alyssa Grenfell produces is that the church is abusive, dishonest, dangerous, harmful, bigoted, and lying about all of its truth claims that it knows are false." [^1] Hansen proposes an operationalized test: scroll the channel's videos and tally positive vs. negative — "I would think you might have 1% that are positive." [^3] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* the "look at the corpus" move is a fair empirical check on a stated disposition, though a "1%" eyeball estimate is impressionistic, not a measured content analysis.

2. **The two-horned dilemma.** "If the church is as bad as you claim, it should be destroyed and John Dehlin should stop acting like it shouldn't, because it's really weird to me when you're saying I don't want to be called an anti-Mormon, but this institution is abusive [etc.]. There's a total disconnect there." [^2] The other horn: "Let's make John, your channel, reflect what you actually believe about the church. Because right now there's a massive disconnect between what you say you believe versus what you produce." [^4] *Rating: strong as an internal-consistency argument;* it does not require Hansen to read hearts — it only requires the critic's words and works to line up, which is the essence of [[Reforming a Thing's Essence Is Destroying It Not Reforming It|"just own it"]].

3. **Distinguishing "not true" from "evil."** Hansen explicitly *respects* the position that the church is simply false: "I can respect people who think that the church is not true. You're one of those people [to Pynakker]." The disconnect arises only at the further claim "that the church is bad, bigoted, dangerous, abusive" while disclaiming the "anti" label. [^5] *Rating: strong;* this keeps the argument off the legitimacy of disbelief and onto the specific incoherence of "it's abusive and dishonest, but I bear it no animus."

4. **The financial incentive sharpens the disconnect.** Hansen notes the structural reason the ratio won't change: a channel "fueled entirely by people who are upset about the church" faces an audience cost for posting positive content — "does it make financial sense for you to begin to make episodes... about the positive fruits of Mormonism?" [^6] He pairs this with a standing offer to come on and walk through the social-science literature (cf. [[The Fruits of the Church - Do Critics Have a Better Program]]). *Rating: moderate;* the incentive analysis is plausible and explanatory, but it edges from the clean coherence argument toward imputing financial motive, which is weaker ground.

**Steelman of the critic.** [[Steven Pynakker]] supplies the rebuttal throughout: a critic can coherently believe the *institution* is net-harmful in specific, documentable ways (abuse handling, financial opacity, LGBTQ harm) while believing individual members and even much of the lived religion are good — so "exposing the bad" is not the same as "wanting the good thing destroyed," any more than an investigative reporter who only covers a hospital's malpractice wants hospitals abolished. The "1% positive" framing also ignores selection: a reform/accountability channel is *supposed* to cover problems, just as a watchdog beat does. Dehlin's reported concession that the church does "more good than bad," and his platforming of faithful and positive voices, is offered as evidence the disconnect is overstated. Hansen's reply is that the *magnitude and totality* of the negative output, plus the refusal to own the implied conclusion, is what makes it incoherent rather than merely critical.
