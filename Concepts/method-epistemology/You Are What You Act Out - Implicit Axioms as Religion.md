# You Are What You Act Out - Implicit Axioms as Religion

#type/claim

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]]'s claim, deployed by the video: your *real* religion is not what you say you believe but the system of implicit axioms that your behavior manifests. Since you cannot act without a hierarchy of values, and "the system of implicit axioms that you hold as primary is your religious belief system," everyone — atheist included — is enacting a faith whether they know it or not.[^1]

## Argument
1. **Behavior reveals true belief better than testimony.** "What you act out is much more what you are than what you say about yourself."[^2] People "are not transparent to themselves"; what you say you believe and what you actually believe "might not be the same thing."[^3]
2. **Action presupposes a value hierarchy.** "You can't act without a hierarchy of values, because you can't act unless you think one thing is better than another — why would you act otherwise? So that means you're embedded within a hierarchy of values whether you know it or not."[^4]
3. **The top of that hierarchy is your religion.** "That hierarchy of values... is based on axioms... and that axiomatic system is essentially your religious system, and there's no way out of that."[^5] Articulated denial of a transcendent deity is "just surface noise."[^2]
4. **Fragmented hierarchies are pathological.** Without a unified value at the top, "your value system will be like a pantheon of gods fighting with each other for dominance, and you will be disintegrated and thus morally confused."[^4] The video glosses this as a "critical point."
5. **Most people enact a Judeo-Christian ethic.** Peterson's test: if you fail to treat someone "as an embodiment of a divine fragment" capable of free choice, or as a sovereign contributing member of the polity, they become "very, very offended" — revealing the ethic they actually hold.[^6]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** The argument trades on an idiosyncratically broad definition of "religion." Equating "the values implicit in one's actions" with "a religious belief system" is largely stipulative — it wins by redefinition, so that an atheist's commitment to, say, evidence or compassion gets relabeled "faith" and "religion." A secularist can grant that humans act on prioritized values while denying that this amounts to anything supernatural, transcendent, or God-directed; functional values need not be axioms held *as primary* in Peterson's quasi-theological sense. The reaction-to-mistreatment test shows people value dignity and autonomy, which has secular philosophical groundings (Kantian, humanist) that don't require attributing a "divine fragment." Critics also note the slide from descriptive psychology ("you act on values") to a normative-theological conclusion ("therefore God / Judeo-Christian ethic") is not licensed by the premises.

## Best Quotes

> "Religion is what you act out. Anything you act out, everything you act out, is predicated on your implicit axioms, and the system of implicit axioms that you hold as primary is your religious belief system."[^1]

> "What you act out is much more what you are than what you say about yourself... we're not transparent to ourselves at all."[^2]

> "You can't act without a hierarchy of values, because you can't act unless you think one thing is better than another."[^4]

> "That axiomatic system is essentially your religious system, and there's no way out of that as far as I can tell."[^5]
