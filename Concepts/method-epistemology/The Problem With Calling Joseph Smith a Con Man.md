# The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man
#type/claim

## The Claim
Labeling Joseph Smith an "outright con man" is the explanation that fits the primary sources *least* well. A con man is a calculating deceiver who exploits marks for gain and whose pretense, once useful, hardens into routine. But evangelical scholar [[Kyle Beshears]] — who immersed himself in Joseph's own journals and does *not* accept him as a prophet — concluded the opposite: Joseph was "an extremely complicated man" whose sincerity was real, even pastoral, and seemed to *increase* rather than wane over time. The con-man category flattens that complexity and is contradicted by the documentary record, even though Beshears still denies Joseph's prophetic claims.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The hostile witness concedes it.** Beshears once thought fraud "obvious — angels, seer stones, gold plates, come on," but after writing his book reached "a different conclusion": "I don't think Joseph Smith was an outright con." See [[Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud]].
- **Sincerity grows, contra the grifter arc.** Against the standard "con man learns to exploit his followers" trajectory, Beshears found Joseph's "sincerity for his desire to see Christianity healed" *increased* "as he grows in this prophetic office" — the reverse of what a calculating fraud's record should show.
- **Pastoral behavior in private sources.** Reading Joseph visiting the sick during an epidemic, mourning with the bereaved, and defending the accused in church trials, Beshears (himself a pastor) "couldn't unsee" a genuine pastoral sincerity grounded in Joseph's own journals, not enemies' affidavits. Beshears still declines all the tidy boxes — "con," "pious fraud," etc. (see [[Refusing the Pious Fraud Category]]).
- **The label also overreaches legally/rhetorically.** The popular "convicted con man" charge overstates the historical record (see [[Convicted Con Man Label Overclaim]]).
- **Steelman.** A critic in the [[Dan Vogel]] "pious fraud" tradition can argue sincerity and deception routinely coexist in charismatic founders — warmth toward the flock is exactly what makes a movement cohere — so pastoral kindness does not show the plates and first-person revelations were genuine. Beshears partly concedes this by refusing the "prophet" verdict; his narrower point, which this note records, is that the cartoonish "con man" is precisely what the documents do *not* support. The honest options remaining are "sincere but mistaken/self-deceived," not "calculating swindler."
