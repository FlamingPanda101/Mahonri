# PIMO Clergy Physically In Mentally Out

#type/claim

## The Claim
The phenomenon of clergy who continue serving while privately disbelieving is named with the LDS-coined acronym **PIMO** — "Physically In, Mentally Out."[^1] The panel debates whether low clergy-belief numbers reflect genuine PIMO leaders across denominations, and whether an LDS bishop could ever be a PIMO.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The acronym and its origin (definitional).** PIMO = "Physically In, Mentally Out" — someone who stays inside a faith institution while no longer believing.[^1] It originated in Latter-day Saint faith-transition discourse and is here generalized to all clergy. *Rating: accurate as a description of a real, observed pattern.*

2. **Self-reported PIMO leaders exist (anecdotal).** The panel cites Bo Euler, who "bragged about being one," and a bishop who praised loving the youth "even though... he resigned the next day."[^3] *Rating: anecdotal; illustrative of the category, not a measure of its size.*

3. **The "why stay?" argument (moderate).** [[Jonah Barnes]] argues an LDS bishop is an unlikely PIMO: "What kind of a psychotic sadist would continue serving as a bishop" — the hardest, unpaid calling in the Church — "if you didn't have a deep true conviction?"[^4] The high personal cost of LDS lay clergy filters out the insincere. *Rating: a real selection effect, but the rebuttal notes self-justifying exceptions.*

4. **Stated-motive rebuttal (steelman of PIMO bishops).** The skeptical host anticipates the counter: PIMO leaders rationalize staying as *altruism* — Bo Euler said he continued "because I saw that I was more able to influence positively." So "why would a non-believer stay?" has a ready answer, and the cost-filter argument is weaker than it first appears.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Even unpaid, high-cost clergy may remain for non-belief reasons: community, identity, family pressure, sunk cost, or a sincere desire to reform from within. The existence of self-described PIMO bishops shows the "no rational non-believer would serve" intuition is empirically false. The honest conclusion is that PIMO clergy exist in every tradition but are hard to count from survey data alone.

**Context.** PIMO complicates both the "grift" reading and the "hollow faith" reading: a PIMO pastor is neither necessarily a cynical money-grifter (see [[Grift Does Not Track With Disbelief Rebuttal]]) nor a simple unbeliever — they often stay for relational or institutional reasons while quietly dissenting on doctrine, which is exactly the kind of nuance the survey's binary format cannot capture (see [[Definitely Believe Wording Skews Survey Results]]).
