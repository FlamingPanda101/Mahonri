# Greek Seeing vs Hebrew Hearing

## The Point
The two cultures privilege different master verbs, and that difference defines two epistemologies. For Greeks, knowing is **seeing** (empirical, static, optical); for Hebrews, knowing is **hearing** (relational, requiring something that *acts* and *speaks*). This is the engine of every later contrast in the talk.

## Argument
1. **The Greek verb: *horaō*, "to see."** Sight is the optimal Greek sense — "optics," "optimal," "optimize" all descend from it. Knowing is empirical: "see it with my own eyes," "seeing is believing." This is "excellent for scientific research, for engineering and empiricism" — which is *why* Greek thought conquered the planet [^1].
2. **The Hebrew verb: *shema*, "to hear."** Hearing is the priority sense. Crucially, "you can't hear something static, you can't hear a Polaroid" — the thing heard must be *acting*. *Shema* is also near the word for *obedience* [^2].
3. **The epistemological consequence.** A Greek "can look at something lifeless and get information from it"; a Hebrew says "if it's not talking I can't hear it; if it isn't acting, I don't know it." Greeks are comfortable with the dead, the abstract, the conceptual; Hebrews require living, acting reality [^3].
4. **This is *us*.** Whenever the contrast names "Greeks," it includes modern Westerners — "we are essentially Greeks in the way that we view the world." The Greek paradigm is "the water we swim in" and don't notice [^4].
5. **The Pilate test case.** This contrast climaxes in [[What Is Truth - Pilate and the Greek Question|Pilate's "What is truth?"]] — the Greek wants truth *shown* (written down, seen); Jesus offers truth *heard* and *lived* ("everyone that is of the truth heareth my voice") [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "For the Greeks the most important verb is *horaō*, or to see... see is believing, right, I want to see it with my own eyes... But the Hebrews have a very different priority on that verb: their most important verb is *shema*, or to hear." [^1]

> "If you're hearing something, that other thing has to be acting. You can't hear something static, you can't hear a Polaroid. Greeks can look at something lifeless and get information from it; Hebrews say it has to be talking and I have to be hearing it." [^2]
