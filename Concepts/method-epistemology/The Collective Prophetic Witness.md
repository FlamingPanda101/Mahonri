# The Collective Prophetic Witness

## Overview

This is a hub note for the LDS epistemological principle of the **collective prophetic witness** — the idea that truth is established not by the inerrancy of any single text or prophet but by the *mutually reinforcing testimony of God's prophets across time*. In the Maybe God debate, Jacob Hansen deploys it as "99 prophets over 1": rather than defend every troubling verse as inerrant, the Latter-day Saint reads Scripture as a network in which major themes corroborate one another, so an isolated passage out of character with the whole can be judged an *outlier* that "may not be inspired" — without granting each reader license to discard whatever they dislike.

The principle does double duty: it dissolves apparent moral contradictions (e.g., Leviticus' death penalty for homosexual acts vs. an unchanging God vs. Jesus) by treating inerrancy as the false premise, and it supplies a *coherentist* alternative to both Protestant inerrancy and self-authentication. Its load-bearing assumption is a sufficiently rich, authoritative extra-biblical canon to define the baseline against which outliers are measured.

## The Steelman

The inerrantist's strongest counter (granted in the cluster): the "outlier" filter risks *circularity* — the modern church's present values become the ruler used to excise inconvenient ancient texts, smuggling cultural change in as revelation, which is functionally the "trajectory hermeneutic" LDS apologists elsewhere criticize. The defense — that the filter is the *prophetic collective under continuing revelation*, not private taste — answers the "who decides?" objection only if one already grants LDS prophetic authority. Honest tension: the method is exactly as strong as the extra-biblical canon is granted to be.
