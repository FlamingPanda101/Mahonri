# Everyone Has a Faith-Choice Bias and Admitting It Is More Objective

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Kerry Muhlestein]] argues that *no one* approaches the [[Book of Abraham]] (or any worldview question) from a position of pure neutrality: everyone has already made a **faith-choice** — to believe, to disbelieve, or to withhold — and that choice colors how evidence is read. The twist is epistemological: the person who *claims* perfect objectivity is actually the *least* objective, because they have hidden their own commitment from themselves. Honestly *naming* one's bias is therefore the more objective posture. As Muhlestein puts it, "cynicism is not intelligence" — reflexive doubt is itself a faith-stance masquerading as neutrality.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "view from nowhere" is a myth.** Everyone reasons from prior commitments, background beliefs, and what they *want* to be true; the believer and the skeptic both bring priors to the Book of Abraham. Pretending otherwise does not remove the bias, it only conceals it — so disclosure is *more* honest, not less. *Rating: strong as philosophy of inquiry — a mainstream point (no presuppositionless inquiry) applied to apologetics.*
2. **Cynicism is a posture, not a proof.** Treating doubt as the automatically "smart" default ("cynicism is not intelligence") confuses an *attitude* with an *argument*. Reflexive disbelief is itself a faith-choice — a bet that the claim is false — and should be owned and defended like any other. *Rating: moderate — a fair rhetorical correction, though it can be overused to dismiss legitimate skepticism.*
3. **It reframes the burden honestly.** Once both sides admit a faith-choice, the debate shifts from "neutral expert vs. biased believer" to "which set of priors better fits the evidence?" — a fairer and more productive frame. *Rating: useful methodological reframing.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The critic warns this is a *tu quoque* that can flatten a real asymmetry: "everyone is biased" is true but risks the false conclusion that all biases are *equal*, licensing the believer to discount any inconvenient evidence as "just your bias too." Methodological naturalism and peer review exist precisely to *constrain* bias, not because anyone is bias-free; calling the disciplined skeptic "the least objective" inverts that. And "cynicism is not intelligence" can become a thought-terminating cliché that immunizes the claim against scrutiny. A measured reply: the argument does not say bias cannot be *disciplined*, only that it cannot be *eliminated*, and that undisclosed bias is more corrosive than disclosed bias — which is compatible with valuing peer review. The honest core: name your priors, then argue evenhandedly; the one who denies having any has simply stopped examining themselves.

## Best Quotes
> Everyone has a faith-choice bias, and the claim of pure objectivity is itself the least objective posture — "cynicism is not intelligence."

## LDS Relevance
This underwrites a Restoration approach to faith-and-evidence: it disarms the framing that the skeptic is the neutral arbiter and the believer the special-pleader, and it sets up Muhlestein's companion defense of [[Revelation Is a Valid Source of Knowledge]]. It also pairs with the vault's guardrail [[A Data Analysis of Scripture Is Only as Objective as Its Model]], which makes the same point from the other direction — that even quantitative "objectivity" carries hidden bias. Per the steelman, the note should *level* the field, not excuse the believer from evidence.
