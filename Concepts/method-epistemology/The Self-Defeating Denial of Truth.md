# The Self-Defeating Denial of Truth

## The Point
The first point of the four-point case is that objective truth exists — and Turek argues it is impossible to deny without self-contradiction.[^1] When someone says "There is no truth," the obvious reply is "Is that true?" The denial of truth is like saying "I can't speak a word of English" (in English) or "Everything I say is a lie."[^1] Such statements violate the **law of noncontradiction** — that opposite ideas cannot both be true at the same time and in the same sense — which is self-evident and undeniable, since even denying the law requires using it.[^1]

## Evidence
Turek's method is simple: apply each relativist claim to itself and see whether it meets its own standard.[^2] "There are no absolute truths!" — *Are you absolutely sure?* "All truth is relative!" — *Is that a relative truth?* "It's true for you but not for me!" — *Is that true for everybody?* "No one knows the truth!" — to know that, you would have to know the truth yourself and survey everyone else.[^2] "You shouldn't judge!" — *Isn't that a judgment?* He notes that Jesus is not forbidding judgment but commanding right judgment ("first take the plank out of your own eye... then you will see clearly"; "judge correctly"), and that everyone — atheist, Buddhist, Christian — makes thousands of judgments daily.[^3] The takeaway is the "intellectual sickle" of noncontradiction: relativists and atheists repeatedly err by *exempting themselves* from their own theories, and learning to spot that exemption quickly exposes the error.[^4] He distinguishes intellectual doubt from emotional doubt, quoting Greg Koukl ("Before my first cup of coffee... I'm an atheist!"), and concludes that the facts don't change with fluctuating feelings.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Self-defeating statements violate the self-evident law of logic known as the law of noncontradiction... No one can think or make any truth claim without the law of noncontradiction." [^1]

> "'True for you but not for me' may be the mantra of our day, but that's not the way the universe really works. If it's really true, it's true for everyone." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints firmly affirm objective truth: scripture defines truth as "knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) — an absolute, mind-independent reality, not a private construction. The self-refutation Turek wields against relativism is the same logical tool LDS apologists use, and his point that one can "love the Lord your God... with all your mind" coheres with the LDS conviction that faith and reason are allies. A complementary LDS emphasis is that some truth is known not only by abstract proposition but by lived experience and the witness of the Spirit — a relational, "Hebrew" way of knowing truth (compare [[What Is Truth - Pilate and the Greek Question]]) that supplements, rather than denies, the law of noncontradiction.
