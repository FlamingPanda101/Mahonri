# Culture Is Soil - Protection and Nourishment Must Be Balanced

## The Point
[[Jeff Strong]]'s organizing metaphor for why people leave is **culture as soil**. Drawing on the etymology of *culture* (Latin *colere*, "to cultivate the dirt — soil for the purpose of growing things"), he argues healthy cultural soil must do two opposite jobs at once: be **firm enough to protect and stabilize** the plant's roots, and **rich enough to nourish** its growth. Faith fails when the soil is imbalanced either way — too hard and depleted, the plant stays in place but starves; too loose and over-fed, the wind blows it over.[^1][^2] The question, he insists, "isn't who's right, good, evil, bad" — it's "how do we get the soil right?"

## Evidence

1. **The two jobs of soil (rated: strong as a model; it is a metaphor, not data).** Soil must supply water, oxygen, nutrients, and minerals — "the nourish side, the soft side" — *and* be "firm and protective" so "if the wind blows it just doesn't rip the plant out of the ground."[^1] Mapped onto the Church: doctrines, authority, priesthood keys, and leaders are the firmness orthodox members "like"; "truth and light... dealing with the real issues of my life" is the nourishment, "or it's dead on arrival."[^2]

2. **Both imbalances are fatal (rated: coherent).** "If there's too much stability and protection and that soil gets depleted, the plants stay in place, but they die... and if there's too much nourishment and no stability and protection, the wind blows and the plants just fall over and die."[^3] Strong's empirical claim is that LDS soil is, on average, imbalanced *toward* protection at the expense of nourishment — though he stresses this "varies a lot by individual family, ward, stake," and many wards are "killing it."[^4]

3. **Four cultural imbalances (rated: interpretive framework).** Strong names four tensions the soil has to balance: **acceptance vs. standards**, **sanctuary vs. transcendence/growth** (is the Church "a bomb shelter" to hide in, or "a gospel of transformation"?), **agency vs. conformity** (the war in heaven was over agency, yet culture can expect "comply and defer"), and **conformity vs. harmony** (everyone different yet "united by a common cause").[^5][^6] He is emphatic this is not an argument for lowering standards: "there's not a single place in the book I advocate for lower standards, weaker commandments, lowering the bar" — he calls balanced soil "a tougher version of the gospel."[^7]

4. **"Prepare them, don't protect them" (rated: pastoral application).** On the sanctuary imbalance, Strong recounts telling a senior church leader who wanted to "protect young people from the world," "I don't want you to protect them. I want you to prepare them... you cannot create a bubble that will last their whole lifetime." Sanctuary is real but is "a rest stop on the highway to transformation."[^6]

5. **Steelman / honest limits.** The soil metaphor is intuitively powerful but **unfalsifiable as stated**: any departure can be retro-fitted as "depleted soil," and any retention as "balanced soil," so it explains everything and predicts little. Brad Whitbeck presses a sharp limit on the nourishment side: over-praising doubt ("you are perfect the way you are") can *reinforce* disaffiliation — Strong concedes this was "hyperbole" and that balance, not affirmation, is the point.[^8] The model also brackets, by Strong's own "ring fence," the institution, the Brethren, and the commandments — which a critic could say places the most consequential variables out of bounds.[^9]

## Best Quotes

> "It comes from the Latin word *colere*, which means dirt... it's soil for the purpose of growing things." [^1]

> "If there's too much stability and protection and that soil gets depleted, the plants stay in place, but they die. And if there's too much nourishment and no stability and protection, the wind blows and the plants just fall over and die." [^3]

> "I don't want you to protect them. I want you to prepare them." [^6]

## LDS Connection
The soil image is scriptural at its root: the parable of the sower turns on whether seed lands on stony ground, among thorns, or in *good ground* ([[Matthew 13.3-9|Matthew 13:3-9]]), and Alma compares the word to a seed that grows only in a heart that gives it "place" ([[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]]). Strong's "firm *and* nourishing" balance mirrors the gospel's own pairing of a "strait and narrow" path ([[2 Nephi 31.18|2 Nephi 31]]) with a Shepherd who feeds the sheep ([[John 21.15-17|John 21]]) — structure and succor together. The call to "succor the weak, lift up the hands which hang down" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 81.5|D&C 81:5]]) is the nourishment side made a commandment, complementing rather than dissolving the standards. Compare [[The Three Levels of the Gospel]], which similarly distinguishes the gospel's enduring core from its cultural accretions.
