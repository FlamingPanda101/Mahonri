# It Takes More Faith to Call Joseph Smith a Fraud Than a Prophet

## The Claim
[[Kerry Muhlestein]] frames the Book of Abraham case as a balance-of-evidence wager: there are now so many details that match what Joseph Smith said — and so few that don't — that the **fraud hypothesis carries the heavier explanatory burden**. "You actually have to have more faith to say Joseph isn't a prophet than ... to say he is." On the prophet hypothesis there are fewer anomalies to explain away than on the fraud hypothesis.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The burden-shift argument (rated: rhetorical–structural)
"There are so many things that match well with what Joseph Smith said from an academic point of view ... and so few things that don't, that I think you actually have to have more faith to say Joseph isn't a prophet than you have to say it is. Intellectually ... there are more things to explain away if you don't accept him as a prophet than there are to explain away if you think he is a prophet. It just is a bigger leap of faith."[^1] This is an explicit **inference-to-the-best-explanation** move: count the anomalies each hypothesis must absorb.

### 2. Backed by the textual hits (rated: cumulative)
The claim is the capstone of Muhlestein's intellectual reasons (10–5): Olishem, Shinehah, the four Canaanite gods, the Sobek/Pharaoh link, the facsimile temple order, and Egyptians associating lion-couch scenes with Abraham. Each forces the fraud hypothesis to posit a lucky guess; the accumulation is what makes "fraud" the higher-faith position. See [[Olishem Is a Linguistic Geographical and Chronological Match]].[^1]

### 3. Parity with secular history (rated: moderate)
The hosts argue we accept far thinner evidence for ordinary ancient claims: "I feel we have just as much historical evidence for the book of Abraham and the plains of [Olishem] as we do for half these cities they say Alexander the Great conquered."[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic answers: (a) the argument quietly omits the *cost* side of the ledger — the surviving papyri read as a standard Book of Breathings, the Kirtland Egyptian Papers exist, and Abraham 3's cosmology looks 19th-century — so "few things that don't match" is contestable (the vault catalogs these at [[The 6% Astrology Outlier and Abraham 3]] and [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]); (b) "more faith to disbelieve" presupposes Muhlestein's weighting of the hits, which a skeptic discounts as phonetic coincidence and post-hoc fit; and (c) the secular-history parity fails because historians don't claim *divine* origin for Alexander's cities — the prior for "ancient toponym" is far higher than for "miraculously revealed scripture." The honest LDS register concedes the unresolved problems (Muhlestein does) and treats the wager as a cumulative-case judgment, not a proof, paired with [[Revelation Is a Valid Source of Knowledge]].

## Best Quotes
> "You actually have to have more faith to say Joseph isn't a prophet than you have to say it is ... there are more things to explain away if you don't accept him as a prophet than there are to explain away if you think he is a prophet." [^1]

## LDS Connection
A method-level companion to [[The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves]] (the same inference applied to the Book of Mormon) and to [[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]]. It crystallizes why LDS apologists frame the [[Book of Abraham]] as a net-positive cumulative case rather than answering objections one at a time.
