# Abductive Reasoning Is How Historians Infer the Best Explanation

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] frames his reading of the leg surgery as an instance of **abductive reasoning** ("inference to the best explanation"), which he distinguishes from deduction (mathematics, syllogism) and induction (the generalizing logic of the sciences). Historians, he says, ask "what would best explain these events?" — and on that standard the best explanation of the boy's alcohol refusal is that "Joseph Jr. is trying to help his dad." Naming the inferential mode is meant to make the argument's *type* explicit and honest, not to disguise interpretation as proof.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The taxonomy is stated explicitly (strong, methodological).** Bradley lays out deductive ("all men are mortal…"), inductive ("hydrogen + oxygen + energy → water"), and abductive reasoning, placing his claim squarely in the third — "what would best explain these events."[^1]
2. **The criteria of a good abduction (interpretive).** Throughout he appeals to standard abductive virtues: *explanatory scope* (it accounts for the refusal, the mother's removal, the undisclosed alcohol context at once), *fit with background knowledge* (dying-children research; the father's documented drinking), and *simplicity* ("ties it all up with a bow").[^2]
3. **Defeasibility is conceded (strong, intellectual honesty).** Bradley repeatedly says a *better* explanation would be welcome — "if you've got a better explanation, that's great" — which is exactly the open, defeasible structure of inference to the best explanation rather than deductive certainty.[^2]
4. **Method generalizes beyond apologetics (interpretive).** The same abductive logic underwrites mainstream historical reconstruction (motives, undocumented questions behind D&C revelations), so the move is not a special pleading invented for the prophet but the ordinary craft of history.[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
Inference to the best explanation is only as good as the field of rival hypotheses considered. A critic can argue Bradley under-populates the alternatives (childish aversion; simple desire for comfort; later narrative embellishment) and over-credits the "noble example for father" hypothesis because it is the one his current lens favors — making the "best" explanation merely the *best among a curated set*. Abduction can launder a preferred conclusion if the comparison class is stacked.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley pre-empts this by naming and engaging the leading rival (mere childish refusal) and rebutting it on the evidence (the child endured the whole operation; the account's undesigned coincidence with undisclosed context). He also invites better hypotheses explicitly, which is the correct response to the comparison-class worry: the remedy for a thin field is more candidate explanations, which he solicits rather than suppresses. The argument remains probabilistic, and he says so.

## Best Quotes
> "Historians do something called abductive reasoning, or inference to the best explanation. That is, what would best explain these events?"[^1]

> "If you've got a better explanation, that's great. But… you've got multiple arrows pointing the same thing and it ties it all up."[^2]
