# Historical Method Over Name Calling

#type/claim

## The Claim
Discrediting labels — "con man," "pedophile," "financial fraud," "fascist," "homophobe" — function as social tools to make an opponent seem evil, stupid, or gullible so that "you don't have to engage with their truth claims." This is rhetoric, not history; good history evaluates evidence rather than slapping on labels.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Labels short-circuit argument (mechanism).** "They want to be able to slap these labels on to immediately discredit and make the opposing side unreasonable. And if they're unreasonable, you don't have to engage with their truth claims."[^2] *Rating: strong — names a recognizable rhetorical strategy.*

2. **The SEC-fine analogy (parallel case).** Christian (who works in finance) compares "convicted con man" to branding the church's SEC settlement "financial fraud": the church received counsel in a vague gray area, followed it, was audited, found outside guidelines, paid the fine, and corrected — yet "fraud" is applied to foreclose engagement.[^3] *Rating: moderate — analogy is acknowledged as "incredibly loose" but illustrates the labeling pattern.*

3. **Anthropological framing.** "Throwing around terms ... that make someone look evil or stupid or gullible ... is a powerful social tool for trying to essentially manipulate someone into agreeing with you. But that's not history."[^4] *Rating: strong.*

4. **Contemporary parallels.** The hosts extend the pattern to modern discourse: calling someone a "fascist," "racist," or "homophobe" can end an argument without engaging it — "game over."[^5] *Rating: contextual — broadens the principle.*

5. **Steelman.** A critic can object that this is a tu quoque / tone argument: sometimes a label is *accurate*, and demanding endless "engagement" can be a stalling tactic that launders genuinely bad behavior. The concept's defense is narrow — it does not claim labels are always wrong, only that an *unestablished* label (con man, where conviction is inconclusive) used to *avoid* examining evidence is bad method. Mark grounds this in the basic, pre-PhD lesson "about the problem of name calling."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "They want to be able to slap these labels on to immediately discredit and make the opposing side unreasonable ... you don't have to engage with their truth claims."[^2]

> "Throwing around terms like that ... is a powerful social tool for trying to essentially manipulate someone into agreeing with you ... But that's not history. I'm a historian."[^4]
