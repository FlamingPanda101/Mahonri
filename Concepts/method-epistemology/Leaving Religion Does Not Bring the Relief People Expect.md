# Leaving Religion Does Not Bring the Relief People Expect

## The Claim
Many who leave religion expect that stepping away from a hard relationship with faith will make things better — "if I leave, things are going to get better for me." A nine-year longitudinal study of youth ([[Sam Hardy]] and [[Justin Dyer]], 2025) suggests the opposite: those who left religion showed *increased* negative functioning, including a weaker sense of purpose that got worse after leaving. The anticipated emotional improvement "doesn't actually come — at least, not in the way they expect." This is the empirical counter to the deconstruction-as-liberation narrative.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Longitudinal panel, not a snapshot (rated: moderate–strong).** The study began in 2016 with 652 religious 12–14-year-olds from California, Arizona, and Utah and surveyed them every two years for nine years, tracking positive functioning (self-esteem, purpose) and negative functioning (sexual risk-taking, suicidal ideation). Tracking the *same* youth over time is what licenses talking about change after leaving rather than mere correlation.[^1]

2. **Purpose declines after exit (rated: moderate).** Youth who left "were reported to have a less robust sense of purpose than their peers," and "such feelings of futility [were] found to get worse since leaving their faith." The deterioration follows the exit rather than merely preceding it.[^1] *Steelman:* observational panels cannot fully rule out that pre-existing distress both predicted leaving and continued worsening for other reasons; the report does not give effect sizes, and "futility getting worse" may reflect the disruption surrounding a faith transition (social loss, family conflict) as much as the loss of belief itself.

3. **Directly contradicts the leavers' expectation (rated: moderate).** Dyer notes the finding "may be contrary to the thought process that many who leave religion experience" — the assumption that relief follows departure.[^1] This is the empirical analogue of the philosophical warning in [[Where Will You Go - Deconstruction Rarely Leads Somewhere Better]].

4. **Coheres with what follows leaving (rated: moderate).** It fits the broader picture that most who leave do not land in confident secularism (see [[Most Who Leave the LDS Church Do Not Become Atheists]]) and that continued private religiosity still benefits the departed (see [[Religiosity Benefits Mental Health Even After Leaving]]).
