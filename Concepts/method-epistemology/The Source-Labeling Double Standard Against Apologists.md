# The Source-Labeling Double Standard Against Apologists

## The Claim
[[Keystone]] notes an asymmetry in how [[Johnny Harris]] flags his sources: a faith-affirming source gets a caveat about bias, while a critical source is presented without one. When Harris references a Keystone video, the viewer "get[s] the clarification that, while notably from an apologist, this summary is well sourced." But when Harris sources an antagonistic figure like [[John Dehlin]], "there's no warning that the information is notably from a critic."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The two-tier labeling.** Apologist sources are pre-labeled as such (with the partial concession "well sourced"); critic sources are not labeled as critics. The framing primes the viewer to discount the believer and trust the skeptic by default.[^1] *Rating: moderate — a specific, checkable observation about one video's editorial choices; it illustrates rather than proves a systematic practice.*

2. **It exemplifies the broader framing critique.** This is a small instance of the larger "enlightened ex-member" posture in which the believer's perspective is bracketed as biased while the critic's is treated as neutral baseline — see [[The Enlightened Ex-Member Frame Treats Believers as Zoo Animals]]. The label "apologist" functions as a soft disqualifier; "critic" is left unmarked as if it were the default, objective stance.[^1] *Rating: moderate — connects the data point to the episode's central thesis about asymmetric framing.*

3. **The principle: symmetry of disclosure.** The implied fix is even-handedness — either flag the standpoint of *all* interested sources or none. A critic has a thesis to advance just as an apologist does; labeling only one side mislabels which claims are "neutral."[^1] This is the practical edge of [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]]. *Rating: strong as a norm — consistent disclosure is a basic fairness standard in sourcing.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Harris can reasonably reply that "apologist" denotes a *confessional commitment to defend* a conclusion, which is a relevant disclosure, whereas a figure like John Dehlin presents as an investigator or journalist rather than a confessional advocate — so the asymmetry tracks a real difference in self-presentation, not bias. One can also argue that flagging the apologist while crediting the work as "well sourced" is *more* generous, not less. The measured conclusion: Keystone identifies a genuine inconsistency worth noting — critics also argue from a standpoint and deserve the same flag — but whether it is unfair *spin* or a defensible distinction between confessional and non-confessional sources is itself contestable, and the point is minor relative to the episode's substantive corrections.

## Best Quotes
> "When Johnny references one of my videos, we get the clarification that 'while notably from an apologist, this summary is well sourced.' But when he sources an antagonistic source like John Dehlin, there's no warning that the information is notably from a critic. Just pointing that out."[^1]
