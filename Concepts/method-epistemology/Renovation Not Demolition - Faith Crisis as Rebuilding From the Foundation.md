# Renovation Not Demolition - Faith Crisis as Rebuilding From the Foundation

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] reframes the experience usually called a "faith crisis" as a **faith renovation**: a deliberate journey down to the foundations of belief to repair and strengthen them, not tear the building down.[^1] Using the Salt Lake Temple — whose renovation had to reach the literal bedrock so the structure could survive coming earthquakes — as his governing image, he argues that doubt handled rightly is construction, not collapse: "this is a renovation, not a demolition, even if that means some old outdated plumbing needs to be replaced."[^2] The crucial discipline is **caution**, because "tearing down walls can bring the whole thing down — and I mean everything, not just the church."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **It is autobiographical, not abstract.** Hansen declines the label "faith crisis" for his own decade (2008–2018), calling it instead "a significant faith renovation where I had to go down and explore and re-explore the very foundations of my belief system."[^1] The renovation framing is the lesson he draws from having survived where his siblings did not.

2. **The bedrock metaphor sets the method.** As a builder knows, "a crack in the foundation will affect everything else"; throwing "paint over and over on a wall that is built on a cracked foundation does not fix the problem."[^4] So a struggling Saint must go to **Level 1** — the existence of God and the nature of belief itself — rather than obsessing over surface issues like Book of Mormon anachronisms. This is the same foundational ordering as the [[Anatomy of Belief - Three Independent Conversations]].

3. **Demolition is dangerous because the alternative is the abyss.** Hansen warns that "there is a dark hole of nihilistic hell awaiting us at all moments," so "how we go about this exploration and renovation matters."[^3] Pure deconstruction does not stop at the church — it can dissolve meaning itself. See [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]].

4. **The aim is to build, not to tear down.** "I am less interested in hearing what people don't believe in than I am in hearing what they do believe in and why."[^5] Critics "are up to" tearing things down; "building is how we avoid the meaninglessness and nihilism that is spreading like a cancer throughout our society."[^5]

5. **Steelman of a critic.** One might object that "renovation" simply privileges a predetermined conclusion — that an honest inquirer must be willing to demolish if the foundation truly fails. Hansen's reply is procedural: he affirms the building "must be exposed to an earthquake" of the critics' best arguments, but insists it would be "crazy to do that without a fully developed foundation" first.[^6] The order, not the avoidance, of testing is his point.

## Best Quotes
> "Don't seek to simply deconstruct. This is a renovation, not a demolition, even if that means some old outdated plumbing needs to be replaced." [^2]

> "Renovation requires caution. Tearing down walls can bring the whole thing down — and I mean everything, not just the church. There is a dark hole of nihilistic hell awaiting us at all moments, so how we go about this exploration and renovation matters." [^3]

> "Ultimately we are trying to build something. We are exploring reality for what we can believe in." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The renovation image is a sanctified version of Alma's counsel to *nurture* a fragile testimony rather than abandon it at the first difficulty: faith begins as an experiment, is tested, and grows into knowledge if cultivated (see [[Alma 32.27-43]]). Hansen's "go down to the bedrock" is the Restoration's own counsel to build on the sure foundation of Christ ("the rock of our Redeemer") so that the devil's "mighty winds" cannot drag the believer down. It also reframes the popular "stay in the boat" exhortation: one stays not by refusing to look at the storm but by reinforcing the hull first — a mature faith that has wrestled with God and come through stronger.
