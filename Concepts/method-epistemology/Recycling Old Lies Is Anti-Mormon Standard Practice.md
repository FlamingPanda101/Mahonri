# Recycling Old Lies Is Anti-Mormon Standard Practice

## The Claim

A recurring [[Ward Radio]] meta-thesis: many anti-Mormon "gotchas" are **recycled old claims** that have already been answered, kept alive by citing pre-rebuttal secondary sources rather than engaging the newer evidence. [[Cardon Ellis]] half-jokingly calls this "rule number one" of his catalog of anti-Mormon tactics — "the recycling of old lies."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Fanny Alger "affair" quote persists after being corrected — rate: moderate (case study)
Even though the manuscript word was shown to be "scrape," not "affair" (see [[The Oliver Cowdery Affair Letter Actually Says Scrape Not Affair]]), critical sites still quote "affair." [[Don Bradley]] explains the mechanism: they cite *Rough Stone Rolling* and other works published *before* the new manuscript evidence, which lets them "get around having to deal with the new evidence." Whether from ignorance or design, the effect is to present a corrected claim as still standing.[^2]

### 2. Winger's Book of Abraham experts are a century old — rate: strong (sourcing audit)
Every Egyptologist [[Mike Winger]] quotes against the Book of Abraham traces to a single 1912 questionnaire circulated by an Episcopal bishop; the scholars cited lived and died in the early 1900s. The "irrefutable scholarship" is over a hundred years old and pre-dates the relevant LDS responses. See [[Wingers Egyptologists All Trace to a 1912 Episcopal Questionnaire]].[^3]

### 3. The "green book" photocopy phenomenon — rate: weak/illustrative
The hosts describe encountering anti-Mormon material that amounts to photocopies of decades-old pamphlets (a 1970s "green book" of recycled gotcha quotes), repackaged and presented as fresh — a vivid, if anecdotal, image of the recycling pattern.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

"It's old" is not a refutation, and the pattern cuts both ways. (a) An argument's *age* says nothing about its *truth*; if the 1912 Egyptologists were right that the facsimiles are ordinary funerary art, repeating that in 2020 is sound, not "recycling a lie." The LDS side must show the old claims are *wrong*, not merely *old* — and for the facsimiles the mainstream Egyptological reading has in fact been *reaffirmed*, not overturned, by later scholars. (b) Apologetics likewise recycles: the same favored rebuttals, talking points, and quotes circulate through faithful media for decades, so "recycling" is a property of *all* popular polemics, not a distinctively anti-Mormon vice. (c) Citing a synthesis like *Rough Stone Rolling* is normal scholarly practice; a critic need not track every micro-update to a manuscript reading to make a fair argument. So the legitimate core is narrow: *specific* corrected claims (the "affair" misquote) should not be repeated as if uncorrected, and sourcing should be current — a fair demand that applies symmetrically to apologists and critics alike.
