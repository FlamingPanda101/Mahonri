# Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge

## The Point

[[Blake Ostler]] meets the religious-pluralism objection — *others, equally intelligent and spiritually sensitive, have confirming spiritual experiences in rival traditions, so LDS experiences are called into question* — by adopting **religious inclusivism** rather than exclusivism or pluralism. On the LDS view, faith in Christ is a "universally and eternally valid principle," so non-LDS spiritual experiences are *expected* and do not undercut the LDS tradition; they represent genuine light God adapts to each culture. [^1]

## Argument

1. **The objection has real force.** It is *true* that people at least as intelligent and spiritually sensitive as Latter-day Saints report confirming spiritual experiences in their own traditions. Ostler explicitly concedes "there's something that is really right about this objection." [^1]
2. **Three available stances.** *Exclusivism* = only we have a direct conduit to God and the truth. *Pluralism* = everyone has equal claim to the truth. *Inclusivism* = others genuinely receive light and truth, integrated within a larger framework that the Restoration completes. Ostler rejects the first two and adopts the third. [^2]
3. **Inclusivism is already built into LDS belief.** It is *part of the tradition* that those outside Mormonism receive spiritual experiences — e.g., [[Wilford Woodruff]] had visions and dreams, and a friend who prophesied the Restoration, before becoming LDS. So an evangelical's experience that "Jesus is the Christ" does not call the LDS faith into question at all. [^2]
4. **Universal scope of faith and light.** "Every person will receive that degree of light that they are willing to receive." Faith in Christ is as valid for Augustine ([[Augustine]]) as for a modern prophet, as operative for Martin Luther as for Joseph Smith. Even one "born in the deepest jungles" who never hears the gospel has full purpose to learn from life and thereby gain light. [^3]
5. **Handling the hard cases.** A vision of the Virgin Mary might seem to conflict (LDS don't expect such appearances); Ostler's inclusivist move is to "look beyond what divides us" and ask *what the experience taught* the person, since "God will adapt his message to any culture... to increase the light of a person." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "LDS adopt what I call religious inclusivism — that is, the experiences... of evangelicals that Jesus is the Christ don't call into question the LDS faith at all." [^2]

> "Every person will receive that degree of light that they are willing to receive, and every degree of light represents salvation for them." [^3]

> "Faith in Christ is as valid a principle for Augustine as it is for President Hinckley, and as operative in the day of Martin Luther as it was in the day of Joseph Smith." [^3]
