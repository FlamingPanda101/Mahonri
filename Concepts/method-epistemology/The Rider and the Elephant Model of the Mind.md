# The Rider and the Elephant Model of the Mind

## The Point

[[Jonathan Haidt]]'s signature metaphor: the human mind is divided like a small **rider** sitting atop a large **elephant**. The rider is conscious, verbal reasoning — the part that uses logic and that you are aware of. The elephant is everything else: the automatic processes, "the 99% of what's going on in your mind that you're not aware of." Reason is not in charge; it sits atop a far more powerful intuitive system. [^1]

## Argument

1. **The mind is divided.** Haidt offers the rider/elephant as the one metaphor he expects to leave behind: "the mind is divided ... like a rider on an elephant." The rider "represents your conscious verbal reasoning ... the stuff that uses logic," and "everything else is the elephant — it's the automatic processes." [^1]
2. **The split is radically lopsided.** The elephant is "the 99% of what's going on in your mind that you're not aware of." Conscious reasoning is the rare exception, not the rule, in mental life. [^1]
3. **Provenance.** Haidt developed the metaphor in his book *The Happiness Hypothesis*, and it was "made famous not by me but by Chip and Dan Heath in their wonderful book *Switch*." [^2]
4. **Consequence for action.** Because the elephant "is a lot stronger than the rider," any account of how minds actually change must reckon with the elephant first — it sets up [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]] and ultimately [[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think]]. [^3]
