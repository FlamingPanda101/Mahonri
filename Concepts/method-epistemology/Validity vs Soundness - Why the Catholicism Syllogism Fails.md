# Validity vs Soundness — Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
In his debate with Jacob Hansen, [[Trent Horn]] offered the syllogism: **(P1)** if Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired; **(P2)** Catholicism is true; **(C)** therefore the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired — adding that if Hansen "can't disprove Catholicism," Catholics should reject the Book of Mormon.[^1] [[Hayden Carroll]] uses this as the course's central example of a **valid but unsound** argument: the logic is fine, but a premise that is a faith claim cannot ground a logical conclusion.[^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The validity / soundness distinction is correctly stated — rate: strong
Carroll defines the terms accurately: an argument is **valid** when the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises (a property of *structure*), and **sound** when it is valid *and* the premises are actually true.[^3] His illustrations are textbook-correct: "All mammals are animals; all dogs are mammals; therefore all dogs are animals" (valid and sound) versus "All birds are blue; all robins are birds; therefore all robins are blue" (valid, unsound).[^4] This is standard first-order logic and is taught correctly.

### 2. The contested move: "unproven" vs. "false" — rate: moderate, slightly overstated
Carroll's precise charge is that P2 ("Catholicism is true") is **unproven**, and that "if the premises are not proven, it cannot be sound."[^5] In the exchange clip, Trent Horn pushes back that "to say an argument is unsound means the premises are false" — not merely unproven.[^6] **Trent is technically right on the textbook definition:** soundness requires the premises to *be* true, not to be *proven* true to the listener. An argument with a true-but-unprovable premise is still sound; what it fails is *dialectical* — it cannot rationally compel an interlocutor who doesn't share the premise. Carroll is conflating *soundness* (a property of the argument) with *cogency/justification* (a property of the case made to an audience). His underlying point — a faith claim can't be wielded as a probative premise against someone who rejects it — is correct, but his terminology overreaches. **Steelman of Trent:** Horn likely intended the syllogism not as a standalone proof but as a *conditional* — "if you grant Catholicism, the rest follows" — which is a legitimate move in a multi-front debate, even if out of scope for a Book-of-Mormon resolution.

### 3. The decisive objection is scope + reversibility, not soundness — rate: strong
Carroll's strongest points are actually adjacent to the logic: (a) the argument is **out of scope** for a debate on "Is the Book of Mormon scripture?";[^7] and (b) it is **reversible** — "If the Book of Mormon is true, Catholicism is false; the Book of Mormon is true; therefore Catholicism is false" has identical form and Trent would reject it.[^8] Reversibility (a principle Carroll credits to [[Frank Turek]]) is the real demonstration that the premise is doing illegitimate work. See [[The Reversibility Test for Bad Arguments]].

## Best Quotes
> "If the first two claims are true, then it provides a logical conclusion, a necessary conclusion."[^3]

> "All birds are blue. All robins are birds. Therefore all robins are blue. Is that valid? Yes... but is it true? It's not sound."[^4]

> "It's not sound because the second premise cannot be proven. So it can never be a sound syllogism."[^5]

> "Don't ever present a syllogism with a faith-based claim. Logic is concerned about proofs, not about faith-based claims."[^9]
