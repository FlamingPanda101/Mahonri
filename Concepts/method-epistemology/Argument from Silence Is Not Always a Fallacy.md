# Argument from Silence Is Not Always a Fallacy

## The Objection Rebutted

Apologists object that the Bethlehem birth's absence from [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] and [[John Mark|Mark]] is "the fallacy of the argument from silence" — just because they didn't mention it doesn't mean it didn't happen.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. McClellan concedes this is the strongest objection

McClellan acknowledges: "Of all the stuff we're going to go through, this probably is like the least illegitimate. Because there is a degree to which it is an argument from silence." He gives this one a rare "touché."[^2]

### 2. But an argument from silence is not automatically a fallacy

"An argument from silence is not a fallacy if we should expect to see it — if they should have mentioned it." The question is whether the silence is significant given what the authors were trying to accomplish.[^3]

### 3. The gospel authors are a different case from Paul

For [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]], the silence is weaker evidence — "Paul's not really about his temporal life. We have a passing reference to him being born of a woman, but that's serving a more theological point." Paul never claims to have met [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] and his letters aren't biographical.[^4]

But for the gospel authors, if we accept traditional authorship, "we would be assuming that the authors had actually met and hung out with Jesus." [[John Mark|Mark]] is supposed to have been [[Peter]]'s interpreter. [[Luke the Evangelist|Luke]] claims to have done careful investigation. "To a degree, yeah, we would expect them to have" mentioned Bethlehem — especially Mark, if it were known.[^5]

### 4. The Bethlehem claim is supported by other evidence, not silence alone

McClellan's argument does not rest on silence alone: "We've talked about other reasons to think this is a secondary edition as well." The silence is one piece of a cumulative case — including the contradictions between Matthew and Luke, the historically impossible census, and the pattern of Matthew constructing prophecy fulfillments. "If we're just isolating that one point, I would say to some degree touché. But there are a lot of other things that contribute to that conclusion."[^6]

## Best Quote

> "An argument from silence is not a fallacy if we should expect to see it, if they should have mentioned it." — [[Dan McClellan]][^7]
