# The Reversibility Test for Bad Arguments

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] teaches a quick diagnostic he attributes to evangelical apologist [[Frank Turek]] ("Frank Turk" in the audio): **if a principle can be flipped back on the person using it, you know it's not a good principle.**[^1] Applied to [[Trent Horn]]'s syllogism, the counter is symmetrical: "If the Book of Mormon is true, Catholicism is false; the Book of Mormon is true; therefore Catholicism is false." Trent would never accept it — which exposes that the original premise was an unjustified faith claim, not a probative fact.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Reversibility is a recognized informal-logic heuristic — rate: strong
A symmetric counter-argument is the standard way to show that a premise is *question-begging* or merely asserts the speaker's own position. If two arguments share identical form but yield contradictory conclusions, the form cannot be what's driving the conclusion — the conclusion is being imported through an unargued premise. This is sound reasoning and Carroll deploys it correctly against Trent. See [[Validity vs Soundness - Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails]]. **Steelman of the opponent:** reversibility only defeats arguments whose premises are *symmetrically unsupported*. If one side's premise is independently evidenced and the other's is not, the "flip" is not really symmetric — so reversibility is a test for unsupported premises, not a universal solvent. Carroll does not flag this limit, which slightly overstates the tool.

### 2. The attribution and a related cousin (the "Tu quoque" risk) — rate: caution
Carroll credits [[Frank Turek]] with the principle and notes he "respects him, but he has some issues" and "doesn't understand Mormonism at all."[^1] Worth noting: a careless reversibility move can collapse into a *tu quoque* ("you do it too") fallacy, which does not establish that *either* side is right — only that both are unjustified. Carroll's use stays on the right side of this line because his goal is precisely to show *mutual* lack of justification and then move the debate to *who can actually justify their standard* (the Book of Mormon for prophets). See [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic]].

### 3. The "Zeus" and "king of the world" extensions — rate: illustrative, valid
Carroll reinforces the test with deliberately absurd valid-but-unsound syllogisms: "If Zeus is king of the gods, Christianity is false; Zeus is king of the gods; therefore Christianity is false," and "The king of the world controls everyone; God chose me as king of the world; therefore I control everyone."[^3] These show that *any* unproven premise can manufacture a valid conclusion — which is the whole point: validity is cheap, soundness/justification is what matters.

## Best Quotes
> "He says if a principle can be flipped on itself, then you know it's not a good principle. So here is the counter that we will give to Trent."[^1]

> "The first way you know that this is a horrible argument is if it can be flipped on its head."[^4]

> "You can make as many valid arguments as you want, but nobody cares until they are sound."[^5]
