# Folk Magic Claims About the Smith Family

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics allege the Smith family engaged in an elaborate world of folk magic: drawing magic circles, animal sacrifice (a dog, pig, or sheep), a necklace charm, a ritual dagger, necromancy, astrology, divining rods, and seer stones.[^1] The objection: this proves the Smiths were occultists, discrediting Joseph's prophetic claims.

## The Rebuttal

1. **Sort the catalog by evidence (core move).** Not all claims are equal. Applying basic source criticism — corroboration, bias, contemporaneity, eyewitness vs. nth-hand — only **three** survive as credibly attested: **seer stones, divining rods, and treasure seeking**, each with multiple attestation from both sides of the bias line and contemporaneous sources.[^2] *Rating: strong — disciplined triage rather than blanket denial or blanket acceptance.*

2. **The sensational items lack good evidence.** Magic circles are "not from friendly sources" and "not from contemporaneous sources"; animal sacrifice (dog/pig/sheep) is similarly unsupported. "Everything else may or may not have happened. We don't know because we don't have good evidence for it."[^3] *Rating: strong — honest agnosticism, not apologetic erasure.*

3. **The source of most claims is hostile and late.** The lurid items come largely from the [[Philastus Hurlbut|Hurlbut]] statements (1833–34), collected by a paid agent "hired to go get the dirt" — see [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem]].[^4] *Rating: strong.*

4. **Uncritical secondary repetition (method critique).** Authors like Richard Van Wagoner reproduce "the laundry list of all of the possible things" [[D. Michael Quinn]] compiled "with absolutely no source criticism," presenting speculation as established fact.[^5] *Rating: strong — names the historiographical failure mode.*

5. **Steelman.** A critic can fairly say the believing side conveniently keeps exactly the three items doctrinally entangled with the Restoration (seer stones, rods) while dismissing the rest as "late and biased," which could be motivated reasoning. Mark's reply is methodological consistency: he applies the *same* evidentiary test to all items and explicitly says "I'm sure some of those accusations are garbage, but which ones? We just don't know" — refusing to claim the unsupported items are *false*, only unproven.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "Magic circles, actually we don't have that from friendly sources ... we don't have that from contemporaneous sources."[^3]

> "In terms of what we actually have good evidence for: seer stones, divining rods, and treasure seeking ... Everything else may or may not have happened. We don't know."[^2]

> "I'm sure some of those accusations are garbage, but like which ones? ... We just don't know."[^6]
