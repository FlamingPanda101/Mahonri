# Deduction Is Top-Down and Induction Is Bottom-Up Reasoning

## The Point
[[Philosophy Tube]] distinguishes the two basic forms of reasoning. **Deduction** is "the top-down approach": it "takes premises and reduces them down to conclusions," and the conclusion is *logically certain* — "given the premises the conclusion must be true." **Induction** is "the bottom-up approach": it "takes premises and draws probable conclusions based on what those premises provide evidence for," and its conclusion is "not certain." [^1][^2] Much of science is built on induction, and even though its conclusions are never certain, "it is a fantastic guide to truth." [^3]

## Argument
1. There are, broadly, two sorts of reasoning: deduction and induction. [^1]
2. **Deduction (top-down):** premises are reduced to a conclusion that follows necessarily. The standard syllogism — "All men are mortal. Earl Grey is a man. Therefore Earl Grey is mortal" — yields a conclusion of which "we can be logically certain": if the premises are true, the conclusion *must* be true. [^1]
3. **Induction (bottom-up):** premises supply evidence that makes a conclusion *probable* but not guaranteed. From "every British person you have ever met has been polite" one infers "the next British person you meet will be polite" — yet "it is possible that the next British person you meet will be a total cad." [^2]
4. Inductive support is graded by accumulated evidence: a cannonball observed to fall 99 times warrants being "fairly but not completely certain that it will fall the 100th time." The conclusion's probability tracks the evidence without ever reaching deductive certainty. [^3]
5. Steelman of induction's worth: the lack of certainty is not a defeater. "Much of science is built on inductive reasoning despite the fact that its conclusions are uncertain. It is a fantastic guide to truth." The right standard for induction is reliability/high probability, not the certainty proper to deduction. [^3]
6. Upshot for epistemology: knowledge claims about the empirical world are characteristically inductive, hence probabilistic rather than certain — connecting to the broader point that knowing is high confidence, not 100% proof. See [[Epistemology Is the Account You Give for How You Know]].

## Best Quotes
> "Generally speaking there are two sorts of reasoning; deduction and induction. Deduction is sometimes called the top-down approach it takes premises and reduces them down to conclusions." [^1]

> "Note we can be logically certain of the conclusion. Given the premises the conclusion must be true." [^1]

> "Inductive reasoning is sometimes called the bottom-up approach. It takes premises and draws probable conclusions based on what those premises provide evidence for. … Note that the conclusion of an inductive argument is not certain." [^2]

> "Much of science is built on inductive reasoning despite the fact that its conclusions are uncertain. It is a fantastic guide to truth." [^3]
