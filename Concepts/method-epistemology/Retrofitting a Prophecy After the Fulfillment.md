# Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment

## The Claim (Objection)
"Retrofitting" (also called *vaticinium ex eventu*, prophecy after the event) is the process by which a vague or non-existent original statement is rewritten, made specific, or backdated once the outcome is known, so that it reads as a precise prediction. The objection holds that several Joseph Smith prophecies show signs of this: the predictive detail is present only in versions written after the fulfillment, while earlier records are either silent or vaguer.[^1]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **A documented textual gap in the Douglas prophecy.** The [[Stephen A. Douglas Presidency Prophecy]] as printed in the *History of the Church* cites William Clayton's journal, "however, when researchers have examined the extant William Clayton journals, the original entry for the 18th of May 1843 does not include the expanded text." The fuller prophecy appears only in the 1856 *Deseret News* printing. This is a textbook case of detail appearing in the later, not the earlier, record. (Evidence rating: strong, and partially mitigated — the expanded text still predates Douglas's 1860 candidacy.)[^2]
2. **The host names the mechanism explicitly and repeatedly.** He warns that a believer might be tempted to think "this is just retrofitted as well," and that a critic could say accounts are "either making it up after the fact and backdating it back to a supposed time when Joseph said it, or he said something really vague and they're trying to make it a really specific prophecy."[^3] (Evidence rating: strong as a framing; the host applies it as a live possibility throughout.)
3. **The 1834 vs Nauvoo dating wedge.** For the Rocky Mountains prophecy, the host finds a good case that Joseph said *something* about going west during the Nauvoo period, "whether or not that was said in 1834 is where I think maybe that could have been added in to the prophecy." Here the retrofit is not the whole prophecy but the early date attached to it. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^4]
4. **Steelman of the believing view.** Not every later, fuller account is a fabrication. Editors of the *History of the Church* worked from multiple sources, some now lost; a fuller printed version can reflect a genuine but separately preserved tradition rather than invention. The host's own concession that the Douglas text predates the 1860 election shows that "later than the journal" does not always mean "later than the fulfillment." The charitable reading is embellishment of a real kernel, not manufacture from nothing.[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Perhaps something like this was said by Joseph, but you could also argue this is just retrofitted as well."[^3]

> "Could this be just many decades after the fact people attributing this back, retrofitting it back to Joseph Smith giving this fulfilled prophecy?"[^4]

> "He said something really vague and they're trying to make it a really specific prophecy."[^5]
