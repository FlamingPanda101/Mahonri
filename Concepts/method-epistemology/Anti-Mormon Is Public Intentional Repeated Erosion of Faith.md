#type/claim

# Anti-Mormon Is Public Intentional Repeated Erosion of Faith

## The Claim
Pressed by [[Steven Pynakker]] to define his terms, [[Jacob Hansen]] offers a content-neutral, conduct-based criterion: an "anti-Mormon" is someone engaged in **public, intentional, repeated erosion of faith in the church's fundamental truth claims**. Disbelief alone does not qualify; neither does publicly announcing one's disagreement; the label attaches only when all three conditions — public, intentional, sustained — converge on the church's *fundamental* claims. This is a deliberately narrower instrument than the common usage that brands any critic "anti," and a different cut than [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea|Beshears' person/idea line]]: Hansen targets a *pattern of activity against core claims* rather than the slide into personal insult. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Unbelief and even open disagreement are explicitly excluded.** Hansen tells Pynakker directly: "It isn't enough just that you don't believe in the church's claims. It isn't even enough that you let people know that you're against the church's beliefs... I don't think that you're an anti-Mormon because you're not engaging in intentional continuous public attacks on the fundamental claims of the church." [^1] *Rating: strong;* by exempting a sincere evangelical interlocutor up front, the definition pre-empts the charge that "anti-Mormon" is just a slur for anyone who disagrees.

2. **The three conjuncts are load-bearing.** Hansen repeats the formula as a fixed triad — "public intentional and repeated erosion of faith in the church's fundamental claims" — when defining it, when applying it to [[John Dehlin]], and when answering an audience super-chat about who gets to define the term. [^2] *Rating: strong as a usable diagnostic;* requiring all three conjuncts filters out one-off remarks, private doubt, and honest "here's why I left" testimony.

3. **A hierarchy of anti-Mormonism, with the "lowest kind" being purely negative.** Hansen says he "sort of gets" evangelical pastors like Mark Driscoll because "at least they believe something and they're trying to propose an alternative worldview." "The lowest kind of anti-Mormonism to me is the kind that just attacks the church and offers nothing." [^3] He argues most such attacks target "general Christian ideas" rather than anything specific to Mormonism, so the honest label would be "anti-Christian" or anti–"Western Judeo-Christian values." [^3] *Rating: moderate;* the ranking is rhetorically effective and the "offers nothing" critique echoes [[Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion]], but the claim that the motive is "more political than anything" is an inference about hearts that outruns the conduct-only criterion he otherwise insists on.

4. **"Who is 'we'?" — nobody owns the word.** Responding to a super-chat ("We define what anti-Mormon is, not non-members"), Hansen disclaims any gatekeeping authority: "I don't care who — nobody has the authority. I just want to know what do you mean when I talk about someone being an anti-Mormon," then restates the triad. [^4] *Rating: strong;* grounding the term in a stipulated, stated definition rather than tribal authority is what lets it function in cross-camp dialogue.

**Steelman of the critic.** A critic can answer that "erosion of faith in fundamental claims" is exactly what *honest disagreement about those claims looks like from the inside* — that the criterion smuggles the church's truth into the definition, so that anyone who effectively argues the church is false is convicted of "erosion" no matter how fair their methods. Kolby Reddish presses precisely this in the chat: "His definition of anti-Mormon doesn't matter if the criticism is accurate." [^5] Hansen's reply is that accurate criticism has *venues* (priesthood channels, sustaining; see [[Reform Through Priesthood Channels Not Public Attack]]) and that he himself criticizes the church publicly on specifics — so the target is not the truth of a claim but the sustained public *project* of dismantling faith in the core. Whether "fundamental claims" can be specified without question-begging is the live weak point.
