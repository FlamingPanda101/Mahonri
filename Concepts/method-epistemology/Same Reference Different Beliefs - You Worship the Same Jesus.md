# Same Reference, Different Beliefs — You Worship the Same Jesus

#type/claim

## The Claim

The standard anti-Mormon move — voiced by [[Mark Driscoll]] and sharpened by [[Gavin Ortlund]] — is that "it's about the **object of your worship**": if you misidentify who Jesus is, you are worshipping a *different* Jesus, the way a man who thinks he is dating Sally is actually with Jane, "even if they were identical twins."[^1] [[Hayden Carroll]] answers that this confuses **reference** with **belief**. The being you are *pointing at* (the historical Jesus of Nazareth) can be one and the same even when two people hold contradictory descriptions of him; differing theology of Christ's relation to the Father does not by itself make it a different referent, and therefore does not by itself make it a false god.[^2]

Carroll's positive standard: "I worship the God the [person] who was Jesus of Nazareth. **Whoever that person is, that's who I'm worshipping.**"[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The atheist still refers to the same Jesus (the reference horn)
Carroll: "even an atheist, if he's talking about Jesus, you don't say you're talking about a different Jesus. No — he's talking about the guy in the New Testament who rose… he believes different things about Jesus, but when he talks about Jesus he's **referring to the same being**."[^3] Reference is fixed by the New-Testament person both parties name; false beliefs *about* that person do not relocate the reference to a new entity. This is the same logic Catholics already accept — see [[Common Reference - Worshipping Adam as God]].

### 2. The Sally's-stepdad analogy (the "wrong description" horn)
Against Ortlund's own Sally/Jane illustration, Carroll inverts it: the disagreement is *not* a Sally-vs-Jane case (two different women) but a **same-woman, mistaken-relation** case — "you think Sally's father is actually her stepdad; I believe it's her biological father."[^4] He pushes the consequence: does wrongly thinking Sally's dad is her stepdad "profoundly affect my relationship with Sally"? "No… I can have a full-blooded relationship with" her.[^4] So a mistaken view of how the Son relates to the Father (separate being vs. same being) is a wrong *description* of the same Christ, not worship of an idol.

### 3. The standard collapses on everyone else
If wrong descriptions made for a different Jesus, then [[Justin Martyr]] and [[Clement of Rome]] — both subordinationists who denied the Son was the same *being* as the Father — worshipped "a different Jesus," as do the Eastern Orthodox who deny the Son is fundamental.[^5] See [[Worshiping the Right Jesus - Created vs Uncreated]] and [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]]. The critic must either exclude the early church or admit that same-reference-with-wrong-belief is still the *same* worship.

### Steelman (Driscoll / Ortlund's side)
The reference move has a real limit, which Carroll concedes: if "I said that this phone is Jesus Christ" and worshipped it, faith in *that* is not faith in Christ — there are boundaries.[^6] Ortlund presses that a false description can corrupt the *relationship itself*: a wrong Christ yields "a different gospel" (works-righteousness, temple ordinances) — "Jesus's cross and blood was not enough for you."[^7] Carroll's reply is that the boundary must be "at the minimum what's explicitly taught in the text," and the metaphysics of consubstantiality is not in the text — leaving the burden on the critic to show *which* New-Testament teaching about Jesus the Latter-day Saint actually denies.[^6]

## Best Quotes
- "Whoever that person is, that's who I'm worshipping." [^2]
- "Even an atheist, if he's talking about Jesus… he's referring to the same being. He just has different beliefs about him." [^3]
- "It's okay — you think Sally's father is actually her stepdad; I believe it's her biological father. That's the sort of difference we're dealing with here." [^4]
