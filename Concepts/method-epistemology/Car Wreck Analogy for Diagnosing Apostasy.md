# Car Wreck Analogy for Diagnosing Apostasy

## The Claim
The wreckage of early Christian history — doctrinal changes, corruption, centuries of schism and religious strife — constitutes good *evidence* that an apostasy occurred, but is not itself the *cause* of the apostasy. The proper method is to argue from evidences (the debris), not from a confident account of causes.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Noel Reynolds quotation (rating: the source of the analogy)
Day reads [[Noel Reynolds]] (*Early Christians in Disarray*) at length: approaching a car wreck, you conclude from "the twisted metal frame, the shattered glass, the injured and dead bodies that an accident occurred. But you would not say that the broken and scattered parts caused the accident. They are only its results. Likewise, all the doctrinal changes... may constitute good evidence that an apostasy occurred, but may not be the cause of that apostasy." [^1]

### 2. It still drives the car (the "is it a car?" extension)
Day extends the metaphor: a wrecked car is still a car — the stereo works, the tires look fine — but "would you take a date on it?" Christianity post-apostasy is recognizable and even beautiful, but damaged. This frames his refusal to denigrate other faiths while still arguing corruption occurred. [^2]

### 3. Methodological humility (rating: strength of the approach)
Because Day argues from evidences rather than causes, he repeatedly defers settling disputed questions ("I'm not here to settle it for you"), treating the debris as "low-hanging fruit" that most observers — LDS or not — can agree is present. [^3]

### 4. Steelman of the opposing view
A critic notes the analogy assumes the "accident" — that the original was a pristine vehicle later wrecked. If, as mainstream scholarship and even Day's own data suggest, early Christianity was *never* a single unified vehicle but a multiplicity from the start ([[Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree]]), then "wreck" presupposes a prelapsarian unity that may never have existed. Day partly concedes this, rejecting the "one perfect mirror that shattered" model that LDS missionaries often use.

## Best Quotes

> "You can conclude from the debris, the twisted metal frame, the shattered glass... that an accident occurred. But you would not say that the broken and scattered parts caused the accident. They are only its results." [^1]

> "Is it a car? Yeah, it's a car. And the stereo probably works... but would you take a date on it?" [^2]

> "To me the evidences is low-hanging fruit." [^3]
