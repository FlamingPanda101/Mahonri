# Mistaking Tradition for the Gospel Sets Up Brittle Faith

## The Point
Jeff Strong argues that a culture which over-emphasizes refuge and unblemished perfection trains members to sink their roots into *tradition* rather than into the *gospel of Jesus Christ* — and that this is a recipe for a brittle faith that shatters under pressure. In his "shallow soil full of stones" tension (sanctuary vs. growth), stones are the traditions that "prevent your roots from growing deep into the soil of the gospel." When heat or a storm comes, "what you think is the gospel, which is really more tradition, fails you, and then you go, 'Oh my gosh, the gospel's not true.'" But "the traditions weren't true. The gospel's still true" — the person simply "based [their] faith more on the traditions" because "they're constantly reinforced in the culture."[^1] His counsel is not to discard traditions but never to "mistake them for the gospel of Jesus Christ."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Cultural perfectionism is a mistranslation.** Strong locates a key cultural distortion in Matthew 5:48 ("Be ye therefore perfect"), which the culture reads as "unblemished... without spot." But the original word "doesn't mean unblemished. It means whole or complete," and wholeness *requires* "blemishes, scars, bumps, bruises, and the learning that goes with that."[^3] The corollary: "the atonement isn't plan B... It's plan A. You go live life. You make mistakes... and it's through repentance that you can learn and grow."[^4] (Cf. [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]].)
2. **Sanctuary-only culture stunts growth.** The church and gospel rightly offer refuge, "but if you stop at that, you don't get to what God really intends, which is transformation or transcendence or growth." A sanctuary mindset teaches people "to hide in the sanctuary," so they "can't grow" and feel un-nourished.[^5] Strong uses the adage "a ship is safe in harbor, but that's not what ships are for": a sanctuary culture "doesn't prepare you for real life."[^6]
3. **Roots in tradition fail under storm.** Because traditions are "constantly reinforced in the culture," people unknowingly build their testimony on them; when the tradition is challenged or proves false, they mistake its collapse for the collapse of the gospel itself.[^1] This is the same failure mode as an [[Inverted Testimony - Why Faith Built Top-Down Collapses|inverted testimony]] built top-down on fragile secondary claims rather than on a core spiritual foundation.
4. **The fix is depth, not demolition.** The solution is to "make more space for myself and others to transform, to grow, to transcend, to make mistakes and come back," while still loving and revering traditions — just refusing to let talk of tradition crowd out the invitation to "sink their roots" into the gospel.[^2] *Steelman:* the line between "tradition" and "gospel" is itself contested — a critic may say Strong reclassifies whatever fails as "mere tradition" to immunize the gospel from disconfirmation. Strong's safeguard is positive, not merely defensive: he names a substantive gospel core (becoming "a new creature in Christ," "a mighty change in your heart") against which traditions can be measured.

## Best Quotes
> "What you think is the gospel, which is really more tradition, fails you, and then you go, 'Oh my gosh, the gospel's not true.' ... The traditions weren't true. The gospel's still true, but you might have based your faith more on the traditions." [^1]

> "If you go back and look at the original word that was translated into perfect, it doesn't mean unblemished. It means whole or complete. And the idea is you have to have blemishes, scars, bumps, bruises, and the learning that goes with that in order to be whole and complete." [^3]

> "Never mistake them for the gospel of Jesus Christ." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Strong's "stones in the soil" reads as a commentary on the parable of the sower: seed on stony ground springs up fast but, "because it had no deepness of earth" and "no root," withers when "the sun was up" (Matt. 13:5–6, 20–21). His point is that the stones are often beloved *cultural* expectations, not the gospel itself. The rehabilitation of "perfect" as *teleios* — "whole, complete" rather than "flawless" — coheres with the Restoration's plan of growth through agency and the Atonement, in which mortality is *meant* to include mistakes ("Adam fell that men might be," 2 Ne. 2:25) and repentance is the ordinary path of progression, not an emergency fallback. Reframing crisis as the failure of tradition rather than of the gospel is precisely the move that turns a would-be demolition into a [[Renovation Not Demolition - Faith Crisis as Rebuilding From the Foundation|renovation]] — clearing out the stones so roots can finally reach the living soil.
