# Spiritual Confirmation Not Data Is Why People Keep Reading Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[The Bald Coder]] closes his data analysis by undercutting its own importance: no technology, however sophisticated, can settle whether scripture is true, because that is not the kind of question data answers. People keep reading the Bible — and the Book of Mormon — "not because of scientific discovery or a data analysis," but because "they feel good when they read it and they have spiritual experiences." His final invitation is experiential, echoing Moroni's promise: read it and "see if you feel good inside."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Truth here is in-principle data-resistant.** "The reason there's so much controversy around this book is because you cannot definitively say whether or not this is true regardless of what technology you're using. And the same can be said for the Bible."[^2] The claim is epistemic, not merely practical: scriptural truth-claims (a prophet, a resurrection, an ancient American Christianity) are not the sort of thing a sentiment score adjudicates. *(Rating: assertion — a reasonable epistemological observation that the metrics measure textual features, not historical or supernatural facts.)*
2. **The actual ground of scripture's hold is experiential.** "At the end of the day, the reason that people keep reading the Bible is... because people feel good when they read it and they have spiritual experiences. And the same thing can be said for the Book of Mormon."[^1] *(Rating: primary — the source's own thesis statement.)*
3. **The invitation is to test experientially.** "If you're curious, maybe try to read it and see if you feel good inside. If you do, great. If not, then don't worry about it."[^1] This mirrors the Book of Mormon's own epistemology (Moroni 10:3–5: ask God, and by the Spirit "ye may know the truth of all things") — an experiential test the creator commends over his own statistics. *(Rating: assertion — coheres with the standard LDS "moroni's promise" epistemology.)*

**Steelman of the skeptical view.** A critic replies that "feeling good when you read it" is a notoriously unreliable truth-detector: people have warm, life-orienting experiences reading the Quran, the Gita, the Stoics, and good novels, so the feeling cannot discriminate which (if any) scripture is *true*; this is the "elevation emotion / burning-bosom" objection. The LDS rejoinder — developed across the vault's epistemology notes — is that the spiritual witness is not bare emotion but a distinct, repeatable, content-bearing form of knowing, and that *every* worldview ultimately rests on some non-inferential starting point (sense perception, rational intuition) that cannot be externally validated. The creator's modest framing ("if not, don't worry about it") sidesteps the strong version of the claim and offers only an invitation to test, which is harder to fault.

## LDS Relevance
This is the proper epistemic home for *all* of the vault's evidential apologetics: intellectual arguments (data analyses, stylometry, archaeology, the witnesses) can remove obstacles and corroborate, but on the LDS model they are not the foundation — the spiritual witness is. [[Brian Hales]] makes the identical move from a very different starting point in the polygamy interview, urging strugglers back to the Book of Mormon as a "tangible miracle" to be confirmed by *spiritual* witness, since "an intellectual witness... won't sustain you." That convergence — a data scientist and a documentary historian both subordinating their evidence to spiritual confirmation — is itself notable. Compare [[A Data Analysis of Scripture Is Only as Objective as Its Model]].
