# Wise as Serpents and Harmless as Doves — Peacemaker Not Pushover

#type/claim

## The Claim

The right posture for online apologetic conflict is to be, in Jesus' phrase, **"wise as serpents and harmless as doves" (Matthew 10:16)** — both halves at once. **Brad Whipple** argues that this means being a *peacemaker* whose aim is to *end* a conflict, not a doormat who ignores it nor a "punisher" who inflames it: "I'm not saying we be pushovers. I am saying that we learn how to better stand for the faith and do it in a way that we recognize other people as children of God — not because we're not willing to fight, but because fighting them isn't effective."[^1] The disagreement on the panel is real and instructive: **Cardon Ellis** presses the corrective that Latter-day Saints are naively slow to admit that some critics simply *hate* them, and that soft rhetoric can give "a false sense of security" when online anti-Mormonism has already produced arson.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. Both halves are load-bearing
Brad's core point is that people drop one half of the verse: "a lot of times people will be like, 'we need to be wise as serpents' and just forget about the harmless as doves part — or 'we should be harmless as doves' and think that means they won't get either."[^3] Effective apologetics requires the serpent (stand firm, correct error) *and* the dove (no contempt, treat the critic as a child of God). See [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]].

### 2. The pragmatic argument: contempt backfires
Why not "go for the throat"? Because anger produces more contention, and when a critic's followers come to watch, "they see, oh, these angry Mormons fit exactly what they told me they'd be like."[^4] Demonizing the demonizer "might serve our own pride, but… I don't think it serves Christ." The dove is not weakness; it denies the critic the caricature he needs.

### 3. The Captain Moroni model — make peace by ending conflict
Asked whether one can "make peace by fighting," Brad answers yes, but on the **Captain Moroni / Zerahemnah** model (Book of Mormon, Alma 44): Moroni's goal is to *minimize loss of life*, repeatedly offering the enemy a covenant of peace and letting all who will lay down their weapons go free, fighting only those "consistently willing to continue fighting."[^5] Applied online: keep offering the off-ramp; some opponents may be ready to make a covenant of peace.

### 4. Bilingualism, not capitulation
Michael adds that Mormon ignorance of the wider 2,000-year theological conversation is *why* he was once anti-Mormon — "Mormons couldn't speak my language." The fix is to become **"bilingual"** with other traditions (learn Christianese, classical theism) so you "don't talk through one another," without adopting their dogma.[^6] This is the serpent's wisdom in practice. Cf. [[Holy Envy - What Latter-day Saints Can Learn from Islam]].

### Counter-voice (Cardon's corrective — the dove can be naive)
The note would be incomplete without the panel's internal tension. Cardon repeatedly pushes back: "Why is this so hard for Mormons to realize that people hate us? They're not mistaken… it's bigotry."[^2] He warns that a reflexive "they're just confused, not thieves" charity does a *disservice* by lulling LDS into a false safety when [[Soft vs Pernicious Anti-Mormonism - The Rhetoric-to-Violence Spectrum|rhetoric has already escalated to violence]]. Brad's synthesis: name the bigotry honestly *and* refuse to mirror it — "be real peacemakers, not pushovers, but also not punishers."[^7] Both agree the goal is to stop "a hundred more [critics] being made," even if a hardened few cannot be reached.[^8]

## Best Quotes
- "We need to be wise as serpents and harmless as doves. And that requires both of those sides." [^3]
- "Not because we're not willing to fight other people, but because fighting them isn't effective." [^1]
- "Be real peacemakers — not pushovers, but also not punishers." [^7]
