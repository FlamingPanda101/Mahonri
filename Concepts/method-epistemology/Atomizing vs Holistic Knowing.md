# Atomizing vs Holistic Knowing

## The Point
Greek knowing works by *deconstruction* — to know a thing, break it into its smallest parts and define it by genus and category. Hebrew knowing is *holistic and functional* — a thing is known by what it does and what it is for. The two methods answer different questions; the error is using the atomizing method where life requires the functional one.

## Argument
1. **The Greek method: atomize.** To know what something *is*, you deconstruct it into "little bits of stuff." Democritus held everything reduces to *atomos* (the word "atom" is Greek, "uncuttable"). The instinct: get a microscope, find the smallest part, and that is the essence — "like saying if I want to know what I am I need to look at my fingernail" [^1].
2. **The Hebrew method: holistic function.** A thing is known by its action in lived reality — "if something isn't acting then it isn't." Knowledge is of wholes-in-operation, not of dissected parts (see [[To Be Means To Breathe - Hebrew Ontology of Action]]) [^2].
3. **The tomato / fruit-salad test.** A Greek puts tomatoes in fruit salad "because they're fruits" (botanical category). A Hebrew is incredulous: you don't put tomatoes in fruit salad, so is it really a fruit? Function over taxonomy — "you are what you do." (The Paul brothers' "how many lemons from an orange tree before you say that's not an orange tree" makes the same Hebrew move.) [^3]
4. **The "borders of the land" case (Mosiah 18:4).** A Greek demands "land A or land B — where is the line?" But Hebrew thought allows "the borders of the land" — a mountain range belonging to neither side; "neither here nor there." Scripture even locates people by *direction headed* ("in the borders of the land, heading north"), i.e. by action, not by a dot on the map [^4].
5. **Where each method is right.** Atomizing is "very, very useful" — for science, engineering, and *law* ("whose corn did the cow eat? get it down to particulars"). The talk does not reject Greek thought; it insists on the *proper integration of both* — science is done in the Greek mind, but **life is lived in the Hebrew experience** [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "Greeks want to atomize everything, they want to split it apart and make categorizations — it's either this or that, heaven and hell, you're either in land A or B. But Hebrew said no, it's the borders of the land — it's neither here nor there." [^4]

> "Science is done in the Greek mind, but life is lived in the Hebrew experience." [^5]
