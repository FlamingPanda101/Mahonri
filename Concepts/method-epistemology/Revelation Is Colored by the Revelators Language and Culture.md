# Revelation Is Colored by the Revelators Language and Culture

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] grounds his Stoicism argument in a model of revelation: God reveals "after the manner of [the recipients'] language," so revelation is unavoidably shaped by the revelator's language, culture, and society. Against the "funnel" model (the revelator as a passive channel through whom God's exact words pass), Thomassen argues the message that comes *out* differs depending on whether it comes through Paul of Tarsus or a 21st-century American — and this is the case for personal revelation and against inerrancy.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Two revelation models (rated: the framework).** Model A (Plato's *Ion*): the rhapsode is a mere "funnel," and "what comes out is what God intended." Model B (the LDS scriptures): D&C 1:24 — these commandments were given "in their weakness, after the manner of their language, that they might come to understanding"; and [[Alma 29.8|Alma 29:8]] — "the Lord doth grant unto all nations, of their own nation and tongue, to teach his word."[^1]
2. **Language informs thought (rated: the argument).** "How we understand things as a 21st-century American who speaks English is not the same as a 4th-century Greek speaker, or a reindeer herdsman in Siberia." So the same divine impulse "comes out" differently through different persons — Paul vs. the modern reader.[^2]
3. **The New Testament authors were Greek-shaped (rated: application).** The NT writers "are also socially, culturally informed. What was the social culture they were writing in? They're writing in Greek… their audience are Greek speakers… that is not insignificant." This sets up the thesis that Stoic categories are embedded in the text. See [[Translation Versus Transformation - The Order of Operations in Hellenization]].
4. **A critique of inerrancy (rated: the payoff).** Because revelation is mediated through layered cultures (the NT is "Greeks capturing something that happened to Hebrews… put into Jacobean English… read 400 years later"), inerrancy "falls apart," and the necessity of *personal* revelation follows — "help me know, Father, what I should be doing."[^3]

**Steelman of the critic.** A defender of verbal inspiration argues that God can superintend human language without overriding it (the "concursive" model), so cultural mediation need not imply error or Stoic doctrinal coloring — the human idiom is the *vehicle*, not a contaminant. A critic also notes Thomassen's own "bald assertions" about Stoic content go beyond what the language-mediation premise licenses: that revelation uses Greek does not entail that Stoic *philosophy* shaped its theology. The mediation premise (revelation comes through culture) is widely granted; the inference to pervasive Stoic shaping is the contested step.

## Best Quotes
> "These commandments are mine, given unto the servants in their weakness, after the manner of their language, that they might come to an understanding."[^1]

> "Language informs thought… what he receives is something, an idea, a sentiment, but what comes out is going to differ on the one hand if it's Paul of Tarsus, on the other hand if it's me."[^2]

## LDS Connection
This is a Latter-day Saint epistemology of scripture: revelation is true but culturally clothed, which both explains Joseph Smith's Jacobean English (and the Book of Mormon's KJV idiom) and motivates ongoing personal revelation. It is the doctrinal cousin of [[A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible]] and frames the whole Stoicism case as an instance of revelation taking the "manner of the language" of its first hearers.
