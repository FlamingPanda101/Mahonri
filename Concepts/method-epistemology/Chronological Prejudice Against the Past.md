# Chronological Prejudice Against the Past

## The Claim
Sound historical reasoning requires an *unnatural* discipline: instead of looking backward at the past through "the lens of everything that has happened since," the historian must mentally reverse position and face *forward* from the subject's own moment, "not knowing what's going to happen." Failing this produces "chronological prejudice" — the modern assumption that "modern" means "better" and that past people were simply benighted.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The fog/lens analogy (rated: moderate, illustrative).** [[Don Bradley]]: the intervening time between us and a historical figure is "a lens," and if that space "were filled with fog, I couldn't help but see you clouded with fog." Natural reading is fogged reading; history "requires something of us that's unnatural."[^1] The practical rule: "what did he see at the time? What did he know? What's his cultural background?" — and "forget what comes after for a while, bracket that."[^2]

2. **"Modern" smuggles in a value judgment (rated: strong, conceptual).** "It's almost baked into the word modern... When we say modern, we tend to mean better. More advanced. People used to think this way, but we're modern, right?" This is "a kind of chronological prejudice that comes with being a modern."[^3] This is essentially C.S. Lewis's "chronological snobbery" applied to LDS history. *Steelman:* the principle cuts both ways and can be abused — bracketing "what comes after" can also bracket *moral* knowledge, letting an apologist excuse genuine wrongs as mere products of their era (the "man of his time" move). Bradley guards against this by insisting Joseph was also *ahead* of his time (anti-slavery, Black ordination), not merely excused by it. The danger remains that the method selectively brackets harms while crediting foresight.

3. **Symmetry: see them fairly *and* see where they exceeded us (rated: moderate).** The corrective is double — "try to see what would it have looked like to be them," and also "where are times when they, surprisingly, they're more advanced than we think."[^4] Applied to [[Joseph Smith]]: human and limited, yet possessing "prophetic foresight" (his presidential emancipation plan that "could have avoided the bloodiest war").

## Best Quotes

> "Doing history, thinking historically requires something of us that's unnatural. ... It requires us to actually reverse direction and be facing back in that time... facing forward, looking to the future, not knowing what's going to happen."[^1]

> "It's almost baked into the word modern... When we say modern, we tend to mean better. ... there's a kind of chronological prejudice that comes with being a modern."[^3]

> "As moderns, we have prejudices against people of the past. That really is the case."[^3]
