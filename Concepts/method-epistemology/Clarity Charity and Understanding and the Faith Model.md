# Clarity Charity and Understanding and the Faith Model

## The Claim
[[Jonathan Neville]] offers a two-part method for the Book of Mormon geography debate (and disputes generally). The epistemic half is a **"faith model"**: people reach different conclusions from the *same facts* because between facts and a conclusion sit *assumptions, inferences, and theories* — so much of what people treat as "fact" is actually assumption. The relational half is a slogan — **clarity, charity, and understanding**: first be clear about what is actually being claimed, then extend charity (don't ascribe bad motives), then seek mutual understanding via side-by-side comparison.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Facts vs. assumptions.** Neville insists "the text is not self-executing" — "everybody who has a model of the Book of Mormon is interpreting the text," so no one's model is simply "what the text says." His running examples: the Chile landing (a Frederick G. Williams note "people assume came from Joseph Smith"), and "tower means pyramid" (Kirk Magleby's reading, which Neville says "is your interpretation, that's not what the text says"). Disentangling fact from assumption is the work.[^1] *(Strong as a methodological point — it is broadly correct that geography models are interpretations, a concession both sides can accept.)*
2. **Side-by-side comparison breaks cognitive dissonance.** Neville argues the best way to surface hidden assumptions is to "lay it out side by side" (his *no more contention* blog does this), and complains that an attempt to run such a comparison was vetoed at Book of Mormon Central.[^2] *(Moderate — a reasonable debiasing technique, though Neville's own comparisons are partisan.)*
3. **Charity polices straw men on both sides.** Notably, Neville applies the method *against his own camp*: he calls it a straw man when Heartlanders say Mesoamerican advocates "believe in two Cumorahs" (they posit one Cumorah plus a nicknamed hill — see [[The Two-Cumorahs Theory]]), and rejects calling Mesoamerican scholars "snobby" or Heartlanders "racist." He also rebuts a bad Heartland argument (that the law of Moses forbade hewn-stone building) by citing Solomon's temple.[^3] *(Moderate-to-strong — self-critical application is the most credible feature of the method; it is the same disposition as [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]].)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The method is admirable in the abstract but uneven in Neville's hands. (a) "Everything but the bare text is assumption" cuts *both* ways: Neville's own load-bearing premises — that [[Oliver Cowdery's Letter VII States Cumorah Is in New York as Fact|Letter VII is decisive]], that [[The Times and Seasons Mesoamerican Articles Were Benjamin Winchester Not Joseph Smith|Winchester wrote the Times-and-Seasons articles]] — are themselves contested *interpretations*, yet he presents some as near-facts, which sits awkwardly with his own model. (b) A "side-by-side comparison" authored by an advocate is not neutral; framing and selection do the persuading, so the technique can launder partisanship as impartiality. (c) "No more contention" can shade into tone-policing that discourages the sharp scholarly disagreement geography questions actually need. The fair, durable core: distinguishing facts from the assumptions layered on them is genuinely clarifying, and *charity toward persons while contesting claims* is a sound debate ethic — Neville's repeated willingness to call out his own side's straw men is the strongest evidence he means it.

## Best Quotes
> "A lot of times people think [something] is a fact [when it] is not really a fact — it's their assumptions... you have assumptions, inferences, theories that lead to your hypothesis. That's what I call the faith model."[^1]

> "It starts with clarity — be clear what you're actually saying. Then charity... you don't ascribe bad motives to people. You don't call Heartlanders racist... you don't call American [scholars] idiots."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the meta-method framing all of Neville's geography arguments, and a useful companion to [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]] and [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]. Its value to the vault is partly cautionary: the same "facts vs. assumptions" scalpel Neville wields against the Mesoamerican model can — and should — be turned on his own conclusions.
