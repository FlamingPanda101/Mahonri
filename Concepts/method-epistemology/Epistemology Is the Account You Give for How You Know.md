# Epistemology Is the Account You Give for How You Know

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] defines epistemology in plain terms: it is "essentially just a fancy word for the explanation that you give as the basis for how you know what you say that you know." [^1] The question "How do I know what I know?" is, he argues, "perhaps the most fundamental of all questions," because the answer "acts as the lens through which we see the world" — and "if our lens is broken we will never clearly see the full nature of reality." [^2]

## Argument
1. People constantly assert what they know or don't know, but rarely examine what they *mean* by "know" — this is "a serious philosophical question at the heart of an entire branch of philosophy known as epistemology." [^1]
2. Epistemology = your account of the *basis* for your knowledge claims. It is the justification layer beneath ordinary talk of knowing. [^1]
3. This account functions as a "lens": it is prior to and shapes every other belief about reality, so getting it wrong distorts everything downstream. [^2]
4. Therefore the right starting point for "any seeker of the truth" — "a Latter-day Saint, a Muslim, a Hindu, atheist, or any sincere person" — is to inspect this lens directly, motivated by "a commitment to want to know the truth." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Epistemology is essentially just a fancy word for the explanation that you give as the basis for how you know what you say that you know." [^1]

> "The question how do I know what I know is perhaps the most fundamental of all questions because the way we answer it acts as the lens through which we see the world and if our lens is broken we will never clearly see the full nature of reality." [^2]

> "The starting point for any seeker of the truth is a commitment to want to know the truth." [^3]
