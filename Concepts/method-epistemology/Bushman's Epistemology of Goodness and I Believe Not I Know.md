# Bushman's Epistemology of Goodness and I Believe Not I Know

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Richard Bushman]] — the foremost academic biographer of [[Joseph Smith]] and a stake patriarch —
describes how he resolved the supposed "faith vs. reason" conflict, and in doing so models an
epistemology several steps subtler than the standard testimony formula. Three moves carry it:

1. **He deliberately does *not* say "I know."** In academic circles, "know implies I have evidence
   that will convince *you*. I don't have evidence that will convince someone else — I have evidence
   that will convince *me*." So he says "I **believe** the gospel is true," or simply states facts
   ("Joseph Smith did this or that"). The "know" language "doesn't work for me."[^1]
2. **An "epistemology of goodness" / pragmatism.** His operative test is not abstract proof of God's
   existence but *functioning* ideas of God: "How do we decide something is truly good, and how do we
   adhere to it?" When a William-and-Mary colleague asked "how can you possibly believe?", his answer
   that *worked* was: as a bishop, people bring me problems too big for me, and "I could not do that
   job if I couldn't call upon God." Testimony, he argues, should say *how it works* — "it helped me
   with my 10-year-old daughter who's filled with fear" — not merely "I know because I had an
   experience." He calls this "pragmatism... what works."[^2]
3. **He reframes his own loss-and-recovery story.** Bushman doubts he ever truly "lost" his testimony
   ("if I had, would I have gone on a mission?"). His real problem was that he "couldn't **explain**
   why" he believed in a way that was *respectable* among his Harvard peers — he didn't need to
   persuade them, only to be a rational person with reasons. Recovery was the lifelong, "piecemeal"
   project of finding articulable ways to describe what Mormonism means.[^3]

The apologetic value: this dissolves the [[Naturalistic Debunking of Spiritual Experience|"you're
stupid if you believe"]] frame without overclaiming. Bushman concedes you cannot *prove* religion by
argument — but neither can you *disprove* it, and the new atheists struck him as "shallow," "clever
and smarty," failing to engage the real human work religion does (suffering, meaning, the yearning to
be good).[^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The two-institutions credibility — rate: strong (ethos)
Bushman has stood "at the top floor of both institutions": Gouverneur Morris Professor emeritus at
Columbia, Bancroft/Guggenheim-decorated early-Americanist, *and* a stake patriarch — "the closest
thing we have to a shaman." His whole stated life purpose has been "to make Mormonism intelligible to
people of all points of view, and perhaps a little attractive," and "to be respectable as a believing
Latter-day Saint in that academic society." That a scholar of his rank holds this position is itself
the counterexample to the faith-vs-intellect dichotomy.[^5]

### 2. "Knowledge" is calibrated to the audience and method — rate: moderate (epistemic distinction)
A trained physicist-turned-historian, Bushman knows the difference between repeatable, sharable
empirical knowledge (a rock accelerates at 9.8 m/s²) and personal spiritual conviction. He declines to
flatten the two: spiritual "evidence will convince *me*," not necessarily another — so honesty requires
"believe," not "know." This is a *more* careful epistemology than either the apologist's "I know" or
the skeptic's "you can't know anything."[^1] Compare [[Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of
Revelation]] and [[Spiritual Gifts Differ - Not Everyone Receives the Burning]].

### 3. Pragmatic verification through lived function — rate: moderate (philosophical pedigree)
The "does it work / functioning God" test has a respectable Jamesian-pragmatist lineage and reframes
testimony as describable evidence ("it helps me teach my primary class") rather than private assertion.
The colleague's reaction — "well, that makes sense" — is offered as proof the reframing communicates
across the believer/skeptic divide.[^2]

### 4. Enchantment vs. redemption — rate: moderate (motive analysis)
Asked what *keeps* him believing for 70 years, Bushman distinguishes **enchantment** (he "loves the
idea" of inexplicable forces that give courage in a bleak world) from the deeper anchor, **redemption**:
"what we really yearn for is to be better people than we are... to be worthy to be loved." Mormonism
provides that through Christ's atonement, a warm community, and prayer in despair. When the Claremont
dean asked "how can you believe in Joseph Smith with gold plates and the whole works?", his answer was:
"When I live the Mormon way, I'm the kind of man I want to be."[^6]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **"It works for me" is not truth.** A pragmatic/"functioning God" test shows a belief is *useful* or
  *consoling*, not that it is *true* — every religion's adherents could say the same. Bushman would
  largely grant this: he explicitly retreats from "know" *because* he cannot bridge to public proof, so
  the position concedes the evidential point and relocates to honest belief.
- **Selection effect.** One eminent believing scholar does not refute the faith-reason tension; there
  are eminent unbelieving scholars too. The claim is only that belief is *respectable/intelligible*, not
  *demonstrated* — a deliberately modest target.
- **Reframing "I never lost it" could rationalize.** A critic may say redefining agnosticism as merely
  "couldn't explain it" protects the testimony from ever having been falsified. Bushman's reply is
  behavioral (he served a mission), which is evidence but not proof.

**What survives:** as an *epistemology*, the position is unusually defensible precisely because it
under-claims — "I believe, and here is how it functions and why it is good," paired with "argument can
neither prove nor disprove religion." It is the mirror image of the overconfident "I know" *and* the
overconfident "you're stupid to believe."
