# Scribal Religion Versus Authoritative Religion

## The Claim
[[Jared Halverson]] distinguishes two fundamentally different kinds of religion. A **scribal (or biblical) religion** grounds every claim in chapter and verse — "the good book says" — and so, lacking independent authority, can only ever *argue over interpretation*. An **authoritative religion** is grounded in prophets who, like Jesus, teach "as one having authority, and not as the scribes" ([[Matthew 7.29|Matthew 7:29]]). Settling disputes by appeal to the Bible, Halverson says, "just makes us one more Protestant denomination" — doing "the exact thing that frustrated Joseph Smith." Latter-day Saints quote the Bible to Protestants only because "when in Rome"; their own religion is not scribal.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Jesus contrasted himself with the scribes.** The crowd marveled that he taught "as one having authority, and not as the scribes" — who always cite Moses, Isaiah, Jeremiah "to give themselves some backup." Jesus "very seldom quoted chapter and verse," and in [[John 5.39|John 5:39]] nearly "apologizes" for scripture: "ye think ye have eternal life" in the texts, but "it's in me."[^2] *(Moderate-to-strong — a real and striking feature of the Gospels' portrayal of Jesus.)*
2. **The Bible is not self-interpreting.** It was "naive of Martin Luther to think... as soon as we abandon the Catholic magisterium the Bible will create the one true church." Because the text does not interpret itself, "all we're left with is interpreters — which is then the question, who's doing the interpretation?" Joseph Smith's own [[Joseph Smith History 1.34|First Vision frustration]] was that "the teachers of the various religions understood the same passage of scripture so differently as to destroy all confidence in settling the question by an appeal to the Bible."[^3] *(Strong — the proliferation of Protestant denominations from a shared Bible is hard to dispute.)*
3. **The slavery precedent.** Halverson cites a pre-Civil-War Southern Baptist conceding that the Bible "at least had prophets and apostles, and that is an advantage no succeeding age has had" — North and South read the same Bible to opposite conclusions on slavery (echoed at the Lincoln Memorial). Living apostolic authority is what could have adjudicated. See [[Abolition Was Read Into the Bible From the Outside]].[^4] *(Moderate — a pointed historical illustration, though one a Protestant can absorb.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The scribal/authoritative dichotomy is sharp but contestable. (a) Protestants do not claim the *bare text* settles everything; classical Protestantism affirms the internal witness of the Spirit and confessional/creedal teaching authority, so "scripture alone" was never "scripture by itself." (b) Naming Jesus as anti-scribal cuts both ways: Jesus also said "it is written" repeatedly and affirmed that scripture "cannot be broken" (John 10:35), so the Gospels present him as *both* authoritative *and* deeply scriptural — the contrast is with the scribes' *misuse*, not with citing scripture as such. (c) The argument relocates rather than removes the interpretive problem: if a prophet's pronouncements also require interpretation (and LDS history shows prophets reinterpreting earlier prophets), then "who interprets the interpreter?" recurs. Halverson partly concedes this by insisting prophets are *fallible* and that members must become "good judges." His strongest, narrower point stands: a tradition with *only* a text and competing interpreters has no in-principle mechanism to resolve doctrinal disputes, which is why the restoration's claim is about a *kind of authority*, not a better commentary.

## Best Quotes
> "Why do we keep trying to settle every question by an appeal to the Bible? ... that just makes us one more Protestant denomination that only has Bible and is stuck with some kind of inerrantist view."[^1]

> "Please don't mistake us for a scribal religion. Yours is a scribal religion — I don't mean that in a derogatory way... yours is a biblical religion. But we have prophets of God who speak by authority, and not as the scribes."[^2]

## LDS Connection
This is Halverson's frame for the entire interface between Latter-day Saints and other Christians: interfaith debate "will always end up being biblical," and so "every time we're going to have to agree to disagree," because it rests on interpretation. The restoration's distinctive is not a superior reading but a different *source* of authority — which is why the argument terminates in [[Revelatory Authority Must Match Interpretive Authority]] and connects to the vault's broader case that [[The Restoration Answers an Authority Problem Scholarship Cannot Solve|scholarship cannot solve the authority problem]].
