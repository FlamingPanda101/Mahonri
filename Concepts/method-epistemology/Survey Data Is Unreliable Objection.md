# Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The skeptical host ("Ryland," who holds a PhD and repeatedly invokes methodology) objects that the clergy-belief survey is essentially **garbage data**: forcing complex theological convictions into a binary "definitely believe / not" format produces unreliable numbers, mood-dependent answers, and artifacts that should not be read as evidence of real disbelief.[^1][^2] If the threshold for "belief" can be set so high that no data can ever count, the objection threatens to nullify *any* conclusion drawn from the survey.

## The Rebuttal

1. **The variance defeats pure-noise (strong).** [[Jonah Barnes]]'s core reply: even granting wording problems, **the variance between traditions is too large to be noise.** A ~60-point gap between Evangelical (80%) and Catholic (23%) clergy on Adam and Eve cannot be explained by survey jitter alone; systematic differences require a systematic cause.[^3] See [[Variance Between Groups Is Real Signal Rebuttal]]. *Rating: strong — this is the decisive counter.*

2. **Source credibility (moderate-strong).** The data are not a random internet poll but the **National Survey of Religious Leaders**, analyzed by tier-A political scientist [[Ryan Burge]].[^4] Professional instrument design and large samples blunt the "garbage" charge, even if no survey is perfect.

3. **Grace can be bounded, not infinite (moderate).** The third host concedes the wording objection but quantifies it: a fair allowance for conservative/nuanced answering might cover **5–10 points, not 15+**, and crucially that grace "should apply equally" to all traditions — yet Evangelicals score ~99% while mainline scores ~75%.[^5] If noise were the explanation, it would depress all groups, not just one.

4. **Self-defeating threshold (logical).** Ryland's own ally points out that if "definitely believe" is held to an impossible standard, "then no data can never mean anything."[^2] An objection that proves too much (invalidating all survey knowledge) is generally too strong to be sound.

**Steelman of the objection.** Ryland is not merely contrarian: response-set effects, social-desirability bias, mood, and the semantics of "definitely" are *genuine* and well-documented threats to survey validity. He explicitly grants that real disbelief "obviously" exists among some clergy; his narrower, defensible claim is that the *headline numbers overstate* it and that triumphalist readings ("hollow faith") outrun what binary data can support.[^6] A careful researcher would agree the point estimates carry uncertainty bands.

**Methodological context.** Both sides are partly right under standard survey methodology: Likert-style or multi-point belief items would indeed yield different absolute figures than a forced binary (Ryland's point), but *between-group comparisons* using the **same instrument** are far more robust than the absolute levels (Barnes's point), because shared wording bias largely cancels out when comparing groups.

## Best Quotes

> "That's exactly one of the biggest problems with survey data. It is garbage... because you run into these types of problems all the time." [^1]

> "If you take it to believe to death, then no data can never mean anything." [^2]

> "You might think this is all garbage data. But look at the variance. The variance is real." [^3]

> "That grace should, in my mind, account for maybe 5 to 10%, not 15. And it should apply equally." [^5]
