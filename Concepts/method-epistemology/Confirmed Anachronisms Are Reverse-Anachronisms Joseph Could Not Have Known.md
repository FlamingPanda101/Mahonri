# Confirmed Anachronisms Are Reverse-Anachronisms Joseph Could Not Have Known

## The Claim

The "shrinking anachronism list" argument is usually run *defensively*: the more alleged errors archaeology eliminates, the less the fraud theory predicts the data, so authenticity becomes more probable. [[Hayden Carroll]] runs the same dataset *offensively*. He argues that each item archaeology has now **confirmed** — pre-Columbian horses, cattle, swine, goats, dogs, ironwork, large-scale warfare, names, and cultural and linguistic details — is not merely a neutralized objection but a positive **hit**: a feature of the genuine ancient world that no one in 1830 knew about, yet which Joseph Smith placed in the text. These are "reverse-anachronisms" — the opposite of what a 19th-century forger leaves behind. Carroll quantifies the swing via [[Matt Roper]]'s 2025 [[Interpreter Foundation]] inventory: roughly **7%** of the Book of Mormon's ancient-world elements were archaeologically confirmable in Joseph's day, against **75%+** confirmed (plus 13% partial, ~9% open) today. The inference is abductive: stacked with the dictation feat and the witnesses, the naturalistic explanation becomes the *least* probable one — "tell me Joseph made this all up. Or tell me the devil made him do it. Any answer would be more compelling than the naturalistic one."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The framing inverts the burden of proof.** A genuine anachronism (iron in a setting that had none) counts *against* a text; a confirmed-after-the-fact detail that was unknown at composition counts *for* it, because a forger cannot plant accurate data he has no access to. Carroll's claim is that the 75%+ confirmed bucket is full of the second kind — Joseph "got so many unknown elements of the ancient world correct before anyone else knew them."[^2] *Rating: structurally sound as an abductive move; its force depends entirely on (a) Roper's classification holding and (b) each "hit" being genuinely unavailable in Joseph's 1830 environment rather than a lucky guess or a King James borrowing.*

2. **The 7% → 75% longitudinal snapshot.** Unlike a bare "the list is shrinking" claim, Carroll cites a *then-vs-now* percentage drawn from Roper's full-corpus inventory, which makes the trend a measured quantity rather than an impression.[^3] The defensive form of this same data is catalogued at [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]] (Roper's 226-item tally, ~174 settled / 31 trending / 21 unsupported) and [[Shrinking Anachronism List as Evidence of Authenticity]]. *Rating: strong if Roper's numbers are reported accurately; a skeptic can contest where the line between "confirmed" and "trending" is drawn.*

3. **The cumulative stack.** Carroll does not rest the abduction on archaeology alone. He couples the confirmations with the dictation rate (4,000+ words/day), the literary complexity, and the 19 witnesses to the plates, so that the naturalistic hypothesis must absorb *several* independent improbabilities at once.[^1] This connects to [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]], [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]], and the [[The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument|keystone argument]]. *Rating: the cumulative case is the real engine here; any single thread (e.g. horses, still [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism|trending not settled]]) is weaker alone than the stack is together.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic grants the trend but denies the *direction* of the inference. (a) "Confirmed somewhere in the ancient world" is not the same as "confirmed in the specific Mesoamerican time and place the Book of Mormon requires," so a generic hit is not a Joseph-couldn't-have-known hit. (b) Many elements Carroll lists (horses, cattle, swine) appear in the King James Bible and in 19th-century frontier vocabulary, so their presence needs no ancient access — undercutting the "reverse-anachronism" label. (c) The most-cited live problems (horses *in period*, steel, chariots, a confirmed Nephite site) sit in the ~9% open bucket, which is precisely where the evidentiary action is; counting the easy confirmations as "predictive hits" risks a Texas-sharpshooter framing. The strongest LDS reply concedes (b) for the King-James-available terms and rests the abduction on the *non-obvious* hits plus the cumulative stack, not on the raw 75% headline.

## Best Quotes

> "If he wrote the Book of Mormon, how did Joseph know about these elements of ancient America before anyone else?"[^2]

> "In Joseph's day, archaeological studies had only confirmed nearly 7% of elements of the ancient world mentioned in the Book of Mormon. Today, we're looking at over 75% confirmed, 13% partially confirmed, and only... 9% unconfirmed."[^3]

> "Tell me Joseph made this all up. Or tell me the devil made him do it. Any answer would be more compelling than the naturalistic one."[^1]

## LDS Connection

This is the offensive complement to the vault's defensive anachronism notes: where [[One Anachronism Does Not Disprove the Book of Mormon]] and [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]] argue that the shrinking list *fails to disprove* the book, Carroll argues the confirmed remainder *positively evidences* it — provided the hits are genuinely unavailable to Joseph. The honest version of the argument therefore lives or dies on filtering King-James-available and frontier-available terms out of the "hit" column, and on the cumulative weight of the residue together with the dictation feat and the witnesses rather than on the bare percentage.
