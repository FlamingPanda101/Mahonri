## The Point
Mark Ellison urges Latter-day Saints to be judicious and cautious in using the term "apostasy." Mere changes in forms of worship or ritual are not necessarily signs of error or deliberate rebellion — Latter-day Saint worship procedures have themselves evolved over a short history. The Greek *apostasia* connotes defiance, rebellion, and breach of faith; many ancient changes that Latter-day Saints disagree with may have arisen from people "trying their best in their circumstances" rather than from outright rebellion.

## Evidence
1. *Apostasia* (from *apo-*, "apart," and *histēmi*, "stand," literally "stand apart") is defined by BDAG as "defiance of established system or authority, rebellion, abandonment, breach of faith."
2. The word appears only twice in the New Testament: Acts 21:21 (a rumor that Paul taught people to "forsake [apostasia] Moses") and [[2 Thessalonians 2.3|2 Thessalonians 2:3]] ("a falling away [apostasia] first"); in context the latter may refer to a more particular, proximate event than a total church apostasy.
3. The word "apostasy" does not appear in the Book of Mormon; instead its standing-related metaphor for post–New Testament Christians is "stumble" (1 Nephi 13:29, 34; 2 Nephi 26:20) — inviting us to picture people making forward progress in rough terrain.
4. Ellison invokes Matthew 7:1–2: if we judge ancient Christians harshly, in the worst possible light, that is how we too will be judged.

## Best Quotes
> "Mere changes in forms of worship or rituals are not necessarily signs of error or apostasy — our own worship procedures have evolved over our short history. Some changes in ancient Christianity that we may not agree with ... might have been motivated by people trying their best in their circumstances, with the amount of understanding they had at the time." [^1]

> "We can choose to be charitable readers, remembering that if we judge ancient Christians harshly, in the worst possible light, that is how we, too, will be judged." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This reframes the Latter-day Saint doctrine of the Great Apostasy in a charitable, evidence-first key: the "stumble" metaphor of the Book of Mormon depicts people striving in difficult terrain rather than willful rebels, encouraging Saints to learn from ancient Christians rather than dismiss them. [^2]
