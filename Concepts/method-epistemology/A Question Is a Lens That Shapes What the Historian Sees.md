# A Question Is a Lens That Shapes What the Historian Sees

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] offers a methodological confession: when he studied [[Joseph Smith]] as an ex-Mormon, his single habitual question — "what was in it for him?" — functioned as a *lens* that pre-selected the evidence he noticed. "A question is a lens… whatever the lenses are that I'm wearing… those are going to shape what I see." His later breakthrough came not from new documents but from broadening the questions he asked (e.g., "Who was he trying to help?"), which let formerly invisible data become visible.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The self-reinforcing slanted question (strong, first-person).** Believing Joseph was "an opportunist," Bradley asked of every sermon and revelation "what was in it for him?" — and "not surprisingly" kept finding confirmation. "You find to some extent what you're looking for."[^2]
2. **A concrete reversal (strong, example).** Using his rigorous micro-chronology method, Bradley *expected* to confirm the critical claim that the Aaronic priesthood restoration was invented in 1834; instead he found Book of Mormon text "preparing for" the John-the-Baptist experience at the right moment — a "faith-promoting" find he made while still an unbeliever, because the data didn't fit his question.[^3]
3. **Method is neutral; the question is not (interpretive).** Bradley stresses he was using a *good* tool (historical-critical micro-context) the whole time; the distortion came from "a very singular, slanted… question," not from sloppy method. Good method plus a narrow question still yields a narrow picture.[^2]
4. **The fix is more questions, not fewer (strong).** The leg-surgery discovery "was the root of starting to raise the question… what else was this guy about?" Broadening the interrogation, not acquiring new sources, is what changed his reading of Joseph.[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A critic could turn the principle against Bradley himself: now that he is a believer, his lens is "how was Joseph trying to serve God / his father?", which is just as capable of manufacturing confirmation as the opportunist lens was. The insight that questions shape findings is double-edged — it equally indicts the faithful historian, so it cannot by itself adjudicate which reading of Joseph is correct.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley would accept the symmetry as exactly the point: the remedy is not to find the one "neutral" question but to *multiply* questions and follow the evidence even when it cuts against one's current lens — which he models by reporting a faith-promoting find made while an atheist and faith-challenging data (the father's drinking, his own anger at God) made while a believer. The principle is offered as a discipline against confirmation bias, not as a relativist solvent.

## Best Quotes
> "A question is a lens… whatever the lenses are that I'm wearing, those are going to shape what I see."[^1]

> "My habitual question was, what was in it for him?… So that was going to shape what I saw."[^2]
