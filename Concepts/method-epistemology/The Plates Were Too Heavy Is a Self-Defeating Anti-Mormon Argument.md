# The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument

#type/claim

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Hayden Carroll]] reports that veteran countercult speaker [[Bill McKeever]] — at a Ratio Christi conference where Carroll spoke after him — spent "30 of his 60 minutes" arguing that the golden plates "would have been too heavy for Joseph Smith to run through the woods with," even bringing a weighted model and inviting people to "come and lift these up."[^1][^2] Carroll's rebuttal is that the argument is **self-defeating for any theist**: it secretly assumes Joseph was *not* a prophet, while purporting to disprove that he was. If you grant — for the sake of argument — that God was behind the work, then "if God wanted Joseph Smith to be able to carry those plates through the woods, do you think God could have allowed him to do that?" A critic who already believes God parted the Red Sea cannot consistently rest his case on Joseph being physically weak.[^3][^4]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The argument smuggles in its own conclusion — rate: strong
The "too heavy" objection only bites if naturalism is assumed: *absent* divine help, a man could not carry that weight while fleeing. But that is exactly the point in dispute. Carroll's move is to surface the hidden premise and suspend it: "Assume Joseph Smith is a prophet… shed your presuppositions for a minute. Be neutral for a second."[^3] Once neutral, a being who can produce plates and translate them by revelation can trivially "allow" their bearer to carry them — so the weight proves nothing about prophethood. This is the same diagnostic Carroll applies generally in [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]]: an attack that presupposes the critic's worldview as the standard is *valid but unsound*.

### 2. It is internally inconsistent with the critic's own miracles — rate: strong (tu quoque that lands)
Carroll's pointed question — "don't you believe that Moses parted the Red Sea? Did God allow him to do something miraculous?"[^3] — exposes a double standard. A Bible-believing evangelical already affirms physically impossible feats by divine enablement ([[Exodus 14.17-18|the Red Sea]], walking on water, etc.). To deny God the far smaller act of strengthening a man to carry metal plates, *while* affirming the parting of a sea, is special pleading. The objection therefore fails by the critic's *own* standard, not merely the LDS one — the mark of a genuinely self-defeating argument.

### 3. The evidential fallbacks are available but secondary — rate: balanced
The panel notes the ordinary apologetic answers exist too: estimates that "the plates actually weren't that heavy," or that a [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|tumbaga (gold-copper) alloy]] would weigh far less than solid gold.[^5] But Carroll deliberately ranks the *theological* rebuttal "almost a better answer," because it works even if one grants a heavy weight — it refuses the frame rather than haggling inside it.[^4] **Steelman of McKeever:** the weight point is not *meant* as a standalone disproof so much as a cumulative-case "implausibility" prompt for an audience that already rejects the supernatural claim — i.e., it preaches to evangelicals who *don't* grant the prophet premise. Carroll's reply concedes this implicitly: the argument only "works" on a pre-committed audience, which is precisely why Carroll judged it weak ("the dumbest argument I've ever heard") in front of people he could ask to be neutral.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "He thought the most important thing they should know is that the golden plates would have been too heavy for Joseph Smith to run through the woods with."[^1]

> "Hey Bill McKeever, don't you believe that Moses parted the Red Sea? Did God allow him to do something miraculous?… If God wanted Joseph Smith to be able to carry those plates through the woods, do you think God could have allowed him to do that?"[^3]

> "I would never rest my argument on the fact that I'm weak. I would never do that."[^6]

## LDS Connection
The exchange is a clean specimen of the panel's master method — make the critic justify (or at least expose) the standard hidden in his attack. It also illustrates a recurring countercult failure mode: arguing *from* a naturalist or creedal baseline that the LDS claim does not share (compare [[The Creeds Cannot Be the Neutral Baseline - The Circularity of Creedal Disqualification]]). For the Latter-day Saint, the takeaway is to refuse the loaded frame: questions about *how* a miraculous event was physically possible presuppose its non-divinity, so the first move is to ask the interlocutor whether he allows God to act at all — and if he does (Red Sea, resurrection), the "too heavy" objection collapses by his own lights.

## Corroboration — McKeever's Manti Pageant Demonstration (Ward Radio)
The same [[Bill McKeever|McKeever]] routine recurs in Ward Radio's gold-plates episode, where the panel plays footage of McKeever and [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] hauling weighted replicas in a red wagon through the Manti pageant crowd, daring families to lift them. The episode adds two things this note's *Salty Saints* source did not: (1) the **witness data** that caps the real weight at 40-60 lb, exposing the 200-lb figure as a solid-gold strawman (see [[Witness Weight Estimates of the Plates Refute the Solid-Gold Strawman]]); and (2) the diagnosis that the move borrows an **atheist evidential standard** the Christian critic doesn't hold (see [[Anti-Mormons Borrow Atheist Weaponry Against the Plates]]). The climactic clip — an ordinary "Mormon mom" stopping McKeever cold by asking who ever said the plates were 200 lb — is the live-action version of the self-defeating point made above.[^7]
