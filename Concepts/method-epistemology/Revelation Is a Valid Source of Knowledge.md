# Revelation Is a Valid Source of Knowledge

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Kerry Muhlestein]] argues that one of the gravest "mistakes" people make in approaching the [[Book of Abraham]] (and scripture generally) is swallowing the **sophistry that revelation is not a valid source of knowledge** — that only instrument-measurable, empirical data counts as real knowing. Against this he defends *revelation as a viable epistemology*: a repeatable, experiential way of acquiring knowledge that is not reducible to laboratory measurement but is no less real for that.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Alma 32 "experiment upon the word."** Muhlestein points to Alma 32's framing of faith as a *repeatable experiment*: plant the word, nurture it, and observe whether it swells, sprouts, and bears fruit. This casts spiritual knowing in quasi-empirical terms — a procedure anyone can run and check against their own experience, not a blind leap. *Rating: scriptural/analogical — a genuine internal model of testable spiritual knowledge, though the "experiment" is first-person and not third-party verifiable.*
2. **The UCLA knee story (felt knowledge instruments missed).** Muhlestein recounts knowing something was wrong with his knee that the instruments and tests at UCLA could not initially detect — a felt, embodied knowledge later *vindicated by surgery*. The point: instruments are not the only reliable detectors of truth; there exist real states of affairs that empirical tools miss but that direct experience registers correctly. *Rating: anecdotal/illustrative — a vivid analogy for non-instrumental knowing; persuasive by analogy, not proof.*
3. **The empiricist exclusion is self-undermining.** The claim "only empirically measurable things count as knowledge" is itself not an empirically measurable result — it is a philosophical commitment. So ruling out revelation *by definition* smuggles in an unexamined assumption, which connects to Muhlestein's broader "questioning assumptions" theme. *Rating: a fair philosophical point — verificationism is famously self-referentially unstable.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The critic replies that "revelation" is epistemically unreliable precisely because it is *private and unfalsifiable*: contradictory revelations across rival traditions cannot all be true, sincere people feel certainty about incompatible claims, and the Alma 32 "experiment" has no failure condition the believer will accept (a null result is reattributed to insufficient faith or nurture). The knee analogy proves only that *some* non-instrumental intuitions are later confirmable *by instruments* — which actually concedes that public verification is the arbiter. A measured reply: the argument does not claim revelation is *infallible* or that every felt impression is knowledge, only that a blanket exclusion of revelation is an unargued dogma; and the cross-tradition disagreement problem afflicts *every* source (perception, testimony, reason) without making them worthless. The honest resting point is modest: revelation can *count* as a source of knowledge alongside reason and evidence, to be weighed, not worshipped.

## Best Quotes
> The fifth mistake is "believing the sophistry that revelation is not a valid source of knowledge."

## LDS Relevance
This is foundational to a Restoration epistemology of *converging witnesses*: spiritual confirmation (Moroni's promise, Alma 32) is placed *alongside* reason and evidence, not against them — see the companion [[Spiritual Confirmation Not Data Is Why People Keep Reading Scripture]] and Muhlestein's paired claim that pure "objectivity" is itself a bias in [[Everyone Has a Faith-Choice Bias and Admitting It Is More Objective]]. It guards against conceding the empiricist's frame at the outset, while (per the steelman) not overclaiming revelation as self-certifying proof.
