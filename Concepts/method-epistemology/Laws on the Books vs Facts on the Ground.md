# Laws on the Books vs Facts on the Ground

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the apologetic comparison between biblical and antebellum slavery commits a category error: it compares the **idealized laws** of the Bible against the **documented horrors** of the American South — "comparing apples to oranges." The fair comparison is law-to-law or practice-to-practice.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two different kinds of evidence (rated: strong; methodological).** For the antebellum South "we are hearing stories of what actually happened... generally ignoring the laws on the books." For the Bible "all we have is the laws that were ostensibly on the books... We don't really have the stories of what actually happened."[^2]

2. **The honest law-to-law comparison reverses the verdict (rated: strong).** Set biblical law beside the [[Louisiana 1825 Code Protected Slaves More Than the Bible|Louisiana Civil Code of 1825]] and the secular code is more protective.[^3]

3. **Likely practice was as brutal (rated: moderate; inference).** As for whether biblical enslaved people were actually treated according to the ostensible laws: "most likely, of course not, because most likely what went on on the ground was probably closer to what went on on the ground in the pre–Civil War American South."[^4]

4. **The laws may not even have been operative (rated: moderate).** McClellan adds that the biblical laws "actually weren't" really on the books in any enforced sense — they are idealized legal literature.[^2]

5. **Steelman.** An apologist could argue that having *more humane ideals*, even if unenforced, still reflects moral superiority. McClellan's reply: unenforced ideals tell us about aspirations, not lived reality, and the biblical ideals themselves (e.g. [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]]) are in places *less* humane than the secular codes.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "When we think of the pre–Civil War American South, we are hearing stories of what actually happened... When we look at the Bible, all we have is the laws that were ostensibly on the books... We don't really have the stories of what actually happened. So this is comparing apples to oranges."[^2]

> "Most likely what went on on the ground was probably closer to what went on on the ground in the pre–Civil War American South."[^4]
