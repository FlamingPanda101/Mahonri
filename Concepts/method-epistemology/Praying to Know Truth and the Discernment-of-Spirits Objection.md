# Praying to Know Truth and the Discernment-of-Spirits Objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A standard evangelical critique of the **Moroni's-promise** missionary practice — inviting people to pray "with real intent" to know whether the Book of Mormon is true — is that prayer is *not* a reliable way to know truth, because the Bible warns of deceiving spirits. The narrator names the objection directly: "How often are you told that praying to God is not a reliable way to know truth?" Critics cite that there are "false spirits... in this world to deceive people," that "Satan goes around looking like an angel of light" ([[2 Corinthians 11.14|2 Cor 11:14]]), that false prophets seem genuine, and that "we cannot trust our own feelings or experiences" [^1].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Reductio: if not direct witness, then what?** The narrator's central rhetorical question: "If a direct witness from God is not the best way to come to know God, then what is?... Is there a greater or more reliable source of knowledge compared to God's direct revelation to your spirit?" [^2]. Rating: **strong as a dilemma** — the critic who denies prayer-knowledge owes an alternative epistemology of God that is *more* reliable than God's own communication, which is hard to supply without circularity (the Bible's own reliability is itself known... how?).
2. **The Bible itself commands testing spirits, not abandoning experience.** [[1 John 4.1|1 John 4:1]] ("try the spirits whether they are of God") presupposes that spiritual experience is real and *evaluable*, not that it is worthless. Rating: **a fair scriptural counter** — the discernment texts the critic cites actually establish a *process* of spiritual epistemology rather than forbidding it.
3. **Pre-canonical and extra-biblical revelation.** The narrator presses: "Is God able to speak to you outside of the Bible? What about to the people before the Bible existed?... Does God only speak to prophets?" [^3]. Rating: **effective** against sola-scriptura epistemology — Abraham, Noah, and Adam had no Bible, so direct revelation must be a valid channel or those figures could not have known God.
4. **Scriptural anchor.** The Lord to [[Oliver Cowdery]]: "Did I not speak peace to your mind concerning the matter?... What greater witness can you have than from God?" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 6.22-23|D&C 6:22-23]]) [^2] grounds the claim that the internal witness is the *highest* evidence.
5. **Steelman the evangelical objector.** The strongest counter the video does **not** fully answer: the discernment problem is symmetric. If Satan can counterfeit "an angel of light" and produce burning-in-the-bosom feelings, then the *feeling itself* cannot certify *which* spirit produced it — that is the whole point of [[1 John 4.1]]. Evangelicals argue the test must be **doctrinal/objective** (does the spirit confess Christ come in the flesh; does the message align with prior Scripture), not merely the *intensity or peace* of the experience. So a sincere prayer producing peace about a false book is exactly the failure mode the warning predicts. Honest tension: the LDS reply ("what greater witness than God?") presumes the witness has been correctly identified as God's — which is the very thing under dispute. The genuine debate is over the *criteria of discernment*, where the LDS side leans experiential and the evangelical side leans propositional.
