# The Heart Levav Is the Basis of Religious Knowledge

## The Point

[[Blake Ostler]] (in a FAIR conference talk spliced into the video) argues that **the human heart — in the full Hebrew sense of *levav* — is the basis of religious knowledge and commitment.** Not the muscle, not mere emotion, but the unified cognitive-and-affective center that Western culture wrongly bifurcates into "mind" vs. "emotions." This is "the very heart of Mormonism."[^1][^2]

## Argument

1. **The invitation is experiential, not argumentative.** Ostler: when Latter-day Saints approach others, "the last thing on earth we would do is send out a bunch of 19-year-olds to argue with people about the Bible." Rather, they offer "a way to enter into interpersonal relationship directly with God." The model is the Gospel of John's disciples who simply said "come and see."[^1]
2. **"Come and see" means deeper sight.** "When they said come and see, they didn't merely mean to use one's eyeballs but to see with deeper and more spiritually insightful eyes" — using "every faculty of human discernment."[^1]
3. **The heart is *levav*: cognitive + affective.** "I speak of the human heart in the fullest Hebrew sense of *levav*, that denotes ... mind and emotions ... it includes both cognitive and affective functions. In fact, it is the very center and seat of spiritual life."[^2] *(Steelman: this is exegetically defensible — Hebrew *lev/levav* does cover intellect, will, and emotion together, so framing religious knowing as heart-knowledge, not bare feeling, is more careful than the caricature of "testimony = warm feelings.")*
4. **The most basic thing in Mormonism is individual experience of the Spirit.** Reasons and scripture have a role, "but at bottom these arguments are not what is most trustworthy and basic ... what is most basic in Mormonism is the individual experience of the Spirit."[^3] *(Cross-examination: locating warrant in private experience raises the standard objection that such experiences are not intersubjectively checkable — which Ostler himself partly concedes in [[Perfect Certainty of Experience Not of Interpretation]] and in his point that the experience is evidence only for the one who has it.)*
