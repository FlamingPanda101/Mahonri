# We Become What We Sacrifice For

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] invokes a documented psychological principle — "sacrifice increases commitment," or "we become what we sacrifice for" — to argue that [[Joseph Smith]]'s pre-anesthetic leg surgery did not merely *reflect* his character but *forged* it. The depth of the childhood sacrifice for his father cemented a value into a commitment and finally into an identity, so that "this boy becomes the man who wanted to honor his father." Bradley extends the same principle to his own grief: how he handles his son's death "is going to shape more than anything else who I become."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A named psychological principle (moderate, social-science).** Bradley presents "sacrifice increases commitment" as "documented in psychological research," not merely a homily — when people sacrifice for a cause "they become more committed to that… it becomes more part of them."[^1]
2. **Paired with post-traumatic growth (moderate).** He couples it with the research literature on *post-traumatic growth*: people can grow "amazingly" through trauma, which "maybe is part of what makes Joseph Smith Joseph Smith." The surgery is read as both trauma and the seedbed of growth.[^2]
3. **Applied reflexively and honestly (strong, first-person).** Bradley applies the principle to himself in real time — his self-hatred after Donnie's death, and his choice of how to bear it, as the thing that "shapes that future more than anything else in my life." This self-application strengthens the claim's sincerity (he is not only theorizing about a dead prophet).[^3]
4. **Symmetry of the formula (interpretive).** The principle cuts both ways: the same mechanism by which a parent's sacrifice deepens love for a child can run "the other direction," deepening a child's commitment to a parent — the move that links the surgery to Joseph's life-long "save the fathers" work.[^1]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The principle, while real, is being deployed as a near-unfalsifiable interpretive frame: almost any later commitment can be retrofitted to an earlier sacrifice. Cognitive-dissonance research does show effort justifies attachment, but it does not license inferring a specific person's life-defining identity from a single childhood event; correlation between hardship and later conviction is not proof that *this* sacrifice *caused* the prophetic identity.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley's claim is calibrated: he says the surgery "both reflects and shapes" character and treats the principle as one strand among several (documented bond, Bushman's verdict, the Danish source). As a *mechanism* it is empirically grounded; as applied to Joseph it is offered as a plausible shaping influence, not a deterministic cause. Its credibility is reinforced by Bradley's willingness to test it on his own life rather than only on a safely distant subject.

## Best Quotes
> "Sacrifice increases commitment… We become what we sacrifice for."[^1]

> "How I handle my son's death is going to shape more than anything else who I become and how I live the rest of my life."[^3]
