# Dating an Idea Differs From Dating Its Manuscript

#type/claim

## The Claim
Pressed that the **Ascension of Isaiah**'s surviving manuscripts are late (the text often dated c. AD 150-200), [[Jonah Barnes]], [[David John Butler]], and [[Cardon Ellis]] argue that dating *the physical manuscript* is not the same as dating *the idea or tradition it records*. The medium can be young while the content is ancient; the burden is to date the idea, which is hard and far more uncertain than dating papyrus. They add a second, stronger move: the Book of Mormon is itself a **datum** that should be fed into how scholars date the pseudepigrapha, rather than the pseudepigrapha being used as a gatekeeper deciding whether the Book of Mormon is "allowed" to exist.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The manuscript/idea distinction is real and standard — rate: strong (mainstream method)
The hosts note that before the Dead Sea Scrolls, the oldest Hebrew Bible manuscript was the Leningrad Codex (c. AD 1008), yet no one dates the Old Testament's *composition* to the 11th century. Likewise the Scrolls (1st c. BC-1st c. AD) attest texts whose ideas are older still. Dating a copy sets only a *terminus ad quem* for transmission, not the origin of the material. *Strong: this is uncontroversial text-critical reasoning; ideas routinely predate their earliest extant witnesses.*[^2]

### 2. Material vs. content analogies — rate: rhetorically apt, logically limited
Butler/Barnes illustrate with anachronistic media: an Elvis song playing on a 2024 iPhone does not date Elvis to 2024; a Kanye beat heard in 2006 may have been recorded earlier. The point: the carrier's date ≠ the content's date. *Moderate — the analogies correctly separate medium from message, but they do not, by themselves, establish that any *particular* idea is early; that still requires argument.*[^3]

### 3. The Book of Mormon as an independent datum — rate: coherent but premise-dependent
If one accepts the Book of Mormon as an ancient record, then [[2 Nephi 11.2-3|Nephi's report]] of an Isaiah ascension vision is *evidence* that such a tradition existed pre-exilically — which should inform (not be overruled by) the dating of the Ascension of Isaiah. "We may reach different conclusions than Schneemelcher… so be it." *Moderate: internally consistent and a fair point about not privileging one scholar's conclusions; but it only carries weight for someone who already grants the Book of Mormon's antiquity, so it cannot be used to *prove* that antiquity without circularity.*[^4]

### 4. Conclusions are revisable; data are primary — rate: sound epistemic caution
The hosts distinguish a scholar's *questions/data* (durable) from their *conclusions* (revisable), warning against treating a 50-year-old consensus as fixed truth, and against "consensus" that is really a large "school of lemmings." *Reasonable as a caution against scientism, though it cuts both ways — it equally warns against treating apologetic reconstructions as settled.*[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic agrees the manuscript/idea distinction is valid *in principle* but presses three points. (a) **It can become unfalsifiable.** "The idea is older than the manuscript" can rescue *any* late text from its date; without independent evidence (a Qumran fragment, a citation in a securely-dated source, a linguistic stratum), the move is a placeholder, not a demonstration. For the Ascension of Isaiah specifically, the "Vision" sections carry developed *Christian* christology (a crucified, resurrected, ascended Son), which is positive evidence the *idea in that form* is not pre-exilic. (b) **It proves too much for the apologist.** If "date the idea, not the manuscript" licenses pushing pseudepigraphal ideas back centuries, the same latitude lets a critic push *Book of Mormon* ideas forward into the 19th century (e.g., "after all we can do" reflecting 1800s grace debates) — the principle is symmetric and does not favor either side. (c) **Using the Book of Mormon to redate the pseudepigrapha is circular** when the parallel is then turned around to authenticate the Book of Mormon. The defensible residue: it is methodologically legitimate to refuse to let a contested manuscript date *automatically settle* a historical question, and to insist that ideas can outrun their earliest copies — but establishing that a *specific* idea is ancient requires independent attestation (which is exactly why the vault leans on Qumran/Jude for Enoch; cf. [[The Enoch Rediscovery Timeline Postdates Joseph Smith]] and [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]]).

## Best Quotes
> "The manuscript on which it is written is different than the ideas written upon it… if you're dating the papyrus you're going to get what you're going to get, but the ideas must have predated it, and it's really hard to date those things."[^3]

> "The Book of Mormon is a datum… so when does the Ascension of Isaiah date to — or, the Book of Mormon has Nephi saying he and Isaiah had the same vision, and that is a datum that should make us think about how we date the Ascension of Isaiah."[^4]

## LDS Connection
This is the methodological backbone under several vault arguments that rely on late-surfacing apocrypha (Enoch/[[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch|Book of the Giants]], the Ascension of Isaiah, the Gospel of Nicodemus): the apologetic only works if a young manuscript can transmit an old idea. Used carefully — paired with independent attestation — it is sound text criticism. Used loosely, it becomes unfalsifiable and, as the steelman notes, symmetric enough to aid critics too. The vault's stronger cases ([[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]]) supply the independent evidence this principle needs.
