# Soft vs Pernicious Anti-Mormonism - The Rhetoric-to-Violence Spectrum

## The Claim
[[Steven Pynakker]] builds his definition of anti-Mormonism by analogy to anti-Semitism, distinguishing a **soft** form (social othering — the country-club exclusion, being eased out of "polite society") from a **pernicious** form whose dehumanizing rhetoric can, at the far end, license violence. The bridge between them is *language*: words like "demonic," "antichrist," and the question "are Mormons even Christian?" do real work, because "ideas and words can have consequences." Pynakker's applied conclusion is that the historically more dangerous rhetoric flows from the Protestant/evangelical anti-Mormon world (the God Makers, [[Mark Driscoll]], Jeff Durbin, Steven Anderson) than from the ex-Mormon community — and that this is why his own reform energy is aimed at *his* side. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The two-tier structure, borrowed from anti-Semitism.** Soft anti-Semitism was social ("you couldn't belong to the country club... eliminated from certain parts of polite society"); pernicious anti-Semitism "led to pogroms and the blood libel" and, ultimately, the death camps. [^1] Pynakker maps the same gradient onto anti-Mormonism. *Rating: moderate-to-strong as a taxonomy;* the soft/pernicious distinction is useful and the analogy is historically grounded, though invoking the Holocaust as the endpoint is rhetorically heavy and risks proving too much.

2. **The escalation thesis: words have consequences.** Pynakker's signature move is the Driscoll-thumbnail parallel: after the Charlie Kirk shooting Driscoll posted AI "slop" asking "are Mormons Christian?"; Pynakker rejoined that "100 years ago, in the 1920s... they were asking the question, are Jews human? I wonder what ever became of that." [^3] The argument is that "this sort of rhetoric... eventually it does end up people going into death camps." [^4] *Rating: contested;* the causal claim that demonizing rhetoric *contributes* to violence is plausible and the timing (rhetoric in the wake of a real massacre) is pointed, but the inference from a provocative thumbnail to genocide is a slippery-slope that the strongest version would need to argue, not assert.

3. **The Michigan shooter as the rhetoric's downstream.** Pynakker repeatedly anchors the thesis in the Grand Blanc massacre: the shooter "was using terminology of they're demonic Mormons, it's antichrist," and entered the meetinghouse believing "Mormons worship Joseph Smith over Jesus." [^5] He argues this is the predictable fruit of "enough propaganda put out by the... evangelical anti-Mormon community" — and that demonic/antichrist framing "is just beyond the pale and it's not Christlike at all." [^6] *Rating: moderate;* a single perpetrator's stated framing is suggestive of how such language can be metabolized by "unhinged people," but it is an anecdote, not evidence of a general rate, as Hansen-style "magnitude" objections would press.

4. **The asymmetry claim and the reformer's self-location.** Pynakker concludes the evangelical world's rhetoric "is much more dangerous than anything I'm seeing from the ex-Mormon community," and that his role is to "bring the temperature down" in his *own* community "which historically has been much more... harder on your people than even the ex-Mormon community has ever been." [^2] He distinguishes well-meaning evangelical interlocutors from "Westboro"-style figures (Driscoll, Durbin, Anderson) who "tend to be bigots in more than just one field." [^7] *Rating: moderate;* the call to police one's own side is ethically strong and connects to [[Extreme Anti-Mormonism Is Reactionary Hatred for Belief Alone|Goldberg's belief-alone diagnosis]], but the comparative ranking ("evangelicals more dangerous than ex-Mormons") is a judgment Hansen contests by pointing to the *volume* and *abuse-framing* of ex-Mormon content.

**Hansen's counter (the steelman against Pynakker).** Hansen resists the asymmetry on two grounds. First, *magnitude*: "in every group of a hundred thousand or a million people, there's someone that's a total lunatic... There's a lot more evangelical Christians than there are ex-Mormons," so extreme cases on the larger side are a base-rate artifact, not proof the rhetoric is worse. [^8] Second, he turns the "rhetoric causes harm" logic on the ex-Mormon side: a steady message that the church is "abusive," "drives people to suicide," and is unsafe for children is *itself* a dehumanizing narrative with consequences (he cites Mormon Stories thumbnails and the "punch Elder Oaks in the face" remark a Dehlin guest made). [^9] The disagreement is therefore not about whether rhetoric matters — both grant it does — but about *which* rhetoric, and at what volume, is doing the damage.

## Best Quotes
> "There was soft anti-Semitism in which you couldn't belong to the country club... but then you have a pernicious anti-Semitism that led to pogroms and the blood libel." [^1]

> "100 years ago, in the 1920s, there were conversations in certain segments of European society in which they were asking the question, are Jews human? I wonder what ever became of that." [^3]

> "We should not be using terms like demonic, antichrist. This is just beyond the pale and it's not Christlike at all." [^6]
