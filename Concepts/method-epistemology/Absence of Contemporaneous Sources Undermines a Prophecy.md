# Absence of Contemporaneous Sources Undermines a Prophecy

## The Claim (Objection)
A sharper, narrower form of the late-reminiscence problem: for several of the most spectacular prophecies, there is a conspicuous *silence* in the contemporary record. If a prediction had truly been fulfilled in dramatic fashion, we would expect believers alive at the time to have trumpeted it. When the earliest mention appears only decades later, the silence itself is evidence against the prophecy's having existed (or having been notable) in the form later claimed.[^1]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **The falling-stars silence.** For the [[Falling of the Stars Prophecy]], the host asks: "Why do we have no one in Joseph Smith's time who is talking about the fulfillment of this prophecy? Surely this would have gotten more attention... if this prophecy really was fulfilled in that way." A miracle that big should have left contemporary traces; it apparently did not. (Evidence rating: strong — an argument from a meaningful, expected silence; compare [[Argument from Silence Is Not Always a Fallacy]].)[^1]
2. **Chained hearsay compounds the problem.** The same account "isn't coming from Philo Dibble himself, but he is recording what was told to him by brother Hancock" — and Dibble's diary is itself dated 1892. So the chain is: event (1833) → Hancock's later telling → Dibble's 1892 record. (Evidence rating: strong; the testimony is both late and secondhand.)[^2]
3. **The Jackson County case is explicitly absent from the record.** For the [[Jackson County Fire and Sword Prophecy]], the "fields and farms and houses... only the chimneys" wording "was not part of the contemporary history of the church" and "appears only in later histories that draw on reminiscences and secondhand testimony many years later." (Evidence rating: strong; concedes the absence directly.)[^3]
4. **The Willard Richards case likewise.** For [[Willard Richards Unharmed in a Hail of Bullets Prophecy]]: "there's nothing contemporaneously written down in 1843, 1844 about this prophecy. This does seem to be later written in to the history of the church." (Evidence rating: strong.)[^4]
5. **Steelman of the believing view.** Arguments from silence are weakest when records are sparse for ordinary reasons. The early Saints were embattled, mobile, and under-documented; many contemporary papers are lost. Absence of a surviving early mention is not the same as proof none existed. Still, the host treats the silence as a real cost, not a fatal blow — he remains "open to it being a fulfilled prophecy."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Why do we have no one in Joseph Smith's time who is talking about the fulfillment of this prophecy? Surely this would have gotten more attention."[^1]

> "There's no contemporary other sources talking about it. This is not even him recording what happened to him firsthand, but he's recording what was related to him by brother Hancock."[^2]

> "There's nothing contemporaneously written down in 1843, 1844 about this prophecy. This does seem to be later written in to the history of the church."[^4]
