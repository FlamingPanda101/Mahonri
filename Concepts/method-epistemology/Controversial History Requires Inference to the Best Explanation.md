# Controversial History Requires Inference to the Best Explanation

## The Claim
Reflecting on Fawn Brodie, Thomas Jefferson, and Columbus, [[Jacob Hansen]] lays out a historical method: for any deeply controversial figure you get **polarized, irreconcilable sources**, and the historian's job is to gather *all* the data and adopt the hypothesis that accounts for the most of it with the fewest wild conjectures — **inference to the best explanation**. The honest historian (his model is [[Don Bradley]]) lays out facts, including the data that cut against his own theory, and resists forcing a verdict. The Restoration apologetic payoff: "he was a prophet" is itself the explanation that absorbs the most data with the least strain.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Controversial figures yield polarized sources (rated: moderate, methodological)
"When you have controversial figures, you end up getting these sides that have very polarized views ... you have to sort through that ... a lot of it relies on figuring out source credibility," which is partly "a judgment call." Imagine writing Donald Trump's history in 50 years from Twitter — that is the difficulty.[^1]

### 2. The honest-historian model (rated: moderate)
Bradley "only wants to ... lay out the facts," gives "the data that doesn't fit what I want it to fit," and when pressed for a conclusion says "I don't know ... all I do is show the pattern." The hosts contrast this with critics who "don't get all the data" — touting evidence against the Book of Mormon while denying any exists for it (e.g. ignoring Nahom).[^1]

### 3. The cumulative inference for the Restoration (rated: apologetic)
"In order to come up with a plausible explanation that accounts for as much of the data as possible," one must handle the witnesses, the text's ancient convergences, and the believers' willingness to die — and "the only way to account for [these] ... is to come up with these wild theories that are way crazier than just saying he was telling the truth." So "he was a prophet" is offered as the parsimonious best explanation.[^1] (Compare [[Inference to the Best Explanation for the Witnesses]] and [[Forensic Reconstruction Eliminates Theories Rather Than Inventing Them]].)

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) inference to the best explanation is sound, but "best" must be scored on *prior plausibility too* — a naturalistic account (even a strained one) may still beat a miraculous one because the prior for "a man received gold plates from an angel" is extremely low, so "fewest conjectures" doesn't automatically favor "prophet"; (b) the hosts apply "get all the data" selectively, foregrounding Nahom and the witnesses while underweighting the data that troubles them (the Book of Abraham papyri, the seer-stone/treasure-digging context); and (c) "they ignore the evidence *for*" is a symmetrical complaint a skeptic levels right back. The shared, defensible core is the *method*: name all the data, weigh source credibility, prefer the most economical explanation — and follow it wherever it leads.

## Best Quotes
> "You get all the facts as best you can understand them and then you come up with a theory that accounts for the data with the least amount of ... wild conjectures." [^1]

> "He never gives it to you ... 'all I'm doing is showing the pattern.' ... that's what a good historian is like." [^1]
