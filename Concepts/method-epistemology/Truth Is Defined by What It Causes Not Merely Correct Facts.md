# Truth Is Defined by What It Causes Not Merely Correct Facts

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Shad Brooks]] argues that truth "actually is far more than simply having correct facts," because truth is also "affected by what the information or facts you present causes."[^1] One can take "correct facts — truths — and leave out a couple of key points of context, arrange them in a certain way" so the hearer reaches "the absolute incorrect conclusion," thereby using true facts to mislead. Conversely, one can tell "a completely fictional story that is not true in the conventional sense, yet teaches something profoundly important and truthful."[^2] Therefore truth "has an essential component in regards to what it causes in other people, in what they end up believing and how they act in the future."[^3] His working definition, stated in a prior stream: **truth is anything that makes you behave more like God; falsehood is anything that makes you behave less like him.**[^4] The strong corollary: "if you use truth to cause someone to be misled, to be deceived, or to commit sin, what you spoke, even if you spoke facts, was not true."[^5]

## Supporting Evidence
- **The Good Samaritan as the test case.** Whether the [[Luke 10.30-37|Good Samaritan]] "factually happened or not is actually completely irrelevant to the reality that it is a very, very truthful story," because of "what it teaches and what it causes."[^6] The Savior "uses fiction to teach very profound truths" — so factuality and truthfulness can come apart. Rate: **the central, clarifying example** — it cleanly separates *correct facts* from *truth*.
- **A lie has two components: intent + effect.** Shad defines a lie as "something with the express purpose and intent of misleading and deceiving someone" *plus* the result "that it causes them to act incorrectly, that makes them move further away from God."[^7] On this definition a figurative parental threat ("if you don't do this, I'll ground you for life") and even Santa Claus are *not* lies, because the intent is to lead the child toward good, not to deceive them for life.[^8] Rate: **the load-bearing move** — it reclassifies figurative and pedagogical speech out of the category "lie."
- **"People dress up just factualism as truth."** [[Ben Kaiser]] and Cardon press that the modern obsession with "correct facts" is not the same as truth in "the bigger word" sense — "real truth, the way that God talks about truth." Paul's "though I... have not charity... I am... sounding brass" (1 Cor. 13) is offered as scriptural support that bare factual truth "doesn't get you anywhere."[^9] Rate: strong internal corroboration that the claim is about *which* concept of truth is primary, not a denial that facts can be correct.
- **Steelman the critic.** This risks collapsing truth into usefulness or consequences — a slide toward pragmatism or "the ends justify the means," where a comforting falsehood could be relabeled "true." The vault's [[Metaphorical Truth as a Useful Fiction]] and [[Sam Harris]]'s rebuttal in [[Metaphorical Truth and Narrative Reading]] press exactly this: "the further you flee the literal text, the more unconstrained your interpretation becomes." Shad's guard is that figurative truth must *still* lead to the genuinely correct understanding and behavior — it is not licence to deceive — but he does not give a sharp criterion separating legitimate figurative truth from rationalized falsehood (see [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]]).

## Best Quotes
> "I can take correct facts — truths — and leave out a couple of key points of context, arrange them in a certain way in which the result is that I can mislead someone completely... and I use truth to achieve it." [^2]

> "Truth as a concept is far more than just correct or incorrect facts. It has an essential component in regards to what it causes in other people, in what they end up believing and how they act in the future." [^3]

> "If you use truth to cause someone to be misled, to be deceived, or to commit sin, what you spoke, even if you spoke facts, was not true." [^5]
