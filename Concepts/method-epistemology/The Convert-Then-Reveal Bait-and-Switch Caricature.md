#type/claim

# The Convert-Then-Reveal Bait-and-Switch Caricature

## The Claim

A favorite Evangelical proselytizing frame — voiced here by [[Mark Driscoll]] — is that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints runs a **bait-and-switch**: missionaries win converts on the appealing common ground (Jesus, grace, family, the good "stuff that you liked"), get them baptized, and only *then* "pull you aside" to reveal that the real religion is works-righteousness and Joseph Smith.[^1] On this telling the Christ-talk is a recruiting veneer and the convert is deceived into "not believing what they actually believe."[^2] The [[All Those In Favor - Three Tactics to Evangelize Mormons|All Those In Favor]] hosts treat this as a caricature that survives only because the critics have never actually talked to a Latter-day Saint about what excites them.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### Why the tactic feels plausible to its users
The strongest version is not pure malice. An Evangelical who holds that grace and works are mutually exclusive will hear LDS grace-language and LDS discipleship-language as a *contradiction*, and will naturally narrate the second as the hidden "true" doctrine concealed behind the first. The "they change the definitions of words" complaint[^3] is the same intuition: it *sounds* like agreement, "but they're not, man." On that prior, a sincere critic can believe a switch is happening even where none is intended — the disagreement is really about whether [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do|grace and discipleship are rivals]] at all.

### The hosts' rebuttal
1. **Nobody hides the distinctives — they volunteer them.** The premise requires Latter-day Saints to be reluctant to discuss what makes them different. The hosts say the opposite is true: "Talk to me about the [[Book of Abraham]]. Talk about the [[Book of Moses]]… Pre-existence, I'll yap about that all day long." "If you come up and ask me about [exaltation], I will go ballistic. That's freaking sick."[^4] A bait-and-switch needs a concealed payload; there is no concealment.
2. **The motivation premise is invented.** "They act like we don't like talking about what makes us different. No — it's like, no dude, talk about the Book of Mormon."[^4] The supposed reluctance is projected, not observed — compare the parallel projection in the [[Dated Anti-Mormon Books Refute the Rebranding Charge|rebranding charge]].
3. **The order is backwards from reality.** Converts encounter the Book of Mormon — "written in 1830 with Jesus on every page"[^5] — at the very *start*, not after baptism. The book that allegedly hides the "real" religion is the first thing handed over.
4. **It conflates anecdote with doctrine.** Where a convert *did* feel blindsided, the honest diagnosis is a cultural or teaching failure, not a doctrinal conspiracy — see [[Conflating Anecdote and Culture With Doctrine Misrepresents the Church]].

### Steelman (the critic's side)
A critic can fairly note that emphasis is real: missionary discussions front-load Christ and family and back-load temple theology, the King Follett material, and exaltation, which *are* taught more fully after commitment. That sequencing is pedagogical (see [[Missionaries Are Tour Guides Not Question Answerers]]), not deceptive — every catechumenate teaches milk before meat — but the critic is right that the *fullest* distinctives come later. The hosts' point stands against the stronger charge: that the early Christ-language is insincere and the later doctrine is *concealed*. It is taught openly, in print, and eagerly.

## Best Quotes

> "Once you get really committed… and then you get baptized, then they pull you aside. They're like, hey, all the stuff that you liked and the reason you joined, we actually don't believe that."[^1]

> "They act like we don't like talking about what makes us different. No, dude — talk about the Book of Mormon. Talk to me about the Book of Abraham, I will go ballistic. That's freaking sick."[^4]

> "This Book of Mormon that was written in 1830 with Jesus on every page — we've just had this thing on the shelf?"[^5]
