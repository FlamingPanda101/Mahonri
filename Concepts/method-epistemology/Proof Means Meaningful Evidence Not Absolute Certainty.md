# Proof Means Meaningful Evidence Not Absolute Certainty

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] flags "proof" as a "problematic term" in everyday epistemic disputes. Appealing to ordinary usage, he argues that "to prove something does not mean that we render it 100 rationally certain"; rather, per the dictionary, "to prove something is merely to provide meaningful evidence for something." [^1] This deflationary reading neutralizes the demand that a claim cannot count as known or proven unless it is established with absolute certainty.

## Argument
1. Some interlocutors treat "proof" as equivalent to 100% rational certainty and use it to dismiss knowledge claims that fall short. [^1]
2. But absolute certainty is essentially impossible (see [[100 Percent Rational Certainty Is Essentially Impossible]]), so that standard would make *nothing* provable. [^2]
3. The actual, dictionary meaning of "prove" is weaker: "to provide meaningful evidence for something." [^1]
4. Therefore a claim can be legitimately "proven" — and known — on the strength of meaningful evidence raising confidence, without ever reaching 100% certainty. This dovetails with treating "know"/"believe" as points on a [[Know and Believe Mark Points on a Confidence Spectrum|confidence spectrum]]. [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Another problematic term is proof. To prove something does not mean that we render it 100 rationally certain. Just look at a dictionary — to prove something is merely to provide meaningful evidence for something." [^1]

> "100 rational certainty is essentially impossible and is not the standard by which we say that we know something." [^3]
