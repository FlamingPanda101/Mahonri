# God Gives Enough Evidence to Seek Not to Compel

## The Claim

Against [[Mike Winger]]'s demand for "irrefutable proof... beyond a shadow of a doubt" before he would consider the [[Book of Abraham]], [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Luke Hansen]] argue that **God deliberately provides enough evidence to invite a seeker, not enough to compel belief** — so a standard of 100% proof misunderstands how God works, and is a standard Winger does not apply to anything else he believes.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Nobody demands 100% proof elsewhere — rate: moderate (epistemology)
The hosts note the inconsistency: people do not demand 100% proof at the pharmacy or in ordinary life, yet when atheists point out there is no 100% proof of God, believers (rightly) appeal to a holistic case — scripture, reason, experience, spiritual confirmation. Winger invokes that holistic epistemology for his own faith but switches to "irrefutable" when Mormonism is in view.[^2]

### 2. Scripture depicts God limiting compelling evidence — rate: moderate (biblical pattern)
If God wanted to *compel*, the risen Christ "could just be walking around today." Instead, per [[Luke 16]] (Lazarus and the rich man), "though one rose from the dead, they will not believe" — even decisive evidence does not force faith. The resurrection is attested by witnesses, but God gives "enough that you decide to seek for yourself spiritually," not a coercive demonstration. See [[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]].[^3]

### 3. Spiritual confirmation is a feature, not a dodge — rate: moderate (framing)
The hosts defend the gospel-topics-essay counsel that the Book of Abraham is "ultimately found through... sincere prayer and the confirmation of the Holy Spirit," arguing this is the same epistemology the New Testament models (Pentecost; hearts "pricked"), not an evidence-free retreat — while conceding feelings *alone* can mislead and must be disciplined.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Winger's underlying worry is legitimate and not fully met. (a) "God gives enough evidence to seek, not to compel" can become *unfalsifiable*: any absence of evidence is reinterpreted as divine pedagogy, which is exactly the move a skeptic distrusts — it can shield *any* religion (including ones the hosts reject) from disconfirmation. (b) There is a real disanalogy between "no 100% proof" (true of everything) and "the central artifact appears to be mistranslated": Winger is not demanding metaphysical certainty but objecting to what he sees as *positive disconfirmation*, which spiritual-confirmation language does not address. (c) If the Holy Spirit's witness is the final arbiter and "can't lie," then contradictory sincere witnesses across religions become a problem the appeal does not solve. (d) The hosts themselves grant feelings can be mistaken, which concedes that spiritual confirmation needs *external checks* — pushing back toward the evidence Winger wants. So the fair core is that Winger's "irrefutable proof or nothing" is an unreasonable and self-inconsistent bar, and a seek-and-confirm epistemology is defensible — while the LDS side still owes an account of how spiritual confirmation is distinguished from error and how it engages, rather than bypasses, the specific evidential objections.

## Best Quotes

> "It seems that God is interested in evidence... but he only wants to give you enough that you decide to seek for yourself spiritually."[^3]

> "You don't ever ask for 100% proof in anything in your life... but then all of a sudden when they're under scrutiny it's like, no, irrefutable."[^2]

> "Though one rise from the dead, they will not believe. So even if you're able to prove something to somebody, they can still choose to believe or not."[^3]
