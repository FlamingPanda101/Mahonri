# Know and Believe Mark Points on a Confidence Spectrum

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that "I know" and "I believe" are not separated by a certainty threshold but are both **reports of subjective confidence** along a continuous spectrum. "Saying you know something is a personal statement about our level of confidence that something is real." [^1] To "know" is simply to have confidence "that is extremely high" — so one should "not be afraid to say that you know something even if that thing is not 100 certain on a rational level." [^2]

## Argument
1. Given that absolute certainty is impossible (see [[100 Percent Rational Certainty Is Essentially Impossible]]), knowing cannot require it. [^3]
2. Both "I know" and "I believe" are first-person statements "to describe our own level of confidence about the reality of something." [^4]
3. Confidence is gradated — "99.999 confident… or 99 confident or 75 percent confident or 60 confident etc." — and "the language we use to describe our level of confidence eventually changes" with the degree of confidence. [^1]
4. At some (vague) point on that spectrum, the natural word shifts from "believe" to "know." The difference is one of degree of confidence, not of a categorical jump to certainty. [^5]
5. Therefore the common claim that "you can't say that you know something unless you are 100 rationally certain… is not true," and high (non-absolute) confidence legitimately licenses the word "know." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Saying you know something is a personal statement about our level of confidence that something is real." [^1]

> "At some level of confidence we switch from saying that we know something to saying that we believe something. The word I believe or I know are statements we make to describe our own level of confidence about the reality of something." [^4]

> "To say you know something is to say that your confidence in the reality of something being real is extremely high so don't be afraid to say that you know something even if that thing is not 100 certain on a rational level." [^2]
