# The Changed-Teachings Gish Gallop Collapses Into a Few Categories

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Anti-Mormon podcaster [[Bill Reel]] presents a rapid-fire list of ~20 "church teachings that have changed" (free agency relabeled moral agency; polygamy ending; no more getting a planet; oral sex; ongoing vs. complete restoration; birth control; masturbation-causes-homosexuality; interracial marriage; less-valiant premortal spirits; Adam-God; the Joseph Smith Translation; alterable temple ordinances; garment protection; the Adamic language; Cumorah's location; the 2015 policy) — to give "the impression that the church has no core unchanging truth." [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Jacob Hansen]] identifies the format as a **Gish Gallop** — a rhetorical dump where "each of these issues individually would really require their own conversation," designed to overwhelm rather than argue (cf. [[Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue]]). Using the [[Doctrine vs Practice vs Culture - The Three-Tier Taxonomy|doctrine/practice/culture taxonomy]] and the [[The Collective Prophetic Witness|collective witness]] standard, the whole list "simplif[ies] into just a few categories": [^2]

1. **Plainly false claims.** E.g., that the Church once taught the restoration was *complete* — when an Article of Faith from Joseph Smith's day explicitly says "many great and important things" are *yet to be revealed*. *Rating: strong on this instance.* [^2]
2. **Not doctrine but practice/policy.** E.g., ending the *practice* of polygamy; changing terminology from "free" to "moral" agency. *Rating: strong; these are application/vocabulary changes.* [^3]
3. **Never canonically endorsed by the whole First Presidency and Twelve.** E.g., the masturbation-homosexuality theory; Adam-God (which [[Orson Pratt]] "opposed very vehemently"). If never ratified by the unanimous body, it was never doctrine. *Rating: strong on the definition; Adam-God's internal opposition is documented.* [^3]
4. **Weak doctrines (one dispensation only).** Even if any item *were* shown to be officially endorsed, it would land in "the weakest of doctrinal categories" — single-dispensation teachings mostly stemming from the two known errors (the Ham/Cain race theory and polygamy-for-exaltation). *Rating: moderate-strong within the model.* [^4]

The decisive observation: **none** of the listed items is a *core* doctrine attested across multiple dispensations. "He didn't talk about any of the core doctrines... well supported and documented throughout the collective witness of God's prophets" — because making "a big deal out of any speculative statement from a church leader has been the go-to anti-Mormon tactic." *Rating: strong as a structural point about what the list omits.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic answers that "it's all just practice / culture / un-ratified speculation" is precisely the **unfalsifiable filter** the method has been accused of: by sorting every changed item into a non-doctrine bin *after* it changed, the Church can claim its *core* never moves while quietly relocating embarrassments. And quantity *can* be evidence: a leadership that repeatedly taught now-disavowed things (some over the pulpit, in official manuals) shows a pattern of fallibility that bears on overall reliability, even if no single item is a "core" doctrine. The Gish-Gallop label, the critic adds, can be a dodge that avoids engaging real instances.

### Counter-objection
Hansen's reply: the sort is *principled*, not post-hoc, because the ruler (cross-dispensational attestation, unanimous ratification) is fixed independently of any particular controversy, and he *invites* item-by-item engagement rather than refusing it — the Gish Gallop objection is to the *format* (20 claims in 60 seconds), not a refusal to discuss. The pattern-of-fallibility point is granted and absorbed: the model never claimed prophetic infallibility, only that the *collective* witness is more reliable than rivals. See [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]].

## Best Quotes
> "This is what's known as a Gish Gallop... a rhetorical tactic where you throw out a bunch of different issues rapidly like this when each of these issues individually would really require their own conversation all their own." [^2]

> "He didn't mention any doctrines that are found across multiple dispensations of time. He didn't talk about any of the core doctrines, the things truly found well supported and documented throughout the collective witness of God's prophets." [^4]
