# Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer

#type/claim

## The Claim
Addressing how local leaders and parents should handle a young person who surfaces a faith challenge, [[Hayden Carroll]] makes a specific pastoral observation: **most youth who bring a hard question to a trusted adult are not, in the first instance, demanding a researched answer — they are checking whether the adult is *okay* with the question.** "Most of the youth who come to adults in the church with questions or concerns actually just want to know if you're okay with it… they want to see what you would say."[^1] The test they are running is whether you "cower" or respond with calm confidence ("Absolutely he did — do you want to talk about it?"). The prescription that follows: don't carry the burden alone, don't rush an answer you don't have, use the church's resources — but *exude confidence*, because the LDS position is strong.[^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "just want to see your reaction" diagnosis — rate: strong, experientially grounded
Carroll frames a concrete script: a youth says "Brother Carroll, I heard Joseph Smith had four wives," and is watching to see "am I going to cower?" When the adult answers without flinching ("Absolutely, he did. Do you want to talk about it?"), the typical response is "No — I just want to see what you would say."[^1] The claim is anecdotal ("this is anecdotal… in my personal experience") but coheres with the wider faith-crisis literature: it is often the *adult's panic*, not the fact itself, that signals to a young person that the fact is dangerous. The corollary danger is **false confidence** — Carroll guards against it: "don't have a false confidence, but you should know the material and the history… and exude some confidence."[^3]

### 2. Don't carry the burden alone — rate: strong, practical
For leaders, the advice is to resist the urge to produce an answer "tomorrow." A leader who "doesn't know anything" should say so and learn *with* the youth — "let's go to your seminary teacher or somebody who knows."[^4] Carroll illustrates with the Church's own video of a girl troubled by polygamy who consulted, in turn, her parents, her bishop, her seminary teacher, and finally her grandfather — none of whom "had all the answers," and that was fine.[^5] The model is a *network* of trusted adults, not a single oracle, which lowers the stakes on any one leader's expertise.

### 3. Confidence flows from the strength of the position, not bravado — rate: balanced
The "exude confidence" counsel only works if it is earned: Carroll ties it to "you've got a very strong position… on any topic. Not to say we don't have hard problems, but we have a pretty good spot."[^3] **Steelman of a worry:** projected confidence can shade into *dismissiveness* — the very "just say a prayer and you'll be fine" hand-wave the panel elsewhere condemns. The note resolves this by distinguishing *posture* from *substance*: confidence is permission for the youth to keep the question open, not a refusal to engage it; the adult still commits to "do you want to talk about it?" and to finding real answers. This is the private-ministry register — gentle, non-combative — that contrasts deliberately with the public-debate teardown in [[Tear Down Worldviews in Public Debate Not Private Ministry]].

## Best Quotes
> "Most of the youth who come to adults in the church with questions or concerns, they actually just want to know if you're okay with it."[^1]

> "They want to see if I'm going to cower, or am I going to say, 'Absolutely he did. Do you want to talk about it?' And usually they're like, 'No, I just want to see what you would say.'"[^1]

> "Don't have a false confidence, but you should know the material and know the history… and exude some confidence, because you've got a very strong position."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This reframes the [[1 Peter 3.15|1 Peter 3:15]] mandate to "give a reason for the hope that is in you" as, first, a matter of *demeanor* — "with meekness and fear," i.e., calm and unafraid. For Latter-day Saint parents and leaders in the home-centered-church era (when the panel argues every dad "should know" apologetics even if he never does it publicly), the load-bearing skill is not encyclopedic recall but a settled confidence that keeps a young person's faith question from hardening into a faith crisis. It pairs with the faith-crisis-as-rebuilding model (see [[Renovation Not Demolition - Faith Crisis as Rebuilding From the Foundation]]): the adult's steadiness is what lets the youth treat doubt as renovation rather than demolition — while the actual conviction is finally the Spirit's work, not the adult's argument (see [[Spirit Over Argument - The Limits of Apologetics]]).
