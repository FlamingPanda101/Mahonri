# The Three Ds Rubric Diagnoses Anti-Mormonism as Bigotry

#type/claim

## The Claim

Anti-religious bigotry can be diagnosed with the same rubric the **Anti-Defamation League (ADL)** uses to flag anti-Semitism — the **three Ds: delegitimization, demonization, and double standards**. **Cardon Ellis** reports getting this rubric directly from the head of the ADL in Israel and argues that [[David Latting]]'s attack on [[Maddy Packer]] satisfies all three in its opening seconds, which moves it out of "criticism" and into bigotry: "you have literally covered two out of three steps of anti-religious bigotry just in the opening two sentences."[^1] The point is to give Latter-day Saints an *objective* test — the same one society already accepts for anti-Semitism — so that anti-Mormonism is named for what it is rather than excused as honest disagreement.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. Delegitimization
Latting opens by saying the Latter-day Saint "completely lacks critical thinking skills" — denying the legitimacy of LDS as thinking believers at all, rather than engaging Packer's actual argument.[^1] Cardon: a critic who "starts out by delegitimizing people… saying they completely lack critical thinking skills" has cleared the first D.

### 2. Double standards
In the same breath Latting "immediately launch[es] into a double standard that cannot be maintained by [his own] church nor the Bible" — demanding LDS pass a Trinitarian/"eternal = self-existent" test that, the panel argues, the New-Testament authors and the ante-Nicene fathers would themselves fail.[^1] Holding Mormons to a standard one's own tradition cannot meet is the second D. See [[The Pre-Bible Post-Bible Epistemology Double Standard]].

### 3. Demonization
The third D is demonization — recast as "you worship a *demon*," the only alternative once you are denied the label Christian. Maddy notes the move directly: "the alternative to you not being a Christian is then you're worshiping a demon."[^2] Cardon's exhibit: within roughly 48 hours of the Grand Blanc / Michigan shooting of LDS worshippers, the [[Mark Driscoll]]s and (per the panel) David Lattings "literally just release a 'Mormons [aren't] Christians' pamphlet to traffic in the human misery of slaughtered worshippers."[^3] If the rhetoric were merely critical it would not pattern-match demonization the way an anti-Semitic equivalent instantly would.

### 4. The parity argument
The rubric's force is its neutrality: society "would recognize it immediately for what it is" if a podcaster engaged Jews "corrosively… every time he did a video on Jews" — he'd be called an anti-Semite.[^4] The same corrosive, "wildly straw-man, meant-to-belittle" pattern aimed at Mormons should earn the same label. See [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea]] and [[Soft vs Pernicious Anti-Mormonism - The Rhetoric-to-Violence Spectrum]].

### Steelman (the critic's reply)
A critic could answer that the three Ds were designed for an *ethnicity*, where belief is not at issue, whereas Mormonism makes contestable doctrinal *truth-claims* that may be legitimately called false — so "delegitimizing" a truth-claim is not the same as delegitimizing a people. The panel's implicit reply is the [[Anti-Mormon Is Public Intentional Repeated Erosion of Faith|stimulus test]]: criticism of the *idea* that updates on evidence is fine; it is the contempt aimed at the *persons*, repeated after correction, that trips the rubric.
