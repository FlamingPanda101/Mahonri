# Linguistic Relativity Shapes Thought

## The Point
The language you speak constrains and shapes *how you think* — your conceptual categories, your moral intuitions, even your sense of what is real. This is the epistemological foundation for the whole Greek-vs-Hebrew thesis: if language shapes thought, then two languages can produce two genuinely different ways of *knowing the world*.

## Argument
1. **The challenge.** A BYU linguistics professor claimed language is "completely irrelevant" to thought — that barriers of language impose no barriers on what can be thought. Jonah (a linguistics graduate) found this "ridiculous" because being fluent in another language "totally changes how you think about things" [^1].
2. **The Russian property example (steelman).** In English the speaker is the *subject* who *has*/*possesses* a thing — "I have it, it's mine." Russian has no such verb; one says the thing "is at me." This removes the speaker from the subject position and makes ownership grammatically slippery — "fantastic for ripping personal property away from the populace" under communism. The same debate is "easy" in Russian and hard in English precisely because of grammar [^2].
3. **The Inuit-snow example.** Where English has the single concept "snow," Inuit has many distinct words — powdery, sleet, icy, drifted, etc. Speakers "readily compartmentalize" into mental bins that English speakers collapse into one. Vocabulary literally changes the granularity of thought [^3].
4. **Vocabulary expands the thinkable.** Learning words is like learning long division after only knowing addition — "there's this whole other world out there." More precise vocabulary gives the *ability* to think more precise, nuanced thoughts; without the words, "you know how smart you are but you can't get it out" [^4].
5. **Whoever controls the words controls the thoughts.** The postmodern insight (and Orwell's, "100 years ago"): if you can control how people talk about a thing, you control what they think about it. Modern example: redefining "woman" changes the very category one reasons with. Using soft words ("had an affair") instead of hard ones ("committed adultery") papers over moral repugnance [^5].
6. **Epistemological upshot.** Therefore language is at least a *symptom of*, and plausibly a *binder on*, a culture's paradigm. So to understand how Hebrews vs. Greeks knew the world, examine their languages — which "turns out big time" different [^6].

## Best Quotes
> "I'm fluent in another language and it totally changes how you think about things... when they said this language has no bearing on your thought parameters, on your paradigm, I thought, you've got to be kidding me." [^1]

> "Language are like the building blocks of the thoughts you have, and if someone controls what those building blocks are, they can control how you think." [^5]
