# The Pre-Bible Post-Bible Epistemology Double Standard

## The Claim
Hayden Carroll's recurring counter to the *sola scriptura* challenger: the Protestant runs **two different epistemologies depending on the date**. *Before* the canon, he freely grants that [[Abraham]], [[Moses]], and the patriarchs knew truth with no book in front of them — by direct divine revelation, taken "by faith." *After* the canon, he insists the 66-book Bible is the sole infallible test of all truth and refuses to let a Latter-day Saint appeal to that same revelatory faculty. If God could communicate truth clearly to a person without a text for the first several thousand years of salvation history, then a fixed text cannot be the *necessary* mechanism of knowing truth — and the standard by which the Evangelical judges Joseph Smith is one he himself abandons the moment he discusses Abraham.[^1] The challenge is symmetrical, not a denial of scripture: Hayden affirms scripture's value but denies it is the *ground* of knowing.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The patriarchs are the Protestant's own admitted counter-example — rate: strong
Pressed "how do you know what's true before the Bible," the Evangelical concedes the point outright: "Of course, Abraham did not have a book in front of him… we have divine revelation."[^3] [[Moses]] "before he wrote anything down" likewise knew God by direct encounter. This is not a Mormon premise smuggled in; it is the challenger's own theology of [[Genesis 15.6|Genesis 15]] and the call narratives. Once granted, the Latter-day Saint only asks that the *same* faculty — God speaking clearly to a person — remain available after AD 100. See [[Personal Revelation Is the Most Legitimate Path to God]] and [[Gods Modus Operandi - Oral Word Then Committed to Writing]].

### 2. It exposes a self-defeating standard — rate: strong (mirrors the canon-justification problem)
The Evangelical admits the Bible "doesn't say directly" which 66 books are the word of God.[^4] So the rule "test all truth by the canon" is itself a truth *not* delivered by the canon — it had to be known by some pre-textual or extra-textual means, the very means the Latter-day Saint is told is illegitimate. This is the live-authority-vs-fixed-text fault line examined in [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]] and [[Authorization - Divine Designation Required for an Infallible Canon]].

### 3. The challenger's own answer is "by faith… for sure" — rate: rhetorically decisive in context
Asked how Moses' contemporaries verified him, the Evangelical answers "it would have been by faith for sure at that time," then immediately concedes "you admit that we don't need a text necessarily to know that God is speaking."[^5] Hayden's point lands: the concession is fatal to the claim that a text is the indispensable test.

### Steelman of the Evangelical rebuttal — rate: this is the real reply, and it is not weak
The challenger's best answer (only partly voiced here) is a **progressive-revelation / closed-era** distinction, not a flat double standard: revelation operated *foundationally* — patriarchs and apostles received it precisely *in order to give* the scriptures, after which the deposit is complete and the canon becomes the standard against which all *subsequent* claimants are measured ([[Acts 17.10-11|the Bereans tested Paul against the existing OT]]; [[Galatians 1.8|"though an angel… let him be accursed"]]). On this view there is no inconsistency: foundation-layers (Abraham, Moses, Paul) and superstructure-dwellers (us) are simply differently situated in redemptive history. See [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]] and [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]]. The Latter-day Saint's rejoinder is that nothing in scripture *announces* that the foundational era has closed — that, too, is an extra-biblical claim — which is why the dispute ultimately routes back through [[Closing the Canon Was Used to Declare Prophecy Ended]].
