# Anti-Mormons Borrow Atheist Weaponry Against the Plates

## The Claim
[[Cardon Ellis]], [[Jonah Barnes]], and [[Oak Norton]] argue that evangelical countercult ministries like CARM ([[Bill McKeever]]) attack the gold plates using the **same epistemology atheists use against Christianity** — and so saw off the branch they sit on. The atheist fixates on an isolated "weird" miracle (Jonah's whale, a talking donkey) while ignoring that the Bible already asks belief in the Resurrection; identically, CARM fixates on a 40-lb "too heavy" detail while granting the Red Sea and the empty tomb. They also demand of Latter-day Saints a standard of proof — accredited archaeologists, physical evidence — that they would never accept for the Resurrection, while denying Latter-day Saints the appeal to *faith* that Christians make for their own miracles. The proposed exit is [[Hugh Nibley]]'s diamond-in-the-field parable: the only test that settles it is to examine the stone itself — **read the book**.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The selective-miracle move is structurally identical — rate: strong analogy
The atheist asks "how can you believe a fish swallowed a man?" while the same book has a man rise from the dead; the countercultist asks "how could Joseph carry the plates?" while granting [[Exodus 14.17-18|the Red Sea]] and the Resurrection. Both pick a small, vivid item and treat it as decisive, ignoring the load-bearing miracle they already accept. The panel's framing meme: "Brad and Chad" — the cessationist and the atheist on a date, agreeing about Mormons.[^1] *Strong as an exposure of the double standard; rhetorically sharp.*

### 2. The evidential double standard — rate: strong, parallels existing note
Christians answer the skeptic's "where's the evidence for the empty tomb / crossing the Red Sea?" with *faith* — but when Latter-day Saints answer "where's the evidence for Zarahemla / the plates?" the same critics deny them the faith move and demand peer-reviewed archaeology. "There is just as much evidence that the gold plates existed as there is that Jesus Christ was resurrected" — i.e., eyewitness testimony — so a Christian who credits the Resurrection on testimony cannot consistently reject the plates' witnesses. This is the vault's [[Apologists Drop the Resurrection Evidential Standard When Judging the Gold Plates|resurrection-standard]] point.[^2] *Strong: a clean tu-quoque grounded in the witness parity.*

### 3. Nibley's diamond parable: examine the stone — rate: moderate, methodological
[[Hugh Nibley]]'s parable (a man finds a diamond in his field; psychologists, historians, geologists, and clergy all weigh in on the *finder* and on *precedent*, but only the jeweler Snite points out the stone is "still available for examination" and should simply be tested) reframes the whole debate: questions about who found it, whether he was sane, or whether diamonds occur in fields are irrelevant to whether *this stone* is a diamond. Applied: stop arguing about plate weight and Joseph's character and **read the Book of Mormon** ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni's promise]]; "by their fruits ye shall know them").[^3] *Moderate: a method/values point, not itself evidence, but a coherent reframe.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's best replies: (a) **the analogy is not symmetric** — the Christian's central miracle (the Resurrection) has, the apologist claims, unusually strong multiply-attested early testimony and was the foundation of a movement willing to die for it, whereas the gold-plates witnesses were a smaller, more socially entangled group, so "just as much evidence" overstates parity; the critic isn't being arbitrary in weighting them differently. (b) **"Read the book" cuts both ways** — a skeptic can read the Book of Mormon and find anachronisms, KJV dependence, and 19th-century concerns, so the diamond parable's "just test the stone" invites exactly the historical-critical examination apologists often resist. (c) **Demanding evidence is not atheism** — asking for corroboration of empirical claims (a buried metal artifact) is ordinary historical method, not a borrowed atheist trick; the Resurrection is a theological claim accepted partly on different (revelatory) grounds, so the "double standard" charge equivocates between historical and faith claims. A Latter-day Saint reply (the episode's): the point is consistency, not anti-evidentialism — the *same* person who grants the Resurrection on eyewitness testimony while denying the plates' eyewitnesses *is* applying a double standard (see [[Witness Weight Estimates of the Plates Refute the Solid-Gold Strawman]] and [[Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates]]); and "read the book" is precisely an invitation to test, with the spiritual witness ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]]) as the decisive, non-archaeological confirmation the tradition has always claimed.

## Best Quotes
> "They use the same weaponry that the atheists use against believers… where's the evidence for crossing the Red Sea, where's the evidence that Jesus Christ rose from the grave? And Christians say, 'I have faith.' But then they turn to us and… we're not allowed to say we have faith."[^2]

> "There is just as much evidence that the gold plates existed as there is that Jesus Christ was resurrected."[^2]

> "The answer… has absolutely nothing to do with who found it or whether the finder was honest or sane… but was to be answered simply and solely by putting the stone to certain well-known tests." — Nibley's parable[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the methodological capstone of the gold-plates episode, sitting above the evidential note [[Witness Weight Estimates of the Plates Refute the Solid-Gold Strawman]] and the logical note [[The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument]]: it diagnoses *why* the "too heavy" attack feels off — it borrows a naturalist standard the Christian critic doesn't actually hold — and redirects to the only dispositive test, reading the book ([[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits|judge it by its fruits]]). Honest takeaway: the consistency charge lands hardest on a *Christian* critic who credits the Resurrection on testimony; against a thoroughgoing skeptic the "just as much evidence" parity is contestable, and "read the book" genuinely invites the critical reading apologists must then also answer.
