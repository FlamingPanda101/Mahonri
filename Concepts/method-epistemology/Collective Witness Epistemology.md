# Collective Witness Epistemology

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s model of knowledge: we never possess 100% rational certainty about anything, so knowing is a matter of *graded confidence*. That confidence is supplied by a set of faculties that function like **independent witnesses** to reality. The more witnesses — and the better-suited their nature — that corroborate a proposition, the higher our justified confidence that it "conforms to reality." Hansen names the model **collective witness epistemology** and notes it has "profound links" to restoration doctrine, while arguing for it on purely rational grounds.[^1]

## Argument
1. **Premise (from [[René Descartes]]):** Perfect rational certainty is impossible; to say one "knows" X is to assert high confidence, not certainty. See [[100 Percent Rational Certainty Is Essentially Impossible]].[^2]
2. **The faculties are witnesses.** Five faculties — intuition, outcomes/fruits, sense data, reason, and authority — each independently testify to the nature of reality, and we use all of them daily to navigate the world. See [[The Five Witnesses of Reality]].[^3]
3. **Courtroom analogy.** Like a judge hearing one witness describe an accident, you have some confidence; when four more witnesses independently describe the same thing the same way, your confidence rises. This is how knowledge works — by the *number and nature* of corroborating witnesses, never by attaining certainty.[^4]
4. **Witnesses are domain-specific and can conflict.** Different witnesses suit different questions; sometimes they disagree (e.g., sense data says time does not slow with speed, but scientific authority says it does), forcing a reduction in confidence in the contested proposition.[^5]
5. **Everyone already appeals to them.** When anyone justifies a claim, they appeal to one or a combination of these witnesses; an arbitrary opinion is one that cannot be backed by any of them. Hansen illustrates with the chain reason → authority → outcomes used to justify "the universe is billions of years old."[^6]
6. **Completeness claim (steelman).** A model that accounts for *all* the witnesses captures reality more fully; those who ignore some witnesses, or over-emphasize/misapply them, end up with a distorted map "even if they're quite sincere" — "as if they will have one eye closed."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "We never have a hundred percent rational certainty, but we can be more or less sure of any given proposition based on the number and nature of the witnesses that convey to us that this is real." [^4]

> "People essentially always look back and appeal to one of these five witnesses or some combination of these witnesses to back up what they're saying and justify what they say they know is true." [^6]

> "It's as if they will have one eye closed to the full scope of the rich and textured reality that we exist in." [^7]
