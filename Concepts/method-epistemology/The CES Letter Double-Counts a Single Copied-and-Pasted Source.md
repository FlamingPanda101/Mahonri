# The CES Letter Double-Counts a Single Copied-and-Pasted Source

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The [[Jeremy Runnells|CES Letter]] presents two separate Palmyra residents who reportedly said [[Martin Harris]] saw the plates "with the eye of faith," implying independent corroboration.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "two" sources are one
"When you follow the sources you'll find that these two references are actually one and the same." What looks like multiple attestation is a single report counted twice.[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a checkable claim about the CES Letter's own citations).

### 2. The duplication came from uncritical copy-paste
"It appears that this quote was just copied and pasted from this anti-Latter-day Saint website, and the author of the CES Letter probably didn't bother to check the sources." The doubling is an artifact of secondhand sourcing, not of the historical record.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the inference of copy-paste is plausible and explains the duplication, though it is reconstructive).

### 3. It is a pattern, not a one-off
"This isn't the only time this happens" — the episode treats the duplication as a representative sourcing failure that "should raise some red flags" about the document's reliability as a whole.[^2] This connects to the broader point that contradictory quotes must be *weighed*, not merely tallied — see [[Contradictory Sources Must Be Weighed Not Merely Counted]] and [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the generalization is asserted here; the specific instance is solid).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A defender of the CES Letter can note that it is a layperson's annotated list of concerns, never claiming each citation is independently vetted, and that a single duplicated citation does not refute the underlying point that *some* contemporaries described Harris's witness as visionary. The strongest neutral takeaway: the double-count is a real methodological error that inflates the apparent weight of the "eye of faith" tradition, and it rightly shifts the burden back onto anyone treating the CES Letter as a reliable source-aggregator — but it does not by itself dissolve every visionary-language datum.
