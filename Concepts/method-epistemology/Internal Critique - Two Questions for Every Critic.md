# Internal Critique — Two Questions for Every Critic

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] gives two opening questions to put to any hostile critic of the Church: **(1)** "Are you able to show that my worldview is wrong *without presupposing your own*?" and **(2)** "Can you give an *internal critique* of my worldview?" He argues these are functionally the same demand and that Protestants "cannot do it" — every critique they offer ultimately assumes their own standard.[^1] An *internal critique* (a concept Carroll borrows from [[Greg Bahnsen|Bahnsenian]] presuppositionalism) means showing a worldview to be *incoherent on its own terms*, without importing outside assumptions.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Internal critique is the gold standard of worldview argument — rate: strong
Carroll's definition is correct: the most powerful refutation of a system is to show it self-destructs by its own commitments (e.g., "if your epistemology were true, you couldn't know it's true"). This is exactly how Bahnsen and [[Cornelius Van Til|Van Til]] argued against atheism. By asking the critic for an internal critique, Carroll forces them to either find a genuine LDS self-contradiction or concede they are merely asserting their own framework. See [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]]. **Steelman of the critic:** a sophisticated Protestant *can* attempt internal critiques of LDS theology — e.g., alleged tensions between an embodied, progressing God and scriptural claims of divine immutability, or between continuing revelation and a "false prophet" test. Whether these succeed is contestable, but the bare claim that "nobody can give an internal critique" is too strong; the Q&A even shows an attendee groping for one.[^2]

### 2. Carroll concedes the LDS side owes the same — rate: balanced
Pressed in Q&A on what an internal critique of LDS belief might look like, Carroll admits the move cuts both ways and distinguishes it from the *epistemological* question "how do you know your prophet is a prophet?" (which he calls "a whole different tangent").[^3] His simplification: an internal critique is "show me that I'm wrong without presupposing things that are unjustified... justify that you're right first."[^4] This is the same engine as [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]] and [[The Reversibility Test for Bad Arguments]].

### 3. The strategic payoff — rate: moderate
Carroll lists what the two questions accomplish: they (a) expose the critic's hidden "I'm right so you're wrong"; (b) force them to justify presuppositions before deploying them; and (c) display that the LDS worldview is internally consistent.[^5] **Caution:** (c) does not follow from (a)–(b). Defeating an opponent's argument shows *their* case fails, not that *yours* is internally consistent — that is a separate burden. Carroll's framing risks treating a successful disarm as a positive proof of LDS coherence, which is a non sequitur the alert listener should flag.

## Best Quotes
> "Are you able to show that my worldview is wrong without presupposing your own?"[^1]

> "Can you give an internal critique of my worldview? ... which nobody can as far as I'm concerned."[^6]

> "All it means is not assuming your own worldview first."[^7]

> "When they have argument 'I'm right, you're wrong,' the internal critique is show me that I'm wrong... without presupposing things that are unjustified."[^4]
