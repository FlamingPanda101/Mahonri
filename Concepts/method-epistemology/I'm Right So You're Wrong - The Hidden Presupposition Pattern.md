# I'm Right So You're Wrong — The Hidden Presupposition Pattern

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that nearly every argument against the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints reduces to a single defective form: **"I'm right, so you're wrong."** The words are almost never said aloud, but the *meaning* is smuggled in as a hidden premise — the critic assumes their own worldview is the standard, then concludes the Church fails to meet it. The fix is to refuse the false premise and require the critic to justify their standard *before* using it as a measuring stick.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The pattern is a buried syllogism with an unjustified major premise — rate: strong (as method)
Carroll reconstructs the typical attack as: (P1) *my worldview is the standard*; (P2) *your teaching conflicts with my worldview*; (C) *therefore you are wrong.* The argument is **valid** but **unsound**, because P1 is an unproven faith claim doing the work of a proof.[^1] This is a genuinely useful diagnostic and mirrors a standard move in informal logic — surfacing a suppressed premise. See [[Validity vs Soundness - Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails]]. **Steelman of the critic:** some critiques are *not* mere question-begging — e.g., "your historical claim X is falsified by evidence Y" is an evidential argument, not a presupposition. Carroll concedes "I could conceive of some" such arguments but estimates "99%" reduce to the I'm-right pattern.[^2] That 99% figure is rhetorical, not measured, and is the weakest link: a method that pre-classifies almost all opposition as question-begging risks the same self-sealing it accuses others of.

### 2. Worked examples generalize the pattern — rate: moderate
Carroll applies it across domains: the "demonic inspiration" charge against Joseph Smith ("any miracle that invalidates my worldview was caused by the devil"),[^3] the Trinity test ("you must believe the Trinity to be a Christian"),[^4] and even a spousal argument ("you did something wrong, therefore you're sleeping on the couch").[^5] The breadth shows the form is real; it also shows the move is content-neutral and can be wielded by *any* side — which is precisely Carroll's point about reversibility. See [[The Reversibility Test for Bad Arguments]].

### 3. The double-edge: it cuts toward LDS claims too — rate: framing / caution
The same demand ("justify your standard first") applies to the LDS appeal to living prophets and the Book of Mormon. Carroll's defense is that the Latter-day Saint standard is *separately justified* ("we justify our standard of prophets through [the Book of Mormon]"),[^6] and he frames a Muslim/Quran and an LDS/prophets parallel to admit the symmetry: "It's bad apologetics when you are simply saying someone is wrong because you are right without justifying your standard first."[^7] **Scholarly context:** this is the classic problem of the *criterion* (Sextus Empiricus / Roderick Chisholm) — every worldview eventually rests on first commitments it cannot non-circularly prove. Carroll's method is strongest as a *leveler* (everyone owes a justification) and weakest if used to imply only the opponent has presuppositions.

## Best Quotes
> "This is his argument in a nutshell... I'm right, so you're wrong. And if you don't disprove my faith-based claims, which is impossible, by the way, then I win the debate."[^8]

> "It's manifested in so many ways. By the way, it's never said like that. Those actual words are never said, but the notion or the meaning of that phrase comes through all the time."[^1]

> "It's bad apologetics when you are simply saying someone is wrong because you are right without justifying your standard first."[^7]

> "You must justify your standard before you can use it against me. And they cannot. That's why it's so effective."[^9]
