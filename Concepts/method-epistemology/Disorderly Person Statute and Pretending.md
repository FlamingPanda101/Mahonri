# Disorderly Person Statute and Pretending

#type/claim

## The Claim
The 1826 charge against [[Joseph Smith]] was almost certainly grounded in New York's **disorderly-person statute** — a catch-all "spitting on the sidewalk" law — under the clause about *pretending* to find lost things. The word "pretending" reflects post-Enlightenment legal categories that classify seer-stone seeing as impossible *a priori*, so the charge says nothing about whether Joseph could actually do what he claimed.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The statute is a catch-all (contextual).** The disorderly-person statute lists ~10 disfavored behaviors; it functions as the "we don't want them in our town" law used "to get rid of someone who hasn't really actually done anything wrong."[^2] *Rating: moderate — characterizes the legal instrument accurately per the JP manual.*

2. **The "pretending to find" clause (specific).** One listed item is "pretending to find stuff" that is lost; this fits Joseph's use of seer stones to locate lost objects and buried treasure, so "this is probably where the charge was getting traction."[^3] *Rating: moderate — inference from later accounts plus statutory text; not 100% certain.*

3. **"Pretending" is a legal-metaphysical verdict, not a factual one (key analysis).** "In the eyes of law, people don't actually find stuff with seer stones, so it's categorically classified as pretended."[^4] The irony: Joseph testified it was *not* pretend ("I can really see in stones") and [[Josiah Stowell]] corroborated him — so the defendants insisted on the reality the statute defines as impossible.[^5] *Rating: strong — dissolves the "con man" inference by exposing the statute's built-in assumption.*

4. **Steelman.** A critic can fairly note that 19th-century law is still law, and society did treat seer-stone money-digging as a punishable nuisance; the statute reflects a genuine social judgment, not merely a technicality. Mark concedes this is exactly the point historians critique as Enlightenment "intolerance towards ordinary people's folk beliefs." See [[Presentism in Folk Belief Judgment]].[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "It's like the spitting on the sidewalk catch-all statute they use to get rid of someone who hasn't really actually done anything wrong."[^2]

> "In the eyes of law, people don't actually find stuff with seer stones. So it's categorically classified as pretended."[^4]

> "In Joseph's testimony ... 'No, it's not pretend. I can really see in stones.' And ... Josiah Stowell's like, 'No, no, it's not pretending. He really saw my place. It's for reals.'"[^5]
