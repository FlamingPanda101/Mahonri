# Stripping Subjectivity From the World Produces Nihilism

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] argues that the scientific "objective truth" maneuver — treating all subjectivity as bias to be eliminated — is "insanely powerful" for gaining control over the world, but carries an **emergent cost**: a world description with all subjectivity stripped out leaves *you*, an irreducible subject, "isolated" among "impersonal facts." The psychological consequence is **nihilism** — the felt sense of being just one unimportant object among objects. The older "mythological landscape" did not pose this problem because every being had a place in a meaningful plan. Hansen uses this as authority-witness that pure objectivism is impoverished and that subjectivity (and hence a witness-based, meaning-laden epistemology) is not mere error.

## Argument
1. Since the dawn of science (and before) we have tended to see ourselves as "subjects in a world of objects" — a "very useful" stance, proven so by science's power to "extend control over the world." [^1]
2. The objective-truth method assumes "everyone's perceptions can be contaminated by their own biases and fantasies," and overcomes this by "stringently specifying the conditions under which an observation takes place" — the experimental method, multiple independent observers, and looking for "commonalities across them." [^1]
3. The commonalities "constitute a description of the objective world," and this has been "insanely / crazily powerful." [^1]
4. But there is an "emergent problem": science "considers anything subjective a form of bias or error… and then gets rid of it," so "what you're left with when you formulate the scientific world is a world that's stripped of subjectivity." [^2]
5. "The problem with that is that **you're a subject**" — so stripping the world of subjectivity "leaves you isolated… with no necessary connection to objective reality in the midst of a set of impersonal facts." [^2]
6. This has "psychological consequences": it becomes "easier to develop a nihilistic sense of being" if you believe you are "fundamentally an object among many and not a particularly important one at that." [^3]
7. Prior to the scientific worldview, people were "embedded in… a mythological landscape" where "every element of being had its place" in a "meaningful plan," so "the meaningfulness of life was not necessarily such a pressing intellectual concern." [^3]
8. **Steelman of the objector.** A scientific realist would respond that the psychological discomfort of nihilism is no evidence against the *truth* of the objective picture — reality owes us no meaning, and bracketing subjectivity is a methodological move, not a metaphysical denial that subjects exist. Peterson's reply is not that objectivism is false but that it is *incomplete*: because the inquirer is ineliminably a subject, a worldview that has no place for subjectivity mis-describes the situation of the knower and predictably breeds nihilism.

## Best Quotes

> "To formulate the idea of an objective truth has been an extraordinarily useful maneuver… you can overcome [bias] by stringently specifying the conditions under which an observation takes place… and then look for commonalities across them… and that's been insanely powerful, crazily powerful." [^1]

> "What science does is consider anything subjective a form of bias or error in the observation, and then get rid of it. And so what you're left with when you formulate the scientific world is a world that's stripped of subjectivity. Now the problem with that is that you're a subject." [^2]

> "When you strip the world of its subjectivity, that sort of leaves you isolated, like an isolated being with no necessary connection to objective reality in the midst of a set of impersonal facts… it's easier to develop a nihilistic sense of being." [^3]
