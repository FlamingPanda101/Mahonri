# Faith Has Become a Debate Sport Not a Salvation Effort

## The Claim

The organizing thesis of the [[All Those In Favor - Three Tactics to Evangelize Mormons|All Those In Favor]] episode is a contrast in **motivation**. Stacking [[Mark Driscoll]] and [[Sean McDowell]] (Evangelicals) against **Noah**, a young Roman Catholic, the hosts argue the two Evangelicals are not actually trying to save anyone — "their faith has just become a debate sport," a contest to "score points in a debate," with "no religious motivation."[^1] The diagnostic is what they would *replace* an LDS testimony with: they try to break a Latter-day Saint's faith in Christ by deploying "some crazy thing from history… true or untrue," but "are you going to replace it with anything? No, that's it."[^2] The Catholic, by contrast, "clearly defined his goal" — he wants Latter-day Saints to become Catholics — which the hosts respect as an honest, salvation-directed aim even though they reject it.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The tell is the absence of a replacement
The hosts grant the Evangelicals may have sincere personal faith; their charge is narrower and behavioral: "it's very obvious in those first two examples that they don't care about our faith… it doesn't seem like you care about us believing in Christ at all. It seems like you just want to tear something down."[^3] An evangelism aimed at *saving* would offer Christ to replace what it dismantles; an evangelism aimed at *winning* only needs the target to lose. The same diagnostic underlies [[Anti-Mormonism Is Attacking the Person Not the Idea]].

### 2. Noah models the alternative: public retraction
What earns the hosts' praise is something they say they had "never in my whole life as a member of the church seen": a critic who went on record to *correct his own past mistakes* about LDS belief.[^4] They name the virtues explicitly — "that's humility, that's not wanting to bear false witness, that's love thy neighbor, that's pray for your enemies"[^5] — and note he did the reading, having "read at least one apologetic Mormon source published in the last 30 years."[^6] This is the charitable-but-critical posture (see [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]]) practiced by an outsider against the LDS rather than by a believer toward his own tradition.

### 3. The reciprocity move
Rather than score against Noah, the hosts reciprocate his charity — "Go steelman the papacy," urging viewers to defend Catholic doctrine to someone who rejects it[^7] — and openly state that "we would definitely be Catholics" if the Church "were to disappear tomorrow."[^8] The contrast is the argument: charitable, goal-honest evangelism invites the same in return; point-scoring invites only counter-attack.

### Steelman (the Evangelical's side)
A defender of Driscoll or McDowell can fairly object that this measures sincerity by tone and that polemic is a legitimate mode: exposing what one believes are soul-destroying errors *is* a salvation effort, even when it sounds combative, and the "no replacement offered" charge ignores that both men preach Christ crucified elsewhere — the clips are rebuttal segments, not full gospel presentations. He can add that the hosts' warmth toward the Catholic is easier precisely because Catholicism shares more LDS premises (sacraments, synergism, apostolic authority), so the "debate sport" label may track *agreement* more than *charity*. The hosts' reply is implicit but real: the test they apply is not politeness but whether the critic engages the actual LDS position (Noah did; the Evangelicals recycled 1830s talking points and discredited sources), and whether the aim is to leave the hearer *with* Christ or merely without a testimony.

## Best Quotes

> "Their faith has just become a debate sport. That's all they care about. It's not about salvation. It's not a religious motivation."[^1]

> "Let me see if I can break down your faith in Christ. Well — okay. Are you going to replace it with anything? No, that's it."[^2]

> "This is the only time I've ever heard someone say, hey guys, I got a lot wrong about you guys, and I want to go on the record and correct some of the things I said. That is Christian. That's humility."[^4]
