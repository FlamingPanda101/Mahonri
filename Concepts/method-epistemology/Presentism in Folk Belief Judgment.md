# Presentism in Folk Belief Judgment

#type/claim

## The Claim
Judging [[Joseph Smith]]'s seer stones, divining rods, and treasure seeking as "silly" or "stupid" commits **presentism** — evaluating past practices through a modern scientific lens. Mocking the practices is not historical analysis; historians refuse to call past folk beliefs silly, recognizing that future generations will view *us* the same way.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **"Silly" is not a historical category (method).** Asked how a prophet could use divining rods, Mark resists the framing: many past beliefs about science and medicine "were just flat-out wrong" and even "harmful," but "are they silly? Are they stupid? Historians don't think like that."[^2] *Rating: strong — disciplinary stance.*

2. **The symmetry argument (key).** "Two centuries from now, people are going to look back on us and have the same kind of natural reaction — 'oh, that's weird, that's strange, that's stupid.'" Presentism is a failure of imagination about one's own contingency.[^3] *Rating: strong.*

3. **Enlightenment intolerance, not enlightenment (reframe).** If Joseph *was* convicted, most historians would read it as "intolerance towards ordinary people's folk beliefs and practices" — Enlightenment rationalism "trickling down" and being weaponized by "social elites against ordinary common folks."[^4] The village-elite editor Abner Cole and the rationalist justice of the peace embody this cultural divide against the rural Smiths.[^5] *Rating: strong — situates the critique in class/cultural history.*

4. **Avoid presentism even on hard cases.** Mark links the principle to slavery law and women's legal standing of the era — we critique those laws without flattening the people into mere villains, the same charity owed to folk-belief practitioners.[^6] *Rating: moderate — analogical.*

5. **Steelman.** A critic can object that anti-presentism can become an all-purpose excuse: if no past practice may be called mistaken, then no historical moral or epistemic progress can be acknowledged, and frauds get a permanent pass. Mark guards against this — he affirms some past beliefs *were* wrong and harmful; the claim is only that *"silly/stupid"* is a manipulative label, not an analysis, and that practices must be understood in context before being judged.

## Best Quotes

> "Are they silly? Are they stupid? ... Historians don't think like that."[^2]

> "Two centuries from now, people are going to look back on us and have the same kind of ... reaction: 'Oh, that's weird. That's strange. That's stupid.'"[^3]

> "That's showing intolerance towards ordinary people's folk beliefs and practices ... Enlightenment rationalism kind of trickling down."[^4]
