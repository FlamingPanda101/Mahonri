# No Infallible Source Only Reliable Guides

## The Claim
Pressed with the obvious objection — "if no book is infallible, doesn't everyone just do whatever they want?" — [[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] answer that the Latter-day Saint epistemology offers **no infallible source but several reliable ones**. There is no single error-proof authority (not the Bible, not the prophet); instead the seeker triangulates among scriptures, living prophets, the Holy Spirit, study, and personal revelation. The system is deliberately *hard* — "the only wrong answer is the easy answer."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The demand for an infallible source is the trap (rate: moderate, framing).** Hansen presses: canons exist "to create a standard by which people could judge truth from error," so "if it's not the Bible, then what is it?" Barnes' reply: those books were compiled "to make it easy, and ... life is hard. You have to take all the witnesses from wherever you can get it ... use your own intuition." See [[Scribalism Is the Idolatry of an Infallible Book]].[^2]

2. **Reliable, not infallible (rate: strong, the distinction).** "So there's no infallible source?" "No infallible — but there are reliable sources." Scripture, "as a collective body, when properly understood in context with one another, while not infallible still gives you the best picture as to what God has done." See [[Reliability Not Infallibility Standard]].[^3]

3. **Multiple witnesses replace one trump card (rate: moderate, method).** "You listen to authorities, you listen to the spirit, you pray and you stay sincere ... you give grace — that's how you find it." This is the five-mediums triangulation model: no single channel is a trump card. See [[Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of Revelation]].[^4]

4. **Prophets are reliable guides, not idols (rate: moderate).** "Don't replace it with an idol of a prophet. Russell Nelson is not God." Prophets are "not perfect but reliable guides ... an iron rod" to hold while you "press forward and figure things out through the mist." See [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]].[^5]

5. **The hard system explains apostasy (rate: framing).** "No wonder people apostatized ... that's a way harder system. Just give me a book ... and I can believe that I'm saved." The ease of book-worship is its seduction; the difficulty of the relational model is a feature, because "the wrong answer is the easy answer."[^6]

## Steelman of the Critic
"Reliable guides, no infallible source" faces a sharp adjudication problem. (a) Without a final court, **how do you settle disputes** when scripture, the Spirit, study, and the prophet seem to conflict? Hansen's own question — "you still need a standard by which to judge" — is not fully answered by "use intuition and pray," which can license the very subjectivism it warns against (the sola scriptura side presses exactly this; see the canon debate-map cluster). (b) The model can be **unfalsifiable**: any error in scripture is "it's not infallible," any error in a prophet is "prophets are fallible," so nothing counts decisively against the system. (c) Appealing to a "living prophet" as the stabilizer reintroduces an authority that *functions* as near-infallible in practice ("follow the prophet"), in tension with "he's not God." (d) A Protestant argues a fixed, public text is a *better* check on private whim and on leaders than an open, Spirit-plus-prophet model. The defensible core is the genuine philosophical point that no source is self-interpreting or self-authenticating, so *some* triangulation is unavoidable; the unresolved cost is the lack of a decision procedure when the reliable guides disagree.

## Best Quotes

> "So there's no infallible source?" — "No infallible. But there are reliable sources, of course. And that's why you still respect the scriptures: because they, as a collective body, while not infallible, still give you the best picture as to what God has done."[^3]

> "Any answer that is 'this is the easy way' is the wrong answer. ... The only wrong answer is the easy answer."[^6]
