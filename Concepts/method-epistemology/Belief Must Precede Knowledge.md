# Belief Must Precede Knowledge

## The Claim
The purpose of the LDS Truth Claims course is **not** to prove the church true beyond doubt, but to present "a fuller case" so a person's decision is more informed and their belief has "more worth and value." Drawing on [[Alma the Younger]] in [[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]], the episode holds that belief is more valuable to God than compelled "sure knowledge," and that rational argument only maintains "a climate in which belief may flourish" [^1][^2].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Alma 32 framework
When people demand a sign so they "can know of a surety, and... don't have to believe," Alma pushes back that "belief must precede knowledge" — sign-compelled certainty has less spiritual worth than freely-chosen faith [^2]. *Rating: an accurate reading of Alma 32's "experiment upon the word"; theologically internal to LDS scripture.*

### 2. The "climate for belief" thesis
> "Rational argument does not create belief, but it maintains a climate in which belief may flourish."

Evidence (including the witnesses) is positioned as a support for faith, not a substitute for it [^3]. *Rating: a coherent fideist-friendly epistemology that pre-empts the demand for proof.*

### 3. Function within the episode
This framing matters because it sets the bar the witness case must clear: not mathematical proof, but enough to make belief "reasonable" — the recurring verdict word applied to each witness [^1].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A critic notes this **insulates the claim from disconfirmation**: if evidence only "maintains a climate" and belief is supposed to precede knowledge, then failures of evidence can always be reabsorbed as opportunities for faith. The co-host's running objection — sincerity/belief does not establish truth — applies here too: prioritizing belief over verification is exactly the posture a naturalist would expect to produce sincere-but-mistaken witnesses.

## Best Quotes
> "The purpose of this course is not to convince you beyond a doubt that the church is true... rather, to present to you a fuller case so that your decision can be more informed." [^1]

> "Belief must precede knowledge, or sure knowledge." [^2]

> "Rational argument does not create belief, but it maintains a climate in which belief may flourish." [^3]
