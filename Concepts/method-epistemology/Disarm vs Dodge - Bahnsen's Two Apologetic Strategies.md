# Disarm vs Dodge — Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] adopts a teaching illustration from Reformed presuppositionalist [[Greg Bahnsen]] to frame the whole course. Imagine you are locked in a room with a man holding a loaded revolver. You can prepare in two ways: practice **dodging the bullets** (learning to answer each individual attack) or practice **disarming the gunman** (taking the weapon — i.e., dismantling the worldview that gives the attacks their force). Bahnsen — and Carroll — argue disarming is the more practical strategy. Carroll's stated method is therefore to "push them on theirs" — challenge the critic's presuppositions before defending his own.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The source is a credentialed presuppositionalist used against his own camp — rate: rhetorically strong, ironic
The illustration comes from [[Greg Bahnsen]], second-generation [[Cornelius Van Til|Van Tilian]] presuppositionalist, recorded in a lecture (Carroll dates it "probably the 80s or 90s").[^2] Carroll relishes the irony that an *evangelical* method is "very easy to take the gun out of his hand" — i.e., he turns presuppositionalism back on Protestants.[^3] **Steelman of Bahnsen:** Bahnsen's own claim is the reverse — that presuppositional apologetics "destroys every worldview except" those with a prophet, a revelation, and a book; Carroll repeats Bahnsen's line that the two coherent worldviews left standing are "Mormons and the Muslims."[^4] (Caution: this is Carroll's paraphrase of a remembered clip, not a verified Bahnsen quotation, and Bahnsen the Calvinist would not have endorsed LDS theology — the "compliment" is being borrowed selectively.)

### 2. Dodging is still necessary for genuine questions — rate: balanced
Carroll does not reject evidential "bullet-dodging." He lists the standard LDS "bullets" (Book of Abraham, polygamy, race and the priesthood, Freemasonry, the King Follett discourse, First Vision accounts, alleged false prophecies) and credits organizations like FAIR for teaching answers.[^5] The distinction is *situational*: a sincere questioner gets answers (dodge); a hostile attacker gets disarmed. See [[Genuine Seekers vs Bullet-Shooters]]. **Scholarly context:** this maps onto the long-standing taxonomy of apologetic schools — *evidentialism* (Habermas, William Lane Craig's cumulative case) vs. *presuppositionalism* (Van Til, Bahnsen). Carroll's contribution is pairing them by audience rather than choosing one.

### 3. Disarming without rebuilding is irresponsible — rate: important caveat (Carroll's own)
Carroll repeatedly warns that breaking a worldview without supplying an alternative produces faith crises: he cites a (loosely-remembered) BYU figure that ~56% of ex-Latter-day Saints become atheists "because they're not provided a good alternative."[^6] Hence the projected fourth class — "how to give an alternative." This is a genuine ethical strength of the framing, though the statistic is presented from memory and uncited.

## Best Quotes
> "One way to prepare to deal with all of those bullets... is to practice dodging bullets... Or another way... would be to practice disarming your opponent so that he's not able to shoot anymore." — [[Greg Bahnsen]], as played in class[^1]

> "What I want you to learn is how to get the gun out of the other guy's hand." — [[Greg Bahnsen]][^7]

> "Even before they can even say anything about my worldview, I push them on theirs... I challenge their presuppositions."[^3]

> "If you just leave them with a broken worldview, they're going to have crisis... so you need to give an alternative."[^8]
