# Hurlbut Affidavits Need Their Hostile Context

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Beshears's chapter on Joseph Smith quotes harsh neighbor statements about the Smith family ("not one of the family had the least claims to respectability"; "poor as well as worthless"; 51 signers calling them "destitute of that moral character"). [[David Snell]] objects that, while the quotes are usable, the book should have explained that these come from the [[Philastus Hurlbut]] affidavits — solicited by a hostile actor specifically to find dirt — because that provenance changes how a reader should weigh them. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The collector was a hostile partisan on a dirt-finding mission.** Snell: Hurlbut "was just raging against Joseph Smith and he convinced these people to sponsor him to go out to Palmyra... to find specifically dirt on the family." [^3] *Rating: strong and standard;* commissioned hostile testimony is the textbook case for source-critical caution (see [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem]] for the fuller treatment).

2. **"Dr. Philastus Hurlbut" — a name, not a credential.** Beshears and Snell jointly note "Dr." was Hurlbut's given name, not a title — a small but telling detail about how the affidavits have been dressed up with false authority. [^4] *Rating: moderate;* corrects a persistent misimpression.

3. **Counter-testimony exists from the same town.** Snell points to neighbors who said Hurlbut "came and interviewed me about the family, but when he didn't find what he was looking for he left" — showing the affidavits were a curated, not random, sample. [^5] *Rating: strong;* it directly undercuts the impression that "everybody" in Palmyra despised the Smiths.

4. **Beshears largely concedes — with a defense.** He agrees the context "is helpful" and that he wishes he could have included it, but explains his paragraph's goal was to show "the array of opinions," and he *did* immediately follow the harsh quotes with positive ones ("big hearty fellows... a good family who made good neighbors"). He says he "dug and dug" for positive material but found it scant because "positivity doesn't sell. Negativity sells," leaving historians with abundant hostile and scant friendly sources. [^6][^7][^8] *Rating: a fair partial concession;* it admits the missing context while explaining the asymmetric source base. He distinguishes painting "who people thought the Smiths were" from "who I think the Smiths were." [^9]

5. **William Smith's framing supplies the causal context.** Snell adds that [[William Smith]] said people were fine with the family "until Joseph had his vision and started talking about his revelations and then everybody just turned against us" — so the hostility post-dates and is colored by the revelations, a context the affidavits hide. [^10] *Rating: moderate-to-strong;* it reframes the reputation data as a reaction to Joseph's claims, not a neutral baseline.

**Steelman of the critic.** A historian can reply that hostility does not equal falsehood — biased witnesses sometimes report true facts, and the sheer number (51+ signers) is not nothing. Snell does not claim the affidavits must be discarded; his and Beshears's shared point is methodological: cite them, but flag their provenance so the reader can weigh bias. That is responsible source criticism, not special pleading.

## Best Quotes
> "Dr. Philastus Hurlbut... which is his name, not a title." [^4]

> "He was just raging against Joseph Smith and he convinced these people to sponsor him to go out to Palmyra... to find specifically dirt on the family." (Snell) [^3]

> "I wasn't trying to paint a picture of who I think the Smiths were. I was trying to paint a picture of who people thought the Smiths were." (Beshears) [^9]

> "Positivity doesn't sell. Negativity sells. And so as a historian I had ample amounts of negative material... and very scant amounts of positive." (Beshears) [^8]
