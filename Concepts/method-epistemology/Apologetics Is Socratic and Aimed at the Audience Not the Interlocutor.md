# Apologetics Is Socratic and Aimed at the Audience Not the Interlocutor

## The Point

[[Jacob Hansen]] frames public apologetics around two linked theses. **(1) Turn every statement into a question.** When you hear an objection, do not answer with a counter-statement; "figure out how to formulate your statement into a question," because — as in the Socratic method — *the one asking the questions is playing offense* and forces the other side to expose the hidden assumptions it cannot defend. **(2) Apologetics is for the watching audience, not the person across the table.** Hansen draws a sharp line: arguing someone into belief "one-on-one... just burns the relationship," and "ministry is not apologetics." The persuasive value of a public exchange accrues to the *onlookers* who watch one worldview fail to answer, not to the interlocutor, who (per the proverb) "convinced against his will is of the same opinion still."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The questioner controls the frame
The Socratic move shifts the burden: instead of defending a thesis, you ask the critic to ground theirs — e.g., to the problem of evil, "is it not possible that unjust suffering is necessary for God to accomplish his purposes?"; to the morality objection, "by what standard do you judge this wrong?"; to the Resurrection, "show me a more plausible account of God interacting with the world." Each question converts the apologist's defensive posture into offense and surfaces an unargued premise. This is the practical engine behind [[Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain]] and [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic]].

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as method — it is a disciplined application of steelmanning ([[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]]) plus presupposition-hunting; weaker as rhetoric, since a skilled interlocutor can simply answer the question.

### 2. Socrates is the model — and the warning
Hansen invokes Socrates "the gadfly of Athens," who exposed that people "don't know what the heck they're talking about" by questioning — but also notes Socrates was executed for it. The cautionary half is that questioning *humiliates*, which is why it is a tool for public forums, not private ministry.

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — illustrative analogy, not argument; the Socrates-was-killed point doubles as the rationale for thesis (2).

### 3. The audience, not the opponent, is the target
Because "a man convinced against his will is of the same opinion still," face-to-face browbeating rarely converts; persuasion happens "when you are observing somebody else having a conversation." So apologetics belongs in the public sphere, while one-on-one relationships call for ministry, which apologetics actively damages. This sharply bounds the discipline and connects to [[Missionaries Are Tour Guides Not Question Answerers]] and [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]].

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as pastoral counsel; it is a self-limiting claim that guards against the failure mode of weaponizing argument against friends.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Socratic questioning can be evasive or manipulative.** A relentless "just asking questions" posture can dodge the apologist's own burden of proof and feel like a debate trick rather than a search for truth — the very "annoying gadfly" the hosts joke about. If your question has an answer, you have gained nothing; if it does not, you have merely concealed your thesis.
- **The audience-only framing can rationalize bad behavior.** Saying "apologetics is for onlookers" risks treating the human across the table as a prop, which is in tension with charity even if the explicit point is to *protect* relationships by keeping argument out of them.
- **Sincere one-on-one persuasion does happen.** The blanket claim that you never change a mind face-to-face is overstated; patient personal dialogue converts people regularly, and the proverb cuts both ways.

**What survives:** the durable insight is the division of labor — *public, audience-facing argument* (where the Socratic, presupposition-exposing style is genuinely effective) is a different activity from *private ministry* (where it backfires). The method is a sharpening of steelmanning and premise-denial already in the vault; its distinctive contribution is the explicit boundary line: **do not do apologetics to the people you are trying to love.**

## Best Quotes

> "Questions are the person who's playing offense. If you're asking the questions, you're the one in the driver's seat." [^1]

> "Figure out how to formulate your statement into a question. Instead of saying 'well, Paul said XYZ,' say 'interesting — why do you think Paul said XYZ?'" [^1]

> "You don't actually help someone through apologetics directly... it just burns the relationship. Ministry is not apologetics. Apologetics is something you do in public forums." [^1]
