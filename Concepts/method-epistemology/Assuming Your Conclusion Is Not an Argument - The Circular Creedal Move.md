# Assuming Your Conclusion Is Not an Argument - The Circular Creedal Move

## The Claim
When an Eastern Orthodox priest (Father Havens) calls Latter-day Saint theology "just insanity," the narrator notes he "is not making an argument. He is simply assuming that the hard Trinitarian theology that he believes is what Jesus and his apostles taught and then claiming anyone who says otherwise is insane." This is **begging the question** — "I'm right because I'm right" — and it is the same move the narrator says [[Trent Horn]] makes with his Catholicism syllogism. Such reasoning persuades a sympathetic "fan base" but "make[s] no sense to anyone else." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The syllogism only works if you grant its disputed premise.** The narrator reconstructs the form: "If Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired. And Catholicism is true. So the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired." It is *valid* but question-begging: premise two ("Catholicism is true") is the very thing in dispute, so the argument "is just assuming your side of the argument in order to claim that you have won the debate." *Rating: strong; this is a textbook petitio principii — see [[Validity vs Soundness - Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails]] for the same structure.* [^1]
2. **Asserting history is not demonstrating it.** Havens claims "it's just objective history... that the Orthodox Church... apostolic succession... Mormons don't know history" — but offers no historical demonstration; he restates the conclusion as if it were the evidence. The narrator's whole video is the reply: *actually review the history* (Hellenization, the late development of *trinitas*, the scholarly consensus). *Rating: strong; substitutes assertion for argument.* [^2]
3. **It is audience-relative rhetoric.** "While this kind of reasoning might be applauded by their fan base, these kinds of arguments make no sense to anyone else." The move functions as in-group boundary maintenance, not persuasion of an outsider. *Rating: moderate; a rhetorical diagnosis.* [^1]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A defender argues this is not viciously circular but *worldview-level reasoning*: every framework must argue from its own first principles, and presuppositional apologetics openly does so (one cannot prove one's ultimate authority without using it). On this view Havens is not "begging the question" but reasoning *from* the rule of faith he holds authoritative, just as the Latter-day Saint reasons from the restored gospel. See [[Presuppositionalism Cannot Refute a Coherent Rival Worldview Like Mormonism]].

### Counter-objection
The narrator's reply: there is a difference between *transparently* arguing from first principles and *smuggling* a disputed conclusion into a premise while claiming you have "won." The fix is to **deny the loaded premise** and demand the actual evidence — which, when supplied for the Trinity's apostolic origin, fails on the historical record. See [[Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain]] and [[Historical Method Over Name Calling]].

## Best Quotes
> "Father Havens is not making an argument. He is simply assuming that the hard trinitarian theology that he believes is what Jesus and his apostles taught and then claiming anyone who says otherwise is insane. This comes off as: we already know the Orthodox Church... is true, so that's why Mormonism is false." [^1]

> "While this kind of reasoning might be applauded by their fan base, these kinds of arguments make no sense to anyone else because it's just assuming your side of the argument in order to claim that you have won the debate. In other words, I'm right because I'm right." [^1]
