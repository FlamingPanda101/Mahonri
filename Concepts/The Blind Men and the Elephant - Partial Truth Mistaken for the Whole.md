# The Blind Men and the Elephant - Partial Truth Mistaken for the Whole

## The Point
John Godfrey Saxe's poem retelling the ancient parable of the **six blind men of Hindustan** — each touching one part of an elephant and confidently declaring the whole creature to be like that part — is used to illustrate a core epistemic failure: taking "one aspect of truth" derived from genuine personal experience and wrongly "applying it to the whole." Each man is *partly right and entirely wrong*. The lesson supports a collective, witness-pooling epistemology: partial truths must be combined, and confident certainty from a single vantage is the trap.

## Argument
1. The frame: "well over 100 years ago an American poet [John Godfrey Saxe] put to rhyme an ancient parable" about "six men of Hindustan, to learning much inclined, who went to see the elephant, though all of them were blind." [^1]
2. Each man seizes one part and over-generalizes: the leg is "round and rough, like a tree"; another (the tusk) is "the spear"; the tail "is like a rope"; the trunk "is like a large snake." [^2]
3. The crucial mechanism: "each is describing truth, and because his truth comes from personal experience, each insists that he knows what he knows." Genuine, experientially-grounded partial truth breeds unwarranted certainty about the whole. [^2]
4. The poem's verdict: the men "disputed loud and long, each in his own opinion exceeding stiff and strong, though each was partly in the right, and all were in the wrong." [^3]
5. The application Hansen draws: "That someone could make a judgment based on one aspect of truth and apply it to the whole seems absurd… on the other hand, have we ever been guilty of the same pattern of thought?" [^3]
6. This connects to the LDS definition of truth as "a knowledge of things as they are, as they were, and as they are to come," and to the prescription to "use spiritual methods appropriate for that search." [^3]
7. **Steelman / limits of the analogy.** A critic notes the parable cuts both ways: it can be deployed as cheap relativism ("every religion grasps part of the elephant, so none is wholly right") — and indeed the parable is classically used *against* exclusive truth-claims. Used that way it would undercut a restored-gospel claim to fuller truth. The video's framing instead treats it as a call to *pool* witnesses and seek the whole (the elephant is one real animal), not to flatten all views as equally partial — which is why it is paired with "greater light reveals pre-existing truth."

## Best Quotes

> "Six men of Hindustan, to learning much inclined, who went to see the elephant, though all of them were blind, that each by observation might satisfy his mind." [^1]

> "Each is describing truth, and because his truth comes from personal experience, each insists that he knows what he knows." [^2]

> "Though each was partly in the right, and all were in the wrong… That someone could make a judgment based on one aspect of truth and apply it to the whole seems absurd or even unbelievable — on the other hand, have we ever been guilty of the same pattern of thought?" [^3]
