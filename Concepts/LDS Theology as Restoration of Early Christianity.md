# LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity

## The Claim
Latter-day Saint doctrines that have long been dismissed as heretical innovations are increasingly vindicated by historical research into early Christianity. Rather than inventing new doctrines, Latter-day Saints "were just remembering old ones."[^1] As scholars learn more about the beliefs and practices of the original Christians, LDS theology "looks better and better,"[^2] and the claim that LDS theology "operates outside of historic Christianity" becomes increasingly difficult to sustain.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Historical Vindication:** "As researchers learn more about early Christianity, the theology of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints looks better and better."[^2] The implication is that LDS distinctives — pre-existence, embodied God, divine council, deification, continuing revelation — find parallels in early Christian and Second Temple Jewish texts that later creedal Christianity suppressed or reinterpreted.

2. **Re-framing LDS as Remembering, Not Inventing:** The video frames LDS distinctives not as [[Joseph Smith]]'s innovations but as recovered memories of authentic early Christianity: "For a long time, Latter-day Saints have felt like the theological outcasts. But as history is uncovered, it turns out we weren't inventing new doctrines. We were just remembering old ones."[^1]

3. **Hardening Position of Critics:** The video acknowledges ongoing resistance ("some will still argue that we are not Christians at all") but claims this position is "becoming harder to defend when you look closely at the beliefs of the original Christians."[^3] The historical evidence is portrayed as eroding the boundary line that excludes LDS from Christianity.

4. **Convergence as Corroboration:** The broader convergence of mainstream Christian scholars toward LDS doctrines — documented in books on embodied God, pre-existence, and the feminine divine — serves as independent corroboration that LDS theology has ancient roots.[^4]

5. **Shift in Strategy:** The source suggests Latter-day Saints should engage these developments by "speaking to [Christians] as fellow Christians instead of restorationists who have the truth and are trying to inform a blighted people"[^5] — implying that the evidence of convergence changes the rhetorical landscape from adversarial to collaborative.

## Best Quote

> "For a long time, Latter-day Saints have felt like the theological outcasts. But as history is uncovered, it turns out we weren't inventing new doctrines. We were just remembering old ones."[^1]

## Counter-Arguments

- **Parallels are not restorations:** Finding parallels in ancient texts does not establish a continuous tradition. Gnostic, Jewish, and pagan ideas about pre-existence or deification existed, but early orthodox Christianity explicitly rejected them as heresies — so discovering that LDS doctrines resemble *rejected* early Christian variants does not make them "restorations" of *authentic* Christianity.
- **The Great Apostasy question:** The claim that LDS is remembering "old" doctrines presupposes a Great Apostasy that corrupted the original faith — a historical claim mainstream Christian scholarship largely rejects.
- **Selection bias in parallels:** LDS apologists may cherry-pick early Christian texts that look LDS while ignoring the vast body of patristic writings that contradict LDS distinctives (e.g., *creatio ex nihilo*, the simplicity and immateriality of God, eternal hell).
