# The Patriarchal Cult Mirrors Pre-Reform Practice That Lehi Revives

## The Claim

Reading [[Gerhard von Rad]] (via [[Julius Wellhausen]]'s *Prolegomena*), [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] present the argument that the patriarchal narratives of Genesis preserve not historical data about Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob but an **anthropological snapshot of the cult of the later age** that told their stories — "projected back into hoar antiquity... like a glorified mirage." The patriarchs meet God *in the countryside* (not in a centralized temple), under **sacred trees** (the oak of Moreh), build **their own altars**, and erect **named standing stones** to mark theophanies (Bethel, "the house of God"). Crucially, every one of these practices is later **prohibited by Deuteronomy** — which is itself evidence the practices were live, since "why would Deuteronomy forbid something if no one's doing it?" Day's payoff: [[Lehi]] does exactly the patriarchs' cult the moment he leaves Jerusalem — builds an altar in the wilderness, worships outside the one chosen place — the very things [[Deuteronomy 12]] bans. The Book of Mormon thus depicts a figure practicing **pre-reform First Temple religion**, matching the picture critical scholarship reconstructs behind the patriarchal legends.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Von Rad's Hexateuch argument (moderate; the core claim).** Day reads von Rad: "It's true we attain to no historical knowledge of the patriarchs, but only of the time when the stories about them arose in the Israelite people... this later age is here unconsciously projected... into hoar antiquity and reflected there like a glorified mirage." The stories tell us what the *narrators*' religion looked like, not what Abraham did.[^2]

2. **The reconstructed cult: countryside, trees, altars, named stones (strong as description).** The patriarchs meet God not in a priest-controlled building but in the open country, "by a tree, by a stone... by the oak of Moreh" ("the oak of the teacher"); they commemorate encounters with **standing stones** they *name* (Bethel = house of God); and they build their own altars (Abraham builds an altar to sacrifice). This is the early Israelite cult of [[Standing Stone Massebah as Material Medium of Divine Presence|sacred pillars and high places]].[^3]

3. **Deuteronomy's prohibitions confirm the practice was current (strong; argument from polemic).** Deuteronomy forbids standing stones (*matzevot*), denies one can see God, bans worship at sacred trees, and ([[Deuteronomy 12]]) forbids building your own altar — restricting sacrifice to the one place Yahweh chooses. Day's logic mirrors evidence of apostasy in the Pauline epistles: Deuteronomy legislates *against* these things in the 7th century precisely because people were doing them. ("Even Moses puts up those standing stones.")[^3]

4. **Lehi reproduces the patriarchal cult (rated: internal-textual; strong as a pattern).** "What does Lehi do the minute he runs out of Jerusalem and gets down to the Gulf of Aqaba? He builds an altar. It's the first thing he does" ([[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:6–7]]) — the very act [[Deuteronomy 12]] prohibits, and the very act Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob performed. The cultural picture of the patriarchs *is* the cultural picture of Lehi. This extends the vault's [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology|Deuteronomistic-reform reading]] from doctrine to *cultic practice*.[^4]

5. **The anachronism marker (supporting).** [[Genesis 12.6|Genesis 12:6]]'s "the Canaanite was then in the land" already signals the narrator writes from a later vantage, consistent with von Rad's claim that the patriarchal material reflects the storytellers' era.[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** (a) Whether the *Deuteronomic reform suppressed an older, legitimate cult* (the [[Margaret Barker]]-style frame the Lehi argument leans on) is a **contested minority thesis**, not consensus. (b) Day and Butler explicitly bracket historicity — "Dave's not saying Abraham didn't exist" — so the argument is about the *picture of the cult*, not events; a conservative could grant historical patriarchs who genuinely worshipped this way without conceding retrojection. (c) That Lehi builds altars and worships outside Jerusalem is also readable as ordinary diaspora necessity (no temple available), not a deliberate theological reversal of Deuteronomy. (d) The reconstruction is built from the same text it reorders. Day frames the convergence as a suggestive lens, not a proof — "the Book of Mormon is an awesome lens to... say, this is interesting, what can we learn?"

## Best Quotes

> "All of the stories of stuff that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob do is really stuff that people found normal... at the time when the stories were written much later."[^2]

> "They met God in the countryside... under sacred trees... and they would put down a stone to mark where they had met God... like Bethel, the house of God. And this is the stuff that Deuteronomy prohibits."[^3]

> "What does Lehi do the minute he runs out of Jerusalem and gets down to the Gulf of Aqaba? He builds an altar. It's the first thing he does. He does the stuff that Deuteronomy prohibits — the stuff that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob did."[^4]
