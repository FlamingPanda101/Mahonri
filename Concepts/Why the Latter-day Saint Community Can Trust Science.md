# Why the Latter-day Saint Community Can Trust Science

## The Point
Biologist [[Steven L. Peck]] argues that Latter-day Saints can trust good science *for the same reasons working scientists do* — not as "just another faith," but because science is a self-policing **ethical and social practice** whose built-in safeguards make its findings reliable.[^1] Science is not the simplistic four-step "scientific method" of textbooks, nor a monolithic procedure that mechanically grinds out truth.[^2] It is "a human social activity" governed by shared **values** (truth, objectivity, prediction, simplicity, repeatability, falsifiability, diversity), **tools** (experiment, observation, modeling, peer review), and above all **ethics** (transparency, openness to revision, tentativeness, research programs, archiving) — and it is precisely "this set of ethical practices that garner trust in its findings."[^3] The Restoration's own embrace of truth "from whatever source" means there is "nothing to fear from good science."[^4]

## Evidence
1. **Truth as the primary value.** Science seeks to "line up our beliefs about the world with ontological realities" — explicitly mirroring D&C 93:24, "truth is knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come."[^5]
2. **Objectivity = transcending self.** Independent investigators reach the same factual conclusions (a tsetse fly's flight distance is the same for any qualified observer), unlike subjective judgments of beauty or a personal testimony. Objectivity is "a collection of virtues that aim to transcend self-centeredness."[^6]
3. **Falsifiability, not proof.** A surviving hypothesis is held as "not yet disconfirmed," never finally "true"; the longer it survives sustained attempts to falsify it, the more warrant it earns. Science values the *ability to be wrong*.[^7]
4. **Peer review polices quality.** Editors send papers to three to five qualified reviewers — often the authors' intellectual opponents — to scrutinize methods, models, analysis, and reasoning; high-prestige journals accept only 2–5%. The process is imperfect (friends, detractors, blind spots) but "provides a screen that prohibits unworthy work from calling itself science."[^8]
5. **Self-correction from within.** Research programs compete for funding, students, and publication space — "a Darwinian selection-like process" — and are "usually self-correcting": the many eyes closest to the data are the ones who catch bias and error. A *static* research program (e.g., astrology) is a marker of pseudoscience.[^9]
6. **Transparency and tentativeness as ethics.** All data, protocols, and funding sources are open to inspection; all claims are "continually challenged, reformulated, refined." Peirce's image: truth is "the asymptotic convergence of scientists doing their work and coming to an agreement."[^10]

## Best Quotes
> "It is this set of ethical practices that garner trust in its findings." [^3]

> "As members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, we have nothing to fear from good science. I have argued that good science can be trusted by members of the Church at least as much as by scientists." [^4]

> "Science, even with all its imperfections, is the best thing people have invented to explain the natural world." [^11]

## LDS Connection
Peck's case is the methodological foundation for the whole in-faith reconciliation project. Far from treating science as a rival faith, the Restoration claims *all* truth: "we are open to truth of every kind, no matter whence it comes" (John Taylor), and the gospel has "room and place for every truth thus far learned by man, or yet to be made known" ([[James E. Talmage]]). The trustworthiness of science is grounded in the same realism D&C 93:24 affirms — "things as they are." The crucial guardrail is the distinction between method and metaphysics: science's working assumption of [[Methodological Physicalism Is Not Ontological Physicalism|methodological physicalism]] brackets God, miracles, and grace as *outside its domain* (Gould's non-overlapping magisteria) rather than denying them — exactly as a Latter-day Saint already expects her mechanic to reason about pistons, not "interstitial elves." Compare the parallel historical thesis in [[Three Theological Presuppositions Behind the Rise of Science]] and the affirmation that [[Theism Makes Science Possible]].
