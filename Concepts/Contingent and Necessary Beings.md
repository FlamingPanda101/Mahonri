# Contingent and Necessary Beings

## The Point
Given PSR, the explanation of anything must lie either in the thing's own nature or in something outside it — there is no third option (a thing explained by *nothing* is ruled out by PSR). [^1] A thing whose explanation lies outside itself is **contingent**: it depends on circumstances beyond itself and would not exist if they did not hold. A thing whose explanation lies in its own nature is **necessary**: there is something in its nature that entails that it cannot fail to exist, so it depends on nothing else. [^1] The world is manifestly full of contingent things — "stones, trees, dogs, human beings, and so on." [^2] The question that drives the proof is whether *everything* could be contingent, or whether contingency must ultimately rest on a necessary being.

## Evidence
1. **The dichotomy is exhaustive (given PSR).** "If a thing is explained neither by its own nature nor by anything outside itself, then it would be explained by nothing at all. But something's having no explanation at all is ruled out by PSR." [^1]
2. **Contingency aggregates (the fallacy-of-composition reply).** It is sometimes objected that even if each member of a collection is contingent, it is a fallacy of composition to infer the whole is contingent. Feser answers that not every part-to-whole inference is fallacious; contingency is, in the relevant sense, "more like color than it is like weight." A collection of three million stones is no less contingent than one stone — "if anything more obviously contingent," since it depends both on its members existing and on their being arranged as they are. The collection is "doubly contingent." [^3]
3. **The necessary being, characterized.** From being necessary it follows that the being is purely actual (no potentials needing actualization), absolutely simple or noncomposite, and "something which just is subsistent existence itself" — for any potentiality, composition, or essence-distinct-from-existence would make it depend on a cause, and so not be necessary after all. [^4] (Feser notes Aquinas also uses "necessary being" in a looser sense — e.g., angels, which have no natural tendency to corruption — but those are necessary only derivatively, since they still require a cause.) [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "In the latter case, we can say that the thing is contingent—that it depends on circumstances outside itself... In the former case, we can say that the thing is necessary—that there is something in its own nature that entails that it cannot fail to exist." [^1]

> "It is quite silly to pretend, then, that when we get to the collection of all the stones there are, or all the contingent things there are, we might somehow suddenly have something that is not contingent." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The contingent/necessary distinction is a genuine ally against atheism: it presses the atheist's "the universe is just *there*" to give an account of why contingent things exist at all, rather than treating the cosmos as a brute fact. Latter-day Saints can affirm that the changing, composite world is contingent and points beyond itself. The honest divergence is over the *content* of the necessary ground. Feser's argument identifies the necessary being with the absolutely simple, purely actual God of classical theism, and argues there can be only one such being. The restored gospel holds that the ultimate, uncreated realities include eternal matter, eternal intelligences, and eternal law (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]) — so what is "necessary" in the LDS frame is not a lone *actus purus*. Some LDS thinkers also press whether the argument really establishes *one* necessary ground rather than a plural one (see [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]]). Feser deserves credit that, on his metaphysics, the uniqueness genuinely follows; the LDS reply is that the metaphysics of divine simplicity it presupposes is itself the point in dispute.
