# Failed Prophecies Show Scripture Is Not Infallible

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues, against [[James White]]'s inerrantist "univocality and uniformity," that some scriptural prophecies simply *failed*, and that this is best explained by denying biblical infallibility rather than by harmonizing "mental gymnastics." Scripture "reflects the views of the culture and times in which the writers wrote" and "is not uniform." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "this generation" second-coming prophecy** — rate: the hardest case, candidly admitted. "The hardest for me as a Christian": Jesus prophesied the temple would be destroyed *and* that he would "return in power and glory before the entire generation passes away" ([[Matthew 24.34|Matt 24:34]]). "One was fulfilled — the temple was in fact destroyed. But he did not come in power and glory before the end of that generation." Redefining "generation" for one clause but not the other is illegitimate. *(scriptural)* [^2]
2. **Internal contradiction on the date** — rate: a sharp logical point. In one breath Christ predicts the timing; in another ([[Mark 13.32|Mark 13:32 / Matt 24:36]]) he says he "didn't know the hour of the second coming, only the Father did." "So you have a contradiction in the text" — unless all prophecies are taken as conditional/provisional. *(internal tension)* [^3] See [[All Prophecies Are Conditional]].
3. **The unbuilt Independence temple** — rate: a parallel LDS case, handled even-handedly. A revelation prophesied the temple in Independence, Missouri would be built "within that generation." It wasn't. The explanation offered — "God adapts to what the people actually do... the people weren't ready" — is itself an implicit conditional. *(restoration scripture)* [^4]
4. **Prophetic precision is uneven** — rate: a literary observation. The Gospel "predictions" of Peter's denial and Judas's betrayal were written down ~50 years *after* the events; the [[1 Nephi 13.40|Book of Mormon]] is "incredibly precise right up until Joseph Smith's time, at which time it gets really vague" — patterns consistent with human authorship in known contexts. *(literary/historical)* [^5]

**Steelman of the inerrantist:** Defenders of inerrancy offer principled (not ad hoc) harmonizations: *preterists* hold the "coming in power and glory" *was* fulfilled in AD 70 (Christ's judgment-coming against Jerusalem, in apocalyptic idiom), so "this generation" is literal and consistent; *futurists* read "this generation" as the generation that sees the final signs. On Mark 13:32, the two-natures distinction (the incarnate Son's human knowledge was limited; cf. [[Philippians 2.5-8|kenosis]]) dissolves the "contradiction." On these readings nothing failed. Ostler's reply: such harmonizations apply a different sense of "generation"/"coming" to adjacent clauses precisely to rescue the text — the very "mental gymnastics" he rejects — and he prefers the simpler conclusion that the writers held genuine, sometimes mistaken, first-century expectations.

## Best Quotes
> "He did not come in power and glory before the end of that generation. I've seen all kinds of mental gymnastics to try to redefine generation." [^2]

> "You have a contradiction in the text anyway, because in one sense he goes 'this is when it's going to happen,' and the other's like 'I don't know when it's going to happen.'" [^3]

> "I don't believe that the scriptures are infallible, so I don't have that problem." [^6]

> "The Book of Mormon... is incredibly precise in its predictions right up until Joseph Smith's time, at which time it gets really vague." [^5]
