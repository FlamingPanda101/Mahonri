# Testament of Naphtali Was Vindicated by the Dead Sea Scrolls

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]] argues that the standard method scholars use to date apocryphal texts — **historic manuscript dating** — is "a glorified guess" that systematically mis-dates Messianic texts late, and that the **Testament of Naphtali** is the documented case proving it. Brought to Western awareness in the 17th century, the Testament was dismissed by scholars as a "foolish pretension to Antiquity" and dated to the 16th–17th century *because* its closing verses sound Christian/Messianic — a figure from c. 2000 BC could not, they reasoned, speak that way [^1]. Then in 1946–47 fragments of it turned up among the [[Book of Enoch - Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation|Dead Sea Scrolls]] at Qumran, forcing the date back to the first century BC — roughly 100 years before Christ [^2]. The text that was "goofy fanfiction nobody took seriously for three or four centuries" was authentically pre-Christian after all.

The deeper point: you cannot date an *idea* by the manuscript that carries it, because ideas are transmitted orally and re-quoted long before they are written down [^3].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Testament of Naphtali is the test case — rate: strong (documented manuscript find)
The Testament of Naphtali belongs to the larger **Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs** — the dying speeches of Jacob's twelve sons. Scholars threw it out as a forgery on internal grounds (its final verse "clearly Messianic language") until the Qumran discovery supplied a physical pre-Christian manuscript, "unmistakably" a portion of the same text [^2]. This is a concrete, checkable instance of the general paradigm shift the vault already records — the Dead Sea Scrolls proving ideas judged "Christian-only" were already circulating in pre-Christian Judaism (see [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]]).

### 2. Historic manuscript dating (HMD) is circular for Messianic texts — rate: strong (logical)
Barnes names the method "historic manuscript dating" and characterizes it as comparison-by-resemblance — "this is a lot like those and a lot like these, so it's probably 300 [AD]" [^4]. When a text mentions a suffering Messiah, the rule auto-dates it after Christ or brands it a hoax, regardless of provenance: "they're reading a text that says I'm from 1000 BC, and they go... oh, never mind, that's a hoax, so they throw it out" [^5]. The Naphtali reversal shows the rule producing a verifiably wrong answer — the same circularity flagged in [[The Parables of Enoch Are Pre-Christian Not a Post-Jesus Forgery]].

### 3. You cannot date an idea by its manuscript — rate: moderate-to-strong (analogy)
Even a manuscript firmly dated to 70 AD does not date the *idea* it records, because "very few ideas, especially in the ancient world, are written willy-nilly on the spot" — they pass verbally before being codified [^3]. The Taylor-Swift-quotes-Elvis analogy: dating the idea by the manuscript is like dating the lyric "hound dog" to 2024 because that's when the earbud playing the song was made, ignoring that Swift would be quoting a 70-year-older source [^6].

### 4. Even an LDS scholar followed the flawed rule — rate: moderate (anecdote)
Barnes notes that [[Steven Robinson]] — an LDS scholar he respects — dated one such text post-Christ purely "because it preaches Christ in that detail," even though, as a Latter-day Saint, Robinson affirmed there *was* Christianity before Christ. The rubric overrode the conviction, illustrating how reflexive HMD has become [^7].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Dating apocalyptic and pseudepigraphal texts without dated manuscripts genuinely *is* inferential, and resemblance-based palaeography is a legitimate (if probabilistic) tool — the Parables of Enoch, also absent from Qumran, remain a real scholarly dispute, not a settled vindication. The Naphtali case shows the method can fail, not that it always does; and the Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs in their *received* form do carry later Christian interpolations, so the critics were not wholly wrong that *some* layer is late. The disciplined conclusion is narrower than the rhetoric: a Messianic phrase is not by itself proof of late composition — the burden is on the dater to show the lateness independently.
