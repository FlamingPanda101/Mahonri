# Docetism and Early Christological Diversity

## The Claim
In the first and second centuries Christians held a wide continuum of views about Jesus's nature, with two extremes: **Docetism** (Jesus was so divine he only *seemed* to be mortal — *dokeō*, "to seem") and **Ebionism** (Jesus was a mortal man, born of a mortal woman). The orthodox "fully God and fully man" position was one option among many, established only later.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Johannine epistles fight Docetism (rating: strong; canonical)
[[1 John 4.1-3|1 John 4:1-3]] insists "every spirit that confesseth that Jesus Christ is come in the flesh is of God" and brands the contrary "the spirit of antichrist." Day: "we learn much about the early church by what [the fathers] preached against" — John is stamping out the already-present teaching that Jesus only *seemed* to have flesh. [^1]

### 2. A spectrum, not a binary (rating: strong; scholarly)
Day stresses Docetism had "different versions," Ebionites were "so hardcore on the other side," and (citing a Catholic scholar in *The Pilgrim Church*) by Nicaea "half of the Christians were... iffy if Jesus was divine." [[Elaine Pagels]] is invoked on Gnostics who denied the apostles saw the risen Jesus or denied physical resurrection. "You can't slice Gnosticism in one slice and stick it on a plate." [^2]

### 3. The "heretics" sincerely believed in Jesus (rating: moderate; charitable)
Day steelmans the Docetists from the inside: "the Docetist did believe in Jesus... 'He's so awesome you can't even kill him.'" They were a competing devotion, not unbelief — and they were numerous enough that the church kept writing against them. [^3]

### 4. Ignatius's witness (rating: moderate; patristic)
[[Ignatius of Antioch]] (disciple of John) explicitly preached against Docetism and against the idea "that God was unknowable," and against those who said the Son is "a mere man" or that "the father, son and holy spirit are the same person." Day notes this list "sounds very much LDS." [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The orthodox response (and the traditional Christian claim) is that the apostolic teaching — the incarnation, "the Word was made flesh" ([[John 1.14|John 1:14]]) — was the *original* deposit, and Docetism/Ebionism are later corruptions the apostles themselves already condemned (hence 1 John). On this view diversity is the noise of heresy around a fixed apostolic signal, not evidence that orthodoxy was undetermined. Day's reply: the sheer volume and persistence of the alternatives, and the late date of doctrinal settlement, show "orthodoxy" was contested in real time, not self-evident.

## Best Quotes

> "Jesus only seemed to die... there were a lot of Christians that thought he was so much God he only seemed to be mortal." [^1]

> "You can't slice Gnosticism in one slice and stick it on a plate and say that's Gnosticism. There's so many different things." [^2]

> "If I walked on the beach unlike the [Docetic] poem... he wouldn't leave footprints." [^3]
