# Paul Invented Christianity as a Cloak for James's Nazirite Judaism

## The Claim
[[Robert Eisenman]]'s radical reconstruction (which Day reports without fully endorsing) holds that the Christianity of Acts and Paul is a *fiction* — a literary cover for what was really an extreme [[Numbers 6|Nazirite]], nationalist, Torah-zealous Jewish movement led by [[James the brother of Jesus|James]]. On this reading, "[[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] invented [Christianity] as a cloak" for James's Judaism; the thing that went out and triumphed in the world is "this illusion created by Paul." Day's stated value of the thesis is not its truth but that it *forced* a scholarly re-evaluation of James after a century of neglect.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Eisenman's source hierarchy (rating: weak; idiosyncratic reconstruction)
Day relays Eisenman's ranking of the sources: the **Dead Sea Scrolls** are honest (but must be read for what they are — records of an extreme Nazirite-Jewish community); the **New Testament** is "telling a lie" that can be read backwards to recover the truth; the **Talmud** is "the other side of the same lie" (the rabbis also hated the Nazirite Jews); and **Josephus** is "sometimes lying but surprisingly true enough" to help synthesize the picture. [^1]

### 2. Whatever James was, Jesus was (rating: moderate; the method's one durable insight)
Eisenman's operative move: since we have far more historical data on James than direct data on Jesus, "if we can figure out who James is, then... whatever James was, Jesus was." James is described in ancient Jewish sources as a priest with camel-knees from constant prayer (implying high-priestly access to the holy of holies), so beloved for his piety that "even the Jews couldn't say anything." See [[James the brother of Jesus]]. [^2]

### 3. Paul as a non-Jewish Herodian (rating: weak; contested)
Day notes Eisenman's argument that Paul was not ethnically Jewish: in Romans 16 Paul greets "Herodion my kinsman" (*Herodion* = "little Herod"), suggesting kinship with the Idumaean/Arab Herodian family. Hence Paul's law-free gospel is read as an outsider's reframing of James's Judaism. [^3]

### 4. The thesis's real payload is the rehabilitation of James (rating: strong; historiographical)
Day stresses the academic effect over the content: Eisenman "threw some really big bombs," and though "I suspect he's probably mostly wrong," he "really forced a re-evaluation of James the brother of Jesus in the last sort of 25 years," reviving the older [[F.C. Bauer]] (Tübingen school) two-party model of a Jamesian church versus a Pauline church. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Mainstream scholarship rejects Eisenman's identification of the Qumran community with James's church and his "read the New Testament backwards" hermeneutic as unfalsifiable — any contrary evidence is absorbed as part of "the lie." The traditional reading takes Acts and Paul as the *primary* witnesses, not a cover story, and explains James's prominence without making Paul a Herodian forger. Day concedes the point by calling the book "really dense," "basically unedited," and the thesis one he is "not really recommending."
