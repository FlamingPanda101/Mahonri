# Becoming Like God Is Not Usurping God

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The most famous anti-Mormon film, *The God Makers*, charges that LDS exaltation is the serpent's lie repackaged — that Latter-day Saints aim to "replace" or "supplant" God, doing exactly what Satan attempted in Eden. [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] rebuts this: exaltation is not rivalry but a father's joy in his children's growth, rooted in a non-zero-sum view of divine power.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Power based on love multiplies, never subtracts.** "God isn't the sort of person that jealously guards his power. God is the sort of person who gives it away and realizes that his power, which is based on love, is the sort of thing that never subtracts — it only multiplies... the more people he's able to share it with."[^2] Exaltation expands God's dominion rather than threatening it.
2. **The good-father analogy.** "A good father doesn't get upset when his children equal or even surpass him... there's a deep sense of pride and accomplishment that comes from seeing your children excel."[^3] Griffiths adds the personal testimony that becoming a father *increased* his love for his own father — relation, not replacement.[^4]
3. **The thesis of the sermon is a question about God's love.** Smith opens by asking everyone "what kind of being God is" and "why does he care about us so much." A human, unlike a squirrel or bear, has "an existential crisis" and the potential to become like God — and God "is so anxious to help us achieve our full potential that he's willing to give us everything he has."[^5] This is grounded in [[Moses 1.39|Moses 1:39]] (God's "work and glory" is human eternal life).
4. **Steelman of the critic.** The critic can reply that "share my power" still entails *additional* gods who organize worlds and beget spirit children — a literal plurality of deities, which monotheists regard as the core problem regardless of how warmly it is framed. Genesis 3:5 ("ye shall be as gods") and Isaiah's "there is no God beside me" are, for the critic, decisive against any program of human deification, however non-competitive. The affective "good father" framing, they argue, does not neutralize the *ontological* claim that men become gods. Griffiths' answer is that classical theosis traditions (Eastern Orthodoxy, Paul's "joint-heirs with Christ") already affirm real participation in the divine without idolatry, so the LDS version differs in degree, not in transgression.
5. **Evidence rating.** Effective at dismantling the *motive* caricature ("you want to dethrone God") and consonant with broader theosis traditions. It does not, by itself, resolve the *metaphysical* monotheism dispute, which turns on the plurality-of-gods question handled elsewhere (see [[Human Deification in Mormonism]]).
