# The ESV Mistranslates Exodus 21.16 to Criminalize Unwitting Buyers

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues the [[Exodus 21.16|Exodus 21:16]] proof-text a critic cited against him ("Whoever steals a man and sells him, **and** anyone found in possession of him shall be put to death") is a **mistranslation lifted from the ESV**, which he calls "a joke" of a translation. Rendering the second clause with "and" turns the verse into a separate capital charge against a third party "found in possession" — making it "a capital offense to unknowingly purchase an enslaved person who had been kidnapped." McClellan: "that's just not how these laws worked in the ancient world." The correct rendering coordinates the two clauses with **"or"**: "whoever steals a man and sells him **or** is found with him in his possession" — i.e., the **kidnapper** is executed whether he has *already sold* the victim or *still holds* him.[^1] See [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "and" reading produces an absurd law (rated: strong; argued).** An ancient slave economy could not function if any buyer were liable to death for unknowingly acquiring a kidnapped person; no slave society imposed strict-liability capital punishment on purchasers. The grammar must therefore be the disjunctive "or."[^1]
2. **Both clauses describe the one kidnapper (rated: strong; primary text).** "The idea is it is a capital offense whether you have already sold him or you have not yet sold him" — the subject of "found in possession" is the *thief*, not a downstream buyer.[^1] This is the same point reinforced by the verse's lexical scope; see [[Covenant Code Lexical Precision on Social Status]].
3. **The ESV gets the verb right even as it botches the syntax (rated: strong; conceded).** McClellan grants the ESV correctly renders "steals a **man**" — a literal rendering of Hebrew *ish* — which is precisely what limits the law to a freeborn Israelite male and so defeats the critic's anti-slavery reading on independent grounds.[^1]

## LDS Relevance
A live example of why the Article of Faith caveat "as far as it is translated correctly" (A of F 1:8) is doing real work: a single conjunction ("and" vs. "or") in a popular English Bible silently rewrites a verse from a narrow ban on man-stealing into a sweeping anti-slavery statute it never was. It cautions Latter-day Saints against building doctrine or ethics on the surface of any one English version without the underlying Hebrew. See [[Covenant Code Lexical Precision on Social Status]].

## Best Quotes
> "This comes from the ESV, which as far as translations go is a joke... it would make it a capital offense to unknowingly purchase an enslaved person who had been kidnapped, and that's just not how these laws worked in the ancient world."[^1]

> "An appropriate translation would say 'whoever steals a man and sells him **or** is found with him in his possession.' In other words... it is a capital offense whether you have already sold him or you have not yet sold him."[^1]
