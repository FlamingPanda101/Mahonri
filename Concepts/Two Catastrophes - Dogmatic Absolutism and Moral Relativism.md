# Two Catastrophes — Dogmatic Absolutism and Moral Relativism

## The Point

[[Sam Harris]] and [[Jordan Peterson]] open by agreeing that any adequate grounding of ethics must steer between **two failure modes**: arbitrary moral absolutism (which Harris ties to fundamentalism, Peterson to a "pathology of order") and moral relativism / nihilism (a "pathology of chaos"). This shared framing is the launch point for the entire debate.

## Argument (steelman)

1. **The first catastrophe — arbitrary absolutism.** A morality grounded in unquestionable axioms (e.g. "God said so") can justify any atrocity, because the axiom is immune to revision. Harris: this is "the catastrophe you identified with religious fundamentalism."[^1]

2. **The second catastrophe — relativism.** If there are no right and wrong answers to questions of human value, then no one who wanted Auschwitz can be said to be *wrong*. Both reject this. Harris: "those who do want Auschwitz are wrong to want Auschwitz."[^2]

3. **Same axis, different vocabulary.** Peterson maps these onto his own scheme: "I think of that as a pathology of order and a pathology of chaos... we're working on the same axis."[^3]

4. **Harris's positive commitment: moral realism.** "I'm a moral realist... there are right and wrong answers to questions of this kind, and you can not know what you're missing." This sets up the later dispute over whether such answers are *facts*. See [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]].[^4]

5. **Suffering vs well-being.** Harris focuses on minimizing suffering as the easier handhold; Peterson notes Harris also wants the positive pole ("well-being") — they agree the goal includes both reducing undue suffering and increasing flourishing.[^5]

## Best Quotes

> "There's two things you want to avoid, two catastrophes... one is the catastrophe that you identified with religious fundamentalism, and the other is the catastrophe that's associated with moral relativism."[^1]

> "Those who do want Auschwitz are wrong to want Auschwitz... I'm a moral realist, and what realism means is that there are right and wrong answers to questions of this kind."[^2]
