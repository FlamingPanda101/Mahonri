# Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture

## The Point

[[Blake Ostler]] argues that the spiritual experience (the "burning in the bosom") is not one more piece of *information within the picture* of our experience but the **lens that makes seeing the picture possible at all**. Because conversion *reorients* one's entire interpretive framework, the experience must be logically and experientially *prior* to interpretation — a "first principle" that cannot itself be derived from, or refuted by, the very framework it grounds. [^1] This directly answers the objection that spiritual experience is "merely interpretation."

## Argument

1. **The lens illustration.** Looking at any photograph, there are things you do not see — the camera lens, and the lens of your own eye — yet they are "what makes seeing the picture possible." Spiritual experiences are like the lens: not the content of the picture, but the condition for seeing it. [^1]
2. **The objection (steelmanned): all experience is interpretation.** (i) All human experience involves interpretation; (ii) interpreting the burning as "from God" is something *we* do as humans; (iii) therefore the divine attribution is a human contribution, and all we really know is that we *experienced it as* coming from God. Ostler grants the premises are largely true, demonstrating it with perceptual illusions — the disappearing peripheral dots, the color-fill afterimage, and the duck/rabbit figure (a [[Ludwig Wittgenstein]] staple) — and notes we filter out "90 to 98% of all of the sense data." [^2]
3. **The rebuttal: the argument proves too much.** If *all* experience were merely the regurgitation of pre-existing categories, then **no genuinely new experience could ever occur** — no new scientific theories, no new inventions, no new revelations. But novel experience plainly happens. A conversion experience is precisely one that *breaks out of* and *replaces* the prior framework, so it cannot be a product of that framework. [^3]
4. **Therefore the experience is interpretive, not interpreted.** Because it rearranges the categories themselves, the spiritual experience is "logically and experientially prior to our interpretive experience." It functions as a "regulatory assertion" that plays a different role than evidence or argument — it makes other meaningful experience and reasoning possible. As such it "resists logical and evidentiary proofs" and "stands on its own." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Our spiritual experiences are like the lens. They're not the information of the picture itself; they're what makes seeing the picture possible." [^1]

> "There would be no possibility of new experiences that break out of the framework of existing paradigms... if all experience were necessarily limited to our prior interpretations. Yet that is precisely what a conversion experience is — it reorients one's entire view of the world." [^3]

> "By the very firstness, these experiences make other meaningful experiences and reasoning possible... they become the lens that give us eyes to see what would otherwise only be a blurry jumble of data without meaning." [^4]
