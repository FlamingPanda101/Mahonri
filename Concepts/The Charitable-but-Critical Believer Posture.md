# The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture

## The Claim
A believing Latter-day Saint can hold two commitments at once: charity, refusing to assume the early Saints were liars or fabricators; and criticism, refusing to accept dramatic prophecy claims uncritically merely because they flatter the faith. The host of *Mormonism with the Murf* models this posture explicitly, declining both naive credulity and reflexive dismissal.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Charity toward the sources.** The host says he would "tend to be more charitable and not believe that they're just completely making these things up." He treats fabrication as a serious charge requiring evidence, not a default. (Evidence rating: a stated disposition, not an argument; it functions as a methodological prior.)[^2]
2. **Criticism toward the claims.** Simultaneously: "I think it's important to be critical as we evaluate them as well, especially if we're going to use these as evidence supporting Joseph Smith's gift of prophecy." He refuses to let belief license a lower evidentiary bar — compare [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]]. (Evidence rating: strong as a principle.)[^1]
3. **Calibrated confidence per item.** Rather than treating the twenty as a uniform bloc, he rates them individually: "highly dubious" of some, finding others "absolutely miraculous if this really did happen." This per-claim calibration is the practical fruit of the posture.[^3]
4. **Anticipating the next step — failed prophecies.** He commits to a follow-up on Joseph Smith's *unfulfilled* prophecies and the [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:20-22]] test for false prophets, showing the posture extends to evidence that cuts against his own view. See [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]]. (Evidence rating: strong — willingness to examine disconfirming data.)[^4]
5. **Steelman of the skeptic's worry.** A critic may answer that "charitable but critical" still smuggles in a thumb on the scale: the believer's charity is selectively extended to faith-promoting witnesses while critical scrutiny is applied just hard enough to look fair. The honest test is whether the same standards would be applied to a rival religion's miracle claims. The host's repeated "if I'm putting on my skeptic hat" framing is an attempt to meet that test, with mixed success.

## Best Quotes
> "I would tend to be more charitable and not believe that they're just completely making these things up. I just wish we did have a contemporaneous source."[^2]

> "I'm obviously a believer so I am more inclined to accept them but at the same time I also think it's important to be critical as we evaluate them as well."[^1]

> "I don't think that there's nothing to any of these... but I think we also do need to be cautious and use critical thinking as well."[^4]
