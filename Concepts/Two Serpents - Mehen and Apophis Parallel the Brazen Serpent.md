# Two Serpents — Mehen and Apophis Parallel the Brazen Serpent

#type/claim

## The Claim
The biblical "good serpent vs. bad serpent" pattern is rooted in **Egyptian serpent iconography**, and the seraph itself is a *winged serpent*. Egypt opposes **Apophis** (the chaos-serpent, enemy of the sun) to **Mehen** (the coiled protector-serpent who guards Ra/Amun-Ra through the twelve hours of night). [^1] This twofold scheme, the hosts argue, illuminates several Israelite texts: (a) the **brazen serpent** of [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21:8-9]] — the people are bitten by "fiery flying serpents" (Hebrew *seraphim*, literally winged/fiery snakes) and healed by looking on a serpent on a staff, the *good* serpent against the *bad*; the hosts insist "fiery" reflects Egyptian *winged-serpent* iconography, not Greek-fire poison, and note the Book of Mormon's roughly five Nehushtan passages; [^2] (b) **Isaiah 6's** throne-theophany, where the *seraphim* are winged serpents flanking the throne — read against winged-cobra (uraeus) throne iconography, specifically **King Tut's throne**, which Butler reads from the hieroglyphs; [^3] and (c) **Exodus 7's** rod-to-serpent contest, where Moses' serpent (priesthood authority) devours the Egyptian lector-priests' serpents — again the good serpent consuming the bad, paralleling the brazen serpent's protection. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The lexical core is sound.** *Saraph* genuinely denotes a serpent (Numbers 21; Isaiah 14:29; 30:6) and *seraphim* the winged throne-beings of Isaiah 6; ANE/Egyptian winged-uraeus iconography is a recognized backdrop for Israelite seraph imagery in mainstream scholarship. The Apophis/Mehen opposition and Mehen's protective role around the solar barque are standard Egyptology. *Rating: the building blocks are well-attested.*
2. **The dichotomy is genuinely structural, not cherry-picked.** A *destructive* serpent set against a *protective/healing* serpent recurs across the three texts (Numbers, Isaiah, Exodus) and in the Egyptian material, so the parallel is a pattern rather than a one-off coincidence. *Rating: a real recurring motif.*
3. **Iconography over etymology.** Reading "fiery flying serpent" as cultural iconography (winged snakes everywhere in Egypt) rather than literal flaming reptiles is a reasonable, even economical, explanation of an odd phrase. *Rating: plausible.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **Shared ANE inheritance, not Egyptian dependence**: winged-serpent and chaos-serpent motifs are pan-Near-Eastern (Mesopotamian *mušḫuššu*, Levantine winged uraei on seals); Israel could draw on a common iconographic pool without Egypt being the source, so "Egyptian origin" overreaches. (b) **The good/bad pairing is near-universal**: protective-vs-destructive serpent symbolism appears worldwide, so its presence in both corpora is weak evidence of any specific link — the [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple|parallelomania caution]] applies. (c) **Apophis and Mehen are not a clean "good vs. bad serpent" doctrine** the way the apologetic implies — Mehen is a protective coil, Apophis a cosmic enemy, but Egypt swarms with *many* serpent deities (Wadjet, Renenutet, the uraeus on every brow) that don't reduce to a binary; selecting two flattens the picture. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is only that Israel's serpent symbolism is *intelligible against* Egyptian iconography (given the Josephite/Egyptian formation argued elsewhere in the source), and that the brazen-serpent-as-Christ type gains depth, not that Egypt is the unique fountainhead.
