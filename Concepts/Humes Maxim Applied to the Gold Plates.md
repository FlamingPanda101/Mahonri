# Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates

## The Claim

The panel's modest conclusion: they are not claiming to *prove* the plates were
real or miraculous, only that fabrication is not the *best explanation*. Invoking
[[David Hume]]'s maxim on miracles, they argue that positing Joseph Smith forged
witness-consistent plates in his garage is itself *more* miraculous than receiving
them from an angel.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The argument is about best explanation, not impossibility

The panel repeatedly refuses to claim fabrication is impossible. The thesis is
that, weighed against the data, it is not "the best explanation for the data" —
the standard of inference to the best explanation, not proof.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as an epistemic framing (it concedes defeasibility and targets plausibility).

### 2. Hume's own criterion, turned around

Hume held that one should accept a miracle only if its denial would be "more
miraculous." Applied here: the cumulative improbability — cheap-but-convincing
gold-look metal, hundreds of finely engraved plates, artificial aging, all by an
unskilled, penniless farmer, undetected — is, the panel argues, *less* credible
than the angelic account on Hume's own terms.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (a rhetorical inversion of a famous skeptical principle; persuasive force depends on accepting the prior improbabilities).

### 3. The dimensions fit a specific ancient artifact

[[Neal Rappleye]] argues the parameters match a pre-Columbian metalsmith's work
of the mid-first century AD — see
[[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation]] — offering a
positive alternative rather than mere denial of fabrication.[^4]

### 4. Free advice to critics: drop the talking point

The panel's bottom line is pragmatic: the Book of Mormon could *still* be false,
but the "I could make them in my garage" objection is a weak, dismissible line of
attack that the parameters defeat.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Hume's maxim cuts both ways: a committed naturalist will always rate "any natural
fabrication, however improbable" as *less* miraculous than "an angel delivered
ancient metal plates," because, on naturalism, the supernatural option has
~zero prior. So the maxim does not adjudicate between worldviews; it only
relocates the disagreement to priors. The panel's strongest residual point is the
narrow one — that the *specific* "easy garage forgery" claim is empirically weak
regardless of one's priors (see
[[Joseph Smith Lacked the Metallurgical Skill and Time]]).

## Best Quotes

> "David Hume basically says... you should only believe a miracle if the counter explanation is more miraculous... I think having Joseph Smith make gold plates in his garage is more miraculous than getting it from an angel."[^3]

> "We're not saying this proves Joseph Smith had gold plates... We're here to say that that criticism just doesn't hold any water. Now... the Book of Mormon can still be made up."[^2]

> "You can just drop this talking point and the Book of Mormon can still be fake... it's just a silly thing to try to criticize it for."[^5]
