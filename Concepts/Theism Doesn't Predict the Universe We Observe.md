# Theism Doesn't Predict the Universe We Observe

#type/claim

## The Claim (skeptical)
[[Sean Carroll]]'s second objection is Bayesian and turns on what theism would have *predicted in advance*. He grants the favorable likelihood — "the likelihood that the parameters of nature would allow for life would be large under theism... I think that's okay" — but says "there's a whole bunch of other things you can ask. Should we expect the universe to look this way if theism were true? And there's a whole bunch of other features of the universe that we would not expect."[^1] His examples: the sheer size of the cosmos, "the fact that there's a hundred billion or a trillion galaxies in our observable universe — that's not what you would expect under theism, and especially the idea that human beings are somehow special in God's eyes."[^1]

The decisive charge is that accommodation is **post hoc**. A theist *could* now say "the whole universe is special in God's eyes" and "this exactly is what God would have made — but too late, because you had the chance for a very, very long time cosmologically." Before modern astronomy "we had no knowledge of the fact that there were extra galaxies out there, that the universe was big, that there was a big bang... You could easily have used your theory of theism to predict that... And nobody did."[^2] [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] agrees it "does seem a bit... post hoc," not "strictly speaking irrational" for the theist, but easy to say only after the fact.[^3] Carroll extends the point to lawful invisibility: modern theists "are pushed to invent a version of God who wants to be completely invisible," wanting "the world just to act according to the laws of physics," then inventing a reason "why oh no, God would have wanted to do it that way so that we would have no idea that God actually existed" — when "it would be very, very trivially easy for God to let us know that God existed."[^4] The conclusion: "God would have wanted it that way is a little bit of a post hoc copout."[^5]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A real Bayesian asymmetry on auxiliary data.** Even if P(life-permitting constants | theism) is high, confirmation requires the *total* evidence. Carroll's point is that the universe's scale and human marginality were *not* predicted by theists and are unsurprising under naturalism, so they pull the posterior back toward naturalism.[^1]
2. **Predictions must precede the data to count fully.** A hypothesis earns the most credit for risky predictions made *before* observation. Theism had "a very, very long time" to predict a vast, ancient, galaxy-filled cosmos and did not; retrofitting it afterward is weak Bayesian confirmation.[^2]
3. **The hiddenness extension.** A God acting only through physics, leaving the world looking exactly as if "governed by purely materialistic rules," is what naturalism predicts; theism has to add an auxiliary ("God wanted to be invisible") to match it, which lowers theism's likelihood. This connects fine-tuning to the [[Schellenberg's Hiddenness Argument and the Christian Reply|divine-hiddenness]] problem.[^4]
4. **O'Connor's concession sets the bar.** Not "irrational," but "post hoc." The skeptic's claim is modest and therefore robust: theism survives this, but it does not *predict* the world, so the world is not evidence *for* it.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the hardest of Carroll's objections to wave away, because it is methodologically fair. The strongest replies are (a) to dispute that theism ever entailed a small, young, human-sized cosmos, and (b) to specify the theism in advance so accommodation is principled, not retrofitted.

- **The "small universe" prediction was never a theistic commitment.** Scripture pictures a God whose works are *uncountable* — stars beyond number (Genesis 22:17), "worlds without number have I created" (Moses 1:33), heavens that "cannot be numbered unto man" (Moses 1:37). LDS scripture in particular *predicts* a vast plurality of worlds and a God whose work is "to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man" across them (Moses 1:39). So a hundred-billion-galaxy cosmos is not an awkward post-hoc patch for the Restoration; it is closer to what its scripture already said. Carroll's "nobody predicted a big universe" is weakest against exactly the LDS tradition. (Cf. [[Fine-Tuning - Book of Mormon Anticipation of Modern Cosmology]].)
- **Human "insignificance" is a category slip.** Cosmic *size* does not entail moral *marginality*; on LDS theology the scale of creation magnifies rather than diminishes the worth of souls ("the worth of souls is great"), and a God for whom each agent matters is not embarrassed by large numbers. The inference "big cosmos ⟹ humans aren't special" is a value judgment, not a datum.
- **Specify theism to defeat the post-hoc charge.** Carroll is right that "God could have done anything" predicts nothing. The reply is to use a *constrained, embodiment-and-agency* theism (the LDS form; cf. [[Philip Goff]]) that predicts in advance: a stable, law-governed, physical world apt for free moral development — which is precisely a lawful, "non-coercive" universe. Lawful regularity is then a *prediction*, not an excuse.
- **Hiddenness is answered, not invented ad hoc.** The "invisible God" objection is the hiddenness argument; the vault's standing replies ([[Schellenberg's Hiddenness Argument and the Christian Reply]], [[Divine Hiddenness Is a Gift That Sanctifies]]) argue that a world *not* coercively saturated with overwhelming proof is exactly what a God seeking freely-given love and moral growth would create — so lawful regularity is predicted by that theism rather than retrofitted to dodge naturalism.
- **Grant the method.** The right posture is to accept Bayesian scoring and *meet* it: state the theistic prediction up front (plurality of worlds, lawful order, real creaturely agency) and show the data confirm it. That converts Carroll's "copout" charge into a head-to-head likelihood contest the theist can actually contest.
