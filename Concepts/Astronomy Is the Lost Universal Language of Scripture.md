# Astronomy Is the Lost Universal Language of Scripture

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Kamron Coleman]] (with [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]]) argues that **naked-eye astronomy was a single universal "language" in which the ancient world's scriptures were written**, and that modernity has rendered us "completely illiterate" in it. Before writing, the night sky was humanity's "first great book" — a shared, memorable, self-authenticating medium. On this view the Christian message spread across the ancient world "like wildfire" not because people were reading the Gospels (statistically almost no one was) but because the Jesus story was a **star story** "plugged into" astronomy everyone already knew. Coleman's *Apotheosis Scroll* aims to teach that lost language so the experience can be recovered.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. A common northern sky made one shared text (rating: moderate; geographic)
Coleman and Butler note that ~68% of habitable land is north of the equator, so "most of humanity for most of its history has seen the same stars" — Egypt, Israel, India, China, Japan, the Mesoamericans all share the northern sky.[^1] A medium visible to everyone, requiring no literacy, solves real problems: it lets you teach and transmit to non-readers, you "don't have to be carrying around books," and it carries built-in **authority** — "Who put the stars up there? God did."[^2] Compare [[Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology Not Modern Science]].

### 2. Astronomy encodes knowledge older than writing (rating: contested; the *Hamlet's Mill* hypothesis)
Following *Hamlet's Mill* (Giorgio de Santillana & Hertha von Dechend), Butler argues that the **precession of the equinoxes** — a ~26,000-year cycle no single observer could detect in a lifetime — appears "in encoded form in a lot of ancient mythology" from Japan to Egypt to the Old Testament, implying a body of star-knowledge "way older than writing."[^3] *Rating: de Santillana/von Dechend is a fringe-but-persistent classic; mainstream scholarship treats much of its pan-mythic decoding as speculative.*

### 3. Christianity transmitted as astronomy, not text (rating: speculative; the load-bearing claim)
"The story of Jesus... did not transmit via the Gospel of Matthew. It transmitted because it's a star story." A convert 1,000 miles from Jerusalem would form a *different* understanding of Christ, yet the Spirit would witness its truth — so early Christian doctrine was "not supplied by the verses in the canon" but by the Spirit confirming a shared astral grammar, only later "correlated into a single doctrine."[^4] See [[The Precession of the Equinoxes Decodes the Lamb Ram and Fish]].

### 4. Scriptural warrant: stars as signs, Abraham the astronomer (rating: moderate as motif)
[[Genesis 15.6|God's promise to Abraham]] of "seed as numberless as the stars," Genesis 1's lights "for signs," the **oth** (sign/token) language linking heaven and earth, [[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3]]'s governing-stars cosmology, and the star-following **Magi** ([[Matthew 2.1-12|Matthew 2]]) are read as biblical endorsement of attending to the stars — distinct from horoscopic astrology. See [[The Wise Men Were Bearers of Preserved First-Temple Covenant]] and [[The 6% Astrology Outlier and Abraham 3]].

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The mainstream reading is that ancient peoples used the sky for the calendar, navigation, and agriculture, and built *culturally specific* mythologies onto it — there was no single decipherable "scripture in the stars." Deuteronomy's polemic against assigning spiritual value to the host of heaven ([[Deuteronomy 4.19]]) shows Israelite religion *resisting* astralization, not encoding it. Christianity's rapid spread is better explained by social networks, martyrdom, and charity (Stark) than by a shared zodiac, and "it transmitted because it's a star story" is unfalsifiable. Coleman concedes the speculative status, calling it "my hypothesis" and insisting he is teaching a *language*, not proving a history.
