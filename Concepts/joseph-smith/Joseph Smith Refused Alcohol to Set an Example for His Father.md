# Joseph Smith Refused Alcohol to Set an Example for His Father

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]]'s signature reading: the boy [[Joseph Smith]] did not simply refuse the anesthetic alcohol — he *paired* the refusal with a request to have his father hold him ("I will have my father sit on the bed and hold me in his arms"). The best explanation of that pairing is that Joseph, a dying child aware of his parents' suffering, was **"parenting his parents"** — protecting his exhausted mother and setting a sober example for his father, [[Joseph Smith Sr]], whose documented struggle with wine the family never mentions in the surgery account. In effect: "if I can do this without alcohol, so can you."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **He linked the two acts, didn't merely refuse (strong, textual).** Per [[Lucy Mack Smith]]: "I will not [take alcohol]… but I will tell you what I will do. I will have my father sit on the bed and hold me in his arms." Bradley insists, "don't just look at the fact that Joseph refused the alcohol. Look at what he paired that with."[^1]
2. **He was demonstrably trying to protect his mother (strong, textual).** Joseph sent Lucy "far away from the house" and twice halted the surgery until she left — direct evidence, "without any inference," that the child was managing his parents' pain, not just his own. If he is parenting his mother, why not also his father?[^2]
3. **The father's weakness for wine is independently documented (strong, documented).** [[Richard Bushman]] describes [[Joseph Smith Sr]] as having "a weakness for wine"; Palmyra store records show him buying liquor/cider; and Joseph Sr's own patriarchal blessing to [[Hyrum Smith]] says "though he has been out of the way through wine, thou hast never forsaken him." The neighboring early revelation to Joseph Sr opens "remember temperance."[^3]
4. **Dying-children research supports the mechanism (moderate, social-science).** A psychologist who interviewed terminally ill children and their parents found that dying children, aware they are watching their parents suffer, sometimes "try to parent their parents." Bradley uses this to argue his reading is not invented from thin air.[^4]
5. **It explains the undisclosed context an honest narrator wouldn't fabricate (strong, logical).** Lucy never mentions the family's alcohol use, yet writes in the boy's refusal-plus-father pairing. "She wouldn't make up a story that reflects a context that she's not disclosing… for no reason." The detail fits a real situation, supporting authenticity.[^5]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
This is, by Bradley's own admission, an **inference**, not a documented motive — Joseph never says *why* he refused. A critic can grant every fact and still resist the conclusion: the boy may have wanted his father simply for comfort (the most natural reading) with no example-setting agenda; the "parenting the parent" psychology, though real, is being retrofitted; and an apologist has obvious motive to find nobility in the prophet's childhood. The reading is plausible but underdetermined by the evidence.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley concedes it is abductive and offers the convergence as its strength: larger research on dying children, the demonstrable care for his mother, the documented paternal context, and the explanatory fit of the otherwise-puzzling refusal all "point the same direction" and "tie it up with a bow." It is offered as inference to the *best* explanation, not proof — and the burden shifts to anyone with a better one. See [[Abductive Reasoning Is How Historians Infer the Best Explanation]].
