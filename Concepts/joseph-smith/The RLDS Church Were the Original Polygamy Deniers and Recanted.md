# The RLDS Church Were the Original Polygamy Deniers and Recanted

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brian Hales]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] note that today's online polygamy-denial movement is not new: the **Reorganized Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints (RLDS)** — founded in 1860 with [[Joseph Smith III]] as president — built its very identity on two claims: that Joseph Smith Jr. was *not* a polygamist, and that the presidency should descend through his son. They were "the OG" deniers — and over more than a century of fighting the issue, their own historians eventually *admitted* Joseph practiced plural marriage, abandoning the founding narrative.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Denial was the RLDS founding principle.** The RLDS Church "was built upon two ideas: Joseph Smith Jr. wasn't a polygamist, and his heir to the presidency should be Joseph Smith III." Their missionary effort targeted *Utah* — sent "to try to get people to join them from the Brighamite tradition" rather than to convert the world — so anti-polygamy was central to their mission, not incidental.[^2] *(Strong — accurately frames RLDS origins and apologetic posture.)*
2. **The 19th-century affidavit databases exist *because* of the RLDS challenge.** The 1869 affidavits, Andrew Jenson's 1886–87 research, and the 1892–93 Temple Lot depositions were all generated *in response to* RLDS missionaries and the RLDS claim to own the temple lot. [[Todd Compton]] told Hales that without the RLDS Church, "we would be highly limited in what we know about Joseph's polygamy" — ironically, the deniers prompted the documentation that refutes them.[^3] *(Strong — a striking historiographical point; the denial movement created its own refutation.)*
3. **An RLDS insider called the denial "a great lie."** [[Ebenezer Robinson]] — an early Nauvoo printer embedded in the RLDS milieu — wrote to [[Joseph Smith III]] that [[Hyrum Smith]] personally "taught me the doctrine," told him and his wife "he heard the voice of the Lord give him the revelation on polygamy... to his brother Joseph," and concluded: "having a perfect personal knowledge of these facts... a denial of them sounds to me like a great lie. I'm sorry it is so, but we cannot undo the past." A man with no motive to lie *to Joseph's own son* affirmed the practice.[^4] *(Strong — an insider primary-source admission against interest.)*
4. **The RLDS historians recanted by the 1980s.** RLDS historians (Richard Howard and others) "came to the realization that he did it, and this actually went through the church and they admitted it." The Community of Christ (the RLDS successor) no longer denies Joseph's polygamy. The original, institutional, century-long denial *collapsed under its own research.*[^5] *(Strong — documented reversal by the very church founded on denial.)*

**Steelman of the denialist view.** A modern denier replies that the RLDS recantation proves nothing about the *facts*: institutions change positions for many reasons (assimilation toward mainstream scholarship, generational drift, a desire for respectability), and the Community of Christ's broader liberalizing trajectory makes its concession unsurprising and not necessarily evidence-driven. The denier also notes that the RLDS *had* the same documents and *still* denied for 120 years — which (the denier claims) shows the documents are genuinely contestable, not dispositive. And appealing to the RLDS as "the original deniers who gave up" is itself a *bandwagon/ genetic* move: that a group abandoned a view doesn't make the view false. The LDS rejoinder is that the RLDS reversal is significant precisely because it came from *hostile* historians with every institutional incentive to *keep* denying (their church was founded on it), working from the primary sources — so their concession is a costly, against-interest admission, exactly the kind of testimony that carries evidential weight, reinforced by insiders like Robinson who never had a motive to incriminate Joseph.

## LDS Relevance
Defuses the rhetorical force of the contemporary denial movement by showing it is a *re-run* of a fight the original deniers already lost on the merits — and that the affidavit record deniers must dismiss exists only because their own predecessors provoked it. It also supplies a pointed *tu quoque* within the [[The Implications Cascade of Polygamy Denial|implications cascade]]: if denial were correct, the RLDS (Community of Christ) "were right from the start," so a consistent denier should question why they remain in the "Brighamite" church at all — a tension Hansen presses. Connects to [[Emma Smith's Polygamy Testimony Splits Before and After 1860]] (the 1860 RLDS founding is the hinge date for Emma's statements).
