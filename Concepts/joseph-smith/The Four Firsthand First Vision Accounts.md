# The Four Firsthand First Vision Accounts

## The Claim

The frequently-cited "four to nine versions of the First Vision" resolves into a finite, well-defined corpus: **four distinct firsthand accounts** (1832, 1835, 1838, 1842) plus several secondhand accounts written by people who heard Joseph tell it. The higher numbers come from counting secondhand reports and brief journal references — not from Joseph telling fundamentally different stories. [[Kerry Muhlestein]], who wrote a book harmonizing them, lays out the inventory for a Protestant pastor. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Four distinct firsthand accounts — rate: strong (documentary)

Muhlestein lists them: **1832** (Joseph's first attempt at a history, in his own hand, in an unfinished publication); **1835** (a visitor came and Joseph told the story while a scribe happened to be present recording it); **1838** (the canonized Joseph Smith—History in the Pearl of Great Price); and **1842** (the **Wentworth letter**, written when John Wentworth of Chicago asked for a history of the church). [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — these four are the standard, published firsthand corpus (now collected in the Joseph Smith Papers).

### 2. The "nine" figure adds secondhand reports — rate: strong

Beyond the four, there are "five accounts of people who heard him who printed something about what they heard," plus scattered "little journal references." So "it depends on what you want to call an account, how many you have" — but only **four** distinct firsthand accounts exist; other firsthand-sounding instances are Joseph "quoting from one of the others." [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — clarifies that the inflated counts mix source types.

### 3. Joseph told the story often; scribal happenstance preserved some — rate: moderate

The 1835 account exists only because "a scribe happened to be with him" while he told it to a visitor — he "tells the story lots and lots and lots of times," so the surviving texts are samples of frequent retelling, not a handful of conflicting drafts. This sets up the audience-and-purpose explanation of their differences. See [[First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction]]. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — contextual, but well-supported by the documentary record.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can press that the *number* of accounts is a red herring; what matters is that the **earliest** (1832), in Joseph's own hand, mentions seeing "the Lord" (one personage) and omits the two-personage theophany and the sweeping condemnation of the churches that dominate the 1838 official version — which could indicate *doctrinal development* rather than stable memory. The reply (see [[First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels]] and [[First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction]]) is that a cursory personal account naturally compresses, that the accounts harmonize once their differing purposes are recognized, and that the core (deity appears, revelation given, remain aloof from existing churches) is constant — so a fixed, knowable corpus undercuts the "the story keeps changing" framing even if the development question remains debated.

## Best Quotes

> "There are four distinct firsthand accounts... 1832 is when he starts to first write a history. 1838 is when he writes the history that we have here in the Pearl of Great Price. In 1835 he has a visitor come to him and he tells him the story... and then in 1842... the Wentworth [letter]." [^1]

> "You only have those four firsthand accounts that are distinct. I mean, there's some other times where you get a firsthand account, but he's quoting from one of the others." [^2]
