# The Interlocking Polygamy Sources Defeat the Forgery Theory

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that the documentary record for [[Joseph Smith]]'s polygamy consists of **independent sources that interlock like puzzle pieces** — pro- and anti-polygamy, in Utah and far from Utah — in ways no coordinated later fabrication could produce. About 30 contemporary (Joseph's-lifetime) sources exist; their *undesigned coincidences* are the strongest argument against the "Brigham framed Joseph" theory.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. An anti-polygamy Nauvoo source names Joseph's wives in 1843 — rate: strong (hostile-witness convergence)
At Yale's Beinecke Library, a document has Relief Society minutes (1843) on the front and, on the back, [[Oliver]] — actually Oliver Olney (*Oliver and Phoebe Wheeler Olney*) — an *opponent* of polygamy, listing names of ~a dozen of Joseph's plural wives. These match women who later, in Utah, testified they were married to Joseph. A Nauvoo *enemy* of polygamy independently corroborates the Utah testimonies — and the Olneys never went west, so the document could not be part of a Utah conspiracy.[^2]

### 2. A private notebook and a marriage record interlock on the levirate point — rate: moderate (undesigned coincidence)
Franklin D. Richards's private "scriptural notes" record that on August 12, 1843, [[Hyrum Smith]] taught the High Council that the ancient *levirate* law (marrying a deceased brother's wife to raise up seed) would be practiced again — a detail *not* explicitly in D&C 132. Independently, Mercy Fielding Thompson reports being sealed the next day in a levirate marriage on behalf of her deceased husband (Hyrum's brother-in-law). A private notebook and a personal testimony, never published side by side, lock together.[^3]

### 3. The Church facilitated, not hid, the research — rate: moderate (access)
Bradley reports being given access to essentially all requested documents except one sealed 1850 disciplinary file (withheld to protect the disciplined individual, not the Church) — which he obtained elsewhere and found *favorable*. The institution publishing the Joseph Smith Papers behaved as a facilitator, not a concealer, which is hard to square with a cover-up.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The "puzzle pieces" framing is powerful but not airtight. (a) Interlock can be partly an artifact of *curation*: the surviving corpus has been assembled and interpreted within an "early polygamy" paradigm, and a denier argues that contrary contemporaneous documents (Joseph's public denials, the 1835 monogamy article) are downweighted while the incriminating ones are foregrounded. (b) Provenance of individual items (the Olney back-side list, a private notebook) still depends on dating and authorship judgments a skeptic can contest. (c) "The Church gave me access" speaks to *recent* institutional openness, not to whether 19th-century testimonies were shaped under Brigham-era pressure. (d) Undesigned coincidences are strong against a *single* coordinated forgery but weaker against a *diffuse* process of memory-conformity in a community that came to expect Joseph had practiced polygamy. So the honest weight is cumulative: the interlocking, geographically dispersed, pro- and anti- sources make a clean "Brigham framed Joseph" conspiracy very expensive to maintain — the denier must discount an implausibly large and varied body of evidence — without that cumulative case reaching the certainty of a single decisive document.

## Best Quotes

> "Both the people who are in favor of polygamy like Brigham... and the people from Nauvoo who were opposed to polygamy both agreed that Joseph was practicing it and who Joseph was marrying."[^2]

> "We just have so many puzzle pieces that fit... nobody's taking Franklin D. Richards's private notebook and publicly lining it up next to Mercy Fielding Thompson's testimony."[^3]

> "My experience was the opposite of them trying to hide information; they seem to be trying to help facilitate the research."[^4]
