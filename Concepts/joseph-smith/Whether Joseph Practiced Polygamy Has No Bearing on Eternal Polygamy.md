# Whether Joseph Practiced Polygamy Has No Bearing on Eternal Polygamy

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that the *historical* question — did [[Joseph Smith]] practice polygamy? — should be **decoupled from the pastoral fear that drives polygamy denial**: namely, that if Joseph practiced it, members are condemned to practice it for eternity. The history is settled by evidence; the eternity question is a separate theological matter that "this guy did it, so we all must" does not actually settle.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The historian's preference is irrelevant to the facts — rate: strong (method)
Bradley repeatedly states that he himself would *prefer* Joseph had not practiced polygamy, but "how comfortable I feel with Joseph Smith's polygamy is not actually relevant to whether he practiced it." The way to learn what happened is openness to data, not drawing a line in the sand about what one will believe. He cites Whitney Horning's stated decision to *begin* by trusting Joseph's public denials as an example of letting a value pre-commit the conclusion.[^2]

### 2. "He did it, so we must do it forever" is a non sequitur — rate: moderate (theology)
Even granting Joseph practiced polygamy, Bradley sees no entailment that everyone must live it eternally: it could have been specific to his life or time, or even a mistake, without condemning all the saved to eternal polygamy — "that's not how eternity works." So the motive for denial (escaping eternal polygamy) does not actually require denying the history.[^3]

### 3. A prophet's wrongdoing does not unmake him a prophet — rate: moderate (scriptural pattern)
Pressed on "how is he not lying?", Bradley argues the biblical prophets routinely did seriously wrong things (Abraham's deceptions, Jacob, Reuben, Judah, Peter's threefold denial) and were still chosen. Demanding that a true prophet never sin "changes the biblical definition" of a prophet. So even the *worst* construal of Joseph's concealment does not, by itself, refute his prophetic claim — removing another motive to deny the history. See [[The Implications Cascade of Polygamy Denial]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The denier's concerns are not as easily separated as Bradley suggests. (a) For many "pneumos," the objection is *not* fear of eternal polygamy but the moral character question: a prophet secretly marrying other men's wives and publicly lying about it looks, to them, incompatible with prophethood — and Bradley's "prophets sinned too" reply, while scriptural, can prove too much (it could excuse almost any conduct). (b) Decoupling history from theology is itself a *theological* move that a believer in continuing revelation may reject: if D&C 132 ties plural marriage to *exaltation*, then whether Joseph practiced it is not obviously severable from what eternity requires. (c) "He might have done it by mistake" sits uneasily with treating Section 132 as binding revelation. So Bradley's decoupling rightly removes one *bad* reason to deny the history (motivated reasoning to escape an eternal mandate) and rightly insists evidence settles the historical question — but it understates how, for the denier, the moral and doctrinal stakes remain entangled with the facts, which is why the debate persists even once the history is granted.

## Best Quotes

> "How comfortable I feel with Joseph Smith's polygamy is not actually relevant to whether he practiced it."[^2]

> "Why couldn't Joseph Smith have practiced polygamy for reasons that were specific to his life or his time... that doesn't mean that we're all going to be condemned somehow to living polygamy forever."[^3]

> "You have to change the biblical definition and presentation of what a prophet is to say he no longer counts as a prophet because of this behavior."[^4]
