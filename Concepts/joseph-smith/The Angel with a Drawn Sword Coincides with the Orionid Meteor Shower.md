# The Angel with a Drawn Sword Coincides with the Orionid Meteor Shower

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that the famous "angel with a drawn sword" — by which [[Joseph Smith]] said he was compelled to proceed with plural marriage — coincides, to the day, with a real astronomical event independently witnessed on the other side of the world, and that this points to a sincere prophet responding to perceived heavenly signs rather than a manipulator inventing a threat. [[Zina Huntington Jacobs]] recorded that Joseph "put it off and put it off" until "an angel with a drawn sword stood by me and told me that if I did not establish that principle… I would lose my position and my life." Joseph sealed Zina on **Oct 27, 1841**, so the angelic episode falls *shortly before* that date — and Bradley finds [[Orson Hyde]], on a ship near Beirut, recorded a "bright glittering sword… an arm with the perfect hand stretched itself out and took hold on the hilt" in the night sky on **Oct 19, 1841 at ~1 a.m.**, which Bradley identifies as the **Orionid meteor shower**.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Two independent sword-in-the-sky reports cluster in mid-October 1841 — rate: striking, source-based
[[Zina Huntington Jacobs|Zina]]'s account places Joseph's "drawn sword" experience just before the Oct 27 sealing; [[Orson Hyde]]'s mission letter records a glittering sword and a hand grasping its hilt in the heavens on Oct 19 — and notes the Arab sailors cried out, indicating a real, shared sky event, not a private vision.[^1][^2] *Striking as a coincidence of independent testimony; the inference drawn from it is interpretive.*

### 2. The astronomy fits — rate: moderate, checkable
Bradley identifies the event as the **Orionids** — debris from Halley's Comet, which Earth passes each mid-October; the radiant sits near Orion's upraised arm and the weapon he is depicted holding, matching "a sword… a hand on the hilt." He states the shower peaked Oct 19 at ~1 a.m., aligning with Hyde's timestamp.[^2] *Moderate: the Orionids are real and seasonally correct; the precise peak time and the "looks like a drawn sword" reading are the softer parts.*

### 3. The inference: signs, not manipulation — rate: interpretive
Bradley's conclusion is dual: either Joseph had a genuine supernatural experience confirmed in the heavens for Hyde, or he sincerely *interpreted* a celestial sign as divine instruction — and **either way he is religiously sincere**, because "a con man doesn't care what the universe is trying to tell him." Soon after, Joseph resumed work on the Book of Abraham (an astronomy-laden text), which Bradley reads as thematically consonant. See [[The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man]] and [[Joseph Smith Was Sincere Not a Fraud]].[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic's strongest replies: (a) **the sources are late and mutually independent in the wrong way** — [[Zina Huntington Jacobs|Zina]]'s "drawn sword" recollection was recorded decades later and uses by-then-stock polygamy-revelation language, and Orson Hyde's meteor sighting has no stated connection to Joseph at all; linking the two is the historian's overlay, not the witnesses'. (b) **A coincidence is not a confirmation** — the Orionids recur *every* October, so "a sword in the sky in mid-October" is unremarkable and predicts nothing; finding a meteor shower near the date of a documented sealing is easy and proves only that the sky behaved normally. (c) Even granting Joseph sincerely read a sign, **sincerity does not establish truth or vindicate the practice** — sincere people misread omens all the time, and the ethical objections to coerced plural marriage (see [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel]]) are untouched by his being non-cynical. A Latter-day Saint reply (in the episode): the argument is modest and aimed only at the *con-man* thesis — it shows Joseph behaving like a man who believed the heavens were speaking to him (delaying, agonizing, then yielding to a perceived sign), which is the opposite of a calculating fraud; the meteor-shower correlation is offered as a suggestive convergence, not a proof, and Bradley frames the whole thing as "either/or," explicitly allowing the natural-sign reading.

## LDS Connection
This pairs with [[Joseph Smith Married Pregnant Women Refuting the Sexual-Motive Thesis]] as the second prong of Bradley's case that Nauvoo polygamy was not opportunistic: the pregnant-bride pattern removes the sexual motive, and the drawn-sword/Orionid convergence supplies a positive picture of a sincere prophet acting under perceived compulsion. It feeds the vault's repeated rebuttal to [[The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man|the con-man frame]]. Honest takeaway: the convergence of two independent "sword in the heavens" reports in the same week is genuinely arresting, but the every-October recurrence of the Orionids is exactly the lever a careful skeptic will pull, and the argument establishes sincerity at most, not the divinity of the command.
