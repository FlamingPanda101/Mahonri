# The Plagiarism Charge Boomerangs on the Critic

#type/claim

## The Claim
Matt Fradd's "fabrication and plagiarism" reason is that the Book of Mormon mirrors Joseph Smith's time and place: period books theorized that American Indians descended from Israel, and Joseph "lifted entire sections from the King James Bible, even copying translation errors." Luke Hansen makes three moves.

1. **The translation-error point assumes a standard the book disowns.** Catholics (and most Christians) hold biblical infallibility "as long as it's translated correctly"; but the Book of Mormon's own title page disclaims this — "if there are faults, they are the mistakes of men." If God permits faults in the *writing* of scripture, he may permit them in the *translation*, especially where they cause no doctrinal harm (which Fradd never alleges). See [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught]]. [^1]

2. **Sharing the era's biblical language is not plagiarism — and the differences refute borrowing.** Per [[Richard Bushman]], other authors who treated Indian origins "were explicit about recognizable Indian practices and the location of particular tribes," whereas the Book of Mormon "deposits its people on some unknown shore... using names that had no connection to modern Indians." *View of the Hebrews* "was an anthropological treatise"; the Book of Mormon "was a narrative, not a treatise" that "refuses to argue its own theory." Decisively, where contemporaries shared Jacksonian racism, the Book of Mormon **overturns conventional American racism**: it makes the Indians "the chosen of God" and "the foundations of civilization in the New World," with European migrants entering late as "Gentiles" and "interlopers." See [[Shared Biblical Sources Are Not Plagiarism]] and [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]]. [^2]

3. **The boomerang.** "If you need plagiarism to explain *some* parts of the Book of Mormon, then what about the parts for which no outside source can be found? And if Joseph didn't need plagiarism for those parts, then why did he need it for the others?" The book contains unique material — the allegory of the olive tree (Jacob 5), ~40 unique doctrinal teachings catalogued by [[Robert Millet]] — for which the borrowing hypothesis has nothing to offer. [^3]

Hansen concedes the broader plagiarism reason was "one of Matt's stronger, albeit very short, points," giving credit where due.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman Fradd)
1. **Real King James material is present.** The Book of Mormon does contain long stretches of KJV Isaiah and other passages, including KJV-specific renderings; that demands an explanation, and "Joseph used the biblical idiom available to him" is one. *Rating: the datum is genuine; only its interpretation is contested.*
2. **The Hebraic-Indian theory was indeed in the air.** That 1820s New York had popular works proposing Israelite origins for the Indians is uncontested (see [[The Hebraic-Indian Theory Was a Common Period Idea]]); the question is whether *availability* of an idea equals *derivation* of a 500-page narrative. *Rating: accurate background; weak as proof of dependence.*
3. **The infallibility framing is fair to engage.** Catholics do qualify inerrancy by correct translation, so pointing to "translation errors" is a coherent internal jab — which is exactly why the title-page disclaimer is the right answer. *Rating: well-posed objection, met on its own terms.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic answers that the boomerang cuts the other way: a 19th-century author *would* mix borrowed scaffolding (KJV diction, the period's Israelite-Indian premise) with original invention, so "some borrowed, some not" is precisely what naturalistic authorship predicts — it is not evidence of antiquity. Bushman's "narrative not treatise" and "overturns racism" observations show *literary sophistication*, not *historicity*; talented authors routinely subvert their culture's assumptions. And "copying KJV translation errors" is harder to wave off than archaic phrasing, since an *ancient* record translated by revelation should not reproduce a *16th-century* mistake. The Latter-day Saint reply is the title-page concession (translation is human and may carry the translator's idiom) plus [[Book of Mormon Lacks the Hebrew Markers View of the Hebrews Uses]]: the specific literary fingerprints of the alleged source are absent, so direct dependence is not demonstrated. The dispute then turns on whether shared *biblical* language plus residual original content better fits inspired translation or skilled fabrication — which this single reason does not settle.
