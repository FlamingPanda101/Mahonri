# The Creeds Abomination Targets Teachings Not Christians

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection (felt personally by the interviewing Protestant pastor): the First Vision calls "all their creeds... an abomination" and "those professors... all corrupt" ([[Joseph Smith History 1.19|JSH 1:19]]) — fighting words that seem to damn creedal Christians. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] answers that the harsh language targets *false teachings about God*, not individual Christians, and that the Lord uses equally strong language toward *insiders*. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Lord uses the same strong language *inward* — rate: strong

Muhlestein: in other revelations the Lord "uses very strong language about Joseph Smith, about his followers" — he "calls Joseph wicked at times. He calls [[Martin Harris]] wicked," then adds "but you can repent, you can come to me." So "while this language may seem strong because it's pointed outward, that strong of language is pointed inward at times as well." The harshness is not anti-Christian animus; "the Lord doesn't mince words." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a documented pattern (e.g., D&C 3, D&C 5) of severe language toward the faithful that still invites repentance.

### 2. "Abomination" attaches to creeds (teachings), not to people — rate: moderate (Muhlestein flags it as his reading)

His "best guess": the Lord is saying "there are teachings about me and my nature and who I am that are not accurate, and I don't like that." The "abomination" is the *creeds'* mischaracterization of the Father, the Son, and their relationship — the theme of John's Gospel, where "Christ talks [about] his relationship with his father more than anything else." Muhlestein is candid that even among LDS scholars "there's not universal agreement" on exactly why the word "creed" is used. See [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — an interpretive reading the speaker explicitly marks as a "best guess," not settled doctrine.

### 3. Joseph Smith defended Christians and saw good in them — rate: strong

Muhlestein is "emphatic": the verse does not make Christians abominable. "Joseph Smith talked about his love for other Christians, that he would defend other Christians and their rights to believe what they believe... the good that came from all of his Christian friends." President [[Gordon B. Hinckley]]: "We invite you to bring all the good that you have and see if we can add to it a little bit." The text condemns *no* "professors of Christianity" as persons. See [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]]. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — consistent with Joseph Smith's documented statements on religious liberty and the good in other faiths.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A creedal Christian can fairly answer that the plain text says the *professors* (not just the creeds) "were all corrupt," and that on Latter-day Saint premises the creeds encode the *nature of Christ* — so to call them an "abomination" is to condemn the very heart of historic Christian confession, which is not a peripheral matter. Muhlestein concedes the language is genuinely offensive on its face ("I can absolutely understand someone being offended at that") and that the Trinity is "as fundamental as it gets." His reply is not that the difference is small but that the *target* is the doctrine, not the disciple: the same God who calls the creeds an abomination calls Joseph "wicked" and bids him repent — so the verse is a critique of teaching, paired with love for the people who hold it. See [[Both Sides Overcorrected in the LDS-Evangelical Divide]] and the existing [[The Nicene Creed Is Not an Abomination]].
