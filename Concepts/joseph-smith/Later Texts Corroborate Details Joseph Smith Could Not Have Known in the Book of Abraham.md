# Later Texts Corroborate Details Joseph Smith Could Not Have Known in the Book of Abraham

## The Claim

[[Luke Hansen]] inverts [[Mike Winger]]'s slogan ("it's not what we don't know, it's what we *do* know that proves the Book of Abraham wrong"): there are several specifics in the [[Book of Abraham]] that ran *against* the scholarship of [[Joseph Smith]]'s day but have since been **corroborated by texts discovered after 1842** — the kind of "reverse-anachronism" a fabricator could not have engineered.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Abraham was literate and an astronomer — rate: moderate (later-text corroboration)
In Joseph's day, scholars pictured Abraham as an illiterate herder whose stories were later written down. The Book of Abraham instead has Abraham *reading*, *writing*, and teaching *astronomy*. Texts unavailable to Joseph corroborate this: Vettius Valens (2nd c. AD) cites "Abraham['s]... books on the study" of the heavens; the Book of Jubilees has Joseph in Egypt recalling "the words of Abraham." See [[The 6% Astrology Outlier and Abraham 3]].[^2]

### 2. Human/child sacrifice in Egypt and the Levant — rate: moderate (later corroboration)
Egyptologists long *denied* Egyptian human sacrifice; the Book of Abraham depicts attempted sacrifice (Facsimile 1) and child sacrifice. Later evidence shows limited Egyptian human sacrifice (e.g., for apostasy/treason, since religion and state were fused in Pharaoh), and a 5th–6th-century-AD text states the first child sacrifice "occurred in the days of Abraham's father" and spread — matching the book.[^3]

### 3. Egyptian sphere of influence reached Abraham's region — rate: weak/moderate (geographic corroboration)
The book has Egyptian-style sacrifice occurring where Abraham lived; evidence that Egypt's sphere of influence extended that far (and later contracted, so Abraham could return to a now-non-Egyptian region) fits the narrative's movements. Offered as supporting texture, not proof.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

"Hits" need to be weighed against the base rate and the misses. (a) Some parallels are loose or cherry-picked: "Abraham as astronomer" is also a *widespread* late-antique and medieval legend (Josephus already calls Abraham a teacher of astronomy), so it was arguably available in the cultural air, not unknowable to a well-read 19th-century American with access to Josephus. (b) Confirmation bias: a long text will inevitably match *some* later discoveries by chance; a fair test must also count the places where the Book of Abraham conflicts with what we now know (the facsimile interpretations Egyptology rejects). (c) Late apocryphal texts (5th–6th c. AD) attesting "child sacrifice in Abraham's father's day" are themselves legendary accretions, not independent historical confirmation of the patriarch's era. (d) The argument proves at most that the book contains *some* plausibly ancient motifs — consistent with a gifted synthesizer drawing on the Bible and available lore — not that it is a translation. So the honest framing (which the hosts adopt) is the cautious one: these are *evidences* that cut against the "obviously fabricated" verdict and deserve weighing, explicitly avoiding Winger's mistake of overstating them into proof.
