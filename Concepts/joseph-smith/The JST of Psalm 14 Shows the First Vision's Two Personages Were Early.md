# The JST of Psalm 14 Shows the First Vision's Two Personages Were Early

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] and [[Walker Wright]] (in a *BYU Studies* article, discussed on [[Ward Radio]]) argue that Joseph Smith's **Joseph Smith Translation** of **Psalm 14**, datable to **1832–33**, already encodes the First Vision — including the involvement of **the Father** seen through the Son. This undercuts the common skeptical claim that Joseph added the second personage (the Father, making the vision *two* personages) only later, in the 1835+ accounts. The earliest layer of his Bible revision, contemporaneous with his 1832 First Vision account, already reflects "no man hath seen God... except he hath borne record of the Son."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The JST rewrites Psalm 14's opening toward the First Vision — rate: moderate (primary text)
Joseph revised "*The fool hath said in his heart, There is no God*" ([[Psalm 14.1|Psalm 14:1]]) to, in effect, "**no man hath seen God**... except he hath... borne record of the Son" — paralleling [[John 1.18|John 1:18]]. The rewrite imports a *seeing-the-Father-through-the-Son* structure precisely matching the First Vision's two-personage theology.[^1]

### 2. The dating is the load-bearing point — rate: strong (chronology)
The JST of Psalm 14 belongs to Joseph's 1832–33 Bible-revision work — the same window in which he penned the 1832 First Vision account. So the "two personages" idea is documented in 1832–33 scripture, not invented in 1835+. The skeptical "he added the Father later" timeline collapses if the Father's involvement is already in the 1832–33 JST.[^1]

### 3. The recast psalm echoes First-Vision language throughout — rate: moderate (parallel)
Beyond the opening, Joseph recast the rest of Psalm 14 with First-Vision-flavored phrasing ("they draw near to me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me"), reinforcing that he was reading his own theophany into the text at this early date. This sits alongside the early-theosis argument from the seer-stone discovery. See [[Joseph's Seer-Stone Discovery Frames Theosis From the Start of the Restoration]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

(a) The JST is a **revision/expansion**, not a manuscript witness, so a critic can say it shows Joseph's *evolving* theology in 1832–33, not a fixed early "two personages" doctrine — and 1832–33 is still *after* the 1820 event, leaving room for development between vision and account. (b) The verbal parallel to John 1:18 ("no man hath seen God") is suggestive but not an explicit narration of two distinct personages; reading the full later doctrine into it may over-claim. (c) The dating of specific JST passages can be debated. What survives is the chronological wedge that matters: the Father's involvement (seeing God through the Son) is attested in *1832–33* material, which is hard to square with the "the Father only appears in the 1835+ retellings" thesis — the claim is dating, and on dating the argument is strong even if the fuller theological reading is contestable.

## Best Quote
> Joseph rewrote "there is no God" into "no man hath seen God... except he hath borne record of the Son" — in 1832–33, already encoding the First Vision. *(argument paraphrase)* [^1]
