# Joseph Smith's Childhood Leg Surgery Was a Rare Experimental Operation

## The Claim
The 1813 operation on seven-year-old [[Joseph Smith]]'s leg was not a routine procedure but one of the rarest surgeries in early American medicine — an experimental limb-saving operation pioneered by Dr. [[Nathan Smith]] at Dartmouth — performed on a child *without anesthetic* at his own insistence. [[Don Bradley]] argues this is precisely the "extraordinary event in early life" a biographer should look for to explain an extraordinary person.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The medical context (strong, documented).** A typhoid epidemic during the War of 1812 left Joseph with a bone infection (osteomyelitis) that migrated from shoulder to leg. The default treatment was amputation; the Smiths "providentially" lived near Dartmouth, where [[Nathan Smith]] had developed a method to remove diseased bone and save the limb.[^2]
2. **The procedure was brutal and pre-anesthetic (strong, documented).** Surgeons used old-fashioned hand-crank drills to break the tibia, then "sawing, hammering, chiseling," removing nine large pieces of bone (with fourteen more fragments later working their way out) on a seven-year-old's shin with no anesthesia.[^3]
3. **It was almost unique in U.S. history (moderate, per Bradley).** Only "a few dozen people in the history of the United States" ever had the surgery; Nathan Smith "was the only one who knew how to do it," and after he died others could not replicate it correctly — so Joseph was "almost certainly" the only child ever to undergo it, and "probably only one [person]… ever had it without anesthetic."[^4]
4. **The child directed the operation (strong, textual).** Per [[Lucy Mack Smith]], Joseph refused brandy and wine, insisted his mother leave, and asked only that his father hold him — then "screamed so loud" that Lucy, though far from the house, came running back twice before being restrained.[^5]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A historian might caution that the "only person ever / only child ever" superlatives rest on Bradley's characterization rather than a cited surgical census, and that [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s memoir, written decades later, may dramatize the scene (the vividness of the screaming, the precise bone counts). The core fact — a serious, pre-anesthetic bone operation — is secure; the rhetorical uniqueness is harder to verify.

## Counter-Objection
Even granting some embellishment, the medical substance is independently plausible: Nathan Smith's limb-conservation work at Dartmouth is documented medical history, amputation was indeed the era's default, and osteomyelitis from typhoid is a recognized sequela. The surgery's extraordinariness does not depend on the exact body count; it is enough that a young child endured major orthopedic surgery without anesthetic, which grounds the "extraordinary event → extraordinary person" biographical inference. See [[We Become What We Sacrifice For]].
