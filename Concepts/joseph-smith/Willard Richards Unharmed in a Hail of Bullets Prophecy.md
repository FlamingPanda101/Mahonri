# Willard Richards Unharmed in a Hail of Bullets Prophecy

## The Claim
[[Joseph Smith]] reportedly prophesied, more than a year before the event, that the time would come when bullets "would fly around [Willard Richards] like hail" and he would "see his friends fall on the right and on the left, but that there should be not a hole in his garment." At Carthage Jail (June 1844), Richards — a large man amid the gunfire that killed Joseph and Hyrum — escaped untouched except for a ball grazing the tip of his left ear.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Richards's near-miraculous escape is historical.** He was present at the Carthage attack and emerged essentially unharmed. (Evidence rating: outcome real and striking.)[^1]
2. **No contemporaneous record of the prophecy.** The host's decisive caveat: "there's nothing contemporaneously written down in 1843, 1844 about this prophecy. This does seem to be later written in to the history of the church." See [[Absence of Contemporaneous Sources Undermines a Prophecy]]. (Evidence rating: this is the central weakness — a vivid prophecy attested only after the fulfillment is a prime retrofit candidate.)[^1]
3. **The grazed ear complicates "not a hole in his garment."** The prophecy specifies his garment would be untouched; a ball grazing his ear is a slight tension with the literal claim, suggesting either fortunate approximation or later shaping. (Evidence rating: minor internal friction.)[^1]
4. **Steelman of the believing view.** A large man standing in a fusillade that killed two others, surviving with only an ear graze, is genuinely remarkable; if Joseph truly foretold it a year prior, it is among the more impressive items. But because the prophecy appears only in the later *History of the Church* — fulfillment-first — the host treats it as likely written in after the fact.
