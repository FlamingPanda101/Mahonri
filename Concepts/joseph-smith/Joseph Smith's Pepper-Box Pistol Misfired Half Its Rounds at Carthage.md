# Joseph Smith's Pepper-Box Pistol Misfired Half Its Rounds at Carthage

## The Claim
The pistol [[Joseph Smith]] fired into the mob at Carthage was an 1827 **Allen & Thurber "pepper-box"** — a multi-barrel revolving cap-and-ball gun. Of its six rounds, only **three actually fired**; the other three failed. [[Shannon Tracy]] explains the mechanism: each barrel was capped, charged with black (not smokeless) powder, and the muzzle dipped in **wax** to keep the powder dry, but the recoil of firing "shakes the whole gun," so percussion caps tended to fall off. Therefore the *first* barrel is the most likely to fire, and reliability decreases down the cylinder.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The misfire pattern is mechanically coherent.** A cap-and-ball pepper-box really is prone to caps loosening under recoil, and a three-of-six result is consistent with that known unreliability. The host notes Ward Radio owns several of the same 1827 Allen & Thurber model. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong as physics; the specific "three fired" count rests on the historical accounts Tracy is summarizing.)[^1]
2. **Black powder, not smokeless, changes the scene.** Tracy stresses the period guns used black powder, which produces a large smoke cloud — so four or five shots in a closed room quickly fill it with a haze, and a ball through the door panel sends out smoke plus wood splinters ("if somebody's face was up against that door they would look like a porcupine"). This bears on visibility and on why accounts of who-shot-whom are murky. (Evidence rating: moderate — the smoke behavior is real and relevant to reconstructing sightlines.)[^2]
3. **Wax-sealing the muzzles is a documented period practice.** Tracy says his gunsmith confirmed shooters dipped the loaded barrel ends in wax to keep powder and ball seated — a plausible field expedient that also explains additional misfire risk if the seal failed. (Evidence rating: low-to-moderate — anecdotal via his gunsmith, but congruent with cap-and-ball handling.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The misfire detail is forensically minor and partly folkloric: the exact "three of six" is reported, not demonstrated, and the wax-dipping claim comes secondhand from Tracy's gunsmith. None of it settles the contested questions (who fired the fatal shots, where Joseph died). A skeptic would also note the framing subtly valorizes Joseph — a prophet defending his brethren with a malfunctioning gun — which is emotionally loaded. The fair, narrow point that survives is technical and unobjectionable: a black-powder pepper-box is an unreliable weapon whose smoke would have degraded visibility in the room, which any reconstruction must model.

## Best Quotes
> "When Joseph shot this, only three of these rounds actually fired and three other ones didn't... your first shot is the one that's most likely to fire, and the probability decreases as you go through the barrel."[^1]

> "If you're in a room and you get four or five shots into the room, you're soon walking in a cloud."[^2]
