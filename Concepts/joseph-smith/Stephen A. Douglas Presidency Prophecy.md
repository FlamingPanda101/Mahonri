# Stephen A. Douglas Presidency Prophecy

## The Claim
On 18 May 1843, in an after-dinner conversation recorded by [[William Clayton]], [[Joseph Smith]] reportedly told Judge [[Stephen A. Douglas]]: "Judge, you will aspire to the presidency of the United States. And if you ever turn your hand against me or the Latter-day Saints, you will feel the weight of the hand of the Almighty upon you." Douglas became the 1860 Democratic presidential nominee, denounced the Mormons during the campaign, lost decisively to Lincoln (12 electoral votes to 108), and died about a year later.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The prediction's content matches Douglas's later career.** He did aspire to the presidency, did turn against the Mormons, and did decline politically thereafter. (Evidence rating: striking fit — *if* the full text predates 1860.)[^1]
2. **The crucial textual problem.** The host fact-checks: the *History of the Church* cites Clayton's journal, "however... the original entry for the 18th of May 1843 does not include the expanded text of the Douglas prophecy as it appears in the history of the church." The fuller prophecy was "first published in 1856 in the Deseret News" and the 1859 *Millennial Star*. See [[Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment]] and [[Absence of Contemporaneous Sources Undermines a Prophecy]]. (Evidence rating: the contemporaneous journal lacks the predictive detail — a serious problem.)[^2]
3. **But the published text still predates the fulfillment.** Importantly, 1856/1859 publication is *before* Douglas's 1860 nomination and defeat. The host: "even if this wasn't put in until 1856 or 1859, this is actually still before he ran for the presidency in 1860... they still had this recorded before this event happened." (Evidence rating: this materially rehabilitates the claim — the prediction is on record pre-outcome, even if not in the 1843 journal.)[^3]
4. **Steelman of the skeptic.** Douglas was already a rising national figure by 1856–59; predicting that a prominent senator would "aspire to the presidency" was a reasonable political read, and the anti-Mormon-then-downfall element could be shaped in the published account. The strongest believing point — pre-1860 publication — is partly offset by the prophecy's plausibility for a known political star.
