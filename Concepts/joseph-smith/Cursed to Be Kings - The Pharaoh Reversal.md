# Cursed to Be Kings - The Pharaoh Reversal

## The Claim
Far from condemning the descendants of [[Ham]] to slavery, the [[Book of Abraham]] *inverts* the pro-slavery curse-of-Ham reading: it blesses them with kingship. [[Pharaoh]] — explicitly a descendant of Ham — is "the most powerful man in the world," so the so-called curse "is the flip side of a blessing. They're going to be kings. They're cursed to be kings." [[Don Bradley]] calls this the maximal reversal: "You can't get more radical."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The internal contradiction of the slavery reading (rated: strong, logical).** If Pharaoh's line is "blessed with the blessings of kingship," it is incoherent to conclude they are "supposed to be slaves." "Obviously, if they're blessed with kingship, they shouldn't be slaves... It would be ridiculous."[^2] The text praises the Hamite line's wisdom and earthly blessings, not their degradation. *Steelman:* a critic can answer that Abraham 1 still *bars* Pharaoh's line from the priesthood, and "blessed with kingship but barred from priesthood" is exactly the dual structure later used to say Black members could be saved but not ordained — so "cursed to be kings" does not actually dissolve the priesthood exclusion, it relocates it. Bradley's rebuttal depends on the "division of labor" frame (see [[The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment]]) and on Joseph's actual ordination of Black men (see [[Joseph Smith Ignored Race-Based Scriptural Distinctions]]).

2. **Joseph applied the reversal politically (rated: strong, documented).** Joseph Smith "opposed slavery and he advocated for African Americans to be able to vote," reversing the traditional curse that "would have given people of African descent no political power."[^3] His logic: "Why would you think that God cursed these people to have no power and be slaves... Look at Pharaoh. He was the most powerful man in the world." He is "seeing it the opposite of what his surrounding cultures are saying."

3. **Restoration extends the reversal universally (rated: moderate, doctrinal).** The further reversal in the Restoration is that "now everybody can be a king. Now everybody can be blessed to be a priest as well" — lineal restriction giving way to universal access.[^4] Bradley frames Joseph as "so far ahead of his time," "setting the precedent."

## Best Quotes

> "He's like, no, actually, the curse is the flip side of a blessing. They're going to be kings. They're cursed to be kings. ... That's opposite. You can't get more radical."[^1]

> "Look at Pharaoh. He was the most powerful man in the world. Why would you think that God cursed these people to have no power and be slaves...?"[^3]

> "It would be ridiculous to think they're blessed with the blessings of kingship, but that means they're supposed to be slaves. No way. It's not even the same conversation."[^2]
