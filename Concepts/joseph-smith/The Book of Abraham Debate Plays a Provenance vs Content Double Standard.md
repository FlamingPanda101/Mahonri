# The Book of Abraham Debate Plays a Provenance vs Content Double Standard

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]] argue that critics of the Book of Abraham run a **selective double standard** between two modes of authentication — *provenance* (where a text came from, its chain of custody) and *content/forensics* (what the text actually says). The critic attacks the **Book of Abraham purely on provenance** (the papyri, the mummies, what Egyptologists say) and pointedly **never reads the text**; but attacks the **Book of Mormon purely on content** (alleged anachronisms like steel) while **waving away its provenance** (the eleven witnesses, the plates, the angel). Whichever mode is inconvenient gets dropped. The honest method is to apply *both* consistently — and Barnes's challenge is simply: "Have you read it?" [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The critic discusses the papyri but never quotes the Book of Abraham — rate: strong (direct observation of the source video)
Barnes notes that across the critic's segment on the Book of Abraham, "he doesn't mention a word of the Book of Abraham — he only talks about the papyrie and the mummies and what did Egyptologists say," never the text's content. The entire attack is on the *mode of production* (Joseph buying papyri, the facsimiles), not on any claim the text makes. [^1]

### 2. For the Book of Mormon the critic inverts the standard — rate: strong (direct observation)
On the Book of Mormon the same critic concedes the witnesses ("eleven witnesses who signed affidavit… three even claimed to have seen the angel Moroni — that's pretty impressive on the surface") and then *dismisses provenance* — "even if several people saw the gold plates, that says nothing about whether those plates contain divine truth" — to attack content instead. So "the content only matters when you want it to matter, and the provenance matters when you want it to matter." [^2]

### 3. Forensics legitimately *can* override provenance — but must be applied evenly — rate: moderate (method)
Ellis frames the two modes with an analogy: provenance is "the story of how something came to be" (e.g., "these are Mick Jagger's glasses"); forensics can override it ("this polymer didn't exist until 1993"). The vault's [[Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance]] grants exactly this — forensics can vindicate *or* unmask in either direction. The objection is not that one mode is illegitimate, but that the critic switches modes to suit the desired verdict. [^3]

### 4. Reading the text reverses the burden — rate: moderate (depends on the parallels case)
Once you *do* read the text, Barnes argues, the Book of Abraham "impossibly predicts" details found only in post-1842 apocrypha (Abraham opposed idols, Chaldea imported Egyptian idolatry with human sacrifice, Abraham as astronomer, Lucifer's envy of Michael). If the translation is a fraud "you have a bigger problem now — you have to explain how Joseph got all these things right without a source text," which makes him a *prophet*, "harder than being a translator." This is the textual case made in [[Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842]] and [[Later Texts Corroborate Details Joseph Smith Could Not Have Known in the Book of Abraham]]. [^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's asymmetry is *defensible*, not mere inconsistency. (a) The two books face different evidentiary situations: for the Book of Abraham we **possess the source object** (the Joseph Smith Papyri, Facsimile 1), and it demonstrably is a standard funerary document — a kind of disconfirmation that the Book of Mormon's lost golden plates can never receive, so attacking the Book of Abraham's provenance is uniquely *available*. (b) "Content parallels" cut both ways: a parallel list with no textual-dependency analysis carries little weight, and many of the "impossible" Abraham details have plausible 19th-century channels (Josephus, *Antiquities*; common Bible-belt lore; Thomas Dick's astronomy), so dismissing the Book of Mormon's anachronisms while crediting the Book of Abraham's parallels could itself be the double standard, run the other way. (c) Witness testimony genuinely *is* weaker than physical evidence for establishing *what a text means* — seeing plates says nothing about the translation's accuracy, which is a fair epistemic point, not a dodge. The strongest reply (which the hosts make) is methodological consistency: whatever standard authenticates or defeats one book should be applied to the other, and to the critic's own scripture — the resurrection rests on witnesses with *worse* provenance than the golden plates (see [[The Eyewitness Double Standard Favors the Gold Plates Over the Resurrection]]).
