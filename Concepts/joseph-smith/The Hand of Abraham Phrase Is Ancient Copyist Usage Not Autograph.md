# The Hand of Abraham Phrase Is Ancient Copyist Usage Not Autograph

## The Claim

[[Mike Winger]]'s repeated "silver bullet" is that the [[Book of Abraham]] says it was written "by [Abraham's] own hand, upon papyrus" ([[Abraham 1]]), yet the surviving papyrus dates to ~the second century BC — roughly **1,800 years after Abraham**. [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Luke Hansen]] reply that "by his own hand" is **standard ancient copyist usage**: a *copy* of an original was routinely said to be "by the hand" of the original author. The phrase does not claim an autograph, so an 1,800-year gap is exactly what one expects of a transmitted text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The same logic indicts the Bible's own books — rate: strong (parallel)
Our oldest manuscripts of Genesis, Exodus, and Deuteronomy postdate Moses by centuries; our oldest copies of Acts postdate Luke. Yet these are accepted as "copies of the original." If "the manuscript is centuries later than the author" disproved authorship, it would disprove the Pentateuch and Acts too. So the dating gap is not a special problem for Abraham.[^2]

### 2. Egyptian and Greek precedent for "wrote it himself" meaning "a copy was made" — rate: moderate (period usage)
The Shabaka Stone — among the most ancient Egyptian writings — opens by announcing that "his majesty wrote this book down anew," yet (per the Egyptologist cited) it is *normal Egyptian usage* to express "the king ordered a copy to be made" as the king writing it himself. Likewise, books were "released" under Pythagoras's name 400 years after his death. Ancient convention attributed copies to the original author.[^3]

### 3. The book's own text says the picture is *not* adjacent — rate: moderate (internal evidence)
A related Winger claim is that the text beside Facsimile 1 is a Book of Breathings, not Abraham — supposedly proving Joseph invented the rest. But the Book of Abraham *itself* says it refers the reader to "the picture at the beginning of this record" — i.e., the text explicitly indicates it is *not* immediately next to its facsimile; and in contemporary papyri the text correlates with the adjacent vignette only ~50% of the time. See [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's case is not trivial. (a) The Book of Abraham's superscription is more *specific* than a generic colophon — "by his own hand, upon papyrus" reads like a claim about *this very document's* origin, which a critic argues invites the autograph reading the apologist resists. (b) The surviving fragments include the very vignette reproduced as Facsimile 1, and the adjacent text *is* a routine Book of Breathings — so the recovered papyrus is *connected* to the project, not random, which strengthens "Joseph thought he was translating *this*." (c) The Shabaka-Stone and Pythagoras parallels show the convention *existed*, but do not prove the Book of Abraham's superscription was *meant* that way rather than literally. (d) Some early Latter-day Saint materials (the Egyptian Alphabet and Grammar) tie specific characters on the surviving fragment to specific Book of Abraham passages, which a critic reads as Joseph treating the extant fragment as his source. So the fair conclusion: "by his own hand" *can* be ordinary copyist idiom — defeating the claim that the 1,800-year gap is *by itself* disqualifying — while whether *this* superscription means that, and whether Joseph's source was the extant fragment or a lost roll, remains contested rather than closed.
