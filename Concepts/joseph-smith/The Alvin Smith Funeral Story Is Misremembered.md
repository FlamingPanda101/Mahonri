# The Alvin Smith Funeral Story Is Misremembered

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A staple of restoration origins is that a Presbyterian minister told the Smith family that [[Alvin Smith]] (Joseph's deceased eldest brother) was burning in hell because he had not received Presbyterian baptism — a perceived act of harsh "unrighteous dominion" said to have motivated Joseph's universalist, family-saving theology. [[Kyle Beshears]] argues this episode "is misremembered in Mormon history." [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **The membership-rolls version is internally inconsistent.** If the minister damned Alvin at the funeral for not being on the church rolls, "why are you going to that pastor" — because [[Lucy Mack Smith]] *later joined* the Presbyterian church "to sit under his spiritual guidance." [^3] *Rating: a sound internal-evidence objection;* Lucy's subsequent membership is hard to square with a funeral insult.

2. **Beshears's reconstruction.** The family had "a positive experience with the Presbyterian church until the new... young gun pastor from out of town came in and took over and then started to teach Calvinistic doctrines of election and reprobation." Lucy then asked "what does that mean for Alvin?" and the pastor "has to say he's probably burning in hell because he wasn't elect." [^4] *Rating: a plausible alternative* grounded in the documented shift to Calvinist preaching, and Beshears notes he "researched it a little bit in a Substack."

3. **Late, secondhand source.** The story comes from William Smith, who "tells that like decades later... and he's a little boy when that happens." [^5] *Rating: a legitimate source-critical caution* about a remembered childhood account.

4. **A pastoral foul, if it happened at all.** Even granting the worst version, a funeral is not the time "to wax eloquent about Calvinistic doctrines of election and reprobation"; the right response is "to weep with those who weep." [^6] *Rating: a fair pastoral judgment* that concedes the emotional core of the LDS memory while disputing the details.

**Steelman of the traditional LDS view.** Host [[Steven Pynakker]] grants the underlying point: the belief that the unbaptized (or unelect) were damned "was a very common thought back then... not made up by Mormons later," and in that climate Joseph's project can be read "in one sense [as] Joseph Smith's attempt to find a way to get Alvin into heaven." [^7] Beshears does not deny that 19th-century reprobation preaching caused real pain or shaped Joseph's concerns; he disputes only the specific funeral-insult narrative's accuracy.

## Best Quotes
> "What's interesting about that episode is I think it's misremembered in Mormon history." [^2]

> "I don't think what happened was that a Presbyterian minister at Alvin's funeral told the Smith family that he was unelect and burning in hell because he was not on the Presbyterian church membership rolls." [^1]

> "The new Presbyterian young gun pastor from out of town came in... and started to teach Calvinistic doctrines of election and reprobation. Lucy asked the question, well, what does that mean for Alvin? And the pastor has to say he's probably burning in hell because he wasn't elect." [^4]

> "Is the funeral the appropriate time to wax eloquent about Calvinistic doctrines of election and reprobation? No. The appropriate thing to do at that point would be to weep with those who weep." [^6]
