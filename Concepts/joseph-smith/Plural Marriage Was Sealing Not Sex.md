# Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex

## The Point
Critics frame [[Joseph Smith]]'s plural marriages as the work of a "manipulative, sex-crazed" predator — comparing him to Warren Jeffs and headlining sealings to other men's wives and to a 14-year-old. Fife argues the "it was all about sex" narrative fails on its own evidence: the actual pattern points to the *sealing power* — the "law of adoption," "dynastic sealings," and "eternity only" sealings — by which Joseph sought to bind families into an unbroken chain to God. A sealing did not necessarily imply a sexual or marital relationship.

## Evidence
1. **The motive cuts against sex.** Fife lists facts the "sex" thesis cannot absorb: Joseph's likely first two Nauvoo plural wives were married women seven months pregnant (when contemporary medical advice discouraged sex in pregnancy); no husband ever filed a complaint; no married woman or husband ever claimed polyandry occurred; and despite being demonstrably fertile (nine children with [[Emma Smith]]), Joseph fathered no children by any plural wife.[^1]
2. **The law of adoption.** Sealings for the deceased were not performed until 1894; before then members were sealed to *living* members — often a Church leader — to be "linked" into the eternal family. [[Parley P. Pratt]] reports Joseph teaching the apostles the sealing power and eternal family organization in 1839.[^2]
3. **"Eternity only" sealings.** Historians' term for the sealing of an already-married woman whose husband was not a Church member; Fife notes "there is no indication" these carried "any of the standard marital expectations" of ordinary marriages — so a "marriage" or "sealing" "did not necessarily imply a sexual or marital relationship."[^3]
4. **[[Helen Mar Kimball]] in context.** Joseph's youngest plural wife (14) was sealed to him "at the behest of her father," [[Heber C. Kimball]], who desired "to be connected with the prophet" — consistent with dynastic sealing — and "very little evidence" supports a sexual relationship (see [[Joseph Smith and Helen Mar Kimball Sealing]]).[^4]
5. **The fruits argument.** Fife asks why, if Nauvoo were "one big orgy," Latter-day Saints today are "among the most 'traditional'" and chaste people, with stronger families and higher fidelity (quoting historian Lawrence Foster). Polygamy demanded "an intense Abrahamic-like sacrifice"; ~20% of living members descend from it, including much of the senior leadership.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Thus, a 'marriage' or a 'sealing' did not necessarily imply a sexual or marital relationship." [^3]

> "If the history of Nauvoo and early Utah was one big orgy, why are the Latter-day Saints some of the most chaste people on the planet?" [^5]

## LDS Connection
Fife ties plural marriage to the doctrine of [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|eternal marriage and the sealing power]] (D&C 132) and to Jacob 2:30's qualifier that the Lord commands the practice only to "raise up seed unto me." Rather than excuse every abuse (he notes John Bennett exploited women under false pretenses), he argues the sealing framework — not lust — explains the Nauvoo and Kirtland practice, an instance of the broader principle that God works through flawed, fallible servants (see [[Mistakes Versus Leading Astray]]).
