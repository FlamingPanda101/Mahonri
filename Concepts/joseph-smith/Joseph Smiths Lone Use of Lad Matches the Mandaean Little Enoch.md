# Joseph Smiths Lone Use of Lad Matches the Mandaean Little Enoch

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that one of the sharpest "quick and dirty" evidences for [[Joseph Smith]] is a single word. In [[Moses 6.31|Moses 6:31]] (revealed 1830), the called prophet [[Enoch]] protests, "Why is it that I have found favor in thy sight, and am **but a lad**…?" Barnes claims this is the *only* occurrence of "lad" in Joseph Smith's entire corpus (no letter, sermon, or other verse). Multiple Enoch traditions unavailable in 1830 — the **Mandaean** Enoch (translated into French 1856 by Jacques Matter, never into English at the time) and the **Bet ha-Midrash** — repeatedly characterize the aged Enoch as small/young: *Petit Anush* ("little Enoch, the lad"), *katan* ("small/juvenile, a lad"). Joseph, he says, "nails this bullseye."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Lad" appears once, at exactly the Enoch call — rate: interesting if the corpus search holds
Barnes states he has "searched everything" and Joseph used "lad" one solitary time, precisely where an ancient Enoch tradition would predict it. The collocation (Enoch + self-deprecating "lad/small" at his prophetic call) matches the Mandaean *Petit Anush* and the Midrashic *katan*. *Moderate-to-interesting: the single-use claim is checkable in the Joseph Smith Papers, and a unique word landing on the "right" figure is a genuine, if small, convergence — the kind the vault files under [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]].*[^2]

### 2. The sources were untranslated/unavailable — rate: the load-bearing chronology
The Mandaean material was rendered only into French (1856), decades after 1830 and not into English Barnes could find; the relevant Enoch corpora were largely undiscovered or untranslated for Joseph. If so, borrowing is excluded and the match is a reverse-anachronism. *Strong on availability, which is the crux; the escape is to deny the match is meaningful (see steelman).*[^3]

### 3. It belongs to a cluster, not a one-off — rate: moderate (cumulative)
The "lad" point sits inside a larger set Barnes parades the same episode (the calling-scene paraphrase in 2 Enoch/Slavonic; the Son of Man vision; the [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch|Mahijah]] name), and the non-LDS scholar [[Salvatore Cirillo]] independently lists "lad-for-the-aged-Enoch" among his "beyond dispute" parallels. *Moderate: cumulative pattern strengthens the case beyond any single item (cf. [[Cirillo Catalogs Substantial Enoch Parallels Beyond Dispute]]).*[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's strongest replies: (a) **"Lad" is a thoroughly ordinary KJV word for a young man** (Genesis 22:5, 12; 1 Samuel 20:21-41) and the *idea* "I am too young/lowly for this call" is a stock **prophetic-commission formula** — Jeremiah's "I am a child" (Jeremiah 1:6), Moses' "slow of speech" (which Moses 6:31 itself echoes), Solomon's "little child." Joseph could generate a humble, youthful Enoch from the King James Bible and the call-narrative pattern alone, with no Mandaean source. (b) **The single-use statistic, even if accurate, is weak** — a word used once is exactly what you'd expect for an uncommon synonym; the "Enoch tradition calls him a lad, Joseph calls him a lad" inference treats a generic humility-of-the-young trope as a fingerprint. (c) **The Mandaean *Petit Anush* identification is contestable** — "Anush/Enosh" and "Enoch" are distinct figures in some traditions, so equating the Mandaean "little Anush" with Joseph's Enoch may conflate them. (d) **Selection effect:** trawling many late Enoch texts for any motif that matches Joseph's short Enoch will turn up hits by chance. The defensible residue: the *availability* point is real (no English Mandaean Enoch in 1830), and a recurring "young/small Enoch" motif does exist in the apocrypha, so this is a *minor, low-probability convergence* that adds a little weight to a cumulative Enoch case — but the strong rhetoric ("bullseye," "he nails it") outruns what one common word can bear, and the commission-formula explanation is hard to exclude.

A representative "quick and dirty" item in the vault's Enoch-parallels file, alongside the stronger [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch|Mahijah]] name-match and the broader [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830|Moses 6-7]] case.
