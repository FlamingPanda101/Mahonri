# Forensic Reconstruction Eliminates Theories Rather Than Inventing Them

## The Claim
[[Shannon Tracy]] proposes reconstructing the Carthage assassination of [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Hyrum Smith]] (June 1844) the way a crime-scene unit works a fresh case: 3D-laser-scan the jail, digitize every room and movable object, run ballistic tests, and gather "voluminous data — both physical and written." The governing rule is that forensics is "more about throwing things out than adding things in": you collect everything, then *eliminate* what does not fit, and "let it talk to us" — rather than starting with a theory and building toward it.[^1] This is pitched as the opposite of rival "who shot Joseph Smith?" documentaries (he name-checks Justin Griffin's theories) that begin from a conclusion.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The method is the standard crime-scene workflow.** A real reconstruction does begin with scene capture and a "what happened / when / how" triage led by a crime-scene specialist, and proceeds by excluding inconsistent hypotheses. As a *description of method*, this is uncontroversial. (Evidence rating: strong as method; the technology — photogrammetry, laser scanning — is genuinely mature and used in engineering and forensics.)[^1]
2. **Refusing to pre-commit is a real epistemic virtue.** Tracy repeatedly declines to state his own hypotheses "until we actually have the facts," which is the correct posture for an investigator and guards against confirmation bias. (Evidence rating: moderate — the *stated* discipline is sound; whether the eventual documentary honors it is untestable now.)[^2]
3. **The scene is unusually well preserved.** Tracy notes Carthage Jail "has not changed a lot for 180 years," making a faithful digital reconstruction feasible; he did an earlier dowel-and-laser trajectory study of the bullet holes in the door over 20 years ago. (Evidence rating: moderate — preservation aids reconstruction but does not by itself yield conclusions.)[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The "we have no theory, we just follow the facts" framing is partly rhetorical. Every reconstruction is theory-laden: which data you collect, how you model bullet resistance through digital "organs," and how you weight conflicting affidavits all embed prior assumptions. A skeptic can note that the project is being crowd-funded by a partisan audience to produce a faith-affirming documentary, which creates exactly the motivated-reasoning pressure the method is supposed to exclude; and that ballistic-gel and avatar simulations can be tuned to yield a desired path (Tracy concedes gels "went wonky" and cannot be controlled "as tight as the 3D models"). The fair residue: the *physical* facts Tracy already cites (two pistols documented, the door that would not lock, the doctor's wound report, the crushed watch) are independent of the documentary and stand on their own; the grander promise to "put it to bed" forensically is, for now, an aspiration, not a result.

## Best Quotes
> "We're not going to have a theory... we're just going to do it like a crime scene. What are we going to find out... and then let that speak."[^1]

> "Forensics is more about throwing things out than adding things in, because you collect it all and then you eliminate what doesn't fit."[^1]
