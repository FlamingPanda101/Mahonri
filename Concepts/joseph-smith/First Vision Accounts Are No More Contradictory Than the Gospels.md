# First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] argues Joseph Smith's First Vision is told inconsistently — first "he saw the Lord" (one personage), later "two personages," with a "very strange history in terms of how many personages there are in the godhead" — implying the story changes every time it is told.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **There is a fixed, finite corpus — not endless mutation (rated: strong, factual).** [[Jacob Hansen]]: "We have four first-hand First Vision accounts and five second-hand," many of them short. It is not the case that "every time he's telling it, it's just changing all over the place."[^2]

2. **The 1832 account is the only one Joseph wrote himself and is deliberately cursory (rated: strong, textual).** The earliest (1832) account, in Joseph's own hand in an unfinished publication, is brief and not "play-by-play"; it simply mentions seeing "the Lord" before moving to the message and effect of the experience — unlike the catechized missionary narrative. Mentioning one personage in a cursory account and two in a fuller one "is not really that big of a deal."[^3]

3. **Different audiences and purposes explain the differences (rated: moderate, hermeneutical).** [[Jasmine Rapley]]: in one account Joseph emphasizes the redemption of his sins; in another, the differences among the churches and which to join — "different purposes, different audiences." The discrepancies are the kind any honest set of retellings shows.[^4]

4. **The Gospels are the parallel case (rated: strong, comparative).** Rapley: "If we're going to talk about different accounts, the four Gospels are not harmonious" — the Synoptics differ from one another and from John on "not minor things," including the nature of God and how omniscient/human Jesus is. A believer harmonizes them while accepting real differences; the same charity applies to the First Vision.[^5]

5. **A non-LDS scholar agrees (rated: moderate, external authority).** [[Steven Prothero]] (non-LDS religion scholar): critics "have delighted in the discrepancies... Such complaints, however, are much ado about relatively nothing. Any good lawyer or historian would expect to find contradictions in competing narratives written down years apart... And despite the contradictions, key elements abide" — in each, Jesus appears, Smith receives revelation, and is told to remain aloof from existing denominations.[^6] *Steelman:* a critic notes the *theologically load-bearing* difference (one God vs. two distinct beings) is not a peripheral detail like the Synoptics' chronology, since it bears on the godhead doctrine itself; the strongest critical version is not "the story changes" but "the *most doctrinally significant* feature is absent from the earliest account and appears later," which could indicate doctrinal development. The LDS reply (Prothero) is that brevity plus differing purpose adequately explains the omission without positing invention.

## Best Quotes

> "We have four first-hand first vision accounts and five second-hand... It's not like every time he's telling it, it's just changing all over the place."[^2]

> "The four Gospels are not harmonious... and it's not minor things — they're differences about the nature of God, how omniscient Jesus is."[^5]

> "Critics of Mormonism have delighted in the discrepancies... Such complaints, however, are much ado about relatively nothing... despite the contradictions, key elements abide." — Steven Prothero[^6]
