# Emma Smith's Polygamy Testimony Splits Before and After 1860

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim
Deniers lean on [[Emma Smith]]'s later flat denials that her husband practiced polygamy. [[Brian Hales]] answers that Emma's testimony **splits at 1860** — the year the RLDS Church organized with her son [[Joseph Smith III]] as president on an explicitly anti-polygamy platform. *Before* 1860, multiple independent, non-pressured witnesses record Emma *acknowledging* the practice; her unambiguous denials come *after*, filtered through RLDS leaders and missionaries for whom any admission would have destroyed the church's founding claim. "Which Emma do you believe — pre-1860 or post-1860?"[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The 1860 hinge.** After the RLDS Church organized (1860) on the premise that Joseph was monogamous, "if Emma had ever admitted that Joseph Smith... was a polygamist, it would have been going against everything that this church stood for." Her post-1860 statements are therefore the *least* reliable — given under maximal institutional pressure and "filtered through RLDS missionaries or leaders."[^2] *(Strong — establishes the motive and filtering that compromise the late denials.)*
2. **At least three pre-1860 witnesses record Emma acknowledging it.** (a) **William McLellin** — the well-known account where Emma discusses Fanny Alger and acknowledges Joseph's plural marriage; (b) **Lovina Smith Walker** ([[Hyrum Smith]]'s daughter, Emma's niece) — recalled a "very plain conversation" in which Emma admitted having participated in the plural marriages of Eliza and Emily Partridge; (c) **Joseph Lee Robinson / Coolidge** — met Emma and "she also freely talked about it," and he shared this *while out of the church*, so he "wasn't biased or working under pressure from some church leader."[^3] *(Strong — three independent witnesses, including an out-of-church one, before the distorting event.)*
3. **The contradiction is acknowledged, not hidden.** Hales is candid: the post-1860 statements "seem to be very unambiguously saying Joseph didn't do it — that's a problem," and "we always have contradictory evidence almost always when we're dealing with historical issues." But the resolution is source-critical: weight the *earlier, unpressured* statements over the *later, motivated* ones, exactly as historians normally do.[^4] *(Strong — models honest source criticism rather than ignoring the counter-evidence.)*
4. **Emma participated in the sealings herself.** The Lovina Walker testimony has Emma *administering* the plural marriages of the Partridge sisters — not merely knowing of them — which is very difficult to reconcile with the later total denials except as motivated revisionism after 1860.[^3] *(Strong — participation is harder to later deny away than mere knowledge.)*

**Steelman of the denialist view.** A denier replies that the pre-1860 "admissions" are themselves *second-hand* recollections (we have McLellin's, Walker's, Robinson's *reports* of what Emma said, not Emma's own words), often recorded years later by people sympathetic to the polygamy narrative, so they are not obviously more reliable than Emma's own repeated, first-person, on-the-record denials to her sons. The denier argues the "1860 filter" cuts both ways: Utah witnesses had their *own* motive (vindicating Brigham-era polygamy) to claim Emma had confessed. And privileging the testimony that fits one's thesis ("believe pre-1860 Emma") risks the same selectivity deniers are accused of. The LDS rejoinder is that (a) the pre-1860 reports include an *out-of-church*, unpressured witness (Robinson/Coolidge), blunting the "Utah motive" charge; (b) Emma's *participation* in the Partridge sealings is attested, not just her knowledge; and (c) the asymmetry of *motive* is real and directional — the RLDS Church's very existence depended on Emma's denial, giving her late statements a specific, documented institutional pressure that the earlier ones lacked. Source-critically, earlier-and-unpressured beats later-and-motivated.

## LDS Relevance
Neutralizes the "but Emma denied it" objection by dating and contextualizing her statements: her credible, unpressured acknowledgments precede the RLDS founding; her categorical denials follow it and serve its institutional need. This is the Emma-specific application of the broader source-critical method in [[The Confidentiality Model Best Fits the Polygamy Evidence]], and it dovetails with [[The RLDS Church Were the Original Polygamy Deniers and Recanted]] (same 1860 hinge, same eventual collapse of the denial).
