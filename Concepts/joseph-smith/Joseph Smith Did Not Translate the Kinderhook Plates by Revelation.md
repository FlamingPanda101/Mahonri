#type/claim

# Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation

## The Claim
The standard critic's charge is that [[Joseph Smith]] "fell for" the [[Kinderhook plates]] — a known forgery — by attempting a revelatory translation, thereby exposing his prophetic gift as fraudulent. The rebuttal: the direct historical sources show Joseph never used any revelatory instrument on the plates and never produced a revealed translation. At most he compared a single character against his secular reference tools; "by translation... we mean he compared it with something that had already been done and saw perhaps a connection there. There doesn't seem to be any sort of divine or revelatory aspect to this process."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **No seer stone, no Urim and Thummim (rated: strong, primary-source).** Per Joseph's own journal and eyewitnesses, when shown the plates he "doesn't say someone fetch me my seer stone or the Urim and Thummim." Instead "he sent for his Hebrew Bible and lexicon" (he had studied Hebrew) and had his "Egyptian alphabet" brought over.[^2] A self-identified "Gentile" eyewitness in the *New York Herald* says Joseph "compared them in my presence with his Egyptian alphabet."[^3] *The reach for academic crib-sheets, not revelatory instruments, is the load-bearing fact.*

2. **The "translation" is one secondhand sentence reconstructible from a single glyph (rated: strong, documentary).** The famous line — Joseph "translated a portion" identifying the buried man as "a descendant of Ham through the loins of Pharaoh, king of Egypt... [who] received his kingdom from the ruler of heaven and earth" — comes from [[William Clayton]], not Joseph firsthand (though *History of the Church* later printed it as if first-person). [[Don Bradley]] and [[Mark Asher McGee]] matched it to one prominent boat-shaped character on [[John Taylor]]'s published facsimiles whose entry in the [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]] reads "honor by birth, kingly power by the line of Pharaoh... possessor of heaven and earth." Every element of Clayton's sentence is already in that one glyph's gloss.[^4] So the whole "translation" plausibly reduces to: spot a familiar character, infer the rest.

3. **The reconstruction (rated: best-available, the panel concedes no scholarly consensus).** Joseph flips through the ~6 plates, calls for his Hebrew lexicon and Egyptian alphabet, notices "that looks similar to this character here," tells Clayton to "make a note," and offers the logical lay inference that the plates record the history of the man they were buried with.[^5] Bradley/McGee present this as the best explanation; the panel stresses that *even without* it, the bare sources still show only single-character comparison, never revelation.[^1]

4. **No sequel, no acquisition, plates abandoned (rated: strong, behavioral).** Despite the open expectation of "a sequel to the Book of Mormon," none came. "We don't get a book of Kinderhook." Joseph let the plates leave Nauvoo and never moved to acquire them — unlike the Egyptian papyri he raised money to buy. See [[Joseph Smith Sought Secular Authentication of Artifacts He Believed Ancient]] and [[A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax]].

5. **Bushman, read in full (rated: strong, scholarly).** Critics quote [[Richard Bushman]] saying Joseph "may not have detected the fraud," but stop there. In full: "he did not swing into a full-fledged translation as he had with the Egyptian scrolls. The trap did not quite spring shut, which foiled the conspirators' original plan."[^6]

**Steelman of the critic.** The maximalist reading is available and not frivolous: Clayton's sentence ties tightly to Book of Abraham / Book of Mormon "lore" (descent from Ham, the line of Pharaoh), Pratt independently connected the plates to the Jaredites, and *History of the Church* did publish the statement in Joseph's voice — so a critic can fairly say Joseph treated a forgery as a genuine ancient record and asserted its content as fact. The honest LDS reply concedes Joseph likely thought the plates *could* be authentic (see [[A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax]]) and possibly erred (see [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]]) — but distinguishes a fallible secular comparison from a claimed *revelation*, of which there is no trace. The argument is therefore narrower than "Joseph was never wrong": it is "Joseph's revelatory gift was not engaged here, and conspicuously produced nothing."

## Best Quotes
> "He doesn't say someone fetch me my seer stone or the Urim and Thummim... he sent for his Hebrew Bible and lexicon."[^2]

> "President Joseph has translated a portion and says they contain the history of the person with whom they were found, and he was a descendant of Ham through the loins of Pharaoh, king of Egypt." (William Clayton)[^4]

> "The extent to which he translated appears to be a single character... There doesn't seem to be any sort of divine or revelatory aspect to this process."[^1]
