# Joseph Smith Predicts His Own Martyrdom

## The Claim
In the days before his death at Carthage (June 1844), [[Joseph Smith]] reportedly made several predictions of his own slaughter: "if I and Hyrum were ever taken again, we should be massacred or I was not a prophet"; "we shall be butchered"; "we are going to be slaughtered"; and most famously, "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter, but I am as calm as a summer's morning... I shall die innocent." He also told [[Dan Jones]] the night before the martyrdom that Jones "would yet see Wales and fulfill the mission appointed" — Jones survived (sent out with a letter the next morning) and served a successful mission in Wales in 1845.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Joseph did die at Carthage, and Jones did go to Wales.** Both outcomes occurred. (Evidence rating: outcomes real.)[^1]
2. **All recorded after the martyrdom.** The host: "this is recorded obviously after the martyrdom happened." Predictions of one's death preserved by survivors after the death are vulnerable to memorializing embellishment. (Evidence rating: late-record caveat.)[^2]
3. **The death prediction was a reasonable read of the danger.** With armed mobs and a hostile jail, anticipating murder was hardly clairvoyant; the "lamb to the slaughter" line is as much resolve as forecast. (Evidence rating: low predictive specificity for the death itself.)[^2]
4. **The Dan Jones strand is self-fulfilling/escapable.** The host: it "could be an easily fulfilled one for him to then go on to Wales after." Jones's survival turned on a mundane contingency (carrying a letter out of the jail). See [[Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Objection]]. (Evidence rating: weak.)[^3]
5. **Steelman of the believing view.** The cumulative, repeated, specific calm-in-the-face-of-death sayings — and the paired survival of Jones — form an emotionally powerful cluster that believers read as genuine foreknowledge. The deflation is that danger made death foreseeable and the records are posthumous.

## Best Quotes
> "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter, but I am as calm as a summer's morning. I have a conscience void of offense towards God and towards all men. I shall die innocent."[^1]

> "You will yet see Wales and fulfill the mission appointed before you die." — to Dan Jones[^1]
