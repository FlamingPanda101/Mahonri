# First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection: the multiple First Vision accounts contradict one another, suggesting Joseph Smith invented or kept revising the story. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] — who wrote a book harmonizing the accounts — answers that the differences track **audience and intent**, not contradiction: assembling the accounts yields "a fuller and fuller picture," and the only genuinely hard part was reconstructing *sequence*, precisely because the experience itself was one of "confusion and fear." [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The accounts harmonize; the hard part was sequence, not contradiction — rate: strong (from a specialist)

Muhlestein: "They come together wonderfully." The one difficult portion to harmonize was the account where Joseph is attacked by Satan — and the difficulty "wasn't that they were contradictory, but to figure out a sequence." His "aha moment": "the essence of that experience was confusion and fear," so "it's not surprising that as he writes about it, it's hard for me to put the pieces together. His confusion is conveyed in the way he talks about it." See [[Trauma Victims Reconstruct Crime-Scene Gaps So Survivor Accounts Vary]]. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a harmonizing scholar reporting that the texts cohere once sequenced.

### 2. Differences track audience and purpose — rate: strong

"They are different depending upon his audience and his intent." The first (1832) is "more of a personal history" focused on what happened to him; the **Wentworth** account is "very outward facing," meant "to help other people understand," so he "tones down language... he doesn't mention abomination" — "probably a good PR move." The 1838 account is "an official history of the church," so it foregrounds "here are the problems with the churches; I'm going to restore a church." See [[The 1838 First Vision Account Was Written to Correct Public Falsehoods]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a coherent, audience-based account of why emphases differ.

### 3. We get a fuller picture, not contradictions — rate: strong

"We don't really get contradictions; as much as we put them together, we get a fuller and fuller picture. It was really pretty easy to harmonize them." The differences are exactly what one expects of honest retellings: "if you were to write or tell a story six years apart... you'd always tell it differently." This is the same charity a believer extends to the four Gospels (see [[First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels]]). [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the everyday-retelling analogy is apt and parallels the Gospel-harmony argument.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The strongest critical version is not "the story keeps changing" but that the *most doctrinally significant* feature — two distinct divine personages — is absent from the earliest (1832) account and appears prominently only later, which could indicate the godhead doctrine *developed* and was read back into the founding theophany. "Audience and purpose" explains *omission* but does not, by itself, rule out *development*. The reply: a cursory personal account written "as if vomiting the experience onto the page" (the pastor's image) naturally compresses to "the Lord" without enumerating personages, and the constant core across all four accounts (a divine visitation, revelation, and the charge to remain aloof from existing churches) is what a developed-and-retrojected doctrine would not reliably preserve.
