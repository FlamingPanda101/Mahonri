# seer stone

#type/term

## The Claim

A **seer stone** is a small stone through which Joseph Smith, like other "seers" in his 1820s Palmyra/Manchester milieu, reported receiving visual revelation — used both for finding lost or hidden objects and, later, as an instrument of translation. The term is central to LDS history because the historical record describes Joseph translating part of the Book of Mormon with a personal seer stone (often placed in a hat), in addition to the "interpreters" (Urim and Thummim) that came with the gold plates.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Joseph owned a personal white seer stone before the plates — rate: moderate (primary source)
In the account [[Don Bradley]] relays from Dr. [[William Purple]]'s notes of the 1826 hearing, the young Joseph looked into neighbor [[Sally Chase]]'s green stone, saw his own white stone ~150 miles away under a tree's roots, and recovered it — and described the new gift as "an all-seeing eye," an "attribute of deity." See [[Joseph's Seer-Stone Discovery Frames Theosis From the Start of the Restoration]].[^1]

### 2. Both the interpreters and the seer stone were used in translation — rate: strong (historiography)
The standard historiographical position (e.g. [[Richard Bushman]]) is that Joseph used **both** the interpreters and the seer stone — the seer stone more heavily in the post-116-pages phase — with strong eyewitness testimony (Emma Smith, David Whitmer, Martin Harris) for the seer-stone method. See [[Joseph Smith Used Both the Interpreters and a Seer Stone]].

### 3. The term carries a deification reading, not only a folk-magic one — rate: moderate (interpretation)
Bradley resists reducing the seer stone to mere folk magic: the "all-seeing eye" language frames seeric power as a *share in a divine attribute*, tying the stone to the restoration's theosis theme from the very beginning rather than to an 1842 borrowing from Freemasonry. See [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Critics note the seer stone is the same kind of object used in **money-digging / glass-looking** folk practice, and read its role in translation as evidence of a 19th-century magical worldview rather than divine instrumentation. The faithful reply distinguishes 19th-century "seeing" language (which described perceived real things) from delusion, and emphasizes the named eyewitnesses to the translation. What survives across views is the *historical fact* that a seer stone was involved; the dispute is over its theological meaning.
