# Abraham 3 Is the Selection of Adam Not the Redeemer

## The Claim
This is the payload of [[Jonah Barnes]]'s argument: [[Abraham 3.22-23|Abraham 3:22-27]] describes the premortal **selection of the first man — Michael, who is Adam** — and Lucifer fell because he was passed over for *that* role (to be the first man / Adam), exactly matching the apocryphal envy-of-Adam motif ([[Lucifer Fell Out of Jealousy of Adam in the Apocrypha]]). The linchpin is the Hebrew: "one like unto God" = **Michael** (*mi-ka-el*, "who is like God"), so the figure standing among the noble and great ones in Abraham 3:24 is named, in the very phrase, as the archangel-Adam.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Like unto God" is the literal meaning of *Michael* — rate: moderate-to-strong (philological)
Barnes' centerpiece: [[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:24]] reads "there stood one among them that was like unto God," and he asks, "how do you say 'like unto God' in ancient Hebrew? *Mikael*." The Hebrew name *Mîkhā'ēl* does parse as "who is like God." So the otherwise-anonymous "one like unto God" can be read as a *name*, identifying the figure as **Michael** — whom LDS doctrine equates with **Adam**.[^1] *Strength: the etymology of Michael is correct; whether Abraham 3:24's descriptive phrase is meant as the proper name is the interpretive leap.*

### 2. The scene is creation, not redemption — rate: moderate (context)
The figure says "we will go down, for there is space there, and we will take of these materials, and we will make an earth whereon these may dwell" ([[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:24]]) — *creation* language. Abraham 4 (the next chapter) is the creation account. So the council of Abraham 3, Barnes argues, is about *who will be the first man on the earth they are about to make*, not who will atone. "One like unto the Son of Man" (3:27) — "first of all, that means he's *not* the Son of Man" — sounds like Michael, not Jehovah. See [[Moses 4 and Abraham 3 Are Two Different Premortal Scenes]].[^2]

### 3. It fits the biblical birthright-forfeiture type — rate: moderate (typology)
A "first-formed" who loses standing to a chosen heir is the recurring scriptural pattern (Esau, Reuben, Manasseh). The *Divine Investiture of St Michael* even calls Lucifer the "first-formed" demoted to "the accuser" — so Lucifer's losing the *Adam* role to Michael coheres with both the apocrypha and the Bible's transferable-birthright logic. See [[The Divine Investiture of St Michael Calls Lucifer the First-Formed]] and [[Two Brothers Where the Younger Is Chosen Are Types of Christ and Lucifer]].[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is the most speculative link in the chain and should be flagged as such. (a) **"Like unto God" is descriptive, not a transliteration.** Abraham 3:24 is an English revealed text; reading the *Hebrew* name *Michael* back into the English phrase "like unto God" assumes a Hebrew substrate the Book of Abraham does not provide, and risks circularity — the etymology is invoked precisely to manufacture the identification. (b) **"One like unto the Son of Man" standardly = Jehovah.** In LDS and biblical usage the premortal Christ is repeatedly the "Son of Man"; the traditional reading takes 3:27's volunteer as the *redeemer* (Jehovah), harmonizing with Moses 4 — Barnes's "not the Son of Man, therefore Michael" inverts the usual sense. (c) **Doctrinal tension with Adam-God.** Elevating Abraham/Michael to a premortally *selected* quasi-redeemer figure flirts with the abandoned Adam-God theory; Barnes is aware (he separately challenges whether Brigham Young even taught it), but mainstream LDS doctrine makes **Christ** the chosen one of the council, not Adam. (d) **Novelty without authority.** Barnes presents this as a personal interpretive breakthrough ("I misread it for years"), not as a reading endorsed by the Church or by LDS scholars — so it carries the weight of one enthusiast's harmonization.

**What survives:** the *question* Barnes raises — whether Abraham 3 narrates the choosing of the first man rather than (or in addition to) the redeemer — is a genuinely interesting exegetical possibility, and the Michael-etymology plus the creation context give it some textual purchase. But it is a bold, non-authoritative reinterpretation that runs against the standard identification of the council's chosen one as Christ; it should be held as a provocative hypothesis, not a settled vindication, even though, *if* granted, it would elegantly close the apocryphal "envy of Adam" gap.
