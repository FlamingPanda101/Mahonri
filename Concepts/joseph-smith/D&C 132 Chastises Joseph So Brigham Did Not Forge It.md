# D&C 132 Chastises Joseph So Brigham Did Not Forge It

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] contains content a [[Brigham Young]] forgery would never include: within ~10 verses it refers to Joseph's *sins*, his *transgressions*, and his *trespasses against [[Emma Smith]]* — conceding that [[Joseph Smith]] did wrong, and even that Emma was wronged. A forger building a pro-Joseph, pro-polygamy revelation would not write this; Joseph's *own* revelations characteristically do.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The text repeatedly faults Joseph — rate: strong (internal evidence)
D&C 132:50 ("I have seen your sacrifices, and will forgive all your sins"), :56 ("let mine handmaid forgive my servant Joseph his trespasses; and then shall she be forgiven her trespasses"), and :60 ("for he shall do the sacrifice which I require at his hands for his transgressions") all attribute *fault* to Joseph and acknowledge he trespassed against Emma. This is the opposite of hagiography.[^2]

### 2. Brigham insisted Joseph "did nothing wrong" — rate: moderate (motive/character)
During the 1844 succession crisis, Brigham repeatedly said he would not extend fellowship to anyone who believed Joseph "died not right with God"; his stance was that Joseph did everything right, especially regarding polygamy. A forger of that conviction would never insert admissions of Joseph's transgressions against Emma. The content cuts against the alleged forger's known commitments.[^3]

### 3. Joseph's genuine revelations *do* chastise him — rate: moderate (stylistic fingerprint)
Joseph's authentic revelations frequently rebuke him for his sins (a known feature of the D&C). Section 132's self-chastisement matches Joseph's pattern, not Brigham's. Bradley adds that Brigham was famously *not* a detail or scripture man — dismissive of biblical narratives, no deep allusive command of scripture — whereas 132, like Joseph's other revelations, is densely allusive to biblical texts. See [[Doctrine and Covenants 132 Wordprint Analysis]] for the stylometric version.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

[[Kwaku El]] presses the strongest counter in the episode: a sufficiently *clever* forger would deliberately include unflattering material precisely to make the document "sound as Josephine as possible." Bradley's replies, and their limits: (a) Doing that well would require Brigham to *out-write* his own documented style — to mimic Joseph's allusive, self-rebuking voice — which Bradley judges beyond Brigham's habits; but "he couldn't have pulled it off" is a judgment call, not a proof. (b) If the self-chastisement were a planted authenticity-marker, the 19th-century Brighamite *defenders* of the revelation should have *pointed to* those features as evidence — yet they only ever discuss its polygamy content, never "look how it admits Joseph's faults"; the marker, if intentional, went unused, which is odd. (c) Still, absence of a tell is weaker than presence of one, and a forger need not consciously exploit every embedded feature. So the argument shifts the burden well — the content is genuinely *surprising* on the forgery hypothesis and *expected* on authenticity — without strictly excluding an unusually shrewd forger, which is why it is paired with the wordprint and the Nauvoo-detail evidence rather than standing alone.

## Best Quotes

> "In the space of 10 or 11 verses you have it referring to Joseph's sins, Joseph's transgressions, and Joseph's trespasses against Emma."[^2]

> "Brigham is never going to give a nod to Joseph having wronged Emma. There's no way. And yet Joseph's own revelations frequently do this."[^1]

> "If you're going to go through all the trouble to plant these things... then the 19th-century Brighamite defenders of the revelation should have been calling attention to those features. Instead they don't even seem to notice them."[^3]
