# Questioning Assumptions Is the First Defense of the Book of Abraham

#type/method

## The Claim

Egyptologist [[Kerry Muhlestein]] (on [[Ward Radio]]) argues that the most important move in evaluating the [[Book of Abraham]] is **method, not data**: the **first** of the recurring mistakes people make is to carry **unexamined assumptions** into the question and treat them as established facts. On his account, most "the Book of Abraham is disproven" arguments smuggle in unargued premises (above all, that the surviving papyrus must be Joseph's source text). Surfacing and questioning those assumptions is the first line of defense, before any Egyptological detail is even discussed.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The five mistakes are method errors, led by hidden assumptions — rate: moderate (epistemology)
Muhlestein's catalogue: (1) making **unexamined assumptions** and treating them as facts; (2) **over-simplifying** a 3,000-year, symbol-laden, multivalent Egyptian record; (3) failing to **question sound bites**; (4) pursuing answers **half-heartedly** — reading only one side and skipping the primary text; (5) accepting the sophistry that **revelation is not valid knowledge**. The first is foundational: bad assumptions poison every later step.[^1]

### 2. The papyrus argument is the prime example — rate: moderate (application)
The leading attack assumes the recovered fragments are the translation source; once that assumption is *questioned*, the historical "missing scroll" evidence reframes the whole debate. So "question your assumptions" is not abstract — it is exactly what dissolves the headline objection. See [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]] and the text-vs-drawing distinction in [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]].[^1]

### 3. Claimed objectivity is itself a bias — rate: moderate (epistemology)
Muhlestein adds that everyone carries a faith-choice bias and that asserting pure objectivity is the *least* objective posture ("cynicism is not intelligence"), and he defends revelation as a viable epistemology (Alma 32's repeatable experiment; his own UCLA knee story). These are companion points to the assumptions critique, treated in the vault's related method notes. (Those notes — on faith-choice bias and on revelation as knowledge — were referenced by the source but are not yet created, so they are named here as plain text rather than linked.)[^1] In a second interview he states the point bluntly: "anyone who tells you they are approaching this objectively is lying to themselves or to you" — because "you either think revelation is possible or not," and that prior commitment is an inescapable starting point.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The method counsel cuts both ways. (a) "Question your assumptions" is **symmetrical**: a defender can have just as many unexamined assumptions (e.g. that a convenient lost scroll existed), so the principle does not by itself favor the apologetic conclusion. (b) Telling critics they are "over-simplifying" or "reading one side" can become a way to **deflect** specific, well-grounded Egyptological objections without answering them. (c) Reframing the dispute as epistemology risks moving the goalposts from "is the translation accurate?" to "are you being open-minded?" What survives is a genuine and fair point: many popular anti–Book-of-Abraham arguments do rest on an unstated premise (the surviving-fragment-as-source) that, once made explicit, is contestable — so demanding that assumptions be surfaced is legitimate intellectual hygiene, even though it establishes the *conditions* for a defense rather than the defense itself.

## Best Quote
> The number-one mistake is making assumptions and treating them as facts. — [[Kerry Muhlestein]] *(paraphrase)* [^1]
