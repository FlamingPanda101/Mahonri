# The 1838 First Vision Account Was Written to Correct Public Falsehoods

## The Claim

The canonized 1838 Joseph Smith—History (Pearl of Great Price) was composed under, and in response to, the **severe 1838 persecution** — a deliberate official record written "to disabuse the public mind and put all inquiries after the truth in possession of the facts." Knowing its occasion and purpose explains its tone (including the "abomination" language) and its outward-facing, church-history framing. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] supplies this context to a Protestant pastor reading the text cold. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The 1838 setting was a crisis of apostasy and false affidavits — rate: strong (historical)

Muhlestein situates the account in Kirtland, 1838: bank failures and "all sorts of things," "bitter persecution," and former intimates who had left the church "printing stories about him that are demonstrably false" and "swearing affidavits" of demonstrable falsehoods to pursue him legally. Joseph writes to set the record straight: "Let me tell you what happened." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the documented Kirtland collapse and the wave of dissent/affidavits are well established.

### 2. The text states its own corrective purpose — rate: strong (primary)

Verse 1 says Joseph writes "to disabuse the public mind, and to put all inquiries after the truth in possession of the facts, as they have transpired, in relation both to myself and the Church, so far as I have such facts in my possession" — exactly because *other* people were circulating their own "facts." The pastor notes this matches the "no man knows my history" theme: Joseph claims to be the one who actually knows what happened. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the stated aim is in the canonized text itself.

### 3. It is an *official history*, not merely a personal memoir — rate: strong

Unlike the more personal 1832 account, the 1838 version is framed as "an official history of the church"; Joseph says "it's about me, but it's really an official history of the church," so he foregrounds "here are the problems with the churches; I'm going to restore a church." This purpose shapes what he includes and emphasizes. See [[First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction]]. [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — accounts for the document's institutional framing.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can argue that a document written *as polemical damage-control* during a legitimacy crisis is exactly the kind of source whose self-serving details (e.g., the dramatic two-personage theophany, the sweeping condemnation of all creeds) should be treated with suspicion — apologetic motive can shape memory and emphasis. The reply (developed at [[First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction]] and [[Trauma Victims Reconstruct Crime-Scene Gaps So Survivor Accounts Vary]]) is that recognizing the document's purpose explains its *selection and tone* without impugning its *core*, which is stable across the independent accounts; purpose-driven emphasis is not the same as fabrication.
