# The Abrahamic Covenant Obligates the Holder to Extend It to All

## The Claim
[[Kerry Muhlestein]], who says he has studied the Abrahamic covenant "for decades," argues that the [[Book of Abraham]]'s account of that covenant is *theologically superior* to the biblical-alone version and better matches the God one would expect. Without the Book of Abraham, the covenant looks **exclusive** — God choosing a narrow few. The Book of Abraham reframes it: receiving the covenant *obligates* the holder to carry it to everyone, so that all may enter. It resolves the bloodline-vs-belief dilemma with a third option — **both**: by making the covenant you bind yourself to give it away, so anyone can be grafted into the lineage.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. It fixes the "exclusive God" problem (rated: theological)
"If you were to say what the Abrahamic covenant is about ... without the book of Abraham, you would have to say God's a bit exclusive ... like he's not planning on saving that many people." Mainstream Christianity "had to wrestle with that" (spiritualizing the covenant as believers-in-Christ); Judaism reads it as "it's us." The Book of Abraham instead says "it's incumbent upon people who have the covenant to go and take the covenant to other people so that everybody can have the covenant. That's what you would expect of God."[^1]

### 2. The both/and resolution (rated: theological)
On whether the covenant is an ethnic bloodline or a state of mind: "the book of Abraham gives us the third option that ... it's actually both. By accepting and making the covenant, you've now [dutified] yourself into giving it to others. ... So we can all be in the bloodline."[^1]

### 3. The book's expanding structure encodes it (rated: literary)
Muhlestein reads Abraham 1→2→3 as widening concentric circles: chapter 1, the *individual's* relationship with God; chapter 2, extending that relationship to the *entire world*; chapter 3, extending it to the *entire cosmos*; chapters 4–5, creation, which makes 1–3 possible. The covenant's outward thrust is built into the architecture of the book.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) the "missionary covenant" reading is also available within the Hebrew Bible itself ("in thee shall all families of the earth be blessed," Gen. 12:3) and in Pauline universalism, so the Book of Abraham is not uniquely required to dissolve exclusivity; (b) this is a *theological-attractiveness* argument ("the God you'd expect"), which is about desirability, not historicity — a more appealing covenant is not thereby an ancient one; and (c) LDS practice ties covenant access to ordinances and lineage assignment, which a critic may argue reintroduces a gatekept exclusivity of its own. The LDS reply: Muhlestein explicitly files this under his *spiritual/revelatory* reasons (4 through 1), not his intellectual ones — it is a claim about the book's truth resonating, evaluated alongside [[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]], not a stand-alone proof.
