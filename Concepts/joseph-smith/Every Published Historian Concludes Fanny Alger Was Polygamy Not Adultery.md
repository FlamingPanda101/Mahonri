# Every Published Historian Concludes Fanny Alger Was Polygamy Not Adultery

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that although critical websites present the [[Fanny Alger]] relationship as an obvious *affair*, **every historian who has done published work on it concludes it was plural marriage** — including non-believing and skeptical scholars. The convergence is the point: the people most informed agree, while the certainty runs in the opposite direction among the least informed.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The list crosses the belief divide — rate: strong (historiographic survey)
Bradley enumerates the published scholars and their priors:
- **[[Dan Vogel]]** — regards Joseph Smith as a pious fraud (emphasis on *fraud*); in his recent Joseph Smith Kirtland biography concludes the relationship was **polygamy** (Joseph performing the marriage himself).
- **[[Todd Compton]]** — a liberal Latter-day Saint who does *not* believe polygamy was inspired; nonetheless concludes Fanny was **polygamy**.
- **[[Brian Hales]]** — concludes **polygamy**.
- **Don Bradley** — concludes **polygamy** (reached while himself an ex-Mormon atheist).
The spread matters: figures with *opposite* motives about Joseph's prophethood land on the same reconstruction.[^2]

### 2. A best-explanation argument from disagreeing sources — rate: moderate (method)
Bradley frames it as inference to the best explanation: some early sources read as polygamy, some as adultery. A "secret polygamy" model explains *both* sets — insiders who knew it was plural marriage saw polygamy; the public, ignorant of the practice, heard "Joseph found with a woman" and assumed adultery. The plain-adultery model cannot explain the 1830s sources that already suggest *polygamy*.[^3]

### 3. The Eliza Jane Webb testimony — rate: moderate (confidante source)
Mrs. Webb (Eliza Jane Webb's family) is the one person who reports that Fanny *herself* confided what happened. She said she could not be certain Joseph had begun formal sealings in Kirtland but was "satisfied that something similar occurred" — i.e., something *like polygamy*, based on what Fanny told her. This is the closest thing to the principal's own account.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Expert consensus is evidence, not proof, and the critic has replies. (a) Scholarly agreement can reflect a shared *framework* (all are working within "Joseph practiced early polygamy") rather than independent confirmation; the question "marriage or adultery?" may be less settled at the *evidentiary* level than the head-count implies. (b) "Polygamy" in these historians' usage can still mean a *self-performed, undocumented, sexual* union that a skeptic would regard as functionally indistinguishable from the adultery being denied — so labeling it "plural marriage" does not by itself rescue the moral picture (Bradley and Galey both concede "either way it doesn't look great"). (c) Vogel's calling it polygamy is *consistent with* his pious-fraud thesis — he can grant the category while denying the divine sanction — so his agreement does not vindicate Joseph's *prophethood*, only the historical *classification*. (d) The Webb testimony is one report of a confidence, hedged ("something similar"). So the strong and fair claim is taxonomic: the scholarship favors classifying the relationship as plural marriage rather than a one-off affair — which defeats the "obvious adultery" gotcha — while leaving the deeper theological and ethical disputes open.

## Best Quotes

> "Dan Vogel thinks Joseph Smith is an imposter, but he still recognizes the evidence points and this is polygamy. ...Why then does everybody who looks deeply into this and publishes on it conclude that it was actually polygamy?"[^2]

> "If the people who are most informed on the issue conclude that this was polygamy, why is it that all the people who are least informed on the issue are so certain of their position?"[^1]

> "Mrs. Webb says... 'I am satisfied that something similar occurred,' because of what Fanny Alger herself told me. It was something like polygamy."[^4]
