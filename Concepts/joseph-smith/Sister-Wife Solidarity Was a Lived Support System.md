# Sister-Wife Solidarity Was a Lived Support System

## The Claim
Beyond the question of sealing mechanics (handled in [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]]), the *lived* relationship among co-wives was, in many recorded cases, a functioning mutual-support network — practical, economic, and emotional. Historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] argues that this sisterhood, visible repeatedly in the women's own writings, is the "rich" and "unique" dimension of plural marriage that the sensationalized narrative erases, and it is what most reshaped her own view. The case is made specific by [[Martha Cox]], whose autobiography ties the bond to an eternal hope.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Childcare across the death of a mother.** When a mother died young — more common in that era — "sister wives could easily step in and help care for the children, so they could remain in the same family unit they had been in before," a genuine reassurance to dying women.[^1]
2. **Division of labor and funded ambition.** Co-wives "divided household responsibilities" and "supported each other financially in... training for professional careers or personal development and growth" — the plural household functioning as a platform that freed individual women to pursue work or education.[^2]
3. **A confidante equally invested in the home.** "Quite a few women write about the value they found in having someone else to talk to in the home about domestic concerns they didn't feel comfortable talking to anyone else about... a person equally invested in their family life who they could confide in."[^2]
4. **Martha Cox as the paradigm case.** [[Martha Cox]] chose plural marriage *against* deep family opposition and community cold-shouldering, yet treasured her sister-wife bond above all her losses — and she had real losses (dead children, desperate poverty).[^3] Her autobiography supplies the load-bearing quote.
5. **Steelman preserved.** Nash does not universalize the rosy picture: the solidarity helped "if sister wives had a good relationship," and she explicitly affirms it is "okay to be upset and grapple with the negative experiences," calling that "just as important."[^4] The argument is that the positive sisterhood was real and common, not that it was universal.

## Best Quotes
> "To me, it is a joy to know that we laid the foundation of a life to come while we lived in that plural marriage. That we three who loved each other more than sisters... will go hand in hand, together down through all eternity. That knowledge is worth more to me than gold and more than compensates for all the sorrow I have ever known." — [[Martha Cox]][^3]

> "You see all the ways that sister wives stepped in to support one another in life and in death."[^1]
