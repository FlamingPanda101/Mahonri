# The Catalyst Theory Is Grounded in How Joseph Translated, Not a Post-Hoc Rescue

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Critics charge that the "Catalyst theory" of the Book of Abraham — the idea that the papyri *prompted* revelation rather than serving as a text Joseph linguistically decoded — was concocted only after the surviving fragments were found not to contain Abraham, an ad-hoc "pivot" to dodge a debunking. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] answers that the theory is not a rescue at all: it is grounded in the *documented* way Joseph Smith already "translated" elsewhere, and it (alongside the Missing-Scroll theory) is simply what honest scholarship concludes once you separate what is known from what is assumed.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The two live theories follow from the data, not from embarrassment
Muhlestein keeps both options open and says which he leans toward depends on what he is currently studying. The **Missing-Scroll** theory: witnesses say Joseph translated the long scroll, which burned, so an Abraham text could have been on it (see [[Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire]]). The **Catalyst** theory: the papyri "opened his mind to revelation" and he received what God wanted to give "regardless of what was on the text." Both arise "from looking at the data we have and don't have," not from a need to reconcile.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — that scholars adopt these positions is clear; that they are evidence-driven rather than defensive is partly a claim about motive, supported by the next point.

### 2. The Catalyst pattern is already attested in the Joseph Smith Translation
The decisive grounding: Joseph demonstrably did exactly this with the Bible. "Joseph Smith looked at an English translation of the Bible and it opened his mind to give him stuff that wasn't in that English translation" — e.g. reading Genesis 1 and receiving Moses 2–4 ([[Book of Moses]]). So receiving revealed content *via* a text without rendering that text word-for-word "is one of the ways Joseph Smith translated." The Catalyst theory is not invented for Abraham; it generalizes a known practice.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the JST's expansion of Genesis into the Book of Moses is a well-documented parallel of the catalyst mechanism.

### 3. "Translate" in Joseph's usage means to convey, not to decode a language
"We're using the word translated very loosely... we're using it the way it was used in Joseph Smith's day" — "I'm getting information that is in one source and giving it to you in another way." A bean "translated" into another bean isn't a Chinese-to-English rendering; the word meant *conveyance/transformation*. So demanding a linguistic decode of the Egyptian misreads Joseph's own category. See [[Translation Means to Convey the Mind Not Decode a Language]].[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the period semantic range of "translate" is real and bears directly on the expectation being imposed.

### 4. Changing one's theory on new data is healthy scholarship, not a pivot
Muhlestein reframes the "pivot" charge: revising a position to better account for recalcitrant data "is a healthy thing," not a scandal (he draws the analogy to the Catholic Church revising on heliocentrism). The strength of having *multiple* viable models is honesty about what the data can and cannot fix.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a sound methodological point; it defends the *practice* of theory-revision rather than proving either theory true.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's strongest reply: a theory that makes the translation *independent of the actual text* is effectively unfalsifiable — if the source can be a lost scroll, or merely a "catalyst" for free-standing revelation, then no examination of any surviving papyrus could ever count against the Book of Abraham, which looks like insulation rather than explanation. Moreover, some of Joseph's contemporaries plainly *believed* he was rendering the characters in front of him (the Kirtland Egyptian Papers put characters in the margin beside the text), so the Catalyst reading must explain away that apparent intent. The honest LDS answer concedes the unfalsifiability cost but argues the catalyst mechanism is not invented for this case — it is read off the independently-documented JST (Moses) and off Joseph's own period usage of "translate" — and that the marginal-character documents are better explained as a later *cipher* project than as interlinear translation (see [[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool]]). On that view the Catalyst theory is a description of a known practice, not a bespoke escape hatch.
