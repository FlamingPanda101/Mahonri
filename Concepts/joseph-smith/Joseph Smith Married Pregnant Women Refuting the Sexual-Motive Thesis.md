# Joseph Smith Married Pregnant Women Refuting the Sexual-Motive Thesis

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that the standard reading of Nauvoo polygamy — that [[Joseph Smith]] instituted it to obtain sex with desirable young women — is refuted by the **pattern of his earliest plural marriages** once the chronology is corrected. The prototype case is not the single [[Louisa Beeman]] (long thought first, sealed April 5, 1841) but [[Zina Huntington Jacobs]], a married woman about **seven months pregnant**. Bradley shows that his first several Nauvoo plural wives were already married and, strikingly, **pregnant or recently so** — and since 1800s medical custom forbade sex late in pregnancy, deliberately sealing about-to-deliver women is the *opposite* of seeking immediate sex. The behavioral pattern, read abductively, points to a non-sexual, "raising-up-seed"/adoption-like sealing logic and a religiously sincere (if puzzling) motive. Notably, Bradley reaches this from a hostile starting point: he spent years researching ~1,500 polygamy sources for [[Brian Hales]] *while out of the Church*, convinced the motive was libido, and says the data changed his mind.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Re-dating the "first wife" dissolves the young-single profile — rate: strong, source-critical
The April 5, 1841 date for [[Louisa Beeman]] rests on [[Joseph Bates Noble]]'s affidavit (published by [[Andrew Jenson]]). Noble is a poor date-witness: his biographer called him "terrible with dates" and "a big boaster"; his claim to have fathered the first plural-marriage child is false ([[Heber C. Kimball]] et al. preceded him); under Temple Lot questioning he gave a 1840-1842 range; and he located the sealing at his Nauvoo house, **not built until after fall 1841**. April 5 is the date of *Noble's own* 1843 plural marriage — suggesting confusion. Re-dating Louisa to spring 1842 makes [[Zina Huntington Jacobs]] first.[^1] *Strong: the date the whole "single young bride" reading depends on is independently unreliable.*

### 2. The pregnant-bride pattern is repeated, not a fluke — rate: strong, primary-sourced
- **[[Zina Huntington Jacobs]]** — sealed Oct 27, 1841, ~7 months pregnant; her son Zebulon born Jan 2, 1842; descendant **DNA testing** confirms the child was her legal husband [[Henry Jacobs]]'s, not Joseph's.[^2]
- **[[Presendia Huntington Buell]]** — sealed mid-Dec 1841 (six weeks after her sister Zina); bore a child in 1842, so ~2/3 likely pregnant at sealing.[^2]
- **[[Mary Elizabeth Rollins Lightner]]** — sealed Jan/Feb 1842; bore a child in March 1842, so ~7-8 months pregnant.[^2]
A man seeking immediate sex with "the hottest chicks in Nauvoo" would not select women he could not have sex with; the repetition makes it deliberate. *Strong as a pattern argument; the gravestone/DNA data are checkable.*

### 3. The husbands did not complain — rate: moderate, corroborating
[[Brian Hales]] notes there are no recorded complaints from the legal husbands, most of whom stayed in the Church — consistent with the sealings being understood as something other than sexual appropriation.[^3] *Moderate: absence of complaint is suggestive, not decisive.*

### 4. The inference is explicitly abductive — rate: candidly interpretive
Bradley is careful that the data tell us strongly what the motive **was not** (sex/opportunism) rather than positively proving what it *was*; he floats an adoption/"born in the covenant," raise-up-seed-to-the-Lord logic ([[Jacob 2.30|Jacob 2:30]]) as the best available inference, while declining to settle the theology ("I'm a historian, not a theologian"). See [[The Best Explanation Beats Cherry-Picked Evidence in History]].[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic's strongest replies: (a) **selection bias** — Bradley features the pregnant married women but Joseph also married demonstrably non-pregnant and very young women (Helen Mar Kimball at 14, et al.), so a pattern drawn from the early "polyandrous" subset may not characterize the whole; (b) **absence of recorded sex ≠ absence of sex** — 19th-century reticence and the lack of surviving children prove little, and at least some later wives plausibly had conjugal relations, so "non-sexual" overgeneralizes from the prototype; (c) the [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel|grooming-pattern critique]] (Bill Reel) reframes the *theological* coercion (eternal-stakes ultimatums, isolation, intermediaries) as the real problem, which the "he wasn't after sex" defense does not touch — a man can be sincerely non-libidinous and still wield prophetic authority coercively. A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument is targeted, not total — it refutes the *specific* and most common claim (that polygamy was *fundamentally about* Joseph's libido) by showing the prototype cases are inexplicable on that thesis; it does not claim every union was celibate, and the sincerity it establishes (a man responding to perceived revelation, see [[The Angel with a Drawn Sword Coincides with the Orionid Meteor Shower]]) is precisely what the "con man / predator-for-sex" frame denies (cf. [[The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man]]). The grooming critique and the motive critique are distinct charges; this note answers the latter.

## Best Quotes
> "Joseph is actually deliberately choosing women who are not only pregnant but… about to give birth. So if Joseph Smith is looking for immediate sex with the hottest chicks in Nauvoo, like he's doing it wrong."[^2]

> "She is seven months pregnant at the time of the marriage… and [DNA shows] he was the child of the legal husband Henry Jacobs, not Joseph Smith's child."[^2]

> "For years I pursued the idea that Joseph Smith's polygamy was motivated by sex… and I was wrong. The data showed that I was wrong, and… I changed my mind."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the historical counter-case to the vault's hardest polygamy challenge, [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel]]: where Reel brackets theology to press a behavioral-grooming pattern, Bradley brackets theology to press a *different* behavioral pattern (pregnant brides) that cuts against the sexual-motive premise underlying the "con man" reading. It connects to [[Polygamy Denialism]] (Bradley affirms Joseph *did* practice it — the question is *why*) and to the sincerity argument in [[The Angel with a Drawn Sword Coincides with the Orionid Meteor Shower]]. Honest takeaway: the re-dating and the DNA/gravestone data are genuinely strong against the libido thesis for the *early* marriages; the defense does not, and does not claim to, resolve the ethical objections to religiously-pressured marriage.
