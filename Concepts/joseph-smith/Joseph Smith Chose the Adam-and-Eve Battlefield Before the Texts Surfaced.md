# Joseph Smith Chose the Adam-and-Eve Battlefield Before the Texts Surfaced

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the most striking thing about Joseph Smith's expansions of Genesis is not any single matched detail but his **choice of subject matter**: in an 1820s–30s religious culture obsessed with *early Christianity* and the Roman Empire, Joseph turned his attention to **Adam** — and a flood of *Adam-and-Eve* apocrypha (the Conflict of Adam and Eve, the Cave of Treasures, the various lives of Adam and Eve) surfaced and were translated only *after* his death, all focused on the very figure he chose to elaborate.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The cultural default was early Christianity, not Adam — rate: moderate (historical framing)
Barnes' framing: "back in Joseph's day the obsession was with early Christianity. They wanted to get back to Jesus Christ. If there was a Netflix in 1830 it would be Roman Empire documentaries and *The Chosen* 24/7… and then Joseph comes along and says, 'let's talk about Adam.'"[^1] Choosing Adam was a non-obvious move against the grain of his environment.

### 2. Joseph emphasized Adam persistently — rate: moderate (documentary)
"Joseph preached Adam from 1830 till the day he died… it was a huge emphasis in the restoration" — the Book of Moses, the Doctrine and Covenants (Adam-ondi-Ahman), and the temple ordinances all foreground Adam and Eve, their expulsion, and their continuing *conflict* with Satan, which is exactly the theme of the *Conflict of Adam and Eve* literature.[^1] (Cf. [[Adam Gathering His Posterity Before Death Matches the Adam Apocrypha]].)

### 3. The matching texts postdate him — rate: moderate (the proleptic structure)
The Conflict of Adam and Eve exists in Latin, Greek, Georgian, Armenian, Ethiopic, and Romanian recensions; Genesis contains none of this material. Barnes' point is the *prolepsis*: "the fact that he even chooses to play on that battlefield shows that he knew something. Any honest scholar would look at these documents and say, how did he know to focus where he did?"[^2] This is the methodological cousin of [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]] and [[The Life of Adam and Eve Fills the Gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses 5]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The argument trades on a *choice-of-topic* coincidence that is weaker than a content match. (a) **Adam was not obscure.** Genesis 1–5 was among the most-read, most-preached text in frontier America; "expanding Genesis" — including the Fall, Cain, and the patriarchs — is the *obvious* place for a Bible-revising prophet to begin (Joseph started a JST revision of Genesis precisely because it is the front of the book). Choosing Adam needs no special knowledge. (b) **Selection bias.** With a large, contradictory apocryphal corpus, one can almost always find *some* later text that resonates with whatever Joseph wrote; the "battlefield" framing counts the hits and ignores the misses. (c) **Availability.** Several Adam traditions (the *Conflict*, Cave of Treasures) were known to Western scholarship and partly translated in the early 19th century, so "surfaced only after his death" overstates their inaccessibility. (d) **Topic ≠ accuracy.** Even granting Joseph chose an apt subject, that says nothing about whether his *content* is correct — the load-bearing work is done by specific data points (treated, with their own caveats, in the related notes), not by the choice of Adam per se.

**What survives:** the observation that Joseph's sustained Adam-and-Eve emphasis happens to align with a genre of apocrypha that became prominent later is a real and interesting *framing* consideration; but as an argument it is softer than the content-level parallels, and it is vulnerable to selection effects and to the simple fact that Genesis is the natural starting point for any scriptural reformer.
