# Lion-Couch Legs Apart Signal a Living Exalted Figure Not a Forgery

## The Claim

[[Cardon Ellis]] relays an observation from Egyptologist [[Bruce Porter]] (made on a "Gospel on the Nile" temple tour) about a specific criticism of **Facsimile 1**. Critics argue that the standard Egyptian **lion-couch scene** depicts an embalming — a *dead* figure whose feet are together — and that because Abraham's **legs are drawn apart**, the facsimile is a forgery or misinterpretation. Ellis answers that Porter photographed **multiple lion-couch scenes in Egyptian temples where the figure's legs are apart**, and that parted legs conventionally signal a *living* or *exalted/resurrected* figure (one who can still *walk*), not a corpse. So the legs-apart feature, far from being an error, fits an *alive/exalted* reading consistent with Abraham being delivered rather than embalmed. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Feet-together = dead; legs-apart = alive — rate: moderate (iconographic convention, asserted via Porter)
The criticism assumes lion-couch scenes are *always* embalming scenes with feet bound together to show death. Ellis reports Porter's counter from on-site photography: at "three different temples" they found lion-couch scenes "where their legs were apart, demonstrating how they were like exalted beings either heading towards the afterlife or whatever" — i.e., the convention is not monolithic, and parted legs read as vitality/exaltation. [^1]

### 2. Walking/legs as a life-symbol across Semitic languages — rate: weak-moderate (supporting wordplay)
Barnes adds a cross-linguistic gloss: in Greek (lambda) and ancient Hebrew, glyphs/letters tie *legs* to *life* and *walking* — "that's why the serpent loses his legs in the garden" (losing legs = losing one's estate, mortality, death). So legs-apart functioning as a life/mobility marker coheres with a broader ancient symbolic grammar. [^2]

### 3. The Karnak provenance tie-in — rate: weak (anecdotal, host flags uncertainty)
Ellis notes one such legs-apart lion-couch scene was at the **temple at Karnak** — near where, he says, the priest whose papyri reached Joseph Smith served (a sub-temple of Karnak) — and immediately hedges: "before I quote myself on that, I have to reassess the map and my pictures." Offered as suggestive, not established. [^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The mainstream Egyptological reply is strong here. (a) The restored Facsimile 1 follows a **well-attested embalming/resurrection-of-Osiris type**, and Egyptologists (e.g., Robert Ritner) read the reclining figure, the jackal-headed priest, and the canopic jars as a standard funerary tableau for the deceased *Hor* — the "legs apart vs together" detail does not convert a funerary scene into an Abrahamic sacrifice. (b) Posture variation in lion-couch vignettes has **conventional explanations internal to Egyptian art** (the ba-soul scenes, the revival of Osiris who does rise) that do not require — or support — the Book of Abraham's specific narrative of a priest of Pharaoh about to knife Abraham. (c) The Porter claim is **relayed second-hand without published citation or images**, so it cannot yet be weighed against the Egyptological literature; "I have to reassess my pictures" is an honest admission that the Karnak-provenance link is not load-bearing. (d) Even granting that parted legs can mark a living/exalted figure, that is *consistent with* but does not *evidence* the Abrahamic reading; multivalent symbols underdetermine the interpretation (see [[Egyptian Symbols Are Multivalent and Hard to Interpret]]). The defensible point is modest: the "legs-apart proves forgery" objection is weaker than critics present it, because lion-couch posture is not as uniform as "always feet-together-because-dead" assumes — but it falls well short of positively vindicating Facsimile 1's Abrahamic interpretation, which the vault locates instead in temple-typology readings like [[The Facsimiles Encode Abraham's Endowment Through the Osiris Myth]].
