# The Hyrum-Smith-at-Dartmouth Theory Fails the Records

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A naturalistic theory (associated with Randy Bell, circulated via Mormon Stories) holds that the distinctive doctrines of Mormonism trace to [[Hyrum Smith]]'s education at **Dartmouth**, where he supposedly absorbed esoteric/scholarly ideas later fed to [[Joseph Smith]] for the Book of Mormon and Doctrine and Covenants. [[Don Bradley]] and the Ward Radio panel ([[Cardon Ellis]], [[Kwaku El]]) rebut it on the documentary record: Hyrum did not attend Dartmouth College at all, the chronology and content don't fit, and the theory is a textbook case of building a sweeping causal claim on a thin, mis-stated fact.[^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Hyrum attended a prep school, not the College — rate: strong, documentary
Hyrum attended **Moore's Charity School** (a preparatory/"Indian charity" school run on the Dartmouth campus), not Dartmouth College. He is **not on Dartmouth's alumni rolls**; [[Lucy Mack Smith]], describing it herself, called it merely "the academy at Hanover" — not even bothering to name Dartmouth. Saying "Hyrum went to Dartmouth" is, in Cardon's words, "actively lying."[^2] *Strong: this is a checkable record claim, and the conflation of a campus prep school with the university is the theory's load-bearing error.*

### 2. The attendance was brief and partly unrecorded — rate: moderate
School records confirm only about **one year** of Hyrum's attendance; [[Lucy Mack Smith|Lucy]] recalled three or four years, but the first year he is absent from the records, and a middle year's records are incomplete (the typhoid epidemic — the same outbreak whose Dartmouth-Medical-School doctors operated on Joseph's leg). So even the duration is far thinner than the theory needs.[^3] *Moderate: limited time on a prep campus is weak soil for "the source of Mormonism."*

### 3. The chronology and target are wrong — rate: strong as a structural objection
The theory requires that a young teenager absorbed advanced religious ideas and then **sat on them for 20-25 years**, slipping them not into the Book of Mormon (1829) but mostly into the later Doctrine and Covenants. Bradley notes this is "a fever dream he had as a 14-year-old at Dartmouth" doing impossible explanatory work — and it ignores that founding and dying for Nauvoo, suiting up against U.S. mortar fire, etc., are not the acts of people running a literary hoax sourced from a summer prep school.[^4] *Strong: the "delayed-release from a teen prep course" mechanism is wildly improbable.*

### 4. It exemplifies the "grandfather's-cousin" fallacy — rate: rhetorical but apt
The panel lampoons the inference chain as the Ferris Bueller "my best friend's sister's boyfriend" telephone game: the actual connection is Hyrum's *grandfather's cousin* who had attended (and likely died before Hyrum arrived). Bradley, a genealogist, notes he doesn't even know his own great-grandfather's cousin's affairs — the degree of separation is doing no real work.[^1] This is the general failure mode of [[Fawn Brodie Popularized the View of the Hebrews Theory|environmental-source theories]]: a tenuous proximity inflated into causation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The strongest version of the theory drops the false "Dartmouth College" claim and argues more modestly: the Smith family *did* have a real, documented connection to the Dartmouth orbit (Hyrum at Moore's; the grandfather's cousin; Dartmouth-trained physicians treating Joseph's leg), New England's religious-intellectual currents were demonstrably in the air, and it is legitimate to investigate whether any specific motifs entered the family through that channel. Bradley explicitly grants this: "because the Smiths did have that connection with Dartmouth, I think sure people ought to explore that." The defensible claim is *exposure to an intellectual environment*; the indefensible one is *that environment generated the Book of Mormon and Mormon doctrine*. The vault's reply is that exposure is not derivation, and the specific, sweeping causal claim collapses on the records (Hyrum's actual school, the 20-year delay, the misdirected output).

## Best Quotes

## LDS Connection
This is a specific instance of the broader rebuttal to environmental-origin theories of the Book of Mormon (cf. [[Fawn Brodie Popularized the View of the Hebrews Theory]] and [[The Consensus of Joseph Smith's Contemporaries That He Was Uneducated]]). It also models Bradley's own method: facts have to be **stated correctly and in order** before they can bear a causal thesis (see [[The Best Explanation Beats Cherry-Picked Evidence in History]] and [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]]). The candid takeaway: investigating the Smiths' New-England milieu is fair game; the "Dartmouth made Mormonism" claim is not what the records support.
