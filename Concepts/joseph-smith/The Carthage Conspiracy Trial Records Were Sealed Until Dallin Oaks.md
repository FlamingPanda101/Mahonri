# The Carthage Conspiracy Trial Records Were Sealed Until Dallin Oaks

## The Claim
The documentary basis for treating the martyrdom as a planned assassination is the 1845 Carthage murder trial. Brian Stutzman recounts that after the 1845 trial the documents were **sealed in wax-type paper with flour paste and stored**, untouched until the late 1960s, when a young law professor named [[Dallin H. Oaks]] — then at the University of Chicago, not identifying himself as a Latter-day Saint — located and opened them, becoming "the first human to touch those documents since they were sealed in 1845." With graduate student Marvin Hill he spent ~10 years producing *Carthage Conspiracy* (early 1970s), the groundbreaking study of the trial.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The book is real and standard (rated: strong).** *Carthage Conspiracy: The Trial of the Accused Assassins of Joseph Smith* (Oaks & Hill, University of Illinois Press, 1975) is a genuine, well-regarded academic study of the trial; the panel's reliance on it is sound. (Note: the panel's "early 70s" dating is slightly off — 1975.)[^1]
2. **The jury-nullification finding (rated: moderate).** Oaks/Hill document that the trial functioned as **jury nullification** — the community's will overrode the law; the closing argument told jurors that to convict the five would mean convicting "every man, woman, and child in the county." The original subpoenaed jury was dismissed and replaced with locals.[^2]
3. **Internal betrayal is documented (rated: moderate).** The conspiracy included former insiders — [[William Law]] (first presidency) and stake president William Marks — not merely outside enemies; the Laws and Higbees published the *Nauvoo Expositor*, and Joseph named conspirators in his "Conspiracies of Nauvoo" discourse.[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Two caveats. (a) The vivid "Oaks pulled out the sealed documents, hair stood up on the back of his neck, first human since 1845" narrative is **Stutzman's retelling of Oaks's anecdote**, polished over decades; the historical *substance* (Oaks researched the Carthage trial records and co-authored the book) is solid even if the dramatic framing is reminiscence. (b) That the trial ended in acquittal via nullification is itself the strongest evidence the panel needs — but it equally shows the legal record does *not* prove a coordinated *national* plot, only a locally-backed killing the community refused to punish; the trial documents indict the five defendants and the community, not "23 delegates from 26 states." So this note supports premeditation-plus-local-political-backing (see [[The Martyrdom Was a Coordinated Political Assassination Not a Mob]]) more securely than it supports any nationwide-caucus claim. It also functions as the inverse of the [[Witnesses Conspiracy Theory Rebutted]] pattern: here the documented conspiracy is against Joseph, whereas critics' "Brigham did it" theory is the unsupported one.
