#type/claim

# Joseph Smith Sought Secular Authentication of Artifacts He Believed Ancient

## The Claim
A behavioral argument from the [[Kinderhook plates]] episode: rather than rushing the plates into revelatory "translation," [[Joseph Smith]] wanted them vetted by outside scholarly bodies *before* he would translate — and he never tried to acquire them. This is the conduct of a man genuinely uncertain about (but open to) an artifact's authenticity, and it parallels how he handled the gold plates (sending the characters to scholars) while contrasting with how he eagerly purchased the Egyptian papyri he already trusted.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Wanted the plates sent to the Antiquarian Society before translating (rated: moderate — late, secondhand source).** [[Wilbur Fugate]], in an 1878 letter to James Cobb, claimed "Joe Smith said they would make a book of 1,200 pages, but he would not agree to translate them until they were sent to the Antiquarian Society of Philadelphia, France, and England." The panel flags this as "a late second- and third-hand account" to take "with a big grain of salt," but notes it *harmonizes* with the independent evidence that Joseph sought secular means to understand the plates.[^2]

2. **He never moved to acquire the plates (rated: strong, behavioral).** Despite ongoing expectation that he would translate them, Joseph let the plates leave Nauvoo and made no effort to keep or buy them — "if he wanted them to stay in Nauvoo, he could have had them stay."[^3]

3. **The contrast with the Egyptian papyri (rated: strong, comparative).** When the papyri appeared, Joseph *wanted* them and raised money from the poor Kirtland Saints to purchase the package (mummies included) — because "he trusted that those were genuinely ancient." He did the opposite with Kinderhook: he wanted an *outside* authentication he never sought for the papyri.[^3]

4. **The same pattern as the gold plates (rated: moderate, paralleled).** With the gold plates Joseph "sent them off to scholars" — the character transcript carried to [[Charles Anthon]] and others in New York and Philadelphia (see [[Charles Anthon Transcript Episode]]). Submitting artifacts to experts is a recurring habit, not a one-off.[^4]

5. **The Hofmann parallel (rated: applied).** When [[Mark Hofmann]]'s forgeries surfaced, the Church bought them "precisely to try to get them authenticated," bringing in experts whose initial read was favorable. Subjecting claimed artifacts to scholarly scrutiny is institutionally characteristic, not evasive.[^5]

**Steelman of the critic.** Each leg is contestable. The Antiquarian-Society detail rests entirely on a hostile forger's decades-late recollection, so it is the weakest possible support for a flattering claim. The "never acquired them" point is an argument from omission: Joseph was demonstrably overwhelmed in 1843, so inaction may reflect distraction, not principled skepticism. And the papyri contrast can be read against the apologist — Joseph *bought and trusted* the (equally non-Abrahamic) Book of Breathings papyri without outside vetting, which suggests his "authenticate first" instinct was inconsistent rather than a settled epistemic virtue. The honest claim is therefore comparative and cumulative: across the gold plates, the papyri, Kinderhook, and (institutionally) Hofmann, the recurring move toward outside examination fits sincerity better than the image of a charlatan who would simply manufacture a translation on demand. On the related but distinct *chain-of-custody* test for genuine artifacts, see [[Provenance Is the Standard for Authenticating Book of Mormon Artifacts]].
