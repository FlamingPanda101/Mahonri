# Joseph Smith Privately Admitted Polygamy to the High Council

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that at [[Oliver Cowdery]]'s April 1838 disciplinary trial in Far West, [[Joseph Smith]] gave the High Council a *private, secreted* explanation of the [[Fanny Alger]] matter — most plausibly an **admission that it was plural marriage, not adultery** — which both exonerated Joseph and convicted Oliver, and which the later disaffected scribe [[Ebenezer Robinson]] remembered as the moment he first heard of polygamy "in 1838."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The charge against Oliver was *falsely insinuating* adultery — rate: strong (court record)
Among nine charges against Oliver was that he had insinuated Joseph committed adultery with Fanny. Witnesses testifying about their conversations with Oliver admitted that when they asked him point-blank whether Joseph committed adultery, Oliver *would not confirm it* — he "refused to answer," or said no while (they claimed) implying yes. So even the hostile record never has Oliver flatly asserting adultery; the matter is left "fuzzy."[^2]

### 2. The scribe recorded that Joseph "gave a history of the girl business" — but not its content — rate: strong (documentary)
When Joseph testified, the High Council scribe ([[Ebenezer Robinson]]) wrote only that Joseph "gave a history of the girl business," said Oliver "was his bosom friend" whom "he had entrusted with many things," and then did *not* record what Joseph said. Bradley reads the setup ("Oliver was my friend and I told him a lot of stuff") as the preface to an *admission*, not a denial — and the omission as deliberate, because the content was meant to stay secret.[^3]

### 3. The High Council acquitted Joseph and condemned Oliver — rate: strong (outcome)
Whatever Joseph said satisfied the council that he had *not* committed adultery (the charge against Oliver was *false* insinuation), yet it was secret enough that the scribe withheld it. Plural marriage — sanctioned, not adulterous — fits both facts: it would clear Joseph of *adultery* while remaining a secret too sensitive to minute.[^4]

### 4. Ebenezer Robinson later dated his knowledge of polygamy to 1838 — rate: moderate (corroborating recollection)
Years later in Nauvoo, the disaffected Robinson wrote to a non-Mormon newspaper that "polygamy had raised its head in Missouri in 1838." Since the Fanny trial was the 1838 Missouri event he scribed, Bradley argues Robinson was most likely referring to what he heard *at that High Council* — i.e., Joseph's confession.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The reconstruction is an *inference to the best explanation*, not a record of words, and the gaps are real. (a) The decisive sentence — Joseph's actual statement — is precisely what the scribe did *not* write; "he set up for an admission" is Bradley's reading of a *preface*, and a denial can also begin "Oliver was my friend and I confided in him." (b) "Acquitted of adultery" is equally explained if Joseph *denied wrongdoing* outright and the partisan council (eager to expel Oliver) believed him; one need not posit a secret polygamy confession. (c) Robinson's "1838" recollection is late and could refer to rumor he absorbed generally, not specifically to courtroom testimony he heard. (d) Most importantly, a *private* admission of polygamy alongside *public* denials raises exactly the honesty problem the hosts themselves wrestle with ("how is he not lying?") — so even if the reconstruction is right, it sharpens rather than dissolves the ethical question. The defensible claim is modest: the High Council record is consistent with — and arguably best explained by — a private acknowledgment of plural marriage, but it does not *document* one.

## Best Quotes

> "The High Council Court scribe does not record what Joseph said. He writes that Joseph gave a history... of 'the girl business,' meaning the whole thing with Fanny Alger."[^3]

> "That doesn't sound like I'm about to give a denial; that sounds like I'm about to give an admission."[^3]

> "Ebenezer Robinson... writes that polygamy had raised its head in Missouri in 1838... I argue [he] was probably referring to what he heard at the High Council."[^5]
