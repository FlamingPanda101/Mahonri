# Joseph Smith's Refusal of Alcohol Cannot Be Explained by the Word of Wisdom

## The Claim
The familiar Latter-day Saint reading of the boy [[Joseph Smith]] refusing alcohol at his 1813 leg surgery — "of course, he's the Word of Wisdom prophet" — is an **anachronism**. [[Don Bradley]] argues the refusal is "something of a mystery" precisely because none of the obvious explanations work: the [[Word of Wisdom]] did not yet exist, there was no temperance movement, and the moment fell at the time of *highest* alcohol consumption in U.S. history. The refusal therefore demands a different, contextual explanation rather than being read backward through 1833.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Word of Wisdom was two decades away (strong, chronological).** The surgery was 1813; the Word of Wisdom ([[D&C 89]]) came in 1833 — "two decades before." Reading the refusal as Word-of-Wisdom obedience is like explaining a 1985 act by a 2005 rule.[^1]
2. **No temperance movement existed yet (strong, documented).** The American Temperance Society was organized in 1826 — "13 years after" the surgery — and the movement only gained steam in the 1830s–40s. Abstinence was not a cultural option Joseph could be conforming to.[^1]
3. **It was the peak of American drinking (strong, documented).** Historians estimate the average adult drank "over 7 gallons of actual alcohol per year" — about three times today's average and twice the modern CDC threshold for heavy drinking. A Smith neighbor recalled, "everybody drank in them days and there was no temperance."[^1][^2]
4. **Joseph himself drank later (rated: moderate, biographical).** Bradley concedes "we know that Joseph Smith Jr. drank some" later in life, ruling out a mere constitutional distaste for alcohol. So "when he actually needed something to dull the pain, why did he refuse it?"[^3]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
A skeptic could say the puzzle is manufactured: a terrified, possibly nauseated seven-year-old might simply have refused a foul-tasting, burning liquid for ordinary childish reasons — no grand motive required. And [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s account was written decades later and is plausibly idealized hagiography, so the "refusal" itself may be embellished. On this reading there is nothing deep to explain.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley anticipates this: the refusal cannot be mere childish aversion because Joseph *endured the entire operation* — nine pieces of bone drilled and chiseled out without anesthetic — which requires "a really strong motive," not a passing dislike. And the account's accuracy is independently supported by its undesigned coincidence with a context Lucy never discloses (the family's alcohol use), which a fabricator would not weave in (see [[Joseph Smith Refused Alcohol to Set an Example for His Father]]). The puzzle is real and the data point is solid; the question is what best explains it.

## Best Quotes
> "He didn't refuse the alcohol because there was a Word of Wisdom at the time. There wasn't a Word of Wisdom at the time… This is the time of highest drinking in U.S. history. Everybody drank."[^2]

> "Why, when he needed it most, when he actually needed something to dull the pain, why did he refuse it?"[^3]
