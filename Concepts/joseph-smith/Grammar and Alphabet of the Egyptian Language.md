# Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language

## The Claim
The *Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language* (a volume within the "Kirtland Egyptian Papers" / "Egyptian alphabet" documents) is a contemporaneous record produced alongside the [[Book of Abraham]] translation that, [[Don Bradley]] argues, reveals how [[Joseph Smith]] and his scribes actually understood the "curse" on [[Pharaoh]] in [[Abraham 1.21-27|Abraham 1]] — as a *division of stewardship* (kingly line vs. priestly line), not a racial punishment.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Provenance and authenticity (rated: strong, documentary).** The documents are on the Joseph Smith Papers website; "there are some parts of these documents in Joseph Smith's handwriting. We know he's involved in producing them." His scribes [[Oliver Cowdery]] and [[W. W. Phelps]] worked with him on the papyri and translation.[^1]

2. **The key passage on Pharaoh (rated: strong, direct quotation).** It calls Pharaoh "a prince of the royal blood, a true descendant from Ham, the son of Noah, an inheritor of the kingly blessings from under the hand of Noah, but not according to the priestly blessing, which fell upon Shem from under the hand of Noah," attributing the division to "the transgression of Ham."[^2] It treats the Genesis curse of [[Ham]] as allocating *kingship* (not priesthood) to his line — see [[The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment]].

3. **It does not cite a verse but plainly tracks Abraham 1 (rated: moderate).** Bradley concedes it is "general in the sense that it's not referencing the verse," but it discusses Pharaoh, descent from Ham, the curse, and lack of priesthood — "plainly talking about the same exact thing, but then it's framing it differently."[^3]

4. **The disputed relationship to the canonical text (rated: contested — steelman).** Bradley acknowledges "there's some controversy over this" and brackets it as "tangential," saying only that "the controversy is how exactly are they related to the text of the Book of Abraham."[^4] *Steelman:* this is the document's chief weakness as evidence. Many scholars argue the Kirtland Egyptian Papers were a derivative attempt to *reverse-engineer* an Egyptian grammar from the already-revealed English text, not a window into Joseph's interpretive intent — and some passages are in scribes' hands, not Joseph's. If the papers post-date or merely gloss the translation, they are weaker evidence of what Joseph "thought the curse meant." Bradley's case stands strongest when this document is corroborated by Joseph's *actions* (see [[Joseph Smith Ignored Race-Based Scriptural Distinctions]]).
