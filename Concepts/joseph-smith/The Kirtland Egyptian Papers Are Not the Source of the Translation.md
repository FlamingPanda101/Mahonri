# The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are Not the Source of the Translation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Cameron Bertuzzi]] argues the "Egyptian Alphabet and Grammar" notebooks (the Kirtland Egyptian Papers) show Joseph Smith's *method*: he lined up Egyptian symbols against English paragraphs, "proving" the Book of Abraham was a fabricated decoding exercise. [[Luke Hansen]] (citing [[Kerry Muhlestein]]) calls this "slide of hand": Joseph never claimed to use the Alphabet and Grammar to translate the Book of Abraham, and the historical record makes it mostly a [[W. W. Phelps]] project — so the documents cannot bear the weight Cameron's whole video rests on.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Joseph never tied the A&G to the translation (rated: moderate–strong)
Joseph called the Book of Abraham a translation/revelation but "made no such statement about the Egyptian alphabet and grammar ... we don't even have a record that a single person involved with the Egyptian papers project made that claim."[^1]

### 2. It was largely Phelps's, and Joseph was often absent (rated: moderate)
Per Muhlestein: "the historical record suggests Joseph wasn't even the one most involved ... it was instead W.W. Phelps." Joseph "wasn't even in Kirtland when some of them were written," which is incompatible with the papers being the product of his unique gift.[^1]

### 3. A Phelps letter predates the papyri (rated: strong, documentary)
Cameron's own video included a document that "is a letter from W.W. Phelps to his wife containing characters with proposed pronunciations and translations" — and "this letter was written weeks before the Saints learned that papyri even existed." The better explanation: the Kirtland papers were a **secular** attempt to work out ancient languages (partly by reverse-engineering the already-revealed Book of Abraham), not the engine of the translation.[^1]

### 4. The argument is built on an assumption (rated: structural)
"Cameron ... simply assumes that they are the source of the Book of Abraham translation and he repeatedly bases his arguments throughout the video on this weak assumption." Remove the assumption and the case collapses — an instance of [[Questioning Assumptions Is the First Defense of the Book of Abraham]].[^1]

## Counter-arguments / related
A critic answers: (a) the temporal correlation between the A&G and the Book of Abraham manuscripts is real, and several Book of Abraham manuscripts were produced in scribes' hands alongside the Egyptian characters, so "the A&G is unrelated" is too strong; (b) "Joseph didn't *claim* to use it" is an argument from silence — he may have used it without a surviving statement; and (c) the reverse-engineering thesis (A&G derived *from* an already-revealed text) is itself a faith-premise. Hansen's reply: even granting messy production, the documentary fact that a "translation" letter predates knowledge of the papyri shows the project's logic doesn't run the way the fabrication thesis needs (see [[Translation Means to Convey the Mind Not Decode a Language]]).

## Best Quotes
> "This is a bit of slide of hand from Cameron. Joseph Smith claimed that the Book of Abraham was both a translation and revelation ... but he made no such statement about the Egyptian alphabet and grammar." [^1]

> "This letter was written weeks before the Saints learned that papyri even existed." [^1]

## LDS Connection
A core plank of the Book of Abraham defense: decoupling the [[Book of Abraham]] from the Kirtland Egyptian Papers (cf. [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]]) and from the surviving Breathings fragments ([[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]), reframing "translation" as revelation rather than decipherment.
