# Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel

## The Point
**Claim ([[Bill Reel]]):** Set aside the labels ("sealings," "eternal marriage") and "focus on behavior, because the pattern is what matters." Reel lays out a grooming sequence and argues [[Joseph Smith]] matches it step for step: target vulnerable, often orphaned teenage girls already inside his circle of trust; create access and build trust as a father-figure; isolate them in his home; desensitize ("normal boundaries don't apply in this higher spiritual context"); invoke prophetic authority and "an angel perhaps with a flaming or drawn sword"; escalate to eternal pressure (exaltation if you comply, "spiritual death" if you don't); use older women as intermediaries to make it "feel sanctioned and not predatory"; impose secrecy; and silence dissenters (Nancy Rigdon, Martha Brotherton) by attacking their credibility.[^1] His case study is Lucy Walker — 16/17, mother dead, father sent away on a mission by Smith himself, brought under Smith's roof, then told God commanded the marriage with a *one-night* deadline, and after no answer a *second* night under "the gates of heaven shall be closed against you."[^2]

**Rebuttal ([[Robert Gurr]]):** Gurr says he is "comfortable" with the Lucy Walker episode because the era did not view marriage as a sexual union; Joseph is "a product of his time period," a colonial frontier where girls married young and many sealings were non-consummated "wards" — "basically adoptions, basically taking on a young girl as another daughter." He concedes Joseph had sexual relations with "a handful... less than 10," and that 14 (e.g. Helen Mar Kimball) lacks evidence of sexual relations. Judging Joseph by modern standards is the fallacy of **presentism**.[^3] See [[Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr]].

## Evidence
1. **The behavioral-pattern frame is Reel's strongest rhetorical move (rated: strong, framing).** By bracketing the theology and listing the predator-grooming checklist, Reel forces the "even Mormons recoil at Warren Jeffs / Samuel Bateman" comparison: "take away your need to believe in him as the prophet, he's committing the same behaviors."[^4] The list of named young brides (Lucy Walker 17, Helen Mar Kimball 14, Nancy Winchester 14, Fanny Alger 17, etc.) gives the pattern its weight.[^1]
2. **The Lucy Walker deadline detail (rated: strong, primary-sourced to her account).** The two-night ultimatum, sleep-deprivation, isolation, and eternal-stakes framing are, by Lucy's *own* words, conditions Reel argues "produce that answer" — making the eventual spiritual confirmation suspect as a "free, informed choice."[^2]
3. **Gurr's "ward / non-consummated" reply (rated: weak under cross-examination).** Reel answers that Gurr is "using the exception": the reasonable read of a man pursuing "husband-wife structures of relationship" is that sexuality was present, and absence of recorded children or testimony reflects 19th-century reticence, not chastity.[^5] *Steelman of Gurr:* Helen Mar Kimball at 14 genuinely lacks evidence of sexual relations, so the flat "all predatory" claim overreaches on that case. But the deeper exchange exposes the *theological* cost: Gurr must hold that "a heavenly father... saw Joseph Smith in a father-daughter relationship and goes, I think I'm going to have him approach her for an intimate one" — a "would God" problem (cf. [[Could God vs Would God on the Book of Mormon]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Whatever language we use — sealings, covenants, eternal marriage, sexual relationship — the behavioral pattern is clear. A powerful adult male in a position of total authority, gaining access to young girls, creating dependency, applying pressure, and reframing it as divine." [^1]

> "If no confirming answer is received, he said, quote, 'The gates of heaven shall be closed against you.' And gives her one more night." [^2]

> "Take away your need to believe in him as the prophet, he's committing the same behaviors." [^4]

> "He didn't view all of his marriages as marriages... they're basically adoptions, basically taking on a young girl as another daughter." — Gurr [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is among the hardest challenges in the vault because the apologetic reply ([[Presentism in Historical Judgment]]) is a *real* historiographical principle yet collides with the eternal-law objection Reel presses: if God's moral law is eternal, why does it "go along with whatever the cultural mores" of 1840s frontier America happen to be? The exchange also feeds the meta-argument that LDS morality "constantly shifts" (polygamy → monogamous celestial marriage; priesthood/temple bans) — see [[The Bible Was Wrong and Consensus Determines Morality]]. A candid LDS response must separate the defensible point (don't anachronistically read modern marriage norms back) from the indefensible (Gurr's on-camera comfort with religiously-coerced teen marriage), which Reel exploits to good effect.
