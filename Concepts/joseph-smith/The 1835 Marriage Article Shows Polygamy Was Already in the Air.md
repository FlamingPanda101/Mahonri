# The 1835 Marriage Article Shows Polygamy Was Already in the Air

## The Claim

A standard critical move is that polygamy was a *later* Nauvoo development "retrojected" onto the [[Fanny Alger]] relationship — i.e., people only *called* it plural marriage after the fact. [[Don Bradley]] counters that the **1835 "Article on Marriage"** ([[Doctrine and Covenants 101]] in the 1835 edition) proves polygamy was already a live issue in the church *before* Emma discovered the Fanny relationship in July 1836 — so plural marriage was not invented out of thin air years later.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The article was published August 1835 — a year before discovery — rate: strong (dated text)
The monogamy statement appeared in the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants, published August 1835. [[Emma Smith]] discovered the Fanny Alger relationship in **July 1836** — eleven months *later*. So whatever prompted the 1835 article predates the discovery.[^2]

### 2. The article itself says the church was *already* "reproached" with polygamy — rate: strong (internal wording)
The text reads: "Inasmuch as this church... has been reproached with the crime of fornication, and polygamy, we declare that we believe that one man should have one wife, and one woman but one husband." The phrasing presupposes that *by August 1835* the church had already been accused regarding polygamy — i.e., *something* involving plural marriage was being discussed. As the hosts put it: why issue this statement "unless there were swirling rumors of something else?"[^3]

### 3. A scriptural-precedent parallel — rate: weak/illustrative
[[Kwaku El]] raises the parallel that [[Jacob 2]] in the Book of Mormon also addresses polygamy — arguably because it was happening then. The exchange concedes this is double-edged: a revelation/statement responding to polygamy can mean polygamy was occurring *or* merely rumored. The hosts leave it as suggestive context, not proof.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the note where the apologetic and critical sides use the *same* logic in opposite directions, so it must be handled carefully. (a) "They denied X, therefore X was happening" is precisely the inference critics deploy against Joseph's *later* public denials of polygamy — so an apologist who leans on "they addressed polygamy in 1835, therefore it was real" cannot consistently dismiss "Joseph denied polygamy publicly, therefore..." as illegitimate reasoning. A denial is genuinely ambiguous between *refuting a real practice* and *refuting a false rumor.* (b) Being "reproached with polygamy" in 1835 is fully explained by *anti-Mormon rumor* (the church was accused of many things it did not do); the article may simply rebut slander, not hint at an actual secret practice. (c) Even if plural marriage was "in the air" in 1835, that does not establish that the Fanny relationship *was* a marriage rather than the very kind of misconduct the article condemns. So the article shows the *topic* predated 1836 — defeating the "invented entirely in Nauvoo" claim — without by itself proving Joseph was practicing polygamy in Kirtland.
