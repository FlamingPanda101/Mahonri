# The Early-1830s Dating of D&C 132 Is Historically Unsustainable

## The Claim

The new LDS polygamy Q&A page states that Joseph Smith "probably received" the revelation later canonized as [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] **as early as the 1830s**, even though he did not dictate it until July 1843. Historian [[Benjamin Park]] argues this early dating is **not a sustainable historical position** — he knows of no historian who actually believes 132 was revealed in the early 1830s. In his reading, the page asserts it not on evidence but on **ideological priority**: an early revelation lets the page place the polygamy doctrine *before* the [[Fanny Alger]] relationship, which then retroactively justifies it as principled plural marriage rather than an affair.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. No historian holds the early-1830s revelation date — rate: strong (expert consensus from within the field)
Park, speaking as a working historian and President of the Mormon History Association, states flatly that he does not know of a historian who believes D&C 132 was *initially revealed* in the early 1830s. The scholarly consensus dates the revelation's content to the Nauvoo period; the 1843 dictation is the documented event (see [[D&C 132 Internal Details Match Nauvoo July 1843]]).[^2]

### 2. The only thread to the 1830s is thematic, not evidentiary — rate: moderate (charitable reconstruction)
Park grants what he thinks the page is reaching for: Section 132 is *framed* around Joseph asking how the biblical patriarchs were justified in having many wives — a question that could plausibly have arisen while he was working on his translation of the Bible (the Joseph Smith Translation) in 1831–1832. But a thematic *connection* to that period is not the same as a *revelation* in that period; "that's not based really on historical evidence."[^3]

### 3. The theology that justifies polygamy does not develop until Nauvoo — rate: strong (internal to LDS doctrinal history)
Park's independent reason for rejecting an early date: the sealing-and-exaltation theology that Joseph uses to *explain and justify* plural marriage does not develop until Nauvoo. If the conceptual apparatus is Nauvoo-era, retrojecting the revelation to the early 1830s is anachronistic. This is also why he reads the Fanny Alger episode as not yet "polygamy" in the developed sense.[^4]

### 4. The dating serves a denial-era apologetic need — rate: moderate (motive analysis)
Park situates the move within the page's audience: a growing group of faithful Latter-day Saints who *deny* Joseph practiced polygamy and want to blame [[Brigham Young]]. Placing the revelation early helps anchor Joseph (not Brigham) as polygamy's originator while still framing it as divinely commanded from the start — an institutional priority shaping the historical claim.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The page's dating is defensible on its own terms. (a) The page says "probably received," not "revealed and recorded" — it may mean only that the *germ* of the doctrine reached Joseph early, which is compatible with a Nauvoo dictation and with the apologetic [[D&C 132 Internal Details Match Nauvoo July 1843|July 1843]] evidence. (b) Joseph himself and later leaders connected the patriarchal-marriage question to his early Bible-translation work, so an early *inquiry* is not invented out of nothing. (c) If the [[The Fanny Alger Relationship Dates to July 1836 Not 1833|Fanny Alger relationship is plural marriage in 1836]], then *some* revelatory warrant must predate 1843, which makes an earlier (even if undocumented) reception more than mere convenience. So the strongest reading is that Park has correctly identified that **a fully formed early-1830s revelation has no evidentiary basis**, while the page's softer "probably received early" can survive as a claim about the doctrine's slow genesis — a claim that is historically thin but not obviously false, and that the apologetic and critical sides interpret in opposite directions.
