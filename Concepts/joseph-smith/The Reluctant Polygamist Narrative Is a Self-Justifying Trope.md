# The Reluctant Polygamist Narrative Is a Self-Justifying Trope

## The Claim

The LDS polygamy Q&A page emphasizes how *challenging* plural marriage was for [[Joseph Smith]], [[Brigham Young]], and other early leaders — the familiar story that they resisted, sought other avenues, and only proceeded after an angel with a flaming sword commanded them. Historian [[Benjamin Park]] argues that "reluctant polygamist" is a **narrative trope**, not a description of behavior: it is how historical actors frame difficult actions to justify them, and how later generations use that framing to feel better. The documented record shows Joseph in the 1840s and Brigham afterward approached polygamy **"recklessly and with full gusto,"** not as men dragged unwillingly into it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "reluctance" language is a recognizable framing device — rate: moderate (historiographic analysis)
Park names the move explicitly: actors frame their own actions in ways that justify them, and those framings get re-deployed by later generations. Treating leaders' professed reluctance as a *literal* account of their disposition mistakes a rhetorical/devotional genre for a behavioral one. The angel-with-a-sword motif functions as divine compulsion that relieves the actor of responsibility.[^2]

### 2. Brigham Young's "casket" quote is the trope's centerpiece — rate: moderate (illustrative primary source)
Park cites the famous Brigham Young statement that on being introduced to polygamy he "would rather have been dead," that he looked at a funeral and wished he were in the casket. Park's point is not that the quote is fabricated but that it is consoling to modern Latter-day Saints precisely because it casts the practice as a heavy cross reluctantly borne — exactly the function a self-justifying trope performs.[^3]

### 3. The conduct contradicts the reluctance — rate: strong (behavioral record)
Against the reluctance narrative, Park sets the actual pattern: Joseph in the 1840s and Brigham after him pursued plural marriage as an "all-consuming practice." The genuine difficulties were *logistical* — keeping it secret, keeping participants satisfied — not motivational. "It's clear that they did not find it a challenge to continue practicing it." Reluctance does not square with the energy and scope of the practice.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The reluctance theme is not pure invention, and the apologetic reading has force. (a) Contemporaneous diaries and the structure of the [[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent|consent process]] do record real anguish — wives and husbands describing dread, delay, and spiritual wrestling (see [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]]); a wholesale "they loved it" reading flattens genuine ambivalence. (b) Initial reluctance and later wholehearted practice are *compatible*: a convert can begin in dread and, once convinced of the doctrine, pursue it earnestly without the early reluctance being insincere. (c) "Full gusto" is itself an interpretive characterization; the same energy can be read as obedience to a commanded principle rather than eagerness for the practice. So Park's strongest and fairest point is that **the page presents reluctance as the whole story and treats it as self-evidently exculpatory**, when historians must read such self-framing critically — while the apologetic side rightly insists the documented anguish was real and not merely performed.

## Best Quotes

> "It wasn't until an angel with a flaming sword came and told them that you had to practice. That's a narrative trope. That's one thing that scholars say — that is how historical actors can frame their actions in a way that justify, and then are used by later generations in order to make us feel better about it."[^2]

> "Joseph Smith during the 1840s, and then Brigham Young after, approached polygamy recklessly and with full gusto, you could say, in a way that is hard to reconcile with the sense that they were reluctant polygamists."[^1]

> "It was a challenge to keep secret. It was a challenge to keep people involved, satisfied. But in the end... it's clear that they did not find it a challenge to continue practicing it. Instead, they saw it as an all-consuming practice."[^4]
