# Winger's Egyptologists All Trace to a 1912 Episcopal Questionnaire

## The Claim

The centerpiece of [[Mike Winger]]'s video is a string of quotations from "renowned Egyptologists" dismissing the [[Book of Abraham]] (Petrie, Mace, Peters, Mercer, von Bissing, Meyer, etc.). [[Luke Hansen]] discovered that **all of these scholars were respondents to a single 1912 questionnaire** circulated by Episcopal Bishop Franklin S. Spalding to discredit the Book of Abraham — so Winger's "irrefutable scholarship" is one century-old, theologically motivated solicitation, not current consensus.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The names and dates line up with the Spalding list — rate: strong (sourcing audit)
Hansen cross-checked each scholar Winger quoted against the people Spalding wrote to; they matched "ding, ding, ding." Their lifespans confirm it: Flinders Petrie (1853–1942), Mace (1874–1928), Peters (1852–1921, himself an Episcopal priest), von Bissing (1873–1956), etc. The "experts" are early-twentieth-century figures whose remarks predate the relevant LDS scholarship by decades.[^2]

### 2. It is the "CES Letter of 1912" — rate: moderate (characterization)
The hosts frame Spalding's *Joseph Smith Jr. as a Translator* as essentially a 1912 anti-Mormon tract — a curated set of negative expert opinions — repackaged and presented by Winger as fresh, decisive scholarship. This is the recycling pattern: a pre-rebuttal source cited to bypass later answers. See [[Recycling Old Lies Is Anti-Mormon Standard Practice]].[^3]

### 3. Newer scholarship grants Joseph some correct hits — rate: moderate (counter-scholarship)
Against the 1912 dismissals, the hosts cite BYU Egyptologists ([[John Gee]], and a "Rhodes") who note Joseph's explanations of certain facsimile figures match present understanding of late Egyptian religion (the period the papyri come from) — too many correct interpretations to dismiss as pure chance. Gee is even shown *correcting his own side's* overreach, modeling scholarly balance. See [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Provenance is not refutation. (a) That the quotes come from 1912 does not make them *wrong*: if Petrie and Meyer correctly identified the facsimiles as ordinary, badly-copied funerary art, the *date* of that judgment is irrelevant — and on the core Egyptological point (the facsimiles are Book-of-the-Dead/Breathings material, not a unique Abrahamic revelation) later mainstream Egyptology has *reaffirmed*, not reversed, the 1912 verdict (cf. [[Robert Ritner]]). (b) Winger could fairly reply that he cited those scholars because their analysis is *representative* of consensus, and that a believer dismissing them as "just a 1912 questionnaire" commits the genetic fallacy. (c) The BYU counter-scholars are themselves interested parties, and "Joseph got some figures right for the period" is contested and, on the skeptical reading, a post-hoc rescue (see the steelman in [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]]). So the legitimate force of the point is narrow but real: Winger *presented* a single, dated, motivated solicitation as if it were the standing weight of modern Egyptology, which overstates his case — even though the underlying Egyptological reading he relies on is not refuted merely by exposing its 1912 origin.
