# Joseph Smith's Father-Son Bond Seeded the Doctrine of Saving the Fathers

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that [[Joseph Smith]]'s unusually intense bond with his father, [[Joseph Smith Sr]], is a key to his prophetic vocation: the boy's drive to "honor" and "save" his father — first demonstrated at the leg surgery — was *cemented* into his identity and flowered later into the Restoration doctrines of baptism for the dead and the Malachi promise to turn "the hearts of the fathers to the children." [[Richard Bushman]] is enlisted: "if there was a personal motive for Joseph Smith Jr.'s revelations, it was… to meet the need of his oft-defeated, unmoored father."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The bond is documented across the life (strong).** Joseph Sr is the first person Joseph tells about the angel Moroni and (Bradley argues, by parallel) likely the first he tells of the First Vision; Joseph weeps for joy baptizing his father in 1830; and a newly-surfaced Danish travelogue records Joseph saying "I wished to honor my father" as a chief motive of his youth.[^1][^2]
2. **The father's affection is on record (moderate, documented).** Joseph Sr's patriarchal blessings to his children "overflow with affection" — an unusually tender masculinity for his generation — fulfilling the "hearts of the fathers to the children" turning in the family itself.[^3]
3. **Bushman supplies independent scholarly support (strong).** Bushman names benefiting the father as a personal motive behind Joseph's revelations, which Bradley treats as corroboration of the surgery-rooted reading rather than mere apologetic projection.[^1]
4. **The doctrine generalizes the principle (moderate, interpretive).** Bradley notes the underlying principle — that "we can do things for those who have passed on" — is usually applied only to a handful of ordinances (baptism, endowment, sealing), but is broader; Joseph's life-long work for the dead is the institutional flowering of the boy's desire to save his father.[^4] (See [[Annidictions - Joseph Smiths Restorations Look Backward Not Forward]] for the broader "look backward" pattern.)

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
This is a psychobiographical reading that risks circularity: the surgery is read as expressing a "save the fathers" motive, and the later doctrine is then read as confirming the surgery's meaning — each propping up the other. A skeptic could note that baptism for the dead has obvious scriptural and theological drivers (1 Cor 15:29, the fate of those who died without the gospel) that need no childhood-trauma origin, and that "honor my father" is a commonplace sentiment, not a unique key.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley does not claim the surgery is the *sole* cause, only that it "reflects and shapes" a motive already present and visible in independent data (the Moroni disclosure, the baptism weeping, the Danish source, Bushman's verdict). The convergence of childhood act, documented adult sentiment, and a respected biographer's independent judgment makes the father-son thread more than a just-so story, even if the strict causal claim remains interpretive.

## Best Quotes
> "If there was a personal motive for Joseph Smith Jr.'s revelations, it was to satisfy his family's religious want and above all to meet the need of his oft-defeated, unmoored father." (Bushman, as quoted)[^1]

> "It's this boy who works to restore the covenant of the fathers… who goes on to institute baptism for the dead to save the fathers, the ancestors."[^4]
