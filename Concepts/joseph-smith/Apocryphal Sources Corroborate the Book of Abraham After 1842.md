# Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842

## The Point
Critics attack the Book of Abraham almost entirely on the *method* of its production (the papyri, the facsimiles) while ignoring the *text itself*. Fife flips the burden: the text contains a long list of narrative details about Abraham — found in *neither* the Bible nor any source available to Joseph Smith in 1842 — that have since turned up in ancient apocryphal and pseudepigraphal Abraham traditions recovered only *after* the Book of Abraham was published. The question is not "did Joseph translate the papyri the way an Egyptologist would?" but "how did the 1842 text anticipate dozens of details that scholars would only later find in ancient extrabiblical sources?"

## Evidence
Fife enumerates a long catalog of Book of Abraham details with no biblical precedent that later surface in post-1842 apocrypha [^1]. Among them:
- **The very existence of a record written by Abraham** — the Bible gives no hint a Book of Abraham ever existed; Abraham is literate and possesses ancient records (Abr. 1:28, 31).
- **Abraham's quest for the priesthood as "the right belonging to the fathers"** and his successful search for God (Abr. 1:1-3; 2:12).
- **His fathers' apostasy** — having once held the priesthood, they turned to idolatry "after the manner of the Egyptians" (Abr. 1:4-10).
- **Human sacrifice, including of children, by Chaldean idolaters**, and the killing of those who refused to worship idols (Abr. 1:7-11).
- **The priest's attempt to sacrifice Abraham himself** on a lion-couch altar described as a "bedstead" (Abr. 1:12-13; Facsimile 1).
- **Terah's role in seeking Abraham's death** (Abr. 1:30), his repentance during a famine, and his relapse into idolatry afterward (Abr. 1:30; 2:5).
- **Angelic deliverance** of Abraham after he cried to the Lord, and the destruction of the altar and idols (Abr. 1:15, 20; 2:13).
- **A famine in Chaldea before the move to Canaan** (Abr. 1:29-30; 2:1).
- **Abraham's possession of the Urim and Thummim** (Abr. 3:1) and his revealed knowledge of astronomy, which he then **teaches in Egypt** (Abr. 3:1-18).
- **The premortal council in heaven** where the plan for this earth was established (Abr. 3:18-28).

Because these details are attested across multiple, independent ancient sources, the case fits the broader pattern of [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]]: a single corroborating text is suspect, but broad independent agreement is the ordinary historiographical basis for credibility. Fife concedes (as the methodology note [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]] insists) that a raw parallel list does not by itself *prove* divine origin — but he argues the critics' refusal to engage the text at all is the tell.

## LDS Connection
This is a textual companion to [[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]] and to [[Abraham as a Backstory to Genesis]]: rather than defending the translation method, it argues that the *content* of the [[Book of Abraham]] carries ancient fingerprints Joseph Smith could not have manufactured in 1842. It mirrors the same evidentiary move Latter-day Saint apologists make for the Book of Mormon ([[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]]).
