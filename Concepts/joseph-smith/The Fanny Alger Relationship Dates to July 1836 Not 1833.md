# The Fanny Alger Relationship Dates to July 1836 Not 1833

## The Claim

[[Don Bradley]] argues that the [[Joseph Smith]]–[[Fanny Alger]] relationship was discovered in **late July 1836** (around July 22), not 1833 — meaning Fanny was **18, not 15**, and that the relationship *began* only shortly before discovery, *after* the sealing power was restored. This redating is load-bearing: it dissolves the "predatory marriage to a 14–15-year-old" charge and reframes the whole episode.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Levi Hancock's datable surrounding events — rate: strong (anchored chronology)
[[Levi Hancock]]'s autobiography says that in the summer of 1836 "Joseph told me to take Fanny Alger and go to Missouri," and brackets this with events we can date independently: it happened right after Levi's brother Solomon married (known date) and right when Joseph received the letter reporting Saints attacked in Clay County, Missouri (known to the exact day). These markers narrow the discovery to **a few days in July 1836**.[^2]

### 2. An Eliza Jane Webb record fixes the night — rate: moderate (single source)
Bradley cites a record created by Eliza (a Webb-family member who lived near the Smith and Hancock households) indicating the specific night the relationship was probably discovered: **July 22, 1836**.[^3]

### 3. Emma's watchfulness rules out a multi-year hidden marriage — rate: moderate (behavioral inference)
[[Emma Smith]] was famously vigilant — in Nauvoo she would not let Joseph be alone with plural wives even living in the house. The idea that Fanny lived in the Smith home as Joseph's wife for ~3.5 years (1833–1836) without Emma noticing is "not believable." A short relationship discovered in mid-1836 fits Emma's documented behavior far better.[^4]

### 4. The timing places it *after* the sealing keys — rate: moderate (theological significance)
If the relationship began shortly before July 22, 1836, it began *after* April 3, 1836 — the day Christ, Moses, Elias, and Elijah are said to have appeared in the Kirtland Temple and Elijah restored the sealing power. Bradley notes this changes the interpretive picture: the keys needed to seal a plural marriage already existed, undercutting the "affair he later disguised as polygamy" reading. (Bradley says he will argue in forthcoming publications that it happened *shortly after* the keys, not on the exact day.)[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

(a) The 1836 case still rests on *inference from surrounding events* and one Eliza-Webb record; a critic could argue the Hancock markers are themselves filtered through imperfect memory. (b) Redating to age 18 removes the most lurid charge but not the structural one: a married 30-year-old prophet entering a secret relationship with an 18-year-old servant in his home, then sending her out of state when discovered, while his wife reacts with distress and Fanny soon marries someone else — this is the pattern the critic emphasizes, and the calendar does not erase it. (c) The "after the sealing keys" point proves only *possibility*, not that a sealing actually occurred; the keys existing in April does not document a July ordinance. So the redating is a real and well-supported correction of the *age/date* facts, while the *moral* and *evidentiary* debate over what the relationship *was* turns on the other notes ([[Every Published Historian Concludes Fanny Alger Was Polygamy Not Adultery]]).
