# Storm at Zion's Camp Prophecy

## The Claim
As armed mobs from Richmond and Clay counties planned to cross the Missouri River near Fishing River to attack Zion's Camp, [[Joseph Smith]] (per Joseph Holbrook) prophesied the Lord would protect them — they should "stand still and see the salvation of God." A sudden, violent storm of darkness, lightning, thunder, and severe hail soaked the mob's ammunition and scattered their horses, preventing the attack. Mob member Colonel John Sconce reportedly said, "I see there is an almighty power that protects this people." Recorded in the *History of the Church*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The storm itself is well attested.** The Fishing River storm is one of the better-documented Zion's Camp episodes; a real meteorological event broke up an imminent confrontation. (Evidence rating: the *event* is solid; whether it was *prophesied* is the question.)[^1]
2. **Recollected after the fact.** The host notes "this is people obviously recollecting after what happened. So you could always say they're trying to make it more miraculous, retrofitting the prophecy." (Evidence rating: standard late-record caveat; see [[The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims]].)[^2]
3. **The prediction is generic deliverance language.** "Stand still and see the salvation of God" (echoing Exodus) is a faith exhortation rather than a specific weather forecast; a storm is then read back as the mechanism. (Evidence rating: weakens the predictive specificity.)[^2]
4. **Steelman of the believing view.** The timing — a storm severe enough to soak powder and scatter horses arriving precisely as the attack was launching — is, even to the skeptic, a fortunate coincidence; the mob's own colonel conceded a protecting power. If Joseph's assurance of deliverance immediately preceded it, the believer reasonably reads providence. The host grants the storm happened "whether or not you want to believe this was a prophecy."

## Best Quotes
