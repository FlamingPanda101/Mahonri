# Egyptians Mixed Jewish and Egyptian Religious Iconography

## The Claim
Egyptologist [[Kerry Muhlestein]] argues that the people who owned and produced [[Joseph Smith]]'s papyri — Greco-Roman-era (c. 200 BC) Theban priests such as **Hor** — are precisely the people known to have **blended Jewish and Egyptian** religious stories and images. So [[Joseph Smith]]'s readings of the [[Book of Abraham]] facsimiles may reflect what a *specific* syncretizing Egyptian priest saw in the drawings, and the fact that Abraham-associations attach to all three facsimiles is what one would expect if the Book of Abraham is genuine.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The owners were documented syncretists (rate: moderate-to-strong, Egyptology).** Around 200 BC the priests/priestesses who owned both surviving sets of papyri "were collecting stories from Greece, from Jews, and from other places ... and incorporating them into their own religious beliefs and practices." Greeks and Jews were the two largest non-Egyptian populations in Egypt; there were spells where, to call God down, "you become Moses," and Abraham appears in their magical texts.[^2]

2. **Hor is exactly who would own a Book of Abraham (rate: moderate, convergence).** Hor was a priest at Karnak (Thebes) for Amun-Ra; Thebes had a sizable Jewish population collecting Judaic tradition. "If the Book of Abraham is what we understand it to be ... a person like Hor is exactly the person we would expect to own it."[^3]

3. **The Vishnu/Jesus analogy (rate: moderate, illustrative).** If a group of Hindus known to mix Christian and Hindu symbols drew "Vishnu walking on the water next to a boat with 12 cows," an expert would correctly say "that's Vishnu" — yet the same image could legitimately be a Jesus image in that community. Likewise an Osiris figure in a syncretizing Theban context can carry an Abraham meaning.[^4]

4. **Jesus himself swapped Osiris for Abraham (rate: moderate, textual).** Per [[John Gee]], the "bosom of Osiris" funerary formula was reworked in Jewish/Christian usage as the "bosom of Abraham" — which is how [[Luke 16.22|Luke 16]] frames the afterlife (Lazarus in Abraham's bosom). Joseph swapping the very pair Jesus swapped is "an odd thing ... to get right if he's just making this all up." See [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]].[^5]

5. **All three facsimiles are elsewhere tied to Abraham (rate: moderate, cumulative).** "All of them at one point or another are in some way associated with Abraham by Egyptians ... what are the odds of that?" Muhlestein: "it takes more faith to disbelieve Joseph Smith is right than to believe he's right on that one."[^6]

## Steelman of the Critic
Syncretism is real but does not license *any* reading: that Egyptians sometimes reinterpreted a figure as Abraham shows a reading is *possible*, not that Joseph's particular identifications are *correct* for this artifact. The owner-identification (Hor) comes from the surviving papyri, but whether those fragments are the source of the Abraham *text* is the separate, contested missing-scroll question. And the strongest critic move is to grant all of this and still press that Joseph presented the facsimile explanations as definitive translations of what the figures mean, where Egyptologists differ — met separately by [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]].
