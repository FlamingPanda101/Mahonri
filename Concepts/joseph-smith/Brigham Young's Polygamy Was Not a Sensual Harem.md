# Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem

## The Point
The critic's charge is that [[Brigham Young]] was a "womanizing polygamist" who kept a sensual harem of dozens of wives — many of them very young — in the Beehive and Lion Houses, a Latter-day Saint version of a Turkish sultan's seraglio. Dr. [[Daniel C. Peterson]] answers that this is one of two equally absurd 19th-century caricatures (the other being that plural marriage was nothing but cold, abusive, transactional misery). The reality sits in between: Brigham "does not come across as much of a romantic," and a striking number of his marriages were *status-conferring* unions that gave women legal and economic standing they could not otherwise hold on the frontier.

## Evidence
- **The "Oriental harem" comparison is racist, not historical.** Peterson notes the 19th-century (and some present-day Christian) habit of comparing Brigham to "the Turkish sultan with his sensual harem, his seraglio," and to "African polygamy" — a depiction he and the host flag as "super popular, and let's be frank, very racist."[^1] It was, he jokes, "a cottage industry in the 19th century to compare Mormonism to the Islam of America."[^2]
- **Many marriages conferred status, not romance.** "A lot of the women that Brigham married... it was to sort of give them status," because in the 19th century women "could not really manage property very well unless they had a man to do it for them." Several wives were elderly by frontier standards; the marriages kept them from being "just on their own on the frontier without a way of making a living."[^3]
- **Ann Eliza Webb was a grifter, not a victim.** The famous apostate wife Ann Eliza Webb married, left Brigham, married again, and abandoned that marriage too. Hugh Nibley's *Sounding Brass* "pokes holes in her story," its contradictions and over-the-top depictions; the last we see of her she has stripped her son's home "bare" and vanished. "She's a grifter."[^4]
- **A historian's verdict: the harem was an outsider's invention.** A [[Keystone]] video cites historian [[Richard Van Wagoner]]: "contrary to popular 19th century notions about polygamy, the Mormon harem dominated by lascivious males with hyperactive libidos did not exist," and "the image of unlimited lust was largely the creation of gentile travelers to Salt Lake City more interested in titillating audiences back home than in accurately portraying plural marriage." Keystone uses this to puncture a sensationalized Scandinavian "trafficking" meme — see [[The Scandinavian Trafficking Claim Is Sensationalized Folklore]].[^6]
- **The one witness who knew real harems found Brigham's household Victorian.** Richard Burton — author of *Narrative of a Pilgrimage to Mecca and El-Medina*, who had masqueraded as a Muslim and knew actual Middle Eastern polygamy first-hand — made a "pilgrimage" to Utah hoping for the great seraglio of Brigham Young. He was *disappointed*: "It was downright Victorian." A very acute observer who knew both worlds "didn't see any resemblance at all" between Latter-day Saint plural marriage and Arabian harems.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "The fact is Brigham Young does not come across as much of a romantic, really... a lot of the women that Brigham married, it was to sort of give them status... in an age where women didn't really have much legal status, that's one of the ways of doing it." [^3]

> "He makes a pilgrimage to the Great Basin to visit the great seraglio of Brigham Young. He was disappointed. It wasn't nearly as sensuous... as he'd hoped it would be. It was downright Victorian." [^5]

> "She's a grifter, I think, you know." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This defends the integrity of plural marriage as practiced under Brigham Young against a caricature that is both historically false and rooted in 19th-century racial-religious prejudice. The same Orientalizing smear was used against Joseph Smith and the Prophet Muhammad alike — see [[Joseph-Muhammad Comparison as Islamophobic Smear]] — and the "judge a 19th-century man by 21st-century romantic norms" error is a species of presentism addressed in [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].
