#type/claim

# Trauma Victims Reconstruct Crime-Scene Gaps So Survivor Accounts Vary

## The Claim
[[Shannon Tracy]] invokes a standard forensic principle to handle the conflicting survivor testimony from Carthage: **the people most involved in a violent event are among the worst sources for reconstructing it.** Caught in the trauma, they remember discrete facts ("I got shot here, there") but have *voids* between them, and they later "fill in" the gaps — sometimes adopting details others suggested. He applies this to both [[John Taylor]] (whose "bullet hit my watch" detail appears only later) and [[Willard Richards]] (who was trapped behind the door, could not have seen everything, and reconstructs the rest). This explains discrepancies *without* implying anyone lied.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **It matches real eyewitness science.** Memory for traumatic events is fragmentary and reconstructive, and post-event suggestion can implant or reshape details — a well-established finding in eyewitness-memory research. Using it to interpret 1844 testimony is methodologically appropriate. (Evidence rating: strong as a general principle.)[^1]
2. **It dissolves a common anti-Mormon move.** Critics have argued that because [[Hyrum Smith]]'s head wound would have severed nerves "immediately," he could not have said "I'm a dead man," so the account must be fabricated. Tracy's framework answers that survivors (here Richards relaying it) reconstruct the scene afterward, so a slightly-off detail is expected human memory, not proof of a cover-up. (Evidence rating: moderate — it neutralizes the inference from one discrepancy to wholesale fabrication.)[^2]
3. **Richards's blocked sightline is concrete.** Tracy reasons the door swung out and "trapped him behind the back," so there were moments Richards physically could not see and later filled in — a specific, checkable claim a scene reconstruction could test. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The principle cuts both ways and can become unfalsifiable: if every inconvenient survivor detail is "trauma reconstruction" while every convenient one is reliable memory, the analyst can keep whichever facts suit the desired reconstruction. A critic can fairly ask for a consistent rule for *which* recollections to trust. And invoking memory-fallibility to rescue the martyrdom narrative is the mirror image of critics invoking it to *doubt* the witnesses — the tool is neutral. The defensible core is simply that variation among Carthage survivor accounts is the normal signature of traumatic eyewitness memory, so discrepancies alone do not establish fabrication on either side; they have to be adjudicated against the physical evidence.

## Best Quotes
> "The worst people to get information from are those who were involved with the crime scene. They will remember certain things... but then they have to try to put it together — there's a void, and they put that together."[^1]

> "I think the door came out and trapped him behind the back, so there are probably moments he didn't see, but he fills them in as he remembers later on."[^2]
