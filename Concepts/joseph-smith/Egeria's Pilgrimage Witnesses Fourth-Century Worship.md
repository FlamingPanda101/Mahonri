# Egeria's Pilgrimage Witnesses Fourth-Century Worship

## The Point
[[Egeria]], a noblewoman from the western Roman Empire (modern Spain or southern France), made a roughly three-thousand-mile pilgrimage through the Christian East in AD 381–384 and left one of the most engaging firsthand accounts of late-fourth-century Christian worship — including the abundance of Sunday preaching, the practice of presbyters preaching alongside bishops, and the intensive catechetical instruction at Jerusalem.

## Evidence
1. **The journey.** Over AD 381–384 Egeria travelled from Sinai to Jerusalem, then to Mount Nebo, Antioch, Edessa, Harran, and to Constantinople via the shrines of Thecla and Euphemia. The account's second part returns to Jerusalem to describe the worship services there. [^1]
2. **Abundant Sunday preaching.** The first thing Egeria noticed in Jerusalem was "a greater abundance of preaching on Sunday … than she was used to." (Wilkinson infers she was used to less preaching at home.) [^2]
3. **Presbyters preach alongside bishops.** "You should note that here it is usual for any presbyter who has taken his seat to preach, if he so wishes, and when they have finished there is a sermon from the bishop." Heal speculates the "if he so wishes" may preserve "a memory of the ancient order in which people preached as they were moved upon by the Spirit (see 1 Corinthians 14:26–31)." [^2]
4. **The purpose of preaching.** Egeria reports its object plainly: "to make sure that the people will continually be learning about the Bible and the love of God" and "will continue to learn God's Law." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Here it is usual for any presbyter who has taken his seat to preach, if he so wishes, and when they have finished there is a sermon from the bishop." (Egeria, quoted) [^2]

> "The object of having this preaching every Sunday is to make sure that the people will continually be learning about the Bible and the love of God." (Egeria, quoted) [^3]

## LDS Connection
Heal's reading of Egeria's "if he so wishes" as a residue of charismatic, Spirit-moved preaching (1 Corinthians 14:26–31) gestures toward an ancient pattern of inspired lay/clergy preaching that resonates with Latter-day Saint expectations of Spirit-led speaking in meetings.
