# The Rediscovered Papyri Are Evidence Against the Book of Abraham

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that, beyond the *lack* of evidence for the Book of Mormon, the Book of Abraham supplies positive **evidence against** [[Joseph Smith]]'s prophetic claims. The Book of Abraham reproduces three facsimiles from an Egyptian papyrus Smith bought in 1835 and said he *translated*. Egyptologists judged his facsimile interpretations wrong; the Church (1913) replied that critics had only the facsimiles, not the originals — but the originals, thought lost in the 1871 Chicago fire, resurfaced in 1966 at the Metropolitan Museum. Both Mormon and non-Mormon Egyptologists now conclude the papyri are ordinary Egyptian funerary texts (the "Book of Breathings," buried with a priest named Hor, c. 2nd century BC), saying nothing about Abraham; the figure Smith labeled Abraham is Osiris, the one he called Pharaoh is Isis. Horn rejects the apologetic that Smith merely *received a revelation* while looking at the papyri, because Smith's own diary says he "translated the original Egyptian," and the Egyptian Alphabet & Grammar shows he was attempting a real translation.[^1] He also plays a clip of [[James White]] pressing an LDS apologist, who repeatedly answers only "God can work miracles."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Strong, factual)** The identification of the surviving Joseph Smith Papyri as a Book of Breathings unrelated to Abraham is the mainstream Egyptological conclusion, conceded by LDS Egyptologists.[^1]
2. **(Strong, against the catalyst theory)** Horn's best point is documentary: Smith's diary language of "translating" and the Kirtland Egyptian Papers cut against a purely revelatory ("catalyst") account.[^1] This is the crux the LDS reply must meet — see [[The Catalyst Theory Is Grounded in How Joseph Translated Not a Post-Hoc Rescue]].
3. **(Weak as argument)** The White clip functions rhetorically; "God can work miracles" is a poor on-the-spot answer, but a bad apologist's stumble is not itself disproof.[^2]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Latter-day Saint reply runs on several tracks. (1) **We do not have the source papyrus.** Eyewitnesses described a long scroll; only fragments survive, so the surviving Book of Breathings may not be what Smith worked from ([[We Possess Only a Fraction of Joseph Smith's Papyri]], [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]], [[Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire]]). (2) **"Translate" did not mean philological decoding** for Joseph Smith — it meant conveying a revealed text, as with the Book of Mormon and the JST ([[Translation Means to Convey the Mind Not Decode a Language]], [[The Catalyst Theory Is Grounded in How Joseph Translated Not a Post-Hoc Rescue]]). (3) **A provenance/content double standard** is in play: the Book of Abraham is judged by the surviving fragments' provenance while ignoring content hits ([[The Book of Abraham Debate Plays a Provenance vs Content Double Standard]]). (4) **Content the text gets right** that Joseph could not have known in 1842 — the place-name **Olishem**, the name **Shinehah** for the sun, Sobek/Pharaoh, and later-corroborated Abrahamic apocrypha ([[Olishem Is a Linguistic Geographical and Chronological Match]], [[Later Texts Corroborate Details Joseph Smith Could Not Have Known in the Book of Abraham]]). (5) The facsimiles plausibly encode a temple/endowment pattern through the Osiris myth rather than a literal Egyptological caption ([[The Facsimiles Encode Abraham's Endowment Through the Osiris Myth]]).

## Counter-objection
Horn's strongest lever is the Egyptian Alphabet & Grammar and the diary's "translated" language, which strain the missing-scroll defense: if the Kirtland Egyptian Papers map specific characters to specific English, then Smith *did* treat the fragments as the source. The LDS counter is that the Kirtland Egyptian Papers are a derivative cipher *project* — scribes reverse-engineering an alphabet *after* the revelation — not the translation mechanism itself ([[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are Not the Source of the Translation]], [[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool]]), and that no single theory yet accounts for all the evidence ([[No Theory Yet Accounts for All the Kirtland Egyptian Papers Evidence]]). On the merits this remains the most evidentially live of Horn's three reasons: the LDS position depends on (a) the genuinely missing long scroll and (b) a non-philological sense of "translate," both defensible but contested. Where Horn overreaches is in calling it one of the *clearest* evidences of fraud while dismissing the content hits (Olishem, Shinehah) as coincidence — a provenance-only standard he would not apply symmetrically to disputed biblical texts.

## Apologetic Engagement
Unlike the geography point, this is a content-and-provenance fight where Horn has real documentary ammunition. The LDS engagement should concede that the surviving fragments are a Book of Breathings, then contest (a) whether they are the source and (b) the meaning of "translation," while pressing the corroborated content the critic must explain. The relevant clusters are the catalyst-theory, missing-papyri, and Olishem/Shinehah notes.

## LDS Relevance
This is the Book-of-Abraham half of Horn's "Reason 2," paired with [[The Book of Mormon Has No Map and No Confirmed Geography]]. It is the place where the Restoration's reply is most method-dependent (what "translate" means), so it doubles as a case study in [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]].
