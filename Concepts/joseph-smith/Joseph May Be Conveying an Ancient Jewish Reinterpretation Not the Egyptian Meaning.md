# Joseph May Be Conveying an Ancient Jewish Reinterpretation, Not the Egyptian Meaning

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The standard facsimile attack runs: "Joseph says this figure means X; Egyptologists say it means Y; therefore Joseph is wrong." [[Kerry Muhlestein]] argues this conceals a hidden assumption — that Joseph was reporting how *the average ancient Egyptian* would read the vignette. But the papyri were owned ~200 BC by a Theban priest in a milieu that demonstrably *blended* Jewish and Egyptian religious traditions, so Joseph may be conveying what these figures meant within an *ancient Jewish reinterpretation* of Egyptian iconography — exactly the kind of reuse the period attests.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. There are no ancient Egyptians to ask — only Egyptologists reconstructing
Muhlestein stresses a double disconnect: critics say "Egyptologists say it means Y," but mean "this is how the *ancient Egyptians* would have seen it" — and "there aren't any ancient Egyptians to ask." On top of that, "we don't know [if] Joseph Smith [is] telling us how *all* ancient Egyptians would have seen this, [or] how ancient Jews would have reinterpreted Egyptian drawings — because we know they did." The whole objection presumes an answer to a question we cannot settle.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the epistemic gap (no living informants; uncertain authorial intent) is real and rarely acknowledged by the sound-bite version.

### 2. Theban priests c. 200 BC were syncretizing Jewish material
Around the date of these papyri, Theban priests "were collecting stories from Greece, from Jews and from other places... and incorporating them into their own religious beliefs and practices" — the two largest non-Egyptian populations in Egypt being Greeks and Jews. There are spells where, to invoke God on a high mountain, "you become Moses," and demonstrable use of Abraham in their rituals. So a reinterpreted, Abraham-laden reading of Egyptian vignettes is not anachronistic for this setting (cf. [[Egyptians Mixed Jewish and Egyptian Religious Iconography]]).[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — Greco-Roman-Egyptian magical/ritual syncretism (incl. Jewish names) is well documented; the specific Abraham usages are published.

### 3. The papyri's owner is exactly who would own a reinterpreted Abrahamic text
The fragments belonged to a Theban priest, Hor (a priest of Amun-Re, Min, and Khonsu, in Karnak), in a region with "a sizable population of Jews" collecting Judaic tradition. "If the book of Abraham is what we understand it to be, a person like Hor is exactly the person we would expect to own it." So the figures may carry the meaning *Hor's* syncretizing circle gave them, which Joseph reports.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the owner-identification is from the papyri; "exactly who we'd expect" is a plausibility argument, not proof the scroll carried Abraham.

### 4. The Abraham–Osiris substitution is the model case (Jesus did it too)
The clearest example that Jews reinterpreted Egyptian figures is biblical: Jesus reworked the Egyptian "bosom of Osiris" into "bosom of Abraham" ([[Luke 16.22|Luke 16:22]]); stelae sometimes label the throned Osiris-figure "Abraham." So when Joseph reads such a figure in Abrahamic terms, he is doing what the sources show ancient Jews *did* (see [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]]).[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the Setne/Luke 16 Osiris→Abraham link is a recognized scholarly hypothesis; it directly models the reinterpretation Muhlestein invokes.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

[[Robert Ritner]] and mainstream Egyptology reply that this is a *post-hoc rescue*: the surviving material is an ordinary Greco-Roman funerary document (Book of Breathings), its figures have conventional meanings, and "some Jews *sometimes* reinterpreted Egyptian iconography" does not show that *this* priest gave *these* figures an Abrahamic meaning, still less that Joseph accurately recovered it. The Abraham-Osiris parallel is a literary-background hypothesis, not evidence that an Osiris-as-Abraham tradition sat on Hor's scroll. The fair LDS position is that this dissolves the *certainty* of the "Joseph is simply wrong" charge — by showing the objection assumes an unprovable claim about authorial intent and ignores a documented reinterpretive culture — without itself proving the facsimiles carry the meanings Joseph assigned. It converts a confident disproof into an open question, which is Muhlestein's actual aim (see [[Questioning Assumptions Is the First Defense of the Book of Abraham]]).
