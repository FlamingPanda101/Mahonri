# Joseph Smith Was Never Charged With Necromancy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Mark Gagnon]] states that in 1826 Joseph Smith was "arrested in Chenango County and charged with practicing necromancy," and ties the charge to his deceased brother [[Alvin Smith]] (rumors that Alvin's body was needed to access the plates).[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Enemies used the word, but it was never a charge
[[Keystone]]: "Did enemies of Joseph Smith call him a necromancer? Yes, absolutely. They called him a lot of things. Was Joseph ever arrested and charged with necromancy? As far as I've been able to discover, no." Gagnon "doesn't cite a source," did not provide one when asked, and an expert Keystone consulted "had never heard this claim."[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — an argument from the absence of any documentary charge; Keystone went to the trouble of asking Gagnon and an expert.

### 2. Necromancy was likely not even a chargeable offense
"I don't even think you could specifically be charged with necromancy at that time." The legal category Gagnon invokes does not match the statutes of 1820s New York.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — a legal-historical point; the relevant statute was the "disorderly person" law, not an anti-necromancy law.

### 3. Gagnon is conflating two separate things
Keystone reconstructs the confusion: Gagnon is probably "conflating vague necromancy rumors with Joseph's 1826 trial and charge for being a *disorderly person*" after accusations that he used treasure-digging to cheat people. That is a different matter, with an uncertain outcome (Keystone thinks acquittal). See [[Joseph Smith 1826 Trial Conviction Question]].[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the 1826 examination was over the disorderly-person statute; the necromancy framing is a category error.

### 4. The Alvin link is itself an unsourced rumor
Gagnon ties the necromancy claim specifically to Alvin. Keystone notes the relevant history is the 1824 rumor that Alvin's body "had been disturbed," which prompted Joseph Smith Sr. to *exhume Alvin and publish a newspaper notice* affirming the body was undisturbed — the opposite of necromancy. Keystone "[hasn't] been able to find" any contemporary source that Joseph needed Alvin's body for the plates.[^5] Compare [[The Alvin Smith Funeral Story Is Misremembered]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the exhumation-and-notice is documented; the "needed the body" motive is an unsourced rumor.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can argue the distinction is partly semantic: Joseph *was* hauled into a legal proceeding in 1826 connected to his folk-magical treasure-seeking, and his reputation as one who dealt in occult "seeing" is well attested, so "charged with necromancy" is a loose-but-not-baseless gloss on a real entanglement with the law over magic-adjacent practices. The disciplined reply: the *specific* claim is false — there was no necromancy charge, the statute was "disorderly person," and the Alvin connection rests on rumor — but the underlying fact that Joseph engaged in folk treasure-seeking and faced a related legal examination is genuine and not denied (see [[Joseph Smith Treasure Hunting Extent]] and [[Folk Magic Claims About the Smith Family]]).
