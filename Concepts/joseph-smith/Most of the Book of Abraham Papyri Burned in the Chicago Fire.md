# Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire

## The Claim
The standard critic's argument is: we have recovered fragments of [[Joseph Smith]]'s Egyptian papyri, Egyptologists read them as ordinary funerary texts (the Book of Breathings), they say *nothing* about Abraham — therefore Joseph's "translation" was bogus. [[Jacob Hansen]]'s rebuttal turns on one word the critics skip: *some*. The surviving fragments are only a portion of what Joseph possessed; the bulk of his papyrus collection was sold to a Chicago museum and destroyed in the Great Chicago Fire of 1871. So the recovered scraps cannot be shown to be the source text Joseph rendered, and the argument-from-the-fragments is an argument from an incomplete record.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The argument hinges on a missing premise.** Hansen's framing: "people say... 'we have' — I'm going to say *some* — 'of Joseph Smith's papyri, and they don't talk about Abraham, therefore Joseph's translation was wrong.' But you noticed I said *some*. We don't have all of the papyri that Joseph had."[^1] *Rating: a valid logical point about the burden of proof* — to indict the translation from the fragments, a critic must first show the fragments *are* the relevant source, which the destruction of the rest blocks.

2. **The historical loss is documentable.** "An enormous amount of the papyri that Joseph owned was bought by the Chicago Museum and was burned in the Great Chicago Fire of the 1870s."[^1] *Rating: substantially historical* — a large part of the original collection did pass into a Chicago museum and was lost; the surviving fragments (the "Joseph Smith Papyri" rediscovered in 1967) are a subset, not the whole. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] specifies that Joseph owned a "larger roll and a shorter roll and a bunch of fragments," that "the larger roll and the shorter roll were destroyed in the Great Chicago Fire," and that the witnesses describe him translating from the (now-burned) larger roll — leaving only the fragments, which are *not* the source of the Book of Abraham.[^3]

3. **The companion "catalyst theory."** Hansen pairs this with the catalyst model — Joseph received genuine revelation *about* Abraham that he believed was tied to the papyri, and may simply have been mistaken about which physical scroll carried it; the revelation stands even if the connection to a given fragment fails. See [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]] and [[The Book of Abraham Has Only Three Apologetic Exits - Reel]]. *Rating: a fallback that survives even if the missing-scroll defense is granted away.*

4. **He concedes the genuine defeater.** Hansen does not claim immunity: he grants that *if* it turned out Joseph rendered a demonstrably false translation, "yes, that would be a big problem."[^2] The argument is therefore narrow — it denies that the *current fragments* prove that, not that the Book of Abraham is unfalsifiable.

**Steelman of the critic.** The missing-scroll defense is weaker than it sounds for the *facsimiles*: Facsimiles 1–3 are reproduced in the Book of Abraham itself, are demonstrably from the surviving Book of Breathings material, and Egyptologists read Joseph's explanations of them as incorrect — so the critic can press the case on the facsimiles without needing the lost scrolls at all. A critic can also note that the "missing length" theory has been challenged on physical grounds (mounting and scroll-length estimates). The honest LDS reply shifts weight onto the text-anticipation case ([[Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842]]) and the fruits ([[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]]) rather than resting on the fire alone.

## LDS Connection
This is a burden-of-proof move within the Book of Abraham debate: it does not prove the translation correct but blocks the most common disproof by denying that the recovered fragments are the demonstrated source. It functions as the historical premise underneath the catalyst theory ([[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]) and is meant to push the conversation toward the *text* of the Book of Abraham, where Latter-day Saint apologists believe the stronger evidence lies ([[Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842]]).
