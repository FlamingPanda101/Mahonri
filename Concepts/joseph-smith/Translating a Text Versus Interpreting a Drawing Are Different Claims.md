# Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims

#type/distinction

## The Claim

Egyptologist [[Kerry Muhlestein]] (on [[Ward Radio]]) draws a methodological distinction that defuses the second major attack on the [[Book of Abraham]] — the charge that Joseph **interpreted the facsimile drawings wrongly**. Muhlestein insists that **translating a text** (rendering the meaning of written characters) and **interpreting a drawing** (explaining what a picture depicts) are *different claims* with different standards. Egyptians deliberately loaded their drawings with **multiple meanings**, so a non-literal or layered reading of a vignette is not a "mistranslation" — and, in fact, some of Joseph's facsimile identifications *do* match what Egyptologists now say.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Texts and drawings are evaluated differently — rate: moderate (method)
A claim about the meaning of a hieroglyphic *sentence* can be checked against grammar and lexicon; a claim about what a *scene* represents is interpretive and admits more than one valid reading. Critics who grade Joseph's facsimile explanations as if they were failed *text* translations apply the wrong yardstick. This is a category distinction, not special pleading.[^1]

### 2. Egyptian iconography is intentionally multivalent — rate: moderate (Egyptology)
Muhlestein (a credentialed Egyptologist) stresses that Egyptian religious art was designed to carry several meanings at once across a 3,000-year, symbol-laden tradition. A given vignette could legitimately be "about" more than one thing, so divergence from the single most common scholarly caption is not automatically an error.[^1]

### 3. Some of Joseph's readings match Egyptology — rate: moderate (convergence)
On Muhlestein's account, several of Joseph's facsimile identifications align with what Egyptologists independently conclude — which a critic must explain away, and which the vault discusses (including [[John Gee]]'s hypocephalus case where the experts were wrong) in [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]]. Combined with the separate missing-scroll point ([[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]) and the prophet-vs-academic distinction ([[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]), the "he got the pictures wrong" attack loses force.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can press back. (a) Joseph did not present the facsimile explanations as loose impressions — he gave **specific identifications** (naming figures, deities, and meanings), some of which mainstream Egyptology rejects, so "it's only interpretation" can look like a retreat from the confidence of the original claims. (b) "Egyptian art is multivalent" can be stretched to immunize *any* reading from falsification, which would make the claim unfalsifiable. (c) The few apparent matches may be coincidental or selectively highlighted. What survives is the legitimate core distinction: judging a *drawing* interpretation by the standards of *text* translation is a methodological error, and Egyptian iconography genuinely is multivalent — even if that distinction limits, rather than positively confirms, the truth of Joseph's specific readings.

## Best Quote
> Translating a text and interpreting a drawing are not the same claim — and Egyptians put multiple meanings into their drawings. — [[Kerry Muhlestein]] *(paraphrase)* [^1]
