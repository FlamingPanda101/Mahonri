#type/claim

# The Fuller Picture of Joseph Smith Undercuts the Self-Conscious-Fraud Model

## The Claim
The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] — who does not believe Joseph Smith was a prophet — nonetheless concludes that the **self-conscious fraud / con-man model is a poor explanation** of him. Having read Richard Bushman's biography *Rough Stone Rolling* (generally regarded as the best biography), Green says the fuller picture of Joseph's day-to-day life shows "a true believer who's flawed like anybody else," not a calculating impostor — and that "I'm actually not alone among skeptics who take that view."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A skeptic's verdict against the fraud model (rate: moderate-to-strong, hostile-witness).** "When you get a full picture of his life and his day-to-day activities and the way that he just operated, I just don't see how you can believe that he was a self-conscious fraud and con man ... the picture that emerges is like a true believer who's flawed." Coming from a non-Mormon agnostic, this is a hostile-witness concession. See the parallel argument in [[The Holy Rebellion - The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man]].[^1]

2. **Flawed like Paul, not uniquely villainous (rate: moderate).** Green: Joseph was "flawed like the Apostle Paul was flawed — actually a lot less flawed than the Apostle Paul" (Paul "went around murdering religious minorities"; "Joseph Smith's crime is that he was horny"). Character attacks "never really do anything for me."[^2]

3. **Some evidence resists pure-fabrication (rate: moderate).** Green grants "a non-zero amount of evidence that he was actually doing what he thought he was doing" — that the Book of Mormon "didn't just purely come from Joseph Smith's head" — which he found "surprising ... not what I would expect" as a non-Mormon.[^3]

4. **"Convenient" moments are real but not decisive (rate: balanced).** Green is candid that parts of Joseph's story "sure are convenient" for him (plural-marriage revelations; the 116-pages command to translate a different record), and that genuine anachronisms remain. But he notes apologists have "not totally implausible" responses and that the anachronism list has been *shrinking*, not growing — see [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]].[^4]

5. **Critics' theory has no stable form (rate: framing).** Green's even-handedness illustrates why the unified "Joseph the con man" narrative struggles: the fuller the biography, the less the fraud model fits. See [[The Critical Theory of Joseph Smith Has No Stable Form]] and [[Joseph Smith's Family Believed Him Against the Long-Con Theory]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
"Not a self-conscious fraud" is not "therefore a true prophet" — it leaves wide middle ground. (a) The **sincere-but-mistaken** option (Joseph genuinely believed he received revelation that did not, in fact, originate with God) is fully compatible with everything Green says and is the default secular reading; it grants his good faith while denying the supernatural claim. (b) Sincerity and self-deception can coexist with *some* knowing embellishment, especially around polygamy and the "convenient" revelations Green himself flags. (c) *Rough Stone Rolling* is a believing scholar's work, however excellent; "best biography" does not make its sympathetic portrait the only defensible read. (d) "A non-zero amount of evidence" is, by Green's own framing, still outweighed for him by the problem of evil — he remains a non-believer. So this concept rebuts the *crude* con-man caricature, not the serious naturalistic case, which simply relocates to "sincere visionary, no real plates."

## Best Quotes

> "When you get a full picture of his life ... I just don't see how you can believe that he was a self-conscious fraud and con man. The picture that emerges is like a true believer who's flawed like anybody else."[^1]
