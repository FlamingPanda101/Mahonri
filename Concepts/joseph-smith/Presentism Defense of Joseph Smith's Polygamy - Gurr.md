# Presentism Defense of Joseph Smith's Polygamy - Gurr

## The Point
**Claim ([[Robert Gurr]]):** Judging [[Joseph Smith]] by 21st-century marriage norms is the fallacy of *presentism*. Smith is "a product of his time period" — a colonial/frontier America with a ~35-year life expectancy where early marriage was common and not unusual, especially in the southern colonies. Crucially, Gurr argues that era "did not view marriage as this sexual union" we assume today; many of Joseph's sealings were non-consummated arrangements in which he took young women in as "wards" — "basically adoptions, basically taking on a young girl as another daughter." He concedes sexual relations in "a handful... less than 10" cases, and cites a peer-reviewed history of U.S. child marriage to argue "no universal appropriate age existed" and marriage could be an exit from abusive homes for girls who couldn't hold property.[^1]

**Rebuttal ([[Bill Reel]]):** Reel grants the historical point in the abstract — there were indeed times and places that normalized such marriages — but refuses the moral conclusion. A 14-year-old's frontal cortex "can't possibly be fully developed until 25 or older," so a child "religiously coerced by theology and people abusing their authority" into an intimate relationship is "wrong, wrong, and wrong... no matter what century." He charges Gurr with "using the exception" (the minority of non-sexual marriages) to launder the pattern, and presses the theological cost: an eternal God whose law "just goes along with whatever the cultural mores" are is incoherent — "God could rise above slavery and teach that slavery's wrong all the time."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Presentism is a legitimate principle (rated: moderate, real but limited).** The vault endorses the anti-anachronism caution in [[Presentism in Historical Judgment]] and [[Presentism in Folk Belief Judgment]]: don't read modern categories back onto a different era. Gurr's life-expectancy and property-law context, and the cited child-marriage scholarship, are genuine historical considerations, not invented.[^1]
2. **The "ward / non-consummated" claim is the weak joint (rated: weak under Reel's cross-examination).** Reel's reply that Gurr is "using the exception" is strong: a pattern of pursuing "husband-wife structures" most reasonably implies sexuality, and 19th-century reticence explains the documentary silence — see the fuller treatment in [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel]]. Gurr's own on-camera concession (sexual relations in up to ~10 cases; "yes, he did have sexual relations with a few of them") undercuts the adoption framing for those cases.[^1]
3. **The eternal-law objection is the deepest problem (rated: strong against the defense as stated).** Presentism answers "don't judge frontier *culture* anachronistically," but it does not answer "why does an *eternal* God's revealed law track the shifting moral fashions of 1840s America?" Reel folds this into his larger thesis that LDS moral teaching "constantly shifts" (polygamy → monogamous celestial marriage; the priesthood/temple ban) — see [[The Bible Was Wrong and Consensus Determines Morality]]. *Steelman of Gurr:* on the narrow factual claim about Helen Mar Kimball (14), the absence of evidence of sexual relations is real, so a blanket "all predatory" charge overreaches on that single case.

## Best Quotes
> "Joseph Smith is a product of his time period... they did not view marriage as this sexual union... Joseph Smith used most of his marriages as wards... basically adoptions, basically taking on a young girl as another daughter." [^1]

> "17 in that time period is not a problem... we can't judge them as a people based on our modern-day morality. That's called presentism. And it's a fallacy." [^1]

> "I'm never going to justify a child with an underdeveloped frontal cortex being manipulated into a relationship with an older man who's using religious coercion... no matter what millennium." — Reel [^2]

> "God could rise above slavery and teach that slavery's wrong all the time." — Reel [^2]

## LDS Connection
This note isolates Gurr's *affirmative* defense so it can be weighed on its own merits against [[Joseph Smith's Polygamy as a Predatory Pattern - Reel]]. The defensible kernel — historians should not anachronistically import modern marriage norms ([[Presentism in Historical Judgment]]) — is a tool the vault uses elsewhere. But the debate exposes its limits: presentism cannot carry the weight of an *eternal* moral law that conveniently mirrors frontier custom, and the "non-consummated ward" framing collapses under Gurr's own admission of sexual relationships. A candid LDS treatment keeps the historiographical caution while declining Gurr's on-camera comfort with religiously-coerced teen marriage, which Reel turns to decisive rhetorical effect.
