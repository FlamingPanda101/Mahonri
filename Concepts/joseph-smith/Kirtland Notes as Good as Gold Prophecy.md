# Kirtland Notes as Good as Gold Prophecy

## The Claim
[[Joseph Smith]] reportedly prophesied that the Kirtland Safety Society notes "would one day be as good as gold." Per the journal history of [[Brigham Young]], when the Saints reached the Salt Lake area in 1847 and needed currency, Mormon Battalion gold dust proved inconvenient to coin, so the Kirtland notes were re-signed and placed "on par with gold," fulfilling the prophecy.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two readings of "as good as gold."** The Salt Lake re-signing is one; the host adds that today Kirtland notes "are worth a lot of money" as collectibles, "some people view that as a fulfillment." (Evidence rating: the elastic phrase admits multiple post-hoc fits, which weakens it.)[^2]
2. **The host is unimpressed.** "I don't really find that too impressive a fulfilled prophecy." (Evidence rating: proponent concession.)[^2]
3. **Self-fulfillment.** Brigham Young's own administration chose to re-circulate the notes at par — the fulfillment was an internal church decision, not an external vindication. See [[Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Objection]]. (Evidence rating: strong deflation.)[^1]
4. **Contemporary expectation mismatch.** The host notes hearers at the time would have read "as good as gold" as "the bank will be successful" — which it was not — so the later re-signing/collectible readings are reinterpretations to salvage the phrase. (Evidence rating: moderate; suggests retrofitting of meaning.)[^2]
5. **Steelman of the believing view.** That defunct notes were later honored at par by the church, and are now genuinely valuable, gives the saying a literal-enough fulfillment for a believer. But because the church controlled the re-signing and the phrase is elastic, the host rates it among the weaker items.
