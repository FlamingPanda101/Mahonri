# The Oliver Cowdery Affair Letter Actually Says Scrape Not Affair

## The Claim

The single most-cited source on [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Fanny Alger]] is an 1838 letter by [[Oliver Cowdery]] to his brother, quoted on critical websites as referring to "a dirty, nasty, filthy **affair** of his and Fanny Alger's." Because Oliver was close to Joseph and (the argument runs) called it an "affair," critics treat this as near-contemporary proof that the relationship was *adultery*, not plural marriage. [[Don Bradley]] argues the quotation is a **misquote** on two independent levels.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The original word is "scrape," not "affair" — rate: strong (manuscript forensics)
We do not have Oliver's original; we have a letterbook copy made by his nephew (the original is at the Huntington Library, with a microfilm at the LDS Church History Library). Examining the copy, Bradley noticed the word "affair" is written *over another word* — darker ink, visible underlying letters (an "s," a "c"). Forensic imaging of the Huntington original (obtained via Chris Smith and [[Brian Hales]]) showed the underlying word is **"scrape."** Oliver actually wrote "dirty, nasty, filthy scrape."[^2]

### 2. "Scrape" was 1830s slang for a mess/difficulty — rate: strong (period lexicon)
The 1828/1838 Webster's defines "scrape" as "a difficulty, perplexity, distress... that which harasses," and labels it a "low word" (slang). Oliver was saying Joseph and Fanny were *in a jam / a hot mess* — expressing moral disapproval ("dirty, nasty, filthy") without specifying the *kind* of relationship. The nephew, copying, replaced the slang "scrape" with the politer, vaguer "affair" to make his uncle sound more educated.[^3]

### 3. "Affair" did not mean "extramarital sex" in the 1830s — rate: strong (period lexicon)
Even the substituted word does not carry the modern meaning. Webster's defines "affair" as "business of any kind; that which is done or is to be done" — "a word of vague and indefinite signification." The sense "extramarital sexual relationship" does not appear in the literature of the 1830s; it develops only toward the end of the 1800s. So the nephew was writing "this dirty, nasty, filthy *thing*," not alleging adultery.[^4]

### 4. Oliver opposed polygamy, so "dirty nasty filthy" fits either reading — rate: moderate (interpretation)
Bradley grants Josh Galey's point that "either way it doesn't look good." But because Oliver is independently known to have *disapproved of polygamy*, his calling the relationship "dirty, nasty, filthy" is exactly what we'd expect *whether* it was adultery or plural marriage — so the moralizing adjectives cannot decide between the two. The word that was supposed to decide it ("affair") turns out not to say what critics claim.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The skeptic still has real cards. (a) "Scrape" remains a strongly *negative* word; whatever happened, Oliver — a first-rank insider — regarded it as shameful, which sits awkwardly with a divinely sanctioned ordinance. (b) The forensic reading of a single overwritten word, while persuasive, is the kind of fine-grained manuscript judgment reasonable experts could contest, and Bradley is an interested party. (c) Even on Bradley's reading, the letter does not *say* "plural marriage" either — it is compatible with adultery; the move from "scrape" to "this was polygamy" requires the *other* evidence (the 1835 article, the High Council minutes, the later testimonies), not this letter. (d) The "affair-means-something-vague" point cuts both ways: a vague word is weak evidence for *any* specific reconstruction. So the honest conclusion is narrow but real: the letter does not establish *adultery*, and the popular "even Oliver called it an affair" gotcha is a genuine mistranslation — but the letter alone does not prove plural marriage either.
