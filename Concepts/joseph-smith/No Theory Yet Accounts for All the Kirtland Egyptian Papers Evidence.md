# No Theory Yet Accounts for All the Kirtland Egyptian Papers Evidence

## The Claim

The Kirtland Egyptian Papers (the Egyptian Alphabet documents and the *Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language*) are genuinely hard to interpret, and [[Kerry Muhlestein]]'s honest verdict is that **no current theory — LDS or critical — accounts for all the evidence**. After detailed analysis he abandoned his own earlier "reverse-translation" model; the critics' "Joseph built a grammar then translated with it" model is mathematically dead (only ~2% character correlation); and the only proposal that fits everything (McKay & Belnap's "wrestling with the natures of beings") is, frustratingly, neither provable nor disprovable. The intellectually honest position is "we don't know what's going on with them — but it's none of the things we thought."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "grammar-first" critical model is impossible on its face
The long-standing anti-Mormon claim is that Joseph "creates a grammar and then translates the Book of Abraham with it." But "who creates a grammar and then translates with it? You always get a translation and *then* you create a grammar — it's impossible to do it the other way." Real philology runs translation→grammar, not grammar→translation.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the methodological direction is a basic fact of how decipherment works.

### 2. The character correlation is below chance (~2%)
Muhlestein's quantitative test: if the grammar/alphabet were used to translate the Book of Abraham, the characters in the grammar should match the characters beside the corresponding Book-of-Abraham text. The measured correlation is "like a 2% correlation... less than what you would expect by happenstance." "It is mathematically demonstrable that he didn't use the grammar to translate the Book of Abraham manuscripts that we have."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a concrete, falsifiable quantitative result against the translation-tool reading (converges with the cipher analysis in [[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool]]).

### 3. Most characters aren't Egyptian, and Joseph's role was peripheral
The documents mix in characters that are not Egyptian — some from Book-of-Mormon characters, some invented — that the makers "know aren't Egyptian." The driving force was W. W. Phelps (an amateur linguist working on a "pure language" project), and Joseph "doesn't pay as much attention... he's skipping things"; the work began ~October 1, 1835 and continued under Phelps (and later Warren Parrish) while Joseph "gets busy with other things." Statements next to such characters cannot be "what these characters mean" — "there's something else going on."[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the non-Egyptian characters and Phelps's lead role are documented; "something else going on" is the honest residue.

### 4. The only fitting theory is unprovable
Muhlestein abandoned his reverse-translation theory; "even some of the anti-Mormons... have had to abandon" theirs. The one proposal that "can account for all the evidence" is Mike McKay and Dan Belnap's: the men were doing an *ontology* — "wrestling with the natures of beings," drawing on the degrees-of-glory of [[D&C 76]] and some Book-of-Abraham priesthood language. "There's nothing that contradicts it; there's nothing that proves it." Best available, but "we'd actually have to be mind readers to know."[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a candid statement of the state of play; its honesty is the point, not a positive proof.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can grant every negative result and still press: the *intent* of the participants looks like decipherment — they put Egyptian-looking characters in margins beside English text and titled the project a "grammar," which is what you build when you think you're cracking a language. "We don't know what they were doing" can sound like a retreat from the most natural reading (an attempted, failed translation) precisely because that reading is embarrassing. And appealing to an unfalsifiable "ontology" proposal is no better than the apologetic uses of unfalsifiability the defender elsewhere criticizes. The strongest LDS answer is that the *negative* findings are robust and independent of any positive theory: the ~2% correlation and the non-Egyptian/invented characters rule out the translation-tool reading *regardless* of what the documents positively were, so the critic's "this proves Joseph faked a translation" overstates what the papers can show. What remains is a genuine, admitted unknown — which is a more honest place to stand than a confident disproof built on an untested assumption (see [[Questioning Assumptions Is the First Defense of the Book of Abraham]]).
