# Kirtland Safety Society Bank Failure Prophecy

## The Claim
The Kirtland Safety Society (legally styled an "anti-banking company") was established 2 November 1836. [[Joseph Smith]] reportedly said that "unless the institution was conducted on righteous principles it would not stand" (from a conference of 8 September 1837, via the *History of the Church*). The bank failed; Joseph attributed the failure chiefly to certain brethren refusing to follow counsel. A widespread national banking panic in May 1837 also collapsed many banks.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The bank did fail.** The outcome matches the conditional warning. (Evidence rating: outcome real but trivially predictable for a shaky frontier bank.)[^1]
2. **The host finds it unimpressive even as a believer.** "I don't know if I really find this too impressive a fulfilled prophecy." (Evidence rating: a notable concession from the proponent.)[^2]
3. **It is an apologetic, not evidence.** The host: it "would probably be used more as an apologetic for how to explain why the bank failed." The "righteous principles" condition means the failure is explained as human disobedience rather than divine misjudgment. See [[Apologetic vs Evidence Distinction for Prophecy]]. (Evidence rating: unfalsifiable conditional; low evidential value.)[^2]
4. **Natural causation dominates.** The 1837 national panic felled "many banks," so the Kirtland collapse needs no prophetic explanation. (Evidence rating: strong natural deflation.)[^2]
5. **Steelman of the believing view.** A founder warning that an enterprise will fail without integrity, followed by its failure amid mismanagement, can be read as moral foresight. But because the condition guarantees an explanation for either outcome, the host rightly rates it weak — "I don't find this a very remarkable prophecy."

## Best Quotes
