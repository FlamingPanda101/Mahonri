# Joseph's Seer-Stone Discovery Frames Theosis From the Start of the Restoration

#type/claim

## The Claim

Historian [[Don Bradley]] (author of *The Lost 116 Pages*), on [[Ward Radio]], argues that the very *discovery* of Joseph Smith's [[seer stone]] is wrapped in **deification (theosis) language** — which means the restoration's "become like God" theme was present from day one, not borrowed from Freemasonry in 1842. Drawing on Dr. [[William Purple]]'s notes from the 1826 hearing: the young Joseph looked into neighbor [[Sally Chase]]'s green stone, saw his **own** white stone ~150 miles away under the roots of a tree, recovered it, and reportedly said he had "acquired one of the attributes of deity, **an all-seeing eye**." Bradley reads the touched-eyes First Vision and this seer gift together as theosis from the start.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "all-seeing eye" is an explicit deity attribute — rate: moderate (primary source)
Per [[William Purple]]'s contemporaneous notes on the 1826 hearing, Joseph framed his new seeric power as having "acquired one of the attributes of deity, an all-seeing eye." Bradley's point: the language is not folk-magic neutral — it casts the gift as a *share in a divine attribute*, i.e. an early, lived instance of becoming like God.[^1]

### 2. The discovery narrative parallels the gift's later use — rate: moderate (source synthesis)
The stone Joseph finds via Sally Chase's stone becomes the instrument of the translation gift; the continuity Bradley draws is from seeing/finding (a divine "eye") to revelation. This connects the seer-stone origin to the same deification arc, rather than treating the stone as mere treasure-seeking apparatus. See [[seer stone]] and [[Joseph Smith Used Both the Interpreters and a Seer Stone]].[^1]

### 3. Theosis predates the 1842 Freemasonry borrowing — rate: moderate (chronological argument)
The episode's larger thesis (with the interpreters-as-compass-and-square and the endowment ascent-pattern material) is that the temple endowment's deification structure was already in place years before Joseph's 1842 Masonic initiation — so critics cannot reduce LDS theosis to a Masonic graft. The seer-stone "all-seeing eye" is one of the earliest data points. See [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]] and [[The JST of Psalm 14 Shows the First Vision's Two Personages Were Early]].[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

(a) The "all-seeing eye"/"attribute of deity" line comes through **Dr. Purple's recollection of an 1826 hearing**, a hostile-context, late-recorded source — so the exact wording is not bedrock. (b) A skeptic reads the whole episode as ordinary **folk-magic glass-looking** (Sally Chase was a "glass looker" too), in which "all-seeing eye" is treasure-seer braggadocio, not a theological program — reading theosis into it may be retrojection. (c) Even granting the language, a single phrase in a treasure-hunting context is thin support for a doctrinal claim about the restoration's origins. What survives is the modest version: the *vocabulary* of sharing a divine attribute attaches to Joseph's earliest seeric activity, which is at least suggestive that deification themes were native to his self-understanding well before 1842 — strengthened (not proven) by the converging endowment and First-Vision evidence Bradley marshals.
