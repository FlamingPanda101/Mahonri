# The Deeply Committed Framing Omits the Duplicity in How Joseph Practiced Polygamy

## The Claim

The LDS polygamy Q&A page acknowledges that [[Emma Smith]] mostly rejected plural marriage, briefly accepted it (allowing [[Joseph Smith]] to be sealed to four women), then rejected it again — but concludes that Emma and Joseph "remained deeply committed to one another." Historian [[Benjamin Park]] argues this framing fails **both in what it says and what it omits**. What it omits is the **duplicity**: Joseph had already been sealed to roughly two dozen women *before* he told Emma, and two of the "four" she approved were women he had *already* secretly married — requiring **mock sealing ceremonies** performed afterward to satisfy her. And the "deeply committed" conclusion does not match a record in which Emma went on an anti-polygamy "rampage" in 1844 and Joseph publicly blamed her.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Joseph was sealed to ~two dozen women before telling Emma — rate: strong (chronology of sealings)
Park stresses that by the time Joseph informed Emma of the doctrine, he had already been sealed to upwards of two dozen women. So Emma's reluctance was not merely about an abstract *doctrine* she was being asked to accept; it was about discovering how her husband had *already been practicing* it behind her. The page's sequencing obscures this.[^2]

### 2. Two of Emma's four approved wives were already secretly married — rate: strong (the mock-sealing detail)
Of the four women Emma said Joseph could be sealed to, **two had already been sealed to him**. To satisfy Emma's apparent grant of permission, a **mock ceremony** was performed afterward for those women. This is the sharpest evidence of duplicity: the ordinance Emma thought she was authorizing had, in two cases, already occurred, and a second ceremony was staged for her benefit.[^3]

### 3. The marriage was not strengthened by the conflict — rate: moderate (contested historiography)
Park grants that [[Richard Bushman]], in *Rough Stone Rolling*, argues that after fall 1843 Joseph and Emma reached an agreement (Joseph would take no more wives; Emma would stop pursuing divorce). But Park says the record is more complicated: Emma launched another anti-polygamy campaign in **March 1844**, and that spring Joseph blamed Emma and the Relief Society for "all the problems." A marriage in that state was not, as the page implies, *pulled together stronger* by the polygamy conflict.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The page's "deeply committed" line is not baseless. (a) Emma never left Joseph, remained with him to his death, and the [[First Wives Could Be Coerced by the Law of Sarah|Law of Sarah]] framework and Bushman's reconstructed fall-1843 accord show a real, if strained, marital bond — "deeply committed" can describe persistence through conflict rather than absence of conflict. (b) A Q&A page is a brief catechetical genre, not a monograph; omitting the mock-sealing detail may reflect *compression* rather than concealment, and the page does concede Emma's repeated rejection. (c) Park's "rampage/blame" reading of spring 1844 is itself an interpretation of fragmentary sources; defenders can read Emma's later loyalty (and her post-1844 stewardship of Joseph's legacy) as evidence the commitment was genuine. So Park's strongest claim is narrow and well-grounded: **the specific assertion that the relationship was strengthened, and the silence about Joseph marrying ~two dozen women before telling Emma and staging mock ceremonies, present a smoothed-over picture** — while "deeply committed," charitably read as enduring attachment despite the rupture, is not simply false.
