# The Martyrdom Was a Coordinated Political Assassination Not a Mob

## The Claim
The standard telling treats the deaths of [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Hyrum Smith]] at Carthage (27 June 1844) as a "mostly peaceful protest that went bad" — a local mob that disliked fast-growing Mormons. Shannon Tracy's panel argues the reverse: the killings were **thought-out, well-planned political assassinations** driven by fear of Joseph's combined religious, economic, and especially *political* power — including a caucus of delegates afraid he could win the presidency in 1844 or 1848.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Oaks framed it as assassination in print (rated: moderate-to-strong, scholarly).** [[Dallin H. Oaks]] opens a chapter of *Carthage Conspiracy* (with Marvin Hill): "The murders of Joseph Smith and Hyrum Smith were not random acts. They were thought-out political assassinations carried on by some of the most notable men in ... Hancock County." The panel rests heavily on this 10-year documentary study. See [[The Carthage Conspiracy Trial Records Were Sealed Until Dallin Oaks]].[^2]
2. **Documented motive: growing bloc political power (rated: moderate).** New members could vote within six months of arriving; tens of thousands gathering meant the Saints already controlled Hancock County and could plausibly control Illinois and the West. Thomas Sharp organized an explicitly **anti-Mormon political party** to counter Nauvoo's bloc voting.[^3]
3. **Pre-meditation in the press (rated: moderate).** Two weeks before the deaths, [[Thomas Sharp]]'s newspaper printed: "War and extermination is inevitable. Citizens arise one and all ... Let it be made with powder and ball" — a specific call directed at Joseph, echoing the 1838 Missouri "extermination order."[^4]
4. **The killing was an execution, not a brawl (rated: moderate).** The panel claims Joseph was shot "execution style by the well" after falling from the window, and that two days later Sharp's paper itself described the deaths — undercutting the "spontaneous riot" framing.[^5]
5. **The foreknowledge angle (rated: weak-to-moderate, theological).** The Liberty Jail promise that "thy days are known" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 122.9|D&C 122:9]]) and the "promise of 5 years" are invoked to argue Joseph knew the timing and "sealed his testimony with his blood" — see [[Joseph Smith Predicts His Own Death Within Five Years]].[^6]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The panel slides between defensible and inflated claims. (a) The **"23 delegates from 26 states" national caucus** to assassinate a presidential candidate rests on Stephen Markham's reminiscence of overhearing a meeting and is not corroborated by the trial documents the panel otherwise leans on; it is far weaker than the documented *local* political organization (the anti-Mormon party, Sharp, the Laws/Higbees). (b) Even Brian concedes "the mob itself that actually did the deed was probably more like" an angry local militia — so "assassination, not mob" is partly a false dichotomy: a locally-executed killing can have political backing without a nationwide conspiracy. (c) The "first presidential candidate assassinated" and JFK-parallel framing is rhetorical color. (d) The "$200,000 in today's money" gloss on the $500 bounty is a loose extrapolation. The honest core that survives: the deaths were **premeditated and politically motivated**, with named conspirators and prior printed intent — not a random riot. The maximalist national-conspiracy gloss is reminiscence-driven and should be held loosely.

## Best Quotes
> "The murders of Joseph Smith and Hyrum Smith were not random acts. They were thought-out political assassinations carried on by some of the most notable men in Carthage in Hancock County." — Oaks, quoted[^2]

> "War and extermination is inevitable. Citizens arise one and all ... Let it be made with powder and ball." — Thomas Sharp's paper, June 1844[^4]

> "Why Carthage? ... that was the headquarters of the caucus ... where the 23 delegates from the 26 states had met to plan the assassination of this political candidate." — Kimberly Watson Smith[^7]
