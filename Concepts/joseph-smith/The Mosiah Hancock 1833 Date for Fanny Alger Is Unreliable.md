# The Mosiah Hancock 1833 Date for Fanny Alger Is Unreliable

## The Claim

A common version of the [[Fanny Alger]] story dates the marriage to **1833**, when Fanny would have been 15, and frames it as a "wife-swap" (Levi gets Clarissa Reed; Joseph gets Fanny). The sole source for this is the late autobiography of [[Mosiah Hancock]]. [[Don Bradley]] argues this account is **full of holes** and cannot bear the weight critics place on it — and that getting the date wrong distorts the entire story.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Written ~60 years later about events before his birth — rate: strong (source criticism)
Mosiah wrote in the 1890s about events that occurred before he was born (1834). He is reconstructing, not remembering. Bradley's maxim: "much of history is just getting the events in the right order"; a source this late and this removed is a weak anchor for a precise date.[^2]

### 2. He added the "first plural marriage" claim to his dead father's autobiography — rate: strong (documentary)
[[Levi Hancock]] wrote his *own* autobiography, mentioning Fanny but *never* claiming to have performed Joseph's marriage to her. Mosiah inserted that claim after Levi died. Bradley: "If I performed the first plural marriage of the restoration, that's going in *my* autobiography — I don't need somebody else to add it after I die."[^3]

### 3. It assumes anachronistic facts — rate: moderate-to-strong (internal anachronism)
Mosiah says it was simply "assumed" that Clarissa Reed, living in Joseph's house as a servant, would *marry* Joseph. In 1833 Ohio, no one would have assumed a servant girl was destined to be a man's plural wife — that is later-polygamy thinking read back onto the 1830s. The account is "a draft" whose blanks "all have to be filled with assumption."[^4]

### 4. His stated reason for trusting his own memory is non-historical — rate: strong (self-undermining)
Mosiah anticipates the objection that he could not remember events from before his birth and answers that he *knows* his memory is reliable because "I have always kept the Word of Wisdom." Bradley: "This is not a good historical argument."[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic can press back. (a) Late and second-hand does not mean *false*: Mosiah was family, grew up among the principals, and a memoirist can preserve genuine family tradition even with garbled details — the *core* (that there was a Joseph–Fanny relationship treated as a marriage) is corroborated elsewhere, so dismissing the source wholesale is too convenient. (b) Bradley *accepts* Mosiah's testimony that it was a *marriage* while rejecting his *date* — a selective use a skeptic will note. (c) The "anachronistic assumption" critique assumes we know 1833 Ohio social expectations precisely; servant-marriage patterns were real. (d) Even granting the 1836 redating, Fanny at 18 marrying a 30-year-old already-married prophet who then sent her away remains, for many, morally troubling regardless of the year. So the redating defeats the "predatory marriage to a 14-15-year-old" framing without resolving the deeper moral and theological questions.

## Best Quotes

> "He's writing about events that happened before he was born that his father should have written about himself if they'd actually happened that way."[^3]

> "He actually even says... some people say there's no way I could remember all these things because they happened before I was born, but I know that my memory is right because I've always kept the Word of Wisdom. ...This is not a good historical argument."[^5]

> "What is this, a draft? ...all the holes here have to be filled with assumption."[^4]
