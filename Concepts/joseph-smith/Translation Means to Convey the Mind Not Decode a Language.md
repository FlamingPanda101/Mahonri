# Translation Means to Convey the Mind Not Decode a Language

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Ether's Elephant]] argues that the word **"translation"** in *Joseph Smith Translation* is being read in the wrong sense by critics. The modern default sense — converting words from one language (Greek, Hebrew) into another (English) — is not what Joseph was doing. Invoking "the most ancient meaning and definition of the word translation" as "**to convey the mind, the meaning, and understanding**," the video reframes the JST: "As a prophet, Joseph Smith was simply trying to convey the mind and meaning of what God wants us to understand."[^1] On this reading, the recurring objection — *the JST does not match the earliest manuscripts* — is an equivocation: it scores a point against language-to-language translation, a task the JST never claimed to perform. The creator openly wishes the Church "would start using different terminology" because the word invites the confusion.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The word "translation" is genuinely broader than language-swapping — rate: moderate
The English *translate* (Latin *trans-* + *latus*, "to carry across") historically extends well past linguistics: a bishop is "translated" to a new see, Enoch and Elijah were "translated" without dying, ideas are "translated" into action. The video leans on this semantic range: the question is whether God's *meaning* has been carried across, not whether two lexicons have been mapped.[^1] This dovetails with [[JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition]], where [[Dan McClellan]] grants the label "translation" works only under "a more expansive understanding… as any kind of transmission."

### 2. It dissolves the manuscript-mismatch objection — rate: strong (internal)
If "translation" means conveying intent, then "you should not be concerned that Joseph Smith's translations sometimes do not exactly match ancient manuscripts."[^3] The critic's strongest empirical card — the JST departs from the critical Greek/Hebrew text — is only damaging if the JST was *aiming* at that text. This is the same structural move as [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]: relocate the goalposts to the goal Joseph actually set.

### 3. Joseph's own practice fits conveyance, not decoding — rate: moderate
The JST was produced with an English (KJV) Bible open in front of him, asking God "basically verse by verse… what does this mean? Am I understanding this correctly? Are we missing something?"[^4] No appeal to source-language manuscripts was involved, which is exactly what a conveyance-of-meaning model predicts and a language-decoding model does not.

**Steelman of the critic:** This is the load-bearing weakness. Joseph did not merely call the work a "translation" in some archaic sense — he and the early Church used the term in contexts (the Book of Abraham facsimiles, the [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]], claims to render specific scrolls) that unmistakably invoke the *modern* language-decoding sense. A skeptic ([[Dan McClellan]]-style) will say the "ancient meaning" defense is a post-hoc semantic escape hatch: redefining a word after its modern sense produced falsifiable claims that failed. The video's own honesty here — wishing the Church used "different terminology" — concedes the term was misleading; it does not show that *Joseph* meant the archaic sense at the time. The argument neutralizes the manuscript objection only if one already accepts the conveyance model on independent (theological) grounds.
