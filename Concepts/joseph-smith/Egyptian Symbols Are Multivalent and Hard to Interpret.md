# Egyptian Symbols Are Multivalent and Hard to Interpret

## The Claim
[[Luke Hansen]] argues the 1912 Egyptologist verdicts against the facsimiles (Breasted's dismissal, Petrie's "not one single word of truth") are both a century stale **and** methodologically naive about how symbols work. Unlike translating words between known languages, interpreting *illustrations* is one of the hardest parts of Egyptology — a single symbol carries many possible meanings, and in the Greco-Roman Theban melting pot (~200 BC) Jews and others actively repurposed Egyptian art with Old Testament characters. So Joseph's readings cannot be dismissed simply because they diverge from a default Egyptological gloss.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Illustration is the hard case (rated: moderate, expert citation)
Quoting Egyptologist [[John Gee]]: "interpretation of illustrations is one of the most difficult parts of understanding Egyptian texts. A given symbol can have many different meanings and trying to decide which one the author was trying to convey is at times nearly impossible," and in many cases "we simply don't yet know what some symbols mean."[^1]

### 2. The dates and the melting pot (rated: moderate)
Most Egyptological knowledge of symbols comes from ~1200 BC, but the Joseph Smith papyri date to ~200 BC, "a time when many cultures were mixing ... Jews, Egyptians and other polytheists were very willing to assimilate new characters and gods ... including stories and characters from the Old Testament." A study of 750+ Egyptian texts found biblical figures, **Abraham and Moses most mentioned**, many from Thebes — the very provenance of Joseph's papyri.[^1]

### 3. Documented Jewish repurposing (rated: moderate)
There are scenes — lion-couch vignettes and Osiris-on-a-throne scenes — that are **labeled** as Abraham, or Abraham/Isaac/Jacob, with someone brought into Abraham's presence (paralleling Facsimile 3). Even Christ repurposes an Egyptian tale, casting Abraham in the Osiris role, in the Lazarus parable ([[Luke 16.22|Luke 16]]). So Jews adapting Egyptian drawings to Abraham is attested, not invented by Joseph.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic answers: (a) "symbols are multivalent" cuts both ways — if a vignette can mean many things, then a "hit" for Joseph's reading is *less* improbable, not more, so multivalence weakens the positive evidentiary claims too; (b) Petrie and Breasted's core point — that Joseph's captions don't match the *standard* funerary meanings — survives, since the burden is on the one asserting a non-standard reading; and (c) the "Jews repurposed this art" cases are debated as to who actually produced them ("is it an Egyptian or a Jew? I don't know," Muhlestein concedes). Hansen's own conclusion is appropriately modest: this does **not** prove Joseph gave an accurate 2nd-century-BC Theban reading — "no, it does not" — it only shows his interpretations aren't the obvious frauds the 1912 quotes imply.
