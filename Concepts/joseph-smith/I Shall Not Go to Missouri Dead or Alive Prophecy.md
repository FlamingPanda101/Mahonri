# I Shall Not Go to Missouri Dead or Alive Prophecy

## The Claim
From the *History of the Church*: on 2 January 1843, after breakfast with Judge Adams in Springfield, Illinois, [[Joseph Smith]] recorded, "I prophesied in the name of the Lord that I should not go to Missouri dead or alive." Missouri had a standing extradition interest in Joseph; he was never successfully taken back there, and he died in Illinois (Carthage) in 1844.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The outcome held.** Joseph was never extradited to Missouri and died in Illinois — neither "dead nor alive" did he return. (Evidence rating: outcome real.)[^1]
2. **Recorded in his own journal/history.** Dated to 1843, this is closer to contemporaneous than many list items, though still mediated through the *History of the Church*. (Evidence rating: relatively better source proximity.)[^1]
3. **The host's terse endorsement.** He simply quips, "Well, I don't think he did" — i.e., the prophecy held — without elaborate defense. (Evidence rating: proponent treats it as a clean, minor hit.)[^1]
4. **Steelman of the skeptic.** Joseph had strong legal and political reasons to avoid Missouri and the active aid of allies to prevent extradition; declaring he would not go can be read as resolve plus favorable circumstances rather than foreknowledge. The prediction is also short-range and within his and his allies' power to influence — partly self-fulfilling.
