# The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool

## The Claim
Since Grant Heward and Jerald Tanner in the 1960s, the strongest argument against the [[Book of Abraham]] has been that the "Kirtland Egyptian Papers" — the Egyptian Alphabet documents and the *Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language* — are [[Joseph Smith]]'s working attempt to *translate* the papyri, with Egyptian characters down the left margin matched to English Book-of-Abraham text. Researcher [[William Schryver]] (re-narrated by [[Hayden Carroll]] and [[Jacob Hansen]]) argues the opposite: a database analysis of the documents shows they are *dependent on* an already-existing Book of Abraham text, and that the whole project was an idiographic "pure language" **cipher** — a substitution key — not a deciphering tool.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "substantial words" are unique proper nouns from the Book of Abraham (rate: strong, quantitative).** Schryver, "a computer programmer by trade," built a data table of every substantial word in the Alphabet's explanations and inventoried them against Abraham 1–5 and Genesis 12/15 (controls). The result: "the substantial words are almost all proper nouns that form a lexicon of people, places, and things, almost all of them from the Book of Abraham." A genuine deciphering tool for an *unknown* text would use generic building-block vocabulary, not unique names from an as-yet-unwritten book.[^2]

2. **The "Lord of the Rings list" analogy (rate: strong, illustrative).** A list reading "Frodo, Bilbo, ring, Gandalf, birthday, fireworks, Shire, 111, Pippin, Merry, Hobbit" is better explained by someone who *already had* chapter one of *The Lord of the Rings* than by someone inventing a story from the list. "There is too little of the story ... for it to have been used to produce the story, yet too much of the story for it not to be dependent on the rest of the story."[^3]

3. **Several explanations depend on D&C 76 and 88, not on Egyptian (rate: strong, internal).** Characters 46–49 track, in order, the celestial, terrestrial, and telestial kingdoms and "a kingdom which is not a kingdom of glory" — language lifted from [[D&C 76|D&C 76]] and [[D&C 88|88]]. "Huge portions of these Egyptian grammar and alphabet documents have nothing to do with the papyri or the Book of Abraham," so they cannot be a tool for rendering Egyptian.[^4]

4. **Most "Egyptian" characters are not Egyptian (rate: strong, paleographic).** Of the 69 characters given explanations, most "are not even Egyptian and do not appear on the papyri." Some are invented, some are composites, and "several of the characters can be traced to ciphers used by the Knights Templar and the Freemasons" — no fewer than seven from the Masonic cipher.[^5]

5. **The "Egyptian Counting" document contains nothing Egyptian (rate: strong, decisive case).** It lists invented characters against English number-words — a textbook substitution cipher key. They called it "Egyptian" because, for W. W. Phelps and the others, "Egyptian" was synonymous with the **pure language** they imagined had escaped the confounding at Babel.[^6]

6. **Phelps had already built a cipher before the papyri arrived (rate: strong, chronology).** In a May 1835 letter to his wife Sally — over a month before Michael Chandler's mummies reached Kirtland — Phelps wrote out "a specimen of some of the pure language": six characters (three identical to the Masonic cipher) with sounds and explanations drawn from prior revelations. He reused those same six characters in the Egyptian Alphabet with *new* sounds and meanings — confirming the documents function as cipher keys.[^7]

**Implication.** If the Alphabet/Grammar are a cipher built *from* the Book of Abraham (and from earlier English revelations), they cannot be the tool that *produced* it. The text "must have been translated by Joseph Smith in the same way he had produced the Book of Mormon ... by revelation" — see [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]] and [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]. The Book-of-Abraham manuscripts with papyri characters in the margin (the Tanners' best exhibit) are then specimens of this cipher work, not interlinear translations.[^8]

**Independent confirmation (Muhlestein).** [[Kerry Muhlestein]], working separately, reaches a converging negative result: a character-by-character correlation between the grammar/alphabet and the Book-of-Abraham manuscripts yields only ~2% — "less than what you would expect by happenstance" — so it is "mathematically demonstrable that he didn't use the grammar to translate." He likewise found the grammar mixes in characters that are *not* Egyptian (some from Book-of-Mormon characters, some invented), which the makers knew weren't Egyptian — and concludes no current theory (LDS or critical) yet explains all the documents. See [[No Theory Yet Accounts for All the Kirtland Egyptian Papers Evidence]].[^9]

## Steelman of the Critic
The cipher thesis is contested. (a) It is a minority reconstruction; the Joseph Smith Papers editors and many scholars still read the Alphabet documents as *some kind of* engagement with translating the papyri, and the direction of dependence is exactly what is disputed. (b) Even granting the cipher reading, the critic can shift entirely to the **facsimiles**, whose published explanations Egyptologists judge incorrect, sidestepping the alphabet documents altogether (the video's own answer is [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]] and [[Joseph Smith Declined to Translate the Hypocephalus Characters of Facsimile 2]]). (c) "Pure language" motivations and a cipher function are not mutually exclusive with the men *believing* they were recovering Egyptian, so the label argument shows confusion in 1835 Kirtland more than it vindicates the modern translation. The honest LDS position keeps both the catalyst theory and the missing-scroll theory live; see [[Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire]].
