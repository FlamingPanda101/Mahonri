# John Taylor's Pocket Watch Was Crushed by a Fall Not a Bullet

## The Claim
A famous relic of Carthage is [[John Taylor]]'s pocket watch, popularly said to have stopped a bullet and saved his life. [[Shannon Tracy]] argues the **physical evidence says it was crushed by compression, not struck by a ball**. The watch shows a small "star" of broken enamel on the *front* and dimples pushed *outward* on the *back*; when examined, those dimples and the star line up with the internal gear-works. So when Taylor was shot in the back, fell, and slammed against the window sill, the watch was compressed between his body and the sill — the gears did not flatten but pushed the casing outward, producing the damage. Crucially, **Taylor's own earliest recollection does not mention a bullet hitting the watch** — only that "something" miraculously kept him from going out the window.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The damage geometry fits compression, not impact.** Outward-pushed dimples on the rear that mirror the internal gear train are what you would expect from the watch being squeezed against a hard surface, not from a ball striking the front. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong as a physical read — the pattern is consistent with crushing; it would be sharpened by Tracy's planned modeling.)[^1]
2. **The bullet story entered Taylor's account later.** Tracy's key forensic claim is that the "watch stopped a bullet" detail is *not* in Taylor's first telling; it was introduced afterward, and Taylor — like any trauma victim — later incorporated it. This is offered as a textbook example of memory reconstruction, not deceit. See [[Trauma Victims Reconstruct Crime-Scene Gaps So Survivor Accounts Vary]]. (Evidence rating: moderate — turns on the documentary record of Taylor's successive accounts, which Tracy asserts.)[^2]
3. **It coheres with the fall sequence.** Taylor, shot from the door, "drops like a deer," pulls back, and his body compresses the watch against the sill — a single mechanism explaining both the wound location and the relic. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This deflates a beloved providential story (a watch literally catching a bullet), and Tracy is candid that he is reconstructing, even floating "maybe he was telling the truth and you're just supporting" the skeptical read — a self-aware hedge. A defender can hold that a glancing ball *and* a hard fall both contributed, or that Taylor's later, fuller memory is the accurate one; the episode does not produce the watch or the primary accounts for inspection. But the apologetic point is subtle and self-effacing: Tracy is *correcting a faith-promoting legend on physical grounds*, which models honest method — and notes the deeper miracle (Taylor surviving the volley at all) is untouched either way.

## Best Quotes
