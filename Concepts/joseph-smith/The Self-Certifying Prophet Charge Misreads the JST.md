# The Self-Certifying Prophet Charge Misreads the JST

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Popularized by [[Mike Winger]] and repeated by evangelical guests on the Dr. Daff show, the "self-certifying prophet" trope runs: Joseph Smith pointed to a prophecy of himself at **Genesis 50:33** — but Genesis 50 ends at verse 26, so he fabricated a verse to validate himself. [[Luke Hansen]] answers that the reference is to the **Joseph Smith Translation (JST)** expansion of Genesis 50, a commentary-like revision *never published in his lifetime, never canonized*, and — decisively — **never used by Joseph or any contemporary to certify his prophetic calling**. You cannot be "self-certifying" with a text you never deployed as certification.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. It's the JST, and it has a Book of Mormon parallel (rated: strong, documentary)
After the church was established Joseph expanded Genesis 50, adding [[Joseph of Egypt]]'s prophecy of a latter-day Joseph — paralleling [[2 Nephi 3]]. "They always skip this part because Mike Winger skipped it in the video." The JST "doesn't get published until after he's dead."[^1]

### 2. He never used it as evidence (rated: strong, argument from absence handled)
"There is no record ... of Joseph Smith or any of his contemporaries using this scripture to be like, you guys need to believe that Joseph Smith is a prophet because of that ... You can't be self-certifying if you don't use the scripture to try to certify yourself." The actual evidence Joseph offered was the Book of Mormon, which the critics sidestep "with a handwave."[^1]

### 3. The status of the JST (rated: moderate)
The JST "is really nothing more than a commentary on the Bible," not canonized, "never quoted or used" liturgically — "maybe tertiary" to LDS faith. Disproving Joseph by it "would be the same as trying to disprove ... Moses ... by quoting a commentary written by John Wesley."[^1] (See [[JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition]].)

### 4. The parity case: Jesus too (rated: moderate)
The hosts note Jesus was "self-certifying" when asked by what authority he cleansed the temple ("My own authority"), and that Matthew's "He shall be called a Nazarene" ([[Matthew 2.23]]) cites a prophecy with no known Hebrew Bible source (cf. [[Nazarene Prophecy Has No Known Hebrew Bible Source]]) — so the standard, applied evenly, would indict the New Testament.[^1]

## Counter-arguments / related
A critic responds: (a) even if never *deployed* as proof, writing a prophecy of oneself into scripture is still a self-referential move a skeptic finds suspicious; (b) the JST/2 Nephi 3 parallel is itself part of Joseph's corpus, so "he had an independent prophecy" is circular; and (c) the Jesus/Nazarene parity is a *tu quoque* that doesn't establish Joseph's authority, only embarrasses a particular critic. The LDS reply concedes the *tu quoque* character but insists the original charge ("he invented a verse to validate himself") is simply false on the documentary record — and that critics who can't engage the Book of Mormon retreat to the JST gotcha.
