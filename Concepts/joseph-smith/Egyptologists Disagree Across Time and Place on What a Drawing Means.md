# Egyptologists Disagree Across Time and Place on What a Drawing Means

## The Claim
The standard one-line attack is: "Joseph Smith says the facsimile means X; Egyptologists say it means Y; therefore Joseph is wrong." Egyptologist [[Kerry Muhlestein]] answers that this "30-second soundbite" hides several false assumptions — chiefly that Egyptologists can reliably tell you what an ancient viewer saw in a picture. In reality, the meaning of an Egyptian drawing shifts across a 3,000-year span and from place to place, and Egyptologists have been **demonstrably wrong** about such identifications before.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The drawings are late and the interpretive evidence is earlier (rate: moderate, method).** The three facsimiles date to roughly 200 BC, but most of the comparative evidence Egyptologists rely on is "from much earlier than that." A figure interpreted one way in 1250 BC "they're going to interpret differently in 200 BC" — yet scholars "keep giving the answers we know from this time period and putting them on top of this time period," which is "shaky ground."[^2]

2. **The same figure can mean different things in different settings (rate: moderate, Egyptology).** "You see a drawing of a person ... and then you find out, oh well actually in this setting it's a different figure that looks the same ... That happens to Egyptologists all the time."[^3]

3. **John Gee's labeled hypocephalus overturned both camps (rate: strong, concrete).** For Facsimile 2's type (a hypocephalus), Egyptologists had split into camps over what certain figures meant, "but we couldn't say for sure because they weren't labeled." [[John Gee]] found an example where those figures *were* labeled — "and it turns out that we were mostly wrong. Both camps were mostly wrong." So the confident "Egyptologists say it means this" had been wrong on exactly such figures.[^4] Muhlestein retells the same Gee hypocephalus episode in a second interview, and adds a structural point: the facsimiles date to ~200 BC while most comparanda are far earlier, so "we often just keep giving the answers we know from this time period and putting them on top of this time period," which "is kind of shaky ground."[^8]

4. **A political-cartoon thought experiment (rate: moderate, illustrative).** Show someone 2,000 years from now a modern political cartoon with no text and "who knows what meaning they would come up with." Pictures are interpretively underdetermined; treating Joseph's reading as a failed *translation* mistakes the category — see [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]].[^5]

5. **Textbooks go obsolete because "what we thought we knew, we actually didn't" (rate: moderate, field-maturity).** Muhlestein notes a favorite Egyptology textbook from ~30 years ago is now unusable: "all textbooks that are 10 years old have wrong information in them," because the field keeps overturning prior consensus — "but people lose their testimonies over what we thought we knew."[^6]

**Implication.** "Egyptologists say it means Y" is not a fixed fact but a moving, sometimes-mistaken consensus about what an ancient viewer perceived — and "there aren't any ancient Egyptians to ask." This does not prove Joseph right, but it dismantles the claim that the facsimiles *definitively* falsify him.

## Steelman of the Critic
That experts have erred on some identifications does not mean they err on *these*; appealing to fallibility-in-general is weak if the specific readings (e.g. the figure on the lion couch, the canopic figures) are well attested. A critic can also note Muhlestein is himself an interested LDS scholar, and that "we can't be sure" cuts both ways — it equally undercuts confident LDS claims that the facsimiles *vindicate* Joseph. The strongest version of the attack concedes interpretive humility yet holds Joseph to his own confident, specific captions. The LDS reply leans on the coincidental hits ([[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]]) and the syncretism context ([[Egyptians Mixed Jewish and Egyptian Religious Iconography]]).

## Best Quotes

> "Both camps were mostly wrong ... people had been saying for a long time, see, Egyptologists say it means this and Joseph Smith says something different, so he must be wrong — when the Egyptologists are now demonstrably wrong on those things."[^4]

> "There aren't any ancient Egyptians to ask. So we go to Egyptologists. And that's already a disconnect."[^7]
