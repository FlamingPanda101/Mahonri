# Public Denials of Polygamy Targeted Bennett's Harem Charge

## The Claim

Polygamy-denial advocates ("pneumos," in [[Don Bradley]]'s coinage) argue that [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Hyrum Smith]] publicly denied practicing polygamy and therefore did not practice it. Bradley and the [[Ward Radio]] hosts respond that several of those denials are best read as responses to **[[John C. Bennett]]'s specific charge** — organized "spiritual wifery" and *harems*, including in the Relief Society — not as blanket denials that *any* plural marriage existed.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The denials are contextual, aimed at a libel — rate: moderate (historical framing)
Bennett's 1842 exposé accused the church of a system of seduction. A denial that the church ran Bennett-style harems can be truthful *and* coexist with confidential, sanctioned plural sealings. Reading the denials as answers to *Bennett* rather than as metaphysical claims about all plural marriage changes their force. See [[Joseph Smith Never Issued a Blanket Denial of Polygamy]].[^2]

### 2. Denial advocates omit the Bennett context — rate: moderate (selective use)
[[Kwaku El]] notes that critics arguing Joseph/Hyrum were "responding to something else" leave out how much Joseph and Hyrum were specifically answering Bennett's harem accusations — "weird omissions" that Bradley and the hosts attribute either to the denial model's blind spots or to selective citation. The interlocking sources (Nauvoo and anti-Nauvoo, pro- and anti-polygamy) line up "like puzzle pieces."[^3]

### 3. The honesty issue is acknowledged, not hidden — rate: moderate (candor)
The hosts repeatedly press "how is he not lying?" Bradley's answer is *esoteric secrecy*: Joseph operated on a principle that some truths are withheld until one is told to disclose (he traces it to the First Vision and Moroni accounts), and concealment of an illegal, life-threatening practice was deliberate. They concede a real ethical tension rather than denying it. See [[Joseph Smith Privately Admitted Polygamy to the High Council]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The denial advocate's strongest point is moral, not historical. (a) Even granting that some denials targeted Bennett's *harem* libel, others were sweeping enough that reading them as narrow rebuttals looks like special pleading — and a prophet who issues public statements engineered to leave a false impression, while privately doing the thing denied, faces a credibility problem the "esoteric secrecy" frame softens but does not erase. (b) "He was answering Bennett" can itself be a *selective* reading chosen to rescue the denials, just as the apologist accuses the critic of selectivity. (c) The pneumo can reply that the cleanest account of a prophet who *says* he doesn't practice polygamy is that he *doesn't* — and that the elaborate secret-confession-plus-targeted-denial reconstruction multiplies hidden entities. So the fair conclusion is that the Bennett context genuinely complicates a naive "he denied it, case closed" reading, while the honesty question remains a live cost that the LDS side must pay rather than wish away.
