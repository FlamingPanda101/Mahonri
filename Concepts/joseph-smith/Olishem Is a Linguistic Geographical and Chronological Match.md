# Olishem Is a Linguistic Geographical and Chronological Match

## The Claim
[[Kerry Muhlestein]] argues that the Book of Abraham's "plains of **Olishem**" ([[Abraham 1.15-19|Abraham 1:10]]) — a place absent from the Bible — is one of the strongest evidentiary "bullseyes" for the text, because decades after Joseph Smith published it scholars found an ancient toponym, **Ulisum / Ulishum**, that matches it on three independent axes at once: **linguistic, geographical, and chronological**. The point isn't a single coincidence but the *conjunction*: "when you get a linguistic, chronological, and geographical match, that's pretty good odds right there."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Linguistic match (rated: moderate–strong)
"Olishem" and "Ulisum" differ only in sounds that routinely interchange in Semitic languages: O↔U and S↔Sh. Muhlestein: "O's and U's switch in Semitic language is easy. S and sh switch. So it's a perfect linguistic match."[^1] Hansen restates it: "in semitic languages O and U are sometimes exchanged as well as the S and Sh sounds making [Olishem] and Ulisum a perfect linguistic match."[^2]

### 2. Chronological match (rated: moderate–strong)
The attesting texts are **from the era of Abraham**, not a random later period. One is an inscription of [[Naram-Sin]] of Akkad; another concerns trade. "These texts are from the day of Abraham. So it's a chronological match."[^1]

### 3. Geographical match (rated: moderate)
The Book of Abraham places Abraham not in Mesopotamia but up in the Syria/Turkey region (consistent with the Chaldeans' actual Bronze-Age location). Ulisum lies in roughly that area — "exactly where you would posit it to be based on the book of Abraham."[^1]

### 4. Hostile-witness concession (rated: strong for what it shows)
Even a non-LDS Assyriologist, [[Christopher Wood]], "grudgingly admitted certainly Ulisum could be superficially linked on phonetic grounds to the Olishem in the book of Abraham."[^2] A concession from outside the apologetic community raises the floor on the linguistic claim.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic notes that (a) a *phonetic* link is the weakest kind — Wood called it "superficial," and short toponyms across the ancient Near East yield many chance near-matches; (b) the exact location of Ulisum is not pinned down ("we know the general area"), so "geographical match" means a broad region, not a coordinate; and (c) absent a controlled base rate of how many ancient place-names *could* be massaged into a Book of Abraham word, the "Las Vegas odds" rhetoric is unquantified. The honest LDS reply (Muhlestein's own register) is that no single item proves divine origin; the cumulative case is what carries weight (see [[Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842]]), and the value here is that critics who attack only the *method* of translation never engage these *textual* hits.
