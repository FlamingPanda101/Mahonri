# Shinehah Matches the Egyptian Word for the Sun's Course in Abraham's Day

## The Claim
Joseph Smith gives "**Shinehah**" as the Egyptian name for the sun (Abraham 3, the astronomical chapter), and says it is an Egyptian word. [[Kerry Muhlestein]] notes that the *usual* Egyptian word for the sun or its course is different — but Egyptologists studying texts from **Abraham's specific time period** later found that the word used then for the ecliptic/course of the sun was **shenha** — the very word Joseph Smith gives. Like [[Olishem Is a Linguistic Geographical and Chronological Match|Olishem]], it is both a linguistic and a **chronological** match: Joseph got the *right word for the right era*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The match is era-specific, not generic (rated: moderate–strong)
"As some other Egyptologists were looking at texts from the time period of Abraham, they figured out that during Abraham's day, the word they use for the elliptical of the sun is Shaneha — the word Joseph Smith says."[^1] Hansen adds the chronological control: "the pyramid texts show that shinha is a plausible rendering for the word they use to describe the sun's orbit during Abraham's time. The text also shows they used a different word just after Abraham's time ... Joseph Smith didn't just get the name right, he even got the chronology right."[^2]

### 2. Not sought as proof (rated: contextual)
Muhlestein stresses the discovery was incidental: "we weren't even looking for this." That the match emerged from independent Egyptological work, not LDS apologetics mining for parallels, slightly insulates it from the cherry-picking charge.[^1]

### 3. Part of a cluster (rated: cumulative)
Muhlestein lists Shinehah alongside Olishem and the four Canaanite gods of Facsimile 2 as a set of low-probability hits: "what are the odds of Olishem and Shinehah ... Like impossible. ... If we were to have Las Vegas calculate the odds for us, they would not tell us to bet on that."[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic replies: (a) transliterating Egyptian (an unvocalized script) into English vowels is loose, so "shinehah ≈ shenha" depends on reconstructed vocalizations that are themselves contested; (b) without a base rate for how many of Joseph's coined "Egyptian" names *fail* to match anything, a few hits could be selection effect; and (c) Joseph also offers astronomical content (Kolob etc.) that critics read as 19th-century, so the "right word, right era" claim must be weighed against the parts that look anachronistic (the vault tracks this tension at [[The 6% Astrology Outlier and Abraham 3]]). The LDS counter is the same as for Olishem: no single datum is decisive, but the *conjunction* of linguistic + chronological precision is hard to get by guessing, and critics who attack only the papyri never address it.
