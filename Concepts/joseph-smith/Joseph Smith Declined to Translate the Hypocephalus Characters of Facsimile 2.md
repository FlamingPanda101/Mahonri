# Joseph Smith Declined to Translate the Hypocephalus Characters of Facsimile 2

## The Claim
Critics (e.g. "KBY"/Kobby Reddish, Nemo, citing Grant Heward and Jerald Tanner) point to specific hieratic characters on the papyrus fragment **JSP XI** that appear, matched to English, in the Book of Abraham manuscripts, and say: these are Joseph's translation, and they are wrong. Tim Barker's rejoinder (re-narrated by [[Hayden Carroll]] and [[Jacob Hansen]]) is that **[[Joseph Smith]] explicitly said the Lord had *not* given him the translation of those very characters** — so they cannot be cited as a failed translation by Joseph at all. The answer "has been under our heads this entire time."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The lacunae of Facsimile 2 were filled from JSP XI (rate: strong; conceded by the critics' own source).** Heward and Tanner (1968) observed that Facsimile 2 (a hypocephalus) was damaged, and "the missing areas on this drawing have been filled in with insertions from other documents." Willard Richards (who wrote the figure explanations) drew the reconstruction; the lacunae text was taken from other Joseph Smith papyri — specifically JSP XI hieratic from rows 2–4. So the very characters critics cite were inserted as *filler* into a drawing, traceable back to JSP XI.[^2]

2. **Joseph deferred the translation of exactly that material (rate: strong; primary source).** Joseph supplied explanations for Facsimile 2, and for the inserted hieratic he wrote that the explanation "will be given in the own due time of the Lord," concluding "the above translation is given as far as we have any right to give at the present time." Every JSP XI hieratic character included in Facsimile 2 was therefore one Joseph **declined** to translate.[^3]

3. **Heward and Tanner stopped short of the implication (rate: moderate; interpretive).** Barker grants the observation originates with the critics, but notes "they stopped just short of its implications, just as every single one of their successors have done." The implication: if Joseph said the translation of these characters was withheld, the standard "he mistranslated JSP XI" attack is aimed at text Joseph never claimed to have rendered.[^4]

4. **It fits the cipher reading of the manuscripts (rate: moderate; coheres).** This dovetails with [[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool]]: [[W. W. Phelps]] and others were using papyri characters as cipher symbols, so the appearance of JSP XI glyphs beside English in the manuscripts need not mean interlinear translation.[^1]

**Implication.** The single strongest "smoking gun" — character-by-character matches between JSP XI and the Book of Abraham manuscripts — loses its force once Joseph's own commentary shows he expressly did not translate those characters. Combined with the missing-scroll point ([[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]), the attack on the *text* (as opposed to the facsimile *drawings*) is blunted.

## Steelman of the Critic
A critic can answer that deferring a translation is convenient *post hoc*, and that the inserted hieratic was nonetheless treated by Joseph's scribes as relevant to the Book of Abraham (else why insert it). The deferral covers the inserted hypocephalus rim text, but the broader dispute over whether the surviving fragments are the source of the Abraham text remains (handled separately). And the facsimile-explanation problem proper — the figure identifications Egyptologists call mistaken — is untouched by this point and must be met on its own ground (see [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]]).

## Best Quotes

> "Literally all of the JSP XI hieratic characters included in Facsimile 2, Joseph Smith deliberately declined from commenting upon because he believed that the translation would at some future time be given in the own due time of the Lord."[^3]

> "Joseph Smith concretely provided direct evidence in Facsimile 2 that he never translated JSP XI, and that the answer has been under our heads this entire time."[^4]
