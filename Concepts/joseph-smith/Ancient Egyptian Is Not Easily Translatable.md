# Ancient Egyptian Is Not Easily Translatable

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Cardon Ellis]] push back on the critic's assertion that the Egyptian text on the Joseph Smith Papyri is "now easily translatable" and "has absolutely nothing to do with Abraham." Their reply has two prongs: (1) **Egyptian is not "easily" translatable at all** — hand a room of Egyptologists any short glyph sequence and they will argue over its meaning; and (2) the surviving **facsimiles have lacunae (holes)**, so no one — Joseph or his critics — can be certain what the missing portions originally depicted, which means "Joseph's guess is as good as your guess" on the damaged sections. The rhetorical confidence of "easily translatable" overstates the actual state of the field. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Give Egyptologists seven glyphs and they argue — rate: moderate (illustrative; real phenomenon of interpretive disagreement)
Ellis's challenge: take "any seven-hieroglyph sequence" from a glyph-dense temple (he cites the relocated temple near the Aswan Dam, the Abu Simbel / Ramesses temples "they had to lift out of the water") and "give it to a room full of Egyptologists and say, 'Translate this' — and you will watch them argue about the meaning of it." Translation of non-formulaic Egyptian involves genuine interpretive latitude; "easily translatable" papers over it. This is the same point the vault makes in [[Egyptologists Disagree Across Time and Place on What a Drawing Means]]. [^1]

### 2. The facsimiles have holes, so the originals are partly unknown — rate: strong (physical fact of the papyri)
On Facsimile 1, the critic claims "that's not what the image shows at all" (asserting it depicts Anubis resurrecting Osiris, with the knife and human head "invented" by Joseph). Barnes answers that the images "have holes in them — you can't see exactly what the images showed… nobody does, because there's holes in them." Where the papyrus is destroyed, the reconstruction is contested on *all* sides, so the flat claim "we now know he's wrong" is unwarranted on the lacunae. [^2]

### 3. The selective trust in Egyptologists — rate: moderate (tu quoque, but a fair consistency check)
Barnes notes critics "love Egyptologists when they say the Book of Mormon isn't true, but then they ask those same Egyptologists if the Book of Genesis is true and they laugh them out of the state." The appeal to Egyptological authority is itself selectively deployed — credited when it cuts against Joseph Smith, discarded when the same scholars would naturalize the Hebrew Bible. [^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic has a real point under the overstatement. (a) **Funerary formulae are not arbitrary**: the Book of Breathings and Book of the Dead are highly formulaic, well-attested genres, and Egyptologists across the spectrum — including believing Latter-day Saint Egyptologists like Robert Ritner's interlocutors — agree the surviving papyrus is a standard funerary text *about a man named Hor*, not Abraham. So "Egyptologists argue about everything" is true of obscure or poetic inscriptions but **false of this genre**, where the reading is secure. (b) The **lacuna argument cuts both ways**: appealing to the holes to say "Joseph's guess is as good as yours" also concedes that the apologetic reconstruction is *itself* a guess; the restored portions of Facsimile 1 (by Egyptologists) follow standard iconographic parallels (the jackal-headed Anubis, the lion-couch embalming scene) that are well-precedented. (c) The point that the *content* survives in Hor's funerary text is exactly what makes the "Joseph translated Abraham from this scroll" claim hard — which is why the strongest LDS responses (the catalyst theory, the missing-scroll theory) *grant* the Egyptological reading and relocate the translation's source (see [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]] and [[Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire]]). The defensible residue of Barnes's point is narrow but valid: "easily translatable, nothing to do with Abraham" is rhetorically inflated, and the *damaged* facsimile details cannot bear the weight of a knockdown "Joseph faked it" claim.
