# The Implications Cascade of Polygamy Denial

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brian Hales]]'s central argument (with [[Jacob Hansen]]) is that denying Joseph Smith practiced plural marriage is **not a free move**: it forces a believer to also discard four further pillars of LDS faith. Using a "fast car with a great engine but no windshield, seats, or sound system" analogy, Hales argues that accepting the "monogamist Joseph" you like comes bundled with consequences deniers rarely confront. Denial is one end of a stick — pick it up, and you pick up the other end too.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(1) The integrity of seven top leaders for 50 years.** Plural marriage and Joseph's authorship of it were affirmed by firsthand eyewitness testimony from the 2nd–4th Relief Society general presidents ([[Eliza R. Snow]], Zina Huntington, Bathsheba Smith) and the 2nd–5th church presidents ([[Brigham Young]], [[John Taylor]], [[Wilford Woodruff]], [[Lorenzo Snow]]). To deny Joseph's polygamy, all seven must be branded liars *while simultaneously* being held worthy to receive revelation and lead the church — a cognitive dissonance the denial requires.[^2] *(Strong — these are firsthand, not hearsay, accounts from the church's highest leaders.)*
2. **(2) [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] and eternal families.** Denial requires attributing Section 132 to Brigham Young or a spurious source. But 132 is the *only* scriptural place teaching Joseph's authority to **seal** marriages to last after death — Hales calls sealing authority Joseph's "zenith teaching." Discard 132 (verse 1 mentions plurality; the bulk teaches sealing), and "there's no way to believe in eternal families because it's the only place we learn Joseph has the authority to seal a marriage." Deniers cannot keep the eternal-family parts they like while excising the polygamy parts — "you pick up one end of the stick, you pick up the other."[^3] *(Strong — sealing authority and plural marriage are textually inseparable in 132.)*
3. **(3) and (4) The temple endowment.** The endowment was written down by [[Wilford Woodruff]] in 1877 as *his recollection* of Joseph's teachings — the *same* late-recollection source whose polygamy testimony deniers reject. If Woodruff's late memory is unreliable on polygamy, it cannot be trusted on the temple either, so the endowment's legitimacy collapses with it. (Developed in [[The Temple Endowment Rests on the Same Witnesses as Polygamy]].)[^4] *(Strong — exposes a methodological double standard.)*
4. **(5) Opposition to the living apostles.** In 2025 the Church released an eight-word statement: "Joseph Smith introduced the practice of plural marriage, not Brigham Young." Vetted (per Hales) through the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve — including historian-apostle [[Dallin H. Oaks]] — it means anyone teaching that Joseph did *not* start polygamy stands in "direct contradiction to our living apostles," which Hales frames as an apostate message. The eight words give local leaders a measuring stick.[^5] *(Strong as an authority argument within the LDS frame; rhetorically weaker outside it.)*

**Steelman of the denialist view.** A denier replies that the cascade is a *coercive* argument from consequences, not evidence: "if you deny X you lose Y and Z you cherish" is emotional leverage, not proof that Joseph practiced polygamy — and a committed denier simply *accepts* the cascade, holding that the later leaders did fabricate or embellish, that 132 (or its polygamy portions) is indeed Brigham-era, and that the temple/eternal-family doctrines can be re-grounded elsewhere (e.g. earlier sealing references, William Clayton's journal — which deniers also reject). The cascade also arguably *proves too much*: that so much hangs on 132 and on late recollections is, to a skeptic, a reason for *unease*, not assurance. The LDS rejoinder is that the cascade is not offered as the *evidence* for Joseph's polygamy (that is the documentary/eyewitness record and the wordprint data — see [[Polygamy Denialism]]) but as a *cost accounting*: it shows denial cannot be quarantined to one uncomfortable practice and demonstrates the denier's method (discount all inconvenient testimony as forgery) is unsustainably selective.

## LDS Relevance
This is the load-bearing apologetic against the growing online "monogamist Joseph" movement: it reframes the debate from "did Joseph practice polygamy?" (a documentary question the denier loses) to "what does denial *cost*?" (a systematic-theology question that exposes denial as faith-destroying rather than faith-protecting). Hales's point is that deniers market their view as a way to *keep* faith in Joseph while jettisoning an embarrassment, but it actually severs eternal families, the temple, prophetic authority, and trust in the living apostles. Pairs with [[Polygamy Denialism]] and [[Brigham Young as the Fall Guy]].

## Best Quotes
> "There are ramifications for faith that I think are in every case faith-weakening, because what you end up with is that you've got to believe the church is led by liars for 50 years. You give up section 132 and eternal families. You basically have to disallow the temple ceremony... and then you find yourself in direct opposition to our current apostles." — [[Brian Hales]] [^1]

> "When we throw away section 132, we give up eternal families, because there's no other [place] — it's the only place we learn Joseph has the authority to seal a marriage." [^3]

> "If somebody tells you Joseph Smith didn't start polygamy, [they] are in direct contradiction to our living apostles." [^5]
