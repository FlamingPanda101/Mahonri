# The Pace Memo Did Not Cause Any Church or State Action

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics credit the Pace memo with triggering institutional responses — implying it was so weighty it moved both the Church and the state of Utah to act on satanic ritual abuse. Ward Radio Part 3 rebuts this: the memo is "not actionable," prompted no documented church action, and did not cause the state task force critics attribute to it.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The timeline disproves the causal claim (strong — dated)
A *Network* periodical writer claimed the Utah Governor's Commission for Women and Families formed a ritual-abuse subcommittee "soon after the memo by Bishop Pace." But "in reality, the subcommittee had been formed in February 1990, before Pace's memorandum" (published July 1990). The effect predates the alleged cause [^1].

### 2. No evidence of church action either (strong)
"There's no evidence the Pace memo actioned any change from the church that it wasn't already making." The memo "suggests no action," and "there's no evidence the Pace memo was accepted or believed by church leaders" [^2].

### 3. The church was already responding like everyone else (moderate — context)
Any responsiveness to SRA reports reflected the same due diligence every large institution exercised during the panic, not a reaction to Pace specifically. "Bishop Pace was asked to complete some due diligence. He returned and recited the catechism of his day." See [[Pace Interviewed Self-Selected Therapy Patients Not a Cross-Section]] [^3].

### 4. Anti-Mormons declared a premature victory (moderate)
"The anti-Mormons at Lighthouse Ministries declared victory" by tying state action to the memo — a claim the February 1990 date falsifies. See [[Even Lighthouse Ministries Doubted the Pace Memo's Credibility]] [^1].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A critic could argue that a leaked internal memo can influence events through informal channels even without a paper trail, and that absence of documented action is not proof of no influence. True in principle — but the *specific* causal claim made by the panic's promoters (that the memo spawned the governor's subcommittee) is decisively refuted by the subcommittee predating the memo by five months, and no alternative documented effect has been produced. The burden lies on the one asserting causation, and the one concrete assertion fails on dates.
