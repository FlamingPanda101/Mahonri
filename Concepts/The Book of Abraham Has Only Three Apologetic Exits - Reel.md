# The Book of Abraham Has Only Three Apologetic Exits - Reel

## The Point
**Claim ([[Bill Reel]]):** Joseph Smith said the papyri were "the writings of Abraham... by his own hand." We still have some of those papyri, his facsimile explanations, and the Kirtland Egyptian Papers (KEP), which line specific Egyptian characters up against English paragraphs of the Book of Abraham. Egyptologists — LDS and non-LDS, as the Church's own Gospel Topics essay concedes — agree the fragments are ordinary funerary texts that name neither Abraham nor any event in the book. Reel argues there are therefore only three apologetic exits, and you should "pay attention to which one" is used: (1) an *unknown translation method* (requires dismissing all of Egyptology; held by "an extremely small number of people"); (2) the *missing scroll* (but the facsimiles and KEP tie the text to the fragments we *have*, and Joseph also claimed the same papyri held the Book of Joseph and a narrative of Princess Katumin — so you now need *three* missing scrolls); (3) the *catalyst theory* (the papyri merely *inspired* revelation), which ignores the facsimiles and KEP and renders the claim unfalsifiable.[^1]

**Concession ([[Robert Gurr]]):** Asked directly which he uses, Gurr answers the **catalyst theory** — Joseph "never actually translated anything," he received revelation; we know red-ink papyri are missing, the surviving fragment is a "book of breathings," but Joseph "was able to use that as revelation to receive the Book of Abraham." He grants it is "not falsifiable" but argues "with all faith-related issues we have to take certain things on faith."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The trichotomy is genuinely exhaustive (rated: strong, framing).** Reel's taxonomy — dismiss the experts / multiply missing documents / redefine "translation" — covers the live LDS options, and his "three scrolls" point is a real cost for the missing-scroll route.[^1] The vault's own best answer largely *steps outside* the trichotomy by relocating the test: judge the book by its doctrinal fruits and by whether Joseph "spoke for God," not by whether he was an Egyptologist — see [[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]] and [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]].
2. **Gurr's catalyst concession is double-edged (rated: strong against Gurr on falsifiability, weak as a positive case).** By owning that the catalyst theory is "not falsifiable," Gurr hands Reel his thesis that apologetics retreats to unfalsifiability under pressure (see [[The Least-Conjecture Test and the Goalpost-Moving Pattern]] and [[Heads Faith Wins, Tails Reason Loses - The Unfalsifiability Charge]]). *Steelman of Gurr:* the surviving "missing red-ink papyri" are attested by eyewitnesses, so the collection demonstrably is incomplete — the catalyst view is not invented from nothing.[^2]
3. **The KEP "glossary-after" rejoinder (rated: contested).** Gurr suggests the Egyptian papers were produced *after* the translation, like a glossary appended to a finished Harry Potter — so the symbol-to-paragraph pairing is reverse-engineered, not the source. He admits "I can't confirm that because we don't have it... is it convenient? Yeah."[^3] Reel's counter: even granting we know *which* pieces are missing, "from the things we do have, all of the written material Joseph Smith is corresponding to shows up with those symbols and those facsimiles," and nothing of the supposed Abraham source survives in the KEP at all.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "There's really only three options... an unknown translation method... the missing scroll theory... [or] a catalyst theory... and at that point the claim becomes unfalsifiable because any mismatch can just be dismissed." [^1]

> "In other words, what you're suggesting is the catalyst theory." — "Yes." [^2]

> "Is it convenient? Yeah. It's not falsifiable. It's not. But we don't do that with all faith-related issues — we have to take certain things on faith." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The most defensible LDS posture is not to win Reel's trichotomy on its own terms but to dispute its premise that "translation" must mean academic Egyptology: the Restoration's revealed scriptures (Book of Mormon, Book of Moses, JST) were *revelations*, not philological renderings ([[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]), and the Book of Abraham's vindication is sought in its doctrinal yield and apocryphal corroborations ([[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]], [[Apocryphal Sources Corroborate the Book of Abraham After 1842]], [[God Found Spirits He Did Not Create - Book of Abraham Solve]]). The cost Reel correctly identifies — and Gurr concedes — is that this trades testability for a fruits-based epistemology, which critics will read as unfalsifiable and believers as appropriately faith-anchored.
