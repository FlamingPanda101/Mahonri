# Morality Is the Well-Being of Conscious Creatures

## The Point
This is the foundational definition of Harris's whole project: questions about values "are really questions about the well-being of conscious creatures," and so values "translate into facts that can be scientifically understood."[^1] He argues, by appeal to reason alone, that **consciousness is the only intelligible domain of value** — any supposed source of value that could not affect the experience of any creature would be "the least interesting thing in the universe."[^2] Given that, "the concept of 'well-being' captures all that we can intelligibly value," and morality just *is* "the intentions and behaviors that affect the well-being of conscious creatures."[^3]

## Evidence
Harris's central move is an argument by elimination: he invites the reader "to try to think of a source of value that has absolutely nothing to do with the (actual or potential) experience of conscious beings," and claims nothing survives the test.[^2] He pre-empts the obvious counterexample — religious moral law followed "for its own sake" — by arguing it is neither honest nor coherent: even believers obey out of concern for consequences (heaven, hell, or this-worldly good), so religious morality is "not an exception to our common concern for well-being."[^4] Likewise, philosophical systems built on "duty, fairness, justice, or some other principle" ultimately "draw upon some conception of well-being in the end."[^5] He answers the charge that "well-being" is too vague by analogy with **health**: the concept "resists precise definition, and yet it is indispensable," and remains "perpetually open to revision" as science advances — without thereby being arbitrary or merely subjective.[^6] He also distinguishes his thesis from an *evolutionary* account: our brains "were not designed with a view to our ultimate fulfillment," so much of well-being "escapes any narrow Darwinian calculus."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Questions about values—about meaning, morality, and life's larger purpose—are really questions about the well-being of conscious creatures. Values, therefore, translate into facts that can be scientifically understood." [^1]

> "I think we can know, through reason alone, that consciousness is the only intelligible domain of value." [^2]

> "'Morality'—whatever people's associations with this term happen to be—really relates to the intentions and behaviors that affect the well-being of conscious creatures." [^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
Harris's definition is doing enormous work that it cannot itself justify. **(1) The grounding/obligation problem.** Even granting that well-being is what we *value*, nothing in the definition tells us why anyone is morally *obligated* to promote it — least of all the well-being of *others*. Describing what conduces to flourishing is a claim about value; morality also includes obligation, and a theory that only ranks states as better or worse "isn't describing morality—it's just describing value" (see [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]]). **(2) Why consciousness?** Harris's elimination argument shows that value is *relative to* conscious experience, but a theist happily grants that — and then asks why conscious beings have the kind of dignity that generates duties. On naturalism, "well-being" is a brute biological fact about certain primates; on theism it is grounded in beings made in the image of God and ordered to a real human good. Classical natural-law and objective-value resources locate the *standard* of flourishing in the nature and end of the human person, not in a stipulated definition (see [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]] on the objectivity of value, [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] on God as the ground of being and goodness). **(3) The smuggled realism.** Harris insists there are "right and wrong answers," i.e. he is a full moral realist — but a Latter-day Saint can press [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]]'s point that robust moral realism is intellectual property the naturalist has borrowed without paying for: facts about brains can tell you *what is*, never on their own *what ought to be*. Notably, the restored gospel agrees with Harris *against* divine-command voluntarism — morality is not arbitrary divine fiat ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]) — yet it grounds the obligatory force of well-being in eternal law and the law of love ([[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]]) rather than in a definition we are free to accept or reject. Represented fairly, Harris is right that morality concerns flourishing; he is missing an account of why that flourishing lays a *claim* on us.
