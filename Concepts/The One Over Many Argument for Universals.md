# The One Over Many Argument for Universals

## The Point
Feser defends **realism** about universals — the view that the shared patterns things have in common (redness, triangularity) are genuinely real and not merely names — by way of the classic **"one over many"** argument and a battery of objections that sink its rival, **nominalism**.[^1] The decisive move is that nominalism, which says there are only general *words* and no real universals, cannot be stated without smuggling universals back in: resemblance is itself a universal, and the very word "red" is a universal repeated across our many utterances.[^2] So the view is self-undermining.

## Evidence
The positive **one over many** argument: a universal like triangularity is not reducible to any particular triangle, nor even to the whole collection of triangles, because every such thing could go out of existence and triangularity could be exemplified again in some new triangle. Universals are also exemplified when no human mind is aware of them. Hence they are neither material things, nor collections of material things, nor mind-dependent.[^1]

The indirect arguments against nominalism:
- **The vicious regress problem.** The nominalist says we call things "red" because they *resemble* each other. But, as Russell noted, resemblance is itself a universal shared by many pairs. Trying to dodge this by saying cases of resemblance merely "resemble" each other only pushes the same problem up a level, *ad infinitum*.[^3]
- **The "words are universals too" problem.** The nominalist claims there is no universal redness, only the general term "red." But "red" uttered by you, me, and Socrates are particular *tokens* of one word *type* that exists over and above the utterances — exactly the "one over many" situation nominalism wanted to avoid. Denying this (claiming we never utter the same word, only resembling words) would make communication impossible.[^4]

Feser's general verdict: "it is notoriously very difficult to defend nominalism in a way that doesn't surreptitiously bring in through the back door a commitment to universals or other abstract objects, in which case the view is self-undermining."[^5] He buttresses realism with further arguments from geometry, mathematics, the nature of propositions, science, and possible worlds — all turning on the objectivity and necessity of what these truths are about.[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "For any particular triangle or red thing, or even the whole collection of these things, could go out of existence, and yet triangularity and redness could come to be exemplified once again in some new triangle or red thing." [^1]

> "The nominalist claims that there are no universals like redness, just general terms like 'red'. Yet this claim seems obviously self-contradictory, since the term 'red' is itself a universal." [^4]

## LDS Connection
This note is squarely an ally against atheism: nominalism is the natural fallback for a thoroughgoing materialist who wants to deny mind-independent abstracta, and Feser shows it collapses under its own weight — objective, shared patterns are real (see [[The Reality of Abstract Objects]] and [[The Laws of Logic Are Immaterial and Point to a Mind]]). For Latter-day Saints there is no tension here at the level of *realism*: scripture treats truth and law as objective and eternal rather than as conventional human labels (D&C 93:24, 36). The honest caveat, keeping Feser at full strength, is that realism about universals is only the *first* step of his argument; the further claim — that these universals must be grounded in one absolutely simple divine intellect — is where the classical and Restoration pictures will diverge ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]).
