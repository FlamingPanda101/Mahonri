# Identical Cross-Case Allegations Point to the Interviewer Not the Crime

## The Claim
Unconnected ritual-abuse cases across different Utah communities produced near-identical allegations and even identical children's drawings — a pattern explicable only by their single shared variable: therapist [[Barbara Snow]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. A judge's affidavit documented the pattern (strong — court record)
A judge noted "bizarre factual correlations" between cases: "In all of the cases, children taken to Barbara Snow for counseling have in turn identified other children and adults in the neighborhood. Nearly identical allegations exist in several of these cases. Even some pictures drawn by some of the children are claimed to be identical" [^1].

### 2. The only common link was Snow plus absent evidence (strong)
The affidavit found "no known connection exists between any of the cases except for the involvement of Dr. Barbara Snow and the inability of law enforcement to discover any corroborating evidence" [^2].

### 3. Stories converged after therapy (moderate — inferential)
Children's accounts started "wild and fantastical" but "after enough therapy, they became homogeneous." Independent sex rings would not converge on the same script; a shared interviewer would produce exactly that [^3].

### 4. Pace's "all the same stories" cuts the same way (strong — added from Part 3)
Ward Radio Part 3 turns Pace's own logic against him. Pace believed the 60 witnesses *because* their stories matched; but "if all the stories are exactly the same, wouldn't that be evidence that they are false? When interrogators get precisely the same story from two criminal suspects, it's evidence that … they are collaborating to give the cops a pre-rehearsed alibi." The shared source here is the saturating media: "perhaps they're drawing from the same news reports that were saturating the airways at that very time." Identical accounts implicate a common input (interviewer or media), not a common crime [^4].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
A believer might argue that ritual-abuse networks could genuinely share methods, training, or even coordination, producing similar disclosures. But that hypothesis predicts some physical or documentary link between the cases, of which investigators found none — while the convergence of independent cases on a single interviewer, with stories homogenizing only after therapy, is the signature of contamination.
