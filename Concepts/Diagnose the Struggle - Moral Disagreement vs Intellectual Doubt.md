# Diagnose the Struggle - Moral Disagreement vs Intellectual Doubt

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that the first task for anyone in a faith struggle is an honest **diagnosis of motive**: is the struggle "primarily due to moral disagreements with the positions of the church or intellectual issues?"[^1] He insists "it's probably both to some extent, but one is probably bigger than the other," and that intellectual arguments cannot resolve what is fundamentally a moral or relational grievance.[^1] His pastoral counsel — the positive-case method — is explicitly addressed only to those whose obstacle is genuinely intellectual, "who do not have serious moral issues with the church but who struggle intellectually."[^2]

## Evidence
1. **Self-examination of motive comes before argument.** "As part of approaching this right, we need to examine our own motivations. Are we resentful right now? Are we in a good head space to even begin an unbiased exploration?"[^3] Hansen quotes the maxim that "people will believe what they want to believe": "if someone is angry and just wants to justify that anger, I can't help."[^3]

2. **The honest sorting question.** "The first thing to do if we are struggling is to be honest about why. Is our struggle primarily due to moral disagreements with the positions of the church or intellectual issues?"[^1] Examples of *moral/relational* obstacles he sets aside: disagreement over "gay marriage or something like that," being "upset with church leaders or policy or church culture" — "those are not primarily intellectual issues."[^4]

3. **Scope is a feature, not evasion.** By naming what his method *cannot* fix, Hansen avoids the common apologetic error of answering an intellectual question that was never the real driver. This is the same insight as [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]]: rational arguments aimed at the "rider" fail when the "elephant" — the affective, moral, relational disposition — is leaning the other way.

4. **Intellectual honesty cuts both ways.** The target audience is people whose desire to believe is checked by "their righteous commitment to intellectual honesty."[^5] The diagnosis is therefore not a way to dismiss doubters but to match the right help to the right problem — and only then to proceed to the [[Start With the Positive Case - Weigh the Scale Before the Earthquake]] method.

5. **Steelman of a critic.** A critic could object that splitting "moral" from "intellectual" lets the apologist dodge hard intellectual problems by reclassifying them as "resentment." Hansen partly anticipates this by conceding the two are entangled ("probably both to some extent") and by insisting the inquirer, not the apologist, must make the honest call about which is "bigger" — locating the diagnosis in self-examination rather than in an accusation leveled at others.

## Best Quotes
> "The first thing to do if we are struggling is to be honest about why. Is our struggle primarily due to moral disagreements with the positions of the church or intellectual issues? It's probably both to some extent, but one is probably bigger than the other. We must be honest with ourselves about this." [^1]

> "We need to examine our own motivations. Are we resentful right now? Are we in a good head space to even begin an unbiased exploration? ... If someone is angry and just wants to justify that anger, I can't help." [^3]

> "This video is for those who do not have serious moral issues with the church but who struggle intellectually to justify saying that they believe that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is the one true church." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The moral/intellectual diagnosis echoes the Restoration teaching that revelation and conviction follow a willing, repentant heart: "If any man will do his will, he shall know of the doctrine" (John 7:17) — disposition precedes knowing. Alma's seed grows only in a heart that does "not cast it out by your unbelief" and gives place for it (see [[Alma 32.27-43]]); resentment is exactly the soil that casts the seed out. The counsel to examine one's own motives before judging the Church is also a posture of charity toward strugglers: it presumes good faith ("people who want to believe but are struggling") rather than condemning doubt, while gently naming that a heart set on grievance will not be reached by evidence alone.
