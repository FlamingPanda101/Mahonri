# Learning by Study and Also by Faith

## The Point
The restored gospel commands seeking truth through two complementary channels at once: "seek learning, even by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118). The editors of the BYU volume take this as their charter — to establish what is known religiously, what is known scientifically, and how the two epistemologies can be brought together, so the book itself can be one of the "best books" from which seekers learn "by study and by faith."[^1] Far from forcing a choice between revelation and research, the restored gospel expects a believer to use both, confident that they ultimately "lead to one great whole."[^2]

## Evidence
- The editors open by stating their belief "that questing for further light and knowledge leads, ultimately, to harmony between revelation and research, between academic and spiritual means of knowing, between scripture and science."[^3]
- They define their two-epistemology structure explicitly, with chapters on "what we know from a religious epistemology" and "what we know from a scientific epistemology" — where "by 'epistemology,' we mean 'a way of knowing truth.'"[^4]
- Scripture is marshaled to favor education: "the glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36); the diligent gain "so much the advantage in the world to come" (D&C 130:19); "to be learned is good if they hearken unto the counsels of God" (2 Ne. 9:29).[^5]
- Joshua Sears closes his journey by recommitting to "seek wisdom 'by study and also by faith' (D&C 88:118), ignoring neither the best books nor the voice of the Spirit."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "We seek learning by study and faith simultaneously." [^7]

> "We accept the truths we have and remain open to more because we expect that there is much yet to be learned by revelation and discovered by scientific research. We believe that ultimately both of those ways of knowing truth lead to one great whole." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This concept is the methodological heart of the restored-gospel approach to evolution. Because the two epistemologies answer different questions — science the *how* and *when* of the natural world, religion the *who* and *why* of the supernatural — they cannot finally contradict; apparent conflict signals "an incomplete knowledge of either science or religion, or both" (Russell M. Nelson).[^8] The editors model the disposition this requires: being "inspired by, not afraid of, ambiguity, unreconciled truths, and unresolved problems," questing "Moses-like" for answers while trusting that "a time [will] come in which nothing shall be withheld" (D&C 121:28).[^9]
