# Sacrifice as the Discovery of the Future

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] offers an evolutionary-psychological genealogy of the sacrificial motif: behavior with survival value (delayed gratification) was selected for, then self-consciously represented and dramatized into ritual and story. "Sacrifice" just *is* "the discovery of the future" — give up something valued now to gain something more valuable later.[^1]

## Argument

1. **Bottom-up emergence.** People first *behaved* in ways with survival significance; because we are self-conscious, we then "make representations of those patterns and dramatize them and encapsulate them in stories." His wolf illustration: a self-conscious wolf watching pack behavior might narrate "each wolf has intrinsic value" — grounded in actual behavior, not imposed rule. *Steelman: morality as observed regularity codified into narrative.*[^2]

2. **Sacrifice = delayed gratification conceptualized.** "If you give up something of value now you can gain something of more value in the future." His students instantly understand it via immigrant parents who "sacrificed to send you to university." Animals may *act out* delay but do not obviously *conceptualize* it.[^3]

3. **The archetypal extreme.** If sacrificing what you value secures future benefit, then "how good could those future benefits be?" (heavenly) and "what's the ultimate sacrifice?" — pushing toward child sacrifice as "a pathological extreme" of "a powerful logic." Peterson reads the biblical trajectory (Abraham/Isaac forbidding it, circumcision as substitute, finally fully *psychologized* abstract sacrifice) as the idea maturing. *Steelman: bloody history as the costly process by which an idea is refined.*[^4]

4. **Harris's pushback.** Much child sacrifice is plain ignorance — misapprehension of the causal structure of the cosmos (burying a firstborn in a post-hole to keep a building up). One needs no "positive gloss" on human sacrifice to form a motivating picture of the future; delayed gratification is "fully separable" from it. See [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]].[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "If you give up something of value now you can gain something of more value in the future... the notion of sacrifice is exactly the same thing as the discovery of the future." [^1]

> "What's the ultimate sacrifice that you have to perform... the child sacrifice fits into that category... it's a pathological extreme — it is a powerful logic extreme." [^4]

> "You don't need any kind of positive gloss on human sacrifice as a meme or as an archetype in order to form a coherent picture of the future that can motivate you." [^5]
