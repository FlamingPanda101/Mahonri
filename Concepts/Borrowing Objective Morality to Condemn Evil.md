# Borrowing Objective Morality to Condemn Evil

## The Point
The "stealing from God" thesis applied to the M of CRIMES: atheists routinely condemn real evils — the Holocaust, child rape and murder, Nazi medical experiments — as *objectively* wrong, yet their own worldview supplies no objective standard by which to do so. To condemn such acts as genuinely (not just distastefully) wrong, "you would again need to appeal to an objective moral law" that atheism denies exists.[^1] In other words, the moral indignation is borrowed capital: it presupposes the very transcendent standard the atheist rejects.[^2]

## Evidence
Turek's centerpiece is his recorded debate exchange with David Silverman, president of American Atheists. Silverman both condemned a position as "wholly immoral" and insisted "there is no objective moral standard"; pressed on Nuremberg, he conceded "the hard answer is you're correct... it is a matter of opinion."[^3] Turek diagnoses two confusions: epistemology vs ontology, and "sociology (how people behave) with morality (how they ought to behave)."[^4] On a purely societal standard, the appeal collapses — hence Turek's question, "Which society? Mother Teresa's or Hitler's?" — since "society" is just a changeable collection of humans, "which is why we had World War II in the first place."[^5]

He drives it home with the chemistry of morality: "Morality isn't made of molecules. What does justice weigh?... Did Hitler just have 'bad' molecules?"[^6] An objective standard requires "an objective, unchanging judge who has supreme authority," which no finite, changeable human can be.[^7] Turek extends the point to *rights*: abortion, same-sex marriage, and the like cannot be grounded as real rights in an atheistic universe any more than the right to life can — "without God they aren't really rights at all," only "subjective preferences."[^8] The consistent atheist, he says, "can't believe that anyone has ever actually changed the world for the better," since reforms by Wilberforce, Lincoln, and King "wasn't really good; it was just different."[^9]

## Best Quotes
> "If David comes to his senses and wants to take back his outlandish assertion that eating babies and murdering six million Jews is just a matter of opinion, he would have to appeal to an unchanging, authoritative standard outside of himself. But that's exactly what atheists don't have. They have molecules. They don't have God." [^2]

> "So at Nuremburg then we really had no right to convict the Nazis... — The hard answer is you're correct. The hard answer is it is a matter of opinion." [^3]

> "Let me go out on a limb and suggest that if your worldview requires you to believe that raping children, murdering children, eating children, and slaughtering six million innocent people is just a matter of opinion, then you have the wrong worldview." [^10]

## LDS Connection
The borrowing argument is fully congenial to LDS apologetics: to condemn the Holocaust as truly evil presupposes an objective, eternal standard that only a divine source can ground. The LDS nuance sharpens the point rather than softening it — because the standard is *eternal law* that God himself honors ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]; [[D&C 130.20-21]]), evil is "really not right" against a fixed measure, not merely against God's say-so. This is precisely Lewis's observation that the moral innovator always smuggles in a fragment of the Tao to attack the rest ([[After Humanity - Michael Ward]]; [[The Innovator Secretly Smuggles In the Tao]]): the atheist's outrage at genocide is itself drawn from the objective value system being denied. And the LDS doctrine of human worth — every person bearing the image of God ([[Genesis 1 Image of God as Seedbed of Rights]]) — supplies the intrinsic value of the victim that Turek says atheism reduces to "zero."[^11]
