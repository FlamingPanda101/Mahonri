# Recognizing the Parallel Is Not the Same as the Theological Conclusion

## The Point
Critics' supposed trump card: if [[Michael Heiser]] and [[Margaret Barker]] see all these parallels to LDS theology, why don't they convert? Arnold answers that recognizing a historical parallel is not the same as accepting a theological conclusion — the historical observation stands regardless of which interpretation one layers on top of it. This both defuses the objection and clarifies what the evidence does and does not prove.

## Evidence
- **The category distinction.** "Recognizing a historical parallel isn't the same as accepting a theological conclusion. A historian who establishes that early American law drew heavily from English common law hasn't thereby become British." [^1]
- **How Barker and Heiser actually interpret it.** Barker holds that Christ "fulfilled and completed what the First Temple pointed toward and that her tradition is the mature form of the original"; Heiser holds the divine-council story "reaches its resolution in the New Testament's understanding of Christ." Both "recognize it as genuinely ancient, and interpret that antiquity as evidence that it represents an earlier stage of a journey that continued further. They simply see the LDS position as stopping short." [^2]
- **The LDS rejoinder.** "The original gospel is the fullness. What apostasy produced wasn't a more developed form of truth. It was an institutionalized, philosophically overlaid version that lost both the doctrines and the priesthood authority structure that the original contained." [^3]
- **What the evidence does and doesn't do.** The scholars "can confirm that the ancient Hebrew text has divine council theology... can verify that the First Temple preserved deification doctrine... But at the end of the day, none of that tells you whether Joseph Smith was a prophet. That's a conclusion that the evidence informs, but it doesn't answer on its own." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "A historian who establishes that early American law drew heavily from English common law hasn't thereby become British." [^1]

> "They see us as ancient and consider it a limitation. We just consider it as the credential." [^5]

> "The LDS position isn't that the evidence proves church is true. The position is that the evidence is inconsistent with the version of the story that Joseph Smith invented something novel and fraudulent." [^6]

## LDS Connection
This note frames the epistemic ceiling on the whole "1844 confirmed" project: the corroboration in [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]] and [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category]] establishes *antiquity and consistency*, not the prophetic claim itself. It is the careful counterpart to the broader trend documented in [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]] and the restoration thesis of [[LDS Theology as Restoration of Early Christianity]] — and it honestly concedes that non-LDS scholars can grant every parallel and still decline the conclusion.
