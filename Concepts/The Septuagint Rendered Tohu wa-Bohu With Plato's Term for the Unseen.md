# The Septuagint Rendered Tohu wa-Bohu With Plato's Term for the Unseen

## The Claim

When the Septuagint (LXX) translators put the opening of Genesis into Greek, the way they handled the chaos-phrase of [[Genesis 1.2|Genesis 1:2]] shows they were reading the text through a **pre-existent-matter cosmology**, not *creatio ex nihilo*. For the Hebrew תֹהוּ וָבֹהוּ (*tohu wa-bohu*, "formless and void") they wrote **ἀόρατος καὶ ἀκατασκεύαστος** (*aoratos kai akataskeuastos*) — "**unseen and unorganized**."[^1] The first of those words, *aoratos*, is not a neutral choice: it is a **distinctive philosophical term used by Plato** to denote the "unseen" preexisting world of Forms (Sophist 246a–c; Theaetetus 155e; Timaeus 51a). The Genesis translator's word for the primordial state therefore "expresses something of Platonic cosmology in biblical guise, perhaps joining the cosmologies of Plato and Moses, as was a commonplace in Hellenistic Jewish thought, particularly in Alexandria."[^1]

The apologetic payoff: the earliest *translation* of Genesis — the Bible of Hellenistic Judaism and of the New Testament authors — pictured the initial state of [[Genesis 1.2|Gen 1:2]] as **unorganized, pre-existent stuff awaiting ordering**, exactly the *creatio ex materia* picture. Even as the Greek beginning ("Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἐποίησεν") moved toward the later independent-clause rendering that [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading|underwrites ex nihilo]], its own vocabulary for the chaos betrays a substrate-not-nothing cosmology.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The lexical fact.** Ron Hendel: "Corresponding to *tohu wa-bohu*, the [Septuagint] translator wrote ἀόρατος καὶ ἀκατασκεύαστος 'unseen and unorganized.'" The phrase is even "rendered in two words joined with a conjunction, exactly like the Hebrew *Vorlage*."[^1] *(Evidence rating: strong — this is the actual LXX text of Gen 1:2, verifiable.)*

2. **Aoratos is a Platonic technical term.** "Scholars have noted that ἀόρατος is a distinctive philosophical term in Greek, used by Plato to denote the 'unseen' preexisting world of ideas (Sophist 246a–c; Theaetetus 155e; Timaeus 51a)."[^1] Hendel cites Hanhart, Harl, and Rösel for the point. *(Evidence rating: strong for the citation; the inference that the translator consciously imported Platonism is reasonable but acknowledged by Hendel as a "glimpse," i.e., suggestive rather than certain.)*

3. **Alexandrian context makes it plausible.** Fusing "the cosmologies of Plato and Moses… was a commonplace in Hellenistic Jewish thought, particularly in Alexandria"[^1] — the same milieu in which [[Philo Did Not Endorse Creation Ex Nihilo|Philo read Genesis through Platonic preexistent matter]]. This connects to the broader pattern that [[Greek Categories Are Anachronistic to the Hebrew Bible|later Greek metaphysical categories were layered onto the Hebrew text]]. *(Evidence rating: corroborating — situates the word choice in a known interpretive culture.)*

**Steelman of the traditional reading:** Hendel himself frames the Platonic resonance cautiously — "within the constraints of a literal translation, *something* of contemporary Platonic cosmology may shine through" — which concedes that the translators were primarily seeking a literal Greek equivalent for an obscure Hebrew phrase, not consciously catechizing readers in Middle Platonism. *Aoratos* can simply mean "invisible/unseen" in ordinary Greek (an earth covered in darkness is, trivially, unseen), so the loaded philosophical sense is an available reading, not a forced one. And it cuts the other way too: that the LXX moved Gen 1:1 toward "In the beginning God made…" is itself part of the trajectory by which the church later read the chapter as *ex nihilo*. The observation is a genuine and elegant tell, but it is a single translational data point, not a doctrinal statement by the translators.
