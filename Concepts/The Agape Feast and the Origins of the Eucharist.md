#type/historical

## The Point
In house-church worship, the group's gathering centered on a shared meal — sometimes called an *agape* feast — provided by the patron(ess) or supplied communally, concluded by partaking of bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus's Last Supper. This communal meal was the original setting of the Eucharist; over time the Eucharistic bread and wine were separated from the fellowship meal, which gradually disappeared.

## Evidence
1. After the kiss of peace in the atrium, the group proceeded to the triclinium to share a meal "provided by the patron(ess) or supplied by others" and concluded with bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus's Last Supper (Mark 14:22–26; cf. 1 Corinthians 10:14–21; 11:17–34) — "a practice that became the precedent for the later Christian Eucharist (the Sacrament)."
2. Brief descriptions of agape feasts and Eucharistic practice survive in the Didache 9–10, 14; Ignatius (Epistle to the Ephesians 5.2–3; Smyrnaeans 8.1); Justin Martyr, 1 Apology 61–67; Tertullian, Apology 39; and the Apostolic Tradition.
3. The communal-meal form preserved the Last Supper setting and recalled Passover; sharing a meal in the biblical world implied "a sharing of one's life."
4. Economic disparity occasionally accompanied such gatherings between upper- and lower-class members (cf. 1 Corinthians 11:33–34).
5. As congregations grew and liturgy formalized, the Eucharist became "the remnant of fellowship meals after the agape feasts had gradually disappeared."

## Best Quotes
> "The group would then proceed to the triclinium to share a meal (sometimes referred to as an agape feast) ... and would conclude the meal with partaking of bread and wine in remembrance of Jesus's Last Supper — a practice that became the precedent for the later Christian Eucharist (the Sacrament)." [^1]

> "A Eucharist of consecrated bread and wine (the remnant of fellowship meals after the agape feasts had gradually disappeared)." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The trajectory from a full shared meal toward a "token meal" of bread and wine illuminates how the modern sacrament's compact form descended from a communal banquet of fellowship.
