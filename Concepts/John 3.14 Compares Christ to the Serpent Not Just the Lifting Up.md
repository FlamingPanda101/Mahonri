# John 3.14 Compares Christ to the Serpent Not Just the Lifting Up

#type/claim

## The Claim

When Jesus says "as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up" ([[John 3.16|John 3:14]]), the meaningful comparison is **Christ to the serpent itself**, not merely the act of "lifting up." [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]], following [[James Charlesworth]]'s *The Good and Evil Serpent*, argue that interpreters have fixated on the verb "lifted up" specifically *to avoid* the scandal of Jesus likening himself to a snake. Charlesworth reports surveying "thousands" of commentaries on John and finding that nearly all discuss raising/lifting, while only "one or two" so much as mention that Jesus is comparing himself to a serpent — a "giant gaping void of silence" produced by modern cultural blinders. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

- **"Lifting up" is not even in the source text.** Charlesworth's decisive philological point: the [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] story John alludes to *never uses "raise up."* God tells Moses to "make a seraph" and "set it on a pole"; Moses sets a serpent on a pole. "He doesn't raise nothing." The "lifted up" language is "entirely an artifact of the New Testament" (and the Book of Mormon), so commentators who anchor the whole verse on a verb that is absent from Numbers are reading their own discomfort into the text. [^3][^4]
- **The cultural blinder is the actual obstacle, not the grammar.** The hosts characterize the standard move as: "Snakes bad, Jesus good, not the same" — a refusal to "try to equate him with the serpent because if you do, you'll just get confused." Charlesworth's remedy is to "get out of our modern world" and recover how the ancients saw the serpent before judging the comparison absurd. [^5][^6]
- **The comparison is coherent once the "good serpent" is recovered.** It is "not some iniquitous, confusing, difficult comparison": Israel's sacred stories knew a serpent that *healed*, *purified from sin*, and *saved from death* (see [[The Nehushtan Is a Healing Divine Serpent the Nephites and Early Christians Valued]]), and that is the serpent Jesus invokes. [^7]
- **Take the early-Christian witness seriously.** The hosts push back on the assumption that rabbinic readings always trump early-Christian ones; the first Christians (and the Nephites) read a divine, saving serpent into the text "for reasons," and "we should pay attention to what the first Christians thought." [^8]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The Greek verb *hypsoō* ("lift up / exalt") is a deliberate Johannine double entendre for the crucifixion-as-glorification (cf. John 8:28; 12:32), so John's emphasis on *being lifted up* is a real, intended theme, not merely an evasion. A defender can grant this: John can mean *both* — Christ is genuinely "lifted up" *and* genuinely likened to the healing serpent — and the point is only that the second half of the comparison has been suppressed, not that the first is illegitimate. A second objection: the analogy may be merely typological (look-and-live → believe-and-live) without implying any ontological "Christ *is* the good serpent." The hosts' reply is that the surrounding Israelite material ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]], the Nehushtan) supplies exactly such a divine serpent, so the stronger reading is available and historically grounded.

## Best Quotes

> "All but one or two… the commentator talks about, oh, this is a verse about raising up, raising up, raising up. What none of them notice is that the passage it's referring to — the story where Moses puts a serpent on a pole in Numbers 21 — doesn't use 'raising up' at all." [^1]

> "Raising up is entirely an artifact of the New Testament… He puts a serpent on a pole. He doesn't raise anything." [^3]

> "They want so bad not to look at Jesus and the serpent that instead they look at 'raising up,' right? So they kind of miss it." [^9]
