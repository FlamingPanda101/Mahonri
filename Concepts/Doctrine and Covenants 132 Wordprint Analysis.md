# Doctrine and Covenants 132 Wordprint Analysis
#type/claim

## The Claim
Stylometric ("wordprint") analysis of [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] — the revelation on plural and eternal marriage — shows that its language matches Joseph Smith's authorship and not Brigham Young's, and that the text was already in circulation while Joseph was alive. This makes the claim that Section 132 is a later, Brigham-era forgery (the core of [[Polygamy Denialism]]) "almost certainly impossible." Authorship statistics, not just provenance, tie the revelation to Joseph.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The statistical study.** Per [[Daniel C. Peterson]], the Interpreter Foundation's wordprint and statistical work (Fields et al.) found that Section 132's distinctive vocabulary and stylistic fingerprint pattern with Joseph Smith's other dictated revelations, defeating the hypothesis of post-Joseph composition.
- **Internal dating corroborates the data.** The revelation is recorded July 12, 1843 — nearly a year before Joseph's June 1844 death — so the stylometric verdict aligns with the documentary timeline rather than contradicting it.
- **Why it matters.** As the argument is summarized in [[Brigham Young as the Fall Guy]]: "If Section 132 goes back to Joseph, then Joseph was involved with plural marriage. There's no way out." The wordprint closes the escape route that would relocate the practice onto Brigham.
- **Steelman.** Wordprint/stylometry is a contested method (sample-size sensitivity, dictation vs. autograph, scribal smoothing), so a critic can fairly say no single stylometric study is dispositive on its own. The vault's point is cumulative: the wordprint is one strand braided with the 1843 dating, multiple independent witnesses, and the [[Succession Crisis of 1844]] evidence — together they overwhelm a forgery hypothesis even if any one strand is individually challengeable.
