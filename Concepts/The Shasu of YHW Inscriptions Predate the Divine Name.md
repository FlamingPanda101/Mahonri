# The Shasu of YHW Inscriptions Predate the Divine Name

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] points to Egyptian inscriptions from the early 14th and 13th centuries BCE that refer to the **"Shasu of the land of YHW"** (spelled with the letters *Y-H-W* plus a glottal stop). Most scholars think this has some **genetic relationship to the divine name Yahweh**, even though in the inscription it is *not yet a deity* — it names a **land or a people**. That is exactly the ingredient the [[The Kenite-Midianite Hypothesis - Yahweh as a Southern Desert God|Kenite-Midianite hypothesis]] needs: a southern nomadic group whose god could be named after their land or tribe. McClellan names **Daniel Fleming**'s *Yahweh before Israel* as the key treatment and his favorite theory of Yahweh's origins.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The inscriptions and their date (rated: strong; epigraphic).** "We have inscriptions from the early 14th century BCE and then the 13th century BCE that refer to the Shasu of the land of, and then we have four letters YHW and then what corresponds to a glottal stop in Egyptian."[^1] These are the oldest texts plausibly connected to the divine name — older than any inscription that names Yahweh *as a god*.

2. **A people/place name, not yet a god (rated: moderate; the crucial nuance).** "Most scholars think that this has some kind of genetic relationship to the divine name, although it is not yet a divine name in this inscription. It is either the name of a land or the name of a people."[^2] McClellan even suggests the Exodus 3 derivation from the verb "to be" is a later folk etymology — "it could just be a name of a place or a people and not actually based on a sentence or a verb."[^3]

3. **It supplies the missing carrier (rated: moderate; fits the hypothesis).** "That would be just the right ingredient for these people to come into the land of Israel with their deity named after their people group or their land. And that might be the origin of the divine name Yahweh, the tetragrammaton."[^2] The Shasu link is what turns the southern-origin idea from speculation into a testable reconstruction.

4. **Fleming's treatment is the lead authority (rated: source-attribution).** "Daniel Fleming's *Yahweh before Israel: Glimpses of History in a Divine Name* discusses the Egyptian inscriptions and their relationship to his theory of the origins of Yahweh."[^4] McClellan adds, "I'm most interested in Fleming's theory about the origins of Yahweh."[^5] See [[Dan Fleming]].

5. **Steelman / limits.** Skeptics note the reading is not airtight: the Egyptian *YHW* could be coincidental homophony, the toponym's location is debated, and a place-name link does not by itself prove the *Israelite* god derives from it. McClellan presents it as the most promising line, not a demonstration — "we don't know for sure."[^3]

## LDS Relevance
The earliest trace of "Yahweh" being a *place/people* later personified as Israel's God reinforces that the biblical deity has a developmental history rather than being the eternal, simple, *ex nihilo* absolute of the creeds. That historical contingency is the wedge Latter-day Saint apologists use to argue the original theology was a divine council/family — see [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]] and [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]]. (McClellan frames this as mainstream critical scholarship, not as an LDS claim.)
