#type/claim

# The Priestly Creation Account Was Composed Later as a Correction

## The Claim

The non-Priestly account ([[Genesis 2.4-7|Genesis 2:4b]]–3) is the **earlier** of the two creation stories. The Priestly account ([[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]]–2:4a) "was almost certainly composed later and in response to and as a correction of some of the things that the priestly authors didn't like about the earlier creation account."[^1] The canonical *order* (Genesis 1 first) is therefore the reverse of the compositional order.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Relative dating (strong).** McClellan states the second account "is almost certainly the earlier of the two," and the Priestly account "almost certainly composed later."[^1] This reflects mainstream Pentateuchal source criticism, in which the Priestly source is typically dated to the exilic/post-exilic period and the non-Priestly (often "J") material earlier. Evidence rating: **strong** (consensus position, though P's absolute date is debated).

2. **Corrective intent (moderate).** The claim that P was written partly to "correct" features of the older account explains the theological "cleanups" in Genesis 1 — e.g., a transcendent God who creates by ordered speech over seven structured days, vegetation and animals in a tidy taxonomy, humanity made last and in the divine image — versus the anthropomorphic, hands-in-the-dirt potter-God of Genesis 2. Evidence rating: **moderate** (reasonable inference; intent is reconstructed, not stated in the text).

3. **Documented in the recommended literature (strong).** McClellan points to [[David Carr]]'s *The Formation of Genesis 1–11: Biblical and Other Precursors* as a detailed discussion of "where the authors seem to be getting all of this."[^2] Evidence rating: **strong** (peer-reviewed source).

**Steelman:** Some scholars (e.g., minimalist or "neo-documentarian" critics) dispute the relative dating of P vs. non-P and whether P was ever an independent narrative or only a redactional layer. "Corrective intent" in particular is an interpretive reconstruction. McClellan's claim is the majority view but not unanimous, and he flags it with "almost certainly" rather than certainty.

## Best Quotes

> "This was almost certainly composed later and in response to and as a correction of some of the things that the priestly authors didn't like about the earlier creation account." [^1]
