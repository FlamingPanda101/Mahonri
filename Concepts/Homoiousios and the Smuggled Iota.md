# Homoiousios and the Smuggled Iota

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] recounts the famous *iota* episode at and after the [[Council of Nicaea|Council of Nicaea]]: the orthodox party adopted **homoousios** (ὁμοούσιος, "of the *same* substance") to define the Son's relation to the Father, while a dissenting bishop "really quickly added a little iota" to make it **homoiousios** (ὁμοιούσιος, "of *like* substance") — a single-letter change that downgraded the Son from identical-in-essence to merely similar.[^1] The anecdote dramatizes how the entire christological settlement turned on one Greek letter, and that the difference was enforced by banishment.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The lexical contrast is real and exactly as described — rate: strong
*Homoousios* (same substance) vs. *homoiousios* (like substance) differ by the single letter iota (ι). This is the most famous one-letter dispute in church history and is the literal source of the English idiom "not one iota of difference." McClellan's gloss — "of like substance rather than of the same substance" — is precise.[^1] *Rating: strong; textbook accurate.* See [[Consubstantiality]].

### 2. The theological stakes were genuine — rate: strong
The two terms encode fundamentally different Christologies. *Homoousios* makes the Son fully and identically divine (Nicene orthodoxy); *homoiousios* — the semi-Arian compromise associated with figures like Basil of Ancyra in the decades after 325 — makes the Son *like* the Father in essence but stops short of numerical identity, preserving a measure of subordination. *Rating: strong; this captures the post-Nicene controversy that raged until Constantinople (381).* This connects to [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]] and [[Two Defining Features of Deity Eternal and Unchanging]].

### 3. The "snuck in a letter, then banished" story is compressed folklore — rate: moderate
McClellan tells it as a quick anecdote ("a little iota... and then, you know, banished"), and Beecher jokes "you can't just sneak in a letter."[^2] Historically the *homoiousios* position was not a single furtive insertion at Nicaea but a sustained party-position that contended for decades; the framing is a memorable simplification rather than a literal event. *Rating: moderate — the lexical and theological content is accurate, the "sneaking" dramatization is folkloric compression.* **Steelman:** a defender would note that the *homoiousian* party included serious theologians making a good-faith case that *homoousios* risked modalism (collapsing the persons), so the iota debate was substantive theology, not a clerical trick.
