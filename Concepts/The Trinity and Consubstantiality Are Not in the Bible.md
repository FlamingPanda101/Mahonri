# The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that what Latter-day Saints reject in the Nicene Creed is *only two things* — **creatio ex nihilo** and **consubstantiality** (*homoousios*) — and that *neither* is taught in the Bible. The Bible never calls the persons "consubstantial," never uses "substance" to define their relation, and uses "Godhead" only in three loosely-glory-laden passages ([[Acts 17.28-29|Acts 17:29]], Romans 1:20, [[Colossians 2.9|Colossians 2:9]]). The biblical word for the Father–Son relation is *hypostasis* (relation/glory), not *ousia* (substance). [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (Supporting Evidence)

1. **"Godhead" renders three *different* Greek words, all about *participation in glory*.** *to theion* (Acts 17:29), *theiotēs* (Rom 1:20), *theotēs* (Col 2:9) — Ostler reads each as "a fullness of glory" / "participating fully in the divine properties," not a multi-personal substance. *Rating: partly sound* (three different lexemes is correct) *but lexically strained* (*theotēs* most naturally = deity). [^3][^4]

2. **No "consubstantial" / "substance" language exists in the NT.** "There's no term ever where God is called consubstantial in the Bible." *Rating: factually correct* — *homoousios* is a 4th-century conciliar term, not a biblical one (cf. [[Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity]]). [^5]

3. **The biblical relational term is *hypostasis*, used of the Father–Son relation.** See [[Hypostasis Describes the Father-Son Relation Not Two Natures]]. The unity the Bible describes is *indwelling glory* (*plērōma*), not shared substance. *Rating: consistent with the rest of the source's case.* [^6]

4. **Ostler concedes most of the creed.** "If we read the Nicene Creed, there's not a lot we're going to disagree with... except for the assertion of creation ex nihilo and the use of the term substance." He even calls "Godhead" a legitimately *biblical* term. *Rating: an unusually irenic concession* that narrows the real dispute to two metaphysical claims. [^7]

5. **Steelman of the Trinitarian.** Doctrines need not appear *verbatim* to be biblical (neither does "monotheism," "Bible," or "incarnation"); *homoousios* was coined precisely to *protect* the biblical data (John 1:1; 8:58; 20:28; Col 2:9; Heb 1:3) against Arian subordinationism. And *creatio ex nihilo*, while debated, is defended from Rom 4:17, Heb 11:3, and 2 Macc 7:28 (see [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]]). *Rating: a strong reply* — "the word isn't in the Bible" is not itself an argument against a doctrine; the question is whether the *concept* best explains the texts. The real fault line is the *prior* metaphysics (creation/eternal matter), which Ostler rightly isolates. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "What is it in the Nicene Creed that we would reject? There's only two things. Creation out of nothing and this term consubstantiality, the homoousios." [^1]

> "One thing we can say with certainty is that the Trinity doesn't appear in the Bible. There's no term ever where God is called consubstantial in the Bible." [^5]

> "It actually uses the term hypostasis to define the relationship between the Father and the Son, not between the divine nature and the human nature of Christ." [^6]
