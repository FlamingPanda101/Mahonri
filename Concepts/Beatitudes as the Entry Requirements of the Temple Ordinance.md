# Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance

#type/claim

## The Claim
Within the reading that [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the Sermon on the Mount is a temple ordinance]], [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] (following [[Jack Welch|Jack Welch]]) treats the **Beatitudes as the ordinance's entry requirements** — "not just anyone can get into this ordinance; people need to . . . meet some kind of moral standard." [^1] He adds two structural observations he says will "matter" when [[Nephi]]'s brother Jacob plays on the text in 2 Nephi 9: there are **nine beatitude statements but only seven distinct blessings** (two blessings are duplicated), and *seven* is the number of branches on the temple [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary|menorah]] — "there is a tree in the temple; how many branches does it have? Seven. We have seven blessings here." [^2] He also recovers the term: Latin *beati* / Greek *makarioi* renders Hebrew *ashrei*, "O the happiness of," and — citing [[LeGrand Baker]] — *makarioi* in antiquity named those "likened to the gods," the **blessed/deified ones**, which loads the Beatitudes with an initiatory promise of becoming "one of the shining ones." [^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **"Entry requirement" fits the ordinance's logic.** If the Sermon is a graded ascent, *something* must screen entrants; a list of dispositional qualifications at the very front is the natural candidate, and it parallels the recommend-interview function described in [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]] ("is your holiness sufficient to get you in the door?"). *Rating: internally coherent given the ordinance premise.*

2. **The 9-vs-7 count is a real textual feature.** Matthew's Beatitudes do run to nine *blessed-are* statements while collapsing to roughly seven distinct promised blessings (the kingdom of heaven and the persecution clauses repeat). That asymmetry is observable in the text and is exactly the kind of structural seam that invites a numerological-ritual reading. *Rating: the count is accurate; the menorah linkage is suggestive, not demonstrative.*

3. **The *makarios* / deification gloss has scholarly backing.** Reading the "blessed" as the god-likened ones is defended in LDS temple scholarship (LeGrand Baker; cf. the *maat/maar* "blessed" line in the channel's [[Egyptian Funerary Texts Democratized From Pharaoh to Everyone|Egyptian]] episode) and dovetails with the promises attached ("they shall see God," "be called the children of God"). *Rating: a defensible semantic enrichment, drawing on the Beatitudes' own apodoses.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The 9-vs-7 numerology is the soft spot: the reduction to "seven" requires choosing *which* duplicates to merge, and a motivated reader can tune the count to hit the menorah's seven (the same elasticity flagged in the parent note's parallelomania objection). Mainstream exegesis reads the Beatitudes as eschatological blessings/wisdom-form *ashrei* sayings with no ritual gatekeeping function, and *makarios* most often just means "fortunate/blessed," the deification sense being a minority specialization. A Latter-day Saint reply concedes the count is interpretively framed but notes that *Jacob's* independent play on the same text in 2 Nephi 9 — a Book-of-Mormon author reworking the beatitude pattern — is the actual payload, and it is that cross-text reuse, not the bare number seven, that carries the weight.
