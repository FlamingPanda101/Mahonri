# God Could Have Created Only the Saved

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Hayden Carroll]] presses a sub-argument inside [[The Creation Dilemma]]: "Is it possible that God could have only created people that he knew would accept him?" If an omnipotent, omniscient, free God can create *some* free-willed person who is never damned, he could in principle create *only* such persons — so the existence of any damned person is a gratuitous choice. In cross-exam he extracts from Gavin (as a Roman Catholic) the admission that God *can* create sinless free agents.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Catholic-doctrine leverage** — rate: strong as an internal critique of Catholicism. Gavin affirms Mary was created sinless and free; and that figures like Enoch, Elijah, Moses (and Mary) were "translated" / saved without normal death. If God can make a free person who ends in heaven, "then why create anybody who's going to go to hell?" *(internal-critique)* [^1]
2. **No coercion required** — because these saved persons are still *free*, the option does not require God to override anyone's will, neutralizing the "forced love" reply. See [[Free Will Is a Red Herring to the Creation Dilemma]]. *(logical)* [^2]
3. **Closing form** — "If you're an all-good God, why do you need people in hell?" *(rhetorical-climax)* [^2]

**Steelman of the affirmative (Molinist core):** this is the crux Molinism is built to answer. Via *middle knowledge*, it may be that there is **no feasible world** in which the persons who freely accept God exist *and* no one freely rejects him — the free choices of others (and the very identities of persons) are bundled across possible worlds, so "create only the saved" may not be a genuinely available option even to omnipotence (you cannot actualize *which* free choices creatures make). A world with the redeemed may be unavailable without also containing the lost. Hayden does **not** engage middle knowledge by name, which is the most significant gap in his case. Mary's sinlessness is also a special Catholic case (immaculate conception by prevenient grace), arguably not generalizable.
