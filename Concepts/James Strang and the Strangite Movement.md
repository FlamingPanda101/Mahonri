# James Strang and the Strangite Movement

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] — whose PhD dissertation was on James Strang's church after his death — calls Strang his "favorite" figure in the diverse landscape of Mormonism and recounts his extraordinary story as a case study in why "Mormonism" cannot be reduced to the Utah church. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (the Strang narrative)

1. **A succession claimant by letter.** Strang converted in February 1844, "just months before Joseph Smith is assassinated," and immediately afterward "produces a letter purportedly from Joseph Smith appointing him to be the next prophet." [^3] *Rating: historically attested;* the authenticity of the "Letter of Appointment" is disputed by historians, which is part of its interest.

2. **A second set of plates.** Strang "claims to discover plates and translates them," receiving "something called the Book of the Law of the Lord from the plates of Laban later." [^4] *Rating: accurate;* the Voree plates and the Book of the Law of the Lord are documented Strangite scripture — and a direct parallel to Joseph Smith's own plates-and-translation pattern (see [[Joseph Smith Did Not Author the Book of Mormon]]).

3. **Monogamy, then not; a disguised wife.** Strang was "a monogamistic alternative to Brigham Young until he's not," secretly wedding Elvira Field and concealing it "by dressing her up in boys' clothing and passing Elvira off as James' 16-year-old nephew." [^5] *Rating: corroborated* by Strangite history.

4. **King on an island; assassinated by his own.** He founded "what can only be described as a proto-Marxist society on an island in Lake Michigan [Beaver Island]," "pronounces himself the king of the kingdom of God on earth," and "is ultimately assassinated by disaffected followers in cooperation with the United States Navy." [^6] *Rating: substantially accurate,* a striking and well-documented episode.

**Steelman of the LDS view.** A Latter-day Saint would point out that Strang's movement collapsed after his death while Brigham Young's succession produced the enduring church — evidence, in the LDS reading, of where the genuine authority lay. Beshears's interest is historical and illustrative (the breadth of the restoration), not an endorsement of Strang's claims, which he treats as parallel to Joseph's rather than vindicating either.
