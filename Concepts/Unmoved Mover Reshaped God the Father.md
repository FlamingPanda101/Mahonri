# Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father

## The Claim
Aristotle's "unmoved mover" — a being that causes all motion but is itself unmoved, "pure actuality without matter," that cannot feel or be changed — was absorbed by Christian apologists and became the template for God the Father: immaterial, impassible, immutable. This is the specific philosophical mechanism behind the broader Hellenization of God ([[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]]).

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Aristotle's concept stated (rating: strong; intellectual history)
[[Aristotle]] (pupil of [[Plato]], teacher of Alexander) defined the ultimate cause as "something that moves things but itself isn't moved" — "pure actuality without matter," with ultimate power but unable to be moved. Day reads the slide concluding "this unmoving mover had to be pure actuality... that idea is going to be who God the Father is going to turn into." [^1]

### 2. The biblical God is the opposite (rating: strong; canonical)
"The Bible describes a God with passions, especially a God that's embodied. Jesus is embodied. But God also has passions in the Old Testament — he's sorry, he gets angry, he smells the sacrifice. This is a Yahweh narrative." Contrast [[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]: the Father has a body of flesh and bones. The impassible/immaterial Mover is irreconcilable with this. [^2]

### 3. The apologists' marketing pitch (rating: moderate; reconstructed)
Day reconstructs the sales pitch to a Platonist: "the *logos*, the *nous*, the mind, is actually God the Father, and God the Father sent his son who is the *logos*... and he took upon himself flesh." The unmoved-mover Father plus the incarnate-*logos* Son lets the apologist "talk the same language" as the philosophically educated. [^3]

### 4. It seeds later doctrines (rating: moderate; downstream effects)
The impassible, immaterial God underwrites *ex nihilo* creation and the metaphysical apparatus of the creeds (*ousia*, *hypostasis*). See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]] and [[Homoousios Same Substance Not Same Person]]. [^1]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Classical theists argue divine impassibility and immateriality are not Aristotle smuggled in but conclusions the church drew from scripture itself: a God who "changes not" (Mal 3:6), who is "spirit" (John 4:24), who is not a man that he should repent (Num 23:19) — the anthropopathic "passions" being accommodative language. Aquinas would later integrate the unmoved mover precisely because he judged it *consonant* with revelation, not a corruption of it. Day's reply: the embodiment of the Son and the passionate Yahweh narrative are too central to dissolve into metaphor, and the result strips God of personhood (see [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]]).
