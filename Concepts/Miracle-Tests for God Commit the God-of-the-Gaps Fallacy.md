# Miracle-Tests for God Commit the God-of-the-Gaps Fallacy

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Many atheists answer "What would convince you God exists?" with a spectacular miracle: regrowing an amputated limb through prayer, rearranging the stars to spell "God exists," or finding "made by Yahweh" written in the human genome [^1]. Concrete versions cited: Emerson Green/"Evan" — if the stars realigned to spell *mene mene tekel upharsin* (the writing on the wall at Belshazzar's feast, [[Daniel 5.25|Daniel 5:25]]), "that would convince me of theism or polytheism. No other explanation is remotely plausible" [^2]; Doug from the Pine Creek channel — since [[Elijah]] called down fire on a soaked sacrifice to beat the prophets of Baal ([[1 Kings 18.24|1 Kings 18:24]]), Christians should be able to ignite "a wet napkin on fire through prayer" [^3].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The core flaw is identical across all versions.** [[Trent Horn]] (quoted) argues every such test reduces to: "I don't know what caused [the limb to heal / the stars to move / the napkin to catch fire]. I can't imagine a natural cause, therefore God must have done it" — i.e., the **God-of-the-gaps fallacy**: *I don't know what caused X, therefore God exists* [^4][^5]. Rating: **strong** — it correctly identifies that an inference from present ignorance of a natural cause to a supernatural one is invalid; a future natural explanation (or an unknown agent) is always live.
2. **It makes the atheist's own position unfalsifiable.** Horn warns that atheists "who lean too much into the God of the gaps objection... will end up making atheism as unfalsifiable as some religions" — because for any proposed sign they can always say "I just don't yet know the natural cause" [^6]. Rating: **a clever judo move** — it turns the unfalsifiability charge (usually leveled at religion) back on the miracle-demanding skeptic.
3. **Why** *these* **signs? — the unexamined premise.** The narrator presses: "Why would these particular things convince you that God exists?" [^5]. The demanded miracles convince only by exploiting a gap in current knowledge, not by positively identifying a divine agent. Rating: **fair**; even a star-rearrangement is, in principle, "just" an unexplained event unless one already imports the inference to a personal God.
4. **The constructive payoff.** Because public-miracle tests are epistemically defective, the better route is the **personal, experiential** one O'Connor himself prefers (see [[Personal Revelation Is the Most Legitimate Path to God]]) [^7]. Rating: the link is **rhetorically effective** — having dismantled the skeptic's preferred evidence, the video redirects to the LDS epistemology.
5. **Steelman the miracle-demander.** The skeptic's strongest reply: not all proposed signs are equal. A truly *agent-revealing*, low-prior, semantically-loaded event — stars spelling a coherent multilingual sentence, or a regrown limb *immediately upon prayer to a specific deity* — is not merely "an unexplained event"; it is improbable on naturalism to a degree that a design/agency inference becomes the best explanation (inference to the best explanation, not gap-stuffing). "Evan's" own framing ("no other explanation is remotely plausible") is precisely an attempt to escape the gaps charge by appealing to probability, not ignorance. Honest tension: the line between "gap fallacy" and "legitimate inference to a designing agent" is exactly where the real debate lives, and the video resolves it more by assertion than argument.
