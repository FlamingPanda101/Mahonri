# Street Preachers Engage the Unprepared but Refuse the Prepared

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] observes a behavioral asymmetry in anti-Mormon open-air preaching: the preachers freely engage uninformed passers-by, but **decline good-faith dialogue with a knowledgeable Latter-day Saint**, sometimes ending the exchange by asserting a privileged spiritual standing the other party supposedly lacks. The methodological charge is that *evangelism aimed only at the unequipped, while avoiding equipped interlocutors, behaves less like truth-seeking persuasion and more like a numbers game* — and that terminating a conversation with "I have the Holy Spirit, you don't" substitutes a status claim for an argument and so concedes the field rhetorically.

> "I just think it's funny that you guys will talk to people who aren't prepared to talk, but when somebody's prepared and ready to chat about it, you say, 'Sorry, I don't want to chat about it.'" [^1]

The conversation-ending move that bookends the video:

> "I got the Holy Spirit living inside me. You don't… And that's the end of the conversation." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The refusal is explicit and repeated.** One preacher tells Carroll plainly, "Correct… I'm here to reach these people. These are my primary mission fields tonight," and "I have no interest in talking to you, man."[^3] Carroll repeatedly offers to defer ("maybe after, man") and to exchange contact information for a later interview, and is declined.[^3]
2. **The asymmetry is the point, not the timing.** Even granting that a given moment is busy, the preachers had, by their own account, "talked to you 20 or 30 times" and judged Carroll to have "a gross misunderstanding" — yet would not let him state his actual position, which is precisely the condition under which a misunderstanding could be corrected.[^4]
3. **The terminating claim is a status assertion, not a reason.** "I got the Holy Spirit living inside me. You don't" ends dialogue by *fiat of spiritual rank*. It is unfalsifiable from the outside and, notably, is the same form of claim the preacher elsewhere rejects when Latter-day Saints appeal to a spiritual witness — so it cuts both ways.[^2]
4. **Pattern, not one-off.** Diego *does* engage at length, but in Carroll's telling never answers the load-bearing question ("You never answer my question… Because the word of God… How do you know?"), looping instead to assertion.[^5] So the video documents two adjacent failure modes: flat refusal, and engagement that evades. Cf. the **sincerity test** in [[Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue]] — a good-faith interlocutor responds to your answer; a bad-faith one moves on.

## Steelman (the preachers' side)
The street preacher's stance is not simply cowardice, and fairness requires saying why:
- **Triage of scarce time.** An open-air evangelist at a crowded venue has minutes and a transient audience. Spending an hour on one well-read apologist who is *not going to convert tonight* is a real opportunity cost against the dozens of curious bystanders who might. "I'm here for them tonight" is a defensible allocation rule, not obviously bad faith.[^3]
- **Asymmetry of purpose.** Their goal is *proclamation to the receptive*, not *debate with the equipped*. Declining a debate one did not come to have is legitimate; not every evangelist is obligated to be a polemicist, and some honestly know they are outmatched on the fine points.
- **Avoiding a hostile-camera setup.** A filmed challenge from a channel host can be a trap as much as a conversation; refusing to perform for someone else's content is prudent, not dishonest.
- **The Spirit claim, charitably.** "The Holy Spirit lives in me" can be a sincere confession of regeneration (their soteriology) rather than mere one-upmanship — though as a *conversation-ender* it still functions as an exit, not an answer.

Where the steelman runs out: none of these justify the specific *asymmetry* Carroll names. Triage explains declining *everyone* equally when busy; it does not explain a standing willingness to engage the uninformed while avoiding the informed. And a movement confident in its case should welcome its best-prepared critics, not screen them out. The honest version of the preacher's position is "I'm not the right person / this isn't the right moment for that debate" — which several of them, to their credit, gestured at ("maybe after").
