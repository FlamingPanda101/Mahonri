# The Satanic Panic Reached the LDS Church in Utah

## The Claim
The satanic ritual abuse panic did not stop at McMartin — it reached over the Wasatch Mountains into the Latter-day Saint community in Utah, where it took its "most lasting and fertile soil." This is the framing premise of the *Glenn L Pace Memo* series, which is named for the SRA controversy involving the LDS Church.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Utah was hit hardest (strong; thesis statement)
"A lesser-known [story] is how the ghoulish fingers of that hysteria reached over the Wasatch Mountains and into the minds of the Latter-day Saints in quiet suburban Utah. Ironically, Utah caught the panic harder than anywhere else" [^1].

### 2. Accusations against clergy and officials (strong)
SRA accusations "are leveled at local lay clergy and high-ranking church officials alike. People have been imprisoned, families shattered, and lives ruined all over again" in Salt Lake City — "the same specter of fear" 300 years after Salem [^2][^3]. See [[Satanic Panic Is a Recurrence of Salem-Style Hysteria]].

### 3. The irony of the setting (moderate)
Salt Lake City — LDS headquarters, in "the most family-friendly state in the nation" — was not protected "from the past and present ravages of the satanic panic." The episode poses the question of whether the faith "famous for happy families" is really shielding demonic abusers, "or is this just the echoes of a satanic panic still reverberating through what locals call Zion?" [^3][^4].

### 4. The promised continuation (moderate)
This episode is part one; it closes by promising that "in the next installment, we'll show how the satanic panic would find its most lasting and fertile soil up in the mountains of the most family-friendly state in the nation" [^1]. The series is hosted at glennlpacememo.com [^5].

### Steelman
A critic could argue that some abuse allegations in Utah were genuine and should not be dismissed as panic. The episode's stance is investigative rather than blanket-dismissive: it asks "is it real this time?" and builds the false-memory framework (see [[False Memory Syndrome Explains the SRA Accusations]]) as the lens for evaluating the Utah cases in later installments.
