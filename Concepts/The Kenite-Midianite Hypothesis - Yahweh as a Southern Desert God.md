# The Kenite-Midianite Hypothesis - Yahweh as a Southern Desert God

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] explains the **Kenite-Midianite hypothesis**: because the name Yahweh matches no regional pantheon and appears only late (see [[The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh]]), Yahweh was probably the **local or ancestral deity of a nomadic group** that migrated from further east or south — around the land of **Midian** — into the central and northern hill country, bringing their god with them. The biblical "Yahweh marches from the south" tradition (Sinai, Paran, Teman) may preserve an archaic social memory of this southern origin.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Yahweh is a stranger to the regional pantheons (rated: moderate; argument from absence).** "This is not a divine name that is known from any other pantheon from anywhere around this area." Scholars have proposed Ugaritic or Akkadian cognates, "but none of these candidates has been widely accepted."[^1] An imported, non-indigenous deity fits the data better than a native one.

2. **The "march from the south" texts (rated: moderate; biblical memory).** Passages "that talk about Yahweh coming forth from lands to the south and to the southeast of Israel, from Sinai, from Paran, from places like that… could preserve an archaic social memory of Yahweh's origins to the south or the southeast."[^2] These archaic poems (e.g., Deut 33, Judg 5, Hab 3) locate Yahweh's home in the southern deserts, not Canaan.

3. **A nomadic carrier group — possibly the Shasu (rated: moderate; working hypothesis).** "A nomadic group may be associated with the Shasu, which is an Egyptian designation for nomadic tribes around the Arabian Peninsula. They might have brought their deity Yahweh into the central and northern hill countries, and that deity then became their patron deity."[^3] This dovetails with the Egyptian "Shasu of YHW" inscriptions — see [[The Shasu of YHW Inscriptions Predate the Divine Name]].

4. **A southern storm/warrior profile (rated: moderate; comparative).** A desert deity who comes in storm and theophany matches the storm-god profile Yahweh shares with Baal elsewhere — the dynamic behind [[Elijah Contest Is About Territorial Storm-Deity Purview]]. The southern-origin theory explains why Yahweh, unlike El, reads as a mobile, marching warrior-god rather than an enthroned patriarch.

5. **Steelman / limits.** McClellan flags this as an active research program, not a settled result: Robert Miller II's attempt to locate Yahweh in Midian's archaeology is "a good discussion… I don't know if he's entirely successful."[^4] Traditional scholars counter that Sinai/Teman simply mark where Israel *met* Yahweh at the Exodus, not where the god originated, and that "social memory" claims are unfalsifiable. The hypothesis remains the most interesting candidate, in McClellan's view, rather than proof.

## LDS Relevance
The hypothesis reinforces that Israel's God has a concrete *history* — entering the land, acquiring a people, merging with El — rather than being the timeless absolute of later creeds. For Latter-day Saints this supports reading the Hebrew Bible's God as one being among a divine plurality whose profile developed over time, consistent with [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]] and the broader case that strict monotheism is a late overlay. (McClellan presents it as critical scholarship, not LDS apologetics.)
