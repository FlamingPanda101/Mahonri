# Divine Unity - There Can Be Only One God

## The Point
Feser opens the systematic treatment of the divine attributes with God's *unity* — "the fact that there is, and in principle can be, only one God." [^1] His claim is stronger than the empirical observation that there happens to be one God; it is that monotheism is *necessarily* true. The argument runs through pure actuality: for there to be more than one thing of a kind, the things must differ by some metaphysical part (a specific difference within a genus, or matter individuating members of a species), and having such parts entails having potentiality. [^2] But God is purely actual, devoid of potentiality, and therefore cannot belong to any kind of which there could be more than one instance. [^3] He is unique not by counting but by His very nature.

## Evidence
1. **Multiplicity requires composition.** "For there to be more than one thing of a certain kind, there must be a distinction between the thing and the species of which it is a member, or … between the species and the genus." [^2] Feser illustrates with *rational animal*: animality and rationality are distinct metaphysical parts, and individual humans are further distinguished by matter. Every such case "will involve a mixture of potentiality and actuality." [^2]
2. **Pure actuality excludes such parts.** Since God is purely actual and "devoid of potentiality … he cannot have parts of the sort in question, and therefore, he does not belong to a kind of which there could be more than one instance." [^3] The five proofs therefore "are not proofs of distinct deities … but rather distinct paths to one and the same God." [^4]
3. **Unity is inseparable from simplicity.** "It cannot be emphasized too strongly that the unity of God is inseparable from his simplicity." [^5] To deny simplicity makes God "a mere instance of a genus," reducing Him "to the status of a member of a pantheon of gods" — even a Zeus or Odin who happened to be the sole occupant of Olympus would still be merely *a* god, "a kind of superangel," and worshiping him "would therefore constitute a kind of idolatry." [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Let us begin with God's unity—the fact that there is, and in principle can be, only one God." [^1]

> "He … does not belong to a kind of which there could be more than one instance. He is, accordingly, unique, so that the theism to which the arguments … lead us is a monotheism." [^3]

> "It cannot be emphasized too strongly that the unity of God is inseparable from his simplicity." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Feser is right that the five proofs aim at a single ultimate, and Latter-day Saints are robust monotheists in worship: they worship the Father, in the name of the Son, through the Spirit, and no other. Where the divergence opens is in *why* and *how* God is one. Feser grounds uniqueness in **divine simplicity** — God cannot be one of a kind because He has no genus/species composition and no potentiality (see [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]] and [[Divine Attributes Follow From Pure Actuality]]). The Restoration instead teaches that God the Father is a perfected, embodied person who shares a *kind* with His children — "God and man are the same species" (see [[God and Man Are the Same Species]]) — and that the divine is a relational fellowship (the Godhead of Father, Son, and Holy Ghost as three persons "one in purpose"). On Feser's terms that very claim makes the Father "a member of a pantheon" or "a superangel"; on the Restoration's terms, Feser has purchased uniqueness at the price of the personal, familial God of scripture. The honest fork: Feser's argument is valid *given* that pure actuality is the right description of the first cause, so the LDS reply must contest that metaphysical step (see [[The Absolutely Simple Source - Why the Regress of Composers Must End in the One]]) rather than the logic — and can note that biblical "monotheism" coexists with a heavenly council and plural *elohim* language (see [[Contingency Argument Permits Plural Elohim]]).
