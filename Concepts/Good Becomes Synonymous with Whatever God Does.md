# Good Becomes Synonymous with Whatever God Does

## The Claim (Objection)
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues the defenses of trinitarian omnibenevolence collapse "good" into a tautology. If permitting/creating-for-hell still counts as "good," then "good" no longer tracks well-being, restoration, or benefit — it just means "whatever God does." At that point "instead of saying that God is good, it's like saying God is God. It renders the word meaningless." This is a Euthyphro-flavored move: divine goodness must remain *morally intelligible* to function as praise rather than tautology.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Intelligibility requirement** — rate: strong. For "omnibenevolent" to be a *substantive* claim (and thus debatable), "good" must carry recognizable content — willing someone's *well-being* / *benefit*. Hayden makes the criterion explicit in cross-exam: "if we want to define... willing someone's good as willing what's a benefit to the individual... I'm not sure creating someone knowing they're going to be eternally consciously tormented is for their well-being. It's the opposite. It's for their destruction." *(definitional)* [^2]
2. **Empties, not defends** — "at that point omnibenevolence has not been defended. It has been emptied." *(closing summary)* [^1]
3. **Whole-debate thesis** — Hayden frames the *entire* debate around this: "whether the word good still means what we say it means when we apply it to God." See [[The Creation Dilemma]]. *(framing)* [^3]

**Steelman of the affirmative:** this is essentially the *Euthyphro dilemma*, and the classical theist takes the third horn — God's *nature* (not his arbitrary will) is the standard of goodness, so "good" is neither arbitrary fiat nor a standard above God. On this view "good" retains content (it is grounded in God's immutable loving nature) while exceeding our full comprehension (analogical predication, see [[Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good]]). The charge of tautology only lands if divine goodness is *pure will*; classical theism denies that. Hayden's rejoinder: even a nature-grounded "good" must remain continuous with the human concept of benefiting persons, which creating-for-hell does not.

## Best Quotes
> "If good can include knowingly and freely creating someone who will enter into hell, then good no longer has a recognizable moral content. It becomes synonymous with whatever God does."[^1]

> "I'm not sure that creating someone knowing that they're going to be eternally consciously tormented is for their well-being. I think it's the opposite. It's for their destruction."[^2]
