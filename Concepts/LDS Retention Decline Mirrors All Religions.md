# LDS Retention Decline Mirrors All Religions

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A common narrative — fueled by family experience, media spotlight, and online voices — holds that Latter-day Saints "are just leaving in droves" and that "retention has never been lower," implying a uniquely catastrophic exodus from the Church.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The raw number is real but partial (Evidence: strong, self-reported by researcher).** [[Justin Dyer]]'s team gathered **five national datasets** and found youth-raised retention dropped from **~82% in the 1980s to ~50% in the 2010s+**.[^2] Dyer is careful to define "retention" narrowly: those raised LDS who self-identified as youth but no longer identify as adults — convert retention is *not* measured because no data exists.[^3] This is an honest scoping caveat that strengthens the claim's credibility.

2. **The comparison class changes everything (Evidence: strong, Pew-backed).** Compared to LDS in the 1980s, 50% "would worry anybody." But compared to other faiths, "many Christian denominations would love today to have a 50% retention," and **Pew ranks Latter-day Saints #1 in retention of active individuals.**[^4] On maintaining identification *plus* active engagement, "we are the highest."[^4]

3. **The decline is civilizational, not LDS-specific (Evidence: moderate-strong).** "That decline is for nearly every single religion. All religions are experiencing a decline in retention over time."[^5] The Church "is not immune to those various social winds" of Western secularization — locating the cause in macro-social forces rather than any Church-specific failing.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** A 32-point drop in retention over a generation is genuinely alarming on its own terms; "we're declining slower than everyone else" is cold comfort to a tradition that claims unique divine truth and once retained 82%. "Best in class" within a shrinking market can still mean absolute decline. Dyer concedes there are "worrying trends," and being #1 in retention does not establish growth in absolute active membership.

## Best Quotes

> "In the 1980s, it was about 82%. ... Today, in the 2010s plus, it looks to be at about 50%."[^2]

> "Many Christian denominations would love today to have a 50% retention. Pew has us at the number one in retention of active individuals."[^4]

> "When you look historically, you see, yes, there is a decline, but that decline is for nearly every single religion."[^5]
