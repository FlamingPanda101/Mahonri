# Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow

#type/claim

## The Claim
The whole weight of Copan and [[William Lane Craig]]'s argument rests on an *initial physical singularity* — a point of zero radius and infinite density at t = 0 that, they say, "both requires and proves creatio ex nihilo."[^1] [[Hollis R. Johnson]] answers that no such perfect point can exist, because quantum mechanics forbids it. "Because fundamental entities or objects cannot be precisely localized, perfect singularities cannot exist. A perfect singularity is, by definition, a perfect point, and quantum mechanics does not allow this. Therefore, the universe cannot have begun at such a (nonexistent) point."[^2] The Hawking–Penrose singularity theorems that Copan and Craig lean on depend on two assumptions "that most physicists now reject: that the general theory of relativity holds everywhere and that the gravitational force is always attractive."[^3] With those assumptions gone — repulsive gravitation apparently drove inflation in the early universe — "the theory that required a singularity is no longer valid," and "without the initial singularity, the claims of Copan and Craig have no scientific basis."[^3] Johnson adds the deeper point: **even if time had a beginning, creation from nothing does not follow.** "A beginning of time means that we can make no measurements or observations regarding any earlier epoch, and the notion of time itself in our universe has no meaning before that moment" — which is a limit on knowledge, not a proof of non-being.[^4]

## Supporting Evidence
- **Discreteness rules out a perfect point.** "Everything in the universe — including energy, matter, space, and time — is ultimately discrete, not smooth and continuous." Space and time are quantized at the Planck length (10⁻³³ cm) and Planck time (10⁻⁴³ s), so "there was always a finite extent to the material in the big bang."[^5]
- **The model only ever began *after* the beginning.** "The standard hot big bang model starts a tiny fraction of a second after a conjectured time zero"; "no statement can be made about the universe before the Planck time, 10⁻⁴³ seconds after the beginning," and "the beginning of time is not defined."[^6]
- **Singularity-free models exist.** "Many inflationary models neither require nor allow an initial physical singularity," and the Hartle–Hawking no-boundary model (1983), which Copan and Craig reject, has no singularity.[^7]
- **A physicist's verdict on ex nihilo.** Fred Adams: "The big bang does not represent creation ex nihilo... before that point we do not assume that there was nothing at all in existence," for otherwise "an extraordinarily large violation of energy conservation took place at the beginning of time."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Because fundamental entities or objects cannot be precisely localized, perfect singularities cannot exist... Therefore, the universe cannot have begun at such a (nonexistent) point. There was always a finite extent to the material in the big bang." [^2]

> "Even if time had a beginning (which is still an open question), creation from nothing does not necessarily follow." [^4]

Johnson is careful to wield this *against ex nihilo*, not against a beginning as such — a Latter-day Saint can still affirm that *this* organized cosmos has a finite past while denying that anything came from absolute nothing. The argument therefore sits beside, but cuts in a different direction from, the apologetic [[Borde-Guth-Vilenkin Theorem - Any Expanding Universe Had a Beginning|Borde–Guth–Vilenkin theorem]] (which argues *for* a cosmic beginning), and it reinforces that [[Modern Physics Is Consistent With Eternal Matter Not Ex Nihilo|modern physics is consistent with eternal matter, not ex nihilo]] and that [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation from nothing is not biblical]]. The misuse of an outdated singularity model is itself a textbook case of the [[The Aquinas Fallacy - Pinning Theology to Current Science|Aquinas fallacy]].
