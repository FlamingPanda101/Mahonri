# Smallness as Asset for Enduring Identity

## The Claim
The diminutive size of Israel and Judah was an *asset* — not a liability — for the development of the biblical project. Small peoples are forced to develop "plan B" — an identity that can survive without military power. Great empires like Assyria and Babylon "had only known success, triumph" and were unprepared for the day after. The biblical project of peoplehood — a portable, text-based identity — required the vulnerability of smallness to emerge.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Empires Cannot Imagine Their Own End:** Wright argues that if America fell today, "I don't think it would be possible for an American people to persist because we just rest on our laurels in terms of imperial power and statehood." Great powers have no "plan B" for when they are no longer militarily superior. The biblical project required the opposite — a people who knew they were Davids, not Goliaths.[^2]

2. **Small Countries Focus on Education:** Wright notes that "a lot of small countries are much more focused on education, like in Europe and elsewhere, where they don't have strong militaries." This maps onto his thesis that the Bible is fundamentally a pedagogical project — education replaces military power.[^3]

3. **The David and Goliath Mentality:** "When you're small you already think of yourself in terms of the David-Goliath mentality — we are Davids, not Goliaths — and we have to prepare for the next day." This mentality is built into the biblical narrative itself.[^4]

4. **Jeremiah's Pushback:** Wright sees this dynamic alive in the biblical text itself: "Jeremiah says Jerusalem was going to be destroyed, and he was put in jail for proclaiming heresy." There was pushback — people saying "our God is big, Jerusalem is big and never going to be destroyed" — and the biblical authors had to insist on vulnerability as reality. Wright notes this continues "into modern Israeli politics and Jewish politics around this fervency that there's something that's always going to save the day."[^5]

5. **Jews as the Ultimate Small People:** Wright frames Jewish history through this lens: "Jews are like 0.1% of the population. So any idea that we have something special on our side can get us into a lot of trouble." The biblical message — that survival comes from wisdom and text, not military exceptionalism — is directly relevant.[^5]

## Best Quote

> "What makes the Bible the Bible also is like we see in Jeremiah — there are a lot of people pushing back against that, not because they said 'no we're not small, we're big,' but rather 'our God is big, Jerusalem is big in the sense that it's never going to be destroyed.' And that continues on into modern Israeli politics … and others saying 'we need to figure out a way to persist and endure.'"[^5]
> — [[Jacob Wright]], *Data Over Dogma* podcast

## Counter-Arguments
- The biblical tradition also contains triumphalist, imperial theology (the Davidic covenant, Zion theology, the "light to the nations" in Deutero-Isaiah) — smallness is one theme, not the only one.
- The Jewish survival narrative as a function of text-based identity is compelling but may overstate the causal role of the Bible vs. other factors (diaspora networks, communal practices, halakha).
- Christianity — a religion that grew from a marginal sect to the religion of an empire — complicates the "smallness is necessary" thesis.
