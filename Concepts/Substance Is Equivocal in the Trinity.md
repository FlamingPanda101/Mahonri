# Substance Is Equivocal in the Trinity

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The credal formula that Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are "one substance" (*homoousios*) — taken to secure that there is exactly one God while preserving three persons. [[Blake Ostler]] argues the word "substance" is doing equivocal work and cannot bear the load. [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Aristotle's "substance" individuates persons (rating: strong; philosophical)
"Aristotle uses the term substance to define each human being. Each human being is a substance." On that standard usage, "if we use the term substance to say that the father, son, and holy ghost are one substance... you'd have to say there are three substances, not one." See [[Aristotle]]. [^2]

> "Aristotle uses the term substance to define each human being. Each human being is a substance. So... you'd have to say there are three substances, not one. And so the word substance is equivocal." [^2]

### 2. What the framers were *after* was equal divinity (rating: moderate; charitable reconstruction)
Ostler offers a generous reading: "what they were after was to say that every way that the father is divine, the son is also divine, and the holy ghost is also divine." If "one essence" just means "the set of properties necessary to be fully divine," then Latter-day Saints can affirm it — and *do*, since all three "share those equally." See [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]]. [^3]

### 3. But they refuse the LDS sense of substance (rating: strong; dialectical)
The Trinitarian "would push back and say, well, whatever we mean by substance, we don't mean what you mean because you're a Mormon and we have to be different." Once they insist on a non-property sense, Ostler says, "you're going to run into the kind of logical problems we've discussed." [^4]

### 4. Substance vs essence vs being are conflated (rating: moderate; terminological)
Apologists use "being, substance, and essence" to describe *what* God is and then "Father, Son, and Holy Spirit" to describe *who* — but never stably define which sense of substance is in play. Ostler's diagnostic question: "When you say God, are you talking about a who or a what?" See [[The Who or What Test for the Word God]]. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Defenders argue *ousia* in Nicene usage is precisely *not* Aristotle's primary substance (the individual) but second substance / nature — the *kind* of thing — so "one ousia, three hypostases" means one divine nature numerically (not three instances). The equivocation charge, they say, is itself the equivocation, treating *ousia* as primary substance. Ostler's counter: a *numerically single* nature shared by three really-distinct persons either reduces to one person (if simplicity is enforced) or to three gods sharing a kind (if it is not) — so the formula buys coherence only by leaving "substance" indeterminate. See [[Latin Trinity and Eastern Trinity Both Fail]].
