# Scripture Cannot Interpret Itself — The Supreme Court Analogy

## The Claim
The Westminster Confession holds that "the infallible rule of interpretation of scripture is the scripture itself." [[Hayden Carroll]] argues this is incoherent by analogy to **law**: a written text never interprets itself; every functioning legal system requires a *living authority of adjudication* — a judge or court — to render a binding interpretation when readers disagree. "Scripture interprets scripture" is therefore "akin to the United States having no Supreme Court or judges and saying that the Constitution and laws of the United States interpret themselves." Because there is no Protestant "seer" with authority to give an official interpretation, the system guarantees the hundreds of mutually-contradicting denominations it has in fact produced.[^1] This note isolates the *analogical / legal* form of the argument; the debate-exchange form is at [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]].

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
- **The Supreme Court parallel.** Laws need a final interpreter "because different people might interpret the same words in our law differently. So we need a final authority to provide an authoritative answer." Courts "perform a function of adjudication"; without a body authorized to give a *binding* meaning, "how could our laws be enforced?"[^2] The LDS claim is that God supplies exactly this office through living prophets, seers, and revelators — the ecclesial analogue of the judiciary.
- **The "borrowed lawnmower" illustration.** A defendant caught on video taking a lawnmower pleads not guilty, claiming he was only "borrowing it to mow his lawn" — *his* private interpretation of the theft statute. The judge, as final authority, "will explain that you can call it whatever you want, but as far as his court is concerned, you stole the lawnmower," and "has the right to enforce his interpretation."[^3] The point: a text plus a private reader is not a legal order; an *authority over the text* is required.
- **The gym locker-room illustration.** A private gym's handbook bars men from the women's locker room; a patron named "Jessica" who has male anatomy insists "I am a woman." Who decides how the handbook defines *woman*? Not each member, nor even all the employees collectively — "the owner is the final authority on how the policy book should be interpreted." A dissenting employee's only recourse is to "start their own gym and provide their own authoritative interpretations" — which Carroll maps onto Protestant denominational splintering.[^4]
- **From analogy to prediction.** Carroll closes the cluster with [[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter]]'s "no prophecy of scripture is of any private interpretation," arguing *sola scriptura* "logically leads a person to advocate that each individual reader of the Bible can decide what the text means," and that this is *why* "we have hundreds of Protestant denominations… none of which believe the exact same things."[^5]
- **Honest assessment / steelman of the Protestant.** The analogy has real bite against the slogan "scripture interprets scripture" taken literally. But a careful Protestant can answer that (a) the legal analogy proves too much — courts famously *disagree*, split, and reverse, so a living magisterium does not in fact end interpretive dispute (the *tu quoque* pressed elsewhere: LDS believers also differ over e.g. the First Vision accounts); and (b) "scripture interprets scripture" was never meant to deny *any* human interpreter, only to deny an *infallible* one — Protestants affirm teachers, creeds, and councils as real but *fallible, correctable* authorities. The vivid analogies score against the caricature; the deeper question — whether *any* system escapes the regress, since the prophet's words must themselves be interpreted — is left open.

## Best Quotes
> "This would be akin to the United States having no Supreme Court or judges and saying that the Constitution and laws of the United States interpret themselves."[^1]
