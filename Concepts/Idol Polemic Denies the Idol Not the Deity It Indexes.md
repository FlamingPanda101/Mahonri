# Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that passages calling foreign gods "the work of men's hands, wood and stone" that "can be destroyed" prove those gods are not real — that the text reduces a deity like Baal, Chemosh, or Asherah *exclusively* to a destructible idol, leaving no real deity behind it. Therefore these are "gods that are not gods."

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The polemic targets the idol, not the deity it indexes (rating: strong; consensus on divine-image theory)
McClellan distinguishes the **idol** (the manufactured material medium) from the **deity** it manifests. The idol can be *called* the deity because it indexes/reifies the deity's presence, but it never exhausts the deity, who exists elsewhere. Denying divinity to the wood-and-stone object is not denying the deity. [^1] This is the ancient Near Eastern "logic of divine images" (cf. [[Melammu - Divine Radiance in Ancient Near East]], [[Divine Images Explain Jesus Relationship to God]]).

> "what that rhetoric is saying is what they refer to as gods are actually just Idols the work of human hands wood and stone and they can be destroyed... an idol is nothing in the world but the idol while it can be identified with the deity is not the fullness of the deity it does not exhaust the presence Identity or being of that deity it is just an index" [^1]

### 2. Paul says exactly the same thing (rating: strong; same author, two clauses)
In [[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Corinthians 8:4–6]] / [[1 Corinthians 10.20|1 Cor 10:20]] Paul declares "an idol is nothing in the world," yet then says that those who sacrifice to idols are actually sacrificing to real entities ("demons... gods they did not know"). The idol is nothing; the deity behind it is not. [^2]

> "that's precisely what Paul says when he says we know that an idol is nothing in the world but then goes on to point out that when they're worshiping Idols they're actually worshiping an entity that is distinct from the idol" [^2]

### 3. Israel's own cult works the same way (rating: moderate; McClellan's monograph)
The Hebrew Bible itself treats material media as the actual presence of Yahweh — e.g., the **Ark of the Covenant**, associated with and spoken of as if it *were* the deity. The idol polemic is the late literary layers denigrating the very practice the rest of the text takes for granted. [^3]

> "the Hebrew Bible itself will also talk about material media as actual deity it's doing the same thing that the authors that are being denigrated by these rather late literary layers in the Hebrew Bible are doing and we see the exact same thing in terms of the Arc of the Covenant" [^3]

### 4. It is a datable rhetorical layer (rating: moderate; literary-historical)
The "idol polemic" is an exilic/post-exilic layer (Deutero-Isaiah, 1–2 Kings). Its authors knew the idol merely indexed a deity understood to exist elsewhere — so the destruction-and-mockery rhetoric "is denying real deity to the idol not to the god that the idol indexes." [^4] See [[Deutero-Isaiah Theory]].

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's reading has surface support: 2 Kings 19:18 *does* say the nations' gods "were not gods... therefore they were destroyed," which can sound like a flat ontological denial. McClellan grants this is the natural English impression but argues the underlying ancient-Near-Eastern idol/deity distinction — confirmed by Paul's parallel reasoning — shows the denial is aimed at the destructible object, not the deity. He notes Ortlund himself later concedes (re: Jezebel/Baal) that "there's a real entity at work" behind pagan worship, which undercuts the flat-denial reading. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "they were denying real deity to the idol not to the deity it was supposed to index" [^6]

> "what this rhetoric is not saying is that a deity like ball or kamos or Ashera is exclusively identified with an idol and therefore because it's the work of human hands it's not an actual God" [^7]

> "it is just material media to reify or manifest the presence of the deity because it is representing something else that is elsewhere and that is the actual God and that cannot be destroyed because that is not the work of human hands" [^1]
