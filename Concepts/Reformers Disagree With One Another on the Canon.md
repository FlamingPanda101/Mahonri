# Reformers Disagree With One Another on the Canon

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The self-authentication thesis holds that any Spirit-led believer can recognize the canon as easily as "black from white" (see [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]]). The rebuttal: the very founders of the doctrine **contradict each other** about which books are in the Bible, which is impossible if the canon is self-evident [^1].

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Calvin kept Baruch; modern Protestants reject it — rate: strong
[[John Calvin]] cited **Baruch** as Scripture, a deuterocanonical book that modern Protestants exclude. On the self-authentication view, "somebody's feelings are wrong there" [^1]. **Scholarly context:** Calvin's relationship to the deuterocanon was complex, but the broader point — that the Reformer who most forcefully argued canon-clarity nonetheless diverged from the eventual Protestant 66-book list — is accurate.

### 2. Luther wanted four NT books out — rate: strong
[[Martin Luther]] argued that four New Testament books (James, Hebrews, Jude, Revelation — the "antilegomena" he relegated) did not belong; he kept seven OT deuterocanonical books out while Calvin wanted to keep some in [^1][^2]. See [[Luther and the Epistle of Straw]] and [[The Orthodoxy Criterion Lets You Cut Scripture You Dislike]]. **Steelman:** a Protestant can say the *eventual consensus* corrected Luther's idiosyncrasy — but that appeal to later corporate consensus is precisely the move that undermines individual self-authentication (see [[Self-Authentication via Church Consensus Backs Into the Catholic Model]]).

### 3. The disagreement is historical, not merely modern — rate: strong
It is "not just you the viewer, it's historically the reformers" and, before them, the early Christians who lacked any method to "look at the whole thing and say, aha, exactly these 66 or exactly these 73 books belong to God." Baptist canon scholar [[Lee Martin McDonald]] confirms canon disputes persisted "even in the fourth and fifth centuries" [^3]. See [[The Early Church Did Not Use Self-Authentication]].

### 4. The disputed books prove the point — rate: strong
There is a class of books the church calls **deuterocanon / antilegomena** ("spoken against") in both Testaments — Hebrews, 2 Peter, Revelation, etc. on the NT side. These "question books" are exactly where individuals' feelings diverge, which is why the church "really has to step in and clarify some confusion." A self-authenticating canon would have no such contested middle [^4].

## Best Quotes
> "Calvin cites to Baruch as scripture. Modern Protestants think he's wrong to do that, so somebody's feelings are wrong there. Martin Luther argues four of the New Testament books shouldn't be in the New Testament. Somebody's feelings are wrong." [^1]

> "John Calvin wanted to keep one of those seven in. Obviously, it's not just you the viewer, it's historically the reformers." [^2]

> "There's a Deutero canon or antilegomena both in the Old Testament and the New Testament... Those are the disputed... the ones where the church really has to step in and clarify some confusion." [^4]
