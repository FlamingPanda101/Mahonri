# Child Rights Confront Sexual Autonomy as the Highest Value

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] offers a diagnosis of *why* child-rights arguments provoke such fierce resistance: they collide with what she calls "the ultimate god of the left… sexual identity and sexual autonomy."[^1] Borrowing [[Jordan Peterson]]'s framing of a "highest value" that "governs all your other values," she argues that on the contemporary left that governing value is sexual autonomy — and a genuine child right would *dethrone* it.

The structure is a conflict of value hierarchies. "If it's true that children have a right to life and they have a right to their mother and father, that means sexually, adults have to change, or their identity is no longer the most important part of the equation when it comes to family building."[^2] Concretely, the child's right would require that "adult self-interest must bow down to child interests" — that the "supposed rights" to marry, to parenthood, to happiness "subject [themselves] to genuine child rights." From that follow the conclusions the left resists: "therefore you cannot get a casual divorce… you cannot create a child on the open fertility market… you can't create a same-sex household if it means children are going to lose their mother and father."[^2] Because this "confronts the highest value," Faust says, "you're confronting somebody's religion. And people… have a pretty irrational response when you challenge their religion."[^3]

[[John Anderson (media)|John Anderson]] adds the anti-egoism rejoinder: others "made way" and "made great sacrifices" for you when you were born, and will again at the other end of life when you can no longer care for yourself, so "this idea of radical selfishness — that I will identify who I am, that I'll be God over my life — flies in the face of just about everything we know about truly finding a workable identity, which is that you don't center it on yourself."[^4] Faust grounds the whole exchange in a theology of human vulnerability: "what it means to be human is you're very vulnerable… at both poles of the human existence," so "we don't get to use other people because they are weak and vulnerable" — but "if sexual identity is God… then [the] vulnerability [of the young] is going to be taken advantage of." She calls for "a very solid Christian theology of what it means to be human."[^5]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **It correctly identifies a genuine value conflict (rate: strong as analysis).** Whatever one concludes, Faust is right that child-rights claims and adult sexual-autonomy claims *can* directly conflict (e.g., a child's claimed right to known biological parents vs. an adult's claimed right to form any family via any means). Naming the conflict as a clash of *hierarchies* — which value is lexically prior — is analytically clarifying and connects to [[A Value Hierarchy Resolves Conflicts Between Moral Principles]].[^2]
2. **The vulnerability principle is widely shared (rate: strong).** "Don't exploit the weak and vulnerable; protect them" is close to a moral universal, and applying it to *both* ends of life (the very young and the very old) is a coherent and attractive ethical anchor that does not depend on contested religious premises to feel binding.[^5]
3. **The anti-egoism point has independent support (rate: moderate).** The claim that a self-centered identity is psychologically unstable, while a self given to others is more durable, echoes a strand of well-being research and a long moral-philosophical tradition; Anderson states it as wisdom rather than data, but it is not idiosyncratic.[^4]

### Steelman of the progressive view

The progressive position is principled, not merely self-interested, and deserves a fair statement. (a) **"Sexual autonomy as god" is a hostile caricature.** Defenders of marriage equality and reproductive freedom typically ground their view in *dignity, equality, and non-discrimination* — the conviction that gay and lesbian people have equal standing to form families and that the state should not privilege one family form — not in worship of autonomy. Framing the opposing value as an idol pre-loads the conclusion. (b) **Autonomy protects the vulnerable too.** The same liberal commitment to bodily autonomy and self-definition is what protects women, sexual minorities, and abuse victims from coercion; it is not obviously the *enemy* of the vulnerable Faust makes it. (c) **Rights can be balanced without lexical priority.** Faust assumes a genuine child right must *categorically* override adult interests ("bow down"), but most rights frameworks weigh competing claims contextually rather than ranking one as an absolute trump; one can take children's interests very seriously without concluding they nullify all adult claims in every case. (d) **The disputed premise is smuggled.** The whole argument runs on "*if* children have a right to their mother and father" — exactly the contested claim (see [[Love Makes a Family Means Child Loss Makes That Family]] and the empirical dispute in [[The No-Difference Same-Sex Parenting Studies Are Methodologically Weak]]); calling the resistance "irrational" / "religious" risks dismissing reasoned disagreement about that premise as mere idolatry. (e) **Charge of "religion" cuts both ways.** Faust herself calls for grounding the view in "a Christian theology of the human person," so the debate is between rival comprehensive worldviews, not reason vs. religion. Faust's reply is that *some* value sits atop everyone's hierarchy, that she is simply naming what she thinks tops the left's, and that her own top value (the protection of the vulnerable child) is one most people would, on reflection, rank highly too.

## LDS Relevance

The argument is a near-perfect secular echo of the LDS doctrine of the **divine worth and primacy of the human person, especially the weak**. *The Family: A Proclamation to the World* frames children as a sacred stewardship and orders family life around their nurture, not adult preference; the broader Restoration teaching that every person is a literal child of God grounds exactly the "don't use the vulnerable" ethic Faust reaches for. Her appeal to a "theology of what it means to be human" parallels the LDS anthropology in which human identity is fixed by *what we eternally are* (children of Heavenly Parents), not by self-construction — which connects directly to [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]]. The "highest value" framing also fits the LDS conviction that consecration — losing one's life to find it, centering the self on God and others rather than on appetite — is the path to a "workable identity," precisely Anderson's point. Where Faust says sexual autonomy must not be the governing god, the Latter-day Saint locates the governing value in God's will and the worth of souls.
