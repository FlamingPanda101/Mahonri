# Self-Defeating Statements Refute Materialism

## The Point
A statement is self-defeating when, for it to be true, it would have to be false — it fails to meet its own standard.[^1] Turek argues that the core claims used to defend atheistic materialism are self-defeating, which is a decisive logical defect. Learning to spot self-defeating statements, he says, lets you defuse a wide range of objections that are "really nonsense."[^2] The recurring pattern is an atheist who quietly exempts himself from his own theory.

## Evidence
Turek gives several examples. To say "the laws of logic are merely human conventions" is self-defeating, because the speaker intends the claim to be true regardless of how human minds conceptualize it — which presupposes the very fixed, mind-independent laws he is denying.[^3] The postmodern slogan "there is no truth" can be disarmed with one question: "Is that true?" Likewise "all truth is relative" ("Is that relative?") and "all truth depends on your perspective" ("Does that truth depend on your perspective?").[^4] Materialism itself self-destructs: any reliable effort to conclude "the mind is the brain" requires a free, rational mind to run and interpret the experiments — so "any intellectually reliable effort to conclude materialism is true must assume that materialism is false."[^5] Turek extends this to [[Daniel Dennett]], who asserts that consciousness is an illusion: detecting an illusion requires being able to see what is real, so Dennett "would need to wake up with some kind of superconsciousness... he'd have to be someone like God in order to know that."[^6] In each case, the atheist "exempts himself from his own theory."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Michael's claim is self-defeating, which means for his claim to be true, it would have to be false. (Read that again.)" [^1]

> "Any intellectually reliable effort to conclude materialism is true must assume that materialism is false!" [^5]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saint epistemology likewise treats reason as a self-evident, God-given starting point rather than something to be explained away — "you reason from [the laws of logic], not to them."[^8] Scripture frames truth as a stable, knowable reality: "truth is knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24), and the Light of Christ "giveth light to every man that cometh into the world" (D&C 84:46; cf. John 1:9). A worldview that must call consciousness or objective truth an "illusion" cuts off the very means by which God invites us to "study it out in your mind" and receive confirmation (D&C 9:8) — so the Restoration affirms, against materialism, that our rational and spiritual faculties are trustworthy because they come from the source of truth.
