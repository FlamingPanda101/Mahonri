# The Institutional Church and the Body of Believers Are Two Senses of One Word

## The Claim

The hinge of the entire debate. [[Jacob Hansen]] opens by distinguishing two referents of the word *church* (*ekklēsia*, "assembly"): **(1)** the **body of sincere believers**, which Latter-day Saints affirm has existed continuously since Christ and through which the Spirit has always worked; and **(2)** the **institutional church** that *governs* believers and holds binding revelatory authority. The Great Apostasy, Hansen stresses, is *not* the claim that the body of believers ever vanished — "Latter-day Saints do not teach that the church as a body of sincere believers was ever lost from the earth" — but only that the *institutional* fullness (apostolic authority to receive binding public revelation) was lost. This single distinction does most of his defensive work: every Catholic indefectibility text ("gates of hell," "pillar of truth," "I am with you always") can be referred to the indestructible body of believers while the *institution* is conceded to have lapsed. [^1]

[[Joe Heschmeyer]]'s counter is to deny that the New Testament licenses the split. He presses Hansen across cross-examination: when Paul calls the church "the pillar and ground of the truth" and tells Timothy how to behave "in the house of God" ([[1 Timothy 3.15|1 Timothy 3:15]]), or when Jesus says "tell it to the church" who then expels the unrepentant ([[Matthew 18.15-17|Matthew 18:17]]), the referent is plainly a *visible, adjudicating institution*, not an amorphous set of seekers. His decisive challenge: **"Where do we find anywhere in the New Testament that distinguishes those two senses?"** — to which Hansen answers only that "sometimes it's clear" from context, citing no passage that marks the distinction. [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The distinction is lexically real (rating: moderate; the word does have a range)
*Ekklēsia* genuinely can denote either a concrete assembly or the wider community of believers, and "body of Christ" language ([[1 Corinthians 12.12-27|1 Corinthians 12]]) supports an organic, more-than-institutional sense. Hansen's two-senses claim is not linguistically baseless. [^1]

### 2. It immunizes the indefectibility texts (rating: strong for the LDS system; defensive value)
Once granted, the distinction lets Hansen affirm Christ's promises to "the church" without conceding the institution's perpetuity — the promises attach to sense (1). This is what lets him say the bride of Christ "is bigger than the Roman papacy" without contradicting the apostasy thesis. See [[The Gates of Hades Are Death and Do Not Besiege the Church]]. [^3]

### 3. It mirrors the Catholic "fullness" concept (rating: moderate; turnabout)
Hansen notes Heschmeyer's *own* framework already splits the body from the institution: valid Orthodox churches have "true churches at the local level" but lack the institutional "fullness" that requires the Pope. If the Catholic can distinguish a real-but-incomplete church from the institutional fullness, the LDS apostasy claim (fullness lost, body remaining) is the same structure. See [[Name the True Church or Concede the Apostasy - the Negative's Burden]]. [^4]

### 4. The textual challenge is genuinely unanswered in the debate (rating: strong; for the Catholic side)
Heschmeyer's "where does the NT distinguish the two senses?" lands: Hansen concedes Matthew 18 refers to an *institution*, that 1 Timothy 3 does too, and that the same word elsewhere means the body of believers — but cannot point to a verse that *flags* which sense is meant or that two distinct churches exist. The distinction is supplied by the interpreter, not the text. [^2]

### 5. Steelman of the LDS reply
The fact that the New Testament does not *thematize* the two-senses distinction does not mean the distinction is illegitimate — the same is true of many later theological categories the Catholic accepts (Hansen presses the "where's the word Trinity in the Bible?" parallel against Heschmeyer's own demand for explicit assumption/infallibility texts). And the visible institution Heschmeyer's verses describe *did* exist in the apostolic era and "helped to guide the body of believers"; the LDS claim is only that this institutional fullness *later lapsed*, not that the NT church was invisible. See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]].

### Steelman of Heschmeyer's counter to that reply
There is an asymmetry: the Trinity is read *out of* the texts' data, whereas Hansen needs the texts to *contain two different churches* so that the institution can die while the body lives — and that bifurcation is exactly what the texts never assert. Worse, the indefectibility promises attach to *the same* church that adjudicates and is "the pillar of truth"; carving off an immortal "body" from a mortal "institution" to absorb the promises is, on Heschmeyer's view, ad hoc. He also presses that "the body of Christ" cannot be a mere collection of well-meaning individuals "wherever they may be" — Paul's organism has *one* visible, growing reality, "the fullness of him who fills all in all" (Ephesians 1:23), with "no separation of head from body."
