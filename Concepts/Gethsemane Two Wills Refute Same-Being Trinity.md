# Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity

## The Claim

The scriptural data presupposes the Father and Son are two distinct *beings*, not one being relating to itself. Jesus' "not my will, but thine," his cry "my God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me," his being *given* authority, and his calling the Father "the only true God" all collapse if Father and Son are the same being. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two wills in Gethsemane.** "Not my will, but thine be done" presents two distinct wills. The same being cannot have a will different from its own will. If Father and Son are one being, the prayer is unintelligible. (Evidence: strong; the orthodox must invoke two-wills dyothelitism to absorb it — see steelman.) [^1]
2. **Jesus has a God.** On the cross ([[Matthew 27.46|Matthew 27:46]]) Jesus addresses "my God." A being cannot have *itself* as its God; "this is my God," not "my person that I call Father." [^2]
3. **Given authority.** Jesus is *given* authority. But if you are God, you already *are* the authority — you cannot be given what you constitutively possess. Being given authority marks a distinct recipient. [^3]
4. **"The only true God" is the Father.** [[John 17.3|John 17:3]]: "this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God" — and the one so named is the person Jesus calls Father, not Jesus. [^4]
5. **Steelman.** Orthodoxy answers all four via *two wills in one person* (Constantinople III) and the *communicatio idiomatum*: the human will of the incarnate Son submits to the divine will of the Father; "my God" and being "given" authority and being "forsaken" are spoken *according to his humanity* / his economic mission; and [[John 17.3|John 17:3]]'s "only true God" is read as distinguishing the Father from idols, not from the Son (whom the same verse pairs as the one "sent"). (Evidence: these are the standard, internally coherent orthodox replies; the dispute is whether they are *ad hoc* or principled.) The LDS rejoinder: each reply requires the nature-indexing the [[The Who or What Test for the Word God|who/what test]] flags, and the plainest reading of distinct wills, a God-above-Jesus, and conferred authority is two beings in a Godhead. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "If Jesus was the same being as the Father, how could the same being not do the will and have a different will from that of the same being that he was? Not my will, but thine." [^1]

> "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me? ... Jesus has a God? I thought he was the same being as God." [^2]

> "If you're God, you already have all the authority. So why is it that Jesus is given authority? ... You can't be given authority." [^3]
