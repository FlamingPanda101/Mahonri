# No Single Biblical Author Describes the Trinity

#type/claim

## The Claim

When asked to "show the Trinity in the Bible," a defender produces five or six verses scattered across different books — Father, Son, and Holy Ghost from the New Testament, then "one God" from Isaiah — and stitches them together. [[Christian (Holy Rebellion)]] presses the decisive point: "What you would need to see actually is for **one author** to describe the Trinity. And you never see that. And so the conclusion is that no one author from the entire biblical corpus understood the Trinity as formulated today by creedal Christians." [^1] Because the Bible "is not a book — it's a collection of texts," cobbling a doctrine out of multiple authors writing in different centuries and contexts does not show that *anyone who wrote scripture* held that doctrine. The diagnostic demand, repeated in a clip the panel plays from debater Chandler Henry, is stark: "Can you show me where one author who wrote the biblical text teaches clearly and identifies **consubstantiality** between the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost?" — a question framed as unanswerable without proof-texting. [^2]

This is sharper than the neighboring notes. [[Trinity Is an Interpretation Not Explicit in Scripture]] argues the *raw data underdetermines* the creedal reading; [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]] attacks the *method* of harmonizing every verse; [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]] notes the *vocabulary* is absent. The present claim adds a positive, falsifiable burden of proof: name **one** biblical author who states the triune-consubstantial relationship. The absence of any such author is offered as direct evidence that the creedal Trinity is post-biblical.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Bible is a collection, not a single authored treatise (rating: strong; method)
"They're cobbling together these texts from a book that — it's not a book, it's a collection of texts." A doctrine assembled by combining Isaiah with the Gospels is a *reader's* synthesis across authors and eras, not the teaching of any one inspired writer. This is why the single-author test is the right test: scripture's authority attaches to what its authors taught, and no author taught the triune formula. Compare [[Canon Cannot Self-Reference - Compilation Postdates the Books]]. [^1]

### 2. The "one author / consubstantiality" demand goes unmet (rating: strong; debate-tested)
In the clip the panel plays, a Catholic interlocutor, asked to name one author who identifies consubstantiality, can only reach for proof-texts — exactly the move the question forecloses. The repeated inability to point to a single author who *describes* (not merely supplies words later read into) the relationship is treated as the empirical payoff of the argument. [^2]

### 3. The vocabulary is admittedly post-biblical (rating: strong; historical)
"Consubstantiality isn't a word found in the Bible," and the concept "gradually comes together throughout the first couple centuries"; the panel notes a Catholic reference work itself states the Trinity "is not found in the Bible but was decided at councils later on." If the term and concept crystallize only in later councils, then by construction no first-century author held it. See [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]]. [^3]

### 4. The salvation stakes make non-explicitness a problem (rating: moderate; theological)
If Protestants hold that the Bible contains the whole "deposit of faith" — everything needed for salvation — then a salvation-defining doctrine like the Trinity "would need to explicitly exist in the Bible," authored, not reverse-engineered. Binding salvation to a doctrine no biblical author states would consign "the authors of the biblical texts and subsequent Christians until the creeds" to the same condemnation aimed at Latter-day Saints. The prooftext [[John 17.3|John 17:3]] ("the only true God" = the Father, distinguished from Jesus) is offered as a single author's own framing that cuts against the creedal synthesis. [^4]

### Steelman (the Trinitarian reply)
The "single author" bar is artificial and would gut doctrines all sides accept. The canon, on the orthodox view, has *one divine Author* speaking through many human ones, so a teaching distributed across books is still Scripture's teaching — and the New Testament authors plainly *do*, individually, supply the raw materials: one author affirms one God (Paul, [[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Cor 8]]), applies divine titles, work, and worship to the Son (John's prologue; Paul's Christ-hymns), and treats the Spirit as personal and divine — so each pillar has single-author attestation even if the *systematized label* "consubstantial" is later. Demanding that an author also use post-biblical philosophical vocabulary is anachronistic: doctrines are legitimately *developed* to defend the data against heresy (the canon of Scripture, the two natures of Christ, even "Bible" itself are likewise not named in a single proof-text). On this view the absence of the *word* and the *systematic treatise* shows only that the church later articulated what the apostles already worshipped, not that no one held it. The LDS rejoinder: developed-doctrine reasoning is exactly what justifies *continuing revelation and a living magisterium* (see [[A Living Magisterium Settles What Sola Scriptura Cannot]]), which Protestants reject; and reading three separate authors' separate claims as one consubstantial God is itself the contested interpretation, not a neutral datum (cf. [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]]).
