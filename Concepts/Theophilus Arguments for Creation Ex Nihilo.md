# Theophilus's Arguments for Creation Ex Nihilo

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Theophilus of Antioch]] (writing c. AD 180, in *Ad Autolycum*) was, per [[Blake Ostler]], the **second person in history** — after [[Tatian]] — to expressly teach *creatio ex nihilo*, and the first to argue *directly against the Middle Platonists* for it. Crucially, Ostler shows Theophilus's reasons were **philosophical, not exegetical**: he accepted the Middle Platonists' own premises about God and reasoned from them. This is direct evidence that *ex nihilo* entered Christianity as a deduction from an adopted Greek concept of God, not as a deposit of revelation.[^1] See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]].

## Supporting Evidence

Ostler isolates **three arguments** Theophilus gives for why matter must be created from absolute nothing (ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων):

1. **God's mode of creation must be unique (the anti-anthropomorphism argument).** If God merely organized pre-existing matter, "the way in which God manifests his power would not be unique" — God would create exactly as a human artisan does, who "when he receives matter from someone, can make what he wants from it." Since both Theophilus and the Middle Platonists agreed God is *not* anthropomorphic, God's creating must be utterly unlike any human creating — hence from nothing.[^2]

2. **Divine monarchy / omnipotence (the sovereignty argument).** "If God is ungenerated and all matter is ungenerated, no longer is God the maker of all... neither is the sovereignty of God shown." If anything existed that God did not create, the divine monarchy (μοναρχία) would be broken; God's omnipotence therefore requires that he be creator of *all*, including matter.[^3]

3. **The eternal must be immutable (the immutability argument).** "Just as God is changeless because he is ungenerated, so also, if matter is also ungenerated, it is also changeless and equal to God. For that which is generated is mutable... The unregenerated is immutable." Since matter manifestly changes, it cannot be eternal/immutable; therefore it must be generated — from nothing.[^4]

Ostler stresses that all three premises (non-anthropomorphism, μοναρχία, eternal=immutable) are **Middle Platonic**, not biblical: the biblical God is unchanging "in character and commitment to justice," whereas Theophilus made him "unchanging in a metaphysical sense."[^5] See [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]].

## Supporting Evidence (Copan & Craig, steelmanned)

For Copan and Craig, Theophilus is not *inventing* a doctrine but *defending* one already latent in monotheism: if God is the sole ultimate source of everything distinct from himself, then matter — being distinct from God — must derive from him, and the only alternative to eternal-uncreated matter (which would rival God) is creation from nothing. The immutability and sovereignty arguments, on this view, are simply making explicit what "Maker of heaven and earth" entailed all along; that Theophilus argues *philosophically* shows the doctrine is *rationally compelling*, not that it is *foreign*.

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

The arguments succeed only on Platonic premises that are themselves the innovation. The immutability argument equivocates: the biblical God's changelessness is moral fidelity, not metaphysical immobility, so "matter changes" does not entail "matter cannot be eternal" unless one has *already* swapped in the Platonic sense of eternity.[^5] The uniqueness argument presupposes the non-anthropomorphic Platonic God the earliest Christians did not hold. And [[Hermogenes]], working from the *same* Platonic premises, drew the *opposite* conclusion (eternal matter), which shows the premises do not force *ex nihilo* — they force a *choice*, and Theophilus chose to protect God's sovereignty at the price of the biblical view of matter and man. See [[Hermogenes Argued for Eternal Matter Because Matter Is Evil]]. May concludes Theophilus "did not... fully realise what a radical break with the theological tradition the doctrine of creatio ex nihilo constituted."[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "If God is ungenerated and all matter is ungenerated, no longer is God the maker of all as the Platonists say, neither is the sovereignty of God shown, by their own account." — Theophilus, *Ad Autolycum* 2.4, quoted by Ostler[^3]

> "The power of God is made manifest in this, that he made what he wanted from the non-existent (ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων)." — Theophilus, quoted by Ostler[^2]

> "Theophilus did not of course fully realise what a radical break with the theological tradition the doctrine of creatio ex nihilo constituted." — Gerhard May, quoted by Ostler[^6]
