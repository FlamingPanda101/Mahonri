# Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics (and Peterson's own former view) hold that [[Brigham Young]] "saw a bill through the territorial legislature in Utah in 1852 that legalized slavery" — a damning blind spot proving Brigham endorsed chattel slavery.[^1]

## The Rebuttal (Numbered)

1. **It was a servitude, not slavery, statute (rated: strong; scholarly).** New research — *This Abominable Slavery* by [[LaJean Carruth]], [[Paul Reeve]], and [[Christopher Rich]] — "totally changed" Peterson's view. The 1852 act recognized *servitude*, not chattel slavery; Brigham "didn't want it called slavery" and went out of his way to say he loathed slavery: "You cannot own human flesh. Chattel slavery is an abomination."[^2] [[Christopher Rich]], reading it as a lawyer, concluded immediately: "This is not a slave bill. Why has everybody told me it was?"[^3] *Steelman:* legal servitude that binds a person to an owner is still a grave deprivation of freedom; the distinction softens but does not erase the moral problem — which Peterson concedes ("It still is [an embarrassment] in a way, but not as bad").

2. **Built-in limits aimed at graduated emancipation (rated: strong; documentary).** Unlike Southern multi-generational chattel slavery: servants could *not* pass the status to their children; were entitled to a set amount of education (~3 months/year, comparable to a frontier white child's schooling) with legal standing to sue if denied; could complain to a magistrate about mistreatment (and magistrates ruled in their favor in real cases); and were even guaranteed "wholesome recreation."[^4] The model was drawn from the *free* states Indiana and Illinois.

3. **The purpose was to defuse, not entrench (rated: strong; historical context).** Southern converts began arriving with slaves; for the first five years the territory simply did not recognize slavery (echoing the English *Somerset*-style principle that without enabling law, slavery has no legal force). The statute was a mediating position between abolitionism-at-any-cost and full recognition — designed so slavery would "die out peacefully" within a generation rather than provoke the kind of violent rupture the nation soon suffered (≈780,000 dead in the Civil War).[^5]

4. **The denomination-splitting fear was real (rated: moderate-strong).** The Saints had watched churches fracture North/South over slavery — "the reason you have a Southern Baptist Convention." They sought a compromise that would neither legitimize slavery nor split the territory or the Church.[^6]

5. **Parallel to Lincoln's compensated-emancipation logic (rated: moderate).** Both [[Abraham Lincoln]] and Joseph Smith had floated using federal land-sale proceeds to compensate owners and resettle freed people (the backdrop to Liberia) — good-faith attempts by slavery's opponents to avoid civil war.[^7] See [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].

## Best Quotes

> "It turned out the 1852 bill wasn't a slave bill. It was a bill for servitude. ... Brigham didn't want it called slavery. He went out of his way to say that he loathed slavery. He said, You cannot own human flesh. Chattel slavery is an abomination."[^2]

> "[Rich said] I began to read it, and I... This is not a slave bill. Why has everybody told me it was? I always thought it was."[^3]

> "It's an attempt to find a mediating position between abolitionism... and the other, which is, let's just recognize it. They're trying to find some middle ground."[^5]
