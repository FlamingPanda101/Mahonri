# High-Demand Religion Retains More Members

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics label the Church a "high-demand religion," implying that its heavy expectations are unhealthy or exploitative. The rebuttal: from a sociological standpoint, demand is a strength — "religions that ask more of their members keep more of their members."[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The investment mechanism (Evidence: established sociology).** "When you have an organization that doesn't really ask very much ... you don't really have much investment in that organization."[^2] This echoes the rational-choice "strict church" thesis (Iannaccone/Stark) that demand screens out free-riders and raises commitment — though Dyer attributes the core idea to Joseph Smith's teaching that "a religion that asks everything of its members ... creates faith."[^3] See [[Joseph Smith]].

2. **Service is intrinsically satisfying (Evidence: strong, replicated).** "That is incredibly satisfying for us humans is to serve. That's been shown over and over again in research."[^3] Demand channels members into service, which is rewarding, producing a "virtuous cycle: the more you're engaged, the more you get engaged."[^3]

3. **Demand drives the health benefits (Evidence: moderate-strong).** "When you have a religion that asks more of its members ... they're going to derive more of the benefits.... low-demand things don't really develop us very much."[^4] Growth occurs in the zone of maximum effort — a near-explicit appeal to Vygotsky's zone-of-proximal-development logic.[^4]

4. **Built-in safeguard (Evidence: candid balance).** Dyer concedes members can be "pushed beyond [their] maximum" and urges self-monitoring — "maybe I need to have a conversation with my bishop ... about my calling" — distinguishing healthy stretch from harmful overload.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view:** "High-demand" is the operative term in the coercive-control / high-control-group literature for a reason — the same demand that builds commitment can suppress dissent, induce guilt, and make exit costly precisely because so much has been invested (the sunk-cost trap). Retention bought by raising exit costs is not obviously a sign of health; it can describe a high-control dynamic. Dyer's "are you being pushed too far?" caveat acknowledges this but treats it as an individual calibration issue rather than a structural one.

## Best Quotes

> "Religions that ask more of their members keep more of their members."[^1]

> "Joseph Smith talked about a religion that asks everything of its members that ... creates faith and that faith then keeps you going."[^3]

> "It's when we're asked to do things that the human being starts to develop."[^4]
