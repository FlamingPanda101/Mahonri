# Embodied God in Christian Scholarship

## The Claim
Mainstream Christian scholars are increasingly publishing books that take seriously the idea that God has a body — a doctrine central to LDS theology and historically dismissed as heresy by creedal Christianity. A "flurry of books" in the last 20 years includes *The Embodied God*, *Heavenly Flesh*, and *Does God Have a Body?*.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### From Ward Radio (General Mainstream Convergence)

1. **Publishing Volume and Recency:** The video cites a burst of books specifically on divine embodiment appearing within the last two decades — *The Embodied God*, *Heavenly Flesh*, and *Does God Have a Body?*.[^1] The titles themselves indicate serious academic and popular engagement with what was once purely a "Mormon heresy."

2. **Convergence Context:** The doctrine of an embodied God is one of several LDS distinctives now drawing mainstream scholarly attention, alongside pre-existence and the feminine divine. [[Terrell Given]] identifies this as part of a broader convergence: doctrines that "really set us kind of outside the margins of mainstream Christianity are now being reinvestigated just vigorously by theologians."[^2]

3. **Ancient Near Eastern Roots:** The renewed interest in divine embodiment may be driven by better understanding of early Israelite religion, where a corporeal deity was more the norm than the exception. The source connects this to "the recognition that in the ancient Middle East" the divine was understood in more physical, embodied terms.[^3]

### From Dan McClellan (Scholarly Consensus with Specific Works)

Dan McClellan provides a significantly more detailed scholarly bibliography confirming the consensus that God was corporeal in the Hebrew Bible:[^4]

4. **[[Benjamin Sommer]], *The Bodies of God in the World of Ancient Israel*** (Cambridge University Press, 2008) — described as "phenomenally influential." Argues for a "fluidity model" of divine embodiment in ancient Israel.

5. **[[Brittany Wilson]], *The Embodied God: Seeing the Divine in Luke-Acts and the Early Church*** (Oxford University Press) — "wonderful work on the visibility of God in the New Testament."

6. **[[Christoph Markschies]], *God's Body: Jewish, Christian, and Pagan Images of God*** (Baylor University Press, 2019) — "a very philosophical, very erudite discussion."

7. **[[Esther Hamori]], *When Gods Were Men: The Embodied God in Biblical and Near Eastern Literature*** (de Gruyter) — situates Israel's embodied God in the broader ANE context.

8. **[[Andreas Wagner]], *God's Body: The Anthropomorphic God in the Old Testament*** (Bloomsbury T&T Clark) — English translation of the German original; systematic linguistic analysis of anthropomorphic language.

9. **[[Luke Irwin]], *Jesus and the Visibility of God: Sight and Belief in the Fourth Gospel*** — examines how John's Gospel navigates divine visibility through "divine image" logic.

10. **Dan McClellan, *Adonized Divine Images*** — open-access academic monograph; plus a chapter in *The Bible Says* (trade book).

This body of scholarship — university-press monographs spanning two decades — represents the established academic consensus that the Hebrew Bible's original conception of God was corporeal and visible, a conception that changed under Greek philosophical influence. The LDS doctrine of an embodied God aligns with the earliest stratum of biblical theology.[^4]

## Best Quote

> "And suddenly there's just this flurry of books that have come out in the last 20 years. The embodied God, heavenly flesh, does God have a body? You can even find books with the title God had a wife or another one did God have a wife."[^1]

## Counter-Arguments

- **Naming the books is not evaluating them:** The video does not cite authors, publishers, or arguments from these books. A book titled *The Embodied God* could be arguing *against* divine embodiment just as easily as for it.
- **Academic interest ≠ doctrinal shift:** Theologians may study divine embodiment as a historical artifact (what ancient Israelites *believed*) without endorsing it as true doctrine.
- **Creedal barriers remain:** The Nicene Creed's affirmation of God as "without body, parts, or passions" (a later confessional formulation) remains binding for Catholic, Orthodox, and most Protestant communions — academic publishing does not change ecumenical creeds.
