# 2 Clement Is the Earliest Surviving Christian Sermon

## The Point
*The Second Epistle of Clement to the Corinthians* (c. AD 140), though transmitted as a letter, is actually a sermon — preached very near the time Justin Martyr described the Sunday service, and the earliest full Christian sermon to survive. Its content is intensely Christ-centered, demanding that hearers "think about Jesus Christ as we think about God."

## Evidence
1. **A sermon disguised as a letter.** "Though a sermon, this work was transmitted to posterity as a letter and is known as *The Second Epistle of Clement to the Corinthians*." It was written "circa AD 140," near the time of Justin's description. (Heal notes scholars likewise judge the New Testament Epistle to the Hebrews to be a sermon — Hebrews 13:22.) [^1]
2. **High Christology from the opening.** The preacher "immediately focuses on Christ, encouraging the congregation to recognize the divinity of Christ, to 'think about (or esteem) Jesus Christ as we think about God,'" and to remember their indebtedness to him for saving them from pagan idolatry. [^1]
3. **Confession of former idolatry.** "We were maimed in our understanding," says the preacher, "worshipping stones and pieces of wood and gold and silver and copper — all of them made by humans." [^2]
4. **Scriptural base in Isaiah 54:1.** The theme of indebtedness to Christ is developed through the preacher's interpretation of Isaiah 54:1, which "he seems to base his sermon on," explaining how the passage "is referring to us." Like Jesus at Nazareth, the preacher "summons the scriptures from some distant historical past and places them firmly within his congregation's present day." [^2]
5. **Ethical exhortation.** The homily ties devotion to Christ to "an ethical life of obedience, … to repent, and to pursue virtue," so that the upright "be crowned in the one to come." [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Think about (or esteem) Jesus Christ as we think about God." (2 Clement, quoted) [^1]

> "We were maimed in our understanding, worshipping stones and pieces of wood and gold and silver and copper — all of them made by humans." (2 Clement, quoted) [^2]

## LDS Connection
2 Clement's explicit instruction "to think about Jesus Christ as we think about God" is offered by Heal as evidence that high Christology and devotion to the divinity of Christ were present in the very earliest surviving Christian sermon — relevant to LDS engagement with claims that Christ's divinity was a later development.
