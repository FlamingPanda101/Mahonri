# Yahweh Cannot Die Objection Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Sam Shamoun]] argues, in support of a non-LDS reading, that **Yahweh cannot die** — "a God can't die" — and uses this to undercut the LDS view that the divine Son became mortal. [[Blake Ostler]] and [[Jacob Hansen]] turn the objection back: the death of God incarnate "is kind of the basis of the entire Christian message," so the premise "God cannot die" proves too much against *Christianity itself*, not just against the LDS. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **Every Christian affirms God died on the cross.** "Yahweh never dies, a God can't die. And I'm thinking but every Christian in the entire world affirms... God himself died. It's kind of the basis of the entire Christian message." *Rating: rhetorically strong* — the crucifixion of the incarnate God is central to historic Christianity; "Yahweh cannot die" sits awkwardly with it. [^2]

2. **It self-destructs.** "Here he is telling us how he's going to eviscerate our view when I'm thinking, I don't have to, you've done it for yourself." Philippians 2 explicitly says the one "in the form of God... became obedient to death, even death on a cross." *Rating: a fair tu quoque* — the objection, taken strictly, indicts the atonement. [^3]

3. **It links to the atonement conundrum.** Shamoun must then say only Jesus' *human* part died while an immortal spirit didn't — reintroducing the partition Ostler attacks (see [[The Hypostatic Union Atonement Conundrum]]). *Rating: coherent connection* between the two objections. [^4]

4. **Steelman of the classical reply.** "Yahweh cannot die" is *true of the divine nature/essence* (immutable, immortal), and the cross is *God the Son dying according to his assumed human nature* — exactly the *communicatio idiomatum*. So the classical Christian and Shamoun can both hold "the divine essence cannot die" *and* "God the Son truly died," without contradiction. The LDS reply succeeds only against a *crudely stated* impassibility, not against the careful Chalcedonian formulation. *Rating: a strong rebuttal* — if Shamoun means the *essence* cannot die (the orthodox claim), Ostler's counter loses its bite; if he means the *person* cannot die, he is the one departing from orthodoxy. The exchange exposes imprecision more than it refutes classical theism. [^5]
