# The Reporting Catch-22 and the Johnson Case

## The Claim
The standard objection — "the bishop should have just reported it; better safe than sorry" — ignores that the church faces *symmetric* legal exposure: it has been sued for **reporting** confessed abuse and sued for **not** reporting it. The episode calls the [[Timothy Johnson]] case "the key piece the Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist Michael Rezendes and his muckrakers missed." The reporting Catch-22 is the actual reason the church operates an abuse helpline (see [[The Abuse Helpline Routes Bishops to Legal Compliance Not Cover-Up]]).

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The church was sued *for reporting* (strong — the omitted case)
In 2019, [[Timothy Johnson]] of the Stayton Ward in central Oregon confessed inappropriate conduct with a minor to his bishop. Oregon law did not then require leaders to report a confessed abuse, "but they felt they should, and they did." After they reported it, "Johnson was sentenced to 15 years in prison for first-degree sodomy, sexual abuse, and unlawful sexual penetration of a girl under 16." Two years later his wife — and the children as plaintiffs — "sued the church *for reporting* the abuse" [^1].

### 2. The suit's logic punishes the report itself (strong)
Per the lawsuit, "church leaders' actions deprived Johnson's wife and children of his companionship, society, love, and income." The family sought $10 million for emotional distress "plus an additional $40,000 to repay Johnson's criminal defense attorney bills from the case in which he had been found guilty" — i.e. damages for the consequences of the church having told the truth to police [^2].

### 3. The dilemma is genuinely two-sided (strong — the core point)
"If you don't report the abuse, you get sued for millions because you're destroying a family. But if you do report the abuse, you're also getting sued for millions because you're also destroying a family. This is why the church has the helpline." The objection "just call the police, what's the harm?" assumes reporting is cost-free; the Johnson case shows it is not [^3].

### 4. The omission is the tell (moderate)
"Isn't it amazing that in all his investigating, Rezendes happened to miss the Johnson case?" A reporter building the narrative that LDS leaders fail to report abuse had strong reason to omit a case where leaders *did* report and were sued for it — because it dissolves the "systemic cover-up" frame into a no-win legal reality [^4]. See [[The Spotlight Analogy Equates a Celibate Cardinal With a Volunteer Bishop]].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
The strongest counter: the existence of a meritless suit for reporting does not justify *failing* to report; mandatory-reporting laws exist precisely because leaving it to clergy discretion lets some abuse continue (as it tragically did in the Adams case); and "we might get sued either way" can become an excuse for inaction that favors the institution over the child. The episode concedes this is "a debate to be had" — its narrower point is that the *reporting decision is not the obvious freebie* critics present, that the church built the helpline to navigate exactly this bind, and that a journalist who suppressed the counter-case (Johnson) was not reporting in good faith. The claim establishes the dilemma's reality, not that non-reporting is always right.

## Counter-objection: "mandatory reporting would solve it"
The episode answers that universal mandatory clergy reporting "would broadcast to the world that the confession is no longer confidential and actual abusers would only sink deeper into the shadows," scrapping the leverage that "hundreds of thousands of local … volunteer … bishops" have to urge abusers to stop [^5]. Whether that trade-off is worth it is contested; see [[The Abuse Helpline Routes Bishops to Legal Compliance Not Cover-Up]] and [[Confessional Privilege Creates a One-Way Street for Accusations]].
