# Whose God Can Ground Morally Reprehensible

## The Point
The debate's deepest disagreement is meta-ethical: *what makes "morally reprehensible" a meaningful charge in the first place?* [[James White]] argues that to even run the resolution you "need to have a God who is transcendent, who can therefore give you an understanding of what morality is" — and "an exalted man from another planet is insufficient for that." [^1] His God, being "the creator of all things," is by His very nature "the definer of goodness, grace, love, mercy, beauty... because there is no standard outside him." [^2] [[Jacob Hansen]] presses the counter-charge that White never *defines* goodness but merely asserts that God *is* goodness — a stipulation that defines goodness "out of existence": "He invents a game... Whatever I choose is automatically good... If he cheats, it's good. If he lies, it's good... Because good has been defined out of existence into whatever he does." [^3] Hansen offers his own standard: morality is grounded in **love** and "the nature of reality itself," where "moral language is what we use to describe something's relationship to the highest and lowest possible states of existence." [^4] White attacks this as ungrounded subjectivism resting on "experience," calling Hansen's claim that love is a "brute fact" self-refuting: "brute facts are irrational." [^5]

## Evidence
- **White's transcendence requirement.** "You need to have a transcendent eternal God to know what righteousness and justice are." [^6] He grounds it in Psalm 89:14 ("righteousness and justice are the foundation of your throne") and Daniel 4 (Nebuchadnezzar: "all his works are true, and his ways just" because He is creator). [^1]
- **Hansen's "inventor of the game" reductio.** The charge is circularity: if "good" just means "whatever God does," the predicate is empty and cannot be used to *praise* God. [^3] This is the practical edge of the Euthyphro worry (see [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]).
- **Hansen's positive account: love as the highest state.** "To love is to will the good of another" (Aquinas); the good is "to be in loving relationship with God and with one another," and acts are moral insofar as they move beings toward or away from that highest state. [^4] Asked for "specific standards," he repeats this relational criterion; White judges it non-responsive. [^7]
- **White's epistemic counterpunch.** When Hansen concedes he judges the Bible "by a standard outside the Bible" and grounds knowledge in direct *experience* of God, White warns that "experience" is an unstable foundation — "do you realize how different modern man interprets his experience than ancient man did?" — and that Jesus held the Sadducees accountable to *written* Scripture, not their experience (Matt 22). [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "You need to have a God who is transcendent, who can therefore give you an understanding of what morality is in the first place. And an exalted man from another planet is insufficient for that." — White [^1]

> "He invents a game and in this game he says, 'Whatever I do is right'... If he cheats, it's good. If he lies, it's good... good has been defined out of existence into whatever he does." — Hansen [^3]

> "Morality is grounded in the nature of love. And as the great Thomas Aquinas said, to love is to will the good of another without thought of oneself." — Hansen [^4]

> "You just said love is a brute fact. Therefore, love is irrational." — White [^5]

## LDS Connection
Both men reject *pure voluntarism* (good = God's bare command), which is exactly the target the Restoration also rejects ([[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]]). But they ground morality differently, and the LDS view sits closer to Hansen while answering White's best objection. The restored gospel locates the good not in God's arbitrary will but in **eternal law that God Himself honors** ([[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]) — the "third horn" of Euthyphro ([[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]). This blunts White's circularity worry from the *other* direction: because goodness is not whatever God stipulates, "God is good" is a substantive, falsifiable-in-principle claim — which is the very leverage Hansen needs to call the Calvinist God reprehensible. Yet White lands a real hit that the LDS apologist should not wave away: grounding morality in "experience" or a "brute fact" invites the [[By What Standard - Morality Requires an Objective Standard|by-what-standard]] challenge and the [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them|borrowed-capital]] charge. The stronger LDS answer is not "love is a brute fact of my experience" but "love is a feature of the eternal moral order both God and man inhabit" — an *ontological* grounding, not a merely experiential one, which is what Hansen gestures at but does not fully articulate under cross-examination.
