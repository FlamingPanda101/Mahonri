# The Law of Restoration as the Single Law of Justice

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that there is exactly **one** law of justice — the **Law of Restoration** of [[Alma 41.3-7|Alma 41]] — under which "we reap what we sow and if we refuse to repent then we reap the punishment attendant that refusal."[^1] This is offered as a "small correction" to [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories|Jacob Morgan's Divine Infusion theory]], which posited *two* laws (a "punitive justice" and a "deserts" justice). Ostler: "There is only one law of justice — the Law of Restoration... If we demand justice and refuse to forgive, then we will receive justice rather than mercy."[^1] It is the metaphysical engine that lets the [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Compassion Theory]] satisfy the "demands of justice" data (point D) *without* retribution — because on this law, justice simply means each person receives what they freely chose and truly desired, "as shown by our works in life."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Justice as natural consequence, not imposed penalty.** The Law of Restoration "returns to us what we truly desire as shown by our works"; the purpose of the law "that decrees that we shall receive according to our works in judgment is to awaken us to the suffering we will endure as a natural result of our actions if we don't repent."[^3] Hence "nothing could be more just than that."[^4] *(Strong: converts "justice" from a creditor's demand into the structure of moral reality — reaping what one sows.)*
2. **It grounds the degrees of glory.** The same law explains why "the degree of light or glory that 'quickens'... our bodies in the resurrection is dependent on whether we abide a telestial, terrestrial or celestial law" (D&C 88:20-32) — the kind of life we live *is* the judgment we receive.[^5] *(Fair: integrates resurrection and eschatology into the one-law framework.)*
3. **An *agape* theory of justice.** Ostler ties it to "an agape theory of ethics which bases moral judgments on the nature of what is required to establish loving relationships," noting he spends "not less than three chapters arguing for this view of justice" in *Exploring Mormon Thought*.[^6] *(Fair: the relational ethic is the deep root — justice and love are not opposed but unified.)*
4. **Steelman of the retributive reading it rejects.** Ostler *concedes* that the Nephite law in [[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34:11-12]] ("the law requireth the life of him who hath murdered") genuinely "assumes a retributive theory of justice." He grants this to a critic (Deidre Green) — but insists he cites that text *only* to establish the **innocence principle** (it is unjust to punish an innocent for the guilty), not to endorse retribution, which he "expressly rejected."[^7] *(Honest: he does not pretend the Book of Mormon is univocally non-retributive; he distinguishes the Nephite civil code's premise from the theory of *atonement's* justice.)*

## Best Quotes
> "There is only one law of justice — the Law of Restoration. According to this law we reap what we sow and if we refuse to repent then we reap the punishment attendant that refusal."[^1]

> "The 'demands of justice' are the demands of the Law of Restoration that each person shall have returned what s/he has sent out, reaping what has been sown."[^4]

> "This is the notion of justice inherent in an agape theory of ethics which bases moral judgments on the nature of what is required to establish loving relationships."[^6]
