# The Compassion Theory of Atonement

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that Latter-day Saint scripture contains a view of atonement that "radically differs from the historical theories and has the resources to avoid all of [their] problems" — the **Compassion Theory**.[^1] Atonement is not a transaction that pays a price but the **healing of sin-caused alienation** through an indwelling, interpenetrating union of life with Christ. It is the master thesis the rest of this cluster unpacks: the union mechanism is at [[Atonement as Indwelling Union That Transforms Darkness Into Light]], Christ's unique qualification at [[Christ Learned Compassion by Suffering Mortal Alienation]], and the rejection of retributive payment at [[Relationship Not Payment - Atonement Rejects Retributive Justice]]. It passes the [[The Desiderata and Abelards Constraint for a Theory of Atonement|desiderata]] that the rivals fail (see [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories]]).

Ostler states the theory in **eight (barely) essential features**:[^2]
1. **Sin is self-absorbed alienation.** We freely (initially innocently) harden our hearts, hiding from God and each other and violating the law of love, convincing ourselves alienation will bring happiness.
2. **Atonement persuades us to give up our alienation.** Absent atonement we'd be "super-fallen," unable to repent. God restores agency by (a) the light of Christ actuating conscience and (b) offering covenant relationship as unmerited grace; accepting it (signified by baptism) is the moment of justification.
3. **Repentance heals and maintains the relationship.** To remain in relationship with a perfect being we must abide the conditions of the law of love — ceasing alienating behavior, making reparation, asking forgiveness.
4. **Union with Christ results in new life and light.** Repenting, we commingle his light with ours, becoming a "new creature" in whom his image is renewed — *at-one* with Christ; sanctification is growth in that light toward **deification**.
5. **A condition of union is willingness to be vulnerable.** Because love entails vulnerability, the darkness in us causes Christ "momentary but excruciating pain" when mingled with his light — but he is *not punished* and does not bear our guilt; he experiences the pain *and subsequent joy* of relationship with imperfect humans.
6. **Christ is uniquely able to accomplish atonement.** He "learned compassion by the things that he suffered," achieved fulness of union with the Father in Gethsemane, and uniquely knew the *fulness of alienation* — abandonment by the Father — on the cross.
7. **Atonement is the mode of relationship God seeks with us.** To be at-one is divine union; Christ seeks to relate to us with the very oneness he has with the Father and Holy Ghost.
8. **Christ satisfied the demands of justice of the Law of Restoration.** "The demands of justice are answered... because we each receive what we freely choose — nothing could be more just than that."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **It saves all four scriptural data points (A-D).** Ostler claims his primary merit is explaining *both how and why* Christ bears our sins without being "unintelligible, arbitrary, illogical, or immoral": "He bears our sins because he takes our lives into his life in union. He suffers for our sins compassionately because... there is darkness and pain in our lives that causes him to suffer."[^3] *(Strong: unlike the moral-influence rivals, the suffering does real, explained work.)*
2. **Pauline / New Perspective corroboration.** Ostler aligns the theory with the New Perspective on Paul (citing NT scholar Morna Hooker): Paul's concern was being "found in Christ" through covenant faithfulness, not the *imputation* of an alien righteousness. Baptism symbolizes dying and rising with Christ ([[Romans 6.3-4|Romans 6:3-4]]); the sacrament symbolizes taking his life into ours. His "best summary" is [[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor. 5:21]]: God "made him to be sin who did not know sin, so that we might become the righteousness of God in him."[^4] *(Strong: independent NT scholarship; "in Christ" union is exactly the model.)*
3. **It explains sanctification, not just forgiveness.** Because the union *eradicates our sinful being with the light of Christ*, the theory accounts for being "sanctified through the atonement" ([[2 Nephi 2.8|2 Nephi 2:8]]) — a desideratum the Empathy and Infusion theories could not meet.[^3] *(Fair: directly targets a gap Ostler identified in the rivals.)*
4. **Steelman of the realist alternative it preserves.** Ostler does *not* deny a completed, past aspect of atonement: Christ "finished his preparations" in Gethsemane, on the cross, and in resurrection, and "all of the sin that existed as of that time" was extinguished. He affirms an "already accomplished" *and* an "ongoing and not yet fully accomplished" aspect — union with us through present repentance.[^5] *(Honest: this two-aspect move is how he keeps the historical anchoring that penal-substitution rightly insists on while relocating the *bearing of my sins* to the moment of justification.)*

## Best Quotes
> "He bears our sins because he takes our lives into his life in union. He suffers for our sins compassionately because he knows of our suffering and actually experiences the pain of our sins as his own."[^3]

> "For our sake he [God] made him [Christ] to be sin who did not know sin, so that we might become the righteousness of God in him [Christ]." — 2 Cor. 5:21, Ostler's "best summary of the Compassion Theory"[^4]

> "Atonement is the story of God's gracious offer of love to accept us as worthy of covenant relationship just as we are and to enter into a relationship in which the energies of our lives are literally mingled as one."[^6]
