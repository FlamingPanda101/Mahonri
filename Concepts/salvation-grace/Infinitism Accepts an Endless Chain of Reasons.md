# Infinitism Accepts an Endless Chain of Reasons

## The Point

**Infinitism** is the response to the [[The Epistemic Regress Problem|regress problem]] that embraces the second horn of the trilemma: the chain of reasons **goes on infinitely**. [[Kevin McCain]] describes it as the view that, so long as the chain *could* continue — there being a further reason for each of your reasons without end — then you have justification. He flags it as a very recent and still-minority position, one historically dismissed by skeptics and non-skeptics alike.[^1]

## Argument

1. **An endless chain is the mark of justification.** On infinitism, justification consists in there being "another reason for each of your reasons infinitely"; the availability of that unending chain is what it is to be justified, rather than a defect to be avoided.[^1]

2. **It is a late arrival in the history of epistemology.** Unlike foundationalism (dominant since Aristotle) and coherentism, infinitism "has only really come about in very recent times in the late 20th century and contemporary times."[^1]

3. **It was long thought to be a non-starter.** McCain stresses that "historically this view was largely dismissed by both skeptics and non-skeptics alike; pretty much everyone thought that infinitism was not a real option" — and that it remains "relatively unpopular" today.[^1][^2] *Steelman:* the infinitist's defenders argue the regress is only vicious if every reason must be *completed* before it justifies; if justification instead requires only that one can always provide a further reason when challenged, an infinite chain of *available* reasons is no more impossible than the infinite series of true propositions we already accept.

## Best Quotes

> "This is roughly the idea that as long as this chain of reasons could continue, where you have another reason for each of your reasons infinitely, then you have justification."[^1]

> "Historically this view was largely dismissed by both skeptics and non-skeptics alike; pretty much everyone thought that infinitism was not a real option."[^1]

> "Infinitism has started to gain some ground but is still relatively unpopular."[^2]
