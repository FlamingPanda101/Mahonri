# The Causal Eis Reading of Acts 2.38 Lacks Greek Support

## The Claim
[[Acts 2.38|Acts 2:38]] — "repent and be baptized... *for* the forgiveness of your sins" (Greek *eis aphesin*) — is a key baptism-saves text. [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] defends the faith-alone reading by arguing *eis* here is **causal**: "be baptized *because* your sins are [already] forgiven," paralleling "they repented *at* (eis) the preaching of Jonah" (Matthew 12:41) and "I baptize you *for* (eis) repentance." [[Joe Heschmeyer]] responds that this causal-*eis* proposal originated with the Baptist grammarian [[Julius Mantey]] specifically to "explain away Acts 2:38," that Mantey admitted "none of the Greek lexicons agree with him," and that [[Ralph Marcus]] decisively refuted it in peer review — so in "no known Greek manuscript... does *eis* ever mean what you're claiming."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The proposal is Mantey's, and contested at birth (rating: strong; documented history).** Heschmeyer traces the causal-*eis* to [[Julius Mantey]] (1950s), who "begins his argument by saying... none of the Greek lexicons agree with him," then adduced passages from Polybius and Josephus. [[Ralph Marcus]] (Journal of Biblical Literature, "On Causal *Eis*," 1951) "flatly state[d]" Mantey was "mistaken in his construing and rendering of all these passages," and the back-and-forth ("The Elusive Causal *Eis*") disproved each example.[^1]
2. **Even faith-alone scholars concede the point (rating: strong; hostile witness).** [[Daniel Wallace]] — who himself *denies* baptismal regeneration — grants that "Marcus ably demonstrated that the linguistic evidence of a causal *eis* fell short of proof." So the concession comes from Ryan's own side.[^2]
3. **Ryan's parallels don't establish causality (rating: moderate; lexical).** Heschmeyer argues "repented *at* the preaching" can mean "at," which is "different than in view of or because of — that's still not causal," and "I baptize you *for* repentance" most naturally means baptism *leading to / with reference to* repentance, not "because you already repented." Ryan also floats a singular/plural argument (repent = plural, be baptized = singular) to detach baptism from forgiveness; Heschmeyer treats this as special pleading.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
On the narrow grammatical question, Heschmeyer has the stronger hand: the causal-*eis* is not accepted by the standard lexicons (BDAG), Marcus's refutation is the recognized result, and even Wallace concedes it "fell short of proof." So Ryan's central move on Acts 2:38 is genuinely weak as *linguistics*. Two caveats keep it from being a knockout: (a) the verse's force can still be parried *without* causal-*eis* — e.g. reading *eis aphesin* as the *goal* of the repent-and-be-baptized complex while tying the actual remission to the faith/repentance it expresses (a move closer to the metonymy reading in [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]]); and (b) "for the forgiveness of sins" echoes the *same phrase* used of John's baptism and of Jesus' blood (Matthew 26:28), so the construction alone doesn't settle the mechanism. Still, as a matter of Greek, "because of" is the part that lacks support. This is a Catholic-vs-Reformed dispute over one preposition with outsized doctrinal weight.

## LDS Relevance
Latter-day Saints read [[Acts 2.38|Acts 2:38]] at face value: Peter couples repentance and baptism *for* (unto) the remission of sins and the gift of the Holy Ghost — precisely the gospel's first ordinances (Articles of Faith 1:4), administered by authority. The collapse of the causal-*eis* reading removes a common Protestant objection to the LDS (and Catholic/Orthodox) understanding that baptism is instrumentally tied to remission. The vault's existing [[Acts 2.38]] note frames the same dispute; this concept adds the specific scholarly adjudication ([[Ralph Marcus]] vs [[Julius Mantey]]) that the grammatical escape lacks support.
