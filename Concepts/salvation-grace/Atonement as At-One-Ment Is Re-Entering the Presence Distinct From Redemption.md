# Atonement as At-One-Ment Is Re-Entering the Presence Distinct From Redemption

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] distinguishes two things often merged under "the atonement." The **redemption** is Christ *paying the bride-price* — the suffering in Gethsemane and on the cross, "purchasing, redeeming our immortal souls" at "the cost of the blood of an eternal being." The **atonement** proper is *at-one-ment*: being brought back into God's presence, "being at one with him," made possible *because* the redemption was paid. On this reading the **Day of Atonement** "isn't the day the sacrifice is offered, it's the day the bride and groom see each other" — the day of reunion. The torn temple veil therefore does not abolish the temple; it inaugurates a *new* veil through which the redeemed now ascend to be at one with God. This sharpens the English-word point the vault already makes at [[Atonement as Covering - Joseph Could Not Have Known]] and grounds [[The Veil Is Christ Reversing the Day of Atonement]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The word *at-one-ment*.** Woodmansee: "think about the word atonement — it's *at-one-ment*, so to me that indicates being in his presence... the atonement is not necessarily the sacrifice that was offered, it's the fact that the redemption was paid so that at some point we can re-enter his presence." See [[Atonement as Covering - Joseph Could Not Have Known]] for the related coverings/*kaphar* reading. *Rating: the English etymology is genuine (Tyndale coined "at-one-ment"); the redemption/at-one-ment *distinction* is the conceptual contribution.*[^1]
2. **John 17 as the goal.** She grounds at-one-ment in [[John 17.20-23|John 17:21-23]] ("that they may be one in me as I am in you... that they may be one together") — oneness/union as the *telos* of the atonement, not merely legal pardon. Coheres with [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]] and [[Relationship Not Payment - Atonement Rejects Retributive Justice]]. *Rating: John 17 does frame salvation as union; the reading is well-supported.*[^1]
3. **The Day of Atonement as a reunion, not a payment.** "The day of atonement isn't the day that the sacrifice is offered, it's the day that the bride and groom see each other... because now they are at one." The bride-price (redemption) is paid *prior*; Yom Kippur enacts the *meeting*. *Rating: a typological reframing that fuses the wedding and Yom Kippur frames; interpretive but internally consistent.*[^2]
4. **It dissolves the "rent veil ends temples" objection.** Because the redemption *opened* the way, the rent veil means "you need a new veil," not "no veil" — the redeemed now cross to be *at one*. See [[The Veil Is Christ Reversing the Day of Atonement]] and [[Hebrews 10.19-22|Hebrews 10:19-20]]. *Rating: coherent with the reversal claim; depends on it.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The redemption/at-one-ment split is a *theological* construction, not a lexical fact about the Hebrew *kippur* (which centers on "covering/purgation of sin," effected precisely *by* the blood rite on Yom Kippur) — so prying "the sacrifice" apart from "the atonement" runs against the Levitical sense, where the Day of Atonement *is* the day the blood is offered. A Protestant reads the rent veil (Matthew 27:51) and Hebrews 10 as teaching that the *finished* sacrifice grants immediate, unmediated access — no further "new veil" required. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim does not deny the blood rite is central; it observes that the *purpose* of the covering is *reunion* (at-one-ment), which Hebrews itself frames as "draw near... enter into the holiest," language of approach into a continuing sanctuary; the wedding/Yom-Kippur fusion is offered as devotional typology cohering with the vault's relationship-centered atonement notes, not as a replacement for the Levitical mechanics.
