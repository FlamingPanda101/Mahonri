# Reading Perfection in Context Cures the Inward Checklist

## The Claim
[[Trevan Hatch]] argues that the *isolated* reading of [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] feeds a "selfish," inward-focused checklist — "am I doing my scripture, am I going to the temple, am I listening to prophets" — whereas reading the verse in its love-context ([[Luke 6.36|Luke 6:36]]; see [[The Therefore in Matthew 5.48 Ties Perfection to Loving Enemies]]) **redirects the disciple outward** to "the least among us." The contextual reading is itself pastorally therapeutic for religious scrupulosity.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The isolated verse produces an inward checklist (rated: strong as diagnosis).** "We take this verse and we say, okay, I have to be perfect... an endless list of a checklist that's inward focused. And Christ is saying... how about first and foremost we look outward." The error is *isolation* — putting the verse "on an island" and loading it with exaltation doctrine "out of context."[^1]

2. **It maps onto a real mental-health problem (rated: strong, corroborated).** Hatch's returned-missionary students "struggle a lot with mental health... religious OCD," and the BYU Counseling Center, BYU Studies, and Faith Matters (Justin Dyer) have all addressed Matthew 5:48 to "pull back on the toxic perfectionism." Crucially, the social scientists find the church's *doctrine* does not *cause* the OCD — see [[Toxic Perfectionism Lower Among Religious Than Non-Religious]] — but a mistranslated verse can "exacerbate" it.[^2]

3. **The fix complements the grace literature (rated: moderate).** Hatch ties the contextual reading to the broader Latter-day Saint recovery of grace (he cites Brad Wilcox's widely-referenced talk) and to decoupling worth from performance — see [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]]. The remedy is not lowering the standard but relocating it from self-auditing to charity.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic of the "just read it in context" fix could say the inward disciplines (scripture, temple, obedience) are *also* commanded, so re-reading one verse will not dissolve a culture that genuinely emphasizes worthiness checklists; the perfectionism may be driven by lived practice (worthiness interviews, "raising the bar" missionary rhetoric) more than by a Greek mistranslation. The pastoral relief is real but partial — it addresses *one* prooftext, not the whole ecology of scrupulosity. Hatch would likely agree: he frames the contextual reading as *one* corrective to be added alongside the grace emphasis, not a complete cure.

## Counter-Objection
"If perfectionism isn't caused by the doctrine, why does the reading matter?" Hatch's answer: because even a non-causal verse can *exacerbate* an existing tendency, and because giving members the accurate, outward meaning replaces a fear-driven self-audit with the actual command — love, including of enemies — which is harder but freeing.

## Best Quotes
> "We take this verse and we say, okay, I have to be perfect... an endless list of a checklist that's inward focused. And Christ is saying... how about first and foremost we look outward."[^1]

> "It's not the church, it's not the doctrine... that pushes them into religious OCD... but it could exacerbate the problem."[^2]

> "Emphasize grace... be therefore loving is the message there."[^3]
