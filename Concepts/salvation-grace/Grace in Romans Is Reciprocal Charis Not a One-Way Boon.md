# Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the **Augustinian/Protestant picture of grace** — hierarchical, a one-directional "boon" given from above to a *passive*, undeserving recipient with nothing returned — is anachronistic to how a first-century Greco-Roman audience would have heard Paul's *charis*. In that world **charis** named a *reciprocal* relationship: a gift created an obligation to respond, depicted in the three **Graces** (Charites) in a circular dance — one gives, one receives, one returns. There is no passivity and no sense that the gift is "unjustified"; rather something of *benefit* is given and reciprocated. So "being justified **freely** by his grace" ([[Romans 3.23-25|Romans 3:24]]) means God was **not compelled** to give (cf. *xenia*/guest-friendship), not that nothing is owed in return. This recasts Paul's gospel as an *active relationship* with reciprocal obligations, against the "passive recipient" reading. It stands in productive tension with the vault's [[Paul Redefined Charis as Unmerited Gift Against Greco-Roman Reciprocity|Cecilia Peek reading]] of the *same* verse.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Charis was reciprocal in the Greco-Roman world.** Thomassen: on the Augustinian view grace is hierarchical, a "boon... given from an above source that is unjustified," with a "passive" recipient — "all four of those notions are anachronistic." Greek *charis* was "a reciprocal relationship... there's a demand on the giver to bestow a favor, [and] something should be coming back." *Rating: tracks the scholarship of John Barclay (*Paul and the Gift*) on the *circularity* of ancient gift, though Barclay argues Paul's innovation is "incongruity" — see steelman.* See [[John Barclay]].[^1]
2. **The three Graces as the image.** The *Charites* (Latin *Gratiae*) are "usually depicted as three young women... in a circular dance — one giving something, one receiving, and the third returning a gift," a model "much older" than the Greeks (Thomassen cites Japanese *on-gaeshi*; Sophocles' *Ajax*: "favor begets favor"). *Rating: the iconography of the Graces (Seneca, *De Beneficiis*, reads them this way) genuinely encodes give-receive-return; a real ancient datum.*[^2]
3. **"Freely" = not compelled, not "no return."** On [[Romans 3.23-25|Romans 3:24]] ("justified *freely* by his grace"), Thomassen: "it's not that it's freely as in no cost... it's God is not *compelled* to do that," analogized to *xenia*/*hospitalia* (guest-friendship) — the host is not obligated to receive the stranger, but having done so, obligations crystallize on both sides. *Rating: a defensible reading of one nuance of *dōrean*; note the vault's scripture note on this verse glosses *dōrean* as "without payment," so the two readings press different senses.*[^3]
4. **It makes the gospel relational and active.** The payoff: "at a core level it's relational... an active relationship, where something is coming and something is going... favor begets favor — it's not singular, not one time." This dovetails with the LDS emphasis on covenant reciprocity. See [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]]. *Rating: coheres with covenant theology; the "active reciprocity" frame is the thesis.*[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The leading scholar of ancient grace, **John Barclay**, agrees *charis* was embedded in reciprocity *but argues Paul's distinctive move is to "perfect" the gift's **incongruity*** — God gives it *without regard to the worth* of the recipient (the ungodly, Romans 5:6-8) — which is much closer to the "unmerited" reading than to a balanced give-and-return. So "grace is reciprocal, therefore not unmerited" is a *non sequitur*: a gift can both create a return-obligation *and* be given to the undeserving. Paul's *dōrean* ("freely/as a gift," Romans 3:24) and his explicit "not of works" (Romans 4:4-5; 11:6) resist collapsing grace into ordinary do-ut-des exchange — which is also why the vault's [[Paul Redefined Charis as Unmerited Gift Against Greco-Roman Reciprocity|Peek note]] reads the verse as *inverting* worthiness-reciprocity. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** Thomassen's point is compatible with Barclay's — grace is *incongruous* (given to the unworthy) *yet still reciprocal* (it obligates a response of loyalty), which is exactly the covenant structure; the target is only the *passive-recipient/nothing-owed* caricature, not the unmerited *initiation* of the gift. The two vault notes thus capture two true facets of the same verse: God's gift is unprompted by our worth *and* it draws us into an active, returning relationship.

## Best Quotes
> "Grace is reciprocal... there's a demand that is on the giver to bestow a favor — so if I give you something, then something should be coming back, which means the passivity doesn't work." — [[Brian Thomassen]][^1]

> "It's not that it's freely as in no cost associated with what you're doing — it's God is not *compelled* to do that... once he has done so, now there are certain obligations that come into play." — [[Brian Thomassen]][^3]
