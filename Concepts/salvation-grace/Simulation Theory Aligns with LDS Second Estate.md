# Simulation Theory Aligns with LDS Second Estate

## The Claim

[[David Butler]] argues that atheists who embrace simulation theory — the
hypothesis that our perceived reality is a computer simulation created by a
more advanced intelligence — are unknowingly affirming the conceptual structure
of LDS theology. LDS doctrine already describes mortal life as a \"second
estate\": a temporary probationary state, away from \"base reality\" (the
pre-mortal existence with God), with a designed purpose (testing and growth),
after which we return to base reality (resurrection and judgment). Every
conceptual building block of simulation theory — a higher intelligence, a
constructed non-ultimate reality, purpose within the simulation, subjects
within a designed system — is already present in the LDS plan of salvation.
Atheists who dismiss theism as irrational but entertain simulation theory as
plausible are applying inconsistent standards.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Core Parallel: Base Reality → Simulation → Return (Evidence Rating: Primary — Doctrinal)

Butler lays out the structural isomorphism between simulation theory and LDS
theology:[^1]

Simulation Theory:
1. **Base Reality:** The \"real\" world where the simulator/programmer exists.
2. **The Simulation:** A constructed, non-ultimate reality inhabited by subjects
   who may be unaware they are in a simulation.
3. **Purpose:** Testing, observation, or experience (depending on the theory).
4. **Exit:** The simulation ends, and subjects either return to base reality
   or cease to exist.

LDS Plan of Salvation:
1. **Base Reality:** Pre-mortal existence (First Estate) — spirits living in
   God's presence.
2. **The Simulation:** Mortal life (Second Estate) — a probationary state where
   we are separated from direct knowledge of base reality by the \"veil of
   forgetfulness.\"
3. **Purpose:** \"We will prove them herewith, to see if they will do all things
   whatsoever the Lord their God shall command them\" ([[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]]).[^2]
4. **Exit:** Death, resurrection, judgment, and return to God's presence (or
   assignment to a kingdom of glory).

> \"We actually believe in the simulation. There was a time given to men of
> probation. Yes. And we're put in right now. We are not in — we are in a state
> that is away from base [reality]. Like we've left base reality and come here
> in our second estate — and then we are going to return back to base reality.
> Reality is eternal life with God.\"[^1]

### 2. Atheists Who Accept Simulation Theory Have Conceded the Conceptual Framework of Theism (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Logical Analysis)

The argument is not that simulation theory *proves* LDS theology, but that it
concedes everything the atheist needs to grant to make theism rationally
permissible:[^1]

1. **A higher intelligence exists** — The simulator/programmer is an intelligent
   agent capable of creating realities. This is theism's core claim: a creator
   intelligence exists.
2. **Our reality is not ultimate** — What we perceive as \"the world\" is not
   base reality. This undermines the atheist's typical objection that \"we only
   observe the natural world, and there's no evidence of anything beyond it.\"
   If you're in a simulation, the \"natural world\" IS the constructed layer.
3. **Purpose is designed, not emergent** — The simulation has a purpose
   determined by the simulator, not by the random processes of physics. This is
   theism's teleology in different language.
4. **Intelligence precedes and creates reality** — The simulator's mind exists
   prior to and independent of the simulated reality, and the simulated reality
   depends on the simulator for its existence. This is exactly the theistic
   claim about God and creation.

If an atheist says \"an advanced alien civilization could be running a
simulation we're in\" but then says \"a divine intelligence creating our
reality is irrational,\" they are making a distinction without a difference.
The only remaining dispute is the *identity* of the creator — not whether the
concept of a creator is coherent.

### 3. The AI Parallel: \"A Form of Godliness but Denying the Power Thereof\" (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Scriptural/Cultural)

Butler extends the simulation/creation parallel to artificial intelligence:[^3]

> \"AI is obviously huge here and it has a form of godliness but denies the
> power thereof, right? It seems intelligent and in many ways it is
> intelligent. There is no way that out there in the universe, atoms are going
> to collide and recreate and then perfectly organize themselves and then all
> of a sudden create an electrical current to be able to power a computer with
> all of its complex chips and then code it all and make AI. That's absolutely
> absurd to think that could happen.\"[^3]

The reference is to 2 Timothy 3:5: \"Having a form of godliness, but denying
the power thereof.\" Butler's point: AI appears intelligent, but it was
created BY intelligence — it didn't spontaneously emerge from non-intelligence.
The atheist who marvels at AI (created by conscious human minds) but insists
human consciousness \"emerged\" from non-conscious matter is applying
inconsistent standards. Intelligent systems come FROM intelligence — whether
we're talking about AI from humans, simulated realities from simulators, or
creation from God.

> \"But when it comes to us, when it comes to our consciousness, when it comes
> to the way that all intelligent life forms operate, then it's like, yeah, of
> course that happened [by chance].\"[^3]

### 4. LDS Theology Uniquely Bridges the \"Natural\" / \"Supernatural\" Gap (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Doctrinal)

A standard atheist objection to the simulation theory parallel is that theism
posits a \"supernatural\" God while the simulator is a \"natural\" advanced
being. Butler's response (implicit) is that LDS theology has already collapsed
this distinction:

- LDS theology teaches that God is an embodied, exalted being who operates
  through eternal law — not a disembodied spirit outside time and space.
- God is not \"supernatural\" in the Platonic sense of being pure form outside
  matter; He is a glorified, perfected physical being who has mastered eternal
  physical and moral law.
- This makes the LDS God more analogous to an advanced simulator-creator than
  the creedal God of classical theism — an exalted being with mastery over the
  laws that govern reality, creating worlds through those laws.

As [[Alex O'Connor]] has noted, LDS theology is more internally consistent than
creedal Christianity precisely because it doesn't have the incoherent
\"immaterial being creating material reality\" problem.[^4] The simulation
parallel works better for LDS theology than it does for classical theism.

### 5. The \"Monopoly Analogy\" Connection: The Game Board Is the Simulation (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Integration)

Butler's [[Monopoly Analogy for Lifes Purpose]] connects here: the \"game
board\" IS the simulation. The rules of Monopoly (the \"physics\" of the game)
are the constants and laws that govern the simulated reality. The meta-purpose
(having fun, building relationships) is the reason the simulation was created.
The atheist who only sees the game board — who treats the simulation as
ultimate reality — is like the player who thinks winning Monopoly IS the
purpose, missing entirely why the game was set up in the first place.[^5]

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Simulation Theory Is Naturalistic; LDS Theology Is Supernatural

**Steelman:** The simulation hypothesis posits a natural, physical computer
running our universe — not a transcendent God. The simulator is just a more
technologically advanced physical being, operating within the same universe (or
a meta-universe) governed by the same physical laws. This is fundamentally
different from theism, which posits a transcendent, uncreated God who exists
outside any physical framework. Conflating the two is a category error. An
alien running a simulation is not \"God\" in any meaningful theological sense.

**Response:** The LDS response to this is distinctive and stronger than most
theistic traditions can offer. LDS theology already teaches that God is an
embodied, exalted being who operates through eternal law — He is not
\"supernatural\" in the Platonic sense of pure spirit outside space and time.
Joseph Smith taught that \"God himself was once as we are now, and is an
exalted man\" (King Follett Discourse). The God of LDS theology is more like a
being who has mastered the laws of reality and can create within them — a
concept much closer to a simulator-creator than to a disembodied Platonic form.
Furthermore, the claim \"the simulator is not God\" depends on defining God in
a way the LDS tradition doesn't require. From the perspective of beings INSIDE
the simulation, the simulator is functionally omniscient (can observe all states
in the simulation), omnipotent (can modify any parameter), and the creator of
their experienced reality. Whether the simulator is called \"God\" or \"an
advanced alien\" is, from inside the simulation, a semantic distinction.

### Objection 2: The Parallel Is Superficial — Many Traditions Have an \"Illusory World\" Concept

**Steelman:** Many religious and philosophical traditions describe the physical
world as less real than a higher reality. Plato's Cave allegory describes
prisoners seeing only shadows of true forms. Hindu Maya describes the physical
world as illusion. Buddhist thought describes samsara as a realm of suffering
from which one seeks liberation. Gnosticism describes the material world as a
prison created by a lesser deity. Finding a parallel between simulation theory
and LDS doctrine is not distinctive — you could say the same about half a dozen
other worldviews. The structural parallel to simulation theory is an artifact of
a near-universal human intuition that the physical world isn't ultimate, not
evidence of LDS doctrinal truth.

**Response:** Butler's point is narrower than the objection assumes. He's
specifically addressing *atheists* who (a) dismiss theism as irrational while
(b) finding simulation theory intellectually respectable. The other traditions
cited (Plato, Hinduism, Buddhism, Gnosticism) are all themselves religious or
metaphysical worldviews — the atheist who accepts simulation theory has joined
their company in positing a non-ultimate reality with a higher intelligence
behind it. The point is not that LDS theology uniquely parallels simulation
theory — it's that the atheist who embraces simulation theory has already
conceded the rational framework for theism. If you can take [[Nick Bostrom]]'s
simulation argument seriously, you have no principled basis for dismissing the
concept of a creator God as inherently irrational. The remaining dispute is
evidence, not conceptual coherence.

### Objection 3: Simulation Hypothesis Doesn't Entail Purpose or Morality

**Steelman:** Even if we are in a simulation, that doesn't tell us WHY the
simulation exists. It could be a high school science project, a video game, an
academic study, or an accident. The simulator could be indifferent, malevolent,
or long dead. Simulation theory provides no basis for objective morality or
purpose — it's just an explanation for why reality has the structure it does.
Butler illegitimately imports LDS theological content (probation, testing,
eternal purpose) into the bare structural parallel.

**Response:** This objection is correct as far as it goes — simulation theory
itself doesn't entail purpose or morality. Butler's argument doesn't claim it
does. The argument is a *defensive* one aimed at the atheist who says theism is
irrational: \"You already accept that we could be in a reality created by a
higher intelligence for a purpose. Theism adds content to that structure
— specifically, that the purpose is moral probation and the creator is good.
You can dispute the added content, but you can't dismiss the structure as
irrational.\" LDS theology fills in the purpose through revealed scripture
([[Abraham 3.25|Abraham 3:25]]); the bare structural parallel opens the door
to taking revealed purpose claims seriously.

### Objection 4: Elon Musk's Simulation Argument Is a Thought Experiment, Not a Belief

**Steelman:** [[Elon Musk]] and other simulation-theory popularizers typically
present it as a probabilistic argument (\"it's more likely that we're in a
simulation than not\"), not as a positive belief. And most atheist philosophers
reject simulation theory as untestable metaphysics. Butler is attacking a
strawman by treating a niche thought experiment as a widespread atheist
commitment.

**Response:** Butler's target is not \"all atheists believe simulation theory\"
but the double standard of the *subset* of atheists who find simulation theory
plausible while dismissing theism. And the argument works even if only 1% of
atheists accept simulation theory: for that 1%, the inconsistency stands. For
the 99% who reject both simulation theory AND theism, the argument doesn't
apply — Butler would instead use the fine-tuning or moral arguments. The
simulation argument is one tool in a broader apologetic toolkit, not a
standalone proof.
