# The Unevangelized Gap Is a Tu Quoque Against the Apostasy Objection

## The Claim

The host's intuitive objection to the Restoration is the **1,800-year gap**: why would Christ found a church, let it lose its authority within a couple of generations, and then wait ~1,800 years to restore it through one man in America? Hansen does not deny the force of the intuition (he grants it is "very strong circumstantial evidence that just makes it seem not right," though "not a fatal argument" because no contradiction is shown). Instead he turns it around with a **tu quoque** on the unevangelized.[^1]

The reversal: on the standard creedal scheme, salvation runs through a *normative path* (gospel → faith/baptism → the visible church). But "most of the world outside of like Europe and a little bit of North Africa and the Middle East didn't have access to" that path until the 1400s–1600s or later. So "most of humans throughout most of history are in the **exception, not the rule** — at that point, is it even a rule?" The creedal Christian who mocks an 1,800-year gap "almost has an 1,800-year gap where that same gospel and church was not available to most of humans throughout human history."[^2]

Then the LDS resolution: the restored **temple and priesthood** "reach back throughout time," and in the **spirit world** the dead "have the opportunity to fully accept the gospel." Vicarious ordinances plus postmortem teaching mean "every single human throughout all human history and time falls under the normative rules for salvation… there's no outliers." So the LDS system is, on Hansen's telling, the *only* one under which everyone is normatively covered — whereas "the Orthodox, Catholics, Protestants… have this gap in God's ability to normatively save most of humans throughout all human history."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. It re-targets the objection instead of absorbing it — rate: strong rhetorically
The apostasy-gap objection assumes the objector's own scheme has *no* comparable gap. Hansen denies exactly that assumption: the [[The Unevangelized Dilemma Refutes Cant-Know-Morality-Without-Scripture|unevangelized]] are a temporal-and-geographic hole in the creedal normative path that dwarfs 1,800 years. Turning a "why the delay?" objection into "your scheme delayed access for *most people who ever lived*" is a clean burden-shift, kin to the conditional-access tu quoque in [[Conditional Access Tu Quoque Against the LDS God]] and the framing in [[Omnibenevolence vs Conditional Access Models]].

### 2. The population point reframes "normative" — rate: moderate
If a "normative" path excludes the numerical majority of humanity, Hansen presses that the word loses meaning ("is it even a rule?"). The LDS answer makes *normative* literally universal via proxy work, which is the positive payoff of [[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]] applied not to the problem of evil but to the **apostasy debate**: the same temple-and-spirit-world machinery that resolves the soteriological triad also neutralizes the gap objection.

### 3. It is distinct from the standard postmortem-salvation note — rate: distinct
[[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]] is *defensive* (it dissolves the inconsistent triad about God's goodness). This is *offensive*: it weaponizes the unevangelized to **reverse an objection aimed at the Restoration**, claiming comparative advantage over creedal Christianity rather than merely answering an internal puzzle. The two share a mechanism but do different argumentative work.

### Steelman of the creedal reply — rate: this is the strongest counter
The two "gaps" are **disanalogous**. (a) *Kind of gap:* the unevangelized problem is about *individuals who never heard*, for which traditional Christianity has its own resources — invincible ignorance, the [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested|harrowing of Hades]], baptism of desire/blood, the *logos spermatikos* — whereas the LDS apostasy is the claim that the *true church itself ceased to exist on earth*, a stronger and more peculiar claim. (b) *Quantifying "most":* Christianity has been the largest world religion for centuries, and counting heads across deep time to label the majority "the exception" is contestable as a measure of what is *normative*. (c) The host's own rejoinder in the dialogue: the unevangelized question still leaves the *historic institution* intact and points "all the more to the importance of being aligned with" that institution, however God ultimately gathers outsiders into the mystical body — so the asymmetry (no church on earth vs. a church that exists but hasn't reached everyone) remains.
