# Guilt Is Corrective but Shame Attacks Identity

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]] draws a clinical line between **guilt** and **shame** that is decisive for treating scrupulosity. **Guilt** focuses on a *behavior*, "sparks enough movement" to correct, and "keeps us on the covenant path" — it is healthy and even necessary, like physical pain warning of injury. **Shame** focuses on the *self* — "we're not at all worthy and never will be," "not good enough for God" — and is "detrimental to our psychological well-being," far harder to repair. Conflating the two ("feeling shame and thinking it's guilt") keeps the scrupulous person trapped.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Guilt is behavior-focused and corrective (rated: strong; standard in the shame literature).** Guilt "is an opportunity for us to look at our behaviors ... it sparks enough movement where we are willing to correct"; it "keeps us in check ... on the covenant path." So "guilt is not necessarily unhealthy — you kind of need to feel it."[^1] This matches the clinical consensus (Tangney, Lewis) that guilt ("I did a bad thing") motivates reparative action.

2. **Shame is self-focused and corrosive (rated: strong).** "Shame causes us to think that we're not at all worthy and never will be ... not good enough to be in this church or in this gospel"; it has "very detrimental psychological issues" and "it's harder to repair."[^2] Shame ("I am bad") attacks identity rather than conduct.

3. **A spiritual mapping (rated: moderate; interpretive but apt).** The host offers, and Allen affirms, that "shame is more focused on me, and guilt is more focused on Christ" — guilt arises "when I'm not aligned with him," signaling a relationship to repair, whereas shame turns inward and severs it.[^3] This reframes godly sorrow (2 Cor 7:10) as the *guilt* pole, and the adversary's accusation as the *shame* pole.

4. **The pain analogy (rated: illustrative).** Guilt is like physical pain: "if I pulled my finger back and don't feel it, I'm going to break my finger" — pain "can be a warning ... corrective."[^4] Removing all guilt would be as dangerous as congenital insensitivity to pain; the goal is to keep guilt's signal while disarming shame's verdict.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A critic could argue the guilt/shame line is cleaner in theory than in lived religion, where worthiness language ("are you worthy?") and public confession can *produce* shame even when only guilt is intended — so the distinction, while true, may not protect the scrupulous from an environment that conflates them. Allen partly grants this: scrupulosity fuses "I'm not good enough" with identity over years, which is precisely shame, and his prescription is therefore not "feel more guilt" but to **decouple worth from performance** (see [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]]) and to **stay with Christ in the moment** (see [[Stay With Christ in the Moment of Scrupulosity]]) so that corrective guilt does not curdle into identity-shame.

## Best Quotes
> "Guilt is not necessarily unhealthy ... it keeps us on the covenant path. Shame is completely different ... shame causes us to think that we're not at all worthy and never will be."[^1]

> "Shame is more focused on me, and guilt is more focused on Christ ... the guilt comes from when I'm not aligned with him."[^3]
