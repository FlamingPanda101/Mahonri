# Foundationalism Ends the Regress in Basic Beliefs

## The Point

**Foundationalism** is the response to the [[The Epistemic Regress Problem|regress problem]] that says the chain of reasons *ends*. According to [[Kevin McCain]], it is the most popular view in contemporary epistemology and arguably the dominant view since Aristotle: trace the reasons for any justified belief back far enough and you reach a **foundation of basic beliefs** that are themselves justified but require no further reasons. All other justified beliefs rest on this foundation.[^1]

## Argument

1. **Justification bottoms out in foundational beliefs.** If we follow the reasons for a justified belief, and the reasons for those reasons, "eventually we hit a foundation of reasons or beliefs that are themselves justified but they don't require any further justification."[^1]

2. **The classic foundation is experience.** McCain notes that contemporary foundationalists often locate the foundation in beliefs justified *by experience*: an experience can justify a belief without itself needing a further reason. When you look out and see a tree, your visual experience *is* your reason for believing there is a tree, "but there's no further reason in support of that experience."[^2]

3. **These basic beliefs stop the regress.** Because foundational beliefs are justified yet do not depend on further reasons, they halt the otherwise endless demand for "reasons for reasons." The regress terminates, and "all other beliefs rest on this foundation of justified beliefs."[^3] *Steelman of the worry:* a critic asks how a non-propositional experience can confer justification without itself being justified; the foundationalist replies that experiences are the right kind of thing to give reasons without themselves standing in need of them — they are reason-givers, not further beliefs requiring support.

## Best Quotes

> "All of your justified beliefs, if they're justified and we trace back the reasons for those beliefs... eventually we hit a foundation of reasons or beliefs that are themselves justified but they don't require any further justification."[^1]

> "Your visual experience is your reason for believing you see a tree, but there's no further reason in support of that experience."[^2]

> "Foundationalists claim those beliefs are justified and all other beliefs rest on this foundation of justified beliefs."[^3]
