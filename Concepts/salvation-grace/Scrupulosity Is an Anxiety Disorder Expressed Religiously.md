# Scrupulosity Is an Anxiety Disorder Expressed Religiously

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]] argues that **religious perfectionism and scrupulosity are, at their core, an anxiety disorder** — clinically a form of religious OCD — rather than excess righteousness. The two dimensions of scrupulosity are the **fear of punishment from God** and the **fear of sinful activity itself** (even an impure thought), and these fears are discharged through repeated, compulsive religious acts (texting the bishop daily, repenting multiple times a day, going to the temple repeatedly to feel relief). Recognizing the condition as anxiety relocates it from the moral register ("I am wicked") to the clinical/spiritual one that grace can address.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **It is diagnosable as anxiety (rated: strong; clinically grounded).** "We have to go back to its fundamental core, and that is scrupulosity and perfectionism really at its core is an anxiety disorder." Mapped to the DSM, it is "an anxiety disorder where [you're] worried and anxious and fearful," only "in religious words or terms" — "religious OCD."[^1]

2. **Two triggering fears (rated: strong; a useful taxonomy).** Scrupulosity "is demonstrated in two different ways": (a) "the fear of punishment from God ... for sinful activity," and (b) "the fear of the actual sinful activity itself ... an impure thought or doing something offensive to God."[^2] The trigger is cognitive — at "the cognitive level, thinking through these things."

3. **Compulsive reassurance behaviors (rated: strong; clinically recognizable).** The fears are "manifested through these sort of repeated religious acts" performed to be "alleviated from that fear": calling or texting the bishop "every day, multiple text messages — I slipped up again"; "praying for excessive hours"; going "to the temple multiple times during the day."[^3] These are reassurance compulsions, structurally like checking or hand-washing in OCD.

4. **It afflicts the most committed (rated: important nuance).** Precisely because devout members "want to do their very best ... keep their covenants and go to the temple and read their scriptures," the anxiety "can be exacerbated" and turns "maladaptive" — the very engines of devotion become the loops of compulsion.[^4] This explains why "the most faithful people feel broken."

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A devout reader could worry that medicalizing scrupulosity "explains away" real sin and the proper godly sorrow that leads to repentance (2 Cor 7:10). Allen guards against this elsewhere by distinguishing healthy, corrective **guilt** from identity-attacking **shame** (see [[Guilt Is Corrective but Shame Attacks Identity]]): the anxiety frame targets the *compulsive, disproportionate, reassurance-seeking* pattern over trivial or imagined "sins," not the ordinary conscience. A second objection notes that not all such distress is brain-based; Allen agrees it is "partly both" — brain chemistry (serotonin/dopamine) *and* core beliefs *and* environment — so the clinical label complements, rather than replaces, the spiritual diagnosis.

## Best Quotes
> "Scrupulosity and perfectionism really at its core is an anxiety disorder ... it's basically an anxiety disorder where [you're] worried and anxious and fearful about this, but now in religious words or terms — religious OCD."[^1]

> "It's the fear of punishment from God ... and then the other one is the fear of the actual sinful activity itself."[^2]

> "They have to call me, have to repent multiple times during the day ... at its core it's an anxiety issue ... it's just manifest religiously."[^3]
