# The Spirit Is a Steady Furnace Not an Intermittent Fire

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]] (relaying a teaching he attributes to Brad Wilcox) addresses a common scrupulous trigger: members who say "I haven't felt the Spirit in years" conclude they have lost it. The fix is a better metaphor. The Spirit is not a **fire** that "burns" intermittently and intensely — that dramatic "burning in the bosom" happens "maybe a handful of times" in a life — but a **furnace** that runs constantly: when it is working the room is simply "comfortable, warm, pleasant, and sustainable." If "comfortable" *is* the Spirit, then the absence of a burning feeling is not the absence of the Spirit.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The false expectation diagnosed (rated: strong; common pastoral problem).** "We expect so much to feel the Spirit all the time and to be perfect and to have joy and happiness all the time, that we forget that part of the Atonement of Jesus Christ is to help us get to that place."[^2] The expectation of constant emotional intensity is itself a perfectionistic trap.

2. **Fire vs. furnace (rated: the central image).** "Instead of a fire burning in us, think of it as a furnace. When the furnace is not working it's uncomfortable; when it's working it's comfortable, warm, pleasant, and sustainable ... it's constant ... always with us. If we're feeling comfortable, that's the Spirit."[^1] The intense burning "happens maybe a handful of times in a whole life."

3. **Especially for those on medication or with depression (rated: important application).** Allen extends the point to members whose brain chemistry or medication blunts strong feeling: "it's partly brain chemistry ... a neurological issue," so a furnace-model of the Spirit frees them from concluding their flat affect means spiritual failure.[^3] This couples the metaphor to his "partly both" account (chemistry + beliefs + environment) in [[Scrupulosity Is an Anxiety Disorder Expressed Religiously]].

4. **The gift you already have (rated: moderate; covenant logic).** The host adds that with the gift of the Holy Ghost "it's literally — you assume that you've got it ... you're swimming in it"; the constant, quiet companionship is the baseline, not the exception.[^4]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A critic notes that scripture *does* use fire and burning for the Spirit (D&C 9:8 "your bosom shall burn within you"; Luke 24:32; Acts 2 tongues of fire), so the furnace re-description, while pastorally helpful, should not flatten the genuine, sometimes vivid manifestations the standard works describe — nor be used to reassure someone that *any* comfortable feeling is the Spirit regardless of conduct. The measured form of the claim is that the *ordinary mode* of the Spirit is a steady, gentle constancy ("a still small voice"; "peace ... not as the world giveth," John 14:27), so the absence of dramatic burning is not evidence of abandonment — the point Allen needs to defuse scrupulous despair, without denying that the fire sometimes falls.

## Best Quotes
> "Instead of a fire burning in us, think of it as a furnace ... when it's working it's comfortable, it's warm, it's pleasant and it's sustainable ... it's constant ... if we're feeling comfortable, that's the Spirit."[^1]

> "We expect so much to feel the Spirit all the time ... that we forget that part of the Atonement of Jesus Christ is to help us get to that place."[^2]
