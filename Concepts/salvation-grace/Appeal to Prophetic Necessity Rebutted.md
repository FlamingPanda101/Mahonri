# Appeal to Prophetic Necessity Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]]'s answer to [[The Judas Counterexample]]: Judas had to exist because his betrayal was *prophesied* and was *necessary for the atonement* — "someone had to fulfill that prophecy." He supports this from [[John 6.70-71|John 6:70-71]] (Jesus knowingly chose a betrayer among the Twelve) and the calling of Matthias "with full foreknowledge that one of the 12 will not be with the church." This shows "a God fully in control of his creation."[^1]

## The Rebuttal
1. **Necessity instrumentalizes Judas** — rate: strong. Hayden gets Gavin to affirm that Christ's betrayal was *necessary for the atonement* and that Judas's damnation was therefore part of God's plan — then notes this is exactly [[The Greater Good Defense Instrumentalizes Persons|the utilitarian/instrumentalizing move]]: Judas is created *for* an outcome (the atonement) at the cost of his own eternal good. *(deontological)* [^2]
2. **It concedes the dilemma's premises** — Gavin's "God is in control / kept the devil close" affirms that God knowingly initiated a person bound for hell, which is precisely what the [[The Creation Dilemma|creation dilemma]] alleges; control is not the same as *willing Judas's good*. Hayden in rebuttal: "I'm not exactly sure what 'God is in control' means if people have free will." *(internal-critique)* [^3]
3. **God could fulfill prophecy without damning anyone / could refrain** — Hayden's parallel: a perfectly omnibenevolent God could have arranged events (e.g., the plates taken by angels in his Laban example) without anyone's eternal ruin; and crucially, God was *not compelled* to create Judas (Gavin concedes nothing external forced him). So "necessary for the plan" does not answer "necessary that *this person* be created and damned." *(modal)* [^4]

**Steelman of the affirmative:** (a) *Compatibilist foreknowledge* — God foreknew Judas's free betrayal without causing it; the prophecy describes, it does not determine, so Judas authored his own fall (see Gavin's "barometer" / real-time-branching picture, [[Divine Foreknowledge as Real-Time Branching]]). (b) *Felix culpa* — the betrayal was woven into the supreme good of redemption; Judas's free wickedness was *used* for good without being *willed* as his ruin. Hayden's reply to both: even granting libertarian freedom and a redemptive overruling, the question remains why *create* the person whose foreknown, freely-chosen end is hell — necessity-of-the-plan answers a different question than necessity-of-the-person.
