# Julian of Norwich's Showings of Divine Love

## The Point

[[Miranda Wilcox]] uses [[Julian of Norwich]] as the chapter's exemplar of medieval mysticism — a Christian to whom "Jesus appeared... personally." [^1] In May AD 1373, while gravely ill, Julian experienced sixteen "showings" in which she witnessed Christ's Passion and conversed with him; over decades she wrote two accounts (*A Revelation in Love*). By 1393 she had become an **anchoress**, enclosed in a cell attached to a Norwich church, advising those who sought her counsel. Her theology is striking for its tenderness, its non-transactional view of the Atonement, and its insistence that love is God's ultimate meaning.

## Evidence

1. **Christ's love manifest in his crucified face.** Julian saw "the red blood trickling down from under the garland hot and afresh and right plenteously" and learned that Christ's suffering and death is "the supreme revelation of the love of God." Christ is "our clothing, that for love wrappeth us... that he may never leave us." [^2]
2. **Sin is necessary, not divine retribution.** Christ assured her: "Sin is behovely (necessary), but all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of things shall be well." [^3]
3. **The Atonement is not an economic transaction.** Julian learned that God "exacts no price from anyone, directly or vicariously, for nothing is owed." Christ told her: "It is a joy, a bliss, an endless liking to me that ever I suffered passion for thee." [^3]
4. **Compassion for others is Christ within.** "I saw that each kind [natural] compassion that man hath on his even [fellow] Christian with charity, it is Christ in him." [^4]
5. **Love is the whole meaning.** "Thus was I learned that love was our Lord's meaning... ere God made us, He loved us, which love was never slaked, no never shall... And in this love our life is everlasting." [^5] Wilcox concludes Julian "fashions herself as a Christian Everywoman who cultivates a personal relationship with Jesus." [^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Sin is behovely (necessary), but all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of things shall be well." — Christ to Julian [^3]

> "Tenderly our Lord God touches us, and blissfully calleth us saying in our soul: Let be all thy love, my dearworthy child. Attend to me. I am enough to thee, and enjoy in thy Savior and in thy salvation." [^4]
