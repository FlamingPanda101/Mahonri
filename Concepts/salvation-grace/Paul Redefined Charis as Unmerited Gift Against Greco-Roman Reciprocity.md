# Paul Redefined Charis as Unmerited Gift Against Greco-Roman Reciprocity

## The Point
Cecilia M. Peek argues that Paul took the ordinary Greek word *charis* ("grace") — a term loaded with **reciprocity** in its pre-Christian usage — and revolutionized it. In Greco-Roman, Jewish, and Homeric culture, a gift (*charis*, Latin *gratia*, Roman *beneficium*) obligated the recipient to a return, and gifts were given **discriminately to worthy recipients**. Paul shattered both expectations: God's grace is "freely given and utterly unmerited" (Greek *dōrean*), conferred precisely on the **undeserving** — "while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us." [^1] This "benefaction ideology" became the template for all later Christian discussion of grace and salvation. [^2]

## Evidence
1. **Lexical history of *charis*.** The word appears in Homer and Hesiod meaning charm, favor, kindness, benevolence, *and* the grateful response those favors inspire — a built-in two-sidedness. [^1] Of 150+ New Testament occurrences, 102 are in the securely Pauline corpus, 26 in Romans alone; the earliest use (1 Thessalonians, AD 50-51) is a mere greeting formula, showing Paul's doctrine of grace developed over time, maturing by Galatians and Romans. [^3]
2. **Reciprocity was universal in Paul's world.** Greek hospitality, the Roman patron/client system (*beneficium* obliging the *client*), and even Jewish almsgiving (expecting God's blessing as recompense) all anticipated a return. John Barclay: "Most gifts and benefits in the ancient world were distributed discriminately to fitting or worthy recipients." [^4]
3. **Paul inverts worthiness.** Romans calls the recipients "without strength," "ungodly," and "enemies" (Romans 5:6, 10) — the categorically *un*deserving. The KJV "freely" in [[Romans 3.23-25|Romans 3:24]] renders *dōrean*, "as a free gift, without payment." [^5]
4. **Grace is a person, not a disposition.** For Paul, "the grace of God is Jesus Christ" — the incarnate Son is the favor given, "the active and physical manifestation of God's kindness." [^6]
5. **Imperial resonance.** Papyri show *charis* designated grants from the Roman emperor, so Paul's Roman audience could hear God's benefaction as a kind of cosmic imperial patronage. [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "God commendeth his love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us." (Romans 5:8) [^5]

> "Most gifts and benefits in the ancient world were distributed discriminately to fitting or worthy recipients." — John Barclay [^4]

> "The grace of God is Jesus Christ. The incarnated Son is the favor given, the active and physical manifestation of God's kindness, expressed in Christ's life, death, and Resurrection." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Peek closes the chapter noting that "Latter-day Saint theology participates in these historical currents." Some Latter-day Saints emphasize works, "wary of cheap grace"; others recognize that overemphasis on works can produce "toxic perfectionism" and miss "the good news" — grace as "the unmerited gift of God." [^7] This connects to the LDS reading of grace in [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]] and the [[2 Nephi 25.23|"after all we can do"]] debate.
