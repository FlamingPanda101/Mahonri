# The New Testament Repeatedly Says Baptism Saves

## The Claim
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] (Catholic Answers) argues the affirmative — "water baptism saves" — on the methodological ground that one should settle a doctrine by reading what Scripture says *about that doctrine*. He marshals roughly eleven New Testament passages that, he contends, say baptism saves, forgives sins, regenerates, joins us to Christ, or gives the Spirit: Mark 16:16; Acts 2:38; 1 Peter 3:21; Acts 22:16; 1 Corinthians 6:11; Romans 6:3-4; Colossians 2:11-12; Galatians 3:27; Hebrews 10:22; Acts 19; and John 3:5. His core charge against the faith-alone side: it argues from a *general principle* ("we're saved by faith") to override the Bible's specific answer, while citing "literally none" of the baptism passages.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Direct "saves" texts (rating: strong on face; the verses say it).** [[1 Peter 3.21|1 Peter 3:21]]: "Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you"; [[Mark 16.16|Mark 16:16]]: "He who believes and is baptized will be saved"; [[Titus 3.5|Titus 3:5]]: saved "by the washing of regeneration."[^1]
2. **Forgiveness/regeneration/union texts (rating: strong; cumulative).** [[Acts 2.38|Acts 2:38]] (baptized "for the forgiveness of your sins"); Acts 22:16 (baptism to "wash away your sins"); Romans 6, Colossians 2, Galatians 3 (baptism uniting us with Christ's death and resurrection — a "past completed action," "as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ").[^2]
3. **The cumulative-case method (rating: moderate; methodological).** Heschmeyer's strongest argumentative move is structural: "If you actually read what scripture says about baptism, you won't believe what Ryan believes," because Ryan's reading requires neutralizing *every* baptism passage one by one.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The cumulative force is real: the New Testament does associate baptism with salvation, forgiveness, and regeneration in a striking number of places, and the faith-alone interpreter must indeed reinterpret each. The vault's Protestant-side note [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]] grants this and offers the most serious reply — these texts use **metonymy** (baptism *names* the larger reality of faith/union it visibly represents, as "the blood of Jesus saves" names Christ's whole saving death). On that reading "baptism saves" is true without baptism being the *efficient cause* of regeneration. Heschmeyer's rejoinder (developed in [[Baptism Is Gods Work Not a Human Work That Earns Salvation]]) is that this metonymy is ad hoc unless independently motivated, since [[Titus 3.5|Titus 3:5]] already denies baptism is a "work." The genuinely strong part of his case is the **method** (read the topical texts, don't deduce from a slogan); the contested part is whether "saves" entails causation or admits the metonymy/sign reading. This is a Catholic vs. Reformed-Baptist debate; the LDS position enters only by analogy.

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint position sits close to Heschmeyer on baptismal *efficacy*: baptism by proper priesthood authority is *required* for the remission of sins (Acts 2:38; 3 Nephi 11; Moroni 8), not a label for a prior faith-only regeneration. Indeed the LDS practice of **proxy baptism for the dead** (1 Corinthians 15:29; D&C 128) presupposes the rite does real saving work that faith alone could not supply — see [[1 Corinthians 15.29 Points to an Actual Rite of Baptism for the Dead]]. Heschmeyer's cumulative case thus reinforces the LDS premise that the ordinance is causally necessary, even though Catholics and Latter-day Saints diverge sharply on *authority* and on the postmortem application of baptism.
