# Almsgiving Became the Economy of Salvation for the Dead

## The Point
[[Catherine Gines Taylor]], drawing on [[Peter Brown]], describes how **offerings and almsgiving** became a quasi-economic mechanism for aiding the dead. Almsgiving could elicit the church's prayers on behalf of the deceased, and wealthy believers were taught they could "ascend into the starry heavens, almost by apotheosis." One's afterlife was "intimately mingled with the material ability to be remembered" — joining heaven and earth by means of money, for better and (Taylor warns) for worse.

## Evidence
1. **Alms elicit prayers.** "Offerings and almsgiving certainly caught the attention of clergy and could elicit prayers on behalf of the dead," connecting Jesus's command to the rich young man (sell all, give to the poor) to funerary practice [^1].
2. **Wealth as ascent.** Peter Brown shows "wealthy believers were encouraged to give and could thereby hope to ascend into the starry heavens, almost by apotheosis" [^1].
3. **Even the vulnerable gave.** "It is not by chance that even the most vulnerable were asked to donate on behalf of their dead; [[Tertullian]] particularly mentions the need for widows to make offerings on behalf of their husbands" [^2].
4. **Funerary feasts as almsgiving.** [[Augustine]], like Paul, urged moderation at funerary banquets and "saw this kind of feasting as an opportunity for almsgiving to the poor as a vicarious act in aid to the dead" — "a solace for the dead" [^3].
5. **The gatekeeping problem.** Only baptized Christians benefited from the institutional church's prayers, leaving families anxious about loved ones — "no wonder early Christians were keeping vigil in cemeteries at night" [^4].
6. **Taylor's caution.** "For better, but almost certainly for worse, arguments about material wealth and salvation would remain intertwined within the interior discourses of the Christian church for centuries" [^2].

## Best Quotes
> "One's experience in the afterlife was intimately mingled with the material ability to be remembered by token, word, and material memorial." [^2]

> "Augustine … saw this kind of feasting as an opportunity for almsgiving to the poor as a vicarious act in aid to the dead … 'a solace for the dead.'" [^3]

## LDS Connection
The principle that *vicarious good works by the living aid the dead* parallels LDS proxy ordinance work, though the medieval drift toward "buying" salvation (which Taylor flags as a corruption) is precisely what the Restoration replaces with covenant ordinances freely performed. The widow's offering for her husband is a poignant analog to a descendant doing temple work for an ancestor.
