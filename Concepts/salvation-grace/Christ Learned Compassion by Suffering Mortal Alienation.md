# Christ Learned Compassion by Suffering Mortal Alienation

## The Claim
The keystone of [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Ostler's Compassion Theory]] — and the reason it answers desideratum 3 (why *Christ's* suffering atones in a way the Father's and the Holy Ghost's do not) — is that **Christ "learned compassion by the things that he suffered"** as a mortal, acquiring a *first-person* experiential knowledge of human alienation that no being united in the Godhead could otherwise possess.[^1] [[Blake Ostler]] argues that "fully divine beings, as such, cannot experience first-hand the alienation that is the essence of our human condition because they abide in a relationship of complete union with the divine persons. Only by becoming mortal and experiencing alienation first-hand can such experiential knowledge be possible."[^1]

The defining moment is therefore not only Gethsemane but the **cross**: "Christ suffered the essence of spiritual death while on the cross when he experienced complete abandonment by the Father following his complete union with the Father. Only he, in all of history, knew the fullness of the loss of that union and the depth of pain of complete abandonment."[^1] Having known the *fulness* of union with the Father in Gethsemane (see [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity]]) makes the abandonment uniquely devastating, and uniquely qualifying. This pairs with Ostler's [[Kenotic Christology - Divine Properties in Potentiality|kenotic christology]]: the divine Son empties himself of glory to "descend below all things."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A three-fold taxonomy of how the divine shares our experience.** Ostler distinguishes (and this is the genuinely new analytic contribution): **Empathetic sharing** — imagining how another feels based on one's own experience; **Omniscient sharing** — knowing from a third-person perspective what another experiences; **Compassionate sharing** — participating from a *first-person* perspective by *having the same experience*.[^2] The Godhead always had the first two; only the incarnate Christ could have the third with respect to *alienation*. *(Strong: a clean conceptual move that locates exactly what the incarnation adds beyond omniscience — and answers his own objection to [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories|Potter's Empathy theory]], which conflated these.)*
2. **What Christ experienced in Gethsemane and on the cross.** On the Compassion Theory Christ experienced three layers at once: (1) *empathetic* suffering for all who have or will suffer; (2) *omniscient* suffering knowing all sins committed to that point; and (3) *compassionate* sharing for all who had repented to that time.[^3] The scriptural anchor for the bodily extremity is "blood cometh from every pore" ([[Mosiah 3.8|Mosiah 3:7]]). *(Fair: the taxonomy is mapped directly onto the Gethsemane data.)*
3. **Resurrection power as part of the unique qualification.** Christ is also uniquely able because "he has power in himself to lay down his life and take it up again" — the resurrection overcomes alienation by returning us to God's presence to be judged by the [[The Law of Restoration as the Single Law of Justice|Law of Restoration]].[^4] *(Fair: ties the christological uniqueness to the resurrection, not suffering alone.)*
4. **Steelman of the objection.** A classical theist objects that an impassible, timeless God cannot "learn" anything and that ascribing acquired knowledge or abandonment-pain to the divine Son threatens his divinity. Ostler's framework presupposes a *passible, relational, kenotic* God for whom genuine experiential growth is possible — the same relational-theism premise underwriting the whole Compassion Theory. The claim that the Father's "abandonment" is real (not merely apparent) is precisely what an orthodox Trinitarian will resist; Ostler turns this into an *asset*, since only a real loss of a real union can ground Christ's unique qualification. *(Honest: the strength of the soteriology is bought with a contested doctrine of God.)*

## Best Quotes
> "Christ learned compassion by the things that he suffered. According to Mormon scripture, Christ learned how to succor us and share our lives fully by the things that he suffered as a mortal."[^1]

> "Christ suffered the essence of spiritual death while on the cross when he experienced complete abandonment by the Father following his complete union with the Father. Only he, in all of history, knew the fullness of the loss of that union."[^1]

> "Compassionate Sharing of Experience: Participating in another's experience from a first-person perspective by having the same experience and sharing the very experience that is experienced by another."[^2]
