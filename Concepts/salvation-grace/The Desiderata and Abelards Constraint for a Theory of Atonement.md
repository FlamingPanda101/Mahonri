# The Desiderata and Abelard's Constraint for a Theory of Atonement

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] argues that no atonement *theory* should be accepted unless it answers a specific list of questions — the **desiderata** — and meets **Abelard's Constraint**. He first distinguishes three things usually conflated: the *doctrine* of atonement (a faith-claim that through Christ's incarnation, death, and resurrection we are saved and reconciled), a *symbolic/metaphorical expression* (which only tells us what atonement is *like*), and a *theory* (an explanation of *how and why* Christ's life, death, and resurrection make a difference for us "here and now").[^1] The earliest scripture offers at least five metaphors — courtroom justification, ransom/redemption from slavery, reconciliation of friends, sacrificial expiation, and military victory — but "such word-pictures give rise to numerous notions that often don't work well together" and leave the central puzzle unanswered.[^2]

The puzzle: "How could the fact that a man suffered and died 2,000 years ago still have some relation to my repentance here and now? How is it possible that the sins I commit now could cause him pain 2,000 years ago?"[^3] Ostler's thesis is that **no theory in the history of Christianity has met this burden** — none explains how Christ bears the pain of sins not yet freely committed (and which, being free, might never occur), and none meets Abelard's Constraint non-arbitrarily.[^4] This is the diagnostic grid against which he tests every rival (see [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories]]) and his own [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement|Compassion Theory]].

The five desiderata questions a theory must answer:[^5]
1. How is Christ's life, death, and resurrection necessary or uniquely beneficial to eradicate the effects of sin so we are reconciled to God now?
2. Why can't we just be forgiven without someone suffering?
3. Why does *Christ's* suffering atone in a way the Father's and the Holy Ghost's do not?
4. How could Christ "bear our sins" committed in the here and now in a way that caused him to suffer?
5. How do the ordinances of sacrament and baptism signify what occurs in atonement?

**Abelard's Constraint:** a model of atonement must be "neither unintelligible, arbitrary, illogical, nor immoral."[^5]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The scriptural data the theory must explain (steelman of the realist intuition).** Ostler insists the primary data are four best-attested Latter-day Saint scriptural claims, which he labels A-D: (A) Christ takes the effects of our sins *into his being*; (B) as a result he suffers physically and spiritually — "blood cometh from every pore" ([[Mosiah 3.8|Mosiah 3:7]]); (C) because he bears our sins we are released and reconciled; (D) his mercy "satisfies the demands of justice."[^6] Any adequate theory must save *all four*, not just the morally comfortable ones. *(Strong: this is a fair, scripturally dense constraint that rules out purely subjective/exemplarist accounts.)*
2. **Faith requires intelligible content.** Borrowing the "bliks can jump over the moon" example, Ostler argues one "must have some grasp of what is asserted to have faith that it is true"; if "Christ atoned for our sins" had no graspable content, it could not be believed at all.[^1] *(Strong as stated; an apophatic theologian — compare the [[Four Ancient Atonement Models|patristic comfort with mystery]] and McConkie's "no mortal mind can conceive" — would push back that saving faith can rest on a doctrine only partly theorized.)*
3. **The "solution to a problem where there is no problem" worry.** Ostler concedes the force of the objection that we may not *need* an atonement to be forgiven — "Do we even need an atonement to be forgiven or to forgive?" — and treats answering it as part of the desiderata, not a question to be dodged.[^7] *(Fair: he grants the rival its strongest form, which is why moral-influence theories that make Christ merely inspiring fail desideratum 1.)*

## Best Quotes
> "One can believe something without understanding it. I believe that quantum physics is more or less accurate, but I don't fully understand quantum theory well. Nevertheless, one must have some grasp of what is asserted to have faith that it is true."[^1]

> "I suggest that no theory in the history of Christianity to date has actually met the burden of explaining how Christ's suffering somehow eradicates our sin in the here and now... None has adequately explained how Christ could possibly bear the pain of sins that haven't even occurred yet."[^4]

> A theory must be "neither unintelligible, arbitrary, illogical, nor immoral."[^5]
