# Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin

## The Point
The early-fifth-century stele of **Theodosius** at Brignoles (southern France) preserves grieving parents' plea that their little boy, who died *before* baptism, "nevertheless be received by God." [[Catherine Gines Taylor]] uses it to expose a live tension: [[Pelagius]] held that unbaptized children were innocent by reason of their youth and "must not go to hell," while [[Augustine]] argued that even infants "carry within them the remnants of original sin and require baptism." The dispute drove much of the urgency behind vicarious and infant baptism.

## Evidence
1. **The Brignoles stele.** Parents of "a little boy named Theodosius ask that their child, who had died before baptism, nevertheless be received by God," marking "the tensions that were prevalent in the church at this time" [^1].
2. **Pelagius's position.** He "defended the idea that children who were unbaptized were innocent of sin because of their fair and young age at death and must not go to hell" [^1].
3. **Augustine's position.** He "argued that even children carry within them the remnants of original sin and require baptism." In *The Grace of Christ, and On Original Sin*, he insists unbaptized infants "are liable to the condemnation of the first man" and require "regeneration (baptism)" [^2].
4. **Baptism as fundamental initiation.** Because baptism "was so fundamental … the question of baptism for infants who had died came into question" — the same logic that made vicarious baptism attractive [^1].

## Best Quotes
> "Pelagius … had defended the idea that children who were unbaptized were innocent of sin because of their fair and young age at death and must not go to hell. This perspective is in contrast to Augustine, who argued that even children carry within them the remnants of original sin and require baptism." [^1]

> "This poignant funerary inscription on the stele at Brignoles marks the tenderness and concern that parents applied in supplication for salvific intercession on behalf of their innocent child." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Restoration scripture decisively resolves this dispute on the *Pelagian* side and beyond: little children are whole, "not capable of committing sin," and need no baptism (Moroni 8; D&C 137:10, "all children who die before they arrive at the years of accountability are saved"). The very anxiety the Brignoles parents felt — and which Augustine's original-sin doctrine intensified — is dissolved by the LDS rejection of inherited guilt (see [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]]).
