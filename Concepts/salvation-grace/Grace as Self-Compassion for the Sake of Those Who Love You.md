# Grace as Self-Compassion for the Sake of Those Who Love You

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] narrates a new understanding of **grace** that came through grief: after his son Donnie's death he fell into "genuine self-hatred," unable to extend himself compassion. The breakthrough came not from feeling he deserved love but from realizing he is *also someone's son* — "may Patricia's little boy be free from suffering" — and that his living son and his late mother *need* him to care for himself. Grace, he concludes, can be received "for their sakes" even when one cannot yet feel it for oneself.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The breakthrough phrase (strong, first-person).** Lying by Donnie's grave, unable to direct loving-kindness to himself, Bradley framed himself as his mother Patricia's child — "that just opened the floodgates of my being able to have compassion for myself."[^1]
2. **Grace as obligation-of-love, not desert (interpretive).** "Even if I didn't feel love for myself… I needed to give myself compassion for their sakes." The warrant for self-grace is *others'* love for him (his sons', his mother's), not his own merit — "I never thought of grace like that before."[^2]
3. **Tied to function, not just feeling (moderate).** His sons "need me to take care of myself so I can do the things I want in this world for Donnie, and… for Nicholas" — self-compassion is enlisted toward continued service to the living and the dead.[^2]
4. **Healing actually occurred (strong, reported).** "I have healed from all that… toxic, terrible hate of myself because of their love for me" — the principle is presented as efficacious in his lived recovery, not merely aspirational.[^2]

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
This is testimony, not a doctrine of grace, and a theologian could press it: grounding self-compassion in *other people's* love (mother, sons) risks making grace contingent and horizontal — what of the person with no loving family to invoke? And classic Protestant grace is precisely *unconditional* divine favor irrespective of any human regard, so Bradley's "for their sakes" framing could be read as substituting human love for the divine gift, or as another subtle work the sufferer must perform.

## Counter-Objection
Bradley's account is offered as one *experiential doorway* into grace, not its definition, and it coheres with the unconditional model rather than competing with it: the love that unlocked his self-compassion is itself depicted as a reflection of God's love ("a reflection to us… of the love that God has for us"), so the horizontal love mediates rather than replaces the divine. For the person without family, the same logic redirects to being a child of God directly. The "another work" worry misreads it — he describes self-compassion as something he finally could *receive*, not achieve, once the lens shifted. (Cf. [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]].)
