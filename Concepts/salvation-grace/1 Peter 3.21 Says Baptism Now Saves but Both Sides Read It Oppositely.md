# 1 Peter 3.21 Says Baptism Now Saves but Both Sides Read It Oppositely

## The Claim
[[1 Peter 3.21|1 Peter 3:21]] — "Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you... not as a removal of dirt from the body but as an appeal to God for a good conscience" — is, for [[Joe Heschmeyer]], about as explicit as it gets: Peter "literally just said... baptism now saves you," and is clearly talking about *water* baptism (else why deny it works by "removing dirt from the body"?). [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] reads the *same* verse oppositely by leaning on the Noah analogy in vv. 18-20: in the flood, "the water was the judgment" and "the ark is the salvation," so baptism saves only as it points to Christ (our ark), "not because you got a bit of water that's gone over your body."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The verse explicitly says "baptism now saves you" (rating: strong; textual).** Heschmeyer: Peter "just said two things — that baptism now saves you... and that baptism corresponds to how Noah and his family were saved through water. So he is explicitly talking about water baptism." The denial that it works by "removing dirt" presupposes a *physical* washing whose effect is *spiritual*.[^1]
2. **The Noah typology cuts toward water-as-judgment (rating: moderate; Ryan's analogy).** Ryan: "In this analogy, was the water saving you or condemning you? If you were in the water, you drowned... The water was the judgment." Baptism saves "as an appeal to God for a good conscience through the resurrection," i.e. via Christ, not the water itself.[^2]
3. **"Appeal to God for a good conscience" (rating: moderate; both cite it).** Heschmeyer reads the phrase as showing baptism saves "not because of its physical effects, but because of its spiritual effects"; Ryan reads it as locating the saving reality in faith's appeal to the risen Christ rather than the rite.[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
This verse showcases how a single text bends to prior commitments. Heschmeyer is right that the *grammar* is blunt ("baptism now saves you," *baptisma... nyn sōzei*) and that Peter is unmistakably discussing *water* baptism (the "not removal of dirt" clause is unintelligible otherwise) — so Ryan cannot make this a non-water passage. Ryan's Noah-typology point is clever but partly self-defeating: 1 Peter says Noah's family was "saved *through* water" and that baptism "corresponds to this," so the water is the saving medium in the type, not merely the judgment. His real gain is the verse's own qualifier — baptism saves "*as an appeal to God for a good conscience*... through the resurrection of Jesus Christ" — which locates the efficacy in the God-ward appeal and Christ's resurrection, not bare ritual. That qualifier is exactly what the vault's [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]] (Gavin Ortlund) builds a non-causal "metonymy" reading on. Net: Heschmeyer wins that the verse asserts baptism saves and is about water; the open question is *how* it saves (instrument vs. faith-appeal it expresses). Catholic-vs-Reformed; LDS by analogy.

## LDS Relevance
For Latter-day Saints, 1 Peter 3:21 straightforwardly affirms that baptism "doth also now save us," consistent with the necessity of the ordinance. The verse's pairing with Christ's preaching "to the spirits in prison" (vv. 18-20) is doubly significant in LDS theology: it grounds both baptismal salvation *and* the postmortem ministry behind work for the dead — see [[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3:18-20]] and [[D&C 138 Restores the Harrowing of Hell Recorded in the Gospel of Nicodemus]]. The Restoration sides with Heschmeyer that baptism really saves, while framing the "good conscience" appeal as the covenant a worthy, faith-filled candidate makes — efficacy and faith together, not at odds.
