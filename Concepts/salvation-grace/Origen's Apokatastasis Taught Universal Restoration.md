# Origen's Apokatastasis Taught Universal Restoration

## The Point

One of [[Origen]]'s most remarkable ideas — **apokatastasis** (Greek for "restoration") — was, in [[Nicholas J. Frederick]]'s words, "the ultimate rejection of a literal, physical eschatology." [^1] For Origen, apokatastasis was a *final restoration of all things*, when "the course of the entirety of human history would return to enjoy union with God, where 'the end is always like the beginning.'" [^1] It amounted to a form of universal salvation in which everyone ultimately receives redemption.

## Evidence

1. **All beings return to unity with God.** "All beings, who, from that single beginning, have been moved, each by their own motion, through various stages allotted them for their deserts, will ultimately be restored to unity with God through subjection to Christ." [^1]
2. **Origen's thought ranged far beyond a future earthly kingdom.** He also "opined about the preexistence of souls and the possibility that Satan and his demons may, eventually, find some measure of salvation." [^1]
3. **It was an esoteric teaching.** Frederick (citing Bart Van Egmond) notes Origen was "very reluctant to teach the doctrine of apokatastasis to everyone, because it might provoke moral laxity" — withholding it from those for whom "the threat of eternal punishment is still useful," seeing the Church "as a pedagogical institute that accommodates to each individual soul." [^2]
4. **Disputed authorship of the idea.** Frederick flags Ilaria Ramelli's argument that Origen "was not necessarily the original developer of apokatastasis." [^3]

## Best Quotes

> "One of Origen's most remarkable ideas, apokatastasis (Greek for 'restoration'), proved to be the ultimate rejection of a literal, physical eschatology." [^1]

> "Origen... opined about the preexistence of souls and the possibility that Satan and his demons may, eventually, find some measure of salvation." [^1]

## LDS Connection

Frederick links Origen's broader thought (preexistence of souls) to chapter 7 of the same volume; the notion of a graded, near-universal redemption and the rehabilitation of nearly all souls offers a striking patristic parallel to Latter-day Saint conceptions of premortal existence and the degrees of glory, though Frederick himself draws the parallel only by cross-reference rather than explicit claim. [^4]
