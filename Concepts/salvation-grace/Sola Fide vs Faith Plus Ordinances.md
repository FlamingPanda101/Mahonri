# Sola Fide vs Faith Plus Ordinances

## The Point
[[Allie Beth Stuckey]] argues from Ephesians 2 that salvation is "by grace... through faith... not a result of works, so that no one can boast." Because we were "dead in sin," she presses the Reformed point: "Dead people can't reach... A dead person has to be revived," so "we contribute nothing to our salvation."[^1] [[Jacob Hansen]] accepts grace as the saving power but rejects monergism with the *car-on-the-cliff* analogy: Christ arrives with a helicopter and extends His hand, "but you have to choose Jesus Christ — you have to take his hand," grounding the human response in Peter's "repent and be baptized" at Pentecost (Acts 2:38).[^2] He reads 2 Nephi 25:23 ("by grace we are saved after all we can do") as "*in spite of* all we can do," quoting an apostle that "after does not equal because of."[^3]

## Evidence
- Stuckey's exegesis is the classic *sola fide* reading of [[Ephesians 2-8-9 - Saved by Grace Not Works Rebutted|Ephesians 2:8-9]] plus total depravity (Ephesians 2:1-3): the dead cannot cooperate.
- Hansen's reply is functionally Arminian on the human side — a freely accepted gift — which Stuckey notes "a lot of Protestants would believe," signaling the *real* divide is not Calvinism-vs-Arminianism but whether priesthood ordinances are *added* (see [[Priesthood Authority Is the Necessary But Not Sufficient Dividing Line]]).
- The "after all we can do" rehabilitation in [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]] is Hansen's move to keep grace primary while preserving covenantal works.

## Best Quotes
> "By grace through faith you have been saved, and this is not your own doing; it is a gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one can boast... Dead people can't reach." [^1]

> "It's after everything you do, Ally, it's still grace that saves you... after does not equal because of." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Both sides claim grace; the wedge is *sufficiency*. Stuckey: Christ's sacrifice received by faith is the whole of salvation. Hansen: grace saves, but the covenant is entered through ordinances administered by authority — which Stuckey reads as faith *plus* the church (see [[Priesthood Authority Is the Necessary But Not Sufficient Dividing Line]]).
