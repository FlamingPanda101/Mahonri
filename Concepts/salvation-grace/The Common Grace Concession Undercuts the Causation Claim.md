# The Common Grace Concession Undercuts the Causation Claim

## The Claim (Objection)

[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] presses a dilemma created by [[Glenn Scrivener]]'s appeal to "common grace." When confronted with pre-Christian instances of a value ([[Hippocrates|Hippocratic]] egalitarianism, [[Hammurabi|Hammurabi's]] protection of the weak), Scrivener says "there's such a thing as common grace." O'Connor argues this *theological* answer concedes the *historical* point.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The dilemma.** O'Connor: "Either there's a common intuition people can have anyway — in which case Christianity is *not the historical force* responsible for those values — or Christianity is responsible, in which case they couldn't have predated Christianity, which they did."[^2] *(Tight — the disjunction is genuinely exclusive: a value that arises by "common grace" in Babylon and Greece is, by definition, not *caused* by Christianity historically.)*

2. **Common grace explains the data only theologically.** Saying Hippocrates' insight came from God ("common grace") may be true within a Christian worldview, but it does not support the *causal-historical* thesis that *Christianity* (the religion, the church, the Bible) produced Western values — it attributes the value to God *independent of* Christianity's historical influence.[^1] *(Strong — it conflates "God is the ultimate source of all good" [theology] with "the Christian tradition is the historical cause" [the thesis under debate]. The two come apart.)*

3. **Scrivener's escape route — the institutionalization move.** Scrivener pivots: common grace gave the *value* (compassion), but "common grace did not institutionalize things like hospitals." So Christianity's unique *historical* contribution is scaling/embedding, not originating, the value. See [[Institutionalization Is the Unique Christian Contribution]].[^3] *(This is the consistent and strongest reply — it accepts the value's pre-Christian existence and relocates the unique Christian claim to institutional form. O'Connor accepts this but notes it shrinks the thesis.)*

4. **Steelman of Scrivener.** The "lens" argument ([[Noticing Compassion Elsewhere Is Itself Christian]]) supplements common grace: even our *recognition* of these values as good is Christianized, so the data O'Connor cites is filtered through a Christian frame. *(But O'Connor's reply stands: the lens shapes *recognition*, not the *historical fact* that the value existed pre-Christianity, which is what the causation claim needs to deny.)*

## Best Quotes

> "There's such a thing as common grace." — Scrivener[^1]

> "Either it's a common intuition people can have anyway — in which case Christianity isn't the historical force responsible — or Christianity is responsible, in which case they couldn't have predated Christianity, which they did."[^2]
