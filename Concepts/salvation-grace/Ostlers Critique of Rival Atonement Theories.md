# Ostler's Critique of Rival Atonement Theories

## The Claim
[[Blake Ostler]] surveys the leading Latter-day Saint atonement theories and argues that **each fails the [[The Desiderata and Abelards Constraint for a Theory of Atonement|desiderata]]** — specifically, none explains how Christ *actually bears our sins* such that his suffering is connected to our repentance "here and now." Most Latter-day Saints, he notes, accept some form of **penal-substitution**, which he rejects along with its underlying **retributive** theory of justice (the notion that "someone must suffer and pay a price in order for someone to be forgiven").[^1] He then steelmans and refutes four newer, "historically unique" LDS proposals.[^1] This note catalogs his critique; the constructive alternative is [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement]].

## Supporting Evidence
*(Each rival is stated in its strongest form, then shown where it fails.)*

1. **Cleon Skousen — The Demand of Eternal Intelligences for Justice.** *Steelman:* all reality is intelligences whose trust constitutes God's power; their unjust demand that "someone must suffer" is appeased when, revolted at the crucifixion of an innocent they love, they undergo a change of heart toward mercy. Ostler grants "there is a lot to like" — there is no eternal law forcing God to demand suffering, and the theory exposes our own unjust refusal to forgive.[^2] *Failure:* it never connects to the scriptural sense in which Christ bears *my* sins; my present sin "seems totally disconnected." It makes God *complicit* in unjust demands, raises the question of why the Father doesn't suffer instead (making him "unjust and a coward"), and rests on a precarious God whose status collapses if intelligences merely withhold honor.[^3] The scriptural warrant in [[Alma 34.32-33|Alma 34]] and [[Alma 42.13|Alma 42]] "clearly has nothing to do with the demands of intelligences."[^3]

2. **Eugene England — The Self-Rejection Moral Theory.** *Steelman:* the "demands of justice" in Alma 42 are *our own* demand that we pay for our moral failures; estranged from ourselves, we cannot self-accept. Christ's unconditional *agape* — "while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us" — pierces this self-rejection by showing we are worthy of acceptance.[^4] *Failure:* Ostler classes it as a **moral-influence** theory and presses three points: (a) Christ is *not unique* as an inspiring example of loving, unjust suffering (why not Peter crucified upside-down, or Gandhi?); England's "ultimate source of justice" is left vague; (b) there is no sense in which Christ *bears our sins* — what is needed isn't suffering but loving acceptance, which "surely can be given... without any suffering"; (c) "I could have all of the benefits of atonement... without anything done by Christ at all" — I need only drop my unjustified self-rejection. England himself disclaims offering a theory.[^5]

3. **Dennis Potter — The Empathy Theory.** *Steelman:* justice is met equally by punishment or by warranted forgiveness; Gethsemane is the miracle by which Christ "experiences exactly what each of us experiences in our sinning," qualifying him to judge mercifully ("to understand all is to forgive all").[^6] *Failure:* it requires Christ to know our free acts and reasons *before we do them* — but Potter (like Ostler) holds foreknowledge incompatible with free will, so this is incoherent; and if God already knows our reasons by omniscience at the time, the Gethsemane miracle is unnecessary. It fails to explain the *extreme pain* of scripture, makes forgiveness *follow* repentance (so we've "earned the right to be forgiven"), and doesn't explain how we are *sanctified through* the atonement.[^7]

4. **Jacob Morgan — The Divine Infusion Theory.** *Steelman:* without atonement we'd be "super-fallen," lacking the conscience to repent; the atonement infuses the **light of Christ** into all things, actuating conscience and thus grounding human agency itself — "pulling the universe far enough out of the darkness to make repentance and growth possible," and (a bonus) making the resurrection "more at home."[^8] *Failure:* "Morgan doesn't present a theory of atonement. Rather, he presents a theory of *prevenient grace*." He never mentions Gethsemane, the cross, or *why* Jesus suffers — indeed Christ's light "was in and through all things before he suffered," so the suffering is left unexplained.[^9] *(Ostler adds a "small correction": there is only **one** law of justice, the Law of Restoration, not Morgan's two; see [[The Law of Restoration as the Single Law of Justice]].)*

5. **The shared diagnosis.** Three of the four (England, Potter, Morgan) collapse into theories where Christ's *suffering* does no real work — "that of course is a general criticism that applies to all moral influence theories."[^10] The penal-substitution majority view fails the opposite way: it makes the suffering essential but renders it unjust (punishing the innocent), failing Abelard's Constraint. The steelman case *for* substitution as self-giving love rather than imposed punishment is developed at [[Substitutionary Atonement Is Not a License to Sin]]; the classic ransom/military-victory family is at [[Christus Victor and the Ransom by Deception]].

## Best Quotes
> "There is a lot to like in this theory... However, the theory doesn't account for the scriptural data... My sins don't seem to be involved in anything that Christ does."[^2]

> "It seems that I could have all of the benefits of atonement given England's theory without anything done by Christ at all. All I have to do is give up my own unjustified demand for self-rejection."[^5]

> "Not once does he mention either Gethsemane, the cross or even reflect on why Jesus suffers... Any theory of atonement that fails to even address the question just isn't a theory of atonement."[^9]
