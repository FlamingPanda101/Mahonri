#type/claim

# Synergism Is Ancient and Sola Fide Is the Real Innovation

## The Claim

When [[Sean McDowell]] warns Latter-day Saints to "beware of changes to the gospel" — citing [[Ephesians 2.8-9|Ephesians 2:8-9]] against [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25:23]]'s "by grace… after all that we can do" — the [[All Those In Favor - Three Tactics to Evangelize Mormons|All Those In Favor]] hosts turn the warning around. The LDS view of salvation, they argue, is **synergism**: saved by grace, while Christ still "demands our allegiance and our submission."[^1] And synergism is precisely what Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, and the Oriental/Coptic churches have always held. The genuinely *new* doctrine — the actual "change to the gospel" — is McDowell's own Protestant **sola fide and forensic justification**, which "did not exist until the Protestant Reformation," roughly 1,500 years after Christ.[^2] So the man invoking Galatians against innovation is himself the innovator.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. Synergism is the broad historic consensus, not a Mormon peculiarity
The hosts list the traditions that share the structure — "that's what Catholicism believes, that's what Eastern Orthodoxy believes, that's what the Oriental and Coptic branches of orthodoxy believe" — placing the LDS position inside the mainstream of pre-Reformation Christendom rather than outside it.[^3] The shape is: grace is primary and saving, *and* the disciple's faith, repentance, and submission are the appointed expression of that grace, not a competing source of merit. Compare the Catholic articulation in [[Faith Alone but Never Alone]] and the early-church stakes in [[The Pelagian Controversy on Grace and Human Ability]].

### 2. Forensic/solifidian justification is datable and late
"The Protestant view of *sola fide* and forensic justification didn't exist until 1,400, 1,500 years after the death of Christ."[^2] The reductio: for McDowell's reading to be the original apostolic gospel, "he has to say that very shortly after the apostles died, Christianity was in a great apostasy for 500 years where they had the wrong view of salvation, and then [[Martin Luther]] restored it"[^4] — an ironic mirror of the very [[Great Apostasy Is Not a Scriptural Term in LDS Standard Works|apostasy-and-restoration]] logic Protestants reject when Latter-day Saints use it. The early church fathers did not teach forensic justification (the hosts note McDowell would be "teaching the gospel that Paul must have taught, except all of the early church fathers did not have that").[^5]

### 3. Even a non-Calvinist Protestant concedes a human role
The deeper point lands without church-history expertise: "if I ask you, can you choose to accept Christ? If you think the answer is yes — congratulations, you play a role, you contributed to your own salvation."[^6] Unless one is a strict predestinarian, accepting Christ is itself a choice, so some synergism is unavoidable; repentance, baptism, and submission "follow faith… that is the expression of faith."[^7] See [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]] for why "after all we can do" is not a wage-earning clause.

### Steelman (McDowell's side)
A careful Protestant can reply that the dispute is not bare chronology: the Reformers claimed to *recover* Paul's plain meaning (Romans, Galatians) that the medieval church had obscured, so "it appeared late" is not the same as "it is novel error" — and Catholic/Orthodox synergism is itself a development, not a frozen apostolic deposit. He can add that *forensic* justification names a specific mechanism (imputed righteousness) the fathers arguably gesture toward even without the vocabulary. The hosts' rebuttal does not need to win that historical debate outright; it needs only to neutralize the rhetorical sting of "beware changes to the gospel" by showing McDowell's own position is at least as vulnerable to the charge — and arguably more so, since synergism, not *sola fide*, is the position the pre-Reformation church actually articulated.

## Best Quotes

> "This view that we have of salvation — synergism — is not the ancient view of the apostles? It did not exist until the Protestant Reformation? The view that you just described is the one that's new."[^2]
