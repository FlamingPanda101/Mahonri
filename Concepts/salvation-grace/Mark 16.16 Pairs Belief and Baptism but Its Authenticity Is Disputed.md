# Mark 16.16 Pairs Belief and Baptism but Its Authenticity Is Disputed

## The Claim
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] leads his case with [[Mark 16.16|Mark 16:16]]: "He who believes and is baptized will be saved; but he who believes not will be condemned" — Jesus plainly conditions salvation on belief *and* baptism. [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] answers on two fronts: (1) **textual** — the Longer Ending of Mark (16:9-20) is, per most textual scholars including conservatives, probably not original, so the verse carries no authority; and (2) **grammatical** — even if retained, only unbelief is given as the condition of *condemnation* ("he that believeth not"), so baptism marks believers without being a condition of salvation.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Textual doubt about 16:9-20 (rating: strong; mainstream text criticism).** Ryan cites [[Daniel Wallace]] ("the text of Mark 16:9-20 is most likely not... the original inspired text") and [[Bruce Metzger]] (the last twelve verses "are absent from the oldest Greek, Latin, Syriac, Coptic, and Armenian manuscripts"). The earliest witnesses end at 16:8.[^1]
2. **The asymmetry argument (rating: moderate; grammatical).** Ryan: the verse says whoever believes-and-is-baptized is saved, "but he doesn't say if you're not baptized, you'll be condemned" — only "whoever does not believe will be condemned." So baptism is "indicative of those who do have faith," like "grab your hat and go to the train station," where the hat wasn't necessary to arrive.[^2]
3. **Heschmeyer's rebuttals (rating: moderate; theological + rhetorical).** (a) On the text: inspiration does not depend on the human author, so "whether Mark wrote it or not, the Holy Spirit still inspired it" — and trusting the Spirit to preserve Mark undercuts Ryan's escape. (b) On the asymmetry: if faith alone sufficed, "why would he add baptism at all?" The mention of baptism is pointless on Ryan's reading.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the rare baptism prooftext where the *textual* status genuinely matters, and Ryan is on solid mainstream ground: the Longer Ending's inauthenticity is the majority scholarly view. That blunts the verse as a standalone proof. But two things limit the win: (a) Heschmeyer's point that the verse was received as Scripture for centuries and that inspiration ≠ authorship is a real (if contestable) theological move, and many traditions still treat the Longer Ending as canonical; and (b) the *grammatical* asymmetry argument is weaker than the textual one — the most natural reading of "believes and is baptized will be saved" pairs the two, and the negative clause's silence about baptism does not by itself prove baptism optional (it may simply be that unbelief is the paradigmatic damning condition). The debate also exposes a tension in Ryan's overall method: he leans on textual criticism here while elsewhere defending the inspiration of contested passages (Deuteronomy 34, the disputed Markan ending's "essential" first clause). As a Catholic-vs-Reformed exchange, this concerns the strength of one verse, not the whole case.

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint tradition reads Mark 16:16 straightforwardly as pairing faith and baptism for salvation, consistent with the Restoration's required baptism (3 Nephi 11; Articles of Faith 1:4). The textual debate is less acute for Latter-day Saints, who locate authority in living revelation and additional scripture rather than in reconstructing the Markan autograph — and who have an independent, unambiguous baptism-saves text in the Book of Mormon. So where Ryan's strongest reply is "this verse may not be original," that move has limited purchase against an LDS interlocutor who is not relying on Mark 16:16 alone.

## Best Quotes
> "The vast majority of textual scholars, including conservative ones, actually think it's unlikely that even Mark 16:9-20 is authentically part of Mark's gospel... Daniel Wallace... says the text of Mark 16:9-10 is most likely not a part of the original inspired text of scripture." (Ryan)[^1]

> "Whether Mark wrote it or not, the Holy Spirit still inspired it, because the human author doesn't give it its inspiration." (Heschmeyer)[^3]
