# The Hypostatic Union Atonement Conundrum

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Blake Ostler]] presses a dilemma against the two-natures (hypostatic-union) Christology: the winning argument at the early councils was that **only a fully divine being can accomplish the atonement** — yet atonement is achieved *by suffering and dying*, and on the classical view **God cannot suffer or die** (impassibility/immutability). So if only the *human* nature suffered and died, a mere human accomplished the atonement; but if the *divine* nature must atone, then God suffered and died, contradicting classical theism. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The councils' own premise creates the bind.** Ostler attributes to Athanasius the argument that "only a fully divine being can atone for the sins of others" — but the atoning act is suffering and death, which the classical God cannot undergo. *Rating: a genuinely sharp dilemma*; the impassibility/atonement tension is a real and much-debated problem (note: Ostler conflates Athanasius/Nicaea with Chalcedon and misdates it "421" — the substantive point survives the slip). [^2]

2. **"Natures don't do things; persons do."** [[Jacob Hansen]]'s analogy: you cannot say "my fatherly nature ate the pizza but not my son nature." Actions are predicated of *persons*, so partitioning suffering to "the human nature only" is incoherent. *Rating: a forceful intuition* against the *communicatio idiomatum* as commonly stated. [^3]

3. **Ostler steelmans the two-natures view with a better analogy.** He offers immortal-spirit/mortal-body as the strongest version: "both immortal spirit and Jacob went to the pizzeria... but do we really say they both ate pizza?" He explicitly wants to "present their arguments in the strongest form possible." *Rating: a notably fair steelman*; even so he holds it "still doesn't work for them." [^4]

4. **Steelman / classical reply.** The doctrine of the *communicatio idiomatum* holds the *one person* (the divine Son) is the subject of both natures' properties: the Son truly died *according to his human nature* while the divine nature remained impassible — so "God the Son died" is true of the *person* without predicating mortality of the divine *essence*. The atonement's *infinite value* comes from the divine *person* who suffers *in* his assumed humanity; this is precisely what Chalcedon was crafted to express. *Rating: a strong, historic answer* — the conundrum assumes a partitioning the classical view denies; whether "person can die while nature cannot" is coherent is the live philosophical question, not an obvious contradiction. [^5]

## Best Quotes
> "Only a fully divine being can atone for the sins of others... The problem is that it's pulled off by suffering and dying. And God can't suffer or die given the classical view of Christianity." [^2]

> "Natures don't do things. Persons do things. And so I've never understood that." [^3]

> "I want to present their arguments in the strongest form possible... You go and you eat a pizza. Can you say that your immortal spirit is eating a pizza?" [^4]
