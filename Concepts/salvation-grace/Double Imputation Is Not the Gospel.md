# Double Imputation Is Not the Gospel

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that the Reformed doctrine of **double imputation** — our sin imputed *to* Christ on the cross, and Christ's righteousness imputed *to us* — is "an interpretation of the gospel," not the gospel itself. He plays clips of [[Voddie Baucham]] ("the righteousness of Christ is actually imputed to us... that's the gospel that we preach") and [[John MacArthur]], who calls [[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Corinthians 5:21]] "the greatest gospel verse in the Bible" and glosses it as "God punished Jesus for my sin, turns right around and treats me as if I lived his life... when he looks at the cross, he sees you." [^1]

Protestant theologian [[Randal Rauser]] is Horn's key witness against this conflation: Baucham "seems to have conflated his summary of the Calvinist plan of salvation with the gospel... it's not the gospel. It's an *interpretation* of the gospel." [^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Bible never calls this "the gospel."** Even though *euangelion* "is used nine times in 2 Corinthians," the text "does not say this is the gospel." And, Horn adds, "even if the passage was teaching the doctrine MacArthur thinks it's teaching, that doesn't make it part of the gospel" — being true and being *the gospel* are different claims. [^3]
2. **The reductio: it would unchurch the Wesleyans.** If double imputation were the heart of the gospel, then followers of [[John Wesley]] — Methodists who hold to an **imparted** rather than imputed righteousness — "would have a false gospel," as would anyone who reads [[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor 5:21]] differently. The critics will not anathematize Wesley, so by their own lights the doctrine cannot be the gospel. [^4]
3. **[[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor 5:21]] is exegetically underdetermined.** The verse "could mean Christ became sin in the sense that Christ became a *sacrificial sin offering*" (the Hebrew word for *sin* doubles as *sin offering*), or a reference to "the incarnation, Christ coming in the form of sinful flesh as [[Romans 8.3|Romans 8:3]] says." These readings deny double imputation yet are not heretical — so the doctrine cannot be a gospel essential. [^4]
4. **MacArthur refutes himself.** In a later interview MacArthur warned against exactly this over-loading: "It's very easy to pile stuff on top of the gospel and then even eliminate the real gospel." Horn uses MacArthur against MacArthur to show the inconsistency. [^5]
5. **Steelman of the Protestant counter.** A Reformed reader will insist the *mechanism* is not optional decoration but the **content** of the good news — "Christ died *for our sins*" ([[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Cor 15:3]]) is unintelligible without a substitution/imputation account, so to get imputation wrong is to misunderstand what the cross accomplished, i.e. to have a different gospel. Horn's reply is twofold: (a) affirming penal substitution or "Christ died for our sins" does not entail the *specific* Reformed double-imputation construction, which many orthodox Christians (Wesleyans, the Eastern churches, Catholics) reject while still confessing the cross; and (b) the disagreement *among Protestants* over [[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor 5:21]] is precisely the evidence that [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture|Scripture is not perspicuous on this point]], so it cannot be a self-evident gospel essential. Because Latter-day Saints likewise reject imputed-alien-righteousness in favor of a participatory, covenant model of justification, Horn's argument doubles as a defense of the legitimacy of non-Reformed soteriologies against the charge of "another gospel."
