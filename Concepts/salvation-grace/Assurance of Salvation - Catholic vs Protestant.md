# Assurance of Salvation — Catholic vs Protestant

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Protestants charge that the Catholic faith-and-works view destroys assurance of salvation. Trent Horn argues the **opposite**: the Protestant "works will always follow true faith" view is what corrodes assurance.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The Protestant bind: which works prove you're saved?
If good works necessarily *prove* true faith ([[John MacArthur]]), then *which* works, and how many? A "mediocre" Christian — prays, works, attends, avoids grave sin, but doesn't volunteer at the soup kitchen — is left perpetually unsure whether he was "ever really saved" [^1].

### 2. The despairing conclusion
Alan Parr's framing: many "claim they have faith... only to one day sadly realize they really never had saving faith at all." That uncertainty is the bitter fruit of tying assurance to an unspecified quantity of works [^2].

### 3. The Catholic alternative: examine your state of grace
You are not damned for skipping the soup kitchen; non-obligatory good works *strengthen* you against sin but aren't required to remain saved. Assurance comes from an honest **examination of conscience** — am I in a state of grace (free of unrepented grave sin)? — not from auditing whether you've done "enough" [^3].

## Best Quote
> "Protestantism creates a lack of assurance when you need to do a certain amount of good deeds to prove you have true faith, instead of just doing an honest examination of conscience that shows if you are in a state of grace or not." — [[Trent Horn]] [^3]
