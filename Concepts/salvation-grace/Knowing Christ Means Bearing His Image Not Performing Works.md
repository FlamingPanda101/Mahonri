# Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Andrea Woodmansee]] and [[Dave Butler|David Butler]] argue that the haunting "I never knew you, depart from me" of [[Matthew 7.21|Matthew 7:21-23]] (and the shut door of [[Matthew 25.1-13|the ten virgins]], and [[Luke 13.6-9|Luke 13:25-27]]) teaches that the dividing line at the door of salvation is **not works performed** — even prophecy, exorcism, and "many wonderful works" — but whether Christ *recognizes* us. And recognition, they argue, ultimately means **bearing Christ's image**: having so taken on his character that he sees himself in us. The test is [[Alma 5.18|Alma 5:14]]'s "have ye received his image in your countenances," answered by [[Moroni 7|Moroni 7:48]]'s "we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is." So the goal is not a checklist of ordinances or deeds but [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula|deification]] — becoming like Christ.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The oil is not the divider — recognition is
In the parable the foolish five *do* obtain oil and reach the door, yet "I know you not." Woodmansee's diagnostic move: "it's not necessarily the oil that's the dividing line between the wise virgins and the unwise virgins; it's the knowing — that's that dividing line." The same recognition-scene recurs in Luke 13 ("I know you not whence ye are") and Matthew 7. *Rating: a real structural observation — the foolish virgins' disqualifier is stated as non-recognition, not lack of oil.*[^3]

### 2. Works are explicitly insufficient
Matthew 7:22-23 has the rejected plead spectacular works — "have we not prophesied in thy name... cast out devils... done many wonderful works?" — and yet "I never knew you." Butler stresses the darkest word is **"never"**: not that they fell away after good service, but that even *while* doing wonders in his name he did not know them. This sharpens, rather than contradicts, [[Jesus Taught Salvation Involves Doing Not Faith Alone]]: doing the Father's will is necessary, but the *content* of that will is knowing/loving, not résumé-building. *Rating: the text does subordinate impressive works to "knowing"; the inference to deification is the interpretive step.*[^4]

### 3. Knowing = keeping commandments = love
[[1 John 2|1 John 2:3]] supplies the test: "hereby we do know that we know him, if we keep his commandments," and 2:10 ties abiding in light to loving one's brother. Butler notes the love-verb is *agapaō*, the same as [[Matthew 22.37-39|the two great commandments]]. So "knowing" Christ is keeping the commandments, which is *to love* — a state of heart, not a performance. *Rating: a sound lexical link between the Johannine and Synoptic "love"; the integration is the argument.*[^5]

### 4. The payoff is the image in the countenance
[[Alma 5.18|Alma 5:14]] ("received his image in your countenances... this mighty change in your hearts") read with "image" of God (Genesis 1:26) suggests *visibly* resembling God. Butler invokes the **family-resemblance** logic — "born of God, you carry a family resemblance from your parents" — and Oscar Wilde's *Picture of Dorian Gray* (the inner self made visible on the face) as the inverse. At the door, the foolish virgins lack his image; they have done the things but not *become* like him. See [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son]]. *Rating: an evocative synthesis; the Dorian-Gray and family-resemblance pieces are illustrations, not proofs.*[^6][^7]

### 5. The gate is a family reunion
[[2 Nephi 9.41|2 Nephi 9:41]] ("the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel... the Lord God is his name"), in an Eden-saturated passage, is read by Butler as the *same* being who oversaw Eden still at the gate — so passing it is "being recognized as returning family," not a stranger applying a test. *Rating: depends on the Eden-identification, but coheres with the recognition theme.*[^8]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Mainstream exegesis reads "I never knew you" as a Semitic idiom for *disowning* (cf. rabbinic "I do not know you" as excommunication) — a verdict on covenant-breaking ("ye that work iniquity"), not a statement that salvation hinges on ontological likeness. Reading "knowing" as *deification* imports a later LDS theology the Matthean text does not require; the Alma/Moroni "image" language is Book-of-Mormon, and chaining it to Matthew is a harmonizing move. And the "works don't count" emphasis can be over-read: Matthew 7 condemns *lawless* workers ("ye that work iniquity"), and the very next verse commends the one who *hears and does* — so doing is not dismissed, only doing-without-relationship. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim is not that obedience is optional but that obedience is the *means* by which one comes to know (and resemble) Christ, so that "knowing" and "doing his will" are the same arc, not rivals — which is exactly how 1 John frames it (you *know* him *if* you keep his commandments).
