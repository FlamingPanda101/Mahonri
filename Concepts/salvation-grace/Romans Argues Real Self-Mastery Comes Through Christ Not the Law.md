# Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues that the *thesis* of Romans, read as Greco-Roman rhetoric for a Hellenized Roman audience, is **self-mastery (*enkrateia*) through Christ rather than through the Mosaic law**. Romans (a martial people prizing discipline) respected the Jews because they *persisted*, and located the source in the Mosaic law as a vehicle of self-discipline — the same appeal that drew the god-fearers (e.g. Cornelius the centurion). Paul's move is to grant the desire for self-mastery, then argue that a *borrowed, cross-civilizational* adoption of the law "lacks force" and dooms the Gentile to failure ("the good that I would I do not," [[Romans 7.19|Romans 7:19]], a *Medea*/*akrasia* allusion) — and finally to **flip** the Roman self-image: *you* who fancy yourselves the civilized masters of reason are actually slaves to your passions, and real mastery is found only "within Christ" ([[Romans 7.4|Romans 7:4]], "married to another... raised from the dead"). The gift of Christ is *civilization in the kingdom of God*. This is the load-bearing reading behind [[Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography]] and [[Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The hook is *enkrateia*.** Thomassen: "what do the Jews have? ...the Mosaic law... a vehicle through which self-mastery could be achieved... Romans are a martial people; what is a key component of military success? Discipline." The earliest Gentile converts (Cornelius; the "god-fearers," likely soldiers) were drawn to that disciplined regimen — paralleling [[Philo of Alexandria]] selling Judaism's self-mastery to Gentiles. *Rating: *enkrateia* was a central Greco-Roman/Stoic value, and the god-fearer phenomenon is historical; the "this is Romans' thesis" framing is the interpretive claim.*[^1]
2. **Romans 7 dramatizes the law's failure.** "The good that I would I do not" ([[Romans 7.19|Romans 7:19]]) is the *Medea*/*akrasia* trope (the opposite of *enkrateia*), staged through a [[Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography|fictive Roman persona]] who adopts the law and fails — because it is "unnatural... you are co-opting something that isn't intrinsic to who you are as your people... it's artificial and therefore it will have no real hold on you." *Rating: the *akrasia* reading is well-grounded (see the paired persona note); the "alien law lacks force" gloss is Thomassen's synthesis.*[^2]
3. **The flip: Romans are the real barbarians.** "You Romans present yourselves as the civilized folk... but in reality you're slaves to your passions, and you won't be able to overcome that through some artificial means" — Paul inverts the Greek/barbarian, citizen/other hierarchy (Rome used **Medea** and **Cleopatra-as-Medea** to define the dangerous "other"). *Rating: the rhetorical inversion is a striking and plausible reading of Paul's strategy toward a Roman audience.*[^3]
4. **The solution is union with Christ.** [[Romans 7.4|Romans 7:4]] ("ye are become dead to the law by the body of Christ, that ye should be married to another, even to him who is raised from the dead") resolves it: real self-mastery is "found within Christ... through baptism and Christ, not through the law." Coheres with [[Moral Formation as Deification - Overcoming the Passions]] and (against faith-alone minimalism) [[Jesus Taught Salvation Involves Doing Not Faith Alone]]. *Rating: Romans 7:4 does pivot from law to union-with-the-risen-Christ; the *enkrateia*-fulfilled reading is the interpretive payoff.*[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Mainstream Pauline scholarship reads Romans' core as **justification / the righteousness of God revealed apart from law** (Romans 1:16-17; 3:21-26) and the Jew/Gentile-into-one-people question (Romans 9-11; the "New Perspective" of Dunn/Wright), *not* primarily as a Greco-Roman treatise on *enkrateia*. Romans 7's "I" and the law's "weakness" are usually read against the *Jewish* Torah and the human plight under sin (Romans 8:3), not as an ethnography of Romans co-opting an alien law; the *Medea*/Stoic-self-mastery frame, while illuminating, is a *minority, classics-driven* lens that risks crowding out Paul's covenantal-salvation argument. And "real self-mastery through Christ" can sound like the very works-righteousness Paul resists if detached from grace. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the readings are complementary, not exclusive — Paul *can* be answering "how does a Gentile attain the disciplined life the law promised?" with "through union with the risen Christ" *while also* teaching justification by grace; the self-mastery frame foregrounds the *transformative* (theosis-adjacent) dimension LDS theology stresses, and the grace it rests on is the [[Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon|reciprocal-but-incongruous]] gift, so the mastery is Christ's gift worked in the believer, not an autonomous achievement. Offered as a recovered *rhetorical* layer, held alongside (not against) the salvation argument.
