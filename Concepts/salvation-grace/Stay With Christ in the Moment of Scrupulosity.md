# Stay With Christ in the Moment of Scrupulosity

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]]'s central intervention for scrupulosity reverses the sufferer's instinct. The scrupulous person says "I'm not going to feel good until I do this [perfectly]," and waits to be clean enough *before* seeking help or grace — "when I learn to swim, then I'll go," while they are drowning *now*. Allen's prescription: in the very moment of the anxious thought, **stay with Christ** — activate the Atonement, call on his name, bear testimony to yourself, "remember" — rather than waiting to qualify. Grace is for the drowning, applied in the moment, not a reward issued after the storm passes.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Don't wait to qualify (rated: strong; corrects the core error).** The destructive pattern: "I'll do it perfectly, I'll never do it again ... and then when I figure it out and I'm perfect at it, then I'll go get help." Allen: "that's a difficult situation ... you're not seeking help in the moment when you need it the most ... it's like drowning. When I learn to swim, then I'll go — no, you're drowning *now*. Use the resources you have now."[^1] See [[Maladaptive Perfectionism Leaves Mercy Out of Becoming Perfect]].

2. **"Stay" — the Lazarus/Thomas lesson (rated: moderate; devotional but concrete).** Allen retells a scene (from *The Chosen*) in which Christ tells Thomas, in effect, "I know it's hard ... but if you just stay with [me], things will make sense ... Stay with the mercy, stay with the compassion, even when you're struggling with these scrupulous thoughts."[^2] The instruction is durational: remain in relationship through the not-understanding.

3. **Call on his name and testify to yourself (rated: strong; a usable practice).** "Call upon his name ... Jesus, take me out of this. Jesus, be with me. Stay with me." Allen and the host commend a practice of *bearing your testimony to yourself* in the moment — naming "Jesus is the Christ, my Savior ... he can heal me, get me through this episode" — drawn from Rev 12:11 ("they overcame him by ... the word of their testimony").[^3]

4. **Christ modeled it in Gethsemane (rated: moderate; christological warrant).** When Christ suffered he "called upon his name" — "Father, if there be any other way, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not my will but thine" ([[Luke 22.42|Luke 22:42]]). Allen: "That's the pattern ... when we're suffering, because he suffered, he showed us the pattern" — look to the Father in the moment of agony.[^4]

5. **"Look unto me in every thought" (rated: strong; scriptural anchor).** The therapeutic "stay" is keyed to [[Doctrine and Covenants 6.22-23|D&C 6:36]] ("Look unto me in every thought; doubt not, fear not") — now read not as a demand to perform but as an invitation to *abide* even mid-struggle, with the resurrected wounds as the assurance of present access.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A clinician might caution that for clinical religious OCD, "call on Christ / bear your testimony" could itself become another **reassurance compulsion** — the very loop scrupulosity feeds on — if used to neutralize anxiety on demand; evidence-based OCD treatment (ERP) often involves *tolerating* the uncertainty rather than performing a ritual to discharge it. Allen's framing partly anticipates this: the "stay" is explicitly *not* "do another perfect behavior to feel clean" but a shift from contingent worth to abiding relationship ("you're enough ... right now, not when you do this perfectly"), pairing the spiritual practice with the decoupling of worth from performance (see [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]]) and with professional help ("use the resources you have now"). The soundest synthesis: "stay with Christ" as an identity-anchoring posture, not as a compulsive anxiety-neutralizer, ideally alongside clinical care.

## Best Quotes
> "It's like drowning. When I learn to swim, then I'll go — no, you're drowning *now*. Use the resources that you have now while you're drowning."[^1]

> "Stay with Christ. Stay with the mercy, stay with the compassion. Even when you're struggling with these scrupulous thoughts ... just stay with Christ."[^2]

> "That's the pattern ... when we're suffering, because he suffered, he showed us the pattern" — Gethsemane's "nevertheless, not my will, but thine."[^4]
