# John 3.5 Born of Water and the Spirit Is Disputed Between Baptism and Spiritual Rebirth

## The Claim
In [[John 3.5|John 3:5]] Jesus tells [[Nicodemus]] one must be "born of water and of the Spirit." [[Joe Heschmeyer]] ("I've saved the best for last") argues these are "the two ingredients... of water baptism" and "this is the true and original meaning of being born again," understood as baptism "by all Christians... for centuries." [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] denies it refers to baptism at all: "born of water" means either *natural* birth (matching v. 6, "that which is born of flesh is flesh") or the [[Ezekiel 36]] sprinkling of clean water that symbolizes forgiveness — accomplished by the Spirit through faith, not the ordinance.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **"Born of water and Spirit" as baptism (rating: moderate; historical reception).** Heschmeyer: this "seems like a clear reference to water baptism," and "as best as I can tell, this is exactly how all Christians understood Jesus's words for centuries" — a reading he later backs with patristic unanimity (see [[The Unanimous Early Church Read Baptism as Saving]]).[^1]
2. **Nicodemus should have known the OT background (rating: moderate; intertextual).** Heschmeyer ties it to [[Ezekiel 36]] (water + new heart + Spirit) and the Jewish *mikvah*, arguing Jesus expected "a teacher of Israel" to recognize the water-and-Spirit motif.[^2]
3. **Ryan's "born of water = natural birth" reading (rating: moderate; contextual).** Ryan: "born of water either means Ezekiel 36 sprinkling clean water on you, which represents forgiveness of sins, or some will interpret it as natural birth," which "seems to link up with the next verse" contrasting flesh and spirit. He stresses the passage "doesn't even use the word baptism."[^3]
4. **Heschmeyer's counter on "born of water" (rating: moderate; lexical).** "Born of water" is never used elsewhere for natural birth ("Born of flesh, sure... but being born of water and the spirit is a very clear reference to baptism"). Ryan concedes the phrase "born of water" appears nowhere else, which Heschmeyer turns against the natural-birth gloss.[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The verse is a genuine standoff. Heschmeyer's **reception-history** argument is his strongest card: the church fathers overwhelmingly read John 3:5 as baptism, and "born of water" as a metaphor for *physical* birth is poorly attested (amniotic-fluid readings are largely modern). Ryan's stronger option is therefore the **Ezekiel-36** reading (water = symbolic cleansing by the Spirit), not natural birth — but that reading, ironically, still ties "water" to a real cleansing event, which fits baptismal imagery and is precisely why both sides cite Ezekiel 36. Ryan's "doesn't use the word baptism" point is weak on its own (neither do many obvious baptism allusions). The deciding consideration is whether one privileges first-millennium reception (favoring Heschmeyer) or a *sola fide* systematic prior that reinterprets the water (favoring Ryan). The vault's [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]] shows a Protestant who concedes far more to the baptismal reading than Ryan does. Catholic-vs-Reformed dispute; LDS by analogy.

## LDS Relevance
Latter-day Saints read John 3:5 as a direct statement that baptism (water) and the gift of the Holy Ghost (Spirit) are both required to "enter into the kingdom of God" — a cornerstone proof-text for the necessity of the ordinance and a natural fit with the two-part pattern of baptism by water and confirmation by the Spirit. The Restoration thus lands squarely with Heschmeyer against Ryan on this verse, while grounding the *authority* to perform both in restored priesthood. Joseph Smith's revisions and LDS usage treat "born of water and of the Spirit" as the literal baptismal covenant, not a metaphor for faith-only regeneration.
