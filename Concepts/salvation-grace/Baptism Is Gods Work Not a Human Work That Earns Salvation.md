# Baptism Is Gods Work Not a Human Work That Earns Salvation

## The Claim
A pivotal sub-dispute: is baptism a "**work**" that, if required for salvation, would make grace into merit? [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] says yes — *ergon* means "deed or action," and "is God getting baptized? No, you are," so baptism is a human work that would give "reason to boast" and cannot be a condition of a free gift (Romans 11:6). [[Joe Heschmeyer]] says no — baptism is something we *receive* (passive), and [[Titus 3.5|Titus 3:5]] settles it: Paul says we are saved "not by deeds done by us in righteousness" *and* "by the washing of regeneration," so baptism saves precisely *as God's work*, not ours.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Titus 3:5 brackets baptism with "not by works" (rating: strong; textual).** Heschmeyer: "St. Paul says we're not saved by deeds done by us in righteousness, but that we are saved by the washing of regeneration and renewal in the Holy Spirit. If that sounds like baptism, it's because it is." So Paul holds *both* that baptism saves *and* that works don't — meaning baptism is not in the "works" category.[^1]
2. **Baptism is received, not performed (rating: moderate; grammatical/voice).** Heschmeyer presses that the verb is passive — "It is an action you're receiving. It's passive. You can see it right there in the verb" — analogous to how an OT Israelite *received* circumcision. Ryan counters that one still "participates," so it remains a deed "you're doing for God."[^2]
3. **Ryan's "circumcision is a work" parry (rating: moderate; analogical).** Ryan gets Heschmeyer to grant circumcision is "a work of the law," then argues: circumcision was received yet still a work; baptism is received; therefore baptism is a work, and requiring it makes salvation non-gracious. Heschmeyer replies that "works" is used in *at least three different ways* in the NT (Romans 2 works unto life; Romans 3 works of the law; general deeds), and Ryan "conflates all three."[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
The disagreement is largely *terminological*, and both men half-concede it. Ryan's syllogism ("*ergon* means deed; baptism is a deed; therefore baptism is a work that would let you boast") has the weakness Heschmeyer names: it equivocates on "works," since Paul plainly *commends* some works (Ephesians 2:10) while condemning others (Ephesians 2:9). Heschmeyer's Titus 3:5 move is strong — Paul really does pair "not by works" with "washing of regeneration" — *if* "washing of regeneration" is baptism, which Ryan disputes (he reads it as Spirit-wrought regeneration through faith). So the argument loops back to whether Titus 3:5 references the ordinance. The deeper resolution is Heschmeyer's category point (developed in [[Works of the Law Means Mosaic Boundary Markers Not Obedience as Such]]): if Paul's "works" polemic targets Mosaic boundary markers, baptism (a Christ-commanded covenant act *received* as grace) simply isn't the kind of "work" that occasions boasting. The vault's [[Baptism Saves but Is Not the Cause of Salvation]] reaches a compatible verdict from the Protestant side (baptism conveys grace; it is God's work, "the means by which God saves us by grace"). Catholic-vs-Reformed dispute.

## LDS Relevance
Latter-day Saints reject the "baptism = meritorious work" framing for reasons parallel to Heschmeyer's: ordinances are the *appointed means* through which God's grace is received, not human achievements that put God in one's debt. The Book of Mormon insists "it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23) and that salvation comes "through the atonement of Christ" (Mosiah 3), while still requiring the covenant ordinance of baptism — exactly the both/and (grace *and* received ordinance) that Heschmeyer defends and Ryan resists. Proxy baptism for the dead underscores that the rite is God's saving instrument applied even to those who cannot "work" at all. See [[The New Testament Repeatedly Says Baptism Saves]].

## Best Quotes
> "St. Paul says we're not saved by deeds done by us in righteousness, but that we are saved by the washing of regeneration... So we're not saved by our deeds, by our works. We are saved by baptism. *Ergon* — go trust Paul, not Ryan, about what Paul means about works." (Heschmeyer)[^1]

> "Is God getting baptized? No, you are. And so therefore it's still a deed being done by you, something you're doing for God." (Ryan)[^2]
