# Old Testament Water Types Prefigure Baptism

## The Claim
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] frames his whole baptism case with **Old Testament typology**: throughout the OT "water and the Holy Spirit [are] repeatedly linked to the death of sin and to the creation of new life," prefiguring baptism. He cites [[Naaman]] washing seven times in the Jordan (2 Kings 5, "his flesh was restored like the flesh of a little child"); the Spirit hovering over the waters of creation (Genesis 1); the flood and the dove (Genesis 8; tied to baptism in [[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3]]); the Red Sea crossing (Exodus 14, tied to baptism in 1 Corinthians 10); and his "personal favorite," [[Ezekiel 36]], where God promises cleansing by water, a new heart, the Spirit, and entrance into His people — the four things the NT says are fulfilled in baptism. [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] rejects the typology as eisegesis: Naaman is "just God cleansing him through that means," not a picture of baptism.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Naaman as a baptismal type (rating: moderate; typological).** A simple, "too ordinary" washing produces cleansing and childlike rebirth — "prefiguring... the rebirth, being like a little child, that we get in baptism."[^1]
2. **The New Testament itself draws the lines (rating: strong; the NT cites the types).** Heschmeyer's strongest support is that *Scripture*, not just later tradition, connects these events to baptism: [[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3:20-21]] explicitly ties Noah's flood to baptism ("baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you"), and 1 Corinthians 10:1-2 says Israel was "baptized into Moses in the cloud and in the sea." So the typology is apostolic, not invented.[^2]
3. **Ezekiel 36's fourfold promise maps onto baptism (rating: moderate; intertextual).** Cleansing-water + new-heart + Spirit + people-of-God — "as we've seen from the New Testament evidence, scripture speaks of each of those four promises being fulfilled in baptism." This is also the OT background Heschmeyer uses to read [[John 3.5]] (see [[John 3.5 Born of Water and the Spirit Is Disputed Between Baptism and Spiritual Rebirth]]).[^3]
4. **Ryan's deflection (rating: moderate; the rebuttal).** Ryan grants Naaman was cleansed "through that means" but denies it pictures baptism; he reads "born of water" types as symbolic cleansing by the Spirit through faith, not the ordinance.[^1]

## Apologetic Engagement
The typological case is stronger than Ryan allows precisely because two of its pillars are drawn *by the New Testament itself*: 1 Peter 3 (flood→baptism) and 1 Corinthians 10 (Red Sea→baptism) are not patristic embellishments but apostolic readings, which makes "that's just eisegesis" too quick. Naaman is more debatable — the text presents a healing, and "type" status is an inference — so Ryan's deflation has some purchase there, but it is the weakest of Heschmeyer's examples, not the load-bearing one. The honest assessment: where the NT explicitly baptizes a type (Noah, Red Sea), the connection is exegetically secure; where Heschmeyer extends the pattern by inference (Naaman, creation), it is suggestive rather than probative. A *sola fide* interpreter can still concede the imagery while contesting whether it makes the *ordinance* the instrument of salvation — the recurring crux of the whole debate. Catholic-vs-Reformed dispute.

## LDS Relevance
Typological readings of OT water as prefiguring baptism are congenial to Latter-day Saint scripture, which is itself typologically dense and which teaches that "all things... are the typifying" of Christ (2 Nephi 11:4; Moses 6:63). The Restoration affirms the necessity of baptism the types point toward, and the Red Sea / flood patterns of salvation-through-water resonate with the LDS view of baptism as a real covenant passage from death to life (Romans 6). Where the typology terminates — that the saving reality is conveyed *through* the ordinance, not merely symbolized — the LDS position again sides with Heschmeyer over Ryan, while grounding the rite in restored priesthood authority.
