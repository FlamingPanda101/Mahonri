# The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric

## The Claim
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] contrasts two orientations of theology. Classical "Sanselmian" theology (his coinage from [[Anselm of Canterbury|Anselm]]) is **theocentric**: God created us "for his own glory because he loves his glory," so even the **atonement** becomes "something about rectifying God's hurt or his dishonor or the offense to his justice." The God restored in [[Moses 7|Moses 7]] is **anthropocentric**: he "weeps in solidarity with us," and declares ([[Moses 1.39|Moses 1:39]]) "my work and my glory" to be "your immortality and eternal life." God's glory is real but a "collateral benefit," not his "primary intent" — "his highest objective is our happiness and joy."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The atonement test — rate: strong as a diagnostic.** Givens makes the orientation concrete: a theocentric frame makes the atonement about God's offended honor (the Anselmian satisfaction model); an anthropocentric frame makes it about lifting humans into God's kind of life. "These are actual statements... made by theologians in this Sanselmian tradition," he says, naming a line "from [[Tertullian]] through [[Jonathan Edwards]] through the Baltimore Catechism" giving "the exact same answer": God created us for his own glory.[^2]
2. **The weeping God as the restored picture — rate: central LDS datum.** "He's a god who weeps in solidarity with us... a God the Father who is the perfect reflection of the Son" of [[John the Evangelist|John]]'s Gospel. This is the passage ([[Moses 7|Moses 7:28]], "the God of heaven... wept") that the vault elsewhere treats as the flagship contradiction of divine impassibility — see [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]] and [[Passibility - Mainstream Theology Now Affirms a God Who Suffers]].[^1]
3. **It reorders the apologetic question — rate: a reframing, not a proof.** Givens: "the question isn't 'is there a god?'... A better question is: assuming there is a God, is that God worthy of my adoration and my commitment?" The weeping, human-centered God is, he argues, the one "worthy in every way."[^3]
4. **Glory as by-product, not goal — rate: a value claim with scriptural anchor.** Human happiness "in turn comes back around and does glorify God," so glory is genuine but downstream of love — the inverse of the glory-first theology the vault critiques in [[Hell for Gods Glory Is Narcissism Not Love]].[^2]

**Steelman of the opposing (classical / Reformed) side:** A theocentric ordering is not cold self-regard but the correct creaturely metaphysics: God, as the highest good, *is* what is best for us, so willing his own glory and willing our good are the *same* act — our beatitude just *is* the enjoyment of God's glory forever (Westminster's "chief end... to glorify God and enjoy him"). The Anselmian satisfaction model, defenders add, takes sin's gravity and God's justice seriously in a way "solidarity" alone does not; and biblical grief-language can be read as anthropopathism rather than literal divine suffering (the impassibilist reply developed at [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]]). On this view "anthropocentric" theology risks making God a means to human flourishing — a domesticated deity.

**Counter-objection (Givens's rejoinder):** If our good and God's glory truly coincide, then *which you name as primary* still reveals the character you ascribe to God — and a tradition that can say God damns infants "for his glory" has, by its own ordering, made glory swallow love. The weeping God of Moses 7 is not God-as-means-to-human-ends; he is a God whose very greatness *consists* in self-giving love (the foot-washing of John 13), so the anthropocentric reading exalts God *more*, not less. The satisfaction model's "offense to God" can be retained as real without making it the *point* of the atonement.

## Best Quotes
> "My work and my glory is to bring to pass your immortality and eternal life. The fact that God declares his highest objective is our happiness and joy... and which in turn comes back around and does glorify God."[^2]

> "He's a god who weeps in solidarity with us. And we have a God the Father who is the perfect reflection of the Son that we see depicted so fully in the gospel of John."[^1]

> "The atonement becomes something about rectifying God's hurt or his dishonor or the offense to his justice, instead of being anthropocentric in the way that the book of Moses tells us God is."[^2]

> "Assuming there is a God, is that God worthy of my adoration and my commitment and my devotion?"[^3]
