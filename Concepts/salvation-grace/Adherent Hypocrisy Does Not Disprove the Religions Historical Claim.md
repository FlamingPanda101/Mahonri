# Adherent Hypocrisy Does Not Disprove the Religions Historical Claim

#type/claim

## The Claim

A student tells [[Hayden Carroll]] that the chief reason she rejects mainstream Christianity is that its adherents are **hypocrites** — they preach one standard and live another (she cites her own disillusionment with Joel Osteen, who allegedly failed to open his church during a Texas flood). [^1][^2] Carroll concedes the experience is genuinely off-putting and that hypocrites populate every organization, but he insists this is **logically severable from the truth of the religion**: the faith stands or falls on a *historical event*, the Resurrection, and no amount of believer hypocrisy can reach back and alter what did or did not happen in first-century Jerusalem. The appeal-to-hypocrisy is, at bottom, a *tu quoque* that changes the subject from the claim to the claimant. [^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The truth-maker is an event, not the believers.** Carroll's load-bearing move: "Either Jesus Christ was crucified and was risen again or he wasn't. ... If I'm a hypocrite ... that doesn't impact the historical event of the resurrection." [^3] The truth of a historical proposition is fixed by the past; the present conduct of those who affirm it is causally and logically downstream and cannot retroactively falsify it. This is the same structure as [[One Anachronism Does Not Disprove the Book of Mormon]] (a peripheral defect leaves the central claim intact) and the inverse of [[Dying for a Belief Proves Sincerity Not Truth]] (there, *good* conduct — martyrdom — equally fails to *prove* the claim true; conduct and truth-value are simply different axes).
2. **The objection proves too much.** "On that standard, you can't be a part of any organization, whether it's religious or not, because you're going to find hypocrites in every organization." [^4] If hypocrisy among members refuted a belief system, it would refute *every* movement with members — secular and religious alike — which is absurd; therefore it refutes none. The argument is a generalized parity rebuttal, kin to the move in [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]] (a debunking method that dissolves anything dissolves nothing).
3. **Concede the pastoral force without conceding the logic.** Carroll does not dismiss the complaint: "it is hard to be a part of a religion or a church where people are hypocritical," and he grants "you and I could look at our lives and see times where we were hypocritical as well." [^4] The admission *strengthens* the argument: hypocrisy is universal to fallen humanity, so it cannot be a differentiator that singles out one faith as false.

**Steelman of the critic.** (a) The objection is better read not as "hypocrisy disproves the Resurrection" but as a **credibility / testimony** challenge: most people cannot independently verify the Resurrection and must trust the community that transmits it; pervasive hypocrisy is *evidence about the reliability of that testimonial chain*, which is epistemically relevant even if it leaves the bare historical fact untouched. (b) For a religion whose own scriptures make transformed lives the promised fruit (e.g., "by their fruits ye shall know them"), systemic hypocrisy is not irrelevant noise but a *failed empirical prediction* of the system, so the believer cannot fully quarantine conduct from the truth-claim. (c) Carroll's framing privileges Christianity-as-historical-proposition; a critic of *Mormonism* could turn the very same severing move against LDS apologetics that elsewhere leans on members' exemplary service (cf. Carroll's own appeal, minutes earlier, to Latter-day Saints out-serving the average American as a point in the faith's favor). [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Either Jesus Christ was crucified and was risen again or he wasn't. ... If I'm a hypocrite, let's say that I'm telling everybody how to live and then behind the scenes I'm doing whatever I want — that doesn't impact the historical event of the resurrection." [^3]

> "On that standard, you can't be a part of any organization, whether it's religious or not, because you're going to find hypocrites in every organization." [^4]

> "Just because somebody doesn't live a certain way doesn't disprove the religion necessarily." [^3]
