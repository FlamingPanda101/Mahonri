# Legalism Tries to Earn Gods Love Through Works Alone

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]] names **legalism** as a driver of scrupulosity: the belief that one must "earn God's love through purely works ... just by their actions" while forgetting faith, mercy, and compassion. The legalist over-emphasizes religious behaviors — "I've got to read scriptures for three hours a day," "I have to go to the temple multiple times during the week" — as the price of being loved and "in favor in his eyes." This is the practical engine behind feeling never "good enough," and (Allen stresses) it leaves Christ and the Atonement *out* of the process it claims to serve.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The clinical/theological label (rated: strong; Allen published on it).** "There's a word for that in the literature — it's called legalism ... we did a paper about it. People feel that they need to earn God's love through purely works ... and they forget faith."[^1] The term bridges the psychology of perfectionism and the theology of grace.

2. **Works without faith — the inversion of James (rated: strong; scriptural).** The legalist exactly inverts [[James 2.14-26|James 2:17]] ("faith without works is dead"): they keep the works but jettison the faith and mercy, when the New Testament binds the two together. See [[Works Are the Fruit of Faith Not the Price of Salvation]] and [[Faith and Works - Two Blades of the Scissors]]. *Rating:* the diagnosis is doctrinally sound — works are meant to be the *fruit* of faith, not the *purchase price* of love.

3. **It invalidates the Atonement (rated: strong; the theological cost).** If "we think that we're going to be perfect" and every self-image "will be just completely ideal," then "that invalidates ... the Atonement ... his grace. Why do we need him then?" Allen: perfectionism is "setting yourself up for failure" precisely because "where's Christ in this?"[^2] Legalism is thus not over-devotion but a subtle functional denial of grace.

4. **Behavior-bound worthiness (rated: strong; ties legalism to identity).** Legalism attaches worthiness "to our behaviors and what we do, not who we are" — collapsing the gospel's "you are a child of God" into "I am loved *if* I perform."[^3] See [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]] and the guilt/shame distinction in [[Guilt Is Corrective but Shame Attacks Identity]].

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A Reformed critic of Latter-day Saint soteriology will say the *system itself* is legalistic — worthiness interviews, temple-recommend questions, and "after all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23) re-attach standing to performance, so Allen is treating a symptom the theology produces. An LDS reply is that legalism is a *distortion* the gospel explicitly rejects: grace is "unconditional love, not an earned wage" (see [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]]), "after all we can do" is read as "*in spite of* all we can do" (see [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]]), and ordinances are covenant-relationship, not merit-accrual. Allen concedes some members internalize the destructive "mental map"; his point is that this map "is not the gospel," not that high standards are bad.

## Best Quotes
> "There's a word for that in the literature — it's called legalism ... people feel that they need to earn God's love through purely works just by their actions ... and they forget faith."[^1]

> "Where's Christ in this? Where's the Atonement of Jesus Christ in this? ... that invalidates him and the Atonement and grace. Why do we need him then?"[^2]

> "The worthiness issue is attached to our behaviors and what we do, not who we are."[^3]
