# James Says Abraham Believed Not That He Completed the Work

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] notes a wording puzzle in [[James 2.24|James 2:21-23]]: after invoking Abraham offering Isaac, James concludes "Abraham **believed** God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness" — *believed*, not "obeyed" or "did the work." On the [[Apocryphal Traditions That Satan Tricked Abraham Into the Sacrifice|Satan-trick reading]], this fits exactly: Abraham was credited for his good-faith *belief* that he was doing the Lord's will, even though the act itself (per the apocrypha) was never the Lord's command and was never carried out. Intention, not the completed deed, was imputed for righteousness.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The verse credits belief, not the act (rated: moderate, exegetical)
"James isn't saying Abraham did the work that God commanded him to do — he doesn't say that. He believed he was doing the Lord's work and it was imputed unto him for righteousness ... even though it wasn't the Lord's work, he thought it was, and that was enough."[^1] The repetition in [[Romans 4.17|Romans 4]] ("Abraham believed God") is read the same way.

### 2. The canonical Akedah leaves "believed" unexplained (rated: contextual)
Barnes argues the standard reading (God really commanded it; Abraham obeyed) makes "believed ... imputed for righteousness" an odd phrasing, since one would expect "obeyed." The apocryphal frame supplies a reason the text stresses *belief*: Abraham never completed the work, so faith is precisely what is counted.[^1]

### 3. Faith and works held together (rated: moderate)
The reading still keeps James's faith-plus-works thrust — "good faith, good belief is imputed unto Abraham for righteousness; he was a friend of God" — so it does not collapse into faith-alone; it relocates what the "work" was (faithful intent under a perceived command).[^1] (Compare [[Faith and Works - Two Blades of the Scissors]].)

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) "believed" in James is standard biblical idiom for trusting obedience — James's *whole* point is that faith was "made perfect" by works (v. 22), and he explicitly says Abraham was "justified by works when he offered Isaac" (v. 21), so reading "believed" as "didn't actually do it" runs against the immediate context; (b) the move depends on the contested Satan-trick apocrypha (see the steelman in that note), so it inherits their evidentiary weakness; and (c) the mainstream LDS and catholic reading already harmonizes James without it — Abraham's faith *was* active in a real, completed-as-far-as-permitted obedience, the lamb being divinely interrupted (cf. [[James - Justification Before Men Reply Rebutted]], [[James 2-24 - Justified by Works Not Faith Alone]]). The narrow defensible observation is lexical and modest: James foregrounds *believed/imputed* language, which is at least *compatible* with an intention-centered reading.
