# Faith Is Trust in a True Future and Fear Is Faith in a False One

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Todd McLauchlin]] reframes the relationship between faith and fear: both are *trust in a future reality that has not yet happened* — the difference is only whether that reality is **true** (from God) or **false** (from "the father of lies"). On this account **"fear is just faith in a false future reality."** Faith means giving voice to something true that "only comes from God"; fear means giving voice to, or believing, a lie. This dissolves the usual "faith vs. fear" opposition into a single category (trust in an unseen future) with two possible objects, and explains why Scripture commands "fear not" — to fear is to *participate in the spirit of the lie* [^1].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Faith and fear are structurally the same act — rate: moderate (definitional)
McLauchlin's symmetry: "faith is having a perception of a future reality that's built on... something true from God... fear is having a perception of a future reality that's built on a lie." Both are *proactive trust in the unseen*; the moral difference is the source. Hence "you're still exercising faith" when you fear — "faith in something false." This complements, rather than contradicts, the evidence-based-faith account in [[Faith Is Blind vs Faith Is Evidence-Based]] (faith here is still about a real object — God's promised future — not wishful thinking) [^1][^2].

### 2. "Fear not" is therefore a doctrinal command, not just comfort — rate: moderate
Because fear is faith in a falsehood, the scriptural command to "divest yourself of your fears" (D&C 67:10, "you shall see my face") is read as a command to stop trusting the adversary's false future. "Faith is not wishful thinking; faith is giving word to something that is true and that only comes from God" — and "fear is the same thing in a false future reality" [^2].

### 3. The same action springs from either faith or fear — rate: moderate (illustrative)
McLauchlin's worked example: paying tithing "out of fear of God's wrath" versus "out of love to build God's kingdom" is the *same outward act* with opposite roots — "one builds its house on sand, the other on rock." Whole industries (insurance, anti-aging cosmetics) run on fear; the adversary's dominion *holds* people through fear, offering them a way to assuage it. Faith in Christ "eradicates the lie of the fear." See [[Worship and Praise Initiates Gods Power Before Feeling Follows]] for the practice that re-aims trust [^3][^4].

### 4. Fear is the first fruit of the Fall — rate: moderate (typological)
He grounds the claim in Eden: the first fruit of the Fall is fear — "run and hide... see that you are naked" — so faith in Christ is the antidote that reverses the Fall's first effect. The "fear of God" that "is the beginning of wisdom" is distinguished as the *awe* of encountering God's reality and one's own insufficiency, which then drives one *to* Christ the Comforter — not the same as the fear that the adversary uses [^5].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A philosophical critic will object that the definition **equivocates on "faith."** Fear is an *emotion* (an affective response to perceived threat); faith, in most theological usage, is *trust* or *belief* — collapsing them by saying "fear is faith in a false future" is a rhetorical move, not an analysis, since one can fear something one *knows* is unlikely (the fear persists against one's beliefs). It also risks pastoral harm: telling an anxious or traumatized believer that their fear is simply "faith in a lie from the father of lies" can shame ordinary, even adaptive, fear and ignore its biological and circumstantial causes. And the neat symmetry understates that some fear is *rational and good* (fear of real danger), which the "fear not" texts do not abolish. A measured Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is a devotional reframe aimed at the chronic, faithless *anxiety* that "is the driving impulse of existence in this life," not at acute, appropriate fear; it preserves a place for the godly "fear of the Lord" as awe; and its practical payoff — that one combats faithless fear not by white-knuckling but by re-orienting trust toward Christ's true promised future — is consonant with [[Moroni 7|Moroni 7]]'s linkage of faith, hope, and the things "which are not seen, which are true."

## Best Quotes

> "Faith is not wishful thinking; faith is giving word to something that is true and that only comes from God." [^2]
