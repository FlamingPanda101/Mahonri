# Works of the Law Means Mosaic Boundary Markers Not Obedience as Such

## The Claim
The whole baptism debate turns on what Paul means by "**works**." [[Ryan (NeedGod)|Ryan]] holds that "works" (*ergon*) means *any* human deed — "deeds or actions... in contrast with rest" — so any required action (including baptism) gives ground for boasting and voids grace. [[Joe Heschmeyer]] counters, drawing on the **New Perspective on Paul** ([[James Dunn]], Matthew J. Thomas's *Paul's Works of the Law in the Perspective of Second-Century Reception*), that Paul's polemical "works of the law" denotes **Mosaic boundary markers** — chiefly circumcision, food laws, Sabbaths — that separated Jew from Gentile, *not* human obedience as such. Hence Paul can in the same breath rail against "works of the law" and command "good works."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Ephesians 2:9-10 distinguishes two senses of "works" (rating: strong; textual).** Heschmeyer: "not because of works, lest any man should boast" (v. 9) is immediately followed by "we are his workmanship created in Christ Jesus for good works" (v. 10). Same word, opposite valence — so "works" in the boasting sense ≠ "good works."[^1]
2. **Romans 4's "works" are circumcision-bounded (rating: moderate; contextual).** When Paul says "to the one who does not work but trusts him who justifies the ungodly" (Rom 4:5), Heschmeyer argues v. 9's contrast of "the circumcised and uncircumcised" shows the works in view are *Mosaic* — "anytime Paul talks about works that are only available to the Jews and not the Gentiles, he's only referring to the Mosaic works like circumcision."[^2]
3. **Romans 2 vs Romans 3 (rating: moderate; intra-Pauline).** Romans 2:6-7 has God rendering "to every man according to his works" and giving "eternal life" to those who "by patience in well-doing seek for glory"; Romans 3 denies "works of the law" justify. The two are reconciled only if "works" is used in different senses — Heschmeyer's whole point.[^3]
4. **Second Peter warns against twisting Paul (rating: moderate; rhetorical).** Heschmeyer frames the faith-alone reading as the very misreading 2 Peter 3:16 warns of — "the ignorant and unstable twist [Paul's hard sayings] to their own destruction."[^3]

## Apologetic Engagement
This is the load-bearing exegetical disagreement, and it is genuinely contested scholarship. Heschmeyer's New-Perspective reading (Dunn, Sanders, Wright; here also Thomas's reception-history work) is mainstream and well-evidenced for "works of the law" as covenant identity markers — and it neatly dissolves the Ephesians 2:9/2:10 tension and the Romans 2/3 tension. But it is not uncontested: many Reformed exegetes (and the "Old Perspective") read "works" more broadly as any human doing/merit, citing Romans 4:4-5's wage/gift contrast and Romans 11:6 ("if by grace, then not of works"). So Ryan is defending a respectable traditional reading, not an idiosyncrasy — though Heschmeyer is right that Ryan's *flat* equation ("works = any action, opposite of rest") struggles with Paul's positive uses of "works." The strongest neutral observation: "works" in Paul is demonstrably *not* univocal, which by itself undercuts any argument (like Ryan's) that treats every *ergon* identically. Whether the boundary-marker sense is the *only* polemical sense is the open question. Catholic/New-Perspective vs Reformed dispute.

## LDS Relevance
The Latter-day Saint reading of "works" aligns more with Heschmeyer than with Ryan: the New Testament's rejection of justification "by the works of the law" is read as targeting the *Mosaic* law (circumcision, sacrifices) fulfilled in Christ, not as excluding gospel obedience and ordinances — which the Restoration affirms are necessary (Articles of Faith 1:3-4; James 2 read straightforwardly). The LDS both/and ("by grace... after all we can do," 2 Nephi 25:23) presupposes exactly the distinction Heschmeyer draws: covenant obedience and ordinances (baptism) are the form faith takes, not boast-worthy "works of the law." This concept thus underwrites the LDS rejection of *sola fide* while honoring grace — see [[Baptism Is Gods Work Not a Human Work That Earns Salvation]].

## Best Quotes
> "It's consistent for Paul to at one and the same time commend good works and rail against works of the law... the same word can mean two different things." (Heschmeyer, summarizing James Dunn)[^1]

> "Works of the law, as Dunn explains, primarily points out to legalistic observances like circumcision, not to what we would normally call good works." (Heschmeyer)[^2]
