# Thecla's Intercession Saved the Pagan Falconilla

## The Point
The *Acts of Paul and Thecla* (c. AD 160–180) gave early Christians — especially women — a popular **model of intercessory prayer answered on behalf of the pagan dead**. [[Thecla]], at Queen Tryphaena's request, prays for the queen's dead daughter Falconilla, who is then "received into heaven by miraculous mercy." Though the text was "not held as historically precise," it "lived and breathed in the lives of early Christians" as an exemplary text of efficacious work for the dead.

## Evidence
1. **Falconilla's dream-request.** The dead daughter appears to her mother: "Mother, receive this stranger, the forsaken Thecla, in my place, that she may pray for me and I may come to the place of the just" [^1].
2. **Thecla's prayer.** Without hesitation Thecla prays: "My God, Son of the Most High, who are in heaven, grant her wish … that her daughter Falconilla may live in eternity" [^1].
3. **The outcome and confession.** After Thecla's miraculous survival of the beasts, Tryphaena proclaims, "Now I believe that the dead are raised!" and "Falconilla is then received into heaven by miraculous mercy because Thecla called upon her God" [^2].
4. **Function in the community.** Though fantastic, the account "offered a model of intercessory prayer answered on behalf of a pagan woman" and functioned as "an exemplary text of faithful action," especially among women [^2].
5. **Chrysostom's generous theology.** While [[Augustine]] restricted who could benefit from prayers, [[John Chrysostom]] "is more generous … about the kind of work that Christians could do for their kin, believers or not," envisioning the world of the dead as the jurisdiction of Jesus rather than the church [^3].

## Best Quotes
> "Mother, receive this stranger, the forsaken Thecla, in my place, that she may pray for me and I may come to the place of the just." [^1]

> "The account of Thecla … offered a model of intercessory prayer answered on behalf of a pagan woman." [^2]

## LDS Connection
A widely-loved early Christian narrative in which a living believer's prayer secures the *posthumous salvation of a non-Christian*. This dovetails with the LDS conviction that salvation is offered to those who died without the gospel, and that the living can act efficaciously on their behalf — Chrysostom's "more generous" view (Jesus, not the church, governs the dead) is especially congenial to Restoration soteriology.
