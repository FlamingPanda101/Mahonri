# God Can Lead People Through Other Religions

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] argues that Latter-day Saints can affirm what most evangelicals cannot: **God can genuinely work, lead, and perform miracles through people in other religions.** When a Protestant says "the Spirit led me here," the LDS reply is not to deny the experience but to ask: "Can God lead someone through a different religion to truth? Yes, he can" — even if that doesn't (yet) make them a Latter-day Saint.[^1] This contrasts with the evangelical "it's the devil" reflex toward LDS spiritual experiences.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Reflects genuine LDS inclusivist theology — rate: strong
This accurately represents LDS teaching that "the light of Christ" is given to all and that good and truth in other faiths can be divinely sourced (cf. Alma 29:8, "the Lord doth grant unto all nations... to teach his word"; the 1978 First Presidency statement on great religious leaders receiving "a portion of God's light"). It is a *non-zero-sum* soteriology. **Steelman of the evangelical:** exclusivist Protestants ground their "it could be deceptive" caution in texts like [[Matthew 10.34-36|Matthew 7:21-23]] (false miracle-workers), [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]] ("another gospel"), and 2 Corinthians 11:14 (Satan as an angel of light) — so their suspicion of rival spiritual experiences is principled, not merely insecure. Carroll's framing ("we are not that insecure") is rhetorically loaded; the evangelical position is a coherent consequence of exclusivism, not necessarily fear.

### 2. Exposes the asymmetry / double standard — rate: strong
Carroll's sharper point is structural: he affirms God can help Protestants perform miracles, but "you would be hard-pressed to find a Protestant who would say the same about us" — because the "I'm right so you're wrong" + "any miracle against my worldview is demonic" syllogism forces them to attribute LDS spiritual fruit to Satan.[^3] See [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]]. He notes "that's what the Pharisees said about Jesus" (attributing his works to Beelzebul).[^4] This is a genuine asymmetry: LDS inclusivism can accommodate Protestant grace; Protestant exclusivism structurally cannot reciprocate.

### 3. Practical de-escalation tool — rate: moderate
The pastoral payoff: when a Protestant invokes a spiritual experience, the apologist need not attack it ("I don't want to say 'the spirit, you know'") — "maybe they did" receive real guidance.[^5] This keeps the conversation charitable and consistent with [[Spirit Over Argument - The Limits of Apologetics]]. **Caution:** taken far enough, affirming "God led you to your current religion" can blunt the missionary claim that the *restored* gospel is uniquely necessary; Carroll holds the tension by saying other-faith guidance is real but may be a *stage* ("what if they needed to be in that experience?"), not a terminus.

## Best Quotes
> "Can God lead someone through a different religion to truth? Yes, he can... But what if they needed to be in that experience?"[^1]

> "Do you think God can help perform miracles with Protestants? Absolutely. You would be hard-pressed to find a Protestant who would say the same about us."[^3]

> "We are not that insecure about our worldview... God can actually help other people in other religions."[^6]

> "That's what the Pharisees said about Jesus anyway. And then they killed him."[^4]
