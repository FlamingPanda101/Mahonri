# Faith Alone Appears Once in Scripture and Is the Verse That Denies It

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] presses a simple structural argument: "the single most important doctrine in Protestant Christianity... appears exactly only one time in the entire Bible, and the one time that it appears, the Bible is explicitly rejecting it."[^1] The phrase is "faith only / faith alone"; the verse is [[James 2.24|James 2:24]] — "a man is justified by works, and **not by faith only**." "The only time that that phrase shows up in all of scripture, scripture is saying, 'No.' ... just no."[^1] [[James 2.14-26|James 2]] is a sustained argument that bare mental assent is not saving faith: "even the demons believe and shudder" ([[James 2.19|2:19]]) — so if intellectual agreement saved, "the demons are saved"; "faith was completed by his works" (2:22); and "as the body apart from the spirit is dead, so also faith apart from works is dead" (2:26) — a corpse, not living faith in hiding.[^2] Arnold's punchline targets the Protestant authority structure: *sola scriptura* says the Bible alone is the rule of faith, "but *sola fide* requires dismissing the most explicit passage in the New Testament on the subject... That's not *sola scriptura*. That's selective scriptura."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The lexical fact is correct.** The Greek phrase *pisteōs monon* ("faith only") occurs in the New Testament uniquely at [[James 2.24|James 2:24]], and it appears inside a negation. *Rating: accurate; this is the rhetorical core and it holds.* [^1]
2. **James 2 is genuinely about dead vs. living faith.** [[James 2.19|2:19]] ("the demons believe and shudder"), 2:22 ("faith completed by works"), 2:26 ("faith without works is dead") form a coherent argument that assent alone does not justify — not an isolated proof-text. *Rating: strong reading of the passage in context.* [^2]
3. **Luther's discomfort corroborates the tension.** That Luther called James "an epistle of straw" and sought to relocate it ([[Luther and the Epistle of Straw]]) is evidence that the *sola fide* tradition itself felt the force of this verse. *Rating: a telling internal admission.* [^2]
4. **The consistency charge bites.** A "Bible alone" authority that must explain away the one verse using its slogan, plus [[Matthew 25.31-46|Matthew 25]], [[Matthew 7.21|Matthew 7]], and [[Romans 2.6-7|Romans 2]], is operating selectively. See [[The Prooftext Double Standard on Grace and Works]]. *Rating: a sharp tu-quoque against the Protestant method.* [^3]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** The Protestant reply is well developed and does not simply ignore James: (a) **James and Paul use "justify" differently** — Paul (Rom 3–4) speaks of justification *before God* (forensic, by faith), while James speaks of justification *before men / demonstration* ("show me thy faith," 2:18) or of the *vindication* of faith's genuineness; "justified by works" = "shown to be righteous," not "earned righteousness," so James 2:24 and *sola fide* are about different questions — see the rebuttal-of-the-rebuttal in [[James - Justification Before Men Reply Rebutted]]; (b) **the "faith" James condemns is not saving faith** — it is the *demons'* bare assent (*nuda fides*), whereas Reformation *fides* is *fiducia* (trust) that necessarily produces works, so "faith alone, but the faith that justifies is never alone" (Westminster) — see [[Faith Alone but Never Alone]] and [[James - Different Kind of Faith Reply Rebutted]]; (c) Abraham in James 2 is "justified by works" *years after* Genesis 15:6 already credited his faith as righteousness, suggesting James means a later vindication, not initial justification. A Latter-day Saint / Catholic reply: these moves are exactly what Arnold and the vault's Catholic cluster contest — James says "a man is justified *by* works," not "a justified man *does* works"; the "before men" reading strains the text (Abraham's offering of Isaac was witnessed by no one but God); and even on the "faith is never alone" view, the dispute reduces to whether works are *constitutive of* or merely *consequent to* justification — which the single explicit verse resolves against the bare-*sola* formula. The narrow point stands: you cannot build a primary doctrine on a phrase and then treat the only verse that uses it as an unfortunate misunderstanding.
