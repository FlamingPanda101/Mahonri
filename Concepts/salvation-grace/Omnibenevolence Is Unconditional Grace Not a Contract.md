# Omnibenevolence Is Unconditional Grace Not a Contract

## The Claim
[[Gavin (Catholic Debater)]] argues a truly omnibenevolent God's love must be *non-contractual*. Christ "died for our sins while we were sinners" ([[Romans 5.8|Rom 5:8]]) — not after repentance, not after covenant obedience, not after meeting worthiness criteria, and "definitely not after all we can do." A God who withholds love "until after all we can do is not omnibenevolent; he is contractual. The triune God is not contractual."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **[[Romans 5.8|Rom 5:8]] timing argument** — rate: strong as exegesis of grace's priority. The cross precedes human merit; benevolence is therefore antecedent. *(scriptural)* [^1]
2. **The "all we can do" jab** — a pointed allusion to 2 Nephi 25:23 ("after all we can do"), framing LDS soteriology as the contractual foil. *(polemical)* [^2] This is an *inter-Christian* contrast, not an argument for the resolution per se. **Steelman of the LDS reply:** "after all we can do" is widely read by LDS scholars as grace-saturated ("we are saved by grace, *after* — i.e., notwithstanding — all we can do"), so the contrast may be a strawman of LDS grace theology.
3. **Downward access** — flows into [[Omnibenevolence vs Conditional Access Models]]: grace is "given downward, not earned upward." *(theological)* [^2]

**Hayden's relevance objection (steelman):** even a perfectly unconditional, non-contractual grace does not touch the creation dilemma. The damned receive that grace too, yet are foreknown to reject it and were created anyway. Unconditional grace toward someone you knowingly created for ruin still fails to *will their good*. See [[The Creation Dilemma]].

## Best Quotes
> "A God who must withhold his love until after all we can do is not omnibenevolent. He is contractual. The triune God is not contractual."[^1]

> "He died for us not after repentance. He didn't die for us after covenant obedience... and definitely he did not die for us after all we can do, but while we were still in sin."[^2]
