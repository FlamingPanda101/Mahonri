# Maladaptive Perfectionism Leaves Mercy Out of Becoming Perfect

## The Claim
Dr. [[Kawika Allen]] distinguishes **adaptive** from **maladaptive** perfectionism, and locates the difference *not* in the standards but in the response to falling short. Both types hold high standards and strive hard. The **adaptive** person who misses the mark says "okay, I'll try again tomorrow — it's not the end of the world." The **maladaptive** person *catastrophizes*: "I'm no nothing because I didn't meet those expectations." Applied to "be ye therefore perfect" ([[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]]), maladaptive perfectionism reads the commandment while editing out "the plan of mercy and compassion ... on the journey of becoming perfect" — demanding that one already *be* perfect to merit grace.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The difference is the response, not the standard (rated: strong; matches the perfectionism literature).** "The high standards [are] not the problem ... the issue is how you respond to them." Adaptive perfectionists "still have those high standards ... and then when they fail ... 'Okay, I'll try again tomorrow ... I'll pick myself up.'" Maladaptive perfectionists "crumble, fall apart ... catastrophize ... 'now I'm just terrible, miserable, nothing.'"[^1] *Rating:* this mirrors the standard adaptive/maladaptive perfectionism distinction (e.g., Frost, Hewitt & Flett) in the clinical literature.

2. **Mercy edited out of "becoming perfect" (rated: strong; the theological core).** "Part of that process of becoming perfect is — there's a plan of mercy and compassion on the journey ... They take that part out: 'I've got to be as perfect as possible, or else I don't get the grace of Christ.'"[^2] The error is treating grace as the *prize for* perfection rather than the *means of* becoming perfect. See [[Legalism Tries to Earn Gods Love Through Works Alone]].

3. **"Perfect" is mistranslated as flawless (rated: moderate; lexical support nearby).** Allen and the host note that the Greek *teleios* in [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]] connotes *complete/whole/mature*, not error-free — Sister Johnson's "we can be whole now." Misreading "perfect" as "never failing" is itself a catalyst for the disorder. (Cf. the temple sense of *teleios* as "initiate/endowed," [[Teleios Means Initiate - You Will Be Endowed Not Be Flawless]].)[^3]

4. **Catastrophizing fuses with identity (rated: strong; links to shame).** The collapse — "I'm just no good now" — is not a judgment about a behavior but about the self, the move from corrective guilt into identity-shame. See [[Guilt Is Corrective but Shame Attacks Identity]] and [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]].[^4]

5. **Flexibility, not lower standards, is the cure (rated: strong; with an athletic illustration).** Allen cites research on athletes: "if you show a little bit of flexibility in your thinking and in your workouts, you're much more prepared with less anxiety." **Psychological flexibility** — being "okay with sometimes being imperfect" — outperforms rigid perfection, in sport and in spirituality alike.[^5]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A worry: emphasizing "it's okay to be imperfect" could be heard as lowering the bar or excusing complacency ("everybody makes mistakes" weaponized into "nothing matters"). Allen names this exact failure mode — the *opposite* extreme of using "everybody sucks" as license — and insists the standards stay high ("we should always use the high expectations"); only the *catastrophic response* changes. The defensible position is a both/and: keep striving (adaptive perfectionism) while anchoring worth in grace, not performance, so that failure prompts "try again tomorrow" rather than "I am nothing."

## Best Quotes
> "The high standards [are] not the problem ... the issue is how you respond to them ... the maladaptive folks respond very debilitating ... 'I'm just no good now.'"[^1]

> "There's a plan of mercy and compassion on the journey of becoming perfect. They take that part out: 'I've got to really be as perfect as possible, or else I don't get the grace of Christ.'"[^2]

> "I'm okay being imperfect sometimes. And that's who we are — we're imperfect. It's okay."[^1]
