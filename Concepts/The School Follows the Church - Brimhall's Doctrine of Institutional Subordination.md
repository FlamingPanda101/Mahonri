# The School Follows the Church - Brimhall's Doctrine of Institutional Subordination

#type/claim

## The Claim

The most quotable institutional legacy of the 1911 BYU evolution crisis is the slogan BYU President [[George Brimhall]] coined to close it out: **"the school follows the church."** Historian [[Benjamin Park]] presents this as the founding articulation of a durable principle — that at BYU there is an **authoritative, unbreakable link** subordinating what may be taught to ecclesiastical authority. When secular learning (here, organic evolution) is judged to cut into core doctrine, the academic mission yields. Park argues this principle, established in the firings of [[Ralph Chamberlain]] and the others, still governs and explains recent Provo disputes over "secular truths being prioritized over doctrinal ideas." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The slogan was a deliberate disciplinary act.** After the professors were removed, Brimhall called a student assembly, chastised the students who had petitioned on the professors' behalf, and announced the new slogan as policy: "there is going to be no breaking of this authoritative link over what can and cannot be taught." [^1]

2. **It crystallized a posture already driving the crisis.** President [[Joseph F. Smith]]'s paradoxical stance — championing Americanization while insisting core doctrines could not be surrendered — set the prior. The [[Horace Cummings]] report's alarm at students "wholesale" adopting evolution, and the apostolic committee's ultimatum, were all expressions of the same conviction that doctrinal authority outranks academic autonomy (see [[The 1911 BYU Evolution Controversy and the Firing of Four Professors]]). [^2]

3. **The principle reproduced a recurring cycle.** Park describes BYU repeatedly being granted autonomy and hiring external scholars, then undergoing retrenchment — the "school follows the church" link reasserting itself each decade. The 1909 statement functioned similarly: a flexible doctrinal boundary that could be tightened or loosened depending on who was reading it (see [[The 1909 Origin of Man Read in Its Historical Context]]). [^3]

4. **The subordination did not freeze the boundary forever.** Park's irony is that the same authoritative link later *permitted* evolution: once BYU matured into a respected institution, leaders reinterpreted the 1909 statement to allow academic study, and BYU built a world-leading evolutionary-biology program (see [[Evolutionary Biology at BYU Is an Academic Success Story]]). The link is to *the church's* judgment, not to any fixed scientific verdict — so the permissible content shifted as leadership's posture shifted. [^4]

5. **Park frames it as live, not antiquarian.** The explicit thesis of the video is that "many of those key tensions still remain as seen in recent disputes... over the teaching of secular truths being prioritized over doctrinal ideas" — i.e., the 1911 principle is the template for present-day BYU controversies. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Brimhall announced a whole new slogan for the church from that point on: the school follows the church. In other words, there is going to be no breaking of this authoritative link over what can and cannot be taught." [^1]

> "Many of those key tensions still remain as seen in recent disputes... over the teaching of secular truths being prioritized over doctrinal ideas." [^5]
