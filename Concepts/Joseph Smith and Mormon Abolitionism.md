# Joseph Smith and Mormon Abolitionism

## The Claim
Against the charge that Mormonism is inherently racist, the panel argues that early Latter-day Saints were on the *progressive* edge of 19th-century abolition — to the point that their anti-slavery stance fueled the violent persecution they suffered in Missouri.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Joseph Smith ran as an abolitionist presidential candidate (rated: strong, historical).** [[Jacob Hansen]]: "Joseph Smith was the first abolitionist candidate for the US presidency."[^1] Smith's 1844 platform proposed compensated emancipation funded by the sale of public lands.

2. **Orson/Parley Pratt's abolitionism provoked the Missouri persecution (rated: moderate, historical).** [[Jasmine Rapley]]: it was "not just Joseph Smith, but Orson Pratt's very strident abolitionism" that, "in small part at least," caused the intense Missouri persecution — "people wanted to kill the Mormons because they thought we wanted to free the slaves." (The panel slips between "Orson" and "Parley" Pratt; the broader point is that Latter-day Saint anti-slavery rhetoric in a slave-bordering state was incendiary.)[^2]

3. **"Abolitionist" carried a radical, violent connotation (rated: moderate, contextual).** [[Cardon Ellis]] notes that in the period, "abolitionist" had a radical tinge — associated with uprisings in which "women and children were killed" — so even *emancipationists* distanced themselves from "abolitionists," much as a modern liberal might distance from "progressive." This complicates the modern instinct to read 19th-century LDS caution about the *abolitionist label* as racism.[^3] *Steelman:* this contextualizing can shade into apologetic excuse-making — some early LDS statements (and Joseph Smith's own earlier ambivalence) were genuinely conciliatory toward slaveholders, and Brigham Young later legalized slavery in Utah (see [[Brigham Youngs Slavery Law as a Transition to Freedom]]). The honest picture is a *trajectory*, not uniform abolitionism.

4. **Racial categories were imported, not native (rated: moderate, linguistic).** Ellis: heinous racial attitudes solidified only when "Christians started trying to justify that view through scripture," using Blumenbach-derived color categories that postdate the relevant texts.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "Joseph Smith was the first abolitionist candidate for the US presidency."[^1]

> "People wanted to kill the Mormons because they thought we wanted to free the slaves, because Orson Pratt was just really inflammatory in how he was going about those speeches."[^2]

> "He actually had a beautiful vision of what Zion was and what a child of God actually meant."[^4]
