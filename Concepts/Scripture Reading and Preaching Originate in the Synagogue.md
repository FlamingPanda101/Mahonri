# Scripture Reading and Preaching Originate in the Synagogue

## The Point
Heal locates the origin of the two-part liturgical structure — (1) reading scripture, then (2) preaching on it — in the Jewish synagogue, of which Luke 4:16–22 is the paradigmatic example. Early Christian worship inherited and continued this synagogue pattern rather than inventing it.

## Evidence
1. **Origin claim.** "The combination of (1) reading and (2) preaching the scriptures finds its origins in the Jewish synagogue." Heal cites Alistair Stewart-Sykes, *From Prophecy to Preaching*, for the search for the homily's origins. [^1]
2. **The Nazareth template.** Luke records Jesus standing to read from the scroll of Isaiah, closing the book and sitting, "and the eyes of all in the synagogue were fixed on him," then declaring, "Today this scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing" (Luke 4:16–22). The interplay of reading then expounding is the visible synagogue pattern. [^1]
2. **Continuity into Christianity.** "In both Judaism and early Christianity, the pattern of reading the scriptures as part of the weekly worship service quickly became established." [^2]
3. **Justin's witness confirms the order.** [[Justin Martyr]]'s description (c. AD 150) of the Sunday assembly shows the same sequence: "the memoirs of the Apostles or the writings of the Prophets are read … Then, when the reader has finished, the one presiding over the assembly verbally admonishes" — reading first, sermon after. [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "The combination of (1) reading and (2) preaching the scriptures finds its origins in the Jewish synagogue." [^1]

## LDS Connection
Heal's framing — that Christian worship is a *continuation* of Jewish synagogue practice rather than a Hellenistic novelty — supports the volume's larger thesis (see Combs's Introduction) that early Christianity grew organically out of its Jewish matrix rather than being corrupted from a pure origin.
