# The Joyce Yost Satanic Murder Tip Was Entirely False

## The Claim
A therapist-relayed "satanic ritual" account of a real murder, presented with vivid specifics, turned out to bear no relation to what actually happened — an early Utah case study in how therapeutic narratives can fabricate detailed but false crime stories.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The detailed satanic tip (strong — named investigator testimony)
After Joyce Yost of South Ogden vanished in 1985, a therapist called police claiming her patient said Yost's murder was a satanic ritual, supplying "places, dates, and disgusting details of how Yost was slowly and ritualistically killed." Police "snapped into action" [^1].

### 2. The actual confession contradicted everything (strong)
The killer eventually confessed, and the murder was "absolutely nothing like" the satanic account. "Nothing that was reported had been true" [^2].

### 3. The investigative cost (moderate — firsthand recollection)
Investigator Terry Christensen recalled the wasted effort chasing the false coven theory. See [[Terry Christensen]] [^3].

## Steelman (Opposing View)
One could argue this is a single anecdote and that a patient occasionally relaying a false rumor proves little about therapy generally. But the case foreshadows the pattern repeated systematically in the Lehi cases: a confident, detailed, therapy-sourced account with zero corroboration. Its value is illustrative, not statistical.

## Best Quotes
> "Eventually, the killer confessed to the murder and it was absolutely nothing like the police had been told by the therapist. Nothing that was reported had been true." [^2]

> "I can't tell you the hours that we put into trying to prove it or disprove it. She thinks Joyce Yost was killed by her dad and the coven and it just didn't happen." — [[Terry Christensen]] [^3]
