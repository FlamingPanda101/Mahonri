# Schellenberg's Hiddenness Argument and the Christian Reply

## The Point
**The argument (J. L. Schellenberg).** P1: if God exists, he is perfectly loving. P2: if a perfectly loving God exists, reasonable (non-resistant) nonbelief does not occur. P3: reasonable nonbelief occurs. Therefore no perfectly loving God exists — and hence no God.[^1] Theists grant P1; the contest is over P2 and P3. Schellenberg's driving claim is that a loving God owes each person *evidence sufficient at least to know he exists*, and that this can be done "without infringing moral freedom."[^2]

**The reply (Michael Jones).** The argument "assumes too little about humanity and so too much about the role of God."[^3] P2 quietly presumes that people who receive adequate evidence will believe — but human beings routinely reject evidence that cuts against what they *want* to be true. So P3 is doubtful too: we cannot establish that today's nonbelievers are genuinely *non-resistant* rather than motivated rejecters, which is precisely what the argument needs.[^4]

## Evidence
- **The hidden premise about rationality.** Schellenberg needs people to be the sort of reasoners who, given sufficient evidence, simply believe. Jones denies this from the psychology of motivated reasoning: "instead of changing our views to match the evidence we often dismiss the evidence to hold what we desire to be true."[^5]
- **Confessions of resistance.** Thomas Nagel concedes the existence of God poses a "cosmic authority problem" and openly hopes God does not exist; Aldous Huxley admitted the "philosophy of meaninglessness was essentially an instrument of liberation" — liberation from a system of morality that "interfered with our sexual freedom."[^6] If even philosophers admit they *want* atheism, P3's claim of widespread *inculpable* nonbelief is not safe.
- **Don't assume the conclusion.** To simply declare the cumulative case from natural theology "not enough" and build an argument on that is to assume what must be shown. The honest test is public discourse: can a non-theistic worldview account for the data of natural theology *more* plausibly? If not, the atheist is "not justified in saying the hiddenness of God is enough to suggest his non-existence."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "If a perfectly loving God exists, reasonable non-belief does not occur. Reasonable non-belief occurs. Therefore no perfectly loving God exists." [^1]

> "What we want to believe is what we often make our reality despite the evidence." [^5]

> "We cannot simply assume there is not enough evidence to infer God exists and therefore create an argument against theism from divine hiddenness — that would be assuming your conclusion." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The Restoration sharpens the reply by locating it in a *probationary mortal estate*. Mortality is by design a state in which the memory of pre-mortal life is withdrawn behind a veil of forgetfulness, so that faith is "exercised by choice" rather than compelled by sight (compare [[The Problem of Evil Does Not Disprove God]] for the parallel "defense vs. theodicy" logic). Latter-day Saints can grant Schellenberg his P1 wholeheartedly — God *is* perfectly loving — while denying P2: a love that respects agency does not overwhelm; it invites. And because agency is rooted in uncreated intelligence (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]), the resistance Jones diagnoses is not a flaw God authored but a genuine choice God honors. The deeper move beyond Jones: hiddenness is not merely *tolerated* by a loving God but is a *positive condition* of the test mortality was meant to be.
