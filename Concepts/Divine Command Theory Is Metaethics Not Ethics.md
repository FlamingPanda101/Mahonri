# Divine Command Theory Is Metaethics Not Ethics

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Calvinist (voiced as [[James White]]) claims to have *an objective basis for moral values and duties* because their conception of God *is* goodness, truth, light, and beauty — so God himself is the standard by which anything is judged good or bad. By contrast, the LDS God allegedly "is not the source of goodness" but a being who must *conform* to eternal laws; his Godhood consists in his obedience/alignment with laws he did not author. [^1] See [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The category error: divine command theory is metaethics, not ethics** — rate: strong, clarifying distinction. [[Blake Ostler]] notes that "metaethics is the theory of the ground of moral rules... and *not* a practical guide to what's right or wrong." White's appeal answers (at best) the *grounding* question while pretending to deliver moral *guidance*; the two are different jobs and conflating them is the first error. *(conceptual distinction)* [^2]
2. **Metaethics is absent from the Bible** — rate: strong as a historical point. "Metaethics are never addressed anywhere in the Bible... Christ is not teaching a metaethics or anything like it." The discipline "falls out much, much later, arising in Christianity after centuries." So the claim that *the biblical God* delivers a metaethic is anachronistic. *(historical)* [^3]
3. **It collapses into the Euthyphro dilemma** — rate: decisive (see [[The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory]]). Either goodness is independent of God's command (refuting divine command theory) or "good" just means "whatever God commands" (making morality arbitrary). *(philosophical)* [^4]
4. **The LDS rejoinder: God is not "subject to" an alien law** — rate: reframes the dichotomy. Ostler concedes "there is a law — it's the law of love," but love "is not independent of God" because "Godhood is totally dependent on God being loving... If he ceased to love, he wouldn't be God." Love inheres *in the relationship* between God and persons, dissolving the "source vs. subject" framing White assumes. *(theological)* [^5] See [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]].

**Steelman of divine command theory:** A sophisticated divine-command theorist (e.g., Robert Adams) does not say "good = whatever God arbitrarily commands," but identifies the *moral good* with the will of a God who is *essentially loving*; commands flow from an unchanging good nature, so the "arbitrariness" horn is blunted because God could not command cruelty without ceasing to be God. This is, notably, close to Ostler's own move — which is why Ostler's real complaint is less that grounding morality in God is impossible than that *Calvinism's* God (who decrees damnation) cannot be the loving God this theory needs.

## Best Quotes
> "The first is that what he adopts is divine command theory. And it's not an ethical theory, it's a metaethical theory. Metaethics is the theory of the ground of moral rules, or oughts... and not a practical guide to what's right or wrong." [^2]

> "It's not — metaethics are never addressed anywhere in the Bible. This is something that falls out much, much later... Certainly Christ is not teaching a metaethics or anything like it." [^3]

> "It's not independent of God because Godhood is totally dependent on God being loving... If he ceased to love, he wouldn't be God." [^5]
