# Ritual Has Been Normal for Four Thousand Years

#type/claim

## The Claim
The hosts' answer to the most common reaction to the temple — "this is weird" — is that the *weirdness is the critic's, not the rite's*. North American Christianity is a historical anomaly: the Protestant sects that "escaped Europe and thrived in North America" produced "a very heavily sterilized" Christianity in which "even baptism itself is kind of neutered," so encountering "actual rituals in Christianity" feels strange. [^1] Latter-day Saint temple worship, they argue, recovers "religious ritual that really Christianity hasn't seen since the 1st through 3rd centuries" — much of it still preserved in Orthodoxy and Catholicism. [^2] The endowment is **"learning through allegory... learning through symbolism,"** drawing on Old- and New-Testament types, which a non-ritual society is simply unpracticed at reading. [^3] Cardon Ellis presses the point: "humankind has embraced ritual for 4,000 years. It's only in the past 40 years post-war that we've kind of stopped... because what? We're too cool and we got Xanax now." American culture is "a society robbed of liturgy, robbed of ritual." [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The historical claim is largely correct.** Liturgical, sacramental, multisensory worship *was* the Christian norm for most of its history and survives in the Eastern churches and Catholicism; the stripped-down, sermon-centered, low-ceremony service is a distinctively post-Reformation, and especially American-revivalist, development. The vault documents the older pattern in [[Medieval Liturgy and Drama Enacted Divine Presence]] and [[Monumental Basilicas as the New Temples of Christianity]]. *Rating: historically sound.*

2. **"Weird" is a cultural judgment, not a theological one.** That a practice is unfamiliar to 21st-century American Protestants says nothing about whether it is biblical or ancient. Veils, anointing oil, sacred vestments, washings, and a holy of holies are *Old Testament temple furniture* (cf. [[Exodus 28.30|Exodus 28]], the priestly garments and breastplate). The move "it's weird → it's wrong" is a chronological/cultural prejudice. *Rating: a fair rebuttal of a weak criticism.*

3. **Ritual as formation, not superstition.** The hosts frame ritual as a *mode of learning and becoming* — consonant with [[Medieval Liturgy and Drama Enacted Divine Presence]] ("a performance is a mode of becoming") and with [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]]. This answers the Protestant suspicion that ritual is empty externalism by recasting it as embodied pedagogy. *Rating: a coherent theology of ritual.*

4. **It explains the convert's experience.** Michael Myrrono's own arc — raised evangelical, first finding the endowment "nonsensical and unchristian," later prizing it — is offered as a case study in the unfamiliarity-not-falsehood thesis.

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A Protestant critic can concede that ancient Christianity was ritual-rich and still object that *antiquity is not authority*: that a practice resembles 2nd-century or Old-Testament rites does not make it commanded for the Church, and the Reformers stripped ceremony precisely because they judged much of it accreted and unscriptural (the "regulative principle" — worship only what Scripture commands). "It's only weird because you're sterilized" can also cut both ways: an Orthodox or Catholic critic notes the LDS endowment is *not* in fact their preserved ancient liturgy but a 19th-century rite, so the "we recovered the ancient" claim still has to be argued, not assumed (see [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]], [[Endowment Elements Predate Joseph Smith's Freemasonry]]). A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument here is narrowly that the *category* of sacred ritual is normal, biblical, and ancient — disarming the cheap "weird" reflex — while the separate question of whether *this particular* rite is restored is handled on its own evidence elsewhere.
