# The Principle of Sufficient Reason

## The Point
The principle of sufficient reason (PSR) holds that everything that exists has an explanation — either in its own nature or in something outside it — so that there are no genuinely inexplicable "brute facts." [^1] PSR is the single load-bearing premise of Feser's "rationalist proof": its rationalism "consists merely in its commitment to the principle of sufficient reason," and does not require the controversial rationalist doctrines (innate ideas, all-knowledge-a-priori) historically defended by Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz. [^2] PSR is distinct from the principle of causality: a cause must be distinct from its effect, but a *sufficient reason* need not be — there could in principle be something self-explanatory; and whereas the principle of causality does not entail that everything has a cause, PSR does entail that everything has a reason. [^3]

## Evidence
Feser argues PSR is more certain than any empirical hypothesis, and defends it by *reductio*:
1. **Inductive support.** We do in fact find explanations when we look, and the world's pervasive intelligibility (which makes science and common sense successful) would be "a miracle if PSR were not true." [^4]
2. **The Pruss/Koons argument.** Denying PSR entails radical skepticism about perception: if PSR were false, there might be no connection at all between our experiences and external objects, and no grounds for calling such a disconnect improbable. [^5]
3. **Self-undermining (Feser's extension).** Whenever we accept a rationally justified claim, we assume our reason for it is also the *cause* of our accepting it, and that our faculties track truth. If PSR is false we could have no reason to think any of this holds — including any reason offered for *doubting* PSR. "To doubt or deny PSR undercuts any grounds we could have for doubting or denying PSR. The rejection of PSR is therefore self-undermining." [^6]
4. **Della Rocca's explicability arguments.** Philosophers routinely defend theses by "explicability arguments" (rejecting a state of affairs because it would be a brute fact). Della Rocca argues there is no non-question-begging way to accept such arguments generally while denying them only for the existence of things — so accepting explicability arguments at all commits one to PSR. [^7]

## Best Quotes
> "If it seems difficult to prove, that is not because it is doubtful, but on the contrary because it is more obviously true than anything that could be said either for or against it." [^8]

> "Hence, to doubt or deny PSR undercuts any grounds we could have for doubting or denying PSR. The rejection of PSR is therefore self-undermining... To reject PSR is to undermine the possibility of any rational inquiry." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Against atheism, PSR is a powerful ally: the demand that reality be intelligible "through and through" — that there be no brute facts — undergirds the cosmological inference from contingency to a necessary ground, which is congenial to any theism. Latter-day Saints affirm a deeply intelligible cosmos governed by eternal law (see [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]). The honest divergence is over *what* terminates explanation. For Feser, only one absolutely simple, purely actual being can be self-explanatory, so PSR drives to classical theism's God. The restored gospel locates ultimacy differently: matter, intelligence, and law are themselves eternal and uncreated (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]]), so the "self-explanatory" terminus is not the lone *actus purus* of Aquinas. A fair reading grants Feser that *something* must ground explanation; the dispute is whether that ground is the simple God of classical theism or includes co-eternal realities.
