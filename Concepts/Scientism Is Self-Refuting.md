# Scientism Is Self-Refuting

## The Point
Scientism is the claim that "we get all our truth from science" — that the scientific method is the only path to knowledge.[^1] Turek argues this is self-defeating, because the claim itself is not a scientific truth: "You can't do science to prove that. It's a philosophical claim."[^1] A statement that "only science yields knowledge" fails its own test, so it refutes itself the moment it is asserted. Worse, science cannot even justify *itself* by its own method, since it rests on unprovable philosophical assumptions.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The Craig–Atkins exchange.** When chemist Peter Atkins insisted in a 1998 debate that "science can account for everything," William Lane Craig listed five things that cannot be scientifically proven yet are rational to accept: (1) logical and mathematical truths (science presupposes them, so proving them by science argues in a circle); (2) metaphysical truths (other minds exist, the external world is real, the past was not created five minutes ago); (3) ethical truths (science can't show the Nazi camp doctors did anything evil); (4) aesthetic judgments; and (5) "most remarkably, science itself," which "cannot be justified by the scientific method, since it is permeated with unprovable assumptions."[^2] Craig's example: the special theory of relativity hinges on the one-way constancy of light's speed, which "strictly cannot be proven."[^2]
2. **Atkins had no answer.** Moderator William F. Buckley Jr. quipped, "So put that in your pipe and smoke it." Atkins "looked stunned... as if he had virtually no knowledge of philosophy or the fact that science is built on philosophical principles."[^3]
3. **The label and the refutation.** "His claim that 'science can account for everything' is another way of saying that we get all our truth from science, which is known as 'scientism.'... scientism is self-defeating because the claim 'we get all our truth from science' is not a scientific truth."[^1]
4. **Many roads to truth.** Turek lists other genuine sources of knowledge scientism ignores: self-evident philosophical principles (logic, math, basic morality), sense perception, introspection, the testimony of others, and divine revelation. "You don't need to run a scientific experiment to learn if there's a book in front of you!"[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "Scientism is self-defeating because the claim 'we get all our truth from science' is not a scientific truth. That truth doesn't come from science. It's a philosophical claim. You can't do science to prove that." [^1]

> "Science cannot be justified by the scientific method, since it is permeated with unprovable assumptions." — William Lane Craig [^2]

## LDS Connection
Restoration epistemology is squarely anti-scientism: it insists that truth reaches us through multiple, convergent channels — reason and study, sense and experience, the witness of others, and especially personal revelation ("by the power of the Holy Ghost ye may know the truth of all things," Moroni 10:5). Latter-day Saint scripture even commands believers to "seek learning, even by study and also by faith" (D&C 88:118), pairing empirical inquiry with revealed knowledge rather than collapsing all truth into one method. So the self-refutation of "only science gives knowledge" lands cleanly for an LDS reader, who already holds that the light of truth fills "all things" and is known in more ways than the laboratory ([[D&C 88]]; [[D&C 93]]). The structural move — exposing a worldview claim that cannot satisfy its own standard — parallels [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]].
