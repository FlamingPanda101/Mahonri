# Masoretic Vowels Reversed the Active Verb to Passive

## The Claim

The Hebrew Bible's consonantal text (which is older than the vowel points) presents the pilgrimage verb *ra'ah* in the **active** voice — "you will see [God's face]" — but the [[Masoretes|Masoretes]], the medieval scribes who added the vowel pointing, supplied vowels that change it to the **passive** ("be seen / appear"). [[Dan McClellan]] argues this is a deliberate vocalization choice motivated by theological discomfort with the idea that humans could see God's face, and that modern translations inherit and entrench the Masoretic passive.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Consonants and vowels are separate layers of the text.** Biblical Hebrew was written consonant-only; the Tiberian Masoretes added vowel points (~7th–10th c. CE) representing a later reading tradition. Where the consonants are ambiguous, the vowels encode an interpretive decision. McClellan: the consonants "want you to understand this in the active," but "the vowels have altered it to make it passive."[^2] *(Evidence rating: strong — this is standard textual-criticism background.)*

2. **The Qal/Niphal ambiguity in the finite forms.** For the finite verb, active (Qal *tir'eh*, "you will see") and passive (Niphal *tera'eh*, "be seen / appear") can share the same consonants. McClellan concedes the finite verses "could go either way" on the consonants alone, which is *why* the Masoretic vowel choice is decisive there.[^3] *(Evidence rating: this concession is intellectually honest and locates the real weight on Isaiah.)*

3. **The motive is theological.** The passive is preferred "because people are uncomfortable with this idea that you could see God's face because you're not supposed to be able to see God's face" — the tradition from [[Exodus 33.20|Exodus 33:20]] that "no one can see me and live."[^4] *(Evidence rating: reasonable inference; the conflicting traditions in the text — see [[Conflicting Traditions on Seeing Gods Face]] — make the motive plausible but not provable.)*

4. **Modern translations follow the Masoretic vowels.** McClellan: none of the translations he checked render "the face of God"; "the Masoretic tradition vocalizes it that way, and so you're kind of overturning what the Masoretes wrote when you translate it differently."[^5] *(Evidence rating: verifiable — standard versions read "appear before.")*

**Steelman of the traditional reading:** The Masoretic reading tradition is an ancient, carefully transmitted witness, not an arbitrary medieval invention; the vocalizers may have preserved the original intended sense. Moreover, the Niphal of *ra'ah* with this idiom ("present oneself / appear before") is well attested elsewhere, so the passive is not strained Hebrew. The decisive rebuttal is the infinitive construct in Isaiah, where the active and passive do *not* share consonants and the consonants are active — see [[Infinitive Construct in Isaiah Proves Active Reading]].

## Best Quotes

> "The consonants of the Hebrew... have it in the active. You will see the face of God. The vowels have altered it to make it passive."[^2]

> "It always gets translated in a passive because people are uncomfortable with this idea that you could see God's face because you're not supposed to be able to see God's face."[^4]

> "The Masoretic tradition vocalizes it that way. And so you're kind of overturning what the Masoretes wrote when you translate it differently. So not a lot of folks are doing that."[^5]
