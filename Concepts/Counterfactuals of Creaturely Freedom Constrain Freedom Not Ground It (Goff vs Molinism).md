# Counterfactuals of Creaturely Freedom Constrain Freedom Not Ground It (Goff vs Molinism)

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Philip Goff]] presses a freedom objection against [[William Lane Craig]]'s Molinist theodicy that he is careful to distinguish from the familiar **grounding objection**. The Molinist holds that for every free creature there is, "in fact for billions of years," a fact of the matter about what that creature *would* freely do in any circumstance — God's *middle knowledge* of true counterfactuals of creaturely freedom, used to plan creation. Goff's complaint: if "there is a fact of the matter independent of my choice about what I would do... the matter's settled independent of my choice," so "I'm not really free."[^1] The objection is not "what makes these conditionals true?" (grounding) but "if their truth is **explanatorily prior to my choice**, my choice is constrained." Goff claims Craig's well-known 1990s reply works against grounding but is defeated by recent work on the nature of explanatory priority.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Independence-from-choice is the crux.** Goff isolates *explanatory* independence: for the Molinist, what I would do "is not grounded in / not explained by my choice. If anything, it's prior to my choice. It goes back, it informed God's creation plan that put me in the situation where I've got this choice in the first place."[^2] He contrasts this with a *growing-block/eternalist* future, where the future fact about my choice is "grounded in my future choice" and so is compatible with freedom. The killer-or-conscience example makes it vivid: the Molinist affirms strong libertarian freedom ("totally up to me") yet also that "prior to my choice... there is a fact of the matter... set in stone completely independent of my choice."[^3]
- **The technical move Goff leans on.** He explicitly grants future contingents can be true, then argues the damage is done by *priority*, not truth: there is now "a broadly accepted understanding of explanatory priority... involving what we call directed acyclic graphs" that is "cross-categorial," "asymmetric," and "transitive," and that "avoids the response Bill made in the '90s." He names a paper "in mind by Rubio and Climenhaga" responding to Craig.[^4] Rate: **a substantive, literature-anchored push** — Goff is not re-running a tired objection but claiming the formal account of explanatory priority has moved since Craig last addressed it.
- **Craig's reply.** Craig first tries to assimilate it: "This is the so-called grounding objection." Goff: "No, I don't think it is... I didn't say anything about what grounds these truths. I said that they *constrain* our freedom. It's a different objection."[^5] Craig then denies the constraint outright: counterfactuals of creaturely freedom "clearly don't constrain our freedom because we have the ability to actualize the... consequence of those counterfactuals... there's no causal constraint," and a true counterfactual ("if I were rich I would buy a Mercedes") no more constrains me than a true future-tense statement ("I will buy a Mercedes") that is already true prior to my choosing.[^6] He concedes he has not read the Rubio paper.[^7]
- **Steelman both sides.** *For Goff:* the distinction is real — Craig's "no causal constraint" answer addresses *causal* determination, whereas Goff's worry is *explanatory* priority, a weaker-than-causal but still freedom-undermining relation; if the asymmetric/transitive account of explanatory priority is correct, a fact explanatorily prior to and independent of my choice does look like it settles the matter over my head. *For Craig:* the symmetry argument is strong — if a true future contingent ("I *will* φ") is compatible with my freely φ-ing, it is unclear why a true subjunctive ("I *would* φ") is not; and the Molinist insists the *only* reason the counterfactual is true is *that* I would freely so choose, which is precisely freedom-preserving. The exchange ends unresolved, with Craig flatly "I just fundamentally disagree" and Goff inviting him to engage the post-1990s literature — "that may be a conversation for another day."

## LDS Relevance
This is an intramural classical-theist dispute (Goff and Craig both want libertarian freedom), but it bears directly on the Latter-day Saint case against an unconstrained, exhaustively-foreknowing God. The vault already argues Molinism is an unstable fix — [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]] and [[Foreknowledge and Free Will Are Logically Incompatible]] — and Goff's "explanatory priority constrains freedom" is a fresh, technically updated reinforcement of that conclusion from a non-LDS philosopher: middle knowledge buys God's providential control only by positing facts about my choices that are *prior to and independent of* those choices. The Restoration relocates the whole problem. Because part of every person is **uncreated and eternally agentive** (D&C 93; see [[Co-Eternality Solves the Problem of Evil]]), the LDS God does not author a creation plan keyed to pre-set counterfactuals of *creatures he made from nothing* — agency is primeval, not a divine design parameter — so the very structure Goff attacks (God arranging history by exploiting fixed truths about manufactured wills) is not the LDS picture. Goff reaches limitation in God to escape evil; the Restoration reaches *co-eternal, self-existent agents* to the same end, and along the way dissolves precisely the Molinist machinery Goff says constrains freedom. His objection is thus a useful borrowed weapon: a secular analytic philosopher independently judging that middle knowledge cannot have it both ways.

## Best Quotes
> "There is a fact of the matter, set in stone, explanatorily prior to my choice about what I would do in those circumstances. That does constrain my freedom." [^3]

> "I didn't say anything about what grounds these truths. I said that they constrain our freedom. It's a different objection." [^5]

> "There's been a lot of work on the nature of explanatory priority... a broadly accepted understanding... involving what we call directed acyclic graphs — that is cross-categorial, is asymmetric, is transitive, and avoids the response Bill made in the '90s." [^4]

> (Craig) "I just fundamentally disagree. I don't think [there's] any good argument to think that the truth of counterfactuals of creaturely freedom... constrains my freedom any more than saying that a future contingent proposition... constrains my freedom because it's true prior to my making that choice." [^6]
