# You Can Only Follow Jesus by Joining the Church He Commands
#type/claim

## The Claim
Answering the student Nick's "spiritual but not religious" move — *can I follow Jesus while rejecting organized religion?* — [[Hayden Carroll]] argues that **if** Jesus has commanded His followers to join a specific church, then "following Jesus" *entails* joining that church, because to follow Jesus is to do what He says. The anti-institutional stance is therefore not a neutral way of keeping Jesus; it is, on its own terms, disobedience to Him *if* He has in fact reestablished His church. The argument's hinge is evidential: the Book of Mormon and living prophets are offered as evidence that God *has* reestablished His church with present-day authority, which converts the abstract "follow Jesus" into the concrete "join the fold." Nick concedes the logic is "consistent."

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Following = obeying.** The core move is definitional: a "follower of Jesus" who declines what Jesus commands is not, in the relevant sense, following Him. "Why call ye me, Lord, Lord, and do not the things which I say?" (Luke 6:46). So one cannot coherently keep Jesus-as-Lord while categorically rejecting an institution He has commanded.
2. **Jesus founded and commanded a church.** Christ organized a church with authority and ordinances (apostles, baptism, the sacrament) and tied discipleship to it — "If thou wilt enter into life, keep the commandments"; baptism as a command (John 3:5; Matthew 28:19-20). A purely private, churchless Christianity is thus not the Christianity Jesus actually taught.
3. **The conditional rests on restored authority.** Carroll does not claim *every* institution is binding; the claim is conditional on *which* church Jesus commands. He grounds that on the Book of Mormon as evidence that God has restored His church with living prophets — so the duty to "join the fold" follows only if that evidential premise holds (the divine-headship premise developed in [[A God-Headed Church Is Guaranteed Against Corruption by Divine Foreknowledge]]).
4. **It disarms the anti-institutional escape.** Nick's complaint is really about *corrupt* institutions; Carroll's reply separates "institutions are often corrupt" (granted, cf. [[Institutional Corruption Is Not Merely a Function of an Institution's Age]]) from "therefore Jesus wants no institution" (non sequitur if Jesus himself established one).

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Nick's position has real force: the New Testament's harshest words fall on religious *institutionalists* (the Pharisees), Jesus saved his sharpest praise for outsiders, and Paul says the body of Christ is constituted by faith and the Spirit, not membership in any one visible organization — so "follow Jesus" may be satisfied by faith, love, and obedience without subscribing to a particular church's authority claims. Many sincere Christians across history had no access to the LDS Church yet plainly followed Jesus. The whole argument also *presupposes* its conclusion: it only yields "join this church" if one already grants that this specific church is the one Jesus commands — which is exactly what the skeptic disputes. Carroll's rejoinder is that the conditional is honest (everything rides on the separately-argued restored-authority claim), not that the obligation is self-evident.
