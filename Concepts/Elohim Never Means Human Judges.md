# *Elohim* Never Means "Human Judges"

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The Hebrew word *elohim* in Psalm 82 (and in passages like [[Exodus 21.6|Exodus 21:6]]) refers to **human judges**, who are called "gods" because they represent God's authority.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The lexical claim: *elohim* is never used of human judges (strong)
**[[Dan McClellan]]**: "The word Elohim in the Hebrew Bible is never used to refer to human judges. That is a post-biblical renegotiation and it really starts popping up in the second and third centuries CE after the gospel of John was written." [^1] The "human judges" gloss is a later interpretive development, not the original meaning.

### 2. Its origin is traceable harmonization (moderate–strong)
McClellan reconstructs *how* the reading arose: it "is an outcome of looking at Exodus 21 and Exodus 22 and Exodus 18 and Genesis 6 and Psalm 82 and trying to figure out what's going on with all these Elohim running around doing weird things," at a time "when there is discomfort arising with references to other gods." [^2] Because [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 18 / 23]] presents Moses as a representative of God, interpreters reasoned: "Well, maybe these are human judges. They are called gods because they represent God." [^3] But "this is very clearly not what's going on in these passages. These are all references to gods or in the singular to God." [^4]

### 3. It is the academic consensus (strong)
"This is the overwhelming academic consensus. The judges interpretation of Elohim is entirely post-biblical... originates in second and 3rd century exegesis of these passages and has absolutely nothing to do with what Psalm 82 originally meant." [^5]

### 4. Steelman of the judges reading
The judges reading is not baseless: Hebrew rendering like [[Exodus 21.6|Exodus 21:6]] ("bring him to *ha-elohim*") were read as referring to a tribunal, and rabbinic and some Christian traditions extended this to Psalm 82. McClellan's reply is chronological and lexical: the *attested* judges-interpretation post-dates the biblical period (2nd–3rd c. CE), arises from discomfort with divine plurality, and never reflects how the term functions in its original contexts, which are populated by actual *elohim* — see [[Genesis 6.1-4|Genesis 6:1-4]] and [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]].
