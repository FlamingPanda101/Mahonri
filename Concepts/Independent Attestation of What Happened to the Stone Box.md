#type/claim

# Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box

## The Claim

Two sources report the same obscure local fact — that the stone box eventually **washed down the hill** and its stones were carried off — by routes that cannot be a relay, making them **independent** attestations. [[David Whitmer]] described it in an **1875** Chicago newspaper interview; [[Edward Stevenson]] heard it from a Palmyra local and recorded it — and Stevenson's *first* interview with Whitmer was not until **1877**, so Whitmer could not have gotten it from Stevenson.[^1] The host frames this as a small undesigned coincidence he personally verified after reading [[Josh Gailey]]'s book.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The chronology forecloses cross-contamination
The natural skeptical move is "Stevenson told Whitmer, and it became Whitmer's story." But Whitmer's account (1875) predates Stevenson's earliest contact with him (1877), so the dependency runs the wrong way in time.[^1] Two people who had not yet spoken report the same outcome for the box.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (turns on the published dates; if accurate, the independence is clean).

### 2. The detail is one "only locals would know"
That the box had washed down and the stones been taken (through plowing and farming) is not the kind of dramatic detail a distant believer would invent — it is mundane, local terrain-knowledge, exactly the sort of incidental convergence that signals real events rather than coordinated legend.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (undesigned, low-glamour details are evidentially valuable, but rest here on a single retelling each).

### 3. It reinforces, rather than contradicts, the earlier box reports
A critic might think "the box is gone now" undercuts the witnesses; in fact a stone box left exposed on a farmed hillside for decades *should* erode and scatter, so its later disappearance is consistent with its earlier reported presence — not evidence against it.[^3] This locks together with the location/age data from hostile sources in [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (coherence argument; explains the present absence without special pleading).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The independence claim hangs entirely on the dating, and a skeptic can press several gaps: Whitmer gave "a million" newspaper interviews, so the relevant 1875 item must actually contain the wash-down detail and be reliably dated; "washed down the hill" is also a fairly obvious thing to *guess* about any hillside feature decades on, so convergence may be coincidence rather than shared memory of a real box. More basically, two independent reports that the box is *gone* establish only that locals believed a box had once been there — the same belief the friendly tradition already asserts — not that it ever held gold plates. The reply is that undesigned, hostile-adjacent, locally-specific convergence is a recognized historiographical marker and adds one more independent strand to the cumulative case; but the believer should grant this is a corroborating detail, not a standalone proof, and that it inherits whatever uncertainty attaches to the box's original existence.
