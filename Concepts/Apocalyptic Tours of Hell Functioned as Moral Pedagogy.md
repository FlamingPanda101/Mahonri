# Apocalyptic Tours of Hell Functioned as Moral Pedagogy

## The Point
[[D. Jill Kirby]] argues that early Christian "tours of hell" — chiefly the *Apocalypse of Peter* (early 2nd c.) and *Apocalypse of Paul* (4th c.) — worked like the Greco-Roman *katabasis* literature of Homer and Virgil: they were **paideia** (moral/cultural education). Using vivid description (*ekphrasis*) with sensory detail (*energeia*) in a guided-tour form (*periēgēsis*), and matching each punishment to its sin, they trained readers to adopt their community's values. Because the two apocalypses were composed two centuries apart, the *changing catalog of sins* reveals how communities and their concerns evolved.

## Evidence
1. **Scripture is sparse; the genre filled the gap.** Synoptic "Gehenna" and Revelation's "lake of fire" give little detail; the verbose katabatic tradition of Homer (*Odyssey*) and Virgil (*Aeneid*) was standard schooling [^1].
2. **The pedagogical machinery.** Texts "persuaded readers by evoking emotion through descriptions (ekphrasis) delivered with vivid details (energeia) of sight, sound, and smell," in a guided "periegetic" form; punishments "matched the sin(s) in some fashion" [^1].
3. **Apocalypse of Peter — abortion/infanticide.** Women who procured abortions are "swallowed up … up to their necks" in a pit of excretions while their aborted children "sit, but both alive and they cry to God," shooting lightning at the parents; congealed breast milk breeds flesh-devouring beasts [^2].
4. **Measure-for-measure "hanging" punishments.** In Peter: women hung by their hair, blasphemers by their tongues, liars' lips cut off [^3].
5. **Peter's sins are generic Greco-Roman; Paul's are Christian.** The *Apocalypse of Peter* has "no sins that were unique to Christianity," but the later *Apocalypse of Paul* devotes "about half" its punishments to Christian failings — congregants who chatter after church, slander fellow worshippers, or "give the nod" to plot evil — plus errant presbyters, bishops, deacons, and lectors [^4].
6. **Mercy intrudes.** In the *Apocalypse of Paul*, Jesus suspends punishment on Sundays "for the rest of eternity as a mark of divine mercy." Augustine, by contrast, was cautious about lurid imagery and most concerned to establish that "eternal" punishment is genuinely eternal [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "Early Christian apocalypses, including Revelation, functioned within the Christian community much as did the katabatic literature of authors such as Homer and Virgil." [^1]

> "The punishment for abortion and exposure of children is aligned with those acts, most strikingly by the presence of the children." [^2]

## LDS Connection
Kirby's framing is apologetically useful in two ways. First, it shows that the most graphic Christian "hell" descriptions are *pseudepigraphal pedagogy*, not canonical doctrine — undercutting the assumption that vivid eternal-torment imagery is bedrock Christianity. Second, it models reading such texts for their *moral and historical* function rather than as literal cosmography — congenial to the LDS view that revelation about the afterlife is deliberately limited so we will be "anxiously engaged" in this life (D&C 58:27–28). The *Apocalypse of Peter*'s condemnation of abortion/infanticide also corroborates [[Abortion and Euthanasia as Pagan Not Christian]].
