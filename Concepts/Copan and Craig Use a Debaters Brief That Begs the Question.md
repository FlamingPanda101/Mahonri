# Copan and Craig Use a Debater's Brief That Begs the Question

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Blake Ostler]]'s meta-level charge against [[Paul Copan]] and [[William Lane Craig]] is **methodological**: their defense of *creatio ex nihilo* is not balanced exegesis but "a lawyer's defense brief," and it begs the question by smuggling in the very conclusion that is supposed to be proved.[^1] Two assumptions, Ostler argues, secretly dictate every result:

1. **The word *create* is assumed to mean *creation ex nihilo*.**
2. **Even if a text says God created by organizing unorganized matter, it must still be read as teaching *ex nihilo*, because it "implicitly" assumes God first created that matter from nothing.**

"Yet these very assumptions are themselves what is at issue. Thus the basic premise of their discussion begs the question in their favor."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **They admit the texts don't expressly say it.** Copan and Craig concede that "even if the doctrine of creation out of nothing is not explicitly stated, it is an obvious inference," and that several texts they cite "[do] not expressly address the issue as to how God created" — yet insist the texts must mean *ex nihilo* anyway because it is "implicit."[^3] Ostler: asserting a view is "implicit" without showing *why* the implication is necessary "amounts to simply reading one's own view into the text."[^4]

2. **The "even if" tell.** Their stance is exposed by repeated "even if" concessions: "The view proposing creation from preexistent matter would not win even if the Bible were silent on the matter."[^5] This reveals a *prior theological commitment* that determines in advance what the evidence must show — a debater's posture with "winners and losers," not an attempt at balanced assessment.[^6]

3. **They ignore better readings and contrary scholarship.** Because the conclusion is fixed in advance, they "ignore more convincing readings of the key texts," omit fellow evangelicals (Bruce Waltke, William R. Lane) who defend *creatio ex materia*, and swim "against the tide" of the majority of biblical and classical scholars.[^7]

4. **The question-begging is what generates the two-stage device.** The need to preserve assumption (2) is precisely what forces the *ad hoc* "two-stage creation" reading onto text after text. See [[Two-Stage Creation Is an Ad Hoc Rescue Device]].

## Supporting Evidence (Copan & Craig, steelmanned)

Copan and Craig openly grant they are *defending* a doctrine, and they argue there is no presupposition-less reading of any text — every interpreter brings a framework, so importing a coherent theological prior is legitimate, not fallacious. They distinguish "implicit" from "ambiguous": a conclusion can be a sound *inference* from what a text affirms (God created everything distinct from himself; God alone is unbegotten) even if the text never spells it out. On this view, inferring *ex nihilo* from "God created all things and God alone is uncreated" is ordinary good reasoning, and demanding the exact phrase "out of absolutely nothing" sets an unreasonable evidential bar.

## Why the Steelman Fails (per Ostler)

Ostler concedes there is no purely "objective" reading, but the problem is not that Copan and Craig *have* a framework — it is that their framework is **the disputed conclusion itself**, so the inference is circular.[^2] The "implicit/inference" defense fails because the supposedly entailing premises do not entail it: the Wisdom of Solomon calls God all-powerful Maker of "the all" *and* says he created "from formless matter," proving that "God made everything, God alone uncreated" is logically compatible with *creatio ex materia*.[^8] So the inference is not necessary; it is imposed. And the bar Ostler asks for is not the magic phrase but a text that *consciously addresses the question* in its own historical context rather than one read through a later lens.

## Best Quotes

> "Their article and book are like a lawyer's defense brief for the view that the scriptures teach creatio ex nihilo." — Blake Ostler[^1]

> "The view proposing creation from preexistent matter would not win even if the Bible were silent on the matter." — Copan and Craig, quoted by Ostler[^5]

> "Asserting that a view is 'implicit' in the text without explaining why the implication is necessary to the text amounts to simply reading one's own view into the text. I believe that is precisely what Copan and Craig have done." — Blake Ostler[^4]
