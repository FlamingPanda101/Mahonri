#type/historical

## The Point
In the house-church period, the head of the household — whether male or female — typically served as patron(ess), and sometimes as deacon(ess), presiding over a small group of believers. New Testament and early Christian writings name numerous men and women of means who opened their homes for gatherings, demonstrating that women held significant patronage and leadership roles in the earliest period before those roles later contracted.

## Evidence
1. The patron(ess) hosted gatherings of roughly ten to fifty believers, who on a given day (Jewish Sabbath or Sunday "the Lord's Day") would offer the "kiss of peace," share a meal (agape feast), and conclude with bread and wine.
2. Named patrons/patronesses include: Lydia, "a dealer of purple cloth" in Philippi (Acts 16:14–15, 40); Jason in Thessalonica (Acts 17:5–9); the evangelist Philip and his prophesying daughters in Caesarea (Acts 21:8–14); Phoebe in Cenchreae (Romans 16:1–2); Prisca/Priscilla and Aquila in Corinth, Ephesus, and Rome (Romans 16:3–5; 1 Corinthians 16:19).
3. Also: Chloe, Gaius, and Stephanus in Corinth (1 Corinthians 1:11, 14–16); Philemon, Apphia, and Archippus in Asia Minor (Philemon 1–2); Nympha in Laodicea (Colossians 4:15–16); Tavia and an unnamed widow in Smyrna (Ignatius, Letter to the Smyrnaeans 13.2; Letter to Polycarp 8.2).
4. The prominence of women as patronesses/deaconesses in this period contrasts with their later restriction (see [[Decline of Women's Roles in Early Christian Liturgy]]).

## Best Quotes
> "It seems that the head of the household — whether male or female — would have served as a patron(ess) or 'deacon(ess)' over a small group of between ten and fifty believers." [^1]

> "New Testament and early Christian writings name several examples of men and women of means who, by opening their homes to small gatherings of believers, functioned as patrons or patronesses (sometimes as deacons or deaconesses) in house churches around the eastern Mediterranean." [^2]

## LDS Connection
The visibility of women in foundational ministry roles offers a point of reflection for Latter-day Saints on the historical breadth of women's participation in the early church.
