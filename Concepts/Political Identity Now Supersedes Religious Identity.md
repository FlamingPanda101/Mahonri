# Political Identity Now Supersedes Religious Identity

## The Claim
Over recent decades, politics has become a more central component of personal identity than religion. People on the *extreme* ends of the political spectrum — very conservative and very liberal alike — are the most likely to disaffiliate, because for them religion increasingly *follows* politics rather than the reverse.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A U-shaped disaffiliation curve by ideology (rated: strong).** Across very conservative, conservative, moderate, liberal, very liberal, "it's actually people on the very end of things — sides on both sides — that are more likely to disconnect."[^1] Political identity can "supersede" religious identity.[^2]

2. **The partisan-religion correlation flipped (rated: strong, historical).** "30 years ago, if you were a religious person, I probably couldn't tell whether or not you were a Republican or a Democrat because Democrats and Republicans were equally religious. That has changed... now Republicans are far more likely to be religious than Democrats."[^3] So becoming more liberal now carries religious implications it did not used to.

3. **Both extremes, with a timing shift (rated: moderate, partly anecdotal).** A decade ago the worry centered on "wokeness," secularism, and progressive/liberal ideologies pulling people out; in the last ten years the panel observes an uptick of the *very conservative* placing politics above their LDS identity. Dyer confirms the data show both ends leaving.[^4] *Steelman:* Dyer explicitly cautions against painting with a broad brush — "Democrats, liberals, many of them are fantastic religious people" — and notes the conservative-side claim is partly anecdotal observation layered on the macro trend.[^5]

4. **A developmental window (rated: moderate).** During the late-teen years there is a dip in religious identity alongside a rise in political identity; the two trade salience precisely when many young people are most malleable.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "It's actually people on the very end of things — sides on both sides — that are more likely to disconnect."[^1]

> "Their religion is starting to follow their politics. If their political leader says something, that seems to have a bigger impact on an individual... than even what their religious leader said."[^2]

> "30 years ago... Democrats and Republicans were equally religious. That has changed in recent years where now Republicans are far more likely to be religious than Democrats are."[^3]
