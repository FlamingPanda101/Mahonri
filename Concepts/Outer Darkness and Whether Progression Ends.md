# Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends

## The Claim
The panel discusses what outer darkness actually is and whether anyone there can ever progress out. Their position: Latter-day Saints **do not have a doctrine on the nature of the suffering** in outer darkness — [[Joseph Smith]] taught that only those who go there will know what it is like.[^1] On duration: [[Luke Hansen]] and [[Hayden Carroll]] hold that the **sons of perdition** "don't have" eternal progression (D&C 76 says they "never be redeemed... unlike other people"), distinguishing them from telestial-and-above heirs who may progress.[^2] Carroll rejects **annihilationism** (God erasing existence): "I don't believe in annihilation, but I think people who will be there infinitely are the ones who want to be there infinitely" — outer darkness is chosen.[^3]

## The Rebuttal
1. **The "chosen, not imposed" framing aligns with free-will theodicy — rate: coherent within LDS doctrine.** Carroll/Hansen agree with the common Christian intuition that hell is for "those who want to stay there," which keeps moral responsibility on the agent.[^3] This is the LDS version of the C.S. Lewis "doors of hell locked on the inside" view. **Steelman of the critic:** if outer darkness is genuinely final for sons of perdition (no redemption "unlike other people"), then the LDS solve to the *creation* dilemma does not fully cover them — God still organized/admitted spirits He foreknew would become sons of perdition. The premortal-consent + eternal-law solve (see [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]]) must carry the weight here, since the "eternal = God's, so temporary" reading (see [[Eternal Damnation Is Gods Not Endless]]) explicitly does *not* apply to perdition.
2. **Annihilationism is raised and rejected — rate: a real fork the panel takes a side on.** Ellis recounts an evangelical apologist moving toward annihilationism (better to erase existence than torture forever).[^4] Carroll declines that route, preferring a chosen-eternal-existence model. This is a substantive intramural difference: annihilationism would *more cleanly* answer the problem of hell, but the panel won't adopt it, citing scripture on the permanence of perdition. **Steelman of the annihilationist:** annihilation arguably *better* preserves God's goodness (no endless torment) — Carroll's rejection rests on scriptural commitments (section 76's language), not on the moral force of the dilemma, which annihilationism would actually relieve.
3. **Epistemic humility about the nature of suffering — rate: appropriately hedged.** The panel repeatedly says "we have no clue" what outer-darkness suffering is like, citing folklore of brief, partial visions and Joseph's reticence.[^1] This avoids overclaiming and matches LDS scripture's deliberate vagueness in D&C 76.

## Best Quotes
> "We have no clue [about the nature of the suffering]... Joseph said God said only those who go there will know what that's like." [^1]

> "I don't believe in annihilation, but I think people who will be there infinitely will are the ones who want to be there infinitely." [^3]

> "You can read in section 76, he says that they'll never be forgiven again... they'll never be redeemed again in the due time of the Lord, unlike other people. So... they don't have it [eternal progression]." [^2]
