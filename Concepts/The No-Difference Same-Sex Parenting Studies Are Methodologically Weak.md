# The No-Difference Same-Sex Parenting Studies Are Methodologically Weak

## The Claim

[[Katy Faust]] argues that the wave of studies (roughly 2005–2015) reporting that children "fare no different" raised by two mothers or two fathers is **methodologically weak**, and that it should be disbelieved precisely because "it flies in the face of everything else that we've already established after decades of research on family structure."[^1]

Her case has two halves. **First, the negative critique** of the no-difference studies, which she says share predictable flaws:[^2]
- **Recruited (non-random) samples** — "people that were invited into the study rather than examining population-based data or getting their participants at random." She credits her debate interlocutor **Josh Zeps** with conceding this point.
- **Small sample sizes** and **inadequate controls.**
- **No real outcome measures** — "most of those studies did not actually look at the outcomes for kids… their school records… whether or not they were on welfare," and they "did not wait till the kids were old enough to ask… for themselves." The structural difficulty is real: only ~10% of same-sex couples are married, a subset have children, you must wait for those children to grow up, and then find them *at random* — "it's hard to do good studies," and "between 2005 and 2015 we didn't have time to wait for good studies. We had an agenda to push."

**Second, the positive consensus** the no-difference studies overturned. Faust says that before 2005 left and right agreed "children fared best in the home of their married biological mother and father in a low-conflict marriage," and she reduces the broader family-structure literature to three "truisms":[^3]
1. **Biology matters** — "biological parents are the most invested in, connected to, and protective of kids" and "advantage children in ways that unrelated adults do not."
2. **Gender matters** — "men and women are different, and those differences benefit children"; mothers and fathers "offer distinct and complimentary benefits."
3. **Losing a parent is harmful** — to death, divorce, incarceration; and "separate studies show that sperm and egg donation among heterosexual couples showed diminished outcomes with kids."

Her conclusion: a same-sex-parented child is "always missing a biological parent… always going to be missing mother or father… always lost a parent," so the claim that none of the three truisms applies to that one case should be "looked at askance." She credits **Paul Sullins** with "the lion's share" of the gold-standard, population-based work showing that "no differences actually meant major differences," especially elevated rates of "challenges in school, high rates of depression, anxiety, daily fearfulness, daily crying."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman both sides)

1. **The methodological critique names real, recognized limitations (rate: strong on the methodology point).** Convenience/recruited samples, small n, absent controls, and proxy-rather-than-outcome measures are genuine weaknesses that the social-science community itself flags; early same-sex-parenting research did lean heavily on small recruited samples, and Faust's interlocutor conceded the point on-air. This is a fair, mainstream methodological observation, not a fringe one.[^2]
2. **The coherence argument has force (rate: moderate).** If three findings about family structure are robust (biology, gender, parental loss), then a fourth finding that *none* of them bites in the one politically charged case is at least *prima facie* surprising and warrants scrutiny. This is a legitimate Bayesian point about prior probability — though "surprising" is not "false."[^1][^3]
3. **The honesty about study difficulty cuts both ways (rate: candid).** Faust concedes it is *intrinsically* hard to study this population well — which is to her credit, but also undercuts strong claims in *either* direction, including confident assertions of harm. The same caution flagged in [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]] applies here.[^2]

### Steelman of the progressive / mainstream view

The opposing position is held by essentially every major professional body and deserves a full hearing. (a) **Professional consensus runs the other way.** The American Psychological Association, the American Academy of Pediatrics, and comparable bodies have reviewed the literature and concluded that children of same-sex parents do not differ on key adjustment outcomes; this is not one rogue study but a standing consensus of the relevant experts. (b) **Sullins is a contested outlier.** Paul Sullins's re-analyses (often of the National Health Interview Survey and Add Health) have been sharply criticized for methodological choices — e.g., classifying families by household composition in ways that misattribute children, and not adequately distinguishing *family instability/transition* from same-sex parenting itself. Citing him as "the gold standard" overstates a disputed minority position. (c) **The deficits may be artifacts of stigma and instability, not parental gender.** Where worse outcomes appear, they often correlate with family disruption, legal precarity, and minority stress (discrimination), which the *absence* of marriage rights historically aggravated — an argument that cuts *toward* same-sex marriage, not against it. (d) **Newer, better data exist.** Faust's framing freezes the debate at 2015; subsequent population-based and longitudinal work (including studies using married same-sex couples now that marriage is legal) has continued to find few or no differences in child wellbeing, with planned (intentional) same-sex families sometimes scoring *well* on parental investment. (e) **Recruited samples are not automatically worthless.** For hard-to-reach populations, well-constructed non-probability samples are a standard tool; the critique identifies a limitation, not a disqualification. Faust's reply is that the *outcome-measuring, random, population-based* studies are the ones that matter and that those (on her reading) show deficits — but a fair adjudication requires weighing the full professional literature, not a single contested researcher.

## LDS Relevance

This is an *empirical* support for the structural commitments of *The Family: A Proclamation to the World* — that children are "entitled… to be reared by a father and a mother" and that gender is essential to identity. The Proclamation states these as revelation; Faust's three "truisms" offer a secular, social-science scaffold a Latter-day Saint can cite in the public square. The argument also pairs with the vault's broader caution about the *limits* of social data: just as [[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]] is honest that correlation cannot establish the gospel's truth-claims (see [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]]), an LDS apologist should present the parenting-studies dispute as *contested*, leaning on the methodological point (recruited vs. population samples) rather than overclaiming a settled scientific verdict that the professional bodies do not grant. The theological weight rests on revelation; the studies are corroborating, not foundational.
