# Victory as an Idol in Apologetics

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] diagnoses the deepest driver of bad apologetics as idolatry: when winning the argument becomes the goal, "victory" functions as "an old god that's being worshipped." The apologist then sacrifices honesty, charity, and even truth to please that god — committing idolatry. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Victory named as a false god.** "There's an old god that's being worshipped here, which is victory. You are committing idolatry when you are willing to sacrifice everything to please victory." [^1] *Rating: a sharp pastoral reframe;* it relocates the problem from technique to worship — what god do you actually fear in the moment, "the King of kings... or is it victory?" [^2]

2. **Victory's appetites are corrosive.** "Victory likes conflict. Victory likes bloodshed. Victory likes losers." [^3] *Rating: strong rhetoric;* it explains why a victory-driven apologist *needs* an enemy and cannot tolerate a respectful draw — the idol feeds on opposition.

3. **It retrenches you against learning.** Snell connects it to the prospective missionary who "needs to know the answers... to counter all of these points": when the motive is to win, "if you don't know an answer, you retrench yourself in your position even more," refusing to research or reconsider. [^4] *Rating: strong;* it shows the practical mechanism by which victory-worship blocks growth and accuracy (cf. [[Inaccuracy as a Form of Disrespect]]).

4. **Love-first frees you to say "I don't know."** Snell's counter-posture: "if your primary focus is on loving the other person, then it's okay if you don't know the answer... 'I'll go research it and I'll get back to you.'" [^5] *Rating: strong;* it offers the concrete alternative to the idol — humility that builds relationship.

5. **A modern variant: the algorithm.** Both note a related idol, "the algorithm," which rewards "aggression" and "bloodshed" in the creator space because that is what audiences feed it. [^6] *Rating: moderate;* a timely extension of the same dynamic to content incentives.

**Steelman of the competitive apologist.** Truth matters, and some debates have real stakes for souls; an apologist might argue that conceding ground or saying "I don't know" can mislead seekers, so a will to "win" the argument for truth is a virtue, not idolatry. Beshears's distinction answers this: contending earnestly for truth in "gentleness and respect" is commanded (1 Peter 3:15); idolatry begins only when *winning over the person* displaces *pleasing God* as the operative aim (see [[Peter's Respect Is Fear of God Not the Interlocutor]]).
