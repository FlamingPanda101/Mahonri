# The Book of Mormon Fullness of the Gospel Is a Misread of D&C 20:9

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that the popular belief that the **Book of Mormon contains "the fullness of the gospel"** — taken to mean the *published* book is doctrinally complete — is a misconception. The introduction's "fullness of the everlasting Gospel" line (added in **1981**) derives from [[D&C 20.6-9|D&C 20:6-9]]. But that passage, though written in 1829, is **recounting [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]]'s 1823 visitation** and describing the *plates* as they then existed — the angel of "lightning" countenance and garments "white above all whiteness." It is not a certification that the 1829 translation Joseph published is the whole record. Barnes uses this to argue there is missing revealed material — opening the door to his theory that the [[Book of Moses]] supplies it.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The intro line is sourced to D&C 20, and D&C 20 is quoting 1823
"In the introduction to the Book of Mormon it says [it] contains the fullness of the gospel… but it actually doesn't say that [of the published book]. That's another misconception." The wording traces to [[D&C 20.6-9|D&C 20:6-9]], which describes Joseph being ministered to "by an angel… whose countenance was lightning, whose garments were white above all whiteness," commanded to "translate the Book of Mormon… which contains the fullness of the gospel." Barnes' key move: "that revelation was not given in 1829 [about the finished book] — that's recounting the visitation of Moroni from 1823."[^1][^2]

### 2. The 1823 account in Joseph Smith—History matches word-for-word
Barnes says he prints the [[Joseph Smith History 1.68-69|1823 account]] and D&C 20 "next to each other so you can see this — D&C 20 is just taking from the 1823 experience." In 1823 Moroni called it "the fullness of the everlasting gospel." So the "fullness" predicate attaches to the **1823 plates as a complete heavenly record**, not to whatever portion was eventually translated and printed.[^3][^4]

### 3. The published book is only ~60% of the 1823 record
"Is there any difference between the Book of Mormon in 1823 and 1829? In 1823 it didn't exist [as a book] — it was all on plates." Between 1823 and the 1829 publication, two large pieces dropped out: the **sealed portion** (Joseph was not allowed to translate it) and the **lost 116 pages**. Net: "by 1829 we only get **60%** of what that original 1823 document was."[^5][^6][^7] So the "fullness" of 1823 and the contents of 1829 are not the same thing.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The standard LDS reading is that "fullness of the gospel" means the Book of Mormon contains the *saving doctrines* (faith, repentance, baptism, the Atonement) in their plainness — a *qualitative* completeness — not that it is *quantitatively* the entire record. On that reading the intro is not erroneous at all: the missing sealed portion and 116 pages are additional history/prophecy, not additional *gospel*, so "fullness of the gospel" and "60% of the plates" are simply answering different questions and there is no contradiction to resolve. Barnes' framing trades on sliding between the two senses to create a "gap" his Book-of-Moses theory can fill; a critic (or a careful believer) can grant the historical point about D&C 20 quoting 1823 while denying that it shows the published book is doctrinally *incomplete*.
