# The Written Word Is Women's Pornography

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] argues that explicit "romance" fiction — euphemized as "spicy" books or "smut" — is **functionally pornographic** and should be named as such, because it serves the same end (arousal sought for its own sake) through the medium best suited to female sexuality: the written word.[^1]

Two myths are targeted. First, **that only men consume pornography**: Horn cites a 2018 study (91% of men and 60% of women consumed explicit material in the prior month), notes women make up ~30% of one major video site's audience (40–50% in some countries), and points to bestselling erotic books "selling hundreds of millions of copies" circulated in "thousands of popular TikTok videos."[^2] Second, **that the written form is harmless** ("something in the written form couldn't be that bad"). Horn calls euphemism itself "one of the devil's favorite tools" and insists "romance novels" is a term Christians should stop using, because it disguises the content.[^3]

His account of *why* written erotica fits female sexuality: "the written word allows women to make sexual fantasies more intimate, private, prolonged, and have a greater emotional connection to what excites them." Where male video might use a thin plot, female novels detail motive and relationship — "why did the mailman choose her," "what broke down in her marriage." The genre's tropes — *bodice rippers* set in lavish historical periods climaxing in a man overcoming a woman's initial refusal, and Beauty-and-the-Beast arcs where a woman "civilizes" a dangerous man — corrupt the good female desire to be pursued (see [[Pornography Corrupts Distinct Gendered Desires]]). He cites examples from *Fifty Shades of Grey* to a viral monster-erotica book, and YouTuber Hillary (Second Story).[^4]

Crucially, Horn argues the written medium is **not a milder substitute but an escalating one**. Citing Hillary's research, "90 to 95%" of women struggling with hardcore addiction "started with erotic fiction or smut and fanfiction"; several had *quit video* and fallen back on written material, finding "functionally no difference between the two"; one preferred written smut because it could depict acts "that would have been illegal in video form." Like men needing ever-more-extreme material, written erotica becomes a "gateway drug" to more perverted fantasy.[^5] Therefore the Catechism's condemnation of pornography — material made "to display [sexual acts] deliberately to third parties," perverting the conjugal act — applies to books no less than videos.[^6]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The consumption gap is narrower than the stereotype (rate: strong, statistical).** Even granting measurement noise, a 60%-vs-91% spread and a 30–50% female share of a major site dismantle the "men only" framing on which the silence around women's use depends. The bestselling status of explicit fiction marketed to women is independently observable (publishing sales, BookTok). This is the load-bearing empirical claim and it does not require Horn's metaphysics.[^2]
2. **The escalation testimony is convergent and primary.** Horn's strongest evidence is not theory but the accounts Hillary gathered from women who *equate* written and video material and report the same tolerance-escalation pattern documented for male users — including the striking datum that text can portray what video cannot legally show, making it in some respects *less* restrained, not more.[^5]
3. **The "euphemism" critique is internally consistent.** Horn applies the same suspicion of soft language to "adult content" generally ("adults should be known for their virtue") and even mocks his own forced euphemism ("corn") under YouTube's filter — so the demand to drop "romance novel" is a principled stance against arousal-content being laundered by genre labels, not special pleading against women.[^3]

### Steelman of the critic

The critic has real ground. (a) **Text is not equivalent to image, and the slide is not inevitable.** Reading a sex scene engages imagination, authorial craft, plot, and character in a way a video clip does not; vast numbers of readers consume "spicy" fiction without addiction or escalation, so generalizing from a self-selected sample of *struggling* women (Hillary's accounts) commits a survivorship/recruitment-bias error — these are the people who escalated, by definition. (b) **The genre is heterogeneous.** "Romance" spans chaste inspirational fiction to explicit erotica; collapsing all of it into "pornography" erases the line Horn himself concedes exists for "authentic" art that "might become arousing for some" — the "I know it when I see it" standard cuts against a blanket genre condemnation. (c) **Female sexual fantasy of being "overcome" is well-studied and does not entail endorsing real coercion**; psychologists distinguish fantasy from desire, so "bodice ripper = corrupted nature" may pathologize a normal, harmless imaginative register. (d) The Catechism passage targets *producing* material that displays real or simulated acts "to third parties"; whether private prose written and read by one person maps cleanly onto that definition is exactly the contested step. Horn's reply: the *purpose* (arousal sought for its own sake outside the marital act) is what the Catechism condemns, and the convergent testimony that written and video are "functionally no difference" is precisely the evidence that the medium distinction is morally thin for those who use it as arousal-content (cf. [[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]]).
