# The Delay of the Parousia Drove Christian Doctrinal Change

## The Point

The single greatest engine of eschatological development in early Christianity, on [[Nicholas J. Frederick]]'s account, was the *delay* of the **Parousia** — Jesus's promised physical return. As the first century turned into the second, "the delay in Jesus's return was enough to cause doubt and anxiety in the minds of some Christians." [^1] By the mid-second century "more than a century had passed since the Crucifixion, yet Jesus had not returned, and the millennial kingdom remained unrealized." [^2] This unmet expectation forced successive generations either to defend a literal future return, to try to hasten it, or to spiritualize it away.

## Evidence

1. **2 Peter answers the "scoffers."** The epistle records skeptics asking "Where is the promise of his coming? For since the fathers fell asleep, all things continue as they were from the beginning" (see [[2 Peter 3.4|2 Peter 3:4]]). Peter's reply: God works on his own schedule, the delay exists so all "should come to repentance" (see [[2 Peter 3.9|2 Peter 3:9]]), and "the day of the Lord will come as a thief in the night" (2 Peter 3:10). [^1]
2. **Parousia was a technical term in the making.** Greek *parousia* ("presence"/"arrival") — Latin *adventus*, used of an emperor's arrival — appears 24 times in the New Testament, most often in Paul, and "has come to be used synonymously with Second Coming in New Testament eschatological scholarship." [^3]
3. **The delay produced a doctrinal fork.** After Justin Martyr, successors split: Irenaeus and Tertullian kept the Second Coming and Millennium as "real events," while Clement and Origen "increasingly downplay[ed] eschatological ideas... effectively spiritualiz[ing] them, thus finding an acceptable way to deal with a topic that was becoming more and more uncomfortable in light of their failed expectations." [^4]
4. **Three responses crystallized.** Frederick names them: a new ethos of "living as Christians in a world no longer infused with eschatological hope"; attempts "to hurry God's timetable"; and conviction "that the kingdom of God was a spiritual one rather than a physical one." [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "As Christianity reached the end of the first century and started into the second, the delay in Jesus's return was enough to cause doubt and anxiety in the minds of some Christians." [^1]

> "Just because Jesus may have delayed his return did not mean that Christians had forgotten the promise." [^5]
