# God's Wrath Is the Natural Consequence of Rejecting Love

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
God must display his *wrath* as a distinct attribute — retributive anger toward sinners — and a God without such wrath-to-be-shown is incomplete; this is why he creates objects of wrath. [^1] See [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Wrath is reframed as natural consequence** — rate: a substantive reinterpretation, not a denial. [[Blake Ostler]]: "My wrath isn't really wrath, it's natural consequences that follow when I reject him and do things that hurt other people." Wrath "in this sense is an expression of his love" — a warning that a path "will lead you to your degradation and injure all of your relationships." *(theological reframe)* [^2]
2. **Concrete examples of self-degradation** — rate: illustrative, grounded in the [[Matthew 5.43-45|Sermon on the Mount]] ethic. Stealing "is going to destroy your relationship, even if they don't find out"; dishonoring father and mother destroys relationship; desiring another woman "in your heart" is already adultery because "to love her is to give yourself to her wholly." Sin's harm is intrinsic, not externally imposed. *(ethical analysis)* [^3]
3. **Commands are relational guidance, not arbitrary tests** — rate: connects wrath to the law of love. The Sermon-on-the-Mount commands (give the extra coat, go the extra mile, turn the other cheek) are "ways of being in a relationship," teaching how to flourish — so "wrath" is simply what happens when one rejects that flourishing. *(systematic)* [^4]
4. **God adapts commands; there is no single fixed code** — rate: a corollary used against fixed retributive law. "Why, if we have a law of Moses, does it change to a law of love? Because God adapts his commands to teach us how to treat each other. If there were a single reality of what's right and wrong, there would be no such change." *(redemptive-historical)* [^5]

**Steelman of the retributive view:** Classical theology (and much of Scripture — [[Romans 6.23|Romans]], the prophets) treats divine wrath as God's *holy, personal opposition to evil*, not merely impersonal "natural consequences." On this view, reducing wrath to consequence risks a deistic, hands-off God and undercuts the cross, where Christ bears wrath *propitiously*; a God who never actively judges would not be just. Ostler's reply is not to deny judgment ("there will be a judgment") but to deny *retributive eternal torment decreed before any act* — his target is the Calvinist double-predestination God, not the biblical claim that God opposes evil.

## Best Quotes
> "What he's saying is my wrath isn't really wrath, it's natural consequences that follow when I reject him and I do things that hurt other people." [^2]

> "God's wrath in this sense is an expression of his love, because what he's doing is saying if you go that way it will lead you to your degradation and it will injure all of your relationships." [^2]

> "God adapts his commands to teach us how to treat each other. If there were a single reality of what's right and wrong, there would be no such change." [^5]
