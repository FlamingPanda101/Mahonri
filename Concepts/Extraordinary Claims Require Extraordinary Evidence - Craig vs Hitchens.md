# Extraordinary Claims Require Extraordinary Evidence - Craig vs Hitchens

## The Point
This note captures Hitchens' *positive* strategy for the negative side — and Craig's reply about where the burden of proof lies. Hitchens declines to argue *that God does not exist*. Instead: "I find all the arguments in favor to be fallacious or unconvincing," and the believer is the one making "a very, very, very large claim... your evidence had better be absolutely magnificent." His banner is the maxim "extraordinary claims require truly extraordinary evidence," and he judges that none of Craig's evidence was "extraordinary enough to justify the extreme claims."[^1] He likens disbelief in God to disbelief in "tooth fairies... witches... Santa Claus" — no special doctrine is needed for it.[^2] He also drives a wedge between deism and theism: even *if* there were a First Cause, "it still wouldn't prove that this entity cared about us, answered prayers, cared what church we went to" — "you cannot get from deism to theism except by a series of extraordinarily generous-to-yourself assumptions."[^3]

Craig presses back hard on the logic. Mere disbelief without a positive argument is not atheism but **agnosticism**: "that doesn't prove atheism, that just leaves you with agnosticism." He insists his five arguments are *deductive*, so Hitchens must "identify which premises of the argument he rejects as false," not merely declare them unconvincing.[^4] On the deism/theism point, Craig says Hitchens "is simply confused": "deism is a *type* of theism," and his arguments are "a cumulative case for Christian theism," not a bare First Cause. He also denies claiming certainty: "I'm saying that this is the best explanation of the data... more probable than not."[^5] Under cross-examination Hitchens admits he does "have the nerve to say" God "does not exist," yet when asked for an argument says he'd "rather state it in reverse" — he finds the arguments *for* God wanting.[^6]

## Evidence
**Hitchens (negative):**
- The burden is on the believer; atheism needs no name or doctrine, like not believing in Santa Claus.[^2]
- "Extraordinary claims require truly extraordinary evidence," and Craig's evidence was not extraordinary enough.[^1]
- Even a proven First Cause is "completely indifferent to us"; deism does not deliver a caring, redeeming God.[^3]
- "Absence of evidence" — when asked if it's "not evidence of absence," he answers "I'm not sure that I would agree."[^7]

**Craig (affirmative):**
- Disbelief without a positive disproof yields agnosticism, not atheism; Hitchens gave "no argument that God does not exist."[^4]
- The arguments are deductive: deny a *premise* or accept the conclusion "on pain of your rationality."[^4]
- "Deism is a type of theism"; the case is cumulative for *Christian* theism, and claims only the *best explanation*, not certainty.[^5]
- In his closing he reframes atheism itself as a worldview bearing a burden: Hitchens "asserts that he alone has the true worldview... the only problem is he doesn't have any arguments for it."[^8]

## Best Quotes
> "Extraordinary claims... require truly extraordinary evidence. I don't think any of the evidence we heard from Dr. Craig... was extraordinary enough to justify the extreme claims." (Hitchens) [^1]

> "That doesn't prove atheism, that just leaves you with agnosticism — namely, you don't know if there's a God or not." [^4]

> "You cannot get from deism to theism except by a series of extraordinarily generous-to-yourself assumptions." (Hitchens) [^3]

## LDS Connection
The burden-of-proof exchange is shared ground for all theists, Latter-day Saints included: against the "I just lack belief" posture, Craig's point holds that a *positive* claim that God does not exist (which Hitchens eventually makes) itself needs justification, and that the theistic arguments must be met premise-by-premise rather than waved off (compare [[Atheism Is a Worldview With the Burden of Proof]]). The deism-to-theism gap Hitchens exploits is actually *narrower* on the Restoration view in one respect: Latter-day Saints hold that God is not a distant abstract First Cause but an embodied, personal Heavenly Father who answers prayer and reveals himself — the very "caring entity" Hitchens says bare cosmology cannot reach. The bridge from "a cause" to "a caring God," on the LDS account, is crossed not only by cumulative philosophical inference but by personal revelation and the witness of the Spirit, which is why the *immediate experience of God* (Craig's fifth argument) carries independent weight.
