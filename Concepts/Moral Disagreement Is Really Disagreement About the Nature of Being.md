# Moral Disagreement Is Really Disagreement About the Nature of Being

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s headline thesis is that "the roots of nearly all disagreement" about morality are not disagreements about *values* but disagreements about **metaphysics** — the nature of reality itself. Because we hold our conception of ultimate well-being as a "moral North Star" that orients our whole worldview, and because "we don't agree about the nature of being itself," we cannot agree about what that ultimate well-being looks like or which road leads to it — "and then we are bound to have conflicts over which way we should go through life." [^1][^2] This is why he says "our beliefs about the nature of reality actually matter immensely": they "frame the entire way that we view morality." [^3]

## Evidence
Hansen's worked example contrasts two metaphysical pictures. For a Latter-day Saint, ultimate well-being is **eternal life**, so one will "sacrifice certain temporal enjoyments for a greater payoff on an eternal timeline." For an atheist, the greatest possible well-being is "likely something akin to maximizing one's satisfaction over an 80-year timeline, at the end of which they cease to exist entirely." [^2] The rhetorical pivot is decisive: "Do you see how different your perspective is going to be if you think you will exist forever versus if you think you only have 30 years left in your existence?" [^3] Two people can share the *value* "pursue well-being" yet reach opposite moral verdicts purely because they disagree about whether there is an afterlife — a factual claim about the nature of being, not a clash of moral foundations. This extends his earlier framework (see [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]]): the "ought" points toward a goal, so once the goal (well-being) is fixed by one's metaphysics, the moral directions follow.

## Best Quotes
> "The issue is that we don't agree about the nature of being itself, so we cannot agree about what ultimate well-being looks like. If we don't agree on the destination, or the nature of the road that gets us there, then we are bound to have conflicts over which way we should go through life." [^1]

> "This is why our beliefs about the nature of reality actually matter immensely, because they frame the entire way that we view morality." [^3]

> "Our conception of what constitutes this ultimate well-being becomes like a moral North Star. It's the thing that orients a person's worldview and acts as the standard for their moral judgments." [^4]

## LDS Connection
The thesis dovetails with the restored gospel's claim that **right metaphysics is morally load-bearing**: because Latter-day Saints take eternal life and an endless timeline as the true nature of being, "wickedness never was happiness" is not a slogan but a structural fact about reality (developed further in [[Pure Rationality Makes Morality Selfish - Only God Connects All Well-Being]]). It also reframes apologetics: many moral disputes with secular interlocutors are downstream of a prior dispute about what exists, so the productive argument is often about the nature of being, not about ethics directly.
