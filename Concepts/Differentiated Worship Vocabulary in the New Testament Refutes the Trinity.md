# Differentiated Worship Vocabulary in the New Testament Refutes the Trinity

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] runs an *offensive* argument against the Trinity from the worship language of the New Testament itself. His concern: "Why are there different forms of worship in the New Testament where some are only reserved for the Father and some are used for the Son and the Father?" [^1] He grants the two Greek terms the Trinitarian appeals to — *proskyneo* (rendered "worship," used of the Son) and *latria* / *latreuo* (a worship "reserved only for the Father") — and turns them against the doctrine. If the Father and Son are one and the same being (one substance), the New Testament should not encode an asymmetry in which the highest worship is directed to the Father alone. The Latter-day Saint [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead model]] predicts exactly this asymmetry: the Son is worshipped, but is worshipped *as subordinate* to the Father, "in the way the Son treated the Father — which is as the only true God" ([[John 17.3|John 17:3]]). [^2]

Carroll's diagnostic framing: Protestants, Catholics, and Eastern Orthodox must say that *latria* — reserved for the Father in the apostolic period — *also* applies to the Son "because they're one being." His rejoinder is historical-exegetical: "Well, why wasn't that in the New Testament?" [^3] The differentiated vocabulary is, on his reading, a fossil of the original subordinationist worship that the later [[Trinity - Historical Development|creeds]] flattened — part of the broader [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God|Hellenization]] of the doctrine of God. See [[The Only True God Is the Father (One God One Lord)]].

## Supporting Evidence
*(Steelman — the strongest case for the claim.)*

1. **The NT does deploy distinct worship verbs.** *Proskyneo* (prostration / homage) and *latreuo* (cultic service) are not synonyms; the LDS apologist can point to passages where *latreuo* is directed Godward without the Son named as its object, and argue the distribution is non-random.
2. **Differentiated worship is what subordinationism predicts.** If the Son is "eternally God" yet *subordinate* to the Father (the pre-Nicene [[Born of the Father Before All Ages Affirms Christs Preexistence and Subordination|subordinationist]] position Carroll claims was "accepted before [the Council of] Nicaea in 325"), then a worship asymmetry is the expected signature — not an embarrassment. The single-substance Trinity, by contrast, has to *explain away* why the apostles did not give the Son the same *latria* as the Father. See [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity]].
3. **It coheres with John 17:3.** Jesus calls the Father "the only true God." Carroll: "we worship the Father in the way the Son treated the Father — correct, which is as the only true God." Differentiated worship is the liturgical face of that christology. [^2]
4. **Burden-shift.** The argument is not that the Son receives *no* worship — Carroll is "comfortable saying we worship the Father and we worship the Son." [^2] It is that the *manner* differs, and that the Trinitarian owes an account of why scripture preserves the difference if there is no difference of person-order in the Godhead. The Trinitarian reply runs through indwelling and exaltation — see [[Worship of the Son Is Warranted Through Indwelling]] — which a critic will call a post-hoc harmonization.

**Counter (for balance):** A Trinitarian denies the lexical premise — *proskyneo* and *latreuo* are not cleanly partitioned by recipient in the NT, and *proskyneo* is in fact rendered to God the Father too — so no stable "latria reserved for the Father" rule exists to be leveraged. On this view the Son receives the worship due to God ([[Philippians 2.9-11|Philippians 2:9-11]], every knee bows), which is precisely what one expects if he is God by nature; the apparent asymmetry reduces to the economic subordination of the incarnate Son, not a difference in being.
