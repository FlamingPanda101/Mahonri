# Syriac Poetry Pictures Heaven as a Garden with Degrees of Glory

## The Point
[[D. Jill Kirby]] presents heaven through the mystical nature-poetry of **Syriac Christianity**, especially [[Aphrahat]] (d. c. 345) and [[Ephrem the Syrian]] (d. 373). Their vision of paradise as a fruitful garden is striking for the LDS reader because **Ephrem explicitly describes heaven as having multiple ascending levels**, allotted "to the degree that accords with [each one's] labors" — a graded reward structure typed by Noah's three-decked ark and Moses's ascent of Sinai.

## Evidence
1. **Garden imagery over apocalypse.** Syriac Christianity had "a certain disinterest in apocalyptic literature," preferring nature themes and wisdom literature; the *Odes of Solomon* describes the righteous as "like the earth that flourishes and laughs in its fruits" [^1].
2. **Aphrahat's paradise.** Trees "whose fruits fail not, and whose leaves fall not," a region spacious and unlimited, where "the inheritance shall not be divided and no man shall say to his fellow: 'This is mine and that is thine'"; the righteous are restored to "youthful vigor" [^2].
3. **Ephrem's cooperative trees.** Trees bend their branches as steps and recline the blessed "in its bosom above"; "fruit of every savor / Ranged for the hand to pluck"; dew to rinse the hands. Christ is the **tree of life** at the garden's center [^3].
4. **Heaven's multiple stories (graded glory).** Ephrem: God "varied its beauties / So that some levels / Were far more glorious than others … With justice he raises up each one / To the degree that accords with his labors; / Each is stopped at the level / Whereof he is worth" — "the lowest for the repentant, / The middle for the righteous, / The heights for those victorious, / While the summit is reserved for God's presence" [^4].
5. **Typed by ark and Sinai.** Noah's ark (animals below, birds middle, Noah "like the Deity" on top) and Sinai (people below, priests around, Aaron halfway, Moses on the heights, "the Glorious One on the summit") are "symbols of the divisions / In that Garden of Life" — built on the tripartite ark of [[Genesis 6.19-20|Genesis 6:16]] [^5].
6. **The Western contrast.** Latin Christianity followed [[Augustine]]'s far more restrained **beatific vision**; though he allowed "different levels of happiness and glory," heaven's peace "would not be marred by discontent or envy" [^6].

## Best Quotes
> "To the degree that one level / Is higher than another, / So too is its glory / The more sublime. … There being sufficient levels in Paradise for everyone: / The lowest for the repentant, / The middle for the righteous, / The heights for those victorious, / While the summit is reserved for God's presence." — Ephrem [^4]

> "When the just ascend its various levels / To receive their inheritance, / With justice he raises up each one / To the degree that accords with his labors." — Ephrem [^4]

## LDS Connection
Ephrem's tiered paradise — graded by one's labors, with explicit lowest/middle/highest levels topped by God's presence — is a remarkable fourth-century parallel to the LDS **three degrees of glory** ([[D&C 76]]; [[D&C 88]]). Kirby's accompanying box cautions there is "no evidence" the ancients understood the degrees "in the same way it was revealed to Joseph Smith" (see [[Three Degrees of Glory in the Bible]]), and the JST of 1 Corinthians 15:40 adapts Paul's bodily-resurrection argument to the modern revelation. Still, the structural resemblance — reward "to the degree that accords with his labors" — is a genuine point of contact often cited in LDS apologetics; compare [[Degrees of Glory vs Heaven]].
