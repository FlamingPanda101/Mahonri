# Presuppositional Apologetics - CS Lewis Sun Analogy

## The Claim

[[David Butler]] articulates a form of presuppositional apologetics: we cannot
*prove* God's existence through neutral reason alone — but if we accept the
existence of God as a starting axiom, everything else falls into harmony. This
is illustrated through [[C.S. Lewis]]'s sun analogy (God is like the sun: you
don't look directly at it, but by its light you see everything else) and
Russell Brunson's geometry analogy (unprovable axioms — point, line, plane —
that make the entire system work). The cumulative case for theism is not a
single knock-down proof but a web of mutually reinforcing evidence: when you
accept God, "Zion will be built" — everything else will be clear.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. C.S. Lewis's Sun Analogy (Evidence Rating: Primary — [[C.S. Lewis]] Source)

Butler recounts:

> "There's a CS Lewis... really kind of like an allegory that he tells where
> he's just like... 'The reason I know Christianity is true isn't because I can
> see it. It's the same reason I know the sun is there at noon day when I'm
> looking out a window. You don't see at noon day. You can't see the sun when
> you're looking out of a window, but you can see everything else.'"[^1]

The analogy:
- The sun at its brightest is not directly visible through a window — but
  it illuminates everything else.
- God is not directly provable — but God's existence makes sense of everything
  else (morality, consciousness, design, purpose, religious experience).
- You don't see the sun; you see BY the sun. You don't prove God; you see the
  world rightly THROUGH God.

This is an "inference to the best explanation" approach: theism is the
hypothesis that, if true, makes the widest range of data coherent.

### 2. Russell Brunson's Geometry Analogy (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Illustrative)

Butler references a conversation with [[Russell Brunson]]:

> "In geometry there are three constants that are unprovable and that's a
> point, a line, and a plane. And I don't really understand how mathematic
> proofs go. So I can't really explain it. But they're unprovable things. But
> if you can just accept those three things, everything else works in geometry,
> right?"[^2]

The analogy:
- In Euclidean geometry, point, line, and plane are primitive notions — they
  can't be defined or proven; they're accepted as starting axioms.
- Once you accept them, the entire system of geometry follows with logical
  coherence.
- Similarly: "We will never be able to prove the existence of God. We will
  never be able to prove that the Book of Mormon is true or that Jesus is the
  Christ and resurrected. But if you accept those three proofs, everything else
  will be in harmony. Zion will be built."[^2]

The geometry analogy makes a specific epistemological claim: some truths are
foundational rather than derived. You don't prove them; you accept them and
observe the coherence they produce.

### 3. The Pragmatic Test: "Everything Else Will Be Clear" (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Experiential)

> "That right there is the testimony that it is true — that God does live, that
> Jesus is the Christ, that the restoration really is from God. And it's
> because everything else will be clear."[^2]

The test for the axiom is not logical proof but pragmatic fruitfulness: living
as if God exists produces clarity, coherence, and flourishing that living as if
He doesn't cannot match. This aligns with Alma 32's "experiment upon the word"
— the proof is in the living.

### 4. Not the Absence of Evidence (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Clarification)

> "And that's not to say that there is not evidence for those things. There is
> evidence for those things."[^3]

Butler is careful to distinguish this from fideism (blind faith without
evidence). The point is not that there's NO evidence — there's plenty (see
[[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]], [[Moral Argument
Against Atheism]], etc.). The point is that evidence alone doesn't compel
belief — at some point, you must accept the axiom. Even [[Alex O'Connor]]
concedes: "I think it would take a personal experience with God" for him to
believe.[^3]

### 5. Hayden Carroll's Endorsement (Evidence Rating: Tertiary)

> "That presuppositional apologetic line of reasoning is masterful."
> — [[Hayden Carroll]][^4]

Carroll, who primarily uses evidentialist arguments (fine-tuning, moral
argument), affirms the presuppositional approach as complementary.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Presuppositionalism Is Circular
**Steelman:** "Accept God as an axiom and then everything makes sense" is
circular: you're assuming what you're trying to prove. If I accept "the Flying
Spaghetti Monster exists" as an axiom, I can make everything "coherent" too.
The test of an axiom should be whether it's independently plausible, not just
whether it produces internal coherence.

**Response:** Butler's claim is not purely presuppositional — he explicitly
says "that's not to say that there is not evidence."[^3] The geometry analogy
is about the LIMITS of proof, not the absence of evidence. Points, lines, and
planes aren't arbitrary — they correspond to spatial reality. Similarly, the
God axiom isn't arbitrary — it's supported by evidence, but evidence alone
doesn't force assent. The relationship between evidence and axiom is
bidirectional: evidence supports the axiom, and the axiom illuminates the
evidence.

### Objection 2: This Makes Religious Belief Unfalsifiable
**Steelman:** If "everything will be clear" once you accept the axiom, then any
apparent counter-evidence can be reinterpreted to fit the axiom. Suffering
becomes "part of the plan," unanswered prayers become "God's timing." This
makes the belief system immune to falsification — a hallmark of pseudoscience.

**Response:** This is a genuine risk for any comprehensive worldview, not just
theism. Naturalism faces the same charge (fine-tuning gets explained away by
multiverse, consciousness by illusion). The test is which worldview has BETTER
explanatory power, fewer ad-hoc patches, and more existential fruitfulness.
Butler would argue theism wins on all three counts.

### Objection 3: Many Atheists Would Say They Once "Accepted the Axiom" and It Didn't Work
**Steelman:** Countless ex-believers testify that they sincerely accepted God,
lived the life, and found it DIDN'T make things clear — it created cognitive
dissonance, moral injury, and psychological harm. The pragmatic test cuts both
ways.

**Response:** Butler would likely respond that this is why [[Alma 32]]'s
"experiment" is ongoing — not a one-time decision. And he notes in the video
that even if someone leaves the Church, they should "still live by the
principles that the church has given you" because those principles lead to
flourishing regardless of belief.[^5] The pragmatic fruits of LDS living
(family stability, purpose, community) are observable even by non-believers.
