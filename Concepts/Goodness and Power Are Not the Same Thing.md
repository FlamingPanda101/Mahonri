# Goodness and Power Are Not the Same Thing

## The Point
Ward's fourth and final heading is **power**, and its lesson is the "cornerstone of the Tao": "goodness and power are not the same thing."[^1] Drawing on the scene in *That Hideous Strength* where Mark Studdock refuses to trample a crucifix, Ward shows Lewis insisting that "'Might' cannot be identified unequivocally with 'right,'" that "powerlessness is not necessarily an evil," and that the right course "may require the giving up of all one's power" — even unto death.[^2] At the book's heart, Ward argues, "is an attempt to assign death a positive value even without recourse to a compensatory afterlife."[^3]

## Evidence
Ward roots this in Lewis's lifelong love of Norse mythology, whose ethos he ranked among "the great myths" for telling "men to serve gods who… would certainly be defeated in the end."[^4] "The whole point about Odin was that he had the right but not the might," and yet "that does not in the least alter the allegiance of any free man."[^5] From this Lewis "knew early on and deep in his imaginative bones that defeat is no refutation"; loss, weakness, and "powerlessness of any kind… are not to be simply equated with moral failure," for "there is such a thing as innocent suffering, honourable defeat; it is possible to die a good death."[^6] Ward grounds the point biographically in Lewis's near-death in a 1918 trench, observing that Lewis "ardently defends the Tao… because it told him how to view death": "it is far more important to die on the right side than to live on the wrong side. The worst that can happen to us is not death, but dishonour."[^7]

The decisive contrast is with **subjectivism**, for which "the value of a good death is invisible because power — self-assertive power — lies at the heart of that philosophy."[^8] When value is "a conferral from one's own irrational or nonrational nature," then "truth thus dissolves into power, and the notion that self-sacrifice could ever be morally obligatory becomes absurd."[^9] Self-destruction remains *possible* for the subjectivist, but only as momentary preference; "reasoned self-sacrifice — a purposeful, generous, self-abandoned laying down of one's life for the good of others as others — is impossible."[^10] Ward seals it with Robert Bolt's line from *The Mission*: "If might is right, then love has no place in the world."[^11]

## Best Quotes
> "Goodness and power are not the same thing. Or, to put it another way, powerlessness is not necessarily an evil." [^1]

> "Defeat, death, loss, poverty, weakness, powerlessness of any kind — these things are not to be simply equated with moral failure… it is possible to die a good death." [^6]

> "Truth thus dissolves into power, and the notion that self-sacrifice could ever be morally obligatory becomes absurd." [^9]

> "If might is right, then love has no place in the world." (Robert Bolt, *The Mission*, quoted by Ward) [^11]

## LDS Connection
That "might is not right" and that "it is possible to die a good death" lie at the centre of Latter-day Saint moral vision: the supreme moral act is the freely chosen, atoning self-sacrifice of Christ, "the Lamb slain" who lays down his life of his own will (John 10:18) — the very "reasoned self-sacrifice" Ward says subjectivism cannot account for. Scripture honours the good death over the bad life (cf. Alma 60:13, the righteous slain "enter into the rest of the Lord their God"). Ward's diagnosis — that where value collapses into self-assertive power, obligatory self-sacrifice "becomes absurd" — is the same realism gap faced by [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]], where well-being maximization struggles to make laying down one's life for a stranger rationally *binding* (see [[Moral Heroism Is Not Rational]] and [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]]). For both Lewis and Latter-day Saint thought, the duty to die well presupposes an **objective, eternal moral law** that no calculus of power or pleasure can supply.
