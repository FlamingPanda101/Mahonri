# James 2:24 — "Not By Faith Alone"

## The Verse (KJV)
> "Ye see then how that by works a man is justified, and not by faith only."

## Why This Matters
"Faith alone" (*sola fide*) appears exactly ONCE in the Bible — and it's
negated. This is the primary Catholic proof-text against the Protestant
distinctive.

## The Two Standard Protestant Responses (and Why They Fail)

### Response #1: James Means "False Faith," Paul Means "True Faith"
**Failure point**: James never treats faith as bad. Throughout the entire
letter, "faith" is always good. The problem isn't defective faith — it's faith
that is **alone**, lacking works. James 2:26: "As the body apart from the
spirit is dead, so faith apart from works is dead." Spirit and body are
distinct — works and faith are distinct.

The NIV's "Can *such* faith save him?" and the ESV's "Can *that* faith save
him?" insert a distinction not present in the Greek *pistis*. The KJV's "Can
faith save him?" is more literal.

**Test**: Replace "faith" with "dead faith" throughout James 2. "Show me your
dead faith apart from your works" — the passage becomes incoherent, proving
James didn't conceive of two kinds of faith.

### Response #2: James Means Vindication Before Men, Not God
**Failure point**: Genesis 22:5 — Abraham took Isaac AWAY from the servants.
No human witnesses to the sacrifice. Abraham's works couldn't have vindicated
him before men who weren't there.

Protestant scholars concede this:
- Robert Zins: "It may be stretching things too far."
- Thomas Schreiner (Reformed): "There is no evidence that justification here
  relates to justification before people rather than God."

James 2:22 doesn't say faith was "shown" by works — it says faith was
**completed** (*eteleiōthē*) by works.

## The Catholic Resolution: Ongoing Justification

James 2:24 refers to **ongoing justification** — the increase of righteousness
after initial salvation. It does NOT describe how a person goes from spiritual
death to life.

- **Initial justification**: Grace alone, received in baptism. "None of those
  things which precede justification, whether faith or works, merit the grace."
  (Council of Trent, quoting Romans 11:6)
- **Ongoing justification**: Good works, performed in grace, increase the
  righteousness God has given. See: [[Ongoing Justification vs One-Time Event]]

**Jimmy Akin's Lamp Analogy**:
- Purity (whiteness of the light) = freedom from sin, received at baptism
- Intensity (brightness) = increased through post-baptismal good works

**Key supporting texts**:
- Romans 6:7 — "He who has died is justified from sin" — Paul uses *dikaioō*
  for a post-conversion state.
- Romans 2:13 — "The doers of the law will be justified" — future-tense
  justification.
- Philippians 2:12-13 — "Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling."

## Luther and James
Martin Luther called James "an epistle of straw" and said he felt like
"throwing Jimmy into the stove." He speculated "some Jew wrote it." But he
never tried to remove James from the canon — he walked up to the line but
didn't cross it.

## Assurance Paradox
Under Protestantism, you must constantly question whether your faith was "true"
in the first place (since true faith allegedly always produces works). Under
Catholicism, you examine your conscience honestly: am I in a state of grace?
No need to question whether your conversion was "genuine" — just whether you're
cooperating with grace right now.
