# The Parables of Enoch Preserve a Pre-Existent Divine Messiah

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] argues that "the most important excluded text" for understanding Jewish non-recognition of Jesus is **1 Enoch**, specifically the **Parables (Book of Parables, chapters 37–71)**. There Enoch sees a figure called the **Son of Man** — "a pre-existent heavenly being who dwells with God before creation, who will sit on the throne of glory, who will judge the wicked and vindicate the righteous, whose name was known before the sun and stars were made."[^1] "This is not just a conquering earthly king motif. This is an explicitly divine, pre-existent cosmic figure. And Jesus used this exact title, Son of Man, to refer to himself more than any other designation in the Gospels, 80 times across the four Gospels."[^1] When Jesus tells the high priest they will "see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of power and coming on the clouds of heaven," he is "invoking a specific figure from a specific tradition" — one the establishment, "working from a canon that had been marginalizing that tradition for centuries, largely did not" recognize.[^1] Arnold then defends the antiquity of the tradition and attacks the standard late-dating as circular. See [[Son of Man - Development of the Title]] and [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Parables do describe a pre-existent heavenly Son of Man.** 1 Enoch 48 (the Son of Man "named before... the stars of heaven were made"), enthroned on the throne of glory and judging — this is a real feature of the text, building on [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7]]. *Rating: accurate description of the Parables; the trajectory is documented in the vault's Son-of-Man cluster.* [^1]
2. **Jesus' self-designation matches.** "Son of Man" is Jesus' most frequent self-title (~80x), and the Sanhedrin scene (Mark 14:62) fuses Daniel 7 with Psalm 110 — the "blasphemy" charge makes best sense against an exalted-Son-of-Man background. *Rating: strong; even critical scholars grant the Danielic/Enochic resonance of the trial saying.* [^1]
3. **The antiquity evidence is physical.** Eleven Enoch manuscripts were among the Dead Sea Scrolls (vs. eight of Ezekiel); [[Jude]] quotes 1 Enoch as *prophecy* ("Enoch... prophesied"); and the [[Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church]] "has always included First Enoch in its canon and never removed it." *Rating: the Qumran, Jude, and Ethiopian data are solid — though note the Parables section specifically is the part **not** found at Qumran (see steelman).* [^2]
4. **The late-dating argument is plausibly circular.** Arnold: dating the Parables after Jesus "rests primarily on their theological precision about a divine pre-existent Son of Man" — i.e., it *assumes* detailed messianic prophecy is impossible, "an ideological assumption dressed up as methodology." *Rating: a fair charge against one form of the argument; current scholarship has largely moved to a pre-70 AD (often Herodian) date on other grounds, which actually helps Arnold.* [^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A scholar pushes back: (a) **the Parables are the missing piece at Qumran** — of all 1 Enoch's sections, chapters 37–71 are precisely the ones *absent* from the Dead Sea Scrolls Enoch fragments, which is why some have argued for a later (even post-Christian) date; so citing "11 Enoch manuscripts at Qumran" is slightly misleading, since they attest the *other* booklets (Watchers, Astronomical, Dreams, Epistle), not the Son-of-Man Parables; (b) **the Son of Man in the Parables may itself be developing Daniel in a milieu shaped by, or parallel to, early Christology**, so "Jews deleted a tradition that pointed to Jesus" overreads what might be *convergent* first-century speculation; (c) **mainstream Judaism read Daniel 7's "one like a son of man" corporately** (the righteous remnant) or as Michael, a *legitimate* reading of the Hebrew, not necessarily a "deletion"; (d) Jesus' "son of man" usage is debated — some sayings clearly evoke Daniel 7, but others may use the Aramaic idiom for "a human/someone," so the count of ~80 lumps distinct usages. A Latter-day Saint reply: even granting the Parables' Qumran absence, the **best current scholarship dates them before 70 AD** (Nickelsburg, VanderKam), so a *pre-existent heavenly Son of Man was conceivable in Second-Temple Judaism independent of Christianity*, which is all the argument needs — the *milieu* existed; and the cautious form (a *conceivable* heavenly mediator, not proven direct borrowing) is exactly what the vault's [[Son of Man - Development of the Title]] already concedes. The point is not that one text was surgically excised but that the *standardized canon marginalized the divine-mediator stream*, making the figure Jesus claimed to be harder to recognize for those reading only the edited corpus.

## Best Quotes
> "Enoch receives a vision of a figure called the Son of Man, a preexistent heavenly being who dwells with God before creation, who will sit on the throne of glory... whose name was known before the sun and stars were made. This is not just a conquering earthly king motif. This is an explicitly divine, pre-existent cosmic figure." [^1]

> "If you begin with the premise that detailed messianic prophecy is impossible before the Messiah arrived, then any text containing it must be dated after him, regardless of what the physical evidence shows. That is an ideological assumption dressed up as methodology." [^2]
