# The Combined Improbability of the Naturalistic Witness Feat

## The Claim

[[Larry Murf|The Murph]] grants the critic almost everything — a real but fabricated plate artifact, a humanly-induced group vision — and then argues that the **conjunction** of what a naturalistic Joseph Smith would have to accomplish is so improbable that it becomes its own kind of evidence for the miraculous. The believer need not deny the [[Metallurgical Fraud Theory Rebutted|fraud hypothesis]] piecemeal; he can stack its required parts and ask: *could anyone actually pull all of this off?*[^1] The three feats:

1. **Prophesy then deliver the Three's shared vision.** Joseph promised in advance (the witnesses' revelation) that three men would see the angel and plates, then produced a simultaneous experience for [[Oliver Cowdery]], [[David Whitmer]], and (separately) [[Martin Harris]] — a coordinated "mass hallucination" on demand.[^2]
2. **Fabricate plates convincing enough to be handled *naturally*.** On Murph's reading of the Eight's collective statements (see [[John Whitmer Spiritual Eyes Objection Rebutted]]), they hefted and examined engraved, gold-appearance plates in daylight — not a bundle under a cloth. That is a far more demanding **metallurgical** fabrication than tin-under-frock.[^3]
3. **Dictate the Book of Mormon.** ~588 pages orally produced in roughly two and a half months — "whether or not you believe it's historical... quite an impressive thing to have done."[^4]

The argument is a probabilistic *conjunction*: each feat is individually hard; their product is "an incredible fraud" no one today could replicate. Murph explicitly parallels this to the **fine-tuning** argument for God — not his primary ground for belief (that remains spiritual experience), but a fallback he "would struggle to intellectually explain" away. See [[Cosmic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer (Turek)]].[^5]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Conceding the artifact strengthens, not weakens, the case
By granting [[Dan Vogel]]'s fabricated-plates theory, Murph removes the easy "there were no plates" exit and reframes the debate around *craftsmanship plus showmanship plus literary genius* combined in one uneducated farm boy. "If you did those three things... my goodness, that's impressive."[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a genuine reframing that shifts the burden onto the *cumulative* plausibility of the naturalistic account, but it is an argument from improbability, not a demonstration, and its force depends on accepting Murph's "handled naturally" reading of the Eight (point 2 of the steelman).

### 2. The "more impressive fraud" sub-point
Against the Mormon Stories panel's inference that the Eight saw plates "covered with a cloth," Murph argues that natural handling of engraved gold-appearance plates is "more of an impressive metallurgical fabrication than just a stack of tin covered in a cloth" — so even the *fraud* branch implies a remarkable feat.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — turns a critical concession into a cost the critic must pay; but a skeptic can simply prefer the cheaper cloth hypothesis.

### 3. Asymmetry of replication
Murph's rhetorical clincher: "I don't know if anyone today could replicate that," so if God can do miracles, this is "a more compelling case" for one.[^6]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate — "no one could replicate it" is an intuition about historical singularity, not a measured probability.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

[[Kolby Reddish]]'s reply (and the strongest objection): the conjunction looks improbable only if each feat is treated as a discrete magic trick, but naturalism doesn't require that. (a) The "prophecy" and the vision were produced inside a high-expectancy religious setting where suggestion and group dynamics do real work — not a parlor trick (cf. [[Mass Hallucination Hypnosis Theory Rebutted]]). (b) The Eight more likely handled a *cloth-covered* bundle, dissolving the demanding-metallurgy premise; "spiritual eyes" and the Burnett letter point that way. (c) Dictating a long book over months is impressive but not supernatural — many have done comparable feats; talent plus a scribe plus revision suffices. (d) Methodologically, **piling up improbabilities can manufacture awe around any complex historical event**; the same "how did they pull it off?" rhetoric could be aimed at any successful 19th-century religious founder. Reddish also notes the argument quietly assumes the *most charitable* reading on every disputed point at once. Murph concedes the case is not proof — "doesn't mean it's true" — and rests his actual belief on spiritual experience, treating this as a reason the miraculous is *not unreasonable* rather than established.

## Best Quotes

> "If you did those three things, I feel like, my goodness, Colby, that's impressive. Doesn't mean it's true, but I think I have to take this quite seriously cuz I don't know if anyone today could replicate that."[^1]

> "I don't think it's impossible that Joseph could have created gold plates with engravings on it that duped the eight witnesses. I just think that's more of an impressive metallurgical fabrication than... a stack of tin covered in a cloth."[^3]

> "Even if I didn't have that spiritual belief, I would still think, man, how did Joseph pull that off? Like that's an incredible fraud."[^6]
