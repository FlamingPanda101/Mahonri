# The No-Consent Objection Proves Too Much

## The Point
**Critic's charge:** Children are "baptized into a religion they never consented to" (Jonah Barnes's paraphrase of the critics); an 8-year-old congratulated on "her choice" did not really have one.[^1]

**Response:** The objection proves too much. If genuine informed consent is the bar for any childhood commitment, then a parent may not have the child say the Pledge of Allegiance, wear a school mascot shirt, brush her teeth under "societal pressure," or be taught any moral framework at all — yet the critic applies the standard *only* to baptism. The hosts press for consistency. They also note that real consent operates at two levels: the child has reached the age of reason and can in fact say no ("plenty of people don't get baptized until later"), and agency is *ongoing* — one remains free to choose or leave. The deeper paradox: you cannot be "informed enough to consent later on if you've never developed the moral framework to operate in" — which is exactly what parenting (and baptism) provide.

## Evidence
1. **The consistency challenge.** "Be consistent, please... 'My child will not be doing the Pledge of Allegiance because they're beyond the age of consent to understand whether they're going to be American the rest of their life.'" Likewise refusing the school mascot shirt or anthem.[^2] *Rate: a clean* reductio *— the consent standard, applied evenly, would forbid virtually all childhood formation.*
2. **Social pressure is not coercion, and is universal.** The hosts concede "we're not going to negate that there's social norms" and "even social norms apply pressure on your psychology" — but compare it to children feeling "a lot of pressure to brush their teeth every night. It's a societal pressure." Pressure to do a wholesome thing is not abuse.[^3]
3. **The bootstrapping point.** "If you don't teach her at a young age, how can they be informed enough to consent later on if they've never developed the moral framework to operate in?" Consent presupposes a formed conscience; withholding all formation does not produce a neutral free chooser, it produces an unformed one.[^4] See [[Eight Is a Developmental Threshold of Moral Accountability]].
4. **Real and ongoing agency.** "First off, yes, she does [have a choice]. You could say no. There's plenty of people that don't get baptized... until later on in life." The panel frames later departure as fully open — even an apostate keeps the gift of the formation (see [[Childhood Formation as a Universal Gift Not Mormon-Specific]]).[^5] This connects to the broader LDS principle that coerced commitment is worthless ([[Coerced Love Is Not Love - Agency Against Irresistible Grace]]).
5. **The mirror religion.** Jonah Barnes turns the charge around: children of secular parents are also "baptized" — into an unchosen creed that "there is no God," consumerism saves, and past harm "can never be fixed." No upbringing is consent-neutral.[^6] *Rate:* tu quoque*, but a fair one — it exposes that "raise them in nothing" is itself a substantive, unconsented formation.*

## Best Quotes
> "Be consistent, please... 'My child will not be doing the Pledge of Allegiance because they're beyond the age of consent to understand whether or not they are going to be American the rest of their life.'" [^2]

> "How can they be informed enough to consent later on if they've never developed the moral framework to operate in, because you parented them? That's why it's such a pernicious argument." [^4]

> "Our children are getting baptized into a religion they never consented to... [but] the modern normie millennial parent religion that all these children are getting baptized into is this idea that there is no God." [^6]

## LDS Connection
Agency is the spine of LDS theology: commitment under compulsion is worthless, and salvation requires a genuine choice (see [[Coerced Love Is Not Love - Agency Against Irresistible Grace]]). The age-of-accountability framework ([[D&C 68|D&C 68:25-27]]) is built precisely so the ordinance lands at the age of reason, after which the covenant can be ratified, renewed (weekly sacrament), or declined. Far from coercive, the design assumes lifelong agency — the convert at 30 and the child at 8 both retain the freedom to keep or leave the covenant.
