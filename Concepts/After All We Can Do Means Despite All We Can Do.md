# After All We Can Do Means Despite All We Can Do

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The verse [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25:23]] — "it is by grace that we are saved, after all we can do" — is the "famous clobber verse that evangelicals love to use" to charge Mormonism with works-righteousness. [[Kyle Beshears]] argues this reading misconstrues 19th-century English: "after all we can do" then meant something "akin to... *despite* what we can do." [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal / Supporting Evidence

1. **The standard (works-merit) reading.** Typically the phrase is taken as: "we do works and then where we fall short, Jesus makes up the difference." [^3] *Rating: the genuine target;* this is the popular evangelical caricature and a common LDS folk-reading alike.

2. **The 19th-century linguistic reading.** "What Second Nephi is saying in 19th-century English is: we know that we are saved by grace despite everything that we do. So even if we did all these great works, that's not what's saving us." [^4] Beshears credits "work done on this by a couple of people." *Rating: linguistically defensible* — this is the reading advanced by LDS scholars such as those at the Maxwell Institute (e.g., the argument that "after" = "apart from / in spite of"), and it aligns with [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25:23]]'s own emphasis on grace.

3. **The surprising payoff — it sounds evangelical.** "As strange as this sounds, that's a very evangelical teaching or doctrine that you see appearing in the Book of Mormon." [^5] *Rating: a striking concession* from an evangelical critic — grace-alone language inside LDS scripture.

4. **It feeds his authorship thesis.** The grace-centric reading "leads to my suspicion that this was written by a 19th-century early-19th-century person" steeped in Protestant soteriology. [^5] *Rating: connects coherently* to [[Joseph Smith Did Not Author the Book of Mormon]] and [[Book of Mormon as American Pseudepigrapha]].

5. **Broader soteriological frame.** On whether Mormonism is "legalistic," Beshears answers "yes and no": all are resurrected by sheer mercy (no works required), and salvation is not *whether* but "to which degree of exaltation"; only those in outer darkness (a tiny number) fall outside glory. [^6] *Rating: a fair, nuanced summary* of LDS degrees-of-glory soteriology.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A traditional evangelical might insist the LDS *system* (covenants, ordinances, temple worthiness, exaltation tiers) is functionally synergistic regardless of how one parses 2 Nephi 25:23, and that the "despite" reading is a relatively recent apologetic. Beshears partly grants this with his "yes and no" — the higher tiers of exaltation are works-conditioned even if baseline resurrection/salvation is grace. The narrow rebuttal stands only for the *verse*, not necessarily the whole system.

## Best Quotes
> "In the 19th century the phrase 'after all we can do' would be something akin to the way that we would describe 'despite what we can do.'" [^2]

> "What Second Nephi is saying in 19th-century English is we know that we are saved by grace despite everything that we do. So even if we did all these great works, that's not what's saving us. What's saving us is God's grace." [^4]

> "As strange as this sounds, that's a very evangelical teaching or doctrine that you see appearing in the Book of Mormon, which leads to my suspicion that this was written by a 19th-century person." [^5]
