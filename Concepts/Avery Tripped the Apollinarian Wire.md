# Avery Tripped the Apollinarian Wire

## The Point
Asked the textbook Christology question — "does Jesus have one mind or two?" — [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery (God Logic)]] answered "one divine mind," and balked at affirming that Christ has a human mind. That is **Apollinarianism**: the 4th-century teaching of [[Apollinarius]] that Christ took a human *body* but that the divine *Logos* stood in for a human mind. It was condemned at the First Council of Constantinople (381). The episode uses this as Exhibit A that a *sola scriptura* Trinitarian, pressed on the metaphysics, lands on a named heresy without realizing it — and notes that Avery later recanted on Twitter ("I must have misspoke"), which the hosts argue only proves the point: the position was defended confidently and was wrong by his own tradition's standard.

## Evidence
1. **The flub on tape:** "Does Jesus have one mind or two? — He has one divine mind... So he doesn't have a human mind? — I wouldn't say that..." The guest: "that's a layup question in terms of Christology," and "due to his Protestantism, he's not engrossed in the mind of the fathers."[^1]
2. **Apollinarianism defined:** "a fourth-century heresy that said that Christ had a human body but not a human mind... what Apollinarius said was that the Logos was like the one divine mind of Christ."[^2]
3. **Conciliar condemnation:** identified as condemned at "the First Council of Constantinople, 381 AD," "a major ecumenical council."[^3]
4. **The recant cuts the other way:** Avery later said on Twitter "it was brought to my attention that I made this error, and yes, I do believe the Orthodox position" — but "that wasn't an 'I think,' that was 'Yes, one divine mind,'" raising whether he changed his belief or only his words.[^4]

## Best Quotes
> "He has one divine mind... So he doesn't have a human mind? — I wouldn't say that... [he] flounders right here."[^1]

> "Apollinarianism was a fourth-century heresy that said that Christ had a human body but not a human mind."[^2]

> "On the stage, the Trinitarian arguing — supposed to be representing Western Christianity — confidently doubles down on a position that is condemned by the council."[^3]

## LDS Connection
The patristic refutation the episode quotes is itself congenial to Latter-day Saints: [[Gregory of Nazianzus]]' axiom "what is not assumed is not healed" — if Christ did not take a human mind, he did not save the human mind — affirms Christ's *full, distinct humanity*, which the LDS Godhead has no trouble holding because the Son is a genuinely distinct person, not one substance time-shared with the Father. The deeper apologetic move: the *one-mind/two-natures* tangle that snared Avery is a problem manufactured by Chalcedonian metaphysics; a model of three distinct persons united in purpose never generates it (see [[One Mind Dilemma Against Two Natures]], [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]], [[The LDS Godhead Sidesteps the Named Heresies]]).
