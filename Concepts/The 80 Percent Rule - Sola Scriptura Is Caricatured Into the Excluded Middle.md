# The 80 Percent Rule — Sola Scriptura Is Caricatured Into the Excluded Middle

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]]'s framing device for the whole debate: what he calls the **"80 percent Rule"** — "about 80 percent of critiques of sola scriptura misframe where it actually departs from the alternatives."[^1] The doctrine asserts only that Scripture is the **only infallible *rule***; it does **not** claim Scripture is the only *authority*, nor that every doctrine must be *explicit* in the text. Critics, he argues, set the alternative (Catholic/Orthodox) positions against "the worst possible constructions of sola scriptura," committing the **fallacy of the excluded middle**: the genuine *magisterial Protestant* view — the conceptual space between the non-Protestant position and the crudest "me-and-my-Bible" caricature — "is then excluded from visibility."[^2]

Three specific caricatures he names: (1) that sola scriptura makes the Bible the only *authority* (it does not — creeds, confessions, councils, ordination vows have *real, fallible* authority, like a baseball umpire who is authoritative but not infallible); (2) that it requires every doctrine be *explicit* in Scripture (that targets *sufficiency*, and even the strongest Reformed version adds "by good and necessary consequence," a clause "lopped off" in criticism); (3) that "sola scriptura is not in the Bible, therefore self-defeating" — which misses that sola scriptura is "not a doctrine proper" but a *prolegomenal* matter.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

**Steelman (Ortlund / Protestant):**
- **The umpire analogy is a clean distinction between authority and infallibility.** "The umpire at a baseball game has authority to make a definitive call... but he's not infallible. Almost 99 percent of authorities are fallible." So denying the *infallibility* of councils does not deny their *authority* — defeating the "you reject all church authority" caricature.[^4]
- **The "good and necessary consequence" clause is documented.** The Westminster tradition explicitly affirms doctrines deduced "by good and necessary consequence," and Article 6 of the 39 Anglican Articles gives even greater latitude — so the "everything must be explicit" charge attacks a position no magisterial Protestant holds.[^3]
- **Prolegomenon, not doctrine.** Treating "sola scriptura isn't in the Bible" as self-refuting assumes it is a first-order *doctrine* that must meet its own sufficiency test; Ortlund classes it as a *methodological* claim about where infallibility is located, so the self-defeat charge misfires.

**Steelman (Horn / Catholic counter):**
- **Naming the caricature does not retire the strongest objection.** [[Trent Horn]] grants the umpire distinction but insists his case targets the *non-caricatured* core: premise 2 ("no other infallible rule") is still unproven, regardless of framing — see [[One Member of a Kind Is Not the Only Member - The Black Swan Reply]]. Relabeling sola scriptura "prolegomenal" does not exempt premise 2 from needing a warrant.
- **The "good and necessary consequence" latitude is double-edged.** The wider the permitted inference, the more the *interpreter's* judgment — not the bare text — is doing the work, which is the [[Conscience Is the Protestants True Ultimate Authority|functional-conscience]] critique. Horn's parallel point: if sola scriptura is true and Scripture is formally sufficient, then this important framework should itself be *clearly* in Scripture, and it is not.
- **"Prolegomenon" can hide the load.** Calling the thesis a methodological prologue rather than a doctrine arguably *shields* its central premise from the evidential scrutiny a doctrine would receive — the move Horn presses as question-begging in [[The Word of God Fallacy - The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven]].

## LDS relevance
The 80% Rule cuts against sloppy LDS polemics too: the Latter-day Saint who attacks "the Bible is your only authority and it interprets itself perfectly" is, on Ortlund's showing, hitting a caricature. The *non-caricatured* magisterial-Protestant target — "the only *infallible* rule" — is still vulnerable to the LDS canon/authority critique (see [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon]] and [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]]), but the LDS apologist should aim there, not at the strawman, lest the same "excluded middle" charge be turned back on him.

## Best Quotes
> "About 80 percent of critiques of sola scriptura misframe where it actually departs from the alternatives... remember: only infallible rule, not only authority or explicit source for every doctrine." [^1]

> "The umpire at a baseball game has authority to make a definitive call on whether the pitch is a ball or a strike, but he's not infallible... almost 99 percent of authorities are fallible. That doesn't mean they're not real authorities." [^4]

> "This is the fallacy of the excluded middle. The conceptual space between a non-Protestant position and... the worst possible caricature — that is, the magisterial Protestant view — is then excluded from visibility." [^2]
