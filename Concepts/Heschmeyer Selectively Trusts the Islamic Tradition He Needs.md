# Heschmeyer Selectively Trusts the Islamic Tradition He Needs

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Hayden Carroll]] turns a **consistency / tu quoque** objection on [[Joe Heschmeyer]]. Heschmeyer's Muhammad–Joseph Smith parallel quietly leans on Islamic *tradition* to supply its premises — that Muhammad is the author who "produced" the Quran — while *rejecting* an adjacent piece of that same tradition: the claim that Muhammad was illiterate and so could not have produced such a text unaided. Heschmeyer in fact downplays the illiteracy ("we don't actually know if Muhammad himself was illiterate... somewhat of a motivated story... it makes the Quran seem all the more miraculous").[^1] Carroll's challenge: **by what criterion** is Heschmeyer keeping the tradition he needs (Muhammad as author) and discarding the part he doesn't (Muhammad as unlettered)? Without a principled rule, the parallel is built on a cherry-picked reconstruction of Muhammad rather than on what the sources actually warrant.[^2]

Carroll's constructive point is that Heschmeyer has stumbled onto the *right* skepticism for the wrong reason. The historically defensible move is not "Muhammad was smart enough to write it," but "**we have no contemporary evidence that Muhammad dictated the Quran we have today** — only narratives that appear generations later." On that footing the better explanation is that followers wrote legend-stories about him long after his death — which is exactly the explanatory pattern Heschmeyer, as a non-Muslim, *already accepts*. "You obviously don't trust [the Islamic tradition]; otherwise you'd be a Muslim."[^3]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)
1. **The asymmetry is real and self-serving.** A parallel argument is only as strong as the facts plugged into it. If "uneducated founder produces miraculous book" is doing the work, then whether the founder was actually uneducated is load-bearing — and Heschmeyer takes the *deflationary* side on Muhammad's illiteracy precisely where the LDS take the *deflationary* side on Joseph Smith's. Carroll presses that you cannot have it both ways: hold Joseph to the hostile record of his "extremely limited" mental powers while granting Muhammad an unprovable competence.[^2][^4] *(Strong — this is a clean charge of inconsistent source-handling, not a factual dispute.)*
2. **It exposes that the parallel imports, rather than argues, its premises.** Heschmeyer must assume the traditional authorship narrative to even state the comparison; but that narrative is the very thing mainstream Islamicists regard as historically insecure — see [[The Quran Has No Contemporary Records Only Sources Centuries Later]]. So the parallel borrows credibility from a tradition Heschmeyer otherwise rejects. *(Strong — connects the consistency charge to the documentary problem.)*
3. **The "right argument" Carroll hands Heschmeyer is genuinely stronger.** Rather than litigating Muhammad's IQ, attack the *evidentiary chain*: no contemporary record that Muhammad recited today's Quran word-for-word. This both wins the point Heschmeyer wanted (the Quran argument is weak) *and* avoids the inconsistency — and, Carroll argues, it does **not** transfer to the Book of Mormon, which has contemporary dictation records and witnesses (see [[Muhammad and Joseph Smith Are Apples to Oranges]]).[^3] *(Moderate-to-strong — the disanalogy is the crux of the whole video and is argued at length elsewhere.)*

**Steelman of Heschmeyer.** Heschmeyer is not being arbitrary in the way the charge implies. (a) A historian routinely accepts the *minimal* core of a tradition (a man named Muhammad founded a movement and is associated with the Quran) while bracketing the tradition's *theological embellishments* (an illiteracy motif that exists to heighten the miracle) — that is ordinary source-criticism, not cherry-picking. (b) Heschmeyer's actual thesis does not even *require* that Muhammad authored the Quran; his point is the reverse — that the "too-unlettered-to-have-written-it" argument is weak for *both* founders, so questioning Muhammad's illiteracy is internally consistent with questioning the parallel claim about Joseph. (c) The deeper Catholic argument never rested on the parallel at all but on a prior commitment (if Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is not inspired — see [[Could God vs Would God on the Book of Mormon]]), so a wobble in the Muhammad premise does not by itself rescue the Book of Mormon. The live question is therefore whether Carroll's *disanalogy* (contemporary records and witnesses for Joseph, none for Muhammad) actually holds — not whether Heschmeyer's source-handling is hypocritical.

> "You should be asking, why should I believe the Islamic tradition? You obviously don't trust it. Otherwise, you'd be a Muslim."[^3]
