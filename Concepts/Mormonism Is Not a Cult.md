# Mormonism Is Not a Cult

## The Claim
Evangelical scholar-pastor [[Kyle Beshears]] argues that, on the dominant cultural meaning of the word, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints should **not** be called a cult. In an academic sense "cult" derives from Latin *cultus* and just means a group "behaving religiously in a specific way," but in ordinary usage it conjures Jim Jones, Heaven's Gate, and David Koresh — a small, controlled following around one charismatic leader. Beshears: "for that reason, I don't think Mormonism can be considered a cult." [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal (why the label fails)

1. **Scale.** "According to LDS church statistics, there's over 16 million Latter-day Saints across the globe... already we have a problem with the term cult. That's a lot of people." [^3] *Rating: strong.* The popular sense of "cult" entails a tiny, controllable group; a 16-million-member, century-old, publicly-audited organization simply does not match the prototype.

2. **Tolerated internal diversity.** Beshears points to the "diversity of opinions that the Latter-day Saint church tolerates," naming [[Dan McClellan]] (a temple-recommend-holding academic-biblical-studies Latter-day Saint) and [[Jacob Hansen]] (a traditional apologist) as occupying "a very wide spectrum of beliefs and approaches to scholarship or faith." [^4] *Rating: strong.* Genuine high-control groups suppress dissent; a church that tolerates such a span resists the controlling-cult definition.

3. **The "read the NIV for yourself" point.** Beshears cannot reconcile "mind control cult" with a leadership in Salt Lake that would say, "You know what? Why don't you go ahead and read the New International Version Bible for yourself?" [^5] *Rating: rhetorically effective.* It targets the specific "mind control" charge rather than mere disagreement.

4. **It strips members of agency.** "Ironically it strips Latter-day Saints of agency, which they're huge on." [^6] *Rating: a fair internal-consistency point.*

**Steelman of the opposing view.** Beshears voices the evangelical objection honestly: "you have a prophet, they control their people, I've watched countless ex-Mormon podcasters tell me their story." [^7] He concedes that genuinely **cultic factions** exist within the Mormon umbrella — naming [[Jodi Hildebrandt]]-style groups that are "definitely building [their] own cult" — and that cultic dynamics also appear inside evangelicalism ("a fallen human condition"). [^8] His claim is about the church-as-a-whole, not a denial that abuse exists.

## Best Quotes
> "For that reason, I don't think Mormonism can be considered a cult, because a cult, the way we think of it, has a strong central charismatic figure at a head of a really small following of people and they're extremely controlling of the people who are within their movement." [^2]

> "According to LDS church statistics, there's over 16 million Latter-day Saints across the globe. So already we have a problem with the term cult. That's a lot of people." [^3]

> "I can't wrap my head around somebody saying on the one hand that Latter-day Saints are a member of a mind control cult, and then on the other hand somebody from Salt Lake City just said, 'You know what, why don't you go ahead and read the New International Version Bible for yourself?'" [^5]

> "Are there cultic factions or cultic groups within the umbrella of Mormonism? Of course there are... but is that all of Mormonism? No." [^8]
