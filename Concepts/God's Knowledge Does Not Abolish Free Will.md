# God's Knowledge Does Not Abolish Free Will

## The Point
Feser answers the classic worry that if God infallibly knows I will do X tomorrow, then I cannot do otherwise, so I am not free. [^1] His reply: merely *knowing* that someone acts is never incompatible with their acting freely — not when the act is present, and not when it is future. [^2] The harder version adds that God *causes* the future, not just foreknows it; Feser meets this with the author analogy: a novelist who writes a story in which a character freely commits murder does not thereby make the murder unfree. [^3]

## Evidence
1. **Knowledge alone is harmless.** If I know — by observation, testimony, or a crystal ball — that you are sitting in the next room, that is plainly compatible with your having freely chosen to sit there, "no matter how certain I am." The same holds for knowledge of a *future* free act. "If there would be no incompatibility when I am the one who knows about your future actions, why would there be when God is the one who knows it?" [^2]
2. **The author is not a character.** A novelist's writing the story is not like one *character* hypnotizing another (which would destroy freedom). "It is perfectly coherent to say that the author wrote a story in which someone freely chooses to commit a murder." [^3] God's causal relation to the world "is radically unlike the relation we and other elements of the world have to each other"; he is the author, not an interfering character. [^4]
3. **Analogical, not univocal.** The appearance of incompatibility arises only when we forget that talk of God's knowledge and action must be understood analogically. God's action would conflict with freedom only "if he was comparable to the hypnotist, who is one character alongside the others." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "It is perfectly coherent to say that the author wrote a story in which someone freely chooses to commit a murder. Similarly, it is perfectly coherent to say that God causes a world to exist in which someone freely chooses to commit a murder." [^3]

> "If there would be no incompatibility when I am the one who knows about your future actions, why would there be when God is the one who knows it?" [^2]

> "God's action would be inconsistent with our having free will if he was comparable to the hypnotist, who is one character alongside the others and interferes with them." [^4]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints prize libertarian agency as the bedrock of the plan of salvation, so any account that preserves real freedom against fatalism is welcome, and against the atheist's hard determinism Feser is a useful ally. Yet the author analogy carries a cost the Restoration resists. On Feser's picture, *every* detail of the story — including the murderer's choice — is sovereignly authored by God in a single timeless act; freedom is rescued only by insisting the Author operates on a different metaphysical level than the characters. Critics within the LDS tradition worry this is a distinction without a difference: if God unilaterally writes the script ex nihilo, creaturely "choices" risk being authored outcomes rather than genuinely originative acts ([[Creation Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism and Precludes Free Love]]). The Restoration instead grounds agency in *co-eternal* intelligences that God did not author into being, acting in a real, open present ([[Divine Foreknowledge as Real-Time Branching]]); on this view God persuades and respects choice rather than authoring it ([[God Will Not Force You Into Heaven Against Your Will]]). Be fair to Feser: his compatibility argument for *foreknowledge* (knowledge does not cause) is sound and valuable; the dispute is narrowly over whether divine *causation* of the future, in his strong sense, leaves room for the kind of ultimate creaturely origination Latter-day Saints affirm.
