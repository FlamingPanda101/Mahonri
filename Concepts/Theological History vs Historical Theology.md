# Theological History vs Historical Theology

## The Claim
[[Kyle Beshears]] draws a structural contrast: evangelicals are a people of **historical theology** (they prize doctrine-makers like Luther, Calvin, Spurgeon but care little for the *story* of how doctrine developed), whereas Latter-day Saints are a people of **theological history** — "a theology unfolding before them" in the latter days. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Evangelicals care about the doctrine, not the story.** Ask a conservative Presbyterian about John Calvin and "they're going to get the basics correct about when he lived... but tell me about the works John Calvin has created — now that's where the conversation's going to go." [^3] *Rating: a fair generalization* of the Reformed-evangelical relationship to tradition.

2. **Latter-day Saints invert it.** They are "not a people of a historical theology. They're a people of a theological history that they see in the latter days... a theology unfolding before them" — the First Vision, the [[Great Apostasy]], a living "prophet, seer and revelator" anticipating the Second Coming. [^4] *Rating: a genuinely illuminating frame;* LDS identity is narrative and ongoing-revelatory.

3. **Self-placement in the ongoing story.** "They see themselves as part of that narrative. Evangelicals don't see themselves as part of a church history narrative... We're very atomized. We're very individualistic. Latter-day Saints see their place in the next chapter of the latter days." [^5] *Rating: ties directly* to his communalism insight (see [[Saints Not Believers - LDS Communalism Lesson]]).

4. **Methodological payoff.** Because of this, Beshears argues the *ideal* way to teach Mormonism would fuse "the history and the doctrine at the same time," since for Latter-day Saints the two are inseparable — and would foreground *practice* over belief. [^6] *Rating: a coherent application* of the thesis to pedagogy.

**Steelman of the opposing view.** An evangelical historical-theologian could object that confessional Protestants (e.g., high-church Reformed or Lutherans) very much locate themselves in a continuous church-historical narrative, so the contrast overstates evangelical atomization. Beshears's claim is a tendency about *popular* evangelicalism, not a denial that confessional traditions exist.

## Best Quotes
> "Evangelicals care very deeply about historical theology... we don't really care about the story of how we got the doctrine. We care about the doctrine maker." [^3]

> "They're not a people of a historical theology. They're a people of a theological history that they see in the latter days — a theology unfolding before them." [^4]

> "Latter-day Saints see their place in the next chapter of the latter days." [^5]
