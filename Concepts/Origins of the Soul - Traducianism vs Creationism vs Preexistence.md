# Origins of the Soul — Traducianism vs Creationism vs Preexistence

## The Point
Early Christians had no settled doctrine on where the human soul comes from. Three competing theories developed, and the dominant one varied by region and era. The very existence of this dispute shows that the origin of the soul was an *open question* in the early Church — and that [[Origen]]'s preexistence model was one live option before being rejected, leaving traducianism and creationism to compete in the West.

## Evidence
1. **Preexistence (Origen):** souls created by God before time, who fell into bodies. Rejected by later Christians as "based on Platonism and not scripture," and condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople (553).[^1]
2. **Traducianism ([[Tertullian]], [[Gregory of Nyssa]]):** souls are "transmitted from parents to children in the act of conception." According to [[Jerome]], this was "the major interpretation in the Western Church in his day."[^2]
3. **Creationism ([[Jerome]]):** "God is continually making souls for each individual at the time of their 'conception, birth, or sometime in between.'"[^3]
4. Tertullian's version is a *seminal identity* theory: souls are corporeal, mirror the physical body, and derive from the parents — so "every soul derives from Adam," and "by reason of its birth, has its nature in [fallen] Adam until it is born again in Christ." This seminal identity "laid one of the foundations for Augustine's idea of original guilt."[^4]
5. The shift away from Origen was driven by rising emphasis on doctrinal unity, literal scripture reading, and the influence of [[Aristotle]] (who held the true self is body *and* soul, inseparable).[^5]

## Best Quotes
> Traducianism was, "according to Jerome, the major interpretation in the Western Church in his day." [^2]

> Every soul, "by reason of its birth, has its nature in [fallen] Adam until it is born again in Christ." — [[Tertullian]] [^4]

## LDS Connection
None of the three patristic options matches the LDS doctrine of literal premortal spirit children of Heavenly Parents (Abraham 3:22–23; D&C 93:29). Origen's preexistence is the closest, but it is a *Platonic* preexistence of disembodied intellects, not begotten spirit offspring. The dispute is useful apologetically: it shows the Church never had a unified, revealed doctrine of the soul's origin, undercutting claims that LDS premortality contradicts a fixed "Christian" teaching.
