# Rationalization C — The Third Thing Harmonization

## The Concept

Rationalization C is Dan McClellan's term for the universal pattern in biblical inerrancy apologetics. When faced with two biblical passages that appear to contradict each other (Passage A and Passage B), the apologist — presupposing that the Bible cannot contradict itself — creates an **entirely new third thing, Rationalization C**, which is not found in either text but functions as a unifying framework to harmonize them. McClellan uses the letter "C" to emphasize that this harmonization is EXTERNAL to both texts — it is imported by the interpreter, not drawn from the texts themselves.[^1]

## The Pattern: A-B-C

1. **Passage A:** "Two of every animal" ([[Genesis 6.19-20|Genesis 6:19-20]])
2. **Passage B:** "Seven pairs of clean animals and birds" ([[Genesis 7.2-3|Genesis 7:2-3]])
3. **Rationalization C (apologist's invention):** "Two was the baseline for survival; the extra five pairs were added for sacrifice."

But C is not stated in either A or B. It is created because the apologist NEEDS A and B to agree. As McClellan puts it: "You have to create idea or argument or rationalization C — the entirely new separate third thing that's not in the text that functions as a unifying framework to try to harmonize text A and text B." [^2]

## Examples from This Source

### Example 1: God's Visibility
- **Passage A:** Jacob saw God face to face ([[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]])
- **Passage B:** No one has ever seen God ([[John 1.18|John 1:18]])
- **Rationalization C1 (limited essence):** "They saw God in a reduced form — like seeing sunlight without staring at the sun." This concept is found NOWHERE in either text. "The notion that there were appearances of God where the glory was ratcheted down and they didn't see the full essence, which is a concept that you cannot find anywhere in the Hebrew Bible, is that separate third thing." [^3]
- **Rationalization C2 (pre-incarnate Christ):** "They saw Jesus, not the Father." Also nowhere stated in either text. The OT authors never identify the being as "the Son." This is a retroactive Christian reinterpretation.

### Example 2: Noah's Ark Animal Count
- **Passage A:** Two of every animal and bird (Gen 6:19–20)
- **Passage B:** Seven pairs of clean animals and birds (Gen 7:2–3)
- **Rationalization C:** "Two was the baseline; seven pairs were extra for sacrifice."
- **Why C fails:** The bird clause in B makes no clean/unclean distinction — ALL birds are seven pairs. "That does not fit the notion that it's just two as a baseline, but for all the clean animals, we're going to do seven pairs. It just doesn't fit." [^4]

## Why Rationalization C Is Always Weak

### 1. It Is Not Drawn from the Text
"These rationalizations are overwhelmingly weak, inadequate, and fallacious." [^5] They are not discovered in the text through careful exegesis; they are INVENTED by the apologist to resolve a cognitive dissonance the text itself does not resolve.

### 2. It Presupposes What It Must Prove
The entire enterprise of creating C only makes sense if you have ALREADY decided A and B cannot contradict. Absent that presupposition, you can simply acknowledge that A and B reflect different authors, different time periods, and different theological conceptions. See [[Begging the Question in Biblical Inerrancy Apologetics]].

### 3. It Ignores the Scholarly Consensus
In both examples, the scholarship overwhelmingly supports the conclusion that the texts genuinely reflect different traditions. The flood account is composed of two interwoven sources ([[Noahs Ark Source Criticism Two Flood Accounts]]). God's visibility changed from corporeal to incorporeal under Greek influence ([[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]]). Rationalization C requires ignoring all of this.

### 4. It Is Driven by Identity, Not Evidence
"The apologetic endeavor is not about finding out what's true. It's not about defending what's true. It's not about what's most likely. It is about defending dogmas and making the people who accept those dogmas feel justified and validated." [^6] See [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]].

## Best Quotes

> "As long as we assume that and we see passage A and passage B and they seem to conflict, well, we have just misunderstood them and we need to seek out rationalization C — the separate third thing that functions as a unifying framework to bring passages A and B together." [^1]

> "The notion that there were appearances of God where the glory was ratcheted down and they didn't see the full essence, which is a concept that you cannot find anywhere in the Hebrew Bible, is that separate third thing." [^3]

> "This is a rationalization that has been jinned up by someone who refuses to accept the existence of a contradiction. And when you refuse to accept the existence of a contradiction and then you engage an argument about whether or not there's a contradiction, you're begging the question." [^7]
