# The Nine Woes of 2 Nephi 9 Are Inverted Beatitudes
#type/claim

## The Claim
The closing "woes" of Jacob's temple sermon in [[2 Nephi 9.30-38|2 Nephi 9:30-38]] are not a Nephite "new Ten Commandments" (as an earlier FARMS reading proposed) but a deliberate **inversion of the Beatitudes**. Where a beatitude runs "Blessed are X, for they shall Y," each woe runs "Woe unto X, for they shall Y" — the same grammatical frame turned to a curse. There are **nine** woes (not ten), matching the **nine** beatitude-statements of the Sermon on the Mount, with **seven** repeated punishments mirroring the Sermon's seven distinct blessings. Because Jacob preached ~550 years before Jesus, the parallel implies the Beatitude pattern is older covenant temple language that Jesus later took up, not a Christian invention.

## Supporting Evidence
- **Structural match, not a commandment list.** [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] notes the Decalogue reads "Thou shalt not X," but Jacob's lines read "Woe unto X, *for they shall* Y" — the inverse of "Blessed are X, *for they shall* Y." The form is beatitude-shaped, so reading it as a commandment list misclassifies the genre.
- **Number correspondences.** Nine woes align with the nine beatitude-statements; the seven repeated punishments ("perish," "thrust down to hell") align with the seven distinct blessings of the Sermon. The counts are presented as too tight to be coincidental.
- **Chronological payoff.** Jacob antedates the Sermon on the Mount by roughly five and a half centuries, so a genuine structural dependence runs *from* older temple covenant rhetoric *to* Jesus — evidence (for the LDS reader) of an ancient Beatitude pattern preserved on the Nephite record. This is set within Jacob's sermon as a [[The Day of Atonement|Day of Atonement]] / Feast-of-Tabernacles temple speech.
- **Steelman.** A skeptic will note that "blessing and curse" pairs are a widespread ancient Near Eastern covenant form (Deuteronomy 27-28), so woe-formulas mirroring blessing-formulas need not point to the specific Matthean Beatitudes; the number-matching can also look like motivated counting (how one delimits "nine" woes or "seven" punishments is interpreter-dependent). The claim is strongest as a literary observation about inverted beatitude *form*, more tentative as proof of direct dependence on the Sermon on the Mount. This is the narrower, form-focused counterpart to the broader argument in [[The Nine Woes of 2 Nephi 9 Are Inverted Beatitudes Not New Ten Commandments]].
