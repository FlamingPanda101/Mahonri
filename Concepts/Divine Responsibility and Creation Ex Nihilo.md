# Divine Responsibility and Creation Ex Nihilo

## The Claim

If God creates everything **from nothing** (*creatio ex nihilo*) and **sustains**
all things in existence at every moment, then God bears **ultimate responsibility**
for every attribute, action, and destiny of his creatures — including their
damnation.

## The Logic

Since:
1. Creatures have no nature to exist apart from God's will
2. God actualizes all potential attributes through creative will
3. God sustains creatures and their properties continuously
4. God has perfect foreknowledge of what each creature will do

Therefore: God is causally and morally responsible for what each creature
becomes. An atheist's failure to believe, a Buddhist's rejection of Christ,
a thief's propensity to steal — all trace back to the attributes God willed
into existence and sustains. [^1]

## The Wind-Up-and-Go Refutation

Some Christians compare God to a **watchmaker** who creates a watch and then
lets it run independently, attempting to sidestep divine blame. This analogy
fails because:

1. **God created the materials** ex nihilo — if they tarnish or diminish,
   that's on him
2. **God created and sustains the universal constants** — gravity, entropy,
   the laws of physics — that govern the watch's operation
3. If a watch hand slips out of place, it's because of the physical laws
   God authored and sustains

[[Jonathan Kvanvig]]:

> "This analogy is defective to the core. What allows the watchmaker to make
> a watch that works without his continued involvement in its operation is
> the structure of the universe the watchmaker uses to his advantage in order
> to secure the continued operation of the watch." [^2]

"Attempting to avoid the implications of creation from nothing is a hopeless
project." [^2]

## Gregory of Nissa's Moral Claim

[[Gregory of Nissa]] recognized that creatio ex nihilo is not merely an
ontological/metaphysical claim — it carries **moral weight** about God's
nature itself. If God is the only uncreated being and creates everything
freely, then **the moral destiny of creation and the moral nature of God are
absolutely inseparable**. [^3]

## David Bentley Hart's Conclusion

[[David Bentley Hart]] argues:

> "Christians must say that God creates freely. They must believe that his
> final judgment shall reveal him for who he is. If God created everything
> from nothing, if God is choosing freely to create, and God is truly the
> all-loving God revealed in Christ, then an eternal hell could not possibly
> exist. These propositions cannot possibly be reconciled." [^3]

If you believe in an omnipotent, omni-benevolent God creating from nothing yet
also believe some souls end in the worst possible state of affairs, then you
must hold that **God is creating defective and imperfect creations** — which
contradicts his claimed perfection. [^3]
