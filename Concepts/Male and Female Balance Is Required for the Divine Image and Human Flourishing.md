# Male and Female Balance Is Required for the Divine Image and Human Flourishing

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jacob Hansen]] frames the secular argument (that masculinity and femininity must *balance* for optimal social outcomes) as "a central theme of LDS theology."[^1] The Latter-day Saint claim is stronger than mere social complementarity: **the union of male and female is itself the fullest image of God, and the ordering of the sexes toward each other is built into eternal identity and the plan of exaltation.** Reading [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]] ("in the image of God... male and female created he them") through [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew 19:4-6]] ("the two shall be one flesh... what God hath joined together") and [[1 Corinthians 11.11|1 Corinthians 11:11]] ("neither is the man without the woman... in the Lord"), Hansen presents man and woman as two halves of a single whole — not interchangeable, not independent, but complementary.[^2]

The Proclamation-style assertion follows: **"Gender is an essential characteristic of individual premortal, mortal, and eternal identity and purpose,"** and the highest degree of celestial glory requires entering "the new and everlasting covenant of marriage" — exaltation "can only be attained through faithfulness to the covenants of an eternal marriage between a man and a woman" ([[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132:19-20]]).[^3] Where most Christian traditions read [[Matthew 22.30|Matthew 22:30]] ("in the resurrection they neither marry nor are given in marriage") as ending gendered marriage, LDS theology distinctively holds that *properly sealed* marriage endures eternally — so the male-female union is not merely a mortal arrangement but the structure of deified life.

Hansen closes with Brett McKay's image (via Aristotelian virtue ethics): manhood is not the opposite of womanhood but the opposite of *childhood*; men and women "strive for the same virtues but often attain and express them in different ways," like "two different musical instruments playing the exact same notes" — different timbre, neither superior, "each adds beauty to the world."[^4]

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman)

1. **The scriptural image-of-God argument has real exegetical footing.** [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]] does pair "the image of God" immediately with "male and female," and a respectable strand of interpretation (Barth, and in a different key Margaret Barker's divine-pair readings) takes the image as *relational/dyadic* rather than individual. [[1 Corinthians 11.11|1 Cor 11:11]]'s "neither without the other in the Lord" supplies a NT mutual-dependence text. *(Strong on the text-pairing; the inference from "image" to "marital complementarity specifically" is a theological reading, not a forced one.)*

2. **It coheres with the well-being case.** If (per [[Womanism Is Misnamed Masculinism That Devalues the Feminine]]) a society starved of feminine values becomes depressed and unbalanced, then a theology that *institutionalizes* the balance of masculine and feminine — in marriage as the basic unit — has a built-in corrective. The empirical claim (declining female happiness amid a masculinist value structure) and the doctrinal claim reinforce each other.[^1] *(Internally coherent; the empirical premise is interpretive — see the parent concept.)*

3. **The "different instruments, same notes" model answers the equality/difference dilemma elegantly.** It affirms *equal worth and equal virtue* (same notes) while preserving *real difference in expression* (different timbre), sidestepping both "the sexes are interchangeable" and "one sex is superior."[^4] This is a genuine middle path in the equality-vs-difference feminism debate the video references. *(Strong as a values statement; under-determines the contested practical questions — ordination, division of labor — which it does not resolve.)*

4. **The eternal-marriage reading of Genesis/Matthew is distinctive and bold.** Treating [[Matthew 22.30|Matthew 22:30]] as denying only *new* marriages contracted by the unsealed (not abolishing sealed marriage) is a minority reading, but it gives the male-female union ultimate ontological weight rather than mortal-only significance. *(Coherent within LDS premises; contested exegetically by the broader Christian tradition.)*

**Honest counterweight.** The argument is a *theological synthesis*, persuasive chiefly to those already granting LDS premises (continuing revelation, eternal sealing, the Proclamation's authority). Three pressure points: (a) the slide from "male and female bear God's image" to "*marriage* between male and female is required for the highest glory" is a substantive leap the biblical texts alone do not compel; (b) reading "gender is essential to eternal identity" as fixed binary sits uneasily with the video's own concession that masculinity/femininity are *psychological admixtures* present in everyone — if every person blends both, the move to a hard binary of *roles* needs more work; (c) critics like [[Julie Hanks]] will say this aesthetically lovely "different instruments" framing has historically been used to justify keeping the second instrument off the stage. The note in [[Modern Gender Equality Is Not Grounded in the Bible]] presses the parallel worry that "equal worth before God" has not, historically, translated into "equal standing," and that the gap was closed by movements, not theology.
