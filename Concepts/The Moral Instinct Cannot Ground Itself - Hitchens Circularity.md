# The Moral Instinct Cannot Ground Itself - Hitchens Circularity

## The Point
[[Christopher Hitchens]]' positive account of morality was an appeal to a shared inner instinct — parental love, Socrates' daemon, Adam Smith's "internal witness," "any person of average moral equipment has the same knowledge" [^1]. [[Alex OConnor]] shows this cannot answer the grounding question because it is *circular*: pressed on how we know whose intuitions are *correct*, Hitchens says you'll have the right instinct "if you have the proper moral equipment" — but the only test offered for having proper equipment is having the correct instinct [^2]. O'Connor: "this is the definition of circularity" [^2]. Worse, Hitchens unwittingly conceded the very gap at issue, granting that the instinct leaves "wide open the question of why it exists and how we can justify it," and that some people genuinely lack it (the "sociopathic" and "psychopathic") — which only sharpens the unanswered question of why *their* equipment is defective and his is sound [^3].

## Evidence
- Hitchens' appeal to authority figures (Socrates, Adam Smith, against C.S. Lewis) is presented as *settling* the matter; O'Connor: "again Hitchens has asked of those moral instincts... how does he know that they are objectively correct[?] His answer: well, Socrates answered this... That's not the question" [^1].
- Hitchens admits not everyone shares the instinct ("there are people to whom those thoughts do not occur... deaf to that idea"), labeling them sociopaths/psychopaths — but, O'Connor notes, "the question is *why* we're justified in saying that they have it wrong, which you can't answer by simply calling them psychopaths" [^4].
- The interviewer's pointed framing ("we have many such innate intuitions, some of which are false, so how can we know that the moral intuition... is actually correct?") is met only with a re-assertion of the instinct: "it just amazes me that this passes for a serious argument" [^5].

## Best Quotes
> "You'll have the correct moral instinct if you have the proper moral equipment. Well, how do we know who has the proper moral equipment? Of course — because they have the correct moral instinct. This is the definition of circularity." [^2]

> "We have many intuitions; how do we know which ones are actually correct? His answer: because we have a moral intuition. It just amazes me that this passes for a serious argument." [^5]

## Apologetic Engagement
The classical moral argument (see [[The Moral Lawgiver Argument]], [[Moral Argument Against Atheism]]) contends that bare evolved instinct cannot generate *binding, objective* obligation — an "is" (we feel this urge) never yields an "ought" (this urge is authoritative). O'Connor, an atheist, here grants the core of that complaint against the most famous popular-atheist answer: appealing to the *existence* of moral feeling is not *grounding* its truth, and labeling dissenters "psychopaths" presupposes the very standard in question. This mirrors the standard reply to Sam Harris ([[Harris Describes Morality But Cannot Ground Concern for Others]]). Be careful not to overclaim: O'Connor does not conclude "therefore God." A consistent atheist could accept the circularity and embrace anti-realism or nihilism (cf. [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]]) — and O'Connor himself leans toward conceding objective morality may be unfounded. The apologetic payoff is the dilemma it forces: *either* find a non-circular naturalist grounding *or* admit morality is ungrounded — Hitchens' confident instinct-talk does neither.
