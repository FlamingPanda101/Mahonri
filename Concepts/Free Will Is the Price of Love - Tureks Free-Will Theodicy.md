# Free Will Is the Price of Love - Turek's Free-Will Theodicy

## The Point
Granting that atheism cannot ground evil, the Christian still faces the question: *If God, why evil?* Turek's first answer is the free-will defense. For God to abolish moral evil He would have to remove human free will — "but if He takes away our free will, then He takes away our ability to love as well. God didn't want robots."[^1] Free will, while it makes evil possible, is the only thing that makes genuine love and "ultimate good" possible. Evil is the cost of a creation capable of love rather than mere mechanism.

## Evidence
The argument arose in a hostile Q&A at Michigan State, where a student demanded, "If there is a good God, then why does evil exist? Why doesn't God stop it?" Turek's reply begins disarmingly: if God stopped all evil, "He might start with you . . . and me because we both do evil every day" — we always picture *other* people's evil, never our own.[^2] Pressed on **natural** evil ("natural disasters aren't the result of free will!"), Turek concedes such disasters are not the result of anyone's free will *today*, but traces them to a freewill choice of Adam, leaving us in "a fallen, broken world where bad things happen."[^3] Crucially, God does not stay aloof from the evil He permits: the one-word theme of the Bible is **redemption** — "Paradise lost in Genesis is paradise regained in Revelation" — and God achieved it by entering the world in Jesus Christ, the only wholly innocent person in history, who "suffered horribly for our redemption" and "brought good from evil."[^4] Turek also reframes the purpose of life: God is "a father, not a grandfather," not obligated to keep us comfortable for eighty years; pain "awakens us to God and then refines us for God," because "comfort is temporary but character is eternal."[^5] In the summary he ties it together: "evil came about through the exercise of free will (which is necessary for love to exist)," yet "God is not doing evil at all—we are. We are the rebels."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "For God to end evil on earth, He would have to take away our free will. But if He takes away our free will, then He takes away our ability to love as well. God didn't want robots." [^1]

> "God is not doing evil at all—we are. We are the rebels. While God holds all things together and is responsible for the fact of freedom, we free creatures are responsible for our acts of freedom." [^6]

> "This world isn't a good resort, but it's a great gymnasium." [^7]

## LDS Connection
The free-will theodicy is where Turek and the Restoration converge most powerfully — and where the LDS framework arguably gives the defense a firmer foundation. Latter-day Saint scripture makes **moral agency** the very architecture of the plan of salvation: man is "free to choose liberty and eternal life... or to choose captivity and death" (2 Nephi 2:27), and a world without genuine opposition would have "no purpose in the end of its creation" (2 Nephi 2:11). Turek's "gymnasium not resort" maps almost exactly onto the LDS view of mortality as a probationary "second estate" for growth (Abraham 3:25–26). Two distinctive Restoration resources strengthen the case: (1) a **premortal council** in which spirits chose to accept a plan involving agency and risk, so freedom is not merely imposed but consented to; and (2) the doctrine that intelligences are **co-eternal and uncaused**, giving libertarian agency a metaphysical grounding that creation-ex-nihilo theism struggles to secure (see [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality]]). One point of difference to flag honestly: Turek roots natural evil in an inherited Fall of Adam in a "broken world," whereas LDS theology reads the Fall more positively — a necessary, foreseen step ("Adam fell that men might be," 2 Nephi 2:25) — so mortal hardship is less a tragic rupture than a designed condition for soul-making. The shared core stands: God permits evil to honor the freedom without which love is impossible, and He redeems rather than merely tolerates it.
