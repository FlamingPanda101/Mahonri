# Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible

## The Point
The [[King Follett Discourse]] and Lorenzo Snow's couplet ("As man now is, God once was. As God now is, man may become") taught a hierarchical cosmos of divine beings and humans progressing toward divine status — dismissed by critics as new-age mythology. Arnold argues that the divine-council theology [[Michael Heiser]] recovered from the Hebrew text, plus the Dead Sea Scrolls and [[Margaret Barker]]'s First Temple work, were unavailable to [[Joseph Smith]] in 1830, yet match what he described rather than what 19th-century scholarship described.

## Evidence
- **Heiser's divine council.** Heiser (PhD in Hebrew Bible and Semitic languages, University of Wisconsin; a Protestant) concluded in *The Unseen Realm* that "the original Hebrew worldview included a cosmos with multiple divine beings in a hierarchical structure with humans created in the image of that divine council and with deification language, humans progressing toward divine status, genuinely present throughout the text." Arnold stresses, "This is all mainstream scholarship." [^1]
- **Psalm 82.** God "stands in a divine assembly telling its members, 'You are gods, sons of the Most High,'" and Heiser argues the contextual, grammatical, and ANE comparative evidence points to "actual divine beings, not a metaphor for human judges." [^2] Jesus quotes the verse in John 10:34 to defend his own divine identity, "an argument that only works if the verse is actually about genuine divine status." [^3] (See [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]].)
- **The Dead Sea Scrolls reading of Deuteronomy 32:8.** The older DSS manuscript "describes God dividing the nations according to the number of the sons of God, not the sons of Israel as later manuscripts read... The Nicene theological framework just filtered it out. The Hebrew still has it." [^4] (See [[Deuteronomy 32 Dead Sea Scrolls Monolatry]].)
- **The timing argument.** "Joseph Smith didn't have access to Heiser's research, the Dead Sea Scrolls, or Barker's reconstruction... He was working in 1830. So when his descriptions of the ancient world turn out to match what scholars recovered a century later from sources that he never saw, that's the argument." [^5]

## Best Quotes
