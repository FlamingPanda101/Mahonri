# Trinity Did Not Exist When John Was Written

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
When Jesus says "I and the Father are one" in [[John 10.30|John 10:30]], "his listeners knew exactly what he meant" — namely that he was declaring the Trinity, the doctrine that Father and Son are co-equal, co-eternal, consubstantial persons of one divine essence.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The doctrine is anachronistic to the text (strong)
**[[Dan McClellan]]** argues the central error is reading a 4th-century doctrine back into a 1st/2nd-century text. "The notion of Jesus as one of three co-equal, co-eternal, consubstantial persons comprising the single substance or essence of the one God of Israel simply did not exist at the end of the 1st century CE or the beginning of the 2nd century CE." [^1] Whatever the listeners understood, it cannot have been a doctrine that did not yet exist.

This is sound on the standard scholarly timeline: even in its "most inchoate form" Trinitarian thinking only emerges "in the 3rd century CE at the earliest," and "the actual formal declaration of the trinity as it is understood today doesn't happen till the beginning of the 4th century CE." [^2] (Conventionally located at the Council of Nicaea, 325 CE, and refined through Constantinople, 381 CE.)

### 2. Even Jesus' followers in the narrative do not grasp it (strong)
Within John's own story, "Jesus's followers didn't even really understand what he meant by saying I and the father are one. He has to explain it to them and convince them about it in John chapter 14." [^3] If the immediate disciples needed the oneness explained ([[John 14.9|John 14:9]]), the claim that hostile bystanders instantly understood it as Trinitarian dogma is implausible.

### 3. Steelman of the apologist
The strongest form of the opposing view is not that the *word* "Trinity" was present, but that John's high christology contains the *raw materials* later systematized as the Trinity (pre-existence, the Logos, "the Word was God," Thomas's confession). McClellan does not deny John has a high christology; he denies that this christology is *consubstantial Trinitarianism* rather than **divine mediation / image christology** — see [[Image Christology in the Gospel of John]]. The dispute is therefore over *which* high christology John holds, not whether he has one.
