# Finite Sin vs Infinite Punishment

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Some Christians (e.g., [[Jonathan Edwards]], [[William Wainwright]]) argue that
**all sin is worthy of eternal punishment because all sin is committed against
an infinite being** (God). Therefore, finite mortal actions — from failing to
believe in the Trinity to genocide — are justly punished by the same infinite
torment.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Violation of Proportionality

> "It is almost a universally held maxim that the punishment should in some
> way match the crime — and that's not the case here." [^1]

All mortal actions receive the identical punishment (eternal torture), which
contravenes every intuitive and legal standard of proportional justice.

### 2. The Finitude Problem

[[David Bentley Hart]]:

> "Retribution consisting in unending suffering imposed as recompense for the
> actions of a finite intellect and will must be by any sound definition
> disproportionate, unjust, and at the last nothing more than an expression
> of sheer pointless cruelty. Why are the actions of a finite being with
> finite consequences being punished eternally?" [^1]

Only an **infinite crime** could warrant **infinite punishment** — and no
mortal action qualifies as infinite.

### 3. The Impassibility Problem

If God is **impassible** (unable to be affected by anything external, "without
passions" as the creeds state), then sin literally **cannot harm or affect
God**. How can an action that leaves God entirely unmoved warrant eternal
suffering for the actor? The punishment becomes even more unjustifiable when
the supposed victim is incapable of being victimized. [^2]

### 4. The Ease-of-Sin Problem

If all sin is against an infinite being, then the system God created makes
infinite sins **remarkably easy to commit**:

- Simply denying or failing to understand the Trinity = infinite sin
- Committing murder = infinite sin
- Both receive the SAME eternal punishment

God created beings from nothing in a system that endows them with **infinite
guilt and condemnation if they make even one single mistake** — often without
even knowing they've done it. The creator of such a system bears responsibility
for its extreme consequences. [^2]

## The Wes Huff Example

[[Wes Huff]] represents the position rebutted:

> "There's no such thing as an innocent person... I deserve hell 100% of the
> time. That's like the wages of sin is death... However, I am not going to
> heaven because of anything I've done." [^3]

The host's response: "If none is good but God, whose fault is that? God is
the creator and sustainer of your soul." Either God knowingly creates depraved
souls doomed for damnation, or he isn't all-powerful enough to create souls
that aren't depraved. [^4]
