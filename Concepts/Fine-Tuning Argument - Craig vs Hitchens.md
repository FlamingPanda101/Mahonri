# Fine-Tuning Argument - Craig vs Hitchens

## The Point
Craig's second argument is teleological: the initial conditions and constants of the Big Bang are "fine-tuned for the existence of intelligent life with a precision and delicacy that literally defy human comprehension." There are only three possible explanations — physical necessity, chance, or design. It is not necessity (the constants are independent of the laws), and not chance (the odds are "vanishingly small," and the multiverse escape predicts we should observe a far smaller orderly region — Penrose's "utter chicken feed" point). Therefore the best explanation is design.[^1]

Hitchens replies that fine-tuning is "essentially another form of pattern-seeking on the basis of extremely limited evidence." Physicists are properly "extremely uncertain" about the universe's earliest spatio-temporal episode; to "see the finger of God in this process" either deserves an unwon Nobel Prize or "a charge of hubris." More forcefully, he turns the data against design: the cosmos is full of "shooting stars, collapsed suns, failed galaxies," the Sun will incinerate the Earth, the Andromeda galaxy is on a collision course, and expansion is accelerating toward heat death — "this is not fine-tuning." If it *is* a designer's work, "then there's an indictment to which that designer may have to be subjected."[^2]

Craig answers that scientists are *not* uncertain about fine-tuning — citing Martin Rees and others — and that "we're headed toward nothingness" is irrelevant to design, since "the temporal duration of something is irrelevant to whether it's been designed." The real objection, he says, is "why would God create mankind only to have it go extinct," and that assumes atheism is true; on Christian theism "life does not end at the grave."[^3]

## Evidence
**Craig (affirmative):**
- Constants (e.g., the gravitational constant) and arbitrary quantities (e.g., entropy, matter/antimatter balance) fall into an "extraordinarily narrow range"; the weak force altered by one part in 10^100 would forbid life.[^1]
- The multiverse is ad hoc and self-defeating: if ours were a random member, it is "inconceivably more probable" we'd observe a region no larger than the solar system (Penrose), which "strongly disconfirms the multiverse hypothesis."[^4]
- Fine-tuning is robust, not vanishing: "the laws governing our universe appear to be finely tuned for our existence" (Martin Rees), and Urn/McMullin say the constraints are "too specific and too numerous to evaporate."[^3]

**Hitchens (negative):**
- It is "pattern-seeking on the basis of extremely limited evidence"; "we hardly know what we don't know about the origins of the universe."[^5]
- The visible cosmos is wasteful, cruel, and incompetent — collapsing suns, failed galaxies, a coming heat death and galactic collision: "is this fine-tuning, or is it extremely random, capricious, cruel, mysterious, and incompetent?"[^2]
- A designer responsible for so much destruction faces "an indictment."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "Were these constants or quantities to be altered by less than a hair's breadth, the balance would be destroyed and life would not exist." [^1]

> "Is this fine-tuning, or is it extremely random, capricious, cruel, mysterious, and incompetent?... if it's the work of a designer, then there's an indictment to which that designer may have to be subjected." (Hitchens) [^2]

> "The temporal duration of something is irrelevant to whether it's been designed. The products of human intelligence... will eventually decay and cease to exist, but that doesn't mean they weren't designed." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The fine-tuning inference — that the life-permitting constants point to an intelligent Designer rather than brute chance — supports theism against atheism and is fully embraceable by Latter-day Saints (compare [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from Alma 30]] and [[Fine-Tuning - Book of Mormon Anticipation of Modern Cosmology]]). Hitchens' "wastefulness and coming nothingness" objection has special bite against a classical *creator ex nihilo* who supposedly made everything from nothing for one species; it lands more softly against the Restoration picture, in which God *organizes* pre-existing eternal matter and works within eternal law over vast time, and in which embodied human life is part of an eternal progression rather than a one-off. Craig's own reply — that "duration is irrelevant to design" and that the grave is not the end because of the resurrection — is a point a Latter-day Saint can affirm even more strongly, given the doctrines of pre-mortal existence and eternal life.
