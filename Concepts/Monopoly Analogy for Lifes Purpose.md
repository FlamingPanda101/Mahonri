# Monopoly Analogy for Life's Purpose

## The Claim

[[David Butler]] presents an analogy to illustrate how a purely temporal,
carnal focus misses the true purpose of life — and how atheism, by denying
eternal purpose, traps people in a meaningless game. Playing Monopoly has two
levels of purpose: (1) the in-game goal (acquire property, bankrupt opponents)
and (2) the meta-purpose (have fun, build relationships). If you treat the
in-game goal as the *only* purpose — taking Monopoly "deadly seriously" — you
destroy the relationships that are the real reason for playing. Similarly,
treating mortal life's temporal goals (wealth, status, pleasure) as the only
purpose — as atheism in effect does — misses the eternal purpose and ends in
ruined relationships and existential emptiness. Only by recognizing the greater
purpose outside the "game board" of mortality can life be lived well.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Analogy (Evidence Rating: Primary — Illustrative)

Butler lays out the scenario:

> "Let's say we break out a board game here. We, us three, we play Monopoly,
> right? What's the purpose of Monopoly? ... When I ask people, I'm like,
> 'What's the purpose of Monopoly? What's the goal?' And they're like, 'Well,
> you got to get all the houses on Park Place. You got to charge the rent. You
> got to get all the money. You got to create a monopoly.' Right? And then
> that's just you thinking within the four sides of the game board, right? If
> you zoom out just a half bit, you're like, 'Oh, this is for us to have fun.
> This is for us to have a good experience with one another.' There's a greater
> purpose outside of just the monotonous things that you do."[^1]

The two levels:
- **In-game purpose:** Acquire wealth and property, win.
- **Meta-purpose:** Enjoy fellowship, build relationships, have fun.

### 2. Taking the Game Too Seriously Destroys Relationships (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Practical)

> "If you take it seriously and the whole purpose, right, is to get all the
> things, you will ruin all your relationships. If everyone takes that deadly
> seriously, you will ruin your relationships and you'll have to find someone
> else to play Monopoly with because they're not going to do it again."[^2]

This is a parable about mortality: if you treat career success, wealth
accumulation, or sensory pleasure as the ULTIMATE purpose — rather than as a
means to an eternal end — you'll destroy the very relationships that make life
meaningful. The person who "wins" the economic game but alienates everyone
they love has missed the point of playing.

### 3. Scriptural Grounding (Evidence Rating: Primary — Scriptural)

Butler ties the analogy to the Book of Mormon:

> "That's why in the Book of Mormon specifically when it talks about... being
> spiritually minded is life eternal and being carnally minded is death.
> Because being carnally minded is just being obsessed with the things that
> we're doing in this world that doesn't have a greater purpose outside of
> it."[^1]

Reference: [[2 Nephi 9.39|2 Nephi 9:39]] — \"To be carnally-minded is death, and to be
spiritually-minded is life eternal.\" The "carnal mind" is the person who only
sees the game board — who treats temporal concerns as ultimate. The "spiritual
mind" sees the meta-purpose — eternal joy, covenant relationships, returning to
God.

### 4. Connection to the LDS Concept of Happiness as the End Goal (Evidence Rating: Primary — Doctrinal)

Butler traces the chain of purpose:

> "What did Joseph say? The object and design of our existence is to find
> happiness. And ultimately you can even take that as far as why did Jesus die?
> It's like so that we could return to the Father. Well, why do you want to
> return to the Father? So that we can be exalted. Well, why do you want to be
> exalted? So we can do what He does. Well, why do you want to do what He does?
> It's like the end goal is joy."[^3]

The Monopoly analogy maps: the in-game mechanics (commandments, covenants,
ordinances) are means to the meta-purpose (joy, eternal life, eternal
relationships). Confusing the mechanics for the purpose is the error of the
Pharisees — and of the atheist who sees only material transactions.

### 5. Atheism Traps You on the Game Board (Evidence Rating: Tertiary)

Without an eternal perspective, there IS no meta-purpose — the game board IS
all there is. On atheism, the Monopoly game ends and the board goes back in the
box and nothing mattered. As Butler says: "We forget why we're here."[^4]

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: The Analogy Presupposes an External Purpose
**Steelman:** The Monopoly analogy only works if you already believe there IS a
meta-purpose. The atheist denies exactly that — they claim the "game board" IS
all there is. Butler is begging the question by using an analogy where the
meta-purpose is obvious and then asserting that life has the same structure.

**Response:** Butler would likely respond that the analogy is not intended as a
standalone proof but as an illustration that makes the theistic framework
intelligible. The evidence for a meta-purpose comes from elsewhere — the
fine-tuning argument, the moral argument, the historical case for the
resurrection, personal religious experience. The Monopoly analogy shows what's
at stake: if you're wrong about the meta-purpose, you've missed the real reason
for playing. It's a Pascal's-wager-type existential argument, not a deductive
proof.

### Objection 2: You Can Find Meaning IN the Game
**Steelman:** The joy of Monopoly is IN the playing — the strategy, the
competition, the shared experience. You don't need an external purpose to enjoy
or find meaning in the game. Similarly, life can be meaningful in the living,
without an eternal payoff. Butler's analogy actually undermines his point: if
Monopoly is fun despite having no cosmic significance, so is life.

**Response:** Monopoly is fun partly *because* you know it's temporary and
low-stakes — the relationships survive the game because the game doesn't
ultimately matter. But if life IS the only game and death is permanent
elimination, the stakes are different. The joy of playing a game that ends in
the destruction of all players and the board is harder to sustain. And more
fundamentally: Monopoly is fun because it's a *shared* activity among
*conscious* agents with *free will* who *choose* to spend time together — all
things atheism struggles to ground (see [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]]).

### Objection 3: Instrumentalizing Life for an Afterlife Devalues Present Life
**Steelman:** If this life is just a means to an eternal end, then present
suffering, injustice, and joy are devalued. This can lead to passivity in the
face of evil ("God will sort it out in the next life") and neglect of present
goods. The atheist's focus on THIS life arguably produces MORE ethical urgency
and appreciation for present experience.

**Response:** Butler would likely reject the premise: in LDS theology, this
life is not a disposable means but an integral phase of eternal progression.
What we do here — the relationships we form, the covenants we make, the
character we develop — persists into eternity. The "game" matters BECAUSE it
connects to the meta-purpose, not despite it. And LDS theology's emphasis on
building Zion, caring for the poor, and establishing justice in THIS life
counters the claim that eternal perspective breeds passivity.
