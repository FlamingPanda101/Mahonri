# Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues that the Deutero-Isaiah / Deuteronomy "negation texts" — "there is none beside me," "I am, and there is no other," idols are "nothing" ([[Isaiah 41.24|Isaiah 41:24]]) — are too strong to be mere trash-talk. They are best read as denying the *real existence* of other gods, especially since they pair with claims of unique power.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The same "nothing" vocabulary is used of things that obviously exist (rating: strong; lexical)
Three Hebrew words carry the negation: *ʾayin* ("there is not" → "nothing"), *ʾefes* ("less than nothing"), and *tohu* ("vanity/emptiness"). Deutero-Isaiah applies the very same words to the **nations** — Babylon, Assyria — which manifestly exist. [^1]

> "let's look at Isaiah 4017 all the nations are as nothing before him they are accounted by him as FS less than nothing and toou emptiness" [^2]

### 2. So "nothing" means "irrelevant / incomparable," not "non-existent" (rating: strong)
Just as huge, intimidating empires are real yet "make them look like nothing" before Yahweh, the idols are called "nothing" to deny their *relevance and power*, not their ontology. It is precisely the rhetoric of incomparability. [^3]

> "it's not to say they don't exist it's to say yeah they're huge they're intimidating they're there they're real but our God makes them look like nothing... it is not saying they don't exist it says they don't matter because our God is so much greater" [^3]

### 3. "Formed" undercuts the existence reading (rating: strong; see linked note)
[[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]]'s "no god was *formed* before me" uses *yatsar* (to mold/sculpt, as pottery or an idol) — the verb for manufacturing idols, not the procreation language by which gods are normally generated in the ancient Near East. The text denies that *manufactured* things are gods. See [[Yatsar Formed in Isaiah Targets Manufactured Idols]]. [^4]

### 4. Nathan McDonald's monograph (rating: strong; peer-reviewed support)
McClellan cites [[Nathan McDonald]]'s *Deuteronomy and the Meaning of Monotheism* (esp. ch. 2 §3 on Deut 4:35, 39): this negation rhetoric expresses **proximity, presence within someone's domain, or rhetorical relevance** — not ontological exclusivity. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
Ortlund's strongest counter (which McClellan engages at length) is that the negation language is tied to predicates that *cannot* be reduced to incomparability: declaring the future, "no god formed before me nor shall there be after me," eternality. The Broncos cannot say they predated all other teams. [^6] McClellan answers: the "formed" predicate is idol-polemic (yatsar), and even pre-existence/sole-creator claims are paralleled in the Amun-Ra material, so they still do not isolate monotheism (see [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]).

## Best Quotes

> "when we get to the reference to the idols as nothing and less than nothing clearly the idea is not to say they don't exist no more than it is to say the Nations around you these enormous empires of Babylon and Assyria... don't exist" [^3]

> "it is precisely a denial of their relevance and of their power compared to Adonai so it is precisely the same as saying the Raiders do not compare to the Broncos" [^1]

> "the existence of the other gods is repeatedly acknowledged and even asserted" — re: appealing to Deuteronomy [^7]
