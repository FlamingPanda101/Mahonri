# The Great Lakes Are the Book of Mormons Seas

## The Claim
[[Rod Meldrum]] argues that the Book of Mormon's four "seas" are the **Great Lakes** and that the "narrow neck of land" lies in northern Ohio/Indiana — placing the whole narrative in the North American **Heartland**. He pairs this with a textual-critical argument that the famous statement putting the Lehite landing at "30° south latitude" (Chile) is **not** Joseph Smith's but a later interpolation, removing the strongest scriptural objection to a North-American setting.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (as Meldrum presents it)

### 1. Hebrew "sea" covers freshwater lakes
Meldrum notes biblical Hebrew uses one word (*yam*) for both the freshwater Sea of Galilee and the salt Mediterranean, so the Great Lakes "would certainly qualify as a sea." He maps Sea West = Lake Michigan, Sea East = Lake Ontario, Sea South = Lake Erie, Sea North = Lake Superior.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate on the lexical point (*yam* genuinely covers lakes); weak on the specific lake-to-direction assignments, which are unconstrained.

### 2. A real "narrow neck" between ancient swamps
When the Great Lakes stood ~50 ft higher, the Great Black Swamp (an arm of Lake Erie) and the Grand Kankakee Marsh (an arm of Lake Michigan) came within ~15 miles of each other, leaving a land corridor crossable "in a day or a day and a half" by an average man — matching the Book of Mormon's narrow neck (and the "serpents infested the land" detail, since swamps host snakes).[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate. The higher Holocene lake levels and the swamps are real geography; that this *is* the narrow neck is an interpretive fit, and "a day and a half" is generous.

### 3. The "Chile 30° south" statement is not Joseph's
Meldrum argues the oft-cited "they landed at 30 degrees south latitude" (read as Chile) traces — via word-print analysis — to **William McLellin**, injected into the Frederick G. Williams / John Bernhisel materials, not to Joseph Smith. He parallels this to Orson Pratt's hemispheric additions to the Wentworth Letter that (he says) Joseph corrected to "the Aboriginal inhabitants of this country."[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate as a provenance *question* (the 30°-south note's attribution to Joseph is genuinely disputed among historians); the McLellin word-print attribution is Heartland-sourced and not consensus.

### 4. Travel-time and scale favor North America
Meldrum contrasts the models' scale: the Mesoamerican lands, overlaid on Utah, would be "less than half the size of the state," making it "kind of hard to understand how the Mulekites and Nephites never met up" for ~400 years; in the Heartland they would be separated by 300–400 miles, raising the probability of non-contact. Travel times between "necks" fit North America better.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak. Both models reconstruct distances to fit the text; this is a plausibility preference, not a measurement.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Mesoamericanists reply that "internal consistency is what matters" (Kirk Magleby) and that the Heartland map fails the text's own constraints: the Book of Mormon describes a land "nearly surrounded by water" with a narrow neck between two *seas*, and equating the Great Lakes with oceans strains *yam* past its breaking point — the Lakes are not "seas" in the directional, surrounding sense the narrative needs, and the lake-to-direction mapping is arbitrary (Erie is not meaningfully "south" of Michigan). The 30°-south provenance debate cuts both ways: Joseph also let stand hemispheric statements, and removing one disputed line does not positively locate the seas in Ohio. Critically, the academically rigorous critique of *all* armchair geographies — see [[The Vernal Holley Map Places Book of Mormon Geography Wrong]] — is that place-name coincidences and "a swamp could be a sea" reconstructions are cheap; the discipline's standard (internal map first, external correlation second) is one the Heartland model meets least well. The honest residue: *yam* can denote a lake and the 30°-south attribution is genuinely uncertain, so the "Joseph said South America" objection is weaker than often claimed — but that is a defensive draw, not affirmative evidence for a Great-Lakes setting.

## Best Quotes
> "In the Hebrew language there's no difference between the word for the Sea of Galilee or the Mediterranean Sea... so the Great Lakes would certainly qualify as a sea."[^2]

> "Word-print analysis has demonstrated that the whole thing about the landing in Chile, 30 degrees south parallel, is actually not from Joseph Smith at all — it was William McLellin."[^4]

> "The entire lands of the Book of Mormon according to the Mesoamerica theory is less than half the size of the state of Utah... which makes it kind of hard to understand how the Mulekites and the Nephites never met up."[^5]
