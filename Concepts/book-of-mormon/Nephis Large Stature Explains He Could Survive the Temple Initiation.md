# Nephi's Large Stature Explains He Could Survive the Temple Initiation

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that [[Nephi]]'s otherwise odd self-description as **"large in stature"** in [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]] is a *deliberate, load-bearing* detail: it tells the reader that Nephi, though "exceedingly young," was physically big enough to **survive a grueling, violent temple initiation** — including being struck repeatedly (the [[A Ritual Smiting of the Cheek Was a Lost Temple Ordinance|seven-fold cheek-smiting]]) — by which he came "to know... the mysteries of God." [^1] On this reading, "I was exceedingly young, nevertheless being large in stature, and also having great desires to know of the mysteries of God... he did visit me" packs a causal chain: *young but big enough to take the physical punishment of the rite, and so admitted to the mystery in which the Lord visits the initiate.* [^2] It is the embodied evidence that anchors the cheek-smiting ordinance in Nephi's own experience.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Nephi flags his size twice, and the other instance is explicitly purposeful.** In [[1 Nephi 4.31|1 Nephi 4]] Nephi says he was "large of stature" precisely *because it does work in the story* — Zoram tries to flee and Nephi physically restrains him ("I... did seize upon" him). Butler's inference: Nephi does not mention his body for vanity; when he notes his size it is because it matters to what happens. So the [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]] mention should likewise *do work*, not be a stray fact. [^3]
2. **The verse couples stature with "the mysteries of God" and a divine visitation.** The clause sits inside [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]] between "exceedingly young" and "great desires to know of the mysteries of God... he did visit me." Butler reads the structure as *answering an implied objection*: why initiate one so young? Because he was big enough to endure it. The pairing of stature with mystery-knowledge and a theophany is, on the temple reading, exactly what an account of surviving an initiation would look like. [^4]
3. **Nephi frames the whole book as the testimony of an initiated man.** [[1 Nephi 1.1|1 Nephi 1:1]] ("having had a great knowledge of the goodness and the mysteries of God, therefore I make a record") makes initiation into the mysteries the *stated reason* for writing — so a detail explaining *how he was admitted* to those mysteries is thematically central, not incidental. This coheres with [[The Detailed Narrative Outranks the Chapter Summary in First Nephi]]: Nephi curates his record around temple meaning. [^5]
4. **A modern analogy clarifies the logic.** Butler likens it to a youth lying about his age to enlist in wartime: a scrawny boy is turned away, but a large one is believed — size is the *qualifier* that lets the young candidate through. Here Lehi (and presumably others) "introduced me into the mysteries of God" because Nephi could withstand what the rite demanded. [^2]

**Steelman of the skeptic.** This is a textbook case of an **incidental detail recruited to a theory it never asked to serve**: "large in stature" is fully explained by [[1 Nephi 4.31|1 Nephi 4]]'s plain narrative use (overpowering Zoram) and by ancient leadership topoi (Saul "from his shoulders upward... higher than any of the people," 1 Samuel 9:2) — big body, fit to lead. Reading the [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]] instance as evidence of a *violent initiation* requires first assuming the [[A Ritual Smiting of the Cheek Was a Lost Temple Ordinance|smiting-ordinance]], which is itself contested — so the argument is **circular**: the stature proves the rite only if the rite is already granted, and the rite is bolstered by the stature. There is no statement in 1 Nephi 2 of any beating, fasting, chains, or ordeal; that scaffolding is imported. And "exceedingly young + great desires + the Lord visited me" reads naturally as *spiritual* qualification (humility, desire, revelation), with "large in stature" a parenthetical aside, not a survival credential. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the move is not offered as standalone proof but as the *expected corroboration* if the smiting-ordinance is real — a young initiate would in fact need to be physically capable — and the doubled, self-conscious way Nephi notes his stature (useful both times) is at least consistent with, and arguably better explained by, a body that had to *do* something demanding. The claim's force rises or falls with [[A Ritual Smiting of the Cheek Was a Lost Temple Ordinance]] and should be held at the same provisional, "bonus material" confidence the authors assign it.
