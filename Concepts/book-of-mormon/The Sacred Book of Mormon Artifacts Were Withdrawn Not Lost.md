# The Sacred Book of Mormon Artifacts Were Withdrawn Not Lost

## The Point

When critics ask "what Book of Mormon artifacts do we have?" they usually mean the sacred objects — and Griffiths answers plainly that those are "not the ones that we really want."[^1] His distinctive framing is that the marquee artifacts are not *lost* (mislaid, faked, or never-existent); they were deliberately **shown to designated witnesses and then withdrawn**. A revelation given to Joseph Smith in 1829 named the objects the witnesses would see, the witnesses attested seeing them, and the objects then passed back out of human custody — so their absence is exactly what the LDS account predicts, not an embarrassment it has to explain away.

## Evidence

- **A pre-specified list of objects.** The 1829 revelation listed several artifacts the [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence|witnesses]] would see: the **plates**, the **sword of Laban**, the **interpreters** used to translate, and the **Liahona** ("sort of compass") — all of which are described inside the book itself.[^2]
- **Attested, then gone.** All three witnesses, "on multiple occasions," testified that they saw these objects; yet "we don't have any of them today."[^3] The pattern is sight-plus-testimony, not custody.
- **The plates were taken back by the angel.** Per Joseph Smith's history, once translation finished and the witnesses had their opportunity, the plates "were taken back by the angel [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] and kept into his care" — which is why combing the Hill Cumorah for them is futile.[^4]
- **Too many independent encounters to be a prop.** Beyond the eleven formal witnesses, a wider circle handled or glimpsed the plates — [[Emma Smith]] (moving them while cleaning), [[Catherine Smith]] and [[William Smith]] (hefting them wrapped in linen), [[Josiah Stowell]] (the cloth slipped as Joseph handed them through a window), and [[Mary Whitmer]].[^5] Griffiths: it is "really difficult to imagine that Joseph Smith could have made something... convincing enough" for that many people. See [[Informal Witnesses of the Golden Plates]] and [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]].

This reframes the missing artifacts from a gap in the evidence into a withdrawal anticipated by the account, complementing the separate point that direct New-World remains are scarce anyway ([[Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence in New World Archaeology]]).

## Best Quotes

> "What Book of Mormon artifacts do we have today? Not the ones that we really want... It would be really easy if we had the gold plates."[^1]

> "Those were all shown to the three witnesses and all three of the witnesses on multiple occasions attested that they saw those, but we don't have any of them today."[^3]

> "Once the translation process was finished and the witnesses had had an opportunity to see the plates, they were taken back by the angel and kept into his care."[^4]

## LDS Connection

The withdrawal of the plates is not a later apologetic patch but part of Joseph Smith's earliest narrative, and the 1829 revelation tied the witnesses' privilege to objects they would *see* rather than *keep*. This is why LDS apologetics rests the case on living testimony and converging documentary evidence ([[The Surviving Book of Mormon Artifacts Are Documentary Not Sacred]]) and treats the absence of the relics as consistent with a faith that "still comes up" ([[God Withholds the Slam Dunk to Preserve Faith]]), rather than as a problem to be hidden.
