# Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors

## The Claim

[[Ether's Elephant]] reports a self-conducted computational analysis comparing the 1830 first edition of the Book of Mormon with the modern edition, classifying every detected change. He argues two things: (1) the "thousands of changes" criticism is overblown — the vast majority are bland grammar fixes (e.g. "what → who" at [[Moroni 10.26|Moroni 10:26]]) that do not alter meaning; and (2) the *distribution* of edits across the book's sub-books is **non-uniform**, which — by analogy to a teacher's red-pen marks being unevenly dense across a multi-author document — is evidence of **multiple authorial voices**, and hence of an authentic, multi-hand ancient record rather than a single 1820s forger.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Most edits are non-substantive grammar.** "I was amazed to see just how little has changed besides some obvious grammatical errors." The representative edit corrects English ("what" to "who") "without altering its original meaning."[^1] *Rating: strong and uncontroversial — matches the scholarly consensus (e.g. Royal Skousen's critical-text work) that the overwhelming majority of changes are spelling/grammar/typesetting.* See [[Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free]].

2. **The single-author uniformity prediction.** "Under the assumptions... that the book is uniformly complex and that the author was equally alert throughout... we would expect a single author to commit consistent mistakes." If error-density varies sharply by section, "either that author had a fever that day... or I'd know that there must have been some other hand."[^2] *Rating: the logic is sound **conditional on** the stated assumptions (uniform complexity, uniform alertness, edits as a clean proxy for original error) — which is exactly where it is vulnerable (see steelman).*

3. **The reported statistical battery.** The creator describes: an edit-distance algorithm + difference library + regex preprocessing; a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test rejecting a uniform distribution of edits across multiple batch sizes; unsupervised clustering yielding ~10 distinct edit patterns; and ANOVA with a Tukey post-hoc finding significant grammar-edit differences between sub-books (e.g. Alma vs 2 Nephi and Jacob).[^3] *Rating: suggestive but uncredentialed and unpublished; the creator concedes limitations. The tests show non-uniformity, not specifically multiple **ancient** authorship — that is an interpretive leap.*

4. **An authentic abridged record predicts mixed voices.** Nephite writers quote older sources (the brass plates) without attribution, "like Paul and like John," so "a lot of hands, a lot of fingerprints" is expected. The "smells like plates" texture — choppy time-skips, doubling back, introducing a story before naming its characters — is offered as corroboration.[^4] *Rating: this connects to genuine internal features but is impressionistic; it dovetails with prior word-print claims rather than establishing them.* See [[Read the Book of Mormon as a Translated Ancient Text]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A statistician-critic grants the non-uniformity and denies the inference. (a) **The proxy is broken:** modern editorial changes are not a clean measurement of *original-author* error. Editors corrected the **printer's-manuscript and 1830 typesetting**, standardized to evolving grammar norms, and worked unevenly across the text; uneven edit-density can reflect uneven *editing* (or uneven scribal/typesetter error — the book had multiple scribes and compositors) rather than multiple original authors. (b) **Genre, not authorship:** different sub-books differ in content (legal, military, sermonic, Isaiah quotation), and Isaiah/KJV-derived passages were edited differently from narrative — content heterogeneity alone predicts non-uniform edit rates under a single author. (c) **A single author writing different registers** would *also* produce uneven error rates, undercutting the "uniform mistakes" premise the argument needs. (d) **Confirmation/garden-of-forking-paths:** an amateur running many tests on one corpus until "significant" patterns appear risks over-fitting; a KS rejection of perfect uniformity is nearly guaranteed for any real text and does not by itself imply *multiple ancient* hands. The honest framing — which the video partly adopts — is that the patterns are *consistent with* multiple voices and "don't definitively prove anything," not that they establish ancient multi-authorship.

## Best Quotes

> "If I was reading a book and it had a bunch of errors in chapter 2 and not so many in chapter 3... I'd know that there must have been some other hand in this, because your voice really is your voice."[^2]

> "Writing in different styles is one thing, but becoming an entirely different writer by altering one's mistakes is another."[^2]

> "There's a lot of hands, a lot of fingerprints all over the Book of Mormon. They get smoothed out... because Mormon took a broad brush and tried to edit them."[^4]

## LDS Connection

This is the *offensive* sibling of the vault's defensive edit notes: where [[Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free]] and [[Book of Mormon Edits May Strengthen Rather Than Undermine Its Origin]] argue the changes *fail to disprove* the book, this video argues the *pattern* of changes positively *evidences* multiple ancient authors — structurally the same abductive move as [[Confirmed Anachronisms Are Reverse-Anachronisms Joseph Could Not Have Known]]. Its force depends entirely on whether modern edit-density is a valid proxy for original-author error, which is the live methodological question.
