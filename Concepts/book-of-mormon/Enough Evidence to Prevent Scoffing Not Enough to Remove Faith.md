# Enough Evidence to Prevent Scoffing, Not Enough to Remove Faith

## The Claim

The Book of Mormon is, by design, never going to be *proven* or *disproven* by archaeology. The expected pattern — articulated in a [[Neil A. Maxwell|Neal A. Maxwell]] aphorism the video quotes — is that "enough plausible evidence will come forth to prevent scoffers from having a field day, but not enough to remove the requirement of faith." Reversed anachronisms (steel, cement) supply the first half; the persistent gaps supply the second. The faithful posture is to expect *convergence*, not a slam dunk.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The aphorism frames evidence as bounded on both ends
By His Fruits quotes the apostle: "All scriptures, including the Book of Mormon, will remain in the realm of faith. Science will not be able to prove or disprove... However, enough plausible evidence will come forth to prevent scoffers from having a field day, but not enough to remove the requirement of faith."[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — this is a theological expectation (a faith claim about *how much* evidence to expect), not itself an empirical finding; but it makes a falsifiable-shaped prediction: neither side gets a knockout.

### 2. The "no evidence for Abraham" parallel checks special pleading
The presenter notes we likewise "have no archaeological evidence that there was ever an Abraham, ever a Joseph in Egypt" — figures "some of the greatest men that have ever been on the planet" — yet their non-attestation does not make most people deny they existed. So demanding decisive archaeology of the Book of Mormon applies a standard the wider biblical world does not meet either (cf. [[Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence in New World Archaeology]]).[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a fair tu-quoque against a double standard; it argues for parity of expectation, not for the book's truth.

### 3. The reversals satisfy the "prevent scoffing" clause
The 77% reversal figure and cases like the [[The Jericho Steel Sword Reverses the Nephi Steel Anachronism|Jericho steel sword]] and [[Nephite Cement Construction Was Confirmed at Teotihuacan|Teotihuacán cement]] are presented as exactly the "enough plausible evidence" the aphorism predicts — accumulating support without ever forcing belief (see [[The Anachronism Reversal Rate Trends Toward the Book of Mormon Over Time]]).[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — coherent with the framework; depends on the reversal data being sound.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic will call this unfalsifiable insurance: any *confirmation* is hailed as the predicted "plausible evidence," while any *disconfirmation* is absorbed as the predicted "requirement of faith," so no finding could ever count against the book. That is a real epistemic cost — a thesis immune to disproof is also unprovable. The believer's reply is twofold: first, the framework still makes a *comparative* prediction (the problem-list should shrink rather than grow, and it has — see [[The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves]]); second, this is the *same* evidential situation as much of the patriarchal-era Bible, so the standard is not a Mormon contrivance but a general feature of ancient history. The honest claim is not "archaeology proves the Book of Mormon" but "archaeology behaves the way an authentic-but-not-provable ancient record would, exactly as the aphorism anticipates."
