# The Synagogue Predates Lehi in the Deuteronomic Reform

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The critic charge that "synagogues" in the Book of Mormon are anachronistic (the institution is usually dated to the exile or later) is answered from an unexpected direction: [[Moshe Weinfeld]] himself argues — on p. 44 of *Deuteronomy and the Deuteronomic School* — that the synagogue's *origin* lies in [[Josiah]]'s reform, **before [[Lehi]] left Jerusalem**. [[Mike Day]] notes that Weinfeld "never read the Book of Mormon," so this is a hostile-to-friendly convergence: the non-LDS scholar dates the institution earlier than the critic assumes.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The reform created a worship vacuum filled by liturgy.** When Josiah destroyed the scattered holy places ([[2 Kings 23.5|2 Kings 23]]), Weinfeld writes, "the abolition of the scattered holy places created this religious vacuum [that] was filled by liturgy" — a "formalized prayer" — so "the religion of Israel underwent a metamorphosis from a religion of cult to a religion of prayer and confession." Prayer replaced sacrifice as the medium of communion, and this is "the beginning of the change... at the time of [the] reform" (not only the exile).[^1] *(Strong as a report of Weinfeld; the dating to the reform is his own argument, quoted from the page.)*
2. **A footnote pushes it earlier still.** Day reads Weinfeld's footnote citing another scholar (Finkelstein/"Finklin") who roots the synagogue at the time of **Manasseh** — two kings before Josiah — when "pious men looked for a new kind of worship that could be conducted in secret." Weinfeld calls this "highly plausible." That places non-sacrificial assembly worship well before Lehi's birth.[^2] *(Moderate — a second-hand citation within Weinfeld, but it strengthens the early dating.)*
3. **It fits the persecuted-remnant picture.** A worship arising "in secret" among people "being hunted down" matches the Book of Mormon's opening, where Lehi is hunted; the Nephite "synagogues" are then best read as that ancient assembly carried into the New World — see [[Translation Renders an Ancient Assembly With a Familiar Word]].[^2] *(Moderate — interpretive, but internally coherent.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This rebuttal is sharper than the standard one but still contestable. (a) Weinfeld is describing the *functional roots* of communal prayer, not a synagogue *institution* with buildings and offices; mainstream scholarship (e.g. Lee Levine, Yonatan Adler — see [[The Synagogue Is a Post-Exilic Institution Absent From the Old Testament]]) still finds no synagogue *buildings* before the 1st century BC, so an apologist could be accused of equivocating between "formalized prayer assembly" and "synagogue." (b) The 1972 monograph is one scholar's reconstruction; the Manasseh-era footnote is a *citation of a citation* flagged only as "plausible." (c) The Book of Mormon's "synagogues" could still be a translation choice (the friendlier reading the vault itself prefers) rather than proof the institution literally existed by 600 BC. The believer's fair point is narrower but real: the common assertion that synagogues are *unambiguously* post-Lehi is overstated — a leading non-LDS source dates the *practice* the institution formalizes to the very reform Lehi fled, which is exactly when and where the Book of Mormon says its record begins.

## Best Quotes
> "This vacuum was filled by liturgy... thus the religion of Israel underwent a metamorphosis from a religion of cult to a religion of prayer and confession." — Weinfeld, p. 44, read aloud[^1]

> "Weinfeld, who's never read the Book of Mormon, [is] like, 'uh-uh — synagogue predates Lehi.' Now he doesn't say Lehi, but he says this time period predates the exile."[^1]

## LDS Connection
This converts a stock anachronism objection into a convergence: it complements [[The Synagogue Is a Post-Exilic Institution Absent From the Old Testament]] (which grants the late *building* date but uses it against critics) by adding that the *origin* of the practice is dated, by a hostile source, to the [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|Deuteronomic reform]] — the very milieu the Book of Mormon reflects.
