# The Sting of Death Is Apocryphal Isaiah from the Brass Plates Not a Pauline Anachronism

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A standard critical objection: [[Abinadi]] in [[Mosiah 16.7-8|Mosiah 16:8]] (~150 BC narrative setting) says "the sting of death is swallowed up in Christ," echoing [[1 Corinthians 15.55|1 Corinthians 15:55]] ("O death, where is thy sting?"), which Paul wrote ~AD 55 — centuries later. The phrase recurs in [[Alma 22.14|Alma 22:14]] ([[Aaron]]) and [[Mormon 7.5|Mormon 7:5]] ([[Mormon]]). Critics call this a transparent anachronism: a Book of Mormon prophet quoting Paul. [[Jonah Barnes]], with [[Cardon Ellis]], [[Oak Norton]], and [[Brad Whitbeck]], answer that **Paul was himself quoting Isaiah** — a now-lost ("apocryphal") Isaiah verse that survives only in the Gospel of Nicodemus and that stood, they argue, on the brass plates Lehi carried. On this reading the shared phrase is not borrowing-forward but a common dependence on an older source, turning the "anachronism" into corroboration.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Gospel of Nicodemus preserves an Isaiah "sting of death" oracle — rate: this is the load-bearing datum
In the *Descensus ad Inferos* portion of the Gospel of Nicodemus (ch. 16), the prophets in the underworld rehearse their messianic prophecies; Isaiah declares, "Did not I rightly prophesy to you when I was alive on earth?… and I said in another place, O death, where is thy victory? O death, where is thy sting?"[^2] If this preserves a genuine Isaianic tradition, then [[1 Corinthians 15.55|1 Corinthians 15:55]] is Paul quoting Isaiah, not coining the line — and Hosea 13:14 ("O death, where are thy plagues?") is the canonical fragment of the same oracle. *This is the strongest leg: a concrete extra-biblical text attributing the phrase to Isaiah.*

### 2. The brass plates carried a fuller Isaiah — rate: coheres with the vault's brass-plates model
The panel argues the brass plates (a Northern-Kingdom record, [[The Surviving Old Testament Is the Southern Kingdom's Edition|pre-dating the Southern edition]]) contained "the complete Isaiah," including verses later dropped from the Masoretic canon. [[Abinadi]], steeped in that text, could therefore quote an Isaiah the surviving Old Testament no longer has. This is the same logic the vault develops in [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]] and [[The Brass Plates Were Begun by Joseph of Egypt]]. *Moderate: depends on the brass-plates premise, but internally consistent.*

### 3. Abinadi had access by lineage — rate: speculative onomastics
The panel reconstructs Abinadi as the vanished heir of the record-keeping royal line: [[Amaleki]] (son of "Abinadom"/[[Abadom]]) had a brother who "went with [[Zeniff]]" and disappeared ([[Omni 1.30|Omni 1:30]]); "Abinadom" ≈ "Abinadi"; and [[Limhi]] reports that [[Ammon]]'s party "mourned the death of Abinadi" — odd unless he were a recognized royal figure. So Abinadi plausibly learned the brass-plates Isaiah "at the feet of his father… a steward of the plates." See [[Abinadi Enacts the Suffering King While Quoting the Servant Song]]. *Weak as proof (the hosts concede "those may be coincidences"); it supplies a mechanism, not a demonstration.*

### 4. The motif is a deliberate Nephite thread, not a one-off slip — rate: internal consistency
The "sting of death… swallowed up" formula appears at [[Mosiah 16.7-8|Mosiah 16]], [[Alma 22.14|Alma 22]], and [[Mormon 7.5|Mormon 7:5]], always tied to resurrection. A forger lifting one Pauline phrase is unlikely to weave it consistently across three prophets and 500 narrative years; a shared source text explains the pattern better. *Moderate.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's strongest reply: the Gospel of Nicodemus is a **4th-5th century AD Christian composition**, so its Isaiah speech is almost certainly a Christian author retrojecting Paul/Hosea into Isaiah's mouth — which makes it evidence of *later* Christian harmonizing, not of a pre-exilic Isaiah verse. On that reading the parallel runs the wrong way: the Book of Mormon and the Gospel of Nicodemus both post-date Paul, so neither establishes an ancient source. Moreover, "swallowed up in victory" at [[1 Corinthians 15.55|1 Cor 15:54-55]] is Paul explicitly stitching **Isaiah 25:8** ("he will swallow up death in victory") to **Hosea 13:14** — both canonical — so no lost Isaiah is needed to explain Paul at all. And the Abinadom→Abinadi identification is a pun, not data. A Latter-day Saint reply (made in the episode): the argument is cumulative and probabilistic — the Book of Mormon independently shows pre-Christian prophets knowing Christ ([[Jacob 4.4-5|Jacob 4:4-5]]; see [[Prophets Were Killed for Preaching the Messiah Before 600 BC]]), the Gospel of Nicodemus may transmit *older* underworld-harrowing material even if its final form is late, and the point is not that one verse was surgically traced but that the "anachronism" has a coherent non-anachronistic reading, which is all the rebuttal needs (cf. [[Critics Compiling Anachronism Lists Did Apologists a Favor]]).

## Best Quotes
> "Paul in 1 Corinthians 15 was quoting Isaiah. He didn't come up with the sting of death prophecy… he was quoting an apocryphal Isaiah that is lost to the Old Testament but was on the brass plates."[^1]

> "It proves Paul was quoting Isaiah, Mormon was quoting Abinadi, Aaron was quoting Abinadi, and Abinadi was quoting Isaiah… it wasn't an anachronism, it's another proof that the Book of Mormon is totally true."[^3]

> "They had the complete Isaiah in them — the Isaiah that we now only find in the Gospel of Nicodemus."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is a textbook instance of the vault's preferred move on Book of Mormon "anachronisms": rather than concede a forward-borrowing, locate an older source the surviving canon lost (here, a fuller brass-plates Isaiah). It dovetails with the broader thesis that the brass plates [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain|bypassed the Deuteronomistic editing chain]], and with [[Abinadi Enacts the Suffering King While Quoting the Servant Song]], where Abinadi both quotes and embodies the suffering-king Servant Song. The honest takeaway: the Gospel-of-Nicodemus parallel is genuinely interesting and the consistent Nephite motif is real, but the dating of the Nicodemus text is the soft spot a careful interlocutor will press.
