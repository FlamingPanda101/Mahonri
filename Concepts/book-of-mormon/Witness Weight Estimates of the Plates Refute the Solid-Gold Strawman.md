# Witness Weight Estimates of the Plates Refute the Solid-Gold Strawman

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Bill McKeever]] (CARM) and [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] argue the golden plates cannot be authentic because, taking the angel's word "gold" to mean **solid gold** and the stated dimensions (~6×8×6 in), the plates would weigh ~200 lb — far too heavy for [[Joseph Smith]] to have carried while fleeing. They stage demonstrations with weighted replicas (and a ~90-lb prop) at the Manti pageant, daring passersby to lift them. [[Jonah Barnes]], [[Oak Norton]], and [[Brad Whitbeck]] rebut that **no witness ever described solid gold**: the ~21 people who handled or hefted the plates estimated **40-60 lb**, described turning the leaves like the pages of a book, and noted a greenish patina and blackened ciphers — all pointing to a thin-leaved [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|gold-alloy]], not bullion.[^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The witness corpus says 40-60 lb, not 200 — rate: strong, multiply attested
A Moroni's-America/independent compilation the panel displays lists estimates from Jonathan Hadley, Josiah Stowell, Orson Pratt, William Smith, [[Joseph Smith]], [[Lucy Mack Smith]], Martin Harris, John Whitmer, and others — dimensions clustering at 6×8×~6 in and weight at **40-60 lb** (others simply "very heavy").[^2] This is exactly the kind of [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis|constraining testimony]] the solid-gold figure ignores. *Strong: the 200-lb premise is contradicted by the very witnesses whose existence the plates' story depends on.*

### 2. "Plates," not a brick — rate: strong, definitional
A book is not a solid block of wood; likewise the record was thin metal *leaves* with air between them (witnesses describe riffling the pages), so computing weight as a solid gold block is a category error. Even granting "gold," a leaved alloy plate of those dimensions weighs a fraction of solid bullion. *Strong.*

### 3. The patina and black ciphers indicate an alloy — rate: moderate, corroborating
Several witnesses noticed a greenish tinge (copper oxidation) and blackness in the engraved characters (consistent with silver), pointing to a **tumbaga** (gold-copper, sometimes silver) alloy — common in Mesoamerican metallurgy — which is both lighter than gold and matches "the appearance of gold" (Joseph's own phrase). See [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation]].[^2] *Moderate: the alloy reading is inferential but fits the descriptions and the weight.*

### 4. Even 40 lb is runnable — rate: strong, common-sense
A 40-lb load is liftable and carriable by a farm laborer; the panel notes the actor in *Witnesses* repeatedly ran with a ~40-lb replica across multiple takes, and that McKeever's own demonstrator hefted ~200 lb one-handed on camera (undercutting the "impossible" framing). The "too heavy" point therefore proves nothing even on a heavy estimate — which is the [[The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument|self-defeating-argument]] note's territory.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's best case: (a) **the angel said "gold," and Joseph's people called them "gold plates,"** so attributing an alloy is itself an apologetic retrofit not stated in the early sources — the tumbaga theory is an inference to rescue the weight, not a witness claim; (b) **the weight estimates are themselves suspect** — most "witnesses" hefted the plates *through a cloth or in a box* and few actually saw bare metal, so "40-60 lb" is a guess by people already committed to (or hostile but secondhand about) the story; (c) at the upper end, 60 lb under one arm while *running and fighting off attackers* is still a real feat, so the objection retains some force against the more dramatic narrative elements. A Latter-day Saint reply (the episode's): "gold" naturally means "of the appearance of gold" in 19th-century usage, and the alloy inference is *driven by* the witnesses' own patina/weight reports, not imposed on them; the estimates converge across friendly *and* hostile observers, which is hard to explain if invented; and the remaining "run while fighting" feat is trivial next to the miracles the *Christian* critic already grants (Red Sea, resurrection), which is the deeper point in [[Anti-Mormons Borrow Atheist Weaponry Against the Plates]]. The narrow win: the *solid-gold 200-lb* figure is a strawman the sources refute.

## LDS Connection
This supplies the *evidential* core that the companion rebuttal [[The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument]] deliberately ranks second to its theological point: the witness corpus constrains any weight claim to 40-60 lb, the [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|alloy]] explains both the appearance and the weight, and the leaved-book structure makes the solid-gold computation a category error. It belongs with the vault's witness-and-fabrication cluster ([[Informal Witnesses of the Golden Plates]], [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]]). Honest takeaway: the 200-lb strawman is genuinely refuted by the sources; the residual debate is over how many witnesses handled bare metal and whether "gold" was ever meant literally.
