# View of the Hebrews Is Not Scripture or a Novel

## The Claim
*View of the Hebrews* (Poultney, VT, 1823; 2nd ed. 1825) by [[Ethan Smith]] is neither a book of scripture nor a novel nor a historical romance. It is a missionary-anthropological tract: Ethan Smith's argument that the modern Native Americans descend from the ten lost tribes of Israel, who crossed the Bering land bridge c. 700 BC, and that this knowledge should motivate Christian missionary efforts to restore the indigenous peoples to a knowledge of their Israelite ancestors as part of the latter-day restoration of Israel.[^1] Because the genre is so different from the [[Book of Mormon]], the very category of "plagiarism" is strained.

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The book's own purpose is missionary, not narrative (rated: strong, primary).** Ethan Smith was a Congregational minister and domestic missionary, a reformer and abolitionist concerned with the welfare of marginalized peoples including Native Americans. *View of the Hebrews* is "partly anthropological and partly theological," "a missionary proselytizing tool to help motivate white European American Christian missionaries to go to Native Americans."[^2] A minister friend wrote a foreword amplifying its missionary usefulness.[^1]
2. **The argument is anthropological evidence-gathering, not storytelling (rated: strong, primary).** Ethan Smith's method is to collect reports from people "who have lived among the Indians" about Jewish-seeming customs, Hebrew words, feast days, purification rites, circumcision, and the name "Jehovah" in indigenous languages.[^3] This is a (pseudo-)scholarly anthropological case, structurally unlike a scriptural narrative with characters and plot.
3. **Genre mismatch undercuts plagiarism (rated: moderate, logical).** A first-person scriptural epic and a third-person ethnographic argument do not share a literary form, so a copy-and-paste account of Book of Mormon origins is implausible on its face. *Steelman:* a critic need not claim wholesale copying — only that Joseph mined the book for *ideas* (Israelite ancestry of Native Americans) and dressed them in narrative. Smoot grants this is conceivable for that single broad idea but argues it explains almost none of the Book of Mormon's actual content (see [[Direct Literary Dependence Requires Exclusivity]]).

## Best Quotes

> "It is not claimed to be scripture, and it is not a novel. ... It is his argument that we know the origins of the Native Americans, and that they come from ... the lost tribes of Israel."[^1]

> "It's partly anthropological and it's partly theological ... and sort of a missionary proselytizing tool to help motivate white European American Christian missionaries to go to Native Americans to bring them Christianity."[^2]

> "He's making kind of an anthropological argument ... for why Native Americans are the descendants of lost tribes of Israel."[^3]
