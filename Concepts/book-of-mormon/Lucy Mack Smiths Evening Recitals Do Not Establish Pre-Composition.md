# Lucy Mack Smiths Evening Recitals Do Not Establish Pre-Composition

#type/claim

## The Claim
The single strongest naturalistic foothold for "Joseph composed the Book of Mormon over years before dictating it" is an account from his mother, [[Lucy Mack Smith]], that as a teenager Joseph would give "amusing recitals" describing the "ancient inhabitants of this continent" — their dress, travel, animals, cities, warfare, and worship — "with as much ease, seemingly, as if he had spent his whole life with them."[^1] [[Jacob Hansen]] **steelmans this as the critic's best move and then rebuts it**: the recitals (a) are uniquely attested by one sympathetic source against dozens of silent contemporaries, and (b) are nothing like the kind of disciplined, years-long oral repetition that actual memorization of a 270,000-word text would require. Notably, Hansen raises this argument *for* the critic himself — "I want to steal Joe's argument" — because [[Joe Heschmeyer]] did not bring it up.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **One witness out of dozens.** Lucy's recitals are dated to roughly 5 years before the dictation (Joseph age 17), ending with brother [[Alvin Smith|Alvin]]'s death in 1823. Dozens of statements were collected from Joseph's acquaintances and neighbors — including 22 gathered for the first anti-Mormon book, *Mormonism Unvailed* — and **none** of them recall him rehearsing fictional stories. "His mother is the only one."[^3] *(Strong — the asymmetry between Lucy's account and the hostile statement corpus is checkable; the critic's best evidence is also his only evidence.)*
2. **Real oral memorization looks nothing like family story time.** Ancient cultures stored large texts through repetition, song, dance, and poetic devices — sustained, *out-loud* practice over years, "not something they did quietly in their head or during a few months of family story time."[^4] To gauge the difficulty: one man (an actor with prior memorization experience) spent an hour a day reciting Milton's ~60,000-word *Paradise Lost* aloud and took **9 years** to memorize it. The Book of Mormon is over four times longer and was produced as new composition, not recited from a fixed prior text.[^4] *(Strong — the *Paradise Lost* benchmark is a concrete, well-known datum; it shows occasional teenage story-telling is the wrong order of magnitude.)*
3. **Heschmeyer's own framework undercuts the "literate Joseph" escape.** Heschmeyer and his cited scholars repeatedly stress that rising literacy *decreases* memorization and oratory skill — yet to make 19th-century Joseph capable, Heschmeyer pivots to "he probably read a lot." Both cannot hold: the oral-memory route and the literate-reader route pull against each other, and neither matches a single-draft 270,000-word narrative.[^5] *(Moderate — an internal-tension / consistency point against the opposing case rather than positive evidence.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic has a real, non-trivial case. (a) Lucy is a *primary* source with direct access, and her detail ("dress, modes of traveling, and the animals upon which they rode... mode of warfare... religious worship") reads like a description of someone *developing* an imaginative world years in advance — exactly the slow-gestation model a naturalist needs. (b) Absence of corroboration is weak evidence: most neighbors had no reason to record private family evenings, and hostile witnesses had every incentive to omit anything that made Joseph sound earnest rather than fraudulent, so the "only his mother" point may reflect *who was asked*, not what happened. (c) The *Paradise Lost* analogy is imperfect — Joseph would be *composing and improvising*, not memorizing a fixed prior text verbatim, and improvisational oral performers (epic bards, freestyle reciters) demonstrably sustain long output without a stopwatch-perfect memory. Hansen's fair concession is that the recitals "could point to him developing the story early on in his life"; his claim is only the narrower one that this *particular* practice is "not really the kind of practice that somebody would need to pull off what Joseph did." The disagreement is about whether casual early story-telling scales to the documented dictation feat — see [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].
