#type/claim

# Lehi's Journey Is a Reverse Exodus That Relocates the Sacred Center

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]]'s geographic leg of the reversal: [[Lehi]]'s departure echoes the Exodus — God leads him three days into the wilderness toward a "promised land," mirroring how God led Israel three days into the wilderness toward Canaan. But it is a **reverse Exodus**: Lehi is led *away* from the biblical promised land, out toward "the islands of the sea." Jerusalem is the sacred center, the *axis mundi*, of the world; here God moves that center from the periphery, relabeling a far, unnamed place as the new "promised land." Bradley reads the Liahona "compass" as marking a polar shift — as a magnetic compass realigns to a new pole, the religious axis of the world swings from Jerusalem to the New World, the way Muhammad later shifts the qibla from Jerusalem to Mecca.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Exodus structure is genuinely echoed and then inverted.** [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2]] does have the three-days-into-the-wilderness motif and a divinely promised land — but directed *out* of the biblical Holy Land, which is a real reversal of the Exodus vector (toward, not away from, Canaan). (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong — the parallel-with-inversion is textually defensible.)[^1]
2. **"Axis mundi" relocation is a recognized category.** Scholars of religion do speak of shifts in the sacred center; the Mecca/qibla example is a clean historical instance of a tradition relocating its directional holy center, making Bradley's framing intelligible rather than idiosyncratic. (Evidence rating: moderate — the analogy is apt; its application to 1 Nephi is interpretive.)[^2]
3. **It coheres with the book's New-World theology.** A reverse Exodus that makes a far land the new promised land fits the Book of Mormon's larger claim that the Americas are covenant land — and dovetails with temple-return readings of Lehi's journey. See [[Lehi's Dream Recapitulates the Eden-Temple Return]]. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Exodus typology saturates the Book of Mormon and the broader scriptural imagination, so a critic can hold that Lehi's wilderness journey simply *applies* the Exodus pattern to a new setting (as countless migration narratives do) without intending a pointed "reversal" of the sacred center; the "compass/polar-shift" reading especially leans on the modern connotations of *compass* and on a religious-studies vocabulary the text never uses. The Mecca analogy illustrates the idea but does not establish that 1 Nephi means to enact it. The fair, narrower point stands: the narrative knowingly evokes the Exodus while sending Lehi the opposite direction toward an unnamed promised land — one more inversion in the opening's pattern (per [[The First Narrative of First Nephi Signals a Reversal of Values]]) — whether or not one accepts the full *axis mundi*-shift gloss.

## Best Quotes
> "It's the same kind of thing, except dramatically they're going the wrong way. They're going away from the biblical promised land. It's a reverse Exodus."[^1]

> "There's a shift in what scholars of religion call the axis mundi... it would be like when Muhammad shifts the sacred center of the world for Muslims from Jerusalem... to Mecca."[^2]
