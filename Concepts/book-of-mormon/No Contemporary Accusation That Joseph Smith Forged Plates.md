# No Contemporary Accusation That Joseph Smith Forged Plates

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The objection is that Joseph Smith easily fabricated metal plates. The rebuttal
turns the historical record against it: among all the hostile affidavits gathered
from Palmyra-area neighbors, *none* accuse Joseph of making or possessing the
metal needed to forge plates. If forgery were so easy and so obvious, his
disgruntled critics would have said so.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The affidavits contain many accusations — but not this one

Hostile neighbors called the Smiths lazy, superstitious swindlers and treasure
diggers. Yet no affidavit says Joseph came home with a "giant cast brass kettle,"
a hoard of weird metal, or was caught hammering sheet metal in a barn.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong as an argument from silence — the silence is in a body of *motivated, hostile* testimony, where the accusation (if available) would have been damaging and easy to make. See [[Argument from Silence Is Not Always a Fallacy]].

### 2. The actual contemporary theories were different

The two contemporary skeptical theories were (a) [[Alexander Campbell]]'s — Joseph
simply *wrote* the book himself and there were no plates; and (b) the [[Sydney Rigdon|Rigdon]]–[[Solomon Spalding]]
theory — he stole a manuscript. Crucially, "he forged metal plates" did not
occur to any of them as plausible.[^3]

### 3. The forgery theory is a much later invention

The idea that Joseph fabricated physical plates arises only long after the
contemporary witnesses are gone — "if he avoided detection, he did a pretty good
job of it," the panel notes wryly, since the people best positioned to catch him
never even floated the possibility.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

An argument from silence is defeasible: neighbors may simply not have witnessed
secret metalworking, or such accusations could have existed and been lost. A
critic might also say the affidavits are unreliable generally, so their silence
proves little. The panel's reply is that these same hostile sources freely
invented other damaging stories, so their failure to reach for the forgery charge
is telling rather than accidental — see also
[[Gold Plates Fabrication Was Prohibitively Expensive]].

## Best Quotes

> "It's interesting that nobody, none of his contemporaries accuse Joseph Smith of being able to make plates."[^2]

> "You never get anybody saying, 'Oh, and he had like this hoard of metal that was really weird and he was trying to make gold plates.'"[^5]

> "It's only much later that people [say], 'well maybe he fabricated gold plates.' ... If he avoided detection he did a pretty good job of it."[^4]
