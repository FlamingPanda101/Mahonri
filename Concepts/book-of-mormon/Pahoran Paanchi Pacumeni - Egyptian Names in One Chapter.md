# Pahoran, Paanchi, Pacumeni — Egyptian Names in One Chapter

## The Claim
The three sons of Pahoran in [[Helaman 1.1-9|Helaman 1]] — **Pahoran, Paanchi, Pacumeni** — all carry plausibly Egyptian names beginning with the Egyptian masculine article *Pa-*, spanning the full spectrum from attested to plausible in a single chapter. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Pahoran — attested.** Corresponds to *Pa-ḥuru* in the **Amarna letters** (Egyptian *Pa-Ḥori*), "the Syrian / the Canaanite." Parallels biblical Egyptian-type names: *Pashhur* (Jeremiah's era), *Panehesy* ("the Nubian," cf. Phinehas). Strength: **high**. [^1]
2. **Paanchi — "dead ringer."** Matches Egyptian *Pa-ʿankh*, "the living one," from *ʿnkh* (ankh, "life"). Bowen has a dedicated article. Strength: **high**. [^2]
3. **Pacumeni — plausible but less certain.** Options: Greek *Pachomios* ([[Hugh Nibley]]); or *Pa-kem*, "the blind one" ([[John Sorenson]], [[Paul Hoskisson]]). Strength: **moderate** (no single etymology settled). [^3]
4. **Three Egyptian-pattern names clustered in one royal/judicial family** is internally consistent with a Lehite elite who "knew the language of the Egyptians." Strength: **moderate**. [^1]

**Steelman:** A critic argues the *Pa-* prefix is a productive enough pattern that coining several *Pa-* names is easy; that "Pacumeni" admits multiple, mutually exclusive guesses (a sign of reaching); and that one chapter's cluster could be selection bias. Bowen's honest grading — attested / dead ringer / plausible — is itself the answer: he does not overclaim the weakest member.
