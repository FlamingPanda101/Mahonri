# Joseph's Coat Was a Coat of Markings Not Many Colors

#type/claim

## The Claim
On [[Ward Radio - Evidence of LDS Temple Garments Found in the Dead Sea Scrolls|Ward Radio]], [[Jonah Barnes]] (with [[Don Bradley]] and host [[Cardon Ellis]]) argues that Joseph of Egypt's famous "coat of many colors" ([[Genesis 37|Genesis 37]]) is a **mistranslation** of a coat of **markings** — a marked sacred garment in the line of the temple garment, not a multicolored fashion item. The Hebrew *kethoneth passim* is obscure, and Barnes argues the better sense is a garment bearing distinctive marks/symbols. His proof-text move: aged, blind Israel (Jacob) "recognizes" the bloodied coat ([[Genesis 37|Genesis 37:33]]) — which he could only do by **feeling raised markings**, not by seeing colors. The coat thus belongs to the Adam-garment tradition (a marked covering, *kethoneth* like the "coats of skins" God made in [[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3:21]]) whose symbols Barnes connects to the temple garment and the divine name.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Hebrew does not say "colors."** *Kethoneth passim* is famously uncertain; "many colors" follows the Septuagint/KJV, but *passim* has been read as "long-sleeved," "ornamented," or marked. "Coat of markings" is a defensible reading of an ambiguous phrase. *Rating: moderate — the philology genuinely supports "not necessarily colors," though "markings" specifically is one option among several.*
2. **Blind Jacob recognizes the coat by touch.** A blind man identifying a specific garment by handling it points to tactile features — raised marks or distinctive construction — rather than a visual color pattern. *Rating: moderate; a clever internal argument, though "recognized it" could also rely on shape, fabric, or the brothers' testimony rather than embroidered marks.*
3. **The same word covers the first sacred garment.** The garment God makes in [[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3:21]] is a *kethoneth* ("coat") of skins — the inaugural divinely made covering. Reading Joseph's *kethoneth* as a marked sacred garment ties it to that tradition and to the temple garment's symbolism. *Rating: coherent within the garment-tradition frame; the link is typological, not lexical proof.* See [[The Garment of Skin and the Garment of Light]].
4. **The garment's marks map onto the divine name.** Barnes/Bradley argue the sacred garment's four symbols (one repeated) correspond to the four-letter Tetragrammaton (with a repeated letter), so a "coat of markings" is theologically loaded — wearing it is "taking upon us the name" of God. *Rating: speculative but internally consistent; this is the payoff that motivates the re-reading.* See [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]] and [[Atonement as Covering - Joseph Could Not Have Known]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The mainstream view that *passim* most likely means a long-sleeved or richly ornamented robe (cf. 2 Samuel 13:18-19, where Tamar's *kethoneth passim* is a princess's garment) does not require, or even favor, "ritual markings." The "blind Jacob feels the marks" argument over-reads the text: Genesis says the brothers brought the coat and Jacob *identified* it as Joseph's — recognition by a grieving father of a one-of-a-kind robe needs no embroidered glyphs, and the "blind" detail at this point in the narrative is itself an assumption. The whole move looks like reading later temple-garment theology back into an ambiguous Hebrew word to manufacture a temple connection. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim is modest at its core — the text does *not* say "colors," and the ancient sense is genuinely uncertain, so the popular "many colors" image is not load-bearing. Rebuilding the garment as a *marked* covering simply restores it to the *kethoneth* tradition that runs from Eden's coats of skins onward; the temple-garment payoff is offered as a coherent fit, not a slam-dunk, and it travels with the better-evidenced remnant-theology point ([[The Joseph-Coat Remnant Theology Matches Jasher and the Book of Mormon Not Genesis]]).
