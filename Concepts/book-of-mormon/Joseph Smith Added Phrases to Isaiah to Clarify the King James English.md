# Joseph Smith Added Phrases to Isaiah to Clarify the King James English

## The Claim
[[Avraham Gileadi]] — translating the Book of Mormon into Hebrew with full command of biblical Hebrew — observed that the Book of Mormon "borrows the King James translation of Isaiah" when it quotes Isaiah, and that "sometimes Joseph Smith would add things here and there" that Gileadi "knew from the internal context of Isaiah… was not from Isaiah." His explanation is not that Joseph corrupted the text but that Joseph "was trying to make sense of the… King James version" — adding "his own little phrase… to try to make it more intelligible" for an audience that already used the KJV.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The additions are recognizable to a Hebraist as non-Isaianic.** Because Gileadi knew Isaiah's Hebrew "well enough" not to need an open Old Testament for cross-reference, he could identify Joseph's added phrases "from the internal context of Isaiah" as material that does not belong to the underlying prophet — yet which serves a clarifying function in the English.[^2]
2. **A deliberate accommodation to a KJV-using audience.** Gileadi reasons that a direct fresh translation from the Hebrew "through the seeing device" would "have caused controversy in the community he was trying to convert to," because "they already had a King James… the one everybody used." Leaning on the familiar 1611 text (and glossing it where it was opaque) was the path that would be received rather than rejected. This is a *rhetorical/pastoral* account of the translation method, not a claim of word-for-word divine dictation.[^3]
3. **The Hebrew translators still had to honor the text as given.** Despite recognizing the added phrases, Gileadi says "to be true to the Book of Mormon, we still had to translate what it said" — i.e., the editorial additions are part of the canonical English and were rendered into Hebrew rather than excised.[^4]

## Why It Matters
This is a believing Hebraist *conceding* that the Book of Mormon's Isaiah rests on a King James substrate with Smith-era clarifying insertions — the very datum critics press (see the objection rebutted in [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Variants Are Not Copied from the KJV]]) — and reframing it as evidence of a *functional, audience-aware translation* rather than fraud. It dovetails with the vault's [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]]: the "translation" delivers an intelligible, covenant-bearing text to 1829 readers, not a critical edition. It also sits in productive tension with the directional "toward older readings" argument: Gileadi grants KJV-dependence *and* (elsewhere in the interview) affirms the book's deep Hebraisms, locating the ancient signal in structure, idiom, and covenant theology rather than in the Isaiah base text being independent of the KJV.

## Best Quotes
> "The Book of Mormon borrows the King James translation of Isaiah when it quotes Isaiah… sometimes Joseph Smith would add things here and there, and I knew from the internal context of Isaiah that was not from Isaiah. But afterwards… I realized that he was trying to make sense of the… King James version — adding his own little phrase in there to try to make it more intelligible." [^1]

> "If he had translated from the Hebrew directly through the… seeing device… it would have caused controversy in the community he was trying to convert to. They already had a King James… the one everybody used." [^3]

> "But to be true to the Book of Mormon, we still had to translate what it said." [^4]

## Counter-arguments / Caveats
- A critic reads the same admission as confirming the simplest hypothesis — Joseph worked from an open KJV — and would press why a genuine ancient plate-text would reproduce 1611 English idiom at all.
- The "clarifying intent" is Gileadi's inference about Joseph's purpose, not a documented statement by Joseph; it is a faith-consistent reconstruction.
- The claim is compatible with, but does not by itself establish, the stronger [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Matches the Dead Sea Scrolls|older-readings]] thesis; the two arguments must be held together carefully.
