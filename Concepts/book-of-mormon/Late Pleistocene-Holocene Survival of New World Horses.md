# Late Pleistocene-Holocene Survival of New World Horses

## The Claim

The horse objection assumes a hard rule — **New World horses went extinct at the end of the Ice Age** (the Pleistocene), well before 600 BC — so any horse in the Book of Mormon is impossible. [[Kirk Magleby]] and [[Daniel Johnson]] argue this "megafauna did not survive the ice age" line is a *canon* defended by inertia, not a settled fact, and that the extinction window is being pushed later by independent data: Alaskan permafrost DNA, the central-Mexico stratigraphy, and genetically anomalous living horses. The point is that the terminal-Pleistocene cutoff is the load-bearing premise of the whole objection, and it is precisely the premise now eroding.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The "canon" is an assumption, not a measurement

Magleby frames the resistance sociologically: "scholars love to have established canons," and "megafauna did not survive the ice age" is treated as "a hard and fast limitation" — "the status quo," not a finding revisited on new data. Naming the assumption is the first move in dislodging it.[^2]

### 2. Permafrost DNA for late survival

Magleby cites ancient-DNA work from the Alaskan permafrost showing **post-Pleistocene survival of megafaunal horses** (and likewise mammoth) — i.e., these animals persisted thousands of years later than the classic extinction date. Combined with the stratified central-Mexico horse bones associated with organics dated into Jaredite–Nephite times (see [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism]]), the survival window is argued to overlap the Book of Mormon.[^3]

### 3. The Bashkir / North American Curly anomaly

The thick, hypoallergenic, curly-coated horse first noticed by a rancher near Eureka, Nevada in the early 1900s was assumed descended from Russia's Bashkir horse (via the Russian Pacific presence as far as Sitka). Genetic studies, however, found **no clear connection to the Russian Bashkir** — prompting registries to propose renaming it the "North American Curly" and to suggest it may have been in the Americas "all along," surviving in tiny pockets until rediscovery. Offered as a tantalizing, not decisive, data point.[^4]

### 4. The Copernicus analogy for slow consensus

Magleby compares the situation to Copernicus: heliocentrism had "extraordinarily solid data" yet took generations to "percolate" through the scientific community. Science is "inertia-bound" — usefully so, to avoid chasing every rabbit — but "in a measured, peer-reviewed... way" will eventually move; the horse data are read as "the pattern... the trend."[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is where the LDS case must be most careful, because it slides between two very different claims. (a) *Genus*-level survival: that *some* equids lingered a few thousand years past the ~11,700-BP boundary in pockets (Alaska, perhaps elsewhere) is genuinely supported by aDNA and not particularly controversial — but the latest robust New World horse dates still fall **millennia before** 600 BC, leaving a large gap exactly over Book of Mormon time. (b) *Continuous domestic* horses in Mesoamerica through the first millennium AD: this is what the text needs and what the evidence does *not* show — no securely dated, undisturbed, directly dated horse bone, and no horse material culture, fills the Nephite window. The Curly horse is a red herring: every genomic study places it firmly within *modern domestic* *Equus caballus*, descended from post-Columbian stock, with no ancient American ancestry. And the Copernicus analogy is rhetorical leverage, not evidence — being a suppressed minority view is equally compatible with being *wrong* (cf. countless fringe paradigms that never "percolated" because the data were against them). The defensible conclusion is the modest one the hosts elsewhere concede: the extinction date is being *revised later in places*, which makes the horse anachronism less airtight than once claimed — "trending," not solved.
