# You Would Believe Abraham as Your Neighbor but Not Joseph Smith

## The Claim
[[Devin Barnes]] presses a consistency challenge: every ancient prophet was called *without* corroborating evidence — Abraham, Noah, Jonah, and even Moses had nothing but "trust me, bro" plus, in some cases, later miracles. A modern person who says they would have believed Abraham if they were his neighbor, yet rejects [[Joseph Smith]] for the same "I saw God" claim, is applying a double standard. The twist: Joseph Smith is in fact the *better*-credentialed case, because God gave him tangible evidence — the gold plates and the witnesses — precisely so that "the world needed evidence" and the restoration could be preserved.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Ancient prophets were called with no evidence
"What evidence did Abraham have that he was called? Or what about Noah or Jonah?" Moses "didn't have any evidence to prove his prophetic calling other than he knew the name of God and later he performed these miracles." If you would have trusted Abraham's bare testimony as his neighbor, consistency demands you weigh Joseph Smith's claim by the same standard.[^1] *(Moderate — a parity / tu quoque argument; its force depends on granting that the ancient cases really were evidence-free, which a critic can contest.)*

### 2. Joseph Smith was given *more* evidence, not less
Unlike the ancient prophets, Joseph received physical and witness evidence. Barnes argues this is *why* the plates existed even though Joseph dictated through the seer stone without looking at them: "Because the world needed evidence. If Joseph Smith didn't have any evidence for his prophetic calling, then very few people would have believed him, and the esoteric would not be preserved."[^2] This answers the critic's "why even have the plates if he wasn't looking at them?" *(This dovetails with [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence]] and the hostile-mob argument that the plates were real metal, not tin.)*

### 3. The mobs corroborate real plates
"Why else would these mobs be trying to get the gold plates from him if they were just made of tin?" — offered as circumstantial confirmation that a valuable object actually existed.[^2] *(Weak-to-moderate; rhetorical, but consistent with the broader witness case.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can break the parity several ways. (a) **Disanalogy of distance:** belief in Abraham is not a live epistemic choice — no one alive could have been his neighbor, so "I would have believed him" is a costless hypothetical, whereas Joseph Smith is a documented 19th-century figure whose claims can be investigated and were contested by contemporaries. (b) **The evidence cuts both ways:** the same nearness that supplies plates and witnesses also supplies affidavits, treasure-digging records, and competing naturalistic accounts unavailable for Abraham, so "more evidence" is not automatically more favorable. (c) **Selective standard:** the believer applies the parity only to Joseph Smith and not to Muhammad, Ellen White, or other claimants who also reported divine calls — see [[Other True Prophets Do Not Undercut Joseph Smith]] for the LDS attempt to manage that asymmetry. The argument is best read as shifting the burden ("on what principled ground do you credit ancient prophets but not modern ones?") rather than as positive proof.

## Best Quotes
> "If I were neighbors with Abraham and Abraham says he saw God, would I believe Abraham without that evidence? ... So then why would you say you would believe Abraham if you were his neighbor back in the day, but you wouldn't believe in Joseph Smith if you were his neighbor?"[^1]

> "If he wasn't looking at the gold plates, a lot of critics of the church say then why even have the plates? ... Because the world needed evidence. If Joseph Smith didn't have any evidence for his prophetic calling, then very few people would have believed him."[^2]
