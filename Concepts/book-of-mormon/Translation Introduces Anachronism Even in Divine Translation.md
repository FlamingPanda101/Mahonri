# Translation Introduces Anachronism Even in Divine Translation

## The Claim

The act of translation routinely introduces anachronism into a text, so the
English Book of Mormon (dated 1829) should not be expected to be free of
out-of-place words — and this remains true even if God is the translator, because
what matters is the translator's *goal*, which is the witness of Christ, not
flawless archaeological vocabulary.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The KJV itself contains translation anachronisms

Sixteenth-century King James translators, not understanding first-century Judea,
rendered "lamps" and "lampstands" as "candles" and "candlesticks" — objects that
did not exist in first-century Palestine. Translation imported the anachronism;
the underlying text was fine.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (concrete, verifiable example from a universally accepted text).

### 2. Modern scholarly translators admit it is unavoidable

The NRSVue's own introduction states the translators were careful to avoid
historically and theologically anachronistic terms, but that "anachronism is
ultimately unavoidable in translation." Professionals working with full source
texts concede the point.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (an admission from a mainstream critical translation).

### 3. A divine translator optimizes for the message, not the props

If God is the translator, the relevant question is the translation strategy and
goal. The Book of Mormon's pervasive, unmistakable witness of Christ comes through
"really, really clearly" — even in places that themselves feel anachronistic, like
Lehi and Nephi naming Christ centuries early. The goal was clarity of testimony,
not preventing questions about horses, wine, or chariots.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (a theological framing, not an empirical claim, but internally coherent).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can grant that translation introduces *terminological* anachronism (wine,
candles) while insisting it cannot conjure *physical referents* — if the underlying
ancient record genuinely lacked horses, no translation choice puts real horses in
Mesoamerica. The reply: many Book of Mormon "anachronisms" are precisely
terminological (what English word was chosen for an unfamiliar plant/animal), which
is exactly where translation slack operates; the genuinely physical cases are
handled separately (see [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism]]).

## Best Quotes

> "Translation introduces anacronism and this is unavoidable to some extent... the King James Bible has anacronisms... they said candles and candlesticks when they should have translated it lamps and lampstands."[^2]

> "We were careful to make sure we use terms in ways that were not historically and theologically anacronistic, but anacronism is ultimately unavoidable in translation."[^3]

> "If we consider God the translator, well, what's God's goal with the Book of Mormon? Is it to make sure nobody ever has questions about horses and chariots, or is it to make sure people understand the witness of Christ?"[^4]
