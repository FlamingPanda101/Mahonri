# Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race (Objection Rebutted)

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Joe Heschmeyer]] asserts that the racism of the priesthood ban "is reflected in the Book of Mormon itself" — that the text teaches being Black is a curse from God.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **The text never equates a "curse of God" with Black skin (rated: moderate-to-strong, textual).** [[Jasmine Rapley]]: "The Book of Mormon never calls black people being under a curse of God. The Lamanites have a curse and there's a skin of blackness, but the best textual reading has nothing to do with the color of your skin" — proposals include metaphor, clothing, or body paint, all "perfectly cohesive with the doctrine." Later readers cherry-picked these passages to justify the ban, "but they are not based in the text."[^2]

2. **There were no Africans in the Book of Mormon world (rated: strong, narrative-internal).** [[Q. Walker Lewis]] (quoted via the panel): the objection "shows their ignorance of the entire premise of the Book of Mormon, because 6th-century-BC Jews aren't urban Black Detroiters. There literally is zero black people in the Book of Mormon."[^3] The Lamanite/Nephite division is intra-Israelite, not African.

3. **The Book of Mormon is an *anti*-racist text (rated: moderate, thematic).** Rapley and Irwin: the narrative's whole point is to "decry the prejudice that the Nephites have against the Lamanites." Irwin cites [[Richard Bauckham|Richard Bushman]]: the Lamanites are the *central* people of focus; the land is *for them*; Samuel the Lamanite comes to tell the Nephites to repent. "Do you think a racist of the day would write a fictional story about a slave from Georgia who broke out and ran up to New York and told the people to repent — and that slave was right?"[^4] *Steelman:* the verses do exist and *have* been read racially for over a century by LDS leaders themselves, and the 1981 edition changed "white and delightsome" to "pure and delightsome" — so the text is at minimum *susceptible* to a racial reading, and the apologetic relies on a particular (defensible but not unanimous) interpretation. Rapley concedes this when she says later Saints "tried to cherry-pick them and apply them to the priesthood restriction."

4. **Even a racial reading condemns the attitude (rated: moderate).** Rapley: "Even if you had a racial interpretation of the skin of blackness, the Book of Mormon is a condemnation of that attitude" — the Nephites are destroyed because of their contempt for the Lamanites.[^5]

5. **The "skin" idiom and the 1981 footnotes (rated: moderate, footnote-anchored).** In a separate, fuller treatment, [[Marvin Perkins]] walks every scriptural use of "black" applied to a person (Job 30:30, Jeremiah 8:21/14:2, Lamentations 4:8, Joel 2:6) and shows the KJV footnotes gloss the Hebrew as "gloomy"/"dejected," while the 1981 LDS edition added footnotes redirecting the Book of Mormon "skin"/"scales" passages to the Topical Guide "Darkness, spiritual." Perkins pairs this with the argument that a **curse** is a separation from God, not a pigment — see [[A Curse Is a Separation From God Not a Skin Color]].[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "The Book of Mormon never calls black people being under a curse of God... the best textual reading of that has nothing to do with the color of your skin."[^2]

> "It shows their ignorance of the entire premise of the Book of Mormon, because 6th-century-BC Jews aren't urban Black Detroiters."[^3]

> "Do not revile against them because of the color of their skins. They're going to be whiter than you at the judgment day. This is not the racism of the day."[^4]
