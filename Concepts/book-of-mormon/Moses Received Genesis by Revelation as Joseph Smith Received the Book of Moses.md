# Moses Received Genesis by Revelation as Joseph Smith Received the Book of Moses

## The Claim
[[Devin Barnes]] argues that the way [[Joseph Smith]] produced scripture is not anomalous but matches the oldest precedent in the canon: [[Moses]] wrote Genesis about events (creation, the Flood, the patriarchs) that all predate his own lifetime, with — on the traditional view — no ancient manuscripts to copy from. Moses therefore received Genesis "the same way Joseph Smith received the Book of Moses": by revelation, "no resources." The parallel is meant to normalize revelatory translation and to undercut the demand that Joseph must have used hidden written sources.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Genesis records pre-Mosaic events Moses did not witness
"Moses came up with the book of Genesis... that took place before Moses. He didn't find any ancient writings with the book of Genesis." On the traditional authorship view Moses had no eyewitness access and (per Barnes) no documentary source for creation through Joseph-in-Egypt, yet he is credited with the text.[^1] *(Moderate as a debating point; note it presupposes Mosaic authorship — which mainstream criticism rejects, see Steelman.)*

### 2. The mechanism is the same: revelation without resources
Asked whether Moses might have relied on oral histories or older manuscripts, Barnes answers: "It was just through revelation. He had nothing, no resources." He then maps this directly onto Joseph: Joseph "translated the Book of Mormon in basically the exact same way that Moses translated... the Book of Genesis specifically, because he wasn't there."[^2] *(This reframes the [[Book of Mormon]] dictation feat as continuous with biblical precedent rather than uniquely suspicious — compare [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].)*

### 3. Rhetorical payoff: the critic's source-hunt is special pleading
If a Bible-believing critic accepts that Moses produced Genesis revelatorily, demanding a hidden written source behind the Book of Mormon (View of the Hebrews, Spalding, etc.) applies a standard the critic does not apply to their own scripture — a [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon|burden-shift]] in the believer's favor.[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The parallel is only as strong as Mosaic authorship, which is the weak link. Mainstream biblical scholarship holds that the Pentateuch is a composite of later sources (J, E, P, D) edited centuries after any historical Moses, so "Moses received Genesis with no resources by pure revelation" is not a neutral fact but a pre-critical assumption — see [[Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch]]. A critic can also note that Genesis plausibly *did* draw on prior oral and written tradition (e.g., shared Near Eastern flood narratives), which is the opposite of "no resources." Even granting the analogy, it establishes only that *if* one accepts revelatory authorship for Genesis, one cannot rule it out a priori for the Book of Mormon — it does not positively demonstrate that Joseph's text is revealed. The argument thus works defensively (consistency) far better than as affirmative evidence.
