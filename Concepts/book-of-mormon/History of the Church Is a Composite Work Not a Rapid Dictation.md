# History of the Church Is a Composite Work Not a Rapid Dictation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] argued that the multivolume *History of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints* shows Joseph Smith was capable of producing "long, complex literary works" — implying that if he could produce that, he could have composed the Book of Mormon, undercutting the dictation-miracle argument. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Stephen Smoot]] argues the two works are **not analogous**, so the comparison fails. The Book of Mormon was a *rapid dictation* (~3 months, spring–early summer 1829), a fact even skeptics concede; the *History of the Church* was a *composite, team-edited compilation* assembled over years from documentary sources under Joseph's supervision. [^2]

1. **The Book of Mormon's rapid dictation is conceded even by skeptics.** "There is universal consensus that Joseph Smith dictated the text of the published Book of Mormon (minus the 116 pages lost by Martin Harris)... in the course of about 3 months in the spring and early summer of 1829. Even skeptics are forced to acknowledge the historical data strongly indicates the Book of Mormon was a rapidly dictated text." *Rating: strong; the timeline is well-documented — see [[Dictation Speed and Method Argue Against Fabrication]] and [[Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier]].* [^2]
2. **The History of the Church is the opposite kind of project.** "It is not the product of a rapid dictation in the course of a few short weeks. Rather, it is a composite, edited work that incorporated a variety of documentary sources under the supervision of Joseph Smith and a team of clerks and secretaries." *Rating: strong; the Joseph Smith Papers documents the multi-year, multi-author editorial process.* [^3]
3. **The categories differ, so the inference is invalid.** "It is simply erroneous to assume... that the History of the Church series was the result of a single dictation by Joseph Smith the way the Book of Mormon is. The two are simply not analogous." Producing an edited compilation over years with a staff is unremarkable; dictating a 270,000-word coherent narrative in months without notes or revision is the phenomenon in question. *Rating: strong; the disanalogy is decisive against Horn's inference.* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic could reply that the dictation *method* does not by itself prove *divine* authorship — rapid oral composition is documented in some gifted individuals and oral-formulaic traditions, and Joseph could have mentally pre-composed or drawn on absorbed sources (the storyteller theory) before dictating. On this view the History/Book-of-Mormon disanalogy is granted, but the underlying point Horn wanted — that Joseph had real compositional ability — is not entirely defused.

### Counter-objection
Smoot's reply (and the surrounding cluster): the dictation argument never rests on speed *alone* but on the *combination* — speed, length, internal complexity (e.g., reversed Jaredite king-lists, hundreds of names, prophetic interweaving), lack of notes or substantive revision, and no naturalistic source — which the storyteller theory does not account for (see [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]] and [[The Evening Recitals Were Joseph Relaying Moroni's Information]]). The History of the Church, being a leisurely staffed compilation, demonstrates *none* of those features and therefore cannot establish the capacity at issue.
