# Book of Mormon Father-and-Son Texts Resolve in Context

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A *tu quoque* against Latter-day Saints: "Your own scriptures teach that the Father and the Son are the same being — Ether 3:14 says 'I am Jesus Christ. I am the Father and the Son'; Mosiah 15 says similar things. Why reject your own modalistic texts?" [^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Each text explains *why* the title is justified (rating: strong; contextual exegesis)
[[Blake Ostler]]: "in each context it explains why the claim to be the father and the son is a justified claim." [^2]
- **Mosiah 15:** justified because "the son has his will swallowed up in the father's will," so whatever the Son does is the Father's will, and "the son did the creation, thus becoming the father and the son." See [[Mosiah 15.1-5|Mosiah 15:1-5]].
- **Ether 3:** Christ "appears and reveals that he's going to come in the flesh and that he will be begotten by the father, and in that sense he is the father and the son." See [[Ether 3.14|Ether 3:14]]. [^2]

> "in the context of these scriptures, if we read them in context, there's actually an explanation of why the claim to be both father and the son is made." [^2]

### 2. The same books radically distinguish Father and Son (rating: strong; textual)
"There are simply in the very same context numerous texts that distinguish the father and the son rather radically — not any more radically than the gospel of John does." Ostler cites his own article *Revisioning the Mormon Doctrine of Deity*. [^3]

### 3. The John parallel disarms the charge (rating: strong; comparative)
"We could make the same argument for modalism from the gospel of John. 'He who has seen me has seen the Father' ([[John 14.9|John 14:9]]). But in the very same pericope it's the father who is giving and sending the son." Seeing Jesus = seeing "the actions of the father because I'm doing the will of the father." So if the John text is not modalist, neither are Mosiah/Ether. See [[The Father and Son Distinct - Even Trinitarians Deny Jesus Is the Father]]. [^4]

### 4. "Modalistic-sounding" ≠ modalism (rating: moderate; methodological)
"On first blush these are modalistic sounding texts, but if we take a close look at the context and how the text is actually functioning, it's not modalism that's being expressed." The agent who bears the divine name can say "I am him" without identity of persons. [^5]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
The critic can argue the contexts do *not* fully neutralize the language: Mosiah 15's "the very Eternal Father of heaven and of earth" and Abinadi's God-who-is-the-Father-and-the-Son read most naturally as a single divine person assuming two modes (a strand some early LDS thinkers did read modalistically), and that appealing to "will swallowed up" is itself a harmonizing gloss. Ostler's reply is that consistent contextual reading—applied evenhandedly to John and to the Book of Mormon—dissolves the modalist surface in both corpora, and that the Book of Mormon's many person-distinguishing texts settle the systematic reading. See [[Yahweh Is the Father Who Gave the Son the Divine Name by Adoption]].
