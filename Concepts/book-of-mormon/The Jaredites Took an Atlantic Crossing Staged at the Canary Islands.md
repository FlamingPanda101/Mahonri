# The Jaredites Took an Atlantic Crossing Staged at the Canary Islands

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jerry Grover]] proposes a concrete **Atlantic** route for the Jaredite migration, against the older Pacific model. Origin: the land of **Sumer** (the Book of Mormon name *Kish* matching the major Sumerian city; the unnamed "tower" of [[Ether 2|Ether 1]] matching the regional ziggurats, notably without the later biblical gloss "Babel"). [^1] The legs: north up the valley of Nimrod (a real biblical name, with an active volcano and Nimrod-legends), across the Syrian desert ("the great deep" / empty quarter where "no man had been"), barge-building, then *many waters* = the Mediterranean, with a staging stop "beyond the sea in the wilderness" = the **Canary Islands** (site of the brother of Jared's mount). [^2] From the Canaries, the **Canary current** carries them south to the **North-Equatorial current** across to Mesoamerica, matching the **344 days** of [[Ether 6|Ether 6:11]]; the islands also supply 130-ft trees for ships "as high as a tree," satisfying that textual detail. John Sorenson himself, Grover notes, shifted from a Pacific to an Atlantic view over his career. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Kish/Kesh and the ziggurat-tower are real anchors.** A ruling dynastic city *Kesh/Kish* existed in early Sumer in the right period, and the Book of Mormon's refusal to call the tower "Babel" fits an early Mesopotamian setting (Babel being a later biblical addition). *Rating: a genuine, if circumstantial, convergence; Grover's Sumerian etymologies are specialist and contestable.* [^1]
2. **The current/duration math is testable.** Average Canary→North-Equatorial current speeds plausibly yield ~344 days, and the route's trade winds and hurricane tracks push in the right direction — a real-world path that *fits* the text's "driven before the wind" and 344-day data ([[Ether 6]]). *Rating: a plausibility argument, explicitly weighted as possibility, not probability.* [^3]
3. **It harmonizes with a Mesoamerican landfall.** The model lands the Jaredites where the vault's other geography material places them (cf. [[Mesoamerican Sources Preserve a Book of Mormon Migration - Gurr]]), and connects to [[The Tower Was a Temple Recontextualized From an Older Story]] for the Babel/tower setting. *Rating: internally coherent with existing vault geography.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The text of Ether gives **almost no navigational detail** — "we get two or three chapters and the brother of Jared is already up the mount" — so any route map is heavily *reconstructed*, and Grover grants everyone's Jaredite map "looks a little different." The Sumerian place-name etymologies (e.g. *Moriancumer* parsed from Sumerian roots) are idiosyncratic and unpeer-reviewed; critics will call the whole exercise a *post hoc* fit of modern oceanography to a sparse narrative. And the deeper objection raised on-air: why seek a naturalistic route at all when the crossing is plainly framed as miraculous? A measured reply: Grover's stated method is to ask whether a *real-world path consistent with the text exists* — moving the claim from "no explanation" to "possibility/plausibility" on a weight-of-evidence scale — while affirming the miracle ("it was a miracle they even crossed the ocean"); the model is offered as one feasible reconstruction, not as the meaning of the text.

## Best Quotes
> "The Atlantic crossing seems the most feasible to me... and the Canary Islands seem to satisfy this 'wilderness beyond the sea.'" [^2]

> "If you do the calculations using average current, it does meet the criteria given in Ether where it says they were three hundred forty-four days upon the water." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The model is a worked example of the vault's recurring apologetic posture: not proving the Book of Mormon by science, but showing that its sparse ancient details (a tower without "Babel," ships "high as a tree," 344 days adrift, being "driven before the wind") *cohere with a real geography* a 19th-century author is unlikely to have engineered — evidence weighted toward plausibility rather than treated as proof.
