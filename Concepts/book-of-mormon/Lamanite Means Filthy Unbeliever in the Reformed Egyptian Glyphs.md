# Lamanite Means Filthy Unbeliever in the Reformed Egyptian Glyphs

## The Claim

[[Jerry Grover]] argues that in the [[The Characters Document Translated as Abbreviated Hieratic and Maya|characters document]] the term **"Lamanite" is a glyph whose layered meanings are "tribe/phyle of the unbeliever"** — with secondary glyph-forms also carrying "filthy / excrement." On his reading "Lamanite" was a *generic* label for non-believers (anyone not aligned with the Nephites), and the harshness of the term reflects genuine ancient tribal animosity, not 19th-century racism. He proposes Book of Mormon names routinely carry multiple meanings (a Hebrew phonetic sense, an underlying Egyptian sense, sometimes a constructed Sumerian sense).[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Laman" = unbeliever in the Hebrew and the Egyptian
Grover follows Matthew Bowen and the *Book of Mormon Onomasticon* in deriving "Laman" from a root meaning **no faith / unbeliever** (he cites a Hebrew *lo-amān* sense found in Deuteronomy), then says the corresponding characters-document glyph (a "bar-hook" form) means "unbeliever" in Egyptian too — a convergence across both languages.[^2][^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate for the Hebrew etymology (it has published onomastic support); weak-to-moderate for the Egyptian glyph identification (Grover's unrefereed decipherment).

### 2. A generic label, matching the text
Grover notes Jacob 1:14 already says the Nephites will not distinguish all the dissenting tribes but call them all "Lamanites" — anyone "not for the Nephites." So a glyph meaning "the unbelievers / those-not-with-us" fits the Book of Mormon's own usage of Lamanite as a politico-religious, not strictly genealogical, category ([[Jacob 1.9|Jacob 1]]).[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a *textual* observation (Jacob 1:14 does generalize the term); the glyph claim rides on top of it.

### 3. The "filthy" overtone reflects ancient animosity, not modern racism
Grover finds additional glyph-forms (Egyptian words for "filthy," and a Mesoamerican hook-glyph in Teotihuacan murals meaning excrement/filth/sin) that he reads as a derogatory overtone in "Lamanite." He argues this is *expected* in a genuinely ancient text written amid centuries of tribal warfare — "ethnocentrism," not race — and that Mormon's editorial message is precisely to *overcome* that hostility (the more-righteous Lamanites, the stripling warriors).[^5][^6]
> **Evidence rating:** The literary/cultural point is reasonable; the specific cross-cultural "filth" glyph identifications are speculative.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the same unreviewed decipherment risk as the parent note, amplified: a translator who finds "filthy unbeliever" packed into one glyph has produced a result that is striking but unfalsifiable to a lay audience and unvetted by Egyptologists or Mayanists. The Hebrew etymology of "Laman" as "unbeliever" is debatable (other proposals exist), and pulling a Teotihuacan excrement-glyph into an Egyptian-Hebrew name is exactly the kind of free cross-cultural association the vault warns against. A critic will also note the apologetic convenience: reading the racial language of the Book of Mormon (the "skin of blackness," [[Jacob 1.14]] context) as merely "ethnocentric tribal labeling" softens a genuinely uncomfortable text. The measured reply: the *textual* claim — that Lamanite is a generic politico-religious label, not a strict pedigree — stands on Jacob 1:14 alone, independent of the glyphs (compare [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]]); the glyph-level "filthy unbeliever" decipherment is a tier-C lead, not evidence.

## LDS Connection

The apologetic payoff is the [[The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves|undesigned authenticity]] move: a forger idealizing a sacred people would not bury an ethnic slur in the script, whereas a real ancient record written amid tribal war would. It also reframes the Book of Mormon's racial language as politico-religious labeling (see [[The Northern Lineage Explains Laman and Lemuel and the Brass Plates]] for the theological-conflict reading of Laman and Lemuel). Tier-C: the *textual* generalization is solid; the glyph decipherment is a single researcher's unreviewed proposal.
