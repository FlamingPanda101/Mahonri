# Witnesses Paid a Real Cost With No Payoff

## The Claim

A liar or con-man profits, or at least avoids loss. The witnesses did the opposite: they bore real costs — lost farms, blocked careers, beatings, destitution, decades of mockery — and never extracted money, power, or status from the testimony. The absence of any payoff guts the fraud hypothesis.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Martin Harris's catastrophic personal cost
[[Martin Harris]] lost his New York farm, ended up destitute for years, out of the church, "a pathetic figure" giving Kirtland Temple tours for a few coins — yet when asked about the Book of Mormon he would "seem twenty years younger" and "glow."[^2] He never profited; he came to Utah in 1870, was re-baptized, and died bearing testimony, buried with a Book of Mormon in his hands.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (well-documented decline with no monetization of the claim).

### 2. David Whitmer gained office *in spite of* the Book of Mormon, not because of it
Whitmer became mayor of Richmond, Missouri — in a region that hated Latter-day Saints — and remained respected. Peterson presses the motive question: "To what end? And what gain? I can't think of any."[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the social setting made the testimony a liability, not an asset).

### 3. The obvious cash-out never happened
Any of them "probably could have made a lot of money going on the lecture circuit or writing an exposé... how we made the plates, how we lied about that story. Never does. None of them ever does."[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the unused, lucrative exit is conspicuous).

### 4. Physical cost: beatings
[[Hiram Page]] was beaten so badly some say he never fully recovered and may have died young as a result — yet he would not recant.[^6] See [[No Witness Ever Recanted]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (traditional account, but consistent with the broader pattern).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can argue "no payoff" is not decisive: people maintain costly false beliefs all the time out of identity, pride, or fear of admitting they were duped — costliness shows *sincerity*, not *truth*. Peterson partly concedes this by pivoting: the costs show the witnesses were not lying for gain, which is exactly the point — it eliminates the *deceiver* hypothesis and pushes the debate onto whether they were *deceived* (handled in [[Witnesses Were Deceived Theory Rebutted]]). The believer should grant that costly sincerity, by itself, does not establish that the plates were real.

## Best Quotes

> "I get the impression... he says, 'Who would have thought that the Book of Mormon did all this?' And he's rebaptized. And he ends his life bearing testimony to anybody who will listen."[^3]

> "He probably could have made a lot of money going on the lecture circuit or writing an exposé about my experiences with Joe Smith, how we made the plates... Never does."[^5]

> "Again, to what end? And what gain? I can't think of any gain. What power?"[^4]
