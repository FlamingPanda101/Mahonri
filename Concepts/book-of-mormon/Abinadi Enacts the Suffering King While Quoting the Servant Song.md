# Abinadi Enacts the Suffering King While Quoting the Servant Song

## The Claim
Drawing on [[Jonah Barnes]]'s *Key to the Keystone*, [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] argue that [[Abinadi]] may be the lost prince of the house of Nephi, and that in [[Mosiah 14]] he both **quotes** Isaiah 53 (a Servant Song — the suffering, dying, redeemed Davidic king) and **enacts** it: standing humiliated, imprisoned, and about to be executed before the usurper King Noah. The doubling is offered as an unconscious literary feat far beyond a 19th-century farm boy dictating to a scribe.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A missing Nephite prince (rated: speculative reconstruction).** In Omni, the record-keeper Amaleki has no sons and gives the plates to Benjamin; his father is "Abinadom," and Amaleki has a brother who "went with [Zeniff]" back to the land of Nephi. The reconstruction: Abinadi (son of Abinadom) was a trained prince of the house of Nephi who went with Zeniff's people and was later called as a prophet — and when Limhi reports Abinadi's death to Ammon, "they mourned the death of Abinadi."[^1]
2. **He quotes the king's song while living it (rated: literary reading).** On trial, chained, beaten, "arguing about the meaning of the law of Moses," Abinadi quotes Isaiah 53 — "which is one of the servant songs, about the Davidic king being beaten, humiliated, imprisoned before he rises again." If [[John Eaton]]'s royal reading is right, Abinadi is "the liturgical Davidic king" citing the suffering-king text "in connection with [his own death]."[^2] See [[The Servant Songs Portray the Suffering King of the Festival]].
3. **A Caiaphas-and-Christ typology (rated: literary parallel).** The scene mirrors Jesus before the high priest: "the true Davidic king in front of the high priest who ain't no high priest" — the true heir reciting the ritual obligations before a usurper who is "getting drunk and fat with his concubines."[^3]
4. **The Zeniff/Noah wordplay (rated: onomastic).** Zeniff names his son Noah ("rest"); David was told to name Solomon a "son of rest." The hosts read Noah as a failed Davidic-rest figure — sharpening the irony that the true heir (Abinadi) stands before him.[^4]
5. **Unconscious sophistication (rated: dictation argument).** The narrative is "astonishingly complex… and utterly unselfconscious" — Joseph dictates it and "doesn't take any notice," 120 years before Engnell hypothesized the suffering servant was the Davidic king. See [[Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier]].

**Steelman of the critic.** The Abinadi-as-lost-prince identification is admittedly speculative (the hosts repeatedly flag "those may be coincidences"), resting on name similarity (Abinadom / Abinadi) and a missing-brother inference, not on any explicit text. A skeptic reads Abinadi quoting Isaiah 53 as the obvious thing a Book-of-Mormon prophet predicting Christ would do, with the "king of the festival" layer imported from a contested scholarly framework. The strength of the argument is literary resonance, not demonstration.

## Best Quotes
> "He is the prince, he's the last of the house of Nephi, he's the liturgical Davidic king… and they are going to kill him, and he knows it, and he quotes this saying effectively, 'I am here for the thing I am here for.'"[^2]

> "Joseph is the time traveler… 120 years ahead of Engnell making the hypothesis that the suffering servant is the Davidic king."[^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the climactic Book-of-Mormon application of Eaton's festal drama: a Nephite prophet who is simultaneously the suffering king of the Servant Song and a type of Christ-before-Caiaphas. It joins the vault's Day-of-Atonement typologies — [[Laban and Nephi Enact the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]] — as evidence that the text encodes first-temple ritual patterns its purported author could not have known.
