# The Book of Mormon Fits on the Plates — Sjodahl's Hebrew Test

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] (Catholic Answers) argues in an Instagram reel that the 270,000-word Book of Mormon could not have fit on the golden plates — each character would have to carry ~80 English words, which ancient scripts don't do. [[Keystone]] answers that this is not a new question: [[Janne Sjodahl]] demonstrated empirically in 1923 that the text fits comfortably, and the physical descriptions support a compact script.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The 1923 parchment experiment.** This is the positive demonstration. [[Janne Sjodahl]] took a 7×8-inch piece of parchment, had a friend translate part of the Book of Mormon into Hebrew, and by hand fit **14 English pages** of the Book of Mormon onto that single page. Extrapolating, "the entire Book of Mormon could fit onto only 21 plates." With ~40 plates available, scribes "could have used a larger font size, even after accounting for the lost pages of the Book of Mormon."[^2] *Rating: strong within the source — an actual physical test, not a thought experiment, that undercuts the "doesn't fit" premise by a wide margin.*

2. **Reformed Egyptian was more compact than Hebrew.** Mormon writes that "if our plates had been sufficiently large we should have written in Hebrew; but the Hebrew hath been altered by us." The implication: whatever reformed Egyptian was, it was "significantly shorter than Hebrew" — so the real script would fit even *more* densely than Sjodahl's Hebrew, "another factor that would have allowed for an even larger font size."[^3] *Rating: moderate — depends on the text's self-description, but is internally coherent and strengthens the fit.*

3. **The engravings were small and fine.** Joseph Smith's Wentworth letter says the plates were "filled with engravings... the characters were small and beautifully engraved"; eyewitness [[John Whitmer]] said there were "fine engravings on both sides"; and Egyptologist [[John Gee]] found "even this font size is comparable to the character size of many other ancient engraved Hebrew characters."[^4] *Rating: moderate–strong — converging eyewitness and Egyptological testimony that small, dense engraving is historically plausible.*

4. **Headroom without exotic theories.** Keystone concludes it is "entirely plausible for the Book of Mormon to have been translated from a relatively small number of plates" even before invoking translation theories like Blake Ostler's expansion theory.[^5] *Rating: moderate — a cumulative-case framing; the fit holds on conservative assumptions.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Horn's underlying instinct is not unreasonable: 270,000 words *is* a large text, and there are genuine upper bounds on plate size and weight, so the content cannot be arbitrarily large. A critic could add that Sjodahl's hand-copying assumes a maximal density and an unknown script, and that comparing a 1923 scribe's deliberate miniaturization to an ancient engraver's practical output is not exact. The Darius plates (~200 words/page) are a real data point for low-density metal inscription.

## Counter-objection
The Darius comparison is one or two artifacts against thousands of examples of ancient metal writing, and the Book of Mormon is a different record with a different (self-described compact) script. Sjodahl's test does not need to match an ancient engraver's *typical* density — it only has to show the text *can* fit in the available area, which it does with room to spare (21 of ~40 plates). Combined with the small-engraving testimony and reformed Egyptian's stated compactness, the burden shifts back to Horn, whose argument also relied on a scaled-down model and an unsupported 42-characters-per-side assumption. See [[Trent Horns Word-Count Argument Uses Wrong Parameters]] and [[Reformed Egyptian Characters Were Small]].
