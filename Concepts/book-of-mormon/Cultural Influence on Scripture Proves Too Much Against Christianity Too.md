#type/rebuttal

# Cultural Influence on Scripture Proves Too Much Against Christianity Too

## The Claim
Cameron Bertuzzi argues that [[Joseph Smith]] "drew heavily from the literature and folklore of his time" — citing Ethan Smith's *View of the Hebrews* (1823) for the lost-tribes idea, the name "Nephi" from travel literature, and "Mormon" from the figure "Mormo" — concluding the Book of Mormon is a product of 19th-century culture rather than revelation. The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] and his host reply that this argument **proves too much**: by the same standard, the Bible and Christianity are "products of their time" too, so the criterion cannot single out Mormonism as false.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the rebuttal)

1. **Paul quoted pagan sources (rate: strong, parallel).** The Apostle Paul quotes Greek poets/Stoics ("in him we live and move and have our being"; "we are his offspring," Acts 17), appealing directly to his hearers' pre-existing pagan thought — yet this does not make his letters uninspired.[^2]

2. **Christianity absorbed Greek philosophy (rate: strong, parallel).** "Was St. Thomas Aquinas influenced by Aristotle in any way?" Neoplatonism shaped patristic theology. The idea that "Aristotelians and Platonists had no influence over Christians or over the Apostle Paul" is plainly false — so cultural influence is not a defeater for divine origin.[^3]

3. **All scripture carries its authors' culture (rate: strong, theological).** The Bible's human authors brought "their own human experiences and their own cultural baggage," and a Christian "should think this is a good thing." If "some of Joseph Smith is in the text," that is no more disqualifying than Paul's personality being in his letters — Paul even flags his own opinions ("this is my opinion ... not the Lord"). See the catalyst/dynamic-translation framing in [[Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism]].[^4]

4. **The specific parallels are weak (rate: moderate).** Green calls the *View of the Hebrews* dependence "a joke ... not defensible," "the most boring book imaginable," with "laughable" parallels — gutter-level anti-Mormon material. The Book of Mormon also *lacks* the Hebrew markers *View of the Hebrews* actually uses. See [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]] and [[Book of Mormon Lacks the Hebrew Markers View of the Hebrews Uses]].[^5]

5. **God can use a prophet's own understanding (rate: framing).** "Why wouldn't God allow Joseph Smith's own understanding to have any influence over the church that he's founding?" Influence is expected, not embarrassing, on any non-mechanical model of inspiration/translation.[^6]

## Steelman of the Critic
The "proves too much" reply does not fully neutralize the strongest form of the cultural argument. (a) There is a difference between scripture *reflecting* its milieu (everyone grants this) and a text being *wholly explicable* by its milieu with no remainder — the critic's real claim is the latter, that 19th-century sources suffice to *generate* the Book of Mormon, which the parallels are meant (however weakly) to support. (b) Granting cultural influence concedes ground a *mechanistic* translation theory cannot afford; it works only if one adopts a looser catalyst/dynamic model, which has its own costs (it makes the text's "ancientness" harder to test). (c) The Paul/Aquinas parallels show influence is compatible with inspiration *in general*, but do not by themselves establish that *this* text is inspired rather than merely derivative. The decisive question is therefore not "was there influence?" (yes, trivially) but "is there a remainder the milieu cannot explain?" — which the names/onomastics and reverse-anachronism evidence are meant to supply.

## Best Quotes

> "If that's an argument against Mormonism, it's an argument against Christianity."[^3]

> "The idea that this was plagiarized in order to write the Book of Mormon is a joke. It is not defensible ... Mormon apologists *beg* you to read *View of the Hebrews*."[^5]
