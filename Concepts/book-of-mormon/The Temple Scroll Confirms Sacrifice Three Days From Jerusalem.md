# The Temple Scroll Confirms Sacrifice Three Days From Jerusalem

## The Point
A standard anachronism charge against the Book of Mormon is that Lehi offers sacrifice in the wilderness (1 Nephi 2:6-7) even though Deuteronomy 12 — the law in force after King Josiah's reforms — restricts sacrifice to the Jerusalem temple. Fife argues this supposed blunder is actually a bullseye: an ancient legal exception, unknown in Joseph Smith's day, permits sacrifice away from the temple when the worshipper is more than a three-day journey from Jerusalem. The same passage also showcases a second buried detail — the ancient Near Eastern legal distinction between a "thief" and a "robber," which Joseph Smith could not have known but which the text uses precisely.

## Evidence
- **The Temple Scroll (11QT).** Discovered among the Dead Sea Scrolls over a century after the Book of Mormon was published, it explicitly carves out the three-day exception: clean animals must be slaughtered at the temple only "within a distance of a three days' journey" — implying that beyond that distance, sacrifice elsewhere is permissible (11QT 52:13-16) [^1]. Lehi, "three days in the wilderness," builds an altar and offers sacrifice — exactly in line with this recovered law.
- **Thieves vs. robbers.** In the ancient Near East a *thief* was an insider who stole from a neighbor (handled by local law), while a *robber* was an outsider who threatened the community and could be met with lethal/military force [^2]. Laban deliberately brands the sons of Lehi as "robbers" ("Behold thou art a robber, and I will slay thee," 1 Nephi 3:13), licensing himself to kill them. Fife notes that this same legal logic illuminates why the Lord later justifies Nephi in taking Laban's life by contrasting Laban (an outsider/robber) with the "nation" of Lehi (1 Nephi 4:13) [^3].

Both points sit alongside Fife's larger "How Did Joseph Smith Know?" argument and complement the theological case in [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]] and the textual-transmission case in [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]].

## Best Quotes
> "How crazy is it that the 'Temple Scroll' reiterates that sacrifices outside the temple are allowed if getting back to Jerusalem is more than a three-day journey?" [^1]

> "By labeling the sons of Lehi as robbers, Laban could take military action against them and slay them. The accusation that they are robbers is deliberate by Laban." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is part of Fife's cumulative case that the [[Book of Mormon]] keeps anticipating details about the ancient Near East that postdate its 1830 publication. It pairs naturally with [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]] — where wilderness sacrifice is itself evidence of pre-reform religion — turning two alleged anachronisms into convergent evidences.
