# Benjamin, "Son of the Right Hand" — Democratized Enthronement

## The Claim
The name **Benjamin** in Hebrew means "**son of the right hand**." King Benjamin's climactic temple speech ([[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5:7-9]]) — delivered at the enthronement of his son Mosiah II — quotes royal coronation psalms and **democratizes** the king's name and station: every covenanter becomes a son/daughter at God's **right hand**, bearing the name of Christ. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The name's meaning.** *Ben-yamin* = "son of the right hand." Strength: **high** (standard Hebrew). [^1]
2. **Biblical control: Psalm 2.** "This day he hath spiritually begotten you" quotes **Psalm 2:7** ("Thou art my Son; this day have I begotten thee"), the Davidic coronation formula — a *verifiable* allusion. Strength: **high**. [^2]
3. **Biblical control: Psalm 110.** "Found at the right hand of God" echoes **Psalm 110:1** ("Sit thou at my right hand"), the *par excellence* enthronement psalm. Strength: **high**. [^3]
4. **Democratization + new name.** Normally an enthroned king receives a **new name**; Benjamin gives the new name (the name of Christ/Messiah, "anointed one") to the *whole people*, making them sons/daughters at the right hand. The "right hand" motif ties the king's own name to the coronation imagery and to the christological Son-at-the-Father's-right-hand. Strength: **moderate-high** (a striking convergence of name, liturgy, and theology). [^4]
5. **Theological payoff.** The episode shows onomastic study yielding *theology*, not just historicity — covenant adoption and deification language emerging from the name and the psalms. Strength: **interpretive**. [^4]

**Steelman:** A critic notes that "right hand" enthronement imagery and Psalm 2/110 were available to any Bible reader, so quoting them needn't involve Benjamin's *name*; the name-to-speech link ("son of the right hand" → "right hand of God") may be the analyst's connection, since the text never flags it. Bowen's strongest point is the convergence: a king *named* "son of the right hand" delivering a coronation speech climaxing on "the right hand of God" is a coherence one would expect a Hebrew narrator to engineer.
