# Nahom Inscription Confirms the Book of Mormon Geography

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Skeptics ask whether there is any "hard physical evidence, something that exists in the ground that supports the Book of Mormon." Arnold answers: "the Nahom inscription is as hard as it gets." The book describes [[Lehi]]'s family traveling south along the Arabian Peninsula from Jerusalem, then turning east; a man named Ishmael dies and is buried at a place called **Nahom** [^20][^21].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The altars.** "Decades after the Book of Mormon was published, archaeologists excavating in Yemen discovered ancient stone altars bearing the inscription NHM dating to the 6th and 5th centuries BC" — the era of Lehi's journey — "located precisely where the ancient frankincense trail turned east toward the coast of Oman." There is also "a burial stone from the region bearing the name Ishmael." *Rating: the NHM altars (excavated by a German team at the Bar'an temple in Marib) are real and genuinely datable to roughly Lehi's period; this is the single strongest physical datum in the video.* [^21]
2. **The consonantal-script point.** "Ancient Semitic scripts like Hebrew, South Arabian, and Phoenician were consonantal... So, NHM in ancient South Arabian script is correctly vocalized as Nahom." The modern **Nehem/Nihm** tribal region in Yemen "still bears those same three consonants today, continuously documented from antiquity." Arnold insists "this isn't a stretch or an approximation. NHM is Nahom by the standard rules of how that script works." *Rating: the consonantal reconstruction is linguistically legitimate; vowel reconstruction always carries some latitude.* [^22][^23]
3. **No 1829 source.** "The Arabian Peninsula's trade routes and inscriptions were not mapped by Western scholars in 1829," so there was no source Joseph could have consulted for where the frankincense trail turned east, what tribal names existed in 600 BC, or local burial traditions [^23]. This is the anachronistic-knowledge core of the argument.
4. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) NHM/Nihm is a *known place and tribe* in some 18th–19th-century European sources (e.g., Carsten Niebuhr's 1763 map labels "Nehhm"), so a determined critic argues the name was not strictly inaccessible, even if obscure. (b) "Turning east at Nahom" is a directional match that the *terrain itself* makes natural along the incense route, reducing the surprise. (c) The "Ishmael burial stone" is a separately dated artifact and a common Semitic name; tying it to the narrative is weaker than the NHM altars. Arnold treats the no-1829-source premise as airtight; the Niebuhr-map counter is the strongest skeptical reply and is not addressed [^23].
