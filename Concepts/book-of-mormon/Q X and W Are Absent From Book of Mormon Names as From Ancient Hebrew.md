# Q, X, and W Are Absent From Book of Mormon Names as From Ancient Hebrew

## The Claim
The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] argues that Cameron Bertuzzi made a "stupid can of worms" mistake by citing Book of Mormon **proper names** as evidence *against* the book — because names are, in Green's view, one of the few lines of evidence *for* it. Two onomastic facts impressed him: (1) of the 337 proper names in the Book of Mormon, none contain the letters **Q, X, or W**, which also do not occur in ancient Hebrew; and (2) about a dozen Hebrew-sounding names that looked like "gibberish" were later independently verified, usually by non-Mormon scholars, as real ancient names.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Q/X/W absence matches ancient Hebrew (rate: moderate-to-strong, structural).** "There are 337 proper names in the Book of Mormon, but none of them have the letters Q, X, or W. And those letters don't appear in ancient Hebrew." Green's point: an uneducated frontier author inventing hundreds of names is unlikely to have known to avoid those letters, let alone to have avoided them consistently. "You can just believe that Joseph Smith is the luckiest guesser of all time."[^2]

2. **Joseph's lack of education sharpens the puzzle (rate: moderate, biographical).** Green stresses Joseph "wasn't a big reader ... an uneducated farm boy ... according to his mom the least educated of her children." Knowing "the Bible" would not teach the Hebrew letter inventory, so the pattern is hard to attribute to absorbed reading.[^3]

3. **A dozen "gibberish" names were later verified (rate: moderate-to-strong, cumulative).** "There are like a dozen Hebrew names that sound like literal gibberish ... and in the past century they've all been independently verified, usually by non-Mormon scholars" — names "we didn't know were real names until decades after Joseph Smith died." This connects to the wordplay and attested-name evidence in [[Book of Mormon Names Show Authorial Wordplay]] and [[Sariah Attested as an Ancient Female Name]].[^4]

4. **Citing names backfires for the critic (rate: framing).** "Bringing up names, specifically names, in the Book of Mormon as evidence against Mormonism is just bizarre ... that's like one of the few pieces of evidence *for* the Book of Mormon." See the parallelomania reply to the names-from-Marco-Polo/Mormo line in [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]].[^5]

> This is a textbook case of [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]]: the onomasticon contains structure and later-verified data that a 19th-century fabricator is unlikely to have produced.

## Steelman of the Critic
The names argument is suggestive but not airtight. (a) **Q/X/W** are English orthographic conventions; their "absence" depends on the transliteration scheme Joseph (or the printer) used, and several Book of Mormon names *were* later regularized in spelling, so the statistic is partly an artifact of how the names are rendered in English. (b) English itself uses Q/X/W rarely in proper names, so the base-rate of accidentally avoiding them across a pseudo-biblical naming style may be higher than "luckiest guesser" implies. (c) "Later verified" names require scrutiny case-by-case: some "verifications" are contested matches on consonantal skeletons, the same loose standard apologists reject when critics use it (parallelomania cuts both ways). (d) Counting and provenance of the "337" and "a dozen" figures should be checked against scholarship before leaning on them. The strongest residue is the *cumulative* improbability when the structural pattern and the best-attested names (e.g. Sariah, Nahom-adjacent names) are taken together — which is a convergence argument, not a proof.

## Best Quotes

> "There are 337 proper names in the Book of Mormon, but none of them have the letters Q, X, or W. And those letters don't appear in ancient Hebrew. So there's no way that Joseph Smith could have known that."[^2]

> "There were a dozen undiscovered Hebrew names in the Book of Mormon, and we didn't know they were real names until like decades after Joseph Smith died."[^4]
