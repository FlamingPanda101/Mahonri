# The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves

## The Claim
[[Alex Arnold]] frames the strongest form of the temple argument as a claim about the *direction* of accumulating evidence: "Fraud gets exposed over time. Always. Evidence will keep accumulating *against* the person making the fraudulent claim." With the endowment the opposite has happened — "each decade of relevant scholarship has actually added to the case rather than undermining it."[^1] Four data points, each **inaccessible to Joseph Smith** and each confirmed **after his 1844 death**, anchor the argument:[^1][^2]
1. The **Dead Sea Scrolls** (discovered 1947) include the **Book of Giants**, which names a figure **Mahujah** — appearing as **Mahijah** in LDS scripture published in **1830**, in the same narrative context (and equivalent under Semitic naming rules).
2. The **[[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8]]** "**sons of God**" variant (vs. Masoretic "sons of Israel") in the Scrolls confirms the **divine-council** framework the Book of Mormon's premortal-council cosmology requires.
3. **[[Margaret Barker]]'s First-Temple reconstruction** (published across the 1980s–2000s) confirmed the specific ritual theology the endowment preserves — garments, veil ceremony, deification, divine council.
4. The **[[Acts of John 94-96|Acts of John]]'s 1924 translation** confirmed that Jesus personally instituted a mystery ritual with structural elements matching the endowment — 82 years after 1842.

"The direction of the evidence is the argument. Fraud always runs the other way."[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The chronology is the whole point and is checkable.** 1830 / 1842 (LDS publications) precede 1924 (*Acts of John* in English), 1947 (Scrolls), and the 1980s–2000s (Barker) — public, datable facts. A forger cannot retrofit content to sources that did not yet exist. *Rating: a real and unusual evidential structure.* [^2]
2. **The Mahujah/Mahijah datum is concrete.** A specific name in a specific narrative setting, matched between 1830 LDS scripture and a Qumran text first available a century later, is harder to attribute to chance than a thematic "parallel." *Rating: striking, though one onomastic correspondence should be weighed alongside the cluster, not alone — see [[Unconscious Absorption Cannot Explain Suppressed Content]].* [^2]
3. **The cumulative pattern is what carries the load, not any single item.** Arnold concedes each piece is "a Costco sample," and the force is in the *convergence* of independent lines (manuscript variants, name onomastics, ritual reconstruction, apocryphal text) all pointing the same way. *Rating: the convergence is the strength; it is also where a critic will press hardest.* [^1]
4. **It connects to the explicit three-options frame.** The episode closes by arguing only three explanations exist — coincidence, Masonic borrowing, fraud — and that post-death confirmation defeats fraud while specificity defeats coincidence and [[The Masonry Objection Must Locate the Specific Content Not the General Pattern|the content gap defeats Masonry]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** (a) **Survivorship / cherry-picking:** "each decade adds to the case" is asserted, not demonstrated; a critic notes the same decades produced findings *against* other LDS claims (Book of Abraham papyri, Book of Mormon population genetics), so selecting the confirming items and narrating a one-directional trend is itself a bias. (b) **Dating disputes:** Barker's reconstruction is a *contested minority* thesis, and the relevant Dead Sea Scrolls dating and the *Acts of John*'s relationship to apostolic practice are debated — so "confirmed by later scholarship" overstates a still-live scholarly situation. (c) **Loose onomastics:** Mahujah↔Mahijah depends on accepting flexible Semitic equivalence and a shared "narrative context" that critics say is read in generously. (d) **Forgery can still front-run scholarship by luck or shrewd reading of what was already available** (Enoch traditions, the *Book of Enoch* was known in Laurence's 1821 English translation, etc.). A Latter-day Saint reply: the argument does not claim *proof*, only that the *direction* of the strongest temple-specific data is anomalous for fraud, and that the burden shifts — a critic must explain the specific correspondences (Deut 32:8 divine council, the *kothoneth* garment, the circle ritual) rather than wave at the general possibility of coincidence; "fraud always runs the other way" is a probabilistic claim about where evidence tends, not a deductive proof.

## Best Quotes
> "Fraud gets exposed over time. Always. Evidence will keep accumulating against the person making the fraudulent claim. But what has happened with the LDS temple argument is the opposite. Each decade of relevant scholarship has actually added to the case rather than undermining it." [^1]

> "These aren't general parallels, they're specific, verifiable correspondences that became available to scholars only after Joseph's death. The direction of the evidence is the argument. Fraud always runs the other way." [^2]
