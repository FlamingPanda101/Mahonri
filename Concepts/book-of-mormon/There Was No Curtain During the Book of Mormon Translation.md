# There Was No Curtain During the Book of Mormon Translation

#type/claim

## The Claim
Johnny Harris pictures Joseph Smith dictating the Book of Mormon from *behind a curtain*, looking through stones at the plates so "no one else could see." Keystone grants the image some grace (even Church art has portrayed it this way), but argues the historical accounts of the actual translation describe **no curtain**: critics who say Joseph translated from behind a curtain are mixing up the earlier moment when Joseph *copied characters* off the plates (probably behind a screen) with the later production of the text we now have.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Two distinct events get conflated (core correction).** Drawing on Gerrit Dirkmaat and Michael MacKay's work, Keystone distinguishes (a) Joseph copying characters from the plates "probably from behind a curtain," which [[Martin Harris]] then carried to [[Charles Anthon]], from (b) the later dictation of the actual text. The curtain belongs to (a), not (b).[^2] *Rating: strong — the documented sequence supports the distinction.*

2. **The text was dictated in the open (key datum).** Later accounts of translating the surviving text "indicate that there was no curtain — that Joseph would look at the interpreters or his seer stone in the hat with no access to notes or reference materials, and dictate the text in front of whoever happened to be there at the time."[^3] *Rating: strong — converges with the seer-stone-in-hat witness accounts.*

3. **Why it matters apologetically.** A curtain invites the suspicion that Joseph was concealing notes, books, or a confederate; the open, no-notes, no-reference dictation removes the "something suspicious behind the curtain" inference and feeds the production-feat argument. Keystone is careful: this "doesn't prove Joseph was a prophet," but it "makes it a lot harder for people to claim he was doing anything suspicious." Harris "leaves the door wide open for people to make assumptions" that are "very likely not accurate."[^4] See [[Dictation Speed and Method Argue Against Fabrication]]. *Rating: moderate — the inference is defused, not the historicity proven.*

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman of Harris)
The curtain image is not invented out of nothing: a screen really was used during the early character-copying phase, the Church itself has produced art showing a curtain, and the sources for the open dictation are reminiscences gathered later, which carry the usual recollection caveats. A critic can fairly say the "behind a curtain" shorthand is a forgivable compression rather than a fabrication, and that the seer-stone-in-hat method (face buried in a hat) is itself strange enough that removing the curtain does not make the scene ordinary. Keystone's point survives this: the strongest sources for the *text* we have describe no curtain and no notes, so the specific "concealed behind a curtain" suspicion is the part that is unsupported.

## Best Quotes

> "Later accounts of the translation of the text we have today... indicate that there was no curtain, that Joseph would look at the interpreters or his seer stone in the hat with no access to notes or reference materials and dictate the text in front of whoever happened to be there."[^3]

> "When people say that Joseph dictated from behind a curtain, they're probably mixing up the time when he was just copying down characters for when he was actually translating the text."[^2]
