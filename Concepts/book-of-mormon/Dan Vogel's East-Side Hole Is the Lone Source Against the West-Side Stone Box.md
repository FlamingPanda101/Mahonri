# Dan Vogel's East-Side Hole Is the Lone Source Against the West-Side Stone Box

## The Claim

[[Josh Gailey]] takes critic [[Dan Vogel]]'s specific position — that the excavation was a money-digger hole on the **east** side of the hill and that there was **no stone box** — and tallies all 16 relevant historical sources under a stated rubric. The result: a lopsided count favoring a **west-side / summit** hole with an empty **stone box**, with Vogel's east-side reading resting on a single late, indirect, unsympathetic source.[^1][^2] This applies the vault's [[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates|minimal-facts method]] against a named opponent, complementing the [[Informed Saints - The Historical Evidence for Gold Plates Critics Cant Answer|Informed Saints]] treatment.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The rubric is borrowed, not bespoke
Gailey grounds the analysis in the logic-of-history work of Behan (B.H.) McCullagh: prefer **early** to late, **firsthand** to hearsay, **enemy/unsympathetic** attestation (which carries an "aura of truth" against interest), and **multiple independent** attestations; then infer the best explanation. He notes [[Don Bradley]] and other historians use similar methods — so this is "not Josh's rulebook."[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a framing move (uses agreed historiographical criteria, neutralizing the "special pleading" charge).

### 2. The 16-source tally
Of 16 sources: nine reference a general hole/excavation; four place it on a side without specifying; four put it near the **top/summit**; **five** place it on the **west side** (Joseph Smith, [[Oliver Cowdery]], [[Edward Stevenson]]'s unnamed local, and the hostile [[Lorenzo Saunders]]); **five** attest a **stone box** (Joseph, Oliver, [[Martin Harris]], [[David Whitmer]], the unnamed local). Exactly **one** source — Frederick Mather (1880), late, indirect, unsympathetic, and laced with thunder-and-lightning embellishment — puts the dig on the **east** side; a second notes an east-side hole made by later **Rochester treasure-seekers**. Vogel's east-side claim relies on the lone Mather source.[^4][^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the asymmetry is the argument; five vs. one, with the "five" including hostile and eyewitness sources).

### 3. The hostile "no fresh dirt" datum dates the dig to 1823
Lorenzo Saunders — a former treasure-hunting associate hostile to the Smiths — went to the hill the Sunday after the plates were taken and found the west-side excavation **old, with no fresh dirt**. Against his own interest, this corroborates Joseph's account of first opening the box in **1823** (under [[Moroni]]'s annual instruction) and merely clearing it in 1827 — an independent attestation of the *timing*. See [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]].[^6]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a hostile source supplying location *and* chronology against interest).

### 4. The east-side hole is conceded — and harmless
Gailey grants there probably *was* a large east-side hole (from money-diggers / the Rochester company) and "sees no reason to reject it." Multiple holes on a hill long known for treasure-digging is expected; the question is narrowly whether a west-side hole with a stone box existed, which the sources overwhelmingly support. Vogel must still account for the **five** independent stone-box attestations he rejects.[^7]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (concession removes a red herring and isolates the real disagreement).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Vogel can press that *counting* sources is not weighing them: most of the "west side / stone box" attestations trace back through a small, interconnected early-Mormon circle (Joseph, Oliver, and those who read them), so they may not be truly independent, and Joseph's and Oliver's accounts are interested. The "stone box" itself is exactly what a pious-fraud narrative would generate, and later hostile sources describing a box (Saunders, the unnamed local) could be echoing decades of published Mormon claims rather than autopsy. "No fresh dirt" confirms only that *a* hole was old, not that it was the plate-box. And a single source is not worthless if it is the most candid: Mather's east-side detail might preserve the unembellished core under the dramatization. The reply is that the rubric explicitly *weights* (early, firsthand, hostile, multiple) rather than mere head-count, that the location was known to *both* friendly and hostile sources, and that the money-digger-hole theory fails on geography — but the believer should concede that source-independence is the live question and that "empty box" remains an inference to the best explanation, not a proven fact (cf. [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]]).
