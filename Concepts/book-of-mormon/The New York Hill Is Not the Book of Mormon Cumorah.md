# The New York Hill Is Not the Book of Mormon Cumorah

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Mark Gagnon]] treats the New York hill where Joseph Smith recovered the plates as the Book of Mormon's hill **Cumorah** — site of "an enormous battle between two Judeo-Christian tribes... in like 400 AD" — and reads that identification into the record itself.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The name was applied late, by early members
[[Keystone]]: "Early Latter-day Saints didn't start to call the New York hill... 'Hill Cumorah' until the mid 1830s" — and notably *Joseph Smith himself did not originally call it that*. The equation is a later folk identification, "probably based on a misreading or misunderstanding of [[Mormon 6.6|Mormon 6:6]]."[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the mid-1830s emergence of the "Cumorah" label for the New York hill is a documented historical point.

### 2. The text separates the burial sites
[[Mormon 6.6|Mormon 6:6]] distinguishes two deposits: Mormon "hid up in the hill Cumorah all the records... save it were these few plates which I gave unto my son Moroni." The abridgment Joseph received was the *exception* Moroni carried away — so "Mormon 6 suggests that the Book of Mormon Cumorah wasn't where the plates were ultimately buried."[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the text itself separates the records-cache from the plates Moroni kept and later deposited.

### 3. The geography does not fit New York
"Book of Mormon geography doesn't match up with the New York location. There's no evidence of a massive battle." So "this Cumorah probably isn't that Cumorah."[^4] This aligns with the mainstream limited-geography view — see [[The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate]] and contrast the misfires of [[The Vernal Holley Map Places Book of Mormon Geography Wrong]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the absence of evidence for a vast 400 AD battle at the New York drumlin is widely acknowledged; the positive geography is debated.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic notes that for over a century many Latter-day Saints — including church leaders — *did* identify the New York hill as Cumorah and located the final battles there, so distancing the Church from that view now can look like a convenient retreat once the New York site proved archaeologically barren. One can also argue that separating "the hill where the plates were found" from "the hill Cumorah" multiplies hills to rescue the geography. The disciplined reply: the *textual* distinction in Mormon 6:6 is real and predates the controversy, the late date of the "Cumorah" label for the New York hill is documented, and the limited-geography reading is independently motivated — so Gagnon's confident identification of the two is an overstatement, even though the popular tradition he echoes was once common among members.

## Best Quotes
> "Early Latter-day Saints didn't start to call the New York hill, where Joseph Smith retrieved the plates, Hill Cumorah, until the mid 1830s — before even Joseph Smith called it that."[^2]

> "Book of Mormon geography doesn't match up with the New York location. There's no evidence of a massive battle... this Cumorah probably isn't that Cumorah."[^4]
