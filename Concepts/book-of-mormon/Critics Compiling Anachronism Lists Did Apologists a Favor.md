# Critics Compiling Anachronism Lists Did Apologists a Favor

## The Claim

The centuries-long critical project of cataloguing Book of Mormon "anachronisms" has, in effect, handed Latter-day Saints a research agenda: each alleged anachronism is a concrete, testable prediction that can be investigated, and as the items get resolved the list itself becomes a record of vindication. On this framing the believer should be *grateful* to critics rather than treat them as enemies — and should reciprocate by **steelmanning** each objection before answering it. The episode stages this as a deliberate contrast between host [[Cardon Ellis]]'s comedic "these are bad people" bit and [[Jordan Bowman]]'s and [[Hayden Holm]]'s more measured counter that the critics created the opportunity.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The list is a free, falsifiable research program
Because each anachronism names something specific that "shouldn't" exist (steel, gold plates, horses, cement), it tells investigators exactly what to look for; the [[Stick of Joseph]] project is built on chasing those items down. Jordan: the critics "created such a good opportunity for us... we got to be thankful to the people that have been critical."[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — a sound methodological point (anachronism claims are testable), though it does not by itself show the tests come out favorably.

### 2. The steelman discipline produces better answers
Hayden repeatedly says the project's rule is to take each argument, "steelman it... and then see what holes are poked in it naturally," and that understanding the critic's side is the more persuasive posture. This is the same charitable-but-critical method the vault commends elsewhere.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — about argumentative virtue, not about the underlying historicity.

### 3. The believer's own honest doubts mirror the critics'
Hayden concedes he reads the Book of Mormon and notices the same problems: "I would have been the one to make [the list]" — so the anachronism inventory is not merely hostile but the natural output of any careful reader. This blunts the "anti-Mormons are just haters" caricature the episode otherwise plays for laughs.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — concession-based; strengthens credibility rather than proving any specific claim.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic answers that "thanking us for the list" reframes a *failed prediction* as a gift: if the Book of Mormon really were an ancient record, the anachronisms should not have been there to catalogue in the first place, and "we'll eventually find it" is exactly the unfalsifiable move (the episode itself jokes about God leaving one item permanently unprovable). The honest version of the critique is that gratitude-plus-steelmanning is good rhetoric but does not change the score: the remaining unresolved items (e.g. New-World steel swords) are still open, and a method that treats every outcome — resolved *or* unresolved — as faith-promoting is not doing evidential work. A Latter-day Saint reply (made in the episode): the *direction of travel* matters, since authentic texts get easier to defend over time and frauds get easier to debunk (see [[The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves]]), and the spiritual witness, not the batting average, is doing the load-bearing work (see [[God Withholds the Slam Dunk to Preserve Faith]]).

## Best Quotes

> "We got to be thankful to the people that have been critical to the Book of Mormon, or else what would we be doing right now? We'd just be right about everything." [^1]

> "Are you trying to steelman the argument first that there were steel swords? Because that's what I try to do... we're taking every argument and we're trying to steelman it, and then see what holes are poked in it naturally." [^2]

> "If this list hadn't already been made while I was doing this project, I would have been the one to make [it]." [^3]
