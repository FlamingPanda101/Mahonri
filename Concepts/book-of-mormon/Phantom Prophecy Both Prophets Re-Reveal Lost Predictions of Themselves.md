# Phantom Prophecy Both Prophets Re-Reveal Lost Predictions of Themselves

## The Claim
Cameron Bertuzzi's ("Capturing Christianity") lead argument against Mormonism — billed as the one to watch "if you watch nothing else" — is the **"phantom prophecy problem."** Both Muhammad and [[Joseph Smith]] claimed earlier scripture foretold them, but the prophecies are conveniently "lost" and then re-revealed by the prophet himself. For Joseph, the example is the prophecy of a latter-day seer named Joseph (Genesis 50:33 in the Joseph Smith Translation, paralleled in [[2 Nephi 3]]), a verse absent from the Masoretic text, Septuagint, and Dead Sea Scrolls. The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] and his host find the argument "weirdly insubstantial."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the rebuttal)

1. **The Bible itself references lost texts (rate: strong, tu quoque).** The reply notes the Old Testament references "the books of Adam," and the scholarly **Q source** that Matthew and Luke are widely held to draw on is likewise lost. "I don't see how there's some huge issue here with lost text. I think the Bible is probably filled with holes ... and things are missing."[^2]

2. **A "convenient, undisprovable" lie is, by design, undisprovable (rate: moderate, undercutting).** Even granting Joseph were "a self-conscious fraud," referencing a text "you can't check ... that you can't disprove the existence of" is a *designed* feature of such a claim — so the inability to check it "isn't some kind of knockout punch." Green: "even on the assumption that Joseph Smith is a straight-up con man ... he's lying in a way so you can't disprove him. So what are you doing?"[^3]

3. **Mormonism does not hold biblical inerrancy (rate: strong, presupposition).** The argument presupposes a complete, inerrant Bible. But Latter-day Saints affirm the Bible "as far as it is translated correctly" and believe plain and precious things were lost — so a *missing* verse is not a problem for them; if anything the loss of suppressed material is expected. See [[The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument]].[^4]

4. **It is not the kind of evidence that would discredit the Book of Mormon (rate: moderate).** Green expected "an anachronism or something ... that would point to the Book of Mormon being solely produced by the imagination of Joseph Smith." A re-revealed lost prophecy is "non-evidential — this isn't anything." For the Muhammad disanalogy generally, see [[Muhammad and Joseph Smith Are Apples to Oranges]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
There is a real point buried in the argument. (a) An *unfalsifiable* self-prophecy is epistemically worthless as positive evidence *for* the prophet, even if it is not evidence *against* him — so the believer gains nothing from Genesis 50:33 either. (b) The pattern is suspicious: a prophet conveniently producing a now-missing prediction *of himself* fits the profile of pious invention better than of genuine restoration, and "you can't disprove it" is exactly what one would say to shield a fabrication. (c) The Bible-also-references-lost-texts move is not perfectly parallel: Q and the "book of Jasher" are referenced or inferred, not *re-revealed verbatim with new self-serving content*. The honest reading is that the phantom-prophecy charge is not a debunking, but neither is the re-revealed verse a *proof* — it is evidentially inert, which is roughly what Green concludes.
