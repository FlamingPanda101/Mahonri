# Book of Mormon Anachronisms Are a Collective Pattern - Reel

## The Point
**Claim ([[Bill Reel]]):** A real ancient American civilization should leave footprints in archaeology, linguistics, and genetics. Instead the Book of Mormon repeatedly names things that "don't show up in the real world that text describes": pre-Columbian steel, swords for mass warfare, chariots, horses, large-scale wheat and barley, a monetary system, society-wide literacy, and Israelite DNA. Any *one* might be explained away, but "collectively, this is a pattern... almost as if they were never there to begin with."[^1] Reel's deeper move is methodological: he flags three apologetic tactics — *reinterpretation* ("maybe horse doesn't mean horse, steel doesn't mean steel"), demanding we *lower our expectations* every time evidence is missing, and offering *weak or misrepresented examples* (Désiré Charnay's wheeled *figurines* for chariots; "little barley," a different species; a single contested horse bone from a site with dating problems).[^2]

**Rebuttal ([[Robert Gurr]]):** There are "no anachronisms... they're all translation issues." Joseph Smith used the best word available in his King James idiom: "steel" is the KJV rendering later corrected to "bronze," and the Book of Mormon's *molten* swords fit bronze (you don't melt steel) — and thousands of bronze artifacts exist in Mesoamerica.[^3] Horses were a *food* source that could have been eaten to local extinction, Native traditions claim horses "all along," and the scientific extinction story (an ice age) is itself implausible in "tropical Mexico" — so the consensus deserves skepticism, not gullible acceptance.[^4]

## Evidence
1. **The list, stacked (rated: strong as a cumulative case).** Reel's enumerated absences — steel, mass-warfare swords, wheeled transport, horses in the period, Old-World grains, coinage, widespread literacy, Israelite DNA — are each individually contestable but jointly form his strongest argument: "their DNA, their animals, their crops, their weapons, their language... everything you would expect from a real civilization is missing."[^1] The vault's standing replies engage each thread: [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]], [[One Anachronism Does Not Disprove the Book of Mormon]], [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism]], [[Mesoamerican Metallurgy Is Trending Earlier Toward Book of Mormon Times]].
2. **The reinterpretation critique cuts both ways (rated: moderate).** Reel is right that endlessly redefining terms can become unfalsifiable; the LDS reply is that *all* translation introduces target-culture vocabulary, so loan-shifting is expected, not ad hoc — see [[Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism]] and [[Translation Introduces Anachronism Even in Divine Translation]]. The honest residue: a reply that *only ever* lowers expectations risks the [[Heads Faith Wins, Tails Reason Loses - The Unfalsifiability Charge]] problem.
3. **The chariot/barley examples (rated: strong for Reel, narrow).** Charnay's finds were wheeled toy figurines, not transport vehicles, and "little barley" is a distinct species — fair points that a careful apologist should concede as *not yet* positive confirmations rather than dismiss. *Steelman of Gurr:* the steel→bronze move is a genuine, checkable philological argument (brass+steel vs. brass+bronze; "molten" swords), not mere hand-waving.[^3]

## Best Quotes
> "Now individually, you might try to explain one of these away... But collectively, this is a pattern... everything you would expect from a real civilization is missing. Almost as if they were never there to begin with." [^1]

> "When apologists use this tactic, notice what's happening. The words stop meaning what they normally mean... We're taught to change our expectations and assumptions every single time when things aren't what we expected." [^2]

> "There's no anachronisms within the Book of Mormon. They're all translation issues... when it talks about making steel swords... they're bronze because it says I made molten swords. You don't molten steel, you molten bronze." [^3]

## LDS Connection
This is the cumulative-case form of the anachronism challenge, and the vault meets it not by denying the gaps but by arguing the list is *shrinking* over time ([[Shrinking Anachronism List as Evidence of Authenticity]], [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]]), that the proper standard is convergence rather than proof ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]), and that "could God" precedes "would the data look exactly like this" ([[Could God vs Would God on the Book of Mormon]]). Gurr's steel→bronze and "eaten to extinction" replies are live but partial; the strongest answer concedes the weak examples and rests the case on the trend lines and on translation theory rather than on a single horse bone.
