# The Spanish Horse Math Cannot Explain Continent-Wide Horses by 1567

## The Claim

Independent researcher [[Daniel Johnson]] (presented by [[Jonah Barnes]] on [[Ward Radio]]) turns the horse objection around: if there were *no* horses in the Americas until the Spanish, then the **demographic arithmetic of how Spanish horses supposedly populated the continent does not work**. Early native horse sightings appear far too soon, and far too widespread, for escaped Spanish stock to account for — which the math, not the Book of Mormon, exposes.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The goalposts keep moving earlier — rate: moderate (documentary chronology)
The popular story credits Columbus (1492) or the Pueblo Revolt (1680) or [[Hernan Cortez]] (1519) as the source. But documented native/wild horse sightings predate the Pueblo Revolt: Blackfoot (1754), western Shoshone (before 1700), wild horses a "nuisance" in the Jamestown records (1669), Kiowa (1590), and the Sonora Valley (1567). Each earlier sighting shrinks the window available for Spanish horses to spread. Wikipedia, Johnson notes, had to revise its claim from 1492 to the 1680 Pueblo Revolt and then to Cortez (1519) to keep the story coherent.[^2]

### 2. The Spanish brought too few, and mostly the wrong sex — rate: moderate (record of expeditions)
The Spanish prized horses above crew and kept careful registers; they typically brought **mares only** (more docile; stallions fight aboard ship) or solid-color stallions for war, in small numbers. First-expedition horses repeatedly died at sea, in battle, or were eaten in extremity. Cortez's own records say he *gave* a few horses to natives and returned to find they had been *eaten*. A handful of horses, skewed by sex, is a poor founding population.[^3]

### 3. The doubling-vs-tripling math — rate: moderate (quantitative back-of-envelope)
The Bureau of Land Management *assumes* feral horses double every ~5 years to justify culling, but ranchers and an Axios review of a 1971-to-present control herd found the population only *tripled in 50 years* (25,000 → ~73,000). Johnson's spreadsheet: if Cortez lost 16 horses in 1519 and they *doubled every 5 years*, you'd have ~16,000 by 1569 (so the Sonora Valley in 1567 is conceivable). But at the *observed* tripling-every-50-years rate, you'd have an absolute maximum of ~48 horses by 1567 — nowhere near enough to seed a continent. The 11-month gestation (no foal without surviving a winter) and high miscarriage rates make rabbit-like expansion impossible.[^4]

### 4. The DNA study's 1519 date is an *assumption* — rate: moderate (methodological critique)
The genetics paper Wikipedia cites identified horses in indigenous contexts *conclusively predating* the Pueblo Revolt, then attributed them to 1519 — but the methodology footnote states they *assumed* the integration was bounded by "the first presence of European horses on the North American mainland in 1519." The early date is a *presupposition*, not a finding, because the genetic diversity is so great they reached for the earliest conceivable European source. See [[If It Looks Like a Horse It Must Be Post-Contact - Circular Dating of New World Horses]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The mainstream account survives most of this, and the math is softer than it sounds. (a) Feral horse herds *can* grow ~18–20% per year under good conditions (the BLM figure is not pure bureaucratic invention); a small founding stock plus *continuous resupply* from Spanish ranches in the Caribbean, Mexico, and (after 1598) New Mexico — not just Cortez's 16 — easily produces large populations within a century, and the 1680 Pueblo Revolt dispersed *thousands* at once. (b) Johnson's "16 horses, tripling every 50 years" model is a strawman of the actual mechanism: nobody claims one escape event seeded everything; horses spread by *trade and theft* between Spanish ranches and native nations, compounding far faster than a single feral herd. (c) The 2023 *Science* study's genomic and isotopic data trace early Plains horses to **post-Columbian Spanish lineages** — so even the "surprisingly early" native horses are Spanish-derived, not survivors. (d) Re-identifying Mercer's Yucatán teeth as extinct *Equus conversidens* concedes those are Pleistocene, not Nephite-era. So the legitimate residue is modest: the *popular* "Columbus's horses bred up over 500 years" story is genuinely sloppy, and the genetics paper's 1519 anchor is an assumption — but a properly specified Spanish-introduction model (multiple sources, trade-driven spread, 1600s timing) fits the evidence without any pre-Columbian horse.

## Best Quotes

> "If Cortez lost 16 horses, which he didn't, and if all of them survive, which they can't, and all of them reproduce at the absolute fastest maximum observed rate, there's still just no way that they could... get up to the Sonora Valley by 1567."[^4]

> "They never took ready males; they only took mares... those aren't good odds."[^3]

> "The reason that they point to 1519 is because they assume that the historic integration of horses was bounded... by the first presence of European horses on the North American mainland in 1519. They just assumed it."[^5]
