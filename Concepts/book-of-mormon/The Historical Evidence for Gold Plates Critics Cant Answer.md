# The Historical Evidence for Gold Plates Critics Cant Answer

#type/claim

## The Claim
On [[Ward Radio - More Archaeological Evidences of the Book of Mormon|Ward Radio]] ("Midnight Strike Through Mormons"), host [[Cardon Ellis]] with the [[Stick of Joseph]] crew argue that **writing on bound metal plates** — once dismissed as an obvious 19th-century anachronism — is now solidly attested in the ancient world, removing one of the original objections to the Book of Mormon. The same segment notes that **steel** (Nephi's "most precious steel" sword of [[Laban]], [[1 Nephi 4.31|1 Nephi 4:31]]) was likewise called impossible and has since been vindicated. The framing: these are evidences critics "can't answer," because the very features that looked fabricated in 1830 now fit antiquity, and the overall anachronism list has shrunk dramatically — the trajectory of an authentic claim, not a fraud.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Dated metal plates from the right era exist.** The hosts cite inscribed **gold plates from Bulgaria (~600 BC)** and the **Pyrgi gold tablets from Italy (~500 BC)** — the latter bound with holes for rods, paralleling the description of Joseph Smith's plates as bound rings. Writing sacred texts on thin gold sheets, and binding them, are genuine ancient practices. *Rating: strong on the bare fact — the Pyrgi tablets and other gold-leaf inscriptions are real, dated artifacts; the "bound like Joseph's plates" detail is a fair comparison.*
2. **Steel was prematurely declared impossible.** A 6th-century-BC **Damascus-steel sword** and related finds show carburized/quality steel existed in the relevant period, so Nephi's steel sword is no longer an automatic anachronism. *Rating: moderate-to-strong; ancient steel is attested, though "most precious steel" remains a translation gloss.* See [[Metallurgical Fraud Theory Rebutted]].
3. **The objection has reversed direction.** Of 200+ catalogued anachronisms since 1830, the great majority (the hosts say "over a hundred and seventy") have been struck off — and metal-plate writing and steel are showcase examples of objections that flipped from liability to corroboration. "Frauds always look worse over time"; the Book of Mormon is trending the other way. *Rating: the trend is real; the precise count is the hosts' figure.* See [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]].
4. **Fabrication faces independent barriers anyway.** Even granting the metal-plate medium is ancient, the *witness-constrained* plates Joseph produced are hard to fake on color, weight, thinness, and engraving, and bulk metal was beyond the Smith family's means. *Rating: a separate, converging line.* See [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]] and [[Gold Plates Fabrication Was Prohibitively Expensive]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** "Metal plates exist in antiquity" answers a claim few serious critics still press; it does not show *the* gold plates existed or that Joseph translated an ancient record — at most it removes one weak objection, which is not the same as positive evidence for the Book of Mormon. The Bulgarian/Pyrgi plates are from the wrong continent and culture (Thracian, Etruscan-Phoenician), so they establish *general* plausibility of metal-plate writing, not a Mesoamerican Nephite practice. And "critics can't answer this" is rhetoric: the standard critical reply — that the plates were never available for examination and the witnesses describe them in visionary terms — is untouched by Old World artifacts. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the apologetic burden here is deliberately narrow — to neutralize a once-confident anachronism — and on that narrow point it succeeds: the medium Joseph described is demonstrably ancient and was *not* known to be so in 1830, which is awkward for a fraud hypothesis. Stacked with the witness and cost arguments, the metal-plate vindication is a real (if modest) contribution to a cumulative case, not the whole case.
