# Haplogroup X2A Is Not Valid Evidence for the Book of Mormon

## The Claim

Some defenders point to mitochondrial **Haplogroup X2A** in certain Native American
populations — which has Old World / Near Eastern roots — as DNA evidence *for* the
Book of Mormon. Rappleye and qualified LDS geneticists reject this: it is bad
evidence and should not be used.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. X2A in the Americas is far too ancient

Scientists can date how far back a haplogroup goes in a population. Haplogroup X in
the Americas dates to roughly **10,000 BC** — pre-Jaredite, pre-Lehite, vastly older
than any Book of Mormon migration. It cannot mark Lehi's arrival ~600 BC.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a dateable genetic fact).

### 2. It is a cousin lineage, not descended from Near-Eastern X

Geneticists can determine whether lineages are parent-descendant or merely siblings/
cousins sharing a common ancestor. American X2A is a **cousin** of Near-Eastern X,
not descended from it — so it would indicate a shared deep ancestor, not a migration
from the Near East. The mainstream reconstruction has it arriving via Siberia and the
Bering land bridge.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (phylogenetic relationship, not surface similarity).

### 3. The qualified LDS geneticists decline to use it

[[Ugo Perego]] (population geneticist specializing in Native American DNA) and [[John Butler (DNA scientist)]] — people with every motive to cite it if it were valid —
explicitly say it cannot be used. Rappleye recounts intervening to stop a promoter
from advancing it as bad science: "We don't need to use bad junk data, junk science,
junk arguments to support the Book of Mormon."[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the most motivated experts rejecting their own potential evidence).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A defender could argue X2A's Old World root is at least *consistent* with a Near
Eastern connection and worth flagging for non-specialists. Rappleye's reply models
intellectual honesty: consistency is not evidence; using a refuted argument damages
credibility and the book's defense. This concept doubles as a methodological norm —
see [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]] — that
apologetics must rigorously vet its own "evidence."

## Best Quotes

> "Haplogroup X in the Americas, it goes back to like 10,000 BC... this is pre-Jaredite. This is super ancient."[^2]

> "What the evidence shows is that they're cousins. They're not directly descended from each other... it would mean someone came from somewhere else who had ancestors who came from the ancient Near East."[^3]

> "I trust the Latter-day Saint geneticists who know the evidence and have every motive in the world to talk about something as evidence for the Book of Mormon if it's there who are telling us no, they don't use it... we don't need to use bad junk data, junk science, junk arguments to support the Book of Mormon."[^4]
