# Direct Literary Dependence Requires Exclusivity

## The Claim
To establish that one text (A) directly drew on another (B), parallels alone are insufficient — you must show that the parallels can be explained by **no other potential source**, plus an evidentiary trail that the author of A actually knew and used B. None of the proposed [[Book of Mormon]]/*View of the Hebrews* parallels meets this standard. This is [[Blake Ostler]]'s methodological point, which Smoot endorses.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Exclusivity is the test for dependence (rated: strong, methodological).** "If you want to posit direct dependence ... the only way you can get that to work with parallels is if you can show no other potential parallels from other potential sources" — "a direct literary dependency that can only be explained as literary dependency by no other potential sources."[^1] The shared-biblical-source and common-milieu categories defeat exclusivity for every Book of Mormon parallel (see [[Shared Biblical Sources Are Not Plagiarism]] and [[The Hebraic-Indian Theory Was a Common Period Idea]]).
2. **An evidentiary trail of acquaintance is required (rated: strong, methodological).** "There's got to be some kind of evidentiary breadcrumb ... that person A knew about source B and was very familiar with source B." No such trail links Joseph Smith to *View of the Hebrews* (see [[The Oliver Cowdery Link Is Chronologically Impossible]]).[^2]
3. **The watered-down "milieu" version loses explanatory power (rated: moderate, logical).** Critics, sensing the dependence claim fails, retreat to "*View of the Hebrews* is just an example of the Hebraic-Indian theory" — but once diluted to a generic concept "all over the place," it "doesn't really have a lot of explanatory power."[^3] *Steelman:* the strongest version of the critic's case (David Persuit's sustained book-length argument, which Smoot calls "the best case") at least attempts in-depth textual parallels rather than scattershot lists, and a Bayesian could argue accumulated weak evidence plus geographic proximity raises the probability of dependence even absent a smoking gun. Smoot's reply: probability talk cannot substitute for the exclusivity and breadcrumb conditions; without them the case "is kind of a nothingburger."

## Best Quotes

> "If you want to posit like direct dependence ... the only way you can get that to work with parallels is if you can show no other potential parallels from other potential sources."[^1]

> "There's got to be some kind of evidentiary breadcrumb trail ... that person A knew about source B and was very familiar with source B."[^2]

> "I don't think that any of the parallels that people have pointed out between Book of Mormon and View of the Hebrews qualifies for that."[^1]
