# Ishmael Was a Common Judahite Name in 600 BC

## The Claim

The Book of Mormon names a Jerusalemite "[[Ishmael]]" in [[Lehi]]'s party (~600 BC). [[Neal Rappleye]] argues this is itself a small convergence: Ishmael was a **very common Hebrew name** in 7th–6th-century-BC Judah, exactly the milieu the Book of Mormon describes — even as the same name was comparatively *rare* in South Arabia, where the [[The Ishmael Funerary Stela in South Arabia|Ishmael funerary stela]] was found. The naming fits the "right name in the right place" profile on both ends. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. ~60 Hebrew attestations from the 8th–6th centuries BC — rate: strong (epigraphic)

Rappleye reports that, citing Ernst Axel Knauf and his own search of seals and bullae, the name Ishmael was "very popular in the sixth and seventh centuries BC"; he personally identified about **60 attestations** of Ishmael in Hebrew epigraphy from the 8th–6th centuries BC. One seal even pairs "Ishmael" with the patronym "Nahum" (NHM) — both Book of Mormon-relevant names on a single artifact. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — a large, period-appropriate corpus of Hebrew attestations.

### 2. The name is rare in South Arabia, sharpening the convergence — rate: moderate-to-strong

Of a corpus of ~8,500 South Arabian inscriptions, only **27** carry "Ishmael," and only **four** date to the early first millennium BC. So the name was common where the Book of Mormon's Ishmael came from (Judah) and rare where he died (near Nehem) — the very pattern a genuine traveler would produce. See [[The Ishmael Funerary Stela in South Arabia]]. [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong — the asymmetry is a real "fingerprint," though it cannot identify any individual.

### 3. It answers the "lucky guess" framing — rate: moderate

A skeptic might say "Ishmael" is just a generic biblical name Joseph Smith borrowed. Rappleye's reply: the *period epigraphy* shows it was specifically a popular Judahite name at 600 BC — so the Book of Mormon putting a common-for-its-time name on a Jerusalem family is a quiet, checkable hit, not an anachronism. [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — corroborative, in the same family as the [[Sariah Attested as an Ancient Female Name|Sariah]] attestation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can answer that "Ishmael" is a famous *biblical* name (the son of Abraham) that any 19th-century author would naturally reach for, so its presence proves nothing about ancient authorship — the Hebrew epigraphic frequency is irrelevant if the simplest source is the KJV Old Testament. Rappleye's reply is that this point does not stand alone: it is one strand in the Arabian convergence (the mandatory eastward turn, the dated NHM region, the Frankincense Trail, Bountiful), and the *combination* — including a period-appropriately-common Hebrew name turning up on a 6th-century stela near the very region the text names — is what is striking. The standard remains convergence, not proof (see [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]).
