# Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier

## The Claim

[[Matt Vallejo]] argues that of all the Book of Mormon anomalies, raw **production speed** is the hardest to wave away — and he quantifies it. [[Joseph Smith]] dictated ~270,000 words in ~65 working days, about **4,000 words per day**. Against the distribution of human writing rates this is not merely high but **~4.66 standard deviations above the mean**, a region of the bell curve where, in his words, "there's like not really that many data points." He illustrates the sigma framing for lay viewers (68% within 1σ, 95% within 2σ, 99% within 3σ), so that 4.66σ is far out past the third deviation.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The modern NaNoWriMo control.** The National Novel Writing Month challenge asks entrants to write 50,000 words in 30 days (~1,667/day) — and only **15–20%** complete it each year, despite having computers, keyboards, and the ability to edit and retype. Joseph's ~4,000/day is ~3.5× that target, achieved by hand. Vallejo also notes second-grade-educated subsistence farmers "aren't typically seeking out that type of competition" in the first place.[^3][^4] *Rating: an apt modern benchmark — it grounds "fast" in a measurable contemporary failure rate rather than intuition.*

2. **The period control.** Even the decade's fastest serious authors were far slower: John Neal self-reported ~1,000 words/day for *My Brother Jonathan*; Cooper ~700/day for *The Last of the Mohicans*. Joseph quadruples Neal and is ~6× Cooper. See [[The 1820s Imprint Baseline Places the Book of Mormon at the Statistical Extreme]].[^5]

3. **The hidden multipliers that make 4,000/day worse.** Vallejo stacks constraints that a bare word rate hides: no surviving manuscript or journal (his wife [[Emma Smith]] never reported one); no formal tutor present at the start (only Emma, her brothers, and [[Martin Harris]] before [[Oliver Cowdery]]); a *single draft* with no edits (confirmed against the printer's manuscript); handwriting (slower than typing); and a **scribe read-back** at each step that roughly doubles the time per line. The "he memorized it beforehand" escape, he argues, fails on its own terms — memorizing 4,000 fresh words a day for 60+ days is itself "probability zero."[^6][^7] *Rating: the conjunction of one-draft + no-manuscript + read-back is the genuinely novel contribution here, and it directly pre-empts the standard "prior rehearsal" rebuttal.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **σ presupposes a distribution that isn't really known.** "4.66 standard deviations" requires a defined mean and variance for "human writing speed," which Vallejo does not rigorously establish; the figure is an evocative approximation, not a measured z-score, and the underlying rates are right-skewed rather than normal, so normal-curve percentiles mislead at the tail.
- **Dictation ≠ original composition.** Reciting already-internalized or improvised oral narrative can run far faster than drafting belletristic prose. Skilled oral performers and preachers sustain high word rates; a fluent KJV-cadenced register lowers the per-word cognitive load.
- **"No manuscript" is an argument from silence.** Absence of a reported manuscript is not proof none existed; long mental rehearsal over the years of "telling stories" (which the family did report) could supply structure without a written draft.
- **One feature, not a proof.** Vallejo himself treats speed as one conditioned input feeding [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions|the combined estimate]], not a standalone demonstration; its weight depends on the disputed priors there. Compare the more qualitative [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]], whose steelman notes stylometry and dictation feats are contested.

## Best Quotes

> "The NaNoWriMo challenge... write a 50,000-word book over a period of 30 days... only between 15 to 20% each year actually complete that. These are people that have computers and keyboards and can actually make edits and type."[^3]

> "That puts Joseph's output to about 4,000 words a day, which is three and a half times what people that enter the NaNoWriMo can even do. That puts him at a 4.66 standard deviation over the mean."[^1]

> "There's no one else that is doing a 265,000-word manuscript in one draft with no edits."[^7]
