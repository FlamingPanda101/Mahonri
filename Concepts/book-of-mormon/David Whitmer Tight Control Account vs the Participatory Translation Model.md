# David Whitmer Tight Control Account vs the Participatory Translation Model

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** [[David Whitmer]] reported that [[Joseph Smith]] would see English
words appear on the seer stone, read them to scribe [[Oliver Cowdery]], and the
words would not disappear until written down and read back correctly. If the words
were divinely fixed letter-for-letter ("tight control"), then later **editing**
of the text contradicts the translation story and implies the words were not from
God after all.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Joseph's own claim is modest

Saints Unscripted stresses that "Joseph only ever said he translated the Book of
Mormon by the gift and power of God" — he never publicly endorsed the
word-on-the-stone mechanism. The detailed "tight control" picture comes from
Whitmer's later reminiscence, not from Joseph.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on the narrow point (Joseph's public statements are
> minimal); weaker as a full account, since many faithful sources *do* cite Whitmer
> approvingly.

### 2. The canonized D&C 9 describes a participatory method

[[D&C 9|Doctrine and Covenants 9]] (the Oliver Cowdery revelation) says you must
"study it out in your mind. Then you must ask me if it be right." The host argues
this canonized text outranks Whitmer's reminiscence and depicts a
**collaborative, effortful** process — which comfortably accommodates later
refinement and editing.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (canonized primary text vs. third-party late
> recollection). Note D&C 9 concerns Cowdery's *failed* attempt to translate, so
> its applicability to Joseph's method is itself debated.

### 3. Whitmer is "mistaken" or "misinterpreted"

The host's stated position: "I do not believe Joseph was simply reading words that
appeared... I think he was involved in the process. That means I think that David
Whitmer is either mistaken or that perhaps we wildly misinterpret what he meant."[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** This is a frank concession that the apologetic requires
> setting aside or reinterpreting Whitmer — honest, but it weakens any argument
> that *also* leans on Whitmer's testimony for the book's authenticity.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic can fairly note that LDS apologists invoke Whitmer's vivid
"words-on-the-stone" account when it bolsters the *miraculous* nature of the
translation, then discard it when it makes *editing* awkward. You cannot have it
both ways: either the testimony is reliable (and edits are a problem) or it is
unreliable (and a frequently cited faith-promoting account collapses). A
participatory model also blurs the line between "translation by the power of God"
and ordinary 19th-century composition — which is the critic's whole point.

## Best Quotes

> "David Whitmer actually says that Joseph would read off the English to Oliver
> Cowdery... and when it was written down and repeated to brother Joseph to see if
> it was correct, then it would disappear."[^1]

> "In section 9, it describes part of the translation process and says... you must
> study it out in your mind. Then you must ask me if it be right."[^3]

> "I think that David Whitmer is either mistaken or that perhaps we wildly
> misinterpret what he meant by his statement."[^4]
