# The Vernal Holley Map Places Book of Mormon Geography Wrong

## The Point
In 1992 Vernal Holley argued — and the [[CES Letter]] reproduces via two maps — that Joseph Smith invented the [[Book of Mormon]]'s place names by lightly altering names of towns and bodies of water around the Great Lakes (e.g. Oneida → Onidah, Antioch → Ani-Anti, Monroe → Moroni, Ripple Lake → Ripliancum).[^1] Fife's rebuttal is twofold and decisive: (1) the geography is internally wrong — placed against the Book of Mormon's own consistent internal map, "virtually everything in the Vernal Holley map is in the wrong place"; and (2) the name list collapses once you check dates, because many of the "parallel" towns did not exist on any map Joseph could have used, several not existing at all until decades after 1830.[^2]

## Evidence
**Geographic incoherence.** Measured against the Book of Mormon's own internal directions, Holley's placements invert the text: Morianton belongs by the *eastern* seashore but Holley puts it near the "sea west"; Ramah (the Jaredite name for Cumorah) is placed in Ontario, far north of New York; Jacobugath is northward in the text but southward on the map; Angola should be north of Zarahemla, not south; Alma north of Lehi-Nephi, not far west.[^2] Many of Holley's parallels are locations named only once in the text and never given a position — so he leans on Jaredite names "because their location is less clear than Nephite ones."[^2]

**The dating problem.** Of 86 place names in the Book of Mormon, Holley claims 28 were lifted from a map spanning five US states and two Canadian provinces, hundreds of miles from upstate New York.[^3] But many of those towns post-date 1830:[^4]
- Angola, New York — established 1854 (24 years after publication)
- Tecumseh — unnamed until 1912 (82 years)
- Alma, West Virginia — settled early 1830s, not on period maps
- Conner, Canada — named 1865 (35 years)
- Mantua Village, Ohio — incorporated 1898 (68 years)
- Minoa — created 1895 (60 years)
- Kiskimentas Township, PA — named 1832 (2 years after)

Eight of the remaining "parallels" are simply **biblical** names (Midian, Noah, Jordan, Boaz, Shilom/Shiloh, Jerusalem, Ephraim) — for which the Bible, sitting on Joseph's own shelf, is a far more plausible source than obscure distant hamlets.[^5] Fife notes that even Jeremy Runnells quietly removed the Monroe→Moroni connection, "yet the rest remains," leaving only about 9% (8 of 86) of the original parallels standing — and those weak.[^6] He pointedly asks whether Joseph "liked the name of a small and obscure body of water hundreds of miles away so much that he kept the first three letters of 'Ripple Lake,' added seven letters, and called it Ripliancum."[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "Here is the problem: The whole thing is bogus. According to the geography of the Book of Mormon, virtually everything in the Vernal Holley map is in the wrong place."[^2]

> "How did Joseph Smith borrow names from small towns and bodies of water hundreds of miles away that did not exist until after 1830?"[^5]

> "With only 9% potential connections remaining (8 out of 86), why do critics still use the Vernal Holley model?"[^6]

## LDS Connection
The Holley map is a vivid case study in the [[The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit|Texas Sharpshooter tactic]]: a polished, official-looking graphic that "at first glance... seemed conclusive," but whose bullseyes were painted around random hits after the fact. For a Latter-day Saint, the lesson is that visual authority is not evidence — the moment one cross-checks the internal geography and the founding dates of the towns, the parallel evaporates. It belongs alongside the broader point that [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon|no proposed naturalistic source survives scrutiny]].
