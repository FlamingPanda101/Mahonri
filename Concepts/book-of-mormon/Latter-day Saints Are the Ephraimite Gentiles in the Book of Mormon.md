# Latter-day Saints Are the Ephraimite Gentiles in the Book of Mormon

## The Claim
[[Avraham Gileadi]] argues that the Book of Mormon consistently and deliberately classifies modern Latter-day Saints as **Gentiles** — specifically *Ephraimite* Gentiles — and never as the "house of Israel." In the book's own scheme, "the house of Israel… is defined always as the Jews, or the ten tribes, or Lehi's descendants. We are never [house of Israel]; we're always defined as the Gentiles."[^1] Because Ephraim "was assimilated into the nations," believing Gentiles of Ephraimite lineage are the gathering-agents *to* Israel rather than members of the biblical house of Israel themselves.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Two chiasms in 3 Nephi 20–21.** Gileadi cites "two classic ones in 3rd Nephi 20 and 21" (published in his book *The Last Days*) that structurally encode the relationship between the house of Israel and the Gentiles — defining who counts as which. He treats these as part of the book's sophisticated Hebrew literary architecture (see [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]]).[^3]
2. **The Kirtland Temple dedicatory prayer self-identifies the Saints as Gentiles.** "Even the Kirtland Temple dedicated prayer… says we who are identified with the Gentiles" — i.e., the early Church, by revelation, placed itself in the Gentile category, "which is correct."[^4]
3. **The early Brethren understood the scheme correctly.** Gileadi holds that "the early brethren of the restoration… understood everything… I can't find fault with any of it scripturally." He contrasts this with "a lot more precepts of men" today — "presuppositions that have no scriptural basis" — that blur the book's own Israel/Gentile definitions. His rabbinic training to "analyze everything to death and make sure that it really is scriptural" is offered as the corrective method.[^5]

## Why It Matters
The claim cuts against a popular informal LDS self-description as literal "house of Israel." Gileadi's reading preserves a sharper biblical category: the Saints are *grafted-in Gentiles* of Ephraim whose covenant role is to gather scattered Israel (the Jews, the ten tribes, and Lehi's seed), not to *be* the house of Israel. Apologetically this (a) showcases the Book of Mormon's internal consistency on a technical point a 19th-century author would be unlikely to track across 3 Nephi and the D&C, and (b) ties the book's eschatology to the [[Isaiah Is Apocalyptic and Applies Simultaneously to Isaiahs Day and the End Time|end-time Isaiah scenario]] in which Gentile and Israel roles are precisely defined.

## Best Quotes
> "We are the Ephraimite Gentiles… Ephraim was assimilated into the nations… and the house of Israel in the Book of Mormon is defined always as the Jews, or the ten tribes, or Lehi's descendants. We are never [that] — we're always defined as the Gentiles." [^1]

> "Even the Kirtland Temple dedicated prayer… says we who are identified with the Gentiles… and which is correct, and the early brethren of the restoration… understood everything." [^4]

## Counter-arguments / Caveats
- Mainstream LDS usage often speaks of members as literal Israel (especially Ephraim) gathered in the last days; Gileadi's point is taxonomic — the Book of Mormon's *narrative label* for the latter-day audience is "Gentiles," even where those Gentiles are ethnically Ephraimite.
- The chiastic and definitional readings depend on Gileadi's structural analysis; a critic would note that "house of Israel" vs. "Gentiles" usage in the Book of Mormon is debated and not uniformly as tidy as the claim implies.
