# The Uto-Aztecan B-to-P and B-to-Kwa Split Maps the Nephite-Mulekite Merger

## The Claim
[[Brian Stubbs]] reports that the Semitic data in [[Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer|Uto-Aztecan]] does not form one uniform system but **two**, differentiated by what Semitic *b* became. In the dominant set, Semitic *b* → Uto-Aztecan *p* (≈700 items; the *p* sub-corpus, including the Egyptian layer). In a separate, sizable set, Semitic *b* → Uto-Aztecan *kwa* (~400 of the remaining items): Hebrew *bala* "swallow" → *qualo*; *bama/bahamat* "back, high hill" → *quahami*; *benay* "children" → *konay*.[^1][^2] Stubbs reads the roughly **3:1 ratio (≈3/4 *b*→*p*, ≈1/4 *b*→*kwa*)** as the fingerprint of the Book of Mormon's **Nephite–Mulekite merger**: two Semitic-derived peoples who developed mutually unintelligible dialects in separate New-World environments, then fused — with the more numerous Nephite dialect dominating (~3/4) and the Mulekite minority (~1/4) surviving as the *kwa* layer.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A real two-system split exists in the data (rated: moderate, contested).** The *b* → *kwa* shift is presented as no stranger than the attested Indo-European Greek-*p* / Latin-*kw* correspondence (Greek *hippos* "horse" vs. Latin *equus*), giving it linguistic plausibility as a genuine alternate reflex rather than noise.[^4] See [[Sound Correspondences Distinguish Descent from Coincidence in Language]].
2. **It fits the Book of Mormon narrative of two diverged Semitic groups (rated: moderate, interpretive).** The text has the Mulekites and Nephites unable to understand one another despite common origin; Stubbs notes each lived "next to other languages that changed them in different ways," and the two carried different stocks (Aramaic/Egyptian in one, "a lot of Phoenician" in the other) — explaining divergence beyond a single sound shift.[^5] This dovetails with the lineage-and-dialect reasoning in [[The Northern Lineage Explains Laman and Lemuel and the Brass Plates]].
3. **The ratio is read as a demographic clue (rated: weak, speculative).** Stubbs infers from the 3:1 corpus split that "the Nephite language was the more dominant — that's what they learned," reconciling the text's "we taught them our language" with the data showing the languages *mixed* rather than one being dropped.[^6] *Steelman:* this is the most theory-laden step. The 3:1 figure is a count within a corpus whose Semitic-origin status is itself the contested premise (see [[Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer]]); a single shift conditioned by ordinary phonological environment (not two source dialects) could in principle produce two reflexes, so mapping *p* onto "Nephite" and *kwa* onto "Mulekite" is an overlay the linguistic data does not compel. The mainstream non-adoption of the underlying thesis carries through to this corollary.

## Best Quotes
> "About three-quarters of the language was the Nephite dialect, and about one-quarter was the Mulekite."[^3]

> "They were living next to other languages that changed them in different ways. So when they came together, they couldn't understand each other."[^5]
