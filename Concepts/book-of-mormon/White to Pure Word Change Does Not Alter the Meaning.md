# White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** The Book of Mormon was edited to swap **"white"** for **"pure"**
(the well-known 2 Nephi 30:6 change, "white and delightsome" → "pure and
delightsome"). The critic charges the change "doesn't seem motivated by
revelation. It seems motivated by the lifting of the priesthood ban just a few
years earlier" — i.e., a public-relations edit on race, not inspiration.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The words are near-synonyms in the relevant idiom

Saints Unscripted argues "pure and white are often used as synonyms, especially
in ancient cultures that had no concept of race the way that we define it today."
On this reading the substitution clarifies the intended sense (moral/spiritual
purity) without changing the meaning of the text.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate. It is true that "white" has a long
> moral/ceremonial-purity range (cf. "whiter than snow," Psalm 51:7) and that
> ancient Near Eastern categories were not modern racial categories. But the claim
> that the *specific* edit changed nothing is contestable (see steelman).

### 2. The word change is a "distraction" from the real issue

The host explicitly de-emphasizes the wording: "the word change isn't even the
real issue that he's raising." He redirects to the deeper objection — how a
supernaturally translated book can be edited at all — which he answers via the
revelation model. See [[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin]].[^3]

### 3. Joseph Smith made the change in 1840, and the *mix* footnote points to Interfaith marriage

In a separate treatment, [[Marvin Perkins]] adds two data points: (a) Joseph Smith
himself changed "white" to "pure" back in **1840** (it simply did not reach the
updated scriptures until 1981), citing a worry that readers would misunderstand
the racial-sounding word; and (b) the 1981 edition added a footnote on *mix* in
2 Nephi 5:23 reading "Topical Guide: Marriage, Interfaith" — so the cursed "seed
of him that mixeth" concerns **Interfaith**, not interracial, marriage, among
people who shared the same earthly father (Lehi). On Perkins' reading the *white →
pure* change is one of a cluster of edits redirecting the racial-sounding language
toward spiritual purity. See [[A Curse Is a Separation From God Not a Skin Color]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's better point is not lexical but **causal**: the timing (the edit
follows the 1978 lifting of the priesthood ban) suggests the motive was
sociopolitical optics about race, not the recovery of an original reading. If a
"revealed" text is quietly revised to track changing 20th-century racial
attitudes, that is exactly what one expects of a human document responding to its
audience — and "the words are synonyms" does not address the *why now*. The
honest LDS reply is the revelation-model reframe, not the synonymy claim. See
[[White to Pure Edit Tracks the Priesthood Ban Not Revelation]].
