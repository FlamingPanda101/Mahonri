# Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation

## The Claim

The leading positive theory for what the plates actually were: a pre-Columbian
**tumbaga** alloy — gold mixed with copper and a little silver — surface-treated
by **depletion gilding** so the exterior looks like pure gold while keeping the
weight and dimensions the witnesses report. This single alloy explains both the
golden color and the puzzling *greenish* cast one witness reported.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Tumbaga matches the weight-vs-color constraint

Pure gold of the reported volume would be too heavy and far too expensive. An
alloy of roughly **70% copper, 10% silver, 20% gold** (less than 20% gold per
[[Josh Coats]]) hits a golden appearance while fitting the ~40–60 lb weight and
6-inch stack.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (materials-science calculation tied to the witness parameters).

### 2. Depletion gilding makes tumbaga look like gold

Ancient American metalsmiths treated tumbaga with acid (depletion gilding) so the
gold migrated to cover the whole surface. [[Josh Coats]]'s paper shows side-by-side
images of pure gold, treated tumbaga, and untreated tumbaga — and the treated
sheet is visually indistinguishable from gold to a layperson.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (reproducible workshop demonstration plus real artifacts).

### 3. Exposed copper explains the greenish cast

[[Josiah Stowell]] glimpsed a corner of the plates under their covering and
described them as having a "greenish cast" like a greenish stone. If a corner or
edge exposed the underlying copper, it would oxidize green — exactly like the
copper Statue of Liberty. The panel notes this is *not* how a fabricator inventing
"gold plates" would describe them, making it an authentic detail.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (an undesigned, counter-intuitive detail that the alloy theory predicts).

### 4. Real ancient artifacts show the same pattern

The panel points to genuine ancient American artifacts that have a golden sheen
with visible green oxidation/corrosion on parts — a physical analogue to what the
witnesses describe.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic can object that tumbaga isn't "gold," so calling the plates gold is
loose. The panel preempts this: depletion-gilded tumbaga *is* reasonably called
golden by anyone not told the alloy. A harder objection is that depletion gilding
is a Mesoamerican technique — invoking it presupposes the very ancient-American
provenance under dispute. The reply is that the theory is offered to explain the
*data*, not to prove provenance — see the modest conclusion in
[[Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates]].
