# Nahom Is the Tabernacle Room of Mourning and Comfort

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler|David Butler]] argues that **Nahom** names the *holy place* (the tabernacle's middle room) understood as the **room of mourning and comfort**. The Hebrew roots behind Nahom (*naham*/*nacham*) bear the meanings "to mourn, comfort/be comforted, murmur/complain/groan, repent" — and the death and burial of Ishmael at Nahom enacts exactly that cycle (they mourn, complain, repent, and are comforted as the Lord provides food). Butler's added observation, missing from prior scholarship (Goff; the Astons), is that the *same* cluster also runs through the **broken-bow episode** — so the entire middle stretch is "the room where they experience mourning," not just the one named stop. Crucially, **comfort** here is not consolation but *empowerment*: drawing on LeGrand Baker & Stephen Ricks (*Who Shall Ascend to the Hill of the Lord?*), Butler reads [[Isaiah 40.1-55.13|Second Isaiah's "comfort ye"]] as being raised from the netherworld and made a king/priest — the movement from mourning to joy that climaxes the second-room feast. This is the holy-place panel of [[Lehis Journey Maps the Tabernacle Not Solomons Temple]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Nahom's roots = mourn/comfort/repent.** Butler: there are two possible roots (two Hebrew H's) behind "Nahom" — *naham*/*nacham* — bearing "to mourn, to comfort or be comforted, to murmur, complain, groan, and to repent." The Ishmael episode ([[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34-39]]) realizes all of them, ending as "the Lord provided us food." *Rating: the NHM/comfort wordplay is well documented (Goff; Astons); a real and oft-cited datum, also tied to the [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn|NHM inscription]].*[^1]
2. **The same cluster in the broken-bow story.** Butler's contribution: "what neither one of them says is the same roots also appear in the broken-bow story" — they mourn, complain, Lehi repents, and are comforted with food. So the *room* (holy place), not just the named stop, is the locus of mourning-to-joy. *Rating: an original synthesis; both episodes do contain murmuring/repentance/divine provision, supporting the room-wide reading.*[^1]
3. **Comfort = empowerment, not a pat on the head.** Via Baker & Ricks (quoting "Anderson"), Butler ties [[Isaiah 40.1-55.13|"comfort ye, comfort ye my people" (Isaiah 40:1)]] to the New Year festival drama and Psalms of lament: "comfort" = "the cessation of mourning through an empowerment," being "raised from the netherworld" (Psalm 71:20-21) and given the rod (scepter/kingship) and staff (priesthood) — "I am empowered by the symbols of priesthood and kingship." *Rating: leans on a devotional LDS source (Baker & Ricks) reading Second Isaiah liturgically; the lexical link "comfort → raised/empowered" is suggestive, the king/priest gloss interpretive.*[^2]
4. **Connection to the Waters of Mormon.** Mike Day links it to [[Mosiah 18|Alma's baptismal covenant]]: "willing to mourn with those that mourn... comfort those that stand in need of comfort," then "stand as a witness" — read as mourning/comforting (Nahom) followed by witnessing (having seen the Lord), the second-room pattern. *Rating: a real verbal echo (Mosiah 18:8-9 uses mourn/comfort); the room-mapping is the overlay.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The Nahom/NHM convergence is the *strongest* Book-of-Mormon geographic datum on its own terms; bolting a *tabernacle-room* allegory onto it risks over-reading a place that the text presents simply as where Ishmael died and was buried. The "comfort = enthronement/empowerment" reading depends on Baker & Ricks' liturgical reconstruction of Second Isaiah — a devotional, not mainstream-critical, framework — and on a chain of Psalm cross-references; "comfort" in Isaiah 40 most plainly means *consolation* of exiles, not coronation. Finding the mourn/comfort cluster in *both* episodes could simply reflect that *any* hardship narrative contains complaint, repentance, and relief. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim does not weaken the NHM evidence but adds a literary layer atop it; the mourn/comfort/repent roots genuinely cluster in the relevant chapters, the "comfort as empowerment" reading has Psalmic warrant (raised from the pit → given rod and staff), and the room-wide pattern coheres with the ordered tabernacle mapping and the [[The Alma Fainting Episodes Are a Ritual Death and Rebirth Ordinance|Alma death-and-rebirth]] note. Held as typology, provisional and cumulative.
