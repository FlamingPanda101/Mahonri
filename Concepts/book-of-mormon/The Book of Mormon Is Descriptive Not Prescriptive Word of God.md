# The Book of Mormon Is Descriptive Not Prescriptive Word of God

#type/claim

## The Claim
On [[Ward Radio - Descriptive vs Prescriptive Word of God|Ward Radio]], host [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Jonah Barnes]] (and Quku) argue that "the word of God" has wrongly become a synonym for "inerrantly true." The Book of Mormon — like most of the Bible — is mainly **descriptive**: the record of ancient prophets giving their spiritual opinions and channeling what they understood God to be communicating, written from their own limited, time-bound viewpoints. It is *not* **prescriptive** in the sense of God's words dictated verbatim (a category closer to the Doctrine and Covenants' revelations). On this reading, tensions and in-text corrections are expected features of a human record, not defeaters. The frame is deployed especially to defuse the "skin curse" as a moral objection (see [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]]).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The record openly self-corrects, at Christ's own direction.** In [[3 Nephi 23.9-13|3 Nephi 23]] the risen Christ rebuks the Nephites for omitting [[Samuel the Lamanite]]'s fulfilled resurrection prophecy, and [[Nephi]] then *adds* it. A text that can omit and later amend — with God directing the editorial fix rather than re-dictating — behaves like a kept account, not a fixed divine transcript. *Rating: strong on the descriptive point; this is the clearest in-text evidence the record is editorially human.*
2. **Prophets in the record disagree with each other.** Nephi's harsh "skin of blackness" framing is partly contradicted by his brother Jacob, who tells the Nephites not to "revile against them because of the darkness of their skins" and warns the Lamanites' skins may be "whiter" at judgment ([[Jacob 3.8|Jacob 3:8–9]]) — imagery that cannot be literal pigment. An in-text prophetic *debate* fits "descriptive viewpoints," not a single dictated decree. *Rating: strong; the Nephi–Jacob tension is on the page.* See [[A Curse Is a Separation From God Not a Skin Color]].
3. **A Lamanite prophet reverses the framing.** Samuel the Lamanite, a dark-skinned outsider, is the righteous voice who declares the *Nephites* the ones ripening for a curse — showing the "curse/skin" language tracks shifting prophetic perspective, not a fixed racial verdict from God. *Rating: supportive; consistent with the descriptive lens.*
4. **Scripture's own usage resists "the written word = the word of God."** The hosts note (with the related vault notes) that the phrase "word of God" in scripture often denotes God's living communication or Christ himself, and that no verse calls a particular human writing "the word of God." *Rating: a real lexical point, though contested by inerrantist readings.* See [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]] and [[No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** This looks like a convenient escape hatch: whenever the Book of Mormon says something embarrassing (a racial curse, a hard saying), the apologist reclassifies it as a mere prophet's opinion, rendering the book unfalsifiable — anything true is "the word of God," anything awkward is "descriptive." It also sits in tension with the book's own self-description ("the most correct of any book") and with how the institutional church has historically treated its statements as binding. A critic adds that "descriptive vs. prescriptive" is a modern hermeneutical move read back onto a text that presents its prophets as speaking *for* God. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the distinction is not invented to dodge — it is *demonstrated* from within the text (3 Nephi 23's commanded edit; the Nephi/Jacob disagreement), and it matches how Latter-day Saints already read scripture (prophets are inspired, not infallible). "Most correct" can mean most correct in *principles unto salvation*, not stylistic or attitudinal inerrancy. The frame constrains itself by the record's own self-correcting behavior, so it is principled rather than ad hoc.

## Best Quotes
> "'The word of God' has come to mean 'true.' But most of scripture is the descriptive record of prophets doing their best to write what they felt God was telling them — not God's dictated words."

> "Christ tells them to add Samuel's words because they left them out. The record fixes itself — that's not how a dictated, fixed text works."
