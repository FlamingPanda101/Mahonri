# Sonia Hazards Printing-Plate Theory Fails the Physical Description

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

[[Sonia Hazard]] proposed a naturalistic origin: [[Joseph Smith]] did not
fabricate plates but *found* real **stereotype printing plates** near the Hill
Cumorah — perhaps fallen from a wagon on the nearby postal road — and built a
religious story around them. The rebuttal: printing plates do not fit the
witnesses' physical description.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Printing plates are far too thick and not pliable

Stereotype/printing plates are much thicker than the "common tin" thickness the
witnesses report; the witnesses describe sheets thin and pliable like thick
paper/parchment. Printing plates are rigid.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (direct physical mismatch on a multiply-attested parameter).

### 2. No binding rings and no punch holes

The plates were bound by D-shaped rings through punch holes. Printing plates would
not naturally have such holes; producing them would require deliberate effort,
undermining the "just found them" premise.[^3]

### 3. Wrong sound and wrong feel

Stacked printing plates *clank* when flipped; the witnesses describe a metallic
*rustling*. Rappleye, who personally examined old hand-carved printing plates at
the French Institute of Oriental Archaeology in Cairo, notes they are "big,
clunky, cumbersome," not "fine little thin" objects.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (firsthand familiarity with real printing plates plus the witness "rustling" detail).

### 4. The transport scenario is admittedly improbable

Hazard herself calls this her "most straightforward though less probable"
scenario — that plates fell off a wagon on the postal road that hugged the western
base of the hill. The panel notes she does not really explain how a collection of
printing plates ended up there.[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Hazard's strongest point is methodological: a *found-object* theory avoids the
need for Joseph to have any metalworking skill, sidestepping
[[Joseph Smith Lacked the Metallurgical Skill and Time]]. And the postal-road
geography is real. The panel grants the theory is "not impossible" but argues it
trades the skill problem for an even worse physical-fit problem, and that
[[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|the tumbaga model]]
fits the data far better. The hosts frame all such naturalistic reaches as
"mental gymnastics" relative to taking the witnesses at face value.[^6]

## Best Quotes

> "The third and final scenario is the most straightforward though less probable. Smith really could have found printing plates not in a printing shop but rather on, in, or near the hill in 1823 or 1827, much as he said he did."[^5]

> "Printing plates... wouldn't have been bound by rings and they wouldn't have naturally had punch holes in them."[^3]

> "They don't make a metallic rustling sound. They make a clanking sound... they don't really work very well as an explanation for what the witnesses said they encountered."[^4]
