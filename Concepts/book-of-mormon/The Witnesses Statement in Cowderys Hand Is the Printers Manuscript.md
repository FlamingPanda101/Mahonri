# The Witnesses Statement in Cowdery's Hand Is the Printer's Manuscript

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] noted that the earliest surviving copy of the Testimony of the Three and Eight Witnesses — including the witnesses' signatures — is in the handwriting of **[[Oliver Cowdery]]**, not in each witness's own hand, implying the signatures may not represent the witnesses' genuine, individual endorsement. [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Stephen Smoot]] argues this is "a particularly weak argument" that misunderstands what the document is. The manuscript in question is the **printer's manuscript** — the copy Cowdery prepared (August 1829–January 1830) *for the typesetter* — so of course the whole thing, witness statement included, is in his hand: he was the scribe who made that copy. [^2]

1. **It's a scribal copy by design.** "The fact that it is in the handwriting of Oliver Cowdery is simply because he was the scribe who prepared this particular manuscript copy of the Book of Mormon." The portion of the *original* manuscript where the witness statement appeared is not extant, "making Cowdery's manuscript the earliest surviving copy." *Rating: strong; this is documented on the Joseph Smith Papers.* [^2]
2. **The witnesses authorized their inclusion.** "[[David Whitmer]] recalled that each of the witnesses themselves either signed their name to the original statement directly or had Oliver Cowdery sign for them." A scribe signing on authorization was ordinary practice. *Rating: moderate-strong; rests on Whitmer's recollection but is consistent with the period.* [^3]
3. **They had every chance to retract — and never did.** "If any of the witnesses felt the printed testimony misrepresented their views, they had plenty of opportunity to publicly retract... but they never did. On the contrary, there are multiple accounts where witnesses independently reaffirm the original printed testimonies." So the handwriting "is in no way [a] refutation of the statement's authenticity." *Rating: strong; non-recantation despite estrangement is well-attested — see [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]].* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A skeptic replies that "the witnesses never retracted the *printed* statement" is weaker than independent autograph signatures would be: the men endorsed a *collective* testimony drafted by others, and several later gave idiosyncratic accounts (visionary "spiritual eyes," seeing the plates "by faith") that complicate a flat, physical reading. So the absence of individual holographs, while not damning, does leave the precise content each man personally affirmed slightly under-determined.

### Counter-objection
Smoot's reply: the relevant evidence is not the *handwriting of one copy* but the witnesses' *lifelong, repeated, independent reaffirmations* — given by estranged and even hostile men with strong incentives to recant. That body of testimony far outweighs a paleographic point about which scribe penned the printer's manuscript. The "spiritual eyes" qualifications are addressed separately (the witnesses also described handling and hefting the plates physically). See [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence]] and [[No Witness Ever Recanted]].
