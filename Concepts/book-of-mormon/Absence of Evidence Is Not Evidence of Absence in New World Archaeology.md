# Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence in New World Archaeology

## The Point

**Critic's claim:** "There is no archaeological evidence of the Book of Mormon" — no cities named Zarahemla or Bountiful in the digs, no Nephite inscriptions. Atheist and religious critics alike treat this silence as decisive: a real history should have left findable remains.

**Fife's response:** The objection rests on the **fallacy of absence** ("the absence of evidence does not mean evidence of absence," attributed to Carl Sagan). New-World archaeology is barely sampled, the relevant cultures were physically destroyed, and many well-attested peoples and events have likewise left little or nothing — so the bar critics set is one ancient history routinely fails to clear.

## Evidence

- **Tiny sample size.** Mayanist George Stuart told *National Geographic* (2011) there are "almost 6,000 archaeological sites, and we've dug at forty of them" — under 1% — adding, "We hardly know anything, really, about the Maya."[^1]
- **The 2% analogy.** [[Tad Callister]] compares the critic to a man who surveys 2% of the United States and then declares there are no large lakes, no mountains above 10,000 feet, no redwoods, no volcanoes — categorically denying what he simply never saw. Two percent of the continental U.S. is roughly the size of Oklahoma.[^2]
- **The cultures were erased.** European disease reduced central Mexico's indigenous population by an estimated 90%+; Spaniards razed Aztec and Maya cities, temples, books, and names, and renamed surviving cities (Coatzacoalcos became Espiritu Santo, etc.). One researcher notes it was "mainly their large-scale destruction that has led to them being erased from the world's memory."[^3]
- **Hostile preservation conditions.** Jungle foliage, hot/humid climate, acidic soil, and recurring earthquakes, volcanoes, and storms make Mesoamerican sites hard to find and artifacts other than stone and metal unlikely to survive — unlike the arid, better-preserved Middle East.[^4]
- **Famous parallels with little or no evidence.** The Huns depended on thousands of horses, yet no horse remains survive in that region; there is essentially no archaeology of the Battle of Hastings (1066); elephants in Syria, the capital city of Thinis, the trading city of Punt, and even the Israelite enslavement and Exodus all leave scant to no physical trace.[^5]

These limits are why historicity should be judged by [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity|cumulative convergence]], and why the inverse demand — "specify what you expect to find" — exposes the objection (see [[No Archaeological Evidence Requires Specifying What to Look For]]).

## Best Quotes

> "How foolhardy to unequivocally claim there were no horses, cattle, steel, and Nephite names in Book of Mormon lands and times when at least 98% of archaeological remains in ancient America have not been unearthed." — Tad Callister [^2]

> "It's mainly their large-scale destruction that has led to them being erased from the world's memory." [^3]

## LDS Connection

The Book of Mormon itself anticipates a destroyed, sealed-up record surfacing among a people whose civilization had collapsed (Mormon 8:1–9). Early Saints expected ruins to be found later (cf. David Whitmer's recollection that the Lord said "people should discover the ruins of the lost cities"). The methodological point — that silence is not disproof — therefore guards against treating a young, under-excavated field as a verdict against revealed history.
