# The Acts of Matthew Mirrors Third Nephi - Christ Appearing in a Distant Land

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] highlights an apocryphal text — the **Acts of Matthew (in the City of the Priests)** — that contains a striking structural parallel to [[3 Nephi]]. In it, the apostle Matthew returns to the other apostles and reports that, while he was in a great city in **Parthia** (Asia, beyond the Holy Land), **Jesus Christ appeared in the heavens and descended** to that city, "opened up the gospel to all these people," and helped establish his church there. [^1] Crucially this is set *after* the Ascension — months or years later. So an apocryphal book independently records **Christ descending to establish his church in a distant land**, exactly the event-type of 3 Nephi, witnessed by an apostle. [^2] Barnes connects this to the roughly **eight-month gap** between Christ's Ascension and his appearance to the Nephites, speculating that Christ may have visited multiple peoples in that window ("did he go to Australia, Ethiopia, China?"), with the Acts of Matthew one surviving trace. The texts survive in Greek, Syriac, and Ethiopic versions. [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Acts of Matthew is a real apocryphal text with the descent-motif.** Apocryphal apostolic Acts circulated widely in multiple language traditions and do contain post-Ascension christophany and church-founding episodes, so Barnes is pointing to genuine literature, not inventing it. *Rating: the text exists; the precise wording Barnes paraphrases is from late legendary material.* [^1]
2. **The structural parallel to 3 Nephi is real.** [[3 Nephi 11.10-11|3 Nephi 11]] has the resurrected Christ descend from heaven to a gathered multitude in a distant land and inaugurate his church/ordinances — so an *independent* "Christ descends to a far city and founds his church, witnessed and recorded" tradition is a legitimate multiple-attestation parallel for the *event-type* (cf. [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]]). *Rating: a genuine and notable resemblance.* [^2]
3. **The eight-month window is canonical real estate.** The interval between the Ascension and the Nephite appearance is textually open, so the "where else did Christ go?" question is a fair one within the Book of Mormon's own frame. *Rating: legitimate as a question, speculative as an answer.* [^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The Acts of Matthew is **late (medieval manuscripts), legendary, and exactly the kind of apocryphal Acts critics — and most Christians — treat as ahistorical** (the same corpus the vault flags in [[Apostolic Martyrdom Traditions Are Late Apocryphal Legend]]). A descending-savior, gathered-crowd, church-founding scene is a *common hagiographic template*, so its resemblance to 3 Nephi may reflect shared genre conventions rather than independent memory of a real event — and a critic will note one cannot use a text one elsewhere dismisses only where it flatters the Book of Mormon. A measured reply: the claim is *typological multiple attestation*, not a historicity argument — it shows that "the risen Christ appearing to peoples outside Judea to establish his church" was an idea *present in the early Christian imagination*, which makes 3 Nephi less of an outlier than critics assume; Barnes offers the visit-itinerary speculation explicitly as "your puny minds blown" fun, not as evidence.

## Best Quotes
> "There's an apocryphal acts of Matthew book that shows Jesus Christ descending to establish his church in a distant land, just like in the Book of Mormon — and Matthew was a witness to it." [^2]

> "Was he, just like pulling a Russell M. Nelson, cruising around to all these places? Because Matthew found one in Parthia, and it's in the apocryphal books." [^3]

## LDS Connection
The note supplies an apocryphal corroboration for the *concept* most distinctive to 3 Nephi — the resurrected Christ ministering to peoples beyond the Old World — feeding the vault's recurring thesis that ideas critics treat as Joseph Smith's inventions turn up in ancient texts discovered later (cf. the apocryphal corroborations of the Book of Abraham and the Garment of Adam). It is best deployed as a "less of an outlier than you think" argument, not as proof of an event.
