# Additional NHM Inscriptions Date the Nahom Region to Lehi's Day

## The Claim

Beyond the three famous **NHM votive altars** from the Bar'an temple near Marib, there is a broader corpus of South Arabian inscriptions naming the **NHM tribe / region** — several of which date to Lehi's period (~600 BC) or the early first millennium BC. [[Neal Rappleye]] argues these lesser-known attestations, mostly unknown to Latter-day Saints, document the long-term stability of the Nehem tribe and make the Book of Mormon's Nahom ([[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34]]) better-evidenced than ever. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The familiar altars are only the baseline — rate: strong (epigraphic)

The three Bar'an altars name a dedicant from the tribe of NHM and were dated by the German excavation team's epigrapher to the 7th–8th century BC. [[Warren Aston]]'s later work noted further NHM-bearing inscriptions but treated them only briefly, so "a lot of people aren't aware that there are some of these additional inscriptions." [^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the altars themselves are uncontroversial published finds.

### 2. A catalog of additional NHM attestations — rate: moderate-to-strong

Rappleye walks through several: a 3rd-century-AD "image-formula" funerary stela naming a Nehemite (Hermann von Wissmann linked it to the Highlands via the deity ʿAthtar Sharqan and vine-motif decoration); an administrative palm-stick text from Nashan listing NHM; two dedicatory texts from Sirwah (one with a dual NHM/Maʿin affiliation, one naming four Nehemites); a **7th-century-BC** dedicatory text naming a father and son as *kabir* (chief) "of the NHM"; and three **7th-century-BC** inscriptions from Haram naming a *kabir* of an NHM group (debated: Christian Robin read NHM as "stonemason," G. Lancaster Harding as a tribe/people). [^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (several solid attestations; a few are debated as to whether NHM there is the tribe or a common-noun "stonemason").

### 3. The 7th-century attestations reach Lehi's time — rate: strong

Among them, the *kabir* dedicatory text and the Haram inscriptions date to the 7th century BC, "specifically back to Lehi's time" — corroborating that the name goes back to ~600 BC, which is exactly what critics once said could not be shown. This lets Rappleye flesh out a pre-Islamic chronology documenting the tribe "throughout antiquity." [^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — multiple independent 7th-century attestations of the name in the right region.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can press that (a) most of the ~thousands of South Arabian inscriptions do *not* mention NHM (Rappleye concedes this), (b) NHM is a common Semitic triliteral root that can mean "stonemason," so some "NHM" attestations may be the common noun rather than the tribe (Robin's reading of the Haram texts), and (c) the densest attestations cluster centuries after Lehi. Rappleye grants the debated cases but argues the *cumulative* picture — multiple 7th-century-BC tribal attestations in the correct region, plus the later continuity — is what carries weight, and that the burden is on the critic to find a competing NHM region elsewhere in Arabia (see [[Nahom Names a Tribal Region Not the Altar Findspot]]). The value remains corroborative convergence, not proof (see [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]).
