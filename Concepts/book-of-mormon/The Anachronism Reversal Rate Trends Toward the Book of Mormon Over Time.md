# The Anachronism Reversal Rate Trends Toward the Book of Mormon Over Time

## The Claim

Plotted across time, the share of alleged Book of Mormon anachronisms that have been *reversed* by archaeology climbs steeply: only about **7%** were reversed between 1830 and 1844; roughly **22%** by 1965; and about **77%** by 2024 — leaving only ~9% the critics can still press. Because the reversals come from non-LDS archaeology, the trend is the argument: a fabrication's problem-list should grow under scrutiny, while an authentic record's shrinks.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The reversal curve (Matt Roper / Scripture Central)
By His Fruits presents [[Matt Roper]]'s inventory as a time series: 1830–1844, 7% reversed (with two "partial"); by 1965, 22%; by 2024, 77%, with a remaining ~9% still attackable and some items "partially" reversing now.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — it rests on Roper's systematic, citation-heavy survey of ~1,000 publications; the exact percentages depend on his classification standards (see the vault's fuller treatment in [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]] and [[Shrinking Anachronism List as Evidence of Authenticity]]).

### 2. The reversals are externally driven, not internal apologetics
The presenter stresses the items were reversed "not by Latter-day Saints. These are by archaeological discoveries," and promises the footnotes will document each — pre-empting the "apologists just argued it away" reply.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — strong *if* the underlying eliminations are genuinely external (as Roper claims); a reader cannot audit all of them from the video.

### 3. The trend direction is the inference
"All of these inacronisms that have been used to attack are now helping us prove" the book; the same items that fueled the objection become evidence as they reverse. This is a Bayesian point: a *shrinking* error list is more probable under authenticity than under fabrication.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the logic is sound; its strength is only as good as the reversal data feeding it.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The numbers invite three challenges. (a) **Selection and definition:** "reversed" can be generous — sometimes meaning a thing merely existed *somewhere* in antiquity, not in the required Mesoamerican place and time; tightening the standard would lower the percentage. (b) **The residue is the hard core:** the ~9% still pressed (and some of the "trending" items) are precisely the load-bearing ones — large horse populations, chariots, a steel industry, a confirmed Nephite city — so a high *count* of reversals can coexist with the *decisive* objections surviving. (c) **Post-hoc reclassification:** with no fixed external referee, a defender can quietly drop or redefine items. The most defensible version of the claim is comparative and directional — *the list is shrinking and the trend runs opposite to how fraud behaves* (see [[The Direction of the Evidence Runs Opposite to How Fraud Behaves]]) — rather than a precise statistical proof; and even the presenter frames the residue honestly via the [[Enough Evidence to Prevent Scoffing Not Enough to Remove Faith|Maxwell aphorism]] that evidence will never be a slam dunk.
