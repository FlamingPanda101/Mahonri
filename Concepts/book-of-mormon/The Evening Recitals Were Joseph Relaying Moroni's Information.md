# The Evening Recitals Were Joseph Relaying Moroni's Information

#type/claim

## The Claim
The "amusing recitals" passage in [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s history — the storyteller theory's flagship evidence — does not show the young Joseph *inventing* a fictional world; read in its own narrative context it shows him *relaying* a history that [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] had already disclosed to him.[^1] Keystone's specific move is chronological: the controversial paragraph comes only "a few sentences later" than Lucy's account of the September 1823 Moroni visits, so the recitals are dated *after* the angelic disclosure, not before it. "Lucy was not admitting that Joseph was an imaginative storyteller. She was saying that Joseph was relaying information that had been already given or shown to him by Moroni."[^2] This is a distinct argument from the parallel rebuttal that occasional family story-time could never scale to memorizing a 270,000-word text (see [[Lucy Mack Smiths Evening Recitals Do Not Establish Pre-Composition]]); here the point is that the source itself, in context, supplies an innocent explanation the critic omits.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Moroni's content included the history, not just the plates.** On the night of September 21, 1823 Joseph reported being visited three times, and per his own history "was also informed concerning the aboriginal inhabitants of this country, and shown who they were and from whence they came. A brief sketch of their origin, progress, civilization, laws, government, of their righteousness and iniquity was made known unto me."[^3] So a source of the recital details *predates the recitals* — the visions, not Joseph's imagination. *(Strong within the LDS frame — it removes the need to posit pre-composition; weaker against a critic who rejects the visions as themselves invented.)*
2. **The family's contemporaneous framing was relay, not fiction.** Joseph told his father, then that evening his "whole family"; [[William Smith]] recalled the angel "had also given him a short account of the inhabitants who formerly resided upon this continent... The whole family were melted to tears and believed all he said," and they reconvened the next evening to hear more.[^4] Lucy then says they "continued to get the children together every evening for the purpose of listening while he gave us a relation of the same" — i.e., a *relation* of what Moroni had shown, which she "considered to be part of the miraculous story of the coming forth of the Book of Mormon," not "evidence of fraud."[^5] *(Strong — the eyewitnesses nearest the events did not read them as Joseph making things up.)*
3. **The critic relies on the reader's imagination to fill the gap.** "Many critics will rely on your imagination" to answer how Joseph could know such things before translation, "instead of just giving you the context."[^6] The argument inverts the burden: the storyteller reading requires *suppressing* the surrounding paragraphs, while the relay reading simply reads Lucy continuously. *(Moderate — a framing / source-handling point rather than independent evidence.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic has a real reply. (a) The whole defense is internal to Lucy's narrative — if one already disbelieves the Moroni visits, then "Joseph relayed what the angel showed him" just relocates the fiction from the recitals to the visions; nothing is gained evidentially. (b) Lucy wrote decades later as a devoted mother building a faith narrative, so her *framing* ("relation of the same") is exactly what a believing reconstruction would say regardless of what actually happened; the contemporaneous detail in the recitals ("dress... manner of traveling... the animals which they rode... mode of warfare") still reads like a developing imaginative world. (c) Some manuscript versions place the recitals language without an airtight timestamp, so "a few sentences later" does not by itself fix strict chronology. (d) Even on the believing reading, the recitals show Joseph capable of vivid, extended, detailed oral narration about ancient American peoples — which is precisely the *capacity* the storyteller theory needs, whatever its ultimate source. The defender's honest claim is therefore the narrower one: in context Lucy is not *confessing* fabrication, and the critic's strongest text does not say what the critic needs it to say.

## Corroboration: Smoot's Open Letter to Trent Horn
[[Stephen Smoot]] makes the identical argument against [[Trent Horn]], who cited the "amusing recitals" to portray Joseph as "a clever storyteller who possessed the requisite imagination to produce the Book of Mormon." Smoot: Lucy "explicitly couches these amusing recitals in the context of Joseph receiving instruction from the angel about the coming forth of the record... never does Lucy hint that her son was an imaginative storyteller spinning yarn within the family. Rather, the family interpreted Joseph's accounts as a sign that he was truly inspired of God." He stresses that until then Joseph "had never read the Bible through" and "was less inclined to the study of books than any of the Smith children" — so "you cannot enlist Lucy Mack Smith or her memoir as evidence" that Joseph had a wild imagination, even if you wish to believe he did.[^7]
