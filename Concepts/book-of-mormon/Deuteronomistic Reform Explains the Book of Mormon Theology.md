# Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics call the theology of [[1 Nephi 1.8-19|1 Nephi 1]] anachronistic — claiming Jews in 600 BC did not believe in seeing God enthroned, a divine council, a Messiah in the flesh, or worship outside the Temple. Arnold argues this is "actually backwards": those were the very ideas being eliminated at that moment [^27].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The reform happened, on a tight timeline.** "In 621 BC, King [[Josiah]] launched what scholars call the Deuteronomistic Reform" — "exactly 21 years before Lehi left Jerusalem." His scribes "edited scriptures, they destroyed altars, and they killed priests who practiced the old religion," specifically eliminating "seeing God face-to-face, the divine council, Messianic theology involving God in the flesh, and worship anywhere outside the Jerusalem Temple." Arnold grounds this in [[2 Kings 22-23|2 Kings]] and the work of [[Frank Moore Cross]], [[Margaret Barker]], and [[Michael Heiser]]. *Rating: the reform and its scholarly framing are mainstream; "killed priests" leans on the 2 Kings 23 account.* [^26]
2. **Lehi maps onto pre-reform religion.** "Open First Nephi chapter 1. Lehi sees God on his throne surrounded by a heavenly council. He prophesies about a Messiah coming in the flesh. Then he builds an altar in the wilderness... Lehi was practicing pre-reform theology at the moment that it became illegal." [^27]
3. **It even explains the family conflict.** "[[Laman]] and Lemuel's opposition to their father also makes sense in this context. They had been raised under the reform and they knew what he was doing was dangerous." *Rating: an elegant internal-coherence point, though it is interpretive rather than evidential.* [^27]
4. **The anachronistic-knowledge twist.** A critic may say "the Josiah reforms are right there in 2 Kings. Joseph could have just read that." Arnold concedes the narrative exists but says Joseph would have needed "the full scope of the Deuteronomistic editorial program" — that a specific scribal school "systematically and specifically suppressed divine council theology, pre-existent Messiah language, and open theophany across multiple biblical books over decades" — and that this is "a 20th century scholarly framework developed by [[Martin Noth]], [[Frank Moore Cross]], and others." Reading 2 Kings tells you Josiah burned altars, not that 1 Nephi maps onto the outlawed theology [^28][^29][^30].
5. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) The "older suppressed religion" reconstruction (esp. Barker's "temple theology") is *itself contested* among biblical scholars; it is a minority interpretive program, not consensus fact. (b) Revival-era American Christianity already affirmed a pre-existent, incarnate Christ and visionary theophany, so Joseph had ample non-academic sources for "God in the flesh" and throne visions. (c) The 621 BC dating and "21 years before Lehi" precision depends on accepting the Book of Mormon's own chronology. (d) Heiser and Barker are doing constructive theology/scholarship, not delivering a settled verdict. Arnold presents the reconstruction as established history rather than a debated school of thought [^29].

## Best Quotes
> "In 621 BC, King Josiah launched what scholars call the Deuteronomistic Reform. His scribes purged the religious landscape of Israel. They edited scriptures, they destroyed altars, and they killed priests who practiced the old religion." [^26]

> "Lehi was practicing pre-reform theology at the moment that it became illegal. Laman and Lemuel's opposition to their father also makes sense in this context." [^27]

> "He would have needed to understand the full scope of the Deuteronomistic editorial program... But that is a 20th century scholarly framework developed by Martin Noth, Frank Moore Cross, and others. It was not available in 1830." [^29]
