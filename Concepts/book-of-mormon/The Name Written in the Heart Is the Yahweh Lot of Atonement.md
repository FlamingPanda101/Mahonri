# The Name Written in the Heart Is the Yahweh Lot of Atonement

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler|David Butler]] argues that [[Mosiah 5.8|King Benjamin's]] charge to "retain the name written always in your hearts" (Mosiah 5:11-13) is the **recognition criterion** that separates the [[The Right and Left Hand of God Are the Day of Atonement Goats|two Day-of-Atonement lots]]. The sheep on the right "know the name by which they're called" and "hear and know the voice of the master"; the goats on the left do not — "how knoweth a man the master whom he hath not served, and who is a stranger unto him?" So bearing the name in the heart is having the *la-Yahweh* lot — being known by, and knowing, the Master. In [[Matthew 25.31-46|Matthew 25]], the further criterion is *mercy to "the least of these"* (feeding, clothing, visiting), and the condemned "don't even know what they did wrong." This ties the right/left judgment to the channel's [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works|"I never knew you"]] recognition theme.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Knowing the name and voice is the divider.** [[Mosiah 5.8|Mosiah 5:12]]: "retain the name written always in your hearts, that ye are not found on the left hand of God, but that ye hear and know the voice by which ye are called, and also the name by which he shall call you." Butler: the sheep "know the name by which they're called... the voice of the master; the goats on the left do not." See [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]]. *Rating: the name/voice/recognition language is explicit in Mosiah 5; the goat-lot framing comes from the paired note.*[^1]
2. **The "stranger" who is far from the heart.** [[Mosiah 5.8|Mosiah 5:13]]: "for how knoweth a man the master whom he hath not served, and who is a stranger unto him, and is far from the thoughts and intents of his heart?" Read as the goat who never knew the Master. Coheres with [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works]] (the bridegroom "far from the thoughts and intents of [the] heart"). *Rating: a direct verbal echo; the cross-text link is sound.*[^1]
3. **Sheep vs goats by temperament — and by mercy.** Butler notes sheep "follow a master who calls them" and develop a relationship, while goats "are all over the place... unruly"; but the *decisive* Matthew 25 criterion is mercy "unto one of the least of these," which the Lord counts as done "unto me." *Rating: the temperament point is illustrative folk-observation; the mercy criterion is the text's own (Matt 25:40,45).*[^2][^3]
4. **The condemned don't know their fault.** In [[Matthew 25.31-46|Matt 25:44]] the goats ask "when saw we thee...?" — "they don't even know what they did wrong." Butler leaves open whether the divider is "just good works" (the clip cuts off mid-question), preserving the relationship-vs-works tension (cf. [[Jesus Taught Salvation Involves Doing Not Faith Alone]]). *Rating: faithfully reports the text's open question rather than over-claiming.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** Mosiah 5's "name written in the heart" is plainly Benjamin's covenant-renewal language (taking the name of Christ at baptism, internalizing it) and stands on its own without Yom-Kippur lots; importing the *la-Yahweh*/*la-Azazel* goats to gloss "right/left hand" is the contested move of the paired note. Matthew 25's criterion is explicitly *works of mercy*, which sits awkwardly with a "knowing, not works" emphasis — the text judges by what was *done* to the least of these. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the two are not rivals — the name in the heart *issues in* mercy to the least, so "knowing the Master" and "doing his will" are one disposition (the merciful are precisely those who bear his name/voice), and the condemned's ignorance of their fault shows the lack was relational/dispositional, not a missing checklist item; the framing coheres with the vault's reading that doing flows from knowing ([[Jesus Taught Salvation Involves Doing Not Faith Alone]]).
