# Anthony Metcalf "Entrancement" Quote Is a Late Doubled Embellishment

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The [[Jeremy Runnells|CES Letter]] cites [[Anthony Metcalf]] to show that [[Martin Harris]] saw the plates only in a "visionary or entranced state," presenting two excerpts as if they were two independent quotations corroborating each other.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "two quotes" are one paragraph
The two excerpts "are literally from the same paragraph of the same 1880 book," so they cannot count as independent attestation — the doubling manufactures an illusion of corroboration from a single source.[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a checkable bibliographic fact about the cited source).

### 2. The interview is misdated to a dead man
Metcalf claimed to have interviewed Harris "in the winter of 1875–76, which interestingly would have been an interview with a corpse"; the actual interview "probably happened in 1873 when Martin was 90 years old." A reminiscence that misdates its own occasion — and was published in 1880 about an aged interviewee — invites caution.[^2] See [[The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong (the dating error is concrete; "corpse" is rhetorical but the point stands).

### 3. "Entrancement" is uncorroborated and idiosyncratic
"No other source that I've seen uses the term entrancement; thus I think this is probably an embellishment on Metcalf's part." The word is doing heavy lifting in the CES Letter but is unique to this one hostile reminiscence.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (argument from the silence of every other witness source; reasonable but not decisive).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The skeptic can reply that Metcalf was a real contemporary who actually met Harris, that an aged witness's own framing of his experience as a trance-like vision is evidence worth taking seriously, and that dismissing every unflattering term as "embellishment" risks a one-sided filter — accepting Harris's faith-promoting statements at face value while explaining away the visionary ones. The strongest neutral conclusion is narrow: the *doubling* is illegitimate and the dating is wrong, which lowers the quote's evidentiary weight without proving Harris never used trance-like language.
