# Second-Temple Judaism Was Assembled in the Hasmonean Period

## The Point

The synagogue is not an isolated anomaly. [[Yonatan Adler]] argues that the *entire* apparatus the Pharisees treated as eternal Mosaic tradition — dietary law (kashrut), ritual purity (mikva'ot), tefillin and mezuzot, Sabbath prohibitions, and the festival system — leaves no significant trace in the material record before roughly 200 BC. What looks like "ancient continuous Jewish practice" was largely a 2nd-century-BC national-identity project under Hasmonean (Maccabean) sponsorship, driven by the pressure of Greek cultural dominance.

The crucial distinction (and the reason this is not an attack on the Bible): Adler is *not* saying the Torah is a fabrication. "The laws are old and the texts are old." He is tracing "the gap between the law being written down and that law being widely practiced in daily life by ordinary people" — two different things that "can actually span centuries." This matters because critics measuring the Book of Mormon "against ancient Judaism" are usually measuring it against this late, assembled system.

## Evidence

- **Kashrut.** The pork prohibition is plain in Leviticus, "but when the archaeologists examine animal bones from Israelite sites in the pre-exilic period, pig bones appear with regularity." Widespread pork avoidance as practice "doesn't actually solidify until the Hellenistic period" [^1]. *Rating: Strong — direct faunal/material evidence.*
- **Tefillin and mikva'ot.** Tefillin "don't show up in any confirmed archaeological context before the Dead Sea Scrolls era, roughly 150 BC onward," and the stepped immersion pools are "essentially absent from the archaeological record before the 1st century BC, and then they appear in abundance afterward" [^2]. *Rating: Strong — abrupt appearance pattern.*
- **A standardization project.** "The Hasmonean dynasty actively promoted Torah observance as a marker of Jewish identity," so "what we tend to think of as ancient continuous Jewish practice was really just a 2nd century BC political and religious project" [^3]. *Rating: Moderate — synthesis/interpretation built on the converging material data.*
- **The prophets corroborate the gap.** Amos, Isaiah, and Jeremiah condemn violations "so widespread and persistent that the scholars now recognize them as the normal condition of the society" — "the prophets corroborated what the archaeology now shows" [^4]. *Rating: Moderate — internal-biblical support for the practice gap.*

## Best Quotes

> "What we tend to think of as ancient continuous Jewish practice was really just a 2nd century BC political and religious project." [^3]

> "He's not arguing that the Torah is a fabrication. The laws are old and the texts are old. What he's actually tracing is just the gap between the law being written down and that law being widely practiced in daily life by ordinary people." [^5]

## LDS Connection

If the system Jesus navigated was largely a Hellenistic-era construction, then the "doesn't match Judaism" objection collapses against a record that left before that construction existed. It also reframes Mark 7, where the Pharisees indict Jesus over "the tradition of the elders" — not the law of Moses — and he answers with Isaiah, "teaching for doctrines the commandments of men" [^6]. The same logic underwrites [[The Book of Mormon Is Measured Against the Wrong Period]] and complements [[Margaret Barker]]'s textual recovery of the suppressed pre-exilic religion.
