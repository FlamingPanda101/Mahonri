# The Book of Mormon Rescued Us From Two Apostasies

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] frames the payoff of the "Great Forsaken" thesis: the Book of Mormon is far more valuable than usually realized because it rescued the Saints **not from one apostasy but from two** — the post-apostolic Great Apostasy *and* the pre-exilic "Great Forsaken" of the scribes. Because [[Lehi]]'s brass plates were taken out of Jerusalem *before* the Deuteronomistic compilation hardened, the Book of Mormon preserves pre-reform, First-Temple theology that the surviving Old Testament edited away.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A deliberate "time capsule" past two corruptions (rate: moderate, framing).** "The Book of Mormon rescued us not from one apostasy but from two ... it was the plan all along, it was the time capsule." Knowing this "changes your perspective on the Book of Mormon" and on the cost paid for the brass plates ("worth a hundred Labans' heads").[^2]

2. **The brass plates predate the Deuteronomistic edition (rate: moderate, chronology).** Lehi left Jerusalem c. 600 BC carrying records that bypassed the post-exilic scribal compilation; the brass plates notably **lack** the Deuteronomistic History books and contain prophets (Zenos, Zenock, Neum) absent from the received canon. See [[The Deuteronomistic History Books Are Absent From the Brass Plates]] and [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]].[^3]

3. **It preserves suppressed First-Temple content (rate: moderate, thematic).** The atoning/suffering Messiah, the olive-tree allegory of the Gentiles' grafting ([[Jacob 5]], Zenos), and a non-ethnocentric covenant all appear in the Book of Mormon — precisely the material Barnes says the scribes excised. See [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]].[^4]

4. **Two distinct apostasies, two distinct scatterings (rate: framing).** Barnes distinguishes the pre-Christian forsaking from the scattering of Israel that loomed over Nephi: "for Nephi it was all about the scattering of Israel and the fact that they had taken these records to preserve them." The Book of Mormon answers both.[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
The "rescue from two apostasies" is a faith-internal payoff, not independent evidence. (a) It **presupposes** the Book of Mormon is an authentic ancient record; a critic reads the same data as a 19th-century author retrojecting his theology onto a fictional pre-exilic source, so the "preservation" is question-begging as an argument *for* the book. (b) The claim that the brass plates preserve *pre-reform* theology depends on the contested Margaret-Barker-style First-Temple reconstruction, which mainstream scholarship does not endorse wholesale. (c) That the Book of Mormon contains "suppressed" content matching what the thesis says was removed is exactly what one would expect whether the book is ancient *or* a sophisticated modern composition built on 19th-century higher-criticism intuitions — so it cannot, by itself, decide between the two. (d) The "time capsule ... plan all along" language is theological narration, not historical demonstration. The defensible core: *if* the Book of Mormon is authentic, its independence from the Deuteronomistic chain is a real and interesting feature; whether it is authentic must be argued on other (e.g. onomastic, Nahom, witness) grounds.
