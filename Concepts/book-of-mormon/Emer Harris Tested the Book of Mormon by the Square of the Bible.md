# Emer Harris Tested the Book of Mormon by the Square of the Bible

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] introduces a primary source he says he "stumbled on" at the Church archives — the minutes of [[Emer Harris]]'s remarks at the April 6, 1856 Provo stake conference — to show two things: (1) Emer (Oaks's direct ancestor, elder brother of [[Martin Harris]]) converted by a rigorous *comparative* method, "laying the Bible as a square" on the Book of Mormon; and (2) because Emer learned details from Martin (the chief scribe of the lost pages), his account preserves **Mulekite material that survives only from the lost 116 pages**, corroborating the reality of that missing text.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A craftsman's test of scripture.** Emer: "I was at that time a mechanic and tried things by the square. The Bible was my square, and I laid my square upon it, and if it fit, I concluded it was true." He made "a concordance to the Book of Mormon and compared it with the Bible and the Apocrypha," word by word — a deliberate, evidential conversion, which may explain the lag before his baptism.[^1]
2. **He found the Book of Mormon fuller, not contradictory.** "When I compare the saying of the Bible in the Book of Mormon, I found them to agree, for the thing spoken of the Bible I found more fully spoken of in the Book of Mormon." His final trigger was seeing "the signs following this people."[^2]
3. **He transmits lost-pages Mulekite detail.** Telling "the history of those that were lost" when "the king from Jerusalem had his eyes put out" (Zedekiah), Emer says Mulek and others of the royal family hid, and "on coming out of their hiding place, they found four females of the royal family who also had hid… there being four males and four females," who settled in "the south part" of the Americas. Our small-plates text gives only a thumbnail of the Mulekites; Bradley notes these specifics "aren't in our Book of Mormon text," and reads them as preserved from the lost pages via Martin — with a deliberate "echo of the flood story" (four couples, eight souls).[^3]
4. **It supports the longer-manuscript thesis.** Emer also says Martin "scribed for near 200 pages before the manuscript was lost," which Bradley cites (as in *The Lost 116 Pages*) as evidence the lost manuscript may have exceeded 116 pages.[^4]
5. **Steelman of the skeptic.** A critic notes this is a single 1856 recollection of events nearly three decades earlier, that Emer could have absorbed later Book-of-Mormon-culture embellishments, and that a flood-style "four couples" detail might be Emer's own harmonizing flourish rather than authentic lost-pages content. Bradley's reply is provenance and oddity: Emer's proximity to Martin gives him plausible access, and the details are *non-canonical specifics* a fabricator imitating the printed text would not invent (they go beyond, and slightly against the shape of, the surviving account) — which is exactly the signature of independent transmission rather than copying. The note treats the source as suggestive corroboration, weighted by its archival primary-document status.
