# Mesoamerican Sources Preserve a Book of Mormon Migration - Gurr

## The Point
**Claim ([[Robert Gurr]]):** Pre-Columbian and early-contact Mesoamerican sources — Diego Durán's *Gods and Rites*, the histories of Ixtlilxóchitl, José de Acosta, Bernardino de Sahagún, and codices (Boturini, Aubin, Telleriano-Remensis, Tovar) — preserve a migration narrative that, Gurr argues, converges remarkably with the Book of Mormon: exiles commanded by God to leave a homeland and cross the sea eastward to a promised land; a directional "idol" that told them when to march (read as the Liahona); fair-skinned ("Aztlan = whiteness") bearded ancestors; *seven* leaders/tribes (paralleling the Nephites, Lamanites, Jacobites, Josephites, Zoramites, etc.); a virgin-born "god of air and water," Quetzalcóatl, who taught peace, opposed sacrifice, promised to return, and was identified with Venus/the morning star (cf. Christ, "the bright morning star"); and a civilization-ending last stand on a hill (Chapultepec, "Grasshopper Hill") in a land of "many waters" — which lets Gurr relocate Cumorah to the Valley of Mexico (Lake Texcoco), where Moroni then took 36 years to walk to New York.[^1]

**Rebuttal ([[Bill Reel]]):** Reel never disputes the chronicles line-by-line but answers with a parallelomania reductio: "if shared themes and parallels are enough to establish truth, then the Epic of Gilgamesh, which predates the Bible by centuries, would have to be accepted as true" — it has the flood, divine destruction, a warned man, an ark, birds sent out, a post-flood sacrifice, even divine regret. "Overlap doesn't establish historicity." The real test is "looking at the truth claims of Mormonism and seeing if they hold up," where the evidence "falls short."[^2] He also flags the absurdity of a lone human trek from central Mexico to Palmyra as "beyond the scope of what human beings are recorded as having done."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **The convergence case is Gurr's most distinctive contribution (rated: weak-to-moderate, and idiosyncratic).** The chroniclers are real primary texts, and the breadth of motifs (sea migration, directional device, seven groups, a returning culture-hero, a hilltop last stand) is rhetorically striking. But the vault's own analysis warns sharply against exactly this method: a heap of thematic parallels without disciplined textual and chronological control "carries no burden" ([[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]]), and most of these chronicles are *post-conquest* records gathered by Spanish friars who were themselves predisposed to find Hebrews and Christianity, raising contamination concerns Gurr waves off.
2. **The Quetzalcóatl identification is the weakest link (rated: weak; the vault rebuts it directly).** Gurr equates Quetzalcóatl with Christ via the Venus/morning-star motif. The vault's standing treatment, [[The Quetzalcoatl Parallel Names Moses Not Christ]], notes the Book of Mormon never names Quetzalcóatl and that the *View of the Hebrews* tradition identified him as **Moses**, not Christ — so the parallel does double duty for critics. Gurr himself concedes "no single pre-Columbian source says this was Jesus Christ."[^1]
3. **Reel's Gilgamesh parity argument is decisive against the *method* (rated: strong, framing).** It shows that detailed narrative overlap cannot, by itself, establish historicity — the same logic the vault applies to *View of the Hebrews* parallels (parallelomania). *Steelman of Gurr:* the legitimate residue is that LiDAR and ongoing Mesoamerican archaeology *do* reveal a dense, advanced civilization compatible with a Book of Mormon setting ([[LiDAR Reveals a Dense Advanced Maya Civilization Matching the Book of Mormon]]), and that *convergence* (not a single parallel) is the right historicity standard ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]) — but convergence still requires controlling for late, contaminated, friar-mediated sources, which Gurr's presentation does not.

## Best Quotes
> "No single pre-Columbian source says this was Jesus Christ. But the convergence of motifs and narratives is so strong... it only becomes Jesus Christ is the only reasonable conclusion." [^1]

> "If shared themes and parallels are enough to establish truth, then the Epic of Gilgamesh... would have to be accepted as true on that basis alone... Overlap doesn't establish historicity." — Reel [^2]

> "It's an absurd claim that a human being walked from central South America up to Palmyra, New York... as if that's like no big deal." — Reel [^3]

## LDS Connection
Gurr's Mesoamerican segment is an aggressive, geography-specific apologetic that most mainstream LDS scholarship would *not* endorse in its particulars (the Valley-of-Mexico Cumorah and the Quetzalcóatl-as-Christ identification are fringe even among Mesoamerican-model advocates). The vault's posture is instructive: it welcomes the genuinely evidentiary threads — advanced civilization, the right kind of warfare and fortifications, trending metallurgy ([[LiDAR Reveals a Dense Advanced Maya Civilization Matching the Book of Mormon]], [[Mesoamerican Warfare and Fortifications Match the Book of Alma]]) — while explicitly disowning parallelomania ([[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]], [[The Quetzalcoatl Parallel Names Moses Not Christ]]). Reel's Gilgamesh rejoinder lands because Gurr's case leans on motif-stacking rather than the convergence-plus-source-criticism the vault treats as the real standard.
