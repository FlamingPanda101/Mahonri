# Book of Mormon Names Show Authorial Wordplay

## The Claim
The authors/narrators of the Book of Mormon (especially Mormon, Nephi, Enos) **knew the Hebrew and Egyptian meanings of their proper names and deliberately played on them (paronomasia)** in the way characters are introduced and their stories told. This goes beyond the prior claim that the names are merely *attested* in the ancient Near East. [[Matthew Bowen]]: he wasn't the first to look at the names, but the one who thought, "Don't you realize the full potential of this vein of inquiry?" [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A repeated introduction-pattern.** Characters are introduced with an oddly specific, name-revealing detail: Alma "was a young man" ([[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]]); Nephi "born of goodly parents" ([[1 Nephi 1.1|1 Nephi 1:1]]); Enos "knowing my father that he was a just man" ([[Enos 1.1-2|Enos 1:1-2]]); Abish converted "on account of a remarkable vision of her father" ([[Alma 19.16|Alma 19:16]]). Strength: **high** — the pattern is consistent and the introductions are otherwise unmotivated. [^2]
2. **Internal control passages.** [[Helaman 5.6-7|Helaman 5:6-7]] has Helaman tell his sons to remember that it was "said and written ... that they were **good**" — explicitly tying the name Nephi to "good," then fulfilled in [[Helaman 8.7|Helaman 8]]. This is an *internal* confirmation, not just a modern reconstruction. Strength: **high**. [^3]
3. **Biblical controls.** Where the text quotes the Bible (e.g. King Benjamin quoting Psalm 2:7), we can verify the allusion is real, validating the method. Strength: **high**. [^4]
4. **Cumulative onomasticon.** The Nephite name-stock is "heavily Hebrew" with Egyptian elements, consistent with a Lehite colony that knew "the language of the Egyptians" and Hebrew. Strength: **moderate**, depends on each etymology. [^1]

**Steelman (the skeptic's view):** A critic (e.g. in the tradition of Dan Vogel) would argue that (a) wordplay can be read into almost any name post hoc; (b) some "meanings" lean on the King James English ("goodly," "good") rather than the supposed Hebrew/Egyptian; (c) Joseph Smith, a Bible-saturated author, could unconsciously produce name-and-meaning pairings; and (d) confirmation bias selects the hits and ignores names with no wordplay. Bowen's strongest reply is the *internal* control passages (Helaman 5/8) and place-name puns ([[Jershon Means Place of Inheritance|Jershon]]) that depend on Hebrew roots not obvious from English — harder to attribute to chance or to KJV priming.
