# Attestation Versus Wordplay — Two Levels of Evidence

## The Claim
There are **two distinct evidentiary payoffs** from Book of Mormon name research, and Bowen's contribution operates at the second, stronger level:
1. **Attestation** — the name exists as a genuine ancient Near Eastern name (Hebrew/Egyptian/Semitic). This is itself notable but limited.
2. **Functional wordplay** — the *text* shows its author **knew the meaning** and built narrative puns on it. This is much harder to attribute to chance or to a 19th-century farm boy. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Alma illustrates both levels.** Level 1: *Alma* is attested as a male Semitic name (at Ebla, via [[Terry Szink]]) — "basically verified, beyond question." Level 2: the text introduces Alma as "a young man" ([[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]]), matching the name's meaning ("lad"). Strength: **high** for the dual demonstration. [^2]
2. **A spectrum of certainty.** Within a single chapter ([[Helaman 1.1-9|Helaman 1]]) Egyptian names range from **attested** (Pahoran) to **dead ringer** (Paanchi) to **plausible** (Pacumeni). Honest grading of confidence strengthens the overall case. Strength: **moderate-high**. [^3]
3. **Place-names raise the bar.** [[Jershon Means Place of Inheritance|Jershon]] and [[Zarahemla Means Seed of Compassion|Zarahemla]] depend on Hebrew roots invisible in English, so the wordplay can't be reduced to KJV vocabulary. Strength: **high**. [^4]

**Steelman:** A critic grants level-1 attestation is real but says it proves little — coincidence and a large name-pool guarantee some hits. Against level-2, the critic argues the "meaning" is supplied by the analyst, and the introduction details are flexible enough to fit. The reply: level-2 cases with *internal* controls (Helaman 5/8) and non-English-derived roots (Jershon's *yarash*) are the ones not easily explained away.
