# Trent Horns Word-Count Argument Uses Wrong Parameters

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

[[Trent Horn]] argues the ~270,000-word Book of Mormon could not have fit on "only
40" 6×8-inch golden plates, concluding: "If the text doesn't fit, then you must
acquit the Book of Mormon... of being an authentic translation of ancient texts."
The rebuttal: Horn's three key inputs — the plate count, the page/word baseline,
and the words-per-square-inch method — are all wrong.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The "40 plates" figure is unsourced

No eyewitness ever gave a total plate count. A few statements say only "many."
The 40 figure is not from the witnesses; it is an assumption Horn imports. With no
witness number, one must derive the count from the *measured* parameters
(weight, height, plate thickness) — see below.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the absence of any witness count is verifiable from the statement corpus).

### 2. Scientific estimates put the stack in the hundreds, not 40

[[Josh Coats]] (garage experiment measuring void space between common-tin-thickness
copper discs) estimates **187–259 plates**; geologist-engineer [[Jerry Grover]]
estimates **300–600**. A 6-inch stack of tin-thin plates simply *is* hundreds of
leaves.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (two independent technical estimates converge well above 40).

### 3. Horn omits the lost 116 pages

Horn's word count does not account for the 116 lost manuscript pages; [[Josh Coats]]'s
model does. Including them only strengthens the case, since the additional text
still fits within the hundreds-of-plates count.[^4]

### 4. The words-per-square-inch method cherry-picks one or two artifacts

Critics extrapolate from one or two ancient metal inscriptions (e.g. the Darius
plates) — but there are thousands of examples of ancient metal writing. A sweeping
generalization from a tiny, unrepresentative sample is methodologically invalid.
See [[Translation Density Shows the Book of Mormon Text Fits]].[^5]

### 5. Keystone: Horn assumes ~42 characters per side and uses a scaled-down model

A separate [[Keystone]] rebuttal of the same Horn reel reverse-engineers his numbers: 270,000 words ÷ 80 words/character = 3,375 characters ÷ 40 plates ÷ 2 sides ≈ **only 42 characters per side** — an unexplained, implausibly low assumption. Keystone contacted the maker of the very plate model Horn shows on screen: it actually holds ~**128 characters per side**, and Horn was displaying the **3/4-scale** model — the full-scale model has ~**230 characters** on the front of the first plate (almost 5½× Horn's figure). Horn's own plate dimensions (6×8 in) and seal fraction (~2/3) are also only *some* of the eyewitness estimates ([[Orson Pratt]], [[Martin Harris]], [[David Whitmer]] gave 7×8 or 6×9, and ~1/2 sealed), leaving far more room than he represents.[^7] The positive demonstration that the text fits is [[The Book of Mormon Fits on the Plates - Sjodahl Hebrew Test]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Horn's instinct is reasonable: 270,000 words *is* a lot of text, and the upper
weight/size bounds are real constraints — the text can't be arbitrarily large. The
panel explicitly credits him ("I'm sure Trent Horn was doing his honest best
work") and warns against the *opposite* error of claiming fabrication is easy. The
disagreement is purely over inputs: use the correct plate count, character-based
density, and a representative artifact sample, and the text fits.[^6]
