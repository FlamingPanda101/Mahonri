# The Book of Mormon Was Countercultural in 1830 Indian Removal America

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] caps his "reversal of values" reading with the book's historical context. The Book of Mormon was published in **1830 — the same year Andrew Jackson initiated Indian Removal**, pushing Native Americans west of the Mississippi. Against that backdrop, the book declares that God covenanted *this land* to the **ancestors of the Native Americans** — that it is *their* land. Bradley argues this "could not have been more countercultural in Joseph Smith's time": at the precise moment the U.S. government was dispossessing and dehumanizing American Indians, a new scripture dignified them as a covenant people and heirs of the land. This, he says, is the external confirmation of his thesis that "this book is meant to be read as countercultural," and that conventional readings have missed it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The chronological collision is real.** The Book of Mormon was published in 1830; the Indian Removal Act passed in 1830. The book's framing of the Americas as covenant land promised to the Lamanites' ancestors does run directly counter to the era's removal policy. (Evidence rating: strong on the bare facts of timing and the book's claim; the rhetorical force is the interpretation.)[^1]
2. **It coheres with the book's pro-indigenous theology.** The Book of Mormon's narrative gives a dignified ancestral identity to American Indians as a remnant of a covenant people — a posture sharply at odds with contemporary attitudes, which lends weight to the "countercultural" reading. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong — the textual theme is uncontested; whether it was *deliberately* subversive is the claim.)[^1]
3. **It supplies a real-world anchor for the literary thesis.** Bradley's four internal reversals (social, economic, moral, geographic) gain plausibility if the book's *content* was also socially subversive in its moment — the 1830 context is offered as that anchor. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A skeptic can argue the "countercultural" framing is read back anachronistically: the Book of Mormon's treatment of the Lamanites also includes the "skin of blackness" / curse language that critics call its own form of racial othering, so calling the book straightforwardly pro-indigenous is selective. One can also note that sympathetic-to-the-Indian "lost tribes" speculation was *common* in 1820s–30s America (e.g., Ethan Smith's *View of the Hebrews*), so the basic idea was not unique to Joseph Smith, even if the removal-era timing sharpens its bite. Bradley's defenders would reply that the book's *covenantal dignifying* of Native peoples — land promised to *them* — still cut hard against Jackson-era policy regardless of how widespread "lost tribes" curiosity was, and that the curse passages are themselves contested as non-racial elsewhere in the literature. The fair residue: the 1830/Indian-Removal coincidence is genuine and striking, and the book does advance a covenant claim on behalf of Native ancestry that ran against the dominant policy of its moment.
