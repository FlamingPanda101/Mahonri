# Book of Mormon Christology Edits Are Not Evidence of Trinitarian Backpedaling

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** The 1830 text read "the virgin... is the mother of **God**"
([[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11:18]]) and "the Lamb of God, yea, even **the Eternal
Father**" ([[1 Nephi 11.21|1 Nephi 11:21]]). Later editions inserted "the Son of"
in both. Critics argue this reflects [[Joseph Smith]]'s **evolving theology** — an
originally Trinitarian (even modalist) Christology that he later revised toward a
plurality-of-gods, social-Godhead view. The edits, on this reading, hide the
earlier theology.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The 1830 text is not actually Trinitarian

The host, "as someone who has read the entire 1830 edition," argues "it's not
Trinitarian at all," and offers [[2 Nephi 31|2 Nephi 31]] as a counter-example:
the Father and the Son **speak separately**, with the Father repeatedly
distinguishing himself from the Son — "that alone makes a strict Trinitarian
reading very difficult to maintain."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a counter-text. A passage staging Father–Son
> dialogue is hard to square with modalism, and it was present in 1830 unchanged.

### 2. "Mother of God" was orthodox, ambiguous language

The original "mother of God" mirrors the historic title *Theotokos* ("God-bearer,"
Council of Ephesus, 431), which never meant Mary birthed the divine nature. The
1837 insertion of "the Son of" *clarifies* for a Protestant readership rather than
reversing a doctrine. (Linguistic/historical context.)

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong. The synonym defense is legitimate, but it
> does not by itself explain *why* the change was made when it was.

### 3. The edits fit divine-investiture Christology

Book of Mormon theology already calls the Son "the Father" representationally
(Mosiah 15: Christ is "the Father and the Son"). Adding "the Son of" disambiguates
the relationship within an existing framework rather than importing a new one.

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (depends on accepting the LDS divine-investiture
> reading of Mosiah 15).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's strongest case: even if 2 Nephi 31 distinguishes the persons, the
*specific edited verses* originally identified the Lamb flatly *as* the Eternal
Father and Mary as the mother *of God* — language that is at minimum
modalist-sounding and was systematically softened exactly as Joseph's public
theology shifted (King Follett discourse, plurality of gods, co-eternal spirits).
A coincidence between the direction of the textual edits and the documented
trajectory of Joseph's later teaching is precisely the pattern a developing-human-
theology hypothesis predicts. Pointing to one unedited verse (2 Nephi 31) does not
neutralize the edited verses; it shows the 1830 text was theologically mixed —
which itself fits an author working out his Christology in real time.
