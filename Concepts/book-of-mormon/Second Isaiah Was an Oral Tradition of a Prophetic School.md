# Second Isaiah Was an Oral Tradition of a Prophetic School

## The Claim
[[John Eaton]] argues that "Second Isaiah" (Isaiah 40–55) is not the work of a single later author but the product of a **school of prophets** in and around the temple who transmitted material **orally** over several generations before it was finally written down around or after Cyrus (539 BC). [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] use this to answer the standard objection that Deutero-Isaiah is too late to appear in the Book of Mormon: if the material was old and oral, Lehi's family could have carried it.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A school, not a guy (rated: Eaton's thesis).** "Second Isaiah is not a guy; second Isaiah is a school of people… a school of prophets in and around the temple deliberately seeing themselves as in the tradition of Isaiah, applying the name Isaiah to the work they do." Eaton "refers to several generations of work eventually being written down" — "essentially an oral tradition."[^1]
2. **The dating objection, bracketed (rated: framing).** The usual reason for dating Isaiah 40–55 late is that it names Cyrus; the hosts grant scholars who reject predictive prophecy will date it post-Cyrus, but note Eaton's point is that the *content* "was already old when it was written down."[^2] Compare the vault's [[Deutero-Isaiah Theory]].
3. **Jacob says Isaiah "spake" it (rated: textual).** In 2 Nephi 6:4–5 Jacob says "I will read you the words of Isaiah" but then, twice, that these are the words Isaiah "spake" — and he quotes Isaiah 49 (a Servant Song) and Isaiah 50, both Deutero-Isaiah. The hosts read this as the Book of Mormon presenting Deutero-Isaianic material as a remembered *oral* tradition, "exactly how we ought to have some pieces of second Isaiah in the Book of Mormon."[^3]
4. **Isaiah 1 is late and absent from the Book of Mormon (rated: corroborating detail).** Scholars treat Isaiah 1 as a late editorial preface; the Book of Mormon's Isaiah quotations begin at Isaiah 2, not Isaiah 1 — a "suspiciously correct" alignment.[^4]

**Steelman of the critic.** The dominant critical position remains that Isaiah 40–55 is **exilic** (6th-century) composition, postdating Lehi's departure (c. 600 BC), and Eaton's oral-school model — while a real scholarly option — does not establish that specific Deutero-Isaianic *texts* existed by 600 BC. A "full Chad" apologetic (Isaiah wrote it all) and a "it's all late, so the Book of Mormon fails" skepticism are the poles; Eaton's view is offered as a middle path, not as proof. The oral-transmission hypothesis is plausible but not demonstrable for the particular passages quoted.

## Best Quotes
> "Second Isaiah is not a guy; second Isaiah is a school of people… he in fact says it's essentially an oral tradition."[^1]

> "Jacob says… 'these are the words which Isaiah spake'… which is exactly right, and that is exactly how we ought to have some pieces of second Isaiah in the Book of Mormon."[^3]
