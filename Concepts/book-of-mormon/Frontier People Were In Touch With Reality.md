# Frontier People Were In Touch With Reality

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The witnesses shared an early-1800s "backcountry magical worldview," making them credulous and susceptible to seeing things that weren't there.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Hard physical labor kept them tethered to the real
These were farmers and laborers who spent their days moving rocks, building fences, digging wells, clearing fields, removing tree stumps, and walking or riding everywhere over rough paths. "Talk about being in touch with reality."[^2] Their lives were the opposite of disassociated.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (a sociological reframing; persuasive but not quantitative).

### 2. Moderns are arguably *more* prone to manufactured unreality
Peterson inverts the charge: "we are more prone to believe nonsense — altered reality, artificial intelligence photos. They never saw any of that. For them it was hard work."[^3] People who lived under the open sun all day knew exactly what the sun looked like and wouldn't mistake it for an angel.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (rhetorical inversion; the comparison is suggestive rather than demonstrated).

### 3. The "credulous rube" stereotype is condescending and unhistorical
The idea that they were "disassociated from reality and we're not... is in some ways laughable." Their small homes meant little time spent indoors imagining things; their economy demanded constant contact with the physical world.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (corrective to a stereotype; the stereotype itself is the thing being challenged).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The serious version of the objection isn't "they couldn't tell sun from angel" but that the *cultural plausibility structure* of the early-1800s American frontier — seer stones, treasure-digging, folk magic, visionary religion — made supernatural interpretations of ambiguous experiences readily available and socially rewarded. Manual competence with rocks and wells does not immunize against culturally-primed religious interpretation. Peterson's strongest reply is indirect: the *eight's* experience was mundane and tactile (nothing ambiguous to interpret supernaturally), so the worldview objection bites mainly on the three, and even there the convergent specificity (see [[No Shared Hallucination]]) resists a purely cultural-projection account. The believer should concede this concept rebuts a caricature more than the best form of the worldview argument.

## Best Quotes

> "They were also hard-headed people... They were out moving rocks, building fences, digging wells. Talk about being in touch with reality."[^2]

> "We are more prone to believe nonsense, I think — altered reality, artificial intelligence photos. They never saw any of that. For them it was hard work."[^3]

> "You're not just seeing fairies appearing all the time... you know what the sun looks like — you're out under it almost all the time."[^5]
