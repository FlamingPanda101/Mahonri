# Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance

## The Claim

[[Jerry Grover]] argues that while **provenance** (a documented chain of custody from a controlled excavation) is the gold standard for authenticating an artifact, **forensic analysis can override poor provenance** in either direction — vindicating a genuine object that *lacks* provenance, or unmasking a forgery that *has* it. The Grolier Codex is the showcase case for vindication.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Grolier Codex — vindicated despite no provenance — rate: strong (case study)
The Maya Codex of Mexico (formerly the Grolier Codex) surfaced in a *private collection* in the 1960s with essentially no provenance; the eminent Mesoamericanist [[Michael Coe]] *argued against* its authenticity, and it was long disputed. Yet a 2018 team of scientists upheld it through multiple forensic studies as **genuine — the oldest surviving book in the Americas**. Forensics overturned the provenance problem and an expert's skepticism.[^2]

### 2. Provenance can be forged faster than forensics — rate: moderate (method)
Grover notes the converse: "there have been lots of times where the provenance has been able to be forged faster than the forensics." A documented chain of custody is *evidence*, not proof; physical analysis (saw marks, hot-rolled copper, desert-varnish dating) can expose a fraud that paperwork made look legitimate — which is exactly how the Michigan Relics and Padilla plates were caught.[^3]

### 3. The honest rule of thumb — rate: n/a (method statement)
Grover's working practice: he simply does *not* use a disputed object in his research until forensics clears it — neither asserting it fake nor citing it as evidence. Authenticity is provisional and revisable as analysis improves; objects "sit on a shelf" pending data. See [[Defending Faith Requires Ruling Out the Fakes]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The principle is sound but easy to abuse. (a) "Forensics can override provenance" can become a *believer's escape hatch*: a defender of any favored fake can say "just wait for forensics to vindicate it," indefinitely deferring an adverse verdict — the Grolier *vindication* is the rare exception, not the rule, and most poorly-provenanced objects that get tested turn out fake. (b) Forensic methods are themselves contestable: desert-varnish dating (Los Lunas), residue carbon dating (Bat Creek), and stylistic analysis all have wide error bars and disputed reliability, so "forensics settled it" can overstate. (c) The Grolier case actually *vindicates the system*: it was upheld only *after* rigorous testing and peer scrutiny, which is the academic process working, not an argument against trusting it. (d) A skeptic notes that the asymmetry the believers want (genuine-but-unprovenanced golden plates) is precisely the scenario forgers exploit. So the defensible takeaway is methodological humility: provenance is strong but not infallible, forensics can move a verdict either way, and the responsible stance — which Grover models — is to *withhold* use of disputed items rather than to treat "forensics might vindicate it someday" as license to cite them now.
