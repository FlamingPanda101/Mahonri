# Mormon Puns on Alma's Name as Virgin, Concealed, and Young Man

## The Claim

[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that the narrator Mormon **puns on the name Alma across several Hebrew roots sharing the consonants *ʿ-l-m*** — and, crucially, adds a layer the vault's existing onomastic note does not: that one of those senses is *ʿalmah*, "**Virgin**" (the very word of [[Isaiah 7.14|Isaiah 7:14]], "the Virgin shall conceive"), and *ʿolam*, "**eternal**." So "Alma" simultaneously evokes **young man** (*ʿelem*/*ʿalmah*), **conceal/hide** (*ʿalam*), **Virgin** (*ʿalmah* — "he is the Virgin"), and **eternal** (*ʿolam*) — making Alma a *type of the Virgin / the eternal woman*, which sets up his baptizing by the authority of [[Alma Baptizes by the Authority of El Shaddai at the Waters of Mormon|El Shaddai the Mother]]. The "young man" and "conceal" senses are the established case (see [[Alma Means Young Man - Wordplay on Hiddenness]], from Matthew Bowen); **the Virgin/eternal divine-feminine layer is what this Ward Radio treatment adds.** [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Mormon puns twice in quick succession at the name's introduction — rate: moderate-high (the patterning is conspicuous)
[[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]] introduces Alma as "**a young man**" — and Butler notes one Hebrew word for young man/stripling is *ʿelem* (also the word behind "stripling warriors," making the stripling warriors "the Alma warriors"). Then "he being **concealed**" (Mosiah 17:4) puns on *ʿalam*, "to conceal." Butler: "Mormon puns not once but twice in quick succession on this name… to be sure we get it." This is the same density Bowen flags. [^1]

### 2. The Virgin sense: *Alma* ≈ *ʿalmah*, "his name is Virgin" — rate: moderate (the new divine-feminine layer)
Butler's added move: the same triliteral root yields *ʿalmah*, "**Virgin**" — the unambiguous word of [[Isaiah 7.14|Isaiah 7:14]] ("*ha-ʿalmah*, *the* Virgin, shall conceive," with the definite article, as the Great Isaiah Scroll confirms). So "his name is something really close to **Virgin**." Combined with *ʿolam* ("eternal") and *taʿalumah* ("a secret"), "Alma is the **Eternal woman**… his name echoes in all of that." This ties Alma directly to the tree-of-life Virgin of [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]]. [^2]

### 3. The payoff at the Waters of Mormon — rate: moderate (motivates the divine-feminine reading)
The name-as-Virgin is not idle wordplay: it is why Butler reads Alma as a *high priest of the Mother*, baptizing "having authority from El Shaddai" in a place marked by the beasts, pure water, and trees of the divine feminine. The onomastics and the ordinance scene reinforce each other. See [[Alma Baptizes by the Authority of El Shaddai at the Waters of Mormon]]. [^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The same cautions apply as to the Bowen version, plus extra for the Virgin layer. (a) **"Young man" is plain KJV English** ([[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]] simply *says* "young man"), so the introduction need not presuppose a Hebrew pun at all. (b) **Combining multiple roots is having it both ways**: *ʿalmah* "young woman/virgin," *ʿelem* "young man," *ʿalam* "conceal," and *ʿolam* "eternal" are *related but distinct* lexemes, and piling all of them onto one male character's name is the kind of maximal wordplay that can be generated for almost any name — the methodological worry the vault raises in [[Attestation Versus Wordplay - Two Levels of Evidence]]. (c) The **Virgin sense is gendered feminine** (*ʿalmah* is a young *woman*), so reading a male prophet's name as "Virgin" requires treating the gender mismatch as deliberate typology rather than a problem — a charitable but unfalsifiable move. (d) A fugitive narrative *naturally* involves concealment, so the "hide/secret" cluster may be plot, not pun. The defensible core: the **"young man / conceal" wordplay is well-grounded** (independently argued by Bowen from Ebla attestation), and the **Virgin/eternal layer is a creative extension** that is *suggestive* given Alma's role at the divine-feminine-marked Waters of Mormon, but rests on conflating several roots and overriding grammatical gender — which is why it functions as typology in service of the Heavenly-Mother thesis rather than as standalone linguistic proof.
