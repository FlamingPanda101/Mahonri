# brass plates

#type/term

## The Claim
The **brass plates** (or "plates of brass") are the metal scriptural record that [[Laban]] held in Jerusalem and that [[Lehi]] sent his sons to retrieve before the family left for the New World (1 Nephi 3–4). [[Nephi]] reports they contained "the five books of Moses," a record of the Jews to the reign of Zedekiah, and the prophecies of the holy prophets, including writings of [[Joseph of Egypt]] not found in the King James Bible ([[1 Nephi 5.11|1 Nephi 5:11–13]]). In Latter-day Saint apologetics the brass plates function as a key explanatory device: a branch of scripture that **left Jerusalem c. 600 BC**, before the textual changes the vault associates with the [[Josiah|Josianic]]/Deuteronomist reform, and so preserves older theology.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **A pre-exilic textual stream that escaped later editing.** Because the plates were carried out of Jerusalem just before the Babylonian destruction, they "bypassed" the exile, the Masoretic standardization, and the canon councils — preserving an embodied, pre-existent Messiah and divine-council material. *Rating: an internal-narrative claim, coherent with the reform thesis but presupposing the plates' historicity.* See [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]].
2. **A northern, Egyptian-language scribal archive.** The custodian family is of the tribe of Manasseh; researchers argue the record was kept in Egyptian by a northern scribal tradition, which explains both "Reformed Egyptian" and the plates' divergence from the southern, Judahite text. *Rating: a strong convergence of details.* See [[The Northern Lineage Explains Laman and Lemuel and the Brass Plates]].
3. **Contents that differ pointedly from Genesis.** The plates carry Joseph-of-Egypt prophecies and (per one argument) a fifth book of Moses other than Deuteronomy — features used to argue the plates began with the patriarch Joseph. *Rating: internal; load-bearing for the related claims.* See [[The Brass Plates Were Begun by Joseph of Egypt]].

**Steelman.** No physical brass plates exist; every claim about their contents derives from the Book of Mormon, so they cannot be cited as independent evidence for it. The "Bible was corrupted, the plates were pure" framing overstates the textual-criticism consensus (the Hebrew text is remarkably stable). **Reply:** the brass plates are not offered as a stand-alone proof but as a coherent *internal* mechanism that makes a cluster of features (older Christology, Egyptian language, Josephite material) hang together, and whose explanatory reach is what an authentic ancient record would display.
