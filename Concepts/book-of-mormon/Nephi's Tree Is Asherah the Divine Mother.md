# Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother

#type/claim

## The Claim
Crediting [[Daniel C. Peterson]]'s 1998 article **"Nephi and His Asherah,"** [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that the tree of life in [[Nephi]]'s vision ([[1 Nephi 11|1 Nephi 11]]) is the goddess **Asherah** — the divine Mother — because when Nephi asks the *meaning* of the white, beautiful tree, the angel answers by showing him a white, beautiful **virgin** bearing a child. The tree *is* a woman, and that woman is the tree of life. The vault's existing Asherah notes cover the archaeology ([[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship]]), the pillar-figurine iconography ([[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight]]), and the dating of her vilification ([[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah]]); this note adds the *specific [[1 Nephi 11|1 Nephi 11]] tree-equals-virgin exegesis* and Peterson's argument.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The vision answers "what is the tree?" with a woman.** Nephi sees a tree whose whiteness "did exceed the whiteness of the driven snow" and asks "to know the interpretation thereof." Instead of a definition he is shown the city of Nazareth and "a virgin... exceedingly fair and white," then "the Son of God" she bears. The structural answer to *what is the tree* is *she is the woman* — both are "white and beautiful." [^1]
2. **Nephi later confirms the tree is the tree of life.** Explaining the vision to his brothers in [[1 Nephi 15|1 Nephi 15:21-22]], Nephi says the tree "was a representation of the tree of life." So the tree is a woman who *is* the tree of life — the Asherah pattern, where a divine woman is symbolized by a tree. [^2]
3. **Asherah was a tree-goddess attested next door and in Israel.** At Ugarit her title *Athirat* ("the goddess," feminine of *El*); she is "known all around the ancient Near East," her "most common symbol a tree." The "Yahweh and his Asherah" inscriptions at **Kuntillet Ajrud** and the **Khirbet el-Qom** tomb ("Blessed be Uriyahu by Yahweh and his Asherah"), plus the hundreds of **Judean pillar figurines** (a woman whose lower half is a tree-trunk) from Lehi's-era Jerusalem, show many Israelites regarded her as legitimate. (Butler cites Ziony Zevit's *The Religions of Ancient Israel* and notes this is "Wikipedia stuff... not obscure.") [^3]
4. **People fall down before the tree as before a divine being.** In [[1 Nephi 8|1 Nephi 8:30]] the dreamers "fell down and partook of the fruit." Prostration is what one does "in the presence of a God or a king" (Butler lists the Apocalypse of Abraham 10, 2 Enoch 22:5, Revelation 1:17, Daniel 10:9, Ezekiel 1:28) — so the tree is treated as "some kind of divine being." [^4]
5. **A "happiness" wordplay names the tree as the Asherah.** The fruit is first introduced as "desirable to make one **happy**" ([[1 Nephi 8|1 Nephi 8:10]]). Hebrew *ashrei* ("O the happiness of," the Beatitude word) sounds and looks like *Asherah* — and scholars on Proverbs 3:18 ("she is a tree of life to them that lay hold upon her") already read its happiness-plus-tree-of-life imagery as an Asherah/wisdom reference. Tellingly, "happiness" is overwhelmingly a Book-of-Mormon word (26× vs. 3× in the Old Testament, **0×** in the New), and the Book of Mormon foregrounds "the plan of happiness." Butler: Nephi "is not just showing the images — he's saying, what tree is this? It's the Happy Tree." [^5]
6. **Peterson got there first.** Butler stresses "this is not all my idea": [[Daniel C. Peterson]], "Nephi and His Asherah" (c. 1998), argued that 1 Nephi makes most sense "against a background where Nephi is familiar with and does not condemn a symbolism of Asherah, who is a divine woman who is also a tree." [^6]

**Steelman.** The hosts repeatedly disclaim certainty — "I don't know... I love the linguistic stuff," and grant the case is "really hard to make" without the Book of Mormon. A critic adds: Nephi never *names* Asherah; the virgin is explicitly Mary, and the tree is later glossed as "the love of God" ([[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11:25]]), so reading the tree *as the goddess* imports a meaning the text does not state; and the prophets condemned Asherah, so any echo could be apostasy rather than revelation (the objection [[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah]] addresses). **But** the argument is about *symbolic substrate*, not a claim that Mary = Asherah: a pre-exilic Israelite who knew the tree-goddess would naturally read a divine-woman-as-tree image, and Peterson's reading explains the otherwise odd move whereby asking "what is the tree?" is answered with "a woman." Butler himself frames Mary as "the perfect type" of the divine feminine, not its literal identity.

## Best Quotes
> "It's clear that somehow the answer to the question 'what is the tree?' is that she is the woman. The tree is white and beautiful; the woman is white and beautiful." [^1]

> "Daniel Peterson, twenty-odd years ago, said this makes the most sense against a background where Nephi is familiar with — and does not condemn — a symbolism of Asherah, who is a divine woman who is also a tree." [^6]

> "Nephi is not just showing the images. He's like, 'Hey, what tree is this? It's the Happy Tree.'" [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the episode's headline payoff and a strong [[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship|Heavenly Mother]] argument: a faithful PhD ([[Daniel C. Peterson|Peterson]]) reads the Book of Mormon's central vision as preserving the very tree-goddess the [[Josiah's Reform Removed the Mother from the Temple|reformers purged]] — so the Restoration's divine Mother is a *recovery*, not an import. It builds directly on [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight]] and [[Wisdom Personified as the Mother in Heaven]], and the violent removal of this same tree-goddess is what Butler reads in [[Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah]].

## Corroboration (Ward Radio)
In the later Ward Radio live stream "Was Alma a High Priest of Heavenly Mother?", [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] re-runs this same tree-equals-Virgin-equals-Asherah argument as the first of "two roads," then extends it: the pure waters and thicket of trees reappear at the **Waters of Mormon**, where Alma (whose name puns on "Virgin") baptizes by the authority of El Shaddai. See [[Alma Baptizes by the Authority of El Shaddai at the Waters of Mormon]] and [[Mormon Puns on Almas Name as Virgin Concealed and Young Man]]. [^7]
