# The Rechabites Are the Prophets of Old Who Inspired Lehi

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]] proposes a specific candidate for the otherwise-unnamed **"prophets of old, whom
they had cast out and stoned and slain"** that Lehi invokes in [[1 Nephi 1.8-19|1 Nephi 1:19-20]]:
**Rechab** and the **Rechabites**, known from [[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35]] and elaborated in the
apocryphal *History of the Rechabites* (the *Zosimus* narrative). Barnes argues the Rechabites are a
**"proto-Lehi"** — a righteous, Jerusalem-era family who fled the doomed city for preaching the
Messiah and crossed a great body of water to a promised land, producing a six-point parallel to the
Lehi story too close to be coincidence.[^1] This is the concrete instantiation of the vault's broader
[[Prophets Were Killed for Preaching the Messiah Before 600 BC]] thesis (also Barnes's): there it is
argued *that* such prophets existed; here a *named apocryphal group* is offered as one of them.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "prophets of old" are unnamed in the surviving canon — rate: strong (textual gap)
In [[1 Nephi 1.8-19|1 Nephi 1:19-20]] Lehi is mocked for denouncing Jerusalem's wickedness but they
seek his life only when he "manifested plainly the coming of a Messiah" — "even as with the prophets
of old, whom they had cast out and stoned and slain." The plural "prophets" implies a *group* the Old
Testament does not name (Zechariah son of Jehoiada predates Lehi and is singular). The Rechabites fill
that gap.[^2]

### 2. Jeremiah 35 fixes the Rechabites in Lehi's exact era — rate: strong (canonical anchor)
[[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35]] presents the Rechabites as contemporaries of Jeremiah (and thus Lehi),
defined by their forefather **Jonadab son of Rechab**'s command to dwell as **nomads** — no houses,
vineyards, or settled cities — "explaining why Lehi was so willing to leave the land of his
inheritance." The Wikipedia/standard summary also ties them to the **Kenites** (wandering smiths) who
accompanied Israel.[^3]

### 3. The apocryphal *History of the Rechabites* mirrors the Lehi narrative — rate: moderate (apocryphal parallel)
In the *Zosimus* text, the monk Zosimus is led by an angel across an impassably large body of water to
a righteous people who say "we are the children of Rechab." Their origin story: "when Jeremiah the
prophet declared that the city of Jerusalem was to be handed over to destroyers, our father Rechab paid
attention" — they pleaded for Jerusalem, were spared a while, then **driven out for "worshiping God the
Son of God himself"** (i.e., for messianism), and departed across the waters. Barnes lists six
parallels: (1) live in Jerusalem just before the Babylonian captivity; (2) descend from a righteous
father after whom they are renamed (Rechabites / Lehites); (3) follow that father who believes the
prophecy that Jerusalem will fall; (4) flee into the wilderness; (5) are led to a promised land across
a wide expanse of waters; (6) believe in the controversial divine sonship of the coming Messiah.[^4]

### 4. The perpetual-posterity promise echoes Enos — rate: moderate (covenant language)
[[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35:19]]'s reward — Jonadab "shall not want a man to stand before me for ever" —
parallels the covenant that a righteous father's "seed will not be utterly destroyed" ([[Enos 1.1-2| Enos 1]]), and post-biblical traditions (the *Mishnah*'s "sons of the water-drinkers," Joseph Wolff's
1839 report of Yemeni Rechabite-claimants near Sinai) extend the line.[^5]

### 5. Why the record was suppressed — rate: moderate (motive theory)
Like the [[Prophets Were Killed for Preaching the Messiah Before 600 BC|parent thesis]], Barnes argues
a text in which Jerusalem's establishment murders Messiah-preachers was "an inconvenient truth" the
[[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|Deuteronomist editors]] would suppress, fitting the
[[The Surviving Old Testament Is the Southern Kingdom's Edition|southern-edition]] pattern and the
general point that the Book of Mormon presupposes a richer, partly-lost pre-exilic record (cf.
[[Jesus Referenced the Apocrypha]]).[^6]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Parallels are not identification.** The six points are *thematic* (righteous father, flight, water
  crossing, messianism) and could reflect a shared *genre* of "righteous remnant flees doomed city,"
  not that Lehi specifically had the Rechabites in mind. Barnes himself frames it as "we're going to
  prove it" but the proof is cumulative resemblance, not a citation.
- **The *History of the Rechabites* is late and Christianized.** Its surviving form post-dates Christ
  and bears Christian redaction (the "Son of God" language), so its "messianism" may be a later overlay,
  not 7th-century-BC belief — weakening parallel #6.
- **Jeremiah 35's Rechabites are not said to be prophets, nor martyred.** The canonical text praises
  their fidelity to a *temperance vow*; the prophet/persecution material comes only from the apocryphal
  expansion. So the identification leans on the contested apocryphon, not the firm canonical anchor.
- **"Prophets of old" may just be the generic rejected-prophet motif** (Jeremiah in the stocks, Uriah
  slain), as the steelman in the parent note grants.

**What survives:** the *modest* claim is strong — there genuinely existed a Jerusalem-era, nomadic,
covenant-bound family (the Rechabites of [[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35]]) whose later tradition tells a
Lehi-shaped story of fleeing doomed Jerusalem across the waters. Whether they are *the* prophets Lehi
named is a plausible, evocative *hypothesis*, not a demonstration — and Barnes's real payoff is the
same turnabout as the parent note: the Book of Mormon's "prophets cast out for the Messiah" presupposes
exactly the kind of lost record the apocrypha is now recovering.
