# The Book of Mormon Has No Map and No Confirmed Geography

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]]'s second reason for not being Mormon is methodological and deliberately *not* an internal-contradiction attack. He explicitly declines to disprove the Book of Mormon by alleged errors — he grants Mormons the same interpretive latitude (genre, translation, non-literal reading) he grants Catholics defending the deuterocanon. The real asymmetry, he says, is geographic: "Most Bibles contain maps... you can visit the ruins of cities described in the Bible," and atheist, Christian, and Jewish scholars can "roughly agree on where the events in the Bible allegedly took place." Nothing comparable exists for the Book of Mormon — Mormons disagree whether events occurred in Mesoamerica, the U.S. heartland, or South America, and (he quotes) the National Geographic Society "does not know of anything found so far that... substantiated the Book of Mormon." His analogy: it would be hard to defend the Bible "if some Christians said Jerusalem was in Israel and others said southern France and others said India."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **(Moderate)** The disagreement among LDS geography models (Mesoamerican vs. Heartland vs. hemispheric) is real and ongoing; the failed Zarahemla expeditions in Iowa are cited as illustration.[^2] This internal disagreement is the strongest part of Horn's case.
2. **(Weak–moderate, dated)** The National Geographic Society quotation is genuine but old and broad; it asserts non-confirmation, not disconfirmation, and predates more recent claimed convergences.[^1]
3. **(Concession-shaped)** Horn concedes some LDS scholars lean toward the book as allegory/non-historical, which he says "threatens to turn Mormonism into something as irrelevant as Unitarianism."[^3] This is a dilemma (historical-but-unlocated vs. admittedly-allegorical), not a positive disproof.

## Opposing View (Steelmanned)
The Latter-day Saint reply distinguishes *non-confirmation* from *disconfirmation* and denies the Bible/Book-of-Mormon symmetry is apt. (1) "Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence" when one has not specified what signature to look for, and only a tiny fraction of the relevant New World has been excavated ([[Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence in New World Archaeology]], [[No Archaeological Evidence Requires Specifying What to Look For]], [[Only Two Percent of the Americas Has Been Excavated]]). (2) The geography is not undetermined in the Old World leg of the narrative: the **Nahom/NHM** altar inscriptions and a matching "Bountiful" due east are a concrete, datable convergence ([[Nahom Inscription Confirms the Book of Mormon Geography]], [[Bountiful Lies Due East of Nahom]]). (3) The internal-model disagreement is overstated as fatal: a *limited* geography (most scholars: Mesoamerica) predates and survives the dispute, and LiDAR has revealed a dense, advanced, fortified Maya civilization of the right scale and period ([[LiDAR Reveals a Dense Advanced Maya Civilization Matching the Book of Mormon]], [[Mesoamerican Warfare and Fortifications Match the Book of Alma]]). (4) The standard is *convergence, not proof* — the same standard applied to many biblical sites ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]).

## Counter-objection
Horn's Jerusalem-in-three-countries analogy equivocates. Biblical geography enjoyed two millennia of continuous habitation, named survival of toponyms, and an unbroken textual-pilgrimage tradition; the Book of Mormon describes a civilization whose names did *not* survive into colonial cartography, so the fair comparison is to other lost-and-relocated ancient sites (Troy, the Hittite capital, Ebla) that were eventually found despite earlier "no evidence" verdicts. That a community still debates the exact map is not unique to Mormonism (the location of Sodom, the route of the Exodus, and the site of Calvary are all contested among believing scholars). The deeper point is dialectical: Horn frames this as a "would God leave us without a map?" expectation, but the LDS literature reframes it as "*could* God have done this, and does the available evidence trend toward or away from authenticity over time?" — where the anachronism-reversal rate is claimed to run *toward* the book ([[Could God vs Would God on the Book of Mormon]], [[The Anachronism Reversal Rate Trends Toward the Book of Mormon Over Time]]).

## Apologetic Engagement
Horn is careful and concedes much, which makes this his most durable Book-of-Mormon point; it is not refuted by a single artifact but by the cumulative-convergence posture the vault already documents. The honest LDS engagement admits the internal-geography disagreement and the absence of a Bible-grade map, then shifts the burden to the trend of the evidence and the Old-World anchors (Nahom, Bountiful, the frankincense trail — [[The Frankincense Trail Fits Lehis Wilderness Journey]]).

## LDS Relevance
This is the geographic core of Horn's "Reason 2." Its companion is [[The Rediscovered Papyri Are Evidence Against the Book of Abraham]] (the Book of Abraham half). Note that Horn deliberately sets aside the internal-contradiction genre of attack — so the LDS reply need not relitigate anachronisms here, only the *geography/confirmation* claim, handled by the Nahom, LiDAR, and limited-geography clusters.
