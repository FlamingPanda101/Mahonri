# Alma Is One of the 72 Boustrophedon Names of God

## The Claim

[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] offers a final, admittedly speculative flourish: that **"Alma" appears among the 72 mystical names of God** ("the Shem ha-Mephorash") derived from [[Exodus 14.17-18|Exodus 14:19-21]]. Those three verses each contain exactly **72 Hebrew letters**, and reading them **boustrophedon** (one letter from verse 1, the *last* letter of verse 2, the first of verse 3 — "the turning ox," alternating direction like plowing a field or "playing Snake on your phone") yields 72 three-letter divine names. In the popular Kabbalistic charts of these names, one of them — read right-to-left, "four from the right on the top row" — is ***ʾLMʿ / Alma***, glossed "healing / eliminating negative thoughts." Butler presents this as a curiosity ("what do we make of that? I don't know — the Book of Mormon is true"), not as proof. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The construction of the 72 names is real and ancient — rate: moderate (the technique is genuinely attested)
The Shem ha-Mephorash is a documented Jewish mystical tradition: the three consecutive 72-letter verses of Exodus 14:19-21, combined boustrophedon, generate seventy-two three-letter names, each meditated upon "to gain spiritual power." Butler ("Dave didn't make this up") locates the tradition in Kabbalistic texts like the *Sefer Yetzirah* and *Sefer ha-Bahir*, ignoring the later Masoretic vowel-points and working from the bare 72 consonants. [^1]

### 2. "Alma" is among them — rate: weak (depends on a Pinterest-style chart; sequence/labels are popular, not critical)
On a common chart of the 72 names (with new-age glosses like "DNA of the soul," "dream state"), Butler points to a square reading ***ʾLMʿ*** — "**Alma**" — labeled "healing / eliminating negative thoughts," located "four from the right" because the chart runs right-to-left. He stresses the *labels* are partly "new-agey mysticism," but the *names themselves* are old. [^2]

### 3. It rhymes with the other Alma wordplay — rate: weak (suggestive, not evidential)
For Butler this is a bonus resonance on top of the [[Mormon Puns on Almas Name as Virgin Concealed and Young Man|Virgin/conceal/young-man puns]]: the name of the Book of Mormon's church-founding prophet also surfaces as a *name of God* in an ancient Jewish meditation tradition. He offers it explicitly as a "fun piece of trivia for those of you who like me are nerds," not load-bearing apologetics. [^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the weakest, most speculative claim in the episode, and the cautions are severe. (a) **The "72 names" are esoteric medieval Kabbalah, not a feature of the biblical text**: the technique post-dates the Book of Mormon's ostensible setting by well over a millennium, so "Alma is a name of God" tells us nothing about how a 6th-century-BC or 2nd-century-BC Nephite would hear the name. (b) **Three-letter combinations are cheap**: with 72 short consonant-clusters drawn from a fixed alphabet, *many* common Semitic names will coincidentally appear — finding "Alma" among them is unsurprising and carries no evidential weight (the Texas-sharpshooter problem). (c) **The chart Butler reads is a popular/Pinterest artifact** with new-age glosses ("diffusing negative energy," "DNA of the soul"), and he says so — the *labels* are modern inventions, and even the *ordering* of the names varies by source, so "four from the right, healing" is not a stable datum. (d) Butler himself frames it as trivia and answers "what do we make of that? I don't know," which is the appropriate confidence level. The honest takeaway: the Shem ha-Mephorash is a *real* and interesting Jewish mystical tradition worth recording, and the coincidence that "Alma" can be read out of it is a *fun* resonance — but it is numerological coincidence, not evidence for the Book of Mormon, and the source treats it as such.
