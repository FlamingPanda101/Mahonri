# Book of Mormon Isaiah Variants Are Not Copied from the KJV

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics say [[Joseph Smith]] simply copied Isaiah straight out of the King James Bible "on a shelf," and that this settles the whole question. The Book of Mormon does contain extensive Isaiah material — "roughly a third of the book appears across several chapters" [^9].

## The Rebuttal
1. **Hundreds of differences, not a handful.** Comparing the two side by side yields "word substitutions, phrase variations, structural changes, additions, and omissions... not a handful, but hundreds." A copyist seeking the path of least resistance would reproduce the familiar text verbatim. *Rating: the existence of variants is uncontested; their *significance* is the contested part.* [^9]
2. **Fabrication-cost argument.** Making hundreds of small changes "adds complexity and creates more opportunities to get caught. People committing fraud don't usually try to make their job harder on purpose." So if Joseph copied Isaiah, "why does it look like he didn't?" *Rating: a plausibility argument, not proof; it assumes a rational, risk-minimizing forger.* [^10]
3. **The variants are directional, not random.** Arnold pre-empts the "he varied it deliberately to look like a translation" reply: random creative changes would scatter randomly, but the actual variants "cluster in a specific direction, toward the older, more ancient readings" — the signature of translation from an older source. This sets up the [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Matches the Dead Sea Scrolls]] argument [^11].
4. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) The Book of Mormon Isaiah *base text is still recognizably KJV*, italics and all — including KJV translation choices and at least some KJV-specific renderings — which strongly suggests the 1611 English was the working substrate, however modified. (b) Many "changes" are minor and could reflect dictation, revision, or harmonization rather than access to a superior Vorlage. (c) "Toward older readings" is a selective summary; scholars dispute how many variants genuinely improve on the Masoretic/KJV text. Arnold presents the directional claim without quantifying the proportion that actually favor an older reading [^11].

## Best Quotes
> "If you actually compare the two side by side, you find hundreds of differences. Word substitutions, phrase variations, structural changes, additions, and omissions. Not a handful, but hundreds." [^9]

> "Making hundreds of small changes adds complexity and creates more opportunities to get caught. People committing fraud don't usually try to make their job harder on purpose." [^10]

> "What we actually find is that the variations aren't random. They cluster in a specific direction, toward the older, more ancient readings, which is typical for what translation from an older source actually looks like." [^11]
