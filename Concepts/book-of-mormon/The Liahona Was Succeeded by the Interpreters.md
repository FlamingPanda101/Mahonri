# The Liahona Was Succeeded by the Interpreters

## The Claim

Historian [[Don Bradley]], on [[Ward Radio]], argues that a single narrative from the lost 116 pages — preserved in [[Fayette Lapham]]'s interview of Joseph Smith Sr. — answers **two** questions the surviving Book of Mormon leaves open: (1) **how the Nephites obtained the interpreters** (the text explains how the Jaredites got theirs in [[Ether 3.14|Ether 3]], but never how the Nephites did), and (2) **why the Liahona stopped working** (the text simply ceases to mention its use after the New World, even when later prophets travel through wilderness and fight wars where it would obviously help). Per Lapham's account, sometime after arrival the Liahona led its bearer — Bradley argues [[Mosiah]] the first, during the exodus from the land of Nephi to Zarahemla — to an object he was instructed to put on his face: the **interpreters**. Once the Liahona had led him to its own replacement and he learned to use it, the Liahona ceased to function. There is thus a **succession from Liahona to interpreters**, and the missing "how the Nephites got the interpreters" story is missing precisely because Mosiah the first's entire narrative was in the lost pages.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Both gaps are genuinely present in the surviving text — rate: strong (textual)
The canonical Book of Mormon really does (a) narrate the Jaredite interpreters' origin ([[Ether 3.14|Ether 3]]) but never the Nephites', and (b) fall silent on the Liahona's use after the early New World period, despite later occasions that cry out for it. Bradley's reconstruction is motivated by real textual silences, not invented problems.[^1]

### 2. The lost-pages location explains the silence economically — rate: moderate
Because Mosiah the first's reign (and Benjamin's early life) fell within the lost 116 pages, the *absence* of a Nephite-interpreters origin story in our text is exactly what we'd expect if that story sat in the missing manuscript — a single explanation covering both gaps. The interpreters' presence in Mosiah/Benjamin's hands (Benjamin interprets the Jaredite stone) presupposes an acquisition the surviving text never shows.[^1]

### 3. The relic-succession motif fits the wider parallel — rate: moderate (thematic)
A revelatory instrument leading to its own successor coheres with Bradley's broader reading of the Nephite relics as a sacred cache modeled on Israel's tabernacle furniture (see [[The Nephite Sacred Cache Parallels the Ark of the Covenant]]), where instruments of divine guidance are handed down and transformed across dispensations.[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic presses the sourcing. (a) **It hangs on Fayette Lapham**, a single late, secondhand interview of Joseph Smith Sr.; one cannot reconstruct lost scripture confidently from one such recollection. (b) **The identification of the bearer as Mosiah the first is inference**, not stated — Bradley reads it "from various clues," so the narrative's anchoring is interpretive. (c) **An argument from silence cuts both ways** — the Liahona's narrative disappearance might simply reflect Mormon's abridging choices rather than the device ceasing to work, and the interpreters could have an origin Mormon assumed readers knew. A Latter-day Saint reply: the Lapham narrative is valuable precisely because it is an *independent, external* report of lost-pages content that happens to resolve two separate canonical puzzles at once — a convergence unlikely from fabrication; and even held as a hypothesis, it shows the surviving text's "gaps" are the kind a lost-manuscript theory predicts, strengthening rather than straining the account. Bradley presents it as reconstruction, not canon.

## Best Quotes

> "The Liahona leads him to its own replacement... the Liahona guides to the interpreters; once it does that, its purpose is fulfilled, it's replaced. There's a succession from Liahona to interpreters." [^1]

> "How do the Nephites get the interpreters? ... once we realize that Mosiah the first's entire narrative was in the lost pages, then it makes perfect sense that we do not have in our text the story of how the Nephites got the interpreters." [^1]
