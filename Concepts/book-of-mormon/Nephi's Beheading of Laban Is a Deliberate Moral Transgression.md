#type/claim

# Nephi's Beheading of Laban Is a Deliberate Moral Transgression

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]]'s moral leg of the reversal: the most disturbing act in the entire Book of Mormon — [[Nephi]] cutting off the head of the helpless, drunk, sleeping [[Laban]] — is placed at the very *beginning* of the book, on what Bradley argues is the night of Passover. He reads this as the text deliberately flagging itself as a transgressive book. Nephi kills Laban to obtain a book (the brass plates), and the very record Nephi himself later writes is modeled on those plates — so the act that obtains scripture is woven into the book's self-presentation. Pointedly, Nephi breaks the **biggest** commandment ("Thou shalt not kill") in order to obtain the tablets *of the law*; the text does **not** argue the killing was legal or justified — "you are supposed to be disturbed by this story. Nephi is disturbed by it; you're supposed to be too."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The killing really is the narrative's moral shock, placed up front.** Decapitating a defenseless man is the book's hardest scene, and it occurs in the opening chapter — consistent with Bradley's claim that the book signals its transgressive register early rather than easing the reader in. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong as a literary observation about placement and tone.)[^1]
2. **The text foregrounds the moral tension rather than resolving it.** Bradley notes the narrative does not build a legal defense ("it's not trying to make that case"); Nephi's own hesitation dramatizes the violation. This is a fair reading of the passage's affect even though the Spirit's command is present in the text. (Evidence rating: moderate — the *discomfort* is genuinely in the text; whether the book wants it left unresolved is the interpretive claim.)[^2]
3. **Law-against-Spirit framing.** Bradley sets [[Laman]]'s appeal to keeping "the law of Moses" against Nephi being told by the Spirit to *break* the law's biggest commandment — reading the episode as posing law versus spirit at the threshold. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The text *does* supply a justification Bradley downplays: Nephi is commanded by the Spirit, reasons "it is better that one man should perish than that a nation should dwindle and perish in unbelief," and the scene has long been read as a providential, even Atonement-typological act rather than a celebrated transgression (see [[Laban and Nephi Enact the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]]). A critic of the "reversal" reading can say Bradley overstates the book's intent to leave you *un*-reconciled, since the narrator clearly frames the killing as divinely sanctioned necessity. Bradley himself clarifies in the after-show that the message is *not* that one should behead people or flout all law — the disorientation is a literary device to "break you out of your conventional ways of seeing" so the book's larger message lands. The fair residue: the episode is undeniably transgressive and placed at the outset, and the text does dwell on Nephi's reluctance; reasonable readers differ on whether the book resolves that tension (providential necessity) or deliberately preserves it (Bradley's reading).

## Best Quotes
