# Mesoamerican Sacred-Number Intervals Structure the Book of Mormon Year-Counts

## The Claim
[[Jerry Grover]] argues that the year-counts in [[4 Nephi]] and the reign-lengths in [[Ether 1.3|Ether]] (and, he says, throughout the Book of Mormon) are deliberately built from a set of **"sacred numbers"** — Hebrew 5, 7, 10 and Mesoamerican 9, 13, 19, 20 (plus multiples) — exactly the way Maya codices structure their divinatory almanacs. On this reading, Mormon's abridgment is "really his codex... using the same techniques they used in Mesoamerica to make it sacred," and so reflects an ancient compositional convention rather than a 19th-century narrative.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The numbers are anchored in the Mesoamerican calendar
13 and 20 derive from the 260-day Maya ritual calendar (13 periods of 20 days); 19 corresponds to the Metonic cycle, at which lunar and solar reckonings realign ("the New Year's Day hits the same day every 19 years"). Grover thus claims the "sacred" set is not arbitrary but tied to real Mesoamerican astronomy/religion.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate for the calendar facts (the 260-day count and Metonic cycle are genuine); the inference that the Book of Mormon *uses* them is the contested step.

### 2. Fourth Nephi's intervals are the sacred set
[[4 Nephi]] lists years non-sequentially, skipping some. Graphing the listed years, Grover finds the gaps between them are 7, 9, 10, 13, 19, 20, 30 — the sacred numbers and multiples — and that each listed year can itself be reached by summing sacred numbers. "Why would you skip one set of numbers but not the other?" is, he says, answered by the sacred-interval scheme.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate (the intervals are real arithmetic, but with a dense menu of "allowed" numbers, hitting them is statistically cheap — see steelman).

### 3. Ether's reign-lengths decompose into sacred sums
[[Ether 1.3|Ether]] gives reign-lengths rather than a calendar; Grover decomposes them (24 = 19+5; 42 = 7+7+7+7+7+7 or 19+10+13) and notes 19 — which he associates with righteousness/Christ — clustering on certain kings (e.g., Coriantum). The pattern, he says, recurs even in Alma's "year of the judges" counts, which skip years to "cut it into 19s."[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak (multiple decompositions per number is exactly the flexibility that makes the test unfalsifiable without strict rules).

### 4. It mirrors documented Maya codex practice
Grover shows the Dresden and Madrid codices working base-13 interval-sets and the Borgia (Aztec) using 13s and 7s to reach 52, establishing that "engineer the intervals to land on sacred numbers" is what Mesoamerican almanacs actually do — including Maya kings "fudging" their birth-years to hit sacred dates. So the *technique* he attributes to Mormon is attested in the target culture.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the codex practice is real and well-described); it supplies a cultural precedent but not proof the Book of Mormon participates in it.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
This is the heart of the problem, and the host [[Cardon Ellis]] names it on-air: "have we just declared so many sacred numbers at some point that any number you could say is a divine combination of 9 and 13 and 20?" With an allowed set of {5,7,9,10,13,19,20} plus all multiples and free addition of multiple terms, almost any integer up to ~60 is expressible — so "the intervals are sacred numbers" risks being a tautology, the numerical analogue of the pareidolia/parallelomania charge leveled at chiasmus-hunting (see [[Pareidolia Objection to Chiasmus Finding]]). Grover concedes "you have to be careful," that some intervals don't match, and that Maya kings themselves fudged — which cuts both ways, since a system flexible enough to absorb royal fudging is flexible enough to absorb coincidence. The decipherment underlying the characters-document leg is unrefereed single-researcher work ([[The Characters Document Translated as Abbreviated Hieratic and Maya]]), and Grover knows the target chronology in advance — a textbook confirmation-bias setup. The honest residue is narrow: *if* a pre-registered, rule-bound statistical test (fixed sacred set, fixed combination rules, a null model of random year-skipping) showed the Book of Mormon's intervals exceeding chance, that would be a genuine datum; absent that, the claim is a provocative lead, tier-C, not demonstrated, and it "won't add anything to the doctrine" by Grover's own admission.

## Best Quotes
> "The year count that's going on there — Mormon is writing this — it's really his codex. It's a religious codex, and he's using the same techniques that they used in Mesoamerica to make it sacred."[^1]

> "You have intervals of seven, intervals of nine, intervals of ten... intervals of thirteen — again, sacred numbers — an interval of nineteen, intervals of twenty."[^3]

> "Have we just declared so many sacred numbers at some point that any number you could say, 'oh well that's just a divine combination of nine and thirteen and twenty'?" — host's objection; Grover: "Yeah, you have to be careful about it."[^6]

## LDS Connection
Offered as evidence the Book of Mormon "is not just something written in the 19th century" but matches an ancient Mesoamerican mode of composing a religious/divinatory text — a numerical companion to the literary-structure arguments in [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]] and to Grover's reformed-Egyptian decipherment in [[The Characters Document Translated as Abbreviated Hieratic and Maya]]. It also functions as a (contested) data point for a **Mesoamerican** geography, since its whole frame presupposes Maya calendrical culture — the opposite pole from the Heartland material in the vault. Like the chiasmus case, its force depends entirely on controlling for chance; the vault flags it tier-C accordingly.
