# The Padilla Plates and Related Stones Were Forged to Target Latter-day Saints

## The Claim

[[Jerry Grover]] and [[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]] argue that the **Padilla plates** (and the related "lock stone" and stele held by [[Jerry Ainsworth]]) are forgeries *specifically crafted to deceive Latter-day Saints* — they copy symbols from the Book of Mormon "characters document" and were fashioned to fit a Mesoamerican reading of the Book of Mormon. Unlike the [[The Michigan Relics Are 100 Percent Forgeries|Michigan Relics]] (a generic money-making scam), these are *uniquely pernicious* because they target believers.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Modern jeweler's saw marks on the metal — rate: strong (forensic)
The plates were given to a physician, Dr. Jesús Padilla Orozco, who claimed authenticity; BYU archaeologist Ray Matheny evaluated them and published that they are fraudulent. The metalwork shows marks "produced by a modern jeweler's fine-tooth saw" — modern tooling, not ancient manufacture.[^2]

### 2. They copy the characters document and Chichén Itzá art — rate: strong (design tell)
Some glyphs on the plates are lifted directly from the Book of Mormon "characters document," and the art is a hodgepodge ripped from Chichén Itzá — a deliberate attempt to look like a Mesoamerican Book-of-Mormon artifact. The forger was the stepson of the esteemed archaeologist Alfonso Caso, lending a false aura of credibility.[^3]

### 3. No provenance, never examined, tied to a known forger — rate: moderate (provenance)
The lock stone and stele (held by [[Jerry Ainsworth]] until his death, present whereabouts unknown) were never subjected to formal examination, lack provenance, and share lettering with the demonstrably fraudulent Padilla plates. Ainsworth's group reportedly tried to get the stele placed in the Mexico City Temple — a "flex." The parallel Brazilian "gold plates" hoax even bore a glyph from [[Mark Hofmann]]'s forged Anthon transcript, showing the forgers worked from internet images of known fakes.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Caution about *unexamined* objects cuts both ways. (a) Grover concedes the lock stone and stele have *not* actually been forensically tested — so "everything about this screams fake" is an *inference from association* (shared symbols, the Padilla link, missing provenance), not a demonstration; his own standard ("let us have them and examine them") implies the case is not yet closed on those two pieces. (b) The Grolier Codex precedent shows objects from private collections with poor provenance *can* prove genuine ([[Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance]]), so dismissal-by-association is fallible. (c) A defender could note that copying a real artifact's symbols is what a *competent* forger and an *authentic* derivative both might do — though here the modern saw marks on the plates settle the matter for the plates themselves. So the well-supported conclusion is narrow but firm: the *Padilla plates* are forgeries (modern tooling, BYU evaluation), and the associated stones are almost certainly from the same source and unusable — while intellectual honesty (which the panel models) admits the stones were never directly tested and could in principle be examined.
