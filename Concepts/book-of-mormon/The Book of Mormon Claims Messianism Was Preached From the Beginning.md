# The Book of Mormon Claims Messianism Was Preached From the Beginning

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]] argues that where the Old Testament barely whispers the Messiah, the Book of Mormon "kicks down the door like the Kool-Aid guy" and proclaims explicit, specific messianism *from its first pages* — six centuries before Christ — and even claims that **all** the prophets testified of the Messiah. This is exactly the bold, canon-contradicting claim that [[Bart Ehrman]]'s scholarship (per [[Ehrman Is Right That the Canon Lacks the Suffering Messiah]]) says *should* be impossible — and the Book of Mormon makes it the centerpiece of its self-understanding.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Book of Mormon front-loads explicit messianism — rate: strong (textual, internal)
From 1 Nephi onward the Nephite prophets name the Messiah, his virgin birth, his ministry, and his atoning death with a directness absent from the surviving Old Testament; [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25]] frames the whole record around being reconciled to Christ. Barnes: "the Book of Mormon from the first page starts spouting messianism… and scares the crap out of people who know the Old Testament."[^1] The contrast with the canon is the point.

### 2. Jacob: *none* of the prophets failed to testify of Messiah — rate: strong (canonical)
Barnes presses the maximal claim: "Jacob says there were no prophets save they did testify of the Messiah… he's not saying there was one prophecy you forgot — he says there *weren't* prophets but prophesied of the Messiah. It was a rash all over the ancient Israelite world."[^2] The Book of Mormon thus asserts pervasive pre-Christian messianism as a historical fact about Israel.

### 3. Lehi is nearly killed for preaching it — rate: moderate (narrative + corroboration)
The Jerusalem reaction to Lehi's messianic preaching is violent ([[1 Nephi]]), which Barnes glosses through the principle "you don't stone a prophet for nonsense; you stone him for the truth." A familiar, threatening claim provokes violence; an absurd one provokes dismissal. This connects to the vault's [[Prophets Were Killed for Preaching the Messiah Before 600 BC]] and the apocryphal corroboration in [[Apocryphal Prophet-Martyrdoms Corroborate the Book of Mormons Slain Messianic Prophets]].[^2]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's reply is the obvious one and it is forceful. (a) **An anachronism is the natural reading.** Eber D. Howe's original objection stands: detailed Christianity 600 years early is precisely what a 19th-century author steeped in the New Testament would retroject; the explicit messianism reads as *evidence of late composition*, not of suppressed antiquity. The very vividness Barnes celebrates is what critics cite as the giveaway. (b) **Circularity.** Using "the Book of Mormon says messianism was everywhere" to *prove* messianism was everywhere assumes the Book of Mormon's historicity — the point at issue. (c) **The maximal claim raises the bar.** Jacob's "no prophet but testified of Messiah" is a *stronger* historical assertion than the apocrypha support; the apocryphal texts (even granting early dates) show *some* suffering-Messiah expectation in *some* streams, not the universal, explicit, Christian-specific messianism the Book of Mormon asserts. The honest position: the Book of Mormon's claim is internally clear and *coherent with* a corruption thesis, and it gains modest external traction from genuinely-early pre-Christian messianic texts (the [[Testament of Naphtali Was Vindicated by the Dead Sea Scrolls|Naphtali]] case); but it does not, on its own, overturn the mainstream judgment that pre-exilic Israel lacked a developed suffering-Messiah doctrine, and it cannot be used as its own proof.
