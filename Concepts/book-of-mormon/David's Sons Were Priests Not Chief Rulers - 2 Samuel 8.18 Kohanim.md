# David's Sons Were Priests Not Chief Rulers - 2 Samuel 8.18 Kohanim

## The Claim

[[Mike Day]] argues that [[2 Samuel 8.18|2 Samuel 8:18]] preserves a buried datum that the KJV mistranslates: the Hebrew says [[David]]'s sons were *kohanim* — **priests** — not "chief rulers" as the King James Version renders it. David therefore ruled as **both king and priest**; he wears the ephod, leads the procession of the ark to establish the temple, and ordains his own sons to the priesthood. This king-and-priest office "until it wasn't" (until the Zadokites monopolized the priesthood) is exactly the office the Book of Mormon ascribes to Nephi, whose record is headed "his reign **and** ministry" — a one-word colophon flagging that Nephi, like David, is a king *and* a priest. The example also shows why a reader must go to the Hebrew: the plain English conceals the point.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Hebrew word is *kohanim*, "priests" (strong; lexical).** Where the KJV of 2 Samuel 8:18 reads "and David's sons were chief rulers," the Hebrew has *kohanim*. The same word everywhere else in the Hebrew Bible means "priest" (a *cohen*). The KJV's "chief rulers" is a smoothing that obscures the more startling claim that the king's sons held priestly office.[^2]

2. **The surrounding narrative shows David acting as high priest (strong; contextual).** In Samuel, David wears the **ephod** (the priestly vestment), leads the ark procession to the threshing floor where the temple will stand, and leads Israel in war, prayer, and liturgical acts. The "priest" reading of 8:18 fits a David the text already portrays in priestly roles, rather than contradicting it.[^2]

3. **Wellhausen reconstructs a king-priest office later narrowed to the Zadokites (moderate).** In *Prolegomena* (Day cites ch. 4, "Priests and Levites," pp. 60–73), [[Julius Wellhausen]] argues David "ruled as a king and a priest," and that this changed only once the Zadokites came to power and the [[Aaron|sons of Aaron]] functioned "like the pope." The royal priesthood is thus an *earlier* stratum overwritten by a later, more hierarchical priestly order — a [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins|standard documentary-hypothesis]] move.[^2]

4. **The Book of Mormon colophon independently encodes king-and-priest (rated: internal-textual; strong as description).** The header to [[1 Nephi 1.1|1 Nephi]] reads "An account of... his reign **and** his ministry" — "reign" (king) plus "ministry" (priest). Day treats this as a "breadcrumb" that Nephi holds the same fused office the Hebrew of 2 Samuel 8:18 attributes to David — a feature a reader would miss without knowing what a *cohen* is. This dovetails with the vault's [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology|pre-reform First Temple]] reading of Nephite religion.[^1]

**Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) The Chronicler's parallel (1 Chronicles 18:17) *itself* rephrases 8:18 as "the sons of David were chief about the king," so an ancient Israelite tradition already read the role as administrative, not cultic — the KJV is following a real interpretive trajectory, not simply blundering. (b) Some scholars read 2 Samuel 8:18 *kohanim* as a non-Levitical "royal priest/chief minister" role precisely because non-Levites holding the priesthood was theologically awkward later — i.e., the word may carry a broader courtly sense here. (c) The Nephi colophon is a chapter heading whose wording reflects the Book of Mormon's own editorial voice; "reign and ministry" need not be a deliberate echo of an Israelite king-priest title. Day presents the parallel as a suggestive "breadcrumb," not a proof.
