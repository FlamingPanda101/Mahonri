# Sea in the Book of Mormon Can Mean a Mighty River

## The Claim
[[Jonathan Neville]] argues that the Book of Mormon's "sea west," "sea east," etc. need not denote oceans: the Hebrew word *yam* ("sea") covers large rivers and even an artificial basin, so the "seas" of a North American geography can be the Mississippi and the Great Lakes rather than the Pacific and Atlantic — which are "too big" to fit the text's distances. He reports arriving at this via an "epiphany" while preparing a geography presentation and checking Strong's Concordance.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Strong's lexical range.** Strong's entry for *yam* (H3220) gives "the Mediterranean Sea... large river... and artificial basin," and Neville notes Isaiah and Nahum use the word for the **Nile**. He and the hosts also point to 2 Kings 16:17, where the temple's bronze "sea" (a basin) is called *yam* — so "sea" can mean a body of water from an ocean down to a font.[^1] *(Moderate — the lexical breadth is genuine; whether the Book of Mormon's translation "sea" reflects *yam* this way is an inference, since we lack the underlying text.)*
2. **"Sea west, south" implies more than one.** Neville reads a Book of Mormon phrase referencing the "sea west, south" as implying multiple "west seas," which fits a river-and-lakes system better than a single ocean coastline.[^1] *(Weak-to-moderate — turns on a particular parsing of the verse.)*
3. **The Mississippi as the "mighty river."** "What river is more mighty than the Mississippi?" The early settlers themselves likened the Mississippi drainage to the Nile (hence Memphis, Cairo, "Little Egypt" in Illinois) — and the Great Lakes, once far larger (perhaps 60 feet deeper), and a region the text calls "nearly surrounded by water," supply the needed bodies of water inland.[^2] *(Moderate — the toponym parallel is real local history; the geographic application remains a model, not a proof.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The lexical move is real but does heavy lifting. (a) We do not possess the Book of Mormon's source text, so invoking *yam* is an *assumption* about what English "sea" translates — the same kind of assumption Neville elsewhere cautions against (see [[Clarity Charity and Understanding and the Faith Model]]). (b) Even granting *yam*'s breadth, the Book of Mormon repeatedly describes crossing "many waters" / "the great deep" to *reach* the promised land by ship, which reads more naturally as an ocean; redefining the internal "seas" as rivers does not by itself relocate the whole geography. (c) Critics note this is a classic case of expanding a definition until it fits the desired map, which a Mesoamerican advocate would call special pleading (the Yucatán, by contrast, is flanked by actual seas — see [[The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate]]). The fair, narrower point survives: "sea" in ancient usage genuinely is broader than "ocean," so the objection that "North America has no seas in the right places" is not automatically fatal — though the lexical possibility falls short of establishing the Mississippi reading.

## Best Quotes
> "Strong's Concordance... one of the definitions it gives is a mighty river. And I thought, well, what river is more mighty than the Mississippi?"[^1]

> "We have to take our mindset away from the word 'sea' and apply it to their definition — it means river, it can be... any number of things."[^1]

## LDS Connection
The "sea" argument is the lexical key that, with the [[The New Madrid Earthquake Explains Third Nephi's Darkness Without Volcanoes|New Madrid earthquake]], makes a Mississippi-valley North American geography textually viable for Neville. It is also a clean illustration of his repeated insistence that "the text is not self-executing": the same word "sea" yields different maps depending on which lexical assumption the reader imports.
