#type/claim

# The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates

## The Claim

[[Josh Gailey]] takes the historiographical method [[Gary Habermas]] uses to defend the resurrection — the **minimal facts** argument — and applies it, unchanged, to the coming forth of the Book of Mormon.[^1] The method: establish a base of **early, independent, unsympathetic sources**; distill them into a short list of **core facts a critical scholar would also grant**; then run a **probability / best-explanation analysis** asking which hypothesis has the greatest explanatory scope and power with the least *ad hoc* contrivance.[^2] Gailey says he searched the literature (BYU Studies and others) for anyone who had crossed from "the witnesses are impressive" to "this is positive evidence for a living miracle like the resurrection," found it was "open space," and stepped in.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. It is a borrowed, pre-existing method — not a bespoke LDS rulebook
The probability analysis comes from historians of logic such as **C. B. McCullagh**; Gailey insists "it's not Josh's rulebook," he is "just applying it within that method that's already been established."[^4] This shifts the debate from "Mormons special-plead" to "do the agreed criteria, applied evenhandedly, license the conclusion?"
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a framing move (uses the critic's own accepted tools).

### 2. A short list of facts even a skeptic should grant
Setting God, angels, and miracles aside, Gailey proposes core facts: the Book of Mormon exists (239 chapters / ~608 manuscript pages that "didn't poof into existence ex nihilo"); many people **sincerely believed** they saw the plates; there was likely an **empty stone box** in a particular hill.[^5] These mirror the resurrection's minimal facts (sincere belief unto death, the empty tomb) and are meant to be the agreed starting line, not the conclusion. See [[The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the *existence* facts are uncontested; "sincere belief" and "empty box" carry the real weight and are contested in degree).

### 3. The conclusion is reached by inference to the best explanation
Run the probability analysis and, Gailey argues, the least-contrived hypothesis covering all the data is that there really was a hole and an artifact of golden plates in the hill, taken up and translated. He claims to have read "every published critique" and found no rival hypothesis that handles the full data set.[^6] This dovetails with the witness-focused [[Inference to the Best Explanation for the Witnesses]] and the modest-conclusion posture of [[Humes Maxim Applied to the Gold Plates]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (IBE is a legitimate virtue; "no rival explains it" is a strong empirical claim that depends on one's prior for the supernatural).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The method's leverage on the resurrection comes from facts granted by *secular New Testament scholars*; there is no comparable guild of non-LDS historians who have vetted and granted a "minimal facts" list for the plates, so "a critical scholar would agree" is doing unearned work. A Bayesian with a low prior for miracles can accept every minimal fact (a book exists, people sincerely believed, a hole existed) and still rate a poorly-evidenced naturalistic story — sincere self-deception around a crafted or imagined object — more probable than a divine artifact, because "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence." On this view the method does not fail so much as *underdetermine*: it gets you to "something happened that the witnesses sincerely reported," not to gold plates. Gailey's reply is that the naturalistic alternatives on offer (tin plates, printing plates) are themselves source-less and *ad hoc*, so the supernatural hypothesis wins on the method's own contrivance criterion — but the believer must concede the conclusion is only as strong as the prior one brings to it.

## Best Quotes

> "Books don't just poof into existence ex nihilo, as I like to teach my students. It came from somewhere. Somebody wrote it."[^5]

> "It's not Josh's rulebook... this is looking at the logic of history from historians of logic like B. H. McCullagh. We're just applying it within that method that's already been established."[^4]

## LDS Connection

The argument is deliberately ecumenical: it asks Christians who accept the resurrection on Habermas-style grounds to apply the *same* method to the Restoration. For Latter-day Saints the upshot is that the gold plates are not a "take Joseph's word for it" claim but a historically argued one, resting on the converging documentary record catalogued across the witnesses cluster (e.g., [[All Three Witnesses Upheld Their Testimony for Life]], [[Witnesses Paid a Real Cost With No Payoff]]) and crowned by the empty stone box.
