# Apocryphal Prophet-Martyrdoms Corroborate the Book of Mormons Slain Messianic Prophets

#type/claim

## The Claim
On [[Ward Radio - There's No Way Joseph Smith Could've Known About This|Ward Radio]], [[Jonah Barnes]] argues that a body of apocryphal "prophet-martyrdom" traditions — pre-eminently the stoning of [[Jeremiah]] in the **Paraleipomena of Jeremiah (4 Baruch)** and related Baruch literature, alongside the broader *Lives of the Prophets* pattern of prophets killed for their message — corroborates the Book of Mormon's repeated portrait of **slain messianic prophets** opposed by the religious establishment. The Book of Mormon places Jeremiah in Lehi's own world (his words are on the brass plates; [[Helaman 8.7|Helaman 8]] cites Jeremiah prophesying Jerusalem's fall) and depicts a recurring cycle of prophets — Lehi, Abinadi, Samuel — who proclaim a coming divine Messiah and are persecuted or killed. Because the relevant apocryphal manuscripts surfaced/were published long after 1830, the convergence is offered as evidence the Book of Mormon preserves authentic pre-exilic material rather than reflecting Joseph Smith's environment. [[Brad Whitbeck]] supplies the steelman caution throughout.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The canonical Jeremiah really was persecuted, and his scroll was burned.** Jeremiah was imprisoned and put in stocks, opposed by false prophets, and King Jehoiakim cut and **burned his prophecy** ([[Jeremiah 36.31|Jeremiah 36]]). The Book of Mormon's picture of a contemporary, hounded Jeremiah whose words a household risked recording is consistent with the biblical record itself — "like having BBC Radio in German-occupied France." *Rating: strong on the canonical core; the persecution and scroll-burning are in the text.*
2. **Joseph Smith plausibly lacked access to the martyrdom apocrypha.** 4 Baruch / Paraleipomena of Jeremiah and the relevant Baruch apocalypses were not available in English in 1829–30; Barnes dates the manuscripts he cites to the 1880s. Straightforward textual borrowing is therefore excluded. *Rating: moderate-to-strong, with a real caveat — the traditions of Jeremiah's death by stoning circulated more widely than any single late manuscript.* See [[Jeremiahs Secret-Death Vision Parallels Nephis Tree of Life]].
3. **The "slain messianic prophet" is a structural Book of Mormon pattern.** Abinadi burned for proclaiming a suffering, divine Messiah; Lehi mocked and threatened for the same; Samuel shot at on the wall — the book independently reproduces the killed-prophet motif that the apocryphal *Lives of the Prophets* tradition assigns to Isaiah, Jeremiah, and others. *Rating: moderate; a thematic convergence, strongest when paired with the no-access point.* Compare [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]].
4. **It fits the Deuteronomist-reform frame.** If [[Josiah]]'s reform suppressed the pre-existent divine Messiah, then prophets who kept preaching him would be exactly the ones persecuted and edited out — which is the role Jeremiah, Lehi, and Isaiah play in this telling. *Rating: coherent within the thesis; the thesis itself is contested.* See [[The Deuteronomists Edited Out the Pre-Existent Divine Messiah]].

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** [[Brad Whitbeck]]'s own cautions are decisive. (a) The martyrdom apocrypha are **late, fluid pseudepigrapha**; 4 Baruch survives in a Christianized form (commonly dated 1st–2nd c. AD with later interpolations), so the messianic and persecution coloring may be *Christian* embellishment, not a recovered pre-exilic datum. (b) **Parallels are cheap without textual analysis** — "prophets get killed for unpopular messages" is a near-universal religious trope and the weakest evidentiary tier (the vault's standing caution, [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]]). (c) Whitbeck explicitly warns against the conspiratorial "the Deuteronomists deleted the Messiah and martyred its prophets" frame, urging that Josiah's priests "may have been doing their best to follow God." **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the claim is meant as one strand in a *cumulative* case, not a stand-alone proof — the force is the conjunction of (i) a genuinely persecuted canonical Jeremiah, (ii) Joseph's lack of access to the later martyrdom texts, and (iii) the book's independent reproduction of the pattern. Held at that altitude — "suggestive corroboration, not demonstration" — it survives the steelman.
