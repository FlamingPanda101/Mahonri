# The Alma Fainting Episodes Are a Ritual Death and Rebirth Ordinance

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dave Butler|David Butler]] argues that the cluster of "fainting" stories in Alma 17-36 — Lamoni, Lamoni's wife, Lamoni's father, Ammon, and Alma all having essentially the *same* experience — is not a series of one-off ecstatic episodes but a single **ordinance of ritual death and rebirth**, communicated from multiple points of view. Each follows the same template: taught the same **four things** (the creation including Adam, the fall, the plan of redemption, the coming of Christ); a **cry for mercy**; **fainting / "taken for dead"**; **seeing Jesus**; and **rising**. Read against the tabernacle journey, this is the *second-room* pattern: brought to (raised) as the Lord descends, clothed in the **robe of righteousness** ([[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9:14]]), and brought forward to the feast — with the spiritual gift of **hope** (confidence in eternal life / the resurrection). This resolves the long-standing puzzle of "why does everyone keep passing out in Alma?" See [[Nahom Is the Tabernacle Room of Mourning and Comfort]].[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The same four-part teaching recurs verbatim.** Ammon teaches Lamoni "the creation including the creation of Adam, the fall, the plan of redemption, and the coming of Christ"; Aaron teaches Lamoni's father "the same exact four things" five chapters later. Repetition of a fixed catechetical sequence across distinct episodes signals a *pattern*, not coincidence. *Rating: the four-part content does recur across Alma 18-22; a genuine structural observation.*[^1]
2. **Cry for mercy → faint → taken for dead → see Jesus → rise.** Across [[Alma 19.16|Alma 19]] (Lamoni and the queen) and the king's conversion, the converts cry for mercy, fall as if dead, and on rising "seem to have seen Jesus" (Lamoni says so; the king preaches as one who has). The whole cluster repeats it "basically four times" — "that's not somebody's ecstatic experience, that's an ordinance." *Rating: the faint/taken-for-dead/vision pattern is textual; the "ordinance" reading is the inference.*[^2]
3. **Alma's first-person version quotes Lehi's throne-vision.** [[Alma 36.1|Alma 36]] gives the *subjective* account: Alma passes out, cries for mercy, is revived — and "didn't just see Jesus, he saw Lehi's vision," quoting "22 words consecutively" of Christ on the throne with the one and the twelve descending. So we get the rite from outside (Lamoni, queen, father) and inside (Alma). *Rating: Alma 36's verbal overlap with 1 Nephi 1's throne-theophany is real; strengthens the patterned reading.*[^2]
4. **Death-and-rebirth with the gift of hope.** Butler ties it to the robe of righteousness = the resurrected body ([[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9]]) and the spiritual gift of **hope** ("the hope of entering into the holy of holies and sitting down with the fathers"). Coheres with [[Early Christian Baptism as Death and Rebirth]] and [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]]. *Rating: a synthesis with the vault's temple/baptism cluster; the hope-as-second-room-gift mapping is interpretive.*[^3]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The Alma conversions read naturally as *dramatic but ordinary conversion experiences* — overwhelmed by guilt, the hearers collapse and revive transformed — a phenomenology well attested in revival history without any ritual coding. The shared four-part teaching is simply *the missionary message* (creation, fall, redemption, Christ), which one would expect to recur because it is *the gospel being preached*, not a secret rite. Reading "robe of righteousness" and "hope" as second-room temple furniture imports the endowment into narrative conversions, and "Alma quotes Lehi's vision" shows literary artistry, not liturgy. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the force is in the *repetition of a fixed sequence* (same catechism → mercy-cry → death-state → theophany → rebirth) across five characters told from both outside and inside — more structured than spontaneous revival fainting — and the convergence with the tabernacle journey's mourning-to-joy room and the robe/hope cluster suggests an intended ordinance pattern; offered as the *best unifying* explanation of an otherwise "weird" feature, held provisionally per [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]].
