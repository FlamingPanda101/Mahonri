# The Surviving Old Testament Is the Southern Kingdom's Edition

## The Point
"Jewish" and "Israelite" are not synonyms. Around 930 BC Israel split into a northern kingdom (Israel/Ephraim, centered on Shechem and Bethel) and a southern kingdom (Judah, centered on Jerusalem and the temple under a Davidic monarchy). Assyria destroyed the north in 722 BC; its scribal and priestly class largely vanished. Judah survived — "and then they wrote the history, and they shaped the canon." So "what we call the Old Testament today reflects in a very large part the theological perspective of the tribe of Judah, just Judah," a perspective that only became dominant *after* the north was gone and could not offer a competing account [^1][^2]. This is the foundation for measuring the Book of Mormon against the *right* tradition.

## Evidence
1. **Mainstream consensus, named scholars.** [[Frank Moore Cross]] at Harvard mapped this as the **Deuteronomistic history** (Deuteronomy through 2 Kings) — an edited theological narrative reflecting "a specific scribal agenda rather than a straightforward historical record." [[Martin Noth]] established the foundational argument in 1943; [[Michael Heiser]] has written on how the surviving text was shaped by that process [^3][^4].
2. **The editorial fingerprints are consistent.** The text "consistently and repeatedly rewards centralized Jerusalem worship, condemns every northern king using the same formulaic language regardless of… their actual deeds, and… interprets every military defeat as punishment for straying from Jerusalem-centered theology." The pattern is "too consistent and too politically convenient to be merely coincidental" [^3].
3. **Josiah's 621 BC reform is the concentrated episode.** A scroll "conveniently discovered" during temple renovations gave [[Josiah]] authority for a program he had already decided to run: local sacred sites destroyed, northern priestly traditions delegitimized, and prophetic traditions emphasizing direct personal encounter with God "progressively pushed to the margins" [^5].
4. **The yardstick is the point.** "When a critic picks up the Bible assembled by the southern kingdom… and then uses it to measure the Book of Mormon and concludes that it doesn't match, they're right. It doesn't match. But the question that they haven't thought to ask is whether it was ever supposed to match." [^6]

## LDS Connection
If the canonical Old Testament is a Judahite redaction, then the Book of Mormon's divergence from it is not evidence of fraud but the expected signature of a *northern* record (see [[Lehi Was of Manasseh So the Book of Mormon Is a Northern Israelite Record]]). This dovetails with the vault's existing treatment of the [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology|Deuteronomistic reform]] and [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|textual editing]], and overlaps with the academic-side claim that Judah retrofitted northern peoplehood traditions (see [[Israel and Judah Never United]]). Steelman to keep in view: the "Judah edited everything" framing is a strong reading of the Deuteronomistic-history thesis, and the degree of suppression (versus ordinary redaction) is debated in the field.
