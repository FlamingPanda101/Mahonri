# The Cologne Mani Codex Parallels Moroni and the Brass Plates

## The Claim

The **Cologne Mani Codex** — a fourth-/fifth-century Greek parchment codex about the life of the prophet [[Mani]], discovered in 1969 and only later successfully opened and read — contains a short section called the **Apocalypse of Enosh** in which an **angel** commands a prophet to write hidden revelation **upon plates of brass**, **hide them in the wilderness**, and reserve them for a future unveiling "to all the brethren." [[Jonah Barnes]] (on [[Cardon Ellis]]'s **Ward Radio**) argues this is "a couple synonyms away from the story of [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]]" and the theophany of [[Nephi]]: a buried-record / angelic-revelation / sealed-future-book motif appearing independently in a non-LDS ancient manuscript, translated by a non-member scholar ([[John C. Reeves]]) long after Joseph Smith, and therefore impossible to dismiss as a believer's invention.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Greek wording (steelman: the resemblance is in the text, not just the gist).** The passage reads *tauta de panta ta apokrypha … graphēn epi ptychas chalkas … apothou en … erēmon*. Barnes parses three load-bearing terms: *apokrypha* = "hidden writings"; *graphēn epi ptychas chalkas* = "writing upon plates of brass"; *apothou … erēmon* = "put them away / hide them in the wilderness." So the resemblance is lexical (hidden writings + brass plates + concealment in the wilderness), not merely a vague thematic echo.[^2]

2. **An angel is the commissioning agent.** In Reeves' rendering the speaker is an angel who takes the prophet "to the northern region … immense mountains" and says, "The preeminent almighty one has sent me to you that I might reveal to you the secret things … write down these hidden things upon … bronze plates and deposit them in the wilderness … for my revelation which shall not pass away … until my revelation is ready to be revealed to all the brethren." Barnes notes this matches the *theophany* pattern of [[1 Nephi 1.8|1 Nephi 1]] — angelic messenger, mountain vision, command to record.[^3]

3. **Independent, hostile-to-neutral provenance.** The translation is by **[[John C. Reeves]]** in *Heralds of That Golden Realm*, p. 142 — "non-member professional Greek experts," and the codex itself was discovered in 1969 and translated "long after Joseph is gone." The argument from authenticity: a forger working in 1830 had no access to a manuscript not opened until the late twentieth century, and the passage advances *no doctrine* that a believer would want to plant — it merely narrates the same motif "in passing."[^4]

4. **A precedent for vindication-by-discovery.** Barnes leans on the **Book of the Giants**: named in Mani's own canon, known of for centuries but unrecovered until it surfaced among the Dead Sea Scrolls (see [[Book of Enoch - Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation]] for the analogous Enochic case). The Manichaean corpus thus has a track record of texts dismissed and later confirmed by manuscript finds — so dismissing the Enosh parallel out of hand begs the question.[^5]

5. **The "it's just Gnostic" deflection is not an argument.** Barnes' methodological point: the moment critics see "hidden / apocryphal," they file the text under the catch-all label "Gnostic" and treat it as a settled dustbin, substituting a category for an examination of the words. Stripped of that reflex, the parallel deserves to be weighed on its lexical merits.[^6]

> **Counter-weight (for honesty).** Taken to the level of *demonstration* rather than resemblance, the claim is vulnerable to the same discipline the vault applies elsewhere: motif-level overlaps (angel + metal plates + concealment + future revelation) are widespread "hidden-book" topoi and do not establish literary dependence without textual analysis (see [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]] and [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]]). The codex postdates Lehi by ~900 years, so any claim is one of *convergent motif* — corroborating that the buried-brass-plates pattern was at home in the ancient Near East / late antiquity — not of a common source. The hosts themselves frame it as suggestive ("there's got to be something there") rather than probative.
