# A Curse Is a Separation From God Not a Skin Color

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The objection is that the Book of Mormon is racist because the "curse of the Lamanites" links a divine **curse** to dark **skin** (2 Nephi 5:21, "a skin of blackness... because of their iniquity"). [[Marvin Perkins]] answers that the objection imports a modern racial meaning onto a word the scriptures use for something else entirely: a **curse** is a *separation from God brought on by individual sin*, and the "skin of blackness" language is the same idiom-for-gloom that runs through the whole biblical corpus. The curse and the "skin" are two different misreadings stacked on one verse, and removing either collapses the racial reading [^1].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The scriptural definition of *curse* is separation, not pigment — rate: moderate-to-strong (textual, cross-canon)
Perkins notes the dictionary lists fifteen senses of *curse*, including ecclesiastical censure / anathema (a "driving away"). The scriptural pattern consistently means **loss of access to God** — His Spirit, temple, and presence — because of sin [^2]:
- **D&C 29:41** — Adam was "cast out from the Garden of Eden, from my presence... wherein he became spiritually dead."
- **Moses 5** — Cain is "driven out... from the face of the Lord."
- **Alma 3:18-19** — the Amlicites "had come out in open rebellion against God; therefore it was expedient that the curse, *or the separation*, should fall upon them... every man that is cursed brings upon himself his own condemnation."

The clincher is that the curse is **individual and revocable**: a whole group is never cursed as a class ("every man... brings it upon himself"), and the [[Anti-Nephi-Lehi|Anti-Nephi-Lehies]] have "the curse of God did no more follow them" the moment they live the gospel and draw near the Lord — proving the curse was relational distance, not melanin [^3].

### 2. The "skin of blackness" is the biblical idiom for gloom — rate: moderate (footnote-anchored)
Perkins surveys every use of *black* applied to a person (eight in the OT/NT) — Job 30:30, Jeremiah 8:21, Jeremiah 14:2, Lamentations 4:8, Joel 2:6, Nahum 2:10 — and shows the KJV footnotes gloss the Hebrew as "gloomy," "dejected," and "blackness = gloom," paralleling the anguished, mourning Job [^4]. The 1981 LDS edition then added footnotes on the Book of Mormon "skin"/"scales" passages redirecting to the Topical Guide "Darkness, spiritual" and "spiritual blindness." See [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]] and [[White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning]] [^5].

### 3. Judgment-day imagery cannot be literal skin — rate: moderate
Jacob (son of Lehi) warns the Nephites that unless they repent "their skins will be whiter than yours... when you shall be brought with them before the throne of God" (Jacob 3:8). Since the judgment weighs righteousness, not appearance, the "skins" being compared *at the bar of God* must be spiritual purity, not pigment — reinforced by 1 Samuel 16:7 ("the Lord looketh on the heart") [^6].

### 4. External LDS anchors — rate: moderate (denominational authority)
Perkins quotes [[David O. McKay]] ("there is not now and there has never been a doctrine in this church that Negroes are under a divine curse") and the Second Article of Faith ("men will be punished for their own sins, and not for Adam's transgression"), arguing both are incompatible with a hereditary racial curse and align with the curse-as-individual-separation reading [^7].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The verses *do* exist, and they *were* read racially for well over a century — by LDS prophets and apostles themselves, who used them to justify the priesthood and temple ban (see [[The Priesthood Ban Was the Development Not Its Reversal]]). The 1981 footnotes and the *white → pure* edit (2 Nephi 30:6) postdate the 1978 revelation and track the lifting of the ban, which is exactly what one expects of a human document updating to its audience rather than recovering an original sense (see [[White to Pure Edit Tracks the Priesthood Ban Not Revelation]]). So the curse-as-separation reading, while textually defensible, is a *particular* interpretation that the Church arrived at late, not the plain or unanimous historic reading — and Perkins concedes as much when he says earlier Saints "cherry-picked" the verses and applied them to the priesthood restriction. The honest form of the argument is that the racial reading was a mistake the text does not *require*, not that no Latter-day Saint ever held it.

## Best Quotes
