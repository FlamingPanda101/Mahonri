# The Three Types of Anachronism — Verified, Alleged, and Reversed

## The Claim

Not all "anachronisms" are equal, and lumping them together is the rhetorical sleight that powers the standard Book of Mormon attack. By His Fruits proposes a three-part taxonomy: an anachronism is **verified** (demonstrably out of period), **alleged** (looks out of place on present evidence but unproven), or **reversed** (once alleged, since confirmed to be correct for the period). The critic's case treats *every* Book of Mormon item as if it were verified; in fact the historically contested items are *alleged*, and archaeology keeps moving them into the *reversed* column.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Verified anachronism — the genuine, settled error
The presenter's illustration: a (fictional) 1781 message in which Washington receives a "telegram" from Cornwallis. It is verified-anachronistic because the telegraph did not exist until the 1830s. This is what a *real* anachronism looks like — something that could not possibly belong to its claimed time.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a definition (a clear, uncontested example of the category).

### 2. Alleged anachronism — unproven, evidence-dependent
A 1911 critic objected that the Book of Mormon has Nephi kill Laban (~600 BC) with "a sword of the most precious steel," "hundreds of years before steel was known to man" ([[1 Nephi 4.9|1 Nephi 4:9]]). At the time this looked out of place — but it was an *allegation*, contingent on the then-current state of archaeology, not a demonstrated impossibility.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong — the steel objection is correctly classified as evidence-dependent rather than settled.

### 3. Reversed anachronism — the allegation overturned by new evidence
The same steel objection is now reversed: an iron, carburized-steel sword dated to ~600 BC was excavated at Jericho in 1982 (see [[The Jericho Steel Sword Reverses the Nephi Steel Anachronism]]). Likewise the cement objection ([[Helaman 3.7|Helaman 3:7]]) was reversed by confirmed pre-Columbian cement use ([[Nephite Cement Construction Was Confirmed at Teotihuacan]]). The taxonomy's payoff is its *direction*: items keep migrating from "alleged" to "reversed," which is the opposite of what a fabrication should produce.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate–strong — the framework is sound; its force depends on the empirical claim (developed in the linked notes) that reversals genuinely outnumber new problems.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic can grant the taxonomy and still resist the inference. (a) The "reversed" label can smuggle in *post-hoc accommodation*: an allegation is sometimes "reversed" merely because the thing is shown to have existed *somewhere* in the ancient world, not in the specific Mesoamerican time and place the text requires. (b) The most-pressed items — horses, chariots, large steel industry, a confirmed Nephite city — remain alleged, and a defender can quietly reclassify the easy ones while the hard ones stay put. (c) Categorizing is not quantifying: the three-bucket scheme by itself says nothing about the *rate* of reversal, which is the actual argument (made separately via [[Matt Roper]]'s inventory — see [[The Anachronism Reversal Rate Trends Toward the Book of Mormon Over Time]]). The honest claim is therefore not "the taxonomy proves authenticity" but "the taxonomy exposes that the critic's items are mostly *alleged*, and the trend line on those allegations runs the wrong way for the fraud thesis."

## Best Quotes

> "There's basically three kinds of anachronisms. First of all, you have the verified anachronism. That means we know it's been proven it's in the wrong time period." [^1]

> "Number two is alleged anacronism. Meaning... based on archaeological discoveries it appears to be an anachronism... but it it's alleged." [^2]

> "Third, you have the reversed anachronism. That's one that's been reversed. It's been proven correct in that time period." [^3]
