# Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection (the Christian critic):** A book revealed by God should not require
editing. If [[Joseph Smith]] translated the gold plates "by the power of God" —
a supernatural, revelatory process, not a human translation — then how can the
text be changed at all? Why a change in 1840 and a change *back* in 1841? Any
post-publication edit is taken as evidence the book is a human product.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The disagreement is really about a model of revelation

Saints Unscripted's central move: "We seem to understand revelation
differently." On the LDS model, revelation must still be **captured in human
words**, and "no human, including Joseph Smith, does that perfectly." What
matters most is "the spirit of the message more than the specific words chosen to
convey it."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Conceptual/theological. This is a coherent dynamic-revelation
> model, not an empirical proof; its force depends on whether one already grants a
> spirit-over-letter view of inspiration.

### 2. Even the Bible's textual history fits this model

(Implicit and by analogy from the broader tradition.) Scripture in every
tradition has a transmission and revision history — translations are revised,
manuscripts differ — without believers concluding the underlying revelation is
false. Holding the Book of Mormon to a stricter "never a single change" standard
is a special pleading the critic does not apply to the Bible.

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (tu quoque / parity argument; rhetorically strong
> but does not by itself establish divine origin).

### 3. The "1840 then back in 1841" point is reframed, not denied

The video does not dispute that wording changed and partly reverted; it argues
the underlying meaning is stable (e.g., *white*/*pure* are near-synonyms) so the
back-and-forth is editorial polishing, not doctrinal flip-flopping. See
[[White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's strongest form: Joseph's own translation **narrative** (per
[[David Whitmer]]) describes words appearing on the stone that had to be exactly
right before they would disappear — a "tight control" account. If that is true,
the *words themselves* were divinely fixed, so changing them later is not "better
capturing the spirit"; it is correcting God. The dynamic-revelation defense works
only by quietly abandoning the tight-control translation story that many Latter-day
Saints also cite as faith-promoting. This is a real tension, not a
misunderstanding. See [[David Whitmer Tight Control Account vs the Participatory Translation Model]].

## Best Quotes

> "The concern is about how a book revealed by God can be edited at all. How can
> something from God go through change? And it really comes down to how we
> understand revelation."[^3]

> "Revelation still has to be captured in human words, and no human, including
> Joseph Smith, does that perfectly. What matters most is the spirit of the
> message more than the specific words chosen to convey it."[^2]

> "So, my question is if the translation was miraculous and supernatural, why the
> change in 1840? Why the change back in 1841?" — the critic[^1]
