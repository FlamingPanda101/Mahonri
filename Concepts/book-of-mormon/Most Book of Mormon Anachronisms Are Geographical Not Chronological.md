# Most Book of Mormon Anachronisms Are Geographical Not Chronological

## The Claim

[[Ether's Elephant]] argues that the "Book of Mormon is full of anachronisms" critique misapplies the word. By the dictionary definition — an anachronism is "a thing belonging or appropriate to a period other than that in which it exists" — a true anachronism is out of place **in time** (Abraham Lincoln with a cellphone). But the contested Book of Mormon items (horses, steel, etc.) *did* exist in the relevant era; the only question is whether they existed in the right **place**. So they are better called "geographical inconsistencies," a materially weaker objection.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The definitional point.** "These items did exist during the time the Book of Mormon is reported to be written. The real question is if they existed in that geographical area at that time... so a more appropriate way to refer to these would be geographical inconsistencies."[^1] *Rating: largely correct as a definition; for many items (horses, elephants, metals) the dispute is indeed about New-World presence in the period, not about whether the thing existed anywhere then.*

2. **Relabeling lowers the stakes.** A genuine anachronism (a 19th-century object in an ancient text) is close to decisive evidence of fabrication; a "wrong geography" claim is contingent on what archaeology has and hasn't recovered. The video concedes it remains "a criticism" but "a lot weaker than you might think."[^2] *Rating: sound — the evidentiary force of the two categories genuinely differs.*

3. **~2% excavated; absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.** "Experts tell us that we've only looked at about 2% of the Americas... 98% is not discovered yet," and not finding elephant bones in the period "doesn't mean there weren't elephants."[^3] *Rating: the "2%" figure is a common apologetic estimate (use with care), but the underlying point — that New-World archaeology is radically incomplete — is fair.* See [[Only Two Percent of the Americas Has Been Excavated]] and [[Absence of Evidence Is Not Evidence of Absence in New World Archaeology]].

4. **A KJV-era translation may use non-literal vocabulary.** Because the book "was translated... using the language of the King James Bible," some terms "shouldn't be taken as literal as people take them" — a translator may render an unfamiliar ancient animal/material with the nearest familiar English word. The title page's "if there are faults they are the mistakes of men" is invoked.[^4] *Rating: this is the strongest sub-point for genuinely contested terms (e.g. "horse," "steel"), and it is developed more rigorously in the vault's translation notes.* See [[Translation Introduces Anachronism Even in Divine Translation]] and [[Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism]].

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic answers that the relabeling concedes more than it gains. (a) **Geography is still falsifying:** "wrong place" is not a free pass — the Book of Mormon makes positive claims about a specific New-World setting, and the sustained *absence* of horses, wheeled vehicles in use, Old-World domesticates, steel, and pre-Columbian Hebrew/Egyptian across a heavily-excavated Mesoamerica is exactly what one expects if the items are not there; "only 2% excavated" understates that the relevant cultures *have* been studied intensively and the expected remains (e.g. horse bones in occupation layers) are conspicuously missing. (b) **Selective literalism:** the translation-vocabulary defense ("maybe 'horse' means tapir") can rescue *any* failed prediction, which makes it [[One Anachronism Does Not Disprove the Book of Mormon|unfalsifiable]] if applied across the board — and it sits in tension with the apologetic *elsewhere* that treats confirmed items as precise predictive "hits." (c) **The pattern, not the item:** critics like [[Bill Reel]] press that it is the *collective* cluster of out-of-place items, not any single one, that is probative (see [[Book of Mormon Anachronisms Are a Collective Pattern - Reel]]). The honest LDS position concedes (a)/(b) for the genuinely open items and rests on the *trend* — the list is shrinking as data accrue (see [[The Anachronism List Is Trending Toward the Book of Mormon]] and [[Shrinking Anachronism List as Evidence of Authenticity]]) — rather than on a definitional reclassification alone.

## Best Quotes

> "These weren't out of place in time. They're out of place in geography. So a more appropriate way to refer to these would be geographical inconsistencies."[^1]

> "We've only looked at about 2% of the Americas as far as archaeology is concerned, meaning 98% of what is out there is not discovered yet."[^3]

> "It was a translation into a modern tongue using the language of the King James Bible... so it's totally possible that some of these words shouldn't be taken as literal as people take them."[^4]
