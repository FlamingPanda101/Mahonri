# The Neanderthal DNA Disanalogy

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

The popular jab — "if scientists can recover DNA from 70,000-year-old Neanderthals, surely they could find Israelite DNA from 2,000 years ago" — is an apples-to-oranges comparison. The Neanderthal case works because the remains are first *identified as Neanderthal* by anthropology and *then* sequenced; for the Book of Mormon there is no identifiable Nephite body to sequence, and "Nephite/Lamanite" are cultural-political terms, not detectable biological categories.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Identification precedes sequencing

Following [[John Butler (DNA scientist)|John Butler]] and [[Ugo Perego]], the Neanderthal workflow is: anthropological tools establish "these are Neanderthal remains" (versus some other hominid), *after which* DNA is extracted using those bones as a reference point. We have no exhumable Lamanite or Nephite bodies to serve as that anchor — "we don't have their bodies that we can exhume and say, okay, here is Nephite DNA."[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (correctly states the method; identification is logically prior to sequencing).

### 2. "Neanderthal" is anthropological; "Nephite" is cultural

Neanderthal is an anthropological category detectable by specific physiological and genetic traits. By contrast, after just a few generations "Nephite" and "Lamanite" are **cultural and political** labels, not ethnic or anthropological ones — so there is nothing biologically distinctive to detect even in principle. (This dovetails with [[No DNA Evidence Requires Specifying What Signature to Expect]]: you cannot search for a signature that is not biologically defined.)[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the cultural/biological distinction is sound and textually grounded).

### 3. Recency does not guarantee data

[[Neal Rappleye]] adds a historian's caveat: more recent does not mean better attested. The 14th-century-BC Levant is richly documented (the Amarna letters — ~300 royal letters), yet the 10th century BC (David and Solomon's era), though *more* recent, is comparatively dark archaeologically. Data comes in pockets; the same patchiness afflicts ancient DNA, and the New World's humid climate and the [[The Genetic Bottleneck Compounds the Loss of a Founding Signal|demographic and political]] barriers to sampling indigenous remains make preservation and access far worse than in, say, Egypt.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the Amarna vs. Solomon contrast is a clean illustration).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can reply that the disanalogy still leaves a real expectation: we need not identify "Nephite" DNA as such — genome-wide methods could in principle flag any *Near-Eastern autosomal* ancestry in pre-Columbian American remains, independent of cultural labels, just as the Neanderthal admixture in modern humans is detected without exhuming a labeled Neanderthal. The reply: that requires securely dated, securely located founder-era American remains to test, which we lack (we do not know where Lehi landed), and the [[Founder Effect and Genetic Drift Erase a Small Founding Signal|founder effect, drift]], and host admixture mean even a genome-wide scan may have nothing distinctive to find. There are also genuine ethical and political barriers — the Kennewick Man controversy and indigenous claims over sacred remains — that constrain the very sampling the objection assumes is trivial.

## Best Quotes

> "With the Neanderthal example, you first identify them as Neanderthals... and then after that you can do your DNA sequencing. We do not have any Lamanites or Nephites around. We don't have their bodies that we can exhume."[^2]

> "Nephite and Lamanite, after just a few generations, these are cultural and political terms. They're not strictly anthropological or ethnic terms. So you can't really compare it in that way either."[^3]

> "In the late Bronze Age... we have a ton of data thanks to the Amarna letters. Fast forward three centuries to the 10th century, about the time of David and Solomon, and it's a lot less clear... even though that's technically more recent."[^4]
