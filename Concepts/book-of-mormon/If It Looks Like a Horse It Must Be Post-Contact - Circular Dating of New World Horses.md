# If It Looks Like a Horse It Must Be Post-Contact - Circular Dating of New World Horses

## The Claim

The strongest reason "no pre-Columbian horses" looks airtight, [[Daniel Johnson]] and [[Kirk Magleby]] argue, is that the conclusion is **built into the method**. When a horse-like image or bone is found in an ambiguous context, the consensus that horses are post-Columbian is used to *date* it as post-contact — and any pre-contact context is taken to prove the image *cannot* depict a horse. The dating and the conclusion thus guarantee each other, so the paradigm can never be falsified by the very evidence that would count against it. This is offered not as proof of horses but as a defeater for the *confidence* of the anachronism objection.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. The petroglyph dilemma stated in print

Looking at Black Mesa petroglyphs (Cimarron County, Oklahoma) that plainly show a horse, researchers say "Oh, that's a horse. It must be post conquest." Johnson reports reading the inference *explicitly* in scientific papers: **"if it looks like a horse then it must be post contact,"** and conversely "if it's pre-contact, it can't be a horse." The mere existence of a horse image is treated as proof of post-contact date — a textbook circle.[^2]

### 2. The "stratigraphic mixing" escape hatch

When horse bone turns up *in situ* with pre-Classic ceramics (as at Loltún, see [[Pre-Columbian Horse Remains Found in Yucatan Caves and Cenotes]]), the response is that "some sort of stratigraphic mixing" must have occurred — invoked, Johnson says, precisely *because* the data violate the consensus, "when the evidence is there, they try and find a way around it." The dismissal is generated by the conclusion rather than by independent evidence of disturbance.[^3]

### 3. Magleby's named-fallacy taxonomy

Magleby reads the "no horses, therefore the Book of Mormon is a fraud, slam dunk" inference as committing several catalogued fallacies at once: **appeal to tradition** ("it's always been this way"), **bandwagon** ("everybody believes it"), **begging the question / petitio** (assuming the answer — "since we know there were no horses before contact, then this must be post-Columbian"), **circular reasoning** (beginning from the conclusion), and **motivated reasoning** (a process "biased because we're defending a pre-established condition," not letting new data speak). Both grant that *everyone* is prone to these — "we all have biases," confirmation bias included — so the point is a call to method, not an ad hominem. See [[Motivated Reasoning and the Tides of Bias]].[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The "circularity" charge cuts both ways and is partly answerable. Dating a lone, undated petroglyph by the *known* faunal and historical context is ordinary inference to the best explanation, not vicious circularity: given overwhelming independent evidence (paleontological, genetic, documentary) that domestic horses arrive with the Spanish, a horse image with no datable context really *is* most probably post-contact, and saying so is Bayesian, not question-begging. "Stratigraphic mixing" is likewise a real, well-documented taphonomic process in caves and cenotes, not an ad hoc dodge — the burden is on the claimant to show a *sealed, undisturbed, directly dated* association, which the cave material generally lacks. And listing fallacy names does not by itself rebut the consensus; one must show the inference actually has no independent support, whereas here it has a great deal. The defensible LDS use is narrow: the circular *rhetoric* ("looks like a horse → must be post-contact") should be retired in favor of context-by-context dating — which, done honestly, is exactly what would let genuine pre-Columbian finds be recognized.
