# Nahom Exceeds Biblical Sites by Archaeology's Own Standards

## The Claim
[[Josh Gehly]] (Penn State anthropology/archaeology) argues that **Nahom is the single strongest archaeological evidence for the Book of Mormon** — and that, judged by the *same standards* archaeologists apply to biblical sites, it "exceeds almost all biblical sites." His move is to set Nahom inside the real, contested field of biblical archaeology (the minimalist/maximalist debates of Finkelstein and Dever) rather than the simplistic "the Bible is proven" framing, and show that the **convergence** at Nahom clears the bar that field uses to identify a site: "from an archaeological standpoint, this is what a bullseye looks like."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Biblical archaeology is itself contested (rated: strong, framing).** Gehly notes "there's tense debates within the archaeological community on events within scripture and just how accurate the Bible is" — so the honest baseline is not "the Bible is proven," but a standard of evidence with real controversy. Nahom is then measured against that standard, not an inflated one.[^2]
2. **Multiple independent convergences (rated: strong).** The case stacks: (a) the south-southeast route matches the only vegetated/fertile corridor on satellite maps; (b) three altars from the Bar'an / Marib temple bear the consonantal **NHM**, naming the tribe; (c) local place-names and **ancient maps unavailable to Joseph Smith** identify the region; (d) the due-east turn reaches a Bountiful match in Dhofar; (e) a funerary stela bears **Ishmael's** name from the right period. "If you just had the maps that's enough ... if you just had the temples ... that's considered abundant ... you have the whole pie." See [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn]], [[The Ishmael Funerary Stela in South Arabia]], and [[Bountiful Lies Due East of Nahom]].[^3]
3. **A "shot in the dark" prediction (rated: moderate-to-strong).** Gehly frames Nephi's report as a falsifiable prediction: the party arrives at a place *already named* Nahom where a foreigner is buried — "either authentic and ancient or 19th-century fiction." That the named place, the foreigner's name, and the eastward geography all land is what makes it a bullseye rather than a lucky parallel.[^1]
4. **Comparative-evidence claim (rated: weak-to-moderate, rhetorical).** Gehly and [[Jerry Grover]] add that this is "harder evidence for Ishmael ... than we enjoy about other major historical figures" who are "readily accepted" (e.g., disputed claims about how far Alexander or Genghis Khan reached). This is a comparison of evidential standards, not a measured metric.[^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic grants the data but resists the "exceeds biblical sites" ranking. (a) **NHM is a common Semitic triliteral** (root for comfort/consolation), so a NHM toponym along a long caravan route is not improbable, and Carsten Niebuhr's 1763 map already labeled "Nehhm" — so the name was not strictly inaccessible to a determined 19th-century compiler (see the Niebuhr caveat in [[Nahom Inscription Confirms the Book of Mormon Geography]]). (b) The **stela was looted**, so its exact findspot is unknown and it cannot be pinned inside NHM territory, only nearby — and a common name on a tombstone is not an identification. (c) "Exceeds almost all biblical sites" is a **subjective ranking**, not a quantified comparison; biblical sites like Lachish or Hazor have stratified, datable destruction layers and textual cross-references that arguably outstrip a name-convergence. The disciplined Latter-day Saint reply is the vault's standing one: the force is **cumulative convergence, not proof** ([[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity]]) — name + mandatory eastward turn + dated epigraphy + matching terminus is a joint match a 1829 fabricator could not engineer, even if no single element is decisive.
