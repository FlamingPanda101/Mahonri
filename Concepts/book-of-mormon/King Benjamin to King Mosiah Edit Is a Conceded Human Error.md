# King Benjamin to King Mosiah Edit Is a Conceded Human Error

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** [[Mosiah 21.28|Mosiah 21:28]] originally named **King Benjamin** as
the seer who could interpret the engravings, but by that point in the narrative
Benjamin has died and his son **Mosiah** reigns — so later editions changed
"Benjamin" to "Mosiah." The presence of such an internal error is taken as
evidence of human authorship.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Multiple innocent explanations exist

Saints Unscripted lists possibilities without insisting on one: "It could be an
ancient scribal error, an editing mistake, or something Joseph introduced himself
by accident. Honestly, it's hard to say. And that's okay to admit." Any of these
is compatible with an authentic but humanly transmitted record.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Reasonable. Scribal-name confusion (father/son with linked
> reigns) is a well-attested class of error in real ancient manuscripts.

### 2. [[Don Bradley]]'s research engages it seriously

The host points to researcher [[Don Bradley]], who "has a lot to say about this
particular change," signaling that the problem has been studied rather than
swept aside.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Appeal to ongoing scholarship; strengthens credibility but
> the video does not summarize Bradley's actual resolution.

### 3. The honest concession

Uniquely among the five changes, the host offers no strong defense: "I do not have
strong responses here other than acknowledging that the Book of Mormon does
contain mistakes of men." This connects to the title-page admission and the
clarified meaning of "most correct book." See
[[Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free]].[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** A candid concession — rhetorically disarming, but it grants
> the critic's basic premise (the text has errors).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic can press that "it's just a human error" is exactly what a naturalist
predicts and exactly what a "tight control, words-from-God" account forbids. If
God controlled the wording tightly enough for apologists to marvel at the
translation, a king's name appearing in the wrong reign is hard to attribute to
ancient scribes — Joseph was reading the *English* off the stone, not copying a
defective manuscript. The "mistakes of men" defense quietly abandons the
high-control translation story when convenient.

## Best Quotes

> "It could be an ancient scribal error, an editing mistake, or something Joseph

> admit."[^2]

> "Researchers like Don Bradley have a lot to say about this particular change,

> "I do not have strong responses here other than acknowledging that the Book of
