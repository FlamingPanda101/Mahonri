# The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Jonah Barnes]], relaying [[Hugh Nibley]], argues that one of the strongest single evidences for [[Joseph Smith]]'s prophetic gift is a **proper name**. In Joseph Smith's [[Moses 6|Book of Moses]] (revealed 1830), a man named **Mahijah** comes to inquire of the prophet Enoch. The Aramaic *Book of the Giants* — a fragmentary Enochic text recovered from the **Dead Sea Scrolls** and published well over a century after Joseph Smith's death — contains a figure with the same consonantal name (**Mahawai / MHWY / Mahujah**) who likewise goes to question Enoch. Because Hebrew/Aramaic was written without vowels, the match is on the consonants. Nibley called it an all-but-proof "slam dunk": Joseph Smith could not have pulled a 3,000–4,000-year-old name "out of a hat."[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The name appears in the same narrative role in both texts — rate: strong (specific, low-probability match)
In *Moses 6–7* Mahijah/Mahujah approaches Enoch with an interrogation ("Tell us plainly who thou art"); in the *Book of the Giants* the analogous figure is dispatched to Enoch to seek the interpretation of a dream/oracle. It is not merely a shared name but a shared *function* — a messenger going to inquire of Enoch — which raises the improbability of coincidence.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong as an instance of [[Authentic Ancient Texts Contain Uncorroborated Unique Data]] — a unique proper name with a matching role is hard to attribute to chance or to 1830 sources.

### 2. The source was unavailable to Joseph Smith — rate: strong (chronology)
The *Book of the Giants* fragments were identified among the Dead Sea Scrolls in the mid-20th century; the relevant scholarship post-dates Joseph Smith by a century or more. The 1830 Book of Moses therefore could not have borrowed the name. This is the classic "reverse-anachronism" structure (see [[Confirmed Anachronisms Are Reverse-Anachronisms Joseph Could Not Have Known]]).[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on availability; the only escape for the critic is to deny the match is meaningful (see steelman).

### 3. Nibley staked his credibility on it — rate: moderate (appeal to expertise)
Barnes recounts Nibley pressing a scholar friend for an alternative explanation and getting only "Joseph must have had documents we don't know about," which Nibley rejected as a cop-out. The point is that a serious Near Eastern scholar regarded the parallel as essentially inexplicable on naturalistic grounds.[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — argument from a single (partisan) expert; persuasive but not independent confirmation.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

- **Mahijah may derive from the Bible, not the Book of the Giants.** Critics note the element resembles **Mehujael** (Genesis 5:21 in some readings) and the interrogative Hebrew *mah* ("what/who"), so Joseph Smith (or a 19th-century source) could have generated a plausibly Semitic name without any access to the Scrolls. If the name is constructible from the King James Bible, the "impossible knowledge" claim weakens.
- **Consonant-only matching inflates hits.** Vowel-less comparison makes coincidental matches easier; *MHWY* and *Mahijah* are not identical, and the equation depends on a chain of transliteration judgments.
- **The Book of the Giants figure is a giant/Watcher, not a human inquirer.** The narrative roles are similar but not identical, so "same name, same role" overstates the parallel.
- **One data point.** Even a striking single name is thin to carry "proof of a prophet"; authenticity arguments need a *pattern* (which is why the vault frames such items cumulatively).

**What survives:** the **availability** point is genuinely strong — the *Book of the Giants* was not on any 1830 frontier bookshelf — so if the Mahijah/Mahujah equation holds, it is a real reverse-anachronism. The contested link is whether the name is *uniquely* tied to that lost text or merely a biblically-plausible coinage. The defensible claim is that this is a *notable, low-probability convergence* that strengthens a cumulative case (cf. [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]]); the strong claim ("a slam dunk, trillion-to-one") is rhetoric the steelman tempers.
