#type/claim

# Trading Gold and Silver for Brass Inverts Material Value

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]]'s economic leg of the reversal: [[Nephi]] offers [[Laban]] the family's **gold and silver** in exchange for the **brass plates**. In every culture gold and silver are rarer and more valuable than brass, so the youngest son is giving away the more precious metals for the less precious — already an inversion. But Bradley pushes further: what Nephi is really buying is the *engraved records*, and engraving *removes* metal. So strictly, he trades gold and silver for the **negative space** carved out of the brass — "a material nothing" that nonetheless holds the symbolic and spiritual wealth of the scriptures. He likens it to the Tao Te Ching's praise of useful emptiness (the hole makes the wheel, the hollow makes the pot useful).[^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The trade is in the text and is a real value-inversion.** Nephi does offer precious metals for brass plates; valuing the *record* over the *gold* is a genuine reordering of material worth, and the narrative makes the prosperity of the people hinge on obtaining the record rather than keeping the wealth. (Evidence rating: moderate-to-strong — the trade and its priority are textual; "negative space" is interpretive flourish.)[^1]
2. **The "negative space" reading is poetically apt.** That engraving is subtraction, so the message lives in absence, is a clever and coherent way to dramatize "spiritual wealth over material wealth"; the Tao Te Ching parallel illustrates the idea that emptiness can be the useful thing. (Evidence rating: low-to-moderate — an illuminating metaphor, not an argument the author intended it.)[^2]
3. **It coheres with the brass plates' larger function.** Elsewhere the brass plates matter precisely as a *preserved record* (their content, not their metal value), which fits the priority Bradley highlights. See [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]]. (Evidence rating: moderate.)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Trading treasure for a sacred record is a straightforward "wisdom over wealth" theme common to scripture (cf. "the pearl of great price"), so a skeptic can say Bradley is dressing an ordinary moral in avant-garde clothing; the "negative space" point especially risks over-reading, since the plates' *content* is plainly what is valued, not literally the absent metal. The Tao Te Ching comparison is evocative but anachronistic as evidence of authorial intent. The defensible core is modest: the episode does narrate a deliberate exchange of the more-valuable for the less-valuable-but-record-bearing, which is one concrete instance of the opening's pattern of inverting expected values — consistent with [[The First Narrative of First Nephi Signals a Reversal of Values]], whether or not the "negative space" gloss is pressed.
