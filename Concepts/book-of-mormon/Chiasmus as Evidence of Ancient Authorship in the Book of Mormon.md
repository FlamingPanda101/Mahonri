# Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Arnold calls chiasmus "the most striking piece of authorship evidence to me." Chiasmus is "an ancient Hebrew literary device where ideas mirror around a central pivot point in an AB CBA structure, sometimes running 15 to 30 layers deep." The fraud theory must explain how a device unknown to Joseph appears, deeply structured, throughout the text [^17].

## The Rebuttal
1. **The discovery timeline.** "Chiasmus wasn't recognized in Western scholarship until 1854, 24 years after the Book of Mormon was published, and it remained obscure for another century." In 1967 — 138 years after publication — BYU student [[John W. Welch]] encountered chiasmus in New Testament scholarship in Germany, returned to the Book of Mormon, and "found it embedded throughout the text." *Rating: the Welch 1967 discovery narrative is accurate; "unrecognized until 1854" is roughly right for systematic Western study (cf. John Jebb / Robert Lowth precursors).* [^17][^18]
2. **The flagship example: [[Alma 36|Alma 36]].** "A 17-layer structure with Alma's conversion at its center, Jesus Christ as the precise pivot point, and perfectly mirrored thematic elements running outward in both directions." [^18]
3. **The probability estimate.** Physicists [[Boyd Edwards]] and [[Farrell Edwards]] ("not LDS apologists") ran a statistical analysis on Alma 36 and put the probability of that structure occurring by chance "at between 1 in 10,000 and 1 in a million." *Rating: a real published study (BYU Studies, 2004); the odds depend on modeling assumptions about element selection and significance — a known point of methodological debate.* [^19]
4. **The fabrication burden.** To construct that while dictating without notes, "you'd need to know what chiasmus was to begin with, and Joseph didn't... nobody did in America in 1829." He would have had to "hold the entire mirrored pattern in his head" across a 269,000-word text produced at 3,000 words/day, and "do it multiple times." Critics analyzing the book for fraud for 138 years "missed it entirely, because nobody knew about it" [^19][^20].
5. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) Chiasmus arises naturally in repetitive, parallelistic, KJV-saturated prose; a person steeped in biblical cadence can produce mirror structures unconsciously. (b) Identifying chiasms involves *researcher discretion* — selecting which elements "count" can inflate apparent symmetry, and critics (e.g., responses to the Edwards study) argue the odds calculations are not robust. (c) Some proposed Book of Mormon chiasms are weak or overlapping. Arnold presents Alma 36 as the strong case and does not engage the literature questioning chiasmus-detection methodology [^19].
