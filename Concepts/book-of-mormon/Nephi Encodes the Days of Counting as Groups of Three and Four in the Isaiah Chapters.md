# Nephi Encodes the Days of Counting as Groups of Three and Four in the Isaiah Chapters

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Christina Dymock]] reports that the **Weeks/Pentecost (Shavuot)** beat of her seven-feasts outline ([[Nephi Encoded the Seven Hebrew Feasts as the Outline of First Nephi]]) nearly broke the thesis: the Gathering-of-Israel harvest theme in the Isaiah chapters of First Nephi felt far too thin compared with how richly the other feasts were realized. Her resolution: between Firstfruits and Shavuot the Israelites observe the **Omer**, the **"days of counting"** — seven weeks the worshipper literally *counts*. She argues Nephi encodes that counting *formally* by composing the Isaiah-chapter section (≈ 1 Ne 19–22) as a string of **lists arranged in groups of three and four (which sum to seven)**, with key items tallied in sevens and multiples of seven — making the reader *count* in order to enact the feast. Examples she cites: prophets named **seven** times, plates mentioned **fourteen** (7×2) times, and the recurring beasts/ores lists of 1 Ne 18:25 broken into a four and a three. [^1] [^2] [^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Omer "days of counting" is a real, count-based observance.** Leviticus 23 commands counting seven weeks (fifty days, the Omer) from Firstfruits to Weeks/Pentecost — Shavuot literally means "weeks," Pentecost "fifty." A literary device that forces the reader to count is therefore a fitting enactment of *this specific* feast rather than a generic flourish. *Rating: the feast genuinely centers on counting; the match of device-to-feast is apt.* [^1] [^4]
2. **The Isaiah chapters do contain conspicuous lists Nephi otherwise avoids.** 1 Ne 18:25 — "the cow and the ox and the ass and the horse and the goat and the wild goat… gold and silver and copper" — is an enumerative style Dymock says is uncharacteristic of Nephi, who "rarely puts a number in." She first attributed it merely to Isaiah-imitation before noticing the three/four-into-seven structure recurring through the section. *Rating: the lists are textually present and stylistically marked; the seven-grouping is the interpretive overlay.* [^2] [^3]
3. **Number-symbolism is an established Book of Mormon authorial feature.** The vault already documents deliberate authorial wordplay and numeric/structural patterning ([[Book of Mormon Names Show Authorial Wordplay]], [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]]); a feast-driven seven-count is continuous with that craftsmanship rather than a novel imposition. *Rating: consistent with patterns independently attested elsewhere in the corpus.* [^2]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic objects that this is the thesis's most **methodologically fragile** beat — exactly where Dymock admits the obvious harvest parallel "falls apart" and she goes looking for a rescue. Counting words and items in *groups of seven* is highly susceptible to **confirmation bias and the multiple-comparisons problem**: in any long text one can find threes, fours, sevens, and multiples of seven by choosing what to tally and where to start; "prophets 7×, plates 14×" is unverified in the episode and depends on counting conventions the reader sets. Lists are also a known feature of the *Isaiah* source being quoted, giving a mundane stylistic explanation that needs no Omer. The reply offered is that the device is *feast-appropriate* (this feast is uniquely about counting) and that it appears precisely where the seven-feasts order predicts Shavuot should fall — but the note flags this as the weakest link, fairly to be tested against an actual statistical tally before it carries evidentiary weight.

[^2]

> "All of a sudden I went, oh my gosh, Nephi wants us to count." [^3]
