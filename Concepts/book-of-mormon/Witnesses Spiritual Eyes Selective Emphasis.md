# Witnesses Spiritual Eyes Selective Emphasis

#type/objection

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Critics claim the witnesses to the gold plates "only saw them with their spiritual eyes" — i.e., the witnesses had visionary, not physical, experiences, so their testimony does not establish that real plates existed.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

1. **It cherry-picks one datum (core charge).** The "spiritual eyes" reading "emphasizes one piece of data and ignores a mountain of data" — the many statements describing physical handling, hefting, and tangible interaction with the plates.[^2] *Rating: strong (as stated) — frames the move as selective emphasis, the same methodological sin diagnosed in [[Source Criticism in Mormon History]].*

2. **Same failure mode as the folk-magic "laundry list."** Christian connects it to the broader pattern: take a data set, "emphasize some pieces, deemphasize some" to reach a predetermined conclusion — bad history whether done by critics or believers.[^3] *Rating: strong — methodological consistency.*

3. **Motivated reasoning (diagnosis).** "They have to believe that ... you have to ignore this in order to believe this piece," which Mark labels "bad history."[^4] *Rating: moderate — characterizes the critic's incentive.*

4. **Steelman (important — this concept is asserted, not argued in detail).** The source does *not* actually present the countervailing witness statements; it only asserts a "mountain of data" exists. A fair critic can respond that some witness language genuinely is visionary ("eyes of our understanding"), that the experiences may have been religiously framed, and that the believing side could equally be accused of emphasizing the tactile statements while downplaying the visionary ones. This concept therefore states a *principle* (don't cherry-pick) more than it adjudicates the witness evidence; the full witness case must be evaluated elsewhere with the primary statements in hand.

## Best Quotes

> "What you're doing is you're emphasizing one piece of data and ignoring a mountain of data to do that. It's just ridiculous."[^2]

> "You have to ignore this in order to believe this piece ... No, it's bad history."[^4]
