# Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity

## The Claim

The sheer production feat — dictating the entire Book of Mormon in roughly two and a half months, 8.5 to 11 pages a day, off the top of his head, with only about two and a half years of patchy formal schooling, while sustaining distinct authorial voices — is itself beyond the capacity of a young frontier farmer, and points away from solo authorship.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The raw output rate
[[Joseph Smith]] dictated the text in a little over two months at 8.5–11 finished pages per day. "My undergraduate students couldn't have done that. I don't think I could have done it."[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (the timeline and page rates are historically attested; the "no one could" claim is comparative judgment).

### 2. Distinct authorial voices, per Grant Hardy
[[Grant Hardy]]'s *Understanding the Book of Mormon* (Oxford University Press) — written for a non-LDS scholarly audience and not intended as apologetics — finds different narrators with different themes and styles inside the book. Peterson reads it as "powerfully apologetic even if he didn't intend it," because creating distinct characters on the fly during rapid dictation is "beyond Joseph's capacity."[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (Hardy is a credentialed scholar; the literary distinctness is a substantive, checkable claim). See [[John Hilton III]] and *Voices in the Book of Mormon* for a parallel study.[^4]

### 3. The "he was a genius" escape doesn't help
If critics retreat to "he was a genius," that still fails to explain how he *also* arranged for people to see and heft objects, etc. — genius at storytelling does not produce tangible plates or absent-witness experiences.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (a conjunction argument linking authorship to the witness problem).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Strong naturalistic replies exist: (a) the dictation may have drawn on long mental rehearsal, oral-narrative skill, and possibly notes or collaborators, so "off the top of his head" overstates the case; (b) computational stylometry is contested — some studies find multiple "voices," others attribute them to a single author's range or to scribes, so "distinct authors" is not settled; (c) prolific improvisational dictation is documented in other oral performers. Peterson's rejoinder is that even granting authorial genius, the *witness* phenomena (tangible plates, absent-Joseph experiences) remain unexplained, so authorship and witnesses must be solved together — which is why this concept feeds [[Inference to the Best Explanation for the Witnesses]].

## Best Quotes

> "He dictates the thing in two and a half months... 8.5 to 11 dictated pages a day. My undergraduate students couldn't have done that. I don't think I could have done it."[^2]

> "He's looking at the book and finding different authors in it with different themes — like, that's beyond Joseph's capacity."[^3]

> "So they say he was a genius. So that doesn't explain a whole lot to me. And how did he arrange for all these people to see things and heft things?"[^5]
