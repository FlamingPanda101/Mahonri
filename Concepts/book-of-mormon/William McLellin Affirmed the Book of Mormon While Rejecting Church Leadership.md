# William McLellin Affirmed the Book of Mormon While Rejecting Church Leadership

## The Claim
[[William McLellin]] — an early apostle who left the church *permanently* — is an unusually probative witness because his quarrel was with *leadership*, not the book. [[Keystone]] argues that his decades-long, post-departure affirmation of the Book of Mormon (and his record of other witnesses reaffirming it under threat of death) separates the truth claim of the book from grievances about the institution.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A hostile, repeatedly-departed insider still affirmed the book.** McLellin was excommunicated or withdrew repeatedly (1832, 1836, finally 1838) and ended in unbelief in organized religion by 1869 — yet in his 1880 reply to the critic [[James Cobb]] he wrote: "I have set my seal that the Book of Mormon is a true, divine record... When a man goes at the Book of Mormon, he touches the apple of my eye. He fights against truth, against purity, against light." He told Cobb to "fight the wrongs of LDS-ism as much as you please, but let that unique, that inimitable book alone."[^2] *Rating: strong within the source — an estranged apostle with every motive to recant instead doubling down decades later; the disentangling of "book" from "church" is the key move.*

2. **His conviction was grounded in firsthand investigation.** While investigating in 1831, McLellin interviewed [[Hyrum Smith]] for four hours about the coming forth of the record, then prayed, and concluded he "was bound as an honest man to acknowledge the truth and validity of the Book of Mormon."[^3] *Rating: moderate–strong — a documented investigative basis, though filtered through his own journal.*

3. **He recorded witnesses reaffirming under threat of death.** During the 1833 Jackson County persecutions, with lives in danger, McLellin asked [[Oliver Cowdery]] and [[David Whitmer]] whether the book was true. Cowdery: "God sent his Holy Angel to declare the truth of the translation of it to us... and though the mob kill us, yet we must die declaring its truth." Whitmer affirmed the same: "we could not be deceived." McLellin also recorded that eight-witness [[Hiram Page]], beaten by a mob and told to "deny that damned book," replied, "How can I deny what I know to be true?"[^4] *Rating: strong — multiply-attested costly testimony; see [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]].*

4. **He refused to fabricate "darkness" against the book.** McLellin had seen [[David Whitmer]] reaffirm his testimony in June 1879 "as sincerely and solemnly as when he bore it to me in Paris, Illinois, in July of 1831." When Cobb urged him and Whitmer to "tell something, some darkness relative to that book," McLellin answered: "We should lie if we did, for we know nothing against its credibility or divine truth."[^5] *Rating: strong — a refusal to manufacture evidence even when invited by a sympathetic critic.*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
"Never recanted" proves **sincere belief, not the truth of the belief.** McLellin could have been honestly convinced (by a four-hour interview, prayer, and high-expectancy social setting) and still mistaken; the witnesses he cites could likewise have been sincere but deceived. His testimony also reaches us through his own partisan record and Keystone's selection, and a man who spent decades in breakoff groups had his own reasons to keep the Book of Mormon while rejecting Brigham Young's church. The book-vs-leadership split could itself be motivated reasoning.

## Counter-objection
The objection concedes the most important point for this note's purpose: McLellin's case isolates the *book's* credibility from institutional loyalty, since he abandoned the institution entirely yet kept the book. Sincere-but-mistaken belief explains *his* conviction but strains against the *cluster* — multiple named witnesses reaffirming under mob violence and on deathbeds, with no recantation across decades of estrangement. That pattern is what pushes critics toward hallucination theories rather than fraud (see [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]]). The partisan-source caveat is real but cuts less here, because McLellin's words run *against* his institutional interest.
