# Everyday Improbabilities More Likely Than Naturalistic Book of Mormon Authorship

## The Claim

Once [[Matt Vallejo]] reaches his headline figure of ~1 in 15 billion for naturalistic Book of Mormon authorship (see [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions]]), he confronts the problem that a bare astronomical number is hard to *feel*. His solution is a **calibration list**: real-world events that are far *more* probable than 1-in-15-billion, yet that no rational person organizes life around. The point is a reductio on lived behavior — if you would not plan your day around these, you should not credit the even-less-likely claim that [[Joseph Smith]] composed the book unaided.[^1]

The list (lifetime/independent odds, as cited):
- **A meteor striking your home** — 1 in 3.6 million.[^2]
- **Being struck by lightning twice** — 1 in 200 million (the single-strike probability multiplied by itself, since the two strikes are independent events in time).[^3]
- **Having identical quintuplets** — 1 in 3.9 billion (narrower than fraternal, since identical multiples past twins are vanishingly rare).[^4]
- **A chimp randomly typing the word "apple"** — about 1 in 10 billion given ~100–120 keys, an untrained animal, and the relevant keystroke combinations.[^5]

Each is "orders of magnitude more probable than Joseph Smith coming up with the Book of Mormon on his own." Vallejo's conclusion: "I'm not going to live my life by the probability that Joe Smith totally created that thing on his own. That's so preposterous." The believer's competing hypothesis — an authentic ancient translation "through the gift and power of God" — is offered as what actually accounts for the length, the multiple voices, and the speed.[^6]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **It targets the right cognitive failure.** People routinely mishandle very small probabilities, treating "improbable but conceivable" as "live." Translating 10⁻¹⁰ into "rarer than a meteor hitting your house" is a legitimate intuition pump that exposes the mismatch between the critic's confident "Joseph just did it" and the number that confidence implies.[^1] *Rating: rhetorically effective and broadly fair as an illustration of scale.*

2. **The lightning-twice teaching moment is correct in form.** Vallejo correctly explains the multiplication of independent probabilities, and the hosts work through the common misreading ("one in 200 million *people*") versus the right reading ("one in 200 million *lifetimes lived by you*"). The independence point is sound probability pedagogy.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

This is the argument's softest section and the critic has several clean replies:

- **The numbers being calibrated against are themselves shaky.** The headline 1-in-15-billion inherits every disputed prior and dependency assumption from [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions|the chain-rule model]]; calibrating a soft number against vivid analogies does not harden it.
- **Post-hoc events are not predictions.** The Book of Mormon *already exists*; computing the prior odds of an event that has occurred commits the lottery-winner fallacy — every actual long book has astronomically low pre-odds under a "random keystrokes" model, yet books get written by *non-random* authors. The relevant question is the likelihood under *intelligent human composition*, which is not ~10⁻¹⁰. This is the same misfire flagged in [[Infinite Monkey Theorem Against Random Chiasmus]]: defeating blind chance does nothing against a literate author.
- **Improbable things do happen.** Vallejo concedes each listed event *does* occur to *someone*; rarity is not impossibility, and someone has to be the author. Low prior probability is not disproof.
- **A low number is not yet an inference to God.** Even granting the figure, "naturalistic authorship is very improbable" does not establish divine translation, which has its own priors and its own explanatory costs — the point [[Trent Horn]] presses with the magic-trick/miracle distinction. See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].

Vallejo partly anticipates this by framing the list as showing belief "is not crazy" rather than as a proof, and by immediately pivoting to spiritual conviction as his real ground.

## Best Quotes

> "The lifetime odds of a meteor hitting your home is 1 in 3.6 million. But you don't go to bed at night thinking there's a risk we wake up in the morning and a meteor strikes our home."[^2]

> "If you sat a monkey in front of a computer and it typed out the word apple... it's likely to do that in one out of every 10 billion instances... that's way more realistic than the Book of Mormon having been [authored by Joseph alone]."[^5]

> "I'm not going to live my life by the probability that Joe Smith totally created that thing on his own. That's so preposterous. I can't do that."[^6]
