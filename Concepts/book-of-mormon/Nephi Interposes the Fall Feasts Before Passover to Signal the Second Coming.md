# Nephi Interposes the Fall Feasts Before Passover to Signal the Second Coming

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Christina Dymock]] notes a deliberate anomaly in her seven-feasts reading of First Nephi ([[Nephi Encoded the Seven Hebrew Feasts as the Outline of First Nephi]]): although the festal *year* begins in spring with **Passover**, and 1 Nephi 1 is set at the commencement of Zedekiah's reign ([[Don Bradley]]'s Passover-season setting), Nephi *opens* his outline with the **fall** feasts — Trumpets, Atonement, Tabernacles — and only reaches Passover late, at the ocean crossing. She argues this re-ordering is intentional and theological: Christ's **first** coming fulfilled the three spring feasts (Passover, Firstfruits, Weeks), so the still-unfulfilled **fall** feasts are the ones that point to the **Second Coming** — and Nephi, "having seen our day," front-loads them. The interleaving is also, she suggests, why no prior reader spotted the festal pattern: the year appears scrambled. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The spring-fulfilled / fall-future framework is a real typological scheme.** It is a widely taught Christian reading that Passover (the cross), Firstfruits (the resurrection), and Pentecost (the Spirit) were fulfilled at the first advent, while Trumpets, Atonement, and Tabernacles await fulfillment at the Second Coming. Nephi's choice to lead with the fall cluster therefore lands precisely on the "still to come" feasts. *Rating: the spring/fall fulfillment scheme is a coherent and recognized typology; attributing the re-ordering to Nephi's foresight is the interpretive step.* [^2]
2. **The text really does open with a fall-feast scene despite a spring setting.** 1 Nephi 1 presents a Yom Teruah / Rosh Hashanah tableau — shofar-like prophets, the enthroned King, the opened book of judgment with ten days to repent — even though the chapter's dating points to Passover season. The tension between setting and content is genuine and demands *some* explanation; Dymock's is that the displacement is purposeful. *Rating: the spring-setting-vs-fall-content tension is textual, not invented; competing explanations exist.* [^3]
3. **An alternative explanation strengthens, not weakens, the thesis.** Co-host Dave Butler observes that the ancient *civil* year may have begun in the **fall** (Tishri/Rosh Hashanah), so a "commencement of the reign" plus an immediate Rosh Hashanah scene could simply be literal fall dating — making [[Don Bradley]]'s Passover reading the casualty. Either way (deliberate interposition *or* literal fall new-year), Nephi's text aligns with the fall festival at the outset. *Rating: the claim survives under two independent calendar models, which is a point in its favor.* [^4]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic replies that (a) "the fall feasts point to the Second Coming" is a *Christian* typological overlay Nephi need not have held, so reading his outline as encoding it is anachronistic; (b) the simplest read of the spring-setting/fall-content tension is Butler's literal fall new-year, which requires no foresight and no interposition; (c) "this is why nobody noticed before" is unfalsifiable — absence of prior scholarship is equally explained by the pattern not being there; and (d) Dymock concedes the book is "art… poetry" and that we "don't have to assume it literally was fall or spring," which loosens the constraints enough to fit almost any ordering. The reply offered is that the *content* of 1 Nephi 1 is unmistakably a coronation/judgment (fall) scene, that the displacement is real on any account, and that the spring/fall fulfillment scheme gives a motivated — not arbitrary — reason for leading with the fall cluster.

## Best Quotes
> "He's starting with the fall festival, which I think is why nobody really caught this before… when Christ came the first time he fulfilled the first three feasts, and so now that we're getting ready for a second coming the fall feasts would be the ones that we would pay attention to. So Nephi I think does this on purpose — I think he's seen our day." [^2]

> "The new year, Rosh Hashanah, is in the fall… maybe what we're seeing is that it is in the fall of the year." [^4]
