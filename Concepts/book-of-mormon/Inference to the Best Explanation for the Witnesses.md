# Inference to the Best Explanation for the Witnesses

## The Claim

Once "they lied," "they were tricked," and "a mixture" are all judged evidentially bankrupt, the remaining option — that the witnesses were telling the truth and the plates were really there — is, by inference to the best explanation, the rational conclusion, however improbable it seems to someone committed to naturalism.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Sherlock Holmes maxim cuts toward the supernatural here
Holmes: eliminate the impossible, and whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth. For the committed naturalist, God and real plates are ruled "impossible," leaving only improbable naturalistic theories. Peterson reverses the calculus: the *naturalistic* explanations are the ones that are "evidentially impossible" — "logically conceivable, but... just nothing to support them" — so the improbable-but-supported divine explanation remains.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a framing; its force depends entirely on one's prior probability for theism.

### 2. The truth-hypothesis dissolves many problems at once
If the Book of Mormon is a genuine ancient record, you no longer have to explain away the witness testimony, the belief that Joseph had plates, the book's complexity, its sources, or the dictation feat. "It settles a lot of questions... the inference to the best explanation."[^3] See [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (explanatory scope is a genuine theoretical virtue; the cost is the large prior improbability of the supernatural).

### 3. It is honest about the residue
Peterson concedes the best explanation "leaves a few" loose ends (e.g., the horses question), but notes every ancient text carries unresolved disputes; a few open questions don't overturn an explanation that handles the whole.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (intellectually candid; the open questions are real and weigh against).

### 4. Peterson reports a good-faith failed attempt to debunk
He says he has genuinely tried to "get rid of" the whole thing and can dispatch small pieces but not the entirety — "the explanation that takes care of that maybe makes this one even more of a problem."[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (personal testimony of method; not independently verifiable).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The decisive skeptical move is exactly the one Peterson names: for a Bayesian who assigns a very low prior to miracles, even a poorly-evidenced naturalistic theory can remain *more probable* than a supernatural one, because "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence." On this view "inference to the best explanation" smuggles in a contested prior — it is "best" only if the supernatural is on the table. A skeptic can also fairly say "I don't know how he did it, and that's okay" — unexplained is not the same as inexplicable. Peterson grants he "would understand and even respect" the position "it's just not enough for me," objecting only to the stronger claim that "there is no evidence," which he says is "flatly not true." See [[Could God vs Would God on the Book of Mormon]].

## Best Quotes

> "I would say these explanations that people give for the witnesses are impossible — I mean, logically conceivable, but evidentially impossible. They're just nothing to support them. So I'm left with the improbable claim that God intervened... but the evidence for me seems to be really clear and I can't explain it away."[^2]

> "The inference to the best explanation is the way logicians talk about it. And to me, this is the best explanation... Joseph's story makes sense of everything. It doesn't leave a lot of loose ends."[^3]

> "I would understand you and even respect your position if you say, well, it's just not enough for me... But if you say there's no evidence, that's flatly not true. There is evidence."[^6]
