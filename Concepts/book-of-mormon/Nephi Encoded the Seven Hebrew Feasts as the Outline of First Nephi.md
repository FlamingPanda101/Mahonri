# Nephi Encoded the Seven Hebrew Feasts as the Outline of First Nephi

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Christina Dymock]] argues that the *outline* of **First Nephi** is the cycle of the **seven Hebrew feasts** instituted in [[Leviticus 23]], walked through in order: Trumpets (Yom Teruah), the Days of Repentance, the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur), Tabernacles (Sukkot) with its Eighth Day (Shemini Atzeret), Passover, and First Fruits leading through the counted weeks to Weeks/Pentecost (Shavuot). She reached the pattern by "reverse-outlining" 1 Nephi the way a novelist dissects another author's book, and reports that no prior commentator had published it. The structural diagnostic is liturgical: in Leviticus 23 each festival block opens with **"and the LORD spake unto Moses"**, and in 1 Nephi each new feast-section opens with the Lord giving [[Lehi]] a vision or command — "and my father had a vision" signals the next feast. If sound, the pattern is evidence that 1 Nephi was authored by someone who *lived* the pre-exilic festal year, not fabricated by [[Joseph Smith]]. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The "and the LORD spake" hinge.** Leviticus 23's spring and fall blocks both begin "and the LORD spake unto Moses"; Dymock reads the recurring openings of 1 Nephi's sections — the Lord appearing to or speaking to Lehi — as the same structural marker, so that each divine address starts a new feast. The first time the Lord speaks to **Nephi** rather than Lehi is the command to build the ship, which she places at the Passover transition. *Rating: a genuine, falsifiable structural claim; its force depends on whether the section boundaries are objectively where she draws them or selected to fit.* [^2] [^3]
2. **The feasts map onto the right episodes in order.** Trumpets ↔ 1 Nephi 1 (prophets crying repentance as the shofar; the enthroned King; the opened book of judgment); the Days of Repentance ↔ Nephi's own turning and witness (1 Ne 2); Atonement ↔ the casting of lots over Laman (1 Ne 4); Tabernacles ↔ the tree-of-life vision with its stick (lulav) and fruit (etrog); the Eighth Day ↔ the Liahona and its rain/provision theme; Passover ↔ the ship and ocean crossing; First Fruits/Shavuot ↔ the planted seeds and the harvest/gathering themes of the Isaiah chapters. The order is preserved throughout. *Rating: the sequence-fit is the strongest part of the case; each link is itself an interpretive judgment.* [^4] [^5] [^6]
3. **A built-in negative control: Hanukkah is correctly absent.** Hanukkah is a second-temple feast instituted only after the Maccabean rededication (~165 BC), so a genuinely pre-exilic Nephi could not include it. Dymock reports she kept looking for Hanukkah and was "thrown off" until she learned the festal calendar had changed over time — and that its absence is exactly what an authentic sixth-century-BC outline predicts. *Rating: a real, non-trivial control; its omission is evidence the pattern tracks the historical calendar rather than the modern one.* [^7]
4. **Subtlety as a marker of authenticity.** Nephi never *announces* the feasts; he assumes an audience living the law of Moses who would recognize the cues — citrons, sukkah tent-posts, shofar blasts — exactly as an insider, not an explainer, would write. *Rating: consistent with ancient authorship but unfalsifiable on its own; it is the cumulative ritual specificity, not the silence, that carries weight.* [^8]
5. **Coheres with an independent temple-pattern reading of 1 Nephi.** The vault already documents Nephi's dream encoding the three temple rooms by Hebrew name-puns ([[Lehi's Dream Encodes the Three Temple Rooms by Hebrew Name-Puns]]) and the Book of Mormon matching pre-reform first-temple theology ([[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]]); a festal substructure fits the same "Nephi writes liturgical Hebrew under the narrative" thesis. *Rating: convergent support from prior, independently sourced claims.* [^1]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** A critic objects that (a) the festival calendar is so rich in themes — repentance, kingship, harvest, provision, deliverance, mourning — that almost *any* devout Israelite narrative will incidentally touch several, so post-hoc mapping risks **parallelomania**; (b) the section boundaries are set by the interpreter, so "each feast opens with a divine address" can be made true by choosing where feasts begin; (c) the strongest single beat (the two goats) rests on a *lamed*-as-"as" reading and an Enochic "Azazel = Satan" identification that are themselves contested (see [[Laban and Nephi Enact the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]]); and (d) the thesis is the author's own forthcoming, unrefereed work, presented as "wave top." The reply offered in the episode is cumulative and falsifiable-in-principle: the *order* is preserved, multiple *ritual specifics* (nine prophets/nine shofar blasts, lulav→iron rod, etrog, the Omer count encoded as groups of seven) line up, and a real negative control (Hanukkah) behaves correctly — a configuration harder to attribute to chance than any one parallel.
