# Gematria and Patterns of Three and Four Structure Alma 32

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Steve Reed]] argues that Alma's sermon to the poor Zoramites ([[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32-33]]) is deliberately built on **gematria** — the Hebrew practice in which letters double as numbers — so that recurring clusters of the numbers **four** and **three** (which sum to **seven**, the number of completion) are keyed to the symbolic meanings of the corresponding Hebrew letters. Because (he claims) a "23-year-old farm boy" dictating into a hat could not have engineered this numeric architecture, its presence is evidence that Alma 32 was "written by someone who knew and understood Hebrew, for an audience that knew and understood Hebrew" — i.e., an authentically ancient text [^1].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Psalm 29 establishes the technique outside the Book of Mormon — rate: moderate (control case)
Reed first demonstrates the pattern in a text no one disputes is Hebrew: [[Psalm 29.1-2|Psalm 29]] (11 verses) has seven occurrences of "the voice of the Lord," seven of God's powers over nature, and (in the Hebrew) three of "ascribe" plus four of "glory" — 3+4=7 — with verse 7 ("divideth the flames of fire") keyed to the sword-meaning of the seventh letter *zayin*. This shows the numeric patterning "isn't isolated to the Book of Mormon" and supplies a baseline for reading Alma 32 the same way [^2].

### 2. Fours saturate Alma 32, keyed to dalet ("the poor") — rate: weak-to-moderate (word-count, hedged)
Reed counts clusters of four: four "poor"s around the hill **Onidah** (which the BYU onomasticon glosses "he knows my affliction"); the four effects of the planted word (*enlarge / enlighten / delicious* — whose first letters he notes spell "seed"); planted/swelleth/sprouteth/grow; the four things "cast out"; four witnesses (Zenos, Zenock, Moses, Alma); and 16 (4×4) mentions each of "seed" and "believe." He keys this to the **fourth** Hebrew letter *dalet*, which means "the weak/poor" (one hanging the head down) and a "door" to a new beginning — the exact condition of the cast-out Zoramites Alma addresses [^3].

### 3. Threes are keyed to gimel ("the benefactor"), and 4+3=7 — rate: weak-to-moderate
Reed then counts threes (experiment ×3, scriptures ×3, heal ×3, the faith-diligence-patience triad ×3) and nine (3×3) mentions of "nourish," keying them to the **third** letter *gimel* — "to deal kindly / nourish," pictured as a camel (endurance) and a rich man who gives to the poor. The synthesis: Alma, rich in the word, is the *gimel* who descends to lift the *dalet* (the poor); four (the poor) plus three (the benefactor) equals seven (completion). He notes Alma 34:3 even carries the "4-3" sequence: "reap the rewards of your faith and diligence and patience and long-suffering" [^4].

### 4. It coheres with the temple-ascension reading — rate: moderate (intertextual)
The number-scheme rides on a temple structure: seed → tree → fruit as the three rooms (plant/nourish/feast), with eight (transition/veil) and ten (completion/mercy-seat → ten "mercy"s, the Day of Atonement) layering the Tree of Life, the Ark, and the *nehushtan* into the sermon (Alma 33's believe-look-heal). This is the same temple-reading method as [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms]] and [[Lehi's Dream Encodes the Three Temple Rooms by Hebrew Name-Puns]], and complements [[Alma 32 Light Alludes to Genesis 1 Creation]] [^5].

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The decisive objection is **pattern-fitting / the Texas-sharpshooter problem** (cf. [[Pareidolia Objection to Chiasmus Finding]]): in any sufficiently long, repetitive text, an analyst free to choose *which* words to tally, *which* synonyms to merge, and *which* counts to keep can almost always surface "meaningful" multiples of three, four, and seven — and Reed selects the words after the fact (he concedes he "left to the side" keywords whose counts did not fit). Three load-bearing weaknesses compound this: (a) the counts are taken from the *English* King James-style translation, not a Hebrew original we possess, so a genuine Hebrew gematria cannot actually be verified; (b) Reed himself repeatedly hedges that individual patterns "might be a coincidence" or "God just kind of winking at you"; and (c) the "Joseph could not have done this" premise is weak, since the patterns are only visible *after* elaborate analysis and need not have been consciously composed at all. The critic's alternative account of Alma 32 (Joseph adapting Jonathan Edwards's well-known "faith is a seed" sermon) explains the surface content without any Hebrew. A measured Latter-day Saint reply: the Psalm 29 control shows the *technique* is real in Hebrew poetry; the convergence of the number scheme with an *independently* motivated temple structure and with named intertexts (the *nahash*/*mashiach* equivalence, the serpent of Numbers 21) is harder to dismiss than any single count; and the claim is offered as cumulative corroboration for those already open to an ancient text, explicitly "scratching the surface," not as a stand-alone proof.
