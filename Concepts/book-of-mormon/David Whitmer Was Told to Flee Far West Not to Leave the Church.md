# David Whitmer Was Told to Flee Far West Not to Leave the Church

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Trent Horn]] quoted [[David Whitmer]]'s 1887 *An Address to All Believers in Christ* — "if you believe my testimony to the Book of Mormon... then I tell you that God spake to me again by his own voice from the heavens and told me to separate myself from among the Latter-day Saints" — to catch [[Jacob Hansen]] in a **dilemma**: if you trust Whitmer's testimony of the Book of Mormon, why not trust him when he says God told him to leave the Church? [^1]

## The Rebuttal
[[Stephen Smoot]]'s open letter argues this is a **misreading produced by quoting out of context**. Whitmer was *already excommunicated* (April 13, 1838) before the divine voice he describes (June 1838), and the full passage shows God told him to **flee Far West, Missouri** — to escape persecution from the Danites — **not** to leave the Church he was no longer part of. [^2]

1. **The chronology defeats the dilemma.** Whitmer was excommunicated in April 1838; he places the heavenly voice in **June 1838**. So when God allegedly spoke, "he had already been excommunicated" — the voice cannot be a command to *leave* the Church. *Rating: strong; the excommunication date is documented.* [^2]
2. **The full context names the real referent: flee the Danites.** In context Whitmer recounts a "secret organization" (the Danites, under Dr. Avard) administering oaths and threatening dissenters; "my persecutions for trying to show them their errors became of such a nature that I had to leave the Latter-day Saints, and as I rode on horseback out of Far West... the voice of God from heaven spake to me." The command was to **leave the place** for safety, "to avoid what he felt was persecution," not to renounce the Church or his witness. *Rating: strong; the quote, read in full, is about fleeing Far West.* [^3]
3. **No contradiction with the Book of Mormon witness.** "There is no difficulty or contradiction in saying that God could have both borne witness to David Whitmer of the authenticity of the Book of Mormon and warned him to flee for his life by leaving Far West in 1838." The two claims are compatible. *Rating: strong; the dilemma dissolves once both halves are stated accurately.* [^4]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic can still press: even granting the *flee-Far-West* reading, Whitmer *did* permanently separate from the LDS Church and spent decades convinced its leadership had gone "into error and blindness," yet never recanted his Book of Mormon testimony — so a skeptic might argue Whitmer's *spiritual discernment* was unreliable (he affirmed the plates but rejected the prophet who produced them), which complicates using his witness as unqualified support. The contradiction Horn sought is real at the level of *which* of Whitmer's spiritual claims one accepts.

### Counter-objection
Smoot's frame (cf. [[Contradictory Sources Must Be Weighed Not Merely Counted]]): that Whitmer kept his Book of Mormon testimony *even while estranged and hostile to the leadership* is precisely what makes it strong evidence — an estranged witness with every incentive to recant did not (see [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]]). Accepting his sensory testimony of the plates does not commit one to every later doctrinal judgment he made, any more than trusting a hostile eyewitness's report of an event requires adopting his opinions about its meaning.

## Best Quotes
> "Whitmer was excommunicated from the church on April 13th, 1838. According to this passage, Whitmer said God told him in June of 1838 to separate himself from the Saints in Far West Missouri — why? Specifically to avoid what he felt was persecution coming from the Danites." — [[Stephen Smoot]] [^2]

> "There is no difficulty or contradiction in saying that God could have both borne witness to David Whitmer of the authenticity of the Book of Mormon and warned him to flee for his life by leaving Far West in 1838." — Smoot [^4]
