# Native American Testimony That Horses Were Always Here

## The Claim

[[Daniel Johnson]] and [[Kirk Magleby]] argue that the standard "Spanish brought the horse" story rests on a **Eurocentric reading that discounts Indigenous testimony**. Many Native nations preserve origin myths in which the horse is co-eval with their own creation ("we've never not had the horse"), and at every European point of contact explorers found nations *already* possessing mature horse cultures — facts hard to square with recent Spanish introduction, and now partly vindicated by a 2023 *Science* reappraisal pushing Plains horses earlier than historians supposed. Magleby ties this to the Book of Mormon as itself a neglected "ancient American codex" (John Sorenson's phrase) that, like the Indigenous voice, deserves a seat at the table.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

### 1. Mature horse cultures at first contact

Wherever Europeans made first contact they recorded nations that *already* had breeding stock, horsemanship, animal husbandry, and horse-based trade — Blackfoot (1754), Ute (c. 1600–1700), Missouri / Arikara (1682), Tejas and the Sonora Valley (1567). Johnson reads the consistency — "as far back as we get first contact... they're finding native nations that already had the horse" and a whole culture around it — as evidence the animal was not a sudden European novelty everywhere.[^2]

### 2. The Spanish expeditions were poor seed populations

Spanish and Portuguese expeditions favored **solid-color stallions** — in one case "550-plus stallions, two mares" — numbers unsuited to founding a breeding herd. First-expedition horses repeatedly died at sea, were killed in battle, were butchered for jerky in extremity, or (in one escape) were left behind and slaughtered by natives. Johnson concludes the odds that escaped Spanish stock seeded the continent-wide Plains horse complex are "not good odds."[^3]

### 3. Yvette Running Horse Collin and the "Eurocentric mythology"

[[Yvette Running Horse Collin]], a Native scholar, wrote her University of Alaska Fairbanks dissertation deconstructing what she calls a **"Eurocentric mythology"** — the assumption that Europeans had to "bring" horses to a benighted people. Her thesis ("we as Native Americans have always had the horse... give us a place at the table") has been controversial precisely because "not everybody's prepared to believe an indigenous voice."[^4]

### 4. The 2023 *Science* cover

The 31 March 2023 cover article in *Science* reported, from "real science" (including Alaskan permafrost DNA and excavations across the American interior), that horses were among Plains tribes (Apache, Pawnee, Comanche, Shoshone, etc.) "at least a century" earlier than the documentary record had shown — back to about **1600** — and "opens the door" to earlier still.[^5] See [[Late Pleistocene-Holocene Survival of New World Horses]].

### 5. Independent corroboration — Ward Radio / Daniel Johnson

The same researcher ([[Daniel Johnson]]) makes a parallel case on [[Ward Radio]] (via [[Jonah Barnes]]): the Spanish brought mostly **mares** in small numbers (poor founding stock), native horse sightings appear before the Pueblo Revolt (Blackfoot 1754, Shoshone before 1700, Jamestown wild horses 1669, Kiowa 1590, Sonora Valley 1567), and horses pervade Native creation myths and clothing at first contact. He pairs this with a demographic-math argument that escaped Spanish stock could not have spread continent-wide so fast. See [[The Spanish Horse Math Cannot Explain Continent-Wide Horses by 1567]].[^6]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The mainstream account survives most of this. (a) The 2023 *Science* study (Taylor et al.) is regularly *misread*: it confirmed rapid Indigenous integration of horses in the 1600s and that horses spread *ahead* of European records — but its own genomic and isotopic data trace those horses to **post-Columbian Spanish lineages**, not to a surviving native population; it explicitly does *not* support a continuous pre-Columbian domestic horse. (b) A breeding pair, not 550, is enough to found a herd, and the Spanish ranches in the Caribbean, Mexico, and New Mexico (esp. after 1598) supplied ample stock; the Pueblo Revolt of 1680 dispersed thousands. (c) Origin myths are sacred testimony about meaning and belonging, not datable chronicles, and many "always had the horse" traditions are post-1600 reframings — using them as paleontological evidence risks the genetic fallacy the LDS side elsewhere warns against. (d) Running Horse Collin's dissertation is from a folklore/Indigenous-studies program and has not been corroborated by the paleogenetics. So the honest reply is that Indigenous testimony rightly corrects a *condescending narrative of dependence* without thereby overturning the *dating* — the horse Native nations mastered so brilliantly can be Spanish-derived and still be authentically "theirs."
