# The Liahona's Second Spindle Pointed to Pictographic Symbols

## The Claim

Historian [[Don Bradley]], on [[Ward Radio]], reconstructs the **function of the Liahona's second spindle** — a detail the surviving Book of Mormon never explains. [[1 Nephi 16.13|1 Nephi 16]] says the ball had two spindles and that "the one pointed the way we should go," but the abridgment drops what the *other* spindle did. Triangulating the text with two documented 19th-century sources, Bradley argues the device combined **three modes of communication**: the **first spindle gave direction**; the **second spindle pointed around the ball's rim to a pictographic symbol** representing *what* they were traveling toward (food, water, game, etc.); and **changing writing on the spindles** added verbal messages. So when the travelers asked God for, say, wild game, the second spindle would indicate the game-symbol and the first would point the heading to it.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The text itself flags the gap and supplies the "writing" element — rate: strong (textual)
[[1 Nephi 16.13|1 Nephi 16]] explicitly describes two spindles but explains only one, and elsewhere notes that "there was also written upon them a new writing... which gave us understanding concerning the ways of the Lord; and it was written and changed from time to time." The changing writing is in the text, anchoring the "verbal" mode Bradley reconstructs.[^1]

### 2. Fayette Lapham (via Joseph Smith Sr.) supplies the second spindle's purpose — rate: moderate (documented source)
[[Fayette Lapham]], a relation of [[Martin Harris]]'s mother, interviewed Joseph Smith Sr. (~1830) and reported the Liahona had "two pointers, one pointing steadily the way they should go, the other the way to where they could get provisions and other necessaries." This is a documented early account that directly fills the textual gap.[^2]

### 3. Francis Gladden Bishop (via Martin Harris) supplies the design — rate: moderate (documented source)
[[Francis Gladden Bishop]], a Kirtland associate of Martin Harris, published a description traceable to Harris: a brass ball ~3 inches across, two steel spindles in opposite directions, **12 squares around each point**, and between the 24 squares **figures of vegetation, animals, running streams, water** — i.e., a pictographic dial. Bradley uses Bishop only as corroboration because his information demonstrably tracks Harris on otherwise-unique details (e.g., plate size). The convergence of independent text + Lapham + Bishop is the argument's strength.[^3]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A skeptic raises source-critical worries. (a) **Gladden Bishop is a compromised source** — he published Harris's information *as his own revelations* while running a breakaway sect, so a critic will distrust anything traced through him, even if Bradley brackets that by cross-checking against Harris's distinctives. (b) **Fayette Lapham's interview is late and secondhand** (recollection of a conversation with Joseph Smith Sr.), so its precision about "provisions and other necessaries" may be reconstruction. (c) **The reconstruction is necessarily speculative** — it fills a gap the canonical text left blank, so it cannot be more than a well-supported hypothesis about a lost narrative, not doctrine. A Latter-day Saint reply: that is exactly the epistemic status Bradley claims for it — a reconstruction, offered because *three independent lines* (the canonical "two spindles" + changing writing, Lapham, and Harris-via-Bishop) converge on the same picture, which is stronger than any one source alone; and a pictographic-plus-verbal instrument coheres with the device's described use of guiding a migrating company to specific resources.
