# The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate

#type/claim

## The Claim

The common charge that Latter-day Saints invented the limited-geography / other-peoples reading *as a retreat* once DNA became a problem is historically false. Latter-day Saint thinkers had abandoned the strict hemispheric, empty-continent reading decades before Watson and Crick — so it is not "moving the goalposts." The 1981 introduction's change from the Lamanites being the "principal ancestors" to "among the ancestors" of the American Indians is a clarification of a modern *editorial* framing, never a revealed text.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The interpretation has a century-plus pedigree

[[Stephen Smoot]] and [[Neal Rappleye]] trace the limited-scope, other-peoples reading well before DNA was an issue: authors in the **1920s** (e.g. "Janne Sjodahl") already doubted the text described the whole hemisphere or every Native American group; [[Matt Roper]] has documented antecedents for both the limited-geography and the other-populations ideas reaching back to the **1840s**. DNA (discovered 1953) post-dates all of this.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a datable intellectual-history claim with named scholarship).

### 2. Senior leaders were already open to other peoples

The hosts cite a general-conference statement by Anthony W. Ivins of the First Presidency that the Book of Mormon is *not* a history of everyone who ever came to the Americas — "there were people here before and there were people who came after." Even [[Bruce R. McConkie]], who chaired the 1981 scripture committee that *added* the introduction, was on record allowing for other migrants. So the very people who wrote the "principal ancestors" line did not hold the empty-continent reading.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (leader statements, reported second-hand from the book).

### 3. "Principal ancestors" still implies others — and was never scripture

Even the original wording "principal ancestors" entails *other* ancestors (principal ≠ exclusive). And the introduction is a 1981 composition reflecting the dominant 1970s interpretation, not text on the gold plates — "no one claimed that introduction was given by revelation." Adjusting an editorial frame as knowledge advances is the honest thing to do, not a concession of error in scripture.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a clean logical + bibliographic point).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can fairly press the [[The Least-Conjecture Test and the Goalpost-Moving Pattern|goalpost-moving]] worry: the *popular* and even some prophetic teaching genuinely was hemispheric and empty-continent, so the limited reading — however old in scholarly circles — looks like a convenient fallback now deployed to absorb the DNA data. Smoot concedes the "heads-I-win, tails-you-lose" bind is real: change with new data and you are accused of retreat; refuse and you are a backward fundamentalist. The reply, following [[Blake Ostler]], is that believers are entitled to read their own scripture against the best available science, and that the limited reading was the *most rigorous* reading on offer long before DNA — so the DNA objection was arguably attacking a popular strawman, not the strongest position. See [[The Empty Continent Assumption Underlies the DNA Objection]].

## Best Quotes

> "There's this narrative that after we discovered DNA... that's when the Mormon apologists retreated into the limited geography theory. No. We can trace as far back as the 1920s authors who are like, well, it doesn't seem to be describing the entire hemisphere... this is before we even knew DNA existed."[^2]

> "Even if you say principal ancestors, that still means other people. They're not the exclusive ancestors."[^4]

> "No one claimed that introduction was given by revelation. It wasn't handed down on gold plates to Bruce McConkie and the scripture committee. The introduction is not the keystone."[^4]
