# Parenthetical Additions to the Book of Mormon Reflect a Developing Translation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** Some later editions **add** text — parenthetical clarifications
inserted by [[Joseph Smith]]. Worse, one such addition may stem from
conversations with [[Parley P. Pratt]]. The critic asks: "If one of Joseph
Smith's friends can suggest an addition to the text, what does that say about its
supernatural translation and origin?... was Joseph allowing others to influence
his translation?"[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Additions fit a developing, spirit-over-letter model

Saints Unscripted: the changes show "the translation process was still
developing," and "Joseph likely would have made more small changes and edits if
he had not been murdered." On the LDS view of revelation, revisiting a passage to
better convey the spirit of the message is normal, not suspicious.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Conceptual. Consistent with the model in
> [[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin]]; persuasive only
> to those who already accept that model of revelation.

### 2. The "words fall short" analogy

The host appeals to ordinary experience: "How do you fully capture your
experiences in words? Some experiences are so powerful and beautiful that words
simply fall short. The same can be true with revelation and translation."
Refining language afterward is what any communicator does.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Rhetorically effective analogy; not independent evidence of
> divine origin.

### 3. Influence ≠ corruption

That a conversation with Pratt may have prompted a clarifying insertion does not
entail that the *content* is false — collaborators can sharpen wording without
inventing doctrine.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic's sharpest version: a genuinely *revealed* translation of an ancient
plate should not be open to friendly suggestions from contemporaries. If Pratt's
input shapes the text, the line between "translating an ancient record by God's
power" and "Joseph composing/redacting with help" has effectively dissolved —
which is exactly the naturalistic thesis. Appealing to "the translation was still
developing" concedes that the 1830 text was *not* the finished word of God, which
is itself a striking admission for a book marketed as scripture brought forth by
miracle.

## Best Quotes

> "If one of Joseph Smith's friends can suggest an addition to the text, what does
> that say about its supernatural translation and origin?" — the critic[^1]

> "Was Joseph allowing others to influence his translation? This question
> highlights that perhaps the translation process was still developing."[^1]

> "Some experiences are so powerful and beautiful that words simply fall short. The
> same can be true with revelation and translation."[^3]
