# Manuscript Count Attests Transmission Not That Events Happened

## The Claim
An evangelical guest argues the Bible is better attested than the Book of Mormon because of its 5,800+ Greek New Testament manuscripts. [[Luke Hansen]] answers that she is **conflating two different things**: a large pile of agreeing copies attests that *the version we have now is close to the version they had then* (faithful transmission) — it does **not** attest that the *events described actually happened*. And on the metric that matters, the Book of Mormon is *better* attested: it is an **original**, not the end of a long copy-chain reconstructed back toward a lost autograph.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The conflation, named (rated: strong, conceptual)
"What she's conflating: if we have a lot of manuscripts that line up well with what we have now, that means the events ... happened. We don't. The only thing that that means is that the version we have now is pretty close to the version they had then. That doesn't mean the events are more likely to have happened or not."[^1]

### 2. The Book of Mormon is the autograph (rated: moderate)
"The Book of Mormon's better attested. We have the original. We don't have a long trail of copies that we're saying ... is pretty darn close to what it originally was. The Book of Mormon is the original thing." Manuscript proliferation is the *symptom of having lost the original*; the Book of Mormon hasn't.[^1]

### 3. The eyewitness asymmetry (rated: moderate)
The "5,000 saw Moses" / mass-attestation appeal fails the same way: a text written centuries later *reporting* that people saw an event is not the same as those people's affidavits. The Book of Mormon, by contrast, has firsthand statements — the [[Three Witnesses|three]] and [[Eight Witnesses|eight]] witnesses — plus the unexplained provenance of the plates themselves, which "is good historical attestation right there." (Cf. [[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]].)[^1]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic responds: (a) manuscript abundance *does* bear on historicity indirectly — multiple early, geographically independent copies constrain how much legend could accrete, narrowing the gap to the events; (b) "the Book of Mormon is the original" assumes its own provenance (the plates were unavailable for examination and the printer's manuscript is a dictation copy), so calling it an unmediated autograph is contestable; and (c) the witness statements are themselves a small, socially-networked group, which critics weigh differently than a broad manuscript tradition. The LDS reply: granting all that, the guest's specific inference — "more manuscripts ⇒ the events happened" — is a category error, and that error is the only thing this note targets.
