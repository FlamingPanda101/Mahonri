# Alma 32 Light Alludes to Genesis 1 Creation

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]]'s famous test of the word — "is it not real? I say unto you, yea, because it is light; and whatsoever is light is good, because it is discernible" — is not only an epistemology of faith but a deliberate allusion to the **creation account of Genesis 1**. Citing biblical scholar [[David Bokovoy]]'s 2014 study "The Theological Use of Biblical Creation in Alma 32," [[Kaden Alvie]] argues that the Book of Mormon here picks up Genesis 1's movement from *unformed to formed*: the Spirit moves over the face of the waters, the earth is "without form and void," and **light is the first discernible thing** ("let there be light"). Alma's logic — *light is what is discernible, therefore real* — mirrors that creative act, where God turns formless chaos into a formed, light-filled, discernible order.[^1]

The apologetic payload is twofold. First, it supplies further Book of Mormon support for [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference|creation *ex materia*]]: Genesis 1, as read here, presupposes pre-existing "waters" that God forms rather than matter called from nothing (see [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]]). Second, it is an argument from the text's sophistication: weaving subtle creation theology into Alma 32 "off the top of his head with no notes and no prior study" is, the hosts say, what critics must attribute to [[Joseph Smith]] dictating into a hat — a high bar for a naturalistic account of the Book of Mormon.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **A peer-style scholarly source, not a bare proof-text (moderate).** The reading is credited to [[David Bokovoy]] (PhD, Hebrew Bible, Brandeis), "a great scholar," in a 2014 paper on Book of Mormon authors' use of the Old Testament — i.e., it is a documented intertextual argument, not an ad hoc parallel.[^1] Bokovoy specifically situates the allusion in the **J source** (Genesis 2–3) while reading the formless-waters imagery of Genesis 1 alongside it.[^3]
2. **The "discernible" hinge matches Genesis 1's structure (supporting).** In Genesis 1, light is created first and is what makes the world *visible/discernible*; Alma's criterion of reality is precisely discernibility ("whatsoever is light… is discernible"). The conceptual overlap (light = the first discernible, ordering act) is the load-bearing link.[^1]
3. **It coheres with the broader *ex materia* case (supporting).** The same primordial "waters" the hosts press elsewhere (against [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo|2 Maccabees]] and in [[2 Peter 3.5-6|2 Peter 3:5–6]]) are the backdrop here: the earth "exists in this sense but it's without form… and then light is the first discernible thing."[^4]

### Steelman of the Skeptical / Catholic Reply

The critic can resist on two fronts. **Exegetically**, allusion-claims are easy to over-read: "light," "discernible," and "good" are common biblical vocabulary, and Alma 32's overt purpose is an experiment in faith (its standard reading; cf. [[Alma 32.27-43]]), not a treatise on cosmogony — so the Genesis 1 connection may be the scholar's construction rather than the text's intent. Even granting the allusion, it does no anti-*ex-nihilo* work unless Genesis 1 *itself* teaches creation from pre-existing matter, which the classical theist disputes (the "waters" can be read as themselves created in 1:1 before being ordered in 1:2). **On Book-of-Mormon origins**, a 19th-century author steeped in the King James Bible could plausibly echo Genesis 1's light-and-waters imagery without revelation, since that imagery was ambient in the culture; sophistication of allusion is suggestive but not probative of antiquity. Thus the argument strengthens the LDS reading for those already open to it, but does not by itself refute *creatio ex nihilo* or settle the Book of Mormon's provenance.
