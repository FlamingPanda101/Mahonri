# Alma Baptizes by the Authority of El Shaddai at the Waters of Mormon

## The Claim

[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues that **Alma the Elder baptizes at the Waters of Mormon by the authority of the divine Mother**. The [[Mosiah 18|Mosiah 18]] scene is, on his reading, densely marked with divine-feminine iconography: the place is "infested by wild beasts" (the *hayot*, the living creatures that belong to **Shaddai**, the Mistress of beasts), it has "a fountain of **pure** water" (the pure waters that flow from the tree of life / the Virgin), and "a thicket of small trees" (the tree-goddess). At the center, the man whose name puns on *ʿalmah* — "**Virgin**" — declares, "I baptize thee, **having authority from the Almighty God**" ([[Mosiah 18.13|Mosiah 18:13]]) — which back-translates to **El Shaddai**, the "breasted God." Butler reads this as Alma acting by the authority of the Mother (or "the father and the mother simultaneously"). This is the episode's headline payoff and is **new to the vault**, building on the existing Shaddai and Asherah notes. [^1] [^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Having authority from the Almighty God" = El Shaddai — rate: moderate (back-translation is the load-bearing premise)
The baptismal formula in Mosiah 18:13 reads "having authority from the Almighty God." Since "Almighty" is the KJV's standard rendering of **Shaddai**, the phrase corresponds to **El Shaddai** — and because [[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother|Shaddai is the "breasted" (mother) name]], Butler reads Alma's authority as deriving from the divine Mother (or the Father-with-the-Mother). Pop-quiz framing in the source: "By what authority does Alma baptize Helam? … having authority from El Shaddai." [^1]

### 2. The beasts mark the presence of Shaddai — rate: moderate (depends on the Shaddai-as-Mistress-of-beasts reading)
Mosiah 18:4 notes the place "having been infested, by times or at seasons, by wild beasts." Butler ties this to his earlier argument that **Shaddai is associated with the *hayot* (living creatures)** — drawn from Isaiah 56:9 ("all ye beasts of the field/Shaddai, come to devour") and Eve as "the mother of every living thing" ([[2 Nephi 9.21|2 Nephi 9:21]]: Christ suffers "the pains of every living creature… who belong to the family of Adam"). So the beasts of Mormon are "the *hayot* that belong to Shaddai," guarding the holy of holies. [^3]

### 3. Pure water + thicket of trees = the Virgin/tree-goddess — rate: moderate (coheres with the Lehi tree-of-life pattern)
The "fountain of **pure** water" and "thicket of small trees" reprise the **pure waters and the tree** that, in Lehi's vision, are "a representation of the love of God" and stand for the Virgin/tree of life ([[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]]). Where the tree of life is, the water is *pure*; where it is not, it is "the filthy waters of hell." Mormon's *pure* fountain beside a *thicket of trees* thus signals the divine-feminine sanctuary. [^2]

### 4. The "how blessed are they" Asherah-echo — rate: weak-moderate (wordplay)
Butler notes Mosiah 18:30 ("how **blessed** are they") — the *ashrei* ("O the happiness of") beatitude-formula that elsewhere puns on **Asher/Asherah** (the same wordplay running through 1 Nephi 8's "desirable to make one **happy**"). So even the benediction over the baptized carries the Asherah resonance. [^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The reading is evocative but heavily inferential, and the hosts themselves disclaim certainty and any "ecclesiastical call to action." (a) **"The Almighty God" is ordinary KJV scripture**: Joseph Smith could render divine authority with a stock biblical phrase that needs no Hebrew throne-name; the El-Shaddai back-translation is the apologist's reconstruction, not anything stated in the text — and the LDS reading of Mosiah 18:13 has always taken "the Almighty God" as the *Father* (or Christ), with priesthood authority, not the Mother. (b) **The "beasts" are plot, not theology**: Mosiah 18:4 explains the beasts as the reason the *place was uninhabited and safe for secret meetings* (Alma is hiding from King Noah) — a mundane narrative detail, not a coded *hayot*-of-Shaddai motif. (c) **Pure water and trees are the natural setting for a baptism**, and "pure water" is exactly what a baptism narrative requires; reading them as the tree-goddess imports a meaning the text never states. (d) The whole edifice rests on a **chain of contested premises** — Shaddai = breasted Mother, Shaddai = Mistress of beasts, Alma = Virgin — each individually disputable, so the cumulative case is only as strong as its weakest link. Butler's defense is *convergence*: the name-pun (Virgin), the beasts, the pure water, the trees, the El-Shaddai authority formula, and the Asherah beatitude all land in the same two chapters, which he takes as designed patterning rather than coincidence — while explicitly granting that the Restoration's living priesthood, not this reading, is doctrinally authoritative.

## Best Quotes

> "By what authority does Alma baptize Helam? … 'I baptize thee, having authority from the Almighty God' — that is El Shaddai. It's there." [^1]

> "Here we are in Mormon and there are the *hayot* that belong to Shaddai… a fountain of pure water… a thicket of small trees. So the water and the trees are the symbol of the Virgin; the beasts belong to Shaddai; Alma is there — his name is Virgin." [^2]

> "Maybe Alma's authority is from the father and the mother simultaneously — but it is marked repeatedly in these two chapters." [^3]

## LDS Connection

This is the Ward Radio episode's thesis statement — the title's "Was Alma a High Priest of Heavenly Mother?" — and it converts a string of existing divine-feminine arguments ([[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]], [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]], [[Yahweh El Shaddai Is a Throne-Name Preserved in the Book of Mormon]]) into a *specific Book of Mormon ordinance scene*. It pairs with the onomastic case in [[Mormon Puns on Almas Name as Virgin Concealed and Young Man]] and presupposes the [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|Deuteronomist erasure]] of the Mother that the Book of Mormon, predating that final edit, is said to preserve.
