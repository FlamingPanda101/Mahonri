# Nephi and Isaiah Shared the Same Ascension Vision

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jonah Barnes]], with [[David John Butler]] and [[Cardon Ellis]], argues that [[Nephi]]'s tree-of-life vision in [[1 Nephi 11]] and the heavenly vision of [[Isaiah the Prophet|Isaiah]] in the pseudepigraphal **Ascension of Isaiah** are the *same* experience — a repeatable, "modular" temple ascension in which an angel-guide walks the seer through the Messiah's birth, ministry, and crucifixion. The decisive Book of Mormon hook is [[2 Nephi 11.2-3|2 Nephi 11:2-3]], where Nephi says Isaiah "verily saw my Redeemer, **even as I have seen him**," and that Jacob saw him too. On this reading, Nephi knew an Isaiah tradition — preserved on the brass plates and surviving today only in apocrypha like the Ascension of Isaiah — that the Masoretic Old Testament no longer contains.[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. "Even as I have seen him" implies a shared visionary form — rate: the load-bearing textual datum
[[2 Nephi 11.2-3|2 Nephi 11:2-3]] does not merely say Isaiah saw the Redeemer; it says he saw him *in the same manner Nephi did* ("even as I have seen him"), and extends the claim to Jacob. Barnes recounts stumbling on the verse and being struck that Nephi frames his own [[1 Nephi 11]] vision as the same kind of seeing Isaiah had. *Strong as the one anchor that ties the apocryphal parallel to an explicit Book of Mormon statement; everything else is corroboration.*[^2]

### 2. Verse-by-verse parallels between 1 Nephi 11 and the Ascension of Isaiah — rate: striking but the hosts supply the table
The panel claims the two visions track "tick for tick": the only-begotten / divine sonship, the Spirit "in the form of a man," a virgin who conceives (Nazareth/Bethlehem), the descent of the Son, the world stretching forth its hand against him, and the lifting-up by "lawless men." In [[1 Nephi 11]] the angel's relief that Nephi *believes* the divine sonship (1 Nephi 11:6) is read as the same anxiety the Ascension of Isaiah dramatizes — a doctrine being lost. *Moderate: the correspondences are real features of both texts, but the curated "table" is Barnes's (from* Key to the Keystone*), and a virgin-birth/suffering-Messiah vision is a common Christian shape.*[^1]

### 3. The angel-as-priest "ordinance" reading — rate: speculative/theological
Following [[David John Butler]], the panel proposes the repeatable vision is a **temple ordinance**: an angel ("messenger") functioning as a priest conducts both Isaiah and Nephi through the ascent (temple = mountain = heaven; angels = stars = priests). This explains how two prophets centuries apart could receive "the same" vision. *Weak as proof — it is an interpretive overlay — but it is the mechanism the claim needs and coheres with the vault's temple-cosmology notes (cf. [[Isaiahs Vision and the Eucharist]]).*[^3]

### 4. Internal "breadcrumbs" of suppression — rate: suggestive
[[Isaiah 30.9-10|Isaiah 30:9-10]] (people telling seers "see not… speak unto us smooth things") is read as Isaiah's own complaint that hard messianic prophecy was censored, dovetailing with the brass-plates / [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes|Great Forsaken]] thesis that a fuller Isaiah once existed. *Moderate as internal corroboration; it shows a motif of suppression, not a specific lost vision.*[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View
The critic's strongest replies: (a) **Dating.** The Ascension of Isaiah is a composite Christian-era work (Norelli and others date the "Vision" sections to c. AD 150-200, with clear Christian christology); its parallels to Nephi's vision are best explained as *shared Christian tradition*, not a pre-exilic Isaiah Nephi could have read — so the parallel, if anything, looks like 19th-century access to broadly Christian motifs rather than ancient dependence (the episode itself concedes the manuscripts are late and pivots to "date the idea"; see [[Dating an Idea Differs From Dating Its Manuscript]]). (b) **Genericness.** A virgin conceiving a divine Son who is rejected and crucified is the basic gospel outline; finding it in two visionary texts does not require a single lost source. (c) **"Even as I have seen him"** can mean simply that both prophets saw *the Redeemer* (the same referent), not that they underwent an identical, transferable rite — the "modular ordinance" is read *into* the verse. (d) The verse-by-verse table is a partisan harmonization; a neutral reader can find as many disanalogies (Nephi's Liahona/tree imagery has no Ascension-of-Isaiah counterpart). The defensible core is modest and genuinely interesting: the Book of Mormon independently claims pre-exilic prophets saw a suffering, divine Messiah (cf. [[Apocryphal Prophet-Martyrdoms Corroborate the Book of Mormons Slain Messianic Prophets]]), and later-surfacing pseudepigrapha show such visions were *imaginable* in antiquity — which blunts the "no one talked about Christ before Christ" objection without proving direct textual descent.

## LDS Connection
This is the Isaiah-specific instance of the vault's brass-plates engine: where the surviving Old Testament looks thin, locate a fuller tradition the canon lost ([[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain]], [[The School of Isaiah Preserved First-Temple Tradition in Exile]]). It also feeds the temple-cosmology reading of prophetic visions as ordinances. The honest takeaway: [[2 Nephi 11.2-3|2 Nephi 11:2-3]] really does claim a shared seeing, and that is a non-trivial datum — but the Ascension-of-Isaiah parallel's force rises or falls on how one dates the *idea* versus the manuscript, which the next note addresses.
