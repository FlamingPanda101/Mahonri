# The Newark Earthworks Encode the Plan of Salvation

## The Claim
[[Rod Meldrum]] argues that the **Newark Earthworks** in Newark, Ohio — a ~2,000-year-old Hopewell complex of a Great Circle, a square, and an octagon connected by walled avenues — geometrically encode the entire Latter-day Saint **Plan of Salvation**, and that no other religion possesses anything comparable. He treats it as a Hopewell "temple complex" (today four square miles in extent, partly under an 18-hole golf course) and as evidence of a Hebraic, covenant people in North America.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (as Meldrum presents it)

### 1. The geometric vocabulary is Hebraic
Meldrum assigns symbolic values: a **square** = the earth (four corners); a **circle** = God / eternity; an **octagon** = the geometric mean between circle and square. He notes the Kirtland and Nauvoo temple spires share this square-base / octagon-middle / dome-or-ball-top progression, which he reads as earth → Christ → God, the octagon being "the seal of Melchizedek" (the priesthood "after the holy order of the Son of God," cf. [[D&C 107]]).[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak. The square/circle/octagon symbolism is imposed interpretively; octagons are common worldwide (baptisteries, towers) and carry no intrinsic "Melchizedek" meaning.

### 2. A walkable Plan-of-Salvation sequence
Reading right-to-left and counterclockwise (as, he says, a Hebrew would), Meldrum traces: Great Circle = premortal existence; a deliberately *broken* path between circle and square = the veil of forgetfulness; the square = telestial earth; from the square, three exits (one dead-ends; one washed out by a creek; one vicarious path with multiple "checkpoints" = spirit prison / proxy work; one direct path with a single checkpoint for those "born under the covenant") leading to the **octagon** (terrestrial / Christ); then a narrow neck into the final circle = celestial. The orders, he says, are "in the right geometric configuration."[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak. This is a richly post-hoc overlay; the earthworks were surveyed in the 1800s and the "checkpoints"/"paths" are interpreted to fit modern LDS cosmology.

### 3. Built in peace by religious conviction
Meldrum stresses the walls (~30 ft) and ditches (14–15 ft) bear no defensive palisade, implying construction "in a time of great peace" by religious motive, not slaves — and cites a Native tradition of a five-tribe "Great Law of Peace" in the area. He dates part of it to the "Jaredite time frame" with a Hopewell-era yellow-clay remodel.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Weak-to-moderate. The non-defensive character and astronomical sophistication of Newark are genuine and academically recognized; the Jaredite/Hopewell dating and religious-meaning inferences are Meldrum's.

## Steelman of the Opposing View
Mainstream archaeology recognizes the Newark Earthworks as a monumental, astronomically aligned Hopewell ceremonial center (the octagon tracks the 18.6-year lunar standstill) — a real and impressive site, now a UNESCO World Heritage inscription. But none of that supports a Plan-of-Salvation reading: the symbolic assignments (square=earth, octagon=Christ) are supplied by Meldrum, not derived from Hopewell culture, and "walk it counterclockwise and it maps premortality→celestial glory" is unfalsifiable pattern-matching of exactly the kind that makes Heartland geography academically isolated. The Nauvoo-temple-spire comparison is anachronistic (a 19th-century building cannot interpret a pre-Columbian earthwork), and the same "no other religion has this" rhetoric could be run on any complex multi-enclosure site. The defensible residue is small: Newark is a sophisticated, non-defensive, astronomically aligned ceremonial earthwork built by a settled people — interesting, but neutral between geographies and carrying no demonstrable Israelite or restoration content. The vault rates the Plan-of-Salvation decoding tier-C and speculative.

## Best Quotes
> "The entire Plan of Salvation is actually encoded in them... it actually has every element of the Plan of Salvation as only the restored Gospel of Jesus Christ has, yet this is a 2,000-year-old earthwork."[^1]

> "The octagon is the seal of Melchizedek... you can only get there through Christ, and that's why there's this symbolism of the octagon."[^2]

> "Show me something like that in Mesoamerica, and I'll eat my hat — mega hat."[^3]

## LDS Connection
The Newark set-piece is the architectural centerpiece of Meldrum's **Heartland** apologetic, paired with his Mound-Builder-suppression narrative (the documentary *Lost Civilizations of North America*) and Brill Hernandez's claim of "major Hebrew symbolism" in Hopewell architecture (see [[The Great Lakes Are the Book of Mormons Seas]]). It functions as the North-American counterpart to Mesoamerican temple arguments and connects to the broader Heartland-vs-Mesoamerica dispute ([[The New York Hill Is Not the Book of Mormon Cumorah]]). As the steelman notes, the site is real and remarkable; the restoration-cosmology decoding is the speculative, tier-C layer.
