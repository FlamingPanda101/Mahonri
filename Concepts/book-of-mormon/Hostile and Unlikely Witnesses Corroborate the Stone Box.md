# Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box

## The Claim

The strongest corroboration for the hole and stone box in the hill comes not from believers but from **enemies and unlikely witnesses** — people with motive to debunk Joseph Smith who nonetheless confirm the artifact's details. Under [[Josh Gailey]]'s **enemies + unlikely testimonies** criteria, these are the data a critic finds hardest to explain away.[^1] See [[The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon]].

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Lorenzo Saunders — the hostile "no fresh dirt" admission
[[Lorenzo Saunders]], a former treasure-hunting associate who "dedicated chunks of his life to disparage the Smiths," gave debunking interviews — yet in one he conceded the hole was "on the summit on the west side," matching [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Oliver Cowdery]] (knowledge he could only have from being there; he never went west).[^2] He further volunteered he examined it weeks after Joseph removed the plates and found the excavation old, with **no fresh dirt** — which, Gailey argues, unintentionally confirms Joseph's account of first opening the box in **1823** and only clearing it on annual visits thereafter.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a hostile source supplying location and chronology against his own interest is the episode's prize exhibit).

### 2. The money-diggers' actions as an enemy admission
Former associates and neighbors went to the hill, became convinced something of value had been taken, and dug, tore up the cooper's shop across the street, and hired diviners to locate it — even attempting to steal the "gold Bible."[^4] As with the Sanhedrin's "the disciples stole the body," their *behavior* concedes the hole and box. Crucially, this rebuts the "it was just the money-diggers' own hole" theory: the money-digger hole was on the wrong (northeast) side, and it was the diggers themselves who went looking because they believed Joseph had found something.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (the counter-explanation is self-defeating; the diggers are the ones inferring a removed artifact).

### 3. Unlikely witnesses to the plates themselves
- [[Isaac Hale]], Joseph's suspicious, never-converting father-in-law, demanded to see the plates and was let to **heft the box** — a hostile attestation to a real, weighty artifact.[^6]
- [[Josiah Stowell]], the financier the family thought Joseph had *swindled*, testified under oath that he glimpsed a corner as the frock slipped, describing it as a "greenish stone" — a detail no fabricator inventing "gold plates" would invent, and one explained by [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|tumbaga oxidation in firelight]].[^7] (His broader court defense is treated in [[Josiah Stowell Defends Joseph Smith]].)
- [[David Whitmer]] first heard of the artifact from **unsympathetic** townsmen on a train who said "we've been to the hill, we've seen" — an enemy source sparking his interest.[^8]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (individually mundane; cumulatively a pattern of skeptics confirming a tangible object).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can grant that hostile neighbors *believed* Joseph had something and still deny it was gold plates: their actions evidence rural treasure-credulity, not a real artifact, and a hole on a hill is over-determined by farming and money-digging. Saunders' "no fresh dirt" line can be read as confirming only *a* hole, not *the* plate-box, and his location detail might be absorbed from decades of published Mormon accounts rather than autopsy. Stowell's "greenish corner" could be a vague or embellished memory recorded long after. The reply is that the location was known to *both* faithful and hostile sources, the money-digger-hole theory fails on geography, and the greenish detail is undesigned and counter-expectation — but the believer should concede that hostile *belief* in an artifact is weaker than hostile *sight* of one, and that only a few of these witnesses (Hale, Stowell) claim actual contact.

## Best Quotes

### 4. A second independent presentation confirms the Saunders material
[[Josh Gailey]] re-runs the same 16-source analysis on a separate Ward Radio episode aimed at [[Dan Vogel]]'s east-side claim, independently recounting Saunders' "couldn't find the hole but knew it was on the west side" and the "large hole... dug a year or two before but no fresh dirt" lines — and adding that Vogel's contrary reading rests on a single late source (Frederick Mather, 1880).[^9] Two separately-produced presentations of the hostile-witness data strengthen its reliability. See [[Dan Vogel's East-Side Hole Is the Lone Source Against the West-Side Stone Box]].
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (corroborates the source-handling; same underlying documents, different presenter and framing).

## Best Quotes

> "He is a very unlikely testimony of the hole in the hill on the west side. But... he drops in one of his notes that the hole was on the summit on the west side. Exactly corroborating Joseph Smith, Oliver Cowdery, and others."[^2]

> "I examined [it]... it had been done years before, no fresh dirt. And I got so excited... You just unintentionally corroborated Joseph Smith's testimony of going every year, starting in 1823."[^3]

> "I can't show you. But here, hold the box. Shake it." (Joseph Smith to [[Isaac Hale]])[^6]

## LDS Connection

This is the load-bearing half of the [[The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon|empty-tomb parallel]]: the empty tomb is persuasive partly because *enemies* conceded the missing body, and the stone box gains the same kind of force from enemies and unlikely witnesses conceding the hole, its location, its age, and the heft of the object. It complements the friendly-witness material (e.g., [[Informal Witnesses of the Golden Plates]], [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]]) by supplying the hostile corroboration critics most demand.
