# The Bat Creek Stone Is Probably a Fake and Unrelated to the Book of Mormon

## The Claim

[[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]] gives a pros-and-cons assessment of the **Bat Creek Stone** — an inscribed stone reported from an 1889 Smithsonian mound excavation in Tennessee, later read as Paleo-Hebrew — and concludes it is **probably a fake**; and that *even if genuine*, its Hellenistic-period script post-dates Lehi, so it has **nothing to do with the Book of Mormon** regardless.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Galey states the case *for* it fairly — rate: n/a (steelman built in)
The arguments for authenticity: the Smithsonian initially logged it as genuine; Dr. Cyrus Gordon (1970s) identified the script as Hebrew (reading roughly "for Judah"), not Cherokee; and carbon dating of associated residue gave ~AD 427 (uncalibrated, ranging ~AD 32–700). Galey concedes the script is not there by accident — so it is either an authentic artifact or a deliberate fraud.[^2]

### 2. The discoverer was later caught forging — rate: strong (credibility)
The stone was found by [[John Emmert]], the *only* person in the mound, who was ambitious to rise at the Smithsonian and was *later associated with forging characters on stones at other sites*. As Galey puts it, if a man with several arson cases is near a burned chapel, the planted-forgery explanation is "more probable."[^3]

### 3. The inscription matches a Masonic encyclopedia plate — rate: moderate (source of the forgery)
A *General History, Cyclopedia and Dictionary of Freemasonry*, widely available at the time, contains essentially the exact phrase written in basically the same way it appears on the stone — a plausible source the forger copied, "kind of a tell" (compare the mint-mark tells on [[Mark Hofmann]]'s forged coins).[^4]

### 4. The carbon date is irrelevant, and the script is too late anyway — rate: strong (method + chronology)
Carbon dating the mound's *organic residue* dates the *mound material*, not the carving — so a stone planted in the mound would inherit an ancient date regardless. And the Hellenistic-period Paleo-Hebrew script post-dates [[Lehi]] and Nephi (c. 600 BC) by centuries; even a *genuine* Hebrew inscription of that type could not be Nephite. No other inscribed stone has ever been found in any Tennessee mound. See [[Provenance Is the Standard for Authenticating Book of Mormon Artifacts]].[^5]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The authenticity case has live defenders and the verdict is not unanimous. (a) Forensic geologist Scott Wolter examined the stone and judged it legitimate, and Cyrus Gordon's Hebrew reading was a serious scholarly proposal — so "probably fake" is a *probabilistic* judgment, not a settled fact; the Grolier Codex shows poor-provenance objects can be vindicated ([[Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance]]). (b) The "it came from a controlled Smithsonian dig" point genuinely distinguishes it from black-market objects, and a defender notes the Smithsonian only declared it fake *after* realizing it was Hebrew — raising the bias question. (c) The Emmert-forged-elsewhere charge requires the citation Jerry Grover promised to supply; until documented, it is an accusation. (d) The Masonic-source match is suggestive, not proof of copying. *However*, Galey's decisive move sidesteps all of this: even granting authenticity, the *script's date* excludes a Book-of-Mormon connection — so for vault purposes the stone is irrelevant either way, which is the cautious and defensible bottom line.
