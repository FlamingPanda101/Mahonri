# The Quran Parallels the Doctrine and Covenants Not the Book of Mormon

## The Claim
If one must compare the Quran to a Latter-day Saint book of scripture, the correct analog is the **Doctrine and Covenants**, not the Book of Mormon — and certainly not the Bible.[^1] Recognizing the right comparison dissolves the "Joseph just copied Muhammad" objection (see [[Muhammad and Joseph Smith Are Apples to Oranges]]).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The Quran is "one voice," not a library.** Peterson notes the word *Bible* comes from Greek *ta biblia*, "the books" — a library of genres (Psalms, Proverbs, gospels, Chronicles). The Quran is, by contrast, a single voice throughout. In Muslim belief it is not even Muhammad's inspired voice but God's voice "coming through Muhammad like through a pipe" — "if you had a red letter edition of the Quran... the entire Quran would be in red."[^2] *(Strong — accurately states mainstream Islamic doctrine of the Quran as uncreated divine speech.)*
2. **Like the D&C, it is a series of dated, occasion-bound revelations.** The Doctrine and Covenants is not a narrative; strip its section headings and "you'd be kind of lost." The Quran is similar: a series of revelations given to or through Muhammad over nearly a quarter century, then arranged in roughly reverse-chronological order (an out-of-order introduction, then longest-to-shortest chapter).[^3] *(Strong — the standard non-chronological mushaf ordering is uncontested.)*
3. **The D&C contains mixed modes of revelation, paralleling how revelation reached Muhammad.** Peterson notes that *some* D&C revelations are the actual words of an angel or of God, but *most* are "inspired accounts" in Joseph's own voice; the biblical authors likewise have distinct voices (Luke ≠ Mark, Isaiah ≠ Jeremiah). The Quran's uniform single voice is the contrast that makes it D&C-like in form but not in voicing.[^4] *(Moderate — the comparison is illustrative rather than a precise one-to-one match.)*
4. **Steelman / nuance.** The parallel is formal (discrete, occasion-bound revelations collected later), not doctrinal — the D&C is explicitly multi-voiced while the Quran is mono-voiced. Peterson grants the analogy is the *best available*, not exact: "it's really rather like the Doctrine and Covenants, I would say, *if you were going to compare it to anything*."[^1]
