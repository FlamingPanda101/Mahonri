# The Eyewitness Double Standard Favors the Gold Plates Over the Resurrection

## The Claim
Cameron Bertuzzi contrasts [[Joseph Smith]]'s "private revelation" with Jesus's "entirely public" ministry, claiming the resurrection was "witnessed by hundreds of people, including his enemies," whereas Mormonism rests on secretive private experiences. The agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]] and his host argue this applies a **double standard** to eyewitness testimony — one that, examined honestly, favors the gold plates over the resurrection.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence (the rebuttal)

1. **Joseph also preached publicly; the Bible's revelations were also private (rate: strong, parity).** "Joseph Smith also preached in public." And the biblical record is full of private/limited revelations: Moses receiving tablets after going up the mountain alone, Paul's vision on the road to Damascus (which was *his* vision, described as light, not a public viewing of the risen Jesus). See [[Muhammad Had No Corroborating Witnesses or Physical Artifacts]] for the contrast with Islam.[^2]

2. **"Hundreds of eyewitnesses" is one man's claim, not hundreds of reports (rate: strong, source-critical).** Bertuzzi's "hundreds saw the resurrection" rests on Paul's single statement that ~500 saw the risen Christer ([[1 Corinthians 15.6|1 Cor 15:6]]). "If one guy says hundreds of people saw it, that's [not] the same thing as hundreds of independent eyewitness reports." We have "no testimony ... no names even of any of these 500 people."[^3]

3. **The plates have 19 named, signed witnesses (rate: strong, asymmetry).** By contrast, the Book of Mormon has signed testimony from the [[Three Witnesses]] and [[Eight Witnesses]] — "19 firsthand eyewitnesses to the golden plates who suffered persecution," who "had personal beef with Joseph Smith" and "every reason to recant and never did." See [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence]] and [[No Witness Ever Recanted]].[^4]

4. **Even the resurrection witnesses are ambiguous (rate: moderate).** The Gospels record people *not recognizing* the risen Jesus, so even the named appearances may not be straightforward physical identifications — whereas the plates were handled and described. The point is not to debunk the resurrection but to deny that "Christianity doesn't have this problem": it too "requires a leap of faith."[^5]

## Steelman of the Critic
The comparison is not a clean win for the plates. (a) The *kind* of claim differs: the plates witnesses attest a metal object (some "with spiritual eyes"), while the resurrection witnesses attest a person raised from death — arguably a more load-bearing, harder-to-mistake claim if genuine. (b) 1 Cor 15 is an *early creedal* formula (within a few years of the events) naming Peter, James, the Twelve, and 500, which many historians treat as strong precisely because it is early and falsifiable in its own day; dismissing it as "one guy's claim" understates that. (c) The plates witnesses were a tight, mutually-acquainted group with shared religious incentives, which a skeptic can frame as collusion-prone (answered elsewhere in the witnesses cluster). The fair conclusion is Green's modest one: the resurrection also rests on faith and limited testimony, so the "public vs private" contrast is not the clean disqualifier of Mormonism it was presented as — not that the plates testimony strictly outranks the resurrection.

## Best Quotes

> "Anonymous secondhand eyewitnesses — wow, what spectacular evidence, as opposed to 19 firsthand eyewitnesses to the golden plates who suffered persecution ... and never recanted."[^4]

> "No one saw the resurrection ... we have no names even of any of these 500 people that supposedly saw Jesus after he was resurrected."[^3]
