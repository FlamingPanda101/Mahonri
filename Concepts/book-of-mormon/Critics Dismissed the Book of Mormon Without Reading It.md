# Critics Dismissed the Book of Mormon Without Reading It

## The Claim

For most of the twentieth century the scholarly default was to dismiss the Book of Mormon *as caricature, without engaging the text*. Two emblematic admissions capture this: Catholic scholar Thomas O'Dea's observation that critics "did not even have to read the Book of Mormon in order to have an opinion about it," and Yale critic [[Harold Bloom]]'s advice that "I cannot recommend that the book be read either fully or closely." The apologetic point is that this is an *epistemically illegitimate* posture — a pre-judgment masquerading as a verdict — and that the book's later academic reception happened precisely as scholars who actually read it closely (Hardy, Skousen, Gutjahr) reversed the dismissal.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The critic's own confession (O'Dea, 1957)

[[Kirk Magleby]] cites Thomas F. O'Dea — a respected sociologist of Mormonism, *not* an LDS apologist — for the 1957 remark that opinions about the Book of Mormon were formed without reading it. Magleby notes "not much has changed since 1957": for most scholars "the Book of Mormon was caricature, and they ignored it."[^1] An opinion admittedly formed without reading the primary text carries no evidential weight against it.

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a hostile-to-neutral witness conceding the method; self-indicting testimony about the critics' own practice).

### 2. The most eminent literary critic told readers to skip it (Bloom, 1991)

[[Harold Bloom]], "the most famous literary critic in the English-speaking world," praised [[Joseph Smith]] as "an authentic religious genius" and even "an authentic prophet" — yet on the book itself said he could not recommend reading it "either fully or closely." Magleby reads this as Bloom telling "his audience to ignore the Book of Mormon... It was not worth their time."[^1] The tension is the point: Bloom rated the *author* a genius while declining to *read the work*, which is an evaluation made in advance of the evidence.

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (a striking illustration; one could read Bloom as a taste judgment rather than a claim the text is unreadable).

### 3. The reversal tracks the reading

Magleby frames the entire half-century arc as the dismissal collapsing once the text was read on its own terms — close literary analysis ([[Grant Hardy]]), a monks-in-a-monastery critical text ([[Royal Skousen]]), and a sympathetic biography by a non-LDS scholar (Gutjahr). The shift from "ignore it" to elite-press study is the practical refutation of the don't-read-it stance. See [[Elite Academic Presses Publishing the Book of Mormon Mark Its Respectability]].[^1]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (the correlation is real; the causal story — engagement drove respect — is plausible but not proven).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can answer that not every text earns a close reading, and that triage is rational: scholars decline to "fully and closely" read countless purported revelations, and doing so is not prejudice but finite attention. Bloom, on this reading, was making an aesthetic judgment (he found the prose repetitive) rather than confessing willful ignorance, and O'Dea was *describing* others' laziness, not endorsing it. The honest minimal claim, then, is not that every dismissal was illegitimate, but that *opinions explicitly formed without reading the text* cannot count as evidence against it — and that the later close readers found enough to justify serious study.
