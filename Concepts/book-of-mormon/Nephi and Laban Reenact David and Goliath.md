# Nephi and Laban Reenact David and Goliath

#type/claim

## The Claim
In the [[Ward Radio - The Sword of Laban Was the Sword of Joshua|Don Bradley interview]], [[Don Bradley]] cites Ben McGuire's argument that [[Nephi]]'s slaying of [[Laban]] ([[1 Nephi 4.9|1 Nephi 4]]) is a deliberate literary reenactment of [[David]] and [[Goliath]] (1 Samuel 17). Roughly a dozen elements of the David/Goliath episode recur in the **same order** in Nephi's account: an unlikely young deliverer, the enemy felled and then **beheaded with his own sword**, the victor taking the enemy's distinctive weapon as a trophy, and a deliverance that founds a new people. Nephi is cast as "the new David," Laban as "the new Goliath." The parallel reinforces the larger thesis that the sword of Laban is itself a kingly relic with a deep Israelite pedigree (see [[The Sword of Laban Was the Sword of Joshua Forged by Joseph in Egypt]]).

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The beheading-with-the-enemy's-own-sword motif matches precisely.** David has no sword of his own and kills the already-downed Goliath with Goliath's blade (1 Samuel 17:51); Nephi, "constrained by the Spirit," takes the sleeping Laban's own "most precious steel" sword and beheads him with it. The shared, unusual detail — the hero uses the *victim's* weapon — is the strongest single point of contact. *Rating: a genuine and specific parallel; the motif is distinctive enough to be more than coincidence.*
2. **The sequence of shared elements is ordered, not just thematic.** McGuire's case (as Bradley relays it) is that the elements line up in the same narrative order, which is the kind of structural correspondence that resists being dismissed as a generic "underdog beats giant" trope. *Rating: moderate-to-strong if the ordering holds; ordered parallels carry more weight than a loose motif list (cf. [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]]).*
3. **The trophy-weapon descends to found a people.** David's victory inaugurates his rise to kingship; Nephi takes Laban's sword, later uses it as the pattern to make more swords (2 Nephi 5:14), and it becomes a dynastic relic carried down the Nephite record-keepers to Moroni — paralleling the way the David story founds the Davidic line. *Rating: a coherent typological reading; the dynastic-relic claim leans on the broader sword-of-Laban reconstruction.*
4. **An ancient author would be expected to type-scene this way.** Hebrew narrative routinely builds episodes on earlier "type scenes" (betrothal-at-a-well, etc.); a 600 BC Israelite consciously echoing the conquest-era hero would be writing exactly as ancient scribes did, whereas a 19th-century farm boy is not an obvious candidate to engineer an ordered David/Goliath substructure. *Rating: supports authenticity by analogy; not proof.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The David-and-Goliath story was wholly available to Joseph Smith in his King James Bible, so a parallel — even an ordered one — is exactly what a 19th-century author steeped in the Bible would produce, consciously or not; it shows literary dependence on the Bible, not antiquity. "Beheaded with his own sword" is a memorable biblical beat that a storyteller would naturally reuse, and "a dozen elements in order" can be inflated by counting loosely (the vault's own caution, [[A Parallel List Without Textual Analysis Carries No Burden]], applies). **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the point is not that the parallel proves antiquity by itself but that a *deliberate, sustained* reenactment is a mark of sophisticated authorial intent that fits an ancient scribe better than dictation-by-accident — and it dovetails with independent lines (the sword's metallurgy, the Josephite-relic descent) that a fabricator had no reason to coordinate. The honest weight is "literary evidence of careful design," contributory rather than decisive.
