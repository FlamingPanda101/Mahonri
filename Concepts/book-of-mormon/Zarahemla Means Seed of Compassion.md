# Zarahemla Means "Seed of Compassion"

## The Claim
The name **Zarahemla** analyzes as *zera* ("seed / offspring") + *hemla* ("compassion / mercy"), "seed of compassion." Passages set among the people of Zarahemla cluster the language of **compassion** — first toward the converted Lamanites ([[Alma 27.22-26|Alma 27]]), then reciprocally from them ([[Alma 53.10-22|Alma 53]]). Notably, even **non-LDS / restoration-adjacent scholars** have analyzed this name. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The etymology.** *zera* "seed/offspring" is well-attested Hebrew; *hemla* relates to *raḥam/ḥemlah*, "compassion, mercy, pity." Strength: **moderate-high** (the *zera* element is solid; *hemla* is plausible). [^1]
2. **Compassion in context (first pole).** In [[Alma 27.22-26|Alma 27]], the people of Zarahemla have **compassion** on the converted Lamanites and grant them Jershon. Strength: **moderate**. [^2]
3. **Reciprocal compassion (second pole).** In [[Alma 53.10-22|Alma 53]], the converted Lamanites (people of Ammon) are "moved with **compassion**" and send their sons to fight for the Nephites — an *inverted* reciprocal of the earlier compassion. These are "the only two places" the expression is used in that way. Strength: **moderate-high** (the inversion is a deliberate-looking literary pairing). [^3]
4. **External corroboration.** That scholars *outside* the LDS tradition have analyzed Zarahemla's Hebrew etymology lends independence. Strength: **moderate**. [^1]

**Steelman:** A skeptic objects that the *hemla* → "compassion" link is the shakier half of the etymology; that "compassion" is a frequent narrative virtue, so finding it near "the people of Zarahemla" is unsurprising; and that "only two places" may reflect a small sample, not design. Bowen's reply is the *structural inversion* — compassion given, then compassion returned — which reads as authored symmetry rather than coincidence.
