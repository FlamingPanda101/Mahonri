# Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn

## The Claim

The place-name **Nahom** in [[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34]] (where Ishmael is buried
and from which the party turns eastward) corresponds to the **NHM** tribal region in
South Arabia — attested epigraphically back to Lehi's era and located precisely where
ancient travel routes turn east, exactly as Nephi's directions require.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. NHM is a real, anciently attested South Arabian tribe/region

A tribe called **Nihm / Nehem / NHM** has occupied the area since pre-Islamic times.
Because South Arabian (a Semitic language) writes no vowels, the consonantal NHM is
transliterated variously (Nehem, Nihm, Nahm); the Book of Mormon's "Nahom" fits. The
pronunciation guide's "Nay-hom" actually overstates the difference — a short-u "Nahm"
is closer. Inscriptions referring to "Nehemites" go back to the **7th century BC**.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (epigraphically dated tribal name in the right consonantal form).

### 2. Tribe and territory are integrated in Yemen

Objection: "NHM is a tribe, not a place." Reply: Yemeni tribes are tightly tied to
their territory; ancient South Arabian inscriptions use tribal names as place names,
and scholars reading "so-and-so was a Nehemite" debate whether it means the *region*
or the *tribe* — because the two are integrated. Rappleye published on this in [[BYU Studies]].[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (cited inscriptions + a peer-reviewed paper answering the objection).

### 3. The location matches Nephi's mandatory eastward turn

Nephi travels south-southeast along what becomes the Frankincense Trail, then turns
**nearly eastward** after Ishmael's burial at Nahom. The only viable place to turn
east (without dying in the Empty Quarter) is the Wadi Jawf area — and the three main
eastward trails all run within ~10 miles of the historical NHM tribal boundaries. The
turn point is fixed by geography and trade routes, not chosen to fit. An inscription
dated to **600 BC** documents the trail's north-south leg.[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (independent geographic constraint converging on the epigraphic site).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can argue NHM is a common Semitic triliteral root (related to comfort/
consolation, cf. Hebrew *nacham*), so finding *some* NHM toponym along a long caravan
route is not improbable, and the "turn east here" inference is partly retrofitted to
known geography. The reply (Rappleye's): he cannot move the tribe, the trails, or the
geology — had NHM sat 300 miles north, eastward travel from there is "death," so the
*joint* match of name + mandatory turn point + dating is what makes it convergence, not
a lone coincidence.

## Best Quotes

> "There is very solid evidence that Nahom or a name similar to it... is in fact a region, an area in South Arabia, and that it dates back to Lehi's time and it's in the approximate area you would expect."[^1]

> "We've got inscriptions going back to the 7th century BC that mention them... and they're right there surrounding this Wadi Jawf area where the trails all turn east."[^4]

> "I can't if we found inscriptions about NHM 300 miles to the north... there's nothing I could do to make that fit, because going east from there is death."[^4]
