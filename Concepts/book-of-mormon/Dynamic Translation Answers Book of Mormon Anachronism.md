# Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
A critic objects: the Book of Mormon (e.g., 2 Nephi 29:3, "a Bible! A Bible! We have got a Bible") references "a Bible" — but the Bible was not compiled or so named at the era the text depicts, so this is an **anachronism** betraying 19th-century authorship.[^1] [[Hayden Carroll]] answers with **dynamic (functional) translation**: Joseph Smith received the meaning and rendered it in words intelligible to *modern readers*, exactly as Bible translators substitute modern equivalents for concepts lacking a period word.[^2]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The translation-theory reply is coherent — rate: strong (internally)
Dynamic vs. formal equivalence is a real and respectable distinction in translation studies (Eugene Nida's "dynamic equivalence"). If the Book of Mormon's English is a *translation* aimed at communicating ideas to 1830 readers, then using the target-language term "Bible" for the source concept ("the record/scriptures of the Jews") is exactly what a functional translator does. Carroll's parallel is apt: "if you read an English Bible, every word in the Bible did not exist at the time it was written" — yet no one calls the English "Esther" or "salvation" an anachronism.[^3] The audience supplies the same logic for "saved by works after all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23) meaning "in spite of all we can do."[^4]

### 2. It depends on accepting the translation premise — rate: moderate, question-begging risk
The reply only neutralizes the objection *if* one grants that the Book of Mormon is a genuine translation of an ancient source. **Steelman of the critic:** that is precisely what is in dispute; a skeptic will say "dynamic translation" is an unfalsifiable escape hatch that can absorb *any* anachronism (steel swords, horses, "adieu") by relabeling it a translator's choice. So the move does not *prove* authenticity — it shows the anachronism is not a *knockdown* disproof, shifting the burden back to the broader evidential case. This is consistent with the course's own validity/soundness lesson: the reply rescues *consistency*, not *proof*. See [[Validity vs Soundness - Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails]].

### 3. Carroll reframes it as "answering," not "dodging" — rate: framing
Notably Carroll calls this "answering it," distinguishing it from defensive bullet-dodging — i.e., he treats the dynamic-translation reply as a positive explanation rather than mere damage control.[^5] He credits [[David Snell]] (Keystone) and others with developing the "in spite of all we can do" reading. See [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]].
