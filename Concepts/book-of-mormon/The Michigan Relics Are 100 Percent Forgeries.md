# The Michigan Relics Are 100 Percent Forgeries

## The Claim

[[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]] argues that the **Michigan Relics** — a corpus of ~3,000 inscribed slate, clay, and copper objects "found" in Michigan from the 1890s to ~1920 — are *entirely* forgeries, and that Latter-day Saints (notably [[Wayne May]]) should stop citing any of them as Book-of-Mormon evidence. Tellingly, two of the best debunkers were themselves Latter-day Saints: apostle-scientist [[James E. Talmage]] and archaeologist Richard Stamps.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Talmage's lab analysis and Stamps's 18 reasons — rate: strong (forensic)
[[James E. Talmage]], a trained geologist, obtained specimens and tested them at the Deseret Museum (c. 1911), observing fresh saw and tool marks; he labeled them frauds. Richard Stamps (Oakland University, a Latter-day Saint) later reviewed hundreds of pieces and published **18 reasons** they are forgeries. The strongest debunkers came from inside the faith.[^2]

### 2. Anachronistic materials and tooling — rate: strong (material science)
The copper objects are *commercially smelted, hot-rolled copper* — a technology unavailable in pre-Columbian America — bearing saw marks from modern fine-tooth blades and cut at precise right angles. The clay pieces were never kiln-fired and dissolve in water, so they could not have survived centuries in the ground. The copper was chemically corroded to fake age.[^3]

### 3. A forger's signature and a controlled "dig" — rate: strong (pattern)
The objects were found only by James Scotford and Daniel Soper (the latter a disgraced ex–Michigan Secretary of State), who controlled every excavation and told paying participants where to dig; in 130 years no related artifact has been found independently anywhere in Michigan. Many pieces bear a repeated nail-slash mark — effectively the forger's signature. A co-author who published with Wayne May reportedly *begged* him to drop the relics and has since fully recanted.[^4]

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A defender (e.g., the live-chat "Latter-day Chad") raises real cautions. (a) *Definition creep*: "Michigan Relics" is sometimes used to dismiss *any* Michigan find an academic dislikes, and genuine Native American artifacts are recovered in Michigan routinely — so a blanket "all fakes" can over-reach if it sweeps in objects outside the Scotford-Soper corpus (Galey explicitly brackets his claim to that scheme). (b) Academic bias is not imaginary: the Smithsonian and others have a documented history of pre-judging finds that challenge their narrative (transoceanic contact, metal plates), so "the experts say fake" is not automatically decisive. (c) Some defenders claim earlier (e.g., 1812) Michigan finds and the existence of period saws, which—*if* substantiated—would need case-by-case evaluation. But none of these rescue the Scotford-Soper objects, whose hot-rolled copper and modern saw marks are dispositive regardless of institutional bias. So the disciplined conclusion: the Scotford-Soper Michigan Relics are forgeries on physical grounds an LDS scientist established, and citing them helps critics — while the broader lesson about not over-trusting academic dismissals is granted and handled separately ([[Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance]]).
