# The Sword of Laban Was the Sword of Joshua Forged by Joseph in Egypt

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Don Bradley]] argues that the **[[Laban|sword of Laban]]** ([[1 Nephi 4.9|1 Nephi 4:9]]) is the **sword of [[Joshua]]** from the biblical conquest — and that it was **forged by [[Joseph of Egypt]]** in Egypt, by divine direction, for the conquest he foresaw. The relic descended the Ephraimite/Josephite royal line (Joseph → Joshua → ... → Laban), traveled out of Jerusalem with the brass plates, and ended with Moroni. Two evidentiary legs: (a) the **internal** case that the relics in Laban's possession have a deep Joseph-of-Egypt history (developed for the plates in [[The Brass Plates Were Begun by Joseph of Egypt]]), and (b) an **external** statement Bradley adduces as corroboration for the lost 116 pages — [[Francis Gladden Bishop]], a Kirtland neighbor of [[Martin Harris]], published in the early 1850s that the sword "was caused to be made by Joseph of old in Egypt, by the direction of God, and was in the hand of Joshua when he led the house of Israel into the land of Canaan, and after him it came down into the lineage of Joseph to Laban."[^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The lineage data converge.** Bradley notes accounts tracing Laban to the tribe of **Ephraim**; **Joshua** is biblically of Ephraim; **Ephraim** is [[Joseph of Egypt|Joseph]]'s birthright son. A military, kingly lineage (Laban "can command fifty," as Joshua commanded armies) running Joseph → Joshua → Laban makes the sword's descent with the plates "make all the sense in the world." *Rating: the tribal data points genuinely line up; the identification remains a reconstruction.*[^3]
2. **Royal relics really were dynastically transmitted.** Bradley appeals to the well-attested practice of heirloom regalia (sword, book, orb) passing in royal/family lines — a real historical pattern that makes "twin relics handed down" antecedently plausible. *Rating: a sound analogy; supports plausibility, not proof.*[^3]
3. **A documented external source corroborates.** The [[Francis Gladden Bishop]] statement is a real 1850s publication, and Bishop's decades of proximity to [[Martin Harris]] (the manuscript's principal scribe) gives a credible transmission path. Because it **converges** with independent internal evidence, Bradley treats it as [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources|corroboration]], not as authority in itself. *Rating: a genuine multiple-attestation move; its force depends on the internal case being right.*[^1]
4. **Joseph's motive is scripturally grounded.** Genesis has Joseph prophesy the return to the promised land ("carry my bones"); the JST/Book of Mormon expand this to detailed conquest prophecy. A seer foreseeing the conquest commissioning a sword for it is internally coherent. *Rating: coheres with the expanded Joseph-prophecy material in [[2 Nephi 3]]; speculative as to the sword specifically.*[^4]
5. **The rust detail carries the theology.** Per Joseph Smith Sr. (to Fayette Lapham) only the **hilt** survived, the blade "cankered and rusted away," while the brass plates [[Alma 37.3-5|retain their brightness]] — "the power is not in the sword, it is in the word." *Rating: a real secondhand account; Bradley flags it as symbolically apt rather than load-bearing.*[^5]

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The whole chain hangs on a **late, hostile-tier witness**: [[Francis Gladden Bishop]] was an excommunicated, self-proclaimed prophet, and his *Vision*-based "history of this sword" could be his own embellishment of Book of Mormon themes rather than a recovered fact — and the parts Bradley himself is "more skeptical of" (Bishop's claim that the sword has a *guard*, an anachronism for the period) show the source is unreliable in detail. A critic notes that "Laban of the tribe of Ephraim" and "the sword came from Joseph" are *also* reconstructions from the lost pages, so the argument risks circularity (lost-pages claims corroborated by a source that may itself depend on lost-pages talk). And metallurgically, a single steel blade surviving from patriarchal Egypt through Joshua to 600 BC strains credulity (the rust account is, ironically, the only physical-state datum and it says the blade did **not** survive). **A Latter-day Saint reply:** Bradley does not rest on Bishop's authority but on **convergence** — the sword-claim is independently expected once the *internal* evidence shows the brass plates (the relic that travels *with* the sword) originate with Joseph; an unreliable man can still transmit, via Martin Harris, an accurate report, and the test is whether it fits data he could not have engineered. The identification is offered as the best reconstruction of a lost narrative, not as a proven artifact history.

## Best Quotes
> "There is an account that tells us that the sword of Laban had previously been possessed by Joshua — was the biblical sword of Joshua used in the Book of Joshua — and that prior to that it had actually been forged by Joseph in Egypt." [^2]

> "It was caused to be made by Joseph of old in Egypt by the direction of God, and was in the hand of Joshua when he led the house of Israel into the land of Canaan, and after him it came down into the lineage of Joseph to Laban." — Gladden Bishop, quoted by Bradley [^1]

> "The brass plates that contain the word of God will never be dimmed, but the sword rusts away... ultimately the power is not in the sword, it's in the word." [^5]
