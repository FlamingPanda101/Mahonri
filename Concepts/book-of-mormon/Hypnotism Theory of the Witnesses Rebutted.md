# Hypnotism Theory of the Witnesses Rebutted

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

[[Joseph Smith]] hypnotized the witnesses into believing they saw and handled the plates.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Hypnotism is largely anachronistic
"Did anybody actually do hypnotism at that level in those days? It comes along a little later."[^1] The technique as imagined wasn't available to a frontier farmer in the late 1820s.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (broadly true that mesmerism/hypnotism in the modern sense post-dates and was not a tool Joseph plausibly wielded; a historian could quibble on dates).

### 2. It cannot reach the eight or the absent informal witnesses
"How does he hypnotize eight witnesses?" — there's no plausible group induction ("hey guys, stand really still while I get this thing out of my pocket, now stare at it"), and none of the eight ever mentions any such episode. And he was not present for the informal witnesses at all.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (scope problem identical to the deception theory; see [[Witnesses Were Deceived Theory Rebutted]]).

### 3. Not everyone is hypnotizable — so a uniform success is suspicious
Some people simply cannot be hypnotized (Peterson reports it failing on himself — "this is ridiculous, I'm getting really bored"). Joseph would have had to find "the handful of yokels in his neighborhood who would all immediately go under," then prophesy in advance that it would work.[^3]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (individual variation in suggestibility makes a clean sweep implausible).

### 4. Prior failed "staring" attempts undercut a sudden success
Peterson alludes to earlier money-digging episodes where men reported standing for half an hour while someone swung an object before their eyes and saw nothing — yet on the decisive occasion Joseph supposedly "nailed it" first try.[^4]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (rests on traditional money-digging accounts).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The defensible version drops the word "hypnotism" and speaks of *heightened suggestibility in a charismatic religious setting* — a real phenomenon. A skeptic can argue the three witnesses' visionary experience fits expectation-driven group suggestion, even if formal hypnosis is anachronistic. Peterson's strongest answers — the eight's mundane tactile experience and the absent-Joseph informal cases — apply here too: suggestion cannot easily manufacture a heavy metal object hefted in daylight, nor an experience Joseph never attended. The believer should concede that "hypnotism proper" is a weak form of an objection that has a stronger "suggestibility" cousin, which is met elsewhere.

## Best Quotes

> "How do you hypnotize eight witnesses? I mean — hey guys, stand really still for a minute while I get this thing out of my pocket, now stare at it for a long time. None of them mentions that afterwards."[^2]

> "He found the handful of yokels in his neighborhood who would all immediately go under hypnotism. And he got extra lucky because he prophesied that that would happen in the Book of Mormon."[^3]
