# Gold Plates Fabrication Was Prohibitively Expensive

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Critics (e.g. [[Frank Turek]]: "I can make golden plates in my garage") claim
fabricating the Book of Mormon plates would have been cheap and easy. The
rebuttal: before any question of *skill*, simply acquiring enough metal to match
the witnesses' weight and dimensions was a prohibitively expensive endeavor for
the dirt-poor Smith family of the 1820s.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The plates weighed 40–60 lbs of metal

The witnesses describe a stack weighing roughly 40–60 pounds (probably toward the
upper end). Whether gold, lead, tin, or copper, that is a large quantity of metal
to purchase — and gold or even lead in that volume was beyond Joseph's means.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (combines witness testimony with period economics). The weight is multiply attested; the cost inference follows directly.

### 2. Martin Harris's "heft" testimony

In his interview with Joel Tiffany, [[Martin Harris]] said he could tell from
hefting the box that the contents were either lead or gold, and reasoned that
Joseph "didn't have enough credit to buy so much lead" — and gold would require
even more credit.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (single 19th-century interview, but a hostile-to-fraud detail from someone who handled the box).

### 3. The Smith family was destitute

Lucy Mack Smith records the family selling scraps of painted cloth for extra
income. Where would the capital for bulk metal come from? The "Martin Harris paid
for it" rejoinder is treated as special pleading.[^4]

### 4. Even modern replicators can't afford real gold

[[David Baird]], a metallurgist who built an authentic-as-possible replica, could
not afford real gold and used brass instead — a present-day demonstration of the
cost barrier.[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a controlled modern attempt confirms the economic obstacle).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic could note that the "treasure-digging" Joseph might have found gold, or
that a cheaper goldish metal like brass was affordable. The panel grants this
"throws a monkey wrench" possibility but counters that any cheap substitute must
still satisfy *all* the other witness parameters (color, thinness, engraving,
oxidation) — see [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]] —
and that no contemporary ever reported Joseph acquiring such metal — see
[[No Contemporary Accusation That Joseph Smith Forged Plates]].

## Best Quotes

> "It's a prohibitively expensive endeavor."[^6]

> "He could tell from the heft that it was either lead or gold and he knew Joseph didn't have... enough credit to buy so much lead. And gold you would need more credit to get the gold."[^3]

> "The Smith family is dirt poor... they're like selling little pieces of cloth, scraps of cloth... So where's he getting the money?"[^4]
