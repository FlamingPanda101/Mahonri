# The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument

## The Point
Carroll's answer to "why are you still a Latter-day Saint?" is the **keystone argument**: the Book of Mormon is "the keystone of our religion" (Ezra Taft Benson) — "the central stone in an arch... if removed, the arch crumbles." If the Book of Mormon is an authentic ancient record and revelation, the doctrines of God and the authority of the Church follow; "if it's not, we have no religion." So the whole question is decidable by examining one testable artifact. [^1]

## Evidence
1. **The stakes are binary and concrete.** Unlike diffuse theological disputes, the Restoration rises or falls on a single, examinable book — "the glue that holds together this entire religion." If true, Carroll argues, it is "both the best evidence for the historical Jesus, and the bodily resurrection of Christ." [^1]
2. **The dictation feat.** The book was dictated by a 23-year-old in ~65 working days in a single draft: 269,320 words, 6,852 sentences, 26+ distinguishable authors, 170+ unique proper nouns, 77 major storylines, 63+ sermons, Christ named on average every 1.7 verses. [^2] (See [[The Dictation Feat - Reversed Jaredite Kings and a Hundred Names of Christ]].)
3. **Joseph's documented lack of education.** Carroll stacks hostile and family witnesses — Pomeroy Tucker ("uneducated and ignorant"), Joseph's mother Lucy ("less inclined to the study of books than any child we had"), Isaac Hale, David Whitmer ("quite illiterate"), even E. D. Howe's *Mormonism Unveiled* — to argue the consensus was that Joseph lacked the faculties to compose such a work. [^3]
4. **The invitation.** The test is reproducible: "study the glue that holds together this entire religion" — read the Book of Mormon and ask whether Joseph had the skills the text would require. [^3]

## Best Quotes
> "If the Book of Mormon is an actual record and revelation from God... then that has serious implications regarding the doctrines of God and the validity and authority of the Church... If it's not, we have no religion." [^1]

## LDS Connection
The keystone framing concentrates the apologetic burden where Latter-day Saints think it belongs — on the Book of Mormon itself — and pairs the "if-true-then-Restoration" logic with the evidential dictation-feat case ([[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]], [[Joseph Smith Lacked the Metallurgical Skill and Time]]). Honest limit: the argument moves from "hard to explain naturally" to "best explained as revelation," which critics contest; Carroll's own epistemology (see [[Triangulating Truth - Five Mediums of Revelation]]) treats the book as one converging witness confirmed by the Spirit, not a standalone proof.
