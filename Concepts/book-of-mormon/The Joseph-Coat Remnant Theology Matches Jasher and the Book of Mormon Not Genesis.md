# The Joseph-Coat Remnant Theology Matches Jasher and the Book of Mormon Not Genesis

#type/claim

## The Claim
On [[Ward Radio - The REAL History of Adams Garment Lost from Genesis|Ward Radio]], [[Jonah Barnes]] (with host [[Cardon Ellis]], drawing on [[Stephen D. Ricks]]) argues that the Book of Mormon's treatment of Joseph of Egypt's coat preserves a **remnant theology** that matches the *Book of Jasher* and other apocrypha rather than the received text of Genesis. [[Alma 46.21-22|Alma 46:23–24]] has Captain Moroni invoke a prophecy of Jacob that **a remnant of Joseph's coat was preserved and had not decayed** — a typological sign that a remnant of Joseph's seed would likewise be preserved. Genesis contains no such "preserved remnant of the coat" theology. Moreover, where [[Genesis 37|Genesis 37]] says only that the brothers dipped Joseph's coat in blood, the Nephites say it was also "trodden under foot" — and *Jasher* independently says the brothers dipped it *and trampled it in the dust*. Both the remnant theme and the trampling detail are absent from Genesis but present in a text (Jasher) widely available only long after Joseph Smith.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The "preserved remnant of the coat" prophecy is in Alma, not Genesis.** Moroni's appeal ([[Alma 46.21-22|Alma 46]]) treats a surviving, undecayed piece of Joseph's coat as a divinely intended type of the surviving remnant of Joseph's lineage. This developed remnant theology has no basis in the Genesis narrative, which drops the coat after the deception scene. *Rating: strong as a textual contrast; the Book of Mormon clearly carries material Genesis lacks.*
2. **The "trodden under foot" detail matches Jasher against Genesis.** Genesis 37:31 says the brothers killed a kid and dipped the coat in blood; it does not mention trampling. The Book of Mormon's "trodden under foot" language aligns with *Jasher*'s account that the coat was trampled in the dust. *Rating: moderate-to-strong — a specific, non-Genesis detail shared with an apocryphal source.* Compare [[Multiple Attestation - Corroborating Apocryphal Sources]].
3. **It belongs to a larger Adam-garment relic tradition.** The coat is one link in an apocryphal chain (the Garment of Adam passing Adam → Noah → Ham → Nimrod → Esau → Jacob → Joseph) reconstructed from the *Life of Adam and Eve*, *Jubilees*, and *Jasher*; the Book of Mormon's coat-theology fits that tradition. *Rating: coherent within the apocryphal frame; the continuous-relic chain is itself a reconstruction.* See [[The Garment of Power Descended From Adam to Nimrod]] and [[The Garment of Skin and the Garment of Light]].
4. **The matching texts post-date Joseph Smith's plausible access.** Barnes' case turns on these details surfacing in apocrypha that an 1829 farm translator is unlikely to have mined. *Rating: contributory; "Jasher was unavailable" is weaker than for some other apocrypha (English Jasher editions circulated in the 19th century), so this leg needs care.*

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The *Book of Jasher* most cited in this period (the 1840 "Pseudo-Jasher," translated and printed in the U.S.) was in fact circulating in the English-speaking world around Joseph Smith's lifetime, so "Joseph couldn't have known" is weakest precisely here — a critic will say the Book of Mormon could reflect, not corroborate, the Jasher tradition (or simply a natural narrative embellishment that a kid's blood would also leave a coat trampled). And "remnant of Joseph" theology is exactly the sort of thing a 19th-century author building an Israelite-remnant epic would generate from the bare Genesis materials. **A Latter-day Saint reply:** the 1840 English Jasher post-dates the 1830 Book of Mormon, and the Hebrew/older Jasher traditions Barnes leans on were not accessible to Joseph; the convergence is therefore still suggestive even if this is the softer of the garment-parallels. The stronger point is the *theological* one — a developed, typological remnant-of-the-coat doctrine that Genesis simply does not contain — which is hard to explain as ordinary embellishment.
