# Informal Witnesses Were Not Primed

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

A standard skeptical move against the three and eight witnesses is that they were "primed" — psychologically prepared to see what they were told they would see (hallucination, hypnotism, suggestion).[^1] The objection assumes a controlled, expectant setting that [[Joseph Smith]] could exploit.

## The Rebuttal

### 1. A third group of *unofficial* witnesses had no expectation at all
Peterson highlights informal witnesses — [[Mary Whitmer]], [[Lucy Mack Smith]], [[Katherine Smith]], [[Josiah Stowell]] — who encountered the plates with no preparation and, in several cases, no desire to.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (these episodes come from independent lines of testimony and break the "primed setting" premise).

### 2. Concrete counter-cases
- **Mary Whitmer** was doing farm chores, overworked and "in a foul mood," when a messenger with a knapsack showed her the plates and let her turn the pages; she told the account on at least two separate occasions, and may even be the *first* witness, before the official ones.[^3]
- **Katherine Smith**, Joseph's 14-year-old sister, was handed the plates covered in a frock as Joseph fled a mob; she staggered under the weight and hid them under the bedcovers — she never forgot there was a real, heavy object.[^4]
- **Josiah Stowell** testified under oath in court that he saw the plates when the cover slipped as they were handed through a window — "they glinted." He was not primed; he did not want the cover to come off.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate-to-strong (multiple independent, undesired, uncontrolled encounters; weight/glint details are tactile and visual rather than visionary).

### 3. It defeats both hallucination and hoax-control
If Joseph did not control the setting and the witness was not expecting anything, then neither suggestion/hallucination nor a staged hoax accounts for the experience.[^5] This is why Peterson "likes having this separate group."

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can note the informal accounts are mostly *late reminiscences*, sometimes second-hand (told *about* the witness rather than *by* them), and shaped by an already-believing family network — so memory drift and social reinforcement could manufacture convergent detail. Peterson's reply is that the encounters are tactile (heft, weight, glint) rather than the kind of fantastical content that hallucination produces, and that the witnesses' lack of expectation is exactly what the "primed" objection denies. The believer should still concede the evidentiary weight here is testimonial and late, not contemporaneous documentation.

## Best Quotes

> "These people clearly were not expecting anything. You know, Mary's doing farm chores and she's in a foul mood."[^2]

> "He says, 'because I saw them.' ... The cover came off just a little bit. ... This is a case where he didn't expect it. He wasn't primed to see anything."[^5]
