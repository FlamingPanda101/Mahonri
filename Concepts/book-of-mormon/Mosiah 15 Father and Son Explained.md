# Mosiah 15 Father and Son Explained

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Mosiah 15.1-5|Mosiah 15:1-5]] — Christ is "the Father and the Son... one God" — shows the Book of Mormon teaches **Modalism / Trinitarianism**, so Latter-day Saints are inconsistent in rejecting the Trinity. (Often pressed via Kate Talks Mormonism and viewer super-chats.)

## The Rebuttal

### 1. Context already defines "Father" as a role of Christ
Two chapters earlier, [[Mosiah 3.8|Mosiah 3:8]] calls Christ "the Father of heaven and earth, the Creator of all things" — i.e. "Father" is a **creator title** of the Son. Mosiah 15 builds on this groundwork; it is not introducing a Father=Son identity but expanding an already-established role-language. *(Strong: the cross-text reading is exegetically responsible.)* [^1]

### 2. The covenant sense: Father by adoption
[[Mosiah 5.7|Mosiah 5:7]]: by covenant (baptism) believers are "spiritually begotten" and become Christ's "sons and daughters." So Christ is also "Father" in a **covenantal/adoptive** sense. Mosiah 15's "Father and Son" thus distinguishes: Father (by divine conception and by covenant) and Son (by the flesh) — two *roles* of one person, Christ, not a merger of the Father and the Son of the Godhead. *(Strong.)* [^2]

### 3. "One God" = the Godhead, as always
The "they are one God" of Mosiah 15:4 uses "God" for the one *what* (Godhead), consistent with the LDS reading everywhere ([[Three Whos and One What Framework]]). The Book of Mormon introduction/witnesses similarly speak of "the Father and the Son and the Holy Ghost... one eternal God" = one Godhead. The same explanation handles [[Alma 11.38-39|Alma 11:38-39]] ("the Son... the very Eternal Father"). *(Moderate-strong; consistent application of the framework.)* [^3]

### Steelman (the opposing view)
A critic responds that the *most natural* reading of Mosiah 15 — "the Father, because he was conceived by the power of God; and the Son, because of the flesh... they are one God" — sounds genuinely **Modalist** (one God playing two roles), and that the early Book of Mormon and 1830s LDS sources arguably reflect a more binitarian/modalistic Christology *before* the later "social" Godhead of Nauvoo (a development noted by scholars of LDS theology). On this reading the role-distinction harmonization is a *later* doctrinal lens imposed on the text. The panel's reply is that the surrounding chapters (Mosiah 3, 5) supply the role-language *internally*, so the harmonization is the text's own, not an anachronism.

## Best Quotes
