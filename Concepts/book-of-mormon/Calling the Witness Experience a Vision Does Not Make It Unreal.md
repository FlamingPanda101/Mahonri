# Calling the Witness Experience a "Vision" Does Not Make It Unreal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
Because [[David Whitmer]] (via [[Zenas Gurley]]) and others described the showing of the plates as a "vision," the [[Jeremy Runnells|CES Letter]] argues the witness was merely visionary or subjective, not a real physical event.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. "Vision" and "real physical event" are not opposites in Latter-day Saint usage
"Describing something as a vision does not mean the experience wasn't real. We refer to Joseph Smith's First Vision as a vision, but we also believe it was a real physical experience." The critic's inference equivocates on the word.[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a sound semantic point; the witnesses' own tradition treats visions as objectively real).

### 2. The witnesses themselves rejected the subjective reading
[[David Whitmer]] directly answered this objection. When Edward Thurman pressed [[Martin Harris]], Harris asked whether Thurman could see a nearby apple tree, and "he was told that the vision was as factual as that simple sight before them." The witnesses analogized their experience to ordinary, indisputable sense perception.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a firsthand analogy from the witness, repeated across accounts).

### 3. The grandson's testimony reinforces the point
David Whitmer's grandson wrote in 1899 that he had "begged him to unfold the fraud... but he has described the scene to me many times of his vision about noon in an open pasture," concluding "there is only one explanation barring an actual miracle, and that is... hypnotism. It was real to grandfather."[^3] Even a "vision at noon in an open pasture" is described in concrete, physical terms.
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate (family reminiscence, but it independently shows the witness used "vision" for something he insisted was real).

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A critic can argue this is special pleading: the apologist accepts physical language ("hefted," "handled") literally but reinterprets "vision" and "spiritual eyes" to mean "objectively real," so no possible statement could ever count as evidence of a merely subjective experience. The honest version of the rebuttal concedes that "vision" is genuinely ambiguous and rests the case not on the word but on the witnesses' own insistence — e.g., the apple-tree analogy — that what they saw was as real as everyday sight. See [[Witnesses Spiritual Eyes Selective Emphasis]].
