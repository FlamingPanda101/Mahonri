# Enos Means "Man" — Wordplay on the Jacob Wrestle

## The Claim
**Enos** is the Hebrew word *ʾenosh* ("man," a poetic synonym of *ish* and *adam*), and the author exploits this both by introducing Enos in terms of "man" and by patterning Enos's "wrestle before God" on his father **Jacob's** wrestle with a "**man**" (*ish*) at Peniel ([[Genesis 32.24-28|Genesis 32]]). [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The name is the Hebrew word for "man."** Of the three main Hebrew words for man — *adam*, *ish*, *ʾenosh* — Enos is *ʾenosh*, the poetic form. Strength: **high** (uncontested linguistically). [^1]
2. **Introduction follows Nephi's pattern and the name.** [[Enos 1.1-2|Enos 1:1]]: "I, Enos, knowing my father that he was a just **man**" — both echoing Nephi's autobiographical opening (signaling deliberateness) and the meaning of *ʾenosh*. Strength: **moderate-high**. [^2]
3. **The Jacob/Peniel allusion.** Enos 1:2 names "the wrestle which I had before God." His father is **Jacob**; Genesis 32's *wayyeʾaveq* ("wrestle") features a "**man**" (*ish*), and Jacob names the place **Peniel** ("face of God") — Enos's "before God" is "in the presence/face of God." Genesis 32:28 has Jacob striving "with God [*ʾelohim*] and with men [*ʾanashim*]," *ʾanashim* being the plural of *ʾenosh* (Enos). Strength: **high** (multiple converging Hebrew links). [^3]

**Steelman:** A critic notes that an English reader of the KJV could connect "Enos / a man," and that re-using a famous Jacob story for a character whose father is named Jacob is a natural literary move available to Joseph Smith. Bowen's reply is the *plural* detail — *ʾanashim* (Genesis) vs. *ʾenosh* (Enos) — a Hebrew morphological subtlety ("Hebrew 101 ... weird plural forms") not visible in English, which is hard to attribute to an English-only author.
