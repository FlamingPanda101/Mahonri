# The Tabernacle Is a Retrojection of the Temple

## The Claim

Discussing [[Julius Wellhausen]]'s *Prolegomena*, [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] present the argument that the elaborate wilderness **tabernacle** is a literary **retrojection** — a backward projection of Solomon's later **temple** onto the Exodus period. Whatever tent existed during the Exodus and settlement, Wellhausen holds, was "something much simpler"; the gold-laden portable sanctuary of the Priestly source was written centuries later to lend Mosaic authority to the temple cult. Wellhausen further notes that "the history of the ark is actually totally different from the history of the tabernacle," a thread [[Gerhard von Rad]] develops into **two distinct Moses traditions** — one of the Exodus and desert journey, one of Sinai. The argument is one instance of the broader pattern that [[Priestly Account Is Later and Corrective|the Priestly material is late]] and reads monarchy-era institutions back into hoary antiquity.[^1][^2][^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The wealth anachronism (moderate).** Butler raises the objection Wellhausen "or somebody" makes: where would newly-freed slaves get "oceans of gold" to build a "fabulously wealthy portable temple"? A richly appointed sanctuary fits a settled monarchy with a temple treasury far better than a band of refugees in the desert — evidence that the description was composed later and projected back.[^2]

2. **The ark and the tabernacle have separate histories (moderate; literary).** Wellhausen observes that the ark's narrative trajectory differs from the tabernacle's, implying they were not originally a single institution but were later combined. Day flags this as a seam in the text.[^2]

3. **Von Rad's two Moses traditions (moderate).** [[Gerhard von Rad]] argues there are two originally independent Moses complexes — the **Exodus / wilderness** tradition and the **Sinai** tradition — "totally different" and later fused. A composite Moses behind whom two traditions stand supports reading the tabernacle (a Sinai-revelation institution) as a separate, later-layered element rather than straightforward history.[^3]

4. **It is the Priestly source at work (strong as a framework).** The tabernacle texts belong to **P**, the source the documentary hypothesis dates latest (exilic, on Wellhausen's reckoning) and reads as projecting the centralized, regimented temple cult backward. This is continuous with the vault's [[Priestly Account Is Later and Corrective|treatment of P]] and the [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins|Wellhausen thesis]] generally.[^1]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** (a) Day himself does **not** fully accept Wellhausen here: he repeatedly argues P (and the atonement/temple theology) is *early*, not exilic — so on Day's own view the tabernacle need not be a late retrojection. (b) Conservatives such as [[Kenneth Kitchen]] argue the tabernacle's design and materials have genuine Late Bronze Age Egyptian and tent-shrine parallels (portable royal/divine tents are attested), which would make an Exodus-era sanctuary plausible rather than anachronistic. (c) The "two Moses traditions" reconstruction is a documentary inference, not external evidence; a single tradition with thematic variety is possible. (d) The whole case is a *literary* reconstruction built from the text it reorders — the recursion problem the episode flags elsewhere.

## LDS Relevance

The argument cuts two ways for a Latter-day Saint, and Day leans into the productive one. If the tabernacle is a retrojection, the *idea* that later writers projected the temple cult backward onto Moses is precisely the kind of editorial activity the Book of Mormon presupposes when it speaks of an edited, humanly-transmitted scriptural record — and it coheres with reading Nephite religion as **pre-reform First Temple** rather than post-exilic Priestly. It also pairs with the companion argument that [[The Patriarchal Cult Mirrors Pre-Reform Practice That Lehi Revives|the patriarchal narratives mirror later cult practice]]: both are cases of monarchy-era religion read back into the patriarchal/Mosaic past. Day's caution is that he accepts the *method* (sources, editing, retrojection as a real phenomenon) while disputing Wellhausen's specific *late* dating of P — illustrating his refrain that the documentary hypothesis "does not hurt the cause for restoration, it strengthens it" when the Book of Mormon is treated as data. See [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration]].
