# Pareidolia Objection to Chiasmus Finding

## The Claim (Objection)
The objection — voiced *by the hosts themselves* — is that LDS enthusiasts have become so primed to find chiasmus that they now see it everywhere, including where none was intended (apophenia / pareidolia). [[Cardon Ellis]] jokes about spotting "a kayazm between the pita bread and the Takis" at Trader Joe's and a "sandwich kayazm" (bread-meat-bread), then directly asks whether the whole enterprise has tipped into pattern-hallucination and how one would even "validate a kayasmus other than just noticing the literary form is there" [^1][^2].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **No validation method offered.** When pressed, [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah]] answers "I don't know. I have no idea... anybody can go read it" — i.e., he supplies *no* statistical or methodological test to distinguish real chiasm from imposed pattern, which is the crux of the objection [^3]. *Rating: this is the strongest critique in the episode, and it lands because the presenter concedes he has no validation criterion.*
2. **Low base-rate bar.** Short ABA structures and keyword symmetry arise readily in any repetitive, rhetorically heightened text; the sandwich gag is a real (if comic) demonstration that the form is trivially "findable" [^1].
3. **The presenter declines scholarly vetting.** Jonah quips "I make a point of not talking to real scholars," removing the very peer check that would catch pareidolia; Cardon notes a PhD's social role is to "vet what is good theory, what is bad theory, what hasn't been figured out already" [^4].
4. **Steelman of the chiasmus-finder.** In fairness to the form: genuine, statistically robust chiasms *do* exist in ancient texts, and the existence of false positives does not prove every instance is illusory. The proper response to pareidolia is not to abandon chiasmus but to apply rigorous criteria (density, boundary control, statistical significance, intentionality) — which is exactly what this episode skips. So the objection indicts the *method here*, not chiasmus as such.
