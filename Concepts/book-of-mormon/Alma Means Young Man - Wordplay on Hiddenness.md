# Alma Means "Young Man" — Wordplay on Hiddenness

## The Claim
The name **Alma** (1) is an attested ancient male Semitic name meaning "lad / young man" (likely a hypocoristicon of "young man of God"), and (2) the Book of Mormon plays on this meaning when it introduces Alma as "a young man," and on a *second* root (*ʿlm*, "to hide") as Alma repeatedly hides and acts in secret. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **Attested as a male Semitic name.** [[Hugh Nibley]] first proposed "young man / a sign"; [[Paul Hoskisson]] argued "lad"; [[Terry Szink]] documented attestation **at Ebla**. [[Neal Rappleye]] has further verified it as an ancient male name. Strength: **high** ("basically verified"). [^2]
2. **Introduction matches the meaning.** [[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]] introduces Alma as "a young man" — and Bowen notes *no one else* in the Book of Mormon is introduced this way. Strength: **high** (unmotivated detail). [^1]
3. **Second-root wordplay on hiding.** The similar Hebrew root *ʿlm* (*alam*) means "to hide/conceal." After Alma is expelled from King Noah's court and his life is sought, he **hides** in the wilderness of Mormon and acts "privately ... secretly" — Mosiah 18 hammers this: he "went about privately," "taught them privately," "did hide himself." A generation later his son Alma the Younger goes about "in secret" to destroy the church. Strength: **moderate-high** (the secrecy emphasis is conspicuous). [^3]

**Steelman:** A skeptic notes (a) "young man" is plain King James English, so the introduction needn't presuppose Hebrew; (b) the *ʿlm* "hide" root is a *different* root from the *ʿalmā* "young" root — combining two roots looks like having it both ways; (c) any fugitive narrative naturally involves hiding, so the "secret" language may be plot, not pun. Bowen concedes the conflation of roots is partly a sound-play, not a strict etymology — but argues the density of "hide/secret" vocabulary clustered on a character named Alma is the kind of patterning a Hebrew narrator would exploit.
