# Stylometry Shows Multiple Authors in the Book of Mormon

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
The fraud theory holds that one man, [[Joseph Smith]], wrote the entire Book of Mormon. Arnold argues that computational analysis of writing style is inconsistent with single authorship [^15][^16].

## The Rebuttal
1. **What stylometry measures.** Arnold defines it as "the computational study of writing style measuring vocabulary, sentence structure, syntax patterns, and word frequency." Applied to the Book of Mormon, it "finds the different books within the Book of Mormon to be inconsistent with a single author" [^15]. *Rating: the method is real and mainstream; results for the Book of Mormon are contested in the technical literature.*
2. **The method is independently validated.** It is "not an LDS-specific methodology" — the same technique resolved the Shakespeare authorship debate, attributed the disputed Federalist Papers to Hamilton vs. Madison, and identifies anonymous authors. "When applied to known authors, it works." [^15]
3. **The 3-D authorship graph.** On a three-dimensional authorship plot, "the different books of the Book of Mormon scatter across completely different regions. Joseph Smith's own writings cluster in one place, but the Book of Mormon's books don't cluster with him or with each other." [^16]
4. **The novelist control.** "Professional novelists who deliberately create different character voices still produce a very consistent stylometric fingerprint across all of their work. The Book of Mormon doesn't." This pre-empts the "Joseph just wrote in different voices" reply. *Rating: a genuine and underappreciated point — wordprint studies generally show an author's signature survives deliberate voice-changes.* [^16]
5. **Steelman of the skeptic.** (a) Stylometry on *translations* is notoriously unreliable: a translator's hand, dictation, and an archaizing KJV register can blur or distort wordprints. (b) Some methods (e.g., Holmes 1991) found the Book of Mormon *consistent with single authorship* or with Joseph's circle; results depend heavily on method, feature set, and which "non-contextual" function words are used. (c) Differing genres/sources within one author's corpus can also scatter on a plot. Arnold reports the pro-LDS wordprint results (e.g., the Jockers-style multi-author findings) without flagging the methodological disputes or the contrary studies [^16].
