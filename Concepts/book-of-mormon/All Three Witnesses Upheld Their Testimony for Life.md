# All Three Witnesses Upheld Their Testimony for Life

#type/rebuttal

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Kyle Beshears]]'s book pairs [[John Whitmer]] (who kept his witness testimony despite excommunication, "lending credence to his word") against [[Martin Harris]] (who "complicated his testimony" with "spiritual eyes" language) — an apparent balance. [[David Snell]] objects that this framing is unbalanced because it singles out one steadfast witness against one wavering one, when in fact *all three* original witnesses upheld their testimonies for the rest of their lives despite leaving or being excommunicated from the church. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **It was not just John Whitmer — it was all three originals.** Snell: "It wasn't just John Whitmer who was excommunicated and upheld his testimony throughout the rest of his life. It was... all three of the original [witnesses]... either leaving the church or being excommunicated and then later coming back or either way just still upholding their testimony throughout their life." [^2] *Rating: strong and historically accurate* — Oliver Cowdery, [[David Whitmer]], and Martin Harris each separated from the church yet never recanted the witness statement; this is the standard, well-documented apologetic datum.

2. **Excommunication strengthens, not weakens, the testimony.** A witness who is alienated from the institution and has every social incentive to recant, yet maintains the testimony, is *more* credible, not less — there is no remaining motive to prop up a lie. *Rating: strong;* this is exactly why Beshears flagged John Whitmer's case as "lending credence," and Snell's point is that the logic applies to all three.

3. **Even non-original witnesses described material detail.** Snell and Beshears note a witness "not even supposed to see them" reported seeing "a corner and actually saw a metallic color to them," which led Beshears "to the conclusion there was, in fact, a material artifact." [^3] *Rating: strong;* it pushes past testimony-count to physical description (cf. [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]]).

4. **Beshears concedes the imbalance.** He admits "you've just pointed out to me that I kind of broke a rule" and that most evangelicals will encounter the steadfast-witness fact "for the first time" through his book; he says his next book has "a massive section devoted to just the witnesses." [^4][^5] *Rating: a candid concession* that the original framing under-served the evidence.

**Steelman of the critic.** A skeptic can grant that all three never *formally recanted* while noting that recantation is psychologically costly (it would mean admitting decades of public self-deception), and that maintaining a testimony is not the same as the experience being veridical. The strongest critical move is not "they recanted" (they did not) but "their unrecanted testimony is compatible with a sincere visionary or suggestible experience" — which is why the "spiritual eyes" debate matters (see [[Spiritual Eyes Described Real Things in the 1800s]]).
