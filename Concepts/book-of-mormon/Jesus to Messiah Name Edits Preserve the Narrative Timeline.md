# Jesus to Messiah Name Edits Preserve the Narrative Timeline

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

**Objection:** In the 1830 edition the name **Jesus** appears in [[1 Nephi 13.40|First Nephi]]
*before* the narrative point at which the name is said to be first revealed
([[2 Nephi 10.3|2 Nephi 10]]). Later editions changed those early occurrences
from "Jesus" to "Messiah." The critic says he "can't think of any other reason
that this might be updated except to preserve the timeline and maintain that
Jesus' name was not first revealed until Second Nephi 10" — i.e., a cover-up of an
internal inconsistency.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The two terms denote the same person

Saints Unscripted: "Both Messiah and Jesus point to the same being, the same
person, the same savior. They're interchangeable." Swapping a title for a name
changes no doctrine.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Linguistically sound. *Messiah* (Hebrew *māšîaḥ*) is a
> title ("anointed one"); *Jesus* (*Iēsous* < *Yēšûaʿ*) is the personal name. They
> co-refer.

### 2. Under a participatory translation model, the edit is *expected*

The host argues that once you accept Joseph was involved in rendering the text
(not passively reading fixed words), "this basically answers itself. If using
Messiah instead of Jesus makes more sense for the narrative, then it should be
changed." A translator noticing that the personal name is revealed later could
reasonably retro-fit the earlier passages to the less specific title.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Coherent given the model in
> [[Editing Revealed Scripture Is Compatible with Divine Origin]]; circular if one
> rejects that model.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic essentially concedes the apologists' explanation and turns it into the
indictment: the edit exists *precisely* to remove an internal anachronism, which
is what an author smoothing his own fiction does. If the text were a faithful
translation of an ancient record, the "anachronistic Jesus" would either have been
authentic (and untouched) or absent from the start. Choosing "Messiah" to protect
2 Nephi 10's claim is editorial damage control, not translation. The synonymy of
the terms is irrelevant to the *motive* the critic is highlighting.
