# The Seer Stone Swap and Jerusalem Walls Show Joseph Was Receiving Not Composing

#type/claim

## The Claim
Beyond the *scale* of the dictation (see [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]]), three concrete behavioral anomalies during translation are hard to reconcile with a man reciting a story he authored or memorized — they look instead like a man *processing information he is receiving in real time*.[^1] If "all of it came from Joseph's own mind to begin with, there shouldn't have been any names or words he couldn't pronounce," and he should not have been *surprised* by the content.[^2]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **He spelled out names he could not pronounce.** When Joseph hit "a name or a long word during dictation that he couldn't pronounce, his wife said he would spell it out as it appeared on his translation tool."[^2] A storyteller drawing from his own imagination would have no unpronounceable proper nouns; spelling-by-sight implies the text was presented *to* him, not generated *by* him. *(Moderate-to-strong — sourced to [[Emma Smith]]; a self-authored text plausibly contains no words its author cannot say.)*
2. **He was surprised that Jerusalem had walls.** [[Emma Smith]] said Joseph "was surprised to learn from the translation that Jerusalem had walls around it, suggesting that he was actively processing at least some of this information for the first time as it was being dictated."[^3] Surprise at one's own invented content is incoherent; surprise at received content is expected. *(Moderate — a single recollected anecdote, but a pointed one: an author is not surprised by his own plot.)*
3. **The Martin Harris seer-stone swap.** Told that Joseph was merely "repeating memorized sentences," scribe [[Martin Harris]] secretly swapped Joseph's [[Seer Stones and Divining Rods as Revelatory Means|seer stone]] for a similar-looking stone. "If Joseph was just making it up, he should have continued with the dictation as if nothing had happened" — instead he was confused and "can't translate a word until Martin finally fesses up."[^4] This is a near-experimental control: a memorized recitation is independent of which stone is in the hat; the failure on substitution implies the stone was functionally load-bearing to Joseph's process. *(Strong as reported — it is a deliberate test by a skeptical insider with a clean predicted-vs-actual contrast; weaker only on the late, faith-friendly provenance of the account.)*

## Steelman of the Opposing View
A naturalist can absorb each anomaly. (a) *Spelling names*: a composer improvising exotic Hebraic-sounding names could readily produce strings he found awkward to pronounce and spell them for the scribe's benefit — no external text required. (b) *Jerusalem's walls*: "surprise" is Emma's later characterization; a skilled improviser can voice discovery as a narrative effect, and the detail may have been embellished in decades-later retellings by a devoted widow. (c) *The stone swap*: the story is attested late and only from sympathetic sources; even taken at face value, a performer deep in a self-induced focused state might genuinely stumble when his expected ritual prop is altered, which is a psychological, not supernatural, explanation. (d) More broadly, all three rest on family/insider testimony recorded years afterward, the same evidentiary base the storyteller theory is challenged for trusting selectively. The defender's fair claim is the conjunction: any one anomaly is explainable, but a self-authored-and-memorized model has to explain *all* of them at once — unpronounceable names, genuine surprise, and a prop-dependent failure under a controlled swap — and each cuts against "he was just telling a story from his head."

## Best Quotes
> "When Joseph came to a name or a long word during dictation that he couldn't pronounce, his wife said he would spell it out as it appeared on his translation tool. If all of it came from Joseph's own mind to begin with, there shouldn't have been any names or words he couldn't pronounce."[^2]

> "She also said that Joseph was surprised to learn from the translation that Jerusalem had walls around it, suggesting that he was actively processing at least some of this information for the first time as it was being dictated."[^3]

> "To test that claim, Martin swapped out Joseph's seer stone with a similar-looking stone. If Joseph was just making it up, he should have continued... but he doesn't. Instead, he's confused and can't translate a word until Martin finally fesses up."[^4]
