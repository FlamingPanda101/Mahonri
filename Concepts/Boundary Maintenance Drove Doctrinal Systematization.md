# Boundary Maintenance Drove Doctrinal Systematization

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that the *reason* doctrine had to be reduced to a sharp, binary, defensible system was **boundary maintenance**: once the church became "a powerful social institution" with "oversight over the entire population," it needed to know "who is in and who is out."[^1] Belief that previously could be loosely held in diverse communities now had to be "systematized... reduced to something we can draw lines around in a very simple binary way," because a strong in-group defined against an out-group requires unambiguous membership criteria.[^2] The Trinity, on this view, functions partly as a boundary marker.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Boundary maintenance is a recognized driver of religious dogma — rate: strong
The sociological principle — that high-tension groups sharpen doctrinal boundaries to police membership — is well established (cf. social-identity theory, the in-group/out-group dynamics studied across religious movements). McClellan applies a standard sociology-of-religion lens to 4th-century Christianity.[^3] *Rating: strong as a general mechanism.* See [[Trinity as Identity Marker and Costly Signal]].

### 2. It explains the move from tolerated diversity to enforced creed — rate: persuasive
Before institutionalization, "everybody just kind of was drawn to the group or the idea that resonated with them"; afterward a "powerful social institution needed to harmonize things... in order to establish boundaries and say this one's in, this one's out."[^1] This dovetails with [[Canonization Forced the Harmonization That Produced the Trinity]] — canon and creed together become the in/out filter. *Rating: persuasive; coheres with the heresiological literature of the period.* **Steelman:** an orthodox historian would grant that creeds did serve boundary functions but argue the *content* was settled by genuine theological conviction about apostolic truth, not invented merely to exclude — heresy was opposed because it was judged false, and the boundary follows from the truth-claim rather than the reverse.

### 3. The "binary" framing may overstate — rate: moderate
McClellan's claim that everything "has to be reduced to something we can draw lines around in a very simple binary way" is a useful generalization, but creedal Christianity tolerated considerable diversity (rites, theological schools, monastic vs. lay practice). The binary applied chiefly to a small set of load-bearing christological/trinitarian propositions, not to belief wholesale. *Rating: moderate; directionally correct but compressed.*

## Best Quotes
> "Boundary maintenance becomes monumentally important in a circumstance like that where there's a very strong in-group and you are opposed to the out-group and you want to make sure that you know who is who. And so it had to be systematized."[^2]

> "Suddenly the ideas that resonated with different groups... you now had a powerful social institution that needed to harmonize things and needed everything to work together in order to be able to establish boundaries and say this one's in, this one's out."[^1]

> "It all has to be reduced to something that we can draw lines around in a very simple binary way."[^2]
