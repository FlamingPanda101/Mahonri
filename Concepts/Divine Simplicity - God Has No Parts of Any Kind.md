# Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind

## The Point
Divine simplicity is the doctrine — central to classical theism and to Feser's Neo-Platonic proof — that God is in no way composed of parts. [^1] He has no material parts, and no metaphysical parts: no composition of form and matter, and no composition of essence and existence. [^1] Because the first cause must be the terminus of the regress of composers, it must be utterly noncomposite. [^1] This drives the further conclusion that God's attributes are not really distinct *parts* of Him: His intellect, power, eternity, goodness, and so forth "are really all one and the same thing, just conceived of or described in different ways." [^2]

## Evidence
- The first cause "must be something noncomposite, something utterly simple in the sense of having no parts of any kind—no material parts, and no metaphysical parts like form and matter or essence and existence." [^1]
- *Immutability* follows: to change is to gain or lose a feature, which a simple thing (having no parts to gain or lose) cannot do; hence God is also eternal and beginningless/endless. [^3]
- *Immateriality* follows: a material entity has parts that must be combined, so the partless One cannot be material. [^4]
- *Pure actuality* follows: if the One had potentiality as well as actuality, it would have an actual part and a potential part; having no parts, it must be purely actual. [^5]
- The attributes are not distinct components. Using Frege's sense/reference distinction, "'the One's omnipotence' and 'the One's intellect' don't have the same sense, but they refer to the very same thing, to a single, simple, or noncomposite reality." [^2]
- We can speak of such a God only *analogically*, not univocally — His power and intellect are not exactly what power and intellect are in us, because in us they are genuinely distinct features and in Him they are one. [^6]

## Best Quotes
> "it must be something noncomposite, something utterly simple in the sense of having no parts of any kind." [^1]

> "The intellect, omnipotence, eternity, immateriality, and so forth of the One are really all one and the same thing, just conceived of or described in different ways." [^2]

## LDS Connection
This is the **central conflict** between Feser's classical theism and the Restoration. Against atheism, divine simplicity coherently secures God's uncausedness, immutability, and ultimacy — it is a serious, ancient doctrine deserving fair representation. But it is the very opposite of Latter-day Saint teaching. The Restoration confesses that "The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's" and worships a God "of body, parts, and passions" (D&C 130:22) — the explicit negation of the classical "without body, parts, or passions." On the LDS view God the Father is an exalted, embodied person (see [[God Was Once a Man]]), with really distinct attributes and a real history, situated among co-eternal realities He organized rather than created from nothing — see [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]] and [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained]]. Feser's simple, partless One is closer to the abstract Absolute of the creeds than to the Father revealed to Joseph Smith; the philosophical reshaping is traced in [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]] and critiqued in [[Classical Theism Cannot Be Reconciled With the Bible]].
