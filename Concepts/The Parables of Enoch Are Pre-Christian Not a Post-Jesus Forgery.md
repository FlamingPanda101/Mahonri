# The Parables of Enoch Are Pre-Christian Not a Post-Jesus Forgery

#type/claim

## The Claim

The strongest critical objection to the Enochic-Messiah argument is that the Book of Parables ([[1 Enoch]] 37–71) — the one section with the pre-existent enthroned Son of Man — is **absent from the Dead Sea Scrolls** and could have been written by Christians after Jesus to manufacture prophecy. Arnold steelmans this, then argues it fails on three counts: the scholarly consensus has moved to a pre-Christian date, the Parables contain no Christian content, and the late-dating argument is circular.

> **Note:** This is the counterpoint to [[Book of Enoch - Pseudepigrapha and Late Composition]], which argues that 1 Enoch *as a whole* is a late, composite, pseudepigraphal text. The two are compatible: the *completed book* accreted late layers, yet the Parables' *Christology* is still pre-Christian. The dispute here is specifically about the Parables' date and origin, not the book's unified antiquity.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Absence Is Real (the steelman)
Every section of 1 Enoch *except* the Parables is represented at Qumran, so the most explicitly Messianic section has no pre-Christian physical manuscripts. J.T. Milik ([[Joseph Milik]]), who published the editio princeps of the Aramaic fragments, argued the Parables were composed late, possibly under Christian influence — "a real scholarly argument from a real scholar."[^1] See [[Book of Enoch - Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation]].

### 2. The Consensus Has Moved Past Milik
> "George Nickelsburg, the leading authority on First Enoch... places the Parables in the late 1st century BC to early 1st century AD, and John Collins at Yale agrees. Gabriele Boccaccini convened an international Enoch seminar specifically to work through these questions, and the contributors overwhelmingly favor pre-Christian composition."[^2]

[[George Nickelsburg]], John J. Collins, Gabriele Boccaccini, and [[James VanderKam]] — none LDS — converge on a pre-Christian or turn-of-the-era date. The field "has completely moved on."

**Evidence rating: secondary** — a real scholarly consensus, though dating apocalyptic texts without manuscripts involves inference.

### 3. The Parables Contain No Christian Content
> "The Parables contain no actual Christian content. There's no Jesus, no crucifixion, no resurrection, no atonement theology... If a Christian wrote this to manufacture Messianic prophecy after the fact, then they forgot to include Christianity."[^3]

A forger fabricating Messianic proof-texts would name the Messiah he was vindicating; the Parables never do. This is the decisive internal evidence.

**Evidence rating: primary** — the absence of Christian markers is observable in the text.

### 4. The Late-Dating Argument Is Circular
The argument "the Parables must be late because they're too theologically precise about a pre-existent divine Messiah to predate Jesus" assumes its conclusion — that such precise Messianism *couldn't* exist before Jesus.[^4] But that is exactly the point in dispute; one cannot date the text late *because* it is Messianic and then cite its lateness as proof Messianism was post-Christian.

## Best Quotes

> "If a Christian wrote this to manufacture Messianic prophecy after the fact, then they forgot to include Christianity. That's a remarkable oversight for someone that's trying to validate Christianity."[^3]
