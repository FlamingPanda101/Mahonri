# Joseph Smith Did Not Author the Book of Mormon

## The Claim
In a concession host [[Steven Pynakker]] calls "a major concession," [[Kyle Beshears]] states plainly: "I do not believe Joseph Smith to be the author of the Book of Mormon." He immediately closes off both misreadings — he is *not* conceding the LDS ancient-scripture narrative, and he is *not* declaring it ancient. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The text is too theologically sophisticated for the young Joseph.** Whoever wrote it "was extremely attuned to the Protestant zeitgeist of the early American Republic," "very theologically sophisticated and intelligent in the Bible," showing evidence of someone "actually trained or... a lay minister after a long season of time." [^3] *Rating: a substantive literary-theological argument,* distinct from mere "he couldn't read."

2. **Encultured ≠ trained.** Beshears carefully distinguishes: Joseph "grew up in a society that was drenched in the Bible," but being "encultured by biblical language and motif is similar but not the same to saying that you were theologically sophisticated." [^4] *Rating: an important and often-blurred distinction* in Book of Mormon debates.

3. **Age and profile mismatch.** Joseph was "in his early 20s" when producing the text; "the sophistication and the level of theological intelligence that would be required... does not fit, in my opinion, what we know about Joseph Smith." [^5] *Rating: an argument from improbability,* explicitly offered as opinion.

4. **The "original beneath the manuscript" hypothesis.** Beshears finds it "much easier to see Joseph Smith as a redactor or an editor" of "some kind of original Book of Mormon beneath the Book of Mormon manuscripts," producing "the finished product." [^6] *Rating: a coherent alternative,* though he admits it is "the best I can go on."

5. **The strongest counter-argument he grants.** The Oliver Cowdery scribal theory — that Joseph's imaginative, charismatic gift was "honed literarily through Oliver Cowdery," explaining why the manuscript was "cranked out in mere weeks" once Cowdery arrived (unlike the slower start with Emma or Martin Harris). "I think it's the closest argument that would persuade me otherwise." [^7] *Rating: notably even-handed* — he names the best LDS-adjacent rebuttal to his own thesis.

**Steelman of the LDS view.** Pynakker presses the obvious reply: Mormon himself was "the redactor and the editor" who "had all these plates and was putting it all together" — so a complex, multi-author, edited text is exactly what the LDS account predicts. [^8] Beshears concedes the text's genuine complexity ("a lot going on there that I struggle to see coming from one person... in his early 20s") even as he attributes that complexity to a 19th-century redactor rather than ancient prophets. The disagreement, both agree, is unresolved — which is "why it's still an important text."

## Best Quotes
> "I do not believe Joseph Smith to be the author of the Book of Mormon." [^2]

> "Being completely encultured by biblical language and motif is similar but not the same to saying that you were theologically sophisticated." [^4]

> "There's some kind of original Book of Mormon beneath the Book of Mormon manuscripts that we have. And it's much easier for me to see Joseph Smith as a redactor or an editor." [^6]

> "I agree with them that it's sophisticated in the sense that there's a lot going on there that I struggle to see coming from one person who's in his early 20s and just riffing as he goes as if it was some kind of jazz concert and Oliver is struggling to keep up." [^7]
