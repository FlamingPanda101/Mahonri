# Pornography Corrupts Distinct Gendered Desires

## The Claim

[[Trent Horn]] argues that pornography is **not a "male sin"** — it is a human sin that simply *attacks men and women along different lines* because male and female sexuality are not identical. The same disordered habit hooks each sex by corrupting a *good, God-given desire* proper to that sex.[^1]

- **Men** have a good natural desire "to want to pursue and woo a woman they love," directing their strength and competence toward being a full gift of self. Pornography aimed at men corrupts this by *sexualizing the competence itself* and inverting the gift: "arousal centers on extracting pleasure from a woman instead of being a gift to her," which is why male-oriented videos focus on male pleasure and frequently involve "demeaning women through acts of violence, humiliation with bodily fluids."[^2]
- **Women** have a good natural desire "to want to be the object of a romantic chase, to be pursued and wooed" — to be so captivating that she can receive a worthy man's strength. Pornographic material for women (overwhelmingly *written*) corrupts this by centering on "a dangerous man dominating, sometimes violently, a woman who is initially opposed to him or even an enemy" — the pirate/rogue/"bad boy" the heroine civilizes.[^3]

Horn grounds the asymmetry in evidence that women's sexuality is more rooted in emotional connection: men are far likelier to pursue relations with a stranger (the 1978/1982 campus studies, recreated by the *Whatever* podcast: ~30% of men said yes to a stranger's proposition, 0% of women).[^4] He invokes John and Stasi Eldredge's *Captivating* — historical paintings depict nude women reclining far more than nude men, because "Adam is captured best in motion, doing something. His essence is strength and action" — and notes women cross-culturally prefer competent, higher-earning men, so "a man becomes more attractive when he puts on more clothes."[^5] Sociologist [[W. Bradford Wilcox]] supplies the corollary that "women civilize men when they are united to them in matrimony," explaining why married men work harder and behave better; pornography hijacks this same civilizing-the-beast impulse for arousal.[^6]

The payoff is pastoral and ecclesial: because the sin is universal but *gendered in form*, the Church should "equally admonish men and women," stop "finger-wagging the men and coddling women," and reframe the message from *men should reject this* to *people should reject this*, uniting the sexes to "carry each other's burdens" rather than setting them against each other.[^7]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The behavioral asymmetry is documented, not asserted.** Horn cites the Clark–Hatfield-style campus experiments (1978, 1982) and a modern recreation (the *Whatever* podcast) showing a stark sex difference in willingness to accept a stranger's sexual proposition — and the Barna Group's finding that men's secondary reasons skew "fun/escape boredom" while women's skew "curiosity / aid to intimacy." This grounds the "emotional connection" premise empirically rather than by stereotype.[^4]
2. **The medium tracks the desire.** That almost all "romance" fiction is written *for* women, and that the written word "allows women to make sexual fantasies more intimate, private, prolonged," is offered as confirmation that female arousal leans on narrative and emotional context — exactly what a corrupted "desire to be pursued" would produce. See [[The Written Word Is Women's Pornography]].[^8]
3. **It avoids the double standard most anti-porn messaging falls into.** By insisting the vice is shared, Horn pre-empts the "this is a man problem" framing that, he argues, keeps women ashamed and isolated ("they think women do not struggle with this problem, which also isn't true"), and removes the asymmetric blame that poisons the conversation.[^1]

### Steelman of the critic

A secular or feminist critic can resist on several fronts. (a) **The "good gendered desires" are smuggled essentialism.** The claim that women's nature is "to be pursued" and "for submission to a husband" (Horn's phrase) is precisely the contested premise — a critic will say he is reading 1950s gender roles back into biology and calling the result God's design; the *Captivating*/Eldredge material is devotional, not science. (b) **Correlation ≠ corrupted essence.** Even if men more often proposition strangers, that may reflect socialization, differential risk (a woman accepting a male stranger faces far more danger — a confound Horn himself notes for the men's "no's"), and reputational cost, not a fixed teleology of the sexual faculty. (c) **Wilcox's "civilizing" thesis is sociology about *outcomes*, not a warrant for a normative account of female nature**, and the marriage-benefits literature is debated for selection effects. (d) The reframing "people should reject this" is welcome, but a critic will note it sits awkwardly beside Horn's simultaneous claim that "God made women for submission" — which reintroduces exactly the asymmetry he says the Church should drop. Horn's reply is that he is describing *complementary* goods, not unequal worth, and that the empirical sex differences are robust enough to survive the confounds; the design inference is offered modestly, as the best explanation of why these particular desires exist and why their corruption takes these particular shapes (cf. [[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]]).

## Best Quotes

> "Explicit materials attack men and women in different ways… Men have a good natural desire to want to pursue and woo a woman they love… Women have a good and natural desire to want to be the object of a romantic chase, to be pursued and wooed." — Horn[^2]

> "In adult content, strength and action is corrupted, so the competence itself is sexualized. Arousal centers on extracting pleasure from a woman instead of being a gift to her." — Horn[^2]

> "What would help is if we stopped saying men should reject this and instead said people should reject this." — Horn[^7]
