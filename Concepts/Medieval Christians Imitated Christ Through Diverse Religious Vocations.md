# Medieval Christians Imitated Christ Through Diverse Religious Vocations

## The Point

[[Miranda Wilcox]] argues that medieval Christians "felt called to imitate the life of Christ," looking to his ministry (see [[Matthew 20.28|Matthew 20:28]] — the Son of Man came "to minister") and discerning vocations "to prayer, poverty, preaching, evangelizing, nursing, celibacy, pastoral care, contemplation, and other good works." [^1] Between the eleventh and thirteenth centuries a great diversity of orders proliferated, modeled on the apostolic community of [[Acts 1.11|Acts]] 2 and 4.

## Evidence

1. **Reform sought the apostolic life.** Reformers "tried to resurrect ancient ideals... by attempting to return to the apostolic life, which meant a life of poverty, the communal sharing of goods, and, in some cases, itinerant preaching." Some imitated the Egyptian and Syrian desert hermits; others observed the Benedictine Rule and the Rule of St. Augustine. [^2]
2. **Orders split between retreat and engagement.** Some "focused on retreat from the world (Cistercians and Carthusians), and others focused on active engagement with the world (Dominicans and Franciscans)." [^2]
3. **Lay penitent movements blurred the boundary.** Communities of lay penitents — the Waldensians (northern Italy), the Humiliati (Lombardy), and the Beguines (Low Countries) — undertook "an active, urban apostolate," living "according to the form of the primitive church" (Acts 4:32) while working in cities by day and praying in solitude by night. [^3]
4. **[[Francis of Assisi|Francis]] and [[Clare of Assisi|Clare]] as exemplars.** Francesco di Bernardone (1181-1226) and Chiara Offreduccio (1193-1253) "renounced their wealthy families and devoted themselves to following Christ's footsteps by practicing physical asceticism, material poverty," service, humility, and love. Francis's encounter with lepers turned bitterness "into sweetness of soul and body" and revealed his vocation "to follow the footprints of Christ." [^4]
5. **Clare's Testament names the central themes.** "Penitence, poverty, humility, love, and mutual charity that joins the sisters, and loving, service-oriented leadership." [^5] The movement organized into the Friars Minor, the Poor Clares, and the Brothers and Sisters of Penance.

## Best Quotes

> "Medieval Christians felt called to imitate the life of Christ. They looked to Christ's ministry as recorded in the Bible for inspiration and discerned vocations to prayer, poverty, preaching, evangelizing, nursing, celibacy, pastoral care, contemplation, and other good works." [^1]

> "The Lord gave me, Brother Francis, thus to begin doing penance... for when I was in sin, it seemed too bitter for me to see lepers. And the Lord himself led me among them and I showed mercy to them. And when I left them, what had seemed bitter to me was turned into sweetness of soul and body." — Francis of Assisi [^4]
