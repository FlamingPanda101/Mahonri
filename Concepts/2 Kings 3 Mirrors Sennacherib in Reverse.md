# 2 Kings 3 Mirrors Sennacherib in Reverse

#type/claim

## The Claim
The Moab campaign of [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3]] and the deliverance of Jerusalem in [[2 Kings 19.18|2 Kings 18–19]] are deliberately constructed as **structural mirror-images**, and reading them together exposes the territorial ("purview") theology both share. In both, a king revolts at the moment of an empire's dynastic weakness; an invasion is mounted; an intercessory prayer / prophet scene precedes a decisive divine act; and the invader fails to take the final capital and "returns home." The difference is *which side wins* — and the rule that decides it is **purview**: the deity defending its own land prevails, the deity invading another's land loses. In 18–19 the messenger of Adonai annihilates 185,000 Assyrians on Yahweh's home soil; in chapter 3 the "great fury" of Chemosh routs Yahweh's coalition on *Moab's* soil. Same grammar, opposite outcome. [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Parallel setup: revolt at the moment of imperial weakness (rating: strong; structural)
2 Kings 3 — Moab revolts against the northern kingdom when Ahab dies and Jehoram accedes. 2 Kings 18–19 — Hezekiah revolts against Assyria when Sargon II dies and Sennacherib accedes. McClellan: "that's the period of greatest weakness. That is when you strike." Both narratives turn the accession of a new sovereign into the revolt's trigger. [^2]

### 2. Shared retreat vocabulary (rating: strong; lexical)
Sennacherib, his mission unsuccessful, is described as "setting out and returning home" using **two of the same verbs** that describe the coalition's retreat in 2 Kings 3. The verbal echo is, on McClellan's reading, a signal that the two episodes are meant to be juxtaposed. [^3]

### 3. Parallel prophet / intercessory-prayer scene (rating: moderate-strong)
Chapter 3 has Elisha sought out to deliver Adonai's word before the decisive turn; 18–19 has the intercessory prayer "in Isaiah as well" before the messenger of Adonai acts. Each decisive divine intervention is framed by a prophetic/petitionary scene. [^4]

### 4. The invader cannot take the last city (rating: strong; structural)
In both, the campaign succeeds *until the final capital*: Sennacherib takes most of Judah's cities but not Jerusalem; the coalition succeeds across Moab until Mesha's last besieged city. The capital is where the defending deity's purview holds and the invasion breaks. [^5]

### 5. The asymmetry of presentation (rating: moderate; redaction-critical)
18–19 is expansive and triumphant (the defending nation's victory is dwelt upon at length); chapter 3 is "very curt... very short and succinct" because it narrates Yahweh's *failure* as invader, which "is embarrassing" and which the author wants to move past quickly. The contrast in length is itself a clue to the theology: triumph in one's own purview is celebrated; defeat outside it is hurried over. See [[Mesha Stele and Biblical Defeat Theology]] for the palace-vs-people inversion of defeat narration. [^6]

### 6. Steelman of the opposing view
A harmonizer reads 18–19 as the paradigm of Yahweh's universal sovereignty and treats chapter 3's brevity and ambiguity as room to deny any defeat — e.g., the coalition withdrew out of moral revulsion at the sacrifice, or for unstated tactical reasons, with no foreign-deity intervention implied. McClellan answers that this severs the deliberate parallel the text builds (same setup, same retreat verbs, same "could not take the capital") and has to supply motives "not in evidence," whereas the purview reading lets both episodes run on a single consistent logic.

## LDS Relevance
The mirror reading reinforces the territorial / divine-council substrate that LDS apologetics finds congenial: nations are the allotted purviews of distinct divine beings (cf. [[Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament]], [[Chemosh Defeats Yahweh Outside His Purview]]). Yet McClellan deploys it to argue that Elisha's guarantee of victory was an **unfulfilled prophecy** — a conclusion creedal inerrantists and LDS triumphalists alike may resist. As a critical scholar he is tracking "what the data indicate is most likely," not building a case for any tradition; the parallel cuts against tidy harmonization in every direction.
