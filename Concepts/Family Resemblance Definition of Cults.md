# Family Resemblance Definition of Cults

## The Claim
There is **no consensus definition** of "cult" (or even "religion"). Scholars use [[Ludwig Wittgenstein|Wittgenstein's]] "family resemblance" method — list characteristic features and judge whether a group has enough of them.

## Supporting Evidence
- No agreed definition exists, just as there's none for "religion" — illustrated by the "define a chair" problem and the awkward case of Buddhism (no soul, no God in its earliest forms) *(scholar)* [^1].
- The consensus feature-set for an NRM/cult *(scholar)*:
  1. a **charismatic leader** (and continued charismatic leadership) [^2]
  2. an **esoteric body of knowledge** — secret, ancient, newly revealed, or latent inner potential [^3]
  3. a **technique** for quick ("more proximate") personal transformation [^4]
  4. focus on **individual transformation**, not broad social policy [^4]
- Apply these to *early* Mormonism and it "looks like a new religious movement" sociologically — but, Dawson stresses, so did early Christianity [^4].

## Best Quote
> "A new religious movement or a cult is usually focused around or started with some highly charismatic leader... usually focused on some kind of esoteric body of knowledge... some kind of quick technique or method by which people can seize upon this knowledge and transform their lives." — [[Lorne Dawson]] [^2]

## Counter-arguments / related
- The "so did Christianity" parallel: [[Christianity Began as a New Religious Movement]].
