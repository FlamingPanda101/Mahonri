#type/claim

# Every Testable Joseph Smith Claim Fails Only Subjective Evidence Survives

## The Claim

This is [[Kolby Reddish]]'s master argument against resting any weight on the gold-plates witnesses, stated as a *critic-side* claim. Eyewitness testimony is **inherently subjective** — non-contemporaneous, un-cross-examinable, and (as he showed from his own embellished mission journal) reshaped by memory. So Reddish looks instead for the **testable, non-subjective parts implicit in the story**, and reports a consistent pattern: *every time* Joseph Smith makes a claim that can actually be checked against external reality, it fails. He concludes that the only evidence that ever survives scrutiny is the subjective kind, which cannot bear the supernatural conclusion.[^1] His exhibits:

- **The stone box.** Unlike the sword, Liahona, or Urim and Thummim, the box had no claimed divine custody requiring its removal — it should simply still be there. Yet within a few years [[David Whitmer]] reports it "washed out" and gone. "Convenient" that the one durable, locatable artifact vanishes. (Cf. the believing reply in [[Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box]].)[^2]
- **The Book of Abraham papyri** — recovered and found not to say what the translation claims.[^3]
- **Zelph; specific Nephite ruins/artifact identifications** — fail when checked.[^3]
- **The Kinderhook plates** — Joseph engaging a 19th-century forgery as ancient.[^4]
- **The Great Isaiah Scroll (the freshest exhibit).** Reddish says he had just recorded an LDS Discussions episode comparing Book-of-Mormon Isaiah to the Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ, ~125 BC, intact, a millennium older than the KJV's base text) and found **not a single instance** where the deviations between the KJV and the Book of Mormon agree with the scroll — though the brass-plates narrative predicts they should. See the directly opposing believing claim in [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Matches the Dead Sea Scrolls]].[^5]

The structure is a falsifiability complaint: "anytime there's a non-subjective testable thing for Mormonism, it just doesn't match up," so the residue of "real legitimacy" is always and only subjective experience.[^6]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The testable/subjective sorting is methodologically clean
Reddish's move — isolate the implicit *checkable* sub-claims of a testimony rather than litigate the testimony itself — is a recognized way to evaluate eyewitness reports (it is how he prepped trial witnesses). It sidesteps the "which quotes say what" stalemate the episode otherwise gets stuck in.[^1]
> **Evidence rating:** Strong as a *method*; its sweep depends entirely on whether the listed exhibits really are uniform losses (see steelman).

### 2. The Great Isaiah Scroll datum directly rebuts a live apologetic
The same Great-Isaiah-Scroll comparison is used by *believers* (e.g. Alex Arnold) to argue the Book of Mormon's Isaiah variants trend toward older readings Joseph couldn't have known — see [[Book of Mormon Isaiah Matches the Dead Sea Scrolls]]. Reddish reports the opposite empirical result: zero KJV-vs-Book-of-Mormon deviations agree with 1QIsaᵃ. The two claims cannot both stand as stated; this is the sharpest point of factual contact in the episode.[^5]
> **Evidence rating:** Potentially strong but **unverified in the source** — Reddish gives a categorical "not a single instance" without the tally, exactly the controlled count the believing note's own steelman says is missing. A reader should treat both directional claims as awaiting a peer-reviewed enumeration.

### 3. The "convenient vanishing" of the stone box
The box is the one artifact with no theological reason to disappear, so its loss is the cleanest test — and it fails.[^2]
> **Evidence rating:** Moderate — the believer's reply ([[Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box]], [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]]) is that hostile and independent sources attest the box *and* its mundane washing-out, which is evidence *for* a real object, not a convenient dodge.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

Murph and the believing tradition reply on several fronts. (a) **Selection / framing:** the "every testable claim fails" generalization is built from a curated list; the believer points to checkable claims he counts as *hits* — Nahom/NHM, chiasmus, the dictation feat, Hebraisms — so the universal quantifier is contestable. (b) **The artifacts are subjective by *category*, not by failure:** demanding a non-subjective, repeatable test for a one-time historical-religious event is itself a [[Miracle-Tests for God Commit the God-of-the-Gaps Fallacy|category error]]; history is not a lab. (c) **The stone box is positive evidence**, hostile-attested, with its disappearance independently corroborated. (d) On Isaiah, the believing claim and Reddish's claim are both under-specified; the honest position is that neither side has published the controlled variant tally, so the datum is *promissory* on both ends. (e) Finally, Murph reframes the whole pattern: even granting the subjectivity, the *cumulative improbability* of the naturalistic account is itself striking — see [[The Combined Improbability of the Naturalistic Witness Feat]]. Reddish concedes his "reverse testimony" does not hang on any single item ("it's not like it hangs on the stone box") — it is a *cumulative* impression, which is as defeasible as the believer's cumulative case.

## Best Quotes

> "Every time there is an actual testable claim for Joseph Smith, for Mormonism, I don't see the evidence that I would expect to see in reality. And so I guess then maybe it just pivots back to, well, that's what faith is for."[^6]

> "There's not a single instance where the deviations between the King James version of Isaiah and the Book of Mormon versions of Isaiah agree with the Great Isaiah Scroll. Like not a single instance. And yet if the brass plates narrative... is true, that is what we would expect to find."[^5]

> "It's very convenient that the stone box is able to be in place for multiple thousands of years and then within just a few short years is completely absent now from the hill."[^2]
