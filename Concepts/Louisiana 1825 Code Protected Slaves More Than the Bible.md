# Louisiana 1825 Code Protected Slaves More Than the Bible

## The Claim
[[Dan McClellan]] argues that, comparing **law to law**, the secular Civil Code of the State of Louisiana (1825) was *more* protective of enslaved people than biblical slave law — the reverse of the apologetic assumption that Scripture is uniquely humane.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Louisiana text (rated: strong; primary legal source).** Article 173 (ch. 3): "The slave is entirely subject to the will of his master, who may correct and chastise him... though not with unusual rigour, nor so as to maim or mutilate him, or to expose him to the danger of loss of life, or to cause his death." Beating a slave to death was prohibited outright.[^1]

2. **The biblical contrast (rated: strong; primary text).** Under [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]], if a beaten slave dies after "a day or two," there is **no punishment at all**.[^2] So the Bible is the *less* protective code.

3. **Durability of the wording (rated: moderate).** McClellan notes the Louisiana wording "is preserved in every subsequent edition of the Civil Code... all the way down to the 1860s."[^2]

4. **Steelman.** A defender could object that ancient and modern legal systems are incommensurable, or that biblical law's "avenged" clause might have carried a severe penalty in practice. McClellan pre-empts this: the comparison is apt because both are *laws on the books*, and even granting the most generous reading of "avenged," v. 21 still removes any penalty for delayed death.[^3] See [[Laws on the Books vs Facts on the Ground]].

## Best Quotes

> "The slave is entirely subject to the will of his master who may correct and chastise him... though not with unusual rigour nor so as to maim or mutilate him or to expose him to the danger of loss of life or to cause his death."[^1]

> "So according to the laws on the books in the pre–Civil War American South, the slave was more protected than the laws on the book in the Bible."[^2]
