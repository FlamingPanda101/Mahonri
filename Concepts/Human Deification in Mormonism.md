# Human Deification in Mormonism

The inverse of the claim that [[God Was Once a Man]]: Joseph Smith taught that
faithful humans can follow the same path of progression and themselves become gods.
This is the uniquely Mormon materialist version of divinization (theosis).

## The Claim

In the [[King Follett Discourse]], Joseph Smith taught that humans must "learn to be
a God yourselves" through a process of eternal progression — moving from "one small
degree to another," from "grace to grace," from "exaltation to exaltation" — until
arriving at "the station of God."[^1] This collapses the boundary between the
Creator God and created humanity, "blending those spheres together."[^2]

## Best Quote

> You have got to learn to be a God yourself... moving from one small degree to
> another and from a small capacity to a great one, from one grace to grace, from
> exaltation to exaltation, until you arrive at the station of God.[^3]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The Lorenzo Snow couplet:** [[Lorenzo Snow]], an apostle at the time, later
   captured the doctrine in the famous phrase: "As man is, God once was. As God
   is, man may become" — a couplet that Park notes "has its own history within
   the broader culture."[^4]

2. **Masonic framework:** Park argues the discourse directly draws from "Masonic
   ideas of advancement from stage to stage, from station to station, now placed
   on the eternities, given a sacred lineage within godhood."[^5]

3. **Modern LDS framing:** The Church's Gospel Topics essay "Becoming Like God"
   (likely authored by [[Terrell Givens]]) connects the doctrine to broader Christian
   traditions of theosis — "emphasizing both the exceptionalism of Mormonism as well
   as its connections to the broader Christian world."[^6]

4. **Scriptural precedent:** [[D&C 76]] spoke about exaltation (though "not as
   specifically as later LDS teachings would clarify"), and [[D&C 93]] taught that
   spirits existed "in the beginning with God."[^7]

## Counter-arguments

- Mainstream Christianity distinguishes sharply between the Creator and the creature;
  even traditions with robust theosis doctrines (Eastern Orthodoxy) maintain an
  ontological Creator/creature gap — humans participate in divine energies, not
  the divine essence, and never become gods *in essence*. Mormon theosis is
  materialist and ontological in a way patristic theosis is not.
- [[Gordon B. Hinckley]], when asked about the Lorenzo Snow couplet in a 1997 national
  interview, said: "I'm not sure we really teach that" — though in other interviews
  he tried to "double down," revealing "constant awkwardness."[^8]
