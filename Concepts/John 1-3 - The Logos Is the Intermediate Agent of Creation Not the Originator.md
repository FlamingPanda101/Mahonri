# John 1:3 — The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation, Not the Originator

#type/claim

## The Claim
A common Trinitarian argument runs: [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]] says the God of Israel created the world; [[John 1.3|John 1:3]] says all things were made through the Logos; therefore the Logos and the God of Israel are one and the same. [[Dan McClellan]] argues this **flatly misunderstands the Greek of John 1:3**. The verse says all things came to be *di' autou* — "**through / via him**" (the preposition *dia* + genitive) — which marks the Logos as the **intermediate, instrumental agent** of creation, *not* the **primary, originating agent**. The originating agent throughout this pattern is God the Father. So John 1:3, far from equating the Logos with the God of [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]], distinguishes the instrument of creation from its ultimate source. See [[John 1-1c Anarthrous Theos and Subordinate Divinity]].

## Supporting Evidence

1. **The preposition is *dia* + genitive ("through"), not *hypo* ("by").** John 1:3 reads *panta di' autou egeneto* — "all things came to be **through** him." Greek distinguishes the ultimate agent (expressed with *hypo* / *apo*) from the intermediate agent (expressed with *dia* + genitive). The Logos takes the *dia* slot, i.e. the instrument, not the source. *(Strong; the Greek datum is uncontested.)* [^1]
2. **BDAG places John 1:3 under "personal agency."** The standard lexicon (*A Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature*) gives a long entry for *dia*; **sense 4** is "marker of personal agency, through, by," with focus on agency *through* someone — and lists, underlined by McClellan, "Christ as intermediary in the creation of the world, John 1:3." The word is thus not the originator of creation and not "in the role of the God of Israel from Genesis 1:1." *(Strong; flagship reference work.)* [^2]
3. **Novakovich: secondary, intermediate agency; the ultimate agent is God.** [[Lydia Novakovich]]'s *John 1–10: A Handbook on the Greek Text* (Baylor Handbook series) glosses the construction as "secondary, intermediate agency," and states that "this formulation suggests that the primary, ultimate agent of creation is God." *(Strong; specialist handbook on the Greek text.)* [^3]
4. **Wallace's three-tier pattern of agency.** [[Daniel Wallace]], *Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics* (1996), on John 1:3: "the Logos is represented as the creator in a hands-on sort of way, with the implication that God is the ultimate agent." He lays out the NT's typical (though not exclusive) pattern: **ultimate agency** ascribed to God the Father with *hypo*; **intermediate agency** ascribed to Christ with *dia*; **impersonal means** ascribed to the Holy Spirit with *en* or the simple dative. *(Strong; from a conservative grammarian, so not special pleading by a skeptic.)* [^4]
5. **McHugh (ICC): "through him," not "by him."** The International Critical Commentary volume by [[John McHugh]] stresses that the text says *through* him, not *by* him, and that *dia* with the genitive "is always used of the contribution made by God's personal agents" — e.g. John the Baptist as agent-witness, Moses as agent-lawgiver. The Logos is an agent of God's work, parallel in *grammatical role* to other commissioned agents. *(Strong; major critical commentary.)* [^5]
6. **The syllogism therefore equivocates.** "God created" (Genesis 1:1, ultimate agency) and "the Logos created" (John 1:3, intermediate agency) are not the same claim, so they cannot be chained to conclude the Logos *is* the God of Israel. The two texts describe **different roles in one act**, which is exactly what a mediator Christology predicts. *(Strong.)* [^6]

**Steelman of the opposing view.** A Trinitarian can reply that *dia* + genitive does not entail *inferiority* of nature, only distinction of *role*: the eternal Son can share the Father's essence while functioning as the agent "through whom" creation occurs (cf. [[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Cor 8:6]] — "one God the Father... and one Lord Jesus Christ, through [*dia*] whom are all things"; [[Colossians 1.16|Col 1:16]]; [[Hebrews 1.1-2|Heb 1:2]]). On this reading the grammar establishes that the Son is *not the Father* (which orthodoxy already affirms) but not that the Son is *less than fully God*. McClellan's point is narrower than it can sound: the syntax refutes a crude *identity* argument that the Logos = the God of Israel, but distinguishing intermediate from ultimate agency does not by itself decide between subordinationism and a two-persons-one-essence reading — that turns on whether John shares the *later* metaphysics of [[Consubstantiality|consubstantiality]] at all.

## LDS Relevance
This is a comfortable text for Latter-day Saint theology, which already distinguishes the **Father as the ultimate source** from the **Son as the agent of creation** acting under the Father's direction (cf. [[Moses 1.39|Moses 1:39]]; the Son creating "worlds without number" by the Father's word). A Godhead of distinct beings united in purpose fits a Christ who creates *through* the Father's commission better than a single-substance Trinity does — the *dia* grammar reads naturally as **divine investiture / agency**, the same category by which the Son bears the Father's name and authority. See [[Divine Mediation Predates the New Testament]] and [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]].
