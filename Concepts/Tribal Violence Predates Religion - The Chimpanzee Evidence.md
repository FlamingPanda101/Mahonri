# Tribal Violence Predates Religion — The Chimpanzee Evidence

## The Point

[[Jordan Peterson]] and [[Bret Weinstein]] argue, against a Rousseauian "culture corrupts us" view, that group-based atrocity is an inherited **primate** capacity that predates religion. Religious or political dogma is therefore an *amplifier* of human aggression, not its root cause.

## Argument (steelman)

1. **Goodall's discovery.** [[Jane Goodall]]'s 1970s observations at Gombe showed chimpanzee "raiding parties" of adolescent males patrolling territory and tearing interlopers — even former troop members — to pieces. Peterson notes Goodall had "trepidation about publishing it."[^1]

2. **Chimps are not religious.** "The fact that chimps do it shows that it can't be a consequence of something like religious belief... they're not religious and they don't hold a secular totalitarian viewpoint, but they act out the atrocity element that's characteristic of human behavior."[^2]

3. **Root vs amplifier.** Weinstein: "the problem of primate aggression, which we've inherited along with the chimps, is deeper or at least different than the problem of religious violence." Harris agrees religion can "channel these primate urges in unhappy ways" — but so can nationalism and racism.[^3]

4. **The dominance hierarchy.** Peterson roots tribal allegiance in the chimpanzee proclivity to identify with the troop's dominance hierarchy — a source of human social aggression "independent of any obvious religious substrate."[^4]

5. **Why it matters for the debate.** This pushes the diagnosis "deeper than mere surface statements" — i.e. deeper than dogma — and sets up the later move (see [[Religious Texts as Evolutionary Heuristics]]) of reading religion in evolutionary-functional terms.[^4]

## Best Quotes

> "The chimps at Gombe would go on raiding parties... if they found an interloper near the border from another troop — even if it was a member of their troop that had emigrated — they would tear them to pieces."[^1]

> "The fact that chimps do it shows that it can't be a consequence of something like religious belief."[^2]
