# Death Is Not the Worst Thing - Reframing the Problem of Evil

## The Point
[[Austin Fife]] ends the letter where his faith journey began: the problem of evil. As an atheist he found the standard answers fell flat — "a god who did nothing to help us was equivalent to no god at all."[^1] The reframe came from one overheard phrase: **"Death is not the worst thing that can happen to us."** It exposed his hidden premise — that this life is the end of all existence. If mortality is the whole story, "any amount of suffering and wrong is unjust"; but if it is "a blip on the eternal timetable," then "our suffering here on earth would be but a small moment," and a just, merciful God can make every wrong right through an atoning Christ.[^2]

## Evidence
1. **The atheist objection assumes finality.** Fife's argument is that the force of the problem of evil depends on treating death as annihilation. Remove that assumption and the calculus changes: "if this life is not the end of our story, then there has to be more to the story."[^2] This is the eternal-perspective counterpart to [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]] and [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]].

2. **Free will explains why a loving God permits harm.** "If there is a loving God, but there is so much evil in the world, then it follows that He likely gives us space to make our own choices, even if our choices harm others. If not, the malevolent dictators would never take power."[^3] God's restraint is the price of genuine agency.

3. **Alma 14 dramatizes the principle.** When Amulek begs Alma to use God's power to save the women and children being burned, Alma answers that the Spirit constrains him: "the Lord receiveth them up unto himself, in glory; and he doth suffer that they may do this thing... that the judgments which he shall exercise upon them in his wrath may be just; and the blood of the innocent shall stand as a witness against them."[^4] Innocent suffering is neither ignored nor wasted; it is received into glory and stands as evidence at judgment.

4. **The Atonement is what makes the wrongs right.** The resolution is Christocentric: "If an atoning Messiah would make all suffering, wrong, heartache, and even death right, then that would be good news indeed. If the gospel story is true, then death is not the worst thing that can happen to us."[^5] Fife reports that reasoning his way back to belief in God amounted to "recreating the story of the gospel" — the restoration "provided a reasonable explanation for how it all worked."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "Death is not the worst thing that can happen to us." [^7]

> "If mortality is a blip on the eternal timetable, then our suffering here on earth would be but a small moment. For God to be just and merciful, a reward must be given so that every man, woman, and child, no matter their toil on earth, would agree that it was all worth it." [^2]

> "The answer hit me all at once as if I never knew - Jesus Christ. If an atoning Messiah would make all suffering, wrong, heartache, and even death right, then that would be good news indeed." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The reframe is built from restoration doctrine: a premortal existence and an eternal soul (Doctrine and Covenants 93:29) mean death is a passage, not a terminus; the Atonement guarantees that "all that is unfair about life can be made right" through Christ. The "small moment" language echoes the Lord's word to Joseph Smith in Liberty Jail — "thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment" (D&C 121:7) — and Alma 14:11 supplies a scriptural case where God permits martyrdom yet receives the innocent "in glory." Fife's version is a distinctly LDS theodicy of agency, eternal scope, and atoning recompense, complementing [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
