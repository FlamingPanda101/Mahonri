# Bara Means Organize, and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention

## The Point
The single most pointed "1844" claim: in the [[King Follett Discourse]], [[Joseph Smith]] said the Hebrew *bara* (translated "created" in Genesis 1:1) does not mean creation from nothing but "to organize" pre-existing eternal matter. He did this with no academic framework and against the scholarly consensus of his day. 134 years later [[Gerhard May]] — a German Protestant theologian with no interest in vindicating Joseph — published the standard academic treatment showing *creatio ex nihilo* was a second-century theological invention, not a reading of the Hebrew Bible.

## Evidence
- **The 1844 claim.** Joseph said *bara* "doesn't mean to create from nothing. It means to organize... that God organized pre-existing eternal matter and that intelligence and matter are co-eternal with God and nothing was made from absolute nothing," working "from his own understanding of the Hebrew language in 1844, with no academic framework to back him up." [^1]
- **May's verdict.** May "published the definitive academic treatment of creation ex nihilo and concluded that the doctrine was in fact a second-century invention, not a reading of the Hebrew Bible. And he definitely wasn't trying to vindicate Joseph Smith. He was just following the scholarship." [^2]
- **The doctrine's actual origin.** Ex nihilo "was developed in the late 2nd century AD specifically to counter Gnosticism" (who taught matter was evil and the OT God a lesser creator). The orthodox response from Irenaeus and Tertullian "was to establish that God was the absolute source of everything, including matter... not from the Hebrew text and not from the Greek New Testament, but from the specific needs of a theological argument in the 2nd century." [^3]
- **The lexicon.** "The Hebrew word bara in Genesis 1:1 does not require creation from nothing. The Brown-Driver-Briggs Hebrew lexicon, the standard academic reference, doesn't define it that way." [^4]

## Best Quotes
> "He said that it doesn't mean to create from nothing. It means to organize... that God organized pre-existing eternal matter and that intelligence and matter are co-eternal with God and nothing was made from absolute nothing." [^1]

> "A German Protestant theologian named Gerhard May published the definitive academic treatment of creation ex nihilo and concluded that the doctrine was in fact a second-century invention, not a reading of the Hebrew Bible. And he definitely wasn't trying to vindicate Joseph Smith." [^2]

> "So Joseph Smith read the Hebrew correctly in 1844. The academic community documented exactly how and when the institutional tradition departed from the text 134 years later." [^5]
