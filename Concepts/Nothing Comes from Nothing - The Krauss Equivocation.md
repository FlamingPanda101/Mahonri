# Nothing Comes from Nothing - The Krauss Equivocation

## The Point
Some atheists answer the cosmological argument by claiming the universe popped into existence *uncaused, out of nothing*. Turek calls this "a miracle with no miracle worker" — clearly absurd.[^1] He targets physicist Lawrence Krauss's *A Universe from Nothing*, arguing that Krauss commits the logical fallacy of **equivocation**: the "nothing" of his title is not real nonbeing but a quantum vacuum, which is *something* — "fields of fluctuating energy."[^2] Strip away the wordplay and the ancient maxim stands: "out of nothing, nothing comes."[^3]

## Evidence
1. **No-cause vs. a miracle worker.** Turek frames the choice as: option one — "no one created something out of nothing" (atheism); option two — "someone created something out of nothing" (theism). Option two is at least a miracle *with* a miracle worker; option one is "a miracle with no miracle worker."[^1]
2. **We do not actually believe things come from nothing.** No one in the audience worries "that a hippopotamus has just appeared uncaused, out of nothing," and (per William Lane Craig) if things really popped into being uncaused, why don't iPads, Teslas, and pizzas do so? Ordering a pizza beats hoping one materializes.[^4]
3. **A quantum vacuum is not nothing.** "A quantum vacuum is something." Whether its particles are caused is unknown, there are at least ten competing models, but "whatever is happening there, it is not creation out of nothing." Moreover the vacuum itself had a beginning and so needs a cause.[^2]
4. **A fellow atheist agrees.** Columbia physicist David Albert, reviewing Krauss in the *New York Times*, says vacuum states are "particular arrangements of elementary physical stuff," and that particles popping in and out as fields rearrange is no more mysterious than a fist forming as fingers rearrange — "none of these poppings... amount to anything even remotely in the neighborhood of a creation from nothing." Albert: "Krauss is dead wrong and his religious and philosophical critics are absolutely right."[^5]
5. **Krauss admits it.** Krauss concedes "nothing is being used in a philosophical sense" and that the laws of physics "are not nothing," eventually granting that his "nothing" is actually *something* — exposing the bait-and-switch title.[^6]
6. **The deeper disease: contempt for philosophy.** Like Dawkins and Hawking, Krauss disdains philosophy, yet "it is impossible to use no philosophy." Science is "done more in the mind than the lab"; defining what counts as "nothing," "relevant," or "rational" is itself a philosophical (metaphysical) act. "Philosophy always buries its undertakers" (Gilson).[^7]

## Best Quotes
> "One of the things about quantum mechanics is not only can nothing become something, nothing always becomes something... Nothing is unstable." (Lawrence Krauss, quoted by Turek) [^2]

> "And none of these poppings — if you look at them aright — amount to anything even remotely in the neighborhood of a creation from nothing." (David Albert, quoted by Turek) [^5]

> "Nothing can't create anything because, as Aristotle put it, 'nothing is what rocks dream about.'... out of nothing, nothing comes." [^3]

## LDS Connection
Turek's deflation of "a universe from nothing" actually lands *closer* to Latter-day Saint cosmology than to his own. His repeated insistence that "out of nothing, nothing comes" (*ex nihilo nihil fit*) is precisely the classical principle that LDS theology uses to *reject* creation *ex nihilo*: God did not summon being from absolute nonbeing but organized eternally existing matter — "the elements are eternal" (D&C 93:29). So a Latter-day Saint can wholeheartedly join Turek against Krauss's vanishing "nothing," while noting that the maxim cuts against Turek's own ex-nihilo framing elsewhere in the chapter. The shared, defensible point is the agent: something orderly does not arise from literally nothing absent a powerful intelligence.
