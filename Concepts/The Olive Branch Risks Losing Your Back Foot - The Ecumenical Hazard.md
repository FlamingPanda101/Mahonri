# The Olive Branch Risks Losing Your Back Foot - The Ecumenical Hazard

## The Claim
[[Greg Matsen]] and [[Hayden Carroll]] raise an *internal* Latter-day Saint warning: the contemporary move to "bridge" toward creedal Christianity — putting out an olive branch to be counted as fellow Christians — can quietly drag creedal assumptions "forward" into LDS thought and so cost a Latter-day Saint their "back foot."[^1] Carroll sharpens it into a deliberately uncomfortable question: given that LDS scripture has Christ call the creeds an *abomination*, "are we trying to draw from those to make common ground? I'm not sure it's the best way to do it."[^2] The claim is not that ecumenical engagement is wrong, but that uncritical bridge-building has a *direction-of-influence* hazard the friendlier rhetoric tends to hide.

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The drift is bidirectional, and the LDS partner is the one usually adopting.** Matsen observes some Latter-day Saints "kind of lean that way" toward trinitarian assumptions — "for some it's acceptance," for others "it's bridging, right? Creating bridges." The worry is structural: "if I'm reaching out and putting an olive branch out to the rest of Christianity, am I losing my back foot and bringing that forward also?"[^1] *Rating: a real sociological risk* — accommodation language ("we believe in the same Jesus") can erode the very distinctives (embodied God, [[Free Will - Atheist Determinism vs LDS Co-Eternality|co-eternal intelligences]], open canon) that motivate the Restoration. Cf. the same "Greek glasses" dynamic of [[Hellenization Was Not Corruption of an Original Pure Church|Hellenization]] that the participants critique historically.

2. **The First Vision sets a high bar against borrowing.** Carroll's lever is canonical: "Do we believe that Christ said the creeds are an abomination? And are we trying to draw from those to make common ground?"[^2] If the creeds are divinely censured, treating them as a shared foundation looks inconsistent. *Rating: rhetorically strong, but turns entirely on what "creeds" denoted* — see [[The Creeds Were an Abomination Meant Sectarian Dogmatism Not the Ecumenical Creeds]], which argues the 1820s sense was sectarian dogmatism, not the Nicene text per se.

3. **Honesty about difference outperforms accommodation anyway.** Carroll reports that being "upfront and honest" about LDS distinctives "almost always resonates," even with those who disagree, more than blurring the lines — so the missionary payoff does not require ecumenical softening.[^3] *Rating: coherent with his practice* (he hands out Books of Mormon, leads with distinctives) and reframes candor as both more principled and more effective.

4. **Steelman of the bridge-builder.** The countervailing LDS position (see [[Ecumenical Foundation - Borrow Non-LDS Christian Resources for Levels One and Two]]) is that levels one and two — *that God exists*, *that the New Testament Jesus saves* — are genuinely shared ground where borrowing [[C.S. Lewis]] or natural-theology resources is rational stewardship, not capitulation; the distinctives live at level three (the Restoration) and are simply deferred, not surrendered. On this view "losing your back foot" is a discipline problem (sloppy apologists conceding too much), not an indictment of bridge-building itself, and [[Latter-day Saints Affirm the Nicene Creed in Its 4th-Century Context|affirming the 4th-century Nicene text]] need not import 5th-century perfect-being metaphysics. *Rating: a strong reply* — Matsen and Carroll partly concede it by still engaging creedal Christians at the "level one" foundation; the disagreement is over *where* on the pyramid borrowing stops being safe, not whether any common ground exists.

## Best Quotes
> "If I'm reaching out and putting an olive branch out to the rest of Christianity, am I losing my back foot and bringing that forward also?" [^1]

> "If you're upfront and honest... people actually appreciate that more, even if they disagree with you on the front side." [^3]
