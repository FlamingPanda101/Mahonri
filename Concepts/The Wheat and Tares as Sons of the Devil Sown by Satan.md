# The Wheat and Tares as Sons of the Devil Sown by Satan

#type/claim

## The Claim
A Calvinist caller on [[Jacob Hansen]]'s show grounds **unconditional election** in an unusually literal reading of the **parable of the weeds** ([[Matthew 13.36-43|Matthew 13:36-43]]). On his exegesis the field is humanity, the good seed are "sons of the kingdom" sown by the Son of Man, and the weeds are "sons of the evil one" *planted directly by the devil*.[^1] Reprobation is therefore **ontological and by origin**: some humans simply *are* "sons of the devil" placed on earth as such, the elect are always sons of the kingdom, and — like the sheep and the goats — "goats do not become sheep."[^2] He fuses this with [[Romans 9.11-14|Romans 9]] (Jacob loved, Esau hated before either had done good or bad; the potter and the clay; vessels of wrath "prepared for destruction"), reading [[Anne Frank]] as one such vessel of wrath who "never accepted Jesus" and so was a daughter of the devil by the parable's "binary."[^3]

Hansen's claim is that this reading **inverts the parable's actual lesson** and proves too much. The point of wheat-and-tares is precisely that the two are *indistinguishable until their fruit manifests* what they are — a doctrine of *agency and growth*, not of fixed natures — and a gospel commissioned "to all nations, kindreds, tongues, and people" is *pointless* if only pre-planted elect can ever respond.[^4] It is the same arbitrariness rebutted in [[Seeking God Versus Arbitrary Election]] and the same "controlling, unloving God" of [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]].

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The parable is about indiscernibility resolved by fruit, not fixed origin.** Hansen: the wheat and the tares "look very similar, and you can't tell which one is actually a wheat and which one is actually a tare until their works are manifest and show what they are and they bear forth fruit."[^4] On this reading the parable *presupposes* that a person's true allegiance is revealed *through their choices over time* — the opposite of a nature stamped at creation. ([[Matthew 7.16-20|"by their fruits ye shall know them"]] supplies the same hermeneutic.)
2. **Universal preaching is incoherent under fixed-origin election.** "Why preach to all nations, kindreds, tongues, and people if only the elect are the ones that are going to be saved?"[^5] A binding Great Commission to *everyone* only makes sense if everyone can in fact respond — "all people have the capacity to either accept or reject Christ." Hansen reframes the elect as "the people who are willing, not the people that are sort of already predestined."[^6]
3. **The reading produces a monstrous, arbitrary God.** If God *allowed the devil to sow* the reprobate and could have stopped it ("if God is truly all powerful, certainly he could have stopped that"), then the non-elect exist only to be "vessels of wrath," which makes condemnation rest on origin rather than act — the textbook definition of injustice.[^7] See [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]] and [[Salvation by Theology Damns Almost Everyone]].
4. **"That people can't change what they are" is the load-bearing error.** Hansen's closing diagnosis: to say "people can't change from what they are… because of their nature is a very scary, scary proposition," because it denies the moral agency the whole missionary enterprise assumes.[^8]

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman of the caller's reading)
The caller's exegesis is stronger than Hansen allows, and several details cut his way:
- **Jesus' own interpretation does assign origin.** In the parable's *explanation* (not just the agricultural image) Jesus says the weeds "are the sons of the evil one, and the enemy who sowed them is the devil" ([[Matthew 13.36-43|Matt 13:38-39]]). The caller's sharp observation — that the parable says only "an enemy has done this," while the *explanation* says the devil *sowed* them — is a real textual datum: the explanation foregrounds *how they got there* (origin), not merely how they end up. Hansen's "fruit reveals" reading is imported from other parables and is not stated in vv. 36-43.
- **It coheres with the binary parables.** Sheep/goats ([[Matthew 25.31-46|Matt 25]]), wheat/tares, and the wise/foolish virgins are all *binary with no crossover narrated* — goats are never said to become sheep. The caller's "different type of animal" point is a fair generalization of the genre.
- **Romans 9 is genuinely pre-natal.** Paul explicitly grounds the Jacob/Esau election "though they were not yet born and had done nothing either good or bad" ([[Romans 9.11-14|Rom 9:11]]). On its face this *does* locate election prior to works, exactly as the caller insists; the burden is on the synergist to show why "fruit" rather than divine choice is decisive. This is the same hard datum behind [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]].

Where the steelman runs out: even granting that the *explanation* names the devil as sower, it does not say the weeds *cannot repent* — the command "let both grow together until the harvest" presupposes the *present* indiscernibility Hansen emphasizes, and the parable is silent on whether a "tare" could become wheat before harvest. And the Great-Commission objection is not answered by exegesis of one parable: a God who commands universal preaching while having pre-sown most of the field as devil's children has, at minimum, organized a deeply misleading economy of salvation. The caller's own admission — "you could question God till you're blue in the face, but… this is where we're at" — concedes that the reading rests finally on *deference to the text as he reads it*, not on a moral or missiological account that coheres.
