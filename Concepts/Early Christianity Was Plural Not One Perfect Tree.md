# Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree

## The Claim
Earliest Christianity was never "one perfect tree" that later fragmented. From the very beginning — within the New Testament itself — there were multiple, competing, often hostile forms of Christianity. The popular LDS image of a "perfect mirror" that dropped and shattered (the pieces being the apostasy) does not match the historical evidence.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. Conflict is visible inside the canon (rating: strong; canonical)
The Paul–Peter–James disputes ([[Galatians 2.14|Gal 2:11-14]]), the Corinthian "divisions and heresies" ([[1 Corinthians 11.18-19|1 Cor 11:18-19]]), and Diotrephes expelling apostolic emissaries ([[3 John 1.9-10|3 John 9-10]]) are first-generation, in-canon evidence of plurality and revolt — "the under-pressure" beneath the whole New Testament. [^1]

### 2. Multiple christologies coexisted from the start (rating: strong; scholarly consensus)
The episode catalogs rival camps: **Docetists** (Jesus only *seemed* mortal), **Ebionites** (Jesus a mortal man born of a mortal woman), **Marcionites**, and various **Gnostic** movements. Per a cited Catholic scholar, even by Nicaea "half of the Christians were... iffy if Jesus was divine," and per some scholars there were more Marcionites than orthodox at points. See [[Docetism and Early Christological Diversity]]. [^2]

### 3. The earliest documents show high diversity of viewpoint (rating: moderate; literary)
Galatians is among the oldest NT documents (late 40s–50s, before the gospels), yet already shows sharp doctrinal war — so the diversity is not a late corruption. The four gospels themselves present materially different portraits of Jesus (see [[New Testament Books Reflect Rival Communities]]). [^3]

### 4. The LDS "dropped mirror" model is explicitly rejected (rating: honesty of the source)
Day and Butler name and reject the missionary object lesson of dropping a perfect mirror: "history doesn't really lend itself to that." This is a notable internal critique of a common LDS apologetic. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the opposing view
A traditional Christian (or traditional LDS) reading holds that there *was* an original apostolic unity — "one faith, one Lord, one baptism" (Eph 4:5) — and that the heresies are precisely deviations *from* it, which is why the apostles wrote to correct them. The very letters Day cites assume a normative gospel against which error is measured. Day's response: the existence of correction-letters proves the conflict was real and ongoing, not that a pristine unity ever held institutionally.

## Best Quotes

> "It wasn't one thing. There were many different ways of understanding it from the get-go." [^4]

> "We have a mirror. It's perfect. We drop it and it breaks... and that's the apostasy, and history doesn't really lend itself to that." [^4]

> "What we have is a canonized fight... a sharp disagreement about how to be a Jesus follower." [^3]
