# The Gospel Is the Good News of God's Kingdom Not a Doctrinal Formula

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Trent Horn]] argues that the biblical **gospel** (Greek *euangelion*, literally "good news") is not a doctrinal formula about how an individual is saved, but the announcement that **God's kingdom is breaking into the world and all opposing powers will be overthrown through his Son**. The word appears across the Synoptics almost always without explanation — "the good news of the kingdom," "good news for the poor" — pointing back to [[Isaiah 40.1-55.13|Isaiah]]'s proclamation of God's victory over his enemies. During Jesus's earthly ministry the gospel was *not yet* a message about his death, resurrection, or how he would redeem humanity from sin; it was simply that "the time for God's kingdom has arrived." [^1]

Following his Catholic Answers colleague [[Jimmy Akin]], Horn says individual salvation is a **happy consequence** of the gospel rather than its essence: "The New Testament's understanding is not about us as individuals and how we can benefit. The gospel is just not about us." The good news is about "what God and his Son are doing in the world... to their enemies like the devil and the pagan world order." Getting saved is how we "get on board with God's plan." [^2]

A minimal definition that does justice to the texts: the gospel is **the good news of God's kingdom entering the world that saves God's people through the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ** — which is why Paul says it is *in Christ* that we have the good news of our salvation ([[Romans 1.16|Rom 1:16]]; [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Cor 15:1-4]]). [^3]

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The earliest "definition" is a creed about events, not doctrines.** In [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|1 Corinthians 15]] Paul reminds the Corinthians of "the gospel... by which you are saved" and then recites what scholars regard as an early creed (formulated within a few years of the crucifixion): "Christ died for our sins, was buried, rose three days later, and appeared to many." Notably there is **no description of individual salvation from damnable sin** — even though that is how the gospel is popularly summarized. [^1]
2. **The kingdom frame is rooted in the Old Testament.** [[Jimmy Akin]]'s treatment traces the gospel back to texts in the Old Testament announcing that "the time for God's kingdom has arrived and all opposing powers will be overthrown"; the *elaboration* is that the kingdom comes through the Son, and the *happy consequence* is our salvation. This layered structure parallels the vault's [[The Three Levels of the Gospel]]. [^2]
3. **No verse equates the gospel with a salvation formula.** The texts about the gospel "never say the good news is about salvation through faith alone or grace alone or any other doctrinal formula." [^3] This is the lever Horn uses against critics who smuggle [[Double Imputation Is Not the Gospel|double imputation]] into the word.
4. **Steelman of the Protestant counter.** A Reformed reader (e.g. [[John MacArthur]], [[Voddie Baucham]]) would object that [[1 Corinthians 15.1-4|"Christ died *for our sins*"]] already presupposes a substitutionary, justification-centered mechanism — so the kingdom/personal-salvation split is artificial; "for our sins" *is* the individual application. Horn's reply is that affirming Christ died for sins does not commit one to any *particular* theory of imputation, and that reading the mechanism back into the bare announcement is exactly the over-definition he warns against in [[A False Gospel Is One That Contradicts the Kingdom Message]]. Because [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority|Protestants who reject sola scriptura's sufficiency for adjudication]] cannot settle which mechanism is essential, this dispute also bears on [[A Living Magisterium Settles What Sola Scriptura Cannot|the need for a magisterium]] — a point of contact with Latter-day Saint critiques of sola scriptura.

## Best Quotes
> "The New Testament's understanding is not about us as individuals and how we can benefit. The gospel is just not about us. Instead, the good news is about what God and his Son are doing in the world." [^2]

> "At minimum, we can say the gospel is the good news of God's kingdom entering the world that saves God's people through the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ." [^3]
