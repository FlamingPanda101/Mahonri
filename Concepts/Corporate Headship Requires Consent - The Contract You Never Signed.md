# Corporate Headship Requires Consent - The Contract You Never Signed

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Jacob Hansen]] presses the **federal-headship** defense of inherited guilt to its breaking point on its own terms. A Calvinist caller defends original guilt with two analogies: [[Adam]] is the "corporate head" of humanity the way a president represents a nation, and just as post-war Germany owed reparations even from citizens who opposed Hitler, all people inherit Adam's debt as a body.[^1] The caller frames this as a **contract**: by being born and reaping its benefits (the knowledge of good and evil), "you did join that contract when you were born."[^2]

Hansen's claim is that this confuses two things justice keeps separate — **inheriting a consequence** versus **inheriting culpability** — and that the contract analogy *destroys itself*, because the defining feature of a just contract is **consent**. "A contract requires consent," and no one consents to be born; therefore "basic justice says that you are not responsible for contracts that you never entered into yourself with your own free will."[^3] A perfectly moral judge "will judge people as individuals, not as groups of people who didn't even consent to be a part of a particular group."[^4] The position is the positive form of the [[The Infant-Damnation Reductio Against Inherited Guilt Plus Required Profession|infant-damnation reductio]] and tracks the historic [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development|late-Western development]] of inherited guilt.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The disease/guilt distinction is conceded, then exploited.** Hansen repeatedly grants that humanity inherits a *disease* — mortality, a fallen body and world — and the caller agrees.[^5] But "you don't put people in prison and punish them because they inherited a disease. You put people in prison and punish them because of the things that they did."[^6] Cure is the response to a disease; punishment is reserved for acts. Inherited guilt illicitly treats a disease as a crime.
2. **The caller surrenders the governing biblical principle.** Asked where Scripture says you are not bound by contracts you never signed, the caller himself volunteers Ecclesiastes: "men will be punished for their own sins and not their fathers," and concedes "Absolutely."[^7] This is the exact content of the LDS Second Article of Faith and the prophetic principle of [[Ezekiel 18.20]] / Deuteronomy 24:16 — a point the federal-headship advocate cannot deny once stated.
3. **The political analogies undercut the conclusion they were meant to support.** Hansen turns the nation analogy: would *you* accept being "punished for a contract that you never signed and never agreed to be a part of"? The caller agrees that would be "an injustice."[^8] The Germany-reparations case, likewise, is a *debt between nations*, not a verdict that innocent German children "morally deserved" the Allied bombs — and Hansen forces the disanalogy: corporate liability for a *debt* is not the same as personal *desert of eternal punishment*.[^9]
4. **It is the live form of a real historical fault line.** This is not a debater's trick but the [[Pelagius]]–[[Augustine]] question of whether guilt (not merely mortality) is transmitted from Adam, the same dispute behind [[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12]] and the Reformed reading of [[Romans 9.11-14|Romans 9]]. See [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]].

## Supporting Evidence (Steelman of the Augustinian / federal view)
Honesty requires giving the headship doctrine its strongest form, which the caller only gestures at:
- **Federal headship is a coherent legal category, not a confusion.** In Reformed covenant theology Adam is the divinely-*appointed* representative of the human race, the way a head of state can bind a nation by treaty whether or not each citizen voted. On this view we are not punished for someone *else's* act in the way a stranger would be; Adam acts *as us*, by appointment, so his guilt is genuinely *ours* by imputation — the mirror image of Christ's righteousness imputed to the elect ([[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12-19]]; cf. [[Double Imputation Is Not the Gospel]]). The "consent" objection assumes an individualism the Bible's corporate categories ("in Adam all die," 1 Cor 15:22) may simply not share.
- **Consent is not the only ground of just liability.** Children inherit the consequences of a parent's choices, and citizens are bound by laws and debts they never ratified; if *all* non-consensual liability were unjust, much of ordinary moral and legal life would collapse. The Augustinian can argue that being a member of a fallen race is a *natural*, not contractual, fact — so the contract framing is the caller's weaker analogy, not the doctrine's core.
- **Romans is real data.** Paul's Adam–Christ parallelism and the potter/clay passage are serious texts; the LDS denial of inherited guilt is itself a contestable theological choice, not a neutral default.

Where the steelman runs out: even granted federal headship, the move from *corporate liability for a debt* to *each individual infant deserving eternal conscious torment* is an additional, much stronger claim, and it is that claim — not mere solidarity in mortality — that Hansen's "punishment must fit a crime the person committed" intuition targets. Imputed guilt can explain why all *die*; it strains to explain why a baby *deserves hell* for an act it could not have performed.
