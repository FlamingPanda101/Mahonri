# Bill Reel's Free-Will Double Standard - Determinism Cannot Ground Moral Blame

## The Point
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that the critic [[Bill Reel]] holds two claims that cannot both be true. (1) Reel denies free will and agency outright: appealing to science ("everything is cause and effect"), genetics, and epigenetics, he says people don't make choices — racists, serial killers, and child predators are "predisposed" rather than choosing.[^1] (2) Yet Reel still wants to morally condemn racism, pedophilia, and the Church as "morally reprehensible."[^2] Hansen's principle is that "people are only morally responsible for the choices that they make" — "we don't hold people morally accountable for things they don't choose to do."[^3] If no one ever chooses, then moral blame has no target, and Reel's condemnations collapse. This is a concrete instance of [[The Sin of an Inconsistent Worldview]], and it mirrors the LDS critique that [[Ex Nihilo Determinism Negates Moral Praise]].

## Evidence
1. **Reel concedes hard determinism.** Asked directly, Reel agrees humans have no free will and "so we don't have agency... we don't actually make choices."[^1]
2. **The premise Hansen anchors to.** "People are only morally responsible for the choices that they make... if you don't make a choice, you're not responsible for it because you didn't make the choice."[^3]
3. **The genetic argument applied.** Reel relays a geneticist's claim that "child predators aren't choosing to do that; they are predisposed genetically," concluding "if there is an agency there just is an agency and we have to deal with that."[^4]
4. **Reel visibly snagged on the contradiction.** Pressed on whether someone can "choose whether or not to actually have sex with children," Reel answers "yes and no" — Hansen notes "you can see Bill is struggling with the contradiction in his worldview here."[^5]
5. **The inconsistency stated.** "Bill wants to condemn people as morally reprehensible for their racism while also holding to a worldview that says people can't choose what they believe or do."[^2]

## Best Quotes
> "How can we have a coherent discussion about moral responsibility if you're not morally responsible for any of your choices because you ultimately don't make choices?" [^3]

> "On one hand he rightly wants to make moral judgments about racism, pedophilia, and a church that he believes is morally bad, but then he also wants to say that human beings can't choose their behavior or their beliefs. Again, this is a classic example of an inconsistent worldview." [^5]

## LDS Connection
The exchange showcases the burden a critic must also bear: Reel deploys moral outrage against the Church while denying the metaphysical agency that makes moral outrage meaningful. Latter-day Saint theology resolves the tension in the opposite direction — agency is real and uncreated. Scripture teaches that intelligences are self-existent and that humanity is "free to choose liberty and eternal life... or to choose captivity and death" (2 Nephi 2:27), grounding genuine moral responsibility. Where a determinist like [[Sam Harris]] must *reconstruct* responsibility as mere social utility ([[Moral Responsibility Without Free Will - Harris's Reconciliation]]; [[The Illusion of Free Will - Harris's Hard Determinism]]), the Restoration treats agency as a foundational good, so its moral judgments do not undercut their own basis. The same move that LDS apologists run against creation *ex nihilo* — that a fully determined creature cannot be justly praised or blamed ([[Ex Nihilo Determinism Negates Moral Praise]]) — is here run against the secular critic.
