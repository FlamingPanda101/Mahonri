# Forced Belief Would Corrupt Our Motives and Defeat Soul-Making

## The Point
Suppose God simply removed our biased desires and gave everyone an undeniable sense of his existence. Jones argues this would "remove too much freedom" and coerce people toward the outcome God wants instead of letting them choose.[^1] Worse, it would *corrupt the motive*: people would conform out of fear of punishment or hope of reward, not love. God's goal is not mere belief *that* he exists but **cognitively robust theism** (Paul Moser) — lovingly trusting God as Lord and Savior of one's life — and that filial trust cannot be produced by overwhelming propositional proof.[^2]

## Evidence
- **The ad hoc burden.** If you concede that *forcing* belief violates self-determination, yet still insist God should give "more" evidence, you must say what kind of evidence could do that "without removing freedom or inspiring the wrong type of knowledge of God." If you can't specify the better way, the objection is ad hoc.[^3]
- **Moser: motive matters more than assent.** "God's desire as to why people believe in him is more important than that we believe in him." Demanding to know God "on our preferred terms" is "nothing more than a self-serving assumption"; we truly come to know God only when we first acknowledge our own unfitness and selfishness.[^4]
- **Murray: coercion erases the moral choice.** Michael Murray argues that if we were forced to accept God's existence, fear of punishment (or of missing a great good) "would compel us" to act accordingly — but then "God would have removed the ability for self-determination, since there are no longer good and evil sources of action between which creatures could freely and deliberately choose."[^5]
- **Knowing God is not like meeting a neighbor.** Discovering "an omnipotent being that could wipe out all life in the universe" forced upon us "can very easily conjure the wrong emotions and lead to fear, hatred, shock, or thoughts of trying to please this being for selfish gains" — the opposite of what God wants. "God refuses to become an idol of our cognition."[^6]

## Best Quotes
> "God wants free creatures to earnestly seek Him because they want to. He doesn't want a world of Stepford Wives or Pleasantville humans following a script." [^1]

> "God's desire as to why people believe in him is more important than that we believe in him." [^4]

> "Our fear of punishment, or at least our fear of the prospect of missing out on a very great good, would compel us... but in doing this God would have removed the ability for self-determination." [^5]

## LDS Connection
This is the heart of the Latter-day Saint case for a non-coercive God. Agency is not a concession God grants but a feature of beings whose intelligence is uncreated and eternal (see [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency]]), and love freely given is the entire point of the plan — the same logic Turek uses in [[Free Will Is the Price of Love - Tureks Free-Will Theodicy]]. Overwhelming proof would manufacture the "fear- and reward-driven" obedience that the Restoration explicitly rejects: God seeks worshippers who choose him, not subjects coerced by a display of power. Moser's "cognitively robust theism" maps almost exactly onto the LDS distinction between bare belief and a *converting* faith that reorders the heart — and the veil of mortality is precisely the condition that keeps the choice real, contrasting genuine seeking with mere compelled assent (compare [[Seeking God Versus Arbitrary Election]]).
