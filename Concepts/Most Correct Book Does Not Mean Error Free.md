# Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free

## The Claim

[[Joseph Smith]] famously called the Book of Mormon "the most correct of any book
on earth." Saints Unscripted argues this phrase has been misread: "When Joseph
Smith called it the most correct book, he didn't mean grammatically perfect or
error-free." Rather, it refers to the book's *correctness in conveying the
principles of the gospel* — its doctrinal and salvific reliability — not its
freedom from grammatical, scribal, or transmission errors.[^1]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The full Joseph Smith quotation is about precepts, not grammar

The historical statement continues that a person would "get nearer to God by
abiding by its precepts, than by any other book." The metric is spiritual
proximity to God, not textual flawlessness.[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong on the textual point — the original statement
> explicitly grounds "most correct" in *precepts*, not orthography.

### 2. The title page itself anticipates human error

The Book of Mormon's own title page states that if there are faults, "they are the
mistakes of men." Latter-day Saints are therefore "open to the possibilities that
small mistakes may have occurred along the way." The doctrine of an imperfect human
medium is built into the book's self-presentation.[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (internal, original-edition admission predating any
> controversy).

### 3. It dissolves the "any change = falsity" objection

If error-freedom was never claimed, then the existence of edits (the
[[King Benjamin to King Mosiah Edit Is a Conceded Human Error|Benjamin/Mosiah]]
slip, grammatical fixes) is not evidence against the book — only against a
strawman version of its claims.

## Steelman of the Opposing View

The critic can argue this is a convenient retreat: the "most correct book"
language has long been used to assert the Book of Mormon's *superiority* over the
Bible, including textual purity, so redefining it narrowly as "most correct in
precepts" only after errors are catalogued looks ad hoc. Moreover, "mistakes of
men" is doing heavy lifting — it absorbs *any* discrepancy (anachronism, doctrinal
shift, name error) into an unfalsifiable category, which makes the book immune to
disconfirmation rather than confirmed by evidence.
