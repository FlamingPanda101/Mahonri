# Saints Not Believers - LDS Communalism Lesson

## The Claim
Asked what *he* learned from Latter-day Saints, [[Kyle Beshears]] names their communalism: studying the LDS has shown him "how community-oriented and communal Christianity is meant to be, but how individualistic and atomized evangelicalism is." This even changed how he reads the New Testament. [^1][^2]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Belief-centric vs. practice/community-centric.** Evangelicals "primarily see themselves [by] what they believe and think and less so in what they do and behave"; the LDS emphasis on family and community forced him "to reconcile with that." [^2] *Rating: a candid self-critique* rather than a polemic.

2. **Re-reading the New Testament as "y'all."** "I started reading the New Testament differently. I don't see them talking to *you*, Kyle. I see them talking to *y'all*, the church. I'm an individual member of it, but my brothers and sisters in my congregation, we are in this together." [^3] *Rating: exegetically sound* — most NT second-person imperatives are plural, a point biblical scholars routinely stress.

3. **The "saints" word-count argument.** "By far and away, Christians are called *saints* in the New Testament more than they're called *believers*... The early Christians saw themselves as a collection of people who were pursuing holiness together, not primarily by what they believe." [^4] *Rating: substantially accurate;* the participial "those who believe" is common, but the noun *hagioi* ("saints") is the dominant collective self-designation, and the point about communal sanctification holds.

4. **A critique of the Enlightenment self.** Evangelicals "have taken for granted the Enlightenment vision of humanity... *homo sapiens*, man who thinks, man who reasons — so everything's about the head. And that's just not what I see in scripture." The LDS emphasis on family and the term "saint" (a *biblical* term, "not a Mormon term") opened his eyes. [^5] *Rating: a thoughtful theological-anthropology point.*

**Steelman of the opposing (evangelical) view.** An evangelical could reply that doctrinal correctness *is* a form of love and faithfulness, that low-church individualism guards against institutional coercion, and that LDS "community" can shade into social control. Beshears is not endorsing LDS ecclesiology wholesale; he is conceding that evangelical individualism has overcorrected against the New Testament's communal vision — a lesson he credits to observing Latter-day Saints.
