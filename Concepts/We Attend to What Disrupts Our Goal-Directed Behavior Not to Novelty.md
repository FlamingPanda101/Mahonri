# We Attend to What Disrupts Our Goal-Directed Behavior Not to Novelty

## The Point
[[Jordan Peterson]] offers a corrected law of attention. The intuitive view is that novel or anomalous events grab our notice. He argues the truer claim is narrower: we orient to events that *interfere with goal-directed action* — "only pay attention to things that make you fail." Novelty as such is filtered out unless it disrupts the relationship between our behavior and its desired outcome, and that disruption typically arrives with an emotional jolt. [^1][^2]

## Argument
1. Mechanism: only the foveal "tiny little point of focus" is information-rich; the eye "zip[s] that center around — snap snap snap," and the brain stitches the snapshots into what "seems… like a continuous movie of consciousness even though it's really not." [^3]
2. The periphery mainly triages for movement, prompting a gaze shift "so you can keep track of what's changing." Everything else "fades into the background." [^3][^4]
3. In the gorilla video, the black-clad gorilla blends with the black-team players, and the curtain color and the missing player "ha[ve] no bearing whatsoever on whether or not you can complete the task," so they are ignored — "it's irrelevant in terms of the… motivated interpretive frame." [^4]
4. Two superseded theories: (a) that we are broadly conscious of the environment, and (b) that radical change automatically draws attention. Both are false. [^5]
5. Corrected claim: "it isn't anomaly or novelty that attracts your attention; it's the unexpected disruption of the relationship between your behaviors and the desired outcome of those behaviors… only pay attention to things that make you fail." [^1]
6. Such disruption "is also associated with an emotional response," which "prepares you for the worst" and "primes you to be curious and to start to explore… so that you can reconstruct your expectations." [^2]

## Best Quotes
> "It isn't anomaly or novelty that attracts your attention; it's the unexpected disruption of the relationship between your behaviors and the desired outcome of those behaviors… only pay attention to things that make you fail." [^1]

> "You have a tiny little point of focus where the information is rich… and your brain sort of makes an amalgamated picture out of all those little snapshots… so it seems to you like it's a continuous movie of consciousness even though it's really not." [^3]

> "Something that emerges that interferes with what you're doing that you don't expect you will instantly orient towards and concentrate on." [^1]
