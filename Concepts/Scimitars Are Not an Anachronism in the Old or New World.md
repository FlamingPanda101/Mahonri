# Scimitars Are Not an Anachronism in the Old or New World

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)

Critics argue the Book of Mormon's "cimeters" (scimitars) are anachronistic: the
word *scimitar* enters English only with the rise of Islam (~600–700 AD), so curved
swords with that name could not exist in Lehi's day (~600 BC) or in the
pre-Columbian Americas.[^1]

## The Rebuttal

### 1. The objection confuses the word with the weapon

The English *word* "scimitar" is post-Islamic, but **curved-bladed weapons** go back
to the third millennium BC in the ancient Near East. The objection is purely
lexical; the object existed long before the loanword. See
[[Translation Introduces Anachronism Even in Divine Translation]].[^2]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (a clean word-vs-referent distinction).

### 2. Archaeologists routinely call Old World curved swords "scimitars"

The Egyptian *khopesh* is a curved sword; standard scholarship calls it a scimitar.
[[Amihai Mazar]], in *Archaeology of the Land of the Bible* (a standard reference),
writes that "Egyptian *khopesh*-shaped scimitars are known from both the actual
finds and from Egyptian artistic depictions."[^3]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (named scholar, standard reference work, direct quote).

### 3. Hebrew and translated Bibles use "scimitar"

The Hebrew *kidon* is read by some scholars as a curved sword (artwork from the
Assyrian conquest shows carried curved swords); the Common English Bible (CEB)
translates *kidon* as "scimitar." Both languages most relevant to the Book of
Mormon — Hebrew and Egyptian — have terms scholars render "scimitar."[^4]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (cited Hebrew term + mainstream translation choice).

### 4. New World scimitar-like blades are attested

On Maya stelae, elites are depicted holding curved-bladed weapons; [[Mary Miller]]
and [[Simon Martin]] — top-tier Mesoamericanists — describe one Maya warrior holding
a "scimitar-like flint blade."[^5]

> **Evidence rating:** Strong (named specialists describing actual iconography).

## Steelman of the Opposing View

A critic can grant curved blades existed everywhere and still note that calling them
"scimitars" is anachronistic *labeling*, and that the Book of Mormon's "cimeter"
need not denote a curved sword at all. The reply: that is precisely the point —
since scholars freely apply the modern label to ancient curved weapons in both
hemispheres, the Book of Mormon's usage cannot be singled out as uniquely
anachronistic. This does not prove the book true, only that this item is not a
problem.
