# Debate: Is Scripture the Sole Infallible Authority?

## The Question
*Sola scriptura* holds that the canonical Scriptures are the **sole infallible rule of faith** for the church — uniquely God-breathed, sufficient, and clear enough to function as the final standard over every rival authority. The dispute is not whether Scripture is authoritative (all sides grant that) but whether it is the *sole* and *self-sufficient* infallible authority, or whether it necessarily presupposes a second authority — sacred tradition and a teaching church (Catholic), or living prophets and an open canon (LDS) — to identify, secure, and interpret it. Almost every thread in the debate runs through one bottleneck: the table of contents of the inspired books is not itself found in any inspired book, so the canon question becomes the hinge on which authority turns.

## Case FOR sola scriptura (Reformed / Protestant affirmative)
- **Scripture self-attests its authority** — Kelly's affirmative: the inspired word testifies to its own authority, corroborated by how Jesus, the apostles, and the early church *used* it. See [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]]. *(tier: live LDS-vs-Protestant debate, [[Hayden Carroll]])*
- **Scripture is self-authenticating because God is self-contained** — [[Anthony Rogers]]' Reformed epistemology: an aseity-possessing, self-referencing God speaks with self-attesting authority and needs no papal stamp. See [[Scripture Is Self-Authenticating Because God Is Self-Contained]]. *(tier: formal debate vs [[Trent Horn]])*
- **Light manifests itself — perspicuity** — Scripture is formally sufficient / clear enough to convey saving truth; the fault for non-understanding lies in the blind reader, not the light. See [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture]].
- **Scripture is the test of prophets and revelation** — even granting continuing revelation, Scripture is the fixed standard to test any claimed prophet (Berea; "an angel from heaven" of [[Galatians 1.8]]). See [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]].
- **The canon is derived from the books, not the books from a list** — the internal-seams argument: structural markers (Hebrew connectives, canonical "seams," [[Deuteronomy 18.15]]'s forward-pointing prophet) let Scripture in principle attest its own canon. See [[The Internal-Seams Argument for the Canon]].
- **Heretics twist Scripture rather than add to it** — the existence of heresy argues *for* going by Scripture alone, since heretics err by hearkening to outside authorities. See [[Heretics Twist Scripture Rather Than Add to It]] (Rogers' side of the exchange).
- **The Trent canon vote was a tiny minority** — the conciliar "infallible declaration" was settled by ~25 of ~800 bishops, puncturing the image of overwhelming consensus. See [[The Council of Trent Canon Vote Was a Tiny Minority]].
- *(Scholar-tier modern formulations rebutted below:* [[James White]]-style proof-texting, [[Michael Krueger]]'s recognition/self-authentication model, the "ontologically unique word of God" argument of Wes Huff / Gavin Ortlund.)*

## Case AGAINST (Catholic / LDS negative)
**Catholic (Trent Horn / Joe Heschmeyer) — formal debate + apologetics tier:**
- **The canon premise is an infallible rule outside Scripture** — "the 66 books are the sole infallible rule" is *itself* an infallible rule of faith, yet is nowhere stated in Scripture; so an infallible authority exists outside it. See [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]].
- **Knowing Scripture is the word of God requires an outside authority** — [[Trent Horn]]'s dilemma: you know Scripture's status either from outside Scripture (a second infallible authority) or circularly. See [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]] and the underlying [[The Word of God Fallacy - The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven]].
- **"Word of God" is not confined to the written word** — the phrase always denotes oral proclamation; a scroll *records* but is never called "the word of God." See [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]].
- **The self-authentication circularity charge** — "inspired because it says so" is the same move Muslims make for the Quran and LDS for the Book of Mormon; falsified by early NT-book doubts. See [[The Self-Authentication Circularity Charge]] and [[Moroni-Calvin Parallel - The Self-Authentication Gambit]].
- **Self-authentication via church consensus backs into the Catholic model** — relocating recognition to the church (Godfrey, [[Michael Krueger]]) concedes the very church-dependence the Reformation denied. See [[Self-Authentication via Church Consensus Backs Into the Catholic Model]]; cf. [[Krugers General Revelation Analogy Fails at the Particular Level]].
- **The church infallibly declares the canon** — God *determines* by inspiring; the church *declares* under the Spirit (thermometer vs thermostat). See [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]] and [[Thermometer vs Thermostat - The Church Declares the Canon]].
- **Perspicuity fails the hard-cases test / no final interpreter** — sincere readers divide on salvation-relevant doctrines, and only 0.1% obscurity can damn; material but not formal sufficiency. See [[Perspicuity Fails the Hard-Cases Test]], [[Material vs Formal Sufficiency - The Home Depot Analogy]], [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]].
- **The early church did not operate under sola scriptura** — Ignatius, Clement, Irenaeus, the Creed, and Pelikan show no ante-Nicene notion of Scripture alone. See [[The Early Church Did Not Operate Under Sola Scriptura]].

**LDS ([[Hayden Carroll]]) — live debate tier:**
- **The canon question is logically prior to canon authority** — whatever identifies the canon stands logically prior to it and comes from outside the list. See [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]].
- **Three unjustified Protestant presuppositions** — infallibility, sufficiency, and ultimacy are each asserted, never divinely designated. See [[The Three Unjustified Protestant Canon Presuppositions]].
- **The canons are not even identical** — Joseph Smith judged the Song of Solomon uninspired; the LDS canon is open, breaking the "we share the same 66" framing. See [[LDS Reject the Song of Solomon as Inspired]]; cf. [[Outliers Against the Collective Prophetic Witness May Be Uninspired]].

## Where it turns
- **The canon-premise defeater** — the strongest negative move and the debate's center of gravity: an infallible identification of *which books* count cannot itself be drawn from those books, so sola scriptura appears self-refuting at the meta-level. Affirmative reply: we *derive the list from the books* (recognition is posterior to inspiration), not the books from a list. See [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]] ↔ [[The Internal-Seams Argument for the Canon]] ↔ [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]].
- **"Word of God" usage** — does the phrase denote the written text or oral proclamation? If the latter, the avalanche of "word of God" proof-texts proves the authority of God's *speech*, not the *sufficiency of the written word*. [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]] (note Rogers' [[Matthew 15.6]] counter).
- **Self-authentication circularity** — is "inspired because self-attesting" *viciously circular* (Horn: same as Quran/Book of Mormon) or *self-evidencing* like the sun and logic (Rogers)? And does relocating it to church consensus quietly rebuild Rome? [[The Self-Authentication Circularity Charge]] ↔ [[Self-Authentication via Church Consensus Backs Into the Catholic Model]].
- **Perspicuity / hard cases** — does the existence of sincere disagreement disprove clarity, or merely reveal blind readers? The hard-cases catalogue and "no final interpreter" press for an authority beyond the text. [[Perspicuity Fails the Hard-Cases Test]] ↔ [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture]] ↔ [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem]].

## Most authoritative view (honest tier assessment)
The **strongest material in the vault is on the negative (Catholic) side**, and it comes from the most rigorous source tier: the formal [[Anthony Rogers]] vs [[Trent Horn]] debate plus the [[Trent Horn]]/[[Joe Heschmeyer]] apologetics treatments. The canon-premise / "how do you know Scripture is the word of God" dilemma ([[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]], [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]]) is structurally tight and the affirmative's internal-seams answer was, by the notes' own assessment, "not revealed tonight" — a promissory IOU rather than a demonstration. The negative's historical defeater (early, sustained doubts about 2 Peter, James, 2–3 John, Revelation) genuinely strains pure self-authentication.

That said, the affirmative is not without a principled reply: *recognition is posterior to inspiration* (we derive the list from the books) and an *ultimate* authority cannot be validated by a higher one without ceasing to be ultimate — so some self-referentiality is unavoidable for **any** position, including the Catholic magisterium and LDS living prophets, each of which must finally authenticate itself. The **LDS negative** ([[Hayden Carroll]]) lands the cleanest rhetorical hits (the "three presuppositions," "no final interpreter") but inherits the symmetric burden: *where did God publicly designate the modern First Presidency / open canon as final?* — the same canon-authorization demand turned around. Honest verdict: the Catholic critique exposes a real epistemic gap in *sola scriptura* as usually stated; whether that gap is fatal depends on whether one grants that an *infallibly known* rule requires an *infallible recognizer* (Horn) or only an inspired reality fallibly but truly recognized (the Reformed "fallible collection of infallible books").
