# curse of Ham

The "curse of Ham" is an interpretive tradition that misreads Genesis 9:20–27 — where Noah curses **Canaan** (son of Ham) to servitude — as a curse on Ham himself and, by extension, on his supposedly dark-skinned descendants. Because Genesis 10 derives the Egyptians (Mizraim) and other African peoples from Ham, the reading was used in the 18th and 19th centuries to give a biblical rationalization for the enslavement of people of African descent. Critically, the biblical text curses Canaan, not Ham, and attaches no statement about skin color.

## Relevance

The vault treats the curse of Ham as an externally imposed misreading rather than what the text says, paralleling its handling of the [[curse of Canaan]] as a division of labor rather than a racial punishment. It also intersects with the Book of Abraham's priesthood-lineage scheme (blessings descending through Noah's sons), making the distinction between Ham, Canaan, and skin-color readings important for LDS apologetics.
