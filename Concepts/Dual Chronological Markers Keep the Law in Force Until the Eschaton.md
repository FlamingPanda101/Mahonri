# Dual Chronological Markers Keep the Law in Force Until the Eschaton

#type/claim

## The Claim
In [[Matthew 5.17-20|Matthew 5:18]] the [[Jesus of Nazareth|Matthean Jesus]] gives **two parallel time-clauses**, not one: "till heaven and earth pass" **and** "till all be fulfilled/accomplished." [[Dan McClellan]] argues these must be read as **synonymous markers of a single future apocalyptic event** — the passing of heaven and earth *is* the moment all is accomplished. The consequence: the Mosaic law (down to the smallest "jot or tittle") remains fully binding **until the end of the age**, and is not rendered obsolete by Jesus' first-coming "fulfillment." This directly undercuts the popular supersessionist reading that "all is accomplished" already happened at the cross/atonement.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The text gives two markers, not one (rating: strong; grammatical-canonical)
The verse does not say merely "until all is accomplished"; it adds "till heaven and earth pass away." Two distinct clauses, most naturally read in synonymous parallelism (a standard Semitic device), point to one future endpoint — an apocalyptic wrap-up, not the crucifixion. [^1]

### 2. Verse 19 forbids treating the law as cancelled (rating: strong; internal consistency)
The "Jesus subsumed all the laws within himself so they technically persist but no longer bind us" reading renders [[Matthew 5.17-20|verse 19]] incoherent: "whoever breaks one of these least commandments... shall be called least in the kingdom." If the law no longer obligated anyone, there would be nothing to "break." Matthew explicitly says one must *do and teach* the commandments. McClellan calls the laws-trapped-inside-Jesus picture "rather ridiculous." [^2]

### 3. "You can't fulfill a negative" (rating: moderate; conceptual)
Dan Beecher observes that a prohibition ("do not murder") cannot be "completed" the way a positive obligation or a prophecy can — you can only keep *not* doing it indefinitely. So the apologetic sense of "fulfill = bring to an end" does not fit the prohibitive commandments at all, weakening the obsolescence reading. [^3]

### 4. Luke shows a *different* theology, exposing the harmonization (rating: moderate; redaction-critical)
Luke's Sermon-on-the-Plain parallel reframes the saying around **difficulty**, not **time**: "it is easier for heaven and earth to pass away than for one tittle of the law to fail." That is a claim Jesus (as agent of creation, in Luke's frame) could in principle override. The divergence shows that forcing all the gospels to agree means "squinting at Matthew until the edges blur." Read on its own, Matthew says the law stays. [^4]

### 5. Steelman of the traditional supersessionist reading
The classic Christian view takes *plēroō* ("fulfil") in [[Matthew 5.17-20|v. 17]] as Christ *completing* the law's intended trajectory — embodying its righteousness perfectly and bringing the ceremonial/typological commands to their telos in himself (cf. Hebrews' fulfilled priesthood and sacrifice). On this reading "till all be accomplished" *was* satisfied at the cross, the moral law abides as an expression of God's character, and verse 19 addresses the interim before the cross or the abiding moral core. The strongest form notes that Matthew's Jesus does *reinterpret* commands ("but I say unto you"), arguably transposing them to a higher, christologically-centered key rather than leaving them mechanically in force. McClellan's reply: the *dual* time-markers and the explicit threat in v. 19 make a present-tense abolition exegetically very hard to sustain from Matthew's own words.

## LDS Relevance
Latter-day Saint theology classically teaches that the **law of Moses was fulfilled in Christ** and superseded by "the law of the gospel" (e.g., 3 Nephi 15:4-8, "the law in me is fulfilled... old things are done away"). On the surface this aligns with the supersessionist reading McClellan critiques. However, the LDS framework differs from cessationist Protestantism in a way relevant here: it keeps an **open canon and continuing revelation**, so the *content* of binding law is not frozen at a closed first-century text but can be re-given (e.g., the Word of Wisdom, the law of tithing restored in D&C 119). Thus the LDS tradition shares Matthew's intuition that *divine law continues to obligate* — it simply locates the authority to specify which laws bind in living prophets rather than in a static Torah or a one-time eschatological accomplishment. McClellan's apocalyptic reading (law binds until heaven and earth pass) is a critical-historical claim about Matthew's author, not a confessional one, and does not settle the LDS doctrinal question.
