# The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms

#type/claim

## The Claim
[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] argues — crediting the seed to [[Jack Welch|John (Jack) Welch]] — that the **Sermon on the Mount is not a sermon but an ordinance**, an initiatory rite that "played out over the course of those same three rooms" of [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple|Solomon's Temple]] (porch/*ulam*, holy place/*heikhal*, holy of holies/*debir*). [^1] The opening verse stages it: Jesus "went up into a mountain, and when he was set, his disciples came unto him" — and since a mountain *is* a temple symbol and Hebrew *yashav* means both "sit" and "dwell," "the sitter on top of the mountain is God," so this is "the ordinance by which you approach God who sits or dwells on top of the mountain." [^2] Butler then reads the chapter in sequence as ritual "stage directions": entry requirements ([[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance|the Beatitudes]]), a [[The Salt-Savor Covenant Penalty Enacted by Korihor and the Title of Liberty|covenant penalty]] (salt that loses its savor), graded moral laws (obedience, then chastity), an oath-formula, a personated [[The Forbidden Oath Is the Dark Mirror of the Covenant Oath|adversary]], officiators in the roles of Moses–Elijah–the Lord, and a closing change of status into [[Teleios Means Initiate - You Will Be Endowed Not Be Flawless|*teleios*]] ("you will be an initiate"). [^3] The framing premise, restated from the channel's recurring thesis, is that "the Book of Mormon is written by temple initiates for temple initiates" — Nephi, Jacob, Mormon, and Moroni all knew this ordinance and wrote expecting the endowed reader to recognize it. [^4]

## Supporting Evidence (steelman)

1. **The sequence really does track an ascent, not a lecture.** Butler's strongest move is that the Sermon resists being delivered as a talk ("it is not a talk . . . it doesn't sound like a talk") — a fact mainstream scholarship handles by calling it a stitched-together anthology of sayings (a Q-source/Gospel-of-Thomas hypothesis). Reading it instead as a *patterned ritual progression* (requirements → penalty → graded laws → new name) gives the unity that the "loose anthology" view denies it. *Rating: a genuine explanatory advantage over the anthology reading, if one grants the temple frame.*

2. **Concrete textual anchors, not just vibes.** The reading hangs on specific, checkable features: the mountain-ascent opening ([[Matthew 5.17-20|Matt 5:1]]), the altar instruction "leave there thy gift before the altar" (stage direction at an altar), the *antidikos*/"adversary" of Matt 5:25 as a courtroom plaintiff (= Hebrew *satan*), and the *téleios* of [[Matthew 5.48|5:48]] as ritual completion. Each is a real lexical or narrative datum, not invented. *Rating: defensible philological footing for several individual points.*

3. **It is a named, published argument with a pedigree.** The "ordinance" thesis is attributed to [[Jack Welch|Jack Welch]]'s book-length treatment (*The Sermon on the Mount in the Light of the Temple*), which Butler says he only understood on a re-reading a decade later — so this is not ad hoc invention but a developed strand of LDS scholarship. *Rating: strengthens provenance; Welch states it "very obliquely," so the bold version here is Butler's amplification.*

4. **It coheres with the vault's temple cluster.** The claim is the New-Testament counterpart of [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]] and presupposes [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]] and [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]] (Butler names the garden story as the myth the ordinance enacts). It does not stand alone.

**Steelman of the skeptical counter.** The decisive objection is **parallelomania / unfalsifiable retrofitting**: the LDS endowment is symbol-dense and elastic, so a believer who learns it first can map almost any sacred ascent text onto its rooms after the fact — and the "you only see it if you're endowed" framing is exactly what confirmation bias predicts. Critical scholarship already has a non-ritual account (the Sermon as Matthean redaction of sayings material), and several of Butler's anchors are arguable: *yashav* "sit/dwell" does not make every seated speaker God; the altar logic of Matt 5:23-24 fits ordinary Second-Temple sacrifice without a three-room rite; and reading *téleios* as "initiate" is a possible but contested gloss (BDAG's primary sense is "complete/mature"). Granting the parallels does **not** establish that Matthew (or Jesus) *intended* an endowment — only that a modern reader can perceive one. A Latter-day Saint reply: the claim is offered as the *best unifying explanation* of features the anthology view leaves inert, and the cross-text density (Old Testament temple architecture, the Book of Mormon's independent covenant-penalty and oath material) is harder to dismiss as coincidence than any single parallel; the right caution is to argue carefully and keep the parallel *functional* (per [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]]), not to declare the pattern empty.
