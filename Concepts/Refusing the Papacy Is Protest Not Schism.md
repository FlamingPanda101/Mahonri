# Refusing the Papacy Is Protest Not Schism
#type/claim

## The Claim
[[Gavin Ortlund]] and [[Jordan Cooper]] argue that the Reformers' refusal to submit to the papacy was *protest*, not *schism*, because "the entities being protested... are not of divine institution." There is no Petrine office of infallible supremacy in the New Testament, nor in 1 Clement, the Didache, or the second-century evidence — even honest Catholic scholars grant the papacy "is an incremental growth within the church." To decline submission to a man-made structure that "yokes our consciences" without divine warrant is the territory of [[Mark 7.1-13|Mark 7]] (Jesus on the Pharisees' traditions nullifying God's word), not departure from the one true church. Cooper adds [[Philip Melanchthon]]'s distinction (from the *Treatise on the Power and Primacy of the Pope*): the Reformers rejected the pope only by *divine right* (necessary for salvation), not necessarily by *human right* (organizational convenience) — and notes the Reformers were "kicked out," not voluntary leavers.

## Supporting Evidence
1. **The office is not apostolic.** The panel claims the New Testament knows no monarchical bishop of Rome wielding infallible jurisdiction over the whole church; the earliest non-canonical witnesses (1 Clement, the Didache) show collegial presbyter-bishops, not a papal monarchy. The case rests on the historical thinness of second-century Roman primacy.
2. **Catholic scholars concede development.** Ortlund leans on the admission (e.g., from Catholic historians like Robert Eno or Klaus Schatz) that papal primacy is an "incremental growth" that developed over centuries rather than springing fully formed from Peter. If the office is a development, then refusing it is not abandoning something Christ instituted.
3. **Human-right vs. divine-right (Melanchthon).** The Reformers could in principle accept a Roman primacy of *order* or *convenience* (human right); what they denied is that submission to the pope is *de iure divino*, required for salvation. Refusing an unwarranted divine claim is obedience to conscience under God, not rebellion against God's church.
4. **They were expelled, not departers.** Excommunication came *to* the Reformers (Luther's *Decet Romanum Pontificem*, 1521). On this telling the institutional break was initiated by Rome, so the schism charge is misdirected.

**Steelman of the Catholic view.** A Catholic answers that Christ did institute Peter's office ("Thou art Peter... I will give unto thee the keys," [[Matthew 16.18-19|Matthew 16:18-19]]; "feed my sheep," John 21), that legitimate doctrinal *development* (Newman) is the unfolding of an apostolic seed, not invention, and that unilaterally judging the visible church to lack authority and erecting rival communions *is* the formal definition of schism — Cyprian's "he cannot have God as Father who does not have the Church as mother." Being excommunicated does not retroactively make the rejected teaching false. So whether this is "protest" or "schism" turns entirely on the contested premise: was the papacy divinely instituted?
