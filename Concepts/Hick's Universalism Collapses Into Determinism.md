# Hick's Universalism Collapses Into Determinism

#type/claim

## The Claim

[[David Paulsen]] argues that [[John Hick]]'s escape from [[Flew's Divine Complicity Argument]] — **universal salvation** — is self-undermining. Because Hick affirms absolute creation and foreknowledge, he can preserve God's perfect goodness "only if not one soul is lost." But to *guarantee* no soul is lost, he must smuggle in a **determinism** that destroys the libertarian (incompatibilist) freedom his own soul-making free-will defense requires. "Hick's way out, as appealing as it first appears, seems on analysis to be incoherent." [^1]

## Supporting Evidence

1. **Hick's only exit is universalism.** Having conceded that, on absolute creation, "God is ultimately responsible for all the evil that occurs in the world," Hick sees "one, and only one, way out": all of us will finally achieve an authentic relationship with God in a postmortal life "the value of which will far outweigh any finite evil suffered here." [^2]
2. **Two problems Paulsen presses.** (a) Since God creates our primal self-centered natures (or the process that yields them) out of nothing, "I see no reason... why God could not have made us significantly better than we are" — Hick's absolute creator "could have made a much better world than ours." (b) Given that genuine relationship needs incompatibilist freedom, how can it be *certain* there won't be, as [[C.S. Lewis]] suggested, "rebels to the end" with "the doors of hell... locked on the inside"? [^3]
3. **Hick waffles into determinism.** Hick replies that God made us with "a nature that can find its perfect fulfilment... only in" God, so He is "free to follow their own deepest desire, which can lead them only to Himself." But, Paulsen notes, "now Hick is waffling, for it appears that **we are not free after all.**" To salvage God's goodness Hick "is forced to accept some mode of determinism that undermines his free-will defense." [^4]
4. **The contrast.** Joseph Smith's "way out... is to **not go in**": by denying absolute creation he never incurs the complicity charge, so he is not driven to a universalism that must quietly cancel freedom. Real freedom (and thus the possibility of the lost) is preserved (see [[Soul-Making Theodicy - Hick and Joseph Smith]]). [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "But now Hick is waffling, for it appears that we are not free after all. If so, Hick's position is inconsistent." [^4]

> "Joseph Smith's way out of the conceptual incoherency generated by the traditional theological premises is to not go in." [^5]
