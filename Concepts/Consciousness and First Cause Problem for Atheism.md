# Consciousness and First Cause Problem for Atheism

## The Claim

Atheism faces an intractable problem with consciousness: if the initial state of
the universe (the Big Bang) was non-conscious matter/energy, and non-conscious
things cannot produce consciousness, then consciousness should not exist. Yet
we ARE conscious. The consistent atheist — [[Daniel Dennett]] — therefore
denies that consciousness is real, calling it an "illusion." But an illusion
requires a conscious subject to BE illuded, making this claim self-defeating.
Theists, by contrast, posit that consciousness is fundamental — God has always
been conscious, and conscious beings can create or organize other conscious
beings. LDS theology goes further: human intelligences are co-eternal with God
and have always been conscious. Thus, consciousness has always existed and
requires no explanation from non-conscious origins.

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The Core Argument: Non-Conscious → No Consciousness (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Philosophical)

[[Hayden Carroll]] frames the argument:

> "I ask, can this table — [knocks on the table] — can this... first of all,
> is this conscious? I ask the atheists and they say, 'Well, no.' Can this
> table, which is not conscious, bring about something that is conscious? Like,
> can this bring about life? And it's not life — it's consciousness. And they
> would always say, 'Well, no. Consciousness does not derive from non-conscious
> things.'"[^1]

The logic:
1. The initial state of the universe (Big Bang / early universe) was not
   conscious — it was purely physical matter/energy.
2. Non-conscious things cannot produce consciousness (ex nihilo nihilo fit
   applied to consciousness).
3. We are conscious.
4. Therefore, atheism cannot account for consciousness.

The strength of the argument depends on premise 2: CAN non-conscious matter
produce consciousness? The materialist says yes — consciousness "emerges" from
sufficiently complex neural arrangements. Carroll challenges this: "How did non
— or I should ask — has there always been a conscious something? Was the Big
Bang conscious? And they always say, 'Well, no.' And I say, 'Then ask yourself
this question.'"[^1]

### 2. Daniel Dennett's Consistent Answer: Consciousness Is an Illusion (Evidence Rating: Primary — Atheist Source)

> "This is why Daniel Dennett says consciousness is an illusion. Because
> consciousness — true consciousness — does not come from something that's
> not conscious."[^2]

Dennett's position (eliminative materialism) is that consciousness as we
intuitively experience it doesn't exist. There is no "what it's like" to be a
bat or a human — there are just physical processes that the brain
misinterprets as conscious experience. Carroll presents this as the HONEST
atheist position: if you start with non-conscious matter, you can't get
consciousness, so you must deny consciousness exists.

> "Daniel Dennett got it right. He said, 'We're not actually conscious. We're
> just — it's an illusion. We're just playing out. There is no self. It's a
> trick the brain plays on us.'"[^2]

### 3. The Self-Defeating Nature of Denying Consciousness (Evidence Rating: Secondary — Philosophical)

Carroll notes the problem:

> "This is why it's so fun to talk to atheists because they have to deny the
> fine-tuning and then if they want to deny it, you say, 'Okay, now you got to
> deal with all these problems.'"[^3]

The denial of consciousness is self-defeating:
- "Consciousness is an illusion" — who is experiencing the illusion? An
  illusion requires a subject.
- "There is no self" — who is making the claim? A claim requires a claimant.
- If thoughts are just physical processes with no subjective character, then
  "thoughts" (including Dennett's own) have no truth value — they're just
  neural events.

### 4. The Theistic Alternative: Consciousness Is Fundamental (Evidence Rating: Primary — Doctrinal)

Carroll presents the theistic solution:

> "We would say that we've always been conscious. Even creedal Christians would
> say that God has always been conscious, so He can create conscious beings.
> Yeah, but in an atheistic worldview, Daniel Dennett got it right."[^2]

Two levels of theistic response:
- **Creedal Christianity:** God (a conscious being) has always existed. God
  creates conscious human souls. Consciousness comes FROM consciousness, not
  from non-consciousness.
- **LDS Theology (stronger):** Human intelligences are co-eternal with God —
  they have ALWAYS been conscious. God did not create consciousness from
  non-consciousness; He organizes eternally conscious intelligences. This is an
  even stronger answer: consciousness never "came from" non-consciousness
  because it has always existed.[^4]

### 5. The Connection to Free Will and AI (Evidence Rating: Tertiary — Integration)

The consciousness problem connects to the other implications:

Butler notes: "AI is obviously huge here and it has a form of godliness but
denies the power thereof... There is no way that out there in the universe,
atoms are going to collide and recreate and then perfectly organize themselves
and then all of a sudden create an electrical current to be able to power a
computer with all of its complex chips and then code it all and make AI. That's
absolutely absurd to think that could happen."[^5]

The point: even artificial intelligence, impressive as it is, was created BY
conscious minds. The atheist who looks at AI and says "this came from
consciousness" but looks at human consciousness and says "this came from
non-consciousness" is applying inconsistent standards.

## Counter-Arguments / Steelmanning

### Objection 1: Emergence — Consciousness Arises from Complexity
**Steelman:** Consciousness is an emergent property of sufficiently complex
information processing. Just as wetness emerges from H2O molecules (none of
which are individually "wet"), consciousness emerges from neural complexity
(none of which is individually "conscious"). This is not mysterious — it's how
emergence works throughout nature. The demand that consciousness must come FROM
consciousness is a category error.

**Response:** The wetness analogy is a response to an argument Carroll didn't
fully develop, but it's still debatable. Wetness is reducible to molecular
behavior — it's not a genuinely NOVEL property; it's just the macro-level
description of micro-level facts. Consciousness, by contrast, has properties
(first-person subjectivity, qualia, intentionality) that appear to be
genuinely novel and not reducible to third-person physical descriptions. This
is the "hard problem of consciousness" (David Chalmers) — and it's extensively
debated. Carroll's argument assumes the hard problem is real and that
materialism can't solve it — a contested but defensible position.

### Objection 2: Panpsychism — Consciousness IS Fundamental in Nature
**Steelman:** Some atheist/naturalist philosophers (Galen Strawson, Philip
Goff) are panpsychists: they argue that consciousness IS a fundamental property
of matter, present in some proto-form even at the level of elementary
particles. On this view, consciousness didn't "emerge" from non-conscious
matter — it was always there. This is a naturalistic position that avoids both
Dennett's eliminativism and theism.

**Response:** Panpsychism is a fascinating development that Carroll doesn't
engage. It would partially answer his argument: consciousness doesn't derive
from non-conscious things if everything is at least proto-conscious. However,
panpsychism faces its own problems: (a) the "combination problem" — how do
micro-consciousnesses combine into unified macro-consciousness? (b) It's a
radically revisionary metaphysics that most atheists reject. (c) If
consciousness is fundamental, why should we expect the universe's physical
constants to be life-permitting? Panpsychism doesn't solve fine-tuning. And (d)
panpsychism's world of ubiquitous proto-consciousness looks remarkably like a
world "full of intelligences" — a concept familiar in LDS theology.

### Objection 3: The Illusion Argument Isn't Self-Defeating
**Steelman:** Dennett doesn't say there's "nobody home" — he says our
introspective model of consciousness (the "Cartesian theater") is wrong.
Consciousness is real AS a set of cognitive processes; what's an illusion is
the idea of a unified, qualitative "what it's like." The claim "there is no
Cartesian theater" doesn't require a Cartesian theater to be true.

**Response:** This is a fair representation of Dennett's actual position.
Carroll's presentation is simplified. However, many philosophers of mind
(including atheists like Thomas Nagel and David Chalmers) argue that
Dennett's position amounts to denying the existence of consciousness — calling
it "consciousness explained" when what he's really done is "explained away."
The debate is live.
