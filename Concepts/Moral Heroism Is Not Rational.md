# Moral Heroism Is Not Rational

## The Point

The highest moral acts — self-sacrifice for a stranger — are not recommended by
reason. Because nothing is more rational than self-preservation, risking one's
life for another's good is, on a purely rational accounting, irrational. Moral
greatness therefore comes from somewhere other than reason.

## Argument

1. Consider a non-Jew in Nazi-occupied Europe risking his or her life to hide a
   Jew. This was "moral greatness of the highest order" [^1].
2. But was it *rational*? "Not really. You can't get much more rational than
   self-preservation." Endangering one's life and family for a stranger does not
   pass a cost-benefit test [^1].
3. Empirically, Prager notes that in the many studies of Holocaust rescuers he
   has read, no rescuer ever said they acted because it was "the reasonable or
   rational thing to do" — "not one" [^1].
4. Therefore the motive for moral heroism is not rational calculation but
   something else (conscience, faith, a sense of the sacredness of life), which
   reinforces that morality is not derived from reason.
5. (Steelman of the opposing view.) A defender of rational ethics might say
   altruism *is* rational under the right premises — kin selection, reciprocity,
   or an impartial principle that every person's life matters equally makes
   rescue the reasonable act. Prager's reply is that those premises are precisely
   the prior value-commitments reason cannot itself justify; and that rescuers'
   own self-reports point to duty or compassion, not deduction.

## Best Quotes

> "You can't get much more rational than self-preservation."[^1]

> "I have never read of any rescuers who said that they did what they did
> because it was the reasonable or rational thing to do. Not one."[^1]
