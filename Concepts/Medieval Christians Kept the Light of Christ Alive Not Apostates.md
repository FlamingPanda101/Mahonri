# Medieval Christians Kept the Light of Christ Alive, Not Apostates

## The Point

In the Afterword to *Ancient Christians*, [[Miranda Wilcox]] makes a pointed revisionist argument for a Latter-day Saint audience: medieval Christians should not be dismissed as "deluded by apostasy" but **revered** for "keeping the light of Christ alive." [^1] Across the thousand-year span between the fifth and fifteenth centuries, Christian communities stretched "from China to Ireland, from Ethiopia and India to Iceland and Poland," enculturating Christianity, preserving scripture, and developing rich traditions of worship. This directly softens the traditional LDS "Great Apostasy" framing of the Middle Ages.

## Evidence

1. **The reframing is explicit.** "Instead of perceiving medieval Christians as deluded by apostasy, we should revere them for keeping the light of Christ alive by enculturating Christianity across the world, preserving and transmitting scripture, and developing rich traditions of worship and diverse modes of religious life. They devoted their lives to Jesus Christ." [^1]
2. **It is honest about the dark side.** Wilcox does not whitewash: "Christendom was not always aligned with Christ's way of peace. The atrocities of the Crusades, Inquisition, and persecution of Jews fueled by supersessionist zeal" are acknowledged as "perhaps, better known than the medieval devotional practices I highlight." [^2]
3. **Four concrete achievements.** The "Why It Matters" box lists what medieval Christians did: "They spread Christianity across Africa, Asia, and Europe. They preserved and transmitted scripture. They developed rich traditions of worship and diverse modes of religious life. They devoted their lives to Jesus Christ." [^3]
4. **A spectrum of religious life.** Wilcox catalogs the diverse modes — clergy (ordained for sacraments), ascetics (monastics, mendicants, hermits, anchoresses), and laity (baptized but not ordained), with penitents and ecclesiastical workers blurring the boundaries. [^4]
5. **Grounded in Wilcox's own scholarship.** She cross-references her article "Narrating Religious Heritage: Apostasy and Restoration" (*BYU Studies Quarterly*, 2021), signaling this is a developed scholarly position on reframing LDS apostasy narratives. [^5]

## Best Quotes

> "Instead of perceiving medieval Christians as deluded by apostasy, we should revere them for keeping the light of Christ alive." [^1]

> "While not every aspect of medieval Christianity is worthy of emulation, much may be admired." [^6]

## LDS Connection

This is the load-bearing apologetic move of the chapter: Wilcox reframes the Latter-day Saint "Great Apostasy" — usually applied wholesale to the medieval church — so that medieval Christians become fellow disciples worthy of "kinship and gratitude" rather than objects of polemic. "Hearing the voices of medieval Christians may have evoked a sense of a kinship and gratitude." [^6]
