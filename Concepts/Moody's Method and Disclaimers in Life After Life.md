# Moody's Method and Disclaimers in Life After Life

## The Point
Moody is unusually candid about the limits of his own project, and his honesty *strengthens* the book's credibility. A philosopher (Ph.D., University of Virginia, 1969) entering medicine, he explicitly states "I am not trying to prove that there is life after death. Nor do I think that a 'proof' of this is presently possible" [^1][^2]. He worked from roughly **150 cases** in three categories, interviewing about fifty people in detail [^3][^4]. As a logician he "disavows that road to belief which proceeds through invalid appeals to authority" and asks readers *not* to believe him on his authority — only to investigate for themselves [^5]. These disclaimers mean the apologetic weight rests on the convergent testimony and later corroboration, not on overselling.

## Evidence
1. **Author's background, openly stated.** Moody lists his "background, opinions and prejudices": never near death himself; not broadly read in occult/paranormal literature; raised Presbyterian, now a Methodist holding "religion" as "a concern with spiritual and religious doctrines… questions" rather than fixed doctrines; Ph.D. in philosophy (ethics, logic, philosophy of language), then medical school toward psychiatry [^6][^2].
2. **Explicit non-proof disclaimer.** "I am not trying to prove that there is life after death. Nor do I think that a 'proof' of this is presently possible" [^1].
3. **No appeal to authority.** "I am not asking that anyone accept and believe the contents of this volume on my authority alone… I specifically ask that no one do so. All I ask is for anyone who disbelieves… to poke around a bit for himself" [^5].
4. **The case base.** "At the present time, I know of approximately 150 cases of this phenomenon," falling into three categories: (1) persons resuscitated after being pronounced clinically dead, (2) persons who came very close to physical death, and (3) deathbed accounts relayed by bystanders [^3].
5. **Deliberate selection.** Moody mostly dropped the third (second-hand) category to stay close to firsthand reports and keep the study manageable, interviewing "in great detail some fifty persons" [^4].
6. **The composite is an ideal type.** Crucially, the "model" or composite near-death experience he builds is not a single person's account — it is constructed from recurring elements; no one informant exemplifies every feature. (Moody flags throughout that he reports on a *search*, not a statistical or laboratory proof.) [^7]
7. **Honest about emotional involvement.** Having heard so many accounts, "I have come to feel almost as though I have lived through them myself," and he "can only hope that this attitude has not compromised the rationality and balance of my approach" [^8].

## Best Quotes
> "I am not trying to prove that there is life after death. Nor do I think that a 'proof' of this is presently possible." [^1]

> "As a logician who disavows that road to belief which proceeds through invalid appeals to authority, I specifically ask that no one [believe on my authority]. All I ask is for anyone who disbelieves what he reads here to poke around a bit for himself." [^5]

## LDS Connection
Moody's invitation to "poke around a bit for himself" rather than accept a claim on authority resembles the Book of Mormon's own invitation to test the witness experientially (Moroni 10:4–5) and Alma's seed experiment (Alma 32) — knowledge sought through personal inquiry, not coerced by appeal to the investigator's credentials. His careful refusal to overstate evidence is a model of intellectual honesty for apologetics.
