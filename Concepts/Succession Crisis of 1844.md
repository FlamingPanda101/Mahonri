# Succession Crisis of 1844
#type/claim

## The Claim
After Joseph Smith's murder in June 1844, the Latter-day Saint movement fractured over who should lead it, producing the "succession crisis" out of which multiple competing factions emerged (the Quorum of the Twelve under Brigham Young, Sidney Rigdon, James Strang, and later the Reorganization). In this vault the crisis carries an evidential payload: research by [[Ron Esplin]] shows that the Quorum of the Twelve was the *only* faction that continued Joseph's *full* program — including plural marriage and the temple endowment — which presupposes that program already existed under Joseph, undercutting claims that Brigham Young invented those doctrines.

## Supporting Evidence
- **The Twelve continued Joseph's whole program.** Per [[Ron Esplin]]'s work on 1844, the breakaway groups each kept only parts of Joseph's teaching, while the Twelve preserved the complete program — most tellingly plural marriage and the endowment. If those practices were already operative enough that only one faction carried them forward intact, they must trace back to Joseph, not to a later Brigham-era invention.
- **It anchors the anti-denialism case.** This is one of the strands the vault braids against [[Polygamy Denialism]]: alongside the [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]] wordprint and the 1843 dating of the revelation, the succession evidence shows plural marriage existing *before* the crisis, so relocating it onto Brigham fails.
- **Leadership through the keys.** The Twelve's claim rested on priesthood keys Joseph had conferred on them, the basis for the argument that Brigham Young legitimately preserved the church (see [[Brigham Young Saved the Church Through the Keys]]).
- **Steelman.** Other claimants had real arguments — Rigdon was the surviving First Presidency counselor; Joseph III had lineal claims later pressed by the Reorganization, which rejected polygamy and read the early record as monogamous. A skeptic can argue the "full program" framing is constructed in hindsight by the winning faction, and that the contemporaneous public record (Nauvoo-era denials of polygamy) complicates any clean back-dating. The vault's reply is that the Twelve's continuation of plural marriage and the endowment is documented behavior, not mere later assertion, and it converges with the stylometric and documentary evidence tying those practices to Joseph himself.
