# Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation

## The Claim (Objection Rebutted)
[[Sam Shamoun]] argues from a "pan-canonical" method: the Bible is one unified whole authored by God, so it must be self-consistent (univocal), and any single verse may be read in the light of every other verse. On that basis Shamoun answers the heavenly-council reading of texts like [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]] ("let us make man") by citing other passages that "Yahweh alone is God," and rereads [[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8-9]] (Yahweh apportioning the nations to the "sons of God") through Paul's "demons" language in [[1 Corinthians 10.20|1 Corinthians 10:20]]. [^1][^2]

## The Rebuttal

1. **It is proof-texting / scripture-bashing, not exegesis.** [[Blake Ostler]] charges that Shamoun "ignores the passage, immediately shifts the ground" to a verse that agrees with him — "I'm not going to address your scripture, I'll address mine." *Rating: a fair description of a real fallacy* (dodging a text by changing the subject); it is a method that prevents learning from any individual text. [^3]

2. **Univocality smuggles in a conclusion.** If you assume in advance that every book agrees, "you can't learn from it because you impose on it what you already know it has to say." Each biblical book is an individual author in individual circumstances who *may* contradict others. *Rating: strong* and aligned with mainstream historical-critical method (cf. [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]], [[There Is No Such Thing as The Bible]]). [^4][^5]

3. **The Deuteronomy 32 / 1 Corinthians 10 conflation produces an absurd God.** Paul's *daimonia* are the idols/gods of *Gentiles in his own day*; importing that back into Deut 32:8, where ancient Near Eastern gods are *apportioned over the nations by Yahweh*, makes God himself "set demons over other nations" — "a God that nobody wants to worship." *Rating: a sharp reductio*; the linguistic and historical context of each text differs. [^6][^7]

4. **Steelman of the inerrantist.** The univocal reader is honoring a genuine theological conviction — that a single divine Author guarantees a coherent canon, and that later revelation (the NT) is the authoritative interpreter of the earlier. On this view "consensus scholarship" is itself a contestable, naturalistic frame. *Rating: a coherent confessional stance*, but it is a *theological* commitment, not a neutral reading; it cannot settle the historical meaning of an ancient text by fiat, and Ostler notes truth is not decided by "a democratic vote of the scholars" either. [^8]

## Best Quotes
> "What he does is he ignores the passage, immediately shifts the ground because what he's doing is proof texting... I'm not going to address your scripture, I'll address mine. And this kind of scripture bashing is juvenile." [^3]

> "Once you make that assumption and then you approach the text with that assumption, you can't do like actual real analysis of what these texts actually are." [^4]

> "The logical upshot of his argument is that God of the God of Israel apportioned over other nations demons. So, God himself set demons over other nations... What kind of God do you believe in?" [^6]

> "Mark Smith has been the president of the Catholic Bible Association... he actually wants to be honest with his evidence." [^9]
