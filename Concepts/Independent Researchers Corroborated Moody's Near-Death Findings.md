# Independent Researchers Corroborated Moody's Near-Death Findings

## The Point
In the quarter-century after *Life After Life* (1975), multiple researchers working independently — by formal scientific methods, in different countries, with different patient populations — reached the same conclusions Moody did: near-death experiences (NDEs) are a real and natural part of the dying process, not hallucinations produced by drugs, oxygen deprivation, or brain pathology [^1][^2]. Writing the 2001 Preface, pediatrician Melvin Morse states that he does not know "of a single mainstream scientific researcher who has not reached similar conclusions," and that three major literature reviews in the prior seven years all agreed with Moody's initial findings [^3]. This convergence of independent investigators is the apologetic heart of the case: the same data keeps surfacing no matter who looks.

## Evidence
1. **First-generation researchers using formal methods.** The first wave included Dr. Ken Ring (professor of psychology, University of Connecticut), Dr. Bruce Greyson (professor of psychiatry, University of Virginia), and neuropsychiatrist Peter Fenwick of the British Royal College of Psychiatrists. "They used formal scientific investigative techniques and essentially reached the same conclusions as Dr. Moody," documenting that the experiences "are real, not the result of hallucinations or brain pathology" [^1].
2. **Morse's controlled pediatric study.** Morse's team at Seattle Children's Hospital ran a *controlled clinical study* of NDEs in children and "again documented that these experiences are a real part of the dying process and not caused by drugs or a lack of oxygen to the brain" [^2].
3. **Young children with no cultural script report the same thing.** The children were "too young to fear death or to know what a near-death experience is," yet they "told the same stories as the adults tell in *Life After Life*" — undercutting the objection that NDEs are merely culturally expected imagery [^2].
4. **NDEs induced under controlled laboratory conditions.** Dr. James Whinnery (National Warfare Institute) found that fighter pilots brought near death by centrifuge G-forces had near-death experiences — reproducing the phenomenon experimentally rather than relying on retrospective testimony [^2].
5. **Independence of investigators.** "Dr. Moody, a psychiatrist from Georgia; Dr. Whinnery, a flight surgeon for the Air Force; and I, a pediatrician from Seattle, each working independently, reached the same conclusions about near-death experiences" [^2].
6. **Shift in the scientific climate.** When the book first appeared "medical scientists laughed and dismissed near-death experiences as hallucinations"; twenty-five years later there were "dozens of articles in mainstream scientific journals," "hundreds of graduate students" studying NDEs, and "more than half of the medical schools in the country" teaching courses on the spiritual aspects of dying [^3][^4].
7. **Drove a research institution.** *Life After Life* "directly resulted in the formation" of the International Association of Near-Death Studies (IANDS), which "actively contributes to and encourages scientific research," with hundreds of chapters in the U.S. and worldwide [^5].

## LDS Connection
The pattern Morse describes — *conscious awareness surviving the moment of clinical death*, a being or "loving light" greeting the dying, and a life "evaluated and interpreted… according to the love we shared" [^6] — coheres with LDS teaching on the post-mortal spirit world (Alma 40), the continued conscious labor of the spirit after death (D&C 138), a life review, and a being of light readily understood in terms of the Light of Christ. The apologetic value is the *kind* of evidence: independent, secular, sometimes laboratory-controlled investigators converging on conscious survival of death — an empirical complement to revealed doctrine rather than a substitute for it.
