# Never Put the Gospel Behind a Paywall

#type/claim

## The Claim
The Salty Saints panel converts a gut reaction into an explicit ethic of LDS content creation: **freely-given gospel teaching should not be monetized or paywalled.** The trigger is a "Think Celestial" merchandise line ("curated… with intentional healing benefits") and the memory of the "ponderize" scandal — a Seventy gave a General Conference talk and "that night" a relative's business launched "ponderize shirts, pens, bracelets… 95% off," forcing the authority to publicly disavow it.[^1][^2] [[Hayden Carroll]] relays [[Jacob Hansen]]'s standing commitment to "never put my content or my testimony behind a paywall," and draws the line precisely: donations and *early access* are fine; *members-only gospel content* is not. The scriptural anchor is Christ cleansing the temple — "make not my Father's house a house of merchandise" ([[John 2.19-21|John 2:16]]).[^3][^4]

## Supporting Evidence

### 1. The "house of merchandise" anchor is apt — rate: strong
The panel locates the unease exactly in [[John 2.19-21|John 2:16]]: a younger brother diagnoses the "ick" as Jesus' rebuke "stop making my father's house a house of merchandise."[^1] The argument is not that ministry must be free of cost, but that *what the prophets or God have given* should not be guarded for profit: "why should I be profiting off of trying to guard what the church or what God has given us."[^3] This tracks a broad scriptural suspicion of priestcraft (2 Nephi 26:29 — preaching "for gain") that the panelists invoke by jokingly naming a hypothetical store "paywall priesthood / priestcrafts."[^5]

### 2. The donation / paywall distinction does real work — rate: strong, practical
The ethic is not a blanket ban on receiving support. Carroll: "I do have a membership thing on YouTube where if people want to donate, they can. But I will never do members-only content… If you're going to get it early and you want to donate for that, okay, that's something different."[^3] The bright line is *access to the gospel message itself*, not the existence of a tip jar. He concedes the limit of the rule — "that's not for everybody; some people are trying to make a business out of this and I can understand that" — so it is offered as a personal/communal norm, not a universal condemnation.[^4]

### 3. The ponderize precedent shows why the instinct matters — rate: moderate, anecdotal
The cautionary tale is concrete: a conference talk immediately spawned a merchandise "sale," members reacted ("what is this?"), and the General Authority had to disclaim affiliation.[^2] The panel reads the public backlash itself as evidence the instinct is widely shared in the church. **Steelman of the other side:** content creation has genuine costs (equipment, time), and walling premium *extras* is standard and defensible; a critic could note the line between "early access for donors" and "members-only content" is fuzzier in practice than the rule implies. The panel half-concedes this by permitting early-access perks — the principle is clearest at the extremes (free core teaching vs. pay-to-hear-the-testimony) and greyer in the middle.

## Best Quotes
> "I don't like monetizing or putting behind a paywall what the general authorities are teaching." — [[Roy Anderson]][^6]

> "[Jacob Hansen] said, 'I've made a commitment to never put my content or my testimony behind a paywall'… I will never do members-only content. Why should I be profiting off of trying to guard what the church or what God has given us?" — [[Hayden Carroll]][^3]

> "Stop making my father's house a house of merchandise. And I'm like, there we go. That's what it is."[^1]

## LDS Connection
The note sits at the intersection of apologetic *posture* and the Latter-day Saint horror of **priestcraft** — "that men preach and set themselves up for a light unto the world, that they may get gain and praise of the world; but they seek not the welfare of Zion" (2 Nephi 26:29). For LDS content creators specifically, it functions as a self-policing norm in an attention economy that rewards monetization: the credibility of apologetics — itself meant to be a [[Spirit Over Argument - The Limits of Apologetics|shield for faith, not a product]] — is undercut the moment the testimony is sold. It pairs naturally with the warning against treating apologetics as a contest to be won (see [[Victory as an Idol in Apologetics]]): both guard the *motive* behind defending the faith.
