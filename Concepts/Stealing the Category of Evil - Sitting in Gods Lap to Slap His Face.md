# Stealing the Category of Evil - Sitting in God's Lap to Slap His Face

## The Point
This is the chapter's expression of the book's title thesis applied to evil. When New Atheists denounce the evil done "in God's name," they must **borrow the category of objective evil from the very God they are arguing against.** A "molecules-in-motion" atheism has no resources to call anything objectively good or evil, so every moral complaint smuggles in a theistic moral standard. Turek's vivid image: the atheist "has to sit in God's lap to slap His face."[^1]

## Evidence
Turek's star witness is **C. S. Lewis**, the former atheist who realized his anti-God argument from the universe's cruelty and injustice was self-undermining: "A man does not call a line crooked unless he has some idea of a straight line. What was I comparing this universe with when I called it unjust?"[^2] The atheist's outrage requires a fixed standard ("a straight line") that atheism cannot supply. Turek applies the point to specific figures: **Christopher Hitchens** was right that religious people have done evil things, but his materialist atheism "has no means by which to establish anything as good or evil"; and when **Richard Dawkins** complains about the "evil" God of the Old Testament, he must "steal goodness from God to even make sense of his complaint."[^3] The deeper move: the atheist *can* convert to theism and argue the God of the Bible is not the true God — a logically possible position — but he "can't maintain his atheism and claim that the God of the Bible violates an objective moral law because no one can violate an objective moral law that doesn't exist!"[^4] Hence the value-judgment "atheism is good and Christianity is evil" is not just empirically dubious but *logically* incoherent, since good and evil don't exist in an atheistic world.[^5]

## Best Quotes
> "He has to sit in God's lap to slap His face." [^1]

> "[As an atheist] my argument against God was that the universe seemed so cruel and unjust. But how had I got this idea of just and unjust? A man does not call a line crooked unless he has some idea of a straight line." — C. S. Lewis, quoted by Turek [^2]

> "No one can violate an objective moral law that doesn't exist!" [^4]

## LDS Connection
Latter-day Saints share Turek's conviction that real moral obligation testifies of a real Lawgiver; the "stolen concept" critique is fully usable in LDS apologetics. The Restoration sharpens *why* the moral standard is not arbitrary: the **Light of Christ** "is given to every man, that he may know good from evil" (Moroni 7:16), so the atheist's inescapable sense of justice — Lewis's "straight line" — is, on LDS terms, the imprint of Christ's light on the conscience, not a cultural accident. This also tempers the polemic charitably: the moralizing atheist is not merely a hypocrite but a person responding, however inconsistently, to genuine divine light he has not yet acknowledged. Note one boundary worth flagging for honesty: Turek (with classical theism) often grounds the moral standard in the *nature of an absolutely simple, ex-nihilo Creator*; LDS theology grounds objective good in the character and eternal law within which even God acts. The apologetic point — you cannot coherently denounce evil while denying its ground — survives that difference intact.
