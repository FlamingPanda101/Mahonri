# Donald W. Parry

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint scholarship
**Role:** Professor of the Hebrew Bible (Brigham Young University); Dead Sea Scrolls editor

## Bio
Donald W. Parry is a Latter-day Saint scholar of the Hebrew Bible and the Dead Sea Scrolls and a longtime BYU professor, best known in temple studies for his work connecting the Israelite temple to creation and Eden symbolism. [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] highlights Parry's article "Garden of Eden: Prototype Sanctuary" as a prime example of widely-available LDS scholarship that most members do not know about.[^1]

## On the Garden of Eden as Prototype Sanctuary
Parry's central argument, as Spackman relays it, is that Genesis 2–3 reflects temple themes: cherubim and a flaming sword guard Eden's eastern entrance, the Holy of Holies is decorated as a garden with cherubim on its doors and veil, and on the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16) the high priest carries blood east-to-west past the guardian figures to re-enter God's presence — "a ritual undoing of the fall."[^2] Spackman repeatedly defers to Parry's published work — "this is not my stuff... read the article and look at the pictures" — and frames the Eden–temple relationship as a symbolic analog rather than a one-to-one historical descent. See [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]] and [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]].
