# Henri Junod

**Era:** 1863–1934
**Role:** Swiss Protestant missionary and ethnographer of southern Africa

## Bio

Henri-Alexandre Junod was a Swiss missionary and pioneering ethnographer who documented
the peoples of Mozambique, Zimbabwe, and the northern Transvaal in the early twentieth
century. In 1908 he recorded the oral tradition and distinctive customs of the **Lemba**
(Balemba) people, whom he described in *The Balemba of the Zoutpansberg (Transvaal)* and
in his larger work *The Life of a South African Tribe*. On the Ward Radio episode his
1908 account is the primary documentary source quoted for the Lemba's migration story and
their Semitic-looking practices. (His surname is auto-rendered "Juno" / "Junod" in the
transcript.)

## On the Lemba's distinctiveness

Junod recorded that the Lemba stood apart from their neighbors: they were skilled
blacksmiths and metallurgists, kept domesticated fowl no one else had, ate no meat unless
ritually slaughtered, drained blood from meat, refused pork, practiced male circumcision
(called *lhoma*) and circumcised willing neighbors, never intermarried, had no monarchs,
and venerated the spirits of their forefathers. He transcribed their oral tradition of
crossing the sea in a great boat that broke in two, with half the people lost. See
[[The Lemba Tribe's Semitic DNA and Oral Migration Tradition Parallel Lehi]].[^1]
