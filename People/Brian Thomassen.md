# Brian Thomassen

**Era:** Contemporary (2020s)
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint (LDS)
**Role:** Apologetics commentator / presenter

## Bio

Brian Thomassen is an independent Latter-day Saint commentator who appears on the [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Stick of Joseph]] channel. He describes [[Truman Madsen]] as his "old boss" and draws his signature presentation from a lineage of BYU scholarship: a 1974 paper by Madsen titled (in effect) "Are Christians Mormon?", expanded in 2006 by Madsen's and Thomassen's teacher [[David Paulsen]]. Thomassen tweaks Paulsen's work into the baseline of his own talk.[^1]

## Topics

### Convergence — Mainstream Christianity Moving Toward LDS Positions
Thomassen's central argument inverts the "Are Mormons Christian?" debate: he contends that over roughly two centuries broader Christianity has been drifting *toward* historically Latter-day Saint positions. He structures the case as seven doctrines, giving for each Joseph Smith's position, the 19th-century divergence against it, and a modern convergence back toward it — charismata/open canon, divine passibility, a social and embodied Godhead, deification, the divine feminine, a self-surpassing God in time, and salvation for the unevangelized.[^2][^3] See [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]], [[Seven Restoration Doctrines the Broader Church Is Drifting Toward]].

His conclusion is apologetically confident: "the trend lines favor us. We are in a strong theoretical position and do not have to feel apologetic on any level," because while all seven doctrines are now live topics in contemporary Christianity, "only with Joseph Smith are all of these brought together in one modeling."[^4][^5] See [[The Deification Publishing Boom Since 1950]], [[Passibility - Mainstream Theology Now Affirms a God Who Suffers]], [[Open Theism and a Self-Surpassing God]].

### Reading Romans as Greco-Roman Rhetoric
In a later episode (hosted by [[Dave Butler]]) Thomassen argues the book of Romans is misread through an anachronistic [[Augustine|Augustinian]] lens and must be recovered as Greco-Roman rhetoric for a Hellenized Roman audience: grace is [[Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon|reciprocal *charis*]], Paul's "I" is a [[Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography|fictive persona (*prosōpopoeia*)]], the Romans 7 trope is a *Medea*/*akrasia* allusion, and the letter's thesis is [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law|self-mastery (*enkrateia*) through Christ, not the law]]. See [[The Stick of Joseph - How the Book of Romans Changes the Way We See the Gospel]].[^6]
