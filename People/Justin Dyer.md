# Justin Dyer

Latter-day Saint social scientist and professor of religious education at Brigham Young University. He holds a PhD in human and community development from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, completed a postdoctoral fellowship at Auburn University, teaches religion-and-family and graduate statistics, is a fellow with the Wheatley Institute at BYU, and is the current editor of *BYU Studies*. He has published over 80 peer-reviewed articles cited more than 3,000 times, and has served as an LDS bishop — an experience he draws on for the pastoral application of his research.[^1]

## Research on Retention and Well-being

Dyer is the lead voice on the paper *"Latter-day Saint Religiousness, Well-being, and Retention in the United States,"* coauthored with Jenna Ericson, Sam Hardy, Barbara Morgan Gardner, and David C. Dollahite, which synthesizes multiple large datasets (Pew, Ryan Burge's 100,000-respondent Spiritual Seismology study, and longitudinal panels) to test the claim that the LDS Church is uniquely losing members. He argues retention has declined but tracks national trends, and that the Church retains *active* members at the top of the field. See [[LDS Retention Is Higher Than Most Other Christian Traditions]] and [[Decline in Religion Tracks the Broader Collapse of Social Connectedness]].

## On Methodology — Forest and Trees

Dyer repeatedly cautions that "big data" describes the macro landscape and cannot characterize any individual, warning against both the fallacy of generalizing one person's experience and the fallacy of imposing macro trends on a specific person. See [[Big Data Cannot Diagnose the Individual]].

## On Predictors of Leaving

His longitudinal work identifies daily connection with God as the leading retention factor and political extremity as a growing predictor of disaffiliation. See [[Daily Connection With God Is the Top Predictor of Retention]] and [[Political Identity Now Supersedes Religious Identity]].

## On the Profile of Disaffiliates

Using the Spiritual Seismology data, Dyer's team statistically clustered former Latter-day Saints into four groups, overturning the loud-online-atheist stereotype. See [[The Four Groups of LDS Disaffiliates]] and [[Faith-Rejectors and Adverse Childhood Religious Experience]].

## On Mental Health

He dismantles the "Utah suicide implicates the Church" narrative via regional comparison. See [[Utah Suicide Rate Implicates the Church]] and [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]].
