# Josh Gailey

Josh Gailey is an ordained evangelist in the **Quorum of Seventy** of the Church of Jesus Christ (headquartered in Monongahela, Pennsylvania — a Restoration branch distinct from the LDS Church), and a guest on the [[Informed Saints]] podcast. He describes his church role as missionary rather than patriarchal.[^1]

## Witnessing Miracles
Gailey is the author of **Witnessing Miracles: Historical Evidence for the Resurrection and the Book of Mormon**, which ports the resurrection apologetics method of [[Gary Habermas]] (the "minimal facts" argument) onto the coming forth of the gold plates. His "lightbulb moment" came over a decade earlier watching a Habermas presentation; finding the argument's application to the plates was "open space" in the literature, he developed a presentation and then a book.[^2] He also contributed an article version to the FAIR volume *Defending the Book of Mormon*.[^3] See [[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]] and [[The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon]].

## Distinctive Contributions
He surfaces several under-used data points — [[Josiah Stowell]]'s court testimony to glimpsing the plates, [[Isaac Hale]] hefting the box, and especially [[Lorenzo Saunders]]' hostile "no fresh dirt" admission — and offers an undesigned-coincidence argument that [[David Whitmer]] and [[Edward Stevenson]] independently reported what became of the stone box. See [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]] and [[Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box]].

## On Fake Book of Mormon Artifacts (Ward Radio)
Appearing as a panelist on [[Ward Radio - Fake Book of Mormon Artifacts]] (where the transcript spells his name "Galey"), Gailey leads the debunking of forged artifacts — the [[The Michigan Relics Are 100 Percent Forgeries|Michigan Relics]], the [[The Bat Creek Stone Is Probably a Fake and Unrelated to the Book of Mormon|Bat Creek Stone]], and the [[The Padilla Plates and Related Stones Were Forged to Target Latter-day Saints|Padilla plates]] — arguing that ruling out fakes is necessary to defend the genuine case ([[Defending Faith Requires Ruling Out the Fakes]]).[^4]
