# Christoph Markschies

**Full name:** Christoph Markschies
**Role:** Professor of Ancient Christianity / Church History
**Era:** Contemporary (active 1990s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic (Humboldt University of Berlin, Germany)

## Bio

Christoph Markschies is a leading German historian of early Christianity, teaching at Humboldt University of Berlin. He served as president of the university from 2006–2010. His research spans early Christian theology, Gnosticism, the formation of the biblical canon, and the intersection of classical philosophy with Christian thought.

Dan McClellan describes Markschies' book *God's Body: Jewish, Christian, and Pagan Images of God* as "a very philosophical, very erudite discussion" of divine embodiment across religious traditions. [^1] The book surveys how Jews, Christians, and pagans in antiquity conceived of God's body — from full corporeality to allegorical interpretation to total incorporeality.

## Divine Corporeality

Markschies' work documents the historical range of views on God's body: from anthropomorphic and corporeal (early Hebrew Bible, some pagan traditions) through allegorizing reinterpretation (Philo, some church fathers) to full philosophical incorporeality (later orthodoxy under Platonic influence). His survey is significant because it demonstrates that divine embodiment was a live, debated question in antiquity — not a settled "heresy" from the beginning.
