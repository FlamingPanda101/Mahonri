# Frances Young

**Role:** Patristics scholar and theologian
**Era:** 20th–21st century (active 1970s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic (British Methodist)

## Bio

Frances M. Young is a distinguished British scholar of early Christianity and patristics. [[Blake Ostler]] relies on her article "'Creatio Ex Nihilo': A Context for the Emergence of the Christian Doctrine of Creation" (*Scottish Journal of Theology* 44, 1991) as a key authority for the thesis that creation out of nothing was **not** an inheritance from Jewish or apostolic-era tradition but a late-second-century development.[^1] Ostler describes her (with Bruce Waltke) as an example of a "brave traditional Christian" — one who accepts *creatio ex nihilo* as doctrine yet is honest that it cannot be found in the Bible itself.[^2]

## Contributions to the Ex-Nihilo Debate

Young's reasons (quoted by Ostler) for rejecting a Jewish origin of the doctrine:[^3]
1. The sparsity and interpretive difficulty of any references to *ex nihilo* in Jewish and earliest-Christian texts;
2. The contrary evidence of the Wisdom of Solomon, [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]], and the early Christians [[Justin Martyr]], [[Athenagoras of Athens]], [[Hermogenes]], and [[Clement of Alexandria]] — all of whom adopt the Platonic "active God plus passive matter" scheme without any sense of tension;
3. The lack of interest in *ex nihilo* in Jewish tradition until the Middle Ages.

She also argues that even [[Basilides]]'s much-cited language is about the limits of language regarding non-being, not a genuine doctrine of *ex nihilo* — a reading Ostler endorses over [[Gerhard May]]'s.[^4]
