# Dan McClellan

**Who:** Biblical scholar (Ph.D.), co-host of the *Data Over Dogma* podcast with [[Dan Beecher]]. Public educator on biblical studies and the academic study of religion via social media.

**Role:** Presents the academic consensus on biblical texts, often challenging traditional theological readings with linguistic and historical-critical analysis.

## Divine Name Theology / John's Christology
McClellan argues that the Gospel of John does not present Jesus as ontologically identical with the God of Israel. Instead, Jesus is portrayed as the authorized bearer of the divine name — an "exalted human" or "name-bearing angel" who manifests God's presence. This is a form of deity by agency, not by essence. See [[Divine Name Theology]], [[John 8-58 - Before Abraham Was I Am (Divine Name Agency)]].

## Trinity Development
McClellan traces the [[Trinity - Historical Development|Trinity]] as a doctrinal framework that developed between the 2nd and 5th centuries CE, crystallized at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE) under [[Constantine]]'s imperial pressure. He argues the Gospel authors could not have intended Trinitarian meanings because the conceptual framework (consubstantiality, persons vs. being) did not yet exist. See [[Consubstantiality]].

## John 1:1 — Qualitative Theos
McClellan presents the academic consensus that *theos* in John 1:1c is qualitative (no definite article), meaning "the Word was deity/divine" — not "the Word was THE God." See [[John 1-1 - Qualitative Theos (And the Word Was God)]].

## John 10:30 — "I and the Father Are One"
The oneness in John 10:30 is the same unity Jesus prays his followers will achieve in [[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]] — a unity of purpose, not substance. See [[John 10-30 - I and the Father Are One Rebutted]].

## Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor
McClellan argues that Jesus's cursing of the fig tree in [[Mark 11.12-25|Mark 11:12-25]] is a "Markan sandwich" — the fig tree bookends the temple cleansing as a metaphor for the temple's failure to "bear fruit" and its coming destruction (~70 CE). He traces how Matthew compresses the story (losing metaphor) and Luke transforms it into an explicit parable. See [[Fig Tree Cursing as Metaphor for Temple]], [[Markan Sandwich Literary Device]].

## Deutero-Isaiah
McClellan presents the academic consensus that Isaiah 40–55 was composed ~520 BCE by a different author than the 8th-century prophet Isaiah. Evidence includes Cyrus being named by name, Aramaic loanwords from the exilic period, the disappearance of Isaiah's name after chapter 39, and the shift from future prophecy to past-tense description. See [[Deutero-Isaiah Theory]], [[Prophecy and Specificity - Cyrus Named by Name]].

## Methodological Naturalism
McClellan argues that critical scholarship must methodologically reject real prophecy — not from anti-supernatural bias, but because accepting it for one text while applying critical methods to all others creates an inconsistent double standard. He distinguishes this scholarly method from personal faith, which he says is entirely compatible with belief in prophecy. See [[Methodological Naturalism in Biblical Scholarship]].

## Apologetics vs. Critical Scholarship
McClellan contrasts the two approaches: apologists begin with conclusions and work backward to show they are "not impossible"; critical scholars follow data where it leads. He coins the term "rhetorical prophylaxis" for the common apologetic tactic of dismissing scholarship as "trying to destroy the Bible." See [[Apologetics vs Critical Scholarship]].

## Bible as Project of Peoplehood (Jacob Wright Interview)
McClellan hosted [[Jacob Wright]] to discuss Wright's Cambridge UP book *Why the Bible Began*. McClellan's contributions include reinforcing the point that "religion" as a discrete sociocultural dimension didn't exist in the ancient world (the cognitive science of religion places its development between Renaissance and Enlightenment), discussing the Mesha Stele parallel, and drawing out Wright's thesis that the Bible is a pedagogical project rather than scripture for a religion. See [[Bible as Project of Peoplehood]], [[Military Defeat as Catalyst for Bible Formation]], [[Religion as Anachronistic Category]], [[Bible as Pedagogical Curriculum]].
