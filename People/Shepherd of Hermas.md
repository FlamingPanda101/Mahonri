# Shepherd of Hermas

The **Shepherd of Hermas** is an early second-century Christian apocalypse of unknown authorship (sometimes identified with the Hermas of Romans 16:14). The "shepherd" of the title is the angelic guide who instructs Hermas. The book contains five visions, twelve commandments, and ten similitudes; it was "very popular and considered authoritative by many until the 4th century."

## Postmortem Preaching and Baptism of the Righteous Dead
In the Ninth Similitude (elaborating the Third Vision's building of the Church as a tower), Hermas sees two sets of stones rise *through water* to be fitted into the tower. His guide explains the first set were those who "died without baptism," who must rise through water "to be made alive. In no other way could they enter the reign of God." The second set, forty in number, were "the apostles and teachers who proclaimed the name of the Son of God, who having fallen asleep … even proclaimed to those who had previously fallen asleep and gave them the seal of the proclamation [baptism]." [[Clement of Alexandria]] cited this to support postmortem preaching to the dead. See [[The Descent of Christ to the Underworld Was Universally Attested]] [^1].
