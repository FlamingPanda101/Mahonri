# Kenneth Kitchen

**Full name:** Kenneth Anderson Kitchen
**Role:** Egyptologist / Old Testament historian
**Era:** Contemporary (1932–; Personal and Honorary Research Fellow, University of Liverpool)
**Tradition:** Academic; conservative/maximalist on Old Testament historicity

## Bio
K.A. Kitchen is one of the most cited academic skeptics of the Documentary Hypothesis, approaching the Hebrew Bible as an Egyptologist and ancient-Near-East historian rather than a literary source critic. [[Mike Day]] presents Kitchen as a serious, fair-minded opponent whose objection deserves to be taken on its merits — "a sound argument against it."[^1]

## Contribution to the lecture
Day quotes Kitchen's central challenge: **there is no external manuscript evidence for J, E, D, or P as independent documents.** In Day's paraphrase/quotation: "There's no objective independent evidence for any of these four compositions anywhere outside the pages of our existing Bibles. If any excavation produces a substantial chunk of a scroll that indubitably contained a copy of J or E, I would welcome it with open arms."[^1] Day glosses the force of it: "the critics are saying, okay, awesome, you have this hypothesis — show me a scroll where it's the Book of J." He connects this to the existence of Harold Bloom's novelization *The Book of J* (which extracts the J material) precisely to underscore that the reconstructed source has no attestation "at Qumran or anything."[^2]

This is the steelman of the anti-DH position in the talk; Day grants its legitimacy while still finding the internal evidence persuasive. See [[Source Criticism Is Not Reducible to the Documentary Hypothesis]] for the related point that critiquing the DH is not the same as overturning all source criticism.

## Key Works
- *Ancient Orient and Old Testament* (Tyndale Press, 1966)
- *On the Reliability of the Old Testament* (Eerdmans, 2003)
