# Helmut Hübner

**Full name:** Helmuth Hübener (1925–1943)
**Role:** Latter-day Saint anti-Nazi resistance member; martyr
**Era:** Nazi Germany (executed 1943)
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS)

## Bio

Helmuth Hübener was a Latter-day Saint teenager in Hamburg who, with two or three friends (some from his branch), illegally listened to BBC broadcasts, transcribed them, and distributed anti-Hitler leaflets beginning around 1942. Arrested in February 1943, he was tried before the Nazi *Volksgerichtshof* (People's Court) under judge Roland Freisler — a kangaroo court — and executed by guillotine at Plötzensee prison in Berlin in late 1943. [[Stephen Smoot]] notes he is, by Hübener's reckoning, still recognized as the youngest resistance member executed by the Nazis, and is honored within German society at large.[^1]

He died a faithful Saint: in final letters to family and branch members he lamented having to break the Word of Wisdom (offered wine with his last meal) and affirmed he would stand before God to be judged. His surviving collaborators — including Karl-Heinz Schnibbe — received prison sentences, were freed when Germany lost the war, and several emigrated to the United States as lifelong Latter-day Saints.[^2]

## A Controversial Posthumous Excommunication

Hübener's pro-Nazi branch president, [[Arthur Zander]], together with a local mission leader, signed a warrant posthumously excommunicating him — a fact later weaponized as proof the Church "excommunicated an anti-Nazi dissident." Smoot reconstructs the fuller context: under Gestapo interrogation of district president [[Otto Berndt]] (because Hübener had used a church-owned typewriter), excommunication functioned as a coerced signal that the boy acted alone, shielding other Hamburg Saints. The First Presidency, on learning the facts after the war, reversed the excommunication in 1948 and restored his blessings. See [[Helmut Hubner's Excommunication Was a Local Survival Measure Later Reversed]].[^3]
