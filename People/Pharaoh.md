# Pharaoh

**Role:** King of Egypt; antagonist of the Exodus narrative
**Era:** Narrative setting in the second millennium BCE (story composed centuries later; see [[Exodus as Post Hoc Memorial Narrative]])
**Tradition:** Hebrew Bible ([[Exodus]])

## Bio
The unnamed Pharaoh of [[Exodus]] is the Egyptian king who refuses to release the enslaved Israelites despite a series of plagues, until the Red Sea drowning of his army. In the narrative he functions less as a developed character than as the foil against whom YHWH demonstrates supremacy. The text never gives his personal name, and McClellan argues the Exodus story (and thus this figure) is a post-hoc literary memorial rather than a historical record.

## The Hardening of Pharaoh's Heart
The defining feature of Pharaoh in the narrative is the repeated **hardening of his heart**. The text attributes this both to God and, at points, to Pharaoh himself. [[Dan McClellan]] argues the apologetic "wax and clay" analogy wrongly makes Pharaoh the sole cause in order to protect divine non-interference, whereas Exodus explicitly has God hardening Pharaoh's heart to display divine power.
