# John Anderson (media)

**Era:** Contemporary (2010s–2020s); Australian politician 1989–2007
**Tradition:** Conservative / Christian
**Role:** Podcast host (*John Anderson Media*), former Deputy Prime Minister of Australia

## Bio

John Anderson is an Australian former politician — Deputy Prime Minister and leader of the National Party — who in retirement built a long-form interview program, *John Anderson Media*, covering social issues, energy, national security, and family policy. In this episode he interviews [[Katy Faust]] for the third time. He brings a personal stake to the family conversation: his mother died of undiagnosed bowel cancer when he was three, and he was raised, unremarried, by his father — an experience he uses to corroborate Faust's distinction between loss-to-tragedy and loss-to-intentionality, and to testify to the "black hole" left by an absent mother and the importance of paternal modeling.[^1]

## Positions in this dialogue

As interviewer and conversation partner, Anderson:

- **Supplies the lived test case** for [[Loss by Intentionality Wounds Differently Than Loss by Tragedy]] — he longed for his deceased mother yet was mourned-with by a whole community who remembered her fondly, unlike a child isolated in grief after a divorce.
- **Presses the role-modeling gap**: a boy with no mother in the home cannot watch how a man should "respect and honor" a woman, and boys learn relationship by copying models. See [[You Become What You Behold - Modeling as the Engine of Family Transmission]].
- **Voices the "old dude harping on" objection** himself (that children are better off if unhappy parents separate), letting Faust answer the high-conflict-marriage data.
- **Frames the anti-egoism rejoinder**: others made way and sacrificed for you when you were born and will again at life's other end, so a self-centered identity "flies in the face of everything we know about a workable identity." See [[Child Rights Confront Sexual Autonomy as the Highest Value]].

He invokes [[Jordan Peterson]] repeatedly as the model "uncle figure" calling young men to responsibility (contrasted with Andrew Tate).
