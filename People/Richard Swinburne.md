# Richard Swinburne

**Role:** Analytic philosopher of religion (Oxford)
**Era:** Contemporary (b. 1934)
**Tradition:** Christian (converted to Eastern Orthodoxy)

## Bio

Richard Swinburne is a prominent analytic philosopher of religion, long associated with Oxford, known for natural-theology arguments for God's existence and for a broadly social account of the Trinity. [[Blake Ostler]] cites him as a "social trinitarian" whose argument most Christians regard as "thoroughly tritheistic," yet whom Ostler treats as making "a good argument." [^1]

## The Argument That Love Requires Another

Ostler invokes Swinburne's claim that **God must be loving, but love requires another to love** — so, as a matter of his essence, the Father generates the Son in order to be God. Ostler uses this to press the Thomistic "God is love" view: if God exists wholly alone with no other being, what does it mean to say he loves? See:
- [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]]

## Aseity and Ontological Subordination

Ostler also reports Swinburne's position that only the Father has *aseity* (existence "in and of himself" without dependence), making the Son in some sense derivative — which, Ostler argues, yields a genuine *ontological* distinction between a self-sufficient and a dependent being, who therefore cannot be "the same being."
