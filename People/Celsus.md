# Celsus

**Role:** Pagan philosopher; early critic of Christianity
**Era:** 2nd century CE (active c. 175 CE)
**Tradition:** Greco-Roman (Middle Platonic)

## Bio

Celsus was a 2nd-century pagan philosopher who wrote *The True Discourse* (*Alēthēs Logos*, c. 175 CE) — the first comprehensive philosophical critique of Christianity. The work itself is lost, but large portions are preserved in [[Origen]]'s rebuttal *Contra Celsum* (c. 248 CE). Celsus was well-read in both the Hebrew Bible and Christian writings and mounted sophisticated criticisms of Christian theology, including its doctrines of creation, incarnation, and resurrection.

## Role in the Development of Creation Ex Nihilo

Celsus's critique of the resurrection was one of the pressures that drove the development of creation ex nihilo. Celsus, operating from the standard Greco-Roman philosophical consensus, mocked the Christian belief in bodily resurrection as impossible — how could a body, scattered and decayed, be reconstituted?

This challenge is part of the context in which Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus formulated creation ex nihilo: if God created matter from nothing in the first place, then reassembling a decomposed body at the resurrection is a lesser act — the same God who creates can certainly recreate. Creation ex nihilo thus provided theological warrant for the resurrection against pagan philosophical objections.

As Dan McClellan notes:

> "They're arguing about the resurrection. They're arguing about the morality of unformed pre-existent matter. And they come up with creation out of nothing. They are the originators of this doctrine."[^1]
