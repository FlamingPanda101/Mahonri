# Blaise Pascal

**Full Name:** Blaise Pascal
**Era:** 1623–1662 (17th-century France)
**Tradition:** Roman Catholic (Jansenist-influenced)
**Role:** Mathematician, physicist, inventor, philosopher, Catholic theologian

## Bio

Blaise Pascal was a French mathematician, physicist, and Catholic theologian who made foundational contributions to the development of probability theory. In his posthumously published *Pensées*, he applied decision-theoretic reasoning to the question of belief in God, producing the argument now known as **Pascal's Wager**. Rather than attempting to prove God's existence, Pascal argued that, given the potential payoffs and the odds, belief in God is the rational bet — a strategy he "lifted from the gambling hall."[^1]

## Positions in this dialogue

In the Thoughtful Faith video, Pascal is the historical anchor for the **pragmatic / decision-theoretic case for faith**:

- **The Wager as a decision problem, not a proof.** Pascal does not argue that God's existence is more likely than not; he argues that belief is "a really good deal even if the chance that God actually exists is low." See [[Pascals Wager Is a Decision Under Uncertainty Not a Proof]].
- **The stronger second version.** The video, following [[Susanna Rinard]], identifies a version of the wager (argued to be closer to Pascal's own intent) in which you are better off as a believer *whether or not* God exists. See [[The Dominance Version of Pascals Wager]].
- **The two reasonable kinds of people.** Pascal: "there are only two kinds of reasonable people in the world: those who serve God with all their heart because they know him, and those who seek God with all their heart because they do not."[^2]
