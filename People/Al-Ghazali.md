# Al-Ghazali

**Full name:** Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali (c. 1058–1111)
**Role:** Muslim theologian, philosopher, and jurist
**Era:** Medieval Islamic world (11th–12th century)
**Tradition:** Sunni Islam (Ash'ari theology; Sufism)

## Bio
Al-Ghazali was one of the most influential theologians and philosophers of the medieval Islamic world. He is best known for *The Incoherence of the Philosophers* (*Tahafut al-Falasifa*), a critique of the Aristotelian philosophers (notably Avicenna) who, following [[Aristotle]], held the universe to be eternal in the past. Against this, al-Ghazali developed the argument that gives the **Kalam cosmological argument** its name (*kalam* = Islamic scholastic theology): a beginningless universe would entail an *actually infinite* number of past events, which is impossible — so the universe must have a beginning, and therefore a cause.

## The Kalam Argument for a Beginning of the Universe
In [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]'s animated explainer, al-Ghazali is the central historical figure. He "pointed out that if the universe did not have a beginning, then the number of past events in the history of the universe is infinite," and that "the existence of an actually infinite number of past events leads to absurdity. It's metaphysically impossible."[^1] Craig presents two distinct al-Ghazali arguments:

1. **The impossibility of an actual infinite**, illustrated by [[David Hilbert]]'s infinite hotel — see [[Hilbert's Hotel - Why an Actual Infinite Cannot Exist]].
2. **The impossibility of forming an actual infinite by successive addition**, illustrated by the planetary orbits and the falling dominoes — see [[An Actual Infinite Cannot Be Formed by Successive Addition]].

Together these support the Kalam's second premise (*the universe began to exist*) and, with premise (1), the conclusion that the universe has a transcendent cause (see [[The Cause of the Universe Must Be Personal, Timeless, and Spaceless]]).
