# Thecla

Heroine of the second-century apocryphal *Acts of Paul and Thecla*. Whether or not historical, her hugely popular cult is a window onto early Christian ideas about virginity, divine protection, and female religious authority.

## The virgin who claims male authority
On hearing Paul preach "the blessedness of virginity," Thecla pledges perpetual chastity and rejects her betrothed. Twice thrown into the arena, she is repeatedly saved by miraculous divine protection (a storm extinguishes the flames; a lioness defends her; a cloud of fire covers her nakedness). She **baptizes herself** in a pool of seals — a baptism Paul later accepts — then dresses as a man and travels as a missionary, healing, exorcising, and interceding for the dead. [[Ariel Bybee Laughton]]: "Thecla's virginity grants her divine favor and the ability to assume the 'masculine' virtues of courage and self-control." See [[Sexual Renunciation Gave Christian Women Spiritual Authority]]. [^1]

## Tertullian's rejection
[[Tertullian]] denounced the *Acts of Paul and Thecla* as fiction and forgery, reasoning Paul would not "give a female the power to teach and baptize," and invoked 1 Corinthians 14:34–35. The rise of a controlled *order of virgins* in the church was in part a response to charismatic women like Thecla. See [[Tertullian Reframed Church Office as Roman Legal Privilege]]. [^2]
