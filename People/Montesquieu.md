# Montesquieu

**Era:** 1689–1755 (18th century)
**Tradition:** French Enlightenment
**Role:** Philosopher, jurist, student of history

## Bio

French jurist and Enlightenment thinker, writing during the absolutist reigns of Louis XIV and Louis XV. A judge and a student of Roman history (especially the late Republic and how it "went off-kilter"), his fundamental work is *The Spirit of the Laws*.[^1]

## Role in the Argument

The **third link** in [[Brian Thomassen]]'s Enlightenment chain ([[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]] → [[John Locke|Locke]] → Montesquieu → the founders). Montesquieu **invents the separation of powers** — executive, legislative, and judicial — as the cure for the human tendency to accumulate power: "It is necessary from the very nature of things that power should be a check to power," because "great men who are moderate are rare, as it is always easier to follow one's strength than to arrest it." Like Locke, he holds that law should reflect the specific community subject to it (hence laws are relative). The founders adopt his checks-and-balances scheme directly. See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]].[^2][^3]
