# Xenophon

**Full name:** Xenophon of Athens (c. 430 – c. 354 BCE)
**Role:** Greek historian, soldier (general), and Socratic writer
**Era:** Classical Greece (5th–4th century BCE)

## Bio

Xenophon was an Athenian of the upper class, a soldier and general, and — like [[Plato]] — a student of [[Socrates]]. He was a prolific author (the *Anabasis*, *Hellenica*, *Memorabilia*, *Cyropaedia*, and more) and was read continuously as a core classical author "as much as Plato or Aristotle all through the classical period all the way up through the 19th century" — the *Anabasis* being a standard first Greek text. [[Brian Thomassen]] stresses this stature to make the point that Xenophon is "a core central thinker within classical thinking," not a fringe figure.[^1]

## Significance for the Creation Debate

Thomassen invokes Xenophon to defuse the strongest *ex nihilo* proof-text, [[2 Maccabees 7.28|2 Maccabees 7:28]]. Xenophon wrote — using the *exact same phrasing* as 2 Maccabees, and *predating* it (5th c. BC) — that parents "bring forth their children out of nothing." No Greek reader took this as a metaphysical claim that children appear from absolute nothingness; it simply means "a thing is now here that wasn't there before." This shows the "out of nothing" idiom "is not an argument for a creation from nothing model" and "was not understood as making some type of ontological claim about reality." The same idiom in 2 Maccabees therefore carries no *ex nihilo* weight.
