# Vibia Perpetua

**Vibia Perpetua** was a young Christian mother martyred in the arena at Carthage (North Africa) in AD 203, alongside her companions in honor of Geta, son of Emperor Septimius Severus. *The Passion of Perpetua and Felicity* preserves her prison experiences in a diary-like first-person account — not a theological treatise but a record of devotion and visions. Her allusions to scripture and classical texts indicate she was educated.

## Visionary Redemption of Her Dead Brother Dinocrates
Perpetua's narrative includes a series of visions, the most striking being her intercession for her dead brother **Dinocrates**, who had died at age seven of a facial skin disease. She sees him in a dark place, hot and thirsty, his wound still festering, unable to reach a high pool of water. "I knew at once that I was worthy and that I ought to intercede on his behalf. And I began to pray for a long time for him and to cry out to God." After praying day and night, she has a second vision: Dinocrates is now "clean, well dressed, and looked refreshed," the wound healed to a scar, able to drink and play. She concludes her prayers won him "a kind of salvific deliverance." A vivid early case of **vicarious work for the dead** through prayer.
