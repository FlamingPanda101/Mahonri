# John Emmert

**Role:** Smithsonian field agent (1880s); discoverer of the Bat Creek Stone
**Era:** 19th century
**Tradition:** Secular

## Bio

John W. Emmert was a field agent for the Smithsonian's Bureau of Ethnology who, during an 1889 mound excavation in Loudon County, Tennessee, reported recovering the **Bat Creek Stone** — an inscribed stone later argued to bear Paleo-Hebrew letters.

## Relevance to the vault

Emmert is central to [[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]]'s case against the Bat Creek Stone in [[Ward Radio - Fake Book of Mormon Artifacts]]. While the stone was found in a controlled Smithsonian excavation (a point its defenders stress), Emmert was the *only* person in the mound at the find, was ambitious to rise at the Smithsonian, and was later **associated with forging characters on stones at other sites** — making a "planted forgery" explanation more probable. Galey adds that the inscription closely matches a Masonic encyclopedia plate available at the time, and that the residue's carbon date is irrelevant (it dates the mound material, not the carving). His overall verdict: even if genuine Hebrew, the Hellenistic-period Paleo-Hebrew script post-dates Lehi, so it is **not** a Book of Mormon artifact. See [[The Bat Creek Stone Is Probably a Fake and Unrelated to the Book of Mormon]].
