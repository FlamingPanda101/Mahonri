# David Wright

**Full name:** David P. Wright
**Role:** Professor of Bible and the Ancient Near East
**Era:** Contemporary (active 1980s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic (Brandeis University); raised Latter-day Saint

## Bio
David P. Wright is a Hebrew Bible scholar specializing in biblical and Near Eastern law and ritual. [[Mike Day]] introduces him as someone he "thinks was a member of the church" ("I don't know if he still is") — Wright was excommunicated from the LDS Church in 1994 over his historical-critical scholarship — and credits Wright's signature thesis on the Covenant Code.[^1]

## Contribution to the lecture
In Day's discussion of the [[Three Divergent Slave Laws Point to Separate Pentateuchal Sources|divergent slave laws]], Wright supplies the claim that the **Covenant Code** (the legal core of Exodus 21–23, which DH scholars treat as the *earliest* legal stratum) is itself **dependent on still-older Near Eastern sources** — his book argues it directly used and revised the Laws of Hammurabi. Day: "David Wright... wrote a whole text on how the Covenant Code is kind of taking stuff from even older sources."[^1] This reinforces the lecture's broader point that the Pentateuch's legal material is layered and derivative rather than a single Mosaic composition.

## On the Age of Patristic Baptisms (cited by Trent Horn)
Separately, Wright's scholarship on early Christian baptism (the book [[Gavin Ortlund]] cited) is invoked by [[Trent Horn]]: Wright found that, apart from a questionable Augustine case, he could identify no children "of seven or older who were baptized answering the questions for themselves"; the non-infant baptisms among the fathers "are all of adult years," the youngest being [[John Chrysostom]] at ~18 — so the 4th-century fathers were not child-credobaptists. See [[Credobaptists Cannot Agree on the Age of Baptism]].[^2]

## Key Works
- *Inventing God's Law: How the Covenant Code of the Bible Used and Revised the Laws of Hammurabi* (Oxford University Press, 2009)
- *Ritual in Narrative* (Eisenbrauns, 2001)
