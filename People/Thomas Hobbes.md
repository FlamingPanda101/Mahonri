# Thomas Hobbes

**Era:** 1588–1679 (17th century)
**Tradition:** English Enlightenment / early-modern political philosophy
**Role:** Philosopher; originator of social-contract theory

## Bio

English philosopher writing against the backdrop of the English Civil War (1640s, "where Charles is going to lose his head"), the Eighty Years' War, and the Thirty Years' War (in which an estimated 25% of central Europe died). His masterwork is *Leviathan*.[^1]

## Role in the Argument

In [[Brian Thomassen]]'s genealogy of the Constitution, Hobbes is the **first link** in the Enlightenment chain that runs Hobbes → [[John Locke|Locke]] → [[Montesquieu]] → the founders. Hobbes **invents the social contract** ("It does not exist before him"): from a state of nature that is a "war of all against all" — life "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — rational self-preservation drives men to surrender self-government to a **sovereign** with the power (the "big stick") to enforce covenants, since "covenants without the sword are but words." The sovereign becomes the source of law and morality. It is not pure absolutism: obligation "last[s] as long and no longer than the power… by which he is able to protect them." See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]] and [[The Constitution Is an Enlightenment Document]].[^2][^3]
