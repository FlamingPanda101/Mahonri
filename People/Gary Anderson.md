# Gary Anderson

**Role:** Theologian and scholar of early Christianity
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2010s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Academic Christian theology

## Bio

Gary Anderson is a scholar of early Christianity and biblical theology. He co-edited with [[Markus Bockmuehl]] the volume *Creation Ex Nihilo: Origins, Development, Contemporary Challenges* (University of Notre Dame Press, 2018), a major recent contribution to the study of the doctrine.

## The Softest Possible Defense of 2 Maccabees 7:28

Anderson's treatment of 2 Maccabees 7:28 in the volume is notable because he approaches the text sympathetically — he WANTS to find creation ex nihilo there — but still cannot claim the text clearly teaches it:

> "Let me begin with the Bible. The two most commented upon texts are Genesis 1:1 and 2 Maccabees 7:28. For many modern scholars, 2 Maccabees appears to be the better candidate of the two, for it seems to contain an explicit denial of the pre-existence of matter... But as scholars have shown, the assertion that God did not make the world out of things that existed could have merely implied that he fashioned the world from unformed matter. For we have contemporary Greek evidence for the use of an almost identical idiom to describe the engendering of children by their parents."[^1]

Anderson's ultimate conclusion is the weakest possible endorsement:

> "This does not mean that the author of 2 Maccabees understood the term this way. At the same time, that possibility cannot be ruled out. As a result, this text fails as a decisive proof text for the doctrine. The most we can say is that 2 Maccabees is patient of the doctrine of creatio ex nihilo."[^1]

McClellan's gloss: "That's somebody who's coming to defend creation ex nihilo and taking as soft an approach as possible, but still saying it's not clear in this text."[^1] The phrase "patient of" is academic language meaning the text does not CONTRADICT the doctrine — not that it teaches it. It could be read consistently with creation ex nihilo, but it does not require or assert it.
