# Aaron

**Role:** Brother of [[Moses]]; first high priest of Israel
**Era:** Exodus / wilderness period (biblical narrative)
**Tradition:** Hebrew Bible / Pentateuch

## Bio
Aaron is the elder brother of [[Moses]] and, in the Pentateuchal narrative, Israel's first high priest and the head of the priestly line ("the sons of Aaron," the **Aaronids**). He is the figure around whom the Priestly source organizes legitimate temple service, yet he is also the antagonist of the [[Exodus 32|golden-calf episode]] — a tension [[Mike Day]] uses to illustrate competing sources and competing priesthoods in the text.

## Role in the lecture
Day uses Aaron as the hinge of a **source-level power struggle over who controls the sanctuary**:
1. **P elevates the Aaronids.** In the Priestly material, only the sons of Aaron may handle the sacred things; a non-Aaronid who touches them is struck dead by Yahweh.[^1]
2. **Exodus 32 indicts Aaron.** In the golden-calf narrative ([[Exodus 32]]), Aaron is the villain who builds the calf and then disclaims responsibility ("they threw their gold in the fire and the calf jumped out"). The **Levites** then take up the sword, are "ordained" by Yahweh, and kill the calf-worshippers — a story that elevates the Levites over the Aaronid line.[^2]
3. **A theological conflict in the text.** Day reads this as two factions writing past each other — P insisting "the sons of Aaron are in charge," and the Deuteronomist tradition insisting "the Levites are the good guys." He then notes the Latter-day Saint resonance: the restored church calls its lesser priesthood the **Aaronic (Levitical) priesthood**, and ties John the Baptist's conferral of it ("the sons of Aaron... of Moses") to this ancient material.[^3]
