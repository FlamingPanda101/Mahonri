# Athenagoras of Athens

**Role:** Late-2nd-century Christian apologist (the "Christian philosopher of Athens")
**Era:** 2nd century CE (writing c. 170–177)
**Tradition:** Early Christianity

## Bio

Athenagoras was a Greek Christian apologist who addressed his *Legatio pro Christianis* (*Embassy/Plea for the Christians*, c. 177) to the emperors Marcus Aurelius and Commodus, defending Christians against the charge of atheism. [[Blake Ostler]] cites him as one of at least four late-second-century Christian philosophers who still held that **creation out of matter** was the established Christian doctrine — alongside [[Justin Martyr]], [[Hermogenes]], and [[Clement of Alexandria]].[^1]

## Position on Creation

Athenagoras taught that the Logos "came forth to be the idea and energizing power of all material things, which lay like a nature without attributes, and an inactive earth" — i.e., God gives form to a pre-existing, attribute-less substrate.[^2] He uses the explicit **potter-and-clay** analogy: "as is the potter and the clay (matter being the clay, and the artist the potter), so is God, the Framer of the world, and matter."[^3] Ostler argues this is flatly inconsistent with *creatio ex nihilo*, and that Copan and Craig "seriously misrepresent" Athenagoras when they read a two-stage creation from nothing into him: Athenagoras distinguishes Creator from creature, but the distinction is between the *imperishable/eternal* and the *organized/perishable*, not the ontological gulf between self-existent being and absolute nothing.[^4] Athenagoras even claims his view of God as uncreated Framer is the *same* as Plato's — who plainly held creation from a pre-existing substrate.[^5]
