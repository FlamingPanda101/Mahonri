# Parmenides

**Full name:** Parmenides of Elea (fl. early 5th century BCE)
**Role:** Pre-Socratic philosopher
**Era:** Classical Greece (5th century BCE)
**Tradition:** Eleatic school

## Bio

Parmenides was the founder of the Eleatic school of philosophy (Elea, southern Italy). His poem *On Nature* survives in fragments and is one of the most influential texts in Western philosophy. He argued that "what is" (Being) is eternal, ungenerated, imperishable, and unchanging, while "what is not" (Non-Being) cannot be thought or spoken of — and certainly cannot be the source of anything.

## The Axiom: *Ex Nihilo Nihil Fit*

Parmenides formulated the principle that would govern virtually all Greco-Roman thought for nearly a millennium:

> "Nothing comes from nothing" (*ex nihilo nihil fit* / οὐδὲν ἐξ οὐδενός)

From Parmenides' fragments (DK 28 B8):
- Being is ungenerated (*agenēton*) and imperishable
- "What is not" cannot be thought, spoken of, or produce anything
- Coming-into-being from absolute non-being is impossible

This axiom was accepted by:
- **[[Plato]]**: The Demiurge orders pre-existent matter; matter is eternal
- **[[Aristotle]]**: Matter is eternal; "non-being" means privation of form, not absolute nothingness
- **Stoics**: God and matter are co-eternal principles
- **Epicureans**: Atoms are eternal; nothing comes from nothing

The axiom was NOT challenged until Tatian, Theophilus of Antioch, and Irenaeus broke with it in the late 2nd century CE to formulate creation ex nihilo. Their assertion that God created matter itself from absolute nothingness was a direct philosophical rejection of Parmenides' principle.
