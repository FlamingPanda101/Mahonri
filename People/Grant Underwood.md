# Grant Underwood

**Role:** Professor (emeritus) of history, Brigham Young University; historian of Mormonism and systematic theologian
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS / Mormon)

## Bio
Grant Underwood is a historian of early Mormonism and, latterly, a systematic theologian. He spent his career at BYU (retiring to emeritus status in 2025), held the **Richard L. Evans Chair of Religious Understanding**, and served as an editor on multiple volumes of the **Joseph Smith Papers**. His *Millenarian World of Early Mormonism* won scholarly awards. He and his wife Sherry have seven children. He has presented on LDS–Orthodox theological parallels at the International Orthodox Theological Association (IOTA).

## Latter-day Saint Theology Among Christian Theologies (Eerdmans, 2024)
Underwood's signature work is a systematic LDS theology published not by an LDS press but by **Eerdmans**, a mainstream (non-dogmatic) Christian academic publisher, and aimed primarily at a non-LDS readership. The book deliberately avoids polemic, scrupulously defines terms, and puts LDS doctrine in dialogue with Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant, and ancient Christian traditions — repeatedly arguing that Latter-day Saints share more with historic Christianity than is commonly assumed, while owning the genuine ontological distinctives (embodiment, divine–human continuity).

## On the Trinity
In [[Informed Saints - Do Mormons Believe in the Trinity]], Underwood argues Latter-day Saints are "trinitarians, just not creedal trinitarians" and coins the irenic label [[Separate Being Trinitarianism]] — built on [[Jeffrey R. Holland]]'s line that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are "one in every conceivable way except physically united." He frames the LDS–creedal difference as primarily one of *ontology* (see [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]]), urges Latter-day Saints to *understand* rather than reflexively dislike trinitarianism, and reads Article of Faith 6 as [[The Same Organization Means Functional Not Structural Continuity with the Primitive Church|functional rather than structural]] continuity with the primitive church. On other faiths he classes the LDS posture as *inclusivist* (see [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]]) and quotes [[John Taylor]] and [[Dallin H. Oaks]] to honor pre-Restoration Christianity.
