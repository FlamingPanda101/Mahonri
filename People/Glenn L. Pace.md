# Glenn L. Pace

Latter-day Saint general authority (a member of the Presiding Bishopric in the late 1980s–early 1990s) whose name is attached to the LDS chapter of the satanic panic. The podcast [[Glenn L Pace Memo - The Satanic Panic and False Memory|Glenn L Pace Memo]] (glennlpacememo.com) takes its name from the controversy surrounding Pace's internal Church memo on satanic ritual abuse allegations.

## Connection to the satanic-panic series
The *Glenn L Pace Memo* podcast investigates how the 1980s satanic ritual abuse panic — built on uncorroborated recovered-memory testimony — reached the Latter-day Saints in Utah, where accusations were leveled at lay clergy and high-ranking officials. See [[The Satanic Panic Reached the LDS Church in Utah]] and [[Satanic Panic Is a Recurrence of Salem-Style Hysteria]] [^1][^2].

> Note: This first installment establishes the general history of the panic; the LDS-specific details of the Pace memo are promised for later installments.

## The 1990 memo itself (Part 3 analysis)
In May 1989 the Strengthening Church Members Committee received a report from LDS Social Services; "pursuant to the committee's request," Pace interviewed one self-reported victim per week for 60 weeks, publishing his 12-page memo in July 1990. He met 60 therapy patients, 45 of whom claimed to have witnessed or participated in human sacrifice, implicating 800+ unnamed people — but he deliberately collected no perpetrators' names, dates, or places. The Ward Radio Part 3 reading argues the memo was therefore not an investigation but a persuasive essay defending recovered-memory therapy. See [[The Pace Memo Was Not an Investigation but a Persuasive Essay]], [[Pace Interviewed Self-Selected Therapy Patients Not a Cross-Section]], and [[The Pace Memo Names No Perpetrators Making Prosecution Impossible]]. [^3][^4]

His own writing betrays its borrowed origins (the "coven of 13" from *Michelle Remembers*, *The Satanic Underground*, and [[Mike Warnke]]'s *The Satan Seller*) and contradicts itself mathematically (60 × 13 ≠ 800 when only 37 victims were from Utah). See [[The Pace Memo Recycled Existing Satanic Panic Tropes]] and [[Pace's Coven Math Refutes His Own 800-Perpetrator Estimate]]. The series frames Pace charitably as "a product of his time" whose lifetime of church service was eclipsed by a "widely misunderstood" memo, and notes his career was in corporate finance, not psychology or law enforcement. [^5][^6]
