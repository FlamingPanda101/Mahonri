# Isaiah

**Era:** 8th century BCE (active under Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, Hezekiah)
**Tradition:** Hebrew prophet of Jerusalem
**Role:** Prophet; founding figure of the "school of Isaiah"

## Bio
Isaiah son of Amoz is the prophet of Jerusalem to whom the book of Isaiah is ascribed. In the cult-functional and Barkerian readings used on The Stick of Joseph, he is the founding figure of a **school of Isaiah** — a circle of prophets (and their descendants) who preserved first-temple, visionary, Melchizedek-priesthood traditions against the Deuteronomist reform, and whose oral and written legacy became Isaiah 1–66 over centuries.

## Topics
### The School of Isaiah and the Old Believers
[[Margaret Barker]] makes Isaiah and his disciples the chief preservers of the "old believers'" religion — vision of God, an incarnate Lord, the mother in heaven, the day of atonement — after the reform purged them. The later *Ascension of Isaiah* describes Isaiah and a band of prophets (Micah, Joel, Habakkuk) leaving the "wicked city" to live in poverty east of the Jordan. See [[Two Streams of Israelite Religion - Vision Versus the Voice Alone]], [[The School of Isaiah Preserved First-Temple Tradition in Exile]].

### Deutero-Isaiah and the Festal Drama
[[John Eaton]] reads "Second Isaiah" (Isaiah 40–55) not as a single later author but as the work of a prophetic school transmitting an old oral tradition rooted in the festal drama, with the Servant Songs portraying the suffering king. See [[Second Isaiah Was an Oral Tradition of a Prophetic School]], [[The Servant Songs Portray the Suffering King of the Festival]].

### The Mother of the Lord
In Barker's reading of the Great Isaiah Scroll, Isaiah 7:11 originally invoked "the mother of the Lord." See [[The Great Isaiah Scroll Reads Mother of the Lord at Isaiah 7-11]].
