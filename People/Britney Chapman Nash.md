# Britney Chapman Nash

Latter-day Saint historian specializing in LDS women's history, working for the Church History Department. Author of *Let's Talk About Polygamy*, a book built around the personal stories and first-person writings of women — and men — who actually lived plural marriage, rather than around statistics or third-party interpretation.[^1]

She is the interview subject of the [[Keystone - Polygamy Expert What Changed My Mind|Keystone polygamy episode]], in which she recounts how her own view of plural marriage shifted — moving from feeling obligated to defend a practice she did not understand toward peace, and changing especially "where men were concerned" after she studied men's lived experience as the neglected "other half of the story."[^2]

In a second Keystone episode, [[Keystone - Polygamy Expert Debunks All the Myths]], she walks through misconceptions about plural marriage one at a time — that men were motivated by sex (many "dragged their feet"), that all women hated it, that exaltation required it, that most Utahns practiced it (peak ~43% in Manti, declining to 7% by 1900, per [[Kathryn Daynes]]), and that monogamy was the "fair" comparison.[^3] See [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]], [[Polygamy Was a Minority Practice That Declined Steadily]], [[Nineteenth-Century Utah Women Had More Rights Than Monogamous American Women]], and [[First Wives Could Be Coerced by the Law of Sarah]].

She is also cited in a third Keystone episode, [[Keystone - What Exmo Reddit Gets Wrong About LDS Polygamy]], on the minimal courtship of plural wives — "many women who became plural wives experienced little to no courtship, which appealed to Victorian sensitivities about the appropriate behavior of married men." See [[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent]].[^4]

Note: distinct from [[Britney Ward Radio|Britney "the Shadow"]], a separate Latter-day Saint commentator on Ward Radio.
