# Basilides

**Era:** Early–mid 2nd century CE
**Role:** Christian / Gnostic teacher at Alexandria
**Tradition:** Gnosticism (self-identified Christian)

## Bio

Basilides was an influential teacher active in Alexandria in the early-to-mid second century. He is conventionally classed among the Gnostics, though he considered himself a Christian; his system is known chiefly through hostile summaries in [[Irenaeus of Lyons]], [[Clement of Alexandria]], and Hippolytus. [[Brian Thomassen]] notes that Basilides, [[Tatian]], and [[Theophilus of Antioch]] all trace to (or studied under people from) the **Antioch** area — suggesting some shared, unknown precursor figure behind the doctrine they each advance.[^1]

## Significance for the Creation Debate

Thomassen names Basilides as the **first** of the three figures he ties to the invention of creation *ex nihilo*: "It starts with the Gnostics. It starts with Basilides." On this account the doctrine begins in the Gnostic-Christian space — not in Greek philosophy — as part of an effort to make God *superior* to and maximally free from his creation, denying any co-eternal material that would constrain him. (The vault's [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]] foregrounds Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus from the Dan McClellan source; Thomassen pushes the origin one step earlier, to Basilides.)
