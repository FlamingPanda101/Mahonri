# Benjamin Sommer

**Full name:** Benjamin D. Sommer
**Role:** Professor of Bible and Ancient Semitic Languages
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2000s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic / Jewish (Jewish Theological Seminary of America)

## Bio

Benjamin Sommer is a professor of Bible at the Jewish Theological Seminary in New York. He is a leading scholar of the Hebrew Bible with expertise in ancient Israelite religion, theology, and the history of biblical interpretation. His work bridges traditional Jewish learning and modern critical biblical scholarship.

Dan McClellan describes Sommer's book *The Bodies of God in the World of Ancient Israel* (Cambridge University Press, 2008) as "phenomenally influential." [^1] The book argues that ancient Israel, like its neighbors, conceived of God as having multiple bodies and simultaneous localized manifestations — a concept Sommer terms "the fluidity model" of divine embodiment — in contrast to the later "unity model" that emerged under Greek philosophical influence.

## Divine Corporeality / Embodied God

Sommer's *The Bodies of God* is one of the foundational academic works establishing that the Hebrew Bible presents God as corporeal and embodied. McClellan cites it as part of the overwhelming scholarly consensus: "God had a body in the Hebrew Bible that was visible and corporeal and could be touched. And then that changed because the authoritative knowledge changed." [^2]

Sommer's "fluidity model" helps explain how God could simultaneously appear to Abraham in Genesis 18 (as a visible, eating, embodied being) while also being present in heaven — not through later Trinitarian frameworks, but through an ancient Israelite understanding of divine fragmentation and simultaneous localization.
