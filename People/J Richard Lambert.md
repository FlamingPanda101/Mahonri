# J Richard Lambert

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
**Role:** Historical / diachronic linguist; PhD candidate (ancient Middle Eastern languages); former linguist, LDS sacred materials division

## Bio

Jared (J Richard) Lambert is a historical linguist, U.S. Army veteran, and self-described "deep diver" into ancient language and scripture. Raised in Salt Lake City, he served an LDS mission in Cape Verde, West Africa — a creole "melting pot" that sparked his love of languages — then was recruited as an army linguist and trained in Persian/Dari. He holds degrees in Middle Eastern studies, Persian, Chinese, and history, plus a master's in international affairs, and is finishing a PhD on how the ancient languages of the Middle East shape religious belief, ritual, and identity (he reports ~13 years of higher education / seven degrees).[^1] He worked as a linguist in the LDS Church's **sacred materials division**, teaching temple content to translation teams worldwide for the Church's multi-language rollout.[^2]

His research re-reads the Adam-and-Eve narrative through a linguistic lens, treating proto-Hebrew as a "soft," conceptual language and using diachronic analysis to detect later editorial layers. The work is captured in his paper (a "recontextualization of the Adam narrative") and a forthcoming book and documentary; his wife Britney, also a linguist, is a co-investigator. He is married with four daughters and posts short scripture-linguistics videos under the tag **J. Richard Lambert**.[^3]

## On Eve and the Fall

Lambert's signature claims appear in [[The Woman in the Wilderness - The True Story of Eve Linguistic Archaeology|this interview]]: that [[Ezer Kenegdo Names Eve as Adam's Salvation Not His Assistant|"help meet" mistranslates *ezer kenegdo*]], a salvation-word; that [[The Serpent Is a Symbol of the Messiah So Eve Foresaw the Savior|the serpent (*nachash*) symbolizes the Messiah]] so Eve "foresaw the Savior"; and that [[Eve Was Recreated in Eden as a Heavenly Mother Figure|Eve was "found" and recreated in Eden]] rather than built from a rib. He frames a self-coined pattern, the "epothenic arc" (his dissertation topic), and stresses repeatedly that he is "just laying out the linguistics," which he admits are not certain but are meant to push readers to study deeper.[^4]

He grounds the project in **concordism** — the idea that the prophet's pure revelation carries pure truth through the "cracks" even when the surrounding text reflects the sexism of its scribes — paired with an [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context|accommodating God]] who meets humans "line upon line."[^5] He invokes [[Margaret Barker]] on the first-temple transition and [[Steven Pinker]] on the cognitive cost of dismantling "truth towers."[^6]
