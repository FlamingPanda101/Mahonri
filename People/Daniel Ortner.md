# Daniel Ortner

**Role:** Protestant apologist and online debate interlocutor; former Latter-day Saint
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2020s)
**Tradition:** Protestant (leans Reformed; not strictly doctrinaire); classical theism

## Bio

Daniel Ortner is a Protestant Christian and former member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints — "I was LDS for 14 years" — who left the Restoration for creedal Christianity and now defends the classical-theist and Trinitarian view of God in cross-confessional dialogue. The host, [[Jacob Hansen]], notes the two "have had a lot of disagreements over the years online," and Ortner says he has *written* on these topics (a blog post arguing the LDS model fails to explain the universe). On Calvinism he says, "I tend to lean reformed in my views, but I'm not very... doctrinaire on this," resisting the hardest predestinarian formulations while granting their internal consistency. [^1][^2]

## View of God: Self-Existent Creator

Ortner's foundation is God's *self-disclosure in scripture*: God identifies himself as Creator, eternal, and self-existent — the "I AM" of [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3:14]] — who alone has aseity, "self-existence... in and of himself," with everything else "derived from his creative act." God is *personal*, non-physical by nature, and revealed most fully in the incarnate Christ. He resists Hansen's claim that "God" is used in multiple senses (nature, ultimate reality, person), insisting God is a personal Creator who *embodies* love rather than being an abstraction. See:

- [[Two Self-Existent Realities Cannot Coexist]]
- [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]]

## Constraint, Evil, and the Atonement

Across the debate Ortner concedes a constraint on God but locates it *internally* in God's nature and justice, not in any external reality: God "cannot lie," "cannot change his nature," yet "is the master of his nature, not a slave to it." He defends the suffering of this world (the cross, the Exodus plagues, the Amalekites) as the way that best vindicates God's justice and mercy and most glorifies God, appealing to [[Romans 9.11-14|Romans 9]] and Job to ground human trust over human judgment of God. See:

## Original Sin, Hell, and Election

Ortner affirms inherited guilt through Adam — infants are "worthy of condemnation" by an inherited "nature of rebellion," though redeemed through Christ via an age of accountability — and defends never-ending punishment as tracking the *ongoing* sin of the unrepentant in hell. Leaning Reformed, he grants that God loves all only in the sense of common grace while having chosen to save some and not all, conceding the consistent Calvinist "does not love everyone and does not want to save everyone."
