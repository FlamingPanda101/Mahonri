# Jacob Wright

**Who:** Professor of Hebrew Bible at the Candler School of Theology, Emory University (Atlanta, GA). Author of *Why the Bible Began: An Alternative History of Scripture and Its Origins* (Cambridge University Press, 2023).

**Role:** Wright is a Hebrew Bible scholar working at the intersection of biblical studies, political theology, and the history of peoplehood. He argues that the Bible is best understood not as scripture for a religion but as a "project of peoplehood" — a collaborative literary effort by a defeated, divided people to form an enduring identity that could survive the loss of kingdom, temple, and territory.

## Central Thesis: Bible as Project of Peoplehood
Wright's core argument, presented on [[Data Over Dogma - Why the Bible Began with Jacob Wright|Data Over Dogma]], is that the Bible emerged from military defeat and political division — not as a religion replacing a kingdom (per [[Julius Wellhausen|Wellhausen]]), but as a *people* (a "state of mind") forming around shared narratives, memories, and future aspirations when the physical state was destroyed. See [[Bible as Project of Peoplehood]].

## Military Defeat as Catalyst
Wright contends that without military defeat there is no Bible — but defeat alone is insufficient. The other essential factor is the division between Israel (north) and Judah (south), two distinct kingdoms that were never historically united. See [[Military Defeat as Catalyst for Bible Formation]], [[Israel and Judah Never United]].

## The Bible as Curriculum
Wright's second major thesis: the Bible is a "pedagogical project" — an educational curriculum designed to form a people without political power. Wisdom literature was democratized from elite court training to family life ([[Proverbs]] shifts from court tutor to biological mother/father). Education replaces military might as the key to enduring identity. See [[Bible as Pedagogical Curriculum]].

## Women's Prominence in Biblical Narrative
Wright argues that women are prominent in biblical narratives because a small, defeated people cannot afford to sideline 50% of the population. Women model the indirect, diplomatic, survival-oriented approach the defeated need — they are "[[Esther]]s not [[Mordecai]]s," "[[Deborah]]s not [[Gideon]]s." Women also represent the shift from battlefield honor to domestic wisdom. See [[Women in Biblical Narrative as Survival Models]].

## Pushback Against Wellhausen
Wright explicitly rejects [[Julius Wellhausen|Wellhausen]]'s answer to the "why" question. Wellhausen saw the Bible emerging as the scripture of a "religion" (a "cult" or "religious community") created by imperialism destroying the political nation. Wright counters: what emerged was *peoplehood* (properly "nation" in the ethnic sense), not religion — and the biblical authors were not passive victims of imperialism but active, creative agents. See [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins]].

## Methodology
Wright's book avoids footnotes and dense scholarly apparatus to remain accessible. He begins with 150 pages of historical background (the rise and fall of Israel/Judah) before reaching the post-fall period where he argues the Bible truly takes shape. He credits Dan Fleming (NYU) as an influence but diverges from Fleming's view that Israel and Judah shared ethnic commonality — Wright says the union was "purely political" and the united monarchy under David and Solomon was "totally fictional."
