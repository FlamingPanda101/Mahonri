## Who
John Chrysostom ("golden-mouthed"; died AD 407) was one of the most celebrated preachers of the early church, eventually Archbishop of Constantinople. His sermons frequently described aspects of the weekly worship services, and the traditional eastern liturgy was later named after him (the Divine Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom).

## Authority and Reason for Citation
Chrysostom is a major source for fourth/fifth-century Eucharistic liturgy and the symbolism of the veiled chancel screen. He is cited for his descriptions of the actions at the veiled screen and the imagery of the apse as heavenly throne room, the altar as heavenly throne, and the veil being drawn aside during the Eucharist to reveal the angelic liturgy in which the congregation joins (Homilies on First Corinthians 36.5; Homilies on Ephesians 3.5; On the Incomprehensible Nature of God 4.4).

## Cited on the Age of Patristic Baptisms
[[Trent Horn]] (via [[David Wright]]'s research) cites Chrysostom as the *youngest* identifiable non-infant baptism among the church fathers — baptized at about age 18, "all of adult years" — evidence that the 4th-century fathers were neither infant-baptizers in those cases nor child-credobaptists. See [[Credobaptists Cannot Agree on the Age of Baptism]].[^3]
