# Apollinarius

**Who:** Apollinarius (Apollinaris) of Laodicea, fourth-century bishop (c. AD 310–390). Originally a defender of Nicene orthodoxy and an associate of [[Athanasius]] against [[Arius]], he is remembered chiefly for the Christological error that bears his name.

## Position — Apollinarianism
Apollinarius taught that in the Incarnation **Christ took a human body but not a human mind/rational soul**: the divine *Logos* occupied the place a human mind would otherwise hold. The motive was to safeguard Christ's unity and sinlessness, but the effect was to deny his full humanity. The view was **condemned at the First Council of Constantinople (381)**.[^1]

## Significance
In the source video, Apollinarianism is the lead example of a *condemned* heresy that a modern *sola scriptura* Trinitarian ([[Avery (God Logic)|Avery]]) backed into when asked whether Christ has one mind or two — answering "one divine mind." See [[Avery Tripped the Apollinarian Wire]] and [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]].

The classic refutation quoted is [[Gregory of Nazianzus]]' soteriological axiom against Apollinarius: **"what is not assumed is not healed"** — if Christ did not take a human mind, he did not save the human mind.[^2] The episode reads this as friendly to the LDS insistence on Christ's full, distinct humanity (the Son is a genuinely distinct person, not one substance shared with the Father), and as one more instance of the one-mind/two-natures tangle that Chalcedonian metaphysics generates but the [[The LDS Godhead Sidesteps the Named Heresies|LDS Godhead]] does not. Compare [[One Mind Dilemma Against Two Natures]] and [[Ephesus and Chalcedon Defined the Two Natures of Christ]].
