# Susanna Rinard

**Full Name:** Susanna Rinard
**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Academic philosophy (epistemology)
**Role:** Professor of philosophy, Harvard University

## Bio

Susanna Rinard is a professor of philosophy at Harvard University whose work centers on epistemology and the rationality of belief. In the Thoughtful Faith video she supplies the scholarly exposition of **Pascal's Wager**, introducing herself: "I'm Susanna Rinard, a philosophy professor at Harvard, and today I want to talk about Pascal's wager, an argument for belief in God."[^1] (The video's narrator pronounces the surname "Reinard.")

## Positions in this dialogue

Rinard's contribution is descriptive and analytic — she lays out both the weak and strong forms of the wager:

- **The lottery analogy.** A bet is good when payoff times probability is favorable; even a low-probability bet is rational if the payoff is large enough. Belief in God, with an infinite payoff, is "an infinitely better deal" than a hypothetical billion-dollar lottery. See [[Pascals Wager Is a Decision Under Uncertainty Not a Proof]].
- **The standard objections.** She catalogues three: the many-gods problem, the involuntariness of belief, and the worry that a self-interested gambler wouldn't merit the reward — calling them "valid critiques" against the *weak* version.[^2]
- **The dominance version.** She presents a second formulation, "closer to the argument as Pascal himself actually intended," whose second premise is that you are better off believing *even if God does not exist*. See [[The Dominance Version of Pascals Wager]].
