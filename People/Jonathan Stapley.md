# Jonathan Stapley

Latter-day Saint historian (award-winning historian and scientist) and author of *Holiness to the Lord*, a book on Latter-day Saint temple worship. His research on the history of LDS liturgy, sealing, healing rituals, and priesthood underlies the catalog of discontinued temple practices in [[Keystone - Top 10 Abandoned LDS Temple Practices]]. Keystone credits him as indispensable: "If you've learned something new in this video, you probably have historian Jonathan Stapley to thank for it, whose work was indispensable for this," and points viewers to a companion interview with him on the temple.[^1]

In the longer [[Keystone - LDS Historian on What We Get Wrong About the Endowment|Keystone endowment interview]], Stapley advances the thesis that "ordinance" meant *law* (not liturgy) for Joseph Smith — so what "cannot change" is the plan of salvation, not the ceremony's form — and walks through the development of the sacrament prayer, the oath of vengeance, the Freemasonry-derived penalties, and the gendered sealing language. See [[Ordinance Meant Law Not Liturgy When Joseph Smith Said Ordinances Cannot Change]], [[The Oath of Vengeance Was a Prayer for Justice Later Removed]], [[The Endowment Penalties Were Borrowed From Freemasonry and Later Removed]], and [[Joseph Smiths Sealing Text Was More Egalitarian Than Brigham Youngs]].[^2]

His documented examples — children's proxy baptisms, baptisms and blessings of healing (including by women), prayer circles outside the temple, and women sealed to multiple living men — supply the concrete, datable backbone for the apologetic argument that temple change is historically routine. See [[Ten Abandoned Temple Practices Show Continuity Through Change]] and [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]].

In the [[All Those In Favor - The History and Theology of Mormon Temples|All Those In Favor interview]] on his Oxford University Press book *Holiness to the Lord*, Stapley develops the theology of the temple: the "endowment of power" as a recapitulation of Luke-Acts and Pentecost ([[The Endowment of Power Recapitulates Luke-Acts and Pentecost]]); heaven as a constructed relational kinship network and hell as disconnection ([[Heaven Is a Constructed Relational Network and Hell Is Disconnection]]); "ordinance" as a Protestant loanword for rites that function as sacraments ([[Ordinance Was a Protestant Loanword for Rites That Function as Sacraments]]); the Restoration as "radically non-dogmatic" with strong orthopraxy ([[Latter-day Saints Are Radically Non-Dogmatic With Strong Orthopraxy]]); and a chapter on race and the temple building on [[Paul Reeve]].
