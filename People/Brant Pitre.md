# Brant Pitre

**Role:** Catholic New Testament scholar (Augustine Institute); Ph.D., Notre Dame
**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Roman Catholic (Second Temple / historical-Jesus studies)

## Bio
Brant Pitre is Distinguished Research Professor of Scripture at the Augustine Institute and a specialist in the New Testament and Second Temple Judaism. His books include *Jesus and the Last Supper*, *Jesus and the Jewish Roots of the Eucharist*, and *Jesus and Divine Christology* (Eerdmans, 2024), the book under discussion in the source interview. His method reads the Gospels through "an ancient Jewish lens" — Second Temple eschatology, messianism, and the targumic/midrashic habit of teaching by riddle and scriptural allusion.

## Contribution to the source
In the [[Alex OConnor - Jesus DID Claim to be God|Alex O'Connor interview]], Pitre defends the thesis of *Jesus and Divine Christology*: that the reason the earliest (all-Jewish) Christians made divine claims about Jesus immediately after his death is that **Jesus himself made divine claims during his lifetime — but in a Jewish way**, through riddles, parables, and allusions to key texts (the *Shema*, the Decalogue, Exodus 3, Isaiah 43, Daniel 7, Psalm 110). He solves the "paradox" of a low historical-Jesus Christology versus a high early-church Christology by positing a historical connection between them ("the smoke of divine Christology comes from the fire of Jesus's own divine messianism"). Key strands he develops:
- Forgiving sins as a divine prerogative ([[Forgiving Sins Was a Divine Prerogative in Second Temple Judaism]]).
- The walking-on-the-sea theophany and the *ego eimi* / "do not fear" allusion to Exodus 3 + Isaiah 43 ([[The Walking on the Sea Is a Theophany Claiming the Divine Name]]).
- The demand for super-parental love as an implicit divine claim ([[Jesus's Demand for Super-Parental Love Is an Implicit Divine Claim]]).
- The blasphemy charge as the most "crucifiable" explanation of Jesus's death ([[The Blasphemy Charge Makes the Divine Jesus the Most Crucifiable Jesus]]).

He repeatedly distinguishes three questions — whether Jesus *was* God, whether the historical Jesus *claimed* to be God, and whether the Gospels *portray* him as claiming it — locating his book on the second.
