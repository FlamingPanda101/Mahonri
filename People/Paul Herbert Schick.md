# Paul Herbert Schick

**Full name:** Paul Herbert Schick (b. 8 May 1906, Freiberg)
**Role:** Latter-day Saint who refused the Nazi salute on religious grounds
**Era:** Nazi Germany (arrested 1936)
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS)

## Bio

Paul Herbert Schick was a toolmaker from Freiberg whose story, [[Stephen Smoot]] emphasizes, appears nowhere else in the literature on the Church in Germany — it surfaces only in the Gestapo dossier, making this the first place his act of conscience has been recovered.[^1] At a compulsory factory reception of a Hitler speech on 27 March 1936, Schick declined to raise his hand for the German (Nazi) salute and would not sing the national anthem or the *Horst-Wessel-Lied*. He was arrested for it.

## His Statement on Zion

His firsthand statement to the interrogating officer grounds the refusal explicitly in Latter-day Saint belief: "I am a member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. As such, I support the establishment of the Kingdom of Zion... It contradicts my personal beliefs to serve both Zion and another Reich... I find only a contradiction between the goals of Zion and the national anthem and the German salute." He affirmed he still felt "as a German" subject to the Führer, "but consider Zion to be the highest ideal."[^2] He lost his job but escaped a concentration camp because Hitler had recently issued a general amnesty for low-level offenders. Smoot urges that, alongside [[Helmut Hübner]], Schick "needs to be remembered" as a hero. His case is the individual mirror image of the institutional posture analyzed in [[The Church's Accommodationist Survival Strategy in Nazi Germany]].[^3]
