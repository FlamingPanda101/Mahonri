# Pope Vigilius

Bishop of Rome from 537 to 555, during the reign of the Byzantine emperor Justinian I. Vigilius is best known for his vacillating and finally self-contradicting role in the **Three Chapters controversy**, which culminated at the **Second Council of Constantinople (553)**, the Fifth Ecumenical Council.

## The Three Chapters and Constantinople II

Justinian sought condemnation of the "Three Chapters" — three persons and their writings — to reconcile non-Chalcedonian (miaphysite) Christians. Vigilius resisted, then wavered repeatedly. He issued a solemn **First Constitutum** forbidding anyone of ecclesiastical rank to condemn the Three Chapters; the Catholic scholar Richard Price observes he "could not have stated more unambiguously that his decree was final." The assembled bishops condemned the Three Chapters regardless, struck Vigilius's name from the diptychs (a functional excommunication "in his person, though not in his office"), and justified themselves by Matthew 18:20 — "no individual... could forestall the decision of the universal church." Vigilius subsequently capitulated, issuing a **Second Constitutum** that *condemned* the Three Chapters, reversing his own prior "final" ruling.

[[Gavin Ortlund]] makes this episode the centerpiece of his case that papal *supremacy* (as later defined at Vatican I) is absent from the early church: an ecumenical council disciplined a sitting pope and explicitly claimed authority over him. Catholic readers respond that Vigilius acted under imperial duress and that the council became valid only through eventual papal confirmation. The episode is developed in [[Constantinople II Excommunicated Pope Vigilius]].
