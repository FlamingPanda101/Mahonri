# John Hick

**Role:** Philosopher of religion and theologian
**Era:** 1922–2012
**Tradition:** Protestant (latterly religious pluralist)

## Bio

John Hick was one of the most influential philosophers of religion of the twentieth century. His *Evil and the God of Love* (Harper & Row, 1966) is, in [[David Paulsen]]'s words, "widely recognized as the watershed work on the problem of evil." [^1] In it Hick revived and systematized the **soul-making (Irenaean) theodicy**: God's purpose in creating us is to let morally immature agents, set at an "epistemic distance" from Him, freely grow into His likeness through an environment of hardship — a structure strikingly parallel to Joseph Smith's. See [[Soul-Making Theodicy - Hick and Joseph Smith]].

## The fault line: absolute creation

Where Hick and the Restoration part ways is **creation ex nihilo**, which Hick affirms and Joseph Smith denies. Because Hick retains all four classical postulates — perfect goodness, absolute power, absolute foreknowledge, and absolute creation — he is confronted head-on by [[Flew's Divine Complicity Argument]], and he candidly **concedes that God is ultimately responsible for all the evil that occurs in the world.** [^2]

## Hick's universalism — and its incoherence

To save God's goodness given that concession, Hick takes his "one and only" way out: **universal salvation** — all will finally be drawn into a loving relationship with God whose value outweighs any finite suffering. Paulsen argues this collapses: to *guarantee* that not one soul is lost, Hick must quietly assume a determinism that undercuts the libertarian ("incompatibilist") freedom his own free-will defense requires. See [[Hick's Universalism Collapses Into Determinism]]. [^3]
