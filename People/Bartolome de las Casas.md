# Bartolome de las Casas

**Role:** Dominican friar and bishop; defender of indigenous peoples
**Era:** 16th century (c. 1484–1566)
**Tradition:** Catholic (Spanish)

## Bio

Bartolomé de las Casas was a Spanish Dominican friar and bishop who condemned the brutality and greed of the conquistadors as an offense against God and argued — drawing on the Gospel rather than on Aristotle — that the indigenous peoples of the Americas were as worthy of respect as anyone and "must be won for Christ," not enslaved.[^1]

## Christian Abolition and International Law

In [[Tom Holland]]'s account, Las Casas is the Catholic taproot of abolition and international law: against the Aristotelian "natural slaves" argument, his Gospel-grounded defense of indigenous dignity fed (alongside radical-Protestant and Enlightenment streams) into the concept of principles transcending confessional lines — the framework that later licensed British ships to interdict the slave trade.
