# Esther Hamori

**Full name:** Esther J. Hamori
**Role:** Professor of Hebrew Bible
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2000s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic (Union Theological Seminary, New York)

## Bio

Esther Hamori is a professor of Hebrew Bible at Union Theological Seminary. Her research focuses on divine embodiment, anthropomorphism, and the intersection of biblical and ancient Near Eastern conceptions of deity.

Dan McClellan cites her book *When Gods Were Men: The Embodied God in Biblical and Near Eastern Literature* as part of the scholarly consensus demonstrating that God was understood as embodied and corporeal in the Hebrew Bible and its cultural context. [^1]

## Divine Embodiment

Hamori's work examines how biblical and ancient Near Eastern texts depict deities in embodied, human-like form — walking, eating, speaking, wrestling, and physically interacting with humans. She situates Israel's anthropomorphic God within the broader ancient Near Eastern cultural matrix where embodied deities were the norm, not the exception. Her title — "When Gods Were Men" — captures the baseline assumption of the ancient world: gods had bodies, genders, and physical presence.

This scholarship directly supports McClellan's argument that the OT/NT contradiction on divine visibility reflects a genuine historical shift from corporeal to incorporeal concepts of God, not a misinterpretation by modern readers.
