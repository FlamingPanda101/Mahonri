# Gerald Edelman

**Role:** Neuroscientist; immunologist; Nobel laureate (Physiology or Medicine, 1972)
**Era:** 1929–2014
**Tradition:** Academic neuroscience

## Bio

Gerald Edelman was an American neuroscientist (originally a Nobel-winning immunologist) known for theories of brain organization and consciousness, including "Neural Darwinism." In this lecture [[Jordan Peterson]] cites him — rendered in the transcript as "Gerard Adelman" — for an estimate of the brain's staggering combinatorial complexity. [^1]

## Relevance to Epistemology

Peterson invokes Edelman to dramatize the scale of the problem perception must solve: "there are estimates for example by [Gerald Edelman] that there are more connections… more patterns of connections in your brain than there are subatomic particles in the universe." [^1] This supports the claim that, because reality and the brain alike are too complex to register exhaustively, perception must discard nearly all available information. See:
- [[Reality Is Too Complex to Perceive So the Brain Ignores Almost Everything]]
