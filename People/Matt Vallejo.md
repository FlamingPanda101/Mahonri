# Matt Vallejo

A Latter-day Saint from Chino, California, and an economics graduate of BYU-Idaho who, with a friend (Chris Long), pursued additional coursework in statistics out of personal interest. He approached [[The Stick of Joseph - The Statistical Impossibility of the Book of Mormons Origin|the Stick of Joseph]] after he and "super smart buddies" ran a probabilistic analysis of the naturalistic-origin claim for the [[Book of Mormon]], producing a slide deck that quantifies the odds that [[Joseph Smith]] composed the book without divine help.

Vallejo is candid about his credentials, calling himself "a tourist in the field of stats" rather than a statistician, and frames his work as a *style of thinking* a statistician would apply rather than a definitive calculation. His method isolates five anomalous features of the Book of Mormon and combines their probabilities with a conditional-probability chain rule, yielding a headline estimate of roughly 1 in 15.4 billion to 1 in 23 billion. He repeatedly invites critics to substitute their own assumptions, predicting any honest methodology still yields an extreme number. See [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions]].

## Positions / Contributions
- Originated the five-variable conditional-probability model presented on the podcast — education, length, dictation speed, distinct voices, and chiasmus. See [[Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier]] and [[The 1820s Imprint Baseline Places the Book of Mormon at the Statistical Extreme]].
- Anchors his estimates in 1820s U.S. publishing data (~41,000 imprints) rather than abstract assumptions, and offers explicit upper/lower bounds.
- Argues the figure is best understood by calibration against everyday improbabilities. See [[Everyday Improbabilities More Likely Than Naturalistic Book of Mormon Authorship]].
- Concedes statistics cannot themselves produce faith; testimony comes through prayer and sacrifice.
