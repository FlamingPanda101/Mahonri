# Seneca

**Full Name:** Lucius Annaeus Seneca (c. 4 BCE – 65 CE)
**Era:** Early Roman Empire (1st century CE)
**Tradition:** Stoicism
**Role:** Roman Stoic philosopher and statesman

## Bio
Seneca the Younger was a leading Roman Stoic philosopher, dramatist, and statesman, tutor and advisor to the emperor Nero. His *Moral Letters to Lucilius* and essays (notably *On Benefits*) are primary sources for Roman Stoic ethics in the very period the New Testament was being written.

## Cited For
[[Brian Thomassen]] cites Seneca as evidence that Stoic ethics anticipated and parallels New Testament moral teaching — the universal "open" ethic (give benefits even to the ungrateful, "for the sun also rises upon the wicked"), the "adverbial" ethic that locates virtue in the intention of the worshipper rather than the size of the sacrifice, and the duty to repay injury with kindness. Because Seneca writes decades before the Gospels, Thomassen uses him to argue Stoicism was a theoretical "preparatory gospel."
