# Brigham Young

Second president of the LDS Church (1801–1877) and successor to [[Joseph Smith]].
Connected to the [[King Follett Discourse]] primarily through his Adam-God theology.

## Adam-God and King Follett

Park notes that [[Brigham Young]] "would draw from the King Follett Discourse when
he would teach his Adam-God theology, taking some seeds that he believes of Adam-God
theology in that sermon and bringing them to their fruition."[^1]

Adam-God — the teaching that Adam is God the Father — was one of Young's most
controversial doctrines. He saw the King Follett Discourse (with its teaching that
[[God Was Once a Man]]) as providing a theological foundation for this idea: if
God was once a mortal who progressed, and Adam was the first mortal, the logical
chain points toward Adam's identity as God the Father.

## Infant Resurrection

The doctrine of [[Infant Exaltation]] from the discourse "was debated among several
church leaders though it slowly faded away" during Young's era.[^1]

## Character Reconsidered (Peterson, *Becoming Brigham*)

In a long-form interview, Dr. [[Daniel C. Peterson]] argues against the modern image of Brigham as uniquely flawed and as the "fall guy" for everything uncomfortable in LDS history.[^2]

- **Loyalty to Joseph:** Brigham's defining trait was total loyalty to [[Joseph Smith]] — laying the Far West cornerstone under threat of death to fulfill a prophecy; answering Joseph's public rebuke with "Joseph, what would you have me do?"; and dying with the words "Joseph, Joseph, Joseph." This makes the claim that Brigham invented doctrines against Joseph implausible.[^3] See [[Brigham Young as the Fall Guy]].
- **Spiritual, not merely practical:** He spoke in tongues to mired oxen and again before Joseph Smith (whom Joseph said would succeed him), claimed revelation more often than commonly realized, and was obsessed with temple-building. See [[Brigham Young as Spiritual Man Not Just Colonizer]].
- **Race and slavery:** Brigham held the racist assumptions of his era (shared by [[Abraham Lincoln]]) but personally loathed chattel slavery, treated individual Black people kindly (helping Isaac James with his coat; freeing Green Flake of debt), and the 1852 statute he supported was a *servitude*, not slavery, bill. See [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]] and [[Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery]].
- **The keys:** Peterson credits Brigham, as head of the Twelve who held the priesthood keys, with saving the Church at the 1844 succession. See [[Brigham Young Saved the Church Through the Keys]].

He had real rough edges — a sharp tongue he admitted to, an authoritarian streak, an abusive frontier upbringing — but Peterson insists these were neither unique nor "wicked," and were partly exaggerated by the Journal of Discourses. See [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]] and [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].

## Polygamy — Consent and Divorce
[[Keystone]] cites Brigham as a gatekeeper who *denied* some plural-marriage petitions (e.g. [[Peter Shirts]], told to "continue to lead the life of a hermit"), and as holding a permissive view of divorce: a wife whose life "became too burdensome" should "leave and get a divorce," and an alienated wife should be given "a bill of divorcement." Utah likely had the nation's highest divorce rate by 1870, and Brigham himself had wives who divorced him. See [[Plural Marriage Proposal Required a Chain of Consent]] and [[Polygamous Utah Had the Nations Easiest Divorce Especially for Women]].[^5]

## Polygamy — Not the Originator
A core move of polygamy denialism is to relocate plural marriage from Joseph onto Brigham (the [[Brigham Young as the Fall Guy|fall-guy]] pattern). [[Brian Hales]] notes Brigham gave firsthand eyewitness testimony that *Joseph* taught and practiced plural marriage, and the 2025 Church statement affirms "Joseph Smith introduced the practice of plural marriage, not Brigham Young." See [[The Implications Cascade of Polygamy Denial]] and [[The Eyewitness Asymmetry Refutes the Polygamy Conspiracy Theory]].[^4]
