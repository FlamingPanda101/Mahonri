# Tatian

**Full name:** Tatian the Assyrian
**Role:** Early Christian apologist; first articulator of creation ex nihilo
**Era:** 2nd century CE (c. 120–180 CE)
**Tradition:** Early Christianity (later associated with Encratite heresy)

## Bio

Tatian was an Assyrian-born Christian convert who studied under Justin Martyr in Rome before breaking with the Roman church and returning to the East. He is best known for his *Oration to the Greeks* (c. 160 CE) and his *Diatessaron* — a harmony of the four Gospels that became the standard Gospel text in Syriac-speaking Christianity for centuries. Later tradition accused him of Encratite tendencies (extreme asceticism, rejection of marriage).

## First Articulation of Creation Ex Nihilo

Tatian is recognized as the FIRST known Christian writer to explicitly affirm that matter itself was created by God — rather than being an eternal co-principle alongside God:

> "Tatian, an Assyrian Christian convert writing around AD 160, stated that matter is begotten and not produced by any other being, but brought into existence by the framer of all things alone."[^1]

This was a radical philosophical departure. The conventional Greco-Roman view (Platonic, Aristotelian, Stoic) held that matter was eternal and that creation consisted of ordering pre-existent material. Tatian broke with this consensus, asserting that God created matter itself.

Tatian's innovation was driven by his apologetic context — defending Christian theology against Greek philosophical critiques that assumed *ex nihilo nihil fit* ("nothing comes from nothing").

## Doctrine of Creation Ex Nihilo

- See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]
- See [[Retroactive Reinterpretation of Creation Texts]]

Along with [[Theophilus of Antioch]] (c. 169 CE) and [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (c. 180 CE), Tatian is one of the three originators of the doctrine of creation ex nihilo. These three figures developed the doctrine in response to Gnostic and philosophical debates, then retroactively reinterpreted earlier Jewish and Christian texts through the new framework.
