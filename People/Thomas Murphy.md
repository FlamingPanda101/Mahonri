# Thomas Murphy

**Era:** Contemporary (2000s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint background; prominent critic of Book of Mormon historicity
**Role:** Cultural anthropologist

## Who

Thomas W. Murphy is the cultural anthropologist whose essay **"Lamanite Genesis, Genealogy, and Genetics"** (2002) is widely credited with igniting the popular DNA-vs-Book-of-Mormon controversy. He framed the absence of Israelite/Near-Eastern DNA among Native Americans as a **"Galileo event"** for the Church — a scientific finding that, in his telling, falsifies the book's claims about Lamanite origins.[^1]

## In This Source

[[Stephen Smoot]] and [[Neal Rappleye]] use Murphy as the lead example of how the DNA objection collapsed from a *scientific* argument into a *hermeneutical* one. Believing Latter-day Saint scientists answered the genetics swiftly (founder effect, drift, host-population admixture); Murphy's next move, they say, was to argue "but limited geography is not in the Book of Mormon — it's hemispheric geography," which is a claim about textual interpretation, not science. His more recent emphasis — that the Book of Mormon is "racist" in how it depicts indigenous peoples — is likewise a moral/textual argument rather than a genetic one. The hosts read this trajectory as evidence the science is essentially settled and the live dispute is now over reading the text. See [[The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate]] and [[The Empty Continent Assumption Underlies the DNA Objection]].[^2]
