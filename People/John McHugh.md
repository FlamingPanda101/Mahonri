# John McHugh

**Role:** Catholic biblical scholar; New Testament exegete and ICC commentator
**Era:** 20th–21st century (1927–2006)
**Tradition:** Roman Catholic

## Bio

John McHugh was an English Roman Catholic priest and New Testament scholar, long associated with Ushaw College and the University of Durham. He wrote on the Gospels and on Mariology (*The Mother of Jesus in the New Testament*), but in this vault he is cited for his posthumously completed contribution to the *International Critical Commentary* — the volume *John 1–4* — a dense, philologically rigorous treatment of the opening chapters of the Fourth Gospel. The ICC is one of the most demanding technical commentary series in English-language biblical studies, and McHugh's work is treated as a standard scholarly reference on the Greek text.

## Relevance to the vault

McHugh is enlisted as one of four standard reference works ([[Dan McClellan]] also cites BDAG, [[Lydia Novakovich]], and [[Daniel Wallace]]) supporting the *intermediate-agency* reading of [[John 1.3|John 1:3]]. McHugh stresses that the text says the world was made "*through* him," not "*by* him," and that the Greek *dia* with the genitive "is always used of the contribution made by God's personal agents." On this reading the Logos is grammatically parallel to other commissioned agents (John the Baptist as witness, Moses as lawgiver) — God's agent of creation rather than its ultimate originator. This undercuts the Trinitarian inference that, because both Genesis and John make "the creator" the agent, the Logos must simply *be* the God of Israel.
