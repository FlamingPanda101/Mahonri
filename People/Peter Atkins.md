# Peter Atkins

**Role:** Physical chemist (Lincoln College, Oxford, emeritus); atheist
**Era:** Contemporary (b. 1940)

## Bio

Peter W. Atkins is a British physical chemist and a fellow of Lincoln College, Oxford, best known as the author of widely used undergraduate chemistry textbooks (his *Physical Chemistry* being a standard worldwide), which host Justin Brierley calls "the benchmark" of the field.[^1] He is an outspoken atheist and a leading popular advocate of **scientism** — the conviction that the scientific method can, in principle, explain everything and that there is no aspect of reality the human mind cannot penetrate.[^2] In this *Unbelievable?* debate over the film *Expelled* he argues against [[Stephen C. Meyer]] that intelligent design is "not science at all," that ordered structures (and even biological information) can arise without an agent through processes like the second law of thermodynamics, and that invoking a designer is "intellectually lazy."[^3] He nonetheless concedes that ID raises "valid," "probing" questions that materialist scientists are obliged to answer.[^4]

## Scientism and the limits of explanation
Atkins frames atheistic materialism as a celebration, not a diminishment, of humanity: the human brain is "capable of understanding seemingly without limit," and to claim there is "an area where the human mind cannot penetrate" is, in his words, "a denial of humanity."[^5] He holds that the simplest explanations — universal laws of nature running their course — are adequate unless shown deficient, and that science is "a reticulation of ideas," an interconnected network in which no field of inquiry is an island.[^6] Meyer's reply is that this is an *a priori* commitment to materialism that may exclude the true explanation by definition; see [[Scientism Cannot Explain the Origin of Information]] and [[Intelligent Design as a Scientific Inference]].
