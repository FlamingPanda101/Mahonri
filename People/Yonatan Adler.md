# Yonatan Adler

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Jewish (non-Christian, no theological stake in LDS questions)
**Role:** Archaeologist, Ariel University (Israel)

## Bio
Yonatan Adler is an Israeli archaeologist at Ariel University and the author of *The Origins of Judaism* (Yale University Press). [[Alex Arnold]] foregrounds him precisely because he "is not a Christian, he's not LDS, and he really has no theological stake in where any of these conclusions land."[^1] His central argument is that the practices Jews in Jesus' era treated as ancient Mosaic commandments — synagogue worship, dietary law, ritual purity, tefillin, Sabbath and festival observance — "show no significant archaeological or textual evidence of widespread popular observance before the Hellenistic period," roughly 200 BC and later.[^2]

## On the Origins of Jewish Practice
Adler is careful — and Arnold relays the caveat — that he is "not arguing that the Torah is a fabrication. The laws are old and the texts are old." What he traces is "the gap between the law being written down and that law being widely practiced in daily life by ordinary people," a gap that can span centuries.[^3] He marshals faunal evidence (pig bones at pre-exilic Israelite sites), the late appearance of mikva'ot and tefillin, and the absence of synagogue buildings before the 1st century BC to argue that the full apparatus of Second-Temple Judaism was largely assembled as a national-identity project under the Hasmoneans.[^4] See [[Second-Temple Judaism Was Assembled in the Hasmonean Period]] and [[The Synagogue Is a Post-Exilic Institution Absent From the Old Testament]]. Arnold pairs his archaeology with [[Margaret Barker]]'s textual reconstruction as independent disciplines converging on the same picture.
