# Luke VanderLaan

**Role:** New Testament scholar (Luke-Acts, onomastics, eyewitness testimony)
**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Conservative / evangelical NT scholarship

## Bio

Luke VanderLaan (the auto-transcript renders the name phonetically as "Luke Vandereay") is cited by [[Michael Jones]] (Inspiring Philosophy) on the Thoughtful Faith channel as the author of **Living Footnotes on the Gospel of Luke**. His work argues that Luke "interviewed eyewitnesses" ([[Luke 1.1-4|Luke 1:1-4]]) and that the Gospel exhibits **onomastic congruence** — its distribution of personal names matches the documented frequencies of first-century Palestinian Jewish names (building on [[Richard Bauckham]]). Most-popular real names (Simon, Joseph) are most frequent in the Gospels; rare names rare. VanderLaan reports that "fictional accounts couldn't do that," while genuine histories (Josephus, Plutarch) can — so the Gospels drew on real eyewitness material.[^1]

He also notes a stylistic split: vivid eyewitness-style narration clusters where the Gospel reports Jesus interacting with named people, distinct from the "we passages" of Acts where Luke writes from his own experience.[^2]
