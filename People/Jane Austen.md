# Jane Austen

**Who:** Jane Austen (1775–1817), English novelist whose works — including *Pride and Prejudice*, *Sense and Sensibility*, and *Emma* — are among the most celebrated in the English literary canon. Her novels provide a detailed window into the social and economic realities of early 19th-century English life, particularly the marriage market and the tensions women faced between romantic love and economic security.

**Role in Apologetics:** Austen's novels are cited by LDS apologists as literary corroboration of 19th-century marriage norms — specifically that marriage in that era was primarily an economic arrangement and that women and their parents prioritized a man's ability to provide over romantic love. This supports the argument that modern judgments about [[Joseph Smith]]'s plural marriages fail to account for the economic logic of 19th-century marriage.

## Marriage and Economics in Her Novels
Austen's works depict women weighing the desire for love against the practical necessity of economic stability. This reflects the reality that, before birth control and social safety nets, a woman's survival depended on marrying an established provider. See [[Nineteenth-Century Marriage Age Norms]].
