# Blake Ostler

**Role:** Latter-day Saint philosopher and theologian; author and apologist
**Era:** Contemporary (active since 1980s)
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS / Mormon)

## Bio

Blake Ostler is a Latter-day Saint philosopher and theologian, for a time an adjunct instructor at BYU, published in academic and philosophical journals, who conducts scholarly research in German, Egyptian, Hebrew, Greek, and Latin. He is best known for the four-volume series *Exploring Mormon Thought* (Greg Kofford Books): Vol. 1 *The Attributes of God*, Vol. 2 on soteriology, Vol. 3 *Of God and Gods* (divine persons and theosis), and Vol. 4 on the problem of evil. He has a drafted but unpublished fifth volume on mind and matter and is considering instead a fifth volume on creation. He has also produced a more accessible podcast walking through the books. [^1]

In Vol. 2 he argues that **free will and creation out of nothing are logically incoherent to assert together**. He addresses the charge of "diminished divinity" against the social Trinity in chapter 9 of Vol. 3, and has written an article, *Revisioning the Mormon Doctrine of Deity*. [^2] See [[Divinity Requires Loving Relationship]], [[No Diminished Divinity Despite Subordination]].

## Divine Name, Adoption, and the Messenger Tradition

Ostler's central exegetical move is that **Yahweh is the Father**, who confers the divine name on the Son by ancient-adoption and the *shaliach* (messenger) tradition; Jesus speaks "I am" and the divine titles as the Father's name-bearing agent, not as a "multi-personal Yahweh." See:

## Negation Texts and the Critique of "Monotheism"

He reads Isaiah's "there is no God besides me" formulas as Ancient Near Eastern taunts of incomparability and demands for sole political allegiance, paralleling the boasts of Persian kings — not Greek metaphysical monotheism imported back into the text. See:
- [[Babylon Taunt Formula Shows Negation Is a Boast of Superiority]]

## Kenotic Christology and Social Trinitarianism

He defends a kenotic Christology (Christ's divine properties held "in potentiality" while mortal), co-inherence/perichoresis as the binding of divine persons in love, and an LDS social trinitarianism he argues is more coherent than both the Latin (simplicity) and Eastern models. See:

## Spiritual Epistemology

Ostler defends the **human heart (Hebrew *leb*) as the basis of religious knowledge** — a God-given faculty/"extra sense," with spiritual experience functioning as the interpretive *lens* rather than a piece of evidence. See:
