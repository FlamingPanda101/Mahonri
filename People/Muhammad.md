# Muhammad

Muhammad (c. 570–632 AD) is the founding prophet of Islam, born in the Arabian Peninsula and dying at about 62. His revelatory experience began in 610 at age 40 when, according to the traditional story, the angel Gabriel appeared to him and commanded him to "recite" (or "read"); when Muhammad protested that he could not, the angel reportedly throttled him three times before he could recite, and showed him a brocaded cloth bearing writing.[^1] [[Daniel C. Peterson|Daniel Peterson]], professor emeritus of Islamic Studies and Arabic at BYU and author of the biography *Muhammad: Prophet of God*, is the source for the discussion below.

## The Prophetic Call
Peterson highlights an early call narrative in which Muhammad, meditating in a cave above Mecca, hears a voice three times — "Oh Muhammad, messenger of God" — then looks up and sees a figure "seated upon his throne," which Peterson reads as a vision of an enthroned **God** (since in the Quran the throne is God's, never Gabriel's).[^2] His prophetic career lasted about 22 years; after the call, Gabriel "doesn't figure very much" and Muhammad "has very few visions."[^3] See [[Muhammad May Have Been a True Inspired Prophet]].

## Production of the Quran
Called *ummi* (usually "illiterate," though Peterson suspects this may mean "lay/unlearned" rather than wholly unlettered), Muhammad received revelations piecemeal over ~22 years and transmitted them to scribes; the unified text was standardized later under the caliph Uthman, who destroyed variant versions.[^4] This makes the Quran, in Peterson's view, structurally more like the LDS Doctrine and Covenants than the Book of Mormon. See [[The Quran Parallels the Doctrine and Covenants Not the Book of Mormon]], [[Quranic Textual History Is Cleaner Than the Bible's]].

## As Political Leader
After the *Hijra* to Medina in 622, Muhammad became a political and military leader, fighting wars until his death in 632. Because he held political office (unlike Jesus), it is "harder in Islam to separate church and state," a fact later invoked by those who justify violence — though Peterson argues classical *jihad* was defensive, not aggressive. See [[Jihad Does Not Originally Mean Holy War]], [[Islamic Violence Is Mostly Political Not Quranic]].

## In LDS Thought
Early Latter-day Saint leaders (George A. Smith, [[Parley P. Pratt]]) spoke warmly of Muhammad as inspired, and the 1978 First Presidency statement honored him as having received "a portion of God's light." See [[Early LDS Leaders Praised Muhammad as Inspired]], [[Other True Prophets Do Not Undercut Joseph Smith]].
