# Samuel T. Wilkinson

**Role:** Associate professor of psychiatry, Yale School of Medicine; author
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2020s)
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS / Mormon)

## Bio

Samuel T. Wilkinson is an associate professor of psychiatry at the Yale School of Medicine, where he also serves as associate director of the Yale Depression Research Program. He earned a B.S. in mechanical engineering (summa cum laude) from Brigham Young University and an M.D. from the Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, then completed residency at Yale, where he joined the faculty. His research on depression and suicide prevention has been funded by the National Institute of Mental Health, the National Institute on Drug Abuse, the Brain & Behavior Research Foundation, and the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention, and his work has been featured in *The New York Times*, *The Washington Post*, and *The Wall Street Journal*. He served a mission in Nevada and is a husband and father of five. [^1]

## Purpose (2024)

Wilkinson is the author of *Purpose: What Evolution and Human Nature Imply About the Meaning of Our Existence* (Simon & Schuster, March 2024), which received praise from figures including [[Francis Collins]] (leader of the Human Genome Project) and Arthur Brooks. The book grew out of an "existential crisis" he experienced as a first-year medical student, when he felt science and faith were at odds over "the meaninglessness of existence." Months of study, thought, and prayer led him to a coherent reconciliation that he eventually wrote up over two to three years around the start of COVID. [^2]

## Argument: Evolution Implies Purpose

Wilkinson's central move is to deny that "life has no purpose" is a scientific result — it is a *philosophical assumption* riding on the premise that evolution is purely random, which he rejects on the basis of evolutionary convergence (drawing on [[Simon Conway Morris]]). From the non-randomness of evolution and the structure of human nature — its "dual potential" for selfishness and altruism, the centrality of free will/agency, and the depth of family love — he reverse-engineers an account of cosmic purpose, which he finds to resonate strongly with the LDS *Family Proclamation*.
