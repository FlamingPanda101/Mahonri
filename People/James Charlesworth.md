# James Charlesworth

**Full name:** James H. Charlesworth
**Role:** Professor of New Testament; scholar of Second Temple Judaism and the Dead Sea Scrolls
**Era:** Contemporary (active 1970s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic Christian (Princeton Theological Seminary)

## Bio

James H. Charlesworth is a leading scholar of the New Testament and Second Temple Judaism, long associated with Princeton Theological Seminary, where he directs the Princeton Dead Sea Scrolls Project. He edited the standard two-volume collection *The Old Testament Pseudepigrapha* (Doubleday) — the reference set, [[Mike Day]] notes, that contains *First Enoch* and the *Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs* and is "the standard collection" owned by graduate students of the field.[^1]

## Contribution to the lecture

The entire [[The Stick of Joseph - The Ancient Meaning of the Serpent|Stick of Joseph episode on the serpent]] is built around Charlesworth's monograph ***The Good and Evil Serpent*** (Anchor Yale Bible Reference Library) — a 400-plus-page study the hosts describe as, at bottom, "a commentary on one verse," [[John 3.16|John 3:14]].[^2]

1. **The "void of silence" in the commentaries.** Charlesworth reports surveying virtually every commentary on John he could find — "thousands" — and discovering that nearly all of them, when treating [[John 3.16|John 3:14]], discuss Jesus being *raised up* while none notice that the verse has Jesus comparing himself to a *snake*. Charlesworth's point: modern "cultural blinders" make readers assume the serpent can only be evil, so they miss the comparison entirely.[^3] See [[John 3.14 Compares Christ to the Serpent Not Just the Lifting Up]].
2. **"Handmade midrash" / visual theology.** Charlesworth invites the reader to "get out of our modern world" and recover how the ancients *saw* the serpent by surveying the archaeology and iconography — temples, altarpieces, coins — rather than reading a five-minute summary. The hosts repeatedly cite his catalogue of "25 or 30" things the serpent could symbolize (power, healing, fertility, renewal, duality) and his examples (the orb-and-serpent monarch motif, Medusa at Ephesus, Asclepius and Hermes, the Capernaum mural of Christ enthroned holding a serpent).[^4][^5] See [[The Serpent on a Staff Is a Shared Ancient Patrimony of Healing]].
3. **The double entendre thesis.** Charlesworth argues the serpent "may symbolize opposites" — "the bearer of evil is neutralized and superseded by the bearer of the good," each symbolized by a serpent — which the hosts read as the scholarly underwriting of [[John 3.16|John 3:14]] and the Eden "two serpents" motif.[^6]

The hosts stress that Charlesworth, a non-Latter-day-Saint academic, draws no LDS conclusions; his survey is invoked as independent mainstream scholarship that recovers an ancient "good serpent" the Book of Mormon also preserves.

## Key Works

- *The Good and Evil Serpent: How a Universal Symbol Became Christianized* (Anchor Yale Bible Reference Library, 2010)
- *The Old Testament Pseudepigrapha*, 2 vols. (ed.; Doubleday, 1983–85)
