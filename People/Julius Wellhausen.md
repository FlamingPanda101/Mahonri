# Julius Wellhausen

**Who:** German biblical scholar and orientalist (1844–1918). Professor at Greifswald, Halle, Marburg, and Göttingen. Most famous for the Documentary Hypothesis (JEDP) of Pentateuchal composition and his *Prolegomena to the History of Israel* (1878).

**Role:** Wellhausen's work on the composition of the Hebrew Bible and the history of Israelite religion remains foundational — "we all are working in the shadows and in the following of Wellhausen," as [[Jacob Wright]] notes.[^1] His answer to *why* the Bible exists set the terms of debate for over a century.

## Wellhausen's "Why" Answer
Wellhausen's thesis, as characterized by [[Jacob Wright|Wright]], was that what emerged in Israel and Judah was a "transition to religion" — from a political community to what Wellhausen called "Judaism," a new artificial construct separating religion from the political sphere. Imperialism wiped out the space for individual nations to flourish, and a religious community ("cult") formed in response. The Bible is thus the scripture *of a religion* that replaced a destroyed nation.[^2]

## Wright's Critique
Wright argues Wellhausen conflated "nation" (the people) with "kingdom" (the state). What actually emerged from Israel and Judah's destruction, Wright contends, was *peoplehood* — a "state of mind" around shared narratives — not religion. Wellhausen's answer was shaped by his post-Napoleonic German context, where he wanted Germany to be a "spiritual nation" uniting religion and nature, and he used the Hebrew Bible as a cautionary tale of what to avoid (religion separated from political life).[^3]

## Legacy
Despite Wright's pushback, Wellhausen's documentary hypothesis and historical-critical methods remain foundational to modern biblical scholarship. Wright acknowledges this: "we all are working in his shadows."[[Data Over Dogma - Why the Bible Began with Jacob Wright|^1]] The debate is over Wellhausen's *why* answer, not his *how*.
