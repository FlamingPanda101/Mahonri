# Jordan Cooper

**Role:** Confessional Lutheran theologian, pastor, podcaster, and apologist; executive director of *Just and Sinner*
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2010s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Lutheran (American Association of Lutheran Churches); Protestant scholasticism

## Bio

Jordan Cooper is an ordained minister in the American Association of Lutheran Churches, executive director of *Just and Sinner*, and a fellow of systematic theology and chair of Lutheran dogmatics at the American Lutheran Seminary (Weidner Institute). He is working on a contemporary Protestant scholastic theology and presents as a recurring confessional-Lutheran voice in online apologetics. [^1]

## The Conservative Reformation

In Sola Media's *Defending the Reformation* roundtable, Cooper supplies the conversation's organizing category, drawn from the American Lutheran theologian [[Charles Porterfield Krauth]]: the magisterial Reformation was a *conservative* reformation that conserved the catholic heritage while correcting abuses, against both dead traditionalism and the "radical revolution" of the Anabaptists. He insists the secularizing forces of modernity (the papal schism, proto-capitalism, the rise of humanism) were already underway and that Luther was an "accidental reformer" acting under an external ecclesiastical call, not a self-appointed charismatic. See:
- [[The Conservative Reformation - Reform Between Traditionalism and Revolution]]
- [[Luther the Accidental Reformer With an External Call]]

## Papacy by Human Right vs Divine Right

Cooper deploys [[Philip Melanchthon]]'s distinction (from the *Treatise on the Power and Primacy of the Pope*) between holding that the bishop of Rome is head of the church by *human right* (organizational convenience) versus by *divine right* (necessary for salvation), arguing the Reformers rejected only the latter — and were expelled rather than voluntarily departing. He also notes the early Lutherans effectively founded the honest scholarly study of patristics. See:
- [[Refusing the Papacy Is Protest Not Schism]]
- [[The Church Before and After Trent Is a Different Institution]]

## On Household Baptisms and Infant Baptism
[[Trent Horn]] points to Cooper's extensive discussion (as a Lutheran who affirms infant baptism) of the New Testament "household" baptism texts — arguing that if "households being baptized" includes children, the same inference extends to infants. See [[The Argument from Silence Cuts Both Ways on Infant Baptism]].[^2]
