# Otto Berndt

**Role:** Latter-day Saint district president in Hamburg under Nazi Germany
**Era:** Nazi Germany (1930s–1940s)
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS)

## Bio

Otto Berndt was [[Helmut Hübner]]'s district president in Hamburg. [[Stephen Smoot]] draws on Berndt's unpublished, untranslated memoir (a copy in Church archives, portions of which Smoot translated) as a key witness to the church's predicament. When the Gestapo arrested Berndt — because Hübener had used a church-owned typewriter for his leaflets — he was interrogated for multiple days as a suspected collaborator. Earlier, under interrogation, Berndt had been pressed about the Church's tithing system, which the Gestapo wrongly suspected was a compulsory 10% levy.[^1]

## Consenting to the Excommunication

Though Berndt personally opposed excommunicating Hübener and could in no way be called pro-Nazi (he was himself a Gestapo target), he relented to the posthumous excommunication because he judged it would signal to the Gestapo — "breathing down the necks" of the Hamburg Saints — that Hübener acted alone, protecting the wider congregation. See [[Helmut Hubner's Excommunication Was a Local Survival Measure Later Reversed]].[^2]

## "Like Saul of Tarsus"

Smoot closes the episode with Berndt's own post-war reflection, which he treats as the moral key to the whole history: the Saints who believed in the ideals of the new Germany "did like [[Saul of Tarsus]] in persecuting the Christians. They did what they did because they believed it to be in the best interest of the church and the country. Repentance and forgiveness have been manifested by all concerned after the war." Smoot notes that if anyone had the right to bitterness toward fellow members who aligned with the regime, it was Berndt — yet he chose charity.[^3]
