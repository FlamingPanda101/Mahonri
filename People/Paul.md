# Paul

**Era:** 1st century CE
**Tradition:** Second Temple Judaism → early Christianity
**Role:** Apostle; author of the Pauline epistles

## Bio

Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is the author of the earliest substantial Christian writings, the undisputed epistles, including the [[The Stick of Joseph - How the Book of Romans Changes the Way We See the Gospel|Epistle to the Romans]] (c. 55-57 CE). [[Brian Thomassen]] stresses three under-appreciated facts that frame how Paul should be read: he was a **Roman citizen** (a status held by perhaps 5% of the empire's subjects); a **thoroughly Hellenized Jew** of the **tribe of Benjamin** who quotes Greek authors (Aratus, Epimenides, and — Thomassen argues — Seneca) off the cuff; and he wrote *in Greek* to Latin-speaking Romans, signalling a socially elevated, classically educated audience. Paul read the Old Testament in the **Septuagint** and belonged, with [[Philo of Alexandria]], to the Hellenized-Jewish stream centered on Alexandria.[^1][^2]

## On reading Romans as Greco-Roman rhetoric

Thomassen argues Romans must be read as a *rhetorical* and *dialectical* piece in dialogue with the Greco-Roman literary tradition, not as a doctrinal proof-text. Paul's "I" in Romans 7 is a **fictive "I"** (*prosōpopoeia*), a persona the reader inhabits, not autobiography; his core trope — "the good that I would I do not, but the evil which I would not, that I do" ([[Romans 7.19|Romans 7:19]]) — is a recognizable allusion to Euripides' *Medea*, the stock Greco-Roman example of *akrasia* (weakness of the will), the opposite of *enkrateia* (self-mastery). See [[Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography]], [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law]], and [[Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon]]. The vault separately documents that [[The Stick of Joseph - Are Christians Becoming Mormon with Brian Thomassen|the church under-reads Romans]] through an [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law|Augustinian lens]].[^3]
