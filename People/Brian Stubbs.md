# Brian D. Stubbs

**Era:** 20th–21st century
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint (LDS)
**Role:** Comparative linguist; Uto-Aztecan specialist

## Bio
Brian D. Stubbs is a Latter-day Saint linguist trained in Semitic languages and specialized in the **Uto-Aztecan** language family. He earned an M.A. in linguistics at the University of Utah and completed PhD coursework and comprehensive exams (ABD) in Hebrew, Arabic, and Aramaic, then spent his career teaching at the College of Eastern Utah and Utah State University.[^1] His *Uto-Aztecan: A Comparative Vocabulary* is regarded as a serious contribution to the field — Kenneth Hill reviewed it for the *International Journal of American Linguistics* as "a monumental contribution raising comparative Uto-Aztecan to a new level."[^2]

## The Semitic/Egyptian-in-Uto-Aztecan Thesis
Stubbs argues that Uto-Aztecan preserves a large, systematic layer of Hebrew, Aramaic, and Egyptian vocabulary — roughly **1,657 cognate sets** (~60% of his comparative corpus), which he reads as linguistic evidence for [[Book of Mormon]] peoples in ancient America.[^3] He frames the data through regular [[Sound Correspondences Distinguish Descent from Coincidence in Language|sound correspondences]] and reads its two-system split as a [[The Uto-Aztecan B-to-P and B-to-Kwa Split Maps the Nephite-Mulekite Merger|Nephite/Mulekite dialect merger]]. He concedes the thesis is **not** the consensus of Uto-Aztecanists, and has defended it against critics in a 44-point rebuttal on the Interpreter website. His most recent treatment is *Changes in Languages from Nephi to Now*; the work came to wider notice when [[John Sorenson]] forwarded an early draft to [[David H. Kelley]].[^4][^5] See [[Uto-Aztecan Contains a Large Semitic and Egyptian Lexical Layer]].
