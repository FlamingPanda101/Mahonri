# Epictetus

**Full Name:** Epictetus (c. 50 – c. 135 CE)
**Era:** Roman Empire (late 1st – early 2nd century CE)
**Tradition:** Stoicism
**Role:** Greek Stoic philosopher (formerly enslaved)

## Bio
Epictetus was a Greek Stoic philosopher who had been born into slavery and later taught at Nicopolis. His teaching survives in the *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion* (handbook), compiled by his pupil Arrian. That one of the great Stoic teachers was himself a former slave illustrates the Stoic claim that the ethical life is **open to all classes**.

## Cited For
[[Brian Thomassen]] cites Epictetus for Stoic parallels to the New Testament: "one must love the one who flogs him as if he were the father or brother" (paralleling "love your enemies"), and "even if you are not yet Socrates, you ought to live as one who wishes to be Socrates" (the **sage as exemplar**, paralleling the Christian imitation of Christ). His own enslavement supplies the backdrop for reading Philemon through the Stoic notion of the *adiaphoron* — that outward status is indifferent.
