# Jennifer Nagel

**Role:** Philosopher; epistemologist
**Affiliation:** Professor of Philosophy, University of Toronto
**Tradition:** Analytic philosophy (epistemology, philosophy of mind)

## Bio

Jennifer Nagel is a philosopher who teaches at the University of Toronto, working in epistemology and the philosophy of mind, including the nature of intuition and the psychology of knowledge attribution.[^1] In the spliced explainer used here she presents **intuition** as a neutral piece of general philosophy, drawing heavily on [[John Locke]].

## Relevance to Epistemology

Nagel characterizes intuition as **immediate, non-inferential judgment** — not necessarily fast, but unmediated by stages of thinking — about abstract, a-modal subject matter (geometry, number, causation, justice, knowledge). Following Locke she distinguishes intuition (direct: a circle is not a triangle) from demonstration (staged reasoning), and observes that every individual step in a demonstrative chain is itself intuitive.[^1] The Thoughtful Faith host uses this to argue that reason is *emergent from* more basic intuition.
