# M. David Litwa

**Role:** Scholar of New Testament and early Christianity; specialist in deification and the Greco-Roman religious world
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2010s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Academic biblical studies (non-confessional)

## Bio
M. David Litwa is a historian of early Christianity whose work concentrates on **deification (theosis)** in its Greco-Roman context. His monograph *We Are Being Transformed: Deification in Paul's Soteriology* (de Gruyter, 2012) argues that Paul's soteriology is a soteriology of *deification* — believers come to share the divine qualities (immortality and power) that constituted divinity in the first-century Mediterranean world.

## Power to Create Is Not Essential to Divinity
[[Dan McClellan]] quotes Litwa at length to rebut the objection that humans cannot be deified because, unlike Christ, they lack the power to create. Litwa's reply is that in the first-century Greco-Roman and Jewish worlds the two defining divine qualities were **immortality and power**, *not* the power to create — "none of the Olympian gods were creators," and "most gods in the ancient Mediterranean basin were not considered to be creators." On this analysis the existence of **created gods** and **non-creating gods** was "fairly staple theology" before the rise of *creatio ex nihilo* and strict monotheism in the 4th century CE. See [[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)]].
