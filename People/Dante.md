# Dante Alighieri

**Role:** Italian poet; author of the *Divine Comedy*
**Era:** Late medieval (c. 1265–1321), Florence
**Tradition:** Roman Catholic

## Bio

Dante Alighieri was a Florentine poet whose three-part epic, the *Divine Comedy* (*Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, *Paradiso*), is among the foundational works of Western literature. Written in the early fourteenth century in the Tuscan vernacular, it narrates the poet's imagined journey through hell, purgatory, and heaven. The *Inferno* in particular fixed an enormously detailed and influential picture of the afterlife in the Western imagination — concentric circles of torment, graded punishments fitted to particular sins, and vivid infernal geography.

## Relevance to the vault

Dante is cited as a key *post-biblical* contributor to the modern concept of hell. In [[English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell]], [[Dan McClellan]] argues that "hell" is an accumulated composite assembled from several inconsistent streams — Greco-Roman Hades, the [[Book of Enoch - Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation|Book of Enoch]], possibly Zoroastrianism — and "later by Dante and others that are giving us additional imagery." The point is that the popular image of eternal conscious torment in a lake of fire is not a single doctrine handed down whole in scripture but a synthesis finalized only after the Bible, with Dante supplying much of its enduring literary furniture.
