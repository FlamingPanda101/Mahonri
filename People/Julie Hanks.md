# Julie Hanks

**Role:** Licensed clinical therapist (LCSW, PhD); progressive/feminist commentator on the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2010s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint background; widely characterized as a progressive or post-/ex-Mormon critic

## Bio

Julie de Azevedo Hanks is a clinical therapist and prolific social-media presence known for feminist and mental-health-framed critiques of Latter-day Saint culture, especially around women's roles, perfectionism, emotional health, and ecclesiastical authority. In the video she is described as "a popular ex-Mormon therapist" whose Instagram posts the host "normally ignore[s]," responding only because one post went "after my Latter-day Saint sisters." [^1] In the clip that prompts the episode she argues that **LDS female apologists "only have a social media platform because of feminism," and that they "dismiss and discount the contributions of feminism"** — pointing out that "not long ago, women and particularly religious women were told to be quiet and submissive and not speak in the public sphere because that was reserved for men." [^2]

The host's strongest pushback is not against her historical premise (which he partly grants for *first-wave* feminism) but against her use of an undifferentiated "feminism": he counters that she does not reckon with feminism's later waves, and replies on the in-house front with [[Doctrine and Covenants 25|the 1830 revelation to Emma Smith]] commissioning a woman "to expound scriptures" and the founding of the [[Eliza R. Snow|Relief Society]] as evidence the Restoration valued women's public voice independently of secular feminism. [^3]

## Steelman of Her Position

Her core claim is historically defensible and worth stating at full strength: the *concrete social permissions* that let any woman — including conservative LDS women defending the faith online — speak, publish, vote, hold a job, or earn a degree were won largely by women's-rights movements, not handed down by the surrounding patriarchal culture of their own accord. On that narrow point she overlaps with mainstream historiography (see [[Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine]] and [[Modern Gender Equality Is Not Grounded in the Bible]]). Her implicit charge of inconsistency — benefiting from feminism while disparaging it — has bite if "feminism" is read as a single thing. As a credentialed therapist, her warnings about perfectionism and emotional suppression in religious settings also carry real clinical weight.

## Contribution / Why She Matters Here

Hanks functions in this episode as the representative of a **progressive, mental-health-inflected critique of LDS gender theology** — the recurring foil to the Church's complementarian account of men and women. The substantive exchange she provokes is captured in [[Womanism Is Misnamed Masculinism That Devalues the Feminine]] and [[Male and Female Balance Is Required for the Divine Image and Human Flourishing]].
