# Elisabeth Kübler-Ross
**Role:** Psychiatrist; pioneering researcher on death and dying
**Era:** 20th century (*On Death and Dying*, 1969; wrote the Foreword to *Life After Life*, 1975)

## Bio
Elisabeth Kübler-Ross, M.D., was the pioneering researcher on death and dying who, in her 1969 book *On Death and Dying*, dared to talk directly with dying patients about their feelings at a time when death "was something that was not discussed." She documented the now-famous emotional **stages of dying** — denial, isolation, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance — observations so countercultural that they "seemed to anger and outrage a healthcare establishment dedicated to saving lives, not facilitating death" [^1]. She exposed and opposed the "loving lie," the practice of withholding the truth of impending death from patients on the theory that it would rob them of hope [^2]. Melvin Morse credits her with making the cultural climate "ready" for *Life After Life* a decade later: "Dr. Kübler-Ross had introduced the 'shocking' concept that dying people had predictable feelings about death and often even accepted it. Ten years later, Dr. Moody explained why" [^3].

Kübler-Ross wrote the **Foreword** to *Life After Life* from Flossmoor, Illinois, having read a pre-publication copy. She reported that her own research "very much coincides" with Moody's: her patients who "died and made a comeback" described "a floating out of their physical bodies, associated with a great sense of peace and wholeness," and most "were greeted by loved ones who had died before them, or by a religious figure who was significant in their life." She predicted Moody would face criticism from clergy (one priest called such work "selling cheap grace") and from scientists who regarded the study as "unscientific," yet she commended him as "a genuine and honest investigator" [^4].
