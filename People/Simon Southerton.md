# Simon Southerton

**Era:** Contemporary (2000s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Former Latter-day Saint (former bishop); critic
**Role:** Geneticist (molecular/plant genetics)

## Who

Simon G. Southerton is a geneticist and former Latter-day Saint bishop who became, alongside [[Thomas Murphy]], one of the two best-known early-2000s critics pressing the DNA objection to the Book of Mormon. His 2004 book **Losing a Lost Tribe: Native Americans, DNA, and the Mormon Church** argued that mitochondrial and Y-chromosome data place Native American ancestry overwhelmingly in Asia, with no detectable Israelite contribution.[^1]

## In This Source

[[Neal Rappleye]] names Southerton (distinguishing him as "the one who's a geneticist," versus Murphy the anthropologist) when describing how the early DNA debate quickly migrated from genetics to *interpretation*: critics' arguments came to depend on a hemispheric, empty-continent reading of the text rather than on the genetic data alone. The qualified believing geneticists [[Ugo Perego]] and [[John Butler (DNA scientist)]] address exactly the mechanisms (founder effect, genetic drift, host-population admixture, bottlenecks) that, the hosts argue, neutralize the objection once the limited-geography reading is granted. See [[Founder Effect and Genetic Drift Erase a Small Founding Signal]] and [[The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate]].[^2]
