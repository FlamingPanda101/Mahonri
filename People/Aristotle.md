# Aristotle

**Full name:** Aristotle of Stagira (384–322 BCE)
**Role:** Greek philosopher
**Era:** Classical Greece (4th century BCE)
**Tradition:** Peripatetic (Aristotelian)

## Bio

Aristotle was a student of [[Plato]] and tutor to Alexander the Great. His works — spanning logic, metaphysics, physics, ethics, politics, biology, and poetics — formed the backbone of Western philosophy and medieval scholastic thought. His *Physics* and *Metaphysics* articulate the framework of matter and form that dominated Greco-Roman thought about creation.

## "Non-Being" to "Being" Framework

Aristotle's framework is essential for understanding the language used in 2 Maccabees 7:28, Philo of Alexandria, and other Hellenistic Jewish texts. In *Physics* 1.8–9 (191a–b):

- **Matter (ὕλη / *hylē*)** is eternal and ungenerated. It does not come into being or pass away.
- **"Non-being" (τὸ μὴ ὄν / *to mē on*)** is the privation of form in matter — not absolute nothingness. A piece of bronze is "not a statue" (non-being qua statue), but the bronze exists.
- **"Creation"** is the actualization of potential in pre-existing matter. Something comes into being when form is imposed on a material substrate — moving from non-being (formlessness) to being (formed).

As Dan McClellan summarizes:

> "Aristotle talks about the creation of things as a process of bringing them from non-being into being and then the destruction of things as a process of taking them from being to non-being. So this is par for the course when it comes to creation out of pre-existing material."[^1]

This framework — where "non-being" means formless matter, not nothing — is precisely the idiom used by 2 Maccabees 7:28 (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων) and Philo. The scholarly consensus that these texts teach creation from pre-existent matter, not from nothing, is grounded in understanding this Aristotelian/Platonic philosophical background.

## Rejection of Creation Ex Nihilo

Aristotle explicitly rejected the possibility of creation from nothing. For Aristotle, *ex nihilo nihil fit* — nothing comes from nothing. Matter is eternal, and all "coming into being" involves pre-existing material. This axiom governed virtually all Greco-Roman philosophical thought until Tatian and Theophilus broke with it in the late 2nd century CE.

## The Eternal Universe (al-Ghazali's Target)
Aristotle held that the universe is **eternal in the past** — it never began to exist. In [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]'s philosophical Kalam explainer, this is the very view the medieval theologian [[Al-Ghazali]] set out to refute: "Aristotle believed the universe was eternal in the past, but Al Ghazali disagreed," arguing that a beginningless universe would entail an actually infinite number of past events, which is metaphysically impossible.[^kalam] See [[Hilbert's Hotel - Why an Actual Infinite Cannot Exist]] and [[An Actual Infinite Cannot Be Formed by Successive Addition]].
