# Anselm of Canterbury

**Full name:** Anselm of Canterbury (Anselm of Aosta / of Bec)
**Role:** Benedictine monk, philosopher, theologian; Archbishop of Canterbury; Doctor of the Church
**Era:** 1033–1109 (medieval / early scholastic)
**Tradition:** Western Catholic; scholasticism

## Bio

Anselm of Canterbury was one of the most influential theologians of the medieval West, often called the father of scholasticism. He is famous for the ontological argument for God's existence (*Proslogion*) and for his satisfaction theory of the atonement, developed in *Cur Deus Homo* ("Why the God-Man"). His motto, *fides quaerens intellectum* ("faith seeking understanding"), captures his method.

## Cur Deus Homo and the Atonement Argument

[[Gavin Ortlund]] cites Anselm as his favorite theologian and invokes *Cur Deus Homo* to argue why the incarnation requires a God-man mediator: "only God can pay the debt, but only man needs to pay the debt," so the mediator must be both fully God and fully man. [^1] Ortlund uses this satisfaction logic to argue that the Trinity and the deity of Christ are bound up with how reconciliation between God and humanity is even possible. See:
- [[Trinity Is a First-Rank Salvation Issue]]

(Note: Ortlund characterizes *Cur Deus Homo* as "basically a commentary on the Council of Chalcedon"; this is his framing of Anselm's relevance to the God-man question, not a historical claim about the book's genre.) [^1]
