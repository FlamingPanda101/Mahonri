# Umberto Cassuto

**Full name:** Umberto (Moshe David) Cassuto
**Role:** Biblical scholar / rabbi
**Era:** 1883–1951
**Tradition:** Academic Jewish biblical studies (Hebrew University of Jerusalem)

## Bio
Umberto Cassuto was an Italian-Israeli rabbi and Bible professor whose *The Documentary Hypothesis and the Composition of the Pentateuch* (Hebrew lectures, 1941) became the landmark scholarly rebuttal of Wellhausen-style source division. He argued the criteria for separating sources (divine names, doublets, stylistic variation) are better explained by the conventions and rhetorical purposes of a unified ancient author. [[Mike Day]] cites him as the representative voice for "let the doublets sit there."[^1]

## Contribution to the lecture
Day invokes Cassuto as the named opponent who says **the doublets are just doublets** — repetition is a feature of Hebrew narrative art, not evidence of stitched-together sources, and the text can stand as a unity. "Cassuto says, 'Listen, the doublets are just doublets. Let them sit there.'"[^1] In the vault he functions as the steelman counterweight within the source-name and doublet arguments — e.g., the conservative reading of the divine-name distribution in [[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses]] and the harmonizing alternative to the doublets surveyed in [[Three Divergent Slave Laws Point to Separate Pentateuchal Sources]].

## Key Works
- *The Documentary Hypothesis and the Composition of the Pentateuch* (Magnes Press; Eng. trans. 1961)
- *A Commentary on the Book of Genesis* (2 vols.)
- *A Commentary on the Book of Exodus*
