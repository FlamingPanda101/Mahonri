# Gregory of Nazianzus

Fourth-century Cappadocian father (c. AD 329–390), one of the great Christian theologians who "helped to craft the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed." Known as "the Theologian" in the Eastern tradition; a contemporary of [[Basil the Great]] and [[Gregory of Nyssa]].

## Significance
Two key contributions in this chapter:
1. **Soteriological axiom:** "For that which [Christ] has not assumed [taken upon himself], he has not saved" — the principle that Christ had to take on full humanity to redeem humanity fully, used to defend the affirmation of Jesus's complete humanity. See [[Adoptionism and Docetism - The Two Christological Extremes]].
2. **Divine reason / theosis:** he taught that knowledge of God comes "when that within us which is Godlike and divine, I mean our mind and reason, shall have mingled with its like, and the image [of God] … shall have ascended to the archetype [God himself]" — linking the *Logos* in us (the light of Christ) to our capacity to know God.
