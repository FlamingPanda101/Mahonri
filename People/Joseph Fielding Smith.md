# Joseph Fielding Smith

Son of [[Joseph F. Smith]], tenth president of the LDS Church (1876–1972), and
church historian. Unlike his father, Joseph Fielding Smith was one of the two
figures "who more than anyone else emphasized the importance of the King Follett
Discourse and moving it more toward the center of LDS teachings."[^1]

## Champion of the Discourse

While serving as church historian, he included the discourse in his very influential
*Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith* volume — a collection that shaped LDS
understanding of Joseph Smith's theology for generations.[^1]

## Selective Editing

However, his version was not unfiltered. Park notes:

> "The portions like the infant exaltation were removed and the discussions of
> spirit birth were correlated into the mainstream LDS teaching."[^2]

This sanitized the discourse's most controversial elements while still elevating
it to prominence.

## Legacy

His son-in-law, [[Bruce R. McConkie]], continued his work — frequently citing the
discourse as the best LDS sermon ever preached and arguing it should be added to
the LDS canon (though McConkie disagreed with Roberts's footnotes on infant
exaltation and spirit birth).[^3]
