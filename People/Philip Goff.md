# Philip Goff

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Theist (formerly atheist)
**Role:** Professor of philosophy, Durham University

## Bio
Philip Goff is a professor of philosophy at Durham University, best known for his work on consciousness and panpsychism and on cosmic purpose. [[Alex Arnold]] cites him as a philosopher who "moved from atheism toward theism because the fine-tuning of the universe's physical constraints point toward a life-centered purpose that atheism can't account for."[^1]

## On Fine-Tuning and a Constrained God
What makes Goff matter to Arnold's thesis is the *kind* of God he moves toward: "Goff's move is specifically toward a God who works within mathematical law rather than above it, because the physical constraints are themselves the structure, not just free choices made from a blank slate." Arnold reasons that "a God who simply chose those constraints from unlimited alternatives faces the same arbitrariness problem as ex nihilo creation," and names this "the connection to LDS cosmology — eternal law that God works within rather than authors."[^1] See [[Finite Theism Is the Honest Answer to the Problem of Evil]] and [[Independent Philosophers Keep Reconstructing LDS Theology]].

## The Limited-Power God (Debate with William Lane Craig)
In a 2026 dialogue with [[William Lane Craig]] (hosted by [[Justin Brierley]]), Goff laid out the full position firsthand. Raised Catholic, atheist by 14, he returned through the fine-tuning argument toward "something godish" rather than the "omni-god," and identifies the non-negotiable core: "what makes it make intellectual sense to me... is God being limited in power."[^2] He spells out the mechanism — God is constrained to create a universe from a singularity with a fixed mathematical structure, has freedom to select the values of the constants, but "can't then fiddle thereafter" — and argues this "wonderfully simple" hypothesis explains both fine-tuning *and* suffering: "the only way God could create intelligent life is by creating a universe with the right numbers... God knew it was going to be messy... this was this or nothing."[^3] He puts the probability of an all-powerful loving creator at "less than 1%" given observed suffering.[^4] Goff adds two further commitments: a panpsychist/Russellian philosophy of mind (he retreats from "the conscious universe" toward a looser **panentheism** for his Christianity), and an Eastern-Orthodox-flavored, *visionary* resurrection identified with the ascension. Craig contests all of it — see [[A Person Cannot Be a Part of Another Person (Craig vs Cosmic Panpsychism)]], [[Counterfactuals of Creaturely Freedom Constrain Freedom Not Ground It (Goff vs Molinism)]], and [[God's Limited Power Restricts the Resurrection to a Visionary Ascension (Goff)]].

## In Dialogue with Mormonism (Hansen)
Goff later sat down with the Latter-day Saint apologist [[Jacob Hansen]] to map where his limited-God/panpsychist views agree and disagree with LDS theology.[^5] The two found striking kinship — both hold that *solving the problem of evil requires limiting God*, and Goff's panpsychism rhymes with the LDS doctrine of **intelligences** — but split on the metaphysics underneath. Three exchanges are distinctive to this dialogue: (1) Goff defends his limit as a **primitive fact about God's own nature** requiring no base reality, and argues one-necessary-being is *simpler* than the LDS substrate (see [[A Primitive Limit in God's Nature vs an External Eternal Constraint (Goff vs LDS)]]); (2) Hansen presses the **crucifixion dilemma** — any answer to "why the cross?" limits God — which Goff grants outright (see [[The Crucifixion Dilemma - Any Answer Limits God]]); and (3) most strikingly, Goff concludes that the ultimate God (the Father) is **not a mind and not conscious** — "God is the only thing I think is not consciousness" — the precise inverse of the LDS personal Father (see [[Goff - The Father Is Not a Mind, God Is the Only Non-Conscious Thing]]). On the resurrection Goff again denies a bodily rising (Easter = the first experience of theosis), against Hansen's emphasis on materiality.
