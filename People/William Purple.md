# William Purple

William Purple was a resident of the Bainbridge, New York area who claimed to have been present at [[Joseph Smith]]'s 1826 court proceeding and to have been asked by the justice of the peace, Albert Neely, to take notes.[^1] His recollection, published in 1877, is one of the five later sources bearing on whether Joseph was convicted.

## Account of the 1826 Trial

Purple's claim that Neely asked him to take notes raises the possibility that the so-called "Neely notes" are actually Purple's notes, or that Purple kept a separate set Neely used for comparison.[^2] Crucially, in his own published account Purple states that "there wasn't enough evidence to convict him and he was discharged" — directly contradicting the conviction line at the end of the published Neely notes.[^3] The conflict between two roughly contemporaneous (half-century-later) accounts is central to why the conviction question is inconclusive. See [[Joseph Smith 1826 Trial Conviction Question]].
