# Dale Tuggy

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Christian (biblical unitarian; non-Trinitarian)
**Role:** Analytic philosopher of religion (formerly SUNY Fredonia)

## Bio
Dale Tuggy is an analytic philosopher of religion who taught at SUNY Fredonia and authored the *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* entry on the Trinity. He is **not LDS** — he is a biblical unitarian who rejects the Trinity — and [[Alex Arnold]] cites him precisely because his conclusions, reached from outside the LDS tradition, "structurally" match LDS positions.[^1]

## On the Trinity and the Social Trinity
Arnold reports that Tuggy "has spent [his] career demonstrating that the Nicene formulation can't be made logically coherent without first importing assumptions that then generate new contradictions," and "has argued in peer-reviewed philosophy of religion that the social Trinity — meaning divine unity understood as relational rather than metaphysical — is the most defensible Trinitarian position available." Arnold's claim: "whether he knows it or not, what he's describing as the best option is structurally what the LDS Church has taught since 1830."[^1] See [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]].

## On the LDS Christ
Arnold also invokes Tuggy on Christology: "Dale Tuggy, analytic philosopher of religion, again, not LDS, has argued that the LDS description of Christ is actually more philosophically coherent and more consistent with the New Testament text."[^2]
