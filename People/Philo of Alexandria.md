# Philo of Alexandria

**Full name:** Philo Judaeus (Philo of Alexandria)
**Role:** Hellenistic Jewish philosopher and exegete
**Era:** c. 20 BCE – 50 CE (1st century)
**Tradition:** Hellenistic Judaism (Middle Platonic)

## Bio

Philo of Alexandria was the most important Jewish philosopher of the Hellenistic period. A member of a wealthy and prominent Alexandrian Jewish family, he produced an extensive corpus of philosophical and exegetical works that sought to harmonize the Torah with Greek philosophy — particularly Middle Platonism and Stoicism. His allegorical method of interpreting Scripture profoundly influenced later Christian exegesis (Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Ambrose).

Philo was contemporary with Jesus and the early apostolic period, making him an invaluable witness to the intellectual world of 1st-century Hellenistic Judaism.

## Philo and Creation Ex Nihilo

Despite Wes Huff's claim that Philo endorsed creation ex nihilo, the scholarly consensus ([[David Winston]], [[Gerhard May]], [[James Hubler]]) holds that Philo taught creation from **pre-existent matter** — consistent with his Middle Platonic philosophical framework.

### Key Evidence

**Philo's Dualism:** Philo posited TWO eternal principles — an active cause (God / Logos) and a passive principle (pre-existent matter):

> "At the very least one principle of being must be active, the other passive, and that the active cause is the universal mind, absolutely pure and unmixed, beyond virtue, beyond knowledge, and beyond the good itself and the beautiful itself. The passive principle, on the other hand, is in itself lifeless and motionless, but when moved, shaped, and quickened by reason, is transformed into the most perfect master work, this world."[^1]

**Matter Excluded from What God Created:** In *Quis Rerum Divinarum Heres Sit*, Philo lists the things God praises at the completion of creation — and matter is not among them. This implies matter is uncreated.[^2]

**Eternal Matter and Theodicy:** Philo makes eternal, intractable matter the source of difficulty and evil in the world — precisely because evil cannot come from God. If God created everything from nothing, Philo's theodicy collapses.[^2]

**"Non-Existent" = Formless Matter:** When Philo describes God "calling the non-existent into existence," this is standard Middle Platonic idiom: "non-existent" refers to formless, chaotic matter that lacks identity and function — not absolute nothingness. God's creative act transforms disorder → order, quality-less → quality-endowed, darkness → light — all operations ON pre-existing material.[^1]

**Reading Genesis Platonically:** Philo believed "Moses had anticipated Plato" by teaching in Genesis 1:2 that water, darkness, and chaos (the *tohu wa-bohu*) existed before creation. For Philo, this was the pre-existent passive matter awaiting divine ordering.[^1]
