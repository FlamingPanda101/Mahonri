# Robert Putnam

Harvard political scientist best known for *Bowling Alone* (2000), the landmark study documenting the long decline of American "social capital" — participation in civic, philanthropic, and religious organizations. In the interview, Dyer cites Putnam as the framework for understanding LDS retention loss as one instance of a society-wide collapse in connectedness, not a Church-specific failure.[^1]

## Bowling Alone and the Connectedness Curve

Putnam (per Dyer's summary) traced social connectedness from a low point in the 1880s — sensible post-Civil War — up to a dramatic peak around the 1960s, followed by a steady decline. His recent update to the book found the same downward trend continuing. Religion, Dyer argues, was "not spared" these general trends. See [[Decline in Religion Tracks the Broader Collapse of Social Connectedness]].

## The Hope Argument

Dyer leans on Putnam's historical curve for an optimistic counterpoint: because connectedness was low in the 1880s, rose, and fell again, there is "no reason to think that we're just going to keep going down." The cyclical pattern leaves room for reversal. See [[Demographic Indicators Predict a Bright LDS Future]].
