# Menahem Haran

**Full Name:** Menahem Haran
**Era:** 1924–2015
**Tradition:** Jewish (Israeli biblical scholar)
**Role:** Professor of Bible, Hebrew University of Jerusalem

## Bio

Menahem Haran was one of the foremost twentieth-century scholars of Israelite religion, the priesthood, and the temple cult. His major work, *Temples and Temple-Service in Ancient Israel: An Inquiry into the Character of Cult Phenomena and the Historical Setting of the Priestly School* (Clarendon Press / Oxford, 1978; Hebrew original 1972), is a detailed study of the priestly source (P), the tabernacle traditions, and the inner workings of the First Temple.

Haran is notable among critical scholars for dating the priestly source **before** the exile — tying P to the reforms of King [[Hezekiah]] much as the Deuteronomist (D) is tied to [[Josiah]] — a position [[Michael Heiser]] embraced and that runs against the dominant Wellhausenian late-dating of P (see [[Julius Wellhausen]] and [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins]]).

## Cited For

- The theory that the tabernacle as described is a "priestly utopia," a retrojection of the Jerusalem temple of Hezekiah's day — see [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms]].
- The argument that the linen priestly vestments make the wearer an angel and that the high priest's robes match the temple veil — engaged in [[The Garment Is the Veil of the Temple and the Body of the Lord]].
- The observation that the temple's inner room held **wine vessels** implying a forgotten bread-and-wine ordinance — see [[The First Temple Had a Hidden Bread-and-Wine Ordinance]].
