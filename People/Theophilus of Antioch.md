# Theophilus of Antioch

**Role:** Bishop of Antioch; early Christian apologist; originator of creation ex nihilo
**Era:** 2nd century CE (d. c. 183–185 CE)
**Tradition:** Early Christianity (Antioch)

## Bio

Theophilus was the 7th bishop of Antioch (the same see later held by Ignatius) and an early Christian apologist. His sole surviving work is *Ad Autolycum* (c. 169 CE), a three-book apologetic treatise addressed to a pagan friend, defending Christianity against Greek philosophical and religious critiques. Theophilus is notable as the first Christian writer to use the term "Trinity" (τριάς) and the first to explicitly formulate creation ex nihilo with precise philosophical language.

## Creation Ex Nihilo Formulation

Theophilus's *Ad Autolycum* contains the first explicit philosophical formulation of creation from nothing:

> "Theophilus of Antioch around AD 169 wrote that God made all things out of things that were not into things that are."[^1]

The key Greek phrase is ἐξ οὐκ ὄντων (*ex ouk ontōn*) — "out of things that are not." This is a deliberate reversal of the earlier formulation in 2 Maccabees 7:28 (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων — "not out of existing things"). Where 2 Maccabees said God did NOT make the world from pre-formed things (implying unformed matter), Theophilus says God made it FROM non-being itself (absolute nothingness). This is the precise linguistic mark of the doctrinal innovation.

Theophilus was arguing against the Platonic and Stoic consensus that matter is eternal and that creation consists of ordering pre-existent material. His assertion that God created from absolute nothingness was a direct philosophical challenge to the *ex nihilo nihil fit* axiom.

Along with [[Tatian]] (c. 160 CE) and [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (c. 180 CE), Theophilus is one of the three originators of the doctrine of creation ex nihilo. His formulation became the basis for the later Nicene affirmation of God as "maker of all things visible and invisible" — which includes matter itself.
