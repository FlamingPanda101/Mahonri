# William Lane Craig
**Role:** Philosopher of religion and Christian apologist

## Bio
William Lane Craig is an analytic philosopher of religion and one of the most prolific public defenders of Christian theism. In the 2009 Biola University debate he describes himself as "a professional philosopher" approaching the question of God "philosophically, from the standpoint of reason and argument," and his book *Reasonable Faith* is featured at the event.[^1] He is best known for reviving and defending the **Kalam cosmological argument** — that whatever begins to exist has a cause, that the universe began to exist, and therefore the universe has a cause that is "an uncaused, timeless, spaceless, immaterial being of unfathomable power."[^2] His standard cumulative case for theism comprises five strands: the cosmological argument, the teleological (fine-tuning) argument, the moral argument, the historical resurrection of Jesus, and the immediate experience of God as a "properly basic" belief.[^3] He holds that rational argument plays the role of a "servant" confirming what the believer knows through the inner witness of the Holy Spirit, a position [[Christopher Hitchens]] presses him on in the debate.[^4]

## Concedes Logos Christology Is a Platonic Relic
Though a committed Trinitarian, Craig grants that early apologists (Athenagoras, Justin Martyr, Tatian) "were misled by this logos christology that they inherited from middle platonism," and that the creedal "generation of the Son and the procession of the Spirit is a relic of logos Christology, which finds virtually no warrant in the biblical text and introduces a subordinationism into the godhead." Latter-day Saint apologists (Thoughtful Faith) cite this as a hostile-witness admission that an unbiblical Platonic element entered Nicaea — though Craig rejects eternal generation precisely to *defend* the co-equality of the three persons. See [[William Lane Craig Concedes Logos Christology Is a Platonic Relic]].[^5]
