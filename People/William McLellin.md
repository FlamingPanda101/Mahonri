# William McLellin

William E. McLellin (1806–1883) was an early member of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles whose relationship with the church was famously turbulent: baptized 1831, excommunicated 1832, returned and served a mission 1833, ordained an apostle in early 1835, briefly disfellowshipped that August and reinstated in September, formally withdrew in 1836, returned in 1837, and was excommunicated for the last time in 1838. He then affiliated with various Latter-day Saint breakoff groups until 1869, when he abandoned organized religion altogether.[^1]

McLellin is historically notable because he witnessed early church history firsthand, built relationships with many early leaders, and left journals and other writings recording what he thought of the church's truth claims before, during, and after his membership.[^2]

## Affirmed the Book of Mormon While Rejecting Leadership
Despite leaving the church multiple times, McLellin never repudiated the Book of Mormon. In an 1880 reply to [[James Cobb]] — who had hoped McLellin would help discredit the book — McLellin instead wrote: "I have set my seal that the Book of Mormon is a true, divine record... When a man goes at the Book of Mormon, he touches the apple of my eye." He told Cobb to "fight the wrongs of LDS-ism as much as you please, but let that unique, that inimitable book alone."[^3] He grounded this in firsthand testimony: while still investigating (1831) he interviewed [[Hyrum Smith]] for four hours, prayed, and concluded he "was bound as an honest man to acknowledge the truth and validity of the Book of Mormon."[^4] During the 1833 Jackson County persecutions he had a blunt exchange with witnesses [[Oliver Cowdery]] and [[David Whitmer]], who reaffirmed the book under threat of death, and he recorded the beating of eight-witness [[Hiram Page]], who refused to deny it.[^5] See [[William McLellin Affirmed the Book of Mormon While Rejecting Church Leadership]].

## Source of the Fanny Alger "Barn" Rumor
McLellin is also the origin point of the famous "barn" story about [[Fanny Alger]]. In [[Ward Radio - Debunking Joseph Smiths Fake Affair with Fanny Alger feat Don Bradley]], [[Don Bradley]] stresses this is a *late, second-hand* source: long after leaving the Church and after Joseph's death, McLellin reported a *rumor* that [[Emma Smith]] discovered Joseph and Fanny together one night, and said he later asked Emma to confirm it by nodding yes or no. Bradley warns against conflating McLellin's hearsay with [[Oliver Cowdery]]'s near-contemporary letter into a single "caught in the act" narrative. (The hosts distinguish William McLellin from the modern scholar Dan McClellan.) See [[The Oliver Cowdery Affair Letter Actually Says Scrape Not Affair]].[^6]
