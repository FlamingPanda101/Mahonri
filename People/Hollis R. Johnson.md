# Hollis R. Johnson

**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint
**Role:** Astronomer (professor emeritus), reviewer for the *FARMS Review*

## Bio
Hollis R. Johnson (PhD, University of Colorado, Boulder) is a professor emeritus of astronomy at Indiana University. By the article's account he "authored or coauthored about 90 articles in refereed journals and lectured at several universities in the United States and abroad."[^1] He acknowledges discussions with BYU physicist B. Kent Harrison and David H. Bailey (UC Berkeley), among others, in preparing the piece.[^1] He writes as a practicing scientist who is also a Latter-day Saint, and is therefore positioned to evaluate apologetic uses of cosmology from inside the field rather than secondhand.

## Contribution in This Article
In "[[Hollis Johnson - The Big Bang What Does It Mean for Us|The Big Bang: What Does It Mean for Us?]]" Johnson rebuts Paul Copan and [[William Lane Craig]]'s claim that the big bang proves creation from nothing and thereby refutes Latter-day Saint eternalism:

- He names the **[[The Aquinas Fallacy - Pinning Theology to Current Science|Aquinas fallacy]]** — treating current (and outdated) science as a final basis for theology — and shows Copan and Craig rely on the abandoned late-1970s standard hot big bang model.[^2]
- He argues that **[[Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow|quantum mechanics forbids a true singularity]]**, so the initial singularity the argument needs does not exist, and even a beginning of time would not entail creation from nothing.[^3]
- He distinguishes scientific "nothing" (the energetic quantum vacuum) from theological "nothing," and notes the **[[The Universe's Net Energy Is Zero So Creation Needed No Energy Input|universe's net energy is ~zero]]**, so the cosmos needed no energy input — undercutting ex nihilo from physics.[^4]
