# Jacob of Serugh

**Full name:** Jacob (Mar Jacob) of Serugh / Sarug (c. AD 451–521)
**Role:** Syriac preacher-poet, later bishop of Batnan in the region of Serugh
**Era:** Late 5th–early 6th century CE
**Tradition:** Syriac Christianity

## Bio
Jacob "preached in Syriac, an Aramaic dialect similar to the one spoken by Jesus." He "was educated in Edessa (modern Urfa in southeast Turkey) almost a century after Egeria visited that famous city," and was "one of the most prolific preachers in the early Christian world" — over four hundred of his metrical homilies survive, many on the Old Testament. [^1]

## Veil-of-Moses typology
Jacob held the maximalist Christocentric conviction that "there is not even one line in the Old Testament that does not proclaim the way of the Son." In his homily *The Veil on Moses' Face* (Exodus 34:29–35), the veil symbolizes that "the words of prophecy are veiled": the Old Testament hides Christ in types "so that the world might not become openly aware of the Son of God," and only Christ's coming "uncovered Moses' face." Heal also candidly notes the homiletic tradition's anti-Judaism, "foundational to Syriac Christianity." [^2]
