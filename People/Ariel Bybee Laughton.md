# Ariel Bybee Laughton

Latter-day Saint historian of early Christianity (PhD, Duke University), specializing in women's roles, asceticism, and church organization in the ancient church.

## Role in *Ancient Christians*
Author of Chapter 4 (in the PDF numbering), **"Church Organization: Priesthood Offices and Women's Leadership Roles."** Her argument: church offices (bishop, elder, deacon) were fluid and undefined in Paul's day and open to women (Phoebe the deacon, Junia the apostle, Lydia the house-church patron); women's leadership was gradually eroded as Greco-Roman gender norms and a Roman legal-rights model of office (Tertullian) entered the church and bishops consolidated patronage (Cyprian); new female orders (deacons, widows, virgins) brought women under bishops' control; and sexual renunciation became a path to female spiritual authority. She frames the trajectory as "not only a narrative of loss but … one of grace," with modern LDS developments "circling back." See [[Women Led the First-Century Church as Apostles Deacons and Patrons]], [[Greco-Roman Gender Roles Pushed Women Out of Church Office]], [[Tertullian Reframed Church Office as Roman Legal Privilege]], [[Bishops Consolidated Patronage and Displaced Women Patrons]], [[Orders of Female Deacons Widows and Virgins Contained Troublesome Women]], and [[Sexual Renunciation Gave Christian Women Spiritual Authority]].
