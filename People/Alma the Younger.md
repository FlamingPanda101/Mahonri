# Alma the Younger

**Era:** ~100–73 BC (Book of Mormon chronology)
**Tradition:** Nephite (Book of Mormon)
**Role:** Prophet, chief judge, high priest, missionary, theologian

## Bio

Alma the Younger is one of the most significant figures in the Book of Mormon.
He was the son of Alma the Elder, the founder of the Nephite church. As a young
man, Alma the Younger actively sought to destroy the church until he was
visited by an angel and underwent a dramatic conversion (Mosiah 27). He
subsequently became the first chief judge of the Nephite nation and later its
high priest, dedicating his life to preaching and theological instruction.

His major recorded sermons include:
- His discourse on faith as an "experiment" (Alma 32)
- The confrontation with Korihor the anti-Christ (Alma 30)
- His teachings on the plan of salvation to his son Corianton (Alma 39-42)
- The great discourse on the Atonement to the people of Gideon (Alma 7)

## Topics

### Teleological / Fine-Tuning Argument
In [[Alma 30.44|Alma 30:44]], Alma confronts Korihor, who denies the existence
of God and demands a sign. Alma's response is a design argument from cosmic
order:

> "All things denote there is a God; yea, even the earth, and all things that
> are upon the face of it, yea, and its motion, yea, and also all the planets
> which move in their regular form do witness that there is a Supreme Creator."

[[Hayden Carroll]] and [[David Butler]] note that this is "the original
teleological arguer way before St. Aquinas or any of the other early church
fathers who used it."[^1] See [[Fine-Tuning and the Teleological Argument from
Alma 30]] and [[Fine-Tuning - Book of Mormon Anticipation of Modern
Cosmology]].

### The Nature of Unbelief
Alma's exchange with Korihor is a case study in the psychology of atheism in
the Book of Mormon. Korihor's arguments — that religious belief is "the effect
of a frenzied mind" and "the traditions of [the] fathers" (Alma 30:16) — mirror
modern atheistic critiques (Freudian projection, cultural transmission of
belief). Alma's response combines evidential (design argument), testimonial
(prophets, scriptures), and pragmatic (the fruits of belief) lines of
reasoning.

### Faith as Experiment
Alma 32 contains the famous discourse comparing faith to a seed that must be
planted and nurtured — an experiential epistemology that [[David Butler (Stick
of Joseph)|David Butler]] connects to [[Presuppositional Apologetics - CS Lewis
Sun Analogy]].
