# Michael Behe
**Role:** Biochemist; Intelligent Design proponent
**Era:** Contemporary (b. 1952)

## Bio
Michael Behe is a professor of biochemistry at Lehigh University who holds an undergraduate degree from Drexel and a doctorate in biochemistry from the University of Pennsylvania.[^1] He is best known for coining the phrase **irreducible complexity** in *Darwin's Black Box*, arguing that molecular machines in the cell (which need all their parts at once to function) cannot be assembled by the "numerous, successive, slight modifications" Darwin's theory requires.[^2] In *The Edge of Evolution* he presses a quantitative case that random mutation and natural selection face exponentially long odds when more than one coordinated mutation is needed — illustrated by the trillions of malaria cells required to evolve chloroquine resistance via two mutations.[^3] On this panel he stresses that Darwinian processes can readily *break* genes (citing Richard Lenski's E. coli deleting their flagellum) but not build the elegant molecular machinery science keeps discovering; he also floats — against [[Stephen C. Meyer|Meyer]]'s objection — the idea that a "subtle God" could act through undetectable quantum events as a *tool* rather than a mechanism.[^4] His Lehigh colleagues publicly disclaim his ID views as non-science, an episode the panel cites as evidence of institutional hostility.[^5]
