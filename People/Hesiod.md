# Hesiod

**Full name:** Hesiod (fl. c. 700 BCE)
**Role:** Archaic Greek epic poet
**Era:** Archaic Greece (c. 8th–7th century BCE)

## Bio

Hesiod is, alongside Homer, one of the two earliest Greek poets whose work survives. His *Theogony* is the canonical Greek account of the genealogy and origin of the gods — "the guy who writes the Greek myths that you're familiar with," giving the origins of Zeus, Hera, Cronus, and the rest, as [[Brian Thomassen]] puts it.[^1]

## Significance for the Creation Debate

Thomassen uses the opening of the *Theogony* as a one-line proof that *no* ancient tradition — not even Greek myth — begins from absolute nothing. The poem starts, "Verily, at the first **chaos** came to be," and then the other gods follow: "there was already a chaos that existed before even the gods." This is the Greek instance of a universal ANE pattern — a primordial *something* (chaos, water, darkness) is always there to be organized; "chaos is never created." It is one more case showing creation *ex nihilo* has no home in the ancient world. See [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]].[^2]
