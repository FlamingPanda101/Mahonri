# Simon Conway Morris

**Role:** Evolutionary paleobiologist, University of Cambridge
**Era:** Contemporary (1951–present)
**Tradition:** Christian

## Bio

Simon Conway Morris is a British evolutionary paleobiologist long associated with the University of Cambridge and a Fellow of the Royal Society. He first became known for his work on the Cambrian-explosion fossils of the Burgess Shale, and later for his sustained argument that **evolutionary convergence** — the repeated, independent appearance of the same biological forms and functions — implies that evolution is channeled toward certain outcomes rather than being open-endedly contingent. As a practicing Christian, he is often cited in discussions of whether an evolutionary account is compatible with purpose and design.

## Role in This Source

In his interview with [[Jacob Hansen]], [[Samuel T. Wilkinson]] makes Conway Morris the linchpin of his case that evolution is non-random. Wilkinson reports that Conway Morris "has discovered these patterns where evolution seems to be going in certain directions and not others," and quotes his phrase that evolution is "a process that is seeded with inevitabilities" — "not just one random thing after another."
