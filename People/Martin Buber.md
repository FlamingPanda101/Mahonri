# Martin Buber

Austrian-born Jewish philosopher and theologian (1878-1965), best known for the short, dense work **_I and Thou_** (*Ich und Du*, 1923). Buber distinguished two fundamental stances a person can take toward the world: the **I-It** relation, in which the other is an *object* — used, analyzed, held at a distance, transactional; and the **I-Thou** relation, in which the other is met as a *presence* in genuine, mutual, reverent encounter. For Buber the God of the Abrahamic faiths is a **personal, covenantal, relational** God who is properly met as the Eternal Thou, never reduced to an It.

## Invoked in LDS Temple Apologetics
On [[Ward Radio - The Best Temple Prep Class You Never Had]], [[Michael Myrrono (OrthoCardia)|Michael Myrrono]] applies Buber's distinction to temple worship: covenant-making with God should be **I-Thou** — relational, reverent, an encounter of beloved with Beloved — and is "blasphemed" when treated as **I-It**, i.e. transactional ("obey so I won't be smitten," prosperity-gospel bargaining). He draws an analogy to sacred marital union being profaned by hookup/pornography culture, which likewise reduces a Thou to an It. See [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]].
