# Plato

**Full name:** Plato of Athens (c. 428–348 BCE)
**Role:** Greek philosopher
**Era:** Classical Greece (5th–4th century BCE)
**Tradition:** Platonic / Academic

## Bio

Plato was a student of Socrates and teacher of [[Aristotle]]. He founded the Academy in Athens and wrote philosophical dialogues that shaped Western thought. His *Timaeus* is the single most important Greek text for understanding ancient views of cosmogony — including the Jewish and Christian texts that engaged with Platonic ideas.

## The Timaeus and Creation from Pre-Existent Matter

In the *Timaeus*, Plato describes creation as the work of a divine Craftsman (Demiurge, δημιουργός) who:

1. Looks to the eternal Forms (the perfect, intelligible patterns)
2. Orders pre-existing chaotic matter (the "Receptacle" — χώρα / *chōra*) according to those Forms
3. Produces the cosmos as "a moving image of eternity"

The cosmos "came into being" (γέγονεν / *gegonen*) — but from pre-existing principles:
- The Forms (eternal, intelligible, perfect)
- The Receptacle (eternal, chaotic, formless, the "nurse of becoming")
- The Demiurge (who imposes order on the Receptacle)

Plato does NOT teach creation ex nihilo. The Demiurge is a craftsman, not a creator of matter. Matter is pre-existent and eternal; creation consists of bringing form to what is formless.

## Influence on Hellenistic Judaism and Early Christianity

Plato's framework profoundly shaped Hellenistic Judaism:
- **Philo of Alexandria** read Genesis through a Platonic lens, treating the *tohu wa-bohu* of Gen 1:2 as the pre-existent passive matter awaiting divine ordering. David Winston notes Philo "thought that Moses had anticipated Plato in this matter."[^1]
- **Wisdom of Solomon 11:17** explicitly says God created "out of formless matter" (ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης) — direct Timaeus vocabulary.
- **2 Maccabees 7:28**, in its original context, reflects this Platonic framework where "not out of things that exist" (οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων) means not from already-formed entities, but from the formless material substrate.

The doctrine of creation ex nihilo, when it emerged through Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus, was precisely a break WITH this Platonic framework — and a deliberate rejection of the Demiurge-as-craftsman model.
