# Sam Hardy

Latter-day Saint developmental psychologist and professor of psychology at Brigham Young University. His research centers on moral, religious, and identity development across adolescence and emerging adulthood — how young people form (and lose) a sense of purpose, values, and faith. He collaborates frequently with [[Justin Dyer]] on the social-scientific study of Latter-day Saint religiosity, retention, and well-being.[^1]

## Research on Deidentification and Well-being

With [[Justin Dyer]], Hardy led a nine-year longitudinal study (begun 2016) of 652 religious 12–14-year-olds from California, Arizona, and Utah, surveyed every two years on positive functioning (self-esteem, purpose) and negative functioning (sexual risk-taking, suicidal ideation). The team found that youth who left religion showed *increased* negative functioning and a declining sense of purpose — contrary to the relief many expect on leaving. See [[Leaving Religion Does Not Bring the Relief People Expect]] and [[BYU Daily Universe - Why People Leave Religion and What Happens Next]].[^1]

## On "High-Cost Religion"

Hardy interprets the steeper post-exit decline among those leaving the LDS Church through the concept of a "high-cost religion": the more commitment a faith demands, the more dramatic the impact of leaving it. See [[High-Cost Religion Makes Leaving More Costly]].[^1]

## On the "Rise of the Nones" and the Mental-Health Cycle

He frames the study against the growth of religiously unaffiliated Americans (single digits in the 1990s to ~30 percent) and describes a bidirectional cycle in which poorer mental health and loss of faith feed one another. See [[A Bidirectional Cycle Links Faith Struggle and Mental Health]].[^1]
