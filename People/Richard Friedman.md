# Richard Friedman

**Full name:** Richard Elliott Friedman
**Role:** Professor of Hebrew Bible / source critic
**Era:** Contemporary (Harvard PhD; active 1980s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic Jewish studies (University of Georgia; formerly UC San Diego)

## Bio
Richard Elliott Friedman is among the most widely read defenders of the Documentary Hypothesis, best known for *Who Wrote the Bible?* (1987) and *The Bible with Sources Revealed* (2003), which color-codes the Pentateuch by source. In [[Mike Day]]'s lecture he is one of the scholars Day says he has actually read ("I've read Wellhausen, and I've read Friedman, and I've read Carr and Baden"), and Friedman supplies two load-bearing points in the talk.[^1]

## Contribution to the lecture
1. **Dating P early.** Against [[Julius Wellhausen]]'s claim that the Priestly source was the *latest* stratum (exilic/post-exilic), Day cites Friedman's model that **P comes around 700–650 BC**, near [[Hezekiah]]'s reign rather than after the exile. Day flags that this dating matters for Latter-day Saints who read the Book of Mormon as literal history, because the late-dating of sources can create historical tension with a pre-exilic [[Nephi]].[^2][^3]
2. **The seven arguments.** Day works through a Friedman-style list of seven evidences for the Documentary Hypothesis — linguistic change (J/E use older Hebrew than D/P, so single authorship would be "like saying a valley girl wrote Hamlet"), distinctive terminology (Sinai vs. Horeb; Jethro vs. Reuel), different theology, narrative seams, and historical fit — praising Friedman's clarity for non-specialists.[^4][^5]

See [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration]], [[The P and E Sources Say Yahwehs Name Was Unknown Before Moses]], [[Internal Anachronisms Show Moses Did Not Write the Whole Pentateuch]].
