# Augustine

**Augustine of Hippo** (354–430 AD) was Bishop of Hippo, a Doctor of the Church, and arguably the most influential Latin church father. His works (*Confessions*, *City of God*, *On Christian Doctrine*) shaped Western theology for a millennium.

## How the Church Determined the Canon
In *On Christian Doctrine* (Book II), Augustine gives explicit **rules** for the "skillful interpreter of sacred writings" to know which books belong in the Bible: follow the canonical writings accepted by the *greater number* of churches, and especially by the churches of *greater authority* — those founded by apostles. [[Joe Heschmeyer]] cites Augustine as decisive proof that the early church determined the canon by **church consensus and apostolic authority**, not by [[John Calvin]]'s "search your feelings" method. See [[The Early Church Did Not Use Self-Authentication]] [^1].

> "He doesn't say, just search your feelings and you'll know it to be true... He's clearly acknowledging that there are discrepancies in which books are and aren't in different Christian canons" — and resolves them by church authority [^2].

## The Saeculum and the Western Concept of the Secular
[[Tom Holland]] traces the Western *concept of the secular* to Augustine's doctrine of the two cities / the *saeculum* (the doomed-to-pass span of earthly order), which institutionalized Jesus's "render unto Caesar" into the church/state distinction — the very category Holland says Islam lacks. See [[Christianity Has a Concept of the Secular Islam Does Not]].[^4]

## Infant Baptism — Clarified, Not Invented
[[Gavin Ortlund]] argues the picture of infant baptism "only really changes with Augustine" and his anti-Pelagian writings (c. 410). [[Trent Horn]] rebuts that Augustine *clarified and unified* an existing doctrine/discipline rather than originating it — proved by the fact that his opponent [[Pelagius]] *also* affirmed infants must be baptized. See [[Augustine Clarified Infant Baptism He Did Not Invent It]] and [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]]. (The Restoration cuts the root premise — inherited guilt — that drove the practice; see [[Infant Baptism Preceded Its Theological Rationale]].)[^3]
