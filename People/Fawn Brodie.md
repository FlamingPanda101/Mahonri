# Fawn Brodie

Fawn McKay Brodie (1915–1981) was an American biographer and historian (later a professor at UCLA), and a former Latter-day Saint. Her 1945 biography of Joseph Smith, *No Man Knows My History*, is the single most influential naturalistic account of [[Joseph Smith]] and the [[Book of Mormon]].[^1]

## Role in the View of the Hebrews Theory

Brodie is the hinge figure who popularized the claim that the Book of Mormon depends on [[Ethan Smith]]'s *View of the Hebrews*. She devoted "two or three pages" to it and wrote that "although a direct dependence cannot be proven, the striking parallels cannot be a coincidence." After 1945, anti-Mormon publications "all just like are citing Brodie," and modern critics largely "piggyback off of Fawn Brodie."[^2] Notably she also "curb-stomps" the rival [[Solomon Spalding]] manuscript theory as incredible — replacing it with *View of the Hebrews*. See [[Fawn Brodie Popularized the View of the Hebrews Theory]].

## Psychobiography and the Jefferson Case
Brodie called her own genre **psychobiography** and applied Freudian psychology in four of her five biographies. A second Ward Radio episode treats her as the prototype of conjecture-dressed-as-fact: in *No Man Knows My History* she asserted "there is some evidence that Fanny Alger bore Joseph Smith a child" while citing none. When she applied the same method to [[Thomas Jefferson]] (reviving the Jefferson–[[Sally Hemings]] paternity claim), the historian [[Gary Wills]] savaged the result, and the 1998 DNA "confirmation" was later walked back by its own lead researcher [[Eugene Foster]]. See [[Psychohistory Dresses Conjecture in the Veneer of Academia]], [[The Jefferson DNA Study Was Misreported as Proof]], and [[Anti-Mormon Pseudo-History Pioneered the Woke History Method]].[^3]
