# Melvin Morse
**Role:** Pediatrician; near-death-experience researcher
**Era:** 20th–21st century (active researcher from the 1980s; wrote the 2001 Preface to *Life After Life*)

## Bio
Melvin Morse, M.D., is a pediatrician and a "second generation" near-death researcher who credits Raymond Moody as "directly responsible" for his work in the field. He first met Moody while doing a fellowship in brain research at the University of Washington, where his primary interest was the effects of radiation and anticancer drugs on the brain; he had been moonlighting as a critical-care physician when he encountered pediatric NDE cases [^1]. With his research team at Seattle Children's Hospital, Morse conducted a **controlled clinical study of near-death experiences in children**, documenting that the experiences "are a real part of the dying process and not caused by drugs or a lack of oxygen to the brain." Strikingly, these children — "too young to fear death or to know what a near-death experience is" — "told the same stories as the adults tell in *Life After Life*" [^2].

Morse initially considered NDEs mere "fascinomas" (interesting but unimportant) and published his first papers only to add a solid scientific paper to his résumé; a three-day conversation with Moody changed his trajectory, leading him to think critically about NDEs and their implications for the nature of human consciousness [^3]. He authored the 2001 Preface to the reissue of *Life After Life*, surveying a quarter-century of scientific corroboration of Moody's findings [^4].
