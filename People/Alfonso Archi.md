# Alfonso Archi

**Role:** Epigrapher and Assyriologist; leading Ebla scholar
**Era:** 20th–21st century (active 1970s–2020s)
**Tradition:** Academic (Italian)

## Bio

Alfonso Archi was the second epigrapher assigned to the Ebla archives after their discovery at Tell Mardikh, Syria in 1975. While [[Giovanni Pettinato]] (the first epigrapher) published sensationalist readings linking Ebla to the Hebrew Bible, Archi undertook the painstaking work of systematic epigraphic analysis and publication. His careful scholarship systematically refuted Pettinato's apologetic claims and established the scholarly standard for Ebla studies.

## Refutation of Pettinato

Archi published *The Epigraphic Evidence from Ebla and the Old Testament*, which dismantled Pettinato's claims point by point. Mark Chavalas summarizes:

> "The second epigrapher, Archi, spent some effort in refuting many of the premises of Dahoud and Pettinato, ignoring most of the sensationalism, primarily from the American press. He argued that the supposed evidence for Adonai at Ebla was questionable and ambiguous, and that the kings of Ebla were not anointed like the kings of Israel... There is no Genesis creation story."[^1]

### On TM.75.G1682

Archi's analysis of the text Pettinato translated as affirming creation is devastating:

> "Now this is a Sumerian composition very difficult to understand. TM.75.G1682 runs in four columns... After the first three words, which mean 'king of heaven and earth,' a well-known Sumerian epithet, the text is quite incomprehensible."[^2]

Archi found Pettinato's philological derivations unsupported:

- Pettinato derived a Sumerian word from Hebrew *shakar* / Arabic *sahar* (dawn) — "an explanation not requiring comment" (i.e., asserted without argumentative basis)[^3]
- Archi demonstrated that if certain orthographic conventions of the Ebla scribes are accounted for, column 3 of the text reads very differently from Pettinato's rendering[^2]

Archi's alternate reading: "Lordship was not in existence and night abundance was not in existence. City was not in existence"[^2] — a standard ANE cosmogonic catalogue, not a creation ex nihilo affirmation.

## Legacy

Archi's epigraphic rigor established that Ebla is fascinating on its own terms — as an incipient Old Syrian civilization at the advent of urbanism — rather than as apologetic ammunition for the Bible. The field has moved decisively in Archi's direction.
