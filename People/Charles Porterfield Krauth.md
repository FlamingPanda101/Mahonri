# Charles Porterfield Krauth

**Role:** Confessional Lutheran theologian, churchman, and educator; vice-provost of the University of Pennsylvania
**Era:** 19th century (1823–1883), American
**Tradition:** Lutheran (Old Lutheran / confessional revival); Protestant

## Bio

Charles Porterfield Krauth was a leading American Lutheran theologian of the nineteenth century and the foremost intellectual architect of the confessional ("Old Lutheran") revival in the United States. He is best remembered for his 1871 work *The Conservative Reformation and Its Theology*, which argued that the magisterial Reformation was not a radical break but a *conservative* renewal — preserving the catholic substance of the faith while correcting later abuses. He helped found the Lutheran Theological Seminary at Philadelphia and served as a professor there, and was also vice-provost of the University of Pennsylvania. His thought shaped the General Council of Lutheran churches and the broader recovery of confessional Lutheran identity.

## Relevance to the vault

Krauth supplies the organizing category for the vault's discussion of whether the Reformers were reformers or revolutionaries. [[Jordan Cooper]], drawing explicitly on Krauth's phrase "the conservative reformation," frames the magisterial Reformation as steering between two errors — *dead traditionalism* that permits no change and *radical revolution* that says "everything is just change" and starts over each generation. In [[The Conservative Reformation - Reform Between Traditionalism and Revolution]] this spectrum is the foil for the Latter-day Saint counter-claim: that the corruption was deep enough to require not conserving-and-correcting a surviving institution but a *restoration* of priesthood authority — a fourth option Krauth's spectrum omits.
