# James Talmage

**Full name:** James Edward Talmage (1862–1933)
**Role:** Apostle of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints; theologian and author
**Era:** Late 19th – early 20th century
**Tradition:** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS / Mormon)

## Bio

James E. Talmage was an LDS apostle and one of the most influential systematic theologians of early-20th-century Mormonism. Though academically trained as a geologist, his lasting influence is doctrinal, through works such as *Jesus the Christ* and *The Articles of Faith*. He played a central role in formalizing official LDS Christology.

## Divine Investiture of Authority

[[Dan McClellan]] identifies Talmage as "probably responsible for the primary configuration / articulation" of the doctrine of [[Divine Investiture of Authority Is an Anachronistic LDS Harmonization|divine investiture of authority]] — the idea that a sovereign can endow a representative to speak on his behalf and even *as* him. McClellan points specifically to Talmage's 1916 First Presidency–Quorum of the Twelve doctrinal exposition **"The Father and the Son"**, which he characterizes as an attempt to systematize how Latter-day Saints should understand Jesus' relationship to God across the Book of Mormon, the Bible, and the temple ceremony — particularly to harmonize LDS non-Trinitarianism with Book of Mormon passages where Jesus speaks as the Father. McClellan argues the framework "doesn't really work incredibly well," leaning "on a lot of authority rather than logic and data." [^1]

## The Michigan Relics as Faithful Skepticism

Talmage is cited in [[Ward Radio - Fake Book of Mormon Artifacts]] as a model of faithful skepticism: applying his scientific training as a geologist, he obtained Michigan Relic specimens, tested them in his Deseret Museum laboratory (c. 1911), observed the freshness of saw and tool marks, and concluded the artifacts were **frauds**. The hosts note the irony — and the integrity — that two of the best debunkers of forged "Book of Mormon" artifacts (Talmage and the LDS archaeologist Richard Stamps) were themselves Latter-day Saints. See [[The Michigan Relics Are 100 Percent Forgeries]] and [[Defending Faith Requires Ruling Out the Fakes]].
