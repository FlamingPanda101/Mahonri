# Pelagius

A British monk (active c. AD 380–420) who went to Rome to study law and became renowned for his asceticism, oratory, and "great moral standing." He fled Rome ahead of the Visigoths in 410, moving to Africa and then Palestine, where he died.

## Significance
The chief opponent of [[Augustine]] on human nature. Pelagius "taught that human nature is fundamentally good and that sin only comes about through personal actions" — denying inherited corruption or guilt. His followers carried on his teaching after his death. Pelagianism was condemned at the First Council of Ephesus (AD 431). He functions in the narrative as the foil whose defeat secured Augustine's doctrine of [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development|original sin]] as Western orthodoxy. (Notably, the LDS rejection of inherited guilt overlaps with Pelagius on that specific point, though not on grace.)
