# Aristides of Athens

**Role:** Early-2nd-century Christian apologist
**Era:** 2nd century CE (Apology reportedly delivered c. AD 124–130)
**Tradition:** Early Christianity

## Bio

Aristides was an Athenian philosopher and one of the earliest Christian apologists; his *Apology* was reportedly addressed to the emperor Hadrian (or Antoninus Pius) around AD 130. [[Blake Ostler]] discusses him because Copan and Craig claim Aristides "expressly taught" creation out of nothing — a claim Ostler calls "seriously flawed" and bordering on "irresponsible."[^1]

## Position on Creation (and the textual problem)

Ostler first notes the **textual uncertainty** Copan and Craig omit: the *Apology* survives in a shorter Greek recension, a longer Syriac version, and Armenian translations, with very late attestation for the Greek.[^2] The disputed line (Greek recension only) says the elements were "produced out of that which did not exist (ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος) at the command of the true God."[^3] Ostler argues the phrase ἐκ τοῦ μὴ ὄντος is precisely Aristotle's language for **relative non-being** — generation from a formless substrate — not absolute nothing; "the elements" (not "matter") are simply what is perishable and changeable as against the incorruptible God. There is no two-stage creation in the text. He quotes [[Gerhard May]]: Aristides "does not appear... [to] consciously distance himself from the philosophical model of world-formation."[^4] See [[Relative Non-Being vs Absolute Nothing - The Greek Distinction Copan and Craig Miss]].
