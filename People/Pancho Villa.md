# Pancho Villa

**Pancho Villa** (born Doroteo Arango, 1878-1923) was a general of the **Mexican Revolution** who controlled much of northern Mexico, briefly held the presidency, and was assassinated in 1923. He operated in the regions where Latter-day Saint colonies (the Mexican colonies near the U.S. border) were established.

## Bio / Significance
In Latter-day Saint folk memory and family history Villa recurs through encounters with the Mormon colonists. The [[Ward Radio - The Secret Mormon Exploits of Pancho Villa|Ward Radio episode]] (drawing on a 2019 *Meridian Magazine* article by Anne Hinton Pratt and on [[Luke Hansen]]'s wife's family history) recounts: Villa raiding and burning colonist property; a colonist great-grandmother chasing him off by seizing her horses' reins and telling his armed posse "you can ride a pig"; and a documented incident in which mission president **[[James Bevan Weetman]]** and a stake president (Bentley) were captured as suspected spies, put on a sham trial, and instead spent hours teaching Villa the gospel — Villa reportedly saying the missionaries "are doing with words and with books what we are trying to do with guns."

## Contribution
The episode's payload is the **posthumous-visitation and temple-work** claim: roughly five decades later (early 1960s) Weetman is said to have woken to see Villa standing at the foot of his bed, asking help to enter "a beautiful city" he could not enter alone; Weetman then obtained First Presidency authorization and performed Villa's (Doroteo Arango's) temple work at the Mesa Arizona Temple. The hosts fold this into the broader Latter-day Saint doctrines of **vicarious work for the dead** and post-mortal repentance. See [[Pancho Villa's Posthumous Visitation and Temple Work]].
