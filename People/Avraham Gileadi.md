# Avraham Gileadi

**Era:** Contemporary (b. 1940, Maastricht, Netherlands)
**Tradition:** Latter-day Saint (LDS); former Catholic, former convert to Judaism
**Role:** Isaiah scholar; Hebraist; translator

## Bio
Avraham Gileadi is a Hebrew-fluent scholar of the book of Isaiah whose life moved through Dutch Catholicism, an Israeli kibbutz, and formal conversion to Judaism before he was baptized into the [[Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints]] in Jerusalem.[^1] Born in 1940 in Maastricht in the south of Holland, his earliest memories were of World War II — planes crashing, his street bombed, his priest-uncle killed by a shell while ministering to the dying — and his father's work in the Dutch underground helping New Zealand pilots escape, which led the family to emigrate to New Zealand when he was about ten.[^2] A devout Catholic [[altar boy]] taught English by monks, he briefly entered a Catholic seminary, left it as "too much ritual and not enough holy spirit," led a rock-and-jazz band ("the Spotlights"), and then — convinced the Jewish return to Israel was fulfilling prophecy — emigrated to Israel at 27.[^3]

On a kibbutz in the Jezreel Valley he learned Hebrew and was handed a Book of Mormon by a Jewish librarian (a South-African immigrant) who insisted "this book is for you" — the conversion-trigger he tells as his central story.[^4] Directed by the Spirit to "learn Judaism, the law of Moses, [the] schoolmaster for the gospel," he formally converted to Judaism (mikvah, symbolic circumcision, Israeli citizenship), kept a [[Nazarite vow]] (long hair and beard), and studied Isaiah under a rabbi at a Jerusalem yeshiva — where the rabbi taught the tradition that became central to his scholarship: that Isaiah applies to Isaiah's day and the end time simultaneously.[^5] He was baptized in the Pool of Siloam by [[David Galbraith]]'s Israel group, "the first [Jewish citizen] that I know of" baptized in Israel before a law barred it.[^6]

## Isaiah Scholarship
Gileadi came to Utah in 1973 and BYU in 1975, where he began a serious translation and structural study of Isaiah under his mentor, religion dean Ellis Rasmussen.[^7] His first book was *The Book of Isaiah: A New Translation with Interpretive Keys from the Book of Mormon*; his second, *The Last Days: Types and Shadows from the Bible and Book of Mormon*; and his doctoral and post-doctoral work (a decade on a literary structure discovered by [[William Brownlee]] in the Dead Sea Isaiah scroll) culminated in *The Literary Message of Isaiah*.[^8] His thesis — see [[Isaiah Is Apocalyptic and Applies Simultaneously to Isaiahs Day and the End Time]] — is that holistic literary structures spanning the whole book transform Isaiah into a single end-time prophecy and refute the [[Deutero-Isaiah Theory|Deutero-Isaiah]] division.[^9] He runs the Isaiah Institute (isaiahexplained.com) and its apocalyptic verse-by-verse commentary.[^10]

He also revised the LDS Church's Hebrew translation of the Book of Mormon (after a draft by Hebrew University adjunct Jonathan Shunary), reporting that the work confirmed the book's "very sophisticated" Hebraisms — dozens of chiasms (including one spanning the whole book of Mosiah and culminating in [[Isaiah 53]]), classic Hebrew figurative language in [[Abinadi]]'s dialogue, and an ancient Near Eastern vassal-treaty covenant theology.[^11] See [[Latter-day Saints Are the Ephraimite Gentiles in the Book of Mormon]] and [[Joseph Smith Added Phrases to Isaiah to Clarify the King James English]].
