# Luke Irwin

**Full name:** Luke Irwin
**Role:** New Testament scholar
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2020s)
**Tradition:** Academic

## Bio

Luke Irwin is a New Testament scholar whose recent publication *Jesus and the Visibility of God: Sight and Belief in the Fourth Gospel* examines how the Gospel of John navigates the tension between God's invisibility (John 1:18: "no one has ever seen God") and the claim that seeing Jesus constitutes seeing God (John 14:9: "he that hath seen me hath seen the Father").

Dan McClellan cites Irwin's work as part of the scholarly literature demonstrating how Jesus functions as a "divine image" in John — a visible mediator who bears God's presence without being identical to God: "Luke Irwin's recent publication, Jesus and the Visibility of God, Sight and Belief in the Fourth Gospel, that talks about how Jesus can help people see God in the Gospel of John." [^1]

## Divine Image Logic in John

Irwin's work is relevant to the [[Pre-Incarnate Christ Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]]. McClellan uses Irwin's "divine image" framework to explain that seeing Jesus is seeing God in the same way one "sees" a king by looking at his statue — the image BEARS the presence but is not identical to the person. This is fundamentally different from the OT theophanies where YHWH is encountered directly, face to face. Irwin's scholarship clarifies that John's "see Jesus = see God" logic operates on ancient divine-image premises incompatible with the direct divine encounters of the Hebrew Bible.
