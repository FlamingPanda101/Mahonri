# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz
**Role:** Rationalist philosopher and mathematician
**Era:** 1646–1716

## Bio
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was a German polymath of the early-modern rationalist school (alongside Descartes and Spinoza) and co-inventor of the calculus. He is most famously associated with the **principle of sufficient reason (PSR)** — the thesis that everything that exists has an explanation, whether in itself or in another. [[Edward Feser]] notes that PSR "is most famously associated with the early modern rationalist philosopher G. W. Leibniz, but has been formulated in many ways by writers of diverse philosophical commitments." [^1]

Leibniz (together with Samuel Clarke) advanced the version of the cosmological argument that Feser develops as the "rationalist proof": even granting an infinite series of contingent things each caused by a prior one, "there is still the question of why the series itself exists at all," since the series is just as contingent as its members. [^2] Feser borrows Leibniz's illustration of an infinite series of geometry books, each copied from a prior one — even with an immediate cause for each book, the question of why the *content* is geometry (rather than Shakespeare or a repair manual) remains unexplained. [^3] Leibniz drew this from his 1697 essay "On the Ultimate Origination of Things." Feser is careful to add that his argument requires only Leibniz's commitment to PSR, not the broader rationalist doctrines (innate ideas, all knowledge a priori) historically defended by Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz. [^4]
