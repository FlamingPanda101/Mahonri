# J.L. Schellenberg

**Full Name:** John L. Schellenberg
**Era:** Contemporary
**Tradition:** Non-theist / philosopher of religion
**Role:** Analytic philosopher

## Bio

J. L. Schellenberg is the contemporary philosopher best known for formulating the **argument from divine hiddenness** (developed at length in his 1993 *Divine Hiddenness and Human Reason*). The argument contends that a perfectly loving God would ensure that every non-resistant person is in a position to relate to him — and therefore would not permit *reasonable* (inculpable) nonbelief; since such nonbelief plainly occurs, no perfectly loving God exists.[^1]

## Topics

### The Argument from Divine Hiddenness
As reconstructed by [[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]], Schellenberg's argument runs: (P1) if God exists he is perfectly loving; (P2) if a perfectly loving God exists, reasonable nonbelief does not occur; (P3) reasonable nonbelief occurs; therefore no perfectly loving God — and hence no God — exists.[^1] Schellenberg grants that what love requires is only *evidence sufficient for belief*, which "need not infringe" moral freedom; his complaint is that God has hidden himself enough that many cannot even make a reasonable choice for or against him.[^2] See [[Schellenberg's Hiddenness Argument and the Christian Reply]].

### Biblical Counter-Examples Objection
Schellenberg further objects that Scripture itself records belief being *forced* on people (e.g., Paul/Saul, Balaam) who nonetheless disobeyed or rejected God — so, he argues, giving everyone forced belief would not interfere with sanctification. Jones (via Michael Murray) replies that this assumes everyone responds identically; God grants such powerful revelations only to those who can receive them and still remain free, so the theist should *expect* exactly such cases while denying they would be common.[^3] See [[Forced Belief Would Corrupt Our Motives and Defeat Soul-Making]].
