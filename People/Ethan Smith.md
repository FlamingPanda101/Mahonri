# Ethan Smith

Ethan Smith (c. 1762–1849) was a Congregational reverend, domestic missionary, reformer, and prolific author, best known today — though not in his own lifetime — for *View of the Hebrews* (1823; 2nd ed. 1825). Born around 1762, he fought in the Revolutionary War and was reportedly at West Point when Benedict Arnold attempted to betray it to the British. He studied theology and classics at Dartmouth ("a learned, bookish" man), became deeply religious under the influence of the First Great Awakening, his mother, and a local pastor, and pastored Congregational churches across Vermont, New York, New Hampshire, and Massachusetts — including Poultney, Rutland County, Vermont.[^1]

In his own day he was most famous not for *View of the Hebrews* but for a dissertation interpreting the prophecies of Daniel and Revelation (a "key to the prophecies"); his obituary highlighted that work. He publicly feuded in the press with the millennialist William Miller over end-times prophecy.[^2]

## View of the Hebrews and the Hebraic-Indian Theory

As a domestic missionary concerned with the welfare of marginalized peoples (including African American slaves, orphans, and Native Americans), Ethan Smith wrote *View of the Hebrews* to argue that Native Americans descend from the ten lost tribes of Israel — a missionary-anthropological tract, not scripture or fiction. See [[View of the Hebrews Is Not Scripture or a Novel]]. His argument drew on the broader [[Hebraic-Indian theory]] also advanced by contemporaries like Elias Boudinot, James Adair, Mordecai Manuel Noah, and [[Josiah Priest]]. See [[The Hebraic-Indian Theory Was a Common Period Idea]].

## Alleged Connection to the Book of Mormon

Critics from [[Fawn Brodie]] onward have argued [[Joseph Smith]] plagiarized the [[Book of Mormon]] from Ethan Smith. Notably, Ethan Smith — a living, combative author — never accused Joseph Smith of theft. See [[No Contemporary Accusation of Plagiarism]] and [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]].
