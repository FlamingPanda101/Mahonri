# Arius

**Who:** Christian presbyter/priest in Alexandria (c.256–336 CE). Central figure in the Arian controversy that led to the Council of Nicaea (325 CE).

**Position:** Arius taught that Jesus/the Son was **subordinate** to God the Father and that **"there was a time when Jesus did not exist"** — i.e., the Son is a creation of God, not co-eternal. This was a common view in the 3rd century CE but became increasingly problematic by the early 4th century.[^1]

**At Nicaea:** Arius and a few supporters were in the minority at the Council of Nicaea. They were overruled, and Arius was exiled. Tradition holds that [[St. Nicholas]] (Santa Claus) slapped Arius at the council.[^2] The Nicene Creed's language of *homoousios* ([[Consubstantiality|consubstantial]]) was formulated in direct opposition to Arianism.

**Significance:** Arius represents a pre-Nicene Christianity where Jesus's subordination to the Father was widely accepted. His defeat marks the institutional enforcement of the [[Trinity - Historical Development|Trinity doctrine]] via imperial power.
