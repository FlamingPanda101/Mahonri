# Giovanni Pettinato

**Role:** Assyriologist and epigrapher; original decipherer of the Ebla tablets
**Era:** 20th century (active 1970s–1990s)
**Tradition:** Italian academic; apologetically motivated readings of Ebla

## Bio

Giovanni Pettinato (1934–2011) was an Italian Assyriologist who served as the first epigrapher assigned to the Ebla tablets after their discovery at Tell Mardikh, Syria in 1975. He had privileged early access to the tablets and published initial translations that generated enormous excitement — particularly among biblical apologists — for their apparent connections to the Hebrew Bible. However, his readings were soon challenged by the second epigrapher assigned to the archive, [[Alfonso Archi]], and the broader scholarly community.

## The Ebla Controversy

Pettinato's *The Archives of Ebla: An Empire Inscribed in Clay* (English translation, 1981) made several claims that scholars immediately rejected:

- That Sodom and Gomorrah appear in the Ebla archives
- That Eblaite kingship installation rituals parallel the Hebrew Bible
- That the divine name *Adonai* appears at Ebla
- That Ebla provides evidence for early monotheism
- That TM.75.G1682 affirms creation ex nihilo — see [[Pettinato Ebla Translation Unreliable]]

Dan McClellan describes Pettinato's book as "notorious for trying to argue apologetically that the Ebla archives are basically a precursor to the Old Testament."[^1]

## Translation of TM.75.G1682

Pettinato's translation of TM.75.G1682 — the text Wes Huff cited as evidence for creation ex nihilo — rendered the Sumerian composition as a "hymn to the Lord of heaven and earth" affirming divine creation. Alfonso Archi's subsequent epigraphic analysis found the text "quite incomprehensible" beyond its opening words and offered a radically different reading — see [[Ebla Archives Do Not Support Creation Ex Nihilo]].

## Scholarly Rejection

Mark Chavalas, summarizing the field's mature assessment in *Mesopotamia and the Bible: Comparative Explorations* (2003/2004):

> "The second epigrapher, Archi, spent some effort in refuting many of the premises of Dahoud and Pettinato, ignoring most of the sensationalism... The excitement concerning the Ebla material has somewhat died down... In the past 15 years, only a minuscule amount of effort has been put to comparisons of Ebla and the Bible compared to the large amount of work on the civilization of Ebla itself. This is preferable."[^2]

## Legacy

Pettinato's translations continue to circulate in apologetic circles (via [[Norman Geisler]] and others) as evidence for creation ex nihilo and biblical monotheism. In mainstream scholarship, his work on Ebla is treated as an early, overly enthusiastic phase of interpretation that has been superseded by more careful epigraphic work.
