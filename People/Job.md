# Job

**Role:** Patriarch, exemplar of righteous suffering
**Era:** Old Testament (setting is patriarchal; book composed later)
**Tradition:** Biblical / Hebrew Bible

## Bio

Job is the title figure of the biblical book of Job, a righteous and wealthy man whom God permits to be stripped of his children, health, and possessions. The narrative frame (Job 1–2) opens in the heavenly court where "the sons of God" — including "the satan" (the accuser) — present themselves before God. The bulk of the book is poetic dialogue wrestling with why a just man suffers, ending in God's whirlwind speeches.

## Relevance to the vault

Job serves two vault purposes. First, he embodies the classic problem of evil — "why do bad things happen to good people" — used in the discussion of how an afterlife of divine reward and punishment emerged as a theodicy. Second, the heavenly-court scene of Job 1–2 (with the "sons of God" and the satan) is cited as evidence of the **divine council**, supporting the vault's reading of passages like Psalm 82 and Genesis 1:26 against strict Trinitarian monotheism.
