# David Hilbert

**Full name:** David Hilbert (1862–1943)
**Role:** German mathematician
**Era:** Late 19th – early 20th century

## Bio
David Hilbert was one of the most influential mathematicians of the modern era, a leader of the Göttingen school whose work spanned the foundations of geometry, mathematical logic, functional analysis, and the axiomatization of mathematics (the "Hilbert program"). In apologetics he is best known not for a theological claim but for a thought experiment — the imaginary **infinite hotel** — devised to illustrate the counterintuitive properties of an *actually infinite* collection.

## Hilbert's Hotel and the Actual Infinite
In [[William Lane Craig|Craig]]'s philosophical Kalam explainer, Hilbert supplies the illustration for [[Al-Ghazali]]'s first argument. A hotel with an actually infinite number of fully-occupied rooms can still take in new guests, and can lose infinitely many guests while the number remaining stays unchanged or becomes finite — yielding contradictions when "we subtract identical quantities from identical quantities, and get different answers." Craig concludes with Hilbert's own verdict: "The infinite is nowhere to be found in reality. It neither exists in nature, nor provides a legitimate basis for rational thought... The role that remains for the infinite to play is solely that of an idea."[^1] The thought experiment is developed in [[Hilbert's Hotel - Why an Actual Infinite Cannot Exist]]; the argument it serves is summarized at [[The Cause of the Universe Must Be Personal, Timeless, and Spaceless]]. (Hilbert intended the hotel to show that the actual infinite belongs to mathematics as an *idea*, not to physical reality — exactly the use Craig and al-Ghazali make of it.)
