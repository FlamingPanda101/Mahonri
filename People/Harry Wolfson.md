# Harry Wolfson

**Full name:** Harry Austryn Wolfson (1887–1974)
**Role:** Historian of philosophy; Philo of Alexandria scholar
**Era:** 20th century
**Tradition:** Academic (Jewish)

## Bio

Harry Austryn Wolfson was a professor of Hebrew literature and philosophy at Harvard University and the first chair of a Judaic studies center in the United States. His two-volume *Philo: Foundations of Religious Philosophy in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam* (1947) is a landmark study that argued Philo effectively arrived at the concept of creation ex nihilo — a position that represents a minority view in Philo scholarship.

## Philo and Creation Ex Nihilo

Wolfson argued that while Philo used Platonic language of a passive material principle, he ultimately subordinated matter so completely to God's creative will that the result was functionally equivalent to creation ex nihilo. Philo's consistent emphasis on God's absolute sovereignty and his descriptions of God creating "out of non-being" (ἐκ μὴ ὄντων), Wolfson contended, point toward the later doctrine even if Philo didn't use the precise formulation.

However, the majority scholarly consensus — represented by [[David Winston]], [[Gerhard May]], and [[James Hubler]] — rejects Wolfson's reading. They argue Philo's explicit dualism (active God + passive matter), his exclusion of matter from the things God created, and his theodicy (which requires eternal matter as the source of evil) demonstrate that Philo did NOT hold creation ex nihilo. Philo "can't quite get there" because the philosophical axiom *ex nihilo nihil fit* (upheld by Platonists and Stoics alike) prevented him from crossing the line.[^1]
