# Bede

**Role:** Monk, priest, biblical exegete, historian
**Era:** 673 – 735 CE
**Tradition:** Early medieval Christianity (Northumbria, England)

## Bio

Bede was "one of the most prolific and learned scriptural exegetes of the early medieval period," a monk and priest at the twin monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow in northeast England. [^1] Placed in the care of Abbot Benedict (Biscop) and then Ceolfrith at age seven, he spent his whole life in the monastery: "it has always been my delight to learn or to teach or to write." [^1] He finished *The Ecclesiastical History of the English People* in AD 731.

## Translator of Christ for His People

[[Miranda Wilcox]] uses Bede as the prime example of how medieval monastics "translated their new faith into their cultural media." His monastery produced the **Codex Amiatinus**, the oldest surviving complete copy of Jerome's Vulgate — a seventy-five-pound Bible his abbot intended for the shrine of Peter in Rome but died en route. [^1] Bede made [[1 Timothy 2.5|1 Timothy 2:5]] one of his favorite texts, preaching Christ as "the very Mediator between God and human beings." [^1] He regarded preachers as "the successors of the prophets and apostles" and was translating Latin scripture into English on his deathbed. [^2] He also recorded the story of Abbess Hild and the cowherd Cædmon, who turned scripture into Old English poetry. See [[Medieval Christians Translated Christ into Vernacular Culture]] and [[Monasteries Preserved and Transmitted Scripture]].
