# Eusebius of Caesarea

Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260–339) was the early church's first major historian (*Ecclesiastical History*) and a bishop present at the Council of Nicaea (325). A close advisor to the emperor Constantine, he held views sympathetic to [[Arius]] and is described in this source as broadly Arian in his leanings, illustrating that the pre-Nicene field was not monolithically pro-homoousios.

## Cited to Show Nicaea Was Contested and Political
In [[Thoughtful Faith - The LDS Godhead in the Bible (Response to Sam Shamoun)]], [[Blake Ostler]] names Eusebius as one of the "two primary protagonists" alongside [[Alexander of Alexandria]], noting that Eusebius "was a close friend" who "had actually advised Constantine, but he was an Arian." [^1] Ostler uses this to support [[Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity]] — that the council was a politically managed event in which an Arian majority of bishops, not a self-evident biblical consensus, had to be overridden by [[Constantine]].
