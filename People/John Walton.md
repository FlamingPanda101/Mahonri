# John Walton

**Full name:** John H. Walton
**Role:** Professor of Old Testament; scholar of ancient Near Eastern backgrounds
**Era:** Contemporary (active 1990s–present)
**Tradition:** Evangelical Christian / Academic (Wheaton College)

## Bio

John H. Walton is professor of Old Testament at Wheaton College and a leading evangelical specialist in the ancient Near Eastern context of the Hebrew Bible. His most influential thesis is that [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1]] is concerned with **functional ontology** — how the cosmos was ordered to *function* as sacred, inhabited space — rather than with the **material** origin of matter. On this reading the seven days describe God assigning roles, boundaries, and functions (and inaugurating the cosmos as a temple), not the manufacture of physical stuff from nothing.

In the FAIR conference paper [[FAIR - Genesis 1 and Joseph Smith's Cosmology]], [[Stephen Smoot]] leans on Walton as the framework-setting authority, citing *The Lost World of Genesis One: Ancient Cosmology and the Origins Debate* and *Genesis 1 as Ancient Cosmology*. Smoot summarizes Walton as arguing that the Genesis creation narrative "is not primarily concerned with how the material universe came into being but rather how it functions as ordered sacred created space" — a reading Smoot uses to argue that Genesis 1 neither teaches nor requires [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation *ex nihilo*]], converging with Joseph Smith's view of God organizing pre-existing matter. [^1]

Notably, Walton himself is an evangelical who does *not* draw Latter-day Saint conclusions; his functional reading is invoked here as independent, mainstream scholarship that happens to align with the LDS organization-not-fabrication model of creation.
