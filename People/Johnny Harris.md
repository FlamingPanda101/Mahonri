# Johnny Harris

Johnny Harris is a popular YouTuber and video journalist, and a former Latter-day Saint, who produced a series of three widely-viewed videos on LDS history that he later re-released as a single long-form video. Keystone characterizes him as "a sincere guy" who is "much more responsible in his videos about our faith than I've seen some others be" and who generally "is trying to get his facts straight," while still leaving "quite a few issues" worth addressing.[^1]

## Role in the Vault

Harris is the foil for [[Keystone - Everything Johnny Harris Gets Wrong About Mormons]], where Keystone rebuts his treatment of Joseph Smith's treasure-digging, the Book of Mormon translation and witnesses, New World archaeology, Brigham Young's polygamy and the Mountain Meadows Massacre, the temple recommend, the "control" thesis, and — most pointedly — his misattribution of a [[B.H. Roberts Was Playing Devils Advocate Not Losing Faith|devil's-advocate B.H. Roberts quotation]] as evidence that faithful scholars lost their testimonies.

Keystone's running critique is less about flat factual error than about [[The Enlightened Ex-Member Frame Treats Believers as Zoo Animals|presentation and framing]] — Harris's animation, music, and word choices are designed to make the viewer "feel the way he wants you to feel" rather than to mislead on the facts.[^2]

Harris's "third and final" video drew a separate response, [[Keystone - What Johnny Harris Gets Wrong About Mormons]], which answers his "control" thesis with [[Movements Must Institutionalize to Survive Their Charismatic Founder|Max Weber's routinization of charisma]], defends the [[The Temple Recommend Interview Is a Reasonable Entry Standard Not Sinister Control|temple-recommend interview]] and the [[Principle-Based Youth Standards Replaced Rule-Based Ones|principle-based youth standards]], and again rebuts the same B.H. Roberts misattribution.[^3]

His consolidated long-form video also drew a [[Hayden Holm|Salty Saints]] reaction, [[Salty Saints - Fact-Checking Johnny Harris's Harm Story]], which targets the *autobiographical/"harm"* frame (rather than the documentary claims): it tallies a "net-positive ledger" of the goods Harris credits to the Church against the harms he names ([[The Net-Positive Ledger Rebuts Johnny Harris's Harm Thesis]]), argues his twenties faith-renegotiation was ordinary young-adult development ([[Identity Reconstruction at 20-Something Is Ordinary Not Cult Damage]]), and contends his "pipeline"/"sugarcoating" charges are overstated ([[The Pipeline and Sugarcoating Charges Against the Church Are Overstated]]).
