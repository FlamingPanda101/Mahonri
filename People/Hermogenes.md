# Hermogenes

**Role:** Late-2nd-century Christian philosopher who defended *creatio ex materia*
**Era:** 2nd century CE (writing c. 180–200, after Tatian and Theophilus)
**Tradition:** Early Christianity (opposed by Tertullian)

## Bio

Hermogenes was a Christian teacher (a painter by trade, traditionally located at Carthage) who argued, against the newly formulated doctrine of creation out of nothing, that matter is eternal and uncreated. His writings do not survive; he is known almost entirely through [[Tertullian]]'s polemic *Adversus Hermogenem* (*The Treatise against Hermogenes*). [[Blake Ostler]] treats him as a key witness that, even at the end of the second century, *creatio ex materia* remained a live Christian position — and that by then all parties were arguing from shared Middle Platonic assumptions about God and matter.[^1]

## Argument for Eternal Matter

Hermogenes reasoned from the Middle Platonic premise that **matter is evil** to the conclusion that matter must be **eternal and uncreated**: a good God would not, by his own choice or will, make anything evil; yet evil things exist in the world; therefore evil arises not from God's will but "from the fault of another thing — from matter without doubt," which God did not create.[^2] This is the inverse of [[Theophilus of Antioch|Theophilus's]] move: both accept the Platonic premises about matter, but Hermogenes keeps matter eternal to protect God's goodness, while Theophilus makes matter created to protect God's uniqueness and sovereignty.
