# David Carr

**Full name:** David M. Carr
**Role:** Professor of Old Testament / Hebrew Bible
**Era:** Contemporary (active 1990s–present)
**Tradition:** Academic (Union Theological Seminary, New York)

## Bio

David Carr is a leading scholar of the Hebrew Bible and the formation of the Pentateuch. He teaches at Union Theological Seminary in New York City. His work focuses on source criticism, redaction criticism, and the compositional history of Genesis.

Dan McClellan recommends Carr as the starting point for understanding the evidence that Genesis is composed of multiple sources: "I'm going to recommend David Carr's book, *Reading the Fractures of Genesis*, which goes through a lot of the evidence and the methods and the theory that lead us to conclude that Genesis is actually composed of a bunch of different sources." [^1]

## Source Criticism of Genesis / Documentary Hypothesis

Carr's *Reading the Fractures of Genesis* (1996) is a foundational text in contemporary source criticism. Rather than simply asserting the Documentary Hypothesis, Carr walks through the evidence — linguistic, thematic, structural, and theological — that demonstrates Genesis is a composite text with multiple originally independent sources.

McClellan also recommends Carr's newer work *The Formation of Genesis 1–11* and his Kohlhammer commentary on Genesis 1–11, describing them as entry points for understanding "the principles behind all of this" — the methods and theory of source criticism. [^2]

Carr's Genesis work is directly relevant to the flood narrative source division: the Priestly (P) account (two of every animal, Elohim, 150-day flood) and the non-Priestly/Jahwist (J) account (seven pairs of clean animals, YHWH, 40-day rain) are two originally separate traditions that a redactor wove together.
