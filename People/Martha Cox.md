# Martha Cox

A 19th-century Latter-day Saint plural wife whose autobiography became, for historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]], the single story that most reshaped her view of polygamy. Cox wrote honestly about both the good and the bad, but on balance recorded a positive experience and a *deliberate* choice of plural marriage made against deep family opposition: it was rarely practiced in her community, and her friends "cold-shouldered" her when they learned she would marry polygamously, yet she felt strong conviction it was the right path.[^1]

She was no stranger to suffering — she lost children and lived at times in desperately poor circumstances — yet drew her deepest strength from her bond with her sister wives.[^2]

## Best Quote
> "To me, it is a joy to know that we laid the foundation of a life to come while we lived in that plural marriage. That we three who loved each other more than sisters... will go hand in hand, together down through all eternity. That knowledge is worth more to me than gold and more than compensates for all the sorrow I have ever known."[^2]
