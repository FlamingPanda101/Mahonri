# Macrina

Fourth-century Christian ascetic (AD 330–379), sister of [[Basil the Great]] (Basil of Caesarea) and [[Gregory of Nyssa]], who wrote her *Life*.

## Ascetic authority and influence
[[Ariel Bybee Laughton]] cites Macrina as a prime example of how sexual renunciation gave Christian women freedom and authority beyond what other women enjoyed. As a virgin of God she "managed her family's vast estates, founded a monastery for women on her property, devoted herself to reading and the study of scripture, and converted her brother Basil … to the ascetic life." She belongs with Melania the Elder, Marcella of Rome, and Olympias among the aristocratic ascetic women whose renunciation opened doors to scholarship, teaching, travel, mentoring, and monastic leadership. See [[Sexual Renunciation Gave Christian Women Spiritual Authority]]. [^1]
