# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

**Era:** 1712–1778 (18th century)
**Tradition:** Counter-Enlightenment / proto-Romantic
**Role:** Philosopher and writer

## Bio

Genevan-French philosopher writing under the *ancien régime*. Major works: *The Social Contract* (1762, "Man is born free, but everywhere is in chains") and *Emile* (1762, on education). His prize-winning *Discourse on the Arts and Sciences* (1750s) argued that civilization morally corrupts: "Our minds have been corrupted in proportion as the arts and sciences have improved."[^1]

## Role in the Argument

[[Brian Thomassen]] casts Rousseau as the **anti-Enlightenment lynchpin of the French Revolution** — the theoretical foil to the [[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]]–[[John Locke|Locke]]–[[Montesquieu]] line. Against [[Steven Pinker]] (whom Thomassen and Dave fault for trying to claim Rousseau and the Revolution for the Enlightenment), Rousseau "rejects it whole cloth" for something "far more akin to romanticism": reason rejected for intuition, the *noble savage*, **property as the root of all corruption** ("The first man who… enclosed a piece of ground… was the real founder of civil society"), and natural rights supplanted by the **general will**, into which "one can be forced to be free." The general will needs a "great soul of the legislator" who may use the **noble lie**. This traces directly through Hegel to Marx (false consciousness) and to Robespierre's terror. See [[Rousseau Is the Anti-Enlightenment Lynchpin of the French Revolution]].[^2][^3]

Thomassen rates Rousseau among the three best-writing philosophers (with [[Plato]] and [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]) — a "rhetorical flourish that is very seductive."[^4]
