# Gordon B. Hinckley

Fifteenth president of the LDS Church (1910–2008). His public comments on the
[[King Follett Discourse]] exemplify the modern LDS tension between embracing the
discourse's radical doctrines and downplaying them to emphasize commonality with
mainstream Christianity.

## The 1997 Interview

In a national interview, Hinckley said of the King Follett Discourse:

> That gets into some pretty deep theology that we don't know very much about.[^1]

When asked about the [[Lorenzo Snow]] couplet ("As man is, God once was. As God
is, man may become"), Hinckley responded: "I'm not sure we really teach that."[^2]

## Ambivalence

Park notes that "in other interviews he does try to double down, which shows just
this constant awkwardness" — the discourse cannot be fully repudiated (it's too
foundational) nor fully embraced in ecumenical settings.[^2]

## Apologetic Significance

Hinckley's comments are frequently cited by critics as evidence that the LDS Church
is embarrassed by its own doctrinal distinctives. LDS apologists counter that
Hinckley was speaking carefully to a non-Mormon audience and that the doctrine of
exaltation remains core LDS teaching (see [[Human Deification in Mormonism]]).
