# Hugo Méndez

**Role:** Associate professor of religious studies, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill
**Field:** New Testament, especially the Gospel and epistles of John; Johannine literature, pseudepigraphy
**Era:** Contemporary (active 2020s)
**Tradition:** Academic biblical studies; self-identifies as a Christian

## Bio
Hugo Méndez is a New Testament scholar at UNC Chapel Hill whose work focuses on the Johannine corpus. He came to prominence with a 2020 open-access article, "Did the Johannine Community Exist?", and developed its program in his book *The Gospel of John: A New History*. He describes himself as "non-conventional on a ton of issues in John" while remaining "extremely conventional" on the date (~90–110 CE) [^1]. He positions himself as a Christian even as he argues that the Gospel is a pseudepigraphal composition [^2].

## The Johannine Community Did Not Exist
Méndez denies the existence of the long-assumed "Johannine community." He argues the similarities between the Gospel and 1–3 John are better explained by later authors imitating an earlier text than by a single tradition-bearing community. See [[Johannine Community Did Not Exist]] and [[Johannine Epistles Are Pseudepigrapha]].

## John as Pseudepigraphy
He argues both that 1–3 John are pseudepigrapha modeled on the Gospel and that the Gospel itself is pseudepigraphal — a single author presenting himself as the eyewitness "disciple whom Jesus loved." See [[Gospel of John Is Pseudepigraphal Not Eyewitness]] and [[Beloved Disciple Was Not John the Son of Zebedee]].

## Authorship, Unity, and Additions
He affirms substantial single authorship of John (developed in drafts/stages) while accepting two later additions — the *pericope adulterae* and chapter 21. See [[Single Authorship of John Against Redaction Layers]], [[Pericope Adulterae Is a Later Interpolation]], [[John 21 Is a Secondary Addition]], and [[The We of John 21-24 Single Author Explanation]].

## Christology, Symbolism, and Genre
He keeps the Logos hymn with the original Gospel, reads John 1:1c as ascribing a subordinate/participatory divinity to the Word, argues John knew and rewrote the Synoptics, and contends John's pervasive symbolism makes it resemble apocryphal gospels like Thomas and Mary. See [[Logos Hymn Belongs to the Original Gospel]], [[John 1-1c Anarthrous Theos and Subordinate Divinity]], [[John Knew the Synoptics]], [[John's Pervasive Symbolism]], [[Gospel of John Resembles Apocryphal Gospels]], and [[John Gave Christianity Its High Christology and Pre-existence]].
