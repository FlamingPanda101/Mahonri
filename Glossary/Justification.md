# Justification

**Definition.** Justification (Greek *dikaioō*, "to declare or make righteous") is the act by which God sets a sinner in a right standing with himself. Protestant theology treats it as a one-time *forensic* (legal) declaration in which Christ's righteousness is *imputed* to the believer apart from works. Catholic theology (Council of Trent) treats it as both the remission of sins *and* an inward *infusion* of righteousness—justification and sanctification are a single renewing reality, received initially and increased over time.

**Why it matters here.** This is the central fault line of the Reformation and a live LDS apologetic issue. If justification is a finished legal verdict (imputation), works and ordinances can only be evidence; if it is an infused, ongoing reality, then baptism, repentance, and obedience genuinely participate in it. LDS writers generally side with the "initial vs. ongoing" and infusion models against the "legal fiction" of imputation.
