# Perichoresis

**Definition.** Perichoresis (Greek *perichōrēsis*, "going around," rendered in Latin as *circumincessio*, "mutual interpenetration") is the doctrine that the three persons of the Trinity mutually indwell and co-inhere in one another without merging or losing their distinctness. Each person is wholly in the others; the whole divine life is shared. The term was developed by John of Damascus and is invoked to safeguard the unity of the persons while preserving their distinction.

**Why it matters here.** Latter-day Saints and Social Trinitarians appeal to perichoresis (mutual indwelling) to read passages like John 17 as relational oneness rather than one-substance identity — making the Godhead's unity one of love, will, and co-inherence. Critics argue that if perichoresis alone secures oneness, the resulting model becomes hard to distinguish from three beings in perfect union.
