# Foundationalism

**Definition.** The theory of justification holding that the chain of reasons supporting a belief eventually *ends* in **basic beliefs** — beliefs that are justified but do not derive their justification from any further belief. All other ("non-basic") beliefs are justified by resting, directly or indirectly, on this foundation. It is one of three structural answers to the epistemic regress problem, alongside coherentism (the web) and infinitism (the endless chain), and has been the dominant view since Aristotle. Classical foundationalists locate the foundation in beliefs grounded immediately in experience.

**Why it matters here.** Foundationalism is the backdrop for nearly every claim to a self-evident or "properly basic" starting point — whether a Reformed appeal to belief in God, a presuppositional appeal to self-attesting Scripture, or a Latter-day Saint appeal to the witness of the Spirit. Critics press the foundationalist to explain how a non-inferential experience can confer justification without itself being justified.
