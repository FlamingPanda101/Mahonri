# Marcionism

**Definition.** Marcionism is the 2nd-century movement founded by Marcion of Sinope (c. 144 AD), who taught that the wrathful creator-god of the Hebrew Bible was a different, lesser being than the wholly loving God revealed by Jesus. Marcion therefore rejected the entire Old Testament and assembled a pared-down canon: an edited Gospel of Luke and ten Pauline epistles. The proto-orthodox church condemned him, and his challenge is often credited with pressuring the wider church to define its own New Testament canon.

**Why it matters here.** Marcionism is doubly relevant in this library. First, the church's canon-forming reaction against Marcion is a case study in how Scripture's boundaries were set by contested human process rather than a simple list handed down. Second, Marcionite communities reportedly practiced baptism for the dead, which LDS writers cite as early external attestation of a rite mainstream Christianity abandoned. The movement is also invoked in debates over reconciling the Old Testament's God with a "God of pure love."
