# Free-will defense

**Definition.** The **free-will defense** argues that much evil is the unavoidable cost of genuine free will: a God who wanted creatures capable of freely chosen love and goodness could not guarantee they would never choose evil, because coerced "love" or "goodness" is a contradiction. In Alvin Plantinga's influential formulation it is offered as a *defense* (a possible reason that defeats the logical problem of evil), not a full theodicy, and it covers only **moral** evil, leaving natural evil to be addressed separately.

**Why it matters here.** It is the most common theistic reply to the problem of evil and hell, and a point of sharp dispute. Defenders argue love must be uncoerced; critics respond that an omnipotent God could create free beings who *always* freely choose good (the "lava pool" objection), and that Plantinga's "maximal autonomy" smuggles in more than ordinary free will requires.
