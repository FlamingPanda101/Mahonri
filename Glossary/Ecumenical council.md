# Ecumenical council

**Definition.** An ecumenical council (from Greek *oikoumenē*, "the whole inhabited world") is a worldwide assembly of bishops convened to settle doctrine and discipline for the entire church, its decrees claimed as binding. The traditional "first seven" run from Nicaea I (325) through Nicaea II (787) and include Constantinople I (381), Ephesus (431), and Chalcedon (451). Different traditions count different totals: the Eastern Orthodox recognize seven, the Roman Catholic Church twenty-one.

**Why it matters here.** These councils produced the creeds and christological definitions that distinguish "creedal" Christianity, so they anchor the dispute over whether Latter-day Saints fall inside it. The councils are double-edged for LDS apologetics: they are cited as evidence of post-apostolic doctrinal drift decided "by votes," yet the very disagreement over which councils were truly ecumenical (and the schisms they caused, such as Chalcedon and Constantinople II) undermines appeals to a single unbroken consensus.
