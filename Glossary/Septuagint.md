# Septuagint

**Definition.** The pre-Christian Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, made for Greek-speaking Jews in Alexandria from roughly the 3rd–2nd centuries BC. The name (Latin *septuaginta*, "seventy," abbreviated **LXX**) reflects the legend that seventy-two elders produced it; it began with the Torah and grew to include the Prophets, Writings, and the deuterocanonical books. Its underlying Hebrew sometimes differs from the later Masoretic Text.

**Why it matters here.** The Septuagint is the Old Testament most New Testament authors quote, and it includes the deuterocanon — a key argument that those books were anciently scriptural. Debates over messianic prophecy (e.g., Isaiah 7:14 "virgin," Isaiah 9:6) hinge on LXX-versus-Hebrew wording. LDS and critical scholars cite the LXX as evidence the Hebrew text was edited over time.
