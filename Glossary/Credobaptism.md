# Credobaptism

**Definition.** Credobaptism (Latin *credo*, "I believe," + baptism), also called *believer's baptism*, is the practice of baptizing only those who can make a personal profession of faith, on the conviction that the New Testament consistently presents baptism as following repentance and belief (Acts 2:38; Mark 16:16). It is the position of Baptists, Anabaptists, and most evangelicals, and it typically pairs with a *symbolic* view of baptism: the rite is the believer's public response, not an efficacious cause of the new birth.

**Why it matters here.** Catholic apologists press a *tu quoque*: child credobaptism has even less early evidence than infant baptism, and credobaptists cannot agree on a minimum age—producing a "gruesome gap" where a child can sin yet be deemed unbaptizable. Latter-day Saints share the credobaptist refusal of infant baptism but ground it in a revealed age of accountability rather than an improvised threshold.
