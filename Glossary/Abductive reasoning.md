# Abductive reasoning

**Definition.** A mode of inference, named by C. S. Peirce, that reasons from a body of data to the hypothesis that would best explain it — commonly called *inference to the best explanation* (IBE). Unlike deduction (which guarantees its conclusion) or induction (which generalizes from instances), abduction weighs rival explanations by criteria such as explanatory scope, simplicity, plausibility, and freedom from ad hoc assumptions, then accepts the strongest. Its conclusions are probable, not certain, and remain open to revision as new candidate explanations or evidence appear.

**Why it matters here.** It is the engine of historical and design arguments alike: historians infer the best explanation of contested events, and intelligent-design and fine-tuning arguments are framed as IBE rather than proofs. The vault uses it both to argue that a designer best explains cosmic fine-tuning and to assess Book of Mormon origins and the witnesses' testimony. Critics counter that "best of a bad lot" need not be true, and dispute which criteria favor design.
