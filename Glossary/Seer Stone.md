# Seer Stone

**Definition.** A small stone (Joseph Smith owned a brown one and a white one) through which a "seer" reported receiving visual revelation. In 1820s New York folk practice such stones were used to locate lost or hidden objects ("glass-looking" or money-digging); Joseph later used a personal seer stone, frequently placed in a hat to exclude light, as an instrument for translating much of the Book of Mormon — distinct from, but used alongside, the "interpreters" that came with the plates.

**Why it matters here.** The seer stone is a flashpoint because critics read its translation role as evidence of a magical, money-digging worldview rather than divine revelation, while defenders accept the historical fact of the stone but contest its meaning — framing seeric "seeing" as a share in a divine attribute rather than folk superstition. Both sides agree a seer stone was involved; the dispute is theological, not factual.
