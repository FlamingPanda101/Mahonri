# Evidentialism

**Definition.** In epistemology, the thesis that a belief is justified only to the degree it fits one's *evidence* — famously captured in W. K. Clifford's dictum that "it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence." In apologetics, **evidentialism** names the method that defends faith by amassing positive public evidence — historical, scientific, and philosophical — and arguing that the theistic or Christian conclusion is the most reasonable. It contrasts with presuppositionalism (which denies neutral ground) and with Reformed epistemology (which holds belief in God can be rational without argument).

**Why it matters here.** The label marks one pole of a recurring apologetic spectrum. A healthy case, the vault suggests, weighs spiritual *and* evidential reasons together rather than relying on either alone — resisting both pure fideism and pure evidentialism. The contested question is whether faith *requires* evidential support to be rational, or whether demanding proof before belief is itself an unwarranted standard.
