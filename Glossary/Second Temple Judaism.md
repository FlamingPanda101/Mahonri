# Second Temple Judaism

**Definition.** Second Temple Judaism denotes the diverse religious world of the Jewish people from the rebuilding of the Jerusalem temple (c. 516 BC) to its destruction in AD 70. It was not monolithic but a field of competing groups (Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, Qumran) and ideas — emerging beliefs about resurrection, angels and Satan, multiple afterlife frameworks, and varied messianic and divine-mediator expectations. The synagogue and much later "rabbinic" structure also took shape in or after this period.

**Why it matters here.** This period is the interpretive battleground for early Christology: apologists argue that ideas later deemed distinctively Christian (a divine Son of Man, forgiving sins as a divine prerogative, divine mediators) were already at home in Second Temple Judaism, making a high view of Jesus authentically Jewish. It also bears on the Book of Mormon, where critics and defenders dispute whether features fit a pre-exilic versus a Hasmonean-era assembled Judaism.
