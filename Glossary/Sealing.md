# Sealing

**Definition.** In Latter-day Saint temple practice, the ordinance by which the Melchizedek "sealing power" (traced to the keys given Peter in Matthew 16, "whatsoever thou shalt bind on earth") binds relationships to endure beyond death — most prominently marriage (a husband and wife "sealed for time and all eternity") and the linking of children to parents into an unbroken family chain back to Adam. A sealing was not necessarily a sexual or marital relationship; nineteenth-century practice included "dynastic," "law of adoption," and "eternity only" sealings.

**Why it matters here.** The sealing power is central to LDS claims about eternal families, baptism for the dead, and the purpose of temples; it also frames the polygamy debate, since defenders argue many of Joseph Smith's plural unions were sealings, not marriages. The "man and woman" wording is invoked against same-sex temple marriage, a contested point.
