# Creatio ex materia

**Definition.** **Creatio ex materia** is Latin for "creation out of matter" — the view that God formed the cosmos by organizing pre-existing, eternal, unformed material rather than producing matter from nothing. It is the Latter-day Saint position (D&C 93:33, "the elements are eternal") and was the dominant assumption of the Greco-Roman philosophical world, governed by the axiom *ex nihilo nihil fit* ("nothing comes from nothing"). On this reading the Hebrew verb *bara* and the *tohu wa-bohu* ("formless and void") of Genesis 1 describe ordering chaos, not creating substance.

**Why it matters here.** This directly opposes [[creatio ex nihilo]]. Critics charge that eternal, independent matter limits God's sovereignty and makes Him a mere arranger, not the ultimate source of all being. Defenders argue it is the older and more biblically faithful reading, that *ex nihilo* was a late second-century innovation, and that it dissolves problems classical theism faces (e.g., God as sole cause of evil).
