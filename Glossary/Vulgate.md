# Vulgate

**Definition.** The Latin Bible (Latin *editio vulgata*, "the common edition") produced largely by Jerome around AD 382–405, who translated much of the Old Testament directly from the Hebrew rather than the Greek Septuagint. It became the standard Bible of Western Christianity for a millennium and was declared the authentic text for the Roman Catholic Church at the Council of Trent (1546). Jerome distinguished the Hebrew-canon books from the deuterocanon, which he translated more reluctantly.

**Why it matters here.** The Vulgate is central to canon arguments because Jerome — its translator — voiced doubts about the deuterocanon yet included it, a fact both sides claim. It is also a source of contested readings (e.g., the Johannine Comma entered later Vulgate manuscripts), feeding debates over how translation choices shape doctrine.
