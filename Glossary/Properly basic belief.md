# Properly basic belief

**Definition.** A belief that is held rationally without being inferred from other beliefs — it sits in the *foundation* of one's noetic structure rather than being argued to. The phrase is associated with Alvin Plantinga, who argued that, like belief in other minds, the external world, or the reality of the past, *belief in God* can be **properly basic**: warranted directly by experience (e.g., a sense of the divine) rather than by an inferential proof. "Properly" signals that the belief is formed under the right conditions, by reliable cognitive faculties functioning as designed.

**Why it matters here.** This concept lets a believer say their belief in God needs no evidential argument to be rational, defusing the evidentialist demand that theism be *proven*. Latter-day Saint apologetics makes a parallel move with the witness of the Spirit. Critics reply that calling a belief "basic" risks immunizing it from challenge and that almost any belief — including rival religious ones — could claim the same status.
