# Divine embodiment

**Definition.** **Divine embodiment** is the claim that God (specifically the Father, and the resurrected Christ) possesses a tangible, corporeal body of flesh and bones, as Latter-day Saints affirm from D&C 130:22. This stands against the classical-theist conception of God as incorporeal, immaterial spirit (cf. John 4:24, "God is spirit"). A growing body of biblical scholarship argues the Hebrew Bible itself anthropomorphically depicts an embodied deity with a form, face, and back (Genesis 32:30; Exodus 33:20–23).

**Why it matters here.** Critics treat embodiment as a category error that reduces God to a finite creature and contradicts the spirituality of the divine nature. Defenders respond that the bodily resurrection of Christ already commits Christians to an embodied divine person, that the Old Testament portrays God in physical terms, and that "image of God" language implies real physical resemblance passed from Father to Son.
