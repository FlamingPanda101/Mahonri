# Higher Criticism

**Definition.** The umbrella term for historical-critical study of the Bible's *origins* — authorship, date, sources, composition, and historical setting — as distinct from "lower" (textual) criticism, which reconstructs the wording. It includes source, form, and redaction criticism, and rose to prominence in 18th–19th-century German scholarship. It characteristically proceeds under methodological naturalism, bracketing claims of miracle or direct divine dictation when reconstructing how a text came to be.

**Why it matters here.** This is the battleground discipline. Traditionalists charge that its naturalistic assumptions prejudge revelation out of existence; critics reply it merely studies texts as historical artifacts. The vault's distinctive stance is that conservative Protestant inerrancy has no stable answer to higher criticism, whereas an LDS model — a fallible, edited, restorable canon — can absorb its findings rather than be threatened by them.
