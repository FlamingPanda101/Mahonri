# Substitutionary atonement

**Definition.** **Substitutionary atonement** is the family of theories holding that Christ bears, in the sinner's place, what the sinner owed. Its sharpest form, **penal substitution**, says Christ takes the punishment (the divine wrath due to sin) on behalf of believers, drawing on texts like 2 Corinthians 5:21 and Romans 8:3. A related Protestant development, **double imputation**, adds that Christ's righteousness is reckoned to the believer while their guilt is reckoned to Christ. It descends historically from Anselm's satisfaction theory.

**Why it matters here.** Two objections recur. Muslim and some philosophical critics call it unjust—punishing an innocent to acquit the guilty—answered by the reply that the substitute is the divine Son giving himself voluntarily, not an imposed victim. Latter-day Saint and other critics argue penal substitution and double imputation distort the gospel into a legal transfer, preferring relational or transformative models of the [[Atonement]].
