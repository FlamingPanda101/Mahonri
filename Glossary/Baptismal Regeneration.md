# Baptismal Regeneration

**Definition.** Baptismal regeneration is the doctrine that baptism is the ordinary, God-appointed means by which a person is spiritually reborn and saving grace is conferred—baptism "now saves" (1 Pet 3:21) and effects the new birth "of water and the Spirit" (John 3:5). Catholic, Orthodox, Lutheran, and Churches-of-Christ traditions affirm it (with differing accounts of *how* it works); most Baptists and evangelicals deny it, reading baptism as a symbolic public profession that follows a salvation already received by faith.

**Why it matters here.** Critics defending baptismal regeneration stress the near-unanimous patristic consensus that baptism is saving; symbolic-only advocates read the saving language as metonymy. Latter-day Saints affirm baptism is necessary and salvific (siding against bare symbolism) while denying it should be given to infants—splitting the Catholic package by locating the error not in baptism's efficacy but in inherited guilt.
