# Deuteronomist

**Definition.** In source and redaction criticism, the author/editor tradition (the "D" source) behind Deuteronomy and the "Deuteronomistic History" (Joshua–Kings), marked by a distinctive style and a program of centralized, Jerusalem-only worship, strict covenant theology, and aniconism. Scholars associate this circle with the reforms of Hezekiah and especially King Josiah (7th century BC), often linking the "book of the law" found in the Temple (2 Kings 22) to a form of Deuteronomy.

**Why it matters here.** This vault leans heavily on the Deuteronomists as the editors who suppressed older First-Temple theology — divine council, a heavenly Mother, an embodied and plural Godhead, a suffering Messiah — which the Restoration and the Book of Mormon then recover. Critically minded readers treat "Deuteronomist" as a neutral literary-historical category; the apologetic notes here read its reforms as a partial apostasy of editing.
