# Contingency argument

**Definition.** The contingency branch of the cosmological argument, associated with Leibniz, Clarke, and Aquinas. A *contingent* being is one that exists but could have failed to exist; a *necessary* being exists of its own nature and depends on nothing. Invoking the Principle of Sufficient Reason, the argument holds that every contingent thing — and the whole series of contingent things, however long — requires an explanation outside itself, terminable only in a necessary being. Crucially, it works even if the universe is past-eternal, since an infinite chain of contingent things is itself contingent.

**Why it matters here.** Because it does not depend on the universe having a beginning, it sidesteps disputes over Big Bang cosmology. LDS apologists wield it forcefully against atheism, but note a divergence: classical theism makes the necessary being absolutely simple and unique, whereas some LDS readers argue the bare argument yields *a* necessary ground without proving its uniqueness, leaving room for the Restoration's eternal matter, intelligences, and law.
