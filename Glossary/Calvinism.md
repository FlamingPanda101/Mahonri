# Calvinism

**Definition.** Calvinism is the Reformed theological tradition descending from John Calvin, classically summarized by the acronym TULIP: **T**otal depravity, **U**nconditional election, **L**imited atonement, **I**rresistible grace, and **P**erseverance of the saints. Its center of gravity is God's exhaustive sovereignty in salvation (monergism): God unconditionally elects whom he will save, and grace, once given, cannot be refused. It stands opposed to [[Arminianism]], which preserves a libertarian human role.

**Why it matters here.** Calvinism is a primary dialogue partner in this library's problem-of-evil and grace material. LDS apologists argue that a God who unconditionally decrees who is saved and damned is morally objectionable and makes God the author of sin, and they treat the very Calvinist–Arminian impasse as evidence the creeds misdescribed God. Calvinists reply that sovereignty and human responsibility are compatible and that the objection imports an alien standard of justice onto God.
