# Creatio ex nihilo

**Definition.** **Creatio ex nihilo** is Latin for "creation out of nothing" — the doctrine that God created the universe, including matter itself, from absolutely nothing, with no pre-existing material. It is the standard view of classical Christian theism, undergirding God's absolute sovereignty and the radical Creator–creature distinction. Historically, the formula did not become explicit until the late second century CE (Tatian, Theophilus of Antioch, Irenaeus), emerging in debates against Gnosticism and Greek philosophy.

**Why it matters here.** This is the pivot of the creation debate. Latter-day Saints reject *ex nihilo* in favor of [[creatio ex materia]] (creation from pre-existing, eternal matter), arguing that Genesis describes organizing chaos rather than producing matter, and that *ex nihilo* is a post-biblical philosophical development. Defenders of *ex nihilo* argue it is the necessary safeguard of monotheism and divine sovereignty and is implied by texts such as Romans 4:17 and 2 Maccabees 7:28.
