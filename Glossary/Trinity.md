# Trinity

**Definition.** The classical Christian doctrine that the one God exists as three distinct, co-equal, co-eternal *persons* (Latin *personae*; Greek *hypostaseis*) — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — who share one indivisible divine *being* or *essence* (*ousia*, *substantia*). The persons are really distinct from one another yet are not three Gods, because there is numerically one substance. The technical vocabulary (one *ousia*, three *hypostaseis*; *homoousios*, "of the same substance") was hammered out at the Councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381) and presupposed by Chalcedon (451).

**Why it matters here.** This is the central front in LDS–creedal debate. Creedal Christians hold the Trinity as the necessary safeguard of monotheism and Christ's full deity; Latter-day Saints affirm Father, Son, and Holy Ghost as one in *will and purpose* but as three distinct *beings* (a "Godhead" or council), denying the one-substance claim and treating it as a post-biblical, Greek-philosophical development rather than apostolic teaching.
