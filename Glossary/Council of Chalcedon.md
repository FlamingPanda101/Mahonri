# Council of Chalcedon

**Definition.** The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) was the fourth ecumenical council. It defined that Christ is one person (*hypostasis*) "in two natures" — fully divine and fully human — "without confusion, change, division, or separation," rejecting both Eutyches (who merged the natures, *monophysitism*) and the Nestorian tendency to divide Christ into two persons. This "Chalcedonian Definition" became the christological standard for Catholic, Orthodox, and most Protestant churches.

**Why it matters here.** Chalcedon is a flashpoint in the LDS critique of the hypostatic union. Critics argue the council solved a *Greek* problem (how an immaterial deity can be embodied) with Greek categories, generating puzzles about whether Christ has one will or two and whether the formula yields "two Jesuses." A historical counter-argument runs the other way: Chalcedon itself fractured the church — the Oriental Orthodox rejected it — so the "creedal consensus" used to disqualify outsiders never actually held.
