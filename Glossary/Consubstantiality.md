# Consubstantiality

**Definition.** Consubstantiality is the Latin-derived English rendering of *homoousios* (*con-*, "with/together," + *substantia*, "substance") — the doctrine that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit share one and the same divine substance or being. In the Nicene tradition it means the three persons are not three Gods but one God, numerically one in *ousia*/substance while distinct in person (*hypostasis*).

**Why it matters here.** Trinitarian apologists name consubstantiality as the primary line dividing them from Latter-day Saints: three persons in one *being*. Latter-day Saints affirm three fully divine persons united in nature, will, and indwelling, but deny they are one substance/being — reading "I and the Father are one" (John 17) as relational oneness. They also note "consubstantial" is itself an awkward translation choice debated since its adoption.
