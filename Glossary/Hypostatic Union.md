# Hypostatic Union

**Definition.** The hypostatic union (from Greek *hypostasis*, "individual subsistence" or "person") is the orthodox christological doctrine that the one person of Christ subsists in two complete natures, fully divine and fully human, "without confusion, change, division, or separation." It was defined at the Council of Chalcedon (451) against rivals such as Nestorianism (two persons) and Eutychianism/monophysitism (one blended nature), building on Ephesus (431) and the Nicene homoousios.

**Why it matters here.** Trinitarian apologists invoke the hypostatic union to explain how Christ can pray to the Father, not know the hour, and die, while remaining God. Critics — including Latter-day Saints — argue it is a Greek metaphysical solution to a Greek-framed problem that the New Testament writers never posed, and that it is asserted as a labeled mystery rather than explained.
