# Universalism

**Definition.** **Universalism** (or universal reconciliation) is the doctrine that all persons will ultimately be saved — that hell, if real, is not permanent and God's redemptive purpose finally embraces everyone. Its classic ancient form is **apokatastasis** (Greek *apokatastasis*, "restoration of all things"), taught by Origen, who held that all rational beings, even the fallen, would at last be restored to union with God. Modern academic defenders include David Bentley Hart (*That All Shall Be Saved*).

**Why it matters here.** Universalism is one proposed escape from both the problem of evil and the problem of hell, and is contested on several sides. Critics argue it makes salvation coercive and human choice ultimately meaningless. The vault notes that Hick's universalism is charged with collapsing into determinism, while LDS thought occupies a middle position: it affirms a near-universal salvation through degrees of glory and postmortem evangelism, yet preserves a small, self-chosen "outer darkness" rather than full universalism.
