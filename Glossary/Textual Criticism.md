# Textual Criticism

**Definition.** The scholarly discipline that compares the surviving manuscripts of a text to reconstruct, as nearly as possible, the wording of the lost original (the "autograph") and to chart how copies diverged. For the Bible it weighs thousands of Hebrew and Greek witnesses, versions, and quotations, using canons such as *lectio difficilior* (prefer the harder reading) and preference for earlier, more widespread readings. It is sometimes called "lower criticism," in contrast to the "higher" criticism of sources and authorship.

**Why it matters here.** Both sides invoke it. Critics (e.g., Bart Ehrman) stress that scribal change means we do not possess an unaltered text; LDS apologists turn the same data toward a Restoration that supplies what transmission lost, while conservative Protestants argue the variants are minor and the text is recoverable. The discipline therefore frames the whole debate over which Bible, if any, is the rule of faith.
