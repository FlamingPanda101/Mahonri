# Kalam cosmological argument

**Definition.** A temporal cosmological argument named for *kalām*, the medieval Islamic tradition of speculative theology in which al-Ghazali developed it. Its syllogism: (1) whatever begins to exist has a cause; (2) the universe began to exist; (3) therefore the universe has a cause. The first premise rests on metaphysical intuition; the second is defended both philosophically (an actual infinite cannot be traversed by successive addition) and scientifically (Big Bang cosmology and thermodynamics). Conceptual analysis of the cause yields a being that is uncaused, beginningless, timeless, spaceless, immaterial, and personal.

**Why it matters here.** Popularized by William Lane Craig, it is the most debated theistic argument of recent decades. Critics (Hitchens, Krauss) attack premise one or the cosmological evidence; defenders reply that "begins to exist" carves God out, since only what begins needs a cause. LDS and creedal apologists both deploy it, though its conclusion of a personal transcendent cause is held in common rather than disputed between them.
