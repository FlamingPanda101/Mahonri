# Gnosticism

**Definition.** Gnosticism (from Greek *gnōsis*, "knowledge") is a label for a diverse family of religious movements, prominent in the 2nd century, that taught salvation through secret saving knowledge of one's divine origin. Many systems held a dualism between an inferior creator-god (the *demiurge*) of the material world and a higher, truly transcendent God; the body and matter were often viewed as a prison. The Nag Hammadi library (discovered 1945) preserves many such texts. Teachers like Valentinus exemplify the movement, which the proto-orthodox Fathers vigorously opposed.

**Why it matters here.** Gnosticism features in the LDS argument that earliest Christianity was *plural*, not a single pristine church that later shattered — competing forms existed from the start. Critics counter that the church's rejection of Gnostic dualism shows a stable rule of faith, not chaos. Latter-day Saints generally distance their own theology (embodied God, good creation) from Gnostic anti-materialism, while noting that some Gnostic groups preserved rites and ideas the mainstream later lost.
