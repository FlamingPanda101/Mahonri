# Eisegesis

**Definition.** Eisegesis (from Greek *eis*, "into," + *hēgeisthai*, "to lead") is the interpretive error of reading a meaning *into* a text that is not warranted by it — projecting the reader's prior beliefs, later doctrines, or alien categories onto the words. It is the standard foil to [[Exegesis]] (drawing meaning *out* of the text). The variant spelling "isogesis" appears in some of this library's notes.

**Why it matters here.** "That's eisegesis" is a frequent move on both sides of LDS apologetics. Critics charge that creedal monotheism and the Trinity are read back into Genesis and the prophets — metaphysical conclusions the authors never held. Apologists level the same charge at localizing or harmonizing readings that bend a passage to fit a prior commitment. Because the accusation is symmetric, debates often turn on which side has actually established its reading from the text's own context.
