# Pericope

**Definition.** From Greek *perikopē* ("a cutting around," a section cut out), a self-contained unit of biblical text — a single story, saying, or paragraph — treated as a coherent whole for study, liturgical reading, or analysis. Scholars speak of "the love-of-enemies pericope" or "the woman-taken-in-adultery pericope." Identifying a passage's boundaries as a discrete unit is a routine first step in form and textual criticism.

**Why it matters here.** Pericope boundaries become apologetic when a whole unit's authenticity is in play. The classic case is the *Pericope Adulterae* (John 7:53–8:11), widely judged a later interpolation absent from the earliest manuscripts — a concrete example of the canon's fluid edges. Reading a verse within, rather than apart from, its pericope also reshapes meaning, as in the vault's treatment of Matthew 5:48 inside its surrounding love passage.
