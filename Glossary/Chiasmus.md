# Chiasmus

**Definition.** From the Greek letter chi (Χ), chiasmus is a literary structure that states a sequence of ideas and then repeats them in reverse order, producing an A-B-C…C′-B′-A′ pattern that often pivots on a central, emphasized element. It is a recognized feature of ancient Hebrew poetry and rhetoric (e.g., parts of Genesis, Isaiah, and Leviticus) and appears in other ancient literatures as well.

**Why it matters here.** John W. Welch's 1967 discovery of an extended chiasm in Alma 36 is offered as evidence that the Book of Mormon reflects genuine ancient Semitic composition rather than 1820s frontier authorship. Critics counter that chiasmus can arise by chance or by an author's unconscious patterning, so the debate turns on statistical rigor, intentionality, and how strict the proposed structures are.
