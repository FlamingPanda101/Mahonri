# Rule of Faith

**Definition.** The rule of faith (Latin *regula fidei*; Greek *kanōn tēs pisteōs*) is the early church's summary of authoritative, apostolic teaching about God, Christ, and the Spirit, used in the second and third centuries to distinguish orthodoxy from heresy. In writers like Irenaeus and Tertullian it was *creed-shaped before there were creeds* — a three-article confession handed down "from the apostles, the apostles from Christ, and Christ from God." Notably, it functioned as authoritative *teaching* before any fixed *list of books* (canon) existed.

**Why it matters here.** Critics of *sola scriptura* use it to argue the earliest Christians operated under transmitted apostolic teaching, not Scripture-alone — the canon itself began as a rule of faith, not a table of contents. The phrase also appears in canon debates: an infallible autograph we no longer possess, the argument goes, cannot serve as the church's working rule of faith.
