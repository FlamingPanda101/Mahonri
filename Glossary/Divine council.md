# Divine council

**Definition.** The **divine council** is the assembly of heavenly beings — "sons of God" / lesser *elohim* — depicted in the Hebrew Bible and the wider ancient Near East as gathered around the high God, who presides over them (e.g., Psalm 82; 1 Kings 22:19; Job 1–2; Deuteronomy 32:8–9). The motif has close parallels in Ugaritic texts, where the high god El convenes the assembly of the gods. Modern biblical scholars (notably Michael Heiser) treat it as a genuine feature of Israelite cosmology, not mere metaphor.

**Why it matters here.** Latter-day Saints invoke the divine council as evidence that ancient Israel pictured a plurality of divine beings under the Father — supporting the LDS Godhead and the plural "let us" of Genesis 1 — whereas traditional readers interpret these beings as angels and the plurals as the Trinity or a "royal we." The dispute is over whether early Israel was strict monotheist or had a tiered divine world.
