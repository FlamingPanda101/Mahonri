# Reformed epistemology

**Definition.** A school in the epistemology of religious belief — developed by Alvin Plantinga, Nicholas Wolterstorff, and William Alston — holding that belief in God can be rational and **warranted without being based on argument or evidence**. Drawing on Calvin's notion of the *sensus divinitatis* (an innate awareness of God), it treats theistic belief as potentially *properly basic*: produced directly by reliable, properly functioning cognitive faculties in the right circumstances. Plantinga's "warranted Christian belief" extends this to specifically Christian doctrines grounded in the internal witness of the Holy Spirit.

**Why it matters here.** It reframes the burden of proof: the believer need not satisfy the evidentialist before believing rationally. This is congenial to a Latter-day Saint epistemology that grounds conviction in the Spirit's witness, though the two traditions differ sharply on theology proper. Critics raise the "Great Pumpkin" objection — that the same strategy would license any sincerely held religious belief as basic.
