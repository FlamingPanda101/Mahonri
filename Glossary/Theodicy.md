# Theodicy

**Definition.** A **theodicy** (from Greek *theos*, "god," + *dikē*, "justice" — literally "justifying God") is a positive attempt to explain *why* a good and powerful God would permit evil and suffering. The term was coined by Leibniz in 1710. A theodicy is usually distinguished from a mere *defense*: a defense only argues that God *might* have a sufficient reason (showing the critic has not proven inconsistency), whereas a theodicy proposes what that reason actually is — e.g., free will, soul-making, or the necessity of opposition.

**Why it matters here.** Theodicy is where apologetics moves from rebutting the problem of evil to offering an account of suffering. The vault catalogues several: soul-making, the "agape" theodicy of learning divine love, and Lehi's Book of Mormon teaching that there must be "opposition in all things." Critics counter that any theodicy which makes suffering instrumental "instrumentalizes persons," and that a truly omnipotent God should not need such trade-offs.
