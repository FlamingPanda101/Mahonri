# Modalism

**Definition.** Modalism (also Sabellianism, after Sabellius, or modalistic monarchianism) is the view that Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are not three distinct persons but three modes, roles, or temporary manifestations of one single divine person. Its strict form, patripassianism, implies the Father suffered on the cross. It was condemned as heresy by the early church precisely for collapsing the personal distinctions that Trinitarianism preserves.

**Why it matters here.** Critics sometimes accuse the LDS Godhead, or certain Book of Mormon "Father and Son" passages (e.g., Mosiah 15), of modalism. Latter-day Saints reject this: their model affirms three numerically distinct beings, which is the opposite error from modalism — and they note that the historic Trinity is itself charged with sliding toward either modalism or tritheism depending on how its analogies are pressed.
