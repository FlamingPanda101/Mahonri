# Exegesis

**Definition.** Exegesis (from Greek *exēgēsis*, "a leading out," from *exēgeisthai*, "to draw out, explain") is the disciplined drawing-out of a text's meaning *from* the text itself — attending to its grammar, original language, literary genre, and historical-cultural setting to recover what the author plausibly intended for the original audience. It is the methodological ideal of responsible interpretation, contrasted with reading meaning *into* a text (see [[Eisegesis]]).

**Why it matters here.** "Exegesis vs. eisegesis" is a recurring charge in apologetic exchange. Critics accuse traditional Trinitarian and creedal readings of importing later Greek metaphysics into the Hebrew text rather than deriving doctrine exegetically; LDS apologists make the same move against proof-texting that lifts verses from context. The dispute is whether a given reading genuinely "leads out" the author's meaning or smuggles in a foreign framework.
