# Two natures of Christ

**Definition.** The **two natures of Christ** is the orthodox teaching that the one person Jesus subsists in two complete natures, fully divine and fully human, united "without confusion, change, division, or separation." Defined at Ephesus (431) and the Council of Chalcedon (451), this **hypostatic union** was set against Nestorianism (two persons), Eutychianism/monophysitism (one blended nature), and Apollinarianism (a truncated humanity). It is the conceptual machinery by which classical Christianity holds Christ to be at once God and man.

**Why it matters here.** Trinitarian apologists use the two natures to explain how Christ can pray, suffer, die, and not know the hour while remaining God—attributing such acts to the human nature. Critics in this library press several objections: that natures do not act (only persons do), that two minds yield "two Jesuses," and that the whole apparatus is a Greek solution to a Greek-framed problem the biblical writers never posed, asserted as a labeled mystery.
