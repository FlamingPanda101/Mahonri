# Council of Nicaea

**Definition.** The First Council of Nicaea (325 AD), convened by the emperor Constantine in Nicaea (modern İznik, Turkey), was the first ecumenical council of the Christian church. Its central act was condemning Arius — who taught that the Son was a created being — and adopting the original Nicene Creed, which declared the Son *homoousios* ("of the same substance") with the Father. It also addressed the dating of Easter and various points of church order.

**Why it matters here.** Nicaea is a focal point of LDS and broader apologetic debate over how doctrine formed. Critics of the creeds stress that an emperor convened it, that the decisive term was non-biblical, and that the dispute was not in fact settled at Nicaea (Arian controversy continued for decades). Defenders argue the council faithfully articulated apostolic teaching against a genuine distortion. LDS writers often treat Nicaea as a marker of post-apostolic drift rather than authoritative clarification.
