# Pseudepigrapha

**Definition.** From Greek for "falsely attributed writings." The term names ancient Jewish and Christian works ascribed to a venerable figure (Enoch, Moses, Abraham, Solomon, or an apostle) who did not actually write them — e.g., 1 Enoch, Jubilees, the Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs. In antiquity such attribution was often a recognized literary convention conferring authority on a tradition, not necessarily a deceptive forgery in the modern sense.

**Why it matters here.** Two debates turn on this word. Critics label disputed biblical books (e.g., the Pastoral or Johannine epistles) pseudepigraphal to question their authenticity, and some call the Book of Mormon modern pseudepigrapha. LDS apologists respond that pseudepigraphic attribution was a legitimate ancient mode and that several pseudepigrapha preserve authentic early material echoed in Restoration scripture.
