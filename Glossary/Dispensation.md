# Dispensation

**Definition.** A period in which God commits priesthood authority, keys, and saving knowledge to a presiding prophet, opening a new era of revelation (from Latin *dispensatio*, "administration, stewardship"; cf. Greek *oikonomia*, Ephesians 1:10). Latter-day Saints typically count seven major dispensations — Adam, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and the latter-day "dispensation of the fulness of times" — each headed by a prophet who received keys and, in one LDS reading, a declaration of divine sonship.

**Why it matters here.** The dispensational framework lets the Restoration present itself as the gathering and culmination of all prior eras rather than a novelty, and undergirds the claim that authority can be lost (apostasy) and conferred anew. Critics note the seven-dispensation grid and the per-prophet "sonship" pattern rest largely on Restoration scripture (the Books of Moses and Abraham) and are read back into the Bible, not derived from it by ordinary exegesis.
