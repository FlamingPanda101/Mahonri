# Covenant

**Definition.** A covenant (Hebrew *berit*; Greek *diathēkē*) is a binding, often oath-sealed relationship establishing mutual obligations between parties — in the Bible, between God and a person or people (Noah, Abraham, Moses/Sinai, David, the "new covenant"). Scholars note its ancient Near Eastern form mirrors suzerain–vassal treaties, where the subordinate's required "love" denotes sworn loyalty rather than affection. The terms "Old/New Testament" themselves translate *covenant*.

**Why it matters here.** Covenant is the organizing category of LDS theology — ordinances are covenant acts, the temple a covenant relationship, salvation a covenant path — which frames the debate with *sola fide* Protestantism over whether obedient works are wages earned or covenant responses to grace. Critics observe that biblical covenant language is borrowed from Assyrian vassal treaties, which apologists may read as either undermining or illuminating its theological weight.
