# Kenosis

**Definition.** ***Kenosis*** (Greek κένωσις, "emptying," from *ekenōsen*, "he emptied himself") names the self-limitation of the divine Son in becoming human, drawn from the "Christ hymn" of Philippians 2:5–8, where Christ "emptied himself, taking the form of a servant." **Kenotic christology** is the modern theory that in the Incarnation the Son temporarily set aside or rendered latent certain divine prerogatives (e.g. omniscience), explaining why Jesus could grow, suffer, and "not know the hour" (Mark 13:32).

**Why it matters here.** Kenosis is a major LDS apologetic lever. Defenders argue the Son genuinely emptied himself of the active exercise of divinity—holding divine properties "in potentiality"—which makes sense of Gethsemane and his ignorance of the hour without a two-natures partition. Trinitarian readers counter that *what* he emptied himself of was glory or status, not deity, since a god who can shed divine attributes was never immutably God.
