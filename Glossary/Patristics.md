# Patristics

**Definition.** Patristics (from Latin *pater*, "father") is the study of the "Church Fathers" — the influential Christian writers, bishops, and theologians of roughly the 1st through 8th centuries whose works shaped doctrine, liturgy, canon, and exegesis. It is often divided into the *ante-Nicene* Fathers (before the Council of Nicaea in 325) and the post-Nicene Fathers. Their texts are the primary witnesses for how beliefs and practices actually developed after the apostles.

**Why it matters here.** Both sides mine the Fathers as evidence. Latter-day Saints cite ante-Nicene writers to argue that early Christianity held ideas (deification, premortal life, embodied worship) later edited out, and that doctrine drifted as apostolic authority lapsed. Traditional apologists cite the same Fathers to show continuity and an unbroken rule of faith. A key methodological caution: a Father *quoting* a book or idea does not by itself prove how authoritatively he ranked it.
