# Pelagianism

**Definition.** Pelagianism is the teaching associated with the British monk Pelagius (c. 354–418) that human beings, by their own unaided free will, can choose the good and attain righteousness without a prior, enabling gift of divine grace; it denies that Adam's guilt is inherited. Augustine opposed it, and it was condemned at the Council of Ephesus (431). *Semi-Pelagianism*—the milder view that the human will takes the *first* step toward God before grace—was condemned at the Second Council of Orange (529).

**Why it matters here.** "Pelagian" is a frequent polemical charge against Latter-day Saints, who reject inherited guilt and stress moral agency. But LDS theology insists exaltation is impossible without Christ's atonement and ordinances, so the label is widely judged inaccurate—one evangelical scholar (Kyle Beshears) publicly retracted it, preferring at most "LDS semi-Pelagianism," since the LDS view is grace-dependent, not works-autonomous.
