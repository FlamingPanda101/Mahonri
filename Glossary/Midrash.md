# Midrash

**Definition.** Midrash (Hebrew, from *darash*, "to seek, inquire, expound") is the classical rabbinic mode of interpreting scripture — filling narrative gaps, harmonizing tensions, and drawing out moral or legal meaning, often by imaginative expansion of the biblical text. As a body of literature it includes works like Genesis Rabbah; as a method it ranges from close legal exegesis (*halakhic*) to homiletical storytelling (*aggadic*). It typically presupposes a text more pregnant with meaning than its surface allows.

**Why it matters here.** This library frequently invokes midrashic and apocryphal traditions (e.g., the garments of Adam, expansions of Genesis) to show that LDS and early-Christian readings have precedent in ancient Jewish interpretation rather than being modern inventions. The double-edge: a parallel in midrash can authenticate a motif as anciently attested, but critics note that midrash is admittedly elaborative legend, so its support is for *tradition-history*, not historical fact.
