# Hermeneutics

**Definition.** Hermeneutics (from Greek *hermēneuō*, "to interpret, translate") is the theory and method of interpretation — the principles by which a reader moves from a text's words to its meaning. It governs which questions to ask (authorial intent, genre, historical setting, canonical context) and which assumptions ("interpretive lenses") the reader brings. Every reading of scripture, however "plain," operates from some hermeneutic, whether acknowledged or not.

**Why it matters here.** Much LDS–Protestant debate is hermeneutical before it is doctrinal: critics of *sola scriptura* argue that a text never simply "speaks for itself" but is always negotiated through prior commitments, so an infallible Bible without an authoritative interpreter underdetermines doctrine. Defenders of perspicuity reply that scripture's core teaching is clear to any sincere reader; LDS apologists counter that interpretive disagreement is pervasive and requires living revelatory authority.
