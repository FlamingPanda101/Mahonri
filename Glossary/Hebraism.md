# Hebraism

**Definition.** A Hebraism is a grammatical, idiomatic, or rhetorical feature characteristic of the Hebrew language that surfaces in another language — typically as an awkward, un-English construction left over when a Hebrew text is rendered literally. Stock examples include the cognate accusative ("dream a dream"), the conditional "if-and" clause, construct-state noun chains ("plates of brass"), and extended parallelism.

**Why it matters here.** Apologists argue that the Book of Mormon's 1830 text contains Hebraisms that read poorly as English but make sense as literal translations of a Semitic original, evidence its author could not plausibly have faked. Critics reply that many such features also occur in the King James Bible (which Joseph Smith knew) or in ordinary English, so the markers must be weighed for distinctiveness, not merely tallied.
