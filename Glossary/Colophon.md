# Colophon

**Definition.** In ancient and medieval manuscripts, a colophon is a scribe's or author's note — typically at the beginning or end of a record — identifying the writer, the source, the subject, or the circumstances of composition ("I, Nephi, having been born of goodly parents…"). The convention is well attested in Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and biblical literature, where such framing formulas mark the seams between originally separate documents.

**Why it matters here.** Apologists point to colophon-style superscriptions throughout the Book of Mormon (and to copyist phrases like "by the hand of") as evidence the text imitates genuine ancient record-keeping conventions Joseph Smith is unlikely to have known. In biblical studies the same concept appears in source-critical arguments — e.g., the toledot formulas of Genesis read as redactional joints rather than authorial summaries.
