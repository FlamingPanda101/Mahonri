# Soul-making theodicy

**Definition.** The **soul-making theodicy** holds that God permits suffering because a world of hardship and moral struggle is the necessary environment for immature souls to grow into mature, virtuous, God-like persons. Developed by John Hick in *Evil and the God of Love* (1966) and traced by Hick to the second-century church father Irenaeus (hence "Irenaean"), it contrasts with the Augustinian model that treats evil purely as a fall from original perfection. On this view humans begin at an "epistemic distance" from God so their growth toward Him can be free and genuine.

**Why it matters here.** LDS apologists (notably David Paulsen) note Hick's scheme is "highly reminiscent" of Joseph Smith's teaching that mortality is a probationary school for eternal progression. The vault highlights one decisive divergence: Hick retains creation *ex nihilo* and must escape the resulting "divine complicity" via universalism, whereas the LDS denial of *ex nihilo* lets soul-making stand without making God the author of evil.
