# Impassibility

**Definition.** The classical-theist attribute that God cannot suffer or be acted upon — he has no *passions* (from Latin *passio*, "something undergone"). A passion is a being-moved by another, which would mean having a potential actualized; since God is pure actuality, nothing a creature does can move him, grieve him, or alter his state. Classical theists insist this does not make God cold or inert: his love is his own unchanging, supremely living *act*, not a reactive emotion he is buffeted by.

**Why it matters here.** Impassibility is one of the clearest LDS–creedal flashpoints. The Westminster Confession's "without body, parts, or passions" denies God passibility; Latter-day Saints affirm a God "of body, parts, *and* passions" (D&C 130:22) who is genuinely grieved and moved with compassion. Creedal theists read biblical grief-language as *anthropopathism* (accommodated figurative speech); LDS and many open theists take it as literal, arguing real relationship requires the capacity to be affected.
