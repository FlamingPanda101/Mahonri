# Eternal conscious torment

**Definition.** **Eternal conscious torment** (ECT) is the traditional Christian doctrine that the damned suffer punishment in hell consciously and without end. It is the majority historic position, defended by figures such as Augustine and Jonathan Edwards, and stands opposed to annihilationism (the damned are destroyed) and universalism (all are eventually saved). Its central rationale is that sin against a being of *infinite* worth merits infinite punishment, so the penalty's endlessness is not disproportionate.

**Why it matters here.** ECT is the specific target of the problem of hell. Critics — including universalists like David Bentley Hart — call unending punishment for finite sins "disproportionate, unjust, and sheer pointless cruelty," and argue it is incompatible with a loving God who creates souls *ex nihilo*. Latter-day Saints reinterpret the scriptural basis: "eternal punishment" names *God's* punishment rather than punishment of infinite duration, and the binary saved/damned scheme is replaced by graded degrees of glory.
