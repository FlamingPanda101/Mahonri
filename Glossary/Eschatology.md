# Eschatology

**Definition.** Eschatology (from Greek *eschatos*, "last," + *logos*, "study") is the theology of "last things" — death, resurrection, judgment, the Second Coming, the millennium, and the final state. It encompasses both personal destiny and the cosmic consummation of history. Christian eschatology inherited and transformed Jewish "Day of the Lord" expectation, and early Christians lived with an acute, sometimes anxious sense of an imminent end.

**Why it matters here.** Eschatology supplies a key restorationist argument: the earliest Christians were predominantly premillennial and physically literal about Christ's return, a hope later allegorized away (by Origen, then Augustine, who recast the millennium as the present church age). This library treats that shift as evidence of doctrinal drift — an apostasy from primitive expectation — whereas mainstream Christianity reads it as legitimate theological maturation after the delayed *parousia*.
