# Urim and Thummim

**Definition.** In the Hebrew Bible, the *Urim and Thummim* (אוּרִים וְתֻמִּים, conventionally "Lights and Perfections") were objects kept in the breastplate of Israel's high priest and used to obtain yes/no oracular answers from God (Exodus 28:30). In Latter-day Saint usage the same label was later applied to the "interpreters" — two transparent stones set in a frame — that accompanied the gold plates and aided Joseph Smith's translation. The Book of Mormon text itself never uses the phrase "Urim and Thummim," speaking only of "interpreters."

**Why it matters here.** Critics allege the Book of Mormon borrows the biblical Urim and Thummim (e.g., from *View of the Hebrews*); defenders answer that the term is a later LDS convention layered onto the text, not part of it, and that any resemblance is inherited from a shared biblical source rather than copied from a contemporary book.
