# Divine Simplicity

**Definition.** Divine simplicity is the classical-theist doctrine that God has no parts of any kind — no composition of matter, no distinction between His essence and existence, and no real distinction among His attributes. On this view God does not *have* goodness, power, or wisdom; He *is* each of them, and they are identical in Him. It is a cornerstone of the classical theism of Augustine, Aquinas, and much Catholic and Reformed thought, often argued from God being pure actuality and the ultimate, uncaused source.

**Why it matters here.** Latter-day Saints reject divine simplicity, holding instead that God is embodied, in time, and constituted of distinct attributes — a position critics say abandons classical theism. The vault presents both sides: defenders argue simplicity follows necessarily from God being the absolute source, while critics argue it cannot be reconciled with the dynamic, anthropomorphic God of the Bible.
