# Eternal progression

**Definition.** **Eternal progression** is the Latter-day Saint doctrine that intelligent beings advance everlastingly in knowledge, capacity, and glory, and that the highest endpoint of human destiny is to become like God (see [[exaltation]]). It frames existence as an ongoing trajectory of growth rather than a static, completed state, and is often paired with the couplet "As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be." The idea has partial analogues in patristic and Eastern Christian thought, notably Gregory of Nyssa's *epektasis* — perpetual stretching toward the infinite God.

**Why it matters here.** Critics object that a God who "progressed" cannot be the eternal, immutable God of classical theism, and that endless human deification collapses the Creator–creature distinction. Defenders distinguish the LDS view (progression toward and within divinity) from a crude "God was once a sinner" caricature, and point to Christian precedents for never-ending advance toward God.
