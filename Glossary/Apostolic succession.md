# Apostolic succession

**Definition.** Apostolic succession is the doctrine that legitimate church authority is transmitted in an unbroken line from the apostles to their successors, traditionally through bishops by the laying on of hands. In Catholic and Orthodox theology it guarantees valid ordination, sacraments, and teaching authority. The concept appears early (e.g., in Irenaeus and Hippolytus), who appealed to lines of succession to refute heretical teachers claiming secret authority.

**Why it matters here.** This is a core fault line. Latter-day Saints argue that the genuine apostolic line — priesthood authority and keys — was lost when the apostles died without successors, producing the Great Apostasy, and was restored only by angelic ordination in the 1830s. They thus reject the claim that an institutional episcopal chain preserved authentic authority. Catholic and Orthodox apologists hold that succession was never broken. The dispute turns on whether continuity of authority is structural (an unbroken chain) or requires divine reauthorization.
