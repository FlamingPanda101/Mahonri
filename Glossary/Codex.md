# Codex

**Definition.** From Latin *caudex* ("tree trunk," then "book of wooden tablets"), a codex is a manuscript bound as leaves between covers — the page-and-spine format that replaced the scroll. Early Christians adopted the codex strikingly early, and the great 4th-century parchment codices **Sinaiticus** and **Vaticanus** are among the oldest near-complete Bibles. A single codex could hold many books together, which helped shape perceptions of a unified "Bible."

**Why it matters here.** Major codices are primary evidence in canon and text debates: what books a codex contains (Sinaiticus includes the Epistle of Barnabas and the Shepherd of Hermas) shows the canon was not yet fixed, and their wording settles disputed readings.
