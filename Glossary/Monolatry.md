# Monolatry

**Definition.** Monolatry (Greek *monos*, "single," + *latreia*, "worship") is the exclusive worship of one god without denying that other gods exist. It is distinguished from henotheism mainly by emphasis — monolatry foregrounds the demand for exclusive devotion (as in "you shall have no other gods before me") — and from monotheism, which asserts that only one god exists at all. Scholars often apply the term to early Israelite religion, especially readings of Deuteronomy 32:8-9 in which the Most High apportions the nations among divine beings.

**Why it matters here.** The Dead Sea Scrolls reading of Deuteronomy 32 (which has "sons of God" where the later Masoretic Text reads "sons of Israel") is used by Latter-day Saints to argue Scripture presupposes a divine council. Critics counter that the commands are practical loyalty rhetoric, not an ontological concession that rival gods are real.
