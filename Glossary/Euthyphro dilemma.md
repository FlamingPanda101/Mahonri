# Euthyphro dilemma

**Definition.** The **Euthyphro dilemma**, from Plato's dialogue *Euthyphro*, poses a question about the relation between God and morality: *Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?* If the first, morality is arbitrary — God could make cruelty obligatory. If the second, then goodness is a standard independent of God, and God is not its source. The dilemma is the classic objection to **divine command theory** (the view that moral obligations just *are* God's commands).

**Why it matters here.** It is a key battleground in the moral argument and in critiques of the Calvinist "moral monster" God. A common reply is a proposed **third horn** (associated with Alston and Adams): God neither consults an external standard nor commands arbitrarily, because God *is* the Good — the standard itself. Critics answer that even a standard-setting God must be *recognizably* loving, or the word "good" is emptied of meaning. LDS thinkers tend to locate moral truth in principles God Himself honors rather than invents.
