# Typology

**Definition.** Typology (from Greek *typos*, "stamp, pattern, foreshadowing") is a mode of interpretation in which an earlier person, event, or institution (the *type*) prefigures a later one (the *antitype*), the two linked by divine design across salvation history. Classic Christian examples treat the Passover lamb, the binding of Isaac, or the brazen serpent as types of Christ. Typology claims a real correspondence in history, distinguishing it from free allegory.

**Why it matters here.** Typological reading is central to how this library connects the Old Testament, temple ritual, and the Book of Mormon to Christ — and to how early Christians like Melito of Sardis read scripture. Critics counter that typology is unfalsifiable: any text can be made to "point to Christ" retroactively, so a typological match is not evidence of authorship or inspiration. The contested question is whether a given correspondence is authorially intended or imposed by the later interpreter.
