# Inference to the best explanation

**Definition.** A pattern of reasoning — a refinement of what C. S. Peirce called *abduction* — in which, from a body of data, one infers the hypothesis that, if true, would best explain that data. Rival explanations are ranked by criteria such as explanatory scope, explanatory power, simplicity, plausibility, and freedom from ad hoc assumptions; the strongest is provisionally accepted. Unlike deduction it does not guarantee its conclusion: IBE yields *probable* conclusions, the same evidential standard a strong scientific theory aspires to, and remains open to revision. (See also the vault's broader entry on abductive reasoning.)

**Why it matters here.** IBE is the engine of the design, fine-tuning, historical, and witness arguments: design is argued as the best explanation of biological information, theism as the best explanation of the cosmos, and the Book of Mormon witnesses' testimony as resisting every naturalistic account. It also offers a "third way" between deductive proof and fideism. Critics warn that the "best of a bad lot" need not be true and dispute which criteria favor a designer.
