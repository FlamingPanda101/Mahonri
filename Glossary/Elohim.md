# Elohim

**Definition.** **Elohim** (אֱלֹהִים) is the most common Hebrew word for "God" in the Old Testament. Though grammatically plural in form, it usually takes singular verbs when denoting the God of Israel, but the same word is also applied to other gods, to members of the divine council, to angels, and even to deceased humans (1 Samuel 28:13). In Latter-day Saint usage **Elohim** is also adopted as a proper name/title for God the Father, distinct from **Jehovah** (the premortal Christ).

**Why it matters here.** The plural form and its application to multiple beings are central to debates over biblical monotheism: critics read the plural as an intensive or majestic singular, while LDS and some scholars argue it reflects a real divine plurality (a head God over lesser *elohim*). The LDS Father=Elohim / Son=Jehovah convention is a later naming scheme, not a claim about Hebrew grammar.
