# Baptism for the Dead

**Definition.** A proxy ordinance in which a living person is baptized on behalf of someone who died without the rite, on the premise that the deceased may then accept or reject it in the spirit world. Latter-day Saints ground the practice in Paul's question, "else what shall they do which are baptized for the dead?" (1 Corinthians 15:29), and in Christ's preaching to "the dead" (1 Peter 4:6); Joseph Smith introduced it in 1840.

**Why it matters here.** Critics dismiss 1 Corinthians 15:29 as a single ambiguous verse and call the practice a Mormon novelty; the vault answers that the Greek most naturally describes an actual rite, that proxy baptism is attested among early groups such as the Marcionites, and that it resolves the fairness problem of those who never heard the gospel. It is a flashpoint in debates over whether baptism is salvific.
