# Image christology

**Definition.** **Image christology** interprets Jesus as a *divine image* (Greek *eikōn*; cf. Colossians 1:15, "the image of the invisible God")—a figure who bears, manifests, and mediates the divine presence, agency, and authority, so that "whoever has seen me has seen the Father" (John 14:9). On this ancient model an image makes a deity *present* to those who behold it without being identical to that deity, drawing on Near Eastern conventions of cult-images and divine agency.

**Why it matters here.** Associated in this library with Dan McClellan, image christology is offered as a third option between "Jesus is merely a man" and "Jesus is the Father." It lets John affirm a high view of Jesus and the Father's invisibility (John 1:18) without contradiction—seeing Jesus is seeing God *because he bears God's presence, not because he is God*. Trinitarian readers take the same image language (Colossians 1:15; Hebrews 1:3) as itself an assertion of full, consubstantial deity.
