# Messiah

**Definition.** "Messiah" (Hebrew *mashiach*, "anointed one"; Greek *christos*, hence "Christ") originally designated anyone ritually anointed for office — king, priest, or prophet — and only later a specific eschatological deliverer. Second Temple Judaism held diverse and competing messianic expectations: a royal Davidic king, a priestly figure, and, in some streams, a pre-existent heavenly "Son of Man." Christianity identifies Jesus as this awaited figure.

**Why it matters here.** The diversity of messianic expectation drives several apologetic arguments here. Critics note that Jewish non-recognition of Jesus is partly a textual problem — the Hebrew Bible does not unambiguously predict him — while apologists argue that pre-Christian Jewish sources (notably the Parables of Enoch) already envisioned a divine, pre-existent Messiah, supporting a high Christology as authentically Jewish rather than a later Hellenistic invention.
