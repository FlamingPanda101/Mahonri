# Creed

**Definition.** A creed (from Latin *credo*, "I believe") is an authoritative, fixed summary statement of Christian belief, used in worship, baptism, and to mark the boundaries of orthodoxy. The major ecumenical creeds are the Apostles' Creed, the Nicene (Nicene-Constantinopolitan) Creed, and the Athanasian Creed. Creeds emerged partly from baptismal confessions and partly as responses to controversies, condensing contested doctrine — especially about God and Christ — into binding language.

**Why it matters here.** "Creedal Christianity" is often the implicit definition of Christianity used to exclude Latter-day Saints. LDS apologetics responds that the creeds embed post-biblical, Hellenized metaphysics; that they were settled by councils and state power rather than apostolic revelation; and that making assent to them the test of Christianity is circular. A historically important distinction: Joseph Smith's harsh language about creeds being an "abomination" is read by LDS writers as condemning rigid sectarian dogmatism that ended further revelation, not the ancient ecumenical creeds wholesale.
