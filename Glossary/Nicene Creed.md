# Nicene Creed

**Definition.** The Nicene Creed is the most widely shared Christian creed, originally issued at the Council of Nicaea (325) and expanded at the First Council of Constantinople (381) into the form most churches recite today. It confesses one God in three persons — Father, Son (*homoousios*, "of one substance," with the Father), and Holy Spirit — and summarizes belief in creation, the incarnation, crucifixion, resurrection, the church, baptism, and the life to come. A later Western addition, the *Filioque*, helped trigger the East–West schism.

**Why it matters here.** "Creedal Christianity" is frequently defined by assent to this creed, and some apologists use it to exclude Latter-day Saints from the label "Christian." LDS writers respond on two fronts: that the creed imports non-biblical, Hellenized categories foreign to the apostles, and that using it as the gatekeeping baseline is circular. Notably, the Restoration's stronger language about creeds targeted sectarian dogmatism, not the ecumenical creeds as such.
