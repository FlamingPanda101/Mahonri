# Deuterocanon

**Definition.** From Greek for "second canon," a term coined by Sixtus of Siena (1566) for the Old Testament books — Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1–2 Maccabees, and additions to Esther and Daniel — that Roman Catholics and (with a wider list) Eastern Orthodox accept as Scripture but that are absent from the Jewish Hebrew Bible. The "second" denotes books whose canonical status was historically disputed or later affirmed, not books of lesser inspiration in Catholic usage. Protestants call these same books the **Apocrypha**.

**Why it matters here.** Whether these books were "removed" by Protestants or "added" at Trent (1546) is hotly contested. LDS and Catholic apologists note that the early Church and Septuagint included them; Protestants reply that Jerome and the Hebrew tradition excluded them. The vault stresses that neither rival canon was definitively closed before the Reformation era.
