# Immutability

**Definition.** The classical-theist attribute that God cannot change (Latin *immutabilis*, "unchangeable"). On the Thomistic account, change just *is* the actualization of a potential; since God is pure actuality with no unrealized potential, he can undergo no real change. Defenders distinguish *real* change (an intrinsic alteration) from mere *Cambridge* change (a relational shift, as when God "becomes" Creator while only the world's status changes), allowing God to act and create without altering in himself.

**Why it matters here.** Strong metaphysical immutability is a sharp dividing line. Classical theists treat it as entailed by divine simplicity and perfection — a changeless God is maximally stable, not inert. Latter-day Saints affirm constancy of God's *character* and covenants (Malachi 3:6) but reject the strong version, holding that the Father genuinely responds to prayer, is moved by his children, and acts at successive moments — and that wholesale reclassifying biblical divine actions as "Cambridge changes" is special pleading.
