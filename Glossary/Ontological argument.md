# Ontological argument

**Definition.** An *a priori* argument that infers God's existence from the concept of God alone, without appeal to empirical evidence. The name (from Greek *on / ontos*, "being") was coined later; Anselm's eleventh-century version reasons that God, "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," must exist in reality and not merely in the mind, since real existence is greater than imagined existence. Descartes recast it (existence is a perfection a supremely perfect being must possess), and modal versions argue that a necessary being, if possible, exists in all possible worlds.

**Why it matters here.** It is the most contested of the classical proofs. Kant's famous objection — that "existence is not a real predicate" — is often extended to claim the *cosmological* argument secretly presupposes it. The vault notes the standard reply: Aquinas and most Thomists reject the ontological argument while affirming the cosmological one, so the two are not bound together, and metaphysical necessity differs from "existence by definition."
