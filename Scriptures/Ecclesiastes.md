# Ecclesiastes

> Vanity of vanities, saith the Preacher, vanity of vanities; all is vanity. What profit hath a man of all his labour which he taketh under the sun? *(Ecclesiastes 1:2–3, KJV)*

> Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. *(Ecclesiastes 12:13, KJV)*

## Key Term: *hebel* ("vanity / vapor / wind")
The book's refrain — Hebrew **הֶבֶל** *hebel* — means literally "breath," "vapor," or "wind": something fleeting and insubstantial. The King James renders it **"vanity"** ("vanity of vanities"); [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] notes that some exegetes argue the best modern rendering is **"absurd,"** aligning the Teacher's complaint with Albert Camus' *Myth of Sisyphus* — reality "shattering all of our expectations." O'Connor's translation of choice: "Absurd, absurd, everything is absurd."[^1]

## Key Figure: Qoheleth ("the Teacher / Preacher")
The speaker (Hebrew *Qoheleth*, "assembler/preacher") is presented as a great king who "denied my eyes no pleasure," drank wine, and lived an illustrious life, yet "saw that it was all *hebel*… it was all just wind." The recurring phrase **"under the sun"** signals the earthly, sub-heavenly vantage from which "there is no new thing" and no ultimate purpose appears (origin of the idiom "nothing new under the sun").[^1]

## Why a skeptic cites it
O'Connor invokes Ecclesiastes to argue, against the common view, that **nihilism is not a modern, post-religious invention** (Nietzsche → Camus/Sartre) but is ancient — and that "religions emerge as a *response* to nihilism," reversing the usual causal story. He calls Ecclesiastes "one of the still greatest expositions of nihilism… in the printed word."[^1] He finds the book's resolution — *fear God and keep his commandments* (12:13) — unsatisfying because it affirms that everything the Teacher said was "upright and true" yet supplies "no explanation as to why that would solve the problem… no mechanism as to what that would change."[^2] This reading is engaged in [[Objective Meaning Requires a Self-Justifying Non-Contingent Terminus]], where the apologetic reply (cf. [[The Absurdity of Life Without God]]) is that the fear of God *is* the self-justifying, non-contingent terminus the Teacher's "under the sun" survey could never reach — the book's structure dramatizes exactly O'Connor's point that meaning evaporates without a transcendent ground and returns only when one is supplied.
