# Hebrews 11:3

> "Through faith we understand that the worlds were framed by the word of God, so that things which are seen were not made of things which do appear." (KJV)

**Greek:** Πίστει νοοῦμεν κατηρτίσθαι τοὺς αἰῶνας ῥήματι θεοῦ, εἰς τὸ μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων τὸ βλεπόμενον γεγονέναι (*Pistei nooumen katērtisthai tous aiōnas rhēmati theou, eis to mē ek phainomenōn to blepomenon gegonenai*)

**Key phrase:** μὴ ἐκ φαινομένων (*mē ek phainomenōn*) — "not out of things that are visible"

## Context

Hebrews 11 is the "faith hall of fame" chapter, and verse 3 serves as a thesis statement: faith enables understanding that the visible world was made by God's word from what is NOT visible. The contrast is between the visible (τὸ βλεπόμενον) and the invisible (τὰ φαινόμενα in the negated form) — NOT between being and non-being.

The verb κατηρτίσθαι (*katērtisthai*) means "to be fitted together, prepared, equipped, restored to proper condition" — it carries the connotation of ordering and arranging rather than generating from nothing. It is the same verb used for mending nets (Matt 4:21) and restoring a fallen brother (Gal 6:1).

## Significance for the Creation Ex Nihilo Debate

This verse has been cited by creation ex nihilo apologists as evidence that the New Testament teaches creation from nothing. However, in its original context:

- The contrast is **visible/invisible**, not **something/nothing**. In Platonic and Hellenistic thought, the invisible realm is MORE real than the visible — the Forms, the intelligible world. Saying the visible world was made "not from visible things" is entirely consistent with creation from invisible pre-existent matter.
- The verb "framed" (κατηρτίσθαι) implies ordering and arranging, fitting with the material-substrate view.
- The verse was among those retroactively reinterpreted by Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus after the doctrine of creation ex nihilo was formulated (see [[Retroactive Reinterpretation of Creation Texts]]).

Markus Bockmuehl articulates the scholarly consensus: "Frequently cited Septuagintal and New Testament passages which assert God's creation of what is seen from what is not seen... should not be short-circuited into statements about creatio ex nihilo. Exegetes today are in widespread agreement on this point."[^1]
