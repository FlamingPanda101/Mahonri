# Romans 7:19

> For the good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do. *(KJV)*

## Key phrase
"**The good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do.**" The core trope of Romans 7 (paralleled in 7:15) — a person who knows the good yet does the opposite.

## Apologetic / historical application
[[Brian Thomassen]] argues this is a deliberate literary allusion that any educated Greco-Roman would catch — "like *to be or not to be*" — sourced ultimately to **Euripides' *Medea*** (430 BCE), the stock example of *akrasia* (weakness of the will), the opposite of *enkrateia* (self-mastery). Thomassen documents the same line echoed by Ovid ("I see the better and approve it, but I follow the worse"), Epictetus, and Seneca's own *Medea* — and notes [[Paul]] directly quotes Seneca's *taliporos... anthropos* ("O wretched man that I am") at [[Romans 7.24|Romans 7:24]]. Medea, the barbarian sorceress who murders her children, is the dangerous non-Greek "other"; Paul flips the trope on his Roman readers — *you* are the slaves to passion.
