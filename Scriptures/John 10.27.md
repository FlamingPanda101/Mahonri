# John 10:27

> My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. *(KJV)*

## Use in the canon debate
[[Aaron Shafovaloff]] uses this as a pillar of the Protestant **recognition** model of the canon: "We do not need an infallible council of sheep to tell us that we've heard the voice of the Shepherd." Christ's sheep recognize His voice in Scripture directly, so the church *receives and recognizes* the word rather than *conferring* authority on it (see [[The Church Recognizes Scripture It Does Not Make It]] and [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]]).

## In context
[[Jacob Hansen]]'s rebuttal is historical: it was a particular *authoritative council*, not the diffuse "sheep," that fixed which books count as the voice of the Shepherd, and reception of the disputed books was genuinely contested. So the "sheep hear my voice" appeal, while it captures real spiritual experience, does not by itself answer the institutional question of *who* drew the canon's boundaries (see [[Canon Formation Was a Power Struggle Over Authority]]). The same self-authentication appeal is critiqued in [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]].
