# Exodus 21:20-21

> And if a man smite his servant, or his maid, with a rod, and he die under his hand; he shall be surely punished. Notwithstanding, if he continue a day or two, he shall not be punished: for he is his money. *(KJV)*

## Key Hebrew terms
- **נָקַם (naqam)** — the verb in v. 20 for the master being "punished/avenged" appears here in the passive: "he shall be **avenged**." The text does **not** specify the death penalty; scholars regard the punishment as deliberately unspecified because it would differ depending on whether the victim was a native Israelite debt slave or a foreign chattel slave.
- **כֶּסֶף (keseph)** — "silver / money," rendered "his money" in v. 21. The enslaved person is the master's **property**, which is the stated reason no punishment applies when death is delayed.

## Significance
[[Dan McClellan]] reads v. 21 as the crux: if the beaten enslaved person survives "a day or two" and then dies, there is **no punishment at all**, because the death is treated as the accidental outcome of a permissible disciplinary beating, and the master has already lost his property. This means the Bible permits beating an enslaved person to within an inch of their life so long as immediate death does not result. Joshua HS could not recall the verse, claimed the punishment was the death penalty, and inadvertently conceded the verse undermines his "slavery was compassionate" thesis.
