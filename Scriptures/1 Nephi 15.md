# 1 Nephi 15

Returning from his vision, Nephi finds his brothers Laman and Lemuel disputing because they cannot understand the words Lehi spoke concerning the olive-tree allegory (the scattering and gathering of the house of Israel). Nephi rebukes their hardness of heart and prayerlessness, then teaches them: the natural branches of the olive tree are the house of Israel, and the gathering in the last days corresponds to the fulness of the Gospel coming to the Gentiles and then to the remnant of Israel. When they ask the meaning of the tree their father saw in his dream, Nephi explains that the tree and its fruit represent the love of God, and that the rod of iron is the word of God which leads to the tree. Most fully, he interprets the river of filthy water that ran beside the tree: it represents the depths of hell and the awful gulf separating the wicked from the righteous and from the tree of life, so filthy that Lehi's mind had been so caught up in the fruit that he had not noticed its filthiness. Nephi closes by teaching the final state of the righteous and the wicked.

## Why it's cited
This chapter supplies Nephi's own interpretation that the river beside the tree is a "river of filthy water" representing hell, while the tree is the love of God — the textual basis for the argument that the river is simultaneously "love and filth," and for reconstructing how the dream's symbols map onto temple and covenant theology. It is cited alongside 1 Nephi 11 to support reading Lehi's tree as Asherah and to show the olive-tree allegory's role in the Book of Mormon's gathering-of-Israel framework.
