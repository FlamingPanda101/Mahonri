# 1 Peter 2:18

> Servants, be subject to your masters with all fear; not only to the good and gentle, but also to the froward. *(KJV)*

## Key Greek term
- **σκολιός (skolios)** — "crooked / perverse / unjust," rendered "froward" (KJV). The text instructs household slaves (οἰκέται, *oiketai*) to submit not only to kind masters but also to "the cruel and unjust ones."

## Significance
[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] cites this (alongside Paul on masters and slaves) to argue the New Testament does **not** teach abolition — at best it regulates and softens slavery while telling slaves to obey even cruel masters. He stresses Peter "or allegedly Peter" wrote it, flagging the disputed authorship of 1 Peter. This is part of his case that the Bible "at the very best" condemns transatlantic *brutality*, not slavery as an institution. [[Glenn Scrivener]] counters with the "yeast working through dough" model and that manumission was legally constrained in the early empire. See [[The New Testament Does Not Teach Abolition]].
