# 1 Corinthians 7:3-5

> Let the husband render unto the wife due benevolence: and likewise also the wife unto the husband. The wife hath not power of her own body, but the husband: and likewise also the husband hath not power of his own body, but the wife. Defraud ye not one the other, except it be with consent for a time, that ye may give yourselves to fasting and prayer; and come together again, that Satan tempt you not for your incontinency. *(KJV)*

## Key Greek term
- **εἰ μήτι ἂν ἐκ συμφώνου (ei mēti an ek symphōnou)** — "except by mutual agreement/consent." The exception clause governs the *abstaining* (defrauding/depriving), not the having of sex. This is the grammatical hinge of the whole debate.

## Significance
[[Glenn Scrivener]] cites this in his book as Christianity "thrusting consent into the bedroom" — radical because Paul grants the *wife* equal claim over the *husband's* body, inverting the one-directional Greco-Roman norm. [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] calls this "sleight of hand": since the mutual consent applies only to *abstaining*, a spouse cannot unilaterally refuse sex — "it's not consent to have sex, it's consent to not have sex" — a structure he argues describes (and historically licensed) marital rape. Scrivener replies the reading is "unnecessarily suspicious" and that the consent applies to both poles, the chapter being a pro-sex "marriage and family program." See [[1 Corinthians 7 Consent Is to Abstain Not to Have Sex]] and [[Paul Pioneered Mutual Bodily Ownership]].
