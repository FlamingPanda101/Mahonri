# Genesis 5:3 — Adam fathers Seth "in his own likeness, after his image"

> And Adam lived an hundred and thirty years, and begat a son in his own likeness, after his image; and called his name Seth. *(KJV)*

## Key Detail

The verse reuses the **exact terms** of [[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]] — *demut* ("likeness") and *tselem* ("image"), here in the phrase *bidmuto ketsalmo* — but now of a human father begetting a human son. The parallel is the crux of [[Stephen Smoot]]'s argument that the *imago Dei* is a **physical, generational resemblance**: just as Seth bears Adam's image the way a child bears a parent's bodily form, so humanity bears God's image — implying "a continuity of nature and form" between God and humanity, not merely a moral or functional likeness. The same image-and-likeness language that links Adam to Seth in Genesis 5:3 links God to humanity in Genesis 1:26-27, suggesting the relationship is read by the priestly author as one of kind and form. (W. Randall Garr and J. Kathleen McFarlane are cited to the effect that the image is *inherited* father-to-son.)

This grounds the Latter-day Saint reading of literal divine parentage and an embodied God ([[D&C 130.20-21|D&C 130:22]]). It is also the verse a defender of the traditional reading must reckon with, since the obvious sense is bodily resemblance.
