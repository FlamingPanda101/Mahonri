# Genesis 6:1–4

> And it came to pass, when men began to multiply on the face of the earth, and daughters were born unto them, that the sons of God saw the daughters of men that they were fair; and they took them wives of all which they chose. And the LORD said, My spirit shall not always strive with man, for that he also is flesh: yet his days shall be an hundred and twenty years. There were giants in the earth in those days; and also after that, when the sons of God came in unto the daughters of men, and they bare children to them, the same became mighty men which were of old, men of renown. (KJV)

## Key Hebrew Terms

- **בְנֵי־הָאֱלֹהִים (benei ha-Elohim):** "sons of God" — literally "sons of the gods" or "sons of God." In the Hebrew Bible's divine-council worldview, these are divine beings / lesser gods in YHWH's heavenly court (cf. Job 1:6, 2:1; Psalm 82:1, 6; Deuteronomy 32:8 in the DSS/LXX reading). They are NOT angels in the later sense — the category "angel" (מַלְאָךְ, malakh) develops later. Dan McClellan notes: "They're Gods, children of Gods... they get renegotiated — the pantheon gets reduced, gets renegotiated, and they become angels." [^1]
- **נְּפִלִים (Nephilim):** "giants" (KJV) — from the root נפל (naphal, "to fall"). The term likely means "the fallen ones." These are the offspring of the divine beings and human women. They are described as "mighty men which were of old, men of renown" (הַגִּבֹּרִים אֲשֶׁר מֵעוֹלָם אַנְשֵׁי הַשֵּׁם).

## The Two Main Interpretations Throughout History

1. **Divine beings / fallen angels** (earliest interpretation): The "sons of God" are heavenly beings who descended and took human wives. This is the interpretation found in 1 Enoch, the Septuagint (ἄγγελοι τοῦ θεοῦ, "angels of God"), Jubilees, Philo, Josephus, and the earliest church fathers. The offspring are the Nephilim/giants.
2. **Sethite interpretation** (later apologetic): The "sons of God" are the godly descendants of Seth who intermarried with the ungodly descendants of Cain ("daughters of men"). This view originated with Julius Africanus (3rd century CE) and was popularized by Augustine to counter the Enochic tradition. It is still common in some conservative Protestant circles.

The academic consensus strongly favors interpretation 1 as the original meaning within its ancient Near Eastern context. The Sethite view is a later rationalization driven by discomfort with the supernatural elements.

## Relationship to 1 Enoch

This four-verse snippet is the seed from which the [[Book of Enoch - The Watchers and Genesis 6 Expansion|entire Book of Watchers (1 Enoch 1–36)]] grows. 1 Enoch takes this brief, cryptic account and expands it into a detailed narrative spanning 108 chapters, naming the leaders of the fallen angels, describing their covenant on Mount Hermon, cataloguing the forbidden knowledge they taught humanity, and narrating their judgment and imprisonment.

The term "Watchers" (עִירִין, 'irin; Greek: ἐγρήγοροι, egrēgoroi) derives from the Aramaic word for "wakeful ones" — heavenly beings who do not sleep — and becomes the Enochic designation for these fallen angels.
