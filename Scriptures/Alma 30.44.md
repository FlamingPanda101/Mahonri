# Alma 30:44

> But Alma said unto him: Thou hast had signs enough; wilt thou tempt thy God?
> Wilt thou say, Show unto me a sign, when thou hast the testimony of all these
> thy brethren, and also all the holy prophets? The scriptures are laid before
> thee, yea, and all things denote there is a God; yea, even the earth, and all
> things that are upon the face of it, yea, and its motion, yea, and also all
> the planets which move in their regular form do witness that there is a
> Supreme Creator.

## Context

Alma 30 records the confrontation between [[Alma the Younger]] and Korihor, an
anti-Christ who preaches that there is no God, no Christ, no atonement, and
that human religious beliefs are the product of "a frenzied mind" and
traditions of the fathers (Alma 30:16). Korihor demands a sign from Alma to
prove God's existence. Alma responds that Korihor has already had "signs
enough" — the testimony of brethren, the holy prophets, the scriptures, and
the natural world itself.

## Key Exegetical Points

1. **Teleological Argument:** Alma's appeal is a classic design argument — the
   order and regularity of the cosmos (earth, its motion, planets in their
   "regular form") witness to a "Supreme Creator." This predates the
   teleological arguments of [[Thomas Aquinas]] (13th century) and [[William
   Paley]] (19th century) by well over a millennium.

2. **"Motion" and "Regular Form":** [[David Butler]] and [[Hayden Carroll]]
   note that references to the earth's "motion" and planets moving in "regular
   form" suggest knowledge of orbital mechanics — the earth is not stationary
   but in motion, and planetary orbits follow regular, mathematically
   describable paths. This aligns with the modern fine-tuning argument's
   emphasis on the precise values of physical constants that permit stable
   orbits and life.[^1]

3. **"All things denote there is a God":** This is taken as a scriptural
   endorsement of the principle that design in nature is evidence of a
   designer — the foundational premise of the teleological argument.

## Cross-Tradition Readings

- **LDS:** Read as a literal historical event and a prophetic articulation of
  the design argument. Also seen as evidence that ancient Nephite prophets had
  knowledge consistent with modern cosmology, suggesting genuine ancient
  origins of the Book of Mormon.
- **Non-LDS Christian:** Typically not read, as the Book of Mormon is not
  accepted as scripture. However, the *form* of the argument (design in nature
  pointing to God) is universally employed in Christian apologetics (cf. Romans
  1:20).
- **Atheist/Critical:** Would view this as Joseph Smith projecting 19th-century
  natural theology into a fictional ancient text, not as actual pre-Columbian
  cosmology.
