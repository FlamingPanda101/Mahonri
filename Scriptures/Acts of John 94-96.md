# Acts of John 94-96

*A 2nd-century apocryphal text (the "Round Dance" or "Hymn of Jesus"), not in any biblical canon. First scholarly English translation by [[Montague Rhodes James]] in* The Apocryphal New Testament *(Oxford, 1924).*

## Summary of the passage
Before his arrest, Jesus gathers his apostles, forms them into a **circle** with himself at the center, and leads them in a **responsive ritual** — they answer "Amen" after each line — instructs them to **imitate his gestures**, and explicitly tells them to **maintain silence about the mystery**. A representative line:

> "I would be saved, and I would save. Amen. / I would be loosed, and I would loose. Amen. / ... A lamp am I to thee that beholdest me. Amen. / A door am I to thee that knockest at me. Amen. / ... Now answer thou unto my dancing." *(Acts of John 95-96, trans. M. R. James, 1924)*

## Why it's cited
[[Alex Arnold]] presents this as the clearest evidence of the **second** ancient suppression of the temple mystery: a text depicting Jesus personally instituting a circle ritual with commanded silence — which the early church councils condemned and ordered burned. The chronology is load-bearing: the endowment was introduced in **1842**, but the *Acts of John* was not translated into English until **1924** (82 years later), so the parallel content was inaccessible to Joseph Smith. Arnold notes the same circle-ritual tradition recurs independently in the Gospel of Bartholomew, the Questions of Bartholomew, and the **Qasr el-Wizz** codex recovered during the 1960s UNESCO salvage excavations in Nubia — multiple independent witnesses, none in Freemasonry. He stresses that the *structural* parallels (not the sacred specifics) are what scholars of the text and endowed members have independently identified.
