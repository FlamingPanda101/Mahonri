# D&C 128 — Epistle on Baptism for the Dead

> Now, what do we hear in the gospel which we have received? A voice of gladness! ... A voice of gladness for the living and the dead; glad tidings of great joy. ... Let us, therefore, as a church and a people, and as Latter-day Saints, offer unto the Lord an offering in righteousness; and let us present in his holy temple, when it is finished, a book containing the records of our dead, which shall be worthy of all acceptation. *(D&C 128:19, 24)*

## Summary

Doctrine and Covenants 128 is a letter Joseph Smith wrote from hiding on **6 September 1842** (a companion to D&C 127), giving further instruction on **baptism for the dead**. It dwells on the proper keeping of records (the "recorder" and witnesses) so that what is "bound on earth" is "bound in heaven" ([[Matthew 16.18-19|Matthew 16:19]]), expounds [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]] ("else what shall they do which are baptized for the dead"), invokes Malachi's promise of Elijah turning the hearts of the fathers and children, and closes with the exultant "Glad tidings from Cumorah!" passage ([[D&C 128.20|D&C 128:20]]).

## Why it's cited

LDS apologists treat D&C 128 (with [[D&C 138]], Joseph F. Smith's vision of the redemption of the dead) as the **revelatory** basis of the doctrine of baptism for the dead — which is why the practice does not rest on a single ambiguous New Testament verse. When critics argue the doctrine is "constructed around" [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]] alone, the reply is that Latter-day Saints are not *sola scriptura*: continuing revelation (D&C 128/138) detailed and confirmed the rite, and 1 Corinthians 15:29 simply *also* attests something revelation independently established. See [[The One Ambiguous Verse Objection to Baptism for the Dead Fails]].
