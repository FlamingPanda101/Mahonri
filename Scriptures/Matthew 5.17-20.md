# Matthew 5:17-20 (KJV)

> "Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil. For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled. Whosoever therefore shall break one of these least commandments, and shall teach men so, he shall be called the least in the kingdom of heaven: but whosoever shall do and teach them, the same shall be called great in the kingdom of heaven. For I say unto you, That except your righteousness shall exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees, ye shall in no case enter into the kingdom of heaven."

**Key Greek term:** *plēroō* (πληρόω), "to fulfil." Per BDAG, the relevant senses are "to bring to completion that which was already begun" and "to bring to a designed end" — of a prophecy, obligation, promise, or **law**. On the Data Over Dogma reading, this denotes the law reaching its appointed completion at the end of the age, **not** its abolition or obsolescence. See [[Pleroo Means to Complete a Law Not to Abolish It]].

**"Jot or tittle"** renders the Greek *iōta* and *keraia* — the smallest letter and the small decorative stroke on a letter. The phrase is a Semitic idiom for "the least conceivable part."

## Significance
The central proof-text of this episode. [[Dan McClellan]] argues that verse 18 contains **two synonymous chronological markers** — "till heaven and earth pass" and "till all be fulfilled/accomplished" — both pointing to a single future apocalyptic endpoint, so the Mosaic law remains binding until then (see [[Dual Chronological Markers Keep the Law in Force Until the Eschaton]]). Verse 19's threat against anyone who "breaks one of these least commandments" undercuts the popular apologetic that Jesus' fulfillment *cancelled* the law's obligation. On McClellan's reconstruction, the Matthean Jesus is *intensifying* Torah-observance against a Pauline, Gentile-friendly, law-free trajectory (see [[Matthew Intensifies the Law as a Polemic Against the Pauline Mission]]). Contrast Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" parallel ([[Luke 4.16-22|Luke]] context), where the saying is reframed as a statement about *difficulty* rather than *time* ("it is easier for heaven and earth to pass away than for one tittle of the law to fail").
