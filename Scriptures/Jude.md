# Jude

The Epistle of Jude is a short, single-chapter New Testament letter urging believers to "earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints" against false teachers who had crept in unawares. It warns of judgment by recalling examples from Israel's history and from extra-canonical tradition, including the archangel Michael's dispute with the devil over the body of Moses. Most notably, Jude directly quotes the Book of Enoch as prophecy, attributing the words to "Enoch, the seventh from Adam." This citation makes the letter a focal point in canon debates, since a New Testament author treats a book later excluded from the canon as authoritative Scripture.

## Why it's cited

Jude is repeatedly invoked in the canon controversy because it quotes 1 Enoch as genuine prophecy and alludes to the Assumption of Moses, showing that an inspired author drew on writings the later church did not canonize. This is used to argue that "Scripture" was a broader and more fluid category than a fixed, closed Bible, undercutting claims of a self-evident or self-authenticating canon. The letter thus serves as a concrete counterexample in disputes over canon criteria, self-reference, and the anachronism of a closed canon.
