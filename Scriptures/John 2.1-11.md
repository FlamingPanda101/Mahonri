# John 2:1–11

> And the third day there was a marriage in Cana of Galilee… And there were set there six waterpots of stone, after the manner of the purifying of the Jews, containing two or three firkins apiece. Jesus saith unto them, Fill the waterpots with water. And they filled them up to the brim… the ruler of the feast… called the bridegroom, And saith unto him… thou hast kept the good wine until now. This beginning of miracles did Jesus in Cana of Galilee. *(KJV, abridged)*

## The Account
At a wedding feast in Cana, the wine runs out. [[Mary (mother of Jesus)]] tells the servants, "Whatsoever he saith unto you, do it." [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]] has them fill six stone water-pots — vessels "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews" — and the drawn-out water has become wine, judged by the governor of the feast to be the best wine of the night. John calls this "the beginning of miracles," Jesus' first sign.

## The Purification-Jar Detail
John's specification that the pots were for *the purifying of the Jews* is the hinge of the LDS devotional reading on the Stick of Joseph channel. Because Jewish ritual hand-washing reused water (the dirtied washing-water drained back into the basin), the claim is that the pots already held the *used, unclean* water of dozens of guests — so Christ's miracle transformed the **accumulated impurities of everyone present** into the finest wine. See [[The Cana Purification Jars Held the Guests' Sins]].

## The Transformation Typology
The same reading takes the miracle as a type of personal sanctification: Christ "does not need a vineyard to make wine," only dirty water brought to him, and can turn repented sin into nourishment — "faith-destroying sin into faith-promoting experience." The wedding feast itself is read as the kingdom of God. See [[Christ Makes Wine From Dirty Water Not From a Vineyard]].
