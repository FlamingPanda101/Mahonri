# 2 Maccabees 7:28

> "I beseech thee, my son, look upon the heaven and the earth, and all that is therein, and consider that God made them of things that were not; and so was mankind made likewise." (KJV Apocrypha)

**Greek (LXX):** ἀξιῶ σε, τέκνον, ἀναβλέψαντα εἰς τὸν οὐρανὸν καὶ τὴν γῆν καὶ τὰ ἐν αὐτοῖς πάντα ἰδόντα γνῶναι ὅτι οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων ἐποίησεν αὐτὰ ὁ θεός, καὶ τὸ τῶν ἀνθρώπων γένος οὕτω γίνεται.

**Key phrase:** οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων (*ouk ex ontōn*) — "not out of things that exist"

**Translation note:** The KJV "of things that were not" is a dynamic rendering of οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων which more precisely means "not out of existing things." The distinction is significant for the creation ex nihilo debate: "not out of existing things" means God did not use already-formed things as material, but does not necessarily mean God used absolutely nothing (see below).

## Context

This verse is part of a mother's speech to her seventh and youngest son during the Maccabean martyrdoms under Antiochus IV Epiphanes (c. 167 BCE). She urges him to accept martyrdom rather than violate the Torah, grounding her appeal in God's creative power — if God formed the cosmos and formed you in my womb, God can raise you from death. The composition of 2 Maccabees is typically dated to the late 2nd or early 1st century BCE.

The broader passage (vv. 22–29, NRSV):
> "I do not know how you came into being in my womb. It was not I who gave you life and breath, nor I who set in order the elements within each of you. Therefore the Creator of the world, who shaped the beginning of humankind and devised the origin of all things, will in his mercy give life and breath back to you again, since you now forget yourselves for the sake of his laws... Look at the heaven and the earth, and see everything that is in them, and recognize that God did not make them out of things that existed. And in the same way the human race came into being."

## Interpretations

### Traditional Christian Reading
Origen (3rd c. CE) and subsequent tradition read this as the first scriptural affirmation of creation ex nihilo — God creating the cosmos from absolute nothingness. This became the dominant Christian reading.

### Academic Consensus (Modern Scholarship)
The scholarly consensus (May, Goldstein, Winston, Bockmuehl, Anderson, Hubler) holds that the verse does NOT teach creation ex nihilo. The phrase οὐκ ἐξ ὄντων reflects standard Hellenistic philosophical idiom for creation from unformed, pre-existent matter — not absolute nothingness. Key arguments:

- **The womb analogy:** The mother's speech parallels cosmic creation with embryonic formation in her womb — a process that clearly uses pre-existing biological material. God forms a baby from "things that are not a baby" (maternal substance), just as God formed the world from "things that were not a world" (unformed matter).
- **Greek philosophical context:** Aristotle and other thinkers used "from non-being to being" language to describe transformation of pre-existent matter, not generation from nothing. The phrase *ouk ex ontōn* fits this idiom.
- **Wisdom of Solomon 11:17** (same milieu) explicitly says God created "out of formless matter" (ἐξ ἀμόρφου ὕλης).
