# Matthew 12:24-26 — "If Satan cast out Satan"

> But when the Pharisees heard it, they said, This fellow doth not cast out devils, but by Beelzebub the prince of the devils. And Jesus knew their thoughts, and said unto them, Every kingdom divided against itself is brought to desolation; and every city or house divided against itself shall not stand: And if Satan cast out Satan, he is divided against himself; how shall then his kingdom stand? *(KJV)*

## Key Detail
This is the locus classicus for the **"explain it away as demonic"** maneuver: confronted with an undeniable supernatural work, the Pharisees do not deny it happened — they reattribute its *source* to the devil ("by Beelzebub the prince of the devils"). Jesus' reply is not a falsification proof but a coherence test: a demonic explanation must make sense of the work's *effect*. Casting out demons advances God's kingdom against Satan's, so attributing it to Satan makes Satan self-defeating ("a house divided"). The text thus cuts both ways in the LDS–Catholic exchange. [[Hayden Carroll]] uses it to show that the demonic charge is the same move first-century Jews made against Jesus — rhetorically available, presupposition-driven, and (he argues) unfalsifiable. [[Trent Horn]] uses the *same* passage to argue the move is legitimate *when the evidence warrants it*: Jesus himself reasons about whether a demonic attribution coheres, which means demonic explanations are evaluable rather than off-limits.
