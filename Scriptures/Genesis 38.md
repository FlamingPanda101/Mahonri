# Genesis 38 — Judah and Tamar

A self-contained narrative "inserted" into the middle of the Joseph cycle (Genesis 37–50): Judah marries **Bath-shua** ("the daughter of Shua"); his sons Er and Onan are "evil in the eyes of Yahweh" and die; his widowed daughter-in-law **Tamar**, denied her levirate due, disguises herself, and Judah unknowingly fathers twins by her at a **sheep-shearing** journey.

> And it came to pass at that time, that Judah went down from his brethren, and turned in to a certain Adullamite, whose name was Hirah. *(Genesis 38:1, KJV)*

## Significance
[[Trevan Hatch]] treats this chapter as a deliberate literary **mirror of the David–Bathsheba scandal**, and a key plank for reading [[Genesis Was Finalized as Davidic-Solomonic Apologetic|Genesis as Davidic apologetic]]: Judah, like David, goes to **Adullam**; his wife **Bath-shua** echoes David's **Bath-sheba** (whom Chronicles likewise calls Bath-shua); both households have a **Tamar** at the center of a sexual scandal; both feature the "I am pregnant" formula, mourning-then-conception, "evil in the eyes of Yahweh," and a sheep-shearing setting. The chapter's abrupt placement marks it as an editorial insertion. See [[Judah and David Are Written as Literary Doubles]] and [[The Younger-Brother Motif Legitimizes Solomons Succession]].
