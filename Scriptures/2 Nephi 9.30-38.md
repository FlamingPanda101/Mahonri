# 2 Nephi 9:30-38

Jacob's sermon (often read as a [[The Day of Atonement|Day of Atonement]] or Feast-of-Tabernacles temple speech) closes with a sequence of **"woes"** that an earlier FARMS article mistook for a Nephite "new Ten Commandments." [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] counters: there are not ten but **nine** woes, and they are not commandments at all — the commandments read "Thou shalt not kill," whereas these read "woe unto X, *for they shall* Y," the exact **inverted structure of a beatitude** ("Blessed are X, *for they shall* Y").

> 36 Wo unto them who commit whoredoms, for they shall be thrust down to hell.
> ...
> 38 And, in fine, wo unto all those who die in their sins; for ... they shall return to God, and behold his face, and remain in their sins.

Nine woes match the **nine beatitude-statements** of [[Matthew 5.17-20|Matthew 5]]; their **seven repeated punishments** ("perish" / "thrust down to hell") match the **seven** distinct blessings. Because Jacob lived ~550 years before the Sermon, the parallel argues the Beatitude pattern is older than Jesus — pre-existing temple covenant language Jacob weaponizes against covenant-breakers.
