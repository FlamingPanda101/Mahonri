# Matthew 17:10-13

> And his disciples asked him, saying, Why then say the scribes that Elias must
> first come? And Jesus answered and said unto them, Elias truly shall come first,
> and restore all things. But I say unto you, That Elias is come already, and they
> knew him not, but have done unto him whatsoever they listed... Then the disciples
> understood that he spake unto them of John the Baptist.

## Context

Spoken after the Transfiguration. The disciples raise the scribal expectation
(from [[Malachi 4.5-6|Malachi 4:5]]) that Elijah must come before the Messiah.
Jesus' reply holds two tenses together: Elias *shall come* (future) and restore
all things, yet Elias *is come already* — whom the disciples understand to be
John the Baptist.

## Key Exegetical Points

- **Two comings, two forerunners.** The future clause ("shall come... and restore
  all things") is not collapsed into the past clause ("is come already"). The
  Latter-day Saint reading treats *Elias* as a forerunner role (cf. Luke 1:17,
  "in the spirit and power of Elias") that John filled before the First Coming,
  while another Elias precedes the Second. See
  [[The Coming of Elias Before the Second Coming Was an Early Christian Doctrine]].
- **"Restore all things" (ἀποκαταστήσει πάντα).** Language of *restoration* /
  restitution (cf. Acts 3:21), read as pointing beyond John to a latter-day
  restoring work — fulfilled for Latter-day Saints in [[D&C 110]].
- **Against the "already fulfilled" closure.** Mainstream readings take v. 12-13 as
  identifying John as *the* promised Elijah, ending the expectation; the future
  tense of v. 11 is the pressure point against that closure.

## Cross-Tradition Readings

- **LDS:** Harmonized so John is one Elias and a future Elias remains — supporting
  Elijah's literal latter-day return.
- **Catholic/mainstream:** John the Baptist is the promised Elijah; the prophecy is
  fulfilled, with no further literal forerunner expected.
