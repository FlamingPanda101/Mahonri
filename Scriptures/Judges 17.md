# Judges 17 — Micah's idol, ephod, and homemade priesthood

Micah of Ephraim makes a cast image with silver consecrated by his mother, sets up a shrine with an ephod and **teraphim**, and installs first his own son and then a wandering Levite as his private priest — folk religion practiced without authorized oversight. In Judges 18 the migrating tribe of Dan steals the idol, ephod, teraphim, and the priest himself on their way to conquer Laish.

> And the man Micah had an house of gods, and made an ephod, and teraphim, and consecrated one of his sons, who became his priest. *(Judg 17:5, KJV)*

## Key Detail
[[Dan McClellan]] highlights this as the clearest case that **teraphim are never condemned** by God or the narrator — Micah's improvised cult (cast idol, ephod for divination, teraphim, do-it-yourself priesthood) is portrayed as the unremarkable "folk religion of the judges period," and the Danites treat the apparatus as a prize worth stealing ("you take my gods that I made, and the priest"). McClellan grants Judges is "not exactly examples of how the Mosaic law is a success," but stresses the text offers no editorial gloss explaining the teraphim away.
