# Genesis 2:4-7

> "These are the generations of the heavens and of the earth when they were created, in the day that the LORD God made the earth and the heavens, And every plant of the field before it was in the earth, and every herb of the field before it grew: for the LORD God had not caused it to rain upon the earth, and there was not a man to till the ground. But there went up a mist from the earth, and watered the whole face of the ground. And the LORD God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and man became a living soul." (KJV)

**Hebrew (v.5, key terms):** שִׂיחַ הַשָּׂדֶה (*siach ha-sadeh*, "plant/shrub of the field," wild vegetation) and עֵשֶׂב הַשָּׂדֶה (*eseb ha-sadeh*, "herb/grass of the field," typically cultivated vegetation). **v.7:** וַיִּיצֶר (*wayyitser*, "formed," as a potter shapes clay) ... אָדָם (*adam*, "the human") from עָפָר מִן־הָאֲדָמָה (*aphar min ha-adamah*, "dust from the ground").

## Context

This passage opens the second creation account. Dan McClellan stresses that the second sentence of 2:4 begins a *temporal clause* whose main clause does not arrive until v.7: vv.4b–6 merely set the stage — describing an earth devoid of both wild vegetation (no rain) and cultivated vegetation (no human to till) — and only in v.7 is the human formed. Crucially, the human is created *before* any plant life exists.[^1]

McClellan reads the verse division as **2:4a** (closing the Priestly account) and **2:4b** (opening the older non-Priestly account), a division that "the overwhelming scholarly consensus" supports.[^2]
