# Genesis 26 — Isaac, Rebekah, and Abimelech (the third wife-sister episode)

A new famine sends [[Isaac]] to Gerar, where he calls Rebekah his sister for fear of being killed. Abimelech sees Isaac "sporting with" (fondling) Rebekah through a window, rebukes him, and decrees that no one harm the couple; Isaac then grows rich by reaping a hundredfold.

> And the men of the place asked him of his wife; and he said, She is my sister: for he feared to say, She is my wife. *(Gen 26:7, KJV)*

## Key Detail
[[Dan McClellan]] reads this as the **latest** telling of the wife-sister story-type (cf. [[Genesis 12]], [[Genesis 20]]), with the narrator "working very hard to convince you that this is not the same story" (a *different* famine; God forbids going to Egypt). It adds the anachronistic **Philistine** setting (the Philistines arrive only with the Sea Peoples, c. 12th century BCE, long after the patriarchs) and drops the abduction: Abimelech never takes Rebekah, so there is no dream, no plague, no closed wombs. The Hebrew verb *m'tsacheq* (from the root behind "Isaac") carries the sense "to fondle/dally," hence "sporting." Isaac's wealth here comes from a hundredfold harvest rather than a king's gifts. See [[The Wife-Sister Story Is a Tripled Doublet]].
