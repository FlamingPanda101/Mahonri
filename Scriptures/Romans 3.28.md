# Romans 3:28

> Therefore we conclude that a man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law. *(KJV)*

## Key term — the absent "alone" (*allein*)
The Greek reads *logizometha gar dikaiousthai pistei anthrōpon chōris ergōn nomou* — "we reckon that a person is justified by faith **apart from works of the law**." There is **no word for "alone"** in the Greek. In his 1522 German New Testament, [[Martin Luther]] rendered the verse "allein durch den Glauben" — "by faith **alone**." When Catholic scholars objected, Luther defended the insertion in his *Open Letter on Translating* (1530), arguing German idiom required it for clarity and, notoriously, that "*sic volo, sic iubeo*" — Dr. Martin Luther will have it so. The phrase *erga nomou* ("works of the law") is the crux: in context Paul is excluding the **boundary-marking works of the Mosaic law** (circumcision, food laws, calendar — the immediate dispute over Gentile inclusion), not good works as such — the same point [[Ephesians 2.8-9|Ephesians 2:8-9]] makes before adding v.10 ("created... unto good works"). The one verse in scripture that uses the actual phrase "faith only," [[James 2.24|James 2:24]], *denies* it.

## How different traditions read this
- **Protestant (*sola fide*):** the verse teaches justification by faith alone; Luther's *allein* makes explicit what Paul means. Works are the fruit, never the ground, of justification.
- **Catholic:** Paul excludes works *of the Mosaic law* and pre-justification merit, not the faith-formed-by-charity that increases justification; the unqualified "alone" is an addition (Trent). See [[James 2-24 - Justified by Works Not Faith Alone]], [[Initial vs Ongoing Justification]].
- **LDS (Moroni's Standard):** *allein* is a literal addition to the text, and reading Paul whole (Romans 2:6-7; Ephesians 2:10) shows he rejects earning righteousness by the law, not covenant faithfulness. See [[Luther Inserted Allein Faith Alone Into Romans 3-28]] and [[Paul Read Whole Rejects the Law of Moses Not Good Works]].
