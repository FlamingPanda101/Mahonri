# Jacob 5

The **Allegory of the Olive Tree** (also called Zenos's allegory), the longest single chapter in the Book of Mormon. The prophet Jacob quotes the otherwise-lost prophet **Zenos**, who likens the house of Israel to a tame olive tree that the **Lord of the vineyard** tends, prunes, grafts, and labors over across the whole sweep of salvation history — scattering and gathering, nourishing the natural and wild branches, and weeping over the loss of fruit before the final burning and renewal.

## Significance for Givens's God
[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] (Keystone) names "Jacob 5, the lord of the vineyard" as one of the texts that shaped his own discipleship, alongside [[Moses 7]]'s weeping God and [[D&C 76]]'s "expansive vision of human potential."[^1] The chapter's portrait of God is precisely the laboring, grieving, relationally invested vinedresser Givens contrasts with the impassible sovereign of [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God|Greek-influenced theology]] — a God who toils and is moved by his creation, not one untouched by it. See [[The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric]].

## Used in (Great Forsaken)
- [[The Great Forsaken - A Pre-Christian Apostasy of the Scribes]] · [[The Book of Mormon Rescued Us From Two Apostasies]] — Jonah Barnes treats the Zenos olive-tree allegory as a suppressed pre-exilic tradition (the Gentiles grafted into the covenant) that the Deuteronomistic scribes excised and the brass-plates record preserved.
