# 1 Nephi 1

The opening chapter of the Book of Mormon. Nephi introduces himself and his record (written in "the language of the Egyptians" with "the learning of the Jews"). In the first year of King Zedekiah, many prophets warn Jerusalem to repent; Lehi prays and is overcome by the Spirit. He sees a pillar of fire and then a vision of **God seated upon his throne**, surrounded by angels, with One descending in brightness followed by twelve others. This heavenly messenger gives Lehi a **book** to read, which fills him with the Spirit and reveals the coming destruction of Jerusalem and the redemption of the world. When Lehi preaches these things, the Jews mock and seek to kill him, but the Lord protects him.

## Why it's cited

1 Nephi 1 is a cornerstone of the "First Temple theology" apologetic: Lehi's throne-theophany, the descending divine figure with twelve followers, the heavenly book, and the prophecy of a Messiah are presented as authentic pre-exilic Israelite motifs — precisely the older temple religion that the Deuteronomistic reformers of Josiah's era were eliminating. Defenders argue this makes the chapter evidence *for*, not against, an ancient origin, since a 19th-century author would not have known to embed reformed-versus-pre-reform tensions.
