# Isaiah 9:6 (KJV)

> For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace.

(Numbered Isaiah 9:5 in the Hebrew/Masoretic versification.)

## Translation Note

The Hebrew of the four traditionally-named titles runs across two lines: *pele yo'etz* (wonder / wonderful counselor or planner), *el gibbor* ("mighty God"), then *avi'ad sar shalom* (everlasting / eternal father, prince of peace).[^1] In the Christian tradition these are read as four throne-names of the messianic child.

The **Septuagint** does not treat the whole string as a name. It renders the first portion as *megalēs boulēs angelos* — "messenger of great counsel" — folding *el gibbor* into "angel/messenger" rather than "mighty God," and then translates the final four words (*avi'ad sar shalom*) as a full **clause**, adding expansive material: "for I will bring peace to the rulers, peace and health to him."[^2] So the Greek is not truncated or ignoring the Hebrew; it is *expansive*, and it evades a human being called "God" the same way the LXX elsewhere turns *elohim* into "angels."[^3] See [[The Septuagint of Isaiah 9-6 Is Expansive Not Truncated]] and [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]].

## Manuscript Note

A page of **Codex Sinaiticus** (4th c.) preserving this verse copies the main Septuagint text ("messenger of great counsel") but carries a **marginal note** supplying a fairly literal rendering of the Hebrew ("Wonderful counselor, strong God, the mighty one, ruler of peace... father of the coming age").[^4] Apologists cite this note as 4th-century Christian interest in the Hebrew; but the note is a later correction (correctors CA/CB/D), dated by scholarly consensus to the **fifth-to-seventh centuries**, i.e. *after* Jerome's pivot to the Hebrew — see [[The Codex Sinaiticus Marginal Note Postdates Jerome]].[^5]
