# Isaiah 5.1

> Now will I sing to my wellbeloved a song of my beloved touching his vineyard. My wellbeloved hath a vineyard in a very fruitful hill:

## Why it's cited

This opens the Song of the Vineyard, an extended parable in which Israel is the vineyard that God tends but which yields only wild grapes, inviting judgment. It is cited as a key example of prophetic allegory and as part of the Isaiah 3–6 complex that apologetic notes read as a unified depiction of covenant failure and the removal of corrupt worship. The vineyard motif also supplies background for later New Testament vineyard parables, making it a bridge text between the Testaments.
