# Genesis 19:24

> Then the LORD rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire from the LORD out of heaven. *(KJV)*

## The "Two Yahwehs" Reading
Trinitarian apologists (here [[Wes Huff]]) read the doubled divine name — "Yahweh… rained fire… from Yahweh out of heaven" — as implying **two distinct Yahwehs**, a Yahweh on earth and a Yahweh in heaven, taken as a hint of plurality within the Godhead and connected to the preceding Genesis 18 episode where Yahweh dialogues with Abraham at the oaks of Mamre while other figures go down to Sodom.

## The Critical-Scholarship Reading
[[Dan McClellan]] rejects the "two Yahwehs" parse. The Hebrew does **not** say "Yahweh on earth rained fire from Yahweh in heaven"; it says "Then Yahweh caused to rain upon Sodom and Gomorrah fire and brimstone, from Yahweh, from the heavens." The redundancy is awkward, but "the overwhelming majority of scholars… agree that the point here is to make clear that Adonai is no longer on earth. Adonai is in the heavens now." The verse is managing a concern for **divine presence**: where Yahweh is when judgment falls.[^1]

McClellan suggests this is likely a **Deuteronomistic redaction**, since Deuteronomy "is very concerned for only hearing a voice on earth, not seeing a form, because Adonai is up in the heavens." (An older alternative — that two versions of the story were spliced — he treats as somewhat outdated.) Either way, "there aren't really critical scholars who would say, 'Oh yeah, this is evidence for the Trinity in the Hebrew Bible.' That is presuppositional… not supported by the data."[^2]

This belongs to the same pattern as the Shekinah glory on the ark: a tangible divine presence on earth while God "is ruling and reigning in heaven" — explained by the intuitive [[Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images|logic of divine images]], not by two persons of one substance.[^3]
