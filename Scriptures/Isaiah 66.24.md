# Isaiah 66:24

> And they shall go forth, and look upon the carcases of the men that have transgressed against me: for their worm shall not die, neither shall their fire be quenched; and they shall be an abhorring unto all flesh. *(KJV)*

> And they shall go out and look at the dead bodies of the people who have rebelled against me; for their worm shall not die, their fire shall not be quenched, and they shall be an abhorrence to all flesh. *(NRSV, as read in the source)*

## Significance

The **final verse of the book of Isaiah** and a key seedbed for later hell imagery. [[Dan McClellan]] notes the line need **not** mean the dead *experience* the worm and fire for eternity — it can simply mean the worm and fire the onlookers *see* never stop; "it doesn't necessarily mean they're going to be feeling that." It is "hyperbolic imagery" of corpses endlessly consumed and burned. This undying-worm / unquenchable-fire motif is picked up in later Second Temple literature (the Apocrypha's Book of Judith alludes to it) and in the New Testament's Gehenna sayings (e.g. Mark 9:48), feeding the developing concept of divine afterlife punishment.
