# Assumption of Moses

> A Jewish pseudepigraphon (also called the *Testament of Moses*) cast as Moses's farewell testament to Joshua before Israel enters Canaan — surveying Israel's future history down to the end. Only an incomplete Latin manuscript survives; its ending (which apparently narrated Moses's "assumption," i.e. his being taken up) is **torn off and lost**. The surviving copy was discovered by Antonio Ceriani in the Ambrosian Library and published in 1861.

## Summary

The work is best known from antiquity for the tradition that **Moses was taken up (translated)** rather than simply dying and being buried — and the early Christian letter of [[Jude]] (v. 9) alludes to a dispute between Michael and the devil "about the body of Moses," widely thought to derive from this text. Its preserved chapters review Israel's apostasy, scattering, and eschatological deliverance.

## Why it's cited

[[Jonah Barnes]] makes the Assumption of Moses the keystone of a Latter-day Saint argument that the **fifth "book of Moses" on the [[brass plates]]** ([[1 Nephi 5.11|1 Nephi 5:11]]) was this dying testament — *not* Deuteronomy, which (he argues) the [[Josiah]]/Deuteronomist reform substituted later. The text's "Moses was translated" tradition matches the Book of Mormon's assumption in [[Alma 45.18-19|Alma 45:18-19]] ("the Lord took Moses unto himself") against the death-and-burial notice of [[Deuteronomy 34.5-6|Deuteronomy 34:5-6]]; and Barnes proposes the missing ending once held the "prophet like me" prophecy ([[1 Nephi 22.20-21|1 Nephi 22:20-21]]).
