# 1 Nephi 1.8-19

> And being thus overcome with the Spirit, he was carried away in a vision, even that he saw the heavens open, and he thought he saw God sitting upon his throne, surrounded with numberless concourses of angels in the attitude of singing and praising their God. And it came to pass that he saw One descending out of the midst of heaven, and he beheld that his luster was above that of the sun at noon-day. And he also saw twelve others following him, and their brightness did exceed that of the stars in the firmament. And they came down and went forth upon the face of the earth; and the first came and stood before my father, and gave unto him a book, and bade him that he should read. And it came to pass that as he read, he was filled with the Spirit of the Lord. And he read, saying: Wo, wo, unto Jerusalem, for I have seen thine abominations! ... And after this manner was the language of my father in the praising of his God; for his soul did rejoice, and his whole heart was filled, because of the things which he had seen. *(1 Nephi 1:8–15, Book of Mormon)*

This is Lehi's inaugural throne-theophany. Overcome by the Spirit, he sees the heavens open and God enthroned amid numberless concourses of angels; One whose brightness exceeds the noonday sun descends, followed by twelve others. The foremost gives Lehi a book and commands him to read; reading it fills him with the Spirit, and the book pronounces woes upon Jerusalem and foretells its destruction and the captivity of its inhabitants. Lehi rejoices and praises God, marveling at his power, goodness, and mercy, declaring that God will be merciful even to those who come unto him.

## Why it's cited
Apologetics notes cite this opening vision as a Book of Mormon prophetic-call narrative that reproduces the ancient Near Eastern divine-council and prophetic-commission pattern — God enthroned among the heavenly host, a commissioning figure, and a heavenly book — paralleling Isaiah 6, 1 Kings 22, and Ezekiel. The "One descending followed by twelve" is read as an early christophany. The argument is that this council theology, published in 1830, reflects pre-exilic Israelite belief rather than later monotheistic editing, supporting the claim that the text preserves a northern/First-Temple religious milieu.
