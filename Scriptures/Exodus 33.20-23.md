# Exodus 33:20–23

> And he said, Thou canst not see my face: for there shall no man see me, and live. And the LORD said, Behold, there is a place by me, and thou shalt stand upon a rock: And it shall come to pass, while my glory passeth by, that I will put thee in a cleft of the rock, and will cover thee with my hand while I pass by: And I will take away mine hand, and thou shalt see my back parts: but my face shall not be seen. *(KJV)*

## Key Hebrew Terms

- **כָּבוֹד (kavod):** "glory" — but Dan McClellan notes the term can literally mean "body" or "heaviness/substance" in its physical sense. Moses asks to see God's *kavod*, which McClellan argues means God's actual body. The KJV translates it "glory" but the underlying concept is physical and corporeal.
- **פָּנִים (panim):** "face" — the seat of God's visible radiance. In the Hebrew Bible, God's face is specifically what cannot be seen because it radiates lethal divine glory.
- **אָחוֹר (achor):** "back parts" — God allows Moses to see His back, but not His face. This presupposes God HAS a back — i.e., a physical, corporeal form with spatial orientation.

## Context

This passage sits within the broader Sinai narrative. After the golden calf incident, Moses seeks reassurance of God's presence. He asks to see God's *kavod* (glory/body). God agrees to pass by but covers Moses' face with His hand, removing it only after passing so Moses sees God's "back parts" but not His face. The stated reason: "no man shall see me, and live."

## Mesopotamian Parallel: Melammu

McClellan connects this to the Mesopotamian concept of **melammu** — a divine radiance with which deities and divine media are clothed. In Mesopotamia, melammu signals deity and is feared because deities can harm humans. In the Hebrew Bible, the divine radiance operates more like looking directly at the sun — it is so overpowering that it kills. Yet the Hebrew Bible also contains *exceptions*: figures who see God's face and survive (Jacob in [[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]], Moses speaking "face to face" in Exodus 33:11). See [[Melammu - Divine Radiance in Ancient Near East]].

## The Internal Hebrew Bible Tension

McClellan argues this passage expresses a *rule* about divine visibility that the Hebrew Bible itself violates in multiple places — Jacob (Gen 32:30), Moses (Ex 33:11), and others. This internal tension is resolved by later editors who inserted *mal'ak* ("angel/messenger") into theophany accounts to distance the encounter from direct divine contact. The contradiction is not just between OT and NT — it exists *within* the Hebrew Bible itself, showing an evolving conception of God's visibility and danger to humans.
