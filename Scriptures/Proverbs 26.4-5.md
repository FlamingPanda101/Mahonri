# Proverbs 26:4-5

> **4** Answer not a fool according to his folly, lest thou also be like unto him.
> **5** Answer a fool according to his folly, lest he be wise in his own conceit. *(KJV)*

## The paradox
The two verses appear to issue contradictory commands — *do not* answer / *do* answer. The classic resolution (b. Shabbat 30b) is **situational**: each applies in different circumstances, and the juxtaposition teaches discernment about *when* to engage folly. The Hebrew **כְּסִיל (kesil)** — "fool" — denotes not low intelligence but moral/spiritual obstinacy, one who rejects wisdom.

## Apologetic application
[[Hayden Carroll]] reads the couplet as a two-step method: **v.4** = do *not grant* a critic's false presuppositions (don't argue on the fool's flawed terms); **v.5** = *do answer* by exposing the incoherence of those presuppositions and showing where they lead, so the critic is not "wise in his own conceit." See [[Proverbs 26 Two-Verse Apologetic Method]]. (Carroll repeatedly mis-cites the reference as "Proverbs 24:4-5" but quotes 26:4-5.)
