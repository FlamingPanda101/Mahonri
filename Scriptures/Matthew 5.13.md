# Matthew 5:13 (KJV)

> "Ye are the salt of the earth: but if the salt have lost his savour, wherewith shall it be salted? it is thenceforth good for nothing, but to be cast out, and to be trodden under foot of men."

## Significance

Standardly read as a discipleship metaphor (be a preserving, flavoring influence; don't go flat). [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]], reading the [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Sermon on the Mount as a temple ordinance]], instead takes the verse as a **covenant penalty** clause: positioned right after the entry requirements (the [[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance|Beatitudes]]), it warns the initiate that abusing or violating the covenants carries a death-sanction figured as being "cast out and trodden under foot of men."

**The load-bearing detail** is that this exact fate is *narrated* twice in the Book of Mormon, which (on Butler's argument) shows the phrase functioning as a known covenant penalty rather than a stray metaphor:
- **Korihor** ([[Alma 30.27|Alma 30]]) loses his confrontation with Alma, is "cast out, and trodden under foot . . . even until he was dead" — read as the death of an *apostate covenant man*, not a secular skeptic. See [[The Salt-Savor Covenant Penalty Enacted by Korihor and the Title of Liberty]].
- The **Title of Liberty** ([[Alma 46.21-22|Alma 46]]): Moroni's people cast their rent garments under their feet and swear that if they break the covenant of liberty they too will be "cast . . . at the feet of our enemies to be trodden under foot" — a deliberate enactment of the same penalty.
