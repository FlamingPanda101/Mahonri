# D&C 101

> **101:77** — "According to the laws and constitution of the people, which I have suffered to be established, and should be maintained for the rights and protection of all flesh, according to just and holy principles; **101:78** That every man may act in doctrine and principle pertaining to futurity, according to the moral agency which I have given unto him, that every man may be accountable for his own sins in the day of judgment."

> **101:80** — "And for this purpose have I established the Constitution of this land, by the hands of wise men whom I raised up unto this very purpose, and redeemed the land by the shedding of blood."

## Why It Matters Here

This is the central canonical text behind the "the Constitution was inspired" idea — and the point of [[Brian Thomassen]]'s argument is precisely that the verb is **"established"** (and "suffered to be established"), *not* "inspired." Webster's 1828 glosses *established* as "to affirm, confirm, or appoint," and "wise men" connotes the *proper application* of preserved principles (utility), not divine dictation.

- Verse 80 supplies the "**by the hands of wise men whom I raised up**" formulation that grounds [[The Constitution Is Inspired in the Sense of Useful Not Divinely Embodied]] and [[The Constitution Is Not Said to Be Inspired in the Canon - Only Established]]. Note "Constitution" *is* capitalized here, unlike the lowercase "constitutional" in [[D&C 98]].
- Verse 77–78 supplies the *teleology of the free space*: the laws exist so "**every man may act… according to the moral agency**" and be "accountable… in the day of judgment." This is the utility behind the document on Thomassen's reading — it creates the arena for free choice and just judgment, the same arena that makes America [[America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration|the seedbed of the Restoration]].
