# 2 Nephi 2.13

> And if ye shall say there is no law, ye shall also say there is no sin. If ye shall say there is no sin, ye shall also say there is no righteousness. And if there be no righteousness there be no happiness. And if there be no righteousness nor happiness there be no punishment nor misery. And if these things are not there is no God. And if there is no God we are not, neither the earth; for there could have been no creation of things, neither to act nor to be acted upon; wherefore, all things must have vanished away. *(2 Nephi 2:13, Book of Mormon)*

A single verse in Lehi's discourse to Jacob, structured as a cascading conditional: deny law and you must deny sin; deny sin and you must deny righteousness; without righteousness, no happiness; without these, no punishment or misery; and if none of these exist, there is no God — and without God, neither we nor the earth could exist at all.

## Why it's cited
This verse is cited as a compact argument that moral realities (law, sin, righteousness) are not free-floating but are bound together and ultimately grounded in God's existence — and conversely that a real moral order entails a real God and a purposeful creation. Apologetics notes use it in debates over the foundations of ethics: against "whatever God does is good" voluntarism, it is read to imply law and justice have genuine standing (an independent-justice argument), and it supports the broader Lehite claim that righteousness, agency, and the divine are mutually entailing.
