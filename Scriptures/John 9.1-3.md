# John 9:1-3

> And as Jesus passed by, he saw a man which was blind from his birth. And his
> disciples asked him, saying, Master, who did sin, this man, or his parents,
> that he was born blind? Jesus answered, Neither hath this man sinned, nor his
> parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him.

## Key Greek Terms

- **ἥμαρτεν (hēmarten)** — "sinned" (aorist active indicative of ἁμαρτάνω).
  The disciples' question uses the aorist tense, indicating a completed action:
  "Who *sinned* [at some point]?" — not "Who *is sinning*?" This is
  significant because the man was *born* blind, meaning the sin in question
  would have had to occur *before birth*.
- **τυφλὸν ἐκ γενετῆς (typhlon ek genetēs)** — "blind from birth." The man
  had never seen. The condition was congenital.

## Exegetical Significance for Pre-Existence

[[Hayden Carroll]] points out that the disciples' question only makes sense if
they presupposed some form of pre-mortal existence in which the man could have
sinned. The logic:

1. The man was born blind (congenital condition).
2. The disciples ask whether *he* sinned (not just his parents) that he was born
   blind.
3. How could he have sinned *before he was born* unless he existed before
   birth?[^1]

Alternative Christian interpretations:
- **The man sinned in the womb?** This is the only other way to make sense of
  the question without pre-existence, but Carroll dismisses it: "How can you
  sin in the womb?"[^2]
- **The disciples were asking a foolish question / operating under a mistaken
  Jewish belief.** Some commentators argue the disciples held a folk belief
  about fetal sin or the transmigration of souls that Jesus does not endorse.
  However, Carroll notes: "He didn't correct them. He didn't say, 'Why are you
  asking that? That's a dumb question. How could he?'"[^2] Jesus's silence on
  the premise is taken as tacit acknowledgment that the question was
  intelligible.

## Cross-Tradition Readings

- **LDS:** Read as implicit evidence for pre-mortal existence consistent with
  other scriptures (Jeremiah 1:5, D&C 138:56, Abraham 3:22-23). The question
  makes perfect sense if spirits existed before birth.
- **Traditional Christian:** Generally interpreted as reflecting contemporary
  Jewish speculation (possibly about sins in the womb, or a generic belief
  about generational punishment) that Jesus corrects — not as evidence for
  pre-existence. The disciples are asking a theologically confused question
  that Jesus gently redirects.
- **Jewish:** Some ancient Jewish texts (e.g., the Talmud) contain speculation
  about prenatal consciousness or sin, but the mainstream Jewish view does not
  teach pre-mortal existence of the soul in the LDS sense. The idea of a
  pre-existent soul choosing a body appears in some Hellenistic Jewish and
  Platonic thought.
