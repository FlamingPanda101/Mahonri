# Exodus 28

> And these are the garments which they shall make; a breastplate, and an ephod, and a robe, and a broidered coat, a mitre, and a girdle: and they shall make holy garments for Aaron thy brother, and his sons, that he may minister unto me in the priest's office. *(v.4, KJV)*

## Key term — *kethoneth* (the priestly tunic)
Exodus 28 prescribes the **holy garments** of the Aaronic priesthood — breastplate, ephod, robe, the broidered **coat** (Hebrew **כְּתֹנֶת, kethoneth/kothoneth**), mitre, and girdle — made "for glory and for beauty" (v.2) so the priest may minister and not "bear iniquity, and die" (v.43). The chapter also places the engraved plate "**HOLINESS TO THE LORD**" on the high priest's forehead (vv.36–38). The same word *kethoneth* is used in [[Genesis 3.21|Genesis 3:21]] for the garments God makes Adam and Eve after the fall — the lexical hinge of [[John Walton]]'s argument (relayed by [[Alex Arnold]]) that Eden is a garden-temple and Adam/Eve are priestly figures, making the garment a priestly investiture rather than mere clothing. See [[The Garment Shares the Hebrew Word of the Priestly Vestments]] and [[The First Temple Contained the Endowment Pattern Barker Reconstructed Independently]]. The white linen the high priest wears entering the Holy of Holies (cf. Lev 16) is, in [[Margaret Barker]]'s reading, the clothing of a heavenly being.
