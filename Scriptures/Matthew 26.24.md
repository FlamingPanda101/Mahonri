# Matthew 26:24

> The Son of man goeth as it is written of him: but woe unto that man by whom the Son of man is betrayed! it had been good for that man if he had not been born. *(KJV)*

## Key phrase
"It had been **good for that man if he had not been born**" — Greek **καλὸν ἦν αὐτῷ εἰ οὐκ ἐγεννήθη** — Jesus' lament over Judas Iscariot, the betrayer ("son of perdition," cf. John 17:12). The verse compares a fate (Judas's) unfavorably with *non-existence*.

## Apologetic application
[[Hayden Carroll]] cites this verse to argue that **even Jesus implies it is better not to exist than to suffer the worst end** — leveraging it against the doctrine of a God who creates persons *ex nihilo* foreknowing their damnation. If non-existence can be preferable to a person's fate, a perfectly good creator could have refrained from creating that person. See [[Even If the Trinity Is True Your God Is Evil]] and [[Problem of Hell and Creation]].
