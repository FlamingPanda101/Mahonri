# 2 Peter 3:15-16

> And account that the longsuffering of our Lord is salvation; even as our beloved brother Paul also according to the wisdom given unto him hath written unto you; As also in all his epistles, speaking in them of these things; in which are some things hard to be understood, which they that are unlearned and unstable wrest, as they do also the other scriptures, unto their own destruction. *(KJV)*

## Key Greek term
**δυσνόητά (dysnoēta)** — "hard to understand," "difficult to grasp" (v.16). The text explicitly admits that parts of Paul's writings are *hard to understand* and are *twisted* (στρεβλοῦσιν, "wrest/distort") by the unlearned. Notably, the verse also classes Paul's letters with "the other **scriptures** (γραφάς)" — an early internal witness to NT writings being regarded as Scripture.

## In context
Protestant apologists cite this verse two ways: (1) as proof that Paul's letters were recognized as Scripture early — which raises the counter-question, *what makes 2 Peter itself Scripture?*; and (2) Catholics like [[Joe Heschmeyer]] cite it to argue that since Scripture *itself* says Paul is "hard to understand," building one's whole rejection of the church's teaching on a private reading of Paul is unwise — and undercuts the "feel it in your bones" clarity claim.
