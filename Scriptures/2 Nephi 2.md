# 2 Nephi 2

This chapter is Lehi's deathbed discourse to his son Jacob, one of the Book of Mormon's central doctrinal sermons. Lehi teaches that redemption comes through the Holy Messiah, who offers himself a sacrifice for sin to answer the demands of the law and bring about the resurrection. He then lays out a connected theology of the Fall and moral agency: there must needs be an opposition in all things, for without opposing alternatives — good and evil, sweet and bitter, life and death — there could be no real choice and no moral or spiritual goods at all. Without law there is no sin; without sin no righteousness; without these no happiness or misery, and creation would have had no purpose. Adam and Eve's transgression brought a fallen world in which all humanity may act for themselves and not merely be acted upon, free to choose liberty and eternal life through the Messiah or captivity and death through the devil. Lehi declares "Adam fell that men might be; and men are, that they might have joy."

## Why it's cited
The whole chapter is the locus classicus for Lehi's theodicy and the Latter-day Saint doctrine of agency: moral goods are logically impossible without genuine opposition and law, so the existence of evil is framed as a necessary condition of a world where free creatures can attain righteousness and joy. Apologetics notes invoke it in problem-of-evil, free-will, and meta-ethics arguments — including critiques that conflate well-being with moral goodness — and to articulate a "felix culpa" reading of the Fall as a purposeful, joy-enabling step rather than a tragedy.
