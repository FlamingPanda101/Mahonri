# 1 Samuel 19 — Michal hides a teraphim in David's bed

To help David escape Saul's henchmen, his wife Michal lays a **teraphim** (household idol) in the bed, puts a net of goat's hair on its head, and covers it with clothes so the messengers think David lies sick in bed.

> And Michal took an image, and laid it in the bed, and put a pillow of goats' hair for his bolster, and covered it with a cloth. *(1 Sam 19:13, KJV)*

## Key Detail
[[Dan McClellan]] uses this as evidence that **teraphim were a normal household object even in pious homes** — the text says Michal took *the* idol (not "an" idol), and the figure is "close to life size," large enough to impersonate a sleeping adult man (a "Ferris Bueller's Day Off" decoy). He presses the question: why does David, "the ostensibly pious and righteous king of Israel... whose heart was after the Lord," simply have an idol about the house? Contrast the small, plural teraphim of [[Genesis 31.19|Genesis 31:19]]. See [[Teraphim Were Normative Divine Images Never Condemned]].
