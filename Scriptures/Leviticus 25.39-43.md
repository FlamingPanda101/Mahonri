# Leviticus 25:39-43

> And if thy brother that dwelleth by thee be waxen poor, and be sold unto thee; thou shalt not compel him to serve as a bondservant: but as an hired servant, and as a sojourner, he shall be with thee, and shall serve thee unto the year of jubilee... For they are my servants, which I brought forth out of the land of Egypt: they shall not be sold as bondmen. Thou shalt not rule over him with rigour; but shalt fear thy God. *(KJV)*

## Significance
This passage governs **Israelite** debt-dependents, immediately preceding the chattel-slavery permission of [[Leviticus 25.44-46|Leviticus 25:44-46]]. [[Dan McClellan]] uses the contrast to show the Hebrew Bible operates a **two-tier system**: a fellow Israelite who falls into poverty must be treated "as a hired servant" and not "with rigour," and is released at the Jubilee — whereas foreigners (vv. 44-46) are permanent heritable property who *may* be ruled "with rigour." He also notes the earlier verses (25:35-38) about supporting a poor brother "as a resident alien," with no interest and no food sold at profit, describe **poverty relief**, not debt slavery — and that Joshua HS conflated these and "hallucinated" a nonexistent Leviticus 24 command to treat enslaved brothers as sojourners.
