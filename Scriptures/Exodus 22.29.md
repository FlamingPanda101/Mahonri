# Exodus 22:29

> Thou shalt not delay to offer the first of thy ripe fruits, and of thy liquors: the firstborn of thy sons shalt thou give unto me. Likewise shalt thou do with thine oxen, and with thy sheep: seven days it shall be with his dam; on the eighth day thou shalt give it me. *(KJV)*

## Key Hebrew term
- **בְּכוֹר (bekhor)** — "firstborn." The command places the firstborn son in parallel with sacrificial firstling oxen and sheep ("on the eighth day thou shalt give it me"). The grammatical parallel is what fuels the reading that an original injunction to sacrifice the firstborn son survives in the text, later "renegotiated" by redemption/ransom laws elsewhere (e.g. Ex 13:13, 34:20, Num 18:15-16).

## Significance
[[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] raises this verse as a surviving trace of commanded human sacrifice in the biblical tradition, "then renegotiated" — citing it as the kind of "bracing" Old Testament text that makes Christianity's claim to ground Western anti-human-sacrifice values paradoxical. [[Glenn Scrivener]] responds that the firstborn was *ransomed* (paid for toward the temple), and objects that lifting a verse out as a "timeless principle that stands by itself" is alien to how Christians have ever read the Bible. Scrivener said at the end he would "go back and think very very much harder about Exodus 22:29." See [[The Narrative Reading of Scripture vs Proof-Texting]] and [[Surviving Traces of Human Sacrifice in the Hebrew Bible]].
