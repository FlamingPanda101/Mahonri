# Genesis 12 — Abram, Sarai, and Pharaoh (the first wife-sister episode)

Driven by famine to Egypt, [[Abraham|Abram]] fears he will be killed for his beautiful wife Sarai and instructs her to say she is his sister; Pharaoh takes her, is afflicted with plagues, discovers the deception, and expels Abram — who leaves wealthy in flocks, slaves, and donkeys.

> Say, I pray thee, thou art my sister: that it may be well with me for thy sake; and my soul shall live because of thee. *(Gen 12:13, KJV)*

## Key Detail
[[Dan McClellan]] reads this as the **earliest and tersest** of three tellings of one story-type (cf. [[Genesis 20]], [[Genesis 26]]) — it "moves in fits and starts," lacks the later explanatory detail (no warning dream, no half-sister excuse), and ends with the deception functioning as an **etiology of Abram's wealth**: "for her sake he dealt well with Abram." Notably, unlike Abimelech in Genesis 20, Pharaoh is given no statement that he refrained from touching her. McClellan also flags that "polygamy is normative in this time period," so the scandal is not plural marriage but two men claiming one wife. See [[The Wife-Sister Story Is a Tripled Doublet]], [[Polygyny Was Normative but Wife-Sharing Was the Transgression]].
