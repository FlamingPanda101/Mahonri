# John 1:18

> No man hath seen God at any time; the only begotten Son, which is in the bosom of the Father, he hath declared him. *(KJV)*

## The Textual Variant
This verse has a significant manuscript variant at the key phrase:

- **Reading 1:** *monogenes huios* — "only/unique Son" (KJV, majority of manuscripts)
- **Reading 2:** *monogenes theos* — "only/unique God" (found in some early manuscripts)

The difference in the earliest period of transmission would have been a single letter due to *nomina sacra* (sacred name abbreviations). The NRSV updated edition renders it "the only Son himself, God" — an attempt to split the difference that [[Dan McClellan]] calls "outright nonsensical."

## The Scholarly Dispute
[[Bruce Metzger]]'s *Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament* notes *lectio difficilior* (the more difficult reading) favors *theos* — but the editors added a note that this is unlikely because "this would require the author actually have written this, and that just doesn't make any sense."[^1]

*Monogenes theos* would be the only occurrence of this phrase anywhere, while John uses *monogenes huios* several times. Consistency favors "Son." But theological commitments drive the preference — Trinitarians gravitate toward *theos*, while non-Trinitarians favor *huios*.[^2]

## "No One Has Seen God" — The Contradiction with Hebrew Bible Theophanies

John 1:18's absolute statement — "No man hath seen God at any time" — contradicts multiple Hebrew Bible passages where figures claim to have seen God directly:

- **Jacob** declares "I have seen God face to face" ([[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]])
- **Moses** spoke with God "face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend" (Exodus 33:11)
- God physically passes by Moses, who sees God's "back parts" ([[Exodus 33.20-23|Exodus 33:20-23]])

Dan McClellan argues this is a genuine contradiction arising from the historical development of God's nature in biblical theology. The Hebrew Bible presents God as anthropomorphic, corporeal, and visible — a conception that changed under Greek philosophical influence, resulting in the New Testament's doctrine of an invisible, immaterial God. See [[Bible Contradiction - Gods Visibility Gen 32 vs John 1]].

The apologetic harmonizations — that OT figures saw a "limited essence" or the pre-incarnate Christ — are circular rationalizations that presuppose inerrancy rather than reading the texts on their own terms. See [[Limited Essence Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]], [[Pre-Incarnate Christ Theophany Rationalization Rebutted]].

Scholars who have demonstrated the Hebrew Bible's conception of a corporeal, visible God include [[Benjamin Sommer]] (*The Bodies of God*), [[Esther Hamori]] (*When Gods Were Men*), [[Andreas Wagner]] (*God's Body*), and [[Christoph Markschies]] (*God's Body*). [^3]
