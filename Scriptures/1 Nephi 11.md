# 1 Nephi 11

Desiring to see and understand the things his father Lehi saw, Nephi is caught up by the Spirit to an exceedingly high mountain and converses with the Spirit of the Lord. He is shown the tree of life that his father beheld, and when he asks its interpretation, he is shown a virgin in Nazareth, "exceedingly fair and white," who is carried away in the Spirit and returns bearing a child in her arms — the Son of God, the condescension of God. The angel teaches that the tree represents the love of God, which is the most desirable and joyous of all things. Nephi then sees the Lamb of God going forth among men: his baptism, his ministry with twelve others, his healings and casting out of devils, and ultimately his being lifted up and slain for the sins of the world. He also sees the multitudes of the world gathered to fight against the Lamb's apostles, and the great and spacious building (the pride and wisdom of the world) fall.

## Why it's cited
Apologetics notes cite Nephi's vision as the interpretive key linking the tree of life to "the love of God" and, by way of the virgin-and-child sequence, to a divine feminine / mother figure — the basis for reading Nephi's tree as the Israelite goddess Asherah, whose iconography was the sacred tree. The chapter is also used to argue the vision encodes ancient temple theology (ascent of a mountain, a guide, tiered visionary stages) and an explicitly christological "condescension of God." It anchors claims that First-Temple Israelite imagery (the tree, the Mother) survives in the Book of Mormon.
