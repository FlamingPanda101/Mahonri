# Jude 1:14–15

> And Enoch also, the seventh from Adam, prophesied of these, saying, Behold, the Lord cometh with ten thousands of his saints, to execute judgment upon all, and to convince all that are ungodly among them of all their ungodly deeds which they have ungodly committed, and of all their hard speeches which ungodly sinners have spoken against him. (KJV)

## Source of the Quotation

This passage is a **direct quotation of 1 Enoch 1:9**. The author of Jude explicitly identifies the source as "Enoch, the seventh from Adam" — the same genealogical position given to Enoch in [[Genesis 5.18-24|Genesis 5:18–24]] (Seth's line, not Cain's line in Genesis 4).

The phrase "the seventh from Adam" is a precise Enochic marker — it confirms the author of Jude is drawing from the Enochic literary tradition, not merely from oral tradition about a biblical figure named Enoch.

## Significance

- **Jude treats 1 Enoch as authoritative and historical**: The author introduces the quotation with "prophesied" (προεφήτευσεν), the same verb used for canonical prophetic utterance. This indicates the author of Jude considered 1 Enoch to carry prophetic authority.
- **The quotation is verbatim from the extant Ethiopic and Greek texts of 1 Enoch**, confirming the textual tradition's stability on this passage.
- Some early Christian writers (Tertullian, c. 160–220 CE) argued that since Jude quoted Enoch, the book should be considered canonical. Others (Augustine, Jerome) rejected this reasoning, noting that quotation does not equal canonization — Paul quotes pagan poets (Acts 17:28, Titus 1:12) without canonizing them.

## Key Greek Terms

- **προεφήτευσεν (proephēteusen):** "prophesied" — aorist active indicative. The standard verb for prophetic utterance in the NT. Its application to a non-canonical text is notable.
- **μυριάσιν (myriasin):** "ten thousands" — lit. "myriads," a term used in both Septuagintal and apocalyptic literature for the innumerable heavenly host.

## How Different Traditions Read This

- **Ethiopian Orthodox**: Sees this as confirming the canonicity of 1 Enoch, which remains in their broader canon.
- **Catholic/Orthodox/Protestant**: Generally holds that apostolic citation of a work does not confer canonicity; the epistle of Jude is inspired, but its source text need not be. The quotation is true *insofar as it is adopted by the inspired author*.
- **LDS**: Notes this as evidence that the biblical canon was fluid in the apostolic era and that extracanonical texts carried authority; consistent with an open-canon paradigm.
