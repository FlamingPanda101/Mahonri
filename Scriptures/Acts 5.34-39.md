# Acts 5:34-39 — Gamaliel's Counsel

> ...if this counsel or this work be of men, it will come to nought: But if it be of God, ye cannot overthrow it; lest haply ye be found even to fight against God. *(Acts 5:38-39, KJV)*

The Pharisee **Gamaliel** (teacher of [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]]) addresses the Sanhedrin about the apostles. He cites two prior would-be messianic movements — **Theudas** (who drew ~400 followers) and **Judas the Galilean** (who arose "in the days of the taxing") — both of whom were killed, after which "all... were scattered, and brought to nought." His principle: a movement of merely human origin disperses once its leader dies; one that is of God cannot be overthrown.

## In context

In the [[Capturing Christianity - Debate The Great Apostasy Joe Heschmeyer vs Jacob Hansen|Heschmeyer–Hansen debate]], [[Joe Heschmeyer]] turns Gamaliel's test into a positive argument against the Great Apostasy: the apostolic church did *not* scatter at the death of its leaders — there were no twelve rival churches — whereas the movements of Joseph Smith (1844 succession crisis) and Muhammad (the Sunni-Shia split) fractured immediately upon the founder's death. By Gamaliel's own standard, then, the early church bears the mark of being "of God."
