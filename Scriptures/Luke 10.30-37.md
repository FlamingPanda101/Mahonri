# Luke 10:30-37 — The Good Samaritan

> And Jesus answering said, A certain man went down from Jerusalem to Jericho, and fell among thieves ... And by chance there came down a certain priest that way: and when he saw him, he passed by on the other side ... But a certain Samaritan, as he journeyed, came where he was: and when he saw him, he had compassion on him ... Go, and do thou likewise. *(KJV, abridged)*

## Significance
[[Glenn Scrivener]] uses the parable to argue that even *noticing* compassion in outsiders (the Samaritan, or [[Hippocrates]]) is "a profoundly Christian thing": Jesus "inserts himself, thinly disguised as this beautiful stranger from outside the system," and the "people of the book" walk by — so the Christian frame *commends* compassion wherever it is found. He says he would "never want to be heard to say, don't look at the good Samaritan, he's a Samaritan." [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] turns this around: he only goes looking for compassion in other cultures *because* Scrivener claims it is uniquely Christian. See [[Noticing Compassion Elsewhere Is Itself Christian]].
