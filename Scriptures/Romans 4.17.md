# Romans 4:17

> "(As it is written, I have made thee a father of many nations,) before him whom he believed, even God, who quickeneth the dead, and calleth those things which be not as though they were." (KJV)

**Greek:** κατέναντι οὗ ἐπίστευσεν θεοῦ τοῦ ζῳοποιοῦντος τοὺς νεκροὺς καὶ καλοῦντος τὰ μὴ ὄντα ὡς ὄντα (*katenanti hou episteusen theou tou zōopoiountos tous nekrous kai kalountos ta mē onta hōs onta*)

**Key phrase:** καλοῦντος τὰ μὴ ὄντα ὡς ὄντα (*kalountos ta mē onta hōs onta*) — "calling the things that are not as (though they) are"

## Context

Paul is discussing Abraham's faith — specifically, Abraham's trust in God's promise that he would become "the father of many nations" despite his and Sarah's old age and barrenness. The "things that are not" refers to the promised offspring that did not yet exist — not to cosmic creation from nothing.

The immediate literary context is covenantal and promissory: God promises what is not yet, and Abraham trusts God to bring it about. Paul applies this to the resurrection (God "gives life to the dead") — the ultimate case of God bringing into being what was not.

## Significance for the Creation Ex Nihilo Debate

This verse is among the texts that Tatian, Theophilus, and Irenaeus retroactively reinterpreted as supporting creation ex nihilo (see [[Retroactive Reinterpretation of Creation Texts]]). The phrase "calling things that are not into being" sounds like creation from nothing when read through a later doctrinal lens.

However, in its original context:
- Paul is talking about Abraham's promised descendants, not cosmic origins
- The language reflects standard Jewish theological idiom for God's power to bring about what does not yet exist (promise, resurrection)
- The phrase uses Middle Platonic "non-being" language that, in its own context, referred to unrealized potential — not absolute nothingness
