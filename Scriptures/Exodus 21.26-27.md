# Exodus 21:26-27

> And if a man smite the eye of his servant, or the eye of his maid, that it perish; he shall let him go free for his eye's sake. And if he smite out his manservant's tooth, or his maidservant's tooth; he shall let him go free for his tooth's sake. *(KJV)*

## Significance
Joshua HS cited these "eye for an eye / tooth" provisions to argue the Hebrew Bible treated enslaved people compassionately, "nothing like the transatlantic slave trade." [[Dan McClellan]] responds that the verse assumes a regime in which beating slaves is normal — it only requires freeing a slave whose **eye or tooth** the master destroys, which (read alongside [[Exodus 21.20-21|Exodus 21:20-21]]) confirms that lesser beatings carry no penalty at all. He contrasts it with the antebellum [[Louisiana 1825 Code Protected Slaves More Than the Bible|Louisiana Civil Code of 1825]], which forbade beating a slave to death — a protection the Bible lacks.
