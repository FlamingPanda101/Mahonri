# John 20:28

> And Thomas answered and said unto him, My Lord and my God. *(KJV)*

## The Divine Image Interpretation
[[Dan McClellan]] argues this is an adaptation of the Shema and reflects **divine image theology**, not Trinitarian ontology. In the ancient Near East, a divine image (when properly consecrated with the divine name) could be addressed as the deity it represented — because it *manifested the presence* of that deity. A sentient divine image could even speak as the deity.[^1]

Thomas is recognizing Jesus as the authorized bearer of the divine name who manifests God's presence — paralleling Exodus 3, where "the angel of the Lord" speaks as God: "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, the God of Jacob."[^2]

## Distance from Trinitarian Confession
McClellan notes a key distinction: this is **not the narrator, not Jesus himself** making the identification — it's Thomas, a follower, recognizing Jesus as manifesting divine presence. Jesus does not say "I am God" anywhere in John. The Trinitarian concept is "a very different concept from what we have in the Gospel of John — even though it gets right up next to it, it does not have the philosophical frameworks available to it."[^3]

In a separate video McClellan reiterates this reading: when Thomas sees the resurrected Jesus he recognizes that Jesus is "manifesting God's presence and so exclaims my Lord and my God. That's the logic of divine images." [^4]
