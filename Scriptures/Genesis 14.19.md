# Genesis 14:19

> "And he blessed him, and said, Blessed be Abram of the most high God, possessor of heaven and earth." (KJV)

**Hebrew:** וַֽיְבָרְכֵהוּ וַיֹּאמַר בָּרוּךְ אַבְרָם לְאֵל עֶלְיוֹן קֹנֵה שָׁמַיִם וָאָֽרֶץ (*way-bārəkēhū way-yōʾmar bārūk ʾaḇrām lə-ʾēl ʿelyōn qōnēh šāmayim wā-ʾāreṣ*)

**Key term:** קֹנֵה (*qōneh*) — participle of *qanah* (קנה), rendered "possessor" (KJV) or "maker / creator" in other versions.

## Context

In the Melchizedek pericope, [[Melchizedek]], king of Salem and priest of *El Elyon*, blesses [[Abraham|Abram]] after the battle of the kings. (The "he blessed him" is grammatically ambiguous as to who blesses whom, though it is usually read as Melchizedek blessing Abram.) The divine title *El Elyon, qōneh šāmayim wā-ʾāreṣ* recurs in Abram's own oath two verses later (Genesis 14:22).

## Significance for the Creation Debate

[[Brian Thomassen]] uses the translation split on *qōneh* to surface the **emanation** model of creation inside the Hebrew Bible:

- The "**possessor**" rendering reflects *qanah* in its sense "to acquire / buy / own" — God owns heaven and earth because he *acquired* them.
- The "**maker**" rendering, Thomassen argues, reflects a sense meaning "**to beget**" — so the title says *El Elyon* **begat** heaven and earth, i.e. the world *emanated from* God as his child.

Either way the verse frames creation as *something-from-something* (acquiring or begetting), not creation from nothing — one of the two logically permissible Greek models (organization vs. emanation). See [[El Elyon Begat Heaven and Earth - Emanation in the Hebrew Bible]] and [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]].

Lexical note: many scholars read *qōneh* here in a cultic "creator / bringer-forth" sense, comparing the Ugaritic epithet of Asherah *qaniyatu ʾilima* ("creatress of the gods") — itself a production/birth word, which still excludes *ex nihilo*.
