# Doctrine and Covenants

The Doctrine and Covenants is one of the four standard works of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Unlike the Book of Mormon or the Bible, it is not a translation of an ancient record but a collection of modern revelations, chiefly those received by Joseph Smith, together with a smaller number issued through later presidents of the Church. Its sections address the doctrines, ordinances, priesthood organization, and governing principles of the Restoration, ranging from the nature of God and the plan of salvation to practical instructions for building up the early Church. First published in 1835 (expanding the earlier 1833 Book of Commandments), it has been added to over time as the canon of continuing revelation. For Latter-day Saints it stands as direct evidence that the heavens are open and that God still speaks to prophets.

## Why it's cited

Apologetics notes reference the Doctrine and Covenants as the doctrinal and organizational charter of the Restoration — the canonical source for distinctively LDS teachings (an embodied God, eternal marriage, the priesthood, the law of common consent) that distinguish the Church from creedal Christianity. Because it is openly a record of *modern* revelation, it also features in arguments about whether revelation continues after the apostolic age, and in comparisons (for example, to the structure and reception history of other scriptural canons) about how revealed texts are gathered and transmitted.
