# Job 9

> Which alone spreadeth out the heavens, and treadeth upon the waves of the sea. *(KJV, Job 9:8)*

## "Treads upon the waves"
In his reply to Bildad, Job recites a poetic litany of God's incomparable power as creator (Job 9:1–10). Verse 8 names, among God's unique acts, that he "alone... treadeth upon the waves of the sea." The Septuagint renders this as God walking on the sea "as on dry land" (*hōs ep' edaphous*). Walking on the water is thus marked in Hebrew scripture as a **uniquely divine** prerogative.

## Significance for Christology
[[Brant Pitre]] argues this is the Old-Testament backdrop for Jesus walking on the sea ([[Matthew 14.28-31|Matthew 14]] // Mark 6): combined with the divine name (*ego eimi*) and the theophanic "do not be afraid," Jesus is depicted doing what Job says only God does — an "epiphany miracle" claiming divine status. [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] presses a caution: Job 9:8 is spoken by *Job*, who elsewhere in his anguish says false things about God, so quoting him as an authority on divine prerogatives is contestable. Pitre answers that the verse belongs to a litany of God's creative attributes not under dispute, and that the act independently signified divinity in the Greco-Roman world (Caligula's bridge).
