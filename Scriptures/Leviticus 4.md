# Leviticus 4

> And the priest shall make an atonement for them, and it shall be forgiven them. *(KJV, Lev. 4:20; the refrain recurs at vv. 26, 31, 35)*

## The divine passive
Leviticus 4 sets out the **sin offering** (*ḥaṭṭāʾt*). After each case (the anointed priest, the whole congregation, a ruler, a common person), the ritual closes with the same refrain: the priest makes atonement and "it *shall be forgiven*." The verb is a **divine passive** — it is never "the priest forgives," always "it shall be forgiven [by God]." The priest *mediates* the sacrifice; God alone *forgives*.

## Significance for Christology
[[Brant Pitre]] (via Jacob Milgrom's Leviticus commentary) argues this divine passive shows that, in a Second Temple frame, *forgiving* sin — as opposed to offering sacrifice — was understood as an exclusively **divine** prerogative, because sin is above all an offense against the LORD. This is the background that makes the scribes' objection in [[Mark 2.19-20|Mark 2]] ("who can forgive sins but God alone?") a reasonable reading, and Jesus's direct declaration of forgiveness an implicit divine claim. See [[Forgiving Sins Was a Divine Prerogative in Second Temple Judaism]].
