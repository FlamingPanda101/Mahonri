# Galatians 2:11–14 — Paul Opposes Cephas to His Face

> "But when Peter was come to Antioch, I withstood him to the face, because he was to be blamed. For before that certain came from James, he did eat with the Gentiles: but when they were come, he withdrew and separated himself, fearing them which were of the circumcision... I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as do the Jews?" (KJV)

**Note:** modern translations read "Cephas" (Greek *Kēphas*, Aramaic for "rock/stone") where the KJV reads "Peter" — both name the same apostle (cf. [[Matthew 14.28-31|Matthew 16]]).

A first-century apostolic conflict preserved in the canon: Peter's table-fellowship varies by audience, and Paul publicly rebukes him. Day and Butler treat this as primary evidence that there were divergent, conflicting forms of Christianity from the start, and that "men from James" carried enough authority to make Peter change his behavior. See [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]].
