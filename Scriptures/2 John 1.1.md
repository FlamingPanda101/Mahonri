# 2 John 1.1

> The elder unto the elect lady and her children, whom I love in the truth; and not I only, but also all they that have known the truth. *(KJV)*

> *ho presbyteros eklektē kyria kai tois teknois autēs...* *(Greek, NA)*

## In context
The opening address of the second-shortest book in the New Testament (13 verses). The phrase *eklektē kyria*, traditionally "elect lady" and read since at least the 6th–7th century as a metaphor for a personified church. [[Lincoln H. Blumell]] argues the Greek as printed is grammatically broken: in a Roman-period letter opening, the recipient's name is followed by a definite article and the honorific *kyria* / *kyrios* ("lady"/"lord"), so the text must originally have read *eklektē tē kyria* — "to the lady Eklekte" — with the article *tē* lost by haplography against the *-ta/-tē* ending of the name. So read, *Eklekte* is a woman's proper name (the earliest commentator, [[Clement of Alexandria]], says exactly this), making this the only NT letter whose principal addressee is a named woman. See [[Eklekte Is a Named Woman Not the Elect Lady of 2 John]].

The same family of grammar is the basis for re-reading the whole letter: v. 10's warning not to receive false teachers "into the house" fits *Eklekte* as the proprietor of a [[Early Christian House Churches|house church]], not an abstract congregation.
