# Omni 1 — The book of Omni

> Behold, I, Amaron, write the things whatsoever I write, which are few ... And it came to pass that Mosiah ... was warned of the Lord that he should flee out of the land of Nephi ... and they were led ... unto the land which is called the land of Zarahemla ... And ... the people of Zarahemla ... had come out from Jerusalem at the time that Zedekiah, king of Judah, was carried away captive into Babylon. *(Omni 1, abridged — Book of Mormon)*

## Key Detail
The short book of Omni — written by a succession of minor record-keepers (Omni, Amaron, Chemish, Abinadom, Amaleki) — closes the Small Plates of Nephi and records a pivotal transition. [[Mosiah|Mosiah I]], warned by the Lord, leads the righteous Nephites out of the land of Nephi to the land of **Zarahemla**, where they discover the **people of Zarahemla** (the "Mulekites"): descendants of [[Mulek]], a son of King [[Zedekiah]], who had come out of Jerusalem at the Babylonian destruction and crossed to the New World ([[Omni 1.22|Omni 1:12-22]]). The two peoples merge under Mosiah. The book also reports Mosiah interpreting the engravings on a **large stone** brought to him, which gave an account of Coriantumr and the destruction of the Jaredites (Omni 1:20-22).

## Why it's cited
Latter-day Saint apologists use Omni 1 as the textual anchor for two figures whose historicity critics once dismissed:
- **[[Mulek]]**, a non-biblical surviving son of Zedekiah — an alleged blunder later supported by the reading *MalkiYahu ben-hammelek* ("son of the king") behind Jeremiah 38:6 and by a clay seal found in Jerusalem. See [[Mulek Son of Zedekiah Was Vindicated by Linguistics and a Seal]].
- **[[Mosiah|Mosiah I]]**, whose lost-116-pages backstory ([[Don Bradley]], via the [[Fayette Lapham]]/[[Joseph Smith Sr]] interview) has him led by the Liahona to the interpreters and covering his face with a skin to use them — a Tabernacle/veil pattern. See [[The Jaredite and Nephite Interpreters Carry the Endowment Ascent Pattern]].
The large-stone episode also makes Mosiah a *seer*-translator before his grandson translates the Jaredite record, fitting the book's interest in record-keeping and interpreters.
