# Ward Radio — Debunking Joseph Smith's Fake Affair with Fanny Alger (feat. Don Bradley)

A [[Ward Radio]] episode hosted by [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Kwaku El]], [[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]], and [[Brad Whitbeck]], featuring historian [[Don Bradley]]. The premise: separate fact from fiction about [[Fanny Alger]] — was it [[Joseph Smith]]'s earliest plural marriage or an adulterous affair? Bradley (who reached his conclusions while an ex-Mormon atheist) argues, with manuscript forensics and a reconstructed timeline, that it was **plural marriage, not adultery**, and that the popular "affair" narrative rests on a misquote and a misdated source. Josh Galey deliberately plays the skeptic throughout. Much of the historical detail is **new to the vault**; the surrounding polygamy material overlaps the existing polygamy cluster.

## Summary

- **The most-cited "proof of an affair" is a manuscript misquote.** Oliver Cowdery's 1838 letter is quoted as calling it "a dirty, nasty, filthy *affair*," but forensic imaging of the Huntington original shows the word is **"scrape"** (1830s slang for a mess), overwritten by a nephew-copyist; and "affair" did not even mean "extramarital sex" in the 1830s. See [[The Oliver Cowdery Affair Letter Actually Says Scrape Not Affair]]. [^1]
- **The relationship dates to July 1836 (Fanny age 18), not 1833 (age 15).** The only source for 1833 is Mosiah Hancock's hole-filled late autobiography; Levi Hancock's datable surrounding events and an Eliza Jane Webb record fix the discovery to ~July 22, 1836 — after Elijah restored the sealing keys (April 1836). See [[The Fanny Alger Relationship Dates to July 1836 Not 1833]] and [[The Mosiah Hancock 1833 Date for Fanny Alger Is Unreliable]]. [^2]
- **Polygamy was already an issue before 1836, and Joseph likely privately admitted plural marriage in 1838.** The 1835 Article on Marriage already answers a "polygamy" reproach; and the 1838 High Council minutes record Joseph giving an unrecorded "history of the girl business" that exonerated him of *adultery* while staying secret — best read as a confidential admission of plural marriage. See [[The 1835 Marriage Article Shows Polygamy Was Already in the Air]] and [[Joseph Smith Privately Admitted Polygamy to the High Council]]. [^3]
- **Every published historian — believer and skeptic — classifies it as polygamy.** Vogel (pious-fraud), Compton (liberal LDS), Hales, and Bradley all conclude plural marriage; the certainty that it was a mere affair runs inversely to how informed the source is. See [[Every Published Historian Concludes Fanny Alger Was Polygamy Not Adultery]]. [^4]
