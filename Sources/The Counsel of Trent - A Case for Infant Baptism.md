# The Counsel of Trent - A Case for Infant Baptism

[[Trent Horn]]'s response to [[Gavin Ortlund]], who had argued that the historical evidence favors **credobaptism** (baptism restricted to those who profess faith) while conceding the case is not conclusive. Trent grants the genuine silence on explicit infant baptism in the first ~150 years, then argues that (1) the *theological* case is primary — baptism regenerates, all need regeneration, so infants should be baptized; (2) the historical evidence, read against arguments from silence, actually favors **pedobaptism** as an apostolic discipline; and (3) credobaptists are inconsistent, since *child* baptism (which they do practice) has even less early evidence than infant baptism. He sent the script to Ortlund for review to ensure he represented the argument fairly.

## Summary

- **Doctrine vs. discipline.** Trent's framing device: *baptismal regeneration* is a **doctrine** (its antiquity is "overwhelming," conceded even by Baptist critics like William Webster and [[George Beasley-Murray]]), while *infant baptism* is a **discipline** that took time to be uniformly applied. See [[Baptismal Regeneration Is the Doctrine Infant Baptism Is the Discipline]] and [[The Theological Argument for Infant Baptism Precedes the Historical One]].
- **The argument from silence cuts both ways.** Because most apostles died before many Christians were born *into* Christian homes, early baptismal descriptions naturally address adult converts; the [[Didache]]'s fasting rules presuppose adult candidates without thereby excluding infants ("ought implies can"). [[Doug Wilson]] and [[Jordan Cooper]] both note the NT is equally silent on the *child* "walk-the-aisle" baptisms Baptists actually perform. See [[The Argument from Silence Cuts Both Ways on Infant Baptism]].
- **Credobaptists are inconsistent on the age of baptism.** Al Mohler, Keith Foskey, and Allie Beth Stuckey give wildly different minimum ages (from 3 to "well into the teens"); historically 18th–19th-century Baptists rarely baptized anyone under 12–13. [[David Wright]] found that the non-infant baptisms among the fathers were all of *adults* (youngest, John Chrysostom at ~18) — so the 4th-century fathers were not credobaptists either. See [[Credobaptists Cannot Agree on the Age of Baptism]].
- **The catacomb inscriptions are emergency deathbed cases, not the norm.** [[Everett Ferguson]] reads early-3rd-century tomb inscriptions recording baptism *shortly before death* as evidence infant baptism grew out of emergency baptism. Trent counters that epitaphs mention baptism only when it was *atypical* (a providential deathbed gift), exactly as modern Catholic graves rarely note a baptism date; one inscription calls a 2-year-old "a believer from believers," implying ordinary early baptism. See [[Catacomb Baptism Inscriptions Are Emergency Cases Not the Norm]].
- **Tertullian and the 4th-century delays don't prove novelty.** [[Tertullian]] (the only prominent pre-Reformation writer recommending *against* infant baptism) still affirmed baptismal regeneration and original guilt; his prudential delay, and 4th-century cases like [[Gregory of Nazianzus]], reflect a fear of post-baptismal sin, not a credobaptist theology. By the 3rd century [[Origen]], [[Cyprian of Carthage]], and [[Hippolytus of Rome]] attest infant baptism as the universal norm. See [[Tertullian's Opposition Presupposes an Established Practice]].
- **Augustine clarified, he did not invent.** Against Ortlund's claim that the picture only changes with Augustine's anti-Pelagian writings (≈410), Trent shows [[Pelagius]] *himself* affirmed infants must be baptized — proving the belief was already universal, not an Augustinian innovation. See [[Augustine Clarified Infant Baptism He Did Not Invent It]] and the existing [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]].
