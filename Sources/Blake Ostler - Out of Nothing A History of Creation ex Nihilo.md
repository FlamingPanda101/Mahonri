# Blake Ostler — Out of Nothing: A History of Creation ex Nihilo

A long (70-page) scholarly review-essay by [[Blake Ostler]] published in the *FARMS Review* 17/2 (2005): 253–320, responding to [[Paul Copan]] and [[William Lane Craig]]'s two defenses of *creatio ex nihilo*: their chapter "Craftsman or Creator? An Examination of the Mormon Doctrine of Creation and a Defense of Creatio ex nihilo" (in *The New Mormon Challenge*, 2002, abbreviated NMC) and their book *Creation out of Nothing: A Biblical, Philosophical, and Scientific Exploration* (2004, abbreviated CON).

Ostler's overarching thesis is that creation out of nothing is **not** biblical and **not** the belief of the earliest church, but a philosophical innovation first formulated around AD 180–200 in arguments with the Gnostics, Stoics, and Middle Platonists. He argues that Copan and Craig have adopted a "debater's brief" stance, reading their own conclusion into texts that, read in their own historical context, teach creation from a pre-existing chaos (*creatio ex materia*). This source is the authoritative scholarly anchor behind much of the vault's existing ex-nihilo cluster, and it supplies the primary-source patristic detail (Greek terminology, Theophilus, Hermogenes, Irenaeus) that the McClellan-based notes summarize. See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]].

## Summary

### Methodological critique: the "debater's brief" and question-begging
Ostler opens by charging that Copan and Craig are not attempting balanced exegesis but a lawyer's defense brief (p.4–5). Their case rests on two question-begging assumptions: (1) the word *create* is assumed to mean creation *ex nihilo*; and (2) even where a text says God created by organizing unorganized matter, it must still be read as teaching *ex nihilo* because it supposedly "implicitly" holds that God first created the matter from nothing. Ostler argues these very assumptions are what is at issue, so the argument begs the question (p.5). See [[Copan and Craig Use a Debaters Brief That Begs the Question]] and [[Two-Stage Creation Is an Ad Hoc Rescue Device]].

### The New Testament does not teach *ex nihilo*
Ostler works through each NT proof-text:
- **2 Peter 3:5–6** (p.6–9): teaches creation of heaven and earth out of pre-existing water/chaos; the verb συνεστῶσα means to organize by combining, not create from nothing. He rejects Copan and Craig's "two-step" reading.
- **Hebrews 11:3** (p.9–13): "things not seen" / "invisible things" are not nothing; they already exist (cf. Colossians 1:16, 2 Corinthians 4:18, 2 Enoch). The verb καταρτίζω means to frame or put in order. See [[Invisible Things Are Not Nothing in Biblical Creation Texts]].
- **Romans 4:17** (p.13–17): "calls into being the things that are not" uses the negative μή (relative non-being), the verb καλέω (to call/summon, presupposing something to call to), and is set in the context of the resurrection — none of which entails creation from absolute nothing. See [[Romans 4.17 Calls Into Being Does Not Mean Ex Nihilo]].
- **John 1:3** (p.17–19): states *who* (the Word) accomplished creation, not *how*; punctuation is undetermined; creation "through" the Word does not imply *ex nihilo*. See [[John 1-3 - The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation Not the Originator]].

### The crucial Greek distinction: relative non-being vs. absolute nothing
The spine of Ostler's argument (developed p.14–16 and throughout) is the distinction, in Greek philosophical usage, between **relative non-being** (μὴ ὄντος / *ek mē ontos*) — formless matter "so shadowy and vague that it cannot be said to have the status of being" — and **absolute nothing** (οὐκ ὄντος / *ex ouk ontos*). Drawing on Aristotle's *De Generatione Animalium*, Henry Chadwick, Edwin Hatch, and Jonathan Goldstein, he shows that the "non-being" language of the biblical and patristic texts is the language of relative non-being (a pre-existing substrate), not creation from nothing. See [[Relative Non-Being vs Absolute Nothing - The Greek Distinction Copan and Craig Miss]].

### Extrabiblical and patristic texts that teach *creatio ex materia*
Ostler surveys a long series of texts Copan and Craig cite, arguing each better supports *ex materia*: the Dead Sea Scrolls, Rabbi Gamaliel, 2 Enoch, the Shepherd of Hermas, Joseph and Aseneth, the Odes of Solomon, 2 Baruch, Aristides of Athens, 2 Maccabees 7:28, the Wisdom of Solomon, [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]], 1 Clement, [[Justin Martyr]], and [[Athenagoras of Athens]] (p.23–51). He notes that the first "scriptural" arguments for *ex nihilo* (Irenaeus c.185, Origen c.220) cited the Shepherd of Hermas and 2 Maccabees 7:28 — not canonical scripture — which betrays that no canonical text supported the doctrine (p.28–29). See [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]] and [[Philo Did Not Endorse Creation Ex Nihilo]].

### The invention of *ex nihilo*: Tatian, Theophilus, Hermogenes, Irenaeus, Tertullian
The constructive historical core (p.51–68). Following Hubler and May, Ostler argues the doctrine appeared suddenly in the late second century as a consequence of Christian apologists adopting a **Middle Platonic view of God** (transcendent, immutable, self-sufficient). [[Tatian]] (c.180) was the first to teach it expressly, to escape the Stoic eternal-recurrence (ἐκπύρωσις) and defend the resurrection. [[Theophilus of Antioch]] (c.180) was the second, arguing from three premises: God's mode of creation must be unique (not human-like), divine monarchy/omnipotence requires God be creator of all, and the eternal must be immutable (so matter, being mutable, cannot be eternal). See [[Theophilus Arguments for Creation Ex Nihilo]]. [[Hermogenes]] resisted, arguing from the Middle Platonic premise that matter is evil to the conclusion that matter must be eternal and uncreated (since a good God would not make evil) — see [[Hermogenes Argued for Eternal Matter Because Matter Is Evil]]. [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] gave the doctrine its most developed anti-Gnostic form, but Ostler argues it rendered his deification soteriology incoherent (created beings cannot be granted the "faculty of the Uncreated"). [[Tertullian]] sealed it with the argument from omnipotence, later taken up by Augustine and Aquinas.

### Conclusion
Ostler's three summary conclusions (p.68–70): (1) the NT does not teach *ex nihilo* — 2 Peter, Hebrews, and Romans teach creation from a pre-existing substrate; (2) the claim that Jewish texts of Jesus's era already taught it is false; (3) the doctrine appeared suddenly c. AD 180 as a philosophical consequence of adopting a Middle Platonic concept of God — which Latter-day Saints read as "the apostasy in action." He notes the downstream consequences: Chalcedonian two-nature Christology and a strong divine determinism. See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]] and [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]].
