# Thoughtful Faith - The Insane Logic of Original Sin

A recording of an **X Spaces** live call-in show hosted by [[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith), in which he debates two Calvinist callers — "Disciple Tulip" and a second, more theologically-read interlocutor — over **original sin, inherited guilt, total depravity, and unconditional election**. Hansen presses a single moral intuition throughout: a perfectly just judge holds persons responsible **only for their own acts**, so it is unjust to condemn an innocent (the recurring test cases are a newborn baby and [[Anne Frank]]) for what [[Adam]] did or for a "contract" they never consented to. The exchange is a popular-level restatement of the historic [[Pelagius]]–[[Augustine]] fault line, framed around the LDS Second Article of Faith ("men will be punished for their own sins, and not for Adam's transgression").

## Summary

**The corporate-headship / contract debate (Disciple Tulip).** The first caller defends inherited guilt through political and covenantal analogies: Adam is the "corporate head" of humanity the way a president represents a nation, and just as post-war Germany owed reparations even citizens who opposed Hitler, so all people inherit Adam's debt.[^1] He argues we are "in the contract" Adam made with God simply by being born and reaping its benefits (the knowledge of good and evil).[^2] Hansen's rebuttal is that **a contract requires consent** and a moral judge judges *individuals*, not groups they never chose to join; he gets the caller to concede the biblical principle (Ecclesiastes / Ezekiel) that "men will be punished for their own sins and not their fathers."[^3] See [[Corporate Headship Requires Consent - The Contract You Never Signed]] and [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]].

**The disease-vs-guilt distinction.** Hansen repeatedly concedes humans inherit a *disease* — mortality, a fallen body, a fallen world — but denies they inherit *guilt*: "you don't put people in prison and punish them because they inherited a disease."[^4] Total depravity claims the infant *deserves* hell, not merely that it suffers consequences; that is the claim Hansen calls incoherent.[^5] This is the same move catalogued in [[Doctrine of Divine Childhood Versus Total Depravity]] and the [[The Infant-Damnation Reductio Against Inherited Guilt Plus Required Profession|infant-damnation reductio]].

**The wheat-and-tares debate (second caller).** The second caller grounds unconditional election in a literal reading of the **parable of the weeds** ([[Matthew 13.36-43|Matthew 13:36–43]]): the non-elect are "sons of the evil one" *sown by the devil*, so reprobation is ontological — goats never become sheep.[^6] He pairs this with [[Romans 9.11-14|Romans 9]] (Jacob loved, Esau hated; the potter and the clay; vessels of wrath). Hansen replies that the parable's actual point is that wheat and tares are **indistinguishable until their fruit manifests**, and that commissioning the gospel to "all nations, kindreds, tongues" is pointless if only pre-planted elect can respond.[^7] See [[The Wheat and Tares as Sons of the Devil Sown by Satan]].

**The Anne Frank test case and the standard-of-goodness argument.** Hansen's sharpest case is [[Anne Frank]]: a Jewish girl who never accepted Christ and so, on the caller's view, was a "vessel of wrath" who deserved both the Holocaust and an eternity worse than it.[^8] When the caller answers that "anything God does is good no matter how unjust it is from your perspective," Hansen presses the meta-ethical point: you **cannot call God good or perfect** without a standard, and "you can't judge the standard by the standard" — so divine-command goodness collapses into the empty tautology "God is God."[^9] He notes even the Calvinist apologist [[Greg Bahnsen]] would call such unmotivated choice *arbitrary*.[^10] This is the argument developed in [[The Appeal to Mystery Empties the Word Good of Meaning]] and [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]].

**Hansen's closing LDS frame.** Hansen ends by rejecting that God "arbitrarily picks winners and losers": Anne Frank is being dealt with mercifully in the spirit world "based on the knowledge that she had," all people retain the capacity to accept or reject Christ, and "the elect are the people who are willing, not the people that are sort of already predestined."[^11] See [[Seeking God Versus Arbitrary Election]] and [[Salvation by Theology Damns Almost Everyone]].
