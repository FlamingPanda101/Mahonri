# Daniel C. Peterson - The Book of Mormon Witnesses Lied Tricked or Telling the Truth

**Format:** Interview | **Guest:** [[Daniel C. Peterson|Dr. Daniel C. Peterson]] (emeritus, Brigham Young University; Interpreter Foundation) | **Topic:** the witnesses of the golden plates

A long-form interview in which LDS scholar [[Daniel C. Peterson]] works through the three naturalistic options for explaining the Book of Mormon witnesses — they **lied**, they were **tricked/deceived**, or they were **telling the truth** — and argues, by [[Inference to the Best Explanation for the Witnesses|inference to the best explanation]], that the naturalistic theories are "evidentially impossible" while the supernatural account, however improbable to a skeptic, best accounts for the whole body of evidence. He surveys the [[Three Witnesses]] (Oliver Cowdery, Martin Harris, David Whitmer), the [[Eight Witnesses]], and a third set of **informal/unofficial witnesses** (Mary Whitmer, Lucy Mack Smith, Katherine Smith, Josiah Stowell).

- The witnesses fall into three distinct groups with very different experiences: the three (angel, voice of God, supernatural light), the eight (a prosaic noon-day hefting of a tangible object with nothing supernatural), and the informal witnesses who were not expecting anything at all.[^1] See [[Three vs Eight Witnesses Different Kinds of Experience]] and [[Informal Witnesses Were Not Primed]].
- The **conspiracy/lying** theory fails for lack of any evidence and for lack of motive: the witnesses bore testimony to their deathbeds, paid real costs (lost farms, beatings, blocked political careers), never recanted, and never cashed in.[^2] See [[Witnesses Conspiracy Theory Rebutted]] and [[No Witness Ever Recanted]].
- The **deceived/tricked** theory requires Joseph Smith to stage at least three completely different kinds of deception — an "industrial light and magic show," an ordinary daylight hefting, and individual experiences when he was not even present.[^3] See [[Witnesses Were Deceived Theory Rebutted]].
- **Hallucination** fails because the literature contains no documented case of a *shared* hallucination, and the plate descriptions are far too specific and convergent (D-shaped rings, thickness, metallic sound).[^4] See [[No Shared Hallucination]].
- **Hypnotism** is anachronistic and unworkable across eight men and absent informal witnesses, and some people simply cannot be hypnotized.[^5] See [[Hypnotism Theory of the Witnesses Rebutted]].
- The "magical 1800s worldview" claim is turned on its head: frontier farmers who cleared fields and dug wells were arguably *more* in touch with reality than screen-bound moderns.[^6] See [[Frontier People Were In Touch With Reality]].
- Peterson recommends Richard Lloyd Anderson's *Investigating the Book of Mormon Witnesses* and Grant Hardy's *Understanding the Book of Mormon* as the dictation feat (8.5–11 pages a day, distinct authorial voices, in ~two months) is itself "beyond Joseph's capacity."[^7] See [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].
