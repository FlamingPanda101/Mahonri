# Ward Radio — Sola Scriptura Debate: Anthony Rogers vs Trent Horn

A formal moderated debate on the resolution of **sola scriptura** — whether Scripture is the *sole infallible rule of faith* for the church. **Anthony Rogers** (Reformed, Presbyterian Church in America pastor) argues the **affirmative**, defending the Westminster Confession's doctrine that Scripture is the self-authenticating, inspired, inerrant, materially and formally sufficient, perspicuous, and ultimate norm of all Christian faith and practice. **Trent Horn** (Catholic Answers apologist) argues the **negative**, contending the word of God was never confined to the written word alone and that no Scripture passage teaches sola scriptura. Marlin organizes/moderates. Format: 20-min openings, 15-min rebuttals, 15-min cross-examinations each, 20-min Q&A, 7-min closings.[^1]

## Summary
- **Rogers' affirmative case** is a five-fold positive doctrine of Scripture built almost entirely from biblical texts: Scripture is (1) **self-authenticating** because the self-contained triune God speaks with self-attesting authority ("I am who I am"; Jesus' "Amen, amen, I say to you"); (2) **inspired and therefore inerrant**; (3) **materially sufficient**; (4) **formally sufficient / perspicuous**; and (5) the **ultimate norm and regulator** of faith and practice, since God's modus operandi is to give His word orally then commit it to writing for future generations.[^2] See [[Scripture Is Self-Authenticating Because God Is Self-Contained]], [[Gods Modus Operandi - Oral Word Then Committed to Writing]], and [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture]].
- **Horn's negative case** runs three arguments: (1) **the problem of defining Scripture** — "the 66 books are the sole infallible rule of faith" is itself an infallible rule of faith not found in Scripture, so an infallible rule exists outside Scripture; (2) **the absence of sola scriptura in Scripture** — no verse says all public revelation will one day be confined to the written word; (3) **the early Christians did not practice sola scriptura** — Ignatius, Clement of Rome, the Apostles' Creed, and Irenaeus point to bishop, succession, and the church.[^3] See [[The Canon Premise Is an Infallible Rule Outside Scripture]], [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]], and [[The Early Church Did Not Operate Under Sola Scriptura]].
- A central clash is over **the word of God**: Horn argues the phrase in Scripture *always* denotes an orally proclaimed prophetic/apostolic message, never a written document ([[1 Thessalonians 2.13]], [[2 Thessalonians 2.15]]); Rogers replies that [[Matthew 15.6]] and [[John 10.33-35]] explicitly call *written* Scripture "the word of God."[^4] See [[The Word of God Is Not Confined to the Written Word]].
- **Material vs formal sufficiency** is sharpened in cross-examination: Horn concedes Scripture is *materially* sufficient (everything needed is there, "like Home Depot has everything to build a house") but denies *formal* sufficiency (you can't assemble it safely without a living teaching authority).[^5] See [[Material vs Formal Sufficiency - The Home Depot Analogy]].
- **Perspicuity is pressed by hard cases**: Horn uses infant baptism (a live-audience hand-raising stunt), the Eucharist, conditional vs unconditional election, surrogacy, and the free-grace and progressive heresies to argue Scripture is *not* clear enough on salvation-relevant matters for the unlearned to attain them; Rogers replies the fault lies in blind readers, not in the light, citing the noetic effects of sin.[^6] See [[Perspicuity Fails the Hard-Cases Test]] and [[Noetic Effects of Sin Explain Interpretive Disagreement]].
- The **canon** is the recurring fault line: Horn says the church *authoritatively declares* (not fallibly discovers, not legislates) the canon via Hippo, Carthage, Florence, Trent; Rogers claims the canon can in principle be demonstrated *internally* from Scripture (Hebrew vav-prefixes, the "seams of Scripture," Deuteronomy 18's forward-pointing prophet) but admits he had no time to lay it out.[^7] See [[The Church Authoritatively Declares the Canon]] and [[The Internal-Seams Argument for the Canon]].
- **Self-authentication is contested as circular vs self-evidencing**: Horn likens "it's inspired because it says so" to the Quran and Book of Mormon, and to a Mormon "burning in the bosom"; Rogers retorts that light is self-evidencing like the shining sun ([[Luke 24.32]]) and that the Quran's god is not self-contained (swears by created things), so its book cannot self-authenticate.[^8] See [[The Self-Authentication Circularity Charge]] and [[Burning in the Bosom vs Self-Evidencing Light]].
