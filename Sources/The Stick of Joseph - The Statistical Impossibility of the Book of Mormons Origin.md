# The Stick of Joseph - The Statistical Impossibility of the Book of Mormon's Origin

Hosts [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] and [[Hayden Carroll]] interview guest [[Matt Vallejo]], an economics graduate from BYU-Idaho, who presents a slide-deck attempt to quantify the probability that [[Joseph Smith]] could have produced the [[Book of Mormon]] naturalistically — without divine help. Rather than argue any single anomaly, Vallejo isolates five "hard-to-explain" features of the book (low formal education, extreme length, extreme dictation speed, distinct authorial voices, and chiasmus), assigns each a probability against the empirical backdrop of 1820s American publishing, and combines them with a conditional-probability chain rule to reach a headline figure of roughly **1 in 15.4 billion to 1 in 23 billion**. He repeatedly invites critics to redo the math with their own assumptions, predicting any honest methodology still yields a "mind-wrecking" number. The episode closes by calibrating that figure against everyday improbabilities (meteor strikes, being struck by lightning twice, identical quintuplets, a chimp randomly typing "apple") and pivoting from statistics to a testimony that spiritual conviction, not math, is what ultimately persuades.

## Summary

- **The framing.** Vallejo grants the critic's premise — that Joseph alone composed the book "out of whole cloth," memorized it, and recited it in ~65 working days — and asks what probability the data actually support. He explicitly steelmans against himself, offers upper and lower bounds, and likens his approach to *Moneyball*: finding the few variables that "matter most," while conceding his method may look "archaic" to future analysts [00:32], [08:04], [09:30].
- **The five variables.** Low formal education (~second-grade schooling); long-form length (~265,000–270,000 words); off-the-charts writing speed (~4,000 words/day); distinct authorial voices (Nephi, Jacob, Alma, Mormon); and structural chiasmus [10:30]. See [[The Bayesian Stack - Combining Five Book of Mormon Anomalies Yields One in Billions]].
- **The 1820s baseline.** Of ~41,000 unique U.S. imprints in 1820–1829, the average word count was ~20,000–21,000 (mostly pamphlets); only ~5–7% came from the farmer/artisan/self-educated class; and the Book of Mormon sits in the top ~0.0001% by length, exceeded only by John Neal's estimated 370,000–400,000-word *My Brother Jonathan* [12:31], [14:30], [18:00]. See [[The 1820s Imprint Baseline Places the Book of Mormon at the Statistical Extreme]].
- **The speed argument.** Joseph's ~4,000 words/day is ~quadruple Neal's rate and ~3.5× the NaNoWriMo target (which only 15–20% of modern, keyboard-equipped entrants complete), putting him ~4.66 standard deviations above the mean — plus the no-manuscript, no-tutor, one-draft, read-back constraints [27:00], [29:00]. See [[Joseph Smith's Dictation Speed Is a 4.66-Sigma Outlier]].
- **Chiasmus and voices.** Vallejo defers to existing scholarship — only the Book of Mormon among 1820s imprints contains a true structural chiasmus (he uses an upper bound of 3-in-41,000 ≈ 0.7%), and the Roper–Fields–Schaalje stylometry study found greater voice distinction than Dickens, Austen, or Twain (~1 in 1,000) [33:31], [35:30]. See [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]] and [[Stylometry Shows Multiple Authors in the Book of Mormon]].
- **The calibration.** A meteor hitting your home (1 in 3.6M), being struck by lightning twice (1 in 200M), identical quintuplets (1 in 3.9B), and a chimp typing "apple" (1 in 10B) are all *more* probable than the naturalistic-authorship figure — yet no rational person organizes life around them [44:02], [47:00]. See [[Everyday Improbabilities More Likely Than Naturalistic Book of Mormon Authorship]].
- **The pivot.** The hosts and guest insist the statistics only show that belief "is not crazy"; genuine conviction comes through sacrifice, prayer, and reading the book — noting that Alma the Younger grounded his own testimony in fasting and prayer, not in his angelic vision [52:00], [53:30].
