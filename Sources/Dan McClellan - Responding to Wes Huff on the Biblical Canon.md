[[Dan McClellan]] responds line-by-line to apologist [[Wes Huff]] (and the J.I. Packer / R.C. Sproul tradition Huff channels) on how the biblical canon formed. Huff's case is the standard evangelical one: the canon was *recognized*, not *created* — the church is a thermometer, not a thermostat ("the Bible made the church, the church didn't make the Bible"; "the church no more gave us the canon than Newton gave us gravity"). McClellan's counter is that this collapses two different things: the early existence of *inspired-scripture-as-a-concept* (true) and the existence of a *closed, delineated list* matching today's Bible (false until the late 4th century CE). He argues canon is by definition a **consensus** — an agreement about which texts count as inspired — and a consensus over disputed boundaries *requires* a centralized institution to facilitate, authorize, and enforce it. So the church genuinely did play a determinative role, not in *writing* scripture but in *bounding* it.

## Summary

McClellan's rebuttals, in order:

- **The gravity analogy fails.** Gravity is a fundamental force independent of human agency; the canon is "entirely contingent upon human agency and authority because it is a consensus view" that "took centuries to arrive at." Books were written, copied, and circulated long before any canon-list debate — but circulation is not the same as a settled boundary. See [[The Church Bounds Scripture It Does Not Write It - A Consensus Requires Enforcement]].
- **The NT-quotes-NT prooftexts don't establish an early closed canon.** [[2 Peter 3.15-16|2 Peter]] calling Paul "scripture" is real but dated ~120–150 CE (majority view: 2 Peter is pseudonymous and among the *latest* NT texts), so it shows the genuine Pauline letters were *widely recognized*, not that a boundary was *formalized*. [[1 Timothy 5.18|1 Timothy 5]] supposedly quoting Luke fails on two counts: Paul died before Luke was written, and 1–2 Timothy and Titus are themselves pseudonymous (late 1st / early 2nd c.). Cf. [[Canon Cannot Self-Reference - Compilation Postdates the Books]].
- **"The first-century Christians already had the Old Testament" is false.** What counted as Jewish scripture "differed from person to person, from time to time, from place to place." No first-century source asserts a closed OT list matching ours, and both Jewish and Christian writers cite books we now exclude *as* scripture — e.g. [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude]] quotes [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude|1 Enoch and calls it prophecy]]; [[Tertullian]] defends 1 Enoch as scripture into the late 2nd c. See [[Scripture Was a Broader Category Than the Bible - The Anachronism of a Closed Canon]].
- **"It contained scripture vs. it was scripture" (re Codex Sinaiticus) is a distinction without a difference in the 4th c.** [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] reports [[Constantine]] commissioning 50 copies of "the divine scriptures"; [[Athanasius]] describes sending volumes "containing the scriptures" to Constantine — the *same* commissioned volumes, described both ways. The modern canonical/non-canonical distinction is read back anachronistically. See [[The Fifty Divine Scriptures Argument - A Distinction Without a Difference]].
- **"Inclusion in a codex ≠ scriptural reception" — true, but a dodge.** McClellan grants the bare point (the great codices were libraries holding books no one canonizes today) but says Huff *collapses chronological distance* and lumps disparate texts together to dismiss them all. The other data show several were explicitly called scripture: the [[Shepherd of Hermas]] (copied more than any OT/NT book except Psalms, Matthew, John; Tertullian quotes it as scripture), the Epistle of Barnabas, and a strong case for 1 Clement (weaker for 2 Clement and 4 Maccabees). See [[The Fifty Divine Scriptures Argument - A Distinction Without a Difference]].
- **"Would you put Barnabas / Hermas / Clement in your Bible today?" is rhetoric, not argument.** It leverages a Protestant's wish to "appear orthodox" rather than engaging whether *early Christians* treated those texts as scripture. Their modern non-canonical status has no bearing on their ancient reception.
- **"The church is the servant of scripture, not its origin" equivocates on "scripture."** First/second-century *scripture* is not isometric with the later *Bible*; the canon undergirds the modern term but not the ancient one. The church did not *write* scripture but did *determine the boundaries* around what could be called scripture — and "without the church you don't have a New Testament canon, or at least you don't have widespread agreement about" one, because enforcement included "the vilification and the criminalization of other texts resulting in their burial and abandonment."
