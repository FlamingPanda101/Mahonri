# Data Over Dogma — Biblical Literalism What's That

- **There is no such thing as a biblical literalist:** McClellan's headline claim is that "biblical literalism is a fiction that is used to try to curate the authority of the Bible and to assert authority over what the Bible actually says." [^1][^2] Nobody applies the plain sense consistently, because consistent plain-sense reading exposes contradictions, scientific falsehoods, and other gods. So-called literalists are really practicing *ideological* literalism — picking which texts get the literal treatment. See [[There Is No Such Thing as a Biblical Literalist]] and [[Literalism Is Ideological Not Textual]].
- **The early church never read the Bible flatly:** From the moment the canon was complete, thoughtful readers like [[Origen]] (3rd c.) and [[Augustine]] (4th–5th c.) recognized that the "plain/literal/historical sense" was the *least* important. Origen treated the imprecatory [[Psalm 137.9|Psalm 137:9]] (dashing Babylonian babies on the rocks) as allegory for crushing impure thoughts, denied 24-hour creation days, and shrugged at gospel disagreements; Augustine warned against asserting literal claims that "make us look stupid." [^3][^4][^5][^6] This matured into the medieval **fourfold sense** — literal, allegorical, moral, anagogical — with the salvific/christological sense ranked highest. See [[Origen's Multiple Senses of Scripture]], [[The Fourfold Medieval Sense of Scripture]], [[Psalm 137 Babylonian Babies as Allegory for Impure Thoughts]].
- **Sola scriptura and the printing press pulled the needle toward the text:** Because four layers of meaning gave the church enormous interpretive authority, the Reformation pushed back with **sola scriptura** — locating meaning in the text itself. [^7][^8] The printing press then changed *how* ordinary people met scripture: from hearing it read aloud, chained to the pulpit, to (eventually) holding and cross-checking it, which made contradictions findable. [^9][^10] Even so, Luther and Calvin still left ample room for metaphor, poetry, and figurative cosmology. See [[Sola Scriptura Arose to Wrest Authority From the Church]] and [[The Printing Press Enabled Literal Reading]].
- **Literalism as we know it is a modern, reactionary invention:** The hard literalism people fight about today is "less than a hundred years old... up until that point, Christianity didn't look like that." [^11][^12] The Enlightenment, historical criticism, geology, dinosaurs, and Darwin provoked a defensive turn: *The Fundamentals* (c. 1910–1917) coined inerrancy/historicity/univocality as identity markers, the 1925 Scopes Monkey Trial fought over teaching evolution, and the 1978 **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** (25 affirm/deny articles) codified it. [^13][^14][^15][^16] See [[Literalism Is a Modern Reactionary Invention]], [[The Fundamentals and the Birth of Fundamentalism]], [[Chicago Statement on Inerrancy Redefines Literal]].
- **"Literal" really means "whatever props up the tradition":** Chicago Article XV affirms the "literal or normal sense" yet folds in figures of speech and literary forms — so "literal" already includes the figurative, which is self-undermining and re-opens the door to interpretation. [^16][^17] When two texts' plain senses contradict (e.g. the acknowledgement of rival gods in [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3]] and in Exodus), the literalist must pick one as "really literal" and wave the other away — proving the real authority is the interpretive tradition, not the text. Pushed past its authors' intent, this same impulse breeds young-earth flat-earth "firmament" believers. [^18][^19][^20] See [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]], [[Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods]], [[Literalism Produces Flat-Earthers as Its Logical End]], [[The Threshold Problem - Where to Draw the Literal-Figurative Line]].
