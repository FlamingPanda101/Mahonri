# Freethinking Ministries — Can an All-Loving God Create Hell-Bound People? (with Hayden Carroll)

A long, friendly discussion between the Evangelical Molinist hosts of Freethinking Ministries (Tim and Josh) and Latter-day Saint apologist [[Hayden Carroll]], author of *The Creation Dilemma*. Carroll's argument: a God who is omnipotent, unbound, omniscient (knows the future), creates *ex nihilo*, and sends people to hell, faces a catch-22 — if such a God could have created only those who would freely accept him but instead created people he foreknew would be damned, he does not truly will their good. The hosts press a Molinist "feasible worlds" reply; Carroll presses back that an *unbound* God could "do it one at a time." The back half turns to whether the same dilemma recoils on Latter-day Saint theology, which Carroll answers with **eternal law** and **premortal consent**.

## Summary

- **The two sides build a real bridge on the Trinity's coherence.** Carroll grants that *social* Trinitarianism (one substance, three centers of consciousness) is not logically incoherent and is even consistent with Joseph Smith's First Vision — but Latter-day Saints still cannot affirm **consubstantiality** (Father and Son as the same *being*), holding instead that each person is co-eternal and numerically distinct. See [[The Creation Dilemma Targets Five Divine Attributes Not the Trinity Specifically]] and the existing [[Social Trinitarianism Better Fits the Distinct Persons of Scripture]]. [^1]
- **The dilemma targets five attributes, not the Trinity per se.** Carroll clarifies he is critiquing *any* God — Trinitarian, Islamic, or Jewish — who affirms that God knows the future, is all-powerful, is unbound, creates from nothing, and sends people to hell. "If you accept that as a Trinitarian, then yes; if not, then no." See [[The Creation Dilemma Targets Five Divine Attributes Not the Trinity Specifically]]. [^2]
- **The dilemma bites hardest on Calvinism and Catholicism.** The hosts and Carroll agree it is strongest where there is "no wiggle room": Calvinists "bite the bullet" (God wills the damned to hell), and Catholics are bound by dogma. It is weaker against nuanced Evangelical philosophers. See [[The Creation Dilemma Is Strongest Against Calvinism and Catholicism]]. [^3]
- **Foreknowledge is not the engine of the argument.** Carroll explicitly denies that divine foreknowledge entails determinism and denies he is an open theist ("I do believe God knows the absolute future and that we are also free"). The hosts had misread him; the real claim is that *initiating creation* while foreknowing who will be damned is itself a morally evaluable act. See the existing [[The First Moral Act Is Gods Choice to Create]] and [[Foreknowledge Is Not Causation Objection]]. [^4]
- **The Molinist "feasible worlds" reply.** Tim/Josh argue (with William Lane Craig's apparatus) that under *possible* worlds lies the narrower set of *feasible* worlds, constrained by creaturely free will: there may be no feasible world in which all free creatures freely choose God. God created "the one feasible freedom-permitting world in which evil is ultimately defeated and everyone who is not transworld-damned is saved" (cf. *Doctor Strange*'s "one of 14 million"). See the existing [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]] and [[Counterfactuals of Creaturely Freedom Constrain Freedom Not Ground It (Goff vs Molinism)]]. [^5]
- **The traducianism move.** Tim raises *traducianism* (souls arise through human generation, not direct *ex nihilo* creation) as a possible escape: if Sam's atheist parents must exist for Sam to exist, God cannot create Sam without them. Carroll's reply: an *unbound, omnipotent* God could simply "snap his fingers" and create Sam directly — so appealing to traducianism concedes a *limited* God, which is exactly the God his book does not critique. See [[Traducianism as a Solve to the Creation Dilemma]] and the existing [[Origins of the Soul - Traducianism vs Creationism vs Preexistence]]. [^6]
- **"Do it one at a time."** Carroll's sharpest formulation: if God is truly unbound, "I would only create a world where I knew that people would follow me, and if I have to do that one person at a time, I'm going to do that." Citing Jesus on Judas ("better for that man had he not been born," Matthew 26:24), he argues the loving act is to *not create* the person foreknown to be damned. See the existing [[God Could Have Created Only the Saved]] and [[The Judas Argument - Better Never to Have Been Born]]. [^7]
- **The Latter-day Saint solve #1 — eternal law.** Carroll says the dilemma does not recoil on LDS theology because Latter-day Saints posit an **eternal law** (reality itself) outside God that he did not create and cannot change; it requires him to respect free will. The hosts press that this makes "eternal law the God of gods" / an abstract object; Carroll likens it to gravity — an impersonal "what is," with a moral as well as physical side. See the existing [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]] and [[A Primitive Limit in God's Nature vs an External Eternal Constraint (Goff vs LDS)]]. [^8]
- **The Latter-day Saint solve #2 — premortal consent.** Because creation is *not ex nihilo* and intelligences are uncreated, each person **consented** in the premortal life to enter this creation cycle — so God is not culpable for a person who, created from nothing without consent, could rightly say "if you knew I'd reject you, why create me?" The hosts press that consent to embodiment "under duress" (reject it and stay in outer darkness with the rebels) is not free consent; Carroll answers the terms were known from the beginning. See the existing [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]] and [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]]. [^9]
- **Hell on the LDS side — outer darkness.** The hosts note Latter-day Saints are "almost universalists" but still affirm **outer darkness** for those who reject Christ, plus the telestial kingdom as the lowest glory; Carroll concedes outer darkness still has to contend with the dilemma but says even the telestial requires accepting Christ. See the existing [[Latter-day Saints Do Affirm Hell - DC 76 and DC 18]] and [[Outer Darkness and Whether Progression Ends]]. [^10]
- **The hosts' positive theodicy — soul-making + Molinist hopeful universalism.** Tim leans on 2 Corinthians 4:17 ("light momentary afflictions... eternal weight of glory"): this world produces the most who will be fit for heaven; God may have created the one feasible world where evil is defeated, leaving the door open to "molinistic hopeful universalism." Carroll replies this seems to value Grandpa over Hitler. See the existing [[Soul-Making Theodicy - Hick and Joseph Smith]] and [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]]. [^11]
- **Agreed framing at the close.** Both agree the argument is not a *proof* of LDS theology, only that LDS theology can answer the dilemma; the comparative question is which worldview is "most parsimonious with ultimate reality." See [[The Creation Dilemma Is a Comparative Test Not a Proof of Mormonism]]. [^12]
