# Mormonism with the Murph — Evaluating the Testimony of the Witnesses of the Gold Plates

A two-hour conversation in which the believing host [[Larry Murf|the Murph]] and ex-member critic [[Kolby Reddish]] evaluate the Three and Eight [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence|witnesses of the gold plates]]. The episode is explicitly modeled as bridge-building between believers and critics in the wake of the 2025 Charlie Kirk assassination, and most of its weight falls on *how to weigh* eyewitness testimony rather than on adjudicating individual quotes. Both men converge on a surprising amount: that a real **plate artifact** existed, that [[Joseph Smith]] very likely fabricated it, that no witness ever recanted, and that the experience was sincere — while diverging on whether subjective testimony can establish the supernatural claims attached to it.

## Summary
- **Reddish's bottom line on the witnesses:** even as a believer he never found them compelling, and as an attorney/former prosecutor he treats eyewitness testimony for *this kind of claim* as inherently insufficient — not because the witnesses lied or conspired, but because memory reframes spiritual experience over time. He found his own embellished mission-journal account as proof that memory drifts toward the faith-affirming without intent to deceive. [^1] (Existing treatments: [[Naturalistic Debunking of Spiritual Experience]], [[Informal Witnesses Were Not Primed]].)
- He distinguishes the **simplified front-of-the-Book-of-Mormon account** from the messier *History of the Church* version (Harris separating from the other two, "it is enough"), arguing the latter is more realistic and complicates the "group experience" framing. [^2]
- **The Murph's central move — the "incredible fraud" inversion:** if Joseph (a) prophesied and then induced the Three's shared vision, (b) fabricated metal plates convincing enough that the Eight handled engraved gold *naturally* (not merely under a cloth), and (c) dictated 588 pages in ~two and a half months, then the *combined* naturalistic feat is so improbable it becomes its own evidence for the miraculous — "how did Joseph pull that off?" See [[The Combined Improbability of the Naturalistic Witness Feat]]. [^3]
- Both **agree there was a real plate artifact**, attested by informal/unofficial witnesses (Katherine, William, the parents, [[Josiah Stowell]], [[Emma Smith]] moving the plates). They follow [[Dan Vogel]] that Joseph likely created **rudimentary modern (tin) plates**, the four-year "delay" giving him time to make them. Murph stresses this corroborates *a plate*, not an *ancient* one. [^4] (Existing: [[Informal Witnesses of the Golden Plates]], [[Metallurgical Fraud Theory Rebutted]].)
- **Where they split on the Eight:** Murph argues the *collective* primary statements show the Eight saw and handled engraved, gold-appearance plates **naturally** — citing [[John Whitmer]]'s "no, I saw them naturally" interview, [[Hyrum Smith]]'s "two eyes and two hands," and the Theodore Turley exchange (John Whitmer, post-apostasy: "I saw and handled... fine engravings on both sides... but I don't know if the characters are authentic"). He reads the [[Spiritual Eyes Described Real Things in the 1800s|Stephen Burnett "never saw... only under a cloth"]] line as Burnett conflating Martin Harris's *own* visionary frame, not the Eight's. [[Dan Vogel]] (and the Mormon Stories panel) instead infer "covered with a cloth." See [[John Whitmer Spiritual Eyes Objection Rebutted]]. [^5]
- **Both fault the critics for selective sourcing:** Murph's complaint is that Grant Palmer, the Tanners, and Brodie present a handful of "spiritual eyes" quotes (Martin's "eye of faith," "Jesus in the form of a deer") as the whole record while omitting the bulk of corroborating primary statements — a *bias on the critical side* he didn't expect to find. Reddish agrees everyone deserves the *full* picture and that church-history is "a moving and evolving target." [^6] (Cf. [[Witnesses Spiritual Eyes Selective Emphasis]].)
- **The "magical worldview" exchange:** Reddish clarifies he raises it not to call the witnesses gullible but to *contextualize* what their claims meant given their world. Murph reframes it as a "visionary culture," argues a belief in dowsing rods/seer stones (cf. [[Seer Stones and Divining Rods as Revelatory Means]]) does not make one easily duped into seeing an angel, and notes [[David Whitmer]] is not recorded claiming visions elsewhere (vs. [[Oliver Cowdery]], who did). [^7]
- **Stowell as a test case:** Reddish reads Stowell's 1826 South Bainbridge trial testimony — that Joseph never found treasure yet Stowell still swore Joseph "had the gift" — as a reason to take his witness *loosely*. Murph distinguishes Stowell's *beliefs* (Joseph's gift, prophethood) from what he *witnessed* (hefting/glimpsing a greenish-gold plate corner), seeing no reason to doubt the latter. See [[Josiah Stowell Defends Joseph Smith]], [[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]]. [^8]
- **Never recanted despite estrangement:** Murph's answer to "all three left the church" — none ever denied the angel/plates; they judged Joseph a *fallen* prophet (Kirtland bank crisis, Fanny Alger) while holding the Book of Mormon; Oliver and Martin returned. A genuine doubt about the *experience* would have been the moment to reinterpret it as hypnosis, and they never did. See [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]], [[No Witness Ever Recanted]]. [^9]
- **Reddish's epistemological thesis — "every testable claim fails":** eyewitness testimony is subjective; whenever Joseph makes a *non-subjective, testable* claim it fails — the vanished **stone box**, the Book of Abraham papyri, Zelph, the Kinderhook plates, and especially his fresh finding that the KJV/Book-of-Mormon Isaiah deviations do *not* agree with the Great Isaiah Scroll in a single instance. So the subjective corroboration can't carry the divine claim. See [[Every Testable Joseph Smith Claim Fails Only Subjective Evidence Survives]]. [^10]
- **The special-pleading / other-miracles problem:** Reddish presses Fatima (thousands of witnesses) as a better-attested but contradictory miracle. Murph agrees Christians often special-plead, won't outright dismiss other visionary/afterlife claims, evaluates each on its merits (his "200 people + radar" UFO sighting on *Unsolved Mysteries* as a parity case), and grants the witnesses are *better*-attested than Fatima for him. See [[Mutual Vulnerability of Supernatural Claims]]. [^11]
- **Closing:** Reddish lands on a deistic-leaning, this-worldly stance (Douglas Adams' "puddle" vs. fine-tuning; beauty needing no overarching narrative); Murph holds spiritual experience as his *primary* ground and the witnesses/fine-tuning as secondary. Both commend President Nelson's talk on contention and agree they "care more how people treat people than the reasons they hold those principles." [^12]
