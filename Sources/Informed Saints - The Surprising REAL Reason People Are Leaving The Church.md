# Informed Saints - The Surprising REAL Reason People Are Leaving The Church

**Channel:** [[Informed Saints]] | **Guest:** Dr. [[Justin Dyer]] (professor of religious education, BYU; editor of *BYU Studies*) | **Date:** ~February 2026

This interview unpacks the peer-reviewed paper *"Latter-day Saint Religiousness, Well-being, and Retention in the United States"* (Dyer, Jenna Ericson, Sam Hardy, Barbara Morgan Gardner, and David C. Dollahite), arguing that the popular online narrative of the LDS Church "hemorrhaging members" collapses once you compare it to national secularization trends.

- Latter-day Saint retention has genuinely declined since the 1980s (from ~80% to ~50% lifetime retention), but this tracks a broad, decades-long American decline in *all* social connectedness — civic, philanthropic, and religious — documented in Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone*; the LDS Church is not uniquely failing and in fact retains members, especially *active* members, at higher rates than most other Christian traditions.[^1][^2] See [[Church Is Hemorrhaging Members at a Unique Rate]], [[Decline in Religion Tracks the Broader Collapse of Social Connectedness]], and [[LDS Retention Is Higher Than Most Other Christian Traditions]].
- The single strongest predictor of staying is a felt daily connection with God; the strongest emerging predictor of leaving is *political extremity* on either end of the spectrum, as politics increasingly supersedes religion as a person's primary identity.[^3][^4] See [[Daily Connection With God Is the Top Predictor of Retention]], [[Political Identity Now Supersedes Religious Identity]], and [[The Online Echo Chamber Accelerates Disaffiliation]].
- The actual profile of an "ex-Mormon" defies the loud-online-atheist stereotype: roughly half of those who leave are "faithkeepers" or "faith-leaners" who still feel warm toward the Church, often drifting away after a move or life disruption — many of whom would return if a friend invited them.[^5][^6] See [[The Four Groups of LDS Disaffiliates]], [[Most Who Leave Drifted Away Rather Than Rejected Faith]], and [[Faithkeepers Would Return if a Friend Invited Them]].
- LDS mental-health panic claims (high Utah suicide, "Mormon depression") collapse under regional context: Utah sits in the national "suicide belt," and neighboring Colorado and Wyoming — with far fewer Latter-day Saints — have *higher* suicide rates.[^7] On positive well-being, Latter-day Saints rank at or near the top among U.S. religious groups, consistent with the dose-response benefit of [[High-Demand Religion Improves Retention and Well-being]]. See [[Utah Suicide Rate Implicates the Church]] and [[Latter-day Saints Rank High in Well-being and Spiritual Peace]].
- Despite the decline, the demographic outlook is strong: high convert-baptism rates, high participation in the church educational system, high birth rates, and a relatively young membership lead demographer Ryan Burge to forecast "a bright future." A "high-demand religion" framing explains why the LDS membership is bimodal (very active or gone) rather than dominated by a nominal middle.[^8][^9] See [[Why the LDS Membership Lacks a Large Nominal Middle]] and [[Demographic Indicators Predict a Bright LDS Future]].
