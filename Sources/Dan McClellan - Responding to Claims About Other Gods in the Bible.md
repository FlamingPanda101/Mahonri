# Dan McClellan — Responding to Claims About Other Gods in the Bible

A duet/reaction video in which McClellan responds to a creator who argues that the Bible explicitly recognizes the existence of other gods — not merely idols, but actual divine beings — over whom Yahweh claims to be supreme. McClellan affirms each proof-text as "totally accurate" and frames the whole picture as uncontroversial among scholars: the Bible recognizes other gods "from beginning to end," and the only way to find monotheism in it is to redefine the word "monotheism" until it matches something one can locate in the text. The same data underwrite the Latter-day Saint divine-council reading, though McClellan presents it as straight academic biblical studies.

## Summary

- **The other-gods texts are read as affirming real divine beings, not idols.** McClellan affirms that [[Exodus 20.3|Exodus 20:3]] ("no other gods before me") presupposes the existence of what it forbids preferring, and stresses the Hebrew says *elohim* ("gods"), not "idols." See [[Other Gods Are Real Divine Beings Not Merely Idols]]. [^1]
- **Psalm 82 is a divine-council court scene.** He affirms [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82:1]] depicts Yahweh standing in "the council of the gods," and points to his own *Journal of Biblical Literature* paper "The Gods' Complaint: Psalm 82 as a Psalm of Complaint" — the *elohim* / *bene elyon* are the patron deities of the nations. See [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]. [^2]
- **"God of gods" and "Who is like you among the gods" are rhetoric of incomparability.** [[Deuteronomy 10.17|Deuteronomy 10:17]] ("God of gods, Lord of lords") and [[Exodus 15.11|Exodus 15:11]] are direct comparisons whose force *depends* on the other gods existing and being powerful — the more powerful they are, the greater Yahweh sounds by ranking above them. See [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]]. [^3]
- **The same rhetoric saturates the surrounding polytheistic cultures.** Egyptian and Mesopotamian texts praise a favored deity as so superior the others are "nothing" by comparison — the "rhetoric of incomparability" found across the larger polytheistic empires. So Israel's praise of Yahweh is the same genre, not a unique monotheistic claim. [^4]
- **"There is only one God" is therefore not what the Bible says.** McClellan affirms the creator's conclusion: the Bible claims many gods exist but that Yahweh is above them — "that's a huge difference," and "it doesn't really sound like monotheism anymore." There is no monotheism anywhere in the Bible if monotheism means "there are no other gods." See [[Monotheism Is Not the Bibles Native Category]]. [^5]
- **Redefining "monotheism" is an ideological move.** Some scholars who engage the contemporary scholarship still want to keep "monotheism" in the Bible, so they renegotiate the term to mean something like Yahweh being "species unique" (a term he borrows from [[Michael Heiser]] / "Mike Kaiser"). McClellan calls this an attempt to maintain ideological continuity between the Bible and present-day religious communities, and notes his essay in the *Oxford Research Encyclopedia of Religion* and a Brown University conference (proceedings forthcoming, with him as an editor) on "monotheism and its absence." See [[Species-Unique Monotheism Is a Redefinition to Preserve Continuity]]. [^6]
- **Scholarly verdict: "pretty standard fare."** "Scholars would really have no objection to the observations made in this video. The Bible recognizes the existence of other gods from beginning to end." The only way to find monotheism is to change what the word means. [^7]
