# The Counsel of Trent — The Argument for God Atheists Fear the Most

[[Trent Horn]] argues that the fine-tuning argument is the one argument for God that prominent atheists repeatedly rank as the strongest, then tackles a comparatively new objection he has not previously addressed: Neil Sinhababu's "electrons in love."

## Summary
- A montage of atheists (Dawkins, Hitchens, Rationality Rules, genetically modified skeptic, [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]], Philip Goff) concede fine-tuning is the most intriguing / top-tier theistic argument; Goff was moved by it toward panpsychism [^1].
- Fine-tuning persuades because it is an *easily understood* phenomenon that cries out for explanation — unlike contingency or morality, which people deflect; it had antecedents in Paley's design argument before the physics was known [^2].
- Sinhababu's objection: if "psychophysical laws" were different, even a hydrogen-only universe could host conscious agents (electrons that "fall in love"), so conscious agents may not be improbable; Horn replies that minds existing apart from bodies (God, angels) does not mean minds can exist in *any* body, and that our complex biological universe still cries out for explanation (John Leslie's red-wall/white-wall fly) [^3].
