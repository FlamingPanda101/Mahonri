# Data Over Dogma — Did Jesus Make the Old Testament Obsolete?

[[Dan McClellan]] and [[Dan Beecher]] take up [[Matthew 5.17-20|Matthew 5:17-20]] — "I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil" — to ask whether the [[Jesus of Nazareth|Matthean Jesus]] actually abrogated the [[Moses|Mosaic]] law. The episode is framed by Beecher's frustration with a common Christian inconsistency: the same believers who say the law of Moses no longer applies (hence shellfish is fine) still treat the Ten Commandments as binding "good law." Reading the passage on its own terms, the hosts argue that Matthew has Jesus *affirming* the continuing validity of Torah until an apocalyptic endpoint, and that the gospel functions as a polemic against the law-free [[Paul the Apostle|Pauline]] mission to the Gentiles. The discussion exemplifies the podcast's recurring thesis that the Bible is a contested library, not a [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal|univocal]] book.

## Summary
- Beecher's setup: Christians give inconsistent answers about which Mosaic laws still bind. The "Jesus fulfilled the law, so it doesn't apply" move collides with the fact that those same Christians keep the Ten Commandments. [^1]
- The hosts flag — but defer to a future segment — the traditional **threefold/categorical division of the law** (ceremonial vs. moral, "laws that were always meant to be fulfilled"), treating it as one of several post-hoc rationalizations for Christian non-observance. [^2]
- The crux is the meaning of "fulfil." McClellan reads BDAG on Greek *plēroō*: "to bring to completion that which was already begun" and "to bring to a designed end" (of a prophecy, obligation, promise, or law) — i.e., the law reaching its appointed completion, **not** being abolished. See [[Pleroo Means to Complete a Law Not to Abolish It]]. [^3]
- Verse 18 contains **two synonymous chronological markers** — "till heaven and earth pass" and "till all be accomplished" — which must refer to the same future apocalyptic moment, implying the law stays in force until the eschaton. See [[Dual Chronological Markers Keep the Law in Force Until the Eschaton]]. [^4]
- The popular "Jesus subsumed all the laws within himself" reading is rejected: it renders verse 19 ("whoever breaks one of these least commandments... least in the kingdom") meaningless. Matthew explicitly says you *must keep* the commandments. [^5]
- **Luke contrasts with Matthew.** Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" version reframes the saying as a statement about *difficulty* ("easier for heaven and earth to pass away than for one tittle to fail"), not *time* — a different theology that only a forced harmonization could square with Matthew. [^6]
- "You can't fulfill a negative." Beecher notes that prohibitions ("do not murder") cannot be "completed" the way a positive obligation can; you can only keep not doing them — which strains the apologetic sense of "fulfill." [^7]
- "The law and the prophets" reflects a **two- or three-part genre division of Jewish scripture** (Law/Prophets, or Law/Prophets/Psalms; cf. the **Tanakh** — Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim — and a later Christian fourfold split adding "histories"). It indicates a general division of scriptural genre, **not** a closed canon yet. [^8]
- The "613 commandments" (Halakhah) plus the prescriptive use of Proverbs and Psalms show the scope of "the law" Jesus is said to uphold extends well beyond the Decalogue. [^9]
- **Historical reconstruction:** rather than a historical Q&A, this is the *author* of Matthew (~80-90 CE) pushing back against followers of [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] (active in the 50s-60s CE) who taught Gentiles they need not keep the law/circumcision. Matthew is "a discursive volley" intensifying Torah-observance — "anyone who teaches otherwise" (read: Paul) is "least in the kingdom." See [[Matthew Intensifies the Law as a Polemic Against the Pauline Mission]] and [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]]. [^10]
- Supporting this, McClellan notes Matthew alone has Jesus *endorse* continued tithing (of "mint and dill," Matt 23:23) while never straightforwardly abolishing a Mosaic law — only *reinterpreting* it ("you have heard... but I say unto you"). The other gospels lack this material. [^11]
- The Pharisees were *not*, in early Jewish sources, the strictest law-keepers — they were sometimes mocked as lax interpreters — which is why "exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees" sets a clearable (not impossible) bar; the Essenes were the rigorists. [^12]
- Broader payoff: the tension is real because the Bible "argues with itself." The presupposition of [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal|univocality]] (inerrancy, harmonization) forces interpreters to "do violence" to authors' intentions; letting each text say what its author meant is more honest and more interesting. [^13]
