A longer-form [[Ether's Elephant]] video in which the creator reports reading the entire 1830 first edition of the Book of Mormon and then writing a program to detect and classify every change between it and the modern edition. The argument has two layers. First, a deflationary one: the "thousands of changes" critique is "frankly nonsense" — the overwhelming majority are grammatical fixes (e.g. "what → who" at [[Moroni 10.26|Moroni 10:26]]) that leave meaning untouched. Second, a constructive one: the creator claims his analysis finds a non-uniform *pattern* in the edits across the book's sub-books, which he argues is evidence of **multiple authors/voices** — and therefore of ancient, multi-hand authorship rather than a single 1820s forger.

The reasoning is explicitly abductive and offered as a dilemma for skeptics: if Joseph wrote it alone, the intricate, non-uniform error patterns are hard to produce unintentionally; if a team wrote it, the narrative coherence and rapid production are hard to explain. The video stresses that the work "doesn't definitively prove anything" and openly flags its limitations, while taking a parting shot at [[John Dehlin]]'s research.

## Summary

- **Most "changes" are bland grammar, not doctrine.** Reading the 1830 edition, "I was amazed to see just how little has changed besides some obvious grammatical errors." The common edit is correcting English (e.g. "what" to "who"), "without altering its original meaning."[^1] → [[Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors]]; cf. the vault's [[Most Correct Book Does Not Mean Error Free]] and [[Book of Mormon Edits May Strengthen Rather Than Undermine Its Origin]].

- **A single author should make uniform errors; the data look non-uniform.** The "meticulous teacher with a red pen" analogy: a 500-page essay by one student should show a roughly constant edit-rate per 1,000 words. "If some sections are heavily marked... while others are nearly untouched this would suggest evidence of multiple authors."[^2] → [[Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors]].

- **The creator's own computational test reports a pattern.** Using a difference library, a scratch edit-distance algorithm, regex preprocessing, a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test for uniformity (significant across batch sizes), unsupervised clustering (~10 distinct edit patterns), and ANOVA + Tukey on sub-books (e.g. Alma differs significantly from 2 Nephi and Jacob in grammar edits), he argues the edits are *not* uniformly distributed.[^3] → [[Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors]]; relates to existing [[Book of Mormon Names Show Authorial Wordplay]] and word-print discussions.

- **This challenges the "literary genius forged distinct voices" thesis.** Even granting Joseph could fake different *styles*, "becoming an entirely different writer by altering one's mistakes is another" — unintentional error-fingerprints are far harder to fake than deliberate voices, and the patterns "don't align perfectly with the natural divisions between books," complicating any collaboration theory.[^4] → [[Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors]]; cf. [[Confirmed Anachronisms Are Reverse-Anachronisms Joseph Could Not Have Known]] as a sibling abductive argument.

- **The book "feels ancient" and the brass-plates layering predicts mixed voices.** Nephite authors quote older sources (the brass plates) "and not say they're doing it," "like Paul and like John" — so "a lot of hands, a lot of fingerprints" is exactly what an authentic, abridged ancient record should show. The "smells like plates" features: choppy time-skips, doubling back, introducing a story before its characters.[^5] → [[Edit-Pattern Stylometry as Evidence of Multiple Ancient Authors]]; cf. [[Read the Book of Mormon as a Translated Ancient Text]] and [[Mormon Is an Ethnocentric Redactor Focused on the Witness of Christ]].
