# Ward Radio — Revenge of the Adam-God Theory (feat. Chris Blyth)

After Ward Radio's earlier episode argued the "Adam-God theory never happened" and drew fire from critics (Radio Free Mormon, Bill Reel, Corbin Volluz), the panel ([[Jonah Barnes]], Brad Whitfield, host "Carden") brings in folklorist [[Christopher Blythe|Chris Blyth]] to "react to their own episode" and correct the record. Blyth — who once spent "a year or two" trying to make the *two-Adams / title-only* reading work before abandoning it — concedes that [[Brigham Young]] really did teach Adam-God, but reframes the whole controversy historically: the modern apologetic shape of the doctrine was forged in the **1950s–60s reaction to LeBaron fundamentalism**, and what Brigham taught is best understood as an extrapolation from [[Joseph Smith]]'s teaching that the **ancient of days is Adam**. The episode is unusually pro-doctrine: rather than minimizing Adam-God, the panel argues the church **kept most of it** and that it carries real theological payoffs (it tells us who Heavenly Mother is).

## Summary
- **The host's actual claim was narrower than critics heard.** "Carden" clarifies that "Adam-God theory never happened" meant the *weaponized* caricature — Adam-God "as wielded against the members of the Church... ad nauseam" — never happened, not that Brigham said nothing [^1]. Blyth gently overrides the panel: "I want to find a way to make this work... but then I ran into a couple of things" where "Brigham Young pretty clearly is like, yes, Adam of the Garden is the father of our spirits" [^2].
- **The two-Adams reading arose to fight the LeBarons.** Blyth's central historical thesis: [[Spencer W. Kimball]] and [[Bruce R. McConkie]] cracked down on Adam-God in the 1960s because the **Church of the Firstborn of the Fullness of Times** (founded 1955 by the LeBaron brothers) was actively proselytizing it — even converting whole groups of missionaries in France. The "two atoms theory" was developed in this era to *explain away* Brigham's teaching [^3]. See [[Two-Adams Theory Arose to Counter LeBaron Fundamentalism]].
- **Brigham extrapolated it from Joseph.** Blyth and Whitfield trace the genealogy: Joseph identified the **ancient of days** (Daniel 7) as Adam and called him **Michael the archangel** (not *a* archangel) — "the top dog." [[Helen Mar Kimball]] reports the identification; [[Charles B. Thompson]] independently extrapolated a similar Adam-exalted scheme. Brigham's 1847 Winter Quarters near-death vision of all humanity sealed back to Adam supplied the rest [^4]. See [[How Brigham Young Extrapolated Adam-God from Joseph Smith]].
- **The church kept most of Adam-God.** Walking a Mormonr.org table point-by-point, the panel argues the church **accepted** heavenly parents, ex-materia creation, Adam as ancient of days, theosis-by-the-Adam-and-Eve-pattern, and a resurrected/godlike Adam — and **rejected** only the specific tenets that Adam is *our* Father in Heaven, that Jehovah (not Christ) fathered Adam, and that Adam fathered Jesus' spirit [^5]. See [[The Church Kept Most of Adam-God Rejecting Only Specific Tenets]].
- **The redemptive payoff: Eve is Heavenly Mother.** Blyth argues Adam-God's benefit is that it "teaches us who Heavenly Mother is" — Eve *is* Heavenly Mother, so the Eden narrative becomes the story of Heavenly Mother choosing to bring forth her offspring [^6]. See [[Eve Is Heavenly Mother as the Payoff of Adam-God]].
- **Brigham's motive: he thought he was imparting a beautiful truth, not innovating.** Against the conspiracy that Brigham invented Adam-God for polygamy or power, "Carden" presses "what's in it for Brigham Young?" — a starving frontier prophet had no incentive. Blyth answers that Brigham "really believed he did" receive it from Joseph and "thinks he's revealing a beautiful truth" [^7].
- **"Two churches within a church."** The host's closing lament: the LDS apologetic world has split into a Protestant-facing camp that downplays Adam-God and an academic-facing camp that embraces infinite regression of gods — "both claim... the real version," and he sees "no consensus" [^8]. See [[Two Churches Within a Church on Adam-God]].
- **The de-escalation precedent.** [[Wilford Woodruff]] is cited as having urged the saints to "leave this alone — Adam is Adam, Michael is Michael, God is God... you don't have to try to force this all together," which Blyth calls wise [^3]. By 1854 (Oct 8 "This Is Life Eternal") Brigham himself softened: "whether Adam is the personage that we should consider our heavenly father or not... I do not care for one moment how that is" [^9].
