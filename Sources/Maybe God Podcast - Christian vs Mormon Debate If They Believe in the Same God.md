# Maybe God Podcast — Christian vs Mormon Debate: Do They Believe in the Same God?

The Maybe God podcast's first debate-style episode, moderated by host Eric, pairs Latter-day Saint apologist [[Jacob Hansen]] against evangelical apologist [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] on whether Christians and Latter-day Saints worship "the same God."[^1] The conversation moves through three battlegrounds — scriptural authority and the canon, the [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|nature of God (Trinity vs. LDS Godhead)]], and salvation — with Hansen repeatedly "taking off his Latter-day Saint hat" to evaluate Protestantism as a seeker, and Shafovaloff pressing that shared *terms* mask different *referents*.

## Participants
- [[Jacob Hansen]] (Latter-day Saint apologist, Thoughtful Faith; known for debating [[Alex OConnor]])
- [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] (evangelical / Reformed Baptist; Mormonism Research Ministry, Salt Lake City)
- Host: Eric (Maybe God podcast)

## Summary
- **Framing: "Christian before Latter-day Saint," but not creedal.** Hansen affirms he is a Christian who centers the risen Christ, but explicitly rejects the *creedal* formulations — the Nicene and Athanasian creeds — as the later fourth-century litmus test that defines him out; he takes "no issue" with the Apostles' Creed.[^2] Shafovaloff embraces the label "creedal Christian" and the Chalcedonian two-natures definition.[^3] See [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]].
- **Whether they worship the same God.** Shafovaloff's thesis: holding "the historic mainstream typical beliefs of the Latter-day Saint faith" calls into question "whether they're worshiping the same God"; the two camps "use a lot of the same terminology," so "there's a lot of work that has to be done... to unpack the definitions of those terms" — an echo of the Council of Nicaea forcing definitions out of Arian biblical language.[^4]
- **Canon and authority — the closed-canon problem.** Hansen argues the Bible is "a library" and presses: nothing *in* the canon lists the books of the canon, so "whoever chose what books go into the library" is the real ultimate authority — and that authority worked by *binding public revelation*. He rejects a closed canon as an unscriptural tradition.[^5] See [[The Canon Question Is Logically Prior to Canon Authority]] and [[An Open Canon Allows Unbounded Ecclesiastical Authority]].
- **Shafovaloff: "radical receiving," not conferral.** Against Hansen, Shafovaloff (citing [[Gavin Ortlund]] and [[Michael Krueger]]) argues Moses at the burning bush "didn't need a second voice... saying, this is the voice of God"; "my sheep hear my voice." The church *receives* and *recognizes* the self-authenticating books rather than conferring authority on them — "Christianity is a religion of radical receiving."[^6] See [[Self-Authenticating Canon - Feel It in Your Bones]], [[Scripture Is Self-Authenticating Because God Is Self-Contained]], and [[The Light Manifests Itself - Perspicuity of Scripture]].
- **"Emptied the canon," not expanded it.** Shafovaloff's signature canon move: in Protestant terms a canonical book is the *infallible, inerrant, very speech of God*; since (he argues) the LDS apologetic framework holds *no* infallible books, "they've emptied the canon... it's an empty canon of any infallible books," not an expanded one.[^7] See [[The LDS Tradition Emptied the Canon Rather Than Expanded It]].
- **Book of Mormon — origin and the "too much Protestantism" problem.** Hansen narrates the Book of Mormon as a revealed rendering (via seer stone / "interpreters") of a pre-exilic Israelite record, dictated by Joseph Smith at ~4,000 words/day in roughly two and a half months.[^8] Shafovaloff counters with intertextuality — Joseph Smith appears to copy-paste KJV New Testament language into texts set ~600 BC — and surveys three LDS explanations (Royal Skousen's early-modern-English rendering, a spirit-world pre-translation, and Blake Ostler's "creative participant" expansion).[^9] See [[Why Is There So Much Protestantism in the Book of Mormon]] and [[Dynamic Translation Answers Book of Mormon Anachronism]].
- **The infallibility / contradiction trade.** Hansen rejects biblical inerrancy because he finds contradictions (e.g., Leviticus' death penalty for homosexual acts vs. an unchanging God vs. Jesus), reconciling them via a *collective prophetic witness* (99 prophets over 1) and the view that not every word of scripture was even inspired.[^10] Shafovaloff replies that inerrancy makes one "pause and patiently reflect," synthesizing the whole via covenant/dispensational structure — "I want to have as high a view of scripture as Jesus himself had."[^11] See [[Rejecting Biblical Inerrancy Solves the Moral Contradiction Problem]], [[The Collective Prophetic Witness|Outliers Against the Collective Prophetic Witness May Be Uninspired]], and [[The Narrative Reading of Scripture vs Proof-Texting]].
- **Prophets: can a true prophet be a "theological disaster"?** Citing [[Blake Ostler]]'s line that [[Brigham Young]]'s theology "was a disaster," Shafovaloff crystallizes the evangelical principle: true prophets do not "fundamentally, publicly and persistently misrepresent God." Hansen counters with Jonah's unfulfilled Nineveh prophecy, contingent prophecy, and the fallenness of all but Jesus.[^12] See [[True Prophets Cannot Be Theological Disasters]], [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]], and [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]].
- **Nature of God — transcendence vs. the same ontological type.** Shafovaloff opens with the *most high*, aseity, and incomprehensibility (Isaiah 40–48; Rev 4): "who God is today is who he always was"; God is "not a big one of us."[^13] Hansen presses the Trinity as incoherent ("you can't be a frog and the number seven"), citing Jesus' distinct will and knowledge (John 17:3; the Father knowing what the Son does not), and affirms God and man are "the same species" while rejecting *creatio ex nihilo*.[^14] See [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive]], [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]], [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity]], and [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]].
- **Did God become God? — the diversity asymmetry.** Hansen insists LDS *scripture* never teaches God became God (sacrament prayer: "God the eternal Father"), and he rejects the infinite regress of gods even where Joseph Smith's [[King Follett Discourse]] / Sermon in the Grove gestures at it. Shafovaloff's rebuttal is methodological: ask 100 Latter-day Saints whether the Father was once a sinful mortal and you get wide diversity; ask 100 evangelical seven-year-olds and you get unanimous "no" — revealing what each community is "willing to tolerate diversity over."[^15] See [[The Diversity Test - What a Community Tolerates Reveals Its God]] and [[LDS Father Was Always Fully Divine (No Eternal Regression)]].
- **"Offspring of God" — Acts 17 dueling readings.** Hansen reads Acts 17:28–29 ("we are also his offspring") as literal divine kinship of the same kind; Shafovaloff reads the prior clause ("in him we live and move and have our being") as creaturely dependence — we "owe our very being to God," not co-eternity.[^16] See [[Humans Are the Same Species as God (Acts 17 Offspring)]] and [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]].
- **Polytheism, henotheism, and the divine council.** Hansen denies polytheism but calls the Bible "monarchical," not "hard metaphysical" monotheism — a head God over real lesser *elohim* (Genesis 1:26 "let us"; Psalm 82). Shafovaloff insists "God is most high" is non-negotiable for Christians and points to LDS prophets who taught a "Heavenly Father's Heavenly Father."[^17] See [[The Godhead Is Not Polytheism]], [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]], and [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]].
- **Salvation — same words, different "taking his hand."** Both say "saved by grace through faith, not of works." Shafovaloff frames it as a Christmas gift basket received by empty-handed faith like David and the thief on the cross (Romans 4).[^18] Hansen uses the car-on-the-cliff / helicopter analogy: grace saves, but we "take his hand" *through covenant* — faith, repentance, baptism — over an *eternal* journey, rejecting unconditional/predestinarian grace.[^19] Host Eric presses whether baptism and obedience are prerequisites. See [[LDS Salvation Is by Grace Not Earned by Works]], [[Car Wreck Analogy for Diagnosing Apostasy|Car-on-the-Cliff Helicopter Analogy]], [[Works Are the Fruit of Faith Not the Price of Salvation]], and [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]].
