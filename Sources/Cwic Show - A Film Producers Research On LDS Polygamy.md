# Cwic Show — A Film Producer's Research On LDS Polygamy (Selene Anderson)

A *Cwic Show* (host [[Greg Matson]]) interview with film producer [[Selene Anderson]], who researched 19th-century Latter-day Saint plural marriage while producing the folk-horror short film *The Angel* (filmmaker Barrett Bergen), set in a 19th-century polygamous family. Anderson frames her project as a "reclaiming" of the history from both fundamentalists and the polygamy-denial movement, arguing the topic should be engaged "loudly and boldly" rather than timidly.

- Anderson argues the LDS polygamy-denial movement has succeeded by "capitaliz[ing] on women's insecurity and men's guilt," and that treating the plural wives as brainwashed victims is itself misogynistic — either women are rational agents or they are not.[^1] See [[Polygamy Denial Capitalizes on Female Insecurity and Male Guilt]].
- She reads plural marriage through an evolutionary-anthropological lens (female hypergamy, the asymmetry of reproductive cost, 83% of societies practicing polygyny, monogamy as a legally-imposed equalizer) — and through "integral theory" as a developmental progression: hunter-gatherer polygyny → agricultural harems → imposed monogamy (enabling democracy and women's rights) → a restored, woman-driven plural marriage.[^2] See [[Plural Marriage Read Through Hypergamy and the Asymmetry of Reproduction]].
- The historical core: early Utah women voted 50 years before the 19th Amendment, owned property, and had the nation's easiest divorce; early Saints explicitly tied women's emancipation to the Restoration. Anderson reads [[George Q. Cannon]]'s 1884 *Juvenile Instructor* essay as casting plural marriage as the mechanism lifting Eve's curse so women would "stand side by side with man."[^3] See [[Early Saints Tied Womens Emancipation to the Restoration]].
- Doctrinally she argues plural marriage was meant to be **woman-driven and bounded**: the existing wife gives the new wife's hand; [[Exodus 21.10|Exodus 21:10]] forbids diminishing the first wife's support; "deal justly or do not marry multiple women." She pairs plural marriage with the law of consecration as its "masculinized version" — both ideals the Church covenants toward without currently practicing.[^4] See [[Plural Marriage and the Law of Consecration Are the Same Law]].
- The closing theological move: the objection "God could never command this" is really a debate about the nature of God; Anderson answers that God "would never command something that's not for our benefit," trusting that His ways are higher.[^5] See [[The Polygamy Objection Is Really a Debate About the Nature of God]].
