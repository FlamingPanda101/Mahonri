A short explainer from [[Ether's Elephant]] arguing that critics — and many Latter-day Saints — fundamentally misunderstand the **Joseph Smith Translation (JST)** of the Bible. The video's thesis is that the JST is an *inspired revision and commentary* meant to convey the mind and meaning God intends, **not** an academic reconstruction of the original Greek or Hebrew manuscripts. Read that way, the standard "gotcha" — that the JST does not match the earliest manuscripts — simply misfires, because reconstructing the autographs was never its goal.

The piece situates the JST in LDS practice: the Church uses the King James Bible rather than the JST largely for a historical reason — the Reorganized Church (now the **Community of Christ**) held the JST's publishing rights, limiting the early Utah church's access — so today only "snippets" survive in the LDS footnotes, while the larger revealed expansions live in the **[[Book of Moses]]** and the Pearl of Great Price. The video also stresses that the act of "translating" the Bible was the *catalyst* for many revelations now in the Doctrine and Covenants: Joseph went through the English text "verse by verse," asking God what each passage meant.

## Summary

- **The JST is not an attempt to restore the autographs.** "Joseph Smith was not trying to restore the original Greek or Hebrew. Not even close."[^1] Some of it does render a lost account "written on parchment," but even that "does not mean it's trying to recreate word-for-word ancient documents."[^2] This reframing — that a "manuscript mismatch" objection targets a goal the JST never had — parallels [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]] and [[JST Is Revision in the Pseudepigraphic Tradition]]. → [[The JST Recovers Revelatory Intent Not Original Wording]]

- **"Translation" here means conveyance of meaning, not language-swapping.** Citing "the most ancient meaning… of the word translation" as "to convey the mind, the meaning, and understanding," the video argues Joseph "was simply trying to convey the mind and meaning of what God wants us to understand."[^3] The English word *translation* is doing equivocal work, and the critic's objection trades on the modern (language-to-language) sense. → [[Translation Means to Convey the Mind Not Decode a Language]]

- **Spirit over words.** Joseph "seemed to grasp later in his life that the importance of scriptures is less in the words and more in the spirit."[^4] Language is "just symbols that point to abstractions of reality"; what the scriptures contain are "only hints of what existed in the prophet's mind."[^5] The operative question "is not what did the original text say? It is what was the intent of the passages from God."[^6] This is developed in [[The JST Recovers Revelatory Intent Not Original Wording]].

- **The "lost plain and precious truths" claim is narrower than critics assume.** Latter-day Saints believe plain and precious truths were lost over time and the Restoration recovers them — but this does *not* mean the JST is "revealing what was written on the original manuscripts."[^2] Compare the inverse framing in [[The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument]].

- **Imperfections are not disqualifying — and can signal authenticity.** The JST was never finished; Joseph "was still making edits prior to his death."[^7] The video addresses critics by name — "Thank you skeptics. Thank you [[Thomas Paine]]" — and reframes Book of Abraham/Egyptologist objections: "you just proved that Joseph wasn't a scholar… and we already knew that."[^8] It closes by previewing that data on Book of Mormon textual edits will be argued as *evidence* of authenticity.[^9]
