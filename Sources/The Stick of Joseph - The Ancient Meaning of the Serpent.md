# The Stick of Joseph — The Ancient Meaning of the Serpent

A long-form conversation on the [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Stick of Joseph]] channel reuniting hosts [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David ("Dave") Butler]] to ask why Jesus compares *himself* to a serpent in [[John 3.16|John 3:14]]. The interpretive engine is [[James Charlesworth]]'s monograph *The Good and Evil Serpent*, read alongside [[John 3.16|John 3:14]], [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]], [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]], [[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]], and the Book of Mormon's roughly five Nehushtan passages, to recover an ancient Israelite "good serpent" — a divine, fiery, sin-purifying being that heals from death and is identified with Christ. The episode closes by re-reading [[Genesis 3.4-5|Genesis 3]] as a "two serpents" drama in which Satan, a counterfeit of the true serpent-God, impersonates the gardener.

## Summary
- **The whole episode is a commentary on one verse — [[John 3.16|John 3:14]] — built on Charlesworth's *The Good and Evil Serpent*.** Charlesworth surveyed "thousands" of commentaries on John and found that nearly all of them talk about Jesus being *raised up* while none notice that he is comparing himself to a *snake*; a "giant gaping void of silence" produced by modern cultural blinders that assume snakes can only be evil [^1][^2]. See [[John 3.14 Compares Christ to the Serpent Not Just the Lifting Up]].
- **"Lifting up" is an artifact of the New Testament, not [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]].** The Hebrew of Numbers 21 never says "raise up"; God tells Moses to *make a seraph* and *set it on a pole*. Commentators fixate on the verb "lifted up" to avoid the real comparison — Jesus to the serpent itself [^3][^4]. See [[John 3.14 Compares Christ to the Serpent Not Just the Lifting Up]].
- **The brazen serpent is a healing, sin-purifying divine being — the [[Two Serpents - Mehen and Apophis Parallel the Brazen Serpent|Nehushtan]] — that the Nephites and early Christians valued.** In [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] the problem is *death* and the cure is a serpent; in [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]] the problem is *sin* and the cure is the same serpent (a seraph touches Isaiah's lips with fire). "Nehushtan" is the disparaging nickname Hezekiah coined ([[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]]); the word is absent from John 3 and the Book of Mormon *precisely because* those who valued the serpent did not insult it [^5][^6][^7]. See [[The Nehushtan Is a Healing Divine Serpent the Nephites and Early Christians Valued]].
- **Hezekiah and Isaiah are in direct conflict over the serpent — evidence of multiple religions in the First Temple.** Hezekiah destroys the Nehushtan for receiving incense ([[2 Kings 18.4|2 Kings 18:4]]), yet Isaiah, his contemporary, sees the seraphim amid clouds of temple incense ([[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]]). The hosts use [[Richard Friedman]]'s E/J source division to argue [[Numbers 21.8-9|Numbers 21]] is a *northern* (E) text, and the Book of Mormon — a northern Josephite record — preserves the pro-serpent strand the southern editors and later Judaism suppressed [^8][^9]. See [[The Nehushtan Is a Healing Divine Serpent the Nephites and Early Christians Valued]].
- **Serpent-on-a-staff is a shared ancient inheritance (Asclepius, Hermes), and gematria ties *nachash* to *mashiach*.** Charlesworth's "handmade midrash" invites readers to see the serpent as a boundary-crossing, skin-shedding, healing symbol across the ancient world; the Greeks did not borrow from Israel but drew on a common patrimony. *Nachash* (358) and *mashiach*/Messiah (358) share the same numerical value [^10][^11][^12]. See [[The Serpent on a Staff Is a Shared Ancient Patrimony of Healing]].
- **Genesis 3 is a "two serpents" / "thunder twins" drama, and the curse rips off wings, not legs.** In the Eden dialogue the serpent says "*Elohim*," but the rest of the garden text says "*Yahweh Elohim*" — the counterfeit serpent impersonates the God of the garden ("transformeth himself nigh unto an angel of light," [[2 Nephi 9.14|2 Nephi 9]]). Because seraphim are *winged* serpents, "upon thy belly shalt thou go" ([[Genesis 3.14-19|Genesis 3:14]]) depicts the cherubim tearing off the impostor's wings and casting him into the dust [^13][^14][^15]. See [[The Two Serpents of Eden and the Divine Name Switch]].
