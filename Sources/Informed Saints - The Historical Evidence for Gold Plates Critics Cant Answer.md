# Informed Saints — The Historical Evidence for Gold Plates Critics Can't Answer

The [[Informed Saints]] hosts (including [[Jasmine Rapley]]) interview [[Josh Gailey]], an ordained evangelist in the Church of Jesus Christ (Monongahela, Pennsylvania) and author of *Witnessing Miracles*, who applies [[Gary Habermas]]' resurrection-apologetics method — the **minimal facts** argument — to the coming forth of the Book of Mormon, casting the empty **stone box** in the New York hill as "essentially the empty tomb of the resurrection for the Book of Mormon."

## Summary

Gailey's project begins with a "lightbulb moment" watching Habermas defend the resurrection: he realized the same method — early, independent, unsympathetic sources boiled down to a set of agreed core facts, then a probability/best-explanation analysis — could be applied to the gold plates, and found that nobody had crossed that threshold, so the space was open ([[The Minimal Facts Method Applied to the Gold Plates]]). The stakes, he argues, are high for any theist: if the plates are real, Christianity gains a "cross-continental independent attestation of the risen Christ."

He first walks through the resurrection's standard minimal facts (sincere belief unto death; the empty tomb supported by his **PET** rubric — **P**roximity, **E**nemies, **U**nlikely **T**estimonies), then ports the whole framework onto the Book of Mormon ([[The Stone Box Is the Empty Tomb of the Book of Mormon]]). The witness case is, he claims, *documentarily richer* than the resurrection's: ~200 extant sources from the three witnesses, 60+ from the eight, and 21 people interacting with the plates — in early, contemporaneous, often handwritten records (e.g., the printer's manuscript in [[Oliver Cowdery]]'s hand). The witnesses paid real costs, were excommunicated from the church they founded, were offered money to recant, and still — even when estranged and states apart — independently affirmed the same artifact down to minute details (size, color, black patina). This material is developed in the existing cluster: [[Witnesses Paid a Real Cost With No Payoff]], [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]], [[Witnesses Conspiracy Theory Rebutted]], [[All Three Witnesses Upheld Their Testimony for Life]], and [[Three vs Eight Witnesses Different Kinds of Experience]].

The distinctive payload is the **stone box** and the **hostile/unlikely** witnesses to it. The movement began within a 10-mile radius of the hill (proximity); curious or greedy locals — including former treasure-hunting associates — went to the hill, saw a hole and an empty stone box, and were driven to dig and to try to steal the "gold Bible," their very actions amounting to an enemy admission like the Sanhedrin's in Matthew 28 ([[Hostile and Unlikely Witnesses Corroborate the Stone Box]]). [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Oliver Cowdery]] give nearly identical *Times and Seasons* descriptions; [[Lorenzo Saunders]], a dedicated critic, inadvertently confirms the hole's location ("summit, west side") and its age ("no fresh dirt," consistent with a 1823 excavation). Unlikely witnesses to the plates themselves include [[Josiah Stowell]] (a court glimpse of a "greenish" corner — explained by [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|tumbaga oxidation]]), the suspicious father-in-law [[Isaac Hale]] (hefting the box), and [[Emma Smith]]. Gailey adds an undesigned-coincidence: [[David Whitmer]] (1875) and [[Edward Stevenson]] (first Whitmer interview 1877) independently report the box washed down the hill ([[Independent Attestation of What Happened to the Stone Box]]).

He closes by dismissing the rival fabrication theories as ad hoc and source-less — "tin plates," and the newer "stereotype printing plates" — echoing [[Joseph Smith Lacked the Metallurgical Skill and Time]], [[Witness Descriptions Constrain Any Fabrication Hypothesis]], and [[Sonia Hazards Printing-Plate Theory Fails the Physical Description]]. The episode's bold conclusion, voiced by the host: on bare historical grounds a *better* case can be made for the gold plates than for the resurrection — said by speakers who affirm the resurrection so strongly they believe Christ visited the Americas.
