# The Stick of Joseph - What Ancient Scripture Reveals About the Family of Heaven

A [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] conversation (with hosts and [[Austin Falter]]), released alongside the children's book *Family: A Rhyming Proclamation for Kids*, arguing that the LDS "family of heaven" — a Father, a Mother, and a Son, with humans as their literal kin — is not a Joseph Smith innovation but the *oldest* layer of Israelite religion, recoverable from the Hebrew Bible read against its ancient Near Eastern background.

## Summary

Butler's frame is that two "very old ideas — older than the Old Testament" stand behind the family proclamation: (1) God is not an impersonal singular force but **a family of beings**, and (2) the relationship between humans and the gods is one of **shared family**. He contrasts this with the creedal God inherited from **Platonism** (God as "the form of goodness") and **Aristotle** (the **unmoved mover**), arguing that such a God cannot be prayed to — nobody "in the foxhole" actually relates to an abstraction, and the intuitive sense that God is a Father who hears us points back to the family of God (see [[The Intuitive Personal God Points Back to the Family of Heaven]]).

The bulk of the episode is exegetical. Drawing on the **Ugaritic** texts (the El–Asherah–Baal divine family; see [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]]) and on [[Frank Moore Cross]]'s *Canaanite Myth and Hebrew Epic*, Butler reconstructs the earliest Israelite picture: **El** the father, **Asherah** the mother, a **bull**-imaged son, and unnamed young gods (the *elim*/striplings and *almot*/virgins) as the heavenly court. He develops the W. Robertson Smith / Cross anthropology of God as **divine kinsman** — the *goel*/redeemer, the senior man of the tribe whom Israel is told to *cleave* to as a bride ([[God Is the Divine Kinsman and Goel-Redeemer]]). The centerpiece is **Genesis 49:25**, which Butler reads as naming **three distinct divine beings** in Jacob's blessing of Joseph — the Bull of Jacob (Yahweh), El, and Shaddai ([[Genesis 49 Names Three Distinct Gods in Jacobs Blessing]]). He argues **Shaddai** is the "**breasted God**," a feminine divinity equated by [[Mark Smith]] with Asherah via the shared title "breasts and womb" ([[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]]), and that **Abir** ("Bull of Jacob") plus the Kuntillet Ajrud iconography show Yahweh was anciently depicted as a **bull** ([[The Bull of Jacob Shows Yahweh Was Depicted as a Bull]]).

Butler closes by tracing the **collapse**: pre-exilic and exilic editors smooshed El, Shaddai, El Berith and the rest into a single, bodiless, unseeable Yahweh whose "name" (*shem*) replaces his person — and the divine family was either absorbed or recast as idolatry. This is the same Deuteronomistic move covered in [[Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament]] and [[The God of Israel Was Originally El Not Yahweh]]. The payoff for him is devotional: a family-centered church *is* a Christ-centered church, because the Son stands at the center of the divine family from which humans came and to which they may return exalted.
