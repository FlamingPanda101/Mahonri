# The Stick of Joseph - Joseph Smith Got Creation Right

Guest [[Brian Thomassen]], in conversation with hosts including [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]], argues that creation *ex nihilo* is not a reading of the biblical text but a second-century Christian invention — and that the Restoration's "organize from pre-existing matter" model is what the Hebrews, Egyptians, Greeks, and earliest Christians actually believed. His distinctive move is to **exonerate the Greeks**: contrary to the standard Latter-day Saint "creation-from-nothing is Hellenization" story, he contends *ex nihilo* is *antithetical* to Greek philosophy and was manufactured *within* the Christian (specifically Gnostic-adjacent) space to maximize God's superiority over his creation.

## Summary
- **Framing the debate.** Latter-day Saints usually meet the *ex nihilo* model by appealing to Genesis 1:1–2 for a *creatio ex materia* reading, locking onto the verb *bara* and Joseph Smith's King Follett teaching on it. Thomassen affirms this but says the knee-jerk Genesis appeal is only the start. (On the Hebrew itself — *bara* as "organize," no article on *bereshit*, *tohu wa-bohu* as pre-existent chaos — see the existing [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]] and [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]].)[^1][^2]
- **The "strongest" proof-text fails.** The opponent's best verse is [[2 Maccabees 7.28|2 Maccabees 7:28]] ("God did not make them out of things that existed") — canonical in the Septuagint, Vulgate, Luther, and the original King James before 19th-century Protestants cut the Apocrypha. Thomassen defuses it with a Greek parallel: [[Xenophon]] (5th c. BC, predating Maccabees) wrote that parents "bring forth their children out of nothing" — obviously not an ontological claim, just "a thing is now here that wasn't before." The "out of nothing" idiom was never a metaphysical statement. The weaker proof-texts (e.g. [[Hebrews 11.3|Hebrews 11:3]]) only equivocate on "create": invisible ≠ non-existent. (The vault already documents this idiom and the 2 Maccabees consensus in depth — see [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]].)[^3][^4][^5]
- **No "nothing" anywhere in the ancient world.** Every theogony — Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, Hittite, Egyptian — starts from a primordial *something* (chaos, water, darkness) that is organized; "chaos is never created." Hebrew has no word for pure absence: *ʾein* ("there is not") denies availability, *ʾefes* denies significance, *tohu* denotes formlessness/purposelessness (a desert, cf. Jeremiah 4:23), none of them an absolute void. Hesiod's *Theogony* opens "at the first chaos came to be" — chaos precedes even the gods.[^6][^7][^8]
- **The inversion: the Greeks are not the culprits.** Logic itself ([[Parmenides]]) begins with *ex nihilo nihil fit* — "nothing comes from nothing." Within the Greek model creation has exactly two possibilities, **organization** (a ship from wood) or **emanation** (a web from a spider) — and *neither* is creation from nothing. *Ex nihilo* "is antithetical to the Greek model." So the usual LDS claim that it came from Greek philosophy is wrong; it had to come from somewhere else. See [[Ex Nihilo Is a Christian Invention Not Greek Hellenization]].[^9][^10]
- **Why irrationality couldn't take root (the Buddhism analogy).** Thomassen uses the Buddhist *tetralemma* (which violates the law of the excluded middle, e.g. "X both is and is not") to illustrate that genuinely irrational claims could not gain a foothold in the rational Greek West — which is why Buddhism, for all its ferocious missionary work eastward, never penetrated Greece. *Ex nihilo*, equally irrational by the Parmenidean standard, is the one exception that *did* take hold — but only inside the Christian space, not the Greek philosophical one. See [[The Tetralemma Test - Ex Nihilo Could Not Take Root in the Rational Greek West]].[^11][^12]
- **Emanation is already in the Hebrew Bible.** Reading Genesis 14:19, *El Elyon* is "possessor [or maker] of heaven and earth." The root behind "possessor" is *qanah* (to buy/own); the root behind "maker" means *to beget*. So one strand of the Old Testament has God *begetting* heaven and earth — the *emanation* model (world as God's child), mirroring the second Greek option and other ANE systems. See [[El Elyon Begat Heaven and Earth - Emanation in the Hebrew Bible]] and [[Genesis 14.19|Genesis 14:19]].[^13]
- **Where it actually came from: a Christian over-correction.** The first crisis was the Judaizers (Acts / Council of Jerusalem, ~50 AD); the second-century crisis was **Gnosticism**. Early Christianity had genuine secret/temple teaching (Thomassen cites [[Clement of Alexandria]]: "what is hidden to the many shall appear manifest to the few"), which gave Gnostic claimants room. [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] beat the Gnostics by declaring *all* esoteric teaching heresy — stripping out the temple-creation material (Genesis 1 as a temple text, per [[John Walton]]) in the process. Three figures from the Antioch area — the Gnostic [[Basilides]], then [[Tatian]] and [[Theophilus of Antioch]] — then argued God must be *superior* to his creation and maximally free, so any co-eternal matter or required "design" had to be denied. The result: *ex nihilo*, a "most literal" reading of Genesis 1 invented to make the best possible super-God, which hardened into creedal orthodoxy within decades (so that by [[Tertullian]]'s time it is simply assumed) and "becomes the model" at the root of the Trinity. (The late-2nd-century origin and anti-Gnostic motive are already covered in [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]]; what is new here is the "Greeks are innocent / Gnostic over-correction" framing.)[^14][^15][^16][^17][^18]
- **The payoff.** The restored gospel comfortably returns to what "a Greek, or a Hebrew, or an Egyptian" would have found obvious — there was always a *something*. The hosts also gesture (without resolving) at the infinite-regress debate, with Thomassen siding with [[Blake Ostler]] that the [[King Follett Discourse]] does *not* actually teach an infinite regress and affirms a God of all gods. (See [[The Infinite Regress of Infallible Interpreters]] for the unrelated epistemic use of that phrase; on the cosmology see [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer]].)[^19][^20]
