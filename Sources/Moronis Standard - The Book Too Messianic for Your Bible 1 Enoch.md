# The Book Too Messianic for Your Bible: 1 Enoch

[[Alex Arnold]] of *Moroni's Standard* argues that [[1 Enoch]] — the Second Temple apocalypse quoted as prophecy in [[Jude 1.14-15|Jude 1:14–15]], copied in 11 manuscripts at Qumran, defended by the architects of Christian orthodoxy, and preserved in full by the Ethiopian Orthodox Church — was excluded from the Western canon precisely because it was "too Messianic." Its Book of Parables contains the most explicit **pre-existent, divinely enthroned Son of Man** in any pre-Christian text, and Arnold contends this portrait both explains the blasphemy charge at Jesus's trial and matches LDS Christology better than the Nicene formula. He leans deliberately on secular and non-LDS scholars ("if you don't like the archaeology or the history, argue with the scholars, not me").[^1]

## Summary

- **The Son of Man is borrowed, not coined.** Jesus's most frequent self-title (~80×, more than "Christ" or "Son of God") originates in the Enochic/Danielic tradition, not with Jesus himself.[^2] In the Parables (1 Enoch 37–71) the Son of Man is a heavenly being seen beside the "Head of Days," **named before creation**, seated on God's throne, called "the Anointed," and the cosmic judge before whom kings are destroyed.[^3] This builds directly on [[Daniel 7 Son of Man Is Enthroned by the Ancient of Days|Daniel 7]]. See [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]].
- **The trial only makes sense with this background.** Before the Sanhedrin, Jesus identifies himself as "the Son of Man... coming in the clouds of heaven," and the high priest tears his robes for blasphemy. Arnold's load-bearing question: if "son of man" meant only a generic human, the reaction is wild overreaction — so the high priest must have heard the *Enochic* claim to be the enthroned divine judge.[^4] See [[The Sanhedrin Blasphemy Charge Presupposes the Enochic Son of Man]].
- **Its credentials are stronger than its exclusion implies.** Quoted as prophecy by Jude;[^2] 11 Qumran manuscripts (vs. 8 each of Genesis and Ezekiel);[^5] defended for the canon by [[Tertullian]], used by [[Origen]] and [[Irenaeus of Lyons]]; retained to this day in the Ethiopian 81-book canon. Its exclusion was "a Western institutional decision," not a consensus of Christianity. See [[Book of Enoch - Exclusion from the Canon]] and [[Book of Enoch - Dead Sea Scrolls Attestation]].
- **The "late forgery" objection fails.** The Parables alone are missing from Qumran, and J.T. Milik ([[Joseph Milik]]) argued for late, possibly Christian, composition. But the field has moved on — [[George Nickelsburg]], John J. Collins, Gabriele Boccaccini, and [[James VanderKam]] favor pre-Christian composition — and the Parables contain **no Christian content** (no Jesus, cross, resurrection, or atonement). A Christian forger "forgot to include Christianity."[^6] See [[The Parables of Enoch Are Pre-Christian Not a Post-Jesus Forgery]].
- **The LDS payoff.** [[Moses 6]]–[[Moses 7]] (Pearl of Great Price, 1830) is the most extensive Enoch material outside 1 Enoch — Son of Man, the translated city of Zion, and the **weeping God**. Richard Laurence's 1821 English translation was too obscure, and the scholarship to synthesize these parallels is 20th-century, so Arnold argues Joseph Smith could not have assembled this from his environment.[^7] The Enochic Son of Man — pre-existent, distinct in person from the Father, sharing divine authority by appointment — maps onto LDS Christology, not Nicaea's *homoousios*.[^8] See [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]] and [[The Enochic Son of Man Matches LDS Christology Not Nicene Homoousios]].
