# Dan McClellan - Engaging Gavin Ortlund on Monotheism

**Full title:** "Engaging Gavin Ortlund on monotheism"
**Length:** ~56:00
**Format:** Point-by-point video response to Gavin Ortlund's 21-minute video, which itself engaged two of McClellan's videos arguing for the absence of monotheism in the Bible.

## Summary

- **The core disagreement is definitional and methodological.** Ortlund proposes that biblical "other gods" reduce to either (1) non-existent foreign idols or (2) spiritual beings Christians call angels and demons, so the Bible "amounts to" monotheism. McClellan argues this reduction is "reductive and simplistic," presupposes univocality (a single unchanging concept of deity), and flattens the **synchronic change** in the conception of deity across the Hebrew Bible's ~1,000-year span. [^1] See [[Synchronic Change in the Conception of Deity]].
- **"Monotheism" is a loaded modern category, not a biblical one.** McClellan traces the word to the Cambridge Platonist Henry More in the 17th century, where it functioned as an **identity marker and value judgment** distinguishing "good" religion from "bad," never simply counting gods. Defining it as "belief in one God" is, he says, an etymological fallacy; scholars now revise the definition precisely so they can locate monotheism in the Bible and preserve ideological continuity with their own communities. [^2] See [[Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category]].
- **The "rhetoric of incomparability" applied to Yahweh is not unique** and does not signal Heiser's "species uniqueness." McClellan reads at length from the Great Cairo Hymn of Praise to Amun-Ra (c. 1400 BCE) — which calls Amun the sole creator who made all gods, before whom all gods bow — and notes nobody treats that as monotheism. To prove the biblical case one must show the *same* rhetoric is qualitatively distinct in Israel, which he says cannot be done. [^3] See [[Rhetoric of Incomparability Is Not Monotheism]].
- **Two distinct rhetorical layers must not be conflated.** (a) *Idol polemic* (2 Kings 19:18; Psalm 115; Isaiah 41:24) denies real deity to the **idol** — the man-made material medium that indexes a deity — not to the deity it represents; Paul says the very same thing in 1 Corinthians 8–10. (b) *Negation rhetoric* ("I am, and there is no other"; "none besides me") asserts an **exclusive relationship and incomparable supremacy within Yahweh's purview**, not the non-existence of other gods. [^4] See [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]], [[Impotence Rhetoric Is Idol Polemic Not Ontological Denial]], [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]].
- **Territory, not existence, is the question** in the Yahweh-vs-Baal narratives. The Elijah contest (1 Kings 18) and the people's cry *Adonai hu ha-Elohim* ("Yahweh, he is THE god") ask which storm deity holds purview over this land — Yahweh and Baal share the storm-deity profile (cf. Psalm 29, "Rider of the clouds"). Conversely, in 2 Kings 3 Yahweh promises total victory but the campaign fails the instant the Moabite king sacrifices his son to Chemosh — Yahweh loses "home-court advantage" outside his inherited purview. [^5] See [[Elijah Contest Is About Territorial Storm-Deity Purview]], [[Chemosh Defeats Yahweh Outside His Purview]].
- **The angel/demon reduction is anachronistic.** Squishing the rest of the Israelite pantheon into angels/demons is a post-exilic, Persian-to-Hellenistic development; appealing to the Septuagint's rendering of *elohim* as *angeloi* (c. 3rd century BCE) says nothing about how the word was used in the preceding 700 years. *Shedim* (Deut 32:17) are a class of foreign deity, not "demons" — that category is a Greco-Roman-period theological innovation. [^6] See [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]], [[Shedim Are Foreign Deities Not Demons]].
- **The Shema and 1 Corinthians 8 confirm exclusive relationship, not sole existence.** *Adonai eloheinu* ("Yahweh OUR god") presupposes other gods; the grammar of *echad* ("one") parallels Song of Songs 6:9, where the beloved is "one" among acknowledged others — meaning "the only one *for me*." Paul's "for us there is one God... from whom are all things" (1 Cor 8:6) sits beside his explicit admission that "there are many gods and many lords." [^7] See [[The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence]], [[1 Corinthians 8 Paul Affirms Many Gods Exist]].
