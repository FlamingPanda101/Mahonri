# The Counsel of Trent — Is Mormonism Demonic?

The Council-of-Trent posting of a friendly recorded dialogue between Catholic apologist [[Trent Horn]] and LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]], occasioned by Carroll's video naming the "Mormonism could be demonic" charge the *strongest* argument against the faith. The two probe whether it is ever legitimate — epistemically or ethically — to call a rival religion demonically inspired, touching the [[Galatians 1-8 - Angel Preaching Another Gospel]] frame, Aquinas on the limits of demonic power, the reversibility of Horn's debate syllogism against [[Jacob Hansen]], and (uniquely on the Catholic side) Horn's positive case from Eucharistic and Marian miracles and his "infallible at calculus" model of papal infallibility. This is the *same conversation* analyzed from the LDS side in [[Lets Talk w Hayden Carroll - Trent Horn Claims Mormonism Could Be Demonic]]; the four core concept notes below were drawn there and are wikilinked rather than duplicated.

## Summary
- **Driscoll vs. Horn on a spectrum.** Carroll separates [[Mark Driscoll]] — who *declares* Joseph Smith was visited by a demon as fact, and whom Carroll accuses of begging the question — from Horn, who keeps the demonic merely *on the table* as one explanation among natural ones. Carroll's worry is the rhetorical power to "keep people away" plus the moral hazard of inflammatory-and-unfalsifiable labels, tied to the Michigan shooter who thought Latter-day Saints were the antichrist; Horn replies that a *true-but-inflammatory* claim (e.g. "abortion is homicide") carries no such culpability.[^1][^2]
- **"When Catholics Argue Like Jews."** Carroll's central charge: deploying "it could be demonic" as a public apologetic reenacts the Pharisees, who granted Jesus' miracles were real and reattributed them to the devil ([[Matthew 12.24-26]]). He calls it a "backup plan" / "get-out-of-jail-free card" that only works by presupposing one's own worldview and that everyone could equally fire. See [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable]].[^3][^4]
- **Horn's falsifiability defense from Aquinas.** Horn answers that the demonic explanation *is* falsifiable because demonic power is bounded: following [[Thomas Aquinas]], the devil is a creature and cannot raise the dead (God alone is author of life), so a demonic story is ruled out for the Resurrection though live elsewhere. He adds the wonder/miracle distinction. See [[Not Even the Devil Can Raise the Dead - The Aquinas Falsifiability Defense]].[^5]
- **"Magic trick, not miracle."** Horn places the Book of Mormon on the natural side of the line: its production asks "how did you do it?" (a hidden method) rather than presenting a suspended law of nature like the Resurrection, and he refuses the gaps-inference "inexplicable, therefore inspired." Carroll counters with the witness-count and conspiracy-cost. See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].[^6][^7]
- **Argument strength and the reversible syllogism.** Both agree the demonic line is "strongest" because it *grants the most premises* (Horn's abortion analogy). Carroll shows Horn's "If Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is false" syllogism is **reversible** and rests on an *unproven* premise — "damage control," not demonstration. See [[The Strongest Argument Grants the Most Premises]].[^8][^9]
- **Horn's positive case — message-bearing Catholic miracles (NEW).** Horn flips the script: the demonic charge is actually "the strongest argument *against Catholicism*," because Eucharistic flesh, incorrupt hosts, the Immaculate Conception apparitions, Zeitoun, and the Miracle of the Sun at Fatima are *message-bearing* miracles distinctively endorsing Catholic theology. He distinguishes them from generic *faith-healings* (citing [[Craig Keener]]'s research) that God may work through anyone, Mormon or Protestant. This creates a trilemma especially for the "pleasant Protestant." See [[Message-Bearing Miracles Are a Trilemma for the Pleasant Protestant]]. Carroll admits he has "no great answer" to these.[^10][^11]
- **Authority, infallibility, and the Great Apostasy.** Horn grounds a text's inspiration in the *authority of Christ's Church*, not its inexplicability; Carroll counters that the debate never *proved* Catholicism's authority. Carroll states the LDS position that no human source — including the biblical text — is *infallible*, since fallible humans cannot author infallible things unless God overrides their free will. Horn's reply is the **"infallible at calculus"** analogy: infallibility is a *negative protection* (it only prevents error, the Pope could score zero by declining every question), so it needs no puppetry of the will. See [[Papal Infallibility Is a Negative Protection Not a Guarantee of Output]]. Both concede the real question is whether Christ's authority left the earth after the apostles, pointing to the forthcoming [[Capturing Christianity - Debate The Great Apostasy Joe Heschmeyer vs Jacob Hansen|Heschmeyer–Hansen great-apostasy debate]].[^12][^13]
- **Tone and Horn's closing.** Strikingly irenic — Carroll has a Catholic fiancée and Catholic family and praises Horn's *sola scriptura* and abortion apologetics. Asked whether the conversation changed him, Horn says it has *not* dislodged the demonic explanation, but he would add caveats so the label never implies LDS individuals are themselves malicious — and reaffirms that what makes Mormonism a candidate is that it advertises a founding *otherworldly encounter* as a virtue.[^14][^15]
