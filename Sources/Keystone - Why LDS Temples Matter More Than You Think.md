# Keystone — "This Is Why LDS Temples Matter More Than You Think"

A [[Keystone]] monologue reframing Latter-day Saint temples — often dismissed as "expensive buildings where Mormons do secret culty rituals" — as an institution that addresses one of society's deepest problems at its *root*. Using the analogy of his own pneumonia (cough medicine treated symptoms; only treating the pneumonia cured the cough), Keystone argues that poverty, addiction, crime, and self-harm are downstream "symptoms" whose underlying "sickness" is the breakdown of marriage and the family — and that the temple's sealing ordinance, by building strong eternal families, treats the disease rather than the symptoms.[^1]

## Summary
- **The root-vs-symptom diagnosis.** Social pathologies should be treated however we can, but the deeper question is the *underlying sickness*. Non-Latter-day Saint professor Nathan Schluter (in *Crisis* magazine): "the single greatest contributor to our social pathologies such as addiction, mental illness, suicide, and violent crime is the breakdown of marriage and the family." Children of family disruption are at higher risk for violent crime, dropping out, abuse, self-harm, depression, drug abuse, jail, teen pregnancy, poverty, and homelessness.[^2] See [[Temples Treat Social Pathology at the Root Not the Leaves]].
- **Temples target the root.** Of the temple's ordinances, celestial (eternal) marriage is "the capstone of the holy endowment" ([[James E. Faust]]) and "the crowning ordinance of the gospel" ([[Bruce R. McConkie]]); the family unit is "the most important organization in time or in eternity." [[Russell M. Nelson]]: "salvation is an individual matter; exaltation is a family matter." F. Burton Howard's silverware analogy: "if you want something to last forever, you treat it differently."[^3]
- **The data tracks the doctrine.** Getting married in the temple does not guarantee success, but "generally speaking, the statistics... reflect the doctrine": in the U.S. Latter-day Saints are more likely to marry, stay married, and report being happier. A Pew survey: 34% of the general public vs **73% of Latter-day Saints** rank a successful marriage among the most important life goals; 50% vs **81%** for being a good parent.[^4] See [[Latter-day Saints Disproportionately Prioritize Marriage and Parenthood]].
- **The chess-vs-checkers payoff.** Critics who say temple money "would be better spent on social issues" miss that "temples do address social issues — they're just doing it at the root instead of the leaves." It is "a this *and* that situation": feed the hungry *and* battle the underlying family breakdown, "potentially generationally."[^5]
