# Hello Saints — Pastor's HONEST Questions About History of Joseph Smith

An irenic interview in which Jeff, an Evangelical pastor in Utah ([[Hello Saints]]), reads through the 1838 Joseph Smith—History in the Pearl of Great Price with BYU scholar [[Kerry Muhlestein]]. They cover the historical context (the 1838 persecution that prompted the account), the family religious tension that framed the young Joseph's search, the multiple First Vision accounts and how they harmonize, and — at length — the verse calling the creeds an "abomination," which the pastor (as creedal Christian) is personally implicated by. The conversation models charitable interfaith engagement and closes with Muhlestein's testimony.

## Summary

- **The 1838 account was written under severe persecution.** Muhlestein situates the canonized history in 1838 Kirtland: bank failures, mass apostasy, former intimates "printing stories... that are demonstrably false" and swearing affidavits. Joseph writes "to disabuse the public mind and put all inquiries after the truth in possession of the facts" — an official, outward-facing history. See [[The 1838 First Vision Account Was Written to Correct Public Falsehoods]]. [^1]
- **The Smith family was religiously divided.** [[Lucy Mack Smith]] leaned Presbyterian; [[Joseph Smith Sr]] had universalist leanings and stayed aloof from churches (his own father had a dream that a descendant would help restore New Testament Christianity). Per [[Steve Harper]] (via Muhlestein): for the mother "it's better to go to any church than no church," for the father "better to go to no church than the wrong church" — and young Joseph was "caught between," unable to get answers from family or ministers. See [[The Smith Family Religious Division Framed the First Vision]]. [^2]
- **There are four firsthand and several secondhand First Vision accounts.** Muhlestein (a specialist who wrote a book harmonizing them) lists the four distinct firsthand accounts: 1832 (Joseph's own hand), 1835 (a scribe recording him telling a visitor), 1838 (the canonized history), and 1842 (the Wentworth letter). The "four to nine versions" figure comes from adding secondhand printed accounts. See [[The Four Firsthand First Vision Accounts]]. [^3]
- **The accounts differ by audience and harmonize easily.** They "come together wonderfully"; the only hard part was *sequence*, not contradiction — and the Satan-attack portion was hard precisely because "the essence of that experience was confusion and fear." Differences track audience and intent: a personal history vs. the outward-facing Wentworth letter (which omits "abomination" — "probably a good PR move"). "We don't really get contradictions; as we put them together we get a fuller and fuller picture" — just as anyone would tell a story differently six years apart. See [[First Vision Accounts Differ by Audience Not Contradiction]] and the existing [[First Vision Accounts Are No More Contradictory Than the Gospels]]. [^4]
- **Two personages — a non-Trinitarian "strike one."** The pastor names the Protestant difficulty plainly: the First Vision presents two distinct personages (non-Trinitarian), calls the creeds "abominable," and has Joseph take on the mantle of "the one" — "strike one, strike two, strike three." Muhlestein agrees the nature of Christ and the Trinity is "as fundamental as it gets," while maintaining "for the most part we're worshiping the same Christ." See the existing [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]] and [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]]. [^5]
- **The "abomination" targets creeds, not Christians.** Muhlestein's core apologetic on [[Joseph Smith History 1.19|JSH 1:19]]: (a) the Lord uses equally strong language *inward* — calling Joseph and [[Martin Harris]] "wicked" yet inviting repentance — so the harshness is not anti-Christian animus; (b) his "best guess" is that the abomination is "teachings about me and my nature... that are not accurate," i.e. the *creeds*' mischaracterization of the Father–Son relationship (the theme of John's Gospel), not individual believers. Joseph Smith defended other Christians' rights and the good in them (President Hinckley: "bring all the good you have and see if we can add to it"). See [[The Creeds Abomination Targets Teachings Not Christians]]. [^6]
- **A two-way history of overcorrection.** Muhlestein concedes Latter-day Saints have at times circled the wagons and seen too little good in others; both groups have reacted in un-Christlike ways (though nothing justifies the violence in Kirtland, Missouri, Utah). He notes the irony that the church "excused itself from the lunch table" at the First Vision and now wants "a seat at the table" — both distinct *and* part of the group. See [[Both Sides Overcorrected in the LDS-Evangelical Divide]]. [^7]
- **The sting of being called "not Christian."** Muhlestein says the hardest thing is to be considered non-Christian "when... we are as Christian as Christian can be"; the seat at the table he wants is Catholics, Orthodox, Protestants, and Latter-day Saints united in believing in Christ despite differing views of what that means. The pastor agrees evangelicals should not say "you believe in a different Jesus" without first understanding what Latter-day Saints testify and *experiencing* their testimony. See the existing [[Same Reference Different Beliefs - You Worship the Same Jesus]] and [[Define Christian First and the Concession Follows]]. [^8]
- **Closing — a testimony of transformation.** Asked why he is a Christian, Muhlestein answers experientially: physical and spiritual healing, forgiveness, and change "from the outside in a way that can only be Jesus Christ" — felt in his home, at church, at the Garden Tomb, contemplating his parents' deaths. "I know it's true... and I can never forget it or deny it." (A teaser flags a future discussion of the seer-stone translation method.) [^9]
