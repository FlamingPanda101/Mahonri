# Ward Radio - The Real Reason Why People Leave the Faith

**Channel:** [[Ward Radio]] | **Host:** [[Cardon Ellis]], with Brad Whitbeck and panel | **Guest:** [[Jeff Strong]], author of *Torn: Why People We Love Are Leaving the Church and What We Can Learn From Them*

The Ward Radio panel interviews [[Jeff Strong]] about the ~15,000-respondent disaffiliation study behind his book *Torn*. Strong presents four cited reasons people leave and his thesis that *culture* — modeled as the "soil" a faith grows in — crashes over the top of all four, while the hosts press him with a sharper version of the same claim: that the lived "church experience" bucket is really the dominant driver and "church history" is usually the soundbite adopted afterward.

## Summary

- Strong's study finds that **40% of once-active Latter-day Saints have stopped participating in the last ~25 years**, a figure he says triangulates with the Harvard CES study and the Pew Religious Landscape Study (both "a little higher"). He is explicit that this was *not* a probability sample and makes no claim of representativeness or cause-and-effect — "you don't need 98% confidence intervals to know a forest fire's hot enough to kill you."[^1][^2] See [[The Four Cited Reasons People Leave Are Crowned by Culture]] and [[Church Is Hemorrhaging Members at a Unique Rate]].
- The **four primary cited reasons** are: depleted/"lifestyle isn't working" (**6%**), church history (**42%**), social disagreement with church positions (sexual identity, women's voice, financial transparency), and church experience (**18%**). Strong's central argument is that "culture crashes over the top of those four... like jaws, making them worse."[^3][^4] See [[The Four Cited Reasons People Leave Are Crowned by Culture]] and the related [[The Four Groups of LDS Disaffiliates]].
- Strong's organizing metaphor is **culture as soil** (Latin *colere*, "to cultivate the dirt"): healthy soil must be *firm enough* to protect and stabilize roots **and** *nourishing enough* to feed growth. Too much protection and the plant stays put but starves; too much nourishment and no stability and the wind blows it over. He names four cultural imbalances — acceptance vs. standards, sanctuary vs. transcendence, agency vs. conformity, and conformity vs. harmony.[^5][^6][^7] See [[Culture Is Soil - Protection and Nourishment Must Be Balanced]].
- The panel pushes the thesis harder than Strong does: host [[Cardon Ellis]] argues bucket four "really is like 81%," that a member with a good cultural experience would "not even start paying attention to these asinine out-of-context recycled tropes, especially the historical ones," and that "church history" is "the soundbite you adopted afterwards because you had a bad experience." Strong agrees in substance while warning the same logic can become an excuse and respecting those who leave over sincere belief.[^8][^9] See [[Church History Is Often the Soundbite Adopted After a Bad Cultural Experience]].
- On the fix, both sides converge on **"crop-dusting vs. gardening"**: top-down, centrally-planned programs and handbooks ("crop dusting") are good and necessary but cannot meet every individual's needs; retention requires local "gardening" — ministering, individualized attention, a friend and a calling. Cardon proposes radical decentralization (returning PR budgets and decision-making to stake presidents); Strong frames it as members at every level cultivating the soil in "your heart, your relationships, your family, your friends."[^10][^11][^12] See [[Decentralize - Crop-Dusting vs Gardening in Member Retention]].
- A recurring honest tension surfaces (raised most sharply by Brad Whitbeck): praising people for "breaking the mold" or telling a doubter "you are perfect the way you are" risks *reinforcing* disaffiliation, and a survey's stated reason ("I don't know, ask a girl why she wants to break up with you — she'll never be honest") may not be the real one. Strong concedes the intangibles "dictate more than we'd like to admit," which is precisely why his macro data cannot diagnose any individual. See [[Big Data Cannot Diagnose the Individual]].
