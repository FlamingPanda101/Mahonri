# Dan McClellan — Satan isn't in the Old Testament

[[Dan McClellan]] argues that "Satan" as a named divine character — a cosmic rebel opposed to God — does not appear anywhere in the Old Testament, because the character had not yet been invented when those texts were written. The figure is a development of Greco-Roman-period Judaism (Enochic literature) firmed up in the New Testament.

- The generic Hebrew noun *satan* means adversary/accuser and is mostly used of humans; it can also denote a divine figure — e.g., the angel of the Lord is "a satan" against Balaam in [[Numbers 22.22|Numbers 22:22]].[^1] See [[Satan in the Hebrew Bible Is a Generic Noun for an Adversary]].
- The definite form *ha-satan* ("the accuser") occurs 14× in [[Job 1]] and 3× in [[Zechariah 3]] — a divine-council prosecutor who works *for* God, not a being named Satan.[^2] See [[Ha-Satan Is a Council Office Not a Personal Name]].
- The "Satan" texts of later Christian reading are misidentifications: [[Genesis 3.14-19|Genesis 3]] is just a snake (Aesop-style etiology); [[Isaiah 14.12-15|Isaiah 14]] taunts the king of Babylon; Ezekiel 28 (see [[Ezekiel 28]]) is about the king of Tyre as the prototypical human. None is identified with Satan until the turn of the era — in McClellan's view, an early *Christian* innovation post-dating the NT.[^3] See [[Genesis 3 Isaiah 14 and Ezekiel 28 Are Not About Satan]].
- The one possible exception scholars debate: [[1 Chronicles 21.1|1 Chronicles 21:1]] ("Satan incited David to number Israel"), rewriting [[2 Samuel 24.1|2 Samuel 24:1]] (where *Yahweh* incites David). McClellan reads the Chronicler as offloading responsibility from God with a generic, un-named "accuser"; but [[Esther Hamori]]'s *God's Monsters* treats it as the first occurrence of Satan as a personal name.[^4] See [[1 Chronicles 21 Is the Only Possible Old Testament Satan]].
