# Keystone — "What Johnny Harris Gets Wrong About Mormons"

A [[Keystone]] response to [[Johnny Harris]]'s "third and final" video on the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Keystone's lead critique is about *framing*, not facts — the "enlightened ex-member" posture that treats believers as a spectacle — and grants Harris generally gets his facts right, before isolating his recurring **"control"** thesis, his treatment of the temple-recommend interview and youth standards, his telling of the Church's institutionalization, and one "egregious error" (the [[B.H. Roberts]] quotation). (This is a *separate* episode from Keystone's earlier response to Harris's previous video, which is cataloged at [[Keystone - Everything Johnny Harris Gets Wrong About Mormons]].)[^1]

## Summary
- **The "enlightened ex-member" frame.** Harris's recurring posture — "I used to believe this, but now that I've left I am enlightened" — "indirectly treats believing Latter-day Saints a bit like zoo animals, a spectacle to be observed rather than real people to be understood," even as Harris asks to be understood. Keystone concedes it is "a two-way street" that believers fall into too.[^2] See [[The Enlightened Ex-Member Frame Treats Believers as Zoo Animals]].
- **The "control" thesis answered by parent-child analogy.** Harris reduces the Church to "control... by a group of men in Salt Lake City." Keystone likens this to the ancient critic Korihor and to "the age-old challenge between parents and children": rules can be protective or feel arbitrary depending on the child, and "is every rule the parents give going to be ideal? Probably not."[^3]
- **Principle-based youth standards replaced rule-based ones.** Harris mocks the old *For the Strength of Youth* booklet's specific dress rules; Keystone agrees some were dated and notes the *current* booklet is deliberately "principle-based" (e.g., 16 is "a good guideline... counsel with your parents") — evidence of "a church that is willing to examine itself and improve."[^4] See [[Principle-Based Youth Standards Replaced Rule-Based Ones]].
- **Institutionalization is normal, per [[Max Weber]].** Harris frames the "corporationifying" of the Church (the J. Reuben Clark moment, correlation) as sinister control. Keystone invokes [[Max Weber]]: movements must "institutionalize" to survive the death of their charismatic founder — "what was once revolutionary becomes orthodox" — citing the early Christian church after Jesus and the United States after Washington.[^5] See [[Movements Must Institutionalize to Survive Their Charismatic Founder]].
- **The temple-recommend interview is a reasonable standard.** Harris frames the 15 recommend questions as coerced "loyalty" affirmation. Keystone replies it is "not weird for there to be a standard" to enter the temple — ancient Israelite temple entry also had righteousness requirements — and that one should not covenant to a law (chastity, consecration) one rejects.[^6] See [[The Temple Recommend Interview Is a Reasonable Entry Standard Not Sinister Control]].
- **The keystone and the [[B.H. Roberts]] error.** Harris rightly calls the Book of Mormon the "keystone" but "never actually undermines it" — never explaining where it came from if Joseph invented it, or what the witnesses saw. His "most egregious error" is quoting [[B.H. Roberts]] as having "sorrowfully concluded" Joseph created the book, when Roberts was playing devil's advocate; his letter to [[Heber J. Grant]] affirms "our faith is not only unshaken but unshakable."[^7] See [[The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument]] and [[B.H. Roberts Was Playing Devils Advocate Not Losing Faith]].
- **A source-labeling double standard.** When Harris cites a Keystone video he warns it is "notably from an apologist" though "well sourced," but when he cites critic John Dehlin there is "no warning that the information is notably from a critic."[^8] See [[The Source-Labeling Double Standard Against Apologists]].
