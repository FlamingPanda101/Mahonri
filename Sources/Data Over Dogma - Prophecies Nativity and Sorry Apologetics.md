# Data Over Dogma — Prophecies, Nativity, and Sorry Apologetics

**Episode format:** Two segments — first critiques an Answers in Genesis article by Dr. Tim Chaffy on Jesus fulfilling prophecies; second role-plays apologetic objections to McClellan's claims about nativity contradictions and answers them.

## Summary

- Isaiah 7:14 does not prophesy a virgin birth of the Messiah: the Hebrew _alma_ means "young woman," the prophecy referred to a contemporary pregnancy (~8th century BCE), and Matthew repurposed it via the Septuagint's _parthenos_ rendering to construct a fulfillment narrative.[^1]
- Matthew and Luke's nativity accounts irreconcilably contradict: Matthew has Mary and Joseph living in Bethlehem, fleeing to Egypt, then settling in Nazareth for the first time; Luke has them traveling from Nazareth to Bethlehem for a census, peacefully fulfilling temple requirements, and returning home to Nazareth — with no flight to Egypt or slaughter of innocents.[^2]
- The census under Quirinius (Luke 2:2) creates an insoluble chronological problem: Quirinius governed Syria starting 6 CE, a full decade after Herod the Great's death (~4 BCE), and there is no evidence of an empire-wide Roman census that would have required travel to ancestral hometowns.[^3]
