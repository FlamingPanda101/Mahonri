# FAIR Conference Podcast 83 - Daniel C. Peterson - Brigham Young and Slavery

**Format:** Conference address (FAIR) | **Speaker:** [[Daniel C. Peterson|Dr. Daniel C. Peterson]] (emeritus, Brigham Young University; Interpreter Foundation) | **Topic:** [[Brigham Young]], race, and the Utah 1852 servitude statute

Peterson responds to the charge — which he says he once "accepted uncritically" — that Brigham Young "presided over the passage of a law in 1852 in Utah territory that authorized and legalized slavery," and to the broader claim that Young's racial views were "essentially indistinguishable from those of the Nazis." He argues the 1852 *Act in Relation to Service* was a **gradual-emancipation** statute patterned on free-state law, not a slave code, and that Young opposed chattel slavery "vocally, repeatedly, loudly, and effectively." He leans on two books that "fundamentally transformed" his view: [[Amy Tanner Thiriot]]'s *Slavery and Zion* and [[Paul Reeve]], [[Christopher Rich]], and [[LeGene Kuth]]'s *This Abominable Slavery*.

- Young said cringe-worthy racist things, but so did his contemporaries — Peterson opens by quoting a hard segregationist passage and reveals it is **Abraham Lincoln**, not Young, to show "this is the way people thought" without religious justification.[^1] See [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]].
- 19th-century labor was a spectrum (serfdom, apprenticeship, indentured servitude), not the modern free/slave binary; Young himself had been an apprentice "very like slavery."[^2] See [[Two Distinct Slaveries Debt-Bondage and Chattel]].
- The territory's first response was *neutrality* under a Somerset-style principle: with no positive law authorizing slavery, slavery had no legal force in Utah.[^3]
- Ute slave-traders forced the issue by threatening to kill captive children if the Saints would not buy them; buying created a market for more.[^4] See [[Native American Slavery and the Buy-or-Watch-Them-Die Dilemma]].
- The 1852 *Act in Relation to Service* (Young insisted it not be called slavery) was patterned on the gradual-emancipation laws of Indiana, Illinois, and the Northeast — limiting terms, forbidding family-breakup and sexual abuse, mandating ~18 months of education, allowing servants to testify and sue, barring slave-hunting posses, requiring consent to sell labor, and (Section 3) making **children of enslaved mothers born free**.[^5] See [[Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery]] and [[The 1852 Act Was Patterned on Northern Gradual-Emancipation Law]].
- Case studies of personal decency: Green Flake (bought, freed, given a farm; told to stop "paying off" his wife's former owners); Jane Manning James and her husband Isaac James (the Elizabeth Kane coat/wagon scenes); and Sally, a Native woman in Young's household whom he freed.[^6] See [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]].
- The motive was conflict-avoidance: Young (like Joseph Smith and Lincoln) favored gradual, compensated emancipation, and his fear was vindicated by the ≈620,000-death Civil War nine years later.[^7] See [[Brigham Youngs Slavery Law as a Transition to Freedom]].
