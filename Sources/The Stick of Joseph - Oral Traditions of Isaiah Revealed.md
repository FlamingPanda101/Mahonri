# The Stick of Joseph - Oral Traditions of Isaiah Revealed

[[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] (with guest Brad Whitbeck) review [[John Eaton]]'s *Festal Drama in Deutero-Isaiah*. The book reconstructs a pre-exilic autumn **festal drama** of enthronement and reads Isaiah 40–55 against it; the hosts use Eaton's view that Second Isaiah is an old *oral* tradition to answer the standard objection that Deutero-Isaiah is too late to appear in the Book of Mormon.

## Summary
- **The festal drama.** Eaton reconstructs (in 38 points) a New Year festival in which Yahweh / the Davidic king defeats cosmic chaos (Rahab, Tiamat, Yam), creates the world from the slain enemies, processes into Zion, suffers and descends to Sheol, is redeemed, ritually married to Zion, anointed, and enthroned in the *debir* — bringing the autumn rains and fertility.[^1][^2] See [[The Festal Drama Reconstructs a Pre-Exilic Enthronement Ritual]].
- **The Servant Songs are the king of the festival.** Following Engnell, Eaton reads the Servant Songs (Isa 42, 49, 50, 52–53) as the suffering, dying, redeemed *king* of the festival, not a single historical figure — Psalm 22 as the liturgical backdrop.[^3] See [[The Servant Songs Portray the Suffering King of the Festival]].
- **Second Isaiah as an oral tradition.** "Second Isaiah is not a guy; it's a school of people," prophets attached to the temple who transmitted material orally over several generations before it was textualized around / after Cyrus (539 BC). Jacob in 2 Nephi 6:5 says these are the words Isaiah "spake," consistent with an oral tradition Lehi's circle could carry.[^4][^5] See [[Second Isaiah Was an Oral Tradition of a Prophetic School]].
- **Psalm 110 and Psalm 2 as enthronement texts.** The two most-quoted Psalms in the New Testament fit the festal enthronement: the king seated at Yahweh's right hand, begotten as God's son, a priest after the order of Melchizedek.[^6] See [[Psalm 110 and Psalm 2 Are Enthronement Texts of the Festal Drama]].
- **Abinadi as the last Davidic prince.** Drawing on [[Jonah Barnes]]'s *Key to the Keystone*, the hosts argue [[Abinadi]] — possibly the son of Abinadom, a lost prince of the house of Nephi — quotes Isaiah 53 (a Servant Song) while himself enacting the suffering-king role before King Noah, an unconscious literary feat for a 19th-century dictation.[^7][^8] See [[Abinadi Enacts the Suffering King While Quoting the Servant Song]].
