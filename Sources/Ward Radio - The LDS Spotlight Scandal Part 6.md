# Ward Radio — The LDS Spotlight Scandal? (Part 6)

Part 6 of the Ward Radio *Glenn L. Pace Memo* series (the second episode under the "LDS Spotlight Scandal?" title; see [[Ward Radio - The LDS Spotlight Scandal Part 5]]). Having closed the satanic-ritual-abuse arc, this installment pivots to the *real* abuse case anti-Mormons fold into it: the [[Paul Adams]] / Bisbee, Arizona case, reported by [[Michael Rezendes]] — the same journalist behind the Catholic *Spotlight* exposé. The episode argues the LDS case is nothing like *Spotlight*, explains the church's abuse helpline, and surfaces the [[Timothy Johnson]] reporting-lawsuit that the coverage omits.

## Summary

- **The Spotlight analogy paints a volunteer bishop with a cardinal's brush.** Rezendes's Catholic *Spotlight* story (paid, trained, lifelong-celibate clergy abusing thousands, with administrators reassigning predators to escape prosecution) is structurally unlike the Adams case (an *inactive lay member* abused his own two daughters; *no clergy* abused anyone; the bishop urged reporting and the abuser was excommunicated). The episode's on-screen table lays the two side by side. [^1][^2] See [[The Spotlight Analogy Equates a Celibate Cardinal With a Volunteer Bishop]].
- **The bishop was the one trying to help — then blamed.** Adams confessed a single past incident; the bishop brought in the wife (hoping she would stop it and report), called the abuse helpline, advised the couple to call police (they refused), and kept checking for two years while both parents lied that the abuse had ceased. Rezendes's headline ("Seven years of sex abuse: How Mormon officials let it happen") upgraded one confessed incident to seven years of rape and made it the church's fault. [^3][^4] See [[The Spotlight Analogy Equates a Celibate Cardinal With a Volunteer Bishop]].
- **The reporting Catch-22 — the Johnson case the coverage omits.** One year before the Adams story, the church *was sued for reporting*: in the Oregon [[Timothy Johnson]] case, leaders reported a confessed abuser (who got 15 years), and his wife and children then sued the church for $10M, alleging the report deprived them of his "companionship, society, love, and income." Report and you are sued for destroying a family; don't report and you are sued for not protecting it. This is why the helpline exists. [^5][^6] See [[The Reporting Catch-22 and the Johnson Case]].
- **The abuse helpline routes clergy to legal compliance, not cover-up.** The helpline exists because volunteer, unpaid, untrained lay bishops face state-by-state reporting laws; it complies with each state's law, urges victims and families to self-report and seek counseling, is staffed by abuse survivors and former government abuse investigators and prosecutors, and *directly reports* any child in imminent danger. Rezendes recast it as a tool to "divert abuse allegations away from law enforcement." [^7][^8] See [[The Abuse Helpline Routes Bishops to Legal Compliance Not Cover-Up]].
- **Confessional privilege creates a one-way street for unfalsifiable accusations.** Because clergy swear not to reveal what is said in confession, an accuser stands to gain millions by alleging (e.g. [[John Dehlin]]'s "he asked about my underwear") while the bishop cannot answer — "an excellent, unverifiable, unfalsifiable accusation." The episode also distinguishes the LDS structural safeguards (bishops must be married; women staff half of local leadership; interviews are open to parents; clergy rotate) from the Catholic lifelong-celibate solo priest. [^9][^10] See [[Confessional Privilege Creates a One-Way Street for Accusations]] and [[Abuse Is Less Likely in the Church Not More]].
