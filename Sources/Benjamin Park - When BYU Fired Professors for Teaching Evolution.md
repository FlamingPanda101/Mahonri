# When BYU Fired Professors for Teaching Evolution

Historian [[Benjamin Park]] narrates the **1911 BYU "evolution controversy,"** the first time LDS leaders and BYU administrators moved to root out secular learning they believed undercut scriptural truth. Four professors — the Chamberlain brothers and the Peterson brothers — were found to be teaching organic ([[Charles Darwin|Darwinian]]) evolution and arguing it could be reconciled with the gospel; they were given an ultimatum and ended up fired or resigning. Park sets the episode in its era of post-polygamy assimilation, traces the personnel crisis and the ecclesiastical investigation behind it, and follows the long legacies — most enduringly the **1909 "Origin of Man" First Presidency statement**, still cited today.

- **Core narrative:** A generation of LDS academics with elite degrees ([[John Widtsoe]], [[James Talmage]], the [[Ralph Chamberlain|Chamberlain]] and [[Joseph Peterson|Peterson]] brothers) tried to fuse cutting-edge science with the gospel; church leaders, led by President [[Joseph F. Smith]], feared the assimilation had gone too far and drew a red line at evolution.
- **The crisis:** [[Horace Cummings]]' January 1911 investigative report found professors teaching evolution "as a demonstrated law" and students "wholesale" adopting it and doubting the Bible. A five-apostle committee interrogated the four professors and issued the ultimatum: **stop teaching evolution or lose your jobs.**
- **The legacies:** [[George Brimhall]]'s new slogan — **"the school follows the church"** — fixed BYU's subordination to ecclesiastical authority; the watered-down 1909 statement endured as the most-cited fruit of the debate; and Park argues that, a century on, "the Chamberlains and the Petersons won," since BYU now runs a world-leading evolutionary-biology program and LDS members accept evolution at higher rates than white evangelicals.

## Summary

**Cultural context (assimilation).** The controversy fell just two decades after the Church renounced polygamy and worked, through the Reed Smoot trial era, to assimilate into American society. Latter-day Saints flocked to the best universities for advanced degrees and brought that learning back to Utah — [[James Talmage]] (first Mormon PhD) and [[John Widtsoe]] (whose 1906 *Joseph Smith as Scientist* argued the Prophet's teachings could be fused with cutting-edge science) being emblematic. Park reads Widtsoe's line about the struggle to reconcile science and religion cutting "deep into the soul" as setting the tone. As Brigham Young Academy became Brigham Young University (1903), President [[George Brimhall]] recruited these higher-degree men and gave them freedom to teach. [^1] [^2]

**The four professors.** Two sets of brothers, all eager to teach evolution as reconcilable with LDS belief: [[Ralph Chamberlain]] (PhD biology, Cornell) and [[William Chamberlain]] (MA philosophy, Chicago; further study at Harvard under William James — "probably the first academic theologian/philosopher Mormonism produced"); and [[Joseph Peterson]] (PhD, University of Chicago) and [[Henry Peterson]] (MA psychology, Harvard). [^3]

**The other side.** President [[Joseph F. Smith]], son of Hyrum Smith, held a "paradoxical" posture — driving Americanization yet defending orthodoxy — and worried Darwinian evolution undercut foundational scriptural truths. Park notes the historical irony that most 19th-century Christian teachers accepted Darwin; the backlash came with early-20th-century fundamentalist retrenchment, of which Mormonism was part. After BYU's 1909 Darwin Day centennial unnerved leaders, the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve drafted what became the famous **1909 "Origin of Man" statement** — but, per feedback from [[John Widtsoe]], the early explicit anti-Darwinian denunciations were watered down to "prioritize scriptural truth even if we allow this evolutionary experimentation." (See the dedicated treatment: [[The 1909 Origin of Man Read in Its Historical Context]].) [^4] [^5]

**The investigation and firings.** The 1909 statement did not settle the question. A 1908 Church Education policy already barred non-member sources in Bible teaching; stake presidents complained BYU was "going haywire." Church Education Superintendent [[Horace Cummings]] investigated and filed a report dated **January 21, 1911** (preserved in Richard Sherlock's *Sunstone* essay), finding evolution "treated as a demonstrated law," the Bible "treated as a collection of myths," and — most alarming — students "wholesale" embracing the teachings and doubting scripture. A committee of five apostles plus Cummings and Brimhall held full-day interrogations of the four professors and delivered the ultimatum. Students pushed back: 95 of BYU's 114 students signed a petition calling the firings "a death blow to our college work" (published, after rejections, by the *Salt Lake Tribune*). The Church did not back down. **[[Henry Peterson]]** was fired first; **[[Joseph Peterson]]** and **[[Ralph Chamberlain]]** resigned; **[[William Chamberlain]]** escaped the ultimatum for "complicated reasons" but soon found his ideas unwelcome and left. Brimhall chastised the students in assembly and announced the slogan **"the school follows the church."** (See [[The 1911 BYU Evolution Controversy and the Firing of Four Professors]] and [[The School Follows the Church - Brimhall's Doctrine of Institutional Subordination]].) [^6] [^7] [^8]

**Legacies.** All four landed elsewhere — the Chamberlains and Henry Peterson at Utah State / University of Utah / Penn; Joseph Peterson resigned from the University of Utah in its own 1915 evolution crisis. The Church seemed to recognize it had overreached: [[James Talmage]] and later [[John Widtsoe]] were called to the Quorum of the Twelve, partly to mediate the bad press. The cycle of autonomy-then-retrenchment repeated the next decade. The longest legacy is the 1909 statement — [[Joseph Fielding Smith]] cited it to strike down evolution in *Man, His Origin and Destiny*, yet later BYU framed it to permit evolution's academic study. Park's payoff: BYU now has a world-leading evolutionary-biology program (see [[Evolutionary Biology at BYU Is an Academic Success Story]]) and LDS members — especially BYU-educated ones — accept evolution at higher rates than white evangelicals, so "in the end, the Chamberlains and the Petersons won," even as some leaders still doubt Darwin. Park frames the whole episode as a direct rhyme with recent Provo disputes over secular truth vs. doctrine. [^9] [^10]

**Park's recommended sources:** Richard Sherlock, "Campus in Crisis: BYU 1911" (*Sunstone*, includes the full Cummings study); James McLachlan, "William H. Chamberlain and the Quest for Mormon Theology" (*Dialogue*); Tom Simpson, *American Universities and the Birth of Modern Mormonism* (UNC Press); and BYU's free PDF *The Restored Gospel of Jesus Christ and Evolution* (2025). [^11]
