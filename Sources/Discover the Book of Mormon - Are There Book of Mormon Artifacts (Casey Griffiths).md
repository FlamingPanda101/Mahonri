# Discover the Book of Mormon — Are There Book of Mormon ARTIFACTS? — Casey Griffiths

An interview with LDS Church-history scholar [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] on the *Discover the Book of Mormon* channel, framed around the question of physical evidence: "What Book of Mormon artifacts do we have today?" Griffiths separates the sacred artifacts people *want* (and which are gone) from the documentary artifacts that actually survive, then turns to translation, the charge of racism, and the book's pastoral value today.

## Summary

Griffiths opens by framing the Book of Mormon as "an ancient record... written by early inhabitants of the Americas" and translated by [[Joseph Smith]] — but insists you cannot "adequately explain the Book of Mormon unless we mention angels and divine intervention": you "have to embrace the supernatural just to see the natural record."[^1] On a secular level he argues the book is hard to dismiss: produced in roughly three months (April–June 1829), it adds "more proper names to the English language than William Shakespeare," and its internal consistency and lasting impact resist a naturalistic account that Joseph "could have made up the book."[^2] He grants that proposed 19th-century influences exist but argues that, examined closely, features like Nephite government (judges chosen by leadership and sustained by the people) "really isn't anywhere near what the United States was using" and instead "matches several ancient models."[^3]

On the central question, Griffiths answers candidly: the artifacts we have are "not the ones that we really want."[^4] A revelation given to Joseph Smith in 1829 listed sacred objects the witnesses would see — the **plates**, the **sword of Laban**, the **interpreters**, and the **Liahona** — and "we don't have any of those" today; all three witnesses repeatedly attested seeing them, but the objects are gone.[^5] What does survive is **documentary**: about 28% of the **original manuscript** (the rest destroyed by water in a Nauvoo cornerstone), the **printer's manuscript** (the earliest full copy, made by [[Oliver Cowdery]] and [[Hyrum Smith]] as a backup), a **seer stone** with a traceable provenance, and the **"characters document"** in [[John Whitmer]]'s hand.[^6] Griffiths treats these as confirmatory data: the manuscripts' total lack of punctuation fits a Semitic/Egyptian source rather than an author "just writing the book," and the seer stone's short four-owner chain of custody (Joseph → Oliver → Elizabeth Whitmer Cowdery → Phineas Young → Church) matches descriptions given by [[Emma Smith]], [[David Whitmer]], and others.[^7] He notes the Church purchased the characters document in 2024 and that, while some scholars suspect those characters belong to a later Book of Abraham project, the "most likely scenario" is that John Whitmer copied them as one of the eight witnesses.[^8]

Asked where the plates went, Griffiths says the angel [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] took them back per Joseph Smith's history.[^9] He argues it is "difficult to imagine that Joseph Smith could craft an artifact that would essentially fool 11 people" — plus a wider circle of informal witnesses ([[Emma Smith]], [[Catherine Smith]], [[William Smith]], [[Josiah Stowell]], [[Mary Whitmer]]) who handled or glimpsed the plates.[^10] He frames the absence of the sacred objects theologically: "I don't think right now God intends for us to have the slam dunk because the question of faith still comes up."[^11]

The interview then shifts away from artifacts. Griffiths argues the Book of Mormon "is not racist, but... doesn't ignore racism" — that its critique of judging by skin color (Jacob; Samuel the Lamanite) and its post-visit society with "neither bond nor free" and "no manner of -ites" make racism a central problem the text condemns.[^12] He closes on the book's pastoral relevance to modern loneliness and identity — a personal God who "knows you" (Christ ministering "one by one" to the multitude) — and bears testimony that the Book of Mormon has drawn him closer to Christ.[^13]
