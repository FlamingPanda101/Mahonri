# Thoughtful Faith - Mormonism vs Protestantism Who Has the Right View of God

A live-streamed debate hosted by [[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith) between Hansen, defending the Latter-day Saint view of God, and **[[Daniel Ortner]]**, a Protestant and former Latter-day Saint of fourteen years. The two had clashed online for years and met to talk through their differences on the nature of God: embodied vs. incorporeal deity, the [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead vs. the Trinity]], divine simplicity, immutability, timelessness, [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference|creation ex nihilo]], the problem of evil, original sin, and Calvinist election.

Both interlocutors open by stressing their shared ground: both are theists, both reject atheism and "vague spiritualism," and both seek to follow the Jesus of the New Testament. The disagreement begins immediately afterward over what the word "God" *refers to* — a person, a nature, or "ultimate reality" itself — and never fully resolves, which structures the whole exchange.

## Summary

- **What "God" refers to.** Ortner anchors his view in God's self-disclosure in scripture: God as Creator, eternal, self-existent ("I AM," [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3:14]]), the one through whom all things were made ([[John 1.3|John 1:1, 3]], [[Colossians 1.16|Colossians 1:16]]). Hansen presses that the word "God" is used in *multiple senses* in scripture — the person of the Father, the Godhead, divinity-as-nature, and "ultimate reality" — and argues you cannot make "God" always denote a single person, since "God is love" and "God is a person" are ontologically different predications. Ortner replies God is *personal* and *embodies* love without contradiction. ([03:30]–[12:30])

- **Self-existence and "the box."** Hansen runs his cosmological argument that *something* has always existed for both sides — "from nothing, nothing comes" — so the only real question is whether God created out of nothing or organized a co-eternal reality he inhabits. He reframes "universe" as the box drawn around all of reality and asks whether "the box of everything that ever existed [has] ever been empty"; since God has always existed, it never has, so demanding an explanation for *why* anything exists is "a nonsensical question." Ortner counters that the LDS view has a *harder* burden than atheism: it must explain a self-existing reality with the built-in capacity to produce gods. (Steelmanned at [[Two Self-Existent Realities Cannot Coexist]], [[A Reality More Fundamental Than God Just Is God]], [[Maybe the Universe Itself Is the Necessary Being]].) ([14:30]–[21:30])

- **Omnipotence and the "snap his fingers" problem.** Hansen's recurring lever: if God is omnipotent in a *radical* (unrestricted) sense, he could "snap his fingers" and place everyone in eternal bliss with no suffering, no cross, no Egypt, no Amalekites. Every time Ortner answers "this is the *best* way," Hansen replies that "best" implies *other ways were unavailable* — i.e., a real constraint on God. Ortner accepts a constraint, but locates it *internally* in God's nature and justice. (Steelmanned at [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]], [[An Unconstrained God Makes the Atonement Pointless]], [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]].) ([21:30]–[28:30], [67:30]–[71:30])

- **Is God a slave to his nature?** When Ortner says God "cannot lie" and "cannot change his nature," Hansen seizes it: that *is* a constraint, and "a truly omnipotent being has the power to be anything they want to be," including the power to change its own nature. Ortner rejects the framing — "God is the master of his nature, not a slave to it." → [[God Is Constrained by His Own Nature - The Master-Not-Slave Reply]]. ([51:30]–[53:00])

- **What makes God "good."** Hansen argues that calling God "good" is *relational* to some conception of ultimate well-being and ultimate suffering: we call the devil evil because he leads toward suffering and God good because he leads away from it. Ortner resists, insisting God *is* goodness itself, the standard, and that well-being is *defined by* God rather than God being measured against an external scale of well-being — though he concedes hell *is* eternal separation from God, the source of joy. (Steelmanned at [[We Call God Good Because He Saves Us From Suffering]], [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]], [[Independent Justice Refutes Whatever God Does Is Good]].) ([30:00]–[36:30])

- **Timelessness and creation.** Ortner holds God created time itself and is "above time"; Hansen presses that in a strictly *timeless* realm nothing can happen, because acting requires a before-and-after, so a timeless God could never have *created* — and he explicitly judges Joseph Smith *wrong* for saying existence is "one eternal now." → [[A Timeless God Cannot Act or Create]]. ([76:30]–[80:00])

- **Original sin and the condemnation of infants.** Using a newborn as the test case for innocence, Hansen asks why a baby who has done nothing merits condemnation; Ortner affirms infants are *worthy of condemnation* through their inherited "nature of rebellion" in Adam, yet are *redeemed through Christ* via an age of accountability. Hansen's reductio: if babies are guaranteed heaven, we should "celebrate when babies die." Ortner answers that God commands us to value life. (Steelmanned at [[Infant Salvation and the Pelagius-Augustine Dispute over Original Sin]], [[Original Sin Was a Late Western Development]].) ([41:00]–[46:30], [63:30]–[67:00])

- **Divine impassibility and emotion.** A super-chat on "no body parts or passions" draws out Ortner's reading of the confessions: "passions" means God is *not driven by arbitrary, fluctuating emotion*, not that he is unfeeling; the Bible's anger/grief is anthropomorphic, describing God from the human side. Hansen presses that a real change in emotional state contradicts strict immutability. (See [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]], [[Divine Immutability - God Cannot Change]], [[Divine Impassibility Is Classical-Theist Heresy]].) ([73:00]–[76:30])

- **Begotten vs. created; image and likeness.** Ortner grounds the Father–Son distinction and Christ's uniqueness in the Greek sense of *begotten* (not *created*) at [[John 1.18|John 1:18]] and the glory of [[John 17.1-5|John 17]], and reads "image and likeness" as shared *communicable* attributes plus humans as God's representatives/temples — not as God possessing a body. (See [[Begotten Implies Source and Origin]], [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]].) ([60:30]–[63:30], [79:30]–[81:00])

- **Election, and whether God loves everyone.** Leaning Reformed, Ortner grants that God "loves everyone" only in the sense of common grace and sustaining them, while having chosen to save some and not all. Hansen's drowning-child analogy presses that withholding an easy rescue is not love; he concludes the consistent Calvinist must admit God "does not love everyone and does not want to save everyone," and offers his own escape: God *is* saving all he can, because agency is a *necessary condition* of mortality. (See [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]], [[Calvinism Is the Logical Conclusion of Classical Theism]], [[Coerced Love Is Not Love - Agency Against Irresistible Grace]].) ([86:30]–[91:00])

- **Why the Trinity matters (Ortner's close).** Ortner ends by tying the Trinity to soteriology: it secures that the *Creator himself* is also the *Savior*, that Christ's sacrifice was real and was God's own — "I really love knowing that God who made me also died for me." (See [[Why the Trinity vs Godhead Debate Actually Matters]].) ([95:00]–[96:30])
