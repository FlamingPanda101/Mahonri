# Ward Radio — Polygamy Actually Works

A "members bonus" [[Ward Radio]] segment hosted by [[Cardon Ellis]] and [[Brad Whitbeck]] with guest statistician/demographer [[Stephen Cranney]]. The premise: rebut the popular anti-Mormon soundbite that polygamy "didn't even raise the birth rate." Cranney argues it did — because it raised the *marriage rate* — and surveys the demographic data, including the Utah Population Database. The episode also riffs on the "polygamy denier / skeptic / truther" discourse around figures like Michelle Stone. The **fertility argument is new to the vault**; the polygamy-culture material overlaps the existing polygamy cluster.

## Summary

- **Polygamy raised fertility by raising the marriage rate, even though individual plural wives bore fewer children.** Critics note that 2nd/3rd wives had fewer kids than first or monogamous wives and conclude polygamy didn't raise overall fertility — but they ignore that polygyny nearly eliminated unmarried women (Kathryn Daynes's Manti study: essentially one woman died single across decades), and in the 19th century childbearing tracked marriage. Societies practicing polygyny show higher fertility, so it is "ipso facto" pro-natal. See [[Polygamy Raised Fertility by Raising the Marriage Rate]]. [^1]
- **The stated scriptural purpose of plural marriage was to "raise up seed," not a hidden motive.** [[Jacob 2.30]] and [[Doctrine and Covenants 132.63|D&C 132:63]] state the purpose openly; the data is consistent with it. A true experimental proof is impossible (no randomized control group living without polygamy), so the honest conclusion is "as much evidence as you could hope for" short of a controlled trial. See [[Polygamy Raised Fertility by Raising the Marriage Rate]]. [^2]
- **Plural marriage maximized a community's reproductive resources and could ease individual women's burden.** Cranney's own polygamous ancestor married a widow at a bishop's call so her child would be provided for, and Brigham Young arranged surrogate-style sealings for infertile couples; second/third wives bearing fewer children individually was, the hosts argue, less taxing per woman while raising population overall. The early Mormon Utah Population Database is one of the best historical demographic datasets in the world (used by cancer geneticists). See [[Polygamy Raised Fertility by Raising the Marriage Rate]]. [^3]
