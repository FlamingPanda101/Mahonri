# Thoughtful Faith - Who Are the REAL Anti-Mormons?

A co-produced livestream simulcast on both *Thoughtful Faith* and *Mormon Book Reviews*, recorded amid the [[Charlie Kirk]] assassination and the Grand Blanc / Michigan meetinghouse massacre. [[Steven Pynakker]] — an evangelical sympathetic to the Latter-day Saints — frames the conversation as one in a series meant to "lower the temperature," and presses [[Jacob Hansen]] on what actually counts as "anti-Mormon" versus good-faith criticism. The two agree on the goal (model that people who disagree deeply can still talk) while disagreeing sharply on where the lines fall, who belongs on the "spectrum" of critics, and whose rhetoric is more dangerous. Handle the surrounding tragedy soberly: real worshippers were killed, and the discussion repeatedly turns on the fear that demonizing rhetoric can move "unhinged people" to violence.

## Summary

- **Hansen's definition of "anti-Mormon" (04:31–08:33, 23:30).** Not mere unbelief, nor even publicly disagreeing. The criterion is **public, intentional, repeated erosion of faith in the church's fundamental truth claims** — and, crucially, "you can be a member of this church and be an anti-Mormon 100%." He distinguishes principled evangelical opponents (who at least "believe something and are proposing an alternative worldview") from "the lowest kind" who "just attack the church and offer nothing," which he reframes as really being anti-Christian / anti-Western. Grounds [[Anti-Mormon Is Public Intentional Repeated Erosion of Faith]] and [[A Member Can Be an Anti-Mormon]].

- **Pynakker's spectrum: soft vs. pernicious (24:00–27:31).** Drawing an anti-Semitism analogy, Pynakker proposes anti-Mormonism runs from "soft" social othering (the country-club exclusion) to a "pernicious" form whose rhetoric ("demonic," "antichrist," "are Mormons even Christian?") can escalate, in the limit, toward violence. He invokes [[Mark Driscoll]]'s post-shooting AI thumbnail and warns that "ideas and words can have consequences." Grounds [[Soft vs Pernicious Anti-Mormonism - The Rhetoric-to-Violence Spectrum]].

- **The collective witness, applied to the priesthood ban and gay marriage (12:30–21:30).** Pynakker stress-tests Hansen's epistemology: wouldn't a 1940s Latter-day Saint have used the same "collective witness" to defend the priesthood ban? Hansen answers that the model reads the prophets **across all of time, looking for contradictions, not novelty** — Brigham's "Adam-God" and the ban were aberrations from the collective witness, not expressions of it; gender complementarity is not. Grounds [[The Diachronic Collective Witness - Read the Prophets Across All Time]].

- **Reform must run through priesthood channels (10:30–12:30, 35:00–37:30).** An ordinary member "is not the arbiter of what is good for the church"; criticism belongs internally to priesthood leaders or in the choice to sustain/not-sustain, not in public attack. Analogies: the military (you don't publicly attack the commander-in-chief), the basketball coach (you don't argue mid-game). John Dehlin's excommunication is cited as the consequence of going outside that structure. Grounds [[Reform Through Priesthood Channels Not Public Attack]].

- **Is [[John Dehlin]] (and the progressive critic) anti-Mormon? (29:00–33:00, 51:00–52:30).** Hansen says yes — Mormon Stories is "literally dedicated" to that erosion. He concedes (per a prior public reversal) that Dehlin is *sincere* and "wants to do good," but argues sincerity is beside the point: to remake the church's fundamentals into "thing B" is to destroy "thing A." Pynakker counters that critics like Dehlin, the Tanners, RFM, and Alyssa Grenfell are more like reformers, and that Dehlin has platformed faithful and positive voices (it was a Mormon Stories episode that returned Pynakker himself to faith, 49:30–50:32).

- **Reforming the fundamentals = destroying the thing (52:00–53:00, 65:30–71:00).** Using "essential characteristics" from philosophy and the Martin Luther / Catholic-pope analogy, Hansen argues that to alter what makes a church *that* church (e.g., "Joseph Smith was a prophet") is not reform but destruction — so critics should "just own it." Grounds [[Reforming a Thing's Essence Is Destroying It Not Reforming It]].

- **The say-vs-do disconnect (62:01–65:30, 73:32–77:32).** "What you say speaks way quieter than what you actually do." If a channel's near-total output paints the church as abusive, dishonest, dangerous, and bigoted, then claiming "I'm not anti-Mormon, the church does a lot of good" is incoherent: if the church really were that bad it *should* be destroyed; and if it does as much good as the critic privately admits (Dehlin reportedly grants it does "more good than bad"), the channel should reflect that ratio. Grounds [[The Say-Versus-Do Disconnect in Critics]].

- **Models of charitable engagement (09:00–10:30, 79:00–82:30, 90:00–end).** Pynakker praises Catholicism's accommodation of internal critics (Gary Wills) and even hostile outsiders ([[Christopher Hitchens]] as devil's advocate at Mother Teresa's canonization), and holds up [[Sandra Tanner]] and the Tanners as an "anti-Mormon ministry" that nonetheless modeled neighborly, honest, cards-on-the-table engagement. Hansen's mantra "hard on ideas, soft on people" frames his close; both publicly condemn personal attacks made on their own "side" (Hansen disavows Robert Boland's insinuations about Pynakker; he offers to apologize to Kolby Reddish).
