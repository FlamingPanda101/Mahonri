# Informed Saints - Do Mormons Believe in the Trinity

**Channel:** Informed Saints | **Panel:** [[Jasmine Rapley]], Neil, Stephen | **Guest:** [[Grant Underwood]] | **Topic:** the LDS Godhead vs. the creedal Trinity, and whether Latter-day Saints can claim the "Christian" label

A long-form interview with [[Grant Underwood]] about his book *Latter-day Saint Theology Among Christian Theologies* — notable for being a systematic LDS theology published by **Eerdmans**, a mainstream Christian (non-LDS, non-dogmatic) academic press, and pitched primarily to a *non*-Latter-day Saint audience rather than as polemic or apologetic. The Trinity segment is Underwood's irenic case that Latter-day Saints are "trinitarians, just not creedal trinitarians," anchored in his coinage [[Separate Being Trinitarianism]]. The wider conversation argues that "Christian" is a contested, un-ownable label and that the Restoration can honor pre-Restoration Christianity rather than denigrate it.

## Summary

- **The real divide is ontology, not the catalog of God's attributes.** Underwood opens his book by stressing how *much* Latter-day Saints share with other Christians about what God is like (love, justice, mercy, omniscience). The genuine distinctive is **ontology** — a different "theory of being." For most Christians there is an "unbridgeable chasm" between Creator and creature (God is *wholly other*); for Latter-day Saints all beings are "on one continuum of the nature of being… the same race, the same species, the same type of being," with God incomprehensibly advanced. This is the vault's [[Humans Are the Same Kind as God (LDS Ontology)]] and the embodied-God thesis of [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]] and [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]]; D&C 130's "tangible body of flesh and bone" is the cited proof-text.
- **"Children of God" — same vocabulary, different definition.** Other Christians say believers become children of God *by adoption through grace*; Latter-day Saints take it "more literally and ontologically" as the literal offspring of deity ("divine DNA," destiny to "become like our heavenly parents"). The panel develops a recurring meta-point — same words, wildly different meanings — and praises Underwood's scrupulous defining of terms. (Cf. the inverse problem in the Ammon/Lamoni "Great Spirit" exchange: *different* vocabulary, *similar* concept.)
- **Separate-being trinitarianism (the centerpiece).** Building on a [[Jeffrey R. Holland]] passage that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are "one in every conceivable way except physically united," Underwood coins **separate being trinitarianism**: keep the word "trinitarianism" to signal camaraderie, qualify it with "separate being" to mark the distinctive. Most historic formulations say "three persons in one being"; Underwood affirms the three persons and the oneness in love/justice/mercy/knowledge while relocating the difference to *being*. This is the new concept [[Separate Being Trinitarianism]]. The host adds that he himself concluded "we actually are trinitarians… just not creedal/Nicene trinitarians," and that the Book of Mormon is "a trinitarian text" — the move catalogued in [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]] and [[Social and Subordinate Trinitarianism Is the Early Church View]]. They also flag **social trinitarianism** as the nearest creedal cousin (see [[The Social Trinity Is the Most Defensible Trinitarianism]]).
- **Compare the LDS view to the *whole* range of Trinitarianisms, not one solution.** Underwood's method: the threeness-and-oneness puzzle has been "solved" many incompatible ways across the centuries (Catholic, Orthodox, Calvinist); judged against that full umbrella, Latter-day Saints "aren't so far outside" it as critics assume. He warns Latter-day Saints against an "anti-trinitarianism… because we haven't really understood trinitarianism" — the term is "to be understood," not disliked. This reframes the [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model]] contrast as a difference *within* a diverse tradition rather than from it.
- **"Christian" is a contested, un-ownable label.** A long segment argues no one owns the word. Underwood marshals scholarship: [[Jan Shipps]] (Methodist Mormon-studies pioneer), Matt Bowman's *Christian: The Politics of a Word in American Life*, and especially David Congdon's *Who Is a True Christian? Contested Identity in American Life* — all showing the term has been "weaponized" to sanctify a group's own values. He notes the early-20th-century *fundamentals* → "fundamentalist" boundary-drawing, and cites Peter Lampe (*From Paul to Valentinus*), Bart Erman's *Lost Christianities*, and Karen King to argue you cannot even fix a single "Christianity" in the first two centuries, let alone 2,000 years later. (See [[Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree]].)
- **Eastern Orthodoxy is surprisingly close.** Underwood reports presenting a paper on LDS–Orthodox similarities at the International Orthodox Theological Association (IOTA), to a non-LDS audience of clerics and theologians whose reaction mirrored the panel's own surprise. Orthodoxy "hues more closely to the earliest centuries," the pre-Nicene/pre-Constantinian patristic ("from the word for fathers") period he regularly quotes.
- **Why Eerdmans published it.** Per editor-in-chief James Ernest (in a *From the Desk* interview): Eerdmans is a Christian but *non-dogmatic* press with "no creed" and a broad tent; it does not do "boundary-set thinking"; prior Eerdmans titles on Latter-day Saints were "polemical or tendentious," and they wanted to raise the bar with a rigorous, fair, dialogical treatment by a scholar competent in both LDS tradition and the history of Christian theology.
- **Restoration without denigration — and the same-organization problem.** Underwood ties LDS distinctives back to ancient Christianity and Judaism, but rejects a "literalistic" reading of Article of Faith 6 ("the same organization that existed in the primitive church"): the first-century "organizational flowchart cannot really be found," so the claim must be read as **functional**, not structural, continuity — the new concept [[The Same Organization Means Functional Not Structural Continuity with the Primitive Church]]. He pairs this with a charitable apostasy posture, quoting [[John Taylor]] ("if that's darkness, give me a little darkness"), [[Dallin H. Oaks]] (others "kept the light alive"), George Albert Smith, and the General Handbook — celebrating the *additional* light of the Restoration rather than damning the "dark ages." This extends [[The Gathering of Truths from All Churches]], [[A New Latter-day Saint Approach to Ancient Christians]], and the disavowal in [[Great Apostasy Is Not a Scriptural Term in LDS Standard Works]].
- **Exclusivist vs. inclusivist vs. pluralist.** Underwood reports being surprised to learn the LDS posture is best classed not as *exclusivist* (despite the "one true church" claim) but as **inclusivist**: God sheds light and grace on the good and just outside the tradition, without conceding the pluralist "many equal paths." This is the framework of [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]] applied to the Restoration.
- **Testimony coda.** Underwood closes on a personal witness (Book of Mormon experience at 15; filming Melanesian pioneers in Papua New Guinea 25 years ago and foreseeing the present growth — a Port Moresby temple, a new mission) and the channel's motto: "study deeply and still believe boldly."
