# Cwic Show — The Official Position on Official Positions (Trevan Hatch)

A *Cwic Show* (Greg Matsen) interview with BYU scholar [[Trevan Hatch]] about his book chapter "What's the Church's Official Position on Official Positions? Grappling with Truth and Authority" (in *The Learning of the Jews*). Modeled on how rabbis debate authoritative texts, the conversation catalogs how Latter-day Saints decide what counts as binding doctrine — and concludes that the question is genuinely hard.

## Summary
- **The real question is "What is God's position?"** Hatch reframes student questions ("what's the church's official position on X?") as "what is God's position, filtered through the prophets?" — which raises the stakes from institutional PR to revelation.[^1]
- **Competing scholarly criteria for an "official position."** Hatch surveys multiple incompatible checklists — [[Robert Millet]] (2003/2007/2013), three BYU professors (2016), Michael Goodman, Scott Woodward — most centering on *repeated* scripture, the *united voice* of the living brethren, current teaching, and recent publications. The criteria themselves are contested (Lloyd Ericson's published rebuttal: "why these four?") and shot through with malleable terms ("united voice," "the Holy Spirit").[^2] See [[There Is No Agreed Criterion for an Official Church Position]].
- **Prophets vs. scripture — two opposing traditions.** One strand exalts the living prophet over scripture (Brigham Young: "those books are nothing to me" vs. the living oracles; Benson: "the living prophets always take precedence"; Orson F. Whitney: "no book presides over this church"). The opposite strand subordinates prophets to canon (Harold B. Lee and Joseph Fielding Smith: if a prophet teaches "out of harmony with the revelations... every member is duty bound to reject it"; McConkie: "prophets do not rank with the standard works"; [[Dallin H. Oaks|Oaks]] 2019: a single leader's statement is "a personal, though well-considered, opinion... not meant to be binding").[^3] See [[The Living Prophet Versus the Standard Works Are Two Competing LDS Authority Traditions]].
- **The Word of Wisdom as a worked example.** Over 80 times (1908–2002) members were counseled to avoid caffeine; yet caffeinated drinks now sell at BYU, and a church PR rationale was that "caffeine is not mentioned in D&C 89" — *siding with scripture over prophetic teaching*. Meanwhile the revelation itself (D&C 89) was "not by commandment," permits "mild barley drink" (beer) and restricts meat, and Joseph Smith bought/drank beer and took wine to Carthage — so both "follow the prophet" and "follow the text" cut against current practice.[^4] See [[The Caffeine Reversal Exposes the Prophet-Versus-Scripture Tension in the Word of Wisdom]] and the existing [[Word of Wisdom]].
- **"Follow the prophet in all things" is rarely qualified.** Statements that members must "promptly and quietly obey the counsel of the prophet in all things and at all times" (Benson, Bednar) are powerful talking points that get weaponized (the COVID-vaccine "the prophet said it, case closed"; Prop 8 reprimands; "the thinking has been done" memes) — but are seldom cashed out concretely.[^5] See [[Follow the Prophet in All Things Is a Slogan That Gets Weaponized]].
- **Prophets are not experts in general.** Hatch stacks the brethren's *own* deflections: McConkie (general authorities "may or may not be authorities in... doctrinal knowledge"); Boyd K. Packer (leaders "scarcely have time to learn" the fundamentals after their call); George Q. Cannon ("do not put your trust in man, though he be... an apostle, or a president"); and especially [[Tim Ballard|Elder M. Russell Ballard]] ("I'm a general authority, but that doesn't make me an authority in general... I seek others... with degrees and expertise").[^6] (Cross-ref [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]] and [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]].)
- **"When the prophet speaks the thinking has been done" was disowned.** The 1940s *Improvement Era* line was repudiated by then-President George Albert Smith, who wrote that even to imply members should not think for themselves "is grossly to misrepresent... the ideal of the church."[^7] See [[The Thinking Has Been Done Was Repudiated by George Albert Smith]].
- **The Benson "14 Fundamentals" caused angst.** Ezra Taft Benson's 1980 BYU devotional ("the living prophet is more vital to us than the standard works"; "the prophet... [needs no] training... to speak on any subject... at any time") reportedly so troubled President [[Spencer W. Kimball]] that he called Benson before the Twelve to explain himself; the First Presidency's spokesman publicly denied "the president's word is law on all issues."[^8] (Cross-ref [[The Infallibility Trap - Critics Manufacture a Standard the Church Never Taught]].)
- **Hatch's stewardship resolution.** His proposed rule: prophets are *stewards of the institution and guardians of the ordinances* — on church structure and how ordinances are run, "no questions asked, 100%." But on non-revelatory matters outside institutional policy (science, history, family, civic life), "consulting prophets... is like asking a lawyer for medical advice." Where two prophets contradict, the member must "step in and grapple" and reach a personal conclusion — because each person has stewardship over their own soul and family.[^9] See [[Prophetic Stewardship Is Over the Institution and Ordinances Not Over All Truth]].
