[[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith), drawing on notes from [[Luke Hansen]] of Ward Radio, responds to Catholic Answers apologist [[Joe Heschmeyer]]'s video *Why the Book of Mormon Is America's Quran*. Heschmeyer's project is to defuse the "argument from translation" — the claim that the difficulty of producing the Book of Mormon points to a supernatural origin — by showing that structurally identical arguments are made by Muslims for the Quran, and that since the two books reach contradictory conclusions, the argument form must be invalid. Hansen's counter is that the argument **backfires**: Heschmeyer compared the *type* of argument on each side when he should have compared the *weight of evidence*, and once you do, the Book of Mormon's dictation is in a league of its own.

## Summary
The note treats two genuinely new contributions of this video; the underlying dictation-feat and Quran-comparison theses are developed in existing vault notes (linked below) and are not re-derived here.

- **The "proves too much" reversal.** Heschmeyer argues that if two similar-sounding arguments lead to contradictory conclusions (Quran *and* Book of Mormon both divine), the argument form is bad. Hansen replies this proves too much: the very same ad-absurdum form is used by mainstream Christians for the Gospels and the Resurrection (he quotes Glenn Scriber's *The Air We Breathe* and [[Jordan Peterson]]'s "impossible task" remark). The fix is not to retire the argument form but to weigh the evidence — see [[Joe Heschmeyers Quran Parallel Proves Too Much Against the Resurrection]].
- **The evidence actually favors the Book of Mormon.** Hansen contrasts the Quran (≈77,000 Arabic words, non-narrative, dictated piecemeal over ~23 years, historically murky transmission, disputed literacy of Muhammad) with the Book of Mormon (≈270,000 words, single sustained narrative with 100+ characters and consistent internal geography, dictated in a single draft in ~65 working days inside an 85-day window, with multiple eyewitnesses and surviving original/printer's manuscript). He argues the correct Quran analog is the Doctrine and Covenants — see [[The Quran Parallels the Doctrine and Covenants Not the Book of Mormon]] and [[Muhammad and Joseph Smith Are Apples to Oranges]].
- **Heschmeyer's natural-feat examples don't parallel the Book of Mormon.** Memorized plant/medicine taxonomies (Dogon, Philippine, Amazonian encyclopedias), Milman Parry's oral-formulaic Homer (~200,000 Greek words, *collectively* composed, not one draft), and "farm boys read while plowing" all fail to match a 270,000-word single-draft narrative. Hansen notes Heschmeyer's own cited scholars say rising literacy *decreases* memorization, which cuts against the "literate Joseph" move.
- **The 1820s imprint data.** Average word count for an 1820s imprint was ~20,000–21,000 words; the decade's single longer-than-BoM work, John Neal's *My Brother Jonathan* (~370,000–400,000 words), took its professionally-trained author about a year (~1,000 words/day). Joseph Smith's ~270,000 words in ~65 days is roughly quadruple that daily output, from someone in the bottom ~5% of likely authors by profile.
- **Lucy Mack Smith's "evening recitals" do not establish pre-composition.** Hansen steelmans and then rebuts the critic's best naturalistic foothold: of dozens of contemporary neighbor statements (incl. 22 in *Mormonism Unvailed*), only Joseph's mother mentions teenage story-telling about ancient inhabitants, and genuine oral memorization of long texts required years of loud repetition (a modern actor took 9 years to memorize *Paradise Lost*). See [[Lucy Mack Smiths Evening Recitals Do Not Establish Pre-Composition]].
- **Joseph's documented limited education.** Lucy said he was "less inclined to the perusal of books," and at dictation "had never read the Bible through"; [[David Whitmer]] called him ignorant of the Bible; [[Emma Smith]] said he didn't know Jerusalem had walls. Even neutral and hostile parties could only explain the book by positing someone else wrote it (Sidney Rigdon, prodigious memory of "some good man's" writings) — theories that contradicted their own evidence. Hansen cautions against overstating the case: a coherent 1829 letter shows Joseph was not illiterate, though it postdates the dictation.
- **Heschmeyer's "growth by the sword" remark.** Hansen flags as egregious the claim that Mormonism, like Islam, grew partly by force (Nauvoo Legion), refusing to dignify it with rebuttal.
- **Trent Horn's shift from naturalistic to demonic.** Hansen notes [[Trent Horn]] argued only naturalistic explanations in his debate with [[Jacob Hansen]], then later invoked a demonic cause for the angelic encounters — see [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable]]. Hansen closes that the translation feat proves nothing by itself, but combined with the witnesses, Hebraisms, and the [[Joseph Smith's Name Had for Good and Evil Prophecy|"name for good and evil"]] prophecy, a naturalistic account becomes less likely — and the best evidence remains [[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni's promise]] to ask God.
