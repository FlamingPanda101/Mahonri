# Thoughtful Faith — The Witness of Authority (Knowledge by Degrees of Probability)

A later installment in [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1 epistemology series (sub-section 1.1), following up on the videos where he made an **argument from reason** for his "**collective witness model of knowledge**." Hansen says that here he is "doing something different": instead of arguing from reason, he is **calling on the witness of authority** — showing how "other trustworthy people and sources seem to be at the very least implying a similar way of viewing knowledge." [^1] The episode is therefore a curated montage: LDS prophetic statements on truth, the John Godfrey Saxe "blind men and the elephant" poem, and extended philosophical clips from [[Robert Barron|Bishop Robert Barron]] and [[Jordan Peterson]], all marshalled to support the thesis that **knowledge runs on degrees of probability, not certainty**, and that **truth is embraced from any source**.

- **Almost everything we hold true is known by degrees of probability, not "epidictic certitude."** In the central clip [[Robert Barron|Bishop Barron]] argues that apart from "certain mathematical givens," the "extraordinary majority" of what we believe — including all the sciences and all of history (e.g. that Julius Caesar was killed in 44 BC) — rests on degrees of probability carried by tradition we have not personally verified. [^2] See [[Knowledge Runs on Degrees of Probability Not Certitude]].
- **The clarity-of-science vs obscurity-of-religion split is a false, "phony" dichotomy.** Barron resists sharply distinguishing the "clarity of the sciences" from the "obscurity of religion." 100% certitude is an "Enlightenment fantasy"; critics of the Enlightenment (Goethe, Pascal) saw that "almost none of life" appears in that "brilliant bright light" — the most important truths are known "in the twilight." [^3] See [[The Clarity of Science vs Obscurity of Religion Is a False Dichotomy]].
- **Science strips subjectivity from the world and risks nihilism.** [[Jordan Peterson]] argues the objective-truth maneuver — stringently specifying observation conditions and keeping only what is common across observers — is "insanely powerful" but treats all subjectivity as bias to be removed. Stripping the world of subjectivity leaves you, a subject, "isolated" among "impersonal facts," with the psychological consequence of nihilism — a problem the older "mythological landscape" did not pose. [^4][^5] See [[Stripping Subjectivity From the World Produces Nihilism]].
- **Greater light reveals truth that existed all along; rejecting it is self-inflicted blindness.** A [[Dieter F. Uchtdorf|Uchtdorf]] clip uses the discovery of other galaxies as an analogy: the universe did not change when telescopes improved — "our ability to see and understand this truth changed." Truth "exists beyond belief… even if nobody believes it"; we "see through a glass darkly" and must trust the Lord, who is the one "complete, correct, and incorruptible" source of truth. Loss of faith is framed (with [[Henry B. Eyring]]) as "self-inflicted spiritual blindness" — "their problem… lies in what they cannot see." [^6][^7] See [[Greater Light Reveals Pre-Existing Truth]].
- **The blind men and the elephant: partial truths mistaken for the whole.** John Godfrey Saxe's poem about six blind men of Hindustan — each grabbing one part of the elephant (leg/tree, tusk/spear, tail/rope, trunk/snake) and insisting his partial truth is the whole — is used to illustrate how "someone could make a judgment based on one aspect of truth and apply it to the whole," each "partly in the right and all… in the wrong." [^8] See [[The Blind Men and the Elephant - Partial Truth Mistaken for the Whole]].
- **The law of witnesses and the mandate to embrace all truth from any source.** Framing montage: "in the mouth of two or three witnesses shall every word be established"; the Church "embraces all truth… whether in a scientific laboratory or by direct revelation"; [[Joseph Smith]]'s "first and fundamental principle… that we have a right to embrace all and every item of truth"; and "one of the grand fundamental principles of Mormonism is to receive truth, let it come from whence it may." [^9][^10] See [[Embrace All Truth From Any Source]] and [[The Law of Witnesses Establishes Truth]].
