# Capturing Christianity — We Need to Talk About the Shroud

A solo explainer by Cameron Bertuzzi (*Capturing Christianity*) weighing the best evidence for and against the **Shroud of Turin's** authenticity, prompted by a new historical paper (Nicola Sarzo) claiming an earlier medieval reference to the cloth. Bertuzzi deliberately steelmans the skeptical case and lands on **agnosticism**.

## Summary
- **What makes the image strange.** The Shroud bears a faint, full-length image of a crucified man that behaves like a *photographic negative* (discovered 1898), encodes *3D depth* information, fades when backlit, and sits only on the topmost fibers — with **no pigment, dye, binder, or brushstrokes** (per the 1978 STURP investigation) — plus apparently real human bloodstains with serum halos matching Roman crucifixion wounds.[^1] See [[The Shroud Image Has No Known Forgery Mechanism]].
- **The skeptic's strongest card: carbon dating.** In 1988, three labs independently dated the linen to **1260–1390 AD** — the Middle Ages, not the first century.[^2]
- **Sarzo's new historical find.** Sarzo uncovered a treatise by Nicole Oresme (c. 1370–1382) using "a shroud of the Lord Jesus Christ" in a church in Champagne as a textbook example of clerical fraud — pushing the documentary record a decade or two earlier than Bishop Pierre d'Arcis's 1389 memo, and reframing it from a local dispute into educated-clergy common knowledge that the cloth was man-made.[^3] See [[The Medieval Documentary Record Converges With the Carbon Dating]].
- **The convergence argument.** History (the cloth surfacing ~1350s, mocked 1370s, denounced 1389) and science (the 1260–1390 radiocarbon window) independently point to the *same century* — "about as close to a bullseye as you can get" for the skeptic.[^4]
- **Johnson's rebuttal.** Jeremiah Johnson argues Sarzo's case is thinner than advertised: (a) Oresme's Champagne reference is a *generic* relic-scam slam, not a precise pointer to Lirey; (b) both Oresme and d'Arcis assume the image is *painted*, which STURP decisively refuted, undercutting their reliability about its origin; (c) the d'Arcis memo exists in two mashed-together drafts and rests partly on hearsay; (d) none of it explains the *image* — Dr. John Jackson cataloged 17 features no one has reproduced.[^5] See [[A Medieval Forgery Verdict Still Cannot Explain the Image]].
- **The two escape routes for authenticity.** To break the science+history deadlock a defender must either (1) place the Shroud *earlier* than the 14th century — but pre-medieval references (Constantinople cloths, the Sudarium of Oviedo) are "thin" and circumstantial — or (2) show the **carbon dating is unreliable**: the 1988 sample was a single corner possibly contaminated by medieval rewoven repair threads, yielding a skewed average; newer **wide-angle X-ray scattering (WAXS)** suggests a first-century date but needs validation before it can overrule the radiocarbon result.[^6] See [[The Carbon Dating May Have Sampled a Medieval Repair]].
- **The honest verdict.** Bertuzzi concludes that agnosticism is the most reasonable stance: a forgery theory must still explain the unreproduced image, the blood trauma, and the pollen/dirt matching ancient Israel; an authenticity theory must still explain the carbon dating and the historical silence before the 14th century. (He signs off, characteristically, "by the way, Christianity is true.")[^7]
