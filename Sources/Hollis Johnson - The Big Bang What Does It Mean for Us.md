# Hollis Johnson - The Big Bang: What Does It Mean for Us?

A review essay in the *FARMS Review* 16/2 (2004): 277–312 by [[Hollis R. Johnson]], professor emeritus of astronomy at Indiana University, responding to Paul Copan and [[William Lane Craig]]'s chapter "Craftsman or Creator? An Examination of the Mormon Doctrine of Creation and a Defense of Creatio ex nihilo" (in *The New Mormon Challenge*, Zondervan, 2002). Copan and Craig argue that the standard hot big bang model proves [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creatio ex nihilo]] via an initial singularity, that this contradicts Latter-day Saint eternalism (God, spirits, and elements are eternal), and that an embodied God within space-time would perish in the heat death of the universe — calling big bang cosmogony "a veritable dagger at the throat of Mormon theology" (p.146). Johnson, writing as a working astronomer, rebuts the science underneath the argument and sketches how a [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer|Latter-day Saint cosmology]] of eternal, organized matter reads modern physics.

## Summary

Johnson's central move is that Copan and Craig commit what he names the **Aquinas fallacy** — treating contemporary (and in their case outdated, late-1970s) science as a final answer on which to pin theology (see [[The Aquinas Fallacy - Pinning Theology to Current Science]]). The standard hot big bang model with its initial singularity is no longer accepted: quantum mechanics forbids a perfect point-singularity, and the Hawking–Penrose theorems rest on two assumptions (that general relativity holds everywhere and that gravity is always attractive) that most physicists now reject, since repulsive gravitation apparently drove inflation (see [[Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow]]). Without the singularity, the argument for creation from nothing "has no scientific basis" (p.284); even a beginning of time would not entail creation from nothing.

Johnson distinguishes **theological/philosophical "nothing"** (a totally empty void that may not exist) from **scientific "nothing"** (the quantum vacuum), which is "a beehive of activity" — virtual particles, fields, CMB photons, neutrinos, and gravitational fields fill even an evacuated box in intergalactic space (pp.293–95). This is the inverse of the apologetic [[Nothing Comes from Nothing - The Krauss Equivocation|Krauss equivocation]]: here a scientist deploys the vacuum's reality *against* ex nihilo. The vacuum's dark energy is ~70% of the universe's mass-energy (5% common matter, 25% dark matter), now seen accelerating the expansion since 1998 — an energy reservoir that "alone may destroy the argument of creation from nothing" (p.285). Most strikingly, the universe's total net energy appears to be ~zero: the enormous positive (rest-mass, kinetic) energy is balanced by the negative gravitational/vacuum energy stored in empty space, so the cosmos is a "free lunch" requiring no energy input (see [[The Universe's Net Energy Is Zero So Creation Needed No Energy Input]]).

On the religious side, Johnson argues Genesis 1:1 concerns the *earth* (formed from pre-existing gas and dust 4.6 billion years ago), not the whole 14-billion-year universe, so reading "universe" and "big bang" into it "requires a spectacular leap of logic" (p.286) — and Christians who discard biblical chronology to accept the big bang cannot then mount a biblical argument for ex nihilo. He surveys inflation, the multiverse, string/M-theory, and the heat death, and closes by encouraging a still-undeveloped Latter-day Saint cosmology that joins revelation with science, while cautioning that such speculation is not Church doctrine. Latter-day Saints, holding spirit to be matter (D&C 131:7) and intelligence/elements coeternal with God ([[D&C 93|D&C 93:33–34, 29]]), read the big bang as the organization — not the absolute origination — of an eternal cosmos whose "work and glory" is the [[Moses 1.39|immortality and eternal life of man]].
