# Hollis Johnson - The Big Bang What Does It Mean for Us

A review essay in the *FARMS Review* 16/2 (2004): 277–312 by [[Hollis R. Johnson]], professor emeritus of astronomy at Indiana University, answering Paul Copan and [[William Lane Craig]]'s chapter "Craftsman or Creator? An Examination of the Mormon Doctrine of Creation and a Defense of Creatio ex nihilo" (in *The New Mormon Challenge*, Zondervan, 2002). Copan and Craig argue that the standard hot big bang model proves [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creatio ex nihilo]] through an initial singularity, that this contradicts Latter-day Saint eternalism (God, spirits, and the elements are eternal), and that an embodied God within space-time would perish in the heat death of the universe — calling big bang cosmogony "a veritable dagger at the throat of Mormon theology" (p.10). Johnson, writing as a working astronomer who is himself a Latter-day Saint, rebuts the physics underneath the argument and sketches how a [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer|Latter-day Saint cosmology]] of eternal, organized matter reads modern cosmology.

## Summary

Johnson's central charge is that Copan and Craig commit what he names the **Aquinas fallacy** — treating contemporary (and in their case outdated, late-1970s) science as a final answer on which to hang theology (see [[The Aquinas Fallacy - Pinning Theology to Current Science]]). The standard hot big bang model with its initial singularity is no longer accepted: quantum mechanics forbids a perfect point-singularity, and the Hawking–Penrose theorems rest on two assumptions — that general relativity holds everywhere and that gravity is always attractive — that most physicists now reject, since repulsive gravitation apparently drove inflation (see [[Quantum Mechanics Forbids a True Singularity So Creation From Nothing Does Not Follow]]). Without the singularity, the argument for creation from nothing "has no scientific basis" (p.10); even a beginning of *time* would not entail creation from nothing.

Johnson distinguishes **theological/philosophical "nothing"** (a totally empty void that may not exist anywhere) from **scientific "nothing"** (the quantum vacuum), which is "a beehive of activity" — virtual particles, fields, CMB photons, neutrinos, and gravitational fields fill even an evacuated box in intergalactic space (pp.19–21). This is the inverse of the apologetic [[Nothing Comes from Nothing - The Krauss Equivocation|Krauss equivocation]]: here a scientist deploys the vacuum's reality *against* ex nihilo, observing that the vacuum's dark energy is ~70% of the universe's mass-energy (5% common matter, 25% dark matter) and has been accelerating the expansion since the 1998 supernova discovery — a background energy that "alone may destroy the argument of creation from nothing" (p.11). Most strikingly, the universe's total net energy appears to be ~zero, the positive rest-mass and kinetic energy balanced by the negative gravitational/vacuum energy stored in empty space, so the cosmos is a "free lunch" needing no energy input (see [[The Universe's Net Energy Is Zero So Creation Needed No Energy Input]]).

On the religious side, Johnson argues that Genesis 1:1 concerns the *earth* (formed from pre-existing gas and dust 4.6 billion years ago), not the whole 14-billion-year universe, so reading "universe" and "big bang" into it "requires a spectacular leap of logic" (p.11) — and Christians who discard biblical chronology to accept a 14-billion-year-old cosmos cannot then mount a *biblical* argument for ex nihilo. He surveys inflation, the multiverse, string/M-theory, the runaway accelerating expansion, and the heat death and red-giant fate of the sun, then closes by encouraging a still-undeveloped Latter-day Saint cosmology that joins revelation with science, while cautioning that such speculation is not Church doctrine. Holding spirit to be matter (D&C 131:7) and intelligence and the elements coeternal with God (D&C 93:29, 33–34), Latter-day Saints read the big bang as the organization — not the absolute origination — of an eternal cosmos whose "work and glory" is the [[Moses 1.39|immortality and eternal life of man]] (Moses 1:39).
