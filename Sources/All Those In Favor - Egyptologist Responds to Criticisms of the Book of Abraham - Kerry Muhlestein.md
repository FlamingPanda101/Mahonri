# All Those In Favor — Egyptologist Responds to Criticisms of the Book of Abraham (Dr. Kerry Muhlestein)

Hosts Kwaku and Cade screen Cameron Bertuzzi's (Capturing Christianity) video "The Book of Abraham Is Fake — Here's the Undeniable Proof" and get reactions from [[Kerry Muhlestein]], a BYU professor of ancient scripture and practicing Egyptologist who directs an excavation in Egypt. Muhlestein's recurring theme is that the popular "undeniable proof" reduces a genuinely complex problem to "great 30-second sound bites" that have been answered in the published literature for a decade. He reframes the two perennial objections — the facsimiles don't match Egyptology, and the surviving papyrus text says nothing about Abraham — as resting on a stack of unexamined assumptions, and he models a "say what we can and can't tell" scholarly posture throughout.

## Summary

- **Egyptologists cannot give "the" meaning of a 3,000-year tradition.** The same figure is interpreted differently across eras; the facsimiles date to ~200 BC, but most comparanda are far earlier, and reading later meanings onto them is "shaky ground." John Gee found a labeled hypocephalus showing that *both* scholarly camps had misidentified figures in Facsimile 2. See [[Egyptologists Disagree Across Time and Place on What a Drawing Means]]. [^1]
- **Joseph's intent is unknown — he may give an ancient-Jewish reinterpretation, not the "average Egyptian" reading.** Around 200 BC, Theban priests demonstrably blended Jewish, Greek, and Egyptian traditions (spells invoking Moses; texts using Abraham); the papyri's owner, the priest Hor, is "exactly the person we would expect to own" a reinterpreted Abrahamic text. See [[Joseph May Be Conveying an Ancient Jewish Reinterpretation Not the Egyptian Meaning]]. [^2]
- **The Abraham–Osiris substitution is independently attested — including in Jesus' own parable.** Stelae sometimes label the throned figure (normally Osiris) as Abraham; Jesus reworked the Egyptian "bosom of Osiris" into "bosom of Abraham" (Luke 16). All three facsimiles are, at some point, linked to Abraham by ancient sources — long odds for a lucky guesser. See [[Facsimile 3 Identifications Vindicate Joseph Smith by Coincidence]]. [^3]
- **We possess under ~10% of Joseph's papyri, and not the source he translated.** Witnesses unanimously describe Joseph translating from the long scroll, which burned in the 1871 Chicago Fire; the surviving fragments are the Book of Breathings (Hor) and a Book of the Dead (Tasheritmin). A mathematician's scroll-length formula (applied by John Gee) estimates the long scroll at ~42 feet. See [[We Possess Only a Fraction of Joseph Smith's Papyri]] and [[Most of the Book of Abraham Papyri Burned in the Chicago Fire]]. [^4]
- **"He translated the text next to Facsimile 1" is an untested assumption.** Text and adjacent illustration are often misaligned even in modern books; the Book of Abraham twice refers to "the drawing at the *beginning* of the record" (suggesting distance); and every detailed eyewitness — speaking *after* Facsimile 1 was glued onto its backing paper — says he translated the long scroll. See [[Joseph Was Not Translating the Surviving Papyrus]]. [^5]
- **The Kirtland Egyptian Papers are not a translation tool; analysis kills every old theory.** The grammar-and-alphabet documents mix in non-Egyptian characters (from the Book of Mormon and invented ones) that the makers *knew* weren't Egyptian, and Muhlestein's character-by-character analysis finds only ~2% correlation between the grammar's characters and the Book of Abraham manuscripts — "less than chance" — so the grammar was not used to translate. See [[The Kirtland Egyptian Papers Are a Cipher Project Not a Translation Tool]] and [[No Theory Yet Accounts for All the Kirtland Egyptian Papers Evidence]]. [^6]
- **The honest options are the Missing-Scroll and Catalyst theories — and "pivot" is a misnomer.** The Catalyst theory (the papyri prompted revelation, as the KJV prompted the JST) is grounded in how Joseph demonstrably "translated" elsewhere, not invented to dodge a debunking. McKay & Belnap's recent "wrestling with the natures of beings" reading (rooted in D&C 76) is the only proposal that fits all the evidence, though it is neither provable nor disprovable. See [[The Catalyst Theory Is Grounded in How Joseph Translated Not a Post-Hoc Rescue]]. [^7]
- **Method over apologetics: stay in the text, and admit assumptions.** Muhlestein recounts a couple whose Book of Abraham doubts dissolved once Steve Harper had them list only what is actually *known* vs. assumed; he charges that critics (and over-confident defenders alike) insert assumptions to fill gaps, and that "anyone who tells you they are approaching this objectively is lying." See [[Questioning Assumptions Is the First Defense of the Book of Abraham]] and [[Translating a Text Versus Interpreting a Drawing Are Different Claims]]. [^8]
