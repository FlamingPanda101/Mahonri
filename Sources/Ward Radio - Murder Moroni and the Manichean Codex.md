A **Ward Radio** episode in which host [[Cardon Ellis]] and guests [[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Austin Falter]] walk through a passage from the **Cologne Mani Codex** (discovered 1969) — specifically its "Apocalypse of Enosh" — and argue that it independently echoes the [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] / [[Nephi]] story: an angel directs a prophet to write hidden revelation on **plates of brass**, deposit them **in the wilderness**, and reserve them for a future unveiling. The hosts read this as a buried-record / angelic-revelation parallel surfacing in a long-misunderstood third-century religion ([[Mani|Manichaeism]]), translated by non-LDS scholar [[John C. Reeves]] decades after Joseph Smith, and they fold it into a wider riff on how the catch-all label "Gnostic" lets critics dismiss apocryphal material.

## Summary

- **The hook (Cologne Mani Codex).** Ellis opens with the claim that the *Cologne Mani Codex* — a tiny parchment codex discovered in 1969 and finally opened without disintegrating — contains a section, the **Apocalypse of Enosh**, that reads strikingly like a Book of Mormon plot beat [^1]. Barnes (introduced as author of *Key to the Keystone* and *The Lost Gems of Genesis*) is the resident "apocryphal" expert who supplies the material [^2].
- **Who Mani was.** Barnes sketches [[Mani]] as a third-century Parthian/Iranian prophet (of partly Jewish descent) who claimed to be the final prophet completing Zoroaster, Jesus, and Buddha, founder of the dualistic [[Mani|Manichaean]] religion. The hosts note that almost everything about it is known secondhand through hostile heresiologists (e.g. [[Hippolytus of Rome|Hippolytus]]) who simply branded him a heretic [^3].
- **A lost book that resurfaced.** Mani's liturgy lists ~five books he studied, one being the **Book of the Giants** — known of for centuries but unrecovered until it turned up among the Dead Sea Scrolls (the hosts date the realization to the 1940s). This is offered as a precedent: a Manichaean-named text long dismissed, later vindicated by manuscript discovery [^4].
- **The Apocalypse of Enosh passage.** Ellis shows the Koine Greek snippet. Key phrases: *tauta de panta ta apokrypha* ("all these hidden writings"), *graphēn epi ptychas chalkas* ("writing upon plates of brass/bronze"), and *apothou en … erēmon* ("put them away / hide them in the wilderness") [^5]. They flag *apokrypha* = "hidden writings" and *chalkas* = the brass/bronze metal word.
- **An angel is the speaker.** The hosts stress that, as in the theophany of [[1 Nephi 1.8|1 Nephi 1]], an **angel** is the one commanding the prophet — taking him to a northern region of immense mountains, telling him "the preeminent almighty one has sent me … write down these hidden things … upon bronze plates and deposit them in the wilderness … for my revelation which shall not pass away … until it is ready to be revealed to all the brethren" [^6].
- **The translation provenance.** To preempt accusations of LDS spin, Barnes attributes the rendering to **[[John C. Reeves]]**, *Heralds of That Golden Realm*, page 142 — "non-member professional Greek experts," translated long after Joseph Smith [^7].
- **The "sounding brass" lexical aside.** They tie *chalkos* to Paul's *ēchōn chalkos* ("sounding brass") in [[1 Corinthians 13.1|1 Corinthians 13]] to argue the underlying metal-word is the same, conceding scholars render it "bronze" (an alloy) while insisting it's the brass-plates vocabulary [^8].
- **The "it's just Gnostic" complaint.** Barnes' broader thesis: academics reflexively file anything labeled "hidden/apocryphal" under "Gnostic" and treat that as a discredited dustbin, an intellectually lazy catch-all — he likens it polemically to 1960s "they all look the same" dismissal [^9].
- **No doctrine, hence no motive to forge.** They emphasize the passage carries *no doctrine* and was discovered/translated long after Joseph Smith, so it can't be a believer's plant; it simply, in passing, narrates "the same type of story" [^10].
- **Manichaean survival and demise.** The Manichaeans spread east to western China (the hosts mention a "Manichaean Book of Enoch tapestry") and were largely destroyed by later Roman persecution and the rise of Islam [^11].
- **Long patristics tangent.** The conversation diverts into early-Christian "Survivor" — [[Tertullian]] (who coined *Trinitas*, later a [[The Montanists Were an Apocalyptic New Prophecy Movement|Montanist]] "heretic" for insisting on living prophecy), [[St. Nicholas]] possibly holding late authentic priesthood, the role of [[Bart Ehrman|Bart D. Ehrman]]'s *Lost Christianities* — arguing the "winners wrote the books" and that many condemned figures held proto-restoration views [^12].
- **The payoff.** Ellis's framing: if Tertullian or the Manichaeans had heard of Joseph Smith and the brass plates, "they'd have been on board" — the buried-brass-plates motif was, the hosts suggest, more widespread in antiquity than critics allow [^13].
