# Dan McClellan — The One Where God Lost to Another God

A short video exegeting [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3:27]], where the Moabite king [[Mesha]] sacrifices his heir on the city wall, "great fury" breaks out against Israel, and the Israelite-led coalition retreats with Elisha's prophecy of total victory unfulfilled. McClellan argues the most defensible reading is that the patron deity Chemosh intervened in his own territory and routed Yahweh — the inverse of the Sennacherib deliverance in [[2 Kings 19.18|2 Kings 18–19]]. As an academic ("data over dogma"), his data here cut both ways: they corroborate the divine-council / [[Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament|henotheism]] picture LDS apologists draw on, even as he himself frames the episode as a *failed prophecy* and resists any harmonizing or metaphysical gloss.

## Summary

- **The setup is a vassal revolt at the moment of dynastic weakness.** Ahab dies and his son Jehoram takes the throne; Moab, a vassal of the northern kingdom, revolts because a new king has not yet consolidated power. Jehoram recruits Jehoshaphat of Judah and the king of Edom to force Moab back into vassalage. A first invasion stalls and the coalition retreats after about a week. [^1]
- **Elisha delivers an unambiguous prophecy of total victory.** Brought in as the Yahwistic prophet, Elisha declares that Adonai will deliver Moab into the coalition's hand — which McClellan stresses can only mean restoring Moab to vassalage (or, at minimum, recouping the campaign's costs). The second invasion succeeds everywhere until the final city, where Mesha is besieged. [^2]
- **The child sacrifice precedes an immediate, unexplained collapse.** In [[2 Kings 3.26-27|2 Kings 3:27]] Mesha offers his heir as a burnt offering on the wall; "the very next words" are "there was great fury against Israel," and the coalition breaks off and returns home. Moab successfully throws off vassalage; the coalition gains neither vassalage nor plunder. [^3]
- **The lexical key is *qetzef gadol*.** The Hebrew *qetzef* ("fury"), in historical-narrative prose, *only* ever denotes **divine** fury. Elsewhere it is Adonai's fury against Israel — but here that reading is implausible, because nothing gives Adonai any reason to suddenly rage against Israel over a Moabite king's sacrifice. See [[Qetzef Gadol Is Always Divine Fury in Hebrew Narrative]]. [^4]
- **Critical scholars widely read the "great fury" as Chemosh's.** McClellan reports the consensus: the sacrifice "worked" — it catalyzed the intervention of Moab's patron deity Kamosh, whose fury drove the invaders out. "Adonai left home court advantage and lost." There is no other plausible way to account for the sequence. See [[Chemosh Defeats Yahweh Outside His Purview]]. [^5]
- **The episode is the deliberate mirror-image of Sennacherib (2 Kings 18–19).** Hezekiah revolts against Assyria at Sennacherib's accession; Sennacherib takes most of Judah but cannot take Jerusalem, where the messenger of Adonai destroys 185,000 troops and the king "sets out and returns home" — described with two of the *same verbs* used for the coalition's retreat in 2 Kings 3, and prefaced by an intercessory prayer (in Isaiah) paralleling the Elisha scene. The defending nation succeeds inside its own deity's purview; the invading nation fails outside it. See [[2 Kings 3 Mirrors Sennacherib in Reverse]]. [^6]
- **Yahweh's success against Egypt is the same logic, not a counterexample.** To the objection "but God succeeded invading Egypt in the Exodus," McClellan agrees and folds it in: the patron deity of one nation leaving his purview to invade and *judge the gods of* another nation — succeeding there, at least within the narrative. 2 Kings 3 is simply the case where that pattern **fails**. [^7]
- **The brevity is itself evidence of embarrassment.** Unlike the expansive treatment of chapters 18–19, 2 Kings 3 is "very curt... very short and succinct" — the author wants to move past an embarrassing failure, accounting for how Moab threw off vassalage despite a divinely guaranteed coalition: it was another deity's purview, Adonai lost home-court advantage, and the prophecy failed. [^8]
- **McClellan's "data over dogma" stance and its apologetic edge.** He insists the harmonizing readings have "no choice but to fabricate scenarios that are not in evidence" to preserve a sliver of "not impossible." The far-and-away most likely conclusion: the God of Israel mounted an invasion and was defeated in battle by another nation's patron deity. The same territorial/purview data underwrite LDS divine-council readings (see [[Henotheism and Divine Pedagogy in the Old Testament]], [[Textual Literalism Forces Acknowledging Other Gods]]), but McClellan himself frames the text as a *failed prophecy*, not as theology to be defended. [^9]
