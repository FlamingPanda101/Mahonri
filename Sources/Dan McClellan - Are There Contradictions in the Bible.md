# Dan McClellan — Are There Contradictions in the Bible?

A short methodological argument by biblical scholar [[Dan McClellan]] that the inerrantist claim "there are no contradictions in the Bible" collapses, under pressure, into the far weaker claim that it is *not impossible* that the contradictions can be reconciled — a standard so low it ceases to be evidence at all.

## Summary
- McClellan's central thesis: inerrantists are not actually prepared to defend "there are no contradictions," but only the retreat position that "it is not entirely outside the realm of possibility that there are no contradictions" — see [[Not Impossible Is Not the Standard for Contradiction]] [^1].
- The harmonizer's working definition — a contradiction exists *only if* there is no logically, humanly, or physically possible way to reconcile two propositions — licenses any unevidenced scenario, "lowering the evidentiary bar and burying it underground": see [[Harmonization Lowers the Evidentiary Bar]] [^2].
- Because textual interpretation is **not subject to empirical verification**, almost nothing is strictly impossible (secret codes, hidden divine meaning the author never intended), so "not impossible" can never settle the question — see [[Interpretation Is Not Empirically Verifiable]] [^3].
- The "horse in my backyard" analogy: it is *physically possible* a stranger's claim is true, yet one can be confident it is false; likewise one can confidently say two passages contradict — see [[The Horse in the Backyard Analogy]] [^4].
- McClellan lists concrete examples (two creation accounts, two accounts of Joseph's sale, who killed Goliath, the deaths of Ahaziah and of Judas, the Matthew/Luke nativities) and argues the harmonizer's real audience is **themselves**, defending a dogma rather than a peer-reviewable claim — see [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]] [^5] and [[Harmonization Defends a Dogma Not a Conclusion]] [^6].
