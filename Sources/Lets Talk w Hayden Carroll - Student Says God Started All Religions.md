# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Student Says God Started All Religions

An unscripted campus conversation in which LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] engages a student, **Alexa**, who holds a broadly **pluralist / perennialist** view: she was raised Catholic, "aligns heavily" with Daoist and Buddhist beliefs, wears the Virgin Mary, and concludes "I think that all of these are true." [^1] Her signature thesis is that **God started all the religions** and "everybody has morphed them" — there is one underlying "capital-T truth" everyone can access, and what divides the traditions is mere "noise." [^2][^3] Carroll's reply is consistently **exclusivist on truth-claims while inclusivist on truth-content**: any given religion can contain real "truth and good things," but rival religions make *contradictory* historical and doctrinal claims (above all, *was Jesus Christ resurrected?*) that cannot all be true, so a feeling-based pluralism cannot adjudicate them and collapses, morally, into practical atheism. [^4][^5] He closes, as in his other dialogues, by pivoting to the [[The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument|Book of Mormon dictation feat]] as the concrete miracle Alexa must now explain. [^6][^7] Compare his structured handling of the same objection cluster in [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]] (Blake Ostler) and his Ratio Christi presentation [[Hayden Carroll - Ratio Christi Campus Talk]].

## Summary
- **Atheism is incoherent (shared opening ground).** Before the pluralism turn, Carroll and Alexa agree against atheism: it denies the **law of non-contradiction** (which one must use even to deny it — "a square circle") and holds "something comes from nothing," and it cannot account for consciousness, which "cannot come from non-consciousness." The theistic answer is that "God has always been conscious." Alexa affirms she believes in "something greater." [^8][^9]
- **The pluralist thesis stated.** Alexa: "I think that all of these are true," and "I think that every religion has truth in it." She declines to affirm any single tradition's distinctive claims (e.g., she is unsure the Resurrection happened — "that's where they lose me"), treating commitment to one religion as "an act of arrogance" given that "you realistically don't know." [^1][^10]
- **Carroll's concession — truth *in* every religion.** Carroll grants the inclusivist half freely: as a Christian who confesses Jesus as Son of God, "I think Islam can have truth and good things in it. Same with Judaism... pick any religion or any worldview. I think you can draw good from it." The dispute is not whether other faiths contain truth but whether their *exclusive, contradictory* claims can all be true. See [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]]. [^4]
- **The genetic / geographic objection — and its rebuttal.** Alexa: Mexicans practice Catholicism "due to culture and colonialism," so her own inherited faith is "a little bit arbitrary." Carroll names this "the genetic argument... where you're born determines what religion you are," concedes it *influences* belief, but denies it disproves any religion: "you do have people who are not raised Christian who then find Christianity." See [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]] and [[The Genetic-Determinism Argument Against Moral Responsibility]]. [^11]
- **The Resurrection as the test case that breaks pluralism.** Carroll's central move: "*Was Jesus Christ resurrected?* The answer is not yes and no." If he rose, "that means Islam is untrue. Judaism is untrue," because Judaism denies he is the Messiah and Islam denies the crucifixion/sonship — "you can't have all of it at the same time." He grounds the Resurrection in the apostles' testimony and willingness to die (see [[Dying for a Belief Proves Sincerity Not Truth]] for the limits of that inference). This is the load-bearing rebuttal — see [[Contradictory Truth Claims Cannot All Be True - The Resurrection Test]]. [^5][^12]
- **"Noise" vs. "capital-T truth."** Alexa's reply: the differences that "differentiate each religion is all noise"; underneath is "a capital-T truth for everybody... the universal things we can all agree on," and "when there's contention, that's noise." Carroll presses that truth cannot contradict and that "Was Jesus resurrected?" is precisely the kind of either/or her framework cannot dissolve into noise. See [[The Noise Theory of Religious Difference]]. [^3][^13]
- **God-as-a-feeling cannot adjudicate.** Alexa repeatedly defines God experientially — "whatever people define that feeling as, it's like that's God," getting "a good feeling" from serving others. Carroll's counter: feeling closer to God "doesn't validate the truthfulness of their religion," and if two people's feelings of "good" contradict, the feeling cannot tell you which is right — pressed with the Satanist, the abortion case, and "what if they think God is Satan?" See [[A Feeling-Defined God Cannot Adjudicate Contradictions]]. [^14][^15]
- **Abstaining collapses into practical atheism.** When Alexa says she follows no religion because "they all contradict," Carroll: "if you're going to abstain from all religion... you're essentially a practical atheist," and "you can't justify morality." The Hitler / Mother Teresa contrast: both "doing what they think is right" cannot be distinguished on a feelings basis — "Didn't Hitler think he was doing the right thing?" Carroll insists Hitler acted as an atheist "justifying his actions," not in the name of a religion. See [[Relativism Kills Itself - The Hitler Argument Against Self-Created Reality]] and [[A Critic Must Ground His Moral Truth Claims or He Is Borrowing Them]]. [^16][^5]
- **The keystone pivot — the Book of Mormon as the miracle to explain.** Carroll offers "the best argument for Mormonism": in 65 days Joseph Smith produced 269,000 words — against Tolkien (109 words/day), Rowling (293), and Stephen King (650), Joseph averaged "over 4,100 words per day," in one draft, containing ancient Hebrew writing styles, with stylometry ("silometry") indicating ~20 authors, plus witnesses to the plates. "How did he produce this book in this way?" If God helped him, "then you'd have to be a Mormon." See [[The Book of Mormon Keystone Argument]], [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]], and [[Stylometry Shows Multiple Authors in the Book of Mormon]]. [^6][^7]
- **Where it lands.** Alexa is unmoved on commitment — "I think you could honestly throw a dart, pick one, and follow it" if it "resonates with you" — and ends maintaining that religions "separate us." Carroll closes irenically: "I think we have something to learn from each other," declining to tell her "the wisdom that you found is wrong." [^17][^18]
