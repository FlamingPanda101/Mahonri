# Informed Saints — Are Book of Mormon Names Real?

The [[Informed Saints]] hosts interview [[Matthew Bowen]], professor of ancient scripture at BYU-Hawaii, on the **onomasticon** (name-stock) of the Book of Mormon — whether the names are "real or fantastical," and whether the text's authors demonstrably *understood the meanings* of the Hebrew and Egyptian names they used.

## Summary
- Bowen's distinctive thesis is not merely that Book of Mormon names are *attested* in the ancient Near East, but that the narrators **knew what the names meant and played on them** (paronomasia/wordplay) in the way characters are introduced and their stories told [^1]. He builds on prior scholars — [[Hugh Nibley]], [[John Tvedtnes]], [[Paul Hoskisson]], [[John Gee]], [[Terry Szink]], [[John Sorenson]] — saying he "stands on the shoulders of giants" [^2].
- A recurring structural device: a character is introduced with an odd, name-revealing detail (Alma "was a young man"; Nephi "born of goodly parents"; Enos "knowing my father that he was a just man"; Abish converted "on account of a remarkable vision of her father"), which only makes sense once the Hebrew/Egyptian meaning is recovered [^3].
- The scholarship has crossed into mainstream venues: Bowen's "Striking While the Irony Is Hot" appears in *Perspectives on Latter-day Saint Names and Naming*, published by **Routledge**, not an apologetic press [^4].
- Place-names show the same wordplay: **Jershon** (Hebrew *yarash*, "to inherit") is given to Lamanite converts "for an inheritance"; **Zarahemla** (*zera* "seed" + *hemla* "compassion/mercy") sits in passages emphasizing compassion [^5].
- A theological payoff: the **King Benjamin** enthronement speech (Benjamin = "son of the right hand") "democratizes" royal coronation language from Psalm 2 and Psalm 110, making every covenanter a son/daughter at God's right hand [^6].
