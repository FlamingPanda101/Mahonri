A **WARD RADIO** episode hosted by **[[Cardon Ellis]]** with **[[Kwaku El]]**, Drew Scarborough, **[[Austin Falter]]** (Fault Media), and Joseph Gongbass. Unlike the exegetical [[Ward Radio - Kindly Correcting Anti-Mormon Claims Against Baptisms for the Dead|"Kindly Correcting" episode]], this one is about **public perception and missionary strategy** — how to "vibe check" baptism for the dead when TikTok/Instagram critics (not evangelicals or ex-Mormons, but "just a vibes perspective") call it weird.

## Summary

- **The "second Mormon moment" and a new kind of critique.** Kwaku frames 2025 as a second wave of Mormon cultural attention (after the early-2010s Romney/Imagine Dragons moment), but now the critique on TikTok/Reels is purely vibes-based: people post a photo of a baptismal font and say "these guys are weird" [^1].
- **You can't make it less weird — so lean in.** Kwaku's thesis: the ordinance *is* weird ("a giant hot tub held up by million-dollar golden oxen") and cannot be sanitized; the move is to **own the weirdness and "gaslight everybody else"** that they have weird rituals too — see [[Normalize the Esoteric by Reframing LDS Rituals as Universal]] [^2].
- **Everyone already performs ritual.** The worked example: a Texas high-school football game where fans weep through the national anthem is a *ritual* whose religion is "American exceptionalism" (and "manifest destiny"). Normalizing ritual generally defuses the weirdness of proxy baptism [^3].
- **A rebrand: "ancestral baptism."** Austin Falter repeatedly proposes renaming it "ancestral baptism / baptism for your ancestors," likening it to East/Southeast Asian ancestral veneration so "all the vibe-check problems go away" [^4].
- **Why it actually unsettles people: latent belief in spiritual authority.** The panel argues people are weirded out *because* they half-suspect it might be real ("you're admitting we have the power... to baptize people in his name") — many are functionally "polytheistic," assuming you get the afterlife you believe in, so a proxy claim on the dead lands as a real claim [^5].
- **Reaching the New Age audience.** Kwaku's strategic pitch: stop fighting "uptight Protestants" and court the large, "cool" New Age community — energy healing ≈ priesthood power, crystals ≈ seer stones, Atlantis/Lemuria ≈ Book of Mormon, channeled texts ≈ the D&C and Revelation. "Translate" the restored gospel into New Age idiom; cohesion "will sort itself out" because "the idea has to win first" [^6].
- **Cardon's qualifier — relaxing the uptight vs. disciplining the chaotic.** Cardon and Austin counter that on mission it was "easier to relax an uptight person than to discipline a chaotic and non-disciplined person," so a New-Age convert may need more structure; Kwaku replies the untouched New-Age millions are a bigger field than expecting mass Protestant conversions — see [[Normalize the Esoteric by Reframing LDS Rituals as Universal]] [^7].
- **The biblical anchor not to lose in a rebrand.** Cardon warns a rebrand shouldn't sever the King James wording of [[1 Corinthians 15.29|1 Corinthians 15:29]] ("baptized for the dead"): it is "the most unexplainably biblical religious ritual," which "no other church can explain" and they "avoid like the plague" — except [[Tertullian]], who *witnesses* (rather than refutes) it [^8].
- **The prophetic vibe.** The panel lands on simply being "straight up front": a prophet telling the worldwide youth their "greatest responsibility is to gather scattered Israel on both sides of the veil," offered as a choice — leaning into the doctrine rather than hiding the font [^9].
