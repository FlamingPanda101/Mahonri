# The Stick of Joseph - The Ultimate Guide to Freemasonry and the Temple

A four-chapter, ~5-hour conversation with [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] (with Jay Davis and Hayden) laying out what the LDS temple endowment is, what Freemasonry is, where Freemasonry came from, and how they relate. The thesis: the endowment's pattern of ascent, sacred clothing, new name, and covenant is anciently attested (text and art), so the "Joseph cribbed it from Masonry" charge fails — though some Masonic *forms* were repurposed as a liturgical vocabulary.

## Summary
- **Ordinance creation gives new meaning to existing acts.** Creating any ordinance "takes activities or actions that could have other meanings or no meaning at all in some other context and gives it a new meaning" (cf. going under water → baptism; bread and wine → Eucharist). On this view Joseph could repurpose Masonic forms without their being the *source* of the temple's content.[^1] See [[Ordinance Creation Recontextualizes Ordinary Actions]].
- **Deuteronomy and the endowment share a vassal-treaty structure.** Day argues the book of Deuteronomy (and King Benjamin's speech) follows the suzerain-vassal treaty form — preamble, history, stipulations, witnesses, penalties, deposition/recital — and so does the endowment; this is presented as an ancient covenant-making pattern, not a Masonic borrowing.[^2] See [[The Vassal-Treaty Covenant Pattern Links Deuteronomy King Benjamin and the Endowment]].
- **The Enoch ascent and garments of glory.** Drawing on Nibley, the hosts cite *2/3 Enoch* and Hekhalot (Merkabah) mysticism: Enoch ascends through palaces, is anointed and clothed, and "looked like one of the glorious ones" — changing clothing to change one's ontology, paralleled to 1 Cor 15 (corruption putting on incorruption) and Rev 3:21.[^3][^4]
- **The Knights Templar safe-lodging origin of the Masonic lodge.** Following John J. Robinson's *Born in Blood*, the Templars ran a medieval pilgrim banking/safe-lodging network requiring a name, a token, and a password at each lodge — a three-part authentication echoed in Masonic degrees and read as a journey of ascent to the Temple Mount under guarded passwords.[^5][^6] See [[The Templar Safe-Lodging Network Is a Candidate Origin of the Masonic Lodge]].
- **Nibley's "robes of a false priesthood."** Hugh Nibley's graduation-prayer gibe ("clad in the robes of a false priesthood… degrees that have no meaning in thy sight") frames academic regalia as a desacralized temple vestige; sacred clothing across traditions (priests, monks, Sikhs, Parsis) is normal, and mockery of LDS garments is a double standard.[^7]
