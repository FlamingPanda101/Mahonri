# Ward Radio - The Identity of the Elect Lady of 2 John

A [[Ward Radio]] interview — host [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Jonah Barnes]] — featuring BYU papyrologist [[Lincoln H. Blumell]], who argues that the "elect lady" addressed in [[2 John 1.1|2 John]] was not a metaphor for a church but a real, named woman: *Eklekte* (Latin *Electa*). The grammatical key is a single dropped definite article, recoverable by the same methods Blumell uses to edit documentary papyri from Roman Egypt — making 2 John the only New Testament letter whose principal addressee is a named woman.

## Summary

- **The thesis.** The opening *eklektē kyria*, rendered "elect lady" for ~1,800 years and read as a personified church, is grammatically broken Greek. Restored as *eklektē tē kyria* — "to **the** lady Eklekte" — it names a woman. See [[Eklekte Is a Named Woman Not the Elect Lady of 2 John]]. [^1]
- **Blumell's qualification.** His research is editing unpublished Greek papyri, inscriptions, wills, and especially private letters from Egypt — constantly emending orthographic and grammatical errors in unspaced, unpunctuated ancient text. That habituation is what let him see 2 John 1:1 as itself an error needing emendation, not a metaphor needing interpretation. [^2]
- **The earliest commentator agreed.** [[Clement of Alexandria]] (2nd c.) explicitly said the letter was written to a woman *Eklekte nomine* ("Eclecta by name"); he survives only via a 6th-c. Latin epitome and had been dismissed — Blumell says he was right. [^3]
- **Simple names look like adjectives.** *Eklektē* is the feminine adjective "chosen/elect" but is also an attested Roman-period woman's name (Blumell collects Greek and Latin inscriptions). Like *Stephanos* = "Stephen"/"crown," only context disambiguates — and modern critical editions (Nestle-Aland) pre-decide by adding accents, capitals, and word spacing absent from the manuscripts. [^4]
- **The letter-opening formula.** Thousands of Roman-period Egyptian letters open: sender → recipient's name → definite article → honorific *kyrios/kyria*. [[3 John]] ("the elder to Gaius") fits exactly; *kyria* (only at 2 John 1 and 5 in the NT) appears in letters only as a substantive title, never a common noun — so "elect lady" is anomalous, "the lady Eklekte" is standard. [^5]
- **Haplography.** *Eklektē tē kyria* — the name ends *-tē*, the article is *tē*; a scribe copying unspaced text dropped one reduplicated syllable. Blumell: in any ordinary papyrus this emendation would be uncontroversial; only because it is scripture do "the rules change." The fuller reading even survives in a *family* of manuscripts (deliberate copying, not accident). [^6]
- **Verse 13 confirms rather than refutes.** Critics say v. 13's "elect sister" would force a shared name; but v. 13 lacks the definite article, so it can *only* be the adjective, while v. 1 (with the article) is the name. Blumell reads v. 13 as a name-pun ("your sister, also *choice*"), like the puns on Peter and Onesimus. [^7]
- **It dissolves the forgery theory.** Once the address is read correctly, 2 John matches 3 John's form, undercutting the scholarship that made 2 John a second author's pseudonymous imitation. See [[The 2 John Forgery Theory Rests on Misreading the Address]]. [^8]
- **Fits a house-church reading.** v. 10's "do not receive [false teachers] into the house" suits *Eklekte* as a [[Patrons and Patronesses of Early House Churches|patroness]] hosting a [[Early Christian House Churches|house church]] — alongside Lydia (Acts 16) and Nympha (Col 4:15, herself mis-rendered as the man "Nymphas"). Parallels the [[Junia Is a Woman Called Prominent Among the Apostles|Junia]] case and the broader thesis that [[Women Led the First-Century Church as Apostles Deacons and Patrons|women led the first-century church]]. [^9]
- **Method as the moral.** Blumell's repeated lesson: get into the primary sources in the original language, and hold even "settled" consensus with skepticism — a new papyrus can force a paradigm shift (cf. [[Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point]]). The hosts extend this with a riff that Latter-day Saints' epistemic culture prizes primary sources and transparency. [^10]
- **Sources & reception.** Blumell published the case as *Lady Eklekte: The Lost Woman of the New Testament* (Fortress Press); he reports overwhelmingly positive scholarly feedback when the argument is laid out, with predictable "how could we have missed this / don't touch the Bible" pushback. Hosts' comedic asides (the "pirate Bible," a naming-contest bounty, Shepherd of Hermas / canon tangents) are entertainment, not argument. [^11]
