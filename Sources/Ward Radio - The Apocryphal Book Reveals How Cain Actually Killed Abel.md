# Ward Radio — The Apocryphal Book Reveals How Cain Actually Killed Abel!

A Ward Radio episode in which host **[[Cardon Ellis]]** interviews **[[Jonah Barnes]]** about the chapter of his book *The Lost Gems of Genesis* on Cain and Abel. Barnes's thesis: the terse Genesis account ("Cain rose up… and slew Abel") is silent on method and motive, but a cluster of apocryphal Adam-and-Eve texts supply a detailed, gruesome narrative — and Joseph Smith's [[Moses 5.10-11|Book of Moses]] drops cryptic "hints" (the throat oath, "murder to get gain," the stolen inheritance) that line up with them. Notably, Cardon plays skeptic at the end, pressing Barnes on the *dating* of the sources.

## Summary
- **The field, the mountain, and the luring of Abel.** Apocryphal tradition has Adam and Eve, forewarned by a dream that Cain would drink Abel's blood, banish Cain "down to the field" (the locus of all evil, opposed to the holy mountain). Cain lures the wary Abel down by feigning need — "come bless my fields and my flocks." See [[Cain Weaponizes Empathy to Lure Abel - The First Crybully]].
- **How the murder happened — the throat.** Multiple accounts (Cave of Treasures, the Armenian/Greek/Georgian lives of Adam and Eve, the Zohar, the Quran) describe Cain struggling to discover how to kill, finally sawing through Abel's windpipe with a sharp flint stone Satan helped him find — some adding that he drank the blood to seize Abel's life (cf. [[Leviticus 17]], "the blood is the life"). Joseph's "swear unto me by thy throat" is read as the matching hint. See [[The Apocrypha Reveals How Cain Killed Abel by the Throat]].
- **Master Mahan and "murder to get gain."** Joseph's Cain becomes "Master Mahan," master of the "great secret" — kill to obtain Abel's flocks, wife, and the patriarchal inheritance of Adam. "Gain" makes no sense from Genesis alone (Cain gains nothing) but is exactly what the apocrypha supply. The vault already treats the inversion-of-sacrifice frame in [[Murder to Get Gain Inverts the Order of Sacrifice]].
- **Joseph's choice of battlefield — the Adam-and-Eve literature.** Barnes's larger argument: in an 1820s–30s culture obsessed with *early Christianity*, Joseph chose to expand *Adam*, and a slew of Adam-and-Eve documents (Conflict of Adam and Eve, Cave of Treasures) surfaced after his death focused on the very figure he emphasized. See [[Joseph Smith Chose the Adam-and-Eve Battlefield Before the Texts Surfaced]].
- **The dating caveat (Cardon's skeptical turn).** Cardon asks for the *oldest* witness to the flint-throat detail; Barnes concedes it is the Cave of Treasures (Ephrem the Syrian, born ~306, died 373; manuscript tradition ~6th–7th c.), and that the Dead Sea Scrolls and the canonical text say nothing of the method — so Joseph's Moses is, for Barnes, the most "dependable" source, and the apocryphal convergence is suggestive rather than dispositive.
