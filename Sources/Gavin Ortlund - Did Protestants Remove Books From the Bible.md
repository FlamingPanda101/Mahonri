# Gavin Ortlund — Did Protestants Remove Books From the Bible?

A response by [[Gavin Ortlund]] (Reformed Baptist theologian, host of *Truth Unites*) to the charge — pressed by Catholic apologist Alex (Voice of Reason) on Lila Rose's podcast against [[Wes Huff]] — that the 66-book Protestant canon "cannot be found anywhere in the first 1500 years" and that Protestants therefore *removed* the deuterocanon/Apocrypha from the Bible. Ortlund argues the opposite: the deuterocanonical books were **disputed all the way up to the Council of Trent (1546)**, so "you can't remove books from a place they don't yet occupy." [^1] He marshals patristic evidence in three movements — [[Jerome]] in the West, the shorter-canon tradition in the East, and the earliest 2nd-century witnesses ([[Melito of Sardis]], [[Origen]]) — to show the Protestant Old Testament has deep historical precedent.

This is a **Protestant** source. It is an *ally* against the Catholic/Orthodox claim that the wider canon was settled and unanimous, but a *foil* for the LDS open-canon position, which differs from both: Ortlund's whole project is to defend a *narrower, closed* Hebrew-aligned canon as the one "closest to the apostles," whereas the LDS view treats canon boundaries as porous and extendable by living revelation.

## Summary

- **The charge restated and reversed.** Ortlund concedes that strict 66-book lists are rare early, but counters that the Roman Catholic canon "was not settled until the Council of Trent," a point "conceded by good Roman Catholic scholarship" (he quotes the *Catholic Encyclopedia* on medieval "hesitation" about the deuterocanon, and notes few medievals — even [[Thomas Aquinas]] — unequivocally affirmed their canonicity). [^2] See [[The Deuterocanon Was Disputed Until Trent Not Removed]].
- **Jerome held a consistent narrower canon.** In his preface to Samuel/Kings, Jerome defends a 22-book Hebrew canon and labels Judith, Tobit, and 1–2 Maccabees "apocryphal." Scholar [[Ed Gallagher]] confirms Jerome "consistently excluded the deuterocanonical books" his whole career — the debate is only whether his *earlier* view was even wider, never that he later accepted them as first-tier. [^3] See [[Jerome Consistently Excluded the Deuterocanon]].
- **The "Jerome submitted to the judgment of the churches" line is debunked.** The famous quote (*Against Rufinus*) concerns two **Greek translations of Daniel** (the LXX vs. Theodotion), not the Catholic-vs-Protestant canon; Jerome states his *own* judgment that the LXX Daniel "with proper judgment is repudiated," and later letters (e.g., Letter 107, after the 382 council) still warn against the apocrypha. [^4] See [[Jerome Did Not Submit on the Canon - The Daniel Translation Quote]].
- **The two-tier canon resolves the citation confusion.** Many fathers had a *tier-one* (canonical, for dogma) and *tier-two* (edifying, readable) distinction; deuterocanonical books often sat in tier two and could even be "cited as scripture" while being explicitly excluded from the canon list. So a father quoting Sirach proves nothing until you see his canon list and his stated classification. [^5] See [[The Two-Tier Canon Explains Patristic Citation of the Apocrypha]].
- **The East independently carried a shorter canon.** [[Cyril of Jerusalem]], [[Athanasius]], Gregory of Nazianzus, Amphilochius, Epiphanius, and John of Damascus all transmit a 22-book Hebrew canon (with minor wrinkles: Baruch folded into Jeremiah, additions to Daniel, occasional omission of Esther). Cyril *forbids* reading the apocrypha and grounds his list in "apostolic tradition." [^6] See [[The Eastern Church Carried a Shorter Old Testament Canon]].
- **The earliest lists tilt to the shorter canon.** Eusebius preserves [[Melito of Sardis]]'s 2nd-century list — "perhaps the earliest" — which excludes most deuterocanon and which Melito frames as Christian ("concerning our entire faith"), having traveled to Palestine to research the Jewish canon; Eusebius likewise reports [[Origen]]'s 22-book canon, and the 2nd-century Bryennios list agrees. [^7] See [[The Earliest Canon Lists Are the Shorter Canon - Melito and Origen]].
- **The real situation: two early traditions, neither dominant by 1500.** Protestants follow the Jerome / Eastern stream; Rome follows the [[Augustine]] / North-African-council stream (Hippo, Carthage). The deuterocanon's status was contested even among 16th-century Catholic cardinals (Cajetan, Ximenes), who appealed to Jerome. Ortlund's ultimate claim is not that any one father is decisive but that the shorter canon "is most likely to be the earliest, closest to the apostles." [^8] See [[Two Rival Canon Traditions Neither Settled Before Trent]].
