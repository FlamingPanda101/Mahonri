# Ward Radio - The Great Apostasy Deep Dive with Mike Day and Dave Butler

**Full title:** "The Great Apostasy" deep dive (apostasy episode 2) on Ward Radio
**Length:** ~3:43:00 (the hosts say "close to four hours")
**Format:** Slide-driven presentation by Mike Day with running commentary/Q&A from David Butler and hosts Hayden and Jackson Paul. Covers roughly 33 AD to ~400 AD only; the Bible-canon, schisms, and ecumenical councils are explicitly deferred to a future episode.

## Summary

- **The case for a Great Apostasy is built from "evidences," not asserted causes.** Day opens with [[Noel Reynolds]]'s car-wreck analogy from *Early Christians in Disarray*: the doctrinal changes and schisms are the *debris* of an accident, evidence that an apostasy occurred but not its cause. He explicitly leans toward apostasy as a **gradual falling away** (per [[Todd Compton]]'s Greek etymology of *apostasia* = "to stand away") rather than an open mutiny, while granting the New Testament shows open rebellions too. [^1] See [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event]], [[Car Wreck Analogy for Diagnosing Apostasy]].
- **The earliest Christianity was already plural and in conflict — visible inside the New Testament itself.** Day and Butler walk through the [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]]–[[James the brother of Jesus|James]]–[[Peter|Cephas]] tensions: the Galatians circumcision fight, Paul rebuking Peter "to his face" at Antioch, and Paul's wish (Gal 5:12) that the Judaizers "emasculate themselves." They argue the "one perfect tree / dropped mirror" model of church history is false; there were multiple competing Christianities from the start (Docetists, Ebionites, Marcionites, Gnostics). [^2] See [[Early Christianity Was Plural Not One Perfect Tree]], [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict]], [[New Testament Books Reflect Rival Communities]].
- **Acts 15 shows James, not Peter, rendering the decision — challenging the LDS "Peter is in charge" reading.** Butler argues a plain historical reading makes the movement Jerusalem-headquartered under James the brother of Jesus, and that "I judge" (Acts 15:19) is James calling the shot, a reading LDS writers like [[Bruce R. McConkie|McConkie]] and Robert J. Matthews resist. [^3] See [[Acts 15 Shows James Deciding Not Peter]], [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles]].
- **Heresy was often decided by votes and imperial politics, not by demonstrable truth.** Day's "I only need three votes" city-council anecdote frames how Marcion, Arius, and others were ruled heretical; he notes scholars (Catholic and evangelical) concede political decisions occurred. The bishop's office (*episkopos*, "overseer") and monarchical episcopacy solidify in the 2nd century, and Roman primacy is claimed only from the late 2nd century onward (Clement 96 AD, Ignatius, then Leo I c. 400). [^4] See [[Heresy Was Decided by Votes Not Demonstrable Truth]], [[Rise of the Monarchical Bishop]], [[Roman Primacy Was a Later Claim Not First-Century]].
- **The decisive corruption is the Hellenization of God: Greek philosophy reshaping the doctrine of God.** Following [[Richard Hopkins]] (*How Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God*) and [[Barry Bickmore]] (*Restoring the Ancient Church*), Day traces Parmenides → Heraclitus' *logos* → Plato's forms → Aristotle's "unmoved mover" into the apologists ([[Justin Martyr]], [[Origen]], [[Augustine]]), producing an immaterial, impassible, *homoousios* God codified at Nicaea (325) and the Athanasian Creed. Butler argues the creeds "apply a vocabulary" to the New Testament without explaining anything, defending the result as "a mystery." [^5] See [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]], [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]], [[Nicene and Athanasian Creeds Apply Labels Not Explanations]], [[Homoousios Same Substance Not Same Person]].
- **The loss of the divine-family theology is the most "pernicious" part of the apostasy.** Drawing on Butler's *In the Language of Adam* and the Ugaritic texts (1929), they reconstruct an original Israelite pantheon — El the father in his tent, his consort, the divine council of sons, Yahweh the son who inherited Israel — and argue creedal *ex nihilo* monotheism estranged believers from a literal Heavenly Father. [^6] See [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]], [[Ugaritic Texts Reveal Israels Original Pantheon]], [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]].
- **The apostasy may nonetheless have "preserved the text."** Day offers a deliberate steelman: the same imperial-Christian apparatus that corrupted doctrine also preserved the Bible, the cathedrals, and the tradition — Jerome's Vulgate (c. 405) and the Catholic Church guarding the canon for a thousand years. He notes Joseph Smith condemned only the *creeds* as an abomination, not all of Christianity. The canon itself is largely a reaction to [[Marcion]], with [[Athanasius]]'s 367 Easter letter being the earliest surviving list of the 27 NT books. [^7] See [[Apostasy Preserved the Text Steelman]], [[The New Testament Canon Is a Reaction to Marcion]].
- **Secondary themes:** Montanus and the "prophecy has ceased" overcorrection (c. 170 AD) prefigures the minister who told Joseph Smith all revelation ended with the apostles; the Didache as the earliest church manual (two-ways, bread/wine prayers, traveling prophets); Donatism vs. the LDS vertical/horizontal model of priesthood; and McConkie's argument that the apostasy was complete when the *keys* departed. [^8] See [[Montanus and the Overcorrection Against Prophecy]], [[The Didache as Earliest Church Manual]], [[Donatism and the LDS Model of Priesthood Validity]], [[Apostasy Was Complete When the Keys Departed]].
