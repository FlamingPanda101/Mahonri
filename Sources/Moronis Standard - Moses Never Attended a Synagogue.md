# Moses Never Attended a Synagogue — And Neither Did Anyone Before the Maccabees

Alex Arnold of *Moroni's Standard* defends the Book of Mormon against the charge that its religious world is anachronistic. His pivot is the synagogue: the word never appears in the Old Testament, and archaeologist [[Yonatan Adler]] (Ariel University, Yale University Press) documents that synagogue buildings, ritual immersion pools (mikva'ot), tefillin, kashrut, and the rest of the Second-Temple "apparatus" leave no significant trace in the material record before roughly 200 BC — the Hasmonean/Maccabean period. The Judaism Jesus argued against was, by the standard of the tradition it claimed to represent, recent. The payoff: critics who say the Book of Mormon "doesn't match ancient Judaism" are measuring it against the wrong period by ~400 years, since [[Lehi]] left Jerusalem (~600 BC) carrying pre-reform scripture before any of that apparatus existed.

## Summary

- **The synagogue is post-exilic, not Mosaic.** The word is absent from the entire Hebrew Bible, and there is no confirmed physical evidence for synagogue buildings in Israel before the 1st century BC. [[Yonatan Adler]] and [[Lee Levine]] (Hebrew University) reached this conclusion independently, both through Yale University Press [^1][^2].
- **The whole Second-Temple "apparatus" appears late and together.** Kashrut (pig bones are common at pre-exilic Israelite sites), tefillin (not before the Dead Sea Scrolls era, ~150 BC), and mikva'ot (essentially absent before the 1st century BC, then abundant) all solidify only in the Hellenistic/Hasmonean period as a national-identity project under pressure from Greek cultural dominance [^3][^4].
- **Ezra–Nehemiah does not rescue the early-date view.** Even granting that Ezra's public Torah reading was a real mass-recommitment (or merely an Aramaic translation aid), the material culture of the following generations shows no widespread sustained observance — it does not appear at scale until the Hellenistic period [^5][^6].
- **This reframes the Gospels and the Book of Mormon.** In Mark 7 the Pharisees indict Jesus over "the tradition of the elders," not the law of Moses; Jesus answers with Isaiah ("teaching for doctrines the commandments of men"). [[Margaret Barker]]'s textual reconstruction of suppressed pre-exilic temple theology converges with Adler's archaeology from a different discipline. The Book of Mormon reflects the *pre-reform* world (divine council, enthroned God), not the late Second-Temple system [^7][^8].
- **Honest framing and limits.** Arnold concedes Adler does not argue the Torah is a fabrication (the laws and texts are old; the *gap* is between law written and law widely practiced), that Barker's reconstruction is her own and she reads it as fulfilled in Christ, and that resemblance alone does not prove descent. He explicitly says the archaeology dismantles a specific objection rather than proving the Book of Mormon true [^9][^10].
