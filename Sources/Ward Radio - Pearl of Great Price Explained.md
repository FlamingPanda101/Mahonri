# Ward Radio - Pearl of Great Price Explained

Ward Radio interviews [[Jonah Barnes]] (the panel's resident "associate professor of all things apocryphal") about his new book *The Lost Gems of Genesis*, which opens by "debunking what people think the Pearl of Great Price is." The episode's thesis is that almost everyone — members included — misunderstands the Pearl of Great Price: it is not a single revealed book, it was never titled by [[Joseph Smith]], and the label "Joseph Smith Translation" applied to the [[Book of Moses]] is simply wrong. Barnes then floats his speculative centerpiece — that the three 1830 Book-of-Moses revelations may be Joseph quietly reconstructing the content of the lost 116 pages (the Book of Lehi) — and corrects the popular "fullness of the gospel" proof-text along the way. This source heavily overlaps the existing [[Stephen Smoot|Steven Smoot]] PoGP-history cluster ([[The Pearl of Great Price Is the Most Unstable Volume of LDS Scripture]], [[Canonization Is a Community Process Not a Unilateral Prophetic Act]]); the genuinely new material is the "annidiction" coinage, the 116-pages-as-Book-of-Moses theory, and the D&C 20:9 misreading.

## Summary

- **Nobody knows what the Pearl of Great Price is.** Barnes frames the whole book around the claim that "everybody thinks they know what the pearl of great price is and everybody's wrong." It is one of the four standard works — "25% of our cannon" — yet it is "a conglomeration of a few different writings… several different documents all kind of smooshed into one" [^1][^2][^3].
- **The anatomy: revelations given separately, decades apart.** The [[Book of Moses]] is three distinct revelations (to Joseph the seer; on Moses; concerning Adam), given by different scribes from **June–December 1830**, right after the Book of Mormon was published. The [[Book of Abraham]] came ~1835; Joseph Smith—Matthew, the [[Joseph Smith History 1.68-69|Joseph Smith—History]], and the Articles of Faith (in the 1838 Wentworth letter) came later still — "all written at different times in different places" [^4][^5].
- **"Annidictions," not predictions.** Barnes coins the word *annidiction* for a backward-looking revelation: a prophet predicts the future, but a seer "annidicts" what already happened. The three 1830 revelations are annidictions — they claim past events (Adam, Enoch) as restorations of ancient text, unlike the future-facing rest of the volume — illustrated by Nephi son of Helaman at the judgment seat in Helaman [^6][^7].
- **It was never the "Joseph Smith Translation."** "He never said 'I'm going to re-translate Genesis.' That's wrong. He didn't do that." The actual JST / inspired version came *later*. The 1830 revelations came with no titles ("a revelation… to the elders of the church") and no explanation for why Joseph received them [^8][^9].
- **The 116-pages theory (flagged "just a theory").** Doesn't the Book of Mormon contain the fullness of the gospel — so why reveal anything more? Barnes' answer: the lost 116 pages (the Book of Lehi) were Genesis-heavy, since [[Lehi]] devotes ~63% of his 5,552 Book-of-Mormon words to Genesis. Joseph couldn't re-produce the 116 pages directly (the forgers around [[Lucy Harris]] were waiting), so "suspiciously" he begins receiving Genesis-centered revelation weeks later — possibly the same content under a new form [^10][^11][^12][^13].
- **The "fullness of the gospel" misconception.** The Book of Mormon introduction's "fullness of the gospel" line (added 1981) traces to [[D&C 20.6-9|D&C 20:6-9]] (1829), but that passage is *recounting [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]]'s 1823 visit* and the plates as they then existed — not certifying the published 1829 text. By 1829 "we only get 60% of" the 1823 document, missing the lost 116 pages and the sealed portion [^14][^15][^16][^17].
- **"Book of Moses" is a posthumous label.** Joseph never titled or assembled these as "the Book of Moses." [[Franklin D. Richards]] "dug up these old revelations" into an 1851 missionary pamphlet; canonization came ~30 years later. The OT1 manuscript headings (per the Joseph Smith Papers) read "a revelation given to Joseph the Revelator," "revelation given to the elders… on the first book of Moses," and "the revelation concerning Adam" — the name "Moses" comes from the opening line about Moses on the mountain, not from Joseph [^18][^19][^20][^21].
- **"Oops, all berries."** Barnes' running metaphor: if the Book of Mormon is Captain Crunch, the Pearl of Great Price is "oops, all berries" — densely packed with apocryphal parallels, the "DLC / director's cut / expansion pack" of the gospel. [[Richard Bushman]] called the Book of Moses "the most incidental" of Joseph's revelations; [[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]] (per Barnes) the most ambitious [^22][^23][^24].
