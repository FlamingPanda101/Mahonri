Ward Radio episode in which host [[Cardon Ellis]] interviews [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah Barnes]] of Plain & Precious Publishing (author of *Key to the Keystone* and *The Lost Gems of Genesis*). The episode has two halves. The first reads [[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:10-11]] as an "epic rap battle" between Adam and Eve — Adam attempts a celebratory chiasmus but omits the word *transgression*, and Eve rebuts him in the next verse by restoring transgression to the center of the figure, asserting the Fall's necessity. It also previews the *Key to the Keystone* argument that the apocryphal *Life of Adam and Eve* parallels and fills the narrative gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses revelation. The second half is an extended roast of BYU professor [[Taylor Halverson]], who reportedly declined unread to read Barnes's book, framed through the Anton Ego "the new needs friends" monologue from *Ratatouille* as a critique of LDS academic "niceness culture."

## Summary
- **The rap battle premise.** Barnes argues that Moses 5 (LDS Pearl of Great Price) contains two surviving chiasms, including one in [[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:10]] where Adam blesses God "because of my transgression" — a figure that *looks like a chiasm missing one line*. The missing element is the second mention of *transgression*; Barnes proposes Adam could not bear to "relive that transgression," so the figure is incomplete [^1][^2].
- **Eve completes it.** In [[Moses 5.10-11|Moses 5:11]] Eve "rebuts" Adam: "Were it not for our transgression we never should have had seed... and known good and evil, and the joy of our redemption." Putting *transgression* at the front, she completes the figure and voices the fortunate-fall logic — fitting, Barnes notes, because Eve is the one who took the fruit and "knew the fall was good." See [[Eve Completes Adam's Broken Chiasmus in Moses 5]] [^3][^4].
- **Chiasmus framing.** Barnes recaps that [[Jack Welch|Jack Welch]] discovered chiasmus in the Book of Mormon in the 1970s, with [[Alma 36.1|Alma 36]] as the monstrous flagship example pivoting on Jesus Christ; not all chiasms must be huge, just as not all poetry rhymes (haiku, meter). See [[Chiasmus as Evidence of Ancient Authorship in the Book of Mormon]] and [[Chiasmus Interruption as Forgery Marker]] [^5][^6].
- **Apocrypha fills the gaps.** Previewing *Key to the Keystone*, Barnes maps Moses 5's sequence (cast out → sacrifice → angel/voice declares the gospel → [a gap] → Adam celebrates the resurrection → Eve's wise conclusion) onto the *Life of Adam and Eve* and related apocrypha, which fill Moses 5's "hole" (the resurrection being preached) while Moses 5 fills theirs (Adam's celebration). The manuscripts are late (~11th c.) but the traditions, he says, "agree with Joseph Smith in impossible ways." See [[The Life of Adam and Eve Fills the Gaps in Joseph Smith's Moses 5]] [^7][^8][^9].
- **The Halverson roast.** The hosts read an email in which BYU Marriott School professor [[Taylor Halverson]] declined to read Barnes's book, calling himself "highly skeptical that Jonah has said anything new or meaningfully accurate." They juxtapose this against Halverson's self-branding ("master learner," "receive truth let it come from whence it may") to charge LDS academic "niceness culture" with hypocrisy [^10][^11].
- **The Ratatouille frame.** They invoke Anton Ego's monologue — "In many ways, the work of a critic is easy... The new needs friends" — applying it to credentialed scholars who lord over popular apologists, and argue academia now "gatekeeps narratives," not knowledge. See [[Niceness Culture Gatekeeps the New - The Ratatouille Critique of LDS Academia]] [^12][^13][^14].
- **The $500 challenge and partial concession.** They offer Halverson $500 to a charity if he reads the book and verifies one new thing (or fails to disprove a flagged claim), and concede academia is genuinely needed — "we need good academia... falsifiable results" — distinguishing rigor from "cruel hypocrisy." They cite [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] as insiders unafraid of "the new," and note [[Joseph Smith]] and [[Brigham Young]] (the university's namesake) lacked degrees [^15][^16].
