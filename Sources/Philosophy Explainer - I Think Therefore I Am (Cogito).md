# Philosophy Explainer — I Think Therefore I Am (Cogito)

A short, non-LDS general-philosophy explainer used as background reading for the [[Thoughtful Faith - Apologetics Course Overview|Thoughtful Faith]] Level 1 course, sub-section 1.1 (**epistemology**). The video unpacks what is "perhaps the most famous phrase in all of philosophy" — *"I think therefore I am"* — and locates it within [[René Descartes]]'s broader project of finding an undoubtable foundation for knowledge. The speaker and channel are never named; the sign-off and the promise of a sequel arguing for a "logical uncertainty principle" identify it as an independent commentary channel. [^1][^6]

- **The phrase is the tip of a larger argument.** Popularly the *cogito argument* (from the Latin *cogito ergo sum*) and a favorite philosophy-undergrad tattoo, but the narrator argues it might better be called the *dubito* ("I doubt") argument, because the full reasoning depends on **methodic doubt** — Descartes' strategy of "finding absolute certainty by doubting everything that is possible to doubt." [^1] See [[Methodic Doubt Seeks an Undoubtable Foundation]].
- **Methodic doubt clears away the external world.** Descartes wanted "an absolute undoubtable truth in order to build a system of knowledge on a solid foundation." By doubting, he found that although unlikely, it is *possible* we are "in some sort of cosmic dream" or being "deceived by a powerful demon," so we cannot know with absolute certainty that the external world is really there. [^2] See [[Methodic Doubt Seeks an Undoubtable Foundation]].
- **The cogito is the one thing that survives the doubt.** Turning the doubt inward on his own mind, Descartes finds the single thing he cannot doubt is that he exists. The argument: *if I attempt to doubt my own existence, then I am thinking; thinking things exist; therefore I exist — at the very least as a thinking thing.* Every attempt to doubt one's own existence "thereby affirm[s] it by thinking." [^3][^4] See [[The Cogito Is Self-Verifying Through the Act of Doubting]].
- **Rational self-awareness is the sought-after bedrock.** "Rational self-awareness then is that undoubtable absolute certainty that Descartes was looking for as a foundation to all of knowledge." Historians "view this as a turning point in the history of philosophy, marking the beginning of the modern philosophy period." [^5]
- **Teaser — the "logical uncertainty principle."** The narrator closes by flagging a future video arguing for what they call the *logical uncertainty principle*: "that everything has a degree of uncertainty, including René Descartes' I think therefore I am" — i.e. a planned challenge even to the cogito's claimed absolute certainty. [^6] See [[The Logical Uncertainty Principle Challenges the Cogito]].
