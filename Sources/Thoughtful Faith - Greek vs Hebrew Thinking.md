# Thoughtful Faith — Greek vs Hebrew Thinking

Course context: **Thoughtful Faith, Level 1, sub-section 1.1 — Epistemology.** An interview between host Jacob (Thoughtful Faith) and [[Jonah (Plain and Precious Publishing)|Jonah]] (Plain & Precious Publishing) on a chapter from a forthcoming book. The thesis is *epistemological*: that **how a culture knows things** is shaped by its language, and that the "Greek" and "Hebrew" mindsets represent two fundamentally different ways of knowing — seeing-as-knowing (empirical, static, atomizing) versus hearing-as-knowing (relational, active, holistic). The Christian apostasy, the meaning of truth, and repentance are all reframed through this lens.

## Summary
- **Linguistic relativity is the foundation.** Jonah argues (against a BYU professor's claim) that language shapes thought: Russian's lack of a possessive "have" suits collectivism; Inuit's many words for snow let speakers compartmentalize what English lumps as one [^1]. Vocabulary expands what one can think; whoever controls the words controls the thoughts (Orwell, postmodernism) [^2].
- **The core contrast.** For Greeks the master verb is *horaō*, "to see" — empirical, "seeing is believing," good for science; a Greek can know a static, lifeless object. For Hebrews the master verb is *shema*, "to hear" — and you can only hear something that is *acting*; near to "obey" [^3]. This makes the Greek world a "dead world" of categories and the Hebrew world a living world of action.
- **"To be" = "to breathe."** The Hebrew verb for existence (*hayah*) carries the sense of breathing; God breathed life into Adam, and "I AM" can be read "I am the Giver of breath." A thing *is* what it *does*: a fig tree that bears no figs is not a fig tree (the cursing of the fig tree) [^4].
- **Greeks atomize; Hebrews see wholes.** Democritus' *atomos*, deconstruction, "either land A or land B" — versus the Hebrew "borders of the land" (Mosiah 18:4), neither here nor there, defined by the direction one is *heading* [^5].
- **Pilate vs. Jesus (John 18:37) as the crossroads.** "What is truth?" is the ultimate Greek question — Pilate wants truth written on a "Polaroid"; Jesus answers that truth is what you *do, live, hear, experience*: "everyone that is of the truth heareth my voice" [^6].
- **You cannot sin in thought, said the Hebrew (Prager) — but Jesus transcends both.** Hebrew action-morality says no thought is sin; Greek morality keeps a ledger of sins. Jesus rejects *both*: it is not about a tally and not merely about outward acts, but about the *heart* — a continuous orientation, "a Celestial being in who you are" [^7].
- **Time is a rhythm, not a line.** Semitic perfective/imperfective verbs ("I am calling" = unfinished) show Hebrews experience time as rhythm, like music. Using *tonal ambiguity* (Beethoven), Jonah argues repentance "rewrites" the past melody — explaining how forgiven sins can "return" (D&C 82:7): the question is always *what melody is your heart playing now* [^8].
- **Repentance and baptism reframed.** The Celestial Kingdom is not for those who cleared a bar of righteousness ("check the boxes") but for the *willing* — "a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (3 Nephi 9:20). Baptism is not washing dots off a ledger; it is *death and rebirth* — "you are a new creature" (Paul) [^9].
- **Pastoral payoff.** Many faith crises ask "Greek questions" (Adam-God, the Hofmann salamander forgery, "what is God's body made of?"). Jonah urges readers to stop philosophizing the gospel away and instead live it — "eat the fruit, sit in the shade" — citing Jordan Peterson ("your religion is what you act out") as a recovery of the Hebrew mind [^10].
