# Blake Ostler — The Problem of Evil

[[Jacob Hansen]] interviews Latter-day Saint philosopher [[Blake Ostler]] on the fourth volume of *Exploring Mormon Thought* (Ostler's treatment of evil). After steelmanning the classical problem from Epicurus, Ostler delivers two original arguments against classical theism — that the standard "God has his reasons" reply collapses into *moral quietude*, and that *creation ex nihilo* logically entails *occasionalism* (God directly causes every event, destroying free will). He then surveys the three live LDS models of God and creation (finitism, process/panentheism, and God-as-organizer) and defends his own constructive **agape theodicy**: a world of real, gratuitous-looking evils is the freely-chosen arena in which uncreated agents learn to love as God loves. The throughline: because the restored gospel rejects ex nihilo, the LDS God is not the all-determining author of evil that the classical problem assumes.

## Summary
- **The classical problem is empirically, not logically, forced — and its usual escapes fail.** Epicurus's dilemma turns on a *matter-of-fact* premise ("evil exists"), not a logical one [^1]. Augustine's "evil is privation of being" cannot account for positive pain or affirmative malevolence (Ponzi schemes are *present* improper thinking, not a mere lack) [^2]. The free-will defense fails for the brutalized-little-girl case: we constantly and rightly override free will to stop harm, so "his free will is too valuable to interrupt" is "just nonsense" [^3]. See [[Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy]].
- **"God has his reasons we can't fathom" leads to moral quietude.** The skeptical-theist appeal (the dog-at-the-vet analogy: God's cognitive distance from us is at least that great) is *true* but self-defeating: if every actual evil must serve a greater good God foresaw, then intervening to stop evil thwarts God's plan, so I may "sit and watch" any atrocity. It "makes nonsense of moral freedom, moral accountability, and any kind of motivation to do any good" — equivalent to Islamic fatalism (*inshallah*) and Calvinist/Thomist determinism [^4][^5]. See [[Moral Quietude - Why God Has His Reasons Backfires as a Theodicy]].
- **Evil is often gratuitous — God could have reached the same ends with less.** With omnipotence, God could have given the murderer small aneurysms removing only the desire to kill, leaving free will intact; could the last person who died of smallpox (of ~4 billion, preying on children) really have been *necessary*? Justifying evil by "greater good" ends up justifying evil itself [^6][^7]. See [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]].
- **Creation ex nihilo entails occasionalism (hidden determinism).** Because contingent things have no power to keep themselves in existence, ex nihilo requires *divine sustenance* — God re-creates everything exactly as it is in every moment, which entails *occasionalism*: virtually every event is directly caused by God. So classical theism (Calvinist, Thomist, even Arminian) cannot coherently locate free will outside God, and foreknowledge compounds the problem [^8][^9]. See [[Ex Nihilo Entails Occasionalism - The Hidden Determinism of Classical Theism]] and [[Creation Ex Nihilo Leads to Determinism]].
- **Three LDS models, and a constructive agape theodicy.** Ostler lays out finitism (God a "super-scientist" who grew up mastering an eternal universe — dissolves evil but neuters God and prayer), process/panentheism (intelligences with their own agency; God has only *persuasive* power — fits quantum physics and prayer but cannot *guarantee* victory over evil), and God-as-organizer (Ostler's own view) [^10][^11][^12]. On the last, evil is the freely-chosen, premortally-consented arena where uncreated agents learn divine love — *love cannot be coerced*, so free will toward a loving relationship is "worth anything that we need to confront to gain it" [^13][^14]. See [[Three LDS Models of God and Creation - Finitism, Process, and Organizer]] and [[The Agape Theodicy - Evil as the Arena for Learning Divine Love]].
