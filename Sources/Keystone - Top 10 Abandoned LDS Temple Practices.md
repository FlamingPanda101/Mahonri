Keystone walks through ten temple practices the Church once observed and later discontinued, deliberately setting aside the "controversial" changes (penalties, washing/anointing wording, the lecture at the veil) to surface lesser-known ones. The recurring thesis, stated explicitly at the top, is that *familiarity is not doctrine*: "just because you're used to doing things a certain way today, that doesn't mean they've always been done that way or have to be done that way in the future."[^1] The historical spine is the work of historian [[Jonathan Stapley]], whom the presenter credits as indispensable.[^11]

This source feeds the broader change/continuity cluster — see [[Apostasia Means Deliberate Rebellion Not Mere Change]], [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]], and [[Stasis Is the Claim That Needs Proof Not Change]] — by supplying concrete, datable examples of practices that came and went without anyone alleging apostasy.

## Summary

The ten discontinued practices, counted down:

- **#10 — Children's baptisms for the dead (pre-1960).** Any baptized child, boys and girls, could perform proxy baptisms; wards organized temple trips for primary kids. President David O. McKay's journal records the end of the policy; today one must be old enough for the youth program.[^1]
- **#9 — Eating fruit in the endowment.** In the Nauvoo Temple garden room and the later Salt Lake Endowment House, records show patrons ate raisins or other fruit during the Eden reenactment, symbolizing the fruit of the Garden and the Fall.[^2]
- **#8 — Baptisms of healing.** The first baptisms in the Salt Lake and Logan temples were not for remission of sins but for healing — modeled on Naaman washing in the Jordan, often by multiple immersions. Phased out in the early 20th century to free fonts for proxy ordinances.[^3]
- **#7 — Temple blessings of healing.** The Salt Lake garden room hosted healing blessings performed by men, by women, or by both together, involving anointing with oil and laying on of hands, not then tied to a specific priesthood office. Largely discontinued in 1921.[^4]
- **#6 — Prayer circles outside the temple.** Prayer circles were not always temple-bound: special circles (1851–1929) whose membership did not depend on church position, plus priesthood circles, all under First Presidency authority; many meetinghouses had dedicated rooms. The First Presidency consolidated prayer circles to the temple on May 3, 1978.[^5]
- **#5 — The temple choir and the annex.** The annex (linked to the temple by an underground tunnel) was where patrons had recommends checked and changed clothes; a temple choir sang there and at special events. Discontinued in the early 20th century under Salt Lake Temple president and apostle George F. Richards, who wanted to streamline and standardize the experience.[^6]
- **#4 — Disney's *Fantasia* in the endowment film.** The first motion-picture endowment (1955), produced by [[Gordon B. Hinckley]], used a few minutes of footage the Church licensed from Disney's *Fantasia* (with the music track swapped) to portray the Creation.[^7]
- **#3 — Temple libraries.** Multiple historians told Keystone that some early temples had libraries holding religious and secular books; details (size, access) are unknown, and the presenter flags he has "next to zero information."[^8]
- **#2 — Temple-consecrated oil.** Consecrated oil for anointing the sick was once preferentially consecrated *in the temple* within a prayer circle; a shop at the temple gate sold temple-consecrated oil, which both men and women could buy and use.[^9]
- **#1 — Women sealed to multiple living men.** The present asymmetry (a man may be sealed to a living second wife while still sealed to a first) once applied to *women* too. Susa Young Gates was sealed to Alma Dunford (1872), civilly divorced, then sealed to Jacob Gates (1880) — sealed to both living men until Wilford Woodruff cancelled the first sealing in 1890. Sealing cancellations normalized in the 1890s; the one-living-man rule for women became policy only in the 1910s–early 1920s.[^10]
