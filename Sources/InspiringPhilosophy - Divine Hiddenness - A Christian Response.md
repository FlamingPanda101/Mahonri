# Divine Hiddenness — A Christian Response
A response by [[Inspiring Philosophy|Michael Jones]] to J. L. Schellenberg's argument from divine hiddenness — the claim that a perfectly loving God would ensure no reasonable nonbelief, yet such nonbelief occurs, so no such God exists.[^1] Jones argues the argument assumes too little about fallen human psychology and too much about God's role: even with sufficient evidence many would still reject God, forced belief would destroy free self-determination and corrupt our motives, and God's "hiddenness" is actually a merciful means of sanctification rather than indifference.[^2] He synthesizes Paul Moser, Michael Murray, and Laura Garcia to argue God seeks *cognitively robust* trust, not mere propositional assent.[^3]

## Summary
- **Schellenberg's argument.** P1: if God exists he is perfectly loving; P2: a perfectly loving God would not allow reasonable (non-resistant) nonbelief; P3: reasonable nonbelief occurs; therefore no perfectly loving God exists. Theists grant P1; the dispute is over P2 and P3 — Schellenberg holds a loving God owes everyone evidence sufficient to *at least* know he exists, without coercing belief.[^4]
- **The argument assumes too little about humanity.** It presumes that, given good evidence, reasonable people would simply believe. But psychology shows we routinely reject data that conflicts with what we *desire* to be true; Jones cites his own debate experience plus Thomas Nagel's admitted "cosmic authority problem" and Aldous Huxley's confession that meaninglessness was an "instrument of liberation" from morality.[^5]
- **Sufficient evidence may already exist.** Natural theology and the historical case for the Resurrection arguably provide enough evidence to make belief reasonable for any genuine seeker, without forcing it; the atheist's "it's not enough for me" is a subjective proclamation, and to assume the cumulative case fails would be to assume the conclusion.[^6]
- **Forced belief would defeat God's purpose.** Removing our subjective desires or overwhelming us with proof would coerce us, producing fear- or reward-driven obedience (Paul Moser's "cognitively robust theism"; Michael Murray on overwhelmed self-determination). God's aim is not mere belief that he exists but *filial* trust in him as Lord, which requires that we first be humbled and freely seek him.[^7]
- **Replies to fairness, hell, and biblical counter-cases.** Unequal revelation is not unfair given God's omniscient calibration to each person; no one is condemned for mere ignorance (CS Lewis, John 9:41); and forced encounters that "didn't work" (Balaam, Paul/Saul) are exactly what the theist should expect — Murray notes such powerful revelations go only to those who can receive them and remain free.[^8]
