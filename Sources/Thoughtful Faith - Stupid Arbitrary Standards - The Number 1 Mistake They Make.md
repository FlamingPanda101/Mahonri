# Stupid Arbitrary Standards — The #1 Mistake They Make

[[Jacob Hansen]] frames a lecture by the late presuppositional apologist [[Greg Bahnsen]] around the single most common error he says unbelievers commit: **arbitrariness** — asserting opinions without grounding them in any standard. Bahnsen argues that "mere opinion" and "relativism" are the two failures you will meet again and again, that both collapse under the question *"by what standard?"*, and that no coherent morality is possible without an objective standard or authority. Hansen overlays an LDS reading throughout, applying the diagnosis to the modern obsession with inclusion and relativism even inside the Church.

## Summary
- The two "great intellectual sins" in any field are **arbitrariness** and **inconsistency**; Bahnsen estimates that roughly 80% of apologetics is handled simply by watching for people "saying anything they want to say."[^1]
- The most obvious form of arbitrariness is offering **mere opinion** instead of an argument; "it's my opinion" carries no intellectual weight, because a rational case requires reasons, proof, or evidence — answering the perpetual question *"why?"*[^2]
- The second form is **relativism** ("true for you but not for me"), which Bahnsen argues is self-refuting and morally catastrophic: if everyone creates their own reality, then Hitler's genocidal "reality" is equally valid, so "relativism kills itself."[^3]
- An **argument**, unlike an opinion, "is backed by some kind of a standard"; you cannot have any coherent moral or ethical philosophy without "some kind of standard or authority by which you judge," so "an arbitrary morality is no morality at all."[^4]
- The diagnostic tool is the question **"by what standard?"** — used to expose how much of what people believe is arbitrary or built on flawed standards; the follow-up problem of worldview *incoherence* is deferred to a later video.[^5]
- Hansen draws LDS applications: Christians tolerate arbitrary opinion because Bible studies often model it; the modern "obsession with inclusion" can become a soft entry to moral and cultural relativism; kindness to all does not mean all worldviews deserve equal intellectual or moral respect.[^6]
