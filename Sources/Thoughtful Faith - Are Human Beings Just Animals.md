# Are Human Beings Just Animals?
[[Jacob Hansen]] takes on the reductive atheist slogan that human beings are "just animals" — equivalent star-dust to every other creature. Rather than deny the label outright, he concedes and weaponizes it: if we are animals, we are "the weirdest freaking animal that has ever existed," because no other creature in the eons of natural history has done anything remotely like what humans do. The sheer scale of human discontinuity, he argues, is something a flat naturalism cannot wave away.

## Summary
- Hansen frames the atheist claim: we are all "Stardust," all equivalent, all just animals — yet "animals don't think about these type of things," and "never in a million years could an animal do this."[^1]
- He presses a question critics ignore: across the eons there have been creatures comparable to house cats (saber-toothed cats), to giraffes, and so on — but "has there ever been a creature that flew to the moon," that "created the Sistine Chapel," anything "even remotely like human beings"?[^2]
- Most animals across history "did animal stuff" — they ate, lived, reproduced, and died; humans are categorically different, "the most" weird animal precisely because there have been so many ordinary ones.[^3]
- Even within human history the leap is abrupt: for most of the past we behaved like animals, then "literally only in the past four or five thousand years... in the blink of an eye we went from basically behaving like animals to flying to the Moon."[^4]
- His preschool analogy: a teacher runs a baby class for a hundred years; the babies always act like babies — until one cohort, in the last minutes, builds a rocket ship and flies to the moon. The sane response is "those are weird babies," not "they're just babies."[^5]
- His punchline to the atheist: "you're just an animal," and "I am the weirdest freaking animal that has ever existed" — the concession that turns the reductive claim into evidence for human exceptionalism.[^6]
