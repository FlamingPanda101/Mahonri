# Alex O'Connor — The Fine-Tuning Argument is Terrible (Sean Carroll)

A naturalist critique of the [[Chance, Necessity, or Design - The Fine-Tuning Trilemma|fine-tuning argument]] in which physicist [[Sean Carroll]] grants that fine-tuning is "the best argument that people have for the existence of God" yet calls it "a terrible argument," pressing instead the [[The Multiverse and Cosmic Selection Reply to Fine-Tuning|multiverse]] and a [[Theism Doesn't Predict the Universe We Observe|Bayesian]] objection that the observed universe fits naturalism better than theism. Interviewer [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] frames the standard trilemma and largely concurs.

## Summary
- O'Connor frames the classic trilemma — apparent fine-tuning of the constants is explained by **chance**, **God**, or **necessity** — and asks which Carroll favors.[^1]
- Carroll adds a fourth option O'Connor "left off": a **multiverse** with regionally varying constants, where a **selection effect** guarantees we observe a life-permitting region. He distinguishes a genuine multiverse from a single universe that "just got lucky," and treats necessity as making no sense — the constants "could very easily have been otherwise."[^2]
- Crucially, Carroll argues the multiverse is **independently motivated** — it falls out of inflationary cosmology, string theory, and the many-worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics, theories "invented for completely different reasons." This, he says, is "very different than the story with theism."[^3]
- Carroll's headline claim: fine-tuning is "the best argument that people have for the existence of God. But it's a terrible argument." First objection — fine-tuning just means the constants permit *physical* information-processing creatures, which is "exactly what you would expect if God didn't exist"; under theism God needs no such physical scaffolding (e.g. life after death), so fine-tuning should *increase* credence in **naturalism**.[^4]
- Second objection (Bayesian): even granting that theism makes life-permitting constants likely, theism does **not** predict the rest of what we see — a vast universe with a hundred billion to a trillion galaxies, and the apparent insignificance of humans. Theists never used theism to predict a big-bang, galaxy-filled cosmos, so claiming "that's just what God would do" is **post hoc**.[^5]
- O'Connor relays Phil Halper's point that fine-tuning fits a Gnostic **evil demiurge** better than the traditional God, since the "meta conditions" make a material world hard to produce — implying the true source of being did not want matter.[^6]
- Carroll closes that modern theists are pushed to "invent a version of God who wants to be completely invisible," acting only through the laws of physics; "God would have wanted it that way" is "a little bit of a post hoc copout."[^7]
