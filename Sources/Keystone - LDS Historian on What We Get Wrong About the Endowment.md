# Keystone — "LDS Historian Reveals What We Get Wrong About the Endowment"

A [[Keystone]] interview with Latter-day Saint historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] (author of *Holiness to the Lord*, on temple worship) addressing how to reconcile [[Joseph Smith]]'s statement that "ordinances instituted in the heavens before the foundation of the world... are not to be altered or changed" with the documented fact that temple ceremonies have changed repeatedly. Stapley's master key is that **"ordinance" did not mean liturgy** for Joseph Smith but *law* (the plan of salvation), so what cannot change is the saving pattern, not its outward form. The conversation then works through specific changes — the **oath of vengeance**, the **penalties** (borrowed from Freemasonry), and **gendered sealing language** — and the meta-question of why a change-averse religion changes.[^1]

## Summary
- **"Ordinance" meant law, not ritual.** For Joseph Smith — as for John Wesley ("the ordinances of the church are fasting and Bible reading") and in the "municipal ordinance" sense — an ordinance was a *law you obey*; he listed faith, repentance, and baptism as "the first ordinances of the gospel." So "ordinances cannot change" means the *principles of salvation* are fixed, not that there is an "ideal baptism in the mind of God" deviation from which voids it.[^2] See [[Ordinance Meant Law Not Liturgy When Joseph Smith Said Ordinances Cannot Change]].
- **Almost every Latter-day Saint ceremony has developed**: the sacrament prayer Joseph Smith likely "never heard... in his entire life" (extemporaneous consecration was the norm), baptismal and ordination wording, white clothing (a St. George / Wilford Woodruff-era development), and three "vastly different" endowment ceremonies during Joseph's life (1831 high-priesthood ordination; the Kirtland solemn assembly with foot-washing and feast; the Nauvoo cosmological ceremony).[^3]
- **"True and living church" implies change**: Stapley reframes the slogan — "the church is true and living, and all living things change; where there is no change, there is only death" — against an implicit fundamentalism that imagines restoring a static first-century church.[^4]
- **The endowment endows power *to act now***. From Luke 24 ([[Luke 24.49|"tarry... until ye be endued with power from on high"]]), the point of the endowment is power to "build up the kingdom of God" in the present, not merely a credential for the afterlife — a reading Keystone's host calls a "light-bulb moment."[^5] See [[The Temple Endows Power to Build the Kingdom Now Not Only in the Afterlife]].
- **The oath of vengeance** originated in an 1845 temple prayer invoking [[Revelation 6.9-11|Revelation 6:9-10]] (the martyrs crying for God to avenge their blood), aimed at the murders of Joseph and Hyrum; antagonists recast it in citizenship-hearing litigation as a personal oath against the United States; it was removed by the 1920s as that generation faded.[^6] See [[The Oath of Vengeance Was a Prayer for Justice Later Removed]].
- **The penalties** were violent secrecy-oaths Joseph Smith kept from the Freemasonic initiation he "Christianized," dumping the Masonic content but retaining the form; "super alienating and scary" to moderns, they were removed in the late 20th century.[^7] See [[The Endowment Penalties Were Borrowed From Freemasonry and Later Removed]].
- **Gendered sealing language changed.** Comparing Joseph Smith's 1842 sealing text (available on the Joseph Smith Papers) with the Nauvoo-temple text [[Orson Pratt]] published in 1852, Stapley argues Joseph's is "more egalitarian" (man and woman take similar obligations) while Brigham Young's "subordinates women" (men become kings and priests *to God*, women queens and priestesses *to their husbands*); recent revisions have reduced but not eliminated the asymmetry.[^8] See [[Joseph Smiths Sealing Text Was More Egalitarian Than Brigham Youngs]].
- **Why a change-averse faith changes**: citing religious-studies scholar [[Steven Taysom]], persisting religions give adherents "a sense that their faith is unchanging while simultaneously changing," because relevance requires adaptation even as believers need to feel God is constant.[^9] See [[Religions Persist by Feeling Unchanging While Actually Changing]].
