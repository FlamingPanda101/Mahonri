# Leibniz' Contingency Argument

A short animated [[William Lane Craig|Craig]] explainer of the Leibnizian cosmological argument from contingency, opening with [[Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Leibniz]]' great question, "Why is there something rather than nothing?" It lays out a three-premise, deductively valid argument — *(1) everything that exists has an explanation of its existence; (2) if the universe has an explanation, that explanation is God; (3) the universe exists; therefore (4) the explanation of the universe's existence is God* — then defends each premise. The heart of the piece is the defense of premise 1 against the "the universe just is" brute-fact reply (the shiny-sphere-in-the-woods illustration) and Leibniz' distinction between things that exist *necessarily* (by their own nature) and *contingently* (caused by something else).

## Summary
- The argument is presented as deductively *airtight* in form: if the three premises are more plausibly true than false, the conclusion is unavoidable; premise 3 (the universe exists) is "undeniable for anyone seeking truth."[^1]
- Premise 1 is defended against the brute-fact dodge ("the universe is just there, and that's all — no explanation needed") with the shiny-sphere illustration: a ball found in the woods plainly demands an explanation, and *enlarging* it — even to the size of the universe — never removes that need.[^2]
- The "who caused God?" objection is met by Leibniz' distinction: things that exist **necessarily** exist by necessity of their own nature and cannot fail to exist (e.g., as many mathematicians hold of numbers and sets), whereas things that exist **contingently** are caused by something else and need not have existed at all.[^3]
- The universe exists *contingently*, not necessarily — it could have developed with no stars or planets, and "it's logically possible that the whole universe might not have existed" — so its existence must rest on a non-contingent, necessarily existing being.[^4]
- Premise 2 is defended by noting the universe is all of space-time, matter, and energy; its cause must therefore be non-physical, immaterial, and beyond space and time — and since abstract objects cannot cause anything, the short list of candidates leaves God (or "the extremely powerful, uncaused, necessarily existing, non-physical, immaterial, eternal being who created the entire universe").[^5]
