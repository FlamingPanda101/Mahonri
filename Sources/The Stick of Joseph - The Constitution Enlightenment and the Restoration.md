# The Stick of Joseph — The Constitution, Enlightenment, and the Restoration

[[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Stick of Joseph]] hosts [[Mike Day]] and Dave welcome guest [[Brian Thomassen]] (self-styled "Master of the Horse of Orange County") for an intellectual-history argument: **why did the Restoration occur in America and not in France?** Following [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s dictum that "ideas have a genealogy" and do not appear in a vacuum, Thomassen traces the theoretical roots of the U.S. Constitution to the **Enlightenment** ([[Thomas Hobbes|Hobbes]], [[John Locke|Locke]], [[Montesquieu]]) and the theoretical roots of the French Revolution to an **anti-Enlightenment, proto-Romantic** thinker, [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Rousseau]]. His thesis: only the American model embedded a *limiting principle* that created the free space — religious liberty, individual conscience, moral agency — required as the "seedbed of the Restoration."

The conversation also mounts a careful argument about what "the Constitution was inspired" can and cannot mean, restricting itself to canon ([[D&C 101]] "I have established… by the hands of wise men"), distinguishing **revelation from inspiration**, and reading "inspired" through the Greek *theopneustos* ("life-giving / useful") rather than divine embodiment. Long tangents cover the Council of Nicaea, the donation of Constantine, and Darwin vs. Lamarck on teleology.

## Summary

- **Framing question** — Why the Americas and not France? Two 18th-century revolutions, two foundational declarations (Declaration of Independence 1776: rights *endowed by their Creator*; Declaration of the Rights of Man 1789: rights asserted by *the document itself*). The question of *where rights come from* is fundamental.[00:00][03:00]
- **Methodological qualifiers** — Thomassen brackets political/economic precursors to deal only with *ideas*; conflates the Declaration and Constitution into one set (following [[Abraham Lincoln|Lincoln]]'s 1776 founding date); and restricts the inspiration question to **canon** only, not general-authority commentary.[05:31][07:01]
- **"Inspired" is not in the text** — The word "inspired" appears nowhere in the canon regarding the Constitution. The closest is [[D&C 101]]:80 ("I have established the Constitution… by the hands of wise men whom I raised up") and [[D&C 98]]:5–6 ("the law of the land which is constitutional"). The predicate is *established*, not *inspired*. See [[The Constitution Is Not Said to Be Inspired in the Canon - Only Established]].[08:31][13:01]
- **Revelation vs. inspiration** — Thomassen surveys [[Thomas Aquinas|Aquinas]] (revelation = formal cause, inspiration = efficient cause, via Aristotle's four causes) and the Eastern *via negativa* / apophatic tradition, in both of which revelation is prior and inspiration is secondary. See [[Inspiration Presupposes Revelation in the Christian Theological Tradition]].[24:01][37:00]
- **Theopneustos** — The lone biblical "inspiration" prooftext, [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]], uses *theopneustos*, which originally meant **life-giving / useful** (applied to sandals, ointments, springs), not "inspired"; Origen converted it to "inspired" in the 3rd century as a defense of poorly-written Christian Greek. Thomassen reads "the Constitution is inspired" in this *utility* sense. See [[The Constitution Is Inspired in the Sense of Useful Not Divinely Embodied]].[39:00][43:30]
- **Canon defined** — Canon is what has passed the quorums and been sustained by the body of the church (binding); a true claim can be non-binding (e.g., [[D&C 138|D&C 138]] true from 1918 but canonized 1976/1981). LDS faith is **orthopraxic, not orthodox** — closer to Judaism than to creedal Christianity. See [[Orthopraxy over Orthodoxy]].[15:32][20:01]
- **The Constitution as an Enlightenment document** — Foundational Enlightenment texts: [[Francis Bacon|Bacon]]'s *Novum Organum* (1620), [[Galileo Galilei|Galileo]]'s *Assayer* (1623, math as the language of science), [[René Descartes|Descartes]]' *Discourse on Method*. Enlightenment dispositions: skepticism toward truth claims, reason over authority, mathematics, empiricism, and **the abandonment of final causes** (Darwin's non-teleological model vs. [[Jean-Baptiste Lamarck|Lamarck]]'s teleological one). See [[The Constitution Is an Enlightenment Document]].[46:31][53:30]
- **Divine right of kings is a late forgery-based innovation** — Rome made no claim to divine kingship (the *imperator* ruled by force), so medieval Europe inherited a gap; the papacy filled it via the 8th-century **donation of Constantine** (a forgery, exposed by its medieval Latin). All Enlightenment thinkers reject divine right.[54:30][60:30]
- **Hobbes → Locke → Montesquieu** — Hobbes invents the *social contract* (state of nature as war of all against all; sovereign with the "big stick"). Locke shifts to natural law, property as fundamental, and the people's right to *change* their government. Montesquieu invents *separation of powers* as a check on power. The founders adopt all three. See [[The Founders Adopted Hobbes Locke and Montesquieu]].[60:30][72:01]
- **Rousseau as anti-Enlightenment lynchpin of the French Revolution** — Rousseau rejects reason for intuition, property as the root of corruption, and natural rights for the **general will**, which "one can be forced to be free" into; the great legislator may use the *noble lie*. This traces directly through Hegel to Marx, and produces Robespierre's terror and Napoleon's "I am the throne." See [[Rousseau Is the Anti-Enlightenment Lynchpin of the French Revolution]].[73:00][96:01]
- **America as the seedbed of the Restoration** — Only the American (Enlightenment, limiting-principle) model created the protected space for religious liberty and individual conscience; the French (Romantic, utopian, unlimited general will) model killed clergy and ended in imperial absolutism. The gospel could not have been restored in France. See [[America Was the Necessary Seedbed for the Restoration]].[96:30][103:00]
