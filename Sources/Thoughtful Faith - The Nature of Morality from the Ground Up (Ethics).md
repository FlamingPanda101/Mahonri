# Thoughtful Faith — The Nature of Morality from the Ground Up (Ethics)

Sub-section **1.3 Ethics & Morality** of [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1 apologetics course (see [[Thoughtful Faith Apologetics Course]]). After an opening shout-out to the *Midnight Mormons* show, Hansen sets out to "explore the nature of morality from the ground up" — building an account that (a) makes sense of ordinary moral language and (b) is "consistent with the truths of the restored gospel." [^1] The video is a popular-level metaethics explainer whose constructive core closely tracks [[Sam Harris]]-style well-being consequentialism (the course lists *The Moral Landscape* as recommended reading), reframed inside a Latter-day Saint theology in which **God himself is subject to eternal law**.

- **The restored gospel rejects divine command theory.** Hansen opens with what he calls "one of the unique features of the restored gospel": its rejection of the idea "that God determines what is right and wrong" — that even animal abuse "could be made moral if God commanded it." Instead, "even God himself is subject to Eternal law," and "it is his Conformity to the celestial order of being that makes him God." We are invited to enter that same order ("godhood"). [^1] See [[Restored Gospel Rejects Divine Command Theory]], and compare [[The Euthyphro Dilemma Defeats Divine Command Theory]] and [[The Law of Love Is the Single Ground of Morality]].
- **Being exists in tension between suffering and well-being.** Starting "even deeper" than reality — with being itself — Hansen takes "we exist" as the most self-evident fact, then claims conscious being is always stretched "between two poles": suffering and its opposite, well-being. Every creature is "flapping their wings against the constant gravitational like pull of suffering." [^2] See [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]].
- **All sane human action seeks well-being.** Every action — eating lunch, watching Netflix, helping a neighbor — is "consciously or subconsciously some move they are making to try and pull themselves away from suffering and towards some conception of increased well-being." He challenges the viewer to find a counterexample and cites [[Blaise Pascal]] ("all men seek happiness… even those who hang themselves") and [[Aristotle]] ("happiness… is the end at which all actions aim"). [^3][^4] See [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]].
- **Sacrifice does not refute the thesis.** Apparent counterexamples — a mother laying down her life for her children — still aim at well-being: "no one ever chooses suffering for suffering's sake," only "because they believe that doing so will ultimately bring them more well-being," even if that future is "in an afterlife." To choose suffering with no expectation of future well-being would meet "the definition of insanity." [^5] See [[All Human Action Aims at Escaping Suffering Toward Well-Being]].
- **Morality is not just value — it adds obligation.** Good and bad are "concepts of value," but morality "also includes obligation in relation to the good": it says you *ought* to do the good. "Any moral formulation that doesn't include obligation simply isn't describing morality — it's just describing value." [^6] See [[Morality Includes Obligation Not Just Value]].
- **The "ought" is a directional statement toward a goal.** The ought is "a statement about a direction toward an end or goal" — "if I want to get to San Diego from Los Angeles I ought to go south." It points to "a behavior we must take if we desire a particular outcome." [^7] See [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]].
- **A universal desire makes morality objective.** If there is "an outcome that all sentient sane creatures shared universally as a matter of objective fact" — namely, to move away from their own suffering — then moral language gets an objective basis: "there are actual right and wrong answers to moral questions because there are right and wrong answers as to what brings about more or less suffering." [^8][^9] See [[The Ought Is Directional and Grounds Objective Morality in Universal Desire]].
- **Cliffhanger: isn't well-being subjective?** Hansen closes by raising the obvious objection — "isn't well-being subjective?" — and defers it to the next video. [^9]
