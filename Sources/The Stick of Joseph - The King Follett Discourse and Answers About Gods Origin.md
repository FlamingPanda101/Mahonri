# The Stick of Joseph - The King Follett Discourse and Answers About God's Origin

A [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] webcast (with the Stick of Joseph hosts) walking paragraph-by-paragraph through [[Joseph Smith]]'s [[King Follett Discourse]] — the 1844 Nauvoo funeral sermon for [[King Follett]] — and using it to answer "the ultimate question… how did God become God?" The episode frames the discourse as the most explicit Latter-day Saint teaching on the [[God Was Once a Man|origin and embodiment of God]], [[Human Deification in Mormonism|human deification]], and the [[Intelligence Is Uncreated and Eternally Has Agency|co-eternity of intelligence and matter]], and reads it throughout as a coded temple/endowment discourse delivered to a mixed audience of initiates and non-initiates.

## Summary

The hosts open with Joseph's insistence that one must "go back to the beginning of creation" and grasp "the character of God," because "if men do not comprehend the character of God, they do not comprehend their own character." Day and Butler treat this as the root reason Latter-day Saints and creedal Christians "talk past each other" — they are reading "different maps" of the very first scene of the story (see [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]], [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father]]). Butler reads the early paragraphs against [[Genesis 3.4-5|Genesis 3:22]] ("the man is become as one of us") to argue the Eden serpent's claim that the fruit makes one "like the gods" was "not a complete lie" — knowledge raises humanity from the "brute beast" toward divinity, which Day ties to the early-Christian [[Theosis as Likeness and Union So Far as Possible|theosis]] tradition (citing Peter Enns, Michael Heiser, and Athanasius' exchange formula; see [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]]).

The doctrinal core is Joseph's "great secret": "God himself who sits enthroned in yonder heavens is a man like unto one of yourselves," with a [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son|corporeal body]] that the Nicene tradition later dissolved into "a mist" (see [[God in Heaven With a Physical Body Implies a Physical Heaven]], [[Greek Philosophy Overwrites Hebrew Divine Corporeality]]). Butler reads the sermon's repeated "open your ears and eyes," enthronement, and "veil rent" language as deliberate **initiatic/temple signals** ("who has ears, let him hear") — a lecture on the endowment for those already endowed, veiled from the rest. They handle the hardest claim — that God "once was a man… and dwelt on an earth" — with Joseph's **ring object lesson** (an unbroken ring as the immortal spirit with "no beginning or end") and the "[[LDS Father Was Always Fully Divine (No Eternal Regression)|gods in embryo]]" reconciliation of "from eternity to eternity" with "once as we are" (see [[Infinite and Eternal Are Not the Same - The Regress Objection Rebutted]]). Day adds the contested "God is a married man… an exalted woman is his wife" corollary pointing to [[Wisdom Personified as the Mother in Heaven|Heavenly Mother]].

The historical center is a long excursus on creation *ex nihilo*: Joseph's claim that "create came from bara… it means to organize," that "element had an existence from the time he had," and that the "pure principles of element… never can be destroyed." Day and Butler defend the bara-as-organize reading with John Walton's *Lost World of Genesis 1* (bara = giving function to inert/chaotic matter, *tohu wa-bohu*) and date *creatio ex nihilo* to its first explicit articulation by **Theophilus of Antioch** in the second century (see [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]], [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]], [[Theophilus Arguments for Creation Ex Nihilo]], and [[Theophilus of Antioch]]). They embed this in the Nicene controversy — **Arius** vs **Alexander**, **Constantine**'s political motive to "stop the killing," and the apologists who blended John 1's *Logos* with Greek philosophy's [[The One Is Identical to the Unmoved Mover|unmoved mover]] (Anaxagoras) — arguing a sovereign emperor had every reason to suppress a doctrine that makes subjects "the offspring of God" (see [[Homoousios Was Coined to Refute Arius]], [[The Creeds Were an Abomination Meant Sectarian Dogmatism Not the Ecumenical Creeds]], [[Constantine]], [[Arius]], [[Athanasius]]). Day repeatedly distinguishes "same *kind* of substance" (which he accepts, citing the D&C "light of light") from "the same substance" (which he rejects) as the critical Latter-day Saint difference from *homoousios* (see [[Homoousios Means Same Essence Which Mormons Extend to All Humanity]]).

The most distinctive exegesis is Butler's treatment of Joseph's **beresheet** analysis ("the head one of the gods brought forth the gods"). Butler triangulates Joseph's reading with **ancient Jewish readings** that likewise made *Elohim* the object rather than the subject of *bara* — a Targum's "with wisdom (*bahukama*) God created" (tying to [[Proverbs|Proverbs 3:19]]), and the *Sefer Bahir* and *Zohar* reading the implied subject of *bara* as "He/It created the gods" — with the feminine *-īt* termination of *reshit* pointing to Wisdom / the divine feminine (see [[Ancient Jewish Readings of Genesis 1.1 Make Elohim the Object Created by the Head God]], distinct from the grammatical case in [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading]] and [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]]). The discussion then opens onto the divine feminine more broadly — **Asherah** as the tree of life, [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother|Nephi's tree vision]], and Alma 32–33's faith-seed/tree-of-life sermon culminating in the Nephites' question "should we believe in one God?" (see [[Alma 32 Light Alludes to Genesis 1 Creation]], [[Loss of the Divine Family Is the Core Apostasy]]). Butler closes by cataloguing further temple echoes in the discourse — "honey is sweet and so do I," "they without us cannot be made perfect" (*teleios*, as in [[Matthew 5.48|Matthew 5:48]]; see [[Teleios Means Initiate - You Will Be Endowed Not Be Flawless]]), and the "uttermost farthing" of Matthew 5 — and the hosts end on Joseph's "the mind of man… is coequal with God himself," pressing the devotional payoff: human exaltation does not diminish God but, as a father is glorified in his children, "God is glorified in the salvation and exaltation of his creatures."
