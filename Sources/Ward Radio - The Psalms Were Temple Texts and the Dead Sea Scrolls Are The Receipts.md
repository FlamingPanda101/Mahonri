# Ward Radio — The Psalms Were Temple Texts

[[Cardon Ellis]] and **[[Jonah Barnes]]** (with **[[Britney Ward Radio|Britney]]**), fresh from the Dead Sea Scrolls exhibit, argue from the **Psalm Scroll (11Q5)** that the Psalms were **temple altar-liturgy** — group prayers led before the altar — and that a rival scribal tradition edited temple and resurrection content out of the canonical Psalms.

## Summary
- **The Psalms are altar-prayers, not just poetry.** 11Q5's prose coda says David, given "a spirit of understanding and light," wrote 3,600 psalms plus songs "to sing before the altar over the whole burnt offering for every day... and for the Day of Atonement" — i.e. liturgical, prophetic temple texts, not a hymnbook [^1].
- **The Day of Atonement was edited out.** 11Q5 highlights the Day of Atonement as supreme; it is absent from Deuteronomy and from the canonical Psalm-list — read as the Deuteronomist scribes (lawyers, anti-temple) suppressing the corporeal-Messiah/covering theology of [[The Day of Atonement|Yom Kippur]] [^2].
- **"Worm of the destitute" — a suppressed resurrection reading.** Where the Masoretic [[Psalm 102]] reads "prayer of the destitute," Qumran reads "the **worm** of the destitute"; Barnes links *worm* (Mark 9:48; the "worm dieth not") to the *disembodied spirit*, so the verse has God hearing the prayers of *the dead* — vicarious-work theology a later scribe doubled "prayer/prayer" to erase [^3].
- **Selah and the high-priestly cross-prayer.** *Selah* is read as a call-and-response cue for a Davidic prayer circle; and Christ's "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani" ([[Matthew 27.46|Matthew 27:46]], quoting [[Psalm 22]]) is read as a high-priestly altar-prayer with hands raised — the gospel's odd Aramaic-plus-translation suggesting an unutterable sacred prayer ([[3 Nephi 19|3 Nephi 17]]) [^4].
