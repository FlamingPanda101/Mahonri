# Alex O'Connor — Your Mind Is Not Your Brain (Robert Greene on NDEs)

A [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] ("Within Reason") interview with author [[Robert Greene]], recorded as Greene researches a book on **the sublime** in the wake of a near-fatal stroke. The conversation ranges over near-death experiences, the "filter" model of consciousness, and the cracks Greene and O'Connor both see in materialist optimism. Both men are secular — O'Connor a "brutally agnostic" philosopher, Greene a non-religious author — which makes the note valuable for steelmanning: two skeptics arguing that **the mind is not (merely) the brain**.

## Summary
- **Experiential knowledge is irreducible.** Greene's stroke turned his abstract chapter on death into a "visceral" knowing he says could not have come from intellect alone; O'Connor connects this to Frank Jackson's **Mary's Room** (the color-scientist who learns something new on first seeing blue) — experience carries knowledge propositions cannot.[^1] (Cross-ref [[Perfect Certainty of Experience Not of Interpretation]].)
- **The brain as a filter, not a producer.** Greene's central claim, drawn from Aldous Huxley and Henri Bergson: the brain *reduces* and *filters* an overwhelming reality down to a survivable trickle; psychedelics and near-death dissolve the filter, so people "see colors they never saw" and feel reality "lit up like a whole warehouse."[^2] See [[The Brain Is a Filter That Reduces Consciousness Not Its Source]].
- **Brain activity goes *down*, not up.** Both cite fMRI work in which measured cortical activity *decreases* under psychedelics even as experience expands — which they take to imply the brain restricts rather than generates consciousness.[^3] (Cross-ref [[Reality Is Too Complex to Perceive So the Brain Ignores Almost Everything]].)
- **NDEs in the brain-dead.** Greene relays Bruce Greyson's *After*: thousands of collected accounts, some from patients declared clinically dead (heart and breathing stopped) whose minds "kept going," reporting common themes (oceanic oneness, life review). His inference: "if your brain is dead but your mind is operating, that indicates the mind is not the brain."[^4] See [[Near-Death Experiences in the Brain-Dead Challenge Materialism]].
- **Veridical NDEs.** O'Connor raises the hardest cases — the patient who floats above her body and reports a detail (the "red shoe on the roof," or a stain on the doctor's tie) she could not have perceived. Greene concedes he cannot explain these and won't try in the book; both treat them as a genuine, unsolved datum rather than dismissing them.[^5] See [[Veridical NDEs Resist Naturalistic Explanation]].
- **The cultural framing of NDEs.** Greyson's caution (which Greene endorses): a dying Christian *interprets* the ineffable experience through inherited "Jesus, white robes, angels" imagery — so the cultural overlay is real, but the underlying experience of stepping outside the self is cross-cultural and "real."[^6] (Cross-ref the existing [[NDE Parallels in the Bible]].)
- **Materialist optimism is cracking.** O'Connor argues science *methodologically* assumes materialism but illegitimately upgrades it into a *metaphysics* ("there is only the material"), which "shuts a door" and is "deeply unsatisfying"; he and Greene note physics itself ("the smartest people on the planet") confesses how little it understands.[^7] See [[Methodological Materialism Smuggled Into Metaphysical Materialism]].
- **The sublime and the hunger for religion.** Greene defines the **sublime** as an experience of mixed pleasure-and-pain that takes you outside the "circle" of cultural convention (Burke's horror, the Alps, the *mysterium tremendum*); he argues art and religion were once one, that modern "algorithmic" culture starves people of it, and that drugs, porn, and Marvel films are a *false* sublime because they are done *to* you rather than cultivated from within.[^8] See [[The Sublime Is the Hunger Organized Religion Once Fed]]. O'Connor cites Rudolf Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* as the same idea.
