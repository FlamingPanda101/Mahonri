# Dawkins vs Lennox — The God Delusion Debate

A 2007 public debate at the University of Alabama at Birmingham, sponsored by Larry Alex Taunton's Fixed Point Foundation and moderated by federal judge Bill Pryor. [[Richard Dawkins]] (atheist) and [[John Lennox]] (Christian) work through the six major theses of Dawkins' book *The God Delusion*, with the judge reading excerpts and each man given roughly five minutes per exchange.[^1] The result is one of the cleaner head-to-head treatments of New Atheism's core claims against a credentialed theistic respondent.

## Participants
- [[John Lennox]] (theist) · [[Richard Dawkins]] (atheist)

## Summary
- **Faith is blind vs. faith is evidence-based.** Dawkins holds that religion "teaches us that it is a virtue to be satisfied with not understanding" and that the very word *faith* implies the absence of evidence. Lennox distinguishes blind faith (e.g. the flying spaghetti monster) from evidence-based faith, arguing Christian faith rests on objective evidence from science, history, and experience, and that even believing one's spouse loves them is a rational, evidence-grounded "faith." See [[Faith Is Blind vs Faith Is Evidence-Based]].[^2]
- **Does science support atheism or theism?** Lennox runs the argument from reason in reverse: if mind is just "the motions of atoms in my brain produced by an unguided random mindless process," there is no reason to trust those thoughts — so atheism "saws off the branch it sits on." He adds that modern science arose from a theistic expectation of law in nature (Whitehead's thesis), and that Dawkins confuses *mechanism* with *agency*. See [[Atheism Undercuts the Rationality Science Requires]] and [[Theism Makes Science Possible]].[^3]
- **"Who designed the designer?" / God is uncreated.** Dawkins' central thesis: a designer god would itself be complex and demand explanation, an infinite regress that makes God "very, very improbable." Lennox replies that the question only bites against *created* gods — "none of us believe in created gods" — and that explanation in science routinely runs from simple to complex (two scratches in a cave imply a complex mind). See [[Who Designed the Designer - God Is Uncreated]] and [[A Designer Would Be as Complex as the Universe]].[^4]
- **Fine-tuning and the beginning of the universe.** Both engage cosmic fine-tuning; Dawkins prefers the anthropic principle plus a multiverse, which Lennox calls logically unsatisfying, citing Penzias and the 1960s discovery of a cosmic beginning the Bible had long affirmed. See [[Anthropic Fine-Tuning Points to a Designer]] and [[Multiverse as Ad-Hoc Escape from Fine-Tuning]].[^5]
- **Does religion poison everything?** Dawkins argues there is a logical path from faith to atrocity (9/11, the madrasa) but no logical path from atheism to evil. Lennox counters with Stalin, Mao, and Pol Pot — "intellectual murder" committed in the name of atheism — and presses Dawkins for "airbrushing out" communist atrocities, asking him to differentiate between religions as he differentiates between atheists. See [[Atheist Atrocities and the Logical-Path Argument]].[^6]
- **Can you be good without God?** Dawkins grounds morality in an evolved "lust to be good" plus a "shifting moral zeitgeist," needing no holy book. Lennox concedes atheists can *behave* morally but argues atheism cannot *ground* morality: Dawkins' own "no good, no evil, nothing but blind pitiless indifference" dissolves the very categories needed to call anything evil (Hume's is/ought gap; Dostoevsky). See [[No One Needs God to Be Moral - The Grounding Problem]].[^7]
- **Jesus and the resurrection.** In closing, Lennox stakes his faith on the resurrection as the central supernatural evidence and as the guarantee that "justice is real." Dawkins replies that the pivot from grand cosmology to "the resurrection of Jesus" is "so petty, so trivial, so local," and that Darwin's achievement was showing the gardener is not needed.[^8]
