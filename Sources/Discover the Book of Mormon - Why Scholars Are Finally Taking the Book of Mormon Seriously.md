# Discover the Book of Mormon — Why Scholars Are Finally Taking the Book of Mormon Seriously

A first-person retrospective by [[Kirk Magleby]], co-founder of FARMS and former executive director of Book of Mormon Central (now Scripture Central), narrating "the incredible journey the Book of Mormon has taken in my lifetime to go from an ignored laughingstock to respectability as an object of considerable academic interest."[^1] Rather than arguing a single evidence (Nahom, chiasmus, Arabia), the episode is a chronological history of the institutions, scholars, and publishers that, over five decades, moved the [[Book of Mormon]] up the scale of academic standing.

## Summary

Magleby frames the episode around a 1976 personal revelation in which he was given three life's-work assignments, the first being "to help make the Book of Mormon become academically respectable." In 1976 the book "was generally considered crude fiction, about like a comic book or a supermarket tabloid."[^1] He anchors the low point in two famous dismissals: Catholic scholar Thomas O'Dea's 1957 remark that critics "did not even have to read the Book of Mormon in order to have an opinion about it," and Yale critic [[Harold Bloom]]'s 1991 verdict that — though Joseph Smith was "an authentic religious genius" and "an authentic prophet" — "I cannot recommend that the book be read either fully or closely." Magleby reads these as a culture of caricature in which the most eminent literary critic in the English-speaking world "told his audience to ignore the Book of Mormon."[^2] See [[Critics Dismissed the Book of Mormon Without Reading It]]. He casts the reversal as fulfillment of Isaiah 29:14 ("wonder upon wonder"), quoted and paraphrased in [[2 Nephi 27]].[^3]

He then traces the institutional build-out, "earned one discovery, one article, one book, one presentation, one conference at a time." Jack Welch's 1977 *Ensign* article "A Book You Can Respect"; the 1979–1980 founding of FARMS in the basement of the BYU law building by [[Jack Welch]], [[John Sorenson]], and Magleby (newsletter circulation rising from 200 to 20,000); FARMS publishing the 19-volume *Collected Works of [[Hugh Nibley]]*; and BYU's 2002 acquisition of FARMS as the Neal A. Maxwell Institute.[^4] A 1981 watershed was *Chiasmus in Antiquity* (Gerstenberg Verlag, Hildesheim), which placed "Chiasmus in the Book of Mormon" alongside chapters on Sumero-Akkadian, Ugaritic, Hebrew, Aramaic, Talmudic, New Testament, Greek, and Latin chiasmus; Anchor Bible general editor David Noel Freedman's preface reportedly quelled a contributor "mutiny" by judging the Book of Mormon chiasmus "every bit as good, if not better" than the biblical examples.[^5] The chiasmus stream continued through [[Donald W. Parry]]'s reformatted text (1982/2007) and *Chiasmus: The State of the Art* (2020).[^6]

Magleby treats the 1982 RSC volume *Book of Mormon Authorship* (ed. [[Noel Reynolds]]) as establishing a genre: highly credentialed contributors (terminal degrees from Berkeley, Duke, Harvard, Stanford, Virginia Polytechnic) examining the book from multiple disciplines — statistical wordprints, the Lachish letters, 40-day literature, Egyptian/Orphic motifs in Lehi's vision, and authentic details of the Arabian journey.[^7] He chains the milestones: [[John Sorenson]]'s *An Ancient American Setting* (1985) and *Mormon's Codex* (2013); [[Royal Skousen]]'s multi-decade Critical Text and the *Joseph Smith Papers Project* (2001), which "puts Joseph Smith in rare company alongside... Shakespeare, George Washington, and Abraham Lincoln"; [[Brant Gardner]]'s six-volume *Second Witness*; Welch's *Legal Cases in the Book of Mormon* (2008); the Book of Mormon Onomasticon (2013) feeding [[Matthew Bowen]]'s wordplay scholarship; and [[Brian Stubbs]]'s Uto-Aztecan work (2015).[^8]

The episode's distinctive thesis is publishing prestige: the Book of Mormon's full text and book-length scholarly treatments have issued from the most elite academic and trade presses on earth — Oxford (at least eight titles since [[Terrell Givens]]'s 2002 *By the Hand of Mormon*), Princeton (Gutjahr's 2012 *Biography*, by a non-LDS scholar calling it "the most important religious text ever to emerge from the United States"), Yale (Skousen's *Earliest Text*), Penguin Classics (2008), Macmillan, Doubleday, and the University of Illinois ([[Grant Hardy]]'s *Reader's Edition*).[^9] See [[Elite Academic Presses Publishing the Book of Mormon Mark Its Respectability]]. Magleby closes with growth figures — the 200-millionth copy printed (2023), church membership at 17.5 million (2025) versus 3.7 million in 1976 — and concedes the book still does not enjoy "universal acclaim," being "viewed as a threat and therefore demeaned by many contemporary religionists and humanists," just as Jesus threatened the Pharisees and Sadducees.[^10]
