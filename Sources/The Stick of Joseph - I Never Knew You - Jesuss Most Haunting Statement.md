# The Stick of Joseph — "I Never Knew You": Jesus's Most Haunting Statement

A conversation with guest [[Andrea Woodmansee]] and [[Dave Butler|David Butler]] (hosted by Ella Paul, standing in for the usual hosts) on what they call Jesus's most haunting words: **"I never knew you"** ([[Matthew 7.21|Matthew 7:23]]; Joseph Smith—Matthew "ye never knew me"). The thesis is that a single **recognition motif** runs through the parable of the ten virgins, Luke 13, the Sermon on the Mount, and the Book of Mormon: the dividing line at the door of salvation is not *works performed* but whether Christ *knows* us — which the speakers ultimately cash out as [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula|deification]], bearing Christ's image in our countenance. The episode is a companion to the channel's [[The Stick of Joseph - The Hidden Hebrew Wedding Ritual and the Temple|Hebrew-wedding]] and [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|Sermon-as-ordinance]] material.

## Summary

- **The knowing, not the oil, is the dividing line.** In [[Matthew 25.1-13|the ten virgins]], the foolish five *do* obtain oil and reach the door — yet "I know you not." So the oil is not the true divider; recognition is. The same scene recurs at [[Luke 13.6-9|Luke 13:24-27]] ("I know you not whence ye are"). See [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works]].[^1][^2]
- **Recognition by voice and token.** Drawing on the Hebrew wedding, Woodmansee notes admission required a token presented at the door and recognition of the bride's *voice* once the door was bolted; the foolish virgins fail both, and are left in the literal "outer darkness" of a night wedding. Builds on [[The Parable of the Ten Virgins Encodes Hebrew Wedding Customs]].[^3]
- **Works are not the qualifier.** Matthew 7:22-23 has people plead prophecy, exorcism, and "many wonderful works," yet are told "I never knew you, depart from me, ye that work iniquity." The darkest word is **"never"** — not that they fell away, but that even *while* working wonders in his name he did not know them. Coheres with [[Jesus Taught Salvation Involves Doing Not Faith Alone]] but sharpens it toward relationship over performance.[^4]
- **The feast is in the second room, the test at the third.** Butler ties Luke 13 to the Sermon's temple ascent: "we ate and drank in thy presence" is the *second-room* covenant feast (the sacrament/betrothal meal), which is not itself sufficient to pass the judgment into the third room. See [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms]] and [[The Three Temple Rooms Map Justice-Mercy-Grace and Moses-Elijah-the Lord]].[^5]
- **Two covenant meals (betrothal and wedding).** Woodmansee maps Luke 13's "eaten and drunk in thy presence" onto the **betrothal meal** of the Hebrew wedding (bread broken to establish kinship; the bridegroom's cup the bride drinks to seal the match) — read as the sacrament/baptismal covenant — which is binding but not the final wedding feast. See [[Two Covenant Meals - The Betrothal Meal and the Wedding Feast]].[^6]
- **Kinship through eating the sacrifice.** Butler invokes W. Robertson Smith's *Lectures on the Religion of the Semites*: a family's animals are *kin*, so eating one is a solemn kinship act — and "one of our kinsfolk is our God." This illuminates eating the flesh and blood of the Lord, the great *goel* (kinsman-redeemer), who is both the sacrifice and the bridegroom. See [[Eating the Sacrifice Establishes Kinship With God]].[^7]
- **"Receive" and "do" as creation verbs.** Butler observes the Greek: "everyone who asks *receiveth*" is *lambanō*, "to take into the hands / grasp"; "whoso *doeth* these sayings" is *poieō*, the verb of creation (Genesis 1 LXX, the root of *poet*). So hearing the Sermon and "doing" it is a creative act — the worshipper must *become a creator*, echoing Eden. See [[Hearing and Doing the Sermon Is a Creative Act]].[^8]
- **Knowing = keeping commandments = love.** [[1 John 2|1 John 2:3-4]] gives the test: "hereby we do know that we know him, if we keep his commandments"; and 2:9-11 ties it to loving one's brother. Butler notes the love-verb is *agapaō*, the same as [[Matthew 22.37-39|the two great commandments]] — so "knowing" Christ is keeping the commandments, which is to *love*. The JST inverts the issue: not whether the bridegroom knows the virgin, but whether the virgin knows the bridegroom.[^9]
- **The payoff is deification — Christ's image in the countenance.** [[Alma 5.18|Alma 5:14]] ("have ye received his image in your countenances... have ye experienced this mighty change") and [[Moroni 7|Moroni 7:48]] ("we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is") reframe the goal: not a checklist of ordinances or deeds but *becoming* like Christ so that he recognizes himself in us. Butler cites the **family-resemblance** logic (born of God → you carry the family likeness) and Oscar Wilde's *Picture of Dorian Gray* (the inner self made visible on the face). See [[Knowing Christ Means Bearing His Image Not Performing Works]] and [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son]].[^10][^11]
- **The gate is a family reunion, not a stranger's test.** [[2 Nephi 9.41|2 Nephi 9:41]] ("the keeper of the gate is the Holy One of Israel... the Lord God is his name") is read as the *same* being who oversaw Eden and the expulsion still waiting at the gate — so returning is being "recognized as returning family," and the test is having the love of God in the heart, which puts his countenance on one's face.[^12]
