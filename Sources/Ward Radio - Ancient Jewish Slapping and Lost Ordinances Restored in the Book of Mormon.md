# Ward Radio — Ancient Jewish Slapping and Lost Ordinances Restored in the Book of Mormon

A long-form [[Stick of Joseph]] / Ward Radio crossover hosted by [[Cardon Ellis]] (with Brad Whick and [[Hayden Paul]]) interviewing authors [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] and [[Mike Day]] about their book *In the Language of Adam*. It is the sequel-in-spirit to the channel's temple series ([[The Stick of Joseph - The Ancient Temple and the Sermon on the Mount Part I|Sermon on the Mount, Part I]] and [[The Stick of Joseph - The Ancient Temple and Lehis Tree of Life Vision|Tree of Life Vision]]), continuing the thesis that **the Book of Mormon was written by temple initiates for temple initiates** and is best read as temple liturgy. [^1] Its distinctive payload is the argument that a **violent ritual smiting of the cheek** was an ancient temple ordinance — now lost or softened in the modern endowment — and that the Book of Mormon preserves traces of it. The episode also maps [[Lehi]]'s wilderness journey onto the **Tabernacle** (not Solomon's Temple), reading the place-name *Shazer* as a pun on the tabernacle's fine-twined-linen door. Butler and Day repeatedly flag the boldest claims as devotional "bonus material," not settled doctrine — see [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]] and [[Avoiding Parallelomania Between Ancient Rites and LDS Temple]] for the broader frame.

## Summary
- **Framing thesis (recap).** Butler restates the series' premise: the Book of Mormon writers "are Temple worshippers themselves and they write for an audience they expect to be also Temple worshippers," so the text is "full of Temple imagery" the endowed reader is meant to recognize. Reading it this way "changes everything." [^1][^2]
- **Lehi as a new Moses; the journey mapped to the Tabernacle.** Where Lehi's *dream* ([[1 Nephi 8|1 Nephi 8]]) is mapped against Solomon's Temple, Lehi's *physical journey* is mapped against the **Tabernacle** — casting Lehi as a new Moses on an Exodus. The Valley of Lemuel is the open courtyard (Lehi immediately builds an altar there, [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2]]); the captured/hostile temple is "ultimately irrelevant" because the tree of life is still accessible. [^3][^4]
- **Layman and Lemuel were deuteronomists, not just prideful.** Their flaw was *ideological*, not a personality defect: they are "good deuteronomists" who deny personal revelation ("the Lord maketh no such thing known unto us," [[1 Nephi 15.9|1 Nephi 15]]) and call Lehi a "visionary man" — the very thing Deuteronomy says to reject. This frames the family conflict as egalitarianism vs. elitism, "just like there might be an anti-Mormon and a pro-LDS person in the same family." See [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]] and [[A Member Can Be an Anti-Mormon]]. [^5][^6]
- **The mysteries vs. the rules (Deuteronomy 29:29).** Citing [[Margaret Barker]], Day reads [[Deuteronomy 29.29|Deuteronomy 29:29]] ("the secret things belong unto the Lord... but those things which are revealed belong unto us") as the deuteronomist charter: the mysteries are *not for you*, just keep the law. Nephi answers that he *has* been "inducted into the Mysteries" ([[1 Nephi 1.1|1 Nephi 1:1]]; [[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11]]). [^7]
- **The Sermon on the Mount as the same ordinance.** Day reprises [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the Sermon-as-ordinance reading]] — a three-part ascent (Matthew 5/6/7) ending at a straight-and-narrow gate and a fruit-bearing tree — and argues Christ's ministry "included initiating people into Mysteries that had mostly been forgotten," possibly as mass events (the feedings of the 4,000/5,000). [^8][^9]
- **A ritual smiting of the cheek (the central new claim).** [[Matthew 5.39|Matthew 5:39]] ("whosoever shall smite thee on thy right cheek") is read as a ritual *action*, not just a moral teaching; [[Helaman 4.12|Helaman 4:12]] lists "smiting their humble brethren upon the cheek" among apostate desecrations of "that which was sacred"; in [[Alma 14.14|Alma 14]] the judge and elite of Ammonihah smite the bound Alma and Amulek on the cheek **seven times**. Butler ties this to the Babylonian **Akitu festival** (the king ritually slapped until he weeps and confesses loyalty before enthronement), to **Psalm 22** (surrounded, mocked, beaten), to **Isaiah** (giving "my back to the smiters," Isaiah 50:6), and to **Hugh Nibley**'s reading of the Joseph Smith papyri (seven ritual "blows of death" in Egyptian initiation). The full argument is in [[A Ritual Smiting of the Cheek Was a Lost Temple Ordinance]]. [^10][^11][^12][^13][^14]
- **Nephi's "large of stature" explains the violence.** [[Nephi]] twice notes he was "large of stature" ([[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]]; [[1 Nephi 4.31|1 Nephi 4]]). Butler reads the [[1 Nephi 2.6|1 Nephi 2:16]] instance as Nephi's explanation that, though "exceedingly young," he was big enough to *survive the physically grueling initiation* — including being struck seven times — by which one is admitted to "the mysteries of God." See [[Nephis Large Stature Explains He Could Survive the Temple Initiation]]. [^14][^15]
- **The endowment morphs over time.** The "anti-deos"/adversary figure and the eating in the middle room still exist in modern worship "but it's been highly modified"; Joseph reportedly told Brigham "we have not got this quite right yet" and they kept "tinkering." This explains why a violent ancient rite would survive only as a "gentle... mock slap" today. See [[Ten Abandoned Temple Practices Show Continuity Through Change]]. [^16][^17]
- **Shazer = the tabernacle door (*shesh mashzar*).** Nothing happens at Shazer, so the name itself must carry the meaning. The Hebrew root behind *Shazer* means "to twist/twine" ([[1 Nephi 16.13|1 Nephi 16:13]] footnote); the related form *mashzar* appears 21 times in Exodus as the "fine twined linen" (*shesh mashzar*) of the tabernacle hangings, priestly garments, veil — and specifically the **door** by which one enters. Shazer therefore marks the family *passing through the tabernacle door* into the holy place. See [[Shazer Names the Fine-Twined-Linen Door of the Tabernacle]]. [^18][^19][^20]
- **Bountiful is Eden / the holy of holies.** Bountiful has many waters (the waters of life), Nephi meets God repeatedly and builds a boat (an ark) holding seeds, honey, and the recovered law; the *nehushtan*, Moses' smitten rock, and Ezekiel's temple-river imagery all locate the holy of holies there. See [[Lehi's Dream Recapitulates the Eden-Temple Return]] and [[The Nehushtan Is a Healing Divine Serpent the Nephites and Early Christians Valued]]. [^21][^22]
- **The book.** The whole episode promotes *In the Language of Adam* (David J. Butler & Mike Day), with QR-code bonus material; the running gag is the "not for resale" barcode on the cover and the claim it was written "in the actual language of Adam... which weirdly enough is Hungarian." [^23]
