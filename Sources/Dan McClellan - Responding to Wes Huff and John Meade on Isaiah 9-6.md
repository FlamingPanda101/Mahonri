# Dan McClellan — Responding to Wes Huff and John Meade on Isaiah 9:6

[[Dan McClellan]], writing from [[Critical Scholarship|academic-biblical-studies]], responds to a ~45-minute video on [[Wes Huff]]'s channel in which Huff hosts the Old Testament / textual-criticism specialist [[John D. Meade]] arguing that [[Isaiah 9.6|Isaiah 9:6]] (and [[Isaiah 7.14|Isaiah 7:14]]) are prophecies of the birth of Jesus. McClellan confines himself to the **Isaiah 9:6 segment**, where Meade builds a case that — McClellan argues — "rests on a claim that is fundamentally misleading."[^1] The core charge is a **text-critical oversight**: Meade presents a marginal note in **Codex Sinaiticus** as if it were 4th-century evidence that Christians already cared about the Hebrew reading, when the note is a fifth-to-seventh-century correction that *postdates* Jerome's pivot to the Hebrew.[^2]

Because Isaiah 9:6's titles ("Mighty God," "Everlasting Father") are central to Trinitarian and messianic apologetics, McClellan's data is congenial to the LDS critique of the creedal reading of these throne-names, though he is not himself making an LDS argument. The two new claims he develops are catalogued at [[The Septuagint of Isaiah 9-6 Is Expansive Not Truncated]] and [[The Codex Sinaiticus Marginal Note Postdates Jerome]]; the LXX-evasion point connects to the existing [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]].

## Summary

- **The setup.** Meade, a Septuagint/canon/textual-criticism specialist (essay in the *Oxford Handbook of Textual Criticism of the Bible*), discusses Isaiah 9:6 with Huff. McClellan flags the segment because Meade "advances a very specific argument" that "seems to rest on a claim that is fundamentally misleading" — possibly inadvertent, but if so "based on a rather egregious oversight that folks with the advanced training... that both Wes and Dr. Meade have really shouldn't be making."[^1]
- **Concern 1 — the truncated Septuagint reading.** Meade reads the LXX as "...his name will be called messenger of great counsel" and then *stops* — "I'll just stop reading there because there's no other name." This gives the false impression that the Greek ignored the rest of the Hebrew. In fact the ancient translators understood the last four words not as a name but as a **clause**, and the LXX is actually **expansive** there ("for I will bring peace to the rulers, peace and health to him"). Meade simply omits it.[^3] See [[The Septuagint of Isaiah 9-6 Is Expansive Not Truncated]].
- **The "weird translation" mischaracterization.** Meade calls the LXX rendering "weird." McClellan: the genuinely odd move (turning the names into a full sentence with added content) is the part Meade omitted; what remains is a standard LXX maneuver — turning a human-called-God into an "angel/messenger" — the translators doing what they do "in a bunch of other places."[^4]
- **The double standard with Psalm 8 and Psalm 29.** The same LSB that Huff prefers *adopts* the LXX's *elohim* → "angels" at [[Psalm 8.5|Psalm 8:5]] ("a little lower than the angels"; Hebrew: *elohim*, "gods") and punts to the KJV "mighty" / "sons of the mighty" at [[Psalm 29.1-2|Psalm 29:1]] (Hebrew *bene elim*, "sons of gods"). Evangelicals accept the LXX's "weird" reading where it suits them and dismiss it as weird where it doesn't. The old etymology deriving *el / elim / elohim* from a root "to be mighty" was "definitively put to rest" by Marvin Pope in 1955.[^5] See [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]] and [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]].
- **The Hebrew was not invisible to early Christians — but that's an exception, not the rule.** The scholarly consensus is that, up to Jerome (late 4th / early 5th c.), most Christians treated the LXX as the authoritative edition over the Hebrew, even accusing Jewish scribes of altering the Hebrew to sound less Christian. Meade's claim that the Hebrew "never factor[ed] into" Christian thought is a straw man; everyone has always known **Eusebius** noted Isaiah 9:6 as a place the Hebrew works *better* for Christians — but that was a flagged exception to his pro-LXX position, not a counter-consensus.[^6] See [[Eusebius of Caesarea]].
- **Meade's thesis: Jerome as the end of a chain, not the start.** Meade adduces the Jewish revisers (Aquila, Symmachus, Theodotion), who re-supplied Greek words for each Hebrew word, and then **Codex Sinaiticus** to argue that concern for the Hebrew predated Jerome — that Jerome was "the last link in a chain" rather than doing "something brand new."[^7]
- **The Codex Sinaiticus marginal note — the main concern.** Sinaiticus (4th c.) copies the LXX "messenger of great counsel" in the main column but carries a marginal note giving a literal rendering of the Hebrew ("Wonderful counselor, strong God, the mighty one, ruler of peace... father of the coming age"). Meade presents this like a modern study-Bible footnote — already available to 4th-century readers.[^8]
- **The egregious oversight: who wrote it, and when?** The first question any text-critic asks of a marginal note is *who added it and when*. Meade never showed the main text with the note visible — and the note is in **different ink by a different scribe**. Ken Penner's LXX-Isaiah commentary attributes it to corrector **CA** (the text), **CB** (the cross-signs), and **D** (red dots).[^9]
- **The dating sinks the argument.** Scholarly consensus (Scrivener 1864; *Scribes and Correctors of Codex Sinaiticus* 1938; Hernández/Jongkind 2013; Malik 2015 — with Skeat dating CA to the 6th c. and Myshrall's paleography concurring) places the C-correctors in the **fifth-to-seventh centuries**, most likely late-5th-to-early-7th. That is **after** Jerome's *Hebraica Veritas*. The note therefore cannot be evidence for pre-Jerome Christian concern for the Hebrew reading.[^10] See [[The Codex Sinaiticus Marginal Note Postdates Jerome]].
- **Verdict and appeal.** McClellan judges the argument "undermined by misinformation and what seems to me to be an intentional effort to mislead." He declines to appeal to Huff — citing Huff's prior refusal to take accountability for misinformation (the "undesigned coincidences" exchange) and calling him "not a good faith actor" — and instead asks **Meade** directly for timestamps showing where he has misread the argument, and what relevance the marginal note has to the 4th-century Christian position on LXX vs. Hebrew.[^11]
