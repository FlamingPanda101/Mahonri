# Let's Get Real - The Racial Curse in Abraham Isn't What You Think

**Show:** Let's Get Real (Episode E0052) | **Host:** [[Stephen Jones]] | **Guest:** [[Don Bradley]] | **Title:** "The 'Racial Curse' in Abraham Isn't What You Think | Don Bradley E0052"

A long-form interview in which historian [[Don Bradley]] argues that the "cursed as pertaining to the priesthood" language in [[Abraham 1.21-27|Abraham 1]] has been catastrophically misread through the later lens of the LDS [[priesthood ban]], and that, read on its own 1835 terms, the [[Book of Abraham]] actually *reverses* the pro-slavery "curse of Ham" reading current in [[Joseph Smith]]'s day — recasting the descendants of [[Ham]] not as slaves but as blessed with kingship. Bradley supplies a primary-source document never before brought into this debate to recover what Joseph Smith himself thought the curse meant.

- Bradley contends the Book of Abraham functions as a *backstory* to Genesis: where Genesis presents Abraham as a "flat character" chosen arbitrarily, [[Abraham 1.1-4|Abraham 1]] gives a first-person account of a man already *seeking* God — a pattern Bradley identifies as "Abraham's first vision," structurally identical to Moses 1 and Joseph Smith's First Vision.[^1] See [[Abraham as a Backstory to Genesis]] and [[Seeking God Versus Arbitrary Election]].
- He insists on a disciplined historical method — facing *forward* from Joseph Smith's 1835 vantage rather than backward through the 1852–1978 priesthood ban — because "we're looking at it through a lens that Joseph Smith didn't have."[^2] See [[Reading Abraham 1 Without the Priesthood-Ban Lens]] and [[Chronological Prejudice Against the Past]].
- Bradley documents how close interracial marriage was to Joseph Smith personally — a first cousin married a Native American woman, and Emma Smith's aunt Antha Hale married a Black man, Joseph Wallace, in a 1789 Connecticut wedding so controversial the officiating minister Elam Potter was expelled from his congregation — arguing this proximity shaped Joseph's lens.[^3] See [[Interracial Marriage in Joseph Smith's Family]].
- The interpretive core: a primary document produced alongside the translation, the [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]], describes Pharaoh as "a true descendant from Ham... an inheritor of the kingly blessings... but not according to the priestly blessing, which fell upon Shem." Bradley reads this as *division of labor / stewardship* (king-line vs. priest-line, normal in the ancient world like David vs. the Aaronic high priesthood), not racial punishment — a "curse" that is the flip side of a kingly blessing.[^4] See [[The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment]] and [[Cursed to Be Kings - The Pharaoh Reversal]].
- He tests interpretation by Joseph Smith's *actions*: Joseph ordained Black men ([[Elijah Abel]], [[Q. Walker Lewis]]), had an 1831 revelation *promoting* intermarriage with Native Americans (against the Nephite norm), prayed for "Hottentots" and "Laplanders" at the Kirtland Temple dedication, opposed slavery, advocated Black voting rights, and ran for president on an emancipation platform — none of which fits a man who read Abraham 1 as a race-based priesthood restriction.[^5] See [[Joseph Smith Ignored Race-Based Scriptural Distinctions]] and [[Testing Interpretation by the Prophet's Actions]].
- Bradley reframes the entire Book of Abraham debate away from Egyptology: a prophet's role is to "bring forth truths from God," not to be an "academic translator," so the book should be judged by its *fruits* (per Matthew 7 / Alma 32), not by which papyrus it was rendered from.[^6] See [[A Prophet Is Not an Academic Translator]] and [[Judge the Book of Abraham by Its Fruits]].
