# The Stick of Joseph — The Ancient Temple and the Sermon on the Mount Part II

Part II of the long-form conversation on the [[The Stick of Joseph - Are Christians Becoming Mormon with Brian Thomassen|Stick of Joseph]] channel with author [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] (joined by [[Mike Day]] and hosts [[Hayden Carroll]] and [[Hayden Holm]]), continuing the reading that [[The Sermon on the Mount Is an Ordinance in Three Rooms|the Sermon on the Mount is a temple ordinance]] rather than a sermon. Where [[The Stick of Joseph - The Ancient Temple and the Sermon on the Mount Part I|Part I]] laid the architecture (the three rooms, the menorah-tree, the foundation stone) and walked Matthew 5, Part II walks **Matthew 6 and 7** — the inner two rooms — arguing that the chapters' "scattered" moral teachings are actually a coherent ritual narrative incorporating the temple furniture, climaxing in a feast with the Lord and an entrance into the holy of holies. The episode's distinctive payloads are a room-by-room scheme of paired virtues, the [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast|Melchizedek feast]], and the claim that Jacob's nine woes in **2 Nephi 9** are [[The Nine Woes of 2 Nephi 9 Are Inverted Beatitudes Not New Ten Commandments|inverted Beatitudes]] — a Book-of-Mormon witness that the Beatitude ordinance predates Jesus.

## Summary
- **The "hinges" between rooms teach Justice then Mercy.** Matthew 6 opens with a teaching on *almsgiving* that is really two Greek words — *dikaiosynē* ("justice," appearing once) and *eleēmosynē* ("mercy") — at the doorway from room one into room two. Butler reads each room as carrying a paired **legal idea** (Justice → Mercy → Grace), a **priest** (Moses → Elijah → the Lord), and a **spiritual gift** (Faith → Hope → Charity). Room one (Moses, Justice, Faith) is "heavy"; the hinge pivots to the room of Mercy. Developed in [[The Three Temple Rooms Map Justice-Mercy-Grace and Moses-Elijah-the Lord]] [^1][^2].
- **Personal prayer, then group prayer, then the Lord's Prayer as an ordinance text.** Matthew 6:5-8 addresses the singular "thou" (your personal prayer); verse 9 shifts to the plural "ye" ("y'all") — a *group* prayer. Butler suggests "after this manner pray ye" may describe not words-to-repeat but "movements or a way to assemble yourself," perhaps a call-and-response — i.e. liturgical staging, not a fixed script [^3].
- **"Daily bread" is Yahweh-bread.** Mike Day's centerpiece: *epiousios* (Matt 6:11) is a hapax legomenon; parsed *epi* + the participle of "to be," it means "upon the existent one," and since Yahweh *is* the verb "to be" ([[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]]; the [[John 8.58|John "I am"]] sayings), the petition asks for "the bread that Yahweh is upon" — the Eucharistic flesh, cashed out by the feast that follows. Full argument in [[Epiousios Is Yahweh Bread Not Daily Bread]] [^4].
- **The middle room stages a feast at which the Lord descends as Melchizedek.** "Take no thought... behold the fowls of the air... consider the lilies" (Matt 6:25-33) is the Lord showing up to *feed* and *clothe* the worshipper — Adam-and-Eve being clothed by God ([[Genesis 3.21|Gen 3:21]]). The presiding figure is read as **Melchizedek** (Genesis 14), "king of Salem/*shalom*," who brings "the feast of his own flesh and blood"; the birds and the clothing are Elijah signs (fed by ravens; the mantle to Elisha; raising the widow's son). Developed in [[Melchizedek Is Yahweh Presiding Over the Temple Feast]] [^5][^6].
- **Eating really happened in the ancient temple.** Butler argues the temple had a literal feast (Exodus 24's elders "ate and drank" with God; Psalm 23's "table... beside the still waters"), and that the [[Moroni 4.3|sacrament prayers]] of Moroni 4-5 — given with no church context — may be *temple* texts. He frames the endowment and the sacrament as "two halves" of one anciently-unified ordinance [^7][^8].
- **Exodus 24 is the same three-tier ascent.** The people stay at the foot of the mountain; Moses and *Joshua* ("literally Jesus") go up with the elders to a middle level to *see God and eat*; then Moses and Joshua alone pass through a cloud to the top. "You're seeing the same thing here: an ascent through three-part space, passing through a veil, on the sacred mountain, and the feast is in the middle part — and everybody's invited" [^9].
- **Matthew 7 is the holy of holies, guarded by a renewed warning of Justice.** "Judge not" reopens with Justice (room three's legal idea); "give not that which is holy unto the dogs... lest they trample them" is the covenant penalty again, a last warning before the most sacred room [^10].
- **Ask, seek, knock — and four Messianic symbols behind the veil.** The triple petition (Matt 7:7-8) is the request for *admission*. Then "if his son ask bread, will he give him a stone? ...a fish, a serpent?" maps onto **four objects behind the veil**: bread vs. a stone (the foundation rock), a fish vs. a serpent (the *nehushtan*). Butler reads these as four Messianic signs that must be correctly given/received. Developed in [[Matthew 7 Hides Four Messianic Symbols Behind the Veil]] [^11][^12].
- **The narrow gate to life is birth through a wall of thorns and thistles.** "Strait is the gate... which leadeth unto life": the Greek *akantha* and *tribolos* ("thorns and thistles") are the very words of [[Genesis 3.21|Eden's curse]] in the Septuagint — so the thorns/thistles are a *wall* around Eden, and passing the narrow gate to **life** (Eve = *ḥavah*/*Zoē*, "mother of all living," tied to the tree) is a *birth*. Mike adds *mitzraim* (Egypt) means "narrow/tight space," read by one rabbi as the womb. Developed in [[The Narrow Gate to Life Is Birth Through the Wall of Thorns and Thistles]] [^13][^14].
- **The Beatitudes describe the ordinance's climax.** Butler maps the seven Beatitude blessings onto the rite's ending: you *see God* (he descends into the *heikhal*), *inherit the earth* (clothed like Adam, given dominion), are *filled with righteousness* (the feast of the Lord's flesh, *melek-tsedeq*), are *comforted* ("my rod and staff... his Spirit to be with them"), are *called God's child* (you ask the Father for a gift), *obtain mercy* (you pass judgment), and *receive the kingdom* (room three). The self-consistency, he argues, corroborates the reading. Builds on [[Beatitudes as the Entry Requirements of the Temple Ordinance]] [^15].
- **2 Nephi 9's nine woes are inverted Beatitudes, not a "new Ten Commandments."** A 1990s FARMS article called Jacob's woes new Ten Commandments, but there are only **nine**, and they are structured "woe unto X *for they shall* Y" — the **inverted form of a Beatitude** ("blessed are X *for they shall* Y"), with seven *repeated* punishments matching the seven blessings. Jacob is "ripping them a hole" in their own authoritative *temple* language, and 2 Nephi 4 (Psalm of Nephi) and 2 Nephi 1 (Lehi to Laman and Lemuel) reuse the same ordinance vocabulary — evidence the rite predates Christ. Developed in [[The Nine Woes of 2 Nephi 9 Are Inverted Beatitudes Not New Ten Commandments]] [^16][^17].
- **The whole ordinance is a journey from two trees to one tree.** Genesis opens with two trees (life; knowledge of good and evil); Revelation 22 ends at the holy of holies with one tree by God's throne. The temple stages the same passage: from the two pillars (Boaz and Jachin, "which are trees") in to the one tree of life — "you're Adam and Eve, you're cast out, and you do what needs to be done to come back into the presence of the one tree" [^18].
