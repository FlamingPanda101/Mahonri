# LDS scholar: We get this WRONG about ancient temples

Latter-day Saint Bible scholar [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] argues on *Keystone* that Saints often go "looking for the wrong thing" when they try to defend the temple's restoration by hunting for a one-to-one match in the Old Testament, early Christianity, or the ancient Near East. The pattern *is* there — but it comes through *adaptation*, *accommodation*, and *progressive revelation*, not as a coherent ceremony dropped intact from year 30 into 1830. He surveys the best widely-available LDS scholarship — especially Donald W. Parry's reading of the Garden of Eden as a prototype sanctuary and the Day of Atonement as a ritual reversal of the Fall — and reframes the recommend interview, gradations of holiness, the ancient covenant pattern, and even the sacrament against their ancient Near Eastern backdrop.

## Summary

- **"Restoration" is not a one-to-one copy.** Spackman cautions that if Saints look "for one coherent ceremony, I think we're not going to find it." The brethren standardizing the temple in the 1800s noted it had separable Aaronic and Melchizedek portions and "obviously some flexibility" in how it could be administered, so the right thing to look for is corresponding *parts*, not a single transplanted rite [00:00]–[02:30]. See [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]].
- **Adaptation and accommodation are the mechanism.** Drawing on the Church's own "finding answers to gospel questions" materials, he distinguishes *adaptation* (God takes something already in the culture and gives it new meaning — circumcision, Psalm 29 from Canaanite material, Deuteronomy structured as an Assyrian vassal treaty, the tabernacle's Egyptian loan-words and solar-boat-like ark) from *accommodation* (God "comes down and meets us where we are," so even direct revelation "will not be eternally ideal") [02:30]–[09:01]. Joseph's encounter with [[Freemasonry]] "evidently served as a catalyst for revelation" rather than a simple source [06:00]–[07:31]. See [[Restoration Is Adaptation and Accommodation Not a One-to-One Copy]] and the existing notes [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context]] and [[Temple Origins Run Deeper Than Freemasonry]].
- **The Garden of Eden as a prototype sanctuary.** Citing BYU professor Donald W. Parry's article "Garden of Eden: Prototype Sanctuary," Spackman lays the tabernacle over the Eden narrative: cherubim and a flaming sword guard Eden's eastern entrance; the Holy of Holies is decorated as a garden with cherubim on its doors; on the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16) the high priest carries blood *east to west* past the guardian figures and "ritually re-enters God's presence through atonement" — "a ritual undoing of the fall." In the temple, "we are all essentially doing what the high priest does" [10:02]–[13:32]. See [[The Garden of Eden Was a Prototype Sanctuary]].
- **Gradations of holiness and the guardian-figure parallel.** The temple worked by "concentric gradations of holiness": the closer you come to the Holy of Holies the holier and more restricted it is. Herod's temple had a Greek plaque warning any Gentile who crossed the dividing wall "shall be responsible for his own death"; on Sinai Moses warns the people not to cross a line "or they will die." Spackman maps this onto the bishop as "the first line as a guardian figure" and the temple recommend interview — "is your holiness and commitment sufficient to get you in the door?" — a "functional equivalence," not a claim that recommend clerks are cherubim [13:32]–[17:33]. See [[Gradations of Holiness Structured the Ancient Temple]].
- **The ancient covenant pattern and the blood of the covenant.** Old Testament covenants follow an ancient Near Eastern template (parties, stipulations, blessings, *cursings*, ratification). Formal covenants were *cut*: an animal's death enacted a "simile curse" — what would happen to a covenant-breaker. In Exodus 24 Moses sprinkles half the sacrificial blood on the people "as if their own throats had been cut," calling it "the blood of the covenant" — the very phrase Jesus reuses at the Last Supper ("this is my blood of the new covenant"). Spackman argues the Masonic penalties later brought into the temple are a mild echo of a far more graphic ancient pattern, and that animal sacrifice is not absent from the modern temple so much as "replaced with Jesus" [18:31]–[26:00]. See [[Ancient Covenant Pattern and the Blood of the Covenant]].
- **Answering "God dwells not in temples made with hands."** Spackman notes this is "more about frameworks" than individual proof-texts, recalling William Hamlin's "A Tract Made Without Evidence" reply to [[James White]]'s *Temples Made Without Hands*. He concedes that *sacrificial* temple worship ended in Christ but insists that ritual enacting covenant and atonement "is certainly something we still need," lamenting that educated, mission-serving Saints "can go through the temple for 20 years and not see any of this," which is why more explicit modern "handholding" toward atonement-in-Jesus is a mercy [26:00]–[29:01].
