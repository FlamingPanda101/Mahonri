# The Stick of Joseph — The Hidden Hebrew Wedding Ritual and the Temple

A conversation in which [[Andrea Woodmansee]] argues that the **ancient Hebrew wedding** — its three phases, its betrothal contract, its dowry, the bridegroom's year-long absence to "prepare a place," and the night-time wedding feast — is a deliberate **type of the temple, of the sealing ordinance, and of Christ as the bridegroom**. Read this way, she contends, the Parables of Jesus (the ten virgins, the king's wedding feast, the lost coin), the betrothal language of Hosea and Joel, the alabaster-box anointing, the "day no man knoweth," and even the swaddling of the infant Jesus all snap into focus as Hebrew wedding speech, and the whole Plan of Salvation reads as a wedding motif culminating in the sealing. The episode is a devotional companion to the vault's temple-theology cluster ([[Ancient Hebrew Weddings Are a Three-Phase Type of the Temple]], [[The Book of Mormon Already Contains the Endowment]], [[The I-Thou Temple Is Covenant Relationship Not Transaction]]) and grows, like its sibling [[The Stick of Joseph - The Secret Identity of the Wisemen]], out of [[Margaret Barker]]'s first-temple reconstruction.

## Summary

- **The framing thesis.** What "that ancient religion looked like... looks a lot like the temple that we know today." The wedding model "really has implications for our Temple covenants" and "adds another layer of evidence for the Book of Mormon." See [[Ancient Hebrew Weddings Are a Three-Phase Type of the Temple]].[^1]
- **Margaret Barker and a pre-Judaism religion.** Woodmansee's testimony was restored during a faith crisis by [[Margaret Barker]]'s *The Great Angel: A Study of Israel's Second God*, which opened her to "a religion that predates Judaism" — the temple religion of Moses, Abraham, and Adam, lost in [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category|Josiah's reforms]] and restored by Christ and again by [[Joseph Smith]].[^2][^3]
- **Adorned as a bride.** [[D&C 109|D&C 109:73-74]], the revealed Kirtland Temple dedicatory prayer, has the Church "adorned as a bride." [[Hosea 2.19-20|Hosea 2:19]] ("I will betroth thee unto me forever") and the New Testament bridegroom/feast imagery are not poetic decoration but the wedding model. See [[D&C 109]].[^4]
- **The ten virgins is a real wedding, not a riddle.** [[Matthew 25.1-13|The parable of the ten virgins]] is "the weirdest wedding you've ever heard of" until you supply the Hebrew customs: the bride does not know the day or hour because the *groom* does not know — only the father does. See [[The Parable of the Ten Virgins Encodes Hebrew Wedding Customs]].[^5]
- **Three phases of the wedding = three phases of the temple.** (1) *Shidduch* — matchmaking: the **father of the groom** selects the bride (as God chose Eve for [[Adam]]) and negotiates terms; the bride may accept or veto. (2) *Erusin* — formal betrothal: signing the **ketubah** and separation for a year. (3) the wedding itself. Woodmansee ties this to the three-part temple procession.[^6][^7]
- **The ketubah and the Jerusalem window.** The ketubah was the written marriage contract, decorated inside an arched "Jerusalem window" on a blue ground, listing what the **groom** would do for the bride (provide food, clothing, redemption from captivity). The 2020 Church symbol — Christ under an arch on blue — *is* a ketubah: Christ is the covenant, the contract, and the bridegroom. See [[The Ketubah and the Jerusalem Window Depict Christ as the Marriage Covenant]].[^8][^9]
- **The dowry = the endowment.** The groom's family gave a *mohar* (dowry) as a permanent fail-safe for the bride — "a gift that keeps on giving." *Dowry* → *endowment*; the temple endowment is the bridegroom's gift of enduring love, not another box to check.[^10][^11]
- **The alabaster box was dowry oil.** Part of the dowry was an alabaster box of ointment the bride kept to anoint her husband's body at death. The woman of [[Luke 7.36-50|Luke 7]] (and the parallel anointings in Matthew 26 and Mark 14), likely [[Mary Magdalene]], was recognizing [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]] as the bridegroom who would redeem her from captivity to sin. See [[The Alabaster Box Was the Bride's Dowry Oil to Anoint the Bridegroom]].[^12][^13]
- **Tokens and the lost coin.** The groom also gave the *mattan*, personal gifts to remember him by during the separation — including coins sewn onto the bride's veil/headdress as a sign of marriage. The woman's "lost coin" was a wedding token, "the equivalent of losing your very expensive wedding ring"; the signs and tokens are a sign of marriage to Christ.[^14]
- **"Behold, I stand at the door and knock."** In the betrothal, the father and groom came to the bride's door; if she opened it, she accepted — a binding marriage, not a soft engagement (hence [[Matthew 1.16|Joseph could not simply break it off with Mary]]). Christ at the door is bridegroom-speech: opening the door is accepting marriage to him.[^15]
- **"I go to prepare a place for you."** After betrothal the groom returned to his father's estate to **build the bridal home** — which is why it took a year, and why [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]] says he goes to prepare a place (mansions).[^16]
- **The covenant sash and swaddling clothes.** The bride embroidered a long two-sided sash (family genealogy, symbols) wrapped around the couple's joined hands — "tying the knot" — and later used to **swaddle their newborn** as a sign of covenant birth. Jesus's swaddling clothes were not rags but this covenant sash, marking him born-in-the-covenant. See [[Swaddling Clothes Were the Embroidered Covenant Sash]].[^17]
- **The day no man knoweth = Rosh Hashanah.** The wedding came in the fall (post-harvest); the bride knew the season but not the day or hour. The groom's friend (best man) ran ahead **blowing a trumpet** — the Feast of Trumpets / *Rosh Hashanah*, "the day no man knoweth," because it begins on the new moon that cannot be seen. [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] (depicted with a trumpet) came to Joseph Smith on Rosh Hashanah. See [[The Day No Man Knoweth Is Rosh Hashanah Wedding Idiom]].[^18][^19]
- **The lifting up of the bride.** The procession carried the bride on a platform (*aperion*) to the father's house — "the lifting up of the bride," echoing Christ's "I will lift you up." The door was then bolted; recognition was by **voice** (cf. "I never knew you"), requiring a real relationship of prayer with the bridegroom.[^20][^21]
- **Outer darkness, terrestrially.** A night wedding meant the only lit house in the village was the feast; the five foolish virgins and the guest without a wedding garment ([[Matthew 22.30|the king's wedding feast parable]]) are cast "into outer darkness" — literally left outside in the dark. The wise virgins' **oil** came from olives pressed from the tree (wisdom/the tree of life).[^22][^23]
- **The four cups and the bitter cup.** A Jewish wedding has four cups of wine; the fourth is the **cup of wrath / bitterness / judgment**. In an ordinary wedding no one drinks the bitter cup — but "the bridegroom of us all" drank it so his bride would not. [[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5]] and [[3 Nephi 11.10-11|3 Nephi 11]] have covenanters spared the cup of wrath because the bridegroom drank it. See [[The Bridegroom Drank the Bitter Fourth Cup So the Bride Would Not]].[^24][^25]
- **Blood on the wedding cloth.** The consummation produced blood on a white embroidered cloth proving the bride's virginity (its absence meant death). Since none of us is innocent, "whose blood's on the cloth? Christ's." When the bridegroom emerges his bride is celebrated, not stoned; in Revelation the saints are clothed in white linen while he wears red. See [[Christ's Blood on the Wedding Cloth Covers the Impure Bride]] and [[Revelation 19.11-16|Revelation 19]].[^26][^27]
- **Cleaving = devekut.** [[Genesis 2.24|Genesis 2:24]] ("a man shall... cleave unto his wife") uses *devak*, the same verb as "cleave unto the LORD" (Joshua 23:8); Jewish mysticism is *devekut*, "cleaving" to Yahweh, contrasted with Eastern mysticism's self-extinction. Marriage and Jesus's prayer that we "be one" share the same oneness. See [[Genesis 2.24]].[^28]
- **Ten witnesses and the temple recommend.** The bride brought **ten** witnesses (hence ten virgins), the groom two; the ketubah was signed by two non-family witnesses. Woodmansee notes the temple recommend carries Christ in a Jerusalem window on one side and two signatures on the other.[^29]
- **The fasting saying.** [[Mark 2.19-20|Mark 2:19-20]] — the disciples do not fast "as long as they have the bridegroom with them," but will fast "when the bridegroom shall be taken away." We fast now partly because the bridegroom is absent; the hunger is a longing for his return. See [[Mark 2.19-20]].[^30]
- **The feasts (moadim) are wedding appointments.** The *moadim* ("appointed times," mistranslated "seasons" in Genesis 1:14) are appointments to feast in God's presence. Passover (the bride price paid in blood), Unleavened Bread (the groom in the tomb / the year of separation), Firstfruits (he emerges), Pentecost (gifts poured out), and the fall feasts — Rosh Hashanah, the Day of Atonement (the bitter cup), and Tabernacles (the wedding booth/*chuppah*) — are prophetic and all carry wedding undertones; Christ fulfills each.[^31][^32]
- **Taking the name.** The bride takes the groom's name; "taking his name upon us" ([[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5]]) is entering a covenantal marriage with Christ, sealed to him so that, "like a bride," one is taken to the father's house.[^33]
- **The Book of Mormon is saturated with it.** [[1 Nephi 11.18|Nephi's tree-of-life vision]] answers "what is the tree?" with **Mary**, tying the woman to the tree; King Benjamin's *Sukkot*-style assembly, [[Mosiah 5.7-9|Mosiah 5]] as a temple/wedding text, and especially [[3 Nephi 11.10-11|3 Nephi 11]] (Christ the light coming in the dark, having drunk the bitter cup) "is all Hebrew wedding." [[Joel 2.30-31|Joel 2:16]] likewise: "let the bridegroom go forth of his chamber, and the bride out of her closet."[^34][^35]
