# The Council of Trent — The Word of God Fallacy

[[Trent Horn]] argues that the modern Protestant case for [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority|sola scriptura]] — as advanced by [[Wes Huff]] and [[Gavin Ortlund]] — rests on a hidden equivocation: it treats "Scripture is the word of God" as a self-evident or self-attested premise, when in fact the biblical phrase **word of God** is never applied to a written human text. If knowing that Scripture *is* the word of God comes from outside Scripture (tradition, the Church), then a second infallible authority exists and sola scriptura collapses.

## Summary
- The contemporary apologetic (Huff, Ortlund) defends sola scriptura **logically rather than proof-textually**: Scripture is "ontologically unique," the "direct speech of God," therefore infallible and the only infallible rule of faith — but the load-bearing first premise (Scripture *is* God's speech) is either assumed or defended with irrelevant examples [^1]. See [[The Word of God Fallacy - The Load-Bearing Premise Is Unproven]].
- Horn's central dilemma: if we know Scripture is the word of God **apart from Scripture** (via tradition or the Church's teaching office), then another infallible authority exists, which **nullifies sola scriptura**; if we know it *from* Scripture, the argument is circular and, worse, no verse actually says it [^2]. See [[Knowing Scripture Is the Word of God Requires an Authority Outside Scripture]] and [[No Verse Calls a Human Writing the Word of God]].
- A lexical survey shows **"word of God" (logos/rhēma) in both Testaments refers to (a) prophetic speech, (b) the apostolic preaching/gospel, (c) God's specific salvation-historical utterances, or (d) Christ himself** — never to a particular human writing; [[1 Thessalonians 2.13|1 Thess 2:13]] explicitly contrasts spoken word received "not as the word of men" but as God's word [^3]. See [[Word of God Means Prophecy Preaching or Christ Not Scripture]] and [[Luke Identifies the Word of God With the Apostolic Preaching and the Church]].
- The standard Protestant proof-texts are reread in context: **Mark 7 / Matthew 15** target Yahweh's *orally delivered* commandments, not the written text (and Matt 15:6 has a "command/law of God" variant) [^4]; **Romans 3:2** says *logia* (oracles), not *logos* [^5]; **John 10:35** uses "word of God" for a prophetic event behind Psalm 82, and "cannot be broken" means *cannot be nullified*, not *inerrant* [^6]; **Hebrews 4:12** describes God's commands or Christ, paired with Rev 19:13 [^7]. See [[Mark 7 and Matthew 15 Concern Oral Commandments Not Scripture]], [[Romans 3.2 Says Oracles Logia Not Words Logos]], [[John 10.35 Cannot Be Broken Means Cannot Be Nullified]], and [[Hebrews 4.12 Word of God Is Christ or Gods Commands Not Scripture]].
- The earliest **church fathers** (Clement, Didache, Barnabas, Ignatius, early Justin) call the word of God either the Christian *message* or *Christ himself*, and do not even call the NT "Scripture" before Justin Martyr; explicit "Scripture = word of God" statements emerge gradually (e.g. Basil, 4th c.), which Horn reads as evidence that this identification is itself a deliverance of **sacred tradition** — confirming Dei Verbum's "Scripture, Tradition, Magisterium" triad [^8]. See [[Calling Scripture the Word of God Is a Late Patristic Development]] and [[The Word of God Was Never Confined to the Written Word]].
