# Matt Fradd — Debates, Apologetics, and Answering Atheism (Gavin Ortlund & Trent Horn) Ep 392

A friendly, cross-tradition roundtable on **[[Matt Fradd]]**'s *Pints with Aquinas*, recorded the morning after a live formal debate between **[[Gavin Ortlund]]** (Reformed Baptist pastor, Ph.D. historical theology) and **[[Trent Horn]]** (Catholic Answers). Two parts: (1) a conversation on **apologetic method** — debate ethics, charity, steelmanning, preparation, and how Catholic-Protestant polemics have evolved; and (2) a turn to **answering atheism**, including the moral argument, an abductive argument from music, the cosmological argument, divine simplicity, and the problem of pre-human animal suffering, followed by audience Q&A. The tone is collaborative throughout: a Catholic and a Protestant largely on the *same team* against naturalism.[^1]

## Summary
- **Debate's value is to begin issues, not end them.** Horn: "for me, debates don't end issues, they begin them" — their virtue is getting audiences "excited about the topic" enough to investigate, not delivering a verdict; "if you make a decision of what to believe based on watching one debate, that's not a sound epistemology."[^2] Ortlund prizes the *structure*: equal time and an "objective process," like a job interview, out of which "this dynamic emerges... that helps us discern the truth."[^3]
- **Rhetoric vs argument is the format's hazard.** Ortlund's one dislike: "for the audience the rhetoric will often be more influential than the arguments." Both stress that *how* you argue is part of the content — 1 Corinthians 13: without love, argument is "a clanging gong... not just insufficient, it's counterproductive."[^4] Captured in the new [[Heinleins Razor - Start With Misunderstanding Not Malice]].
- **Charity as method — Heinlein's razor.** Horn's rule: "don't attribute to malice what can be attributed to ignorance"; the first instinct toward an opponent's error should be "this person *misunderstood* me," not "misrepresented me," while preserving the option to charge misrepresentation when warranted. He also resists the label "anti-Catholic"/"anti-Protestant" for mere critics, and limits mockery to a "salt"-like sparing use (Christ with the Pharisees, Elijah vs the prophets of Baal). See the new [[Heinleins Razor - Start With Misunderstanding Not Malice]], and existing [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]], [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]].
- **Advice for would-be debaters.** Preparation is "95%" of a debate; learn the medium (debate ≠ sermon ≠ academic paper); start in your own "division," not against the heavyweight champion; surround yourself with honest counselors ("a plan with many counselors succeeds") who will tell you if it isn't your gift; and not every conversation needs to be "for the cameras." Don't be a "Craig clone" — be yourself.[^5]
- **Both sides misrepresent each other on sola scriptura.** Horn names Catholic errors (the John 21 "not enough books in the world" retort fails — Scripture need not record everything) and Protestant ones (quoting verses that praise Scripture, or leaning on a single verse like 2 Timothy 3:17, doesn't by itself establish *sola scriptura*, since a Catholic affirms Scripture's inspiration too).[^6] (This episode is the next-day debrief of the formal debate covered in [[Matt Fradd - Gavin Ortlund vs Trent Horn Is Sola Scriptura True]].)
- **Peace amid disagreement is not from study alone.** Both grant that for any major position you can find an intelligent defender you can't personally refute — but "it doesn't follow that none of them are true... someone has to be right." Conviction comes from a "holistic engagement at every level of your being," locating identity in "the core of your faith, the gospel itself," not in peripheral beliefs; Horn even commends, when evidence is 50/50, "living out that thing that you want to be true."[^7] Adjacent to existing [[Living With Uncertainty Is a Faith Skill]], [[Diagnose the Struggle - Moral Disagreement vs Intellectual Doubt]].
- **The affective turn — apologetics for despair.** Both target the *axiology* of theism (Ortlund's *Why God Makes Sense in a World That Doesn't* casts classical arguments as a narrative; Pascal-influenced: reach 65% probability *plus* the sense that "it would really be an expansive wonderful thing if God does exist"). Ortlund's growing interest is "cultural apologetics" addressing modern despair, loneliness, and a "spiritual restlessness"; both note the rise (and waning) of new atheism, [[Jordan Peterson]] as a "gateway drug," and that the religiously unaffiliated rose from ~5% to ~30% in 30 years.[^8] Adjacent to existing [[Nihilism as Logical Endpoint of Atheism]], [[Heat Death of the Universe Voids Naturalistic Meaning]], [[Meaninglessness Is a Philosophical Assumption Not a Scientific Result]].
- **"Atheism" names no single worldview.** Knowing someone is an atheist doesn't tell you their metaphysics: [[Philip Goff]] defends fine-tuning and cosmic teleology while non-religious; some atheists are reductive materialists, others admit immaterial reality.[^9] The argument that atheism's reductive story is "a rather grim one" overlaps existing [[Atheisms Four Bad Implications]].
- **The moral argument, pressed on grounding.** Horn's pro-life-rooted version: atheism struggles to ground the *intrinsic dignity* and *equality* of humans that even secular people swear by — "we ought to treat equals equally and unequals unequally," so what is equal about us? Not cognitive capacity (that would exclude the severely disabled and demote infants/embryos), so a "more metaphysical, transcendent ground" is needed.[^10] Overlaps existing [[Equality Loses Its Grounding Without God and Souls]], [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]]. Ortlund presses *Justice* and *Love*: feel the deepest injustice or the love for your child, then reconceive it as "only there because it helped your animal ancestors survive" — "I would say that's devastating."[^11]
- **Horn's Euthyphro-for-atheists.** A new dilemma mirroring Euthyphro: "is morality true because it's useful, or is it useful because it's true?" — true-because-useful collapses into utilitarian horror (historic euthanasia of the marginalized); useful-because-true concedes a prior moral truth needing a deeper ground. The math parallel ("you didn't invent mathematics by evolving to count tigers") blocks the evolutionary debunking move. Ortlund: "a nice twist on the moral argument." See the new [[Is Morality True Because Useful or Useful Because True]].
- **Abductive arguments from music (and math).** Ortlund's modest, inference-to-best-explanation arguments: music conveys transcendence non-representationally, a genuine puzzle for philosophers of music, better explained by the Christian tradition of music as "the first language" of creation than by uncertain evolutionary psychology. He deliberately offers skeptics "the back door" rather than an airtight proof. See the new [[Music Conveys Transcendence Better Explained by Theism]].
- **Cosmological argument — avoid the beginning-of-the-universe trap.** Both prefer contingency/necessity formulations over the Kalam so as to sidestep multiverse and infinite-oscillation technicalities (Ed Feser's "first way" doesn't depend on a temporal beginning; [[Aristotle]]'s eternal universe would still require God). Horn adds Andrew Loke's and Alex Pruss's causal-finitism arguments and a dark-energy point against perpetual oscillation.[^12] Fully covered by existing [[The Leibnizian Cosmological Argument from Contingency]], [[Infinite and Eternal Are Not the Same - The Regress Objection Rebutted]], [[Who Made God - Only What Begins Needs a Cause]].
- **Is God's non-existence possible? Anselm and modality.** Horn: no — God is logically necessary, so "there is no possible world where God does not exist," contrasting epistemic vs metaphysical possibility (you can *imagine* God's non-existence the way you can imagine an unsolved conjecture being false, but only one is metaphysically possible; "when people are thinking of nothing, usually they're not actually thinking of nothing"). [[Anselm of Canterbury|Anselm]]: God "cannot even be thought not to exist"; [[Richard Swinburne]] by contrast holds God factually but not logically necessary.[^13] Relates to existing [[Contingent and Necessary Beings]].
- **Divine simplicity is not a Catholics-only doctrine.** Horn: God "has no parts"; it protects God's aseity and absoluteness (if love and righteousness existed *external* to God, that would puncture divine absoluteness). Ortlund stresses it is "a historic Protestant doctrine... in all the Protestant traditions" until the 19th–20th centuries — so [[Cameron Bertuzzi]]'s objection that Lateran IV's infallible teaching of simplicity is a *Catholic* distinctive misfires.[^14] Covered by existing [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind]].
- **Problem of pre-human animal suffering — the hardest one.** Both grant the traditional theodicies (esp. free-will defense) don't reach the animal realm before the human fall. Horn offers multiple reasons (animal suffering as a necessary consequence of a material world per Aquinas; pain as needed for survival response — pain-free animals would be "furry robots"; possible animal resurrection/compensation). Ortlund offers his distinctive proposal that the **satanic fall may have disordered the natural order** (Balthasar, Plantinga, Lewis, Tolkien), giving "conceptual space" but admittedly "not... for actual apologetics with a skeptic." See the new [[The Satanic Fall May Have Influenced the Natural Order]], plus existing [[Animal Suffering Is the Hardest Test for Finite Theism]], [[No Death Before the Fall Is a Twentieth-Century Interpretation Not Settled Doctrine]].
- **Q&A grab-bag.** Most-experienced God: Horn at Golgotha/Church of the Holy Sepulchre (faith "rooted in a time and place"); Ortlund through answered prayer and seeing lives changed in ministry. "What if it's all a figment of imagination?" — bare possibilities are "cheap" and don't move probabilities (the Matrix, solipsism). Infinite-past/multiple-Big-Bangs — use contingency, not Kalam. On doubt: a "stab of doubt now and again" can be "a sign of a healthy faith" (Lewis on moods; Keller on antibodies); the cosmological argument is a "handrail" on a steep staircase. Science-vs-God is "more apparent than substantive" — loud celebrity scientists, plus Elaine Ecklund's finding that atheists are drawn to science rather than science producing atheists.[^15] Scientism's "metal detector" / sandpit-diamonds image (the wrong tool can't find what it isn't built to detect) is already in existing [[Scientism - The Metal Detector Fallacy]]. The "stoned ape" theory (visions as psychedelics) fails: explaining *some* cases doesn't explain *all*; Jesus doesn't sound like "a stoned first-century carpenter" (the trilemma), and psychedelic "deepities" (Dennett) only sound profound.[^16]
- **How belief informs life.** Horn: keep your "circle of influence" (wife, children) from being crowded out by a "circle of concern" over things you can't control (what the Pope said on a plane). Ortlund: theism makes "every single minute of your life" matter with "literally eternal consequences," whereas atheistic meaning is at best self-made — "a kind of playful nihilism." Both confess the temptation to treat God "more like a philosophical argument... than an omnipotent being," admiring charismatic Christians who "act as if God exists."[^17]
