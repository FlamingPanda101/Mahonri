# These Philosophers Weren't Looking for Mormonism. Neither Was I. But Here We Are...

Alex Arnold of *Moroni's Standard* — an Army veteran who came to the restored gospel "through history and archaeology rather than philosophy" — argues that secular and non-LDS thinkers, working independently in different fields and for different reasons, keep reconstructing the distinctive doctrines of the Restoration. He opens with the agnostic philosopher [[Emerson Green]], who set out to answer Sam Harris's claim that Mormonism is "the least plausible form of Christianity" and concluded the opposite: that LDS theology, *precisely because of what it removes from classical Christianity*, is "among the more philosophically defensible options on the table."[^1] The thesis is comparative, not evidential: the convergence does not prove the Church true, but it dismantles the "[[Sam Harris]] framing" that the Restoration is "Christianity plus implausible things, therefore obviously worse."

- **The Restoration replaced the foundation; it didn't add a floor.** Harris's "Christianity plus" assumes LDS theology is classical Christianity with extra claims layered on. Arnold's counter: the Restoration *removed* the later philosophical imports — creation *ex nihilo*, the Nicene Trinity, eternal conscious torment — and returned to a "pre-creedal, pre-edited theological baseline" that matches both the oldest sources and the conclusions careful thinkers keep reaching [^9][^10][^11].
- **Independent thinkers keep landing on LDS-shaped positions.** [[Emerson Green]] (agnostic), [[Dale Tuggy]] (the social Trinity is the most defensible Trinitarianism; the LDS Christ is more coherent), [[David Ray Griffin]] (finite theism is the only honest answer to evil, 1976), [[Philip Goff]] (fine-tuning points to a God *within* mathematical law), and [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] (the LDS framework answers the problem of evil better than classical Christianity) — none LDS, none "doing us any favors" [^7][^8][^14][^15][^17].
- **Removing *ex nihilo* changes the character of the problem of evil.** Under an unconstrained, ex-nihilo God, every instance of suffering is a free design choice from a blank slate; under LDS eternal matter and finite theism, suffering reflects the *constraints of a process* God did not author — the move [[David Ray Griffin]], [[Alex OConnor|O'Connor]], and Harold Kushner all make [^12][^13][^16].
- **The hardest remaining objection is animal suffering.** [[Paul Draper]], Green, and O'Connor press hundreds of millions of years of predation before humans as the toughest case for finite theism. Arnold concedes the LDS framework "doesn't claim to have resolved this," only that it relocates suffering from a chosen design feature to the cost of a constrained process [^16][^18].
