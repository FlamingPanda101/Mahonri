# Apotheosis Narrative — Ex Materia or Ex Nihilo? Creation in Genesis 1:1-2

A blog post by **Jaxon** at *The Apotheosis Narrative* (an LDS apologetics blog), arguing that Genesis 1:1-2 does not describe *creatio ex nihilo* (creation out of nothing) but *creatio ex materia* (creation from pre-existing, unformed matter). Its method is almost entirely a **compilation of authority**: the author assembles **40 quotations from highly-reputable academic sources** — Oxford, Cambridge, Yale, Brill, Eerdmans, and university-press scholars, many of them Jewish or secular and none of them LDS — that converge on reading Genesis 1 as the *ordering* of pre-existent chaos rather than absolute origination. The rhetorical force is the convergence: if the people with no Restoration axe to grind keep concluding "ex materia," then the Latter-day Saint reading of creation as God organizing eternal matter is not a sectarian eccentricity but the mainstream of critical scholarship.

## Summary

The post organizes its 40 quotations around several interlocking claims, every one of which is already covered elsewhere in the vault *except* its signature grammatical argument and one Septuagint observation:

- **The grammatical core (new).** Following the medieval Jewish commentator **Rashi**, several quoted scholars (Marvin Sweeney, Ronald Hendel, John Levenson, Ziony Zevit, Edwin Good, John Collins) argue that the first word *bereshit* ("in beginning of") is a **construct form lacking the definite article**, so the verb *bara* cannot be a finite perfect ("created") but must form a construct chain — yielding "When God began to create…" The whole of 1:1-2 then becomes a **dependent/circumstantial clause** describing the state of things *before* the first creative act, with the *tohu wa-bohu*, darkness, the deep, and the waters already present. The familiar independent-clause rendering ("In the beginning God created…") is later — it "dates to the Hellenistic period (as in the Septuagint), when the details of classical Hebrew grammar had been forgotten" (Hendel). This is developed in [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading]].
- **A Septuagint tell (new).** Ron Hendel notes that the LXX rendered *tohu wa-bohu* with **ἀόρατος καὶ ἀκατασκεύαστος** ("unseen and unorganized"), and that *aoratos* is **Plato's** technical term for the unseen world of Forms — so the Greek translators read Genesis through a Platonic, pre-existent-matter cosmology. See [[The Septuagint Rendered Tohu wa-Bohu With Plato's Term for the Unseen]].
- **Bara does not mean "create from nothing"** (Walton, Westermann, VanGemeren) — the absence of any material in *bara* contexts reflects that *bara* is a *functional* activity, not evidence of material creation from nothing. Already covered in [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]] and [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]].
- **The starting state is chaos, not nothing** — *tohu wa-bohu*, *tehom* ("the deep," cognate with the Babylonian *Tiamat* of *Enuma Elish*), darkness, and the primordial waters are simply *there* when the narrative opens; creation is separation and ordering (Brettler, Brueggemann, Levenson, Cassuto, Rendsburg, Whybray, Crotty). Covered in [[Genesis 1 Creation Is Ordering and Separation Not Ex Nihilo]] and [[The Temple Reenacts the Primordial Mound Rising from the Waters of Chaos]].
- **Ex nihilo is a late doctrine** — it "appeared suddenly in the latter half of the second century" against the entire Greco-Roman philosophical world (Hubler, May, McGrath, Krach), with **2 Maccabees 7:28** the first text later read that way. Covered in [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]], [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]], and [[Greco-Roman Jewish Creation View Was Material Substrate]].

The author's caveat-by-inclusion is notable: he quotes the **steelman** voices too — the rabbinic anxiety in *Genesis Rabbah* (Gamaliel, Bar Kappara) that admitting pre-existent matter would liken God to "a king who built his palace on a garbage dump," and modern scholars (Mangum/Custis/Widder, Anderson) who stress that the biblical writers were simply *not asking* where matter came from, so the text neither teaches nor refutes ex nihilo. This is the honest shape of the evidence and is reflected in the Supporting Evidence sections of the linked concept notes.
