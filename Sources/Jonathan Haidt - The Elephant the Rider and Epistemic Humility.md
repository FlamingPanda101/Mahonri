# Jonathan Haidt — The Elephant, the Rider, and Epistemic Humility

An interview with social psychologist [[Jonathan Haidt]] in which he lays out his core model of the mind (the conscious "rider" atop the automatic "elephant"), explains why persuasion must address the elephant first, summarizes [[Moral Foundations Theory|Moral Foundations Theory]] and his research on libertarian psychology, and closes with a plea for **epistemological humility** — the recognition that individuals are not as smart as they think, and that wisdom emerges from systems, not lone reasoners. Assigned in the Thoughtful Faith course (Level 1, sub-section 1.1, Epistemology) as a non-LDS philosophy/psychology explainer.

## Summary
- **The mind is a rider on an elephant.** The conscious, verbal, logical mind (the rider) is dwarfed by the automatic, emotional ~99% of mental life (the elephant); this is Haidt's signature metaphor from *The Happiness Hypothesis*, popularized by Chip and Dan Heath's *Switch* — see [[The Rider and the Elephant Model of the Mind]] [^1].
- **Persuasion must speak to the elephant first.** Most people argue to the rider with reasons, but the elephant is far stronger; if the elephant "leans in," persuading the rider is effortless, and if it digs in, no argument works — see [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]] [^2][^3].
- **Political opponents are not irrational idiots.** Studying morality across the left/right divide led Haidt to listen to conservatives and find "they know things that you don't know"; talking only to those who agree teaches little — he describes stepping "out of the Matrix" and ceasing to be a partisan liberal — see [[Listening to Those Who Disagree Reveals Your Blind Spots]] [^4][^5].
- **Moral Foundations Theory.** Morality varies across cultures around a set of innate "taste buds": care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, and (sixth) liberty — yielding three "moral cuisines": progressive, social-conservative, and libertarian — see [[Moral Foundations Theory]] [^6][^7].
- **Libertarian psychology.** Haidt's research finds libertarians highest on systemizing and lowest on empathizing — the "most masculine cognitive style" — receptive to the idea that genius emerges from systems (markets) without a central mind [^7][^8].
- **Epistemological humility (the epistemic payoff).** "As individuals we're actually not all that smart on our own ... not as smart as we think we are"; we are like neurons whose genius comes from getting the system right — so a little epistemological humility from everyone would help — see [[Epistemological Humility - We Are Not As Smart As We Think]] [^9][^10].
- **Three conversational modes.** Discussions run in combat mode (battle stations — look for why the other is wrong), discovery/curiosity mode, or relationship mode; acknowledgement is the key that melts defenses [^9].
