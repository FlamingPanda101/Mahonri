A **WARD RADIO** episode hosted by **[[Cardon Ellis]]** with **[[Sean Bailey]]** (author on Second Coming / Book of Revelation themes), **[[Brad Whitbeck]]**, the historian **[[Don Bradley]]**, and **[[Josh Gailey|Joshua Gailey]]** (a [[William Bickerton|Bickertonite]], The Church of Jesus Christ, Pennsylvania). The panel walks through **who the lost ten tribes of Israel were and where they are now**, alternating a serious historical baseline with a running comedy bit about hollow earth and the North Pole.

## Summary

- **The baseline (Sean Bailey).** Jacob/Israel's twelve sons became twelve tribes; under [[Joshua]] they inherited the land; after Solomon the kingdom split into a northern kingdom of ten tribes (Ephraim, Manasseh, and others) and a southern kingdom of two (Judah and Benjamin). The northern kingdom apostatized into idolatry [^1].
- **The Assyrian conquest and three outcomes.** Assyria overran the northern kingdom (~721 BC). Three things happened: some northerners fled south (e.g., [[Lehi]], of Manasseh — see [[Lehi Was of Manasseh So the Book of Mormon Is a Northern Israelite Record]]); some intermarried with the Assyrians to become the **Samaritans**; and the rest were deported and scattered northward [^2].
- **The Assyrian scatter strategy (Don Bradley).** Bradley explains the deportation *method*: Assyria broke conquered peoples' capacity to resist by scattering them through the empire and importing others to mix in, dissolving their distinct ethnic identity — so the deportees' descendants are simply the peoples of that region, not a preserved hidden nation [^3].
- **Samaritans → Palestinians (Bradley).** The Samaritans were *not* lost — known in Jesus' day and for centuries after (estimated ~2 million by the 700s AD), then largely force-converted to Islam, leaving only a few hundred self-identifying Samaritans today. Their descendants in the land are, Bradley argues, the **Palestinians** — so "when the Jews and the Palestinians are fighting they're actually both descendants of the House of Israel" — see [[The Palestinians and Jews Are Both House of Israel]] [^4].
- **Jews always intermarried.** The panel notes Jewish communities took on the appearance of host populations (Ethiopian Jews, a long-vanished Chinese Jewish community, Sephardic vs. Ashkenazi), illustrating that diaspora Israel blended widely — bearing on [[Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent]] [^5].
- **Ephraim in the British Isles.** Patriarchal blessings of British-Isles converts overwhelmingly declared the tribe of Ephraim; combined with British "House of Israel" traditions, the panel infers Ephraimites migrated there — see [[Patriarchal Blessings Reveal Tribal Lineage and Cannot Be Faked]] [^6].
- **A second Israelite temple with cannabis residue.** Bradley relays archaeology of an Israelite temple outside Jerusalem (a deuteronomistic "apostasy" of competing worship sites) whose altar residue tested positive for **cannabis** — paralleling the Oracle at Delphi's natural hallucinogenic fumes [^7].
- **Myth-busting the location.** The hosts "annihilate" three myths: that the ten tribes are all in one body somewhere; that we have *no* clue where they are (we partly do — the Palestinians; Ephraim/Manasseh among the Latter-day Saints); and that they will have their own prophet and religion (they will share the one prophet and priesthood line, gathered to their lands of inheritance per [[3 Nephi]] 21) [^8].
- **Gathering as missionary work, focused on Israel.** Both the LDS and Bickertonite churches "passionately pursue Israel" as the focus of the gathering; Bradley notes (from his book *The Lost 116 Pages*) that early Joseph Smith framed himself more as a **gatherer of Israel** than as a restorer of the New Testament church. Israel cannot be called *gathered/restored* until it accepts Jesus Christ as Messiah [^9].
- **Hollow-earth comedy.** Threaded throughout: jokes that the tribes are in the hollow earth / North Pole / among "lizard people," anchored to a real factoid — Bradley/Whitbeck cite an early-20th-c. Scandinavian LDS convert's science-fiction story about an entrance to a hollow earth in pursuit of the ten tribes, recognized as an early Hollow-Earth reference [^10].
