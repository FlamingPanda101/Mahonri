# Ward Radio — Debunking Attacks on the Golden Plates with REAL Golden Plates

Host [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Jonah Barnes]], [[Oak Norton]], and [[Brad Whitbeck]] rebut the "golden plates were too heavy to be real" argument, using a 40-lb replica and clips of [[Bill McKeever]] (CARM) and [[Aaron Shafovaloff]] doing weighted-replica demonstrations at the Manti pageant, plus a Mormon Stories listener's galvanized-tin replica.

## Summary

- **The "solid gold = 200 lb" premise contradicts the witnesses.** Critics ([[Bill McKeever]], [[Aaron Shafovaloff]]) build a ~200-lb solid-gold block from the plates' stated dimensions and argue Joseph could not have run with it. The panel answers that no witness ever described solid gold: roughly 21 informal witnesses (Martin Harris, Emma Smith, William Smith, Orson Pratt, John Whitmer, [[Lucy Mack Smith]], hostile observers, etc.) estimated **40-60 lb**, described turning the leaves like pages, and noted a greenish patina (copper) and blackened ciphers (silver) — consistent with a [[Tumbaga Alloy Explains Golden Appearance and Green Oxidation|tumbaga alloy]], not bullion. See [[Witness Weight Estimates of the Plates Refute the Solid-Gold Strawman]].
- **The "too heavy" argument is self-defeating and easily falsified physically.** The hosts note that even 40 lb is liftable and runnable (the Farm-boy Joseph; the actor in *Witnesses* did multiple takes), so the demonstration proves nothing — and that the argument secretly assumes Joseph was not a prophet while purporting to disprove it. This is the live-action version of the existing rebuttal [[The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument]]; in the climactic clip an ordinary "Mormon mom" stops McKeever cold at the pageant by asking who said the plates were 200 lb.
- **The cessationist/atheist parallel and Nibley's diamond parable.** The panel's framing claim is that CARM-style countercult apologists "argue like atheists": they grant a worldview with the Red Sea and the Resurrection but balk at a 40-lb miracle and demand academic-archaeological proof while denying Latter-day Saints the appeal to faith. They close with [[Hugh Nibley]]'s diamond-in-the-field parable: the only test that matters is to examine the stone itself — i.e., *read the book*. See [[Anti-Mormons Borrow Atheist Weaponry Against the Plates]].
