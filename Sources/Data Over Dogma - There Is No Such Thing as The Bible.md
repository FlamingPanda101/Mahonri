# Data Over Dogma — There Is No Such Thing as The Bible

- **No "the Bible," only Bibles:** The thesis of this *What Is That?* episode is that "there is no such thing as *the* Bible" — only competing collections that different groups call a Bible, none of which can claim to be objectively, universally, or authoritatively *the* one. McClellan compares it to "the dictionary": people speak as if one universally authoritative reference exists, but there are only many dictionaries.[^1][^2] See [[There Is No Such Thing as The Bible]] and [[The Dictionary Analogy for the Bible]].
- **Three prior judgments produce "your" Bible:** Before anyone can point to a Bible, three undecidable questions must be answered — **(1) canon** (which books), **(2) manuscripts** (which textual base to cobble the books from), and **(3) translation** (which rendering to read) — and none has an objectively correct answer; each is a preference of you or your group.[^3][^4][^5] See [[Three Judgments Stand Between You and a Bible]], [[The Canon Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]], [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]], [[The Translation Question Has No Objectively Correct Answer]].
- **Canon is arbitrary and post hoc:** Canon boundaries differ across Samaritan (Pentateuch only), Jewish, Protestant (66), Catholic (Deuterocanon), Eastern/Ethiopian Orthodox (many more) traditions; the "criteria" for inclusion were rationalizations invented *after* the books were chosen, with exceptions to every criterion. Luther even tried to remove Hebrews, James, and Revelation.[^6][^7] See [[Canon Criteria Were Post Hoc Rationalizations]] and the existing [[Reformers Disagree With One Another on the Canon]].
- **Dogmatic translation imposes univocality:** Translations can be deliberately bent to a theological agenda; the diagnostic case is [[Genesis 2.18-19|Genesis 2:19]], where the NIV, ESV, and the Legacy Standard Bible render the verb as a pluperfect ("had formed") to hide the contradiction with Genesis 1's creation order. This "imposes the dogma of univocality" on a text that refuses to agree.[^8][^9][^10] See [[Genesis 2-19 Pluperfect Is a Test for a Dogmatic Translation]], [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text]], and the existing [[Genesis 2 Is a Zoomed-In Sixth Day - Rebutted]].
- **A library that changed over time, not a single divine book:** The Bible is "at best a compendium," a library of very different books with different aims, anonymous authors writing to different audiences; scribes added and removed material (e.g., the woman caught in adultery) for centuries, so "what the Bible is changes over time."[^11][^12][^13] McClellan notes his own LDS tradition's recent shift to endorsing multiple translations, which he influenced.[^14] See [[Manuscripts Were Altered by Scribes Over Centuries]], [[KJV Onlyism and the Textus Receptus vs Critical Text]], [[LDS Multi-Translation Policy Shift]], and the existing [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]].
