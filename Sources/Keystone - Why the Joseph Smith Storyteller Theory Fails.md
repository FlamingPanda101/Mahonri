# Keystone - Why the Joseph Smith Storyteller Theory Fails

A short Keystone rebuttal to the naturalistic "[[Joseph Smith]] was a creative genius / storyteller who simply invented the [[Book of Mormon]]" hypothesis — the version popularized when a "top ex-Mormon Reddit user" claimed Joseph "was supposedly regaling his family with Book of Mormon-style stories long before he received the plates."[^1] The host grants the theory its strongest evidence and answers it in three moves: the recital evidence is presented *without its context*, it has *no historical corroboration* beyond that one scene, and even if granted it leaves the central puzzles — the witnesses and the dictation — unexplained.

## Summary
- **Problem 1 — lack of context.** Critics anchor the storyteller claim to a single passage in [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s family history, the "amusing recitals" in which the young Joseph described "the ancient inhabitants of this continent, their dress, their manner of traveling, the animals which they rode, the cities that they built... their mode of warfare, and their religious worship."[^2] The host supplies the surrounding narrative the critics omit: this passage comes *after* the [[Angel Moroni|Moroni]] visits of September 21–22, 1823, in which (per [[Joseph Smith History 1.68-69|Joseph Smith's own history]]) Joseph said he "was also informed concerning the aboriginal inhabitants of this country, and shown who they were and from whence they came."[^3] Read in context, Lucy is describing Joseph *relaying* what Moroni had already shown him to a family that "were melted to tears and believed all he said" ([[William Smith]]) — not inventing fiction.[^4][^5] See [[The Evening Recitals Were Joseph Relaying Moroni's Information]] and the parallel rebuttal-by-implausibility [[Lucy Mack Smiths Evening Recitals Do Not Establish Pre-Composition]].
- **Problem 2 — lack of historical corroboration.** Several prominent critics assert Joseph was a renowned storyteller, but none documents it beyond this one scene. Dale Morgan wrote that accounts "agree that the youthful Joseph Smith had a remarkable imagination and a well-developed talent as a teller of tales"; researcher Brian Hales noted "it is unfortunate Morgan provided no documentation to support this statement."[^6] A storyteller needs an audience, yet the dozens of hostile community affidavits gathered to discredit Joseph — saying "all sorts of terrible things about him" — never once called him a known storyteller.[^7] See [[Lucy Mack Smiths Evening Recitals Do Not Establish Pre-Composition]] (the "only his mother / 22 *Mormonism Unvailed* statements" asymmetry) and [[Anti-Mormon Tropes Trace Back to a Few 1830s Forged Affidavits]].
- **Problem 3 — unexplained and contradictory evidence.** Even granting Joseph "a Walter Mitty-esque imagination," that does not resolve the Book of Mormon. (a) The plate witnesses "saw, handled, and inspected" the plates — three of them shown by an angel — and an active imagination explains none of it.[^8] See [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence]]. (b) The dictation: ~269,000 words, no reference materials, no major revisions, face in a hat, ~60 working days, no prior authorship, in King James English "which was not the way Joseph naturally spoke."[^9] See [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]] and [[Dictation Speed and Method Argue Against Fabrication]]. (c) Three "contradictory evidence" anomalies a self-authored text shouldn't produce: Joseph spelled out names he couldn't pronounce; he was *surprised* to learn Jerusalem had walls ([[Emma Smith]]); and when [[Martin Harris]] secretly swapped the seer stone, Joseph could not translate and was confused until Harris confessed.[^10][^11] See [[The Seer Stone Swap and Jerusalem Walls Show Joseph Was Receiving Not Composing]].

The host's overall posture is concessive — "Of course, believe whatever makes the most sense to you" — and he closes by pointing to a companion video defending the plate witnesses against the claim that they never really saw the plates.[^12]
