# FAIR — Genesis 1 and Joseph Smith's Cosmology

A FAIR conference paper by [[Stephen Smoot]] arguing that three pillars of Latter-day Saint cosmology — rejection of [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical|creation *ex nihilo*]], a physically resembling [[Genesis 1.26-27|image of God]], and a [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible|divine plurality]] — can be grounded in the text of [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1]] and converge with the [[King Follett Discourse]] and the [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]] cosmology. Smoot reads the priestly creation account through the "functional ontology" lens of [[John Walton]], treating Genesis 1 as the ordering of pre-existing chaos and the inauguration of a cosmic temple.

## Summary

- **Thesis: three convergence points.** Smoot frames the paper around three foundational elements of LDS cosmology he says are supportable from Genesis 1 — (1) creation from pre-existing matter (rejection of *ex nihilo*), (2) the *imago Dei* of Gen 1:26 connoting a *physical/anthropomorphic* likeness (not merely moral/spiritual), and (3) the plural language of Gen 1:26 evidencing a divine plurality against strict ontological monotheism. [^1]
- **Joseph Smith keeps returning to Genesis 1.** It is reworked in the [[Moses 6|Book of Moses]], recontextualized in the [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]], woven into the Nauvoo temple endowment liturgy, and expounded (from the Hebrew) in the [[King Follett Discourse]]; for Smith, Genesis 1 was a "springboard for doctrinal and even liturgical development," not merely a narrow literal history. [^2]
- **Genesis 1 is the "priestly" creation account (Gen 1:1–2:4).** On the documentary hypothesis its *Sitz im Leben* is plausibly the Jerusalem temple (compare *Enuma Elish* recited at the Akitu festival); its language, structure (forming realms days 1–3, filling them days 4–6), and Sabbath/*moadim* interests mirror priestly concerns, and it depicts the cosmos itself being inaugurated as God's temple. [^3]
- **Hebrew syntax of the opening verses undercuts *ex nihilo*.** *Bereshit* is most likely a construct/dependent temporal clause ("when God began to create…"), the scholarly-consensus reading; verse 2 then describes a pre-existing *tohu wabohu*, *choshek*, the *tehom*, and the *ruach Elohim* sweeping over chaotic waters — i.e. matter already exists when God speaks light into being on day one. (See [[Ruach Elohim - The Feminine Spirit Hovering Over Creation]].) [^4]
- **The verb *bara* emphasizes differentiation, not bringing matter from non-existence.** Genesis 1 shows God separating light/dark, waters above/below, sea/land — assigning boundaries and roles. The "creation from non-existence" reading is a philosophical extrapolation the text does not require. (See [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]].) [^5]
- **A day-by-day cosmology of ordering chaos.** Day 0 = uncreated chaotic cosmos; Day 1 = light (with no physical source, since luminaries come on Day 4) to initiate time; Day 2 = the *raqia*/firmament as a solid dome (illustrated by the Egyptian sky-goddess Nut held up by Shu over Geb) dividing upper/lower waters; Day 3 = seas gather, land and self-generating vegetation "after their kind"; Day 4 = sun/moon/stars set as unnamed "greater/lesser light" *for signs and seasons* (*moadim* = liturgical festivals), demoting/dethroning astral deities; Day 5 = sea and air life, including the *tanninim* (mythic sea-monsters) recast as part of the harmonious order; Day 6 = land animals and humanity. [^6]
- **Humanity is set apart ontologically.** Unlike plants and animals made "after their kind," humans are made "in the image and likeness of God"; the seventh-day Sabbath shifts the account from ordering *space* to consecrating sacred *time*, completing the "space-time matrix" of the cosmic temple. [^7]
- **Walton's functional ontology reframes the whole account.** Citing [[John Walton]]'s *The Lost World of Genesis One* and *Genesis 1 as Ancient Cosmology*, Smoot argues Genesis 1 is "not primarily concerned with how the material universe came into being but rather how it functions as ordered sacred created space" — which dovetails with Joseph Smith's emphasis on eternal matter and divine intelligences organizing the cosmos. (See [[Scripture Reflects Ancient Cosmology Not Modern Science]].) [^8]
- **Convergence 1 — *creatio ex materia*.** This is "one of the clearest points of agreement"; critical scholars (even those with creedal commitments) increasingly grant that *ex nihilo* is "simply not in the Genesis creation text." Joseph Smith's King Follett claim that God had pre-existing materials to organize is, Smoot says, "vindicated." (See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]] and [[King Follett Is Arrangement Not New Substance]].) [^9]
- **Convergence 2 — the image of God is physical.** *Tselem* (image; ~17×, usually a statue/effigy) and *demut* (likeness; ~25×, a visual resemblance, as in Ezekiel's vision of the *demut* of a man = God) carry a bodily sense. Smoot marshals a Catholic (Richard J. Clifford), a Jew ([[Benjamin Sommer]]), and a Protestant (David Noel Freedman) all conceding a shared physical appearance, and presses the [[Genesis 5.18-24|Genesis 5:3]] parallel: Adam fathers Seth "in his image and likeness," so the *imago* is a generational, form-sharing resemblance — converging with D&C 130:22. (See the new concept [[The Image of God Is Physical Resemblance Passed Father to Son]].) [^10]
- **Convergence 3 — divine plurality in "let us make."** Smoot runs through and rejects three alternatives to the plural cohortative of Gen 1:26 — *pluralis majestatis*/royal "we" (unattested for God anywhere else in Hebrew), the Trinity (anachronistic eisegesis), and (implicitly) self-deliberation — in favor of God addressing his **divine council**, a ubiquitous Hebrew-Bible motif (Gen 11; 1 Kings; Job; Psalms; Isaiah). (See the new concept [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]].) [^11]
- **Garr fuses points 2 and 3.** Following W. Randall Garr (and Clifford's "most natural explanation"), humans resemble God *because* they resemble the angelic/godlike beings of the council who resemble God; *tselem* and *demut* thus mark a being "intermediate between the two worlds." This ties the physical-image and divine-council arguments together. [^12]
- **Link to the King Follett / Abraham council.** Smoot connects this to Smith's teaching that "the head of the gods called a council of gods" to organize the world, echoed in the Book of Abraham where "the gods took counsel among themselves" to organize the heavens and the earth. (See [[King Follett Discourse]] and [[Bene Elohim Are Sons Not a Different Species]].) [^13]
- **Honest framing, not an appeal to consensus.** Smoot explicitly disclaims arguing "from consensus" that therefore Joseph Smith is right; rather, he wants Smith's contributions recognized as weighty rather than dismissed as oddities or heresies — closing with Nibley's "time vindicates the prophets." [^14]
