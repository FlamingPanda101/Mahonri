# Keystone — "What This Early Apostate Revealed About the Book of Mormon"

A [[Keystone]] narration of the case of [[William McLellin]], an early apostle who left the church permanently yet never repudiated the Book of Mormon. The frame is a backfired attack: [[James Cobb]], a stepson of [[Brigham Young]] bent on disproving the book, wrote to McLellin in 1880 hoping for damaging material — and got the opposite.

## Summary
- **McLellin's turbulent membership.** Baptized 1831, excommunicated 1832, returned and served a mission, ordained an apostle in early 1835, briefly disfellowshipped and reinstated that year, withdrew in 1836, returned in 1837, excommunicated for the last time in 1838, then joined breakoff groups until giving up organized religion in 1869. His issue was always with *leadership*, not the book.
- **A firsthand investigator.** While investigating (1831), McLellin interviewed [[Hyrum Smith]] for four hours about the coming forth of the record, prayed, and concluded he "was bound as an honest man to acknowledge the truth and validity of the Book of Mormon."
- **Witnesses under threat of death.** During the 1833 Jackson County persecutions, McLellin asked [[Oliver Cowdery]] and [[David Whitmer]] point-blank, with their lives in danger, whether the Book of Mormon was true. Cowdery: "God sent his Holy Angel to declare the truth of the translation of it to us... and though the mob kill us, yet we must die declaring its truth." Whitmer affirmed the same. McLellin recorded that eight-witness [[Hiram Page]], beaten by a mob and told to "deny that damned book," answered, "How can I deny what I know to be true?"
- **The 1880 reply to Cobb.** "I have set my seal that the Book of Mormon is a true, divine record... When a man goes at the Book of Mormon, he touches the apple of my eye." "Fight the wrongs of LDS-ism as much as you please, but let that unique, that inimitable book alone." "Cease your opposition... you might just as well fight against the Rocky Mountains." He had seen David Whitmer reaffirm his testimony in June 1879 as solemnly as in 1831, and refused to invent "some darkness" against the book: "We should lie if we did, for we know nothing against its credibility or divine truth."
