# The Moral Landscape

Sam Harris's 2010 argument that **science can, in principle, determine human values**. Harris holds that morality concerns the *well-being of conscious creatures*, that well-being is a fact about the states of brains and the world, and that there are therefore objective right and wrong answers to moral questions — a "moral landscape" of peaks and valleys of flourishing. He rejects both religious morality and secular moral relativism.

## Summary
- **Morality = the well-being of conscious creatures.** Once values are defined as facts about well-being, science (especially neuroscience and psychology) can study them, and moral questions have objectively better and worse answers [^1].
- **The "moral landscape" metaphor:** a space of possible experiences whose peaks are heights of flourishing and whose valleys are the worst possible misery; multiple distinct peaks may exist, but not every position is as good as every other [^1].
- **Against the is/ought gap and relativism:** Harris argues Hume's fact/value distinction is overstated, that "ought" reduces to facts about well-being, and that cultural/ moral relativism is incoherent and harmful [^2].
- **On free will, belief, and religion:** later chapters argue free will is an illusion, examine the neuroscience of belief, and attack religion as a poor and dangerous guide to morality [^3].
- **Apologetic use (TF Level-1 ethics):** this is the **naturalist foil** to engage, not an ally. It pairs directly with [[After Humanity - Michael Ward]] / C.S. Lewis on objective value (the *Tao*) and with [[Stealing from God - Frank Turek]] and [[Five Proofs of the Existence of God - Edward Feser]] on whether objective morality can be *grounded* without God. The central apologetic question Harris raises but (critics argue) does not answer: what makes well-being *obligatory*?

## Chapters
- Introduction: The Moral Landscape
1. Moral Truth — values as facts; the case that science can speak to "ought"

4. Religion — why religion is a poor foundation for morality
5. The Future of Happiness — prospects for a science of human flourishing
