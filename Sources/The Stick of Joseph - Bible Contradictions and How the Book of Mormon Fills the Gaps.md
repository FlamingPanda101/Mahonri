# The Stick of Joseph — Bible Contradictions and How the Book of Mormon Fills the Gaps

A "Reading Books with Mike and Dave" episode of the Stick of Joseph channel in which [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] read [[Julius Wellhausen]]'s *Prolegomena to the History of Israel* (1878) — the foundational statement of the [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration|Documentary Hypothesis]] — and argue that the apparent contradictions and editorial seams Wellhausen identifies are best read through the [[Documentary Hypothesis Strengthens the Restoration|Book of Mormon as a lens]]. Hosts Brad Whitbeck and Austin ask the "so what?" questions for a lay audience.

## Summary

- **The book and its argument.** *Prolegomena* ("things that must be said beforehand," a Greek neuter plural) is the standard work behind the documentary hypothesis. Wellhausen (German, Lutheran, 1878) argues Israel's religion began simple and grew highly "detailed" later — during and after the Exile — and that one must reconstruct *how the text came to be* before writing a history of Israel. He stands on earlier thinkers (Astruc, de Wette, Spinoza). Day's verdict on the book: it is "the OG," the foundational but not the *best* entry point — he recommends [[Richard Friedman]]'s *Who Wrote the Bible* for newcomers.[^1][^2][^3]
- **What "critical" reading means.** Wellhausen reads the Bible with a critical eye — not attacking it but asking what it claims and where those claims clash: What is God's name? Where is sacrifice made? Who is in charge — Levites or [[Aaron|sons of Aaron]]? What does the king do? He identifies "seams," "discrepancies," and "doublets," then posits a layered history in which a later writer modified an earlier one.[^4][^5]
- **The recursive problem (steelmanned).** Day and Butler grant the method's weaknesses: the history into which the texts are fitted is itself derived *from* the texts, and our knowledge of ancient history is imperfect. They cite [[Umberto Cassuto]]-style and [[Kenneth Kitchen]]-style objections elsewhere; here Butler notes Wellhausen "was not the first" — this is "the great work because it became the standard work," footnoted by everyone who agrees or disagrees.[^6][^7]
- **Setting in life (*Sitz im Leben*).** A recurring tool: every text has a setting. Day notes LDS scholars read King Benjamin's speech (Mosiah 1–6) as set at the temple around a New Year / Feast of Tabernacles festival — booths, atonement, covenant, newness of life — whereas the Psalms give no setting, raising the question of who wrote them and when.[^8]
- **The Book of Mormon is *data*, not a second hypothesis.** Day's central methodological move: "The Book of Mormon is a datum... a whole bunch of data." It is not a rival hypothesis that may exist only if Gentile scholarship grants it permission. Whatever the Book of Mormon shows about 600 BC Israel "ought to affect how you think about the documentary hypothesis." It depicts Lehi and Nephi interacting with an Israel that was not what people thought in Joseph Smith's day but that critical scholarship now suggests it was.[^9][^10]
- **The four sources (flyover).** J and E are oldest (think Judah and Ephraim), named for the divine names common in them; both are hypothetical — "we've never found a copy of J." **J** (Yahwist) is southern, uses Yahweh/Jehovah, and is more "loosey-goosey" and oral — God approached by dreams, visions, prayer. **E** (Elohist) is northern (Shechem, Samaria), uses Elohim. After the north fell (722 BC), the two national epics were deliberately interwoven into one. **P** (Priestly) supplies ritual detail, gives specific dates ("the fourth day of the third month" — "sounds like the book of Alma"), and Wellhausen dated it late (exilic). **D** (Deuteronomy / the Deuteronomistic History: Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings) centralizes worship and reflects [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms|Josiah's reform]] (~640–620 BC).[^11][^12][^13][^14]
- **The brass plates as J or E.** Because Lehi's family is of Manasseh and the brass plates carry their lineage, Day and Butler float that the plates may preserve **E** (or J) — and "if we found it, that would be game, set, match." Day's nuance: the Book of Mormon has "the best of" all four — some J, some E, some P (it mentions sacrifice without ritual detail), and "the best of D" (the prosperity-for-obedience theme). See [[The Northern Lineage Explains Laman and Lemuel and the Brass Plates]].[^15][^16]
- **Wellhausen's Lutheran bias.** Day reads Wellhausen as a Lutheran who treats the P-code's regimentation as a *degeneration* from the joyous J-religion: "the ancient offerings were holy, a joyous nature, a merry making before Jehovah" that the priestly code turned into "proper business" and centralized control. Wellhausen "over and over says J is cooler" ("J slaps"). Day flags this as bias coloring the *why*, not the *how*.[^17][^18]
- **Tabernacle as retrojection.** Wellhausen argues the wilderness tabernacle is a backward projection of Solomon's temple ("where did slaves get oceans of gold for a fabulously wealthy portable temple?"), and that the history of the ark differs from the history of the tabernacle. [[Gerhard von Rad]] develops this into **two Moses traditions** — Exodus vs. Sinai. See [[The Tabernacle Is a Retrojection of the Temple]].[^19][^20]
- **Patriarchs as a mirror of later cult.** From the Hexateuch narratives: the stories of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob preserve "no historical knowledge of the patriarchs" but an anthropological picture of the cult of the *later* age that told them — projected back like "a glorified mirage." The patriarchs meet God in the countryside under sacred trees (the oak of Moreh), build their own altars, and erect named standing stones (Bethel = "house of God") — all of which Deuteronomy 12 / Deuteronomy later *prohibits*, which itself confirms people were doing it. Lehi does the same the moment he leaves Jerusalem. See [[The Patriarchal Cult Mirrors Pre-Reform Practice That Lehi Revives]].[^21][^22][^23]
- **The "last to touch the text" principle.** Butler's true-crime analogy: the last editor to modify a text is the prime suspect — examine his motives. The Deuteronomist (~640–620 BC) overlaid sources with his theology: one place for Yahweh's name, one altar, and "God can't be seen — keep the law." The Book of Mormon shows those very arguments being contested in real time in the first twelve chapters, with Lehi as a true messenger answering *how* God is approached, *who* God is, and *what* wisdom is.[^24][^25]
- **Two Goliaths — and "David" as a throne-name.** A worked contradiction: [[1 Samuel 17.4-7|1 Samuel 17]] has David kill Goliath of Gath ("spear like a weaver's beam"); [[2 Samuel 21.19]] has Elhanan kill the same Goliath. The KJV's "the brother of" in the Chronicles parallel is italicized — not in the Hebrew. Butler floats that "David" (meaning "beloved," uniquely never reused as a name) may be a *throne-name*, and the man's birth name may have been Elhanan ("God has been gracious"), written over the records once he became the prototype king. See [[Elhanan Killed Goliath - Chronicles Harmonized the Contradiction]].[^26][^27][^28]
- **David ruled as king *and* priest.** The episode's sharpest new example: [[2 Samuel 8.18|2 Samuel 8:18]] says David's sons were "chief rulers," but the Hebrew says they were *kohanim* — **priests**. David wears the ephod, leads the ark procession, and makes his sons priests; the king was king-and-priest "until he wasn't" (the Zadokites). The 1 Nephi 1 colophon — "his reign and ministry" — marks Nephi as a king *and* priest. See [[David's Sons Were Priests Not Chief Rulers - 2 Samuel 8.18 Kohanim]].[^29][^30]
- **Why learn Hebrew / why the Bible is hard.** Day relays his own teenage failure to get past Genesis 1:6 ("a firmament in the midst of the waters") and argues the KJV translators were "brilliant but didn't know what they didn't know" — they lacked the temple and the Book of Mormon. Hence 2 Samuel 8:18's *kohanim* hidden under "chief rulers."[^31][^32]
- **Bible as battleground; Book of Mormon as Urim and Thummim.** Pastoral payoff: don't Bible-bash (proof-text against proof-text on whether God can be seen) — mission president Joseph Fielding McConkie told missionaries to leave their Bibles home and use the Book of Mormon. Day loves the Bible "for its messiness" but uses the Book of Mormon "as a lens because of the clarity it brings." The standard for *Prolegomena* isn't whether it's "true" but whether it "advances the conversation" and "moves the football forward." The takeaway is gratitude for the Book of Mormon — an edited anthology whose editors "reveal their hand," unlike the Bible's, and which lets readers recover the [[The Comma Inverts the Plain and Precious Truths Were Removed Argument|plain and precious things]] [[1 Nephi 13.40|Nephi]] said were taken out — an unfinished restoration.[^33][^34][^35]
