# The Stick of Joseph — Clues Reveal the Unknown Editor of Genesis

A conversation in which [[Trevan Hatch]], a BYU Near-Eastern-studies scholar and librarian, argues that the book of **Genesis was given its final, edited form during (or shortly after) the Davidic–Solomonic monarchy** as a piece of pro-Davidic apologetic — and that the "clues" to its unknown editor are embedded in the text itself. The episode is a companion piece to the vault's other [[The Stick of Joseph - The Documentary Hypothesis (Mike Day)|Documentary-Hypothesis]] material: where [[Mike Day]] surveys the JEDP *mechanics*, Hatch presses a single *when-and-why* thesis and reads Genesis as a literary mirror of the house of David.

## Summary

- **Why Genesis can't be straight Mosaic history.** The hosts open with the familiar internal seams — Moses writing of his own death and burial, third-person self-reference — and frame the "mindset shift" away from a child's face-value reading toward asking *who wrote this, for what audience, and why.* Hatch's distinctive answer: the [[Genesis Was Finalized as Davidic-Solomonic Apologetic|final form of Genesis was assembled under the monarchy]] to legitimize David's line, even if many traditions (flood, Eden, the patriarchal cycles) predate it.[^1][^2]
- **Rendsburg's "national epic" frame.** Hatch quotes Genesis scholar [[Gary Rendsburg]]: the scribes "wrote a national epic incorporating all of the earlier traditions back to Abraham and embedded into that narrative anticipations of the present" — telling the story of the past while reflecting on the present. He illustrates with *MASH* (a 1969 film ostensibly about Korea but really about Vietnam) and *The Name of the Rose*: a sophisticated reading asks what a text *meant* to its real author and audience, and whether external data (e.g. archaeological boundary evidence) can be brought to bear.[^3][^4][^5]
- **The Book-of-Mormon analogy for redaction.** Just as [[Mormon Is an Ethnocentric Redactor Focused on the Witness of Christ|Mormon]] is a visible editor stitching sources ("and thus we see"), the Genesis editor pulled in sources — sometimes a single verse of stitching, sometimes whole cycles (Abraham, Isaac, Joseph) composed or placed wholesale. Dating is "on the table" for any given block.[^6]
- **Geography dates the text.** The boundaries of Canaan promised to Abraham in [[Genesis 15.6|Genesis 15]] match the heyday of David and Solomon; Mount Moriah ([[Genesis 22]]) is the later Temple Mount; the Gihon spring (David's water source) appears in Eden; [[Melchizedek]] is "king of righteousness" stationed at *Salem* (Jerusalem) receiving Abraham's tithe. Priesthood, sacrifice, and the Gihon together "legitimize Jerusalem as the current administrative and sacrificial capital."[^7][^8]
- **Polemic against David's rivals.** Genesis explains the origins of David's three subject-rivals — Ammon, Moab (born of Lot's incest), and Edom/Esau — using the ancient polemical trick of accusing an enemy nation of sexual perversion. Isaac's blessing that Jacob be "lord over your brothers" is read as a direct jab at Esau. The [[Mesha Stele and Biblical Defeat Theology|Mesha/Moabite inscription]] shows the rival side doing the same kind of rhetoric in reverse.[^9][^10]
- **The younger-brother motif.** Genesis relentlessly favors the younger son (Abel, Isaac, Jacob, Judah, Joseph, Ephraim) and stages fratricide "in the field" (Cain/Abel, Absalom/Amnon). Hatch argues this [[The Younger-Brother Motif Legitimizes Solomons Succession|pattern climaxes — and then disappears — at Solomon over Adonijah]], exactly what a scribe defending a non-firstborn king's legitimacy would manufacture.[^11][^12]
- **Judah and David as literary doubles.** Both are shepherds who separate from their brothers to *Adullam*; both are tied to Bethlehem and Mahanaim; and the "random" insertion of [[Genesis 38|Judah and Tamar]] mirrors the David–Bathsheba scandal point for point (a wife named Bathshua/Bath-sheba, a daughter/in-law named Tamar at the center of a sexual scandal, the "I am pregnant" formula, mourning-then-conception, "evil in the eyes of Yahweh," the sheep-shearing setting). The Chronicler's renaming of Bathsheba to "Bath-shua" looks deliberate, not a mistake. See [[Judah and David Are Written as Literary Doubles]].[^13][^14]
- **Joseph and David as doubles; Jacob–Laban and David–Saul.** Joseph and David are both favored younger shepherds, both 30 at their ascent, both "handsome," both have "Yahweh was with him." The Jacob/Laban marriage-deception and stolen-teraphim escape parallels Saul's shifting bride-price (the 100/200 Philistine foreskins for Michal) and Michal's idol-in-the-bed ruse — a [[Genesis Was Finalized as Davidic-Solomonic Apologetic|transfer-of-authority]] theme.[^15][^16]
- **The Restoration tie-in.** The Book of Mormon's [[Nephi]] — a younger brother who builds a temple and becomes king ~300 years after David — replays the same Davidic motif; Hatch reads 1 Nephi as Nephi positioning himself as a "new David." [[Jonah Barnes]]'s *Lost Gems of Genesis* notes the motif even in extra-biblical Adam/Lucifer "younger brother" traditions, and the New Testament resurrects it in [[Matthew 20.28|Jesus]] as a younger kinsman of John.[^17][^18]
- **Wellhausen and the J-source date.** Walking through the [[Wellhausen Thesis on Hebrew Bible Origins|Documentary Hypothesis]] (JEDP), Hatch notes scholars date **J to the 10th century (the 900s)** partly on the same boundary evidence, E to the 8th-century northern prophets, and that "every single one of these texts" reaches its *final form after the reign of David and Solomon" — even on [[Richard Friedman|Friedman]]'s and Rendsburg's early datings. He flags the standard anachronisms: "as far as Dan," "to this day," and the trans-Jordan "across the Jordan" orientation that locates the writer in Canaan.[^19][^20]
- **Scripture as a spectrum.** Hatch closes by posing his classroom taxonomy — biblical texts as (1) literal revelation, (2) inspired human explanations of revelation, or (3) unrevealed cultural explanations — and argues all three can still count as scripture. He extends this to a wide-ranging exchange (with Dave Butler) on whether a text its author never meant to canonize, or even "inspired fiction," can be scripture; he and the hosts apply the same lens to Paul's letters, *Jesus the Christ*, and the Book of Mormon. See [[Scripture as a Spectrum Not a Binary]].[^21][^22]
- **Pastoral payoff.** Responding to an education-week attendee who heard all this as a "deficit" requiring prophets to "fix" the Bible, Hatch insists that is not a prophet's job; quoting Joseph Smith ("a man is saved no faster than he gets knowledge"), he argues that wrestling with the Bible's "messiness" is an adult act of faith that brings the text — and Christ — closer, not further.[^23][^24]
