# Blake Ostler - Atonement in Mormon Thought

A 22-page scholarly essay by [[Blake Ostler]] surveying and critiquing the major theories of atonement and defending his own **Compassion Theory** as the only model that satisfies a set of explanatory criteria (the *desiderata*) drawn from Latter-day Saint scripture. The essay condenses the argument of chapters 6-7 of his *Exploring Mormon Thought: The Love of God and the Problems of Theism* (Greg Kofford Books, 2006). Ostler treats atonement as the healing of sin-caused **alienation** through an indwelling, interpenetrating loving union with Christ — explicitly contrasted with penal-substitution, satisfaction/ransom, and moral-influence frameworks. It is the authoritative scholarly anchor in this vault for the LDS "compassion/relationship" theory of atonement and its [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity|Gethsemane]]-centered focus.

## Summary

**Sin and atonement defined (p.1).** Sin is "injury to human relationships and resulting alienation"; atonement "consists in eradicating from our lives whatever gets in the way of loving relationships with God and each other," thus healing alienation. The traditional metaphors (court acquittal, ransom-from-slavery, reconciliation, sacrificial expiation, military victory — pp.1-2) raise questions they do not answer: why is forgiveness impossible without "punishments, dealings and cosmic contraptions"?

**The desiderata and Abelard's Constraint (pp.2-3).** Ostler distinguishes a *doctrine* of atonement (faith claim) from a *metaphor* and from a *theory* (explanation). A theory must answer five questions — chiefly how Christ's suffering 2,000 years ago eradicates *my* sin here and now, and how he could "bear" sins not yet freely committed — and must meet **Abelard's Constraint** (be "neither unintelligible, arbitrary, illogical, nor immoral"). He grounds the desiderata in four best-attested scriptural data points (A-D, pp.2-4): Christ takes our sins *into his being*; consequently suffers body and spirit; thereby releases us and reconciles us; and his mercy "satisfies the demands of justice." See [[The Desiderata and Abelards Constraint for a Theory of Atonement]].

**Critique of rival LDS theories (pp.4-10).** Ostler steelmans then rejects four novel LDS proposals — Skousen's *Demand of Eternal Intelligences*, England's *Self-Rejection Moral* theory, Potter's *Empathy* theory, and Morgan's *Divine Infusion* theory — each for failing to explain how Christ actually bears our sins. See [[Ostlers Critique of Rival Atonement Theories]]. He also rejects the **retributive** notion of justice common to penal substitution, the standard LDS view (p.4).

**The Compassion Theory (pp.10-13).** Eight features: sin is self-absorbed alienation; atonement persuades us to surrender it; repentance heals/maintains the relationship; union with Christ commingles his light with ours ("new creature," at-one-ment, deification); the condition of union is willingness to be vulnerable, so our sin causes Christ momentary-but-excruciating pain (not punishment, not guilt-bearing); Christ is *uniquely* able because he **learned compassion by suffering** as a mortal and knew first-hand the alienation of abandonment on the cross; atonement is simply the *mode of relationship* God seeks; and the "demands of justice" are the [[The Law of Restoration as the Single Law of Justice|Law of Restoration]] (we reap what we sow), satisfied because each receives what he freely chooses. See [[The Compassion Theory of Atonement]], [[Atonement as Indwelling Union That Transforms Darkness Into Light]], [[Christ Learned Compassion by Suffering Mortal Alienation]], [[Relationship Not Payment - Atonement Rejects Retributive Justice]].

**Pauline corroboration (pp.13).** Ostler ties the theory to the New Perspective on Paul (citing Morna Hooker): being "in Christ" through covenant faithfulness, symbolized by baptism (dying/rising with Christ) and the sacrament (taking his life into ours). 2 Cor. 5:21 — God "made him to be sin... so that we might become the righteousness of God in him" ([[2 Corinthians 5.21|2 Cor. 5:21]]) — is his "best summary."

**Reply to Deidre Green (pp.13-22).** Ostler answers feminist critic Deidre Green, who charges that repentant sinners "sadistically" foist pain on Christ. He distinguishes *pointless* from *redemptive* suffering (the 1850 childbirth and the confessing-adulterer analogies), re-reads the unpunctuated [[D&C 19|D&C 19:16-17]] so "all" = "all those who repent" (avoiding backward causation, denial of free will, double punishment, and violation of the innocence principle), and argues Green's own counter-proposal makes Christ suffer the *maximum possible* pointless pain. See [[The Pointless versus Redemptive Suffering Distinction]].
