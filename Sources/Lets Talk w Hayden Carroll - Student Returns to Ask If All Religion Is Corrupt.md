# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Student Returns to Ask If All Religion Is Corrupt

A returning Arizona State University student, **Nick**, sits back down with LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] for a follow-up to their first conversation. Nick is warm toward Carroll personally and even open to attending church on Sunday, but he presses a single recurring thesis: **every religious institution is corrupt in some way** — the Vatican hoarding its archives, clergy preying on children, the "greed of man" turning tithing into extortion. He frames himself as a "follower of Jesus" who has been "disgusted" by organized religion and wants to know whether he can keep Jesus while rejecting the institutions. Carroll concedes far more than a defensive apologist usually does — granting human flaws, the "Utah bubble," and the critical plausibility that any man-run church will eventually corrupt — while holding a narrow line: corruption by *individuals* is not the same as an *organization's* sanctioned malfeasance, and a church genuinely *headed by God* is the one exception to the rule that institutions decay.

## Summary

The conversation moves through a connected chain of the student's challenges, each met by a distinct Carroll move:

- **The opening (accountability/morality).** Nick recaps the health, wellness, and low-divorce statistics that "stuck with" him, then asks whether morality is so eroded that people "would just be heathens" without religion. Carroll answers from the natural-man doctrine (the natural man is "an enemy to God") and concedes Mormonism is "not the only system" for a productive life but "takes the cake." [00:01–05:30]
- **"Can you deny that every religious institution is corrupt?"** Nick's central claim, anchored on the Vatican "sitting on an archive that would do nothing but benefit humanity." Carroll answers with a **corruption distinction**: unavoidable individual "human flaws in the system" (an abusive bishop) versus "an organizational widespread acceptance of malfeasance." He would not remain LDS if he believed *his* church were corrupt in the second sense. [05:30–07:30]
- **The Utah-bubble / brainwashing aside.** Nick raises "a little bit of brainwashing" and sheltered Utah upbringings; Carroll concedes the "Utah bubble" is real and that shielding children from disagreement is "probably not the best way," while noting the Church does not *prohibit* engagement. [07:30–08:30] (Wikilinked to the existing cluster, not re-argued.)
- **"Newer institutions haven't had time for the evil to infiltrate."** Nick's novel argument: corruption is a function of an institution's *age*, so the relatively young Restoration simply hasn't had "enough time for the evil to infiltrate." Carroll grants this is "exactly right" *if* one assumes the church is not God's — and pivots to the theological exception. → [[Institutional Corruption Is Not Merely a Function of an Institution's Age]]
- **"It's God's church, so it can't become corrupt — but it's run by men all the way to the top."** The sharpest exchange. Carroll argues a God-headed church "will not become corrupt because he heads it... unless Jesus can become corrupt." Nick's rejoinder: "it is God's church but it is run by men all the way to the top." Carroll's solution: "Can God call someone who will be faithful?" — divine foreknowledge lets God staff His church with those He knows will not fall. → [[A God-Headed Church Is Guaranteed Against Corruption by Divine Foreknowledge]]
- **"Can I follow Jesus and disrespect institutions in general?"** Nick's spiritual-but-not-religious move. Carroll's answer: "you can only follow Jesus by joining the church" *if* Jesus tells you to join it — so the Book of Mormon, as evidence that God has reestablished His church with living prophets, converts "follow Jesus" into "join the fold," and Nick concedes the logic is "consistent." → [[You Can Only Follow Jesus by Joining the Church He Commands]]
- **Tithing as the engine of "greed."** Nick's visceral complaint — churches that spend "the first 30 minutes... asking for tithing" until he doesn't "feel holy in a house of God anymore." Carroll distinguishes LDS practice (no plate passed, voluntary, temple-tithing tied to a covenant to "give all things") and reframes the duty as personal revelation felt "in the form of a feeling." Nick's pointed line: "if God asked me, sure, but if a man says God told me to ask you, that's a problem." (Wikilinked to the wealth/tithing cluster.) [10:30–12:32]
- **Close.** Carroll likens Nick's seeking to Joseph Smith's first-vision question and invites him to walk to church together; they exchange numbers. [12:32–14:00]
