# FAIR — "Ishmael and Nahom in Ancient Inscriptions" by Neal Rappleye (2022 FAIR Conference)

A 2022 FAIR Conference presentation by [[Neal Rappleye]] (Book of Mormon Central) surveying the South Arabian inscriptional evidence for the names **NHM (Nahom)** and **Ishmael** beyond the three famous Bar'an temple altars. Rappleye argues that several additional, lesser-known inscriptions document the NHM tribal region back to Lehi's day, and that a roughly-6th-century-BC funerary stela bearing the name Ishmael (South Arabian *Yasmaʿil*) from the Nehem region fits — without proving — the Book of Mormon's account of Ishmael's burial at Nahom ([[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34]]). The Q&A addresses the "Joseph could have used a map" and "NHM is only a tribe, not a place" objections.

## Summary

- **The three Bar'an temple altars are the familiar baseline.** First brought to Latter-day Saints' attention by S. Kent Brown (1999) and [[Warren Aston]] (2001), the altars name a dedicant from the tribe of NHM and were dated by the German excavation team's epigrapher to the 7th–8th century BC; LDS researchers (Ross Christensen 1978; Aston) had associated the Book of Mormon Nahom with the Nehem tribal region even before the altars. [^1]
- **Many additional NHM inscriptions exist and are not well known.** Rappleye catalogs further attestations: a 3rd-century-AD funerary "image-formula" stela naming a Nehemite (associated with the Highlands by Hermann von Wissmann via the deity ʿAthtar Sharqan and decorative vine motifs), an administrative palm-stick text from Nashan, two dedicatory texts from Sirwah (one with a dual NHM/Maʿin affiliation), a 7th-century-BC text naming a father and son as *kabir* (chief) of the NHM, and three 7th-century-BC inscriptions from Haram. Several date to Lehi's period (~600 BC) and reinforce the long-term stability of the Nehem tribe's presence. See [[Additional NHM Inscriptions Date the Nahom Region to Lehis Day]]. [^2]
- **The NHM evidence is "stronger and better than ever."** Fleshing out the pre-Islamic chronology shows the Nehem tribe documented throughout antiquity — extending the timeline Aston published. See [[Additional NHM Inscriptions Date the Nahom Region to Lehis Day]] and the existing [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn]]. [^3]
- **The serendipitous discovery of an Ishmael stela.** Browsing a collection of looted "carved face" funerary stelae (a low-cost type used by travelers/foreigners, fitting Lehi's party), Rappleye realized the stelae bear names and searched for "Ishmael." He found a stela reading *Yasmaʿil* — the exact South Arabian cognate of Hebrew *Yishmael* ("may God hear") — dated by script to the 6th–5th century BC and by carving style to the 7th–6th century, i.e. roughly 6th century BC, from the Nehem region. See [[The Ishmael Funerary Stela in South Arabia]]. [^4]
- **He explicitly limits the claim.** "I am not claiming this is the Ishmael from the Book of Mormon... I'm not even willing to say it's likely him" — only that it is a "tantalizing possibility." The stela confirms *a* man named Ishmael (or its South Arabian equivalent) was buried near Nehem ~600 BC, and the style/onomastics fit an outsider from the north along the trade routes — consistent with the Book of Mormon. [^5]
- **The name is rare in South Arabia but common in Judah.** Of ~8,500 South Arabian inscriptions, only 27 carry "Ishmael," and only four date to the early first millennium BC — so it is a relatively rare name there. By contrast Rappleye counts ~60 attestations of Ishmael in Hebrew epigraphy (8th–6th century BC); one Hebrew seal even pairs "Ishmael" with "Nahum" (NHM). So a Jerusalemite named Ishmael in 600 BC is exactly what the period record predicts. See [[Ishmael Was a Common Judahite Name in 600 BC]]. [^6]
- **Q&A — the "old map" objection.** To "Joseph Smith could have found Nahom on an old map," Rappleye answers (citing Kent Brown 2002 and [[Jeff Lindsay]]) that the nearest known map was ~320 miles from Joseph with no evidence he traveled to it, that it spelled the name differently, and — crucially — that a map gives only a name: it cannot supply the 600 BC dating (which critics originally demanded), the eastward turn to Bountiful, or the trade routes. See [[Joseph Smith Could Not Have Used a Nehem Map]]. [^7]
- **Q&A — "NHM is a tribe, not a place."** Rappleye replies that Yemeni tribal identity is very strongly linked to territory; the *nisba* ("the Nehemite") ties a person to both tribe and region, and Islamic-period and inscriptional usage routinely treats tribal names (NHM, Hadramawt) as place names. "There's no critical reason to split those hairs." See [[Nahom Names a Tribal Region Not the Altar Findspot]]. [^8]
- **His bottom line: prophet, not scholar.** Pressed whether Joseph Smith was a "scholar of South Arabian history or a prophet," Rappleye answers "my money's on prophet" — the convergence of First Nephi with ancient Arabia is striking, "however you want to explain that." [^9]
