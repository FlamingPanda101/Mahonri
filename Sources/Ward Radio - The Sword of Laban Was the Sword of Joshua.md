A **Ward Radio** interview with historian **[[Don Bradley]]** (author of *The Lost 116 Pages*), hosted by **[[Cardon Ellis]]** with **[[Brad Whitbeck]]**, presenting Bradley's argument that the **[[Laban|sword of Laban]] was the sword of [[Joshua]]** — forged by **[[Joseph of Egypt]]** in Egypt for the foreseen conquest of Canaan, carried by Joshua, and handed down the Ephraimite/Josephite royal line to Laban and then to [[Nephi]]. The method is two-step: first establish from internal Book of Mormon evidence that the **brass plates were begun by Joseph of Egypt**, then apply the same logic (plus an external 1850s source) to the sword that travels with them.

## Summary

- **The thesis.** Bradley argues the sword of Laban is the biblical sword of Joshua, forged by Joseph in Egypt by divine direction, used in the conquest, preserved through the line of kings to Laban, and ultimately deposited with the plates by Moroni [^1].
- **Method: internal then external.** Reconstructing the lost narrative uses both internal evidence (the Book of Mormon text we still have) and external sources; both point to Laban's relics having "a deep history that goes all the way back to Joseph in Egypt" [^2].
- **The brass plates were begun by Joseph of Egypt.** Every distinctive feature of the brass plates points to Joseph: they were kept by a line descending from Joseph ([[1 Nephi 5]]); they contain his family's detailed genealogy (so detailed Lehi locates himself in it 11 centuries later); they preserve the otherwise-unknown prophets **Zenos and Zenock**, who must be Josephites (3 Nephi 10 makes the Nephites/Lamanites their descendants); they contain the **first-person prophecies of Joseph** ([[2 Nephi 3]]; Alma quotes further Joseph-prophecies); and they are written in **Egyptian** — all explained at once if Joseph started the record [^3].
- **A different Genesis.** Because the brass-plates Bible differs from the Judahite King James Genesis (Josephite prophets, first-person Joseph material), Lehi had access to a "josephite" rather than "judahite" version — consistent with [[The Northern Lineage Explains Laman and Lemuel and the Brass Plates|the northern/Josephite lineage]] [^4].
- **Egyptian-speaking Josephites (travel anecdote).** Whitbeck relays a Muslim antiquities dealer's claim that Moses was "slow of speech" because he grew up speaking **Egyptian**, not Hebrew, and needed Aaron (a Josephite who spoke Egyptian) as mouthpiece — offered as a (speculative) illustration of Egyptian persisting in Joseph's line [^5].
- **The external source: Gladden Bishop via Martin Harris.** [[Martin Harris]]'s decades-long Kirtland neighbor **[[Francis Gladden Bishop]]** published (early 1850s) that the sword "was caused to be made by Joseph of old in Egypt, by the direction of God, and was in the hand of Joshua when he led the house of Israel into the land of Canaan, and after him it came down into the lineage of Joseph to Laban" — apparently derived from Harris [^6].
- **Why Joseph forged it.** Genesis has Joseph prophesying the return to the promised land ("carry my bones"); the JST/Book of Mormon expand this to detailed Exodus and conquest prophecies. Foreseeing the conquest, Joseph had a sword forged for it — the sword his descendant Joshua would carry [^7].
- **Nephi as the new David and new Joshua.** Ben McGuire's parallels: ~a dozen elements of David-and-Goliath recur in order in Nephi-and-Laban; Nephi beheads Laban with Laban's own sword (as David did Goliath), becomes the sword's possessor, and wields it "in defense of his people" as the new Joshua entering a new promised land [^8].
- **The rusted blade and the enduring word.** Per Joseph Smith Sr. (to Fayette Lapham), only the **hilt** survived in the box — the blade had "cankered and rusted away." Bradley reads symbolic significance: the **[[Alma 37.3-5|brass plates retain their brightness]]** while the sword rusts — "the power is not in the sword, it is in the word" [^9].
