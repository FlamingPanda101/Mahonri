# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Trent Horn Claims Mormonism Could Be Demonic

A friendly recorded dialogue (originally on the Council of Trent) between LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] and Catholic apologist [[Trent Horn]], occasioned by Carroll's video naming the "Mormonism could be demonic" charge the *strongest* argument against the faith. The two probe whether it is ever legitimate — epistemically or ethically — to call a rival religion demonically inspired, touching the [[Galatians 1-8 - Angel Preaching Another Gospel]] frame, Aquinas on the limits of demonic power, and the reversibility of Horn's debate syllogism against [[Jacob Hansen]].

## Summary
- **The framing — Driscoll vs. Horn on a spectrum.** Carroll distinguishes Mark Driscoll (who *declares* Joseph Smith was visited by a demon as fact) from Horn, who keeps the demonic merely *on the table* as one explanation. Carroll's worry is the rhetorical power to "keep people away" and the moral hazard of inflammatory-and-unfalsifiable labels, tying it to the Michigan shooter who thought Latter-day Saints were the antichrist — while granting neither man wishes violence.[^1][^2]
- **"When Catholics Argue Like Jews."** Carroll's central charge: deploying "it could be demonic" as a public apologetic reenacts the Pharisees, who granted Jesus' miracles were real and reattributed them to the devil ([[Matthew 12.24-26]]). He calls it a "backup plan" / "get out of jail free card" that only works by presupposing one's own worldview, and that everyone (Islam, Judaism, Mormons) could equally fire. See [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable]].[^3][^4]
- **Horn's falsifiability defense from Aquinas.** Horn answers that the demonic explanation *is* falsifiable because demonic power is bounded: the devil, a creature, cannot raise the dead (God alone is author of life), so a demonic story is ruled out for the Resurrection though live elsewhere. He adds the wonder/miracle distinction — "not everything that's a wonder is a miracle." See [[Not Even the Devil Can Raise the Dead - The Aquinas Falsifiability Defense]].[^5][^6]
- **"Magic trick, not miracle."** Horn places the Book of Mormon on the natural side of the line: its production asks "how did you do it?" (a method we can't see) rather than presenting a suspended law of nature like the Resurrection. He refuses the gaps-inference "inexplicable, therefore inspired." Carroll counters with the witness-count and conspiracy-cost. See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].[^7][^8]
- **Argument strength and the reversible syllogism.** Both agree the demonic line is "strongest" because it *grants the most premises* (Horn's abortion analogy). But Carroll shows Horn's "If Catholicism is true, the Book of Mormon is false" syllogism is **reversible** and rests on an *unproven* premise (the truth of Catholicism) — "damage control," not demonstration. See [[The Strongest Argument Grants the Most Premises]].[^9][^10]
- **Where it actually bottoms out — authority and the Great Apostasy.** Horn grounds a text's inspiration in the *authority of Christ's Church*, not its inexplicability; Carroll counters that the debate never *proved* Catholicism's authority, and both concede the real question is whether Christ's authority left the earth after the apostles — pointing to the forthcoming Heschmeyer–Hansen great-apostasy debate. Notable LDS positions stated: no human source (including the biblical text) is *infallible*, though humans can teach *authoritatively*; Horn's reply uses the "infallible at calculus" analogy (infallibility as a *negative protection*, not a guarantee of output).[^11][^12]
- **Tone.** Strikingly irenic — Horn calls Carroll gracious; Carroll has a Catholic fiancée and Catholic family, praises Horn's *sola scriptura* and abortion apologetics, and admits he has "no great answer" to Eucharistic and Marian miracles. Horn closes by saying the conversation has *not* dislodged the demonic explanation for him, but he would add caveats so the label never implies LDS individuals are themselves malicious.[^13][^14]
