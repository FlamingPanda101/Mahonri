# Data Over Dogma - Is Hell Actually in the Bible

In this "Data Over Dogma Classic" segment, biblical scholar [[Dan McClellan]] walks co-host [[Dan Beecher]] through the historical development of "hell," arguing that the modern concept — **eternal conscious torment in a lake of fire** — is a *post-biblical composite*, not a single doctrine handed down whole in scripture. He traces the arc from ancient Israel's morally-neutral [[Sheol Was the Undifferentiated Realm of All the Dead|Sheol]] (where everyone, good or bad, went), through the Hellenistic-period emergence of afterlife punishment as a [[Divine Punishment in the Afterlife Emerged as Theodicy Under Imperial Oppression|theodicy under imperial oppression]], through the diverse and unsystematized frameworks of Second Temple texts and the New Testament, to the 4th–5th-century institutional systematization and the later imagery of [[Dante]]. Framed by the story of Pentecostal pastor Carlton Pearson, who lost his megachurch after rejecting hell.

- **Ancient Israel had no heaven-or-hell — only Sheol, the common realm of all the dead.** The dead persisted in a dreary, neutral state; the living fed, lit, and petitioned them, and **necromancy was "phenomenally common"** (the necromancer of Endor, [[1 Samuel 28|1 Samuel 28]]). [^1] See [[Sheol Was the Undifferentiated Realm of All the Dead]].
- **English Bibles flatten four distinct words into "hell"** — Sheol (Hebrew), Hades (Greek), Gehenna, and Tartarus — each from a different stream of tradition, creating a false impression of one stable doctrine. [^2] See [[English Bibles Flatten Four Distinct Words Into Hell]].
- **Gehenna was the literal Valley of Hinnom**, site of child-sacrifice (Tophet), turned into a symbol of wickedness — and the "perpetually-burning garbage dump" claim is a late myth with **no archaeological or textual support**. [^3] See [[Gehenna Was the Valley of Hinnom Not a Burning Garbage Dump]].
- **Afterlife punishment emerged as a theodicy under empire.** Its earliest Hebrew-Bible appearances ([[Daniel 12|Daniel 12]], [[Isaiah 66.24|Isaiah 66:24]]) cluster with Seleucid persecution; it answered the scandal that the wicked prosper unpunished in this life — McClellan invokes the LDS "eternal perspective." [^4] See [[Divine Punishment in the Afterlife Emerged as Theodicy Under Imperial Oppression]].
- **Second Temple texts present three competing frameworks, none dominant** — annihilation; temporary torment then annihilation; eternal conscious torment — reflected in [[1 Enoch]], Judith, [[Josephus]], rabbinic literature, the Apocalypse of Abraham, and 2 Baruch. [^5] See [[Book of Enoch - Postmortem Punishment and Reward Varieties]], [[Book of Enoch - Origin of Hell and Judgment Imagery]].
- **The New Testament is not univocal on hell.** Jesus speaks variously of eternal torment, of annihilation, and of temporary torment then annihilation; **Paul does not discuss it at all**; Revelation supplies the lake-of-fire imagery — yet "even death and hell" there come to an end. [^6]
- **Systematization came only after the Bible.** A single concept of hell (reduced to eternal conscious torment) was enforced only once Christianity gained political power in the **4th–5th centuries CE**, supplemented later by [[Dante]] and other literature. [^7]
- **Whether "there is no hell" is defensible.** On McClellan's [[Dogmatic Translation Imposes Univocality on the Text|rejection of univocality]], *any* position — from no postmortem punishment to eternal conscious torment — is arguable if you insist on reducing the plural text to one answer; what the authors actually believed ranges across all of them. [^8]
