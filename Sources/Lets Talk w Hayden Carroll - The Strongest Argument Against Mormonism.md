# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — The Strongest Argument Against Mormonism

A scripted advocacy monologue in which LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] takes up what critics call *the strongest argument against Mormonism* — the charge, voiced bluntly by [[Mark Driscoll]] and more cautiously by [[Trent Horn]], that Joseph Smith's revelations were **demonic rather than divine**. Carroll's thesis is that the argument is rhetorically devastating yet logically empty: it reduces to "my view is true, so your view is false," presupposes the critic's worldview, and never engages the Book of Mormon. He then pivots to his positive case — the Book of Mormon as evidence Joseph could not have authored on his own — and frames the whole exchange against the [[Galatians 1-8 - Angel Preaching Another Gospel]] and Great Apostasy questions. This monologue is the standalone companion to the recorded dialogue in [[Lets Talk w Hayden Carroll - Trent Horn Claims Mormonism Could Be Demonic]].

## Summary
- **The argument, steelmanned.** Carroll opens with the critics in their own words: Driscoll calls Mormonism "founded on lies, demonic deception," a "counterfeit fake Jesus" begun by "a demon"; Horn keeps the demonic merely *on the table* — a witnessed angel "could be something demonic rather than inspired," "superhuman but not divine." Carroll concedes its *rhetorical* force ("the most rhetorically effective argument") and even grants that *if* the critics' premise were true, disgust with the Church might be warranted ("Maybe"). He soberly ties the rhetoric to a Michigan church shooting (a gunman who rammed and burned a packed LDS chapel) without claiming the critics want violence.[^1][^2]
- **Aquinas as the critic's backstop.** Carroll fairly relays Horn's appeal to [[Thomas Aquinas]] (*Summa Theologica*): demons can work "wonders" beyond a man's power and knowledge so that "in a way he seems... to have worked a miracle," even though such works "are not real miracles" — they are "nevertheless something real." This is the strongest version of the case Carroll is answering.[^3]
- **The hidden syllogism.** Carroll reconstructs the demonic charge as a buried argument: *my belief is true, so your belief is false* — concretely, Horn's "Catholicism is true, so the Book of Mormon is not divinely inspired. This is a valid argument." Carroll grants the **validity** but denies the **soundness**: it rests on the unproven premise that Catholicism (or creedal Christianity) is the default truth. See [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]] and [[Validity vs Soundness - Why the Catholicism Syllogism Fails]].[^4][^5]
- **Unfalsifiable and reversible.** Because it presupposes its own conclusion, the charge is the "least credible form of apologetics" — "fingers in your ears... I can't hear you" — and any worldview (Islam, Judaism, the Latter-day Saints) could fire it back unchanged. Carroll shows the reversal explicitly: if his own case against creedal Christianity were merely "they're wrong because Joseph is a prophet," critics would *rightly* demand he justify that premise first. See [[The Strongest Argument Grants the Most Premises]], [[When Catholics Argue Like Jews - The Demonic Charge Is Unfalsifiable]], and [[The Reversibility Test for Bad Arguments]].[^6][^7]
- **Driscoll never does the work.** Carroll's specific complaint against Driscoll: he "doesn't even contend with the LDS claims that if true would validate Joseph as a prophet," substituting "scream demons from your megachurch" for engagement — "weak and frankly cowardice." He asks when Driscoll last sat with a well-informed Latter-day Saint.[^8]
- **The positive case — Joseph could not have written it.** Carroll pivots to the Book of Mormon's production statistics (≈269,320 words and 6,852 sentences in ~65 working days, one draft; Christ named ~4,000 times; 26+ stylistically distinct authors; 77 major storylines) and stacks them against a documented consensus of Joseph's *contemporaries* — friend, foe, and family alike — that he was barely educated. See [[The Consensus of Joseph Smith's Contemporaries That He Was Uneducated]] and [[Book of Mormon Witnesses Are the Strongest Evidence]].[^9][^10]
- **The tell — "the devil made him do it."** Carroll recounts his Ratio Christi talk: after he laid out the evidence to ~200 creedal Christians, the audience agreed Joseph "could not have done this on his own" — but concluded he "could have been demonically possessed." For Carroll this is the whole argument in miniature: the natural evidence is so strong the only escape is the devil. See [[The Book of Mormon Is a Magic Trick Not a Miracle]].[^11][^12]
- **"Yeah, seems really demonically inspired, Mark."** Carroll reads aloud the Book of Mormon passages a demon supposedly authored — that salvation is free through "the merits and mercy and grace of the holy Messiah" ([[2 Nephi 2.8]]), that all are lost "except it be through the atonement," that hope comes "through the atonement of Christ, and the power of his resurrection" — and lets the Christ-centered content rebut the demonic claim by contrast.[^13]
- **Where it bottoms out.** Carroll closes on his recurring counter-question: if the Trinity, consubstantiality, and *sola scriptura* are true and even necessary for salvation, "why didn't Jesus or any prophet of any age teach it?" He frames the creeds as the [[Hellenization Was Not Corruption of an Original Pure Church|Hellenization]] of the faith — Greek metaphysics read back into scripture by a church that "didn't have apostles to guide them" — which the Restoration corrects. See [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]].[^14][^15]
