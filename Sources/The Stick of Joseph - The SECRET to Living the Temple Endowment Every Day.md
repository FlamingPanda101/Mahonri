# The Stick of Joseph — The SECRET to Living the Temple Endowment Every Day (Todd McLauchlin)

LDS devotional interview on the [[Stick of Joseph]] channel: host [[Jackson Paul]] with [[Todd McLauchlin]] and [[Mike Day]] on the "Wednesday-morning question" — how to *embody* the temple endowment in ordinary life rather than treat the ceremony as a finished trophy. McLauchlin reads the **initiatory** (the washing-and-anointing blessing of the body's parts) as a daily **order of operations** for drawing down grace, and argues that **worship and praise** is the act that *initiates* God's power in a person, framing all of life as a choice between two **dominions** (Christ vs. the adversary) governed by **faith vs. fear**. Complements [[The Endowment Is Learning Heaven Not Earning Heaven]] and McLauchlin's [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]]; the practical-discipleship payload is distinct.

## Summary
- **The endowment is an *order* (pattern) you enter, not a finish line.** A priesthood is "an order, a pattern"; the ceremony is the start of a pattern that "must continue forever in order to be like God," and ordinances become "false idols" if confused with the reality they pattern. See [[Priesthood Is an Order Patterned on the Atonement Not a Power]] [^1].
- **Spiritual power has an order of operations — like starting a jet.** McLauchlin's recurring analogy: people get frustrated trying to obtain spiritual power "midstream," like shoving the throttle of a cold C-130 — but if you hit the right buttons in sequence (generator, avionics, throttle) you reach altitude fast. Transformation "can happen way faster than we think." The **initiatory** supplies that sequence [^2].
- **"What do you want?" is the first question — and you are always under a dominion.** The endowment's opening question (and Nephi's, from the angel) is "what do you want?"; not knowing leaves you ruled by something else, because "there's no such thing as sovereignty" — you are always being brought under a power, in darkness or in light. The gospel goal is to bring oneself "into complete full subjection to Jesus Christ voluntarily" [^3].
- **The initiatory mapped onto a daily order of operations.** McLauchlin walks the body-blessing as a morning practice: **mind** first (the seat of agency; "your minds are the portals between heaven and earth"), then **ears** (prepared to hear the word — write down and act on the first impression), **eyes** ("single to my glory," D&C 88:67-68; drawing energy only from Christ), **nose** (breathe in the Spirit), **mouth** (speak the word as given — the true order of prayer). New concept: [[The Initiatory Is a Daily Order of Operations for Drawing Down Grace]] [^4][^5].
- **Worship and praise is the "generator button."** The biggest game-changer he reports: on waking, with no spiritual desire, make an active choice to **worship** — the "sacrifice of praise" (Hebrews 13:15) — *regardless of feeling*, because "motivation is the adversary's tool" and "acting against motivation is the power of faith." Like a missionary forcing out "excuse me," initiating God by free will makes grace flow (D&C 121:45-46). New concept: [[Worship and Praise Initiates Gods Power Before Feeling Follows]] [^6][^7].
- **Two dominions, governed by faith vs. fear.** All power derives from Christ; any power sought outside his will is "a thief that breaks in and steals." You are always in one of two dominions, held by either grace or **fear** — and "fear is just faith in a false future reality." Faith in Christ (giving voice to what is true from God) is proactive; fear (faith in a lie, from the father of lies) is reactive. The same action (paying tithing) can be done from fear or from love — "one builds its house on sand, the other on rock." See [[Faith Is Trust in a True Future and Fear Is Faith in a False One]] [^8][^9].
- **Power is in alignment, not in fighting sin.** "You do not have power to subdue darkness as an individual"; the only power you have is to *enter a dominion* and be animated by it. So "stop fighting sin; follow Jesus Christ with everything you have," and sin loses its hold because Christ already overcame it — you "drift toward whatever you put your attention on, which is what you worship" [^10].
- **The broken heart is the culminating condition.** The sequence ends in the broken heart and contrite spirit: a heart "broken of any desire the Lord doesn't share," which shuts out the adversary (2 Nephi 4) and is the only condition by which one accesses the atonement (2 Nephi 9). The "fear of God" (encountering his reality and one's own insufficiency) is meant to drive one to Christ, who is the Comforter; God "shatters our image of him" (C.S. Lewis) so faith can grow into true knowledge — "a man is saved no faster than he gains knowledge" [^11][^12].
