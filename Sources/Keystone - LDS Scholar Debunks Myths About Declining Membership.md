# Keystone — LDS Scholar Debunks Myths About Declining Membership

- **Retention has dropped but LDS still lead the pack:** Youth-raised LDS retention fell from ~82% (1980s) to ~50% (2010s+), but that decline tracks *every* religion in the secularizing West, and Pew ranks Latter-day Saints #1 in retention of *active* members — undercutting the "leaving in droves" narrative.[^1] See [[LDS Retention Decline Mirrors All Religions]].
- **Those who leave are not a monolith:** Dyer's analysis of Ryan Burge's 100,000+ "spiritual seismology" dataset finds four distinct ex-member groups; ~50% remain favorable toward the Church and many "would come back if a friend invited them," while only a "loud 10%" are antagonistic faith-rejectors.[^2] See [[Four Distinct Groups of Those Who Leave the LDS Church]] and [[Most Who Leave the LDS Church Do Not Become Atheists]].
- **Politics, not church history, is the strongest leaving-predictor:** Tying identity to politics (especially far-left or far-right extremes) predicts disaffiliation; experiencing God regularly predicts staying; church history was a predictor but not a top one.[^3] See [[Politics Predicts Religious Disaffiliation]] and [[Experiencing God Regularly Predicts LDS Retention]].
- **High-demand religion is a feature, not a bug:** "Religions that ask more of their members keep more of their members"; demand drives investment, service, and the documented mental/physical/family-health benefits of religiosity — the opposite of Korihor's "keep their heads down" charge.[^4] See [[High-Demand Religion Retains More Members]] and [[Religious Demand Versus Korihor Priestcraft Objection]].
- **The theology of grace neutralizes toxic perfectionism:** LDS and religious people score *lower* in toxic perfectionism than the non-religious (~12% vs >20%), because toxic perfectionism is about self-worth collapsing on failure, and the gospel message "God loves you no matter what / you can become 100% clean" decouples worth from performance.[^5] See [[Toxic Perfectionism Lower Among Religious Than Non-Religious]] and [[Self-Worth Decoupled From Performance Combats Perfectionism]].
- **LDS lead in religious literacy, practice, and reported happiness — but with humility:** "Mormon Envy" (from the book *Almost Christian*), top scores in scripture/prayer with children, youthful demographics, and highest "very happy with life" rating (42%, tied with Hindus/Jews) — paired with Oaks's "Church of the Lamb" framing that other faiths offer real benefits too.[^6] See [[LDS Excel in Religious Literacy and Practice]] and [[LDS Highest in Self-Reported Happiness]].
