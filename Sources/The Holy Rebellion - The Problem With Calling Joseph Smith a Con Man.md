# The Holy Rebellion - The Problem With Calling Joseph Smith a Con Man

**Host:** [[Christian (Holy Rebellion)]] | **Guest:** [[Mark (Holy Rebellion)|Mark]] (historian, PhD, ~24 years on the Joseph Smith Papers) | **Date:** 2026-02-16

## Summary

- **Treasure hunting was real but normal and modest in extent.** [[Joseph Smith]] did hunt for treasure and admitted it (1825 [[Josiah Stowell]] silver-mine dig; the 1838 *Elders' Journal* "money digger" Q&A), but serious historians place his involvement at perhaps "a dozen or so" digs, set within a centuries-long Western culture of treasure seeking driven by the pre-banking practice of burying valuables and the New World gold/silver imagination.[^1] See [[Joseph Smith Treasure Hunting Extent]], [[Treasure Hunting as Historical Cultural Practice]], [[Treasure Law and Biblical Precedent]], [[Josiah Stowell Hires Joseph Smith 1825]].

- **"Convicted con man" is an overclaim.** Whether Joseph was even convicted at the 1826 Bainbridge proceeding is inconclusive — five conflicting later sources, no contemporaneous conviction record, no billed conviction record in the itemized Neely notes, and no incarceration evidence — and even a conviction would only reflect a post-Enlightenment statute classifying seer-stone seeing as "pretended," not a finding of fraud.[^2] See [[Convicted Con Man Label Overclaim]], [[Joseph Smith 1826 Trial Conviction Question]], [[Disorderly Person Statute and Pretending]], [[Josiah Stowell Defends Joseph Smith]].

- **Sort the folk-practice claims by evidence.** Of the lurid catalog (magic circles, animal sacrifice, necromancy, daggers, astrology), only seer stones, divining rods, and treasure seeking are credibly, contemporaneously, multiply attested; the sensational items derive largely from the biased, commissioned [[Philastus Hurlbut|Hurlbut]] statements and uncritical secondary repetition.[^3] See [[Folk Magic Claims About the Smith Family]], [[Hurlbut Affidavits Bias Problem]], [[Source Criticism in Mormon History]], [[Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin]].

- **Seer stones and divining rods are owned as revelatory means, not explained away.** For Latter-day Saints these are God-ordained instruments in continuity with the Urim and Thummim and the rods of Moses and Aaron; the early revelations to [[Oliver Cowdery]] ([[D&C 6]]/8/9) call the divining rod a spiritual gift ("gift of Aaron").[^4] See [[Seer Stones and Divining Rods as Revelatory Means]], [[Oliver Cowdery Gift of the Rod]], [[Prophets Are Fallible Not Divine]].

- **Method over name-calling, and charity across faiths.** "Magic," "con man," and "fraud" are pejorative labels used to avoid engaging truth claims; good history evaluates corroboration, bias, and contemporaneity, and recognizes that all faiths' supernatural claims are equally "impossible" to a skeptic — so mockery should give way to graciousness.[^5] See [[Avoiding the Pejorative Magic]], [[Historical Method Over Name Calling]], [[Presentism in Folk Belief Judgment]], [[Mutual Vulnerability of Supernatural Claims]], [[Witnesses Spiritual Eyes Selective Emphasis]].
