# Ward Radio — Debunking God Loves Mormons on the Pre-Existence

Host [[Cardon Ellis]], apocrypha author [[Jonah Barnes]], and Scripture Notes owner [[Oak Norton]] react to the evangelical channel "God Loves Mormons," which argues from [[1 Corinthians 15.45-46|1 Corinthians 15]] ("not the spiritual… but the natural first") and from "no one has seen the Father" texts that humans did not premortally exist and are not literally God's children.

## Summary

- **The "no pre-existence" prooftexts are metaphor, not mechanism.** The hosts answer that Paul's "natural first, then spiritual" ([[1 Corinthians 15.45-46|1 Cor 15:45-46]]) contrasts the first Adam with the second Adam (Christ, "the Lord from heaven"), not premortal vs. mortal existence; and that the "no one has seen the Father / heard his voice" texts ([[John 5.37-38|John 5:37-38]]) cannot be flatly true given Moses, Isaiah, and Jeremiah saw God — so either those theophanies were of a distinct Son (undercutting strict Trinitarianism) or the verse is being misread. "If God were your Father, ye would love me" is read as a statement about spiritual likeness, not biology. See [[The Pre-Existence Prooftexts Against It Are Misread Metaphors]].
- **Divine sonship is explicit across the canon.** Against the claim that we are not literally God's children, the panel stacks [[Hebrews 12.9|Hebrews 12:9]] ("the Father of spirits"), [[Acts 17.28-29|Acts 17:28-29]] ("we are the offspring of God"), [[Romans 8.16|Romans 8:16]] ("the Spirit… beareth witness… that we are the children of God"), and [[Jeremiah 1.5|Jeremiah 1:5]] ("before I formed thee in the belly I knew thee"). They argue the creedal denial of literal divine sonship — that God is "Father" only by adoption — is a Hellenistic overlay that makes God cruel and distant. See [[The Canon Affirms Literal Divine Sonship and Premortal Existence]].
- **Apocrypha as corroboration, and the James 2:24 irony.** [[Jonah Barnes]] runs a chain of extra-biblical texts said to teach premortal spirits (Slavonic Palea — spirits created "of fire and light" on day one; 3 Enoch; 2 Baruch 21:6; the Apocalypse of Abraham), paralleling [[Abraham 3.22-23|Abraham 3]]'s "noble and great ones." The episode closes on the recurring irony that the evangelical "faith alone" critique collides with [[James 2.24|James 2:24]] — the only verse using "faith only," and it *denies* it. See [[The Canon Affirms Literal Divine Sonship and Premortal Existence]] and the existing [[Faith Alone Appears Once in Scripture and Is the Verse That Denies It]].
