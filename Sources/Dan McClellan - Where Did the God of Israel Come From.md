In this short video [[Dan McClellan]] asks where Yahweh, the God of Israel, historically came from. His answer separates two originally distinct deities — **El**, the Northwest Semitic high god embedded in the very name *Yisra-EL*, and **Yahweh**, a latecomer with no attestation in the region until the 9th century BCE — and traces how they merged. He favors a southern, nomadic origin for Yahweh (the Kenite-Midianite hypothesis) supported by Egyptian "Shasu of YHW" inscriptions, drawing especially on Daniel Fleming's work.

## Summary

McClellan argues there were really **two** gods of Israel. The original patron, indicated by the theophoric name *Israel*, was **El** — the patriarchal high deity Israel borrowed from the broader Northwest Semitic world, the god named on the Merneptah Stele context (~1208 BCE) when no deity "Yahweh" is attested anywhere nearby. A deity named **Yahweh** appears only around the mid-9th century BCE (the Mesha/Moabite inscription, Shalmaneser III's black obelisk, the Tel Dan stele, and Yahwistic personal names), then becomes Israel's patron — probably by **conflating with El** around 1000 BCE, yielding one god who is both El and Yahweh.

Where did Yahweh come from? The name matches no other regional pantheon and has no widely accepted Ugaritic or Akkadian cognate. McClellan favors the view that Yahweh was the local/ancestral deity of a nomadic group from further east or south (around Midian) brought into the northern hill country — the **Kenite-Midianite hypothesis**, which leans on biblical passages depicting Yahweh marching from the south (Sinai, Paran, Teman). Supporting this, 14th–13th c. BCE Egyptian inscriptions reference the **"Shasu of the land of YHW"** — at that stage a land/people name, not yet a deity, but plausibly the seed of the divine name. He notes the Exodus 3 folk-etymology from the verb "to be" is likely secondary; the name may simply derive from a place or people. He closes with a reading list (Robert Miller II, *Yahweh: Origins of a Desert God*; Thomas Römer, *The Invention of God*; *The Origins of Yahwism*, ed. van Oorschot & Witte; Daniel Fleming, *Yahweh before Israel*; Ted Lewis, *The Origin and Character of God*), naming Fleming's theory as his favorite.
