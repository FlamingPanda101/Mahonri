# Keystone - Top 10 Attacks on Joseph Smith That Backfire

A first-person Keystone countdown of ten frequently-repeated criticisms of [[Joseph Smith]] and the [[Book of Mormon]] that, on the host's telling, "ended up backfiring and strengthening my faith." The unifying move is a burden-of-proof reversal: each attack, pressed to its conclusion, leaves the critic with a *less* plausible naturalistic story than the prophetic claim it was meant to defeat. Most items are compressed pointers to debates the vault already covers in depth; two carry framings worth isolating — the historiographical instability of the critical theory itself (#10) and Joseph's own family as a hostile-to-fraud test of the long-con hypothesis (#5).

## Summary
- **#10 — "Joseph made the Book of Mormon up on his own."** The host notes this charge has *reversed itself* over ~200 years: first "simplistic trash" from an ignorant farmer, then too complex for him (so [[Sydney Rigdon]] + [[Solomon Spalding]] wrote it), then — after the [[Spalding Theory of Book of Mormon Origin|Spalding theory collapsed]] — back to lone authorship, but now by a Joseph "a lot smarter and well read than his friends, family, and even foes claimed." The lack of a cohesive explanation after two centuries is itself treated as evidence.[^1] See [[The Critical Theory of Joseph Smith Has No Stable Form]] and [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]].
- **#9 — Mocking the seer-stone-in-a-hat.** Make fun of the hat all you want, "the words had to have come from somewhere" — 269,000 words, complex timelines, fulfilled prophecies, realistic political and economic systems, dictated with no reference materials in front of witnesses.[^2] See [[Dictation Speed and Method Argue Against Fabrication]] and [[Dictation Feat Exceeds Joseph Smith's Capacity]].
- **#8 — "He was separated from his scribe by a curtain."** The man-behind-the-curtain theory backfires: multiple sources put Joseph in full view of the scribe and visitors during the dictation of the surviving text, removing the "concealed notes" suspicion.[^3] See [[There Was No Curtain During the Book of Mormon Translation]].
- **#7 — "Many plate witnesses left the church."** Their leaving makes the testimony *more* credible: despite estrangement, ridicule, and loss, none ever denied it — and the more people in on a conspiracy, the harder it is to keep, especially once they go rogue.[^4] See [[Witnesses Never Recanted Despite Estrangement]].
- **#6 — Hypnotism (Brodie, Vogel).** When the best a critic of [[Fawn Brodie]]'s and [[Dan Vogel]]'s caliber can offer is that Joseph *accidentally* hypnotized the witnesses into hallucinating, "at what point do you just say, maybe it's all just real."[^5] See [[Hypnotism Theory of the Witnesses Rebutted]] and [[Mass Hallucination Hypnosis Theory Rebutted]].
- **#5 — "He was scheming since he was a teenager."** The long-con theory predicts the people who knew him best would be suspicious; instead every member of his family believed him, some had tangible experiences with the plates, and they followed him through poverty, persecution, and death.[^6] See [[Joseph Smith's Family Believed Him Against the Long-Con Theory]].
- **#4 — The 1843 Kinderhook fake-plate sting.** Pranksters expected a miraculous revealed translation of forged plates; Joseph instead attempted a secular comparison against Egyptian characters he was studying ([[William Clayton]] recorded a partial), got nowhere, and published nothing. "They tried to trap a con man, but Joseph didn't respond like a con man."[^7] See [[Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation]] and [[A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax]].
- **#3 — CES Letter place names.** Of 86 Book of Mormon place names, the [[CES Letter]] flags ~20 as resembling sites near Joseph; but several "didn't exist yet" in 1830 and others were obscure and remote, while studying the names yields finds like Gerson/Jershon from a Hebrew verb meaning "to inherit."[^8] See [[The Vernal Holley Map Places Book of Mormon Geography Wrong]] and [[Jershon Means Place of Inheritance]].
- **#2 — Plagiarism from *The First Book of Napoleon*.** The CES Letter's matched phrases are pulled from the first 10 pages of the Book of Mormon versus 21 scattered pages of [[Napoleon|the Napoleon book]], are not actually in order, and there is no evidence Joseph knew the book existed — "condemn not" and "the land of Jerusalem" are weak loot.[^9] See [[The Plagiarism Charge Boomerangs on the Critic]] and [[No Naturalistic Source Accounts for the Whole Book of Mormon]].
- **#1 — Parallels to *View of the Hebrews* ([[Ethan Smith]]).** Having finally read every page, the host judges it "much ado about nothing" — after 200 years, if this is the best the critics have, the Book of Mormon is easier to believe.[^10] See [[The View of the Hebrews Parallels Are Parallelomania]], [[No Contemporary Accusation of Plagiarism]], and [[BYU Published View of the Hebrews]].
