# Suffering and Evil — The Probability Version

A short animated [[William Lane Craig|Craig]] explainer (part two, following the logical version) that takes up the *evidential* or *probabilistic* problem of suffering and evil: the concession that God and evil are not logically incompatible, paired with the claim that the sheer amount of seemingly pointless suffering makes God's existence highly *improbable*.[^1] Craig answers with three moves — that we are in no position to judge God lacks morally sufficient reasons, that probability is always relative to the *full* scope of evidence (including the arguments for God), and that Christianity itself entails doctrines that *raise* the probability of evil's coexistence with God.[^2] It closes by conceding that even when the intellectual problem is answered, the *emotional* problem remains, and points to the suffering Christ as God's answer of presence.[^3]

## Summary
- The probability version grants that God and suffering can coexist but argues there is "just so much pointless suffering" that "it seems improbable that God could have good reasons for permitting it."[^1]
- **Reply 1 — limited vantage:** we are "limited in space and time, and in intelligence and insight," whereas God "sees every detail of history from beginning to end" and orders it through free decisions; suffering that "appears pointless within our limited scope" may be justly permitted within His wider framework.[^4]
- **Reply 2 — assess against all the evidence:** probabilities are always relative to background information (the sumo-wrestler illustration), so "improbable relative to what?" — weighed against the powerful arguments for God's existence, theism may well be probable.[^5]
- **Reply 3 — Christian doctrines raise the probability:** the purpose of life is to know God, not to be happy; humanity is in rebellion (so moral evil is *expected*); God's purpose spills over into eternal life ("this slight momentary affliction"); and the knowledge of God is an incomparable, infinite good.[^6]
- The intellectual problem of evil thus "does not disprove God's existence," but the *emotional* problem remains powerful — answered not by an argument but by the God who suffers with us in Christ and promises to "wipe every tear from their eyes."[^3]
