# The Council of Trent — The Self-Authenticating Canon Refuted

[[Trent Horn]] and guest [[Joe Heschmeyer]] dismantle the Protestant doctrine of the **self-authenticating canon** — the claim, traced from [[John Calvin]] and [[Martin Luther]] to modern apologists like [[Michael Krueger]], that Scripture's divine authority and the boundaries of the canon can be recognized without any infallible Church or tradition.

## Summary
- Calvin's "we feel a divine energy living and breathing in it" model of canon recognition is **self-refuting**: if every Spirit-led believer just *knows* which books belong, why do Calvin (who cited Baruch), Luther (who wanted four NT books out), and modern Protestants flatly contradict one another? Somebody's "feelings" are wrong [^1].
- The **historical record refutes self-authentication**: the earliest Christians (e.g. around [[Ignatius of Antioch]]) rarely cited the NT as Scripture, and [[Augustine]] and [[Jerome]] settled canon questions by appeal to *church consensus* and *apostolic authority* — never by "search your feelings" [^2].
- Calvin's gambit is structurally identical to **Moroni's "burning bosom"** test in the [[Angel Moroni|Book of Mormon]] ([[Moroni 10.4-5|Moroni 10:4-5]]): both make doubt a sign that *you* are not among the elect, insulating the claim from scrutiny — a move [[Jeremiah]] 17:9 ("the heart is deceitful") undercuts [^3].
- Modern defenders ([[Robert Godfrey]], [[Michael Krueger]]) relocate "self-authentication" from the individual to the *church*, which quietly **backs into the Catholic model** while attacking a strawman ("thermostat") view that Vatican I explicitly condemned [^4].
- Pinning canonicity to objective criteria (apostolicity, orthodoxy) is the **Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy** and leaves the canon a "fallible collection of infallible books" ([[R.C. Sproul]]) hostage to critical scholarship [^5].
