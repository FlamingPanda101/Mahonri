# Thoughtful Faith — Julie Hanks Went After My Friends

[[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith) responds to LDS therapist and progressive commentator [[Julie Hanks]] after one of her Instagram posts claimed LDS female apologists "only have a social media platform because of feminism." The framing is personal — Hansen opens by honoring a recently deceased "Latter-day Saint sister," Sarah Allen — but the body of the episode is **substantive, not gossipy**: it is a long-form philosophical argument (drawing on James Cussen / Living Philosophy, Martin Seligman, and Brett McKay) about the difference between feminism and femininity, the well-being data on the modern West, and the LDS theology of male-female complementarity.

> **What kind of episode is this?** Honestly, mostly an *idea-driven cultural apologetic*, not interpersonal drama. The "went after my friends" hook and the in-house jabs at Hanks ("this is why you don't go to Instagram life coaches for history or philosophy") are a thin wrapper around a real thesis: that so-called feminism became a *masculinist* movement which devalued the feminine, and that LDS theology offers a corrective by valuing male and female as complementary halves. The grievance framing is minimal; the philosophical content is the point. Two genuine concepts were extracted; the parasocial/Instagram-feud element was not inflated into doctrine.

## Summary

- **The trigger.** Hansen normally ignores Hanks's posts but answers this one. Hanks's claim, steelmanned: LDS women defending the faith online enjoy a platform won by feminism, yet "dismiss and discount the contributions of feminism," forgetting that "not long ago... religious women were told to be quiet and submissive." See [[Julie Hanks]]. [^1]
- **In-house counter from Restoration history.** Hansen replies that the Restoration valued women's public voice early and independently: the [[Doctrine and Covenants 25|1830 revelation to Emma Smith]] commissions her "to expound scriptures and to exhort the church"; [[Eliza R. Snow|the Relief Society]] was founded as "a kingdom of priests"; and 19th-century LDS women (e.g., Ellis Reynolds Shipp) earned medical degrees. [^2]
- **The core thesis — feminism is misnamed "womanism."** Granting that *first-wave* feminism (liberty and legal equality) "was great," Hansen/Cussen argue later feminism became a masculinist movement: it told women to adopt competitive, dominant, "masculine" traits in a value-landscape that already over-valued the masculine, thereby *devaluing the feminine itself.* "Nothing has done more to devalue the feminine than so-called feminism." See [[Womanism Is Misnamed Masculinism That Devalues the Feminine]]. [^3]
- **The empirical leg — declining well-being.** Seligman: depression is "ten times as prevalent as in 1960," onset age dropped from ~29 to ~14½; a University of Pennsylvania study finds women, once *happier* than men, are now the *less* happy sex despite gains in representation. Hansen reads this as a society "unbalanced" by minimizing feminine virtues — carefully noting it does not "fully explain" the rise. See [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]] and [[LDS Highest in Self-Reported Happiness]]. [^4]
- **The LDS theological payoff — complementarity.** Male and female together are the image of God ([[Genesis 1.26-27|Genesis 1:26-27]], [[Matthew 19.3-9|Matthew 19:4-6]], [[1 Corinthians 11.11|1 Cor 11:11]]); "gender is an essential characteristic of... eternal identity"; exaltation requires eternal marriage ([[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]]). Closing image (Brett McKay): the sexes are "two different musical instruments playing the exact same notes" — equal worth, different timbre. See [[Male and Female Balance Is Required for the Divine Image and Human Flourishing]]. [^5]
- **Where critics push back.** The strongest opposing case is that the concrete liberties Hansen's friends enjoy were *won by* women's movements — closer to Hanks's point than the rebuttal admits. See [[Feminism Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Doctrine]] and [[Modern Gender Equality Is Not Grounded in the Bible]]. The disagreement may be narrow: Hanks credits feminism for women's permissions; Hansen credits first-wave feminism for the same while charging later feminism with abandoning femininity. [^3]
