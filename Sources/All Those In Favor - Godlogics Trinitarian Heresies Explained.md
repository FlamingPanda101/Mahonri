# All Those In Favor — Godlogic's Trinitarian Heresies Explained

A reaction episode in which the All Those In Favor hosts, joined by "LJ" of the *Rational Theology* podcast, review clips of Trinitarian YouTuber [[Avery (God Logic)|Avery (God Logic)]] — both from his recent Trinity debate with [[Jacob Hansen]] and from older "found footage" of him pressing Latter-day Saint missionaries. The throughline: a Protestant defending the creedal Trinity *from the Bible alone* keeps stumbling into the very heresies the councils named, because the creedal system is a narrow ridge between contradictory errors that Scripture by itself cannot navigate. The LDS Godhead — three distinct persons, one in purpose — is offered as the model that never had to walk that ridge in the first place.

## Summary
- **Avery floundered on Christ's mind**, answering "one divine mind" to the question "does Jesus have one mind or two?" — which the guest identifies as **Apollinarianism**, the 4th-century heresy that Christ had a human body but no human mind, condemned at First Constantinople (381) [^1]. See [[Avery Tripped the Apollinarian Wire]].
- **The diagnosis is structural, not personal**: "if you rely on the Bible alone for understanding the Trinity, you're going to inevitably commit all those heresies that he did." A Catholic or Orthodox debater, drawing on tradition, would have avoided the trap; the Protestant cannot [^2]. See [[Sola Scriptura Cannot Navigate the Heresy Minefield]].
- **The patristic answer cited**: [[Gregory of Nazianzus]]' axiom against Apollinarius — "what is not assumed is not healed" — meaning if Christ did not take a human mind, he did not save the human mind [^3].
- **Avery's *autotheos* contradiction**: he said the Son "is not generated by anything outside of his own essence" (Son as *autotheos*, "of himself God"), which collides with eternal generation — in the first millennium the Father generates the Son, so the Son depends for his nature on the Father; calling the Son *autotheos* yields "two gods separately existing," a heresy by Eastern Orthodox lights [^4]. See [[Autotheos Collides With Eternal Generation]].
- **The salvation critique**: under "salvation by theology," getting these niche metaphysical questions wrong is damnable — so "1% of people that think they are saved are actually saved," and historic fathers (Origen, the Origenists) get condemned posthumously. LDS salvation is by grace, not by passing a metaphysics exam [^5]. See [[Salvation by Theology Damns Almost Everyone]].
- **The "play it safe, don't read" absurdity**: if God reveals dogma through the fathers but understanding-then-rejecting it damns you while ignorance is excused, the safest path is willful ignorance — which the hosts note is not a coherent salvation system [^6].
- **John 1:1 with the missionaries**: Avery pressed missionaries on "the Word was God" vs "a god," conceding the Greek lacks the definite article (*kai theos en ho logos*) so it is not an identity statement (*ho theos* = the Father as a heuristic); the hosts note the missionaries' "one in purpose, he's God" answer actually tracks the best reading ("the Word was divine") better than Avery's own position [^7]. See [[Anarthrous Theos in John 1.1 Undercuts the Identity Reading]].
- **The King Follett ambush**: Avery cites Joseph Smith's "God was not always God" as anti-scriptural, but the hosts show the sermon is parsed *against* its caricature — the Bullock source even records "He was God from... all eternity, and I do not refute it"; Joseph's point is a kenotic/condescension reading (Christ "emptied himself," Philippians 2), and D&C 121 already names the Father "the eternal God of all other gods" five years earlier [^8]. See [[King Follett Is Misread as God Had a God]].
- **The Godhead alternative**: the recurring contrast is three distinct divine persons united in one purpose — the LDS Godhead — which never generates the modalism/Arianism/tritheism/partialism paradoxes the creedal Trinity must dodge [^9]. See [[The LDS Godhead Sidesteps the Named Heresies]].
