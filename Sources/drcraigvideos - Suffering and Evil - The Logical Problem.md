# Suffering and Evil — The Logical Problem
A short animated ReasonableFaith explainer in which [[William Lane Craig]] dismantles the *logical* version of the problem of evil — the claim, framed classically by Epicurus, that the existence of God and the existence of suffering are strictly logically incompatible. Following [[Alvin Plantinga]]'s Free Will Defense, Craig argues there is no explicit contradiction between "God exists" and "suffering exists," so the atheist must smuggle in two hidden assumptions that turn out to be unprovable, leaving the logical argument "bankrupt."

## Summary
- **Epicurus' dilemma.** The video opens with Epicurus' classic framing: if God is willing but unable to prevent evil he is not all-powerful; if able but unwilling, not good; if both willing and able, how can evil exist? The atheist concludes it is logically impossible for God and suffering to coexist, yet suffering plainly exists, so God does not.[^1]
- **No explicit contradiction.** "God exists" and "suffering exists" are not explicitly contradictory the way "David is married" and "David is a bachelor" are. So the atheist needs **hidden assumptions** to expose the alleged contradiction: (1) an all-powerful God *can create any world he wants*, and (2) an all-loving God *prefers a world without suffering*.[^2]
- **Both hidden premises fail.** The first is false because it is logically impossible even for an omnipotent God to *force* people to freely choose the good — a "square circle," not a limit on power. The second cannot be known to be true, since we routinely permit suffering to secure a greater good, so God may have morally sufficient reasons.[^3]
- **The logical argument is conceded dead.** To make the logical problem work the atheist would have to prove it *logically impossible* both that free will exists and that God has good reasons for permitting suffering — a burden "too heavy to bear." Craig closes by noting (quoting Plantinga and Mackie's concession) that philosophers, even atheists, have abandoned the logical problem as "bankrupt," though the *probability* version remains.[^4]
