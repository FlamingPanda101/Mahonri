# Where Will You Go? — Confronting the Meaning Crisis and the Deconstruction of Belief

[[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith) frames the human condition with an extended allegory: stripped of all narratives, "man is adrift in a sea without any definite purpose or direction" — an ocean that is beautiful, terrifying, lonely, and unjust. Faith traditions are the *boats* (and even chunks of driftwood) people build and cling to so they are not tossed about and drowned; to abandon faith is to abandon the boat and swim in calm water, naive to the storms that always come. Drawing on [[Jordan Peterson]] and [[Friedrich Nietzsche]], Hansen argues that deconstruction does not just collapse one belief system but can collapse one's "belief in belief systems" — the road to nihilism — and that meaning, hope, and happiness are found not in comfort but in building a seaworthy craft, plotting a course, and taking on the "sailor's toil" toward a worthy destination.

## Summary
- **The human condition is the ocean — vast, beautiful, terrifying, and unjust.** When all narratives are stripped away, man is "adrift in a sea without any definite purpose or direction," where "chance alone rules your well-being or suffering"; to deny the "perverse injustice" of this plight is to be "either naive or heartless." [^1] See [[The Ocean as the Human Condition]].
- **Faith is grabbing onto a boat; deconstruction abandons it.** "Faith is when you grab onto one of these boats... because the alternative leaves you adrift and without recourse." Those who abandon faith are swimmers who say "the water is not so bad" — but they "don't understand the ocean," its power and danger. [^2]
- **Deconstruction rarely lands somewhere better — it risks nihilism.** Quoting Peterson channeling Nietzsche: when a belief system is challenged, "not only does the belief system collapse but... your belief in belief systems collapses." One can jump from one structure to another, but concluding that "protective structures themselves are not to be trusted" is "the pathway to nihilism." [^3] See [[Where Will You Go - Deconstruction Rarely Leads Somewhere Better]].
- **Meaning is found in building and sailing, not in comfort.** "Happiness is found in meaning not comfort, and meaning is only found in building a seaworthy craft, plotting a course, and taking on the sailor's toil." The paradoxical solution to tragedy and malevolence is "the willingness to face them." [^4] See [[Happiness Is Found in Meaning Not Comfort]].
- **Swimmers vs. ship-builders — the ethics of deconstruction.** Hansen contrasts those who build boats and invite others aboard with "swimmers" who mock the boats and "take great pleasure in trying to sink them"; citing the Charles Benvegar poem, he asks whether one is a careful builder or "a wrecker who walks to town, content with the labor of tearing down." [^5] See [[Swimmers vs Ship-Builders - The Ethics of Deconstruction]].
