# Let's Get Real - Why It's Easier to Flatten Brigham Young

**Show:** Let's Get Real (Episode E0051) | **Host:** [[Stephen Jones]] | **Guest:** Dr. [[Daniel C. Peterson]] | **Title:** "Why It's Easier to Flatten Brigham Young | Dr. Dan Peterson E0051"

A long-form interview tied to Peterson's *Becoming Brigham* documentary series and the film *Six Days in August*, defending [[Brigham Young]] against the modern tendency to make him the "fall guy" for everything uncomfortable in LDS history, and using him as a case study for how God works through fallible humans.

- Peterson argues that the cultural urge to "flatten" heroes — to insist every great figure secretly has "feet of clay" — is a corrosive modern disease (illustrated by the banishment of Aristides the Just and the valet proverb), and that Brigham Young is being scapegoated by people who want to preserve [[Joseph Smith]] as blameless while blaming Brigham for polygamy, the priesthood-and-race policy, and authoritarianism.[^1] See [[Hero-Flattening as a Cultural Disease]].
- Drawing on recent scholarship (*This Abominable Slavery* by LaJean Carruth, Paul Reeve, and Christopher Rich; *Slavery in Zion* by Amy Tanner Thiriot), Peterson reverses his own prior view: Utah's 1852 act was a *servitude* statute aimed at graduated emancipation, not a chattel-slavery bill, and Brigham personally loathed slavery and treated individual Black people kindly.[^2] See [[Utah 1852 Act Was Servitude Not Slavery]] and [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]].
- He distinguishes Brigham's *limitations of his era* (racist assumptions shared by Abraham Lincoln and abolitionists alike) from being "uniquely wicked," rejecting both the "man of his time" excuse-stopper and the presentist condemnation, and warning that the Journal of Discourses (via scribe George Watt) made Brigham sound harsher than the shorthand originals show.[^3] See [[A Man of His Time Without Excusing the Sin]] and [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]].
- The interview's theological spine is that God deliberately works through flawed, fallible prophets — including living ones — because He wants humans to *grow by experience* rather than be "robots," so the proper response to prophetic mistakes is humble discipleship, not hunting for an "out."[^4] See [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]], [[Prophets Are Fallible Today]], and [[Learning by Experience over Book Learning]].
- Peterson recovers a *spiritual* Brigham (speaking in tongues to mired oxen and before Joseph Smith; claiming revelation; obsessed with temple-building) against the flat "colonizer/organizer" stereotype, and frames the 1844 succession as the Twelve holding the keys.[^5] See [[Brigham Young as Spiritual Man Not Just Colonizer]] and [[Brigham Young Saved the Church Through the Keys]].
