# What Is Morality? (Part 4) — Is There Such a Thing as a Moral Absolute?
This is the next installment in [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* metaethics series (see [[Thoughtful Faith - The Nature of Morality from the Ground Up (Ethics)]] and [[Thoughtful Faith - What Is Morality Part 2 - Is Morality Subjective]]). Opening with the conundrum "is it always wrong to lie?", Hansen proposes a model he calls **algorithmic morality**: morality is a formula that converts situational inputs into the right output by ordering deeper *principles* toward the universal end of escaping suffering and reaching well-being. Commandments are time- and place-bound expressions of those principles, so the moral "absolute" is not a universal list of do's and don'ts but the fixed objective *end* (well-being) that the value-hierarchy is calibrated toward — a frame that tracks [[Sam Harris]]-style well-being realism reframed inside Latter-day Saint thought and Jesus' critique of Pharisaic legalism.

## Summary
- **Algorithmic morality, not a list of rules.** Hansen rebrands a corrected "situational ethics" as *algorithmic morality*: "an algorithm will give you an output based on inputs according to a given formula in order to achieve a desired outcome." Morality is "a complex algorithmic formula that leads us away from suffering and towards ultimate well-being." [^1] See [[Algorithmic Morality - Principles as Code Converting Situations to Right Action]].
- **Principles are the deep code; commandments are surface expressions.** "Specific commandments are really just expressions of deeper principles and values given to a particular person or group of people at a particular time," but it is the principles that are "more fundamental than any particular commandment." Principles are "the hard code that we take into ourselves," and spiritual development "is when we encode these principles into our psyche and soul." He quotes an unnamed apostolic teaching that "a true principle makes decisions clear even under the most confusing and compelling circumstances." [^2][^3] See [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]].
- **This is the model Jesus taught against the Pharisees.** Jesus "rejected the law-based moral legalism of his day," reaching past "thou shalt not kill" to the principle (anger) behind it, and defending Sabbath healing of "a daughter of Abraham." The Pharisees "placed a higher value on the letter of the law over the spirit of the law." [^4][^5] See [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]].
- **A value hierarchy resolves moral conflicts.** When commandments collide — telling your insecure wife she does *not* look fat (charity vs honesty), or stealing bread to feed a starving child (property vs life) — "the algorithm helps. It takes all of these principles and arranges them into a value hierarchy." This "can actually justify a white lie." Hansen concedes it is "a dangerous slippery slope" but asks "what's the alternative?" since "life is too complex for a complete list of do's and don'ts." [^6][^7] See [[A Value Hierarchy Resolves Conflicts Between Moral Principles]].
- **Quoting C.S. Lewis: Christianity gives principles, not a program.** Hansen reads [[C.S. Lewis]]: Christianity "has not and does not profess to have a detailed political program," because it is "meant for all men at all times"; "when it tells you to feed the hungry it does not give you lessons in cookery." [^8] See [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]].
- **Authorities on the primacy of principles.** [[Wilford Woodruff]]: the principles of salvation "are principles you cannot annihilate... beyond the reach of man to handle or to destroy." Another unnamed apostolic teaching: "procedures, programs, the administrative policies... are subject to change... but the principles never change." [^9][^10] See [[Principles Are More Fundamental Than Commandments]].
- **Varying application does not make morality relative.** Does ordering principles differently per situation make morality "relative or subjective"? "No, because we all ultimately value the same thing above all else: we desire to escape suffering." That universal end is "the very bedrock of all moral reasoning"; the only question is "if one's hierarchy of values is properly calibrated towards ultimate well-being." [^11][^12] See [[The Moral Absolute Is the Universal End Not the Particular Rule]] and compare [[Moral Relativism Is Self-Contradictory]].
