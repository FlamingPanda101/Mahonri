# Ward Radio — Was Joseph Smith a Trinitarian?

The Ward Radio panel ([[Cardon Ellis]], [[Jonah Barnes]], and [[Ryland Hutchinson]] of Ethers Elephant) takes up the ex-Mormon talking point that [[Joseph Smith]] began as a Trinitarian/modalist and only later evolved a distinct-beings Godhead — and answers it with a single dated document, Doctrine and Covenants 76. The twist of the episode is that the cleanest statement of the rebuttal came from critic John Dehlin himself.

## Summary

- **The steelmanned critic claim.** Ryland reads the argument straight from "an anti-Mormon who's explaining the argument": in the 1820s–early 1830s Joseph believed "God and Jesus were essentially the same being" (a Trinitarian/modalist view), wrote that God into the 1830 Book of Mormon (1 & 2 Nephi), and dictated an 1832 First Vision account mentioning only "the Lord." Then in the mid-1830s he allegedly evolved to a separate-beings view, changed the Book of Mormon text to insert "the Son of" / "the son of the Eternal Father," and altered the First Vision by 1838 to include two personages instead of one.[^1][^2][^3]
- **The debunk — D&C 76 (February 1832).** The panel's "biggest debunk" is that Doctrine and Covenants 76 was dictated in **February 1832**, *before* the 1832 First Vision account (written summer 1832), and already describes the persons distinctly: "we beheld the glory of the Son on the right hand of the Father… we saw him even on the right hand of God… he is the only begotten of the Father" (D&C 76:20, 23). So Joseph was demonstrably "capable of describing God and Jesus as appearing separately in a vision" in early 1832 — which "softens, complicates, or possibly kills" the claim that the separate-beings view only emerged mid-1830s.[^4][^5][^6]
- **The "most unlikely apologist."** Every word of the slide Ryland shares comes from **John Dehlin** (Mormon Stories), who, after watching an Informed Saints episode, publicly posted that D&C 76 "neutralize[s] many of the concerns" about the 1832-vs-1838 discrepancy and admitted: "the way I've been telling the story has been incomplete or possibly misleading… where do I have things wrong?" The panel treats this as a hostile witness conceding the point.[^7][^8]
- **Methodological moral.** The hosts generalize: recycled subreddit talking points ("I just know too much about church history") tend to collapse when you "dive in deeper" to the primary sources, journals, and dating — Dehlin's reversal being the case in point. They also note (citing a 2025 survey) that most self-identified Christians cannot correctly define the Trinity, so the very people calling Latter-day Saints non-Christian often describe modalism themselves.[^9][^10]
- **Extended digression (≈ two-thirds of the runtime).** A long, largely ad hominem exchange about Dehlin's motives, repentance, the Apostle Paul analogy, and whether he should delete his channel. One host repeatedly cautions against "assigning causality" and imputing motive — conceding it "makes it very easy for him to just debunk." This portion is commentary, not argument, and is not load-bearing for the godhead question.[^11][^12]
