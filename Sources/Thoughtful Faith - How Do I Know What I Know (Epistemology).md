# Thoughtful Faith — How Do I Know What I Know (Epistemology)

The opening installment of [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1 series, sub-section 1.1, framing the whole course around the question **"How do I know what I know?"** Hansen presents this as the most fundamental question of all because the answer "acts as the lens through which we see the world" — and a broken lens means we "will never clearly see the full nature of reality." [^1] The video is a deliberately non-LDS, general-philosophy primer on **epistemology**, applicable equally to "a Latter-day Saint, a Muslim, a Hindu, atheist, or any sincere person," with the stated prerequisite being a "commitment to want to know the truth." [^2]

- **Epistemology is just the explanation you give for how you know what you claim to know.** Hansen demystifies the term: it is "essentially just a fancy word for the explanation that you give as the basis for how you know what you say that you know." The point of the series is to examine that lens directly. [^1] See [[Epistemology Is the Account You Give for How You Know]].
- **100% rational certainty is essentially impossible — Descartes' lesson.** Recounting [[René Descartes]]'s project of doubting everything, Hansen notes Descartes found that the senses and even reason "are not 100% reliable," and that the only thing one can be 100% certain of is that "you the entity doing the thinking actually exist" — *cogito ergo sum*. He ties this to *The Matrix* and *The Truman Show* as popular illustrations of radical doubt. The upshot: 100% certainty is "not what we are describing when we say that we know something." [^3][^4] See [[100 Percent Rational Certainty Is Essentially Impossible]].
- **"Know" and "believe" mark degrees of confidence, not a certainty threshold.** Saying "I know" or "I believe" is "a personal statement about our level of confidence that something is real." Confidence runs on a spectrum (99.999%, 99%, 75%, 60%…); at some point the *language* shifts from "believe" to "know," but both words simply report subjective confidence about something being real or true. So "don't be afraid to say that you know something even if that thing is not 100% certain on a rational level." [^5][^6] See [[Know and Believe Mark Points on a Confidence Spectrum]].
- **"Proof" means meaningful evidence, not rendering something 100% certain.** Hansen flags "proof" as a "problematic term": appealing to a dictionary, "to prove something is merely to provide meaningful evidence for something," not to render it 100% rationally certain. This dissolves the demand that one cannot "know" without absolute proof. [^7] See [[Proof Means Meaningful Evidence Not Absolute Certainty]].
- **Setup for the next question: what grants legitimate confidence?** Having argued that knowledge is high confidence rather than certainty, Hansen closes by teeing up the sequel: what is it that "grants us confidence that something is real," giving "legitimate 50% confidence that proposition X is true and 99% confidence that proposition Y is true"? That question of *justification* is deferred to the next video. [^8]
