# Does God Have Limited Power? — Craig vs Goff

A moderated dialogue ([[Justin Brierley]] hosting) between [[William Lane Craig]], defending the classical-theist omnipotent God, and [[Philip Goff]], the Durham panpsychist who has moved from atheism to what he calls a "slightly heretical Christianity" built on a **God of limited power**. Goff's thesis is that the problem of evil is decisive against an omnipotent deity (he puts the probability of an all-powerful loving creator at "less than 1%" given the suffering we see), but dissolves if God is *constrained*: God could select the values of the cosmic constants from a singularity but then **cannot intervene thereafter**, so suffering reflects God's limitations, not God's choices.[^1][^2] Craig replies that this is a "small-g god," not God in the maximal sense, and presses five disagreements — Goff's philosophy of mind, his fine-tuning explanation, his theodicy, his reinterpretation of Christianity, and his reading of the New Testament.[^3] The exchange is highly relevant to Latter-day Saint cosmology: Goff is a non-LDS analytic philosopher independently arguing that a God who *cannot do otherwise* is more defensible than the omnipotent God of the creeds — the same move the vault tracks under finite/constrained theism.

## Summary
- **Goff's journey and the limited-God core.** Raised Catholic, atheist by 14, Goff returned via the fine-tuning argument but toward "something godish," not the "omni-god."[^4] The non-negotiable is limitation: "what makes it make intellectual sense to me... is God being limited in power."[^5] He adds the Eastern Orthodox mystical/theosis tradition and a non-standard (visionary) resurrection as the other two "jigsaw pieces."[^6]
- **Philosophy of mind (Craig's #1).** Craig casts Goff's panpsychism as non-reductive physicalism and a "category mistake" (brains have mass, not moods); presses that biology shows consciousness needs complex neural substrate (ants, oysters, bacteria, chairs have none), that causal closure rules out free agency, and lands a part/whole objection — "one person cannot be a part of another person," so the universe cannot be a person of which we are parts.[^7] Goff replies from Russellian monism (physics gives only mathematical structure, not what "breathes fire into the equations"), notes the conscious-part-whole question is a "hotly open debate," and retreats from the cosmic-mind view toward a looser **panentheism** (a Venn overlap of God and world) that he stresses is not the "deal breaker" for his Christianity.[^8] Developed in [[A Person Cannot Be a Part of Another Person (Craig vs Cosmic Panpsychism)]] and (on combination) [[Panpsychism Collapses into Reductionism]].
- **Fine-tuning (Craig's #2).** Craig: limiting God to the constants but not the laws is "ad hoc," and there's a "vicious circularity" — the universe can't *choose* life-permitting values because it must already have them to be a conscious agent; Goff's necessary-but-contingently-physical universe just smuggles back "a necessarily existing non-physical transcendent personal agent" — i.e. the classical God turning himself into matter, which Craig calls metaphysically impossible.[^9]
- **The theodicy clash (Craig's #3).** Goff: the *only* way God could make intelligent life is to fix the physics and let it evolve messily; "this was this or nothing."[^10] Craig: you don't need omnipotence to prevent the Lisbon earthquake or stop Hitler — "if God can dematerialize Jesus' body from the tomb... God could certainly dematerialize Adolf Hitler," so limited theism still leaves gratuitous evil unanswered.[^11] Craig offers his own Molinist/middle-knowledge theodicy plus the moral argument ("evil actually proves the existence of God").[^12] See [[Gratuitous Evil - God Could Have Achieved His Purposes With Less]].
- **Molinism counter-attack (Goff).** Goff grants future contingents can be true but argues counterfactuals of creaturely freedom are "set in stone... explanatorily prior to my choice" and therefore *constrain* freedom — explicitly **not** the grounding objection — citing recent work on explanatory priority (directed acyclic graphs; a Rubio/Climenhaga paper) that he says defeats Craig's 1990s reply.[^13] Developed in [[Counterfactuals of Creaturely Freedom Constrain Freedom Not Ground It (Goff vs Molinism)]]; cf. [[Molinism Does Not Solve Predestination]].
- **Resurrection = ascension (Craig's #4–5).** Goff identifies the resurrection *with* the ascension: Easter morning is the moment Jesus "returns to the father," thereafter experienced in Pauline-style **visions** — a view forced by the limited-God hypothesis (a God who could raise bodies and reveal himself to millions revives the problems of evil and divine hiddenness).[^14] Craig answers with N.T. Wright's *Resurrection of the Son of God*: *anastasis* never meant assumption-into-heaven for pagans or Jews, so the view is "unJewish" and "at odds with the historical evidence," and Goff's incarnation mechanism is "self-referentially incoherent."[^15] See [[God's Limited Power Restricts the Resurrection to a Visionary Ascension (Goff)]].
- **Aftershow.** Brierley and Peter Byron read the debate as a deliberately "left-hemispheric" stress-test (Ian McGilchrist framing), grant Goff is "on a journey... traveling in the right direction" (a C.S. Lewis "semi-Christianity" quote), and note the problem of evil — not panpsychism — is plainly the engine of Goff's whole position.[^16]
