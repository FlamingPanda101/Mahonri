Keystone hosts Latter-day Saint historian [[Britney Chapman Nash]] — author of *Let's Talk About Polygamy* — for a conversation about Joseph Smith's and the early Church's plural marriage, and about what *changed her own mind* and brought her peace on the subject. Rather than re-litigate the hard cases through data, the episode is built around the lived experience and first-person voices of the women and men who actually practiced polygamy. Nash's central testimony: "I was not always at peace with polygamy as I am now. And what made the difference for me was hearing the voices of women and later men who actually practiced polygamy."[^1]

The episode's distinctive contribution to the vault's plural-marriage cluster is *methodological and pastoral* rather than evidentiary. It does not re-argue that plural marriage was [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex|sealing rather than sex]], that [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem|Brigham Young's household was no Oriental harem]], or that rejecting the practice would [[Rejecting Polygamy Unravels Sealing and the Plan of Salvation|unravel the sealing doctrine]] — those are handled elsewhere. Instead it advances a path *to peace*: listen to the practitioners (see [[Listening to Practitioners' Own Voices Dissolves the Need to Defend]]), recognize that [[Their Covenants Are Not Mine to Defend|their covenants are theirs to defend, not yours]], and let [[Understanding Replaces Fear - the Knowledge Cure for the Polygamy Stigma|understanding replace fear]]. Along the way it surfaces two empirical findings from the women's records: [[The Father's Presence Was the Decisive Variable in Polygamous Family Wellbeing|the father's involvement was the decisive variable in family wellbeing]] and [[Sister-Wife Solidarity Was a Lived Support System|sister-wife solidarity functioned as a real support system]].

## Summary

- **What changed her mind (the thesis).** Peace came not from winning arguments but from "hearing the voices" of practitioners; reading their own defenses meant "I no longer felt like I needed to defend a principle I didn't understand. They had already taken on that role."[^1] Her view shifted especially "where men were concerned," after she studied men's experience as the neglected "other half of the story" to polygamy.[^2] See [[Listening to Practitioners' Own Voices Dissolves the Need to Defend]].
- **Personal documents over data.** Private journals carry "raw emotion" and connect you "on a human emotional level" with "real people struggling with something or choosing something difficult in the name of religious faith" — less filtered than public sermons or a historian's interpretation.[^3]
- **Wife counts and status-sealings.** Most polygamous men had only two wives; five-or-more was "a unicorn," concentrated among high-standing leaders. [[Brigham Young]] had ~55 sealings (living/childbearing with maybe 17–19); many were women seeking *connection* to a leader for next-life standing, reflecting an evolving understanding of how sealing worked. Leaders tended to be wealthier and faced ecclesiastical pressure to marry plurally. (This material overlaps the existing [[Brigham Young's Polygamy Was Not a Sensual Harem]] and [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]] and is not separately noted.)[^4]
- **The father as the key variable.** Families ranged from functional to "just chaos"; the father's presence — emotional and economic — most affected wives' and children's experience. Absentee husbands left wives recording that the husband "felt more like a visitor than a spouse." Even Brigham Young "wasn't necessarily as involved as maybe his families would have hoped."[^5] See [[The Father's Presence Was the Decisive Variable in Polygamous Family Wellbeing]].
- **The story that most changed her: [[Martha Cox]].** Cox chose plural marriage against family opposition and community cold-shouldering, wrote honestly of good and bad, and treasured her sister-wife bond above all her sorrows (lost children, deep poverty), tying it to an eternal hope.[^6] Her quote anchors [[Sister-Wife Solidarity Was a Lived Support System]].
- **Sister-wife support.** Co-wives stepped in "in life and in death" — caring for children when a mother died young (keeping them in the same family unit), dividing household labor, funding each other's professional training, and serving as a confidante "equally invested" in the home.[^7] See [[Sister-Wife Solidarity Was a Lived Support System]].
- **Balance against rose-colored history.** Nash's stated priority was a "balanced and relatively normal view," correcting *both* sensationalized-negative *and* "overly good stories that aren't really representative." She affirms it is "okay to be upset and grapple with the negative experiences."[^8]
- **Finding peace (pastoral core).** Naming the tension — "what will get you kicked out of the church today, we teach was commanded by God 150 years ago" — she rejects both shame-driven disowning ("Mormons don't practice polygamy anymore") and the burden of defending the past: "It's not my burden to bear... I don't have to make and keep their sacred covenants, but can I keep mine?"[^9] See [[Their Covenants Are Not Mine to Defend]].
- **The knowledge cure.** Fear of polygamy is fear of the unknown filled with worst-case speculation; "putting a face on" it and getting "the bare facts" dispels the dread. She closes on [[Marie Curie]]: "Nothing in life is to be feared. It is only to be understood."[^10] See [[Understanding Replaces Fear - the Knowledge Cure for the Polygamy Stigma]].
- **Reported effect.** Many readers told Nash, "I've struggled with polygamy for so long, but somehow by the end of your book, I was okay" — which she attributes to "hearing from the people themselves, not having it be filtered through someone else."[^11]
