# Mormonism with the Murph — Josiah's Reforms, Asherah, First Temple and Atoning Messiah

A long-form interview in which the LDS host "Murph" hosts [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]] and [[Mike Day]] to walk through the [[Hezekiah Josiah and the Deuteronomist Reforms|Deuteronomistic reforms]] of [[Josiah]], the suppression of the goddess [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight|Asherah]] / the divine feminine, the worldview of [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology|first-temple theology]], and a pre-exilic **atoning Messiah** — Margaret Barker's territory — arguing throughout that the Book of Mormon preserves the pre-reform theology that the reformers edited out. It largely re-treads the vault's deep reform/Asherah/Barker cluster, but adds several specific Book-of-Mormon onomastic and ritual readings.

## Summary
- **The reform, text vs. reconstruction.** Butler carefully separates what 2 Kings 22–23 says (Josiah finds a "book of the law" — likely some form of Deuteronomy — in Solomon's Temple, then smashes shrines, burns priests' bones, grinds the Asherah to dust in the Kidron) from the near-consensus *scholarly* reconstruction (Weinfeld's Anchor Bible Dictionary calls it "near canonical") that Deuteronomy is Josiah's charter and the Deuteronomistic History (Joshua–Kings) retroactively grades every king against it. See [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]], [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]], [[2 Kings 23.5|2 Kings 23:5]]. [^1] [^2]
- **The Deuteronomists were lawyers and scribes, not priests.** Their pre-eminent reform is **centralization** (Deuteronomy 12: Yahweh puts only his *name* — not his body — at one sanctuary), which simultaneously consolidates political power *and* transforms God into a nameless, bodiless, wifeless deity who is never on earth (Deuteronomy 4:12 — "you saw no similitude, only a voice"), against texts like [[Exodus 24.10|Exodus 24]] where elders see God and eat. [^3] [^4]
- **Lehi as the other side of the argument.** [[Josiah]]'s reforms fall while [[Lehi]] is a young man (~640–620 BC), so [[1 Nephi 1.8|1 Nephi 1]]'s throne-vision of God amid numberless concourses of angels, Lehi building altars outside Jerusalem, and Nephi's claim to "the mysteries" are read as the Book of Mormon deliberately *contradicting* Deuteronomy — with Laman and Lemuel cast as the Deuteronomists ("the Lord maketh no such thing known unto us"). [^5] [^6]
- **Asherah survives in the Book of Mormon.** The archaeology (Kuntillet Ajrud, Khirbet el-Qom inscriptions pairing "Yahweh and his Asherah"; the Lachish ewer; thousands of Judean pillar figurines) shows a feminine divine was worshipped — what Dever calls a "minority report" being overwritten. Following Dan Peterson's "Nephi and his Asherah," Butler reads [[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11]]'s tree/virgin as twin puns on the name Asherah (the fruit "desirable to make one happy" — *ʾshr*, "to make happy"; "blessed art thou" — *ʾashrê*). See [[No Pre-Josiah Text Vilifies Asherah]], [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight]], [[1 Nephi 11.21|1 Nephi 11:21]]. [^7] [^8]
- **A preserved throne-name.** Butler argues "Lord God Almighty" = *Yahweh El Shaddai* occurs four times in the Book of Mormon and four times in Revelation, always at the throne or in judgment — a name acknowledging Father–Mother–Son that was written out of the Old Testament. See the new note [[Yahweh El Shaddai Is a Throne-Name Preserved in the Book of Mormon]]; builds on [[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother]] and [[Genesis 49 Names Three Distinct Gods in Jacobs Blessing]]. [^9] [^10]
- **Alma as "the Virgin."** Mormon puns on Alma the Younger's name (Mosiah 17 — *ʿelem* "young man" / *ʿalam* "concealed"); Butler/Day read Alma as "the Virgin / the concealed one" (cf. *ʿalmah*, [[Mosiah 17.2|Mosiah 17:2]]) who baptizes at a place of water, trees, and "beasts" (the *sadai*/Shaddai of Isaiah 56) "by the authority of Almighty [Shaddai] God." See [[Alma Means Young Man - Wordplay on Hiddenness]], [[Book of Mormon Names Show Authorial Wordplay]]. [^11] [^12]
- **The atoning Messiah was first-temple, not anachronistic.** Quoting Barker ("the Lord was the son of God Most High… present on earth as the Messiah"), Day argues an atoning Messiah was *expected* in first-temple religion, so finding Christ in the Old Testament is what we should expect; the Deuteronomists removed the Day of Atonement (no Yom Kippur in Deuteronomy's calendar) and the royal-cult liturgy. See [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]]. [^13] [^14]
- **The royal liturgy of Psalm 110/22.** Day (drawing on the myth-and-ritual school) reads [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110]] — the most-quoted Hebrew text in the New Testament — as a New Year liturgy in which the Davidic king is ritually slain, descends, and is enthroned ("a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek"; he revocalizes it as "after **my** order, Melchizedek"), with promises of invulnerability echoed in 3 Nephi and D&C 109. [^15] [^16]
- **The two goats and the Ark in John.** Following Barker, Butler reads Leviticus 16's two identical goats (one *la-Yahweh*, one *la-Azazel*) as the atoning Lord vs. the driven-out Satan, mapped onto **Jesus vs. Barabbas** ("son of the father") in John's Passion, with the empty tomb's two angels (John 20) = the Ark of the Covenant where the blood is sprinkled, and Jesus' "I am" (John 18:6) collapsing the crowd as the high priest's pronouncing of the Name. See the new note [[Barabbas and Jesus Are the Two Goats of the Day of Atonement]]. [^17] [^18]
- **The Kemarim as Melchizedek priests.** The *kemarim* ("idolatrous priests") Josiah puts down in [[2 Kings 23.5|2 Kings 23:5]] are read as the Melchizedek priesthood — Barker's argument that they were the Melchizedek priests, plus the Peshitta's calling Melchizedek a *kumra* (Genesis 14) — with a proposed Cumorah = "place of the kemarim" wordplay around the stone box/Nephite ark. See the new note [[The Kemarim Were the Melchizedek Priests Josiah Purged]] and [[The Aaronic-Melchizedek Dualism Maps the Temple's Kings and Priests]]. [^19] [^20]
