# The Stick of Joseph — The Secret Identity of the Wisemen

A Christmas-season conversation in which [[Andrea Woodmansee]] advances a single thesis about the magi of [[Matthew 2.1-12|Matthew 2]]: that the "wise men from the east" were not Persian astrologers paying a courtesy call on a newborn king, but **bearers of preserved first-temple covenant knowledge** who travelled to *anoint* the infant Jesus as the great High Priest — performing an ordinance, not merely offering worship. Framed against President Henry B. Eyring's call to spiritual preparation for the Second Coming, the episode reads the magi as a pattern for how covenant-keepers recognize and receive the Messiah. It is a devotional companion to the vault's temple-theology cluster ([[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]], [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]]) and stands in deliberate contrast to the critical-academic reading in [[Bethlehem Birth Is Likely an Invention to Fulfill Prophecy]].

## Summary

- **The story makes no sense on its face.** Woodmansee opens by defamiliarizing the nativity: why would wealthy men a thousand miles east of Jerusalem "load up and follow a star to go see a baby" they weren't invited to see, hauling expensive gifts on an arduous journey? She urges reading scripture by entering the characters' motives — the magi's intent must explain the effort.[^1]
- **The text gives almost nothing.** [[Matthew 2.1-12|Matthew 2]] never says how many magi there were (three is inferred from three gifts), never explains *why* they are "wise," and only says they came "from the east." The Greek *magoi* means sorcerer/magician/"oriental scientist"; the King James "wise men" is a loose rendering.[^2][^3]
- **A magician is one who knows secrets.** Stripping modern connotations, Woodmansee reframes a *magos* not as a worker of supernatural tricks but as "somebody who knows the secrets." She cites [[Philo of Alexandria]], who described the magi as a priestly class "initiated in divine virtues" — something secret *and* divine, not mere sorcery.[^4]
- **The Book-of-Mormon lens: a preserved temple diaspora.** Rolling back to ~600 BC, she ties the magi to the Babylonian captivity. Babylon deported the upper classes and *temple priests* — the men who knew the **first temple** (Solomon's) and its original "rites, understandings, and prophecies" before it was lost and replaced by the Second Temple. [[The Wise Men Were Bearers of Preserved First-Temple Covenant|These captive priests preserved the ancient temple covenant]] that [[Lehi]] said was being lost in [[1 Nephi]] under Josiah's reforms.[^5][^6]
- **The Reish Galuta.** She names a Babylonian Jewish priestly class, the *Reish Galuta* ("Resh"), who remained in Babylon after the exile and traced their lineage to King David — a concrete diaspora group claiming to still hold the "wisdom of Solomon," i.e. temple knowledge, not mere cleverness.[^7][^8]
- **"From the east" may mean "from ancient times."** The Hebrew behind "from the east" (*qedem*) can also be rendered "from ancient times"; so "magi from the east" doubles as "keepers of secrets from ancient times." And Eden — the temple-prototype — lay "eastward," so the magi approaching from the east are, allegorically, *coming to/from the temple*.[^9][^10]
- **Not astrologers, not fortune-tellers.** Woodmansee resists the Zoroastrian-astrologer reading. The stars are God-given "for signs and seasons" (Genesis 1:14) — a legitimate channel of divine signs — but modern astrology/tarot has "bastardized" this by mistaking one branch for the whole tree, a cautionary pattern for the last days.[^11][^12]
- **A fun Restoration aside: the magi as Nephites?** The host floats the popular speculation that the magi could be Lehite/Nephite figures (even [[Samuel the Lamanite]], who vanishes after prophesying Christ's birth); Woodmansee doubts it but grants Samuel is "in the pattern of the wise men."[^13]
- **The gifts are temple items.** Gold (everything in the temple was gold-plated — candlestick, table of showbread, mercy seat), frankincense (compounded into the incense that "invoked the presence of the Lord"), and myrrh (the holy anointing oil that "imparted holiness," usable *only* in the temple) are all first-temple materials. Tradition holds the anointing oil disappeared from the Holy of Holies ~600 BC. See [[The Gifts of the Magi Were Temple Items for Anointing the High Priest]].[^14][^15]
- **The Cave of Treasures.** The apocryphal *Cave of Treasures* says Adam carried gold, frankincense, and myrrh out of Eden; at his death they were sealed in a cave. Woodmansee ties this to [[Margaret Barker]]'s reading of Eden as the first temple, and to the recurring motif of sacred objects hidden away before a temple's destruction (cf. the brass plates).[^16][^17]
- **The central claim — an anointing, not a visit.** If the magi were temple-covenant bearers who knew what gold, frankincense, and myrrh were *for*, then bringing them to the infant Jesus — "the great High Priest" — means they came to **perform an ordinance**: to anoint him as Messiah. This, Woodmansee argues, is what finally makes the whole strange story cohere, and may mark the first time the text moves from "Jesus" to "the Christ" (the Anointed).[^18][^19]
- **The star was a sign, not a GPS.** [[Matthew 2.1-12|Matthew]] never says the magi *followed* the star to Jerusalem; trade routes made Jerusalem easy to find. The star was the *sign of the coming of the Son of Man*, and later the confirmation they had found the right place. The prooftext is [[Numbers 24.17|Numbers 24:17]] ("there shall come a Star out of Jacob"), which some Aramaic Targums render "a *Messiah* out of Jacob" — equating the star with the Messiah. The prophecy circulated widely (Qumran, Josephus, Justin Martyr).[^20][^21]
- **Christ is the morning star.** Revelation 22:16 and 2:28 identify Jesus himself as "the bright and morning star," so the Bethlehem star was the fulfillment of ancient prophecy announcing the Son of Man's *first* coming.[^22]
- **Signs in the heavens for the Second Coming.** Citing Cameron Coleman's earlier episode, Woodmansee notes the ~24,000-year precession of the equinoxes shifting from the age of Aries (the ram/lamb — Old Testament sacrifice) to Pisces (the fish — New Testament fishermen/gathering) right at Christ's birth, and now the close of the age of Pisces. Luke 21:25 promises "signs in the sun, and in the moon, and in the stars" for the last days; she notes the Salt Lake Temple's astronomical stonework (Big Dipper pointing to the North Star) as a reminder to "watch the heavens."[^23][^24]
- **The eastern-gate tradition.** Jewish tradition holds the Messiah will enter Jerusalem through the (now bricked-up) eastern gate; Ottoman sultan Suleiman walled it and placed a Muslim cemetery before it to ritually bar a Jewish Messiah — both Messiah and magi "come from the east," reinforcing the temple/east motif.[^25]
- **Pastoral payoff — be like the wise men.** The magi were "ready" because they had preserved and stayed true to ancient covenant when the sign came. [[1 Nephi]] 2:16 shows Nephi craving "the mysteries of God" — the temple secrets — and the application is to *hunger for the temple* and heed living prophets (Woodmansee cites President Russell M. Nelson's October 2024 temple-building address: "do you see what is happening right before our eyes?"). Nelson's promise — "every sincere seeker of Jesus Christ will find him in the temple" — is offered as the bow on the thesis: the temple is exactly what the magi were looking for.[^26][^27]
- **Sources named.** Woodmansee credits [[Margaret Barker]]'s *Christmas: The Original Story*, [[Mircea Eliade]] on cross-cultural initiation rites as "fragments of the original temple covenant," and [[Hugh Nibley]]'s *Temple and Cosmos*.[^28]
