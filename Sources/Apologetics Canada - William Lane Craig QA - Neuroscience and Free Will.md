# William Lane Craig Q&A — Neuroscience and Free Will

In this short Q&A clip from Apologetics Canada, philosopher William Lane Craig responds to the claim that psychological and neurological experiments cast doubt on free will. He argues that the famous Benjamin Libet experiments are not convincing as evidence against freedom, and in fact are precisely what a dualist-interactionist would predict, before pressing his argument from intentionality that mental states require a mind distinct from the brain.

## Summary
- The Libet button-press experiments — where a neural "readiness" signal precedes the agent's conscious awareness of choosing — do not disprove free will; given the finite velocity of nerve signals, a delay between the soul's decision and conscious awareness of it is exactly what dualist-interactionism expects.[^1]
- At most such experiments show *correlations* between brain states and mental states, not that mental states are unreal or causally inert; the brain can be an instrument the mind uses without being identical to the mind.[^2]
- The argument from intentionality shows there must be mental states belonging to a self/soul/mind: a "glob of tissue" cannot be *about* or *of* something, so intentionality requires a mind distinct from (though using) the brain.[^3]
