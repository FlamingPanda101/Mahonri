# The Stick of Joseph — The Ancient Temple and Lehi's Tree of Life Vision

Part II of a long-form [[The Stick of Joseph - The Ancient Temple and the Sermon on the Mount Part I|conversation]] on the Stick of Joseph channel with author [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|David Butler]] (joined by [[Mike Day]] and hosts [[Hayden Carroll]] and [[Jackson Paul]]), recorded in the same sitting as the Sermon-on-the-Mount episode. Where Part I read **the Sermon on the Mount** as a three-room temple ordinance, this episode turns to **[[Lehi]]'s and [[Nephi]]'s tree-of-life vision** ([[1 Nephi 8|1 Nephi 8]], [[1 Nephi 11|1 Nephi 11]]) and to **[[Isaiah]] 3–6**, arguing that both are written in temple "code" by initiates who assumed temple-literate readers. The episode's distinctive payload is that the **tree of life is the goddess [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight|Asherah]] / the divine Mother**, drawing on [[Daniel C. Peterson]]'s 1998 article "Nephi and His Asherah," and that Isaiah witnesses and laments her violent removal from the temple. Butler and Day stress throughout that these are hypotheses, "deep in the shadows," not settled doctrine — see [[The Book of Mormon Matches Pre-Reform First Temple Theology]] and [[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship]] for the broader frame.

## Summary
- **Recap and new thesis.** Butler restates Part I's claim — the prophets are "Temple men" who encode endowment imagery for temple-literate readers — and adds the new hypothesis that [[Lehi]], [[Nephi]], and Jesus are all self-conscious followers of [[Isaiah]], which is why Nephi quotes the oldest chapters of Isaiah at length [^1].
- **Lehi's dream maps the three temple rooms by Hebrew name-puns.** The "large and spacious field" wide "as a world" puns *olam* (world) on *ulam* (the porch); the "great and spacious building" above the earth is *heikhal* (the Hebrew word for a great/spacious building, which is also "above" everything); the tree beside which Lehi calls out "with a loud voice" sits in the *debir*, the "place of speech/Oracle." Full argument in [[Lehi's Dream Encodes the Three Temple Rooms by Hebrew Name-Puns]] [^2][^3][^4].
- **The great and spacious building holds corrupt temple insiders, not outsiders.** Their "fine" clothing is, throughout the KJV Old Testament, the language of priestly and temple furnishings; Lehi — a hunted Visionary — is depicting the Jerusalem temple establishment that wants him dead. The original printer's manuscript reads people who "had come **up**" (temple-ascent language). See [[The Great and Spacious Building Is Apostate Temple Insiders Not Outsiders]] [^5][^6].
- **The iron rod is Christ, grasped four times.** Since [[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11:25]] glosses the rod as "the word of God," and John identifies the Word with Christ, the rod is less a banister than a staff of authority / the Melchizedek priest / Jesus — and the text counts people grasping it **four times** before they reach the tree [^7][^8].
- **The tree is the goddess Asherah / the virgin Mary.** Asherah, attested at Ugarit and in the "Yahweh and his Asherah" inscriptions (Kuntillet Ajrud, Khirbet el-Qom) and the Judean pillar figurines, was a tree-goddess. In [[1 Nephi 11|1 Nephi 11]] Nephi asks the meaning of the white, beautiful tree and is shown a white, beautiful virgin bearing a child — the tree *is* a woman who is the tree of life. Per [[Daniel C. Peterson]]'s "Nephi and His Asherah" (1998), this fits a milieu where Nephi knows and does not condemn Asherah symbolism. See [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]] [^9][^10][^11].
- **A "happiness" wordplay names the tree.** The fruit is "desirable to make one **happy**"; Hebrew *ashrei* ("O the happiness of," the Beatitude word) sounds and looks like *Asherah*, and "happiness" is overwhelmingly a Book-of-Mormon word (26× vs. 3× in the Old Testament, 0× in the New). Nephi is plausibly hinting "this is the Happy Tree — the Asherah." Developed inside [[Nephi's Tree Is Asherah the Divine Mother]] [^12].
- **Isaiah 3–6 is a chiasm encoding the violent removal of the Asherah.** Crisis (ch. 3) → response (ch. 4) → crisis (ch. 5) → response (ch. 6). The wicked "draw out" something with ropes that Isaiah calls *etsah* (counsel, punning *ets* "tree" → "the Tree Lady"), using the very words of the men of [[Genesis 19.24|Sodom]] ("bring her out that we may know her," [[Genesis 19.5|Gen 19:5]]) — Isaiah sees the dragging-down of the Asherah as a rape. "Calling evil good" and moving "light for darkness" and "bitter for sweet" describe the reformers; Isaiah 5 even opens with *ashira* ("I will sing") and Isaiah 6:13 ends with the goddess (*elah*/oak) who "shall return." See [[Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah]] [^13][^14][^15][^16].
- **The tree sweetens bitter water — so the river is both "love of God" and "filthy."** [[Exodus 15.23-25|Exodus 15]]: at Marah the Lord shows Moses a tree (*ets*) that turns bitter water sweet. This resolves why Nephi calls the waters "the love of God" ([[1 Nephi 11.18|1 Nephi 11:25]]) yet "the filthiness... the depths of hell" ([[1 Nephi 15|1 Nephi 15:27]]): the same water is sweet in the tree's presence and bitter in its absence. See [[The Tree Sweetens the Bitter Waters So the River Is Both Love and Filth]] [^17][^18].
- **Isaiah was commanded to speak in code, and Jesus quotes him to justify parables.** Isaiah 6:9–10 commissions the prophet to make the people "hear... but understand not"; in [[Matthew 13.10-17|Matthew 13]] Jesus quotes exactly this to explain why he teaches the crowd in parables while the initiated disciples "know the mysteries of the kingdom." This grounds the whole "written in code" reading. See [[Isaiah Was Commanded to Speak in Code and Jesus Quotes Him for Parables]] [^19][^20].
- **The seven-fold woman and a matriarchal order.** [[Isaiah 4.1|Isaiah 4:1]]'s "seven women shall take hold of one man" is read not as polygamy but as the seven-branched menorah-tree-woman (cf. Hannah's "seven sons," Bathsheba "daughter of seven," Mary Magdalene's seven spirits) — hinting at a "daughters of Zion" women's priesthood and a matriarchal order paralleling the patriarchal. Hosts repeatedly mark this as speculative; carried inside [[Isaiah 3 to 6 Encodes the Violent Removal of the Asherah]] [^21][^22].
