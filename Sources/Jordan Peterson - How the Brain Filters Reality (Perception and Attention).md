# Jordan Peterson — How the Brain Filters Reality (Perception and Attention)

A [[Jordan Peterson]] lecture segment curated into the *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1 course, sub-section **1.1 Epistemology**, as a non-LDS, general cognitive-science treatment of a foundational epistemic question: **how reliable is human perception, and how does the brain decide what to perceive at all?** Peterson moves "from general to narrow," starting with the brain's basic predicament and ending with a precise rule for what captures attention. The throughline is that knowledge begins with perception, and perception is radically more limited and more *motivated* than common sense assumes. [^1]

- **Reality is complex beyond comprehension, so the brain's real problem is what to ignore.** Peterson stacks the layers of reality — subatomic, atomic, molecular, organ, the whole person, families, tribes, nations, the cosmos — and notes the brain alone is "so much more complex than everything else in the universe that it's not even in the same category," citing [[Gerald Edelman]]'s estimate that there are more patterns of connection in the brain than subatomic particles in the universe. Because reality is "complex beyond comprehension," the perceiving system must "simplify it down" and "screen all of that out"; the standing question is "what can I ignore?" and "the answer to that is well you need to ignore almost everything." [^1][^2] See [[Reality Is Too Complex to Perceive So the Brain Ignores Almost Everything]].
- **We are far blinder than anyone guessed — the invisible gorilla.** Sensory hardware already limits us to "a very small little slice of the whole electromagnetic spectrum," but the deeper point comes from [[Daniel Simons]]'s "monkey business / invisible gorilla" experiment: when counting basketball passes, about half of first-time viewers miss a gorilla walking through the scene, a player leaving, and the backdrop changing color. The old hypothesis was that anomalous events automatically grab attention; the experiment "just wrong"-footed that — "radical things can happen and you won't notice them unless they interfere with what you're doing." [^3][^4][^5] See [[Inattentional Blindness Shows We Are Blind to the Unattended]].
- **Attention is allocated by a motivated value hierarchy.** Peterson presses *why* viewers obey the instruction to count passes: the instruction "taps into your hierarchy of values" (wanting to be "smart and competent"), plus social compliance built into the lecture-room "implicit story." Once the task becomes motivationally significant, "you focus your very limited attentional resources precisely on what it is that you're supposed to do." Perception is thus goal-relative, not neutral. [^6][^7] See [[Attention Is Allocated by a Motivated Value Hierarchy]].
- **The corrected rule: we orient to disrupted goals, not to novelty.** Mechanistically, only the foveal "tiny little point of focus" carries rich detail; the eye darts ("snap snap snap") and the brain weaves an "amalgamated picture" that feels like a continuous movie, while the periphery mostly flags movement. The upshot is a narrowed law of attention: "it isn't anomaly or novelty that attracts your attention; it's the unexpected disruption of the relationship between your behaviors and the desired outcome of those behaviors" — "only pay attention to things that make you fail," typically with an accompanying emotional response. [^8][^9][^10] See [[We Attend to What Disrupts Our Goal-Directed Behavior Not to Novelty]].
