# James 2:24 — "Not By Faith Alone" (Catholic Rebuttal)

Council of Trent podcast episode dismantling the two standard Protestant
responses to James 2:24 ("a man is justified by works and not by faith alone")
and articulating the Catholic doctrine of ongoing justification.

## Protestant Response #1: "Different Kind of Faith"

**The claim**: James is condemning a false/dead/merely-professed faith, while
Paul speaks of true saving faith. "Can THAT faith save him?" (NIV/ESV
translation choice).

**The rebuttal**:
- James NEVER speaks of faith as bad. Throughout the entire letter, faith is
  always a good thing. The problem isn't the faith — it's faith being ALONE.
- "For as the body apart from the spirit is dead, so faith apart from works is
  dead" (2:26). The spirit is DISTINCT from the body and gives it life; works
  are DISTINCT from faith and give it life.
- Replace "faith" with "dead faith" or "false faith" in any verse in the
  chapter and James's message breaks: "Show me your dead faith apart from your
  works..." — nonsensical.
- The Greek *pistis* doesn't carry a "that/this faith" qualifier — the KJV's
  "Can faith save him?" is more literal than the NIV/ESV.
- Jordan Cooper (Lutheran) correctly notes James never uses "true faith" but
  wrongly maps "faith without works" → "false faith" instead of seeing it as
  faith in an incomplete state.
- Scott McKnight: "No matter how hard we Protestants might try to work this
  out, the bottom line for James is having works."
- Jimmy Akin: "The faith isn't the problem. Its being alone is the problem."

## Protestant Response #2: "Different Kind of Justification"

**The claim**: James means vindication/proof of righteousness before MEN, not
justification before GOD (Calvin). Abraham was "shown to be righteous" to
others.

**The rebuttal**:
- Genesis 22:5 — Abraham and Isaac went AWAY from the servants. No human
  witnesses were present at the sacrifice. Abraham wasn't vindicated before men.
- Protestant apologist Robert Zins: "The word 'shown' is not in the passage...
  it may be stretching things too far."
- Reformed scholar Thomas Schreiner: "There is no evidence that justification
  here relates to justification before people rather than God."
- James uses "save" and "justify" with God as the reference point.
- James 2:22 doesn't say faith was "shown" or "demonstrated" — it says faith
  was "completed" by works.

## The Catholic Alternative: Ongoing Justification

**Initial justification**: By grace alone, received in baptism. No work
(including circumcision or good deeds) can move a person from spiritual death
to life. The Council of Trent: "We are justified freely by faith because none
of those things which precede justification, whether faith or works, merit the
grace itself of justification."

**Ongoing justification**: After baptism, good works increase the righteousness
God has given. Justification is a PROCESS, not a one-time event.

**Jimmy Akin's lamp analogy**:
- Purity (whiteness) = received at baptism; made free from sin
- Intensity (brightness) = increased through good works after baptism

**Council of Trent**: Cites James 2:24 ONLY for the increase/ongoing element of
justification — never for initial justification.

**Key scripture**:
- Romans 6:7 — "He who has died is justified [dedikaiōtai] from sin" — Paul
  uses "justify" for an ongoing state, not a one-time declaration.
- Romans 2:6-13 — God "will render to every man according to his works... the
  doers of the law will be justified."

## The Assurance Inversion
The Catholic view paradoxically provides MORE assurance:
- Protestant: "Good works always flow from true faith" → you're always
  wondering if your faith was "true" in the first place (Alan Parr quote).
- Catholic: Honest examination of conscience — am I in a state of grace? If
  you're not in grave sin, you don't need to question whether your initial
  conversion was genuine.

## Luther and James
- Luther called James "an epistle of straw" (1522 preface), said "I almost feel
  like throwing Jimmy into the stove," and speculated "some Jew wrote it."
- However, he did NOT try to have James removed from the canon — he walked up
  to the line but didn't cross it.
- His frustration came from Catholics using James against sola fide.

## Catholic/Protestant Common Ground
- Pope Benedict XVI: "Luther's phrase 'faith alone' is true, if it is not
  opposed to faith in charity, in love."
- Both agree: initial salvation is gratuitous, unearned.
- Disagreement: whether post-baptismal works increase righteousness (Catholic)
  or merely evidence pre-existing righteousness (Protestant).
- Council of Trent condemned the view that "works are merely the fruits and
  signs of justification obtained."
