# Blake Ostler — The Heart as the Basis of Religious Knowledge

A lecture by Latter-day Saint philosopher [[Blake Ostler]] (introduced with his BYU philosophy/psychobiology degrees, Utah JD, and academic publications) [^1] curated into the [[Thoughtful Faith - Apologetics Course Overview|Thoughtful Faith]] Level 1 (sub-section 1.1, **epistemology**) sequence. Despite the course's framing as general philosophy, this is an explicitly *LDS* spiritual-epistemology talk: Ostler defends **the human heart — in the Hebrew sense of *leb*, uniting mind and emotion — as the basis of religious knowledge and commitment**, and answers the strongest philosophical objections to grounding a life on spiritual experience. His recurring invitation, after the Gospel of John, is "come and see." [^2]

Ostler frames spiritual experience not as *evidence* one can hand to others but as a personal, relational mode of knowing, and addresses three audiences whose presuppositions differ so sharply he "can't even approach the subject in the same way": the LDS, the evangelical (deferred for time), and the naturalistic/agnostic/atheistic. [^3]

## Summary

- **It is a relationship, not an argument; interpersonal (*ginōskō*) knowledge, not propositional.** What Mormonism offers is "a way to enter into interpersonal relationship directly with God," not superior theology, scholarship, or empirical proof. Eternal life is to *know* God ([[John 17.3|John 17:3]]) in the relational sense, and one person's experience is not transferable as *evidence* to another. [^4] See [[Interpersonal Knowledge - Ginosko Not Just Propositions]].
- **The heart is a God-given faculty — an "extra sense" — for knowing God.** Taken as *leb* (cognitive + affective), divinely implanted ([[Deuteronomy 30.6,11-14|Deut 30]]; [[Romans 10.6-9|Rom 10]]), functioning like a personal *liahona* that works only with a soft, open heart. [^5] See [[The Heart Is a God-Given Faculty for Knowing - Extra Sense Argument]].
- **Spiritual experience is the lens, not the picture.** It does not sit *within* the interpretive framework as one datum; it *reorients the whole framework*, so it is logically prior to interpretation — which defeats the "it's all just interpretation" objection (illustrated with perceptual illusions and Wittgenstein's duck/rabbit). [^6] See [[Spiritual Experience Is the Lens Not the Picture]].
- **The brain-in-a-vat problem dissolves under a pragmatic standard of knowledge.** The skeptical argument is valid but demands an "insane" certainty; properly functioning experience yields "pragmatically meaningful knowledge," and experiencing something *as from God* is good reason to believe it is. [^7] See [[Pragmatically Meaningful Knowledge vs Insane Certainty]].
- **Religious pluralism is met with inclusivism, not exclusivism.** Non-LDS spiritual experiences are *expected* and genuine; "every person will receive that degree of light that they are willing to receive." [^8] See [[Religious Inclusivism Answers the Pluralism Challenge]].
- **Against secular materialism: reason presupposes emotion.** Citing [[Antonio Damasio]] and [[Oliver Sacks]], emotion is a necessary substrate of rational decision-making, so dismissing spiritual experience as "mere emotion" is self-undermining; materialism also struggles with qualia and the reliability of reason. [^9] See [[Reason Presupposes Emotion - The Neuroscience Reply to Materialism]].
- **Pastoral caution: not everyone receives the burning.** By [[Doctrine and Covenants 46.11-14|D&C 46]], gifts differ — some know directly, others believe on the words of those who know; the experience can also be doubted or lost. [^10] See [[Spiritual Gifts Differ - Not Everyone Receives the Burning]].

## Personal narrative & illustrations
Ostler grounds the talk in his own conversion at age 14 — reading the Doctrine and Covenants about Brigham Young, feeling "washed... from the inside out," then a burning heart he "knew" he wasn't creating [^11] — and a later prompting in which he relayed a message to a near-stranger that she stop contemplating suicide, which she said saved her life. [^12] He uses perceptual demonstrations (a hidden-lens photo, peripheral-dot/color-fill illusions, the duck/rabbit) to show experience is partly interpretive. [^6] He treats a former bishop's "God spot in my brain" testimony loss as a case study of materialist over-reach. [^9]
