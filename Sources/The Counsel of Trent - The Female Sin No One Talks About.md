# The Counsel of Trent - The Female Sin No One Talks About

[[Trent Horn]] (Catholic Answers, *The Counsel of Trent*) addresses what he calls a "widespread female sin that almost everyone ignores": **pornography use among women** — including the explicit "romance"/"smut" fiction he insists is functionally pornographic. The video euphemizes the term as "corn" to evade YouTube's algorithmic throttling. Horn's aim is pastoral: to bust the myth that only men struggle, to show how the same sin corrupts men and women along *different* lines, and to call the Church to admonish and heal both sexes equally rather than "finger-wag the men and coddle women." He thanks [[Matt Fradd]] (author of *The Porn Myth*) and an anonymous female panel for reviewing the script.

## Summary

- **The problem is not male-only.** A 2018 study found 91% of men and 60% of women consumed explicit material in the prior month; women are ~30% of one major site's audience (40–50% in some countries); explicit fiction sells hundreds of millions of copies via BookTok. See [[The Written Word Is Women's Pornography]]. [00:32]–[02:01]
- **Naming the euphemism.** "Romance novels," "spicy," "adult content" disguise arousal-content; euphemism is "one of the devil's favorite tools." [02:01]–[02:31]
- **Why women's use differs.** Barna data: men's secondary motives skew "fun/boredom," women's "curiosity / aid to intimacy"; female sexuality is more rooted in emotional connection (campus stranger-proposition studies, 1978/1982 and the *Whatever* recreation: ~30% of men vs ~0% of women said yes). The *written* word fits this by making fantasy "intimate, private, prolonged." [02:31]–[05:30]
- **Pornography corrupts distinct gendered desires.** Men's good desire to *pursue and woo* (strength as gift) is corrupted into extracting pleasure and demeaning women; women's good desire to *be pursued* is corrupted into bodice-ripper and Beauty-and-the-Beast fantasies of taming a dangerous man. Draws on John & Stasi Eldredge's *Captivating* and sociologist [[W. Bradford Wilcox]] on marriage "civilizing" men. See [[Pornography Corrupts Distinct Gendered Desires]]. [05:30]–[10:31]
- **Escalation, not a mild substitute.** Per YouTuber Hillary (Second Story), 90–95% of women in hardcore addiction began with erotic fiction/fanfiction; written and video are "functionally no difference"; text can depict what video cannot legally show. [10:31]–[12:00]
- **Moral grounding.** The Catechism condemns pornography for perverting the conjugal act by displaying it "to third parties"; even depictions of married couples, if made to arouse, are evil. Borderline "tasteful" art exists ("I know it when I see it," Potter Stewart), but arousal sought for its own sake outside the marital act is the line. [12:31]–[13:30]
- **Solutions.** Reject stereotypes; remove near occasions of sin (dumb phone, no smartphone by the bed); accountability software (Covenant Eyes); same-sex accountability friendships for women, which the Church under-provides; licensed Catholic therapy for marital/sexual frustration. Books recommended: *Holy Sex* (Greg Popcak), *Delivered* (Matt & Cameron Fradd), the free ebook *New Fruit*, Magdala Ministries. [13:30]–[19:31]
- **Marital frustration is real and must not be ignored.** Cites [[Paul the Apostle]] on conjugal rights ([[1 Corinthians 7.3-5]]) and pre-papal [[Karol Wojtyla (John Paul II)|Karol Wojtyła]]'s *Love and Responsibility* that climax "must be reached in harmony… with both partners fully involved" — yet frustration "never justifies" arousal outside the marital act. [14:30]–[16:01]
- **A note on modesty.** A woman's testimony about self-pleasure can be a stumbling block for men in ways a man's testimony is not, so such testimony belongs in venues geared toward women (with a disclaimer) — but Horn *rejects* the "women shouldn't even say the word" position as burka-logic, praising Leila Miller's chapter in their co-authored *Made This Way*. [19:31]–[21:01]
- **Reframe the message.** Stop saying "men should reject this"; say "people should reject this," uniting the sexes to "carry each other's burdens" ([[Galatians 6.2]]). [21:01]–[21:31]
