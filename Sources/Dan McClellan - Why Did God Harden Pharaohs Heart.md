# Dan McClellan — Why Did God Harden Pharaoh's Heart?

[[Dan McClellan]] reacts to a viral apologetic illustration (the "sun and the wax/clay heart" analogy) that attempts to explain away the repeated biblical statements that God hardened Pharaoh's heart. He argues the apologetic is a fabricated framework imposed on the text to protect the presupposition of human free will and divine non-interference, and that the book of Exodus straightforwardly depicts God overriding Pharaoh's agency to achieve God's own rhetorical goals.

- **The "wax and clay" apologetic is entirely invented.** The clip's argument claims God is like an unchanging sun, and human hearts are vessels filled with either heart-softening "wax" or heart-hardening "clay" by their own possessors — so the *same* divine glory hardens or softens depending on what a person has chosen. McClellan responds that there is "nothing anywhere in the Bible that supports this notion," and its only function is to shift blame from God to Pharaoh.[^1] See [[Wax and Clay Heart Analogy Rebutted]] and [[Hardened Heart Apologetic Shifts Blame to Pharaoh]].
- **Exodus repeatedly and explicitly attributes the hardening to God himself.** Across [[Exodus 4.21|Exodus 4:21]], [[Exodus 7.3-5|Exodus 7:3-5]], [[Exodus 9.12|Exodus 9:12]], [[Exodus 10.1-2|Exodus 10:1-2]], [[Exodus 11.9-10|Exodus 11:9-10]], and [[Exodus 14.4|Exodus 14:4]] / [[Exodus 14.17-18|Exodus 14:17-18]], God says "I will harden his heart" and acts to *prevent* Pharaoh from releasing Israel. This is selective, time-specific divine action, not an automatic byproduct of exposure to glory.[^2] See [[God Explicitly Hardens Pharaohs Heart in Exodus]].
- **The stated purpose is for God to display power — to "show off."** The text repeatedly gives God's own motive: to multiply signs and wonders, to "gain glory for myself," and so that Egypt, Israel, and the world "shall know that I am the Lord." In [[Exodus 9.12|Exodus 9:14-16]] God says he could have ended it but kept Pharaoh alive "to show you my power." McClellan: the hardening exists "so that God would have the opportunity to flex."[^3] See [[Hardening Pharaohs Heart Was to Display Gods Power]].
- **Pharaoh also hardens his own heart — which collapses the analogy.** Because Exodus elsewhere has *Pharaoh* hardening his own heart, the hardening cannot be a mechanical, constant effect of God's glory on a poorly-curated heart; it is something God *selectively* does at specific moments for specific reasons.[^4] See [[Pharaoh Also Hardens His Own Heart]].
- **The hardening exposes the apologetic method and the memorial framing of Exodus.** The "free will" defense is precluded by the text: Exodus 4–14 plainly has God "meddling in human agency." McClellan further notes the narrative is saturated with memorial language ("tell your children and grandchildren") — evidence the story was crafted *post hoc* by later generations as a memorial, and that the Exodus is not historical. The whole apologetic is "putting dogma over data."[^5] See [[Hardened Heart Refutes the Free Will Defense]], [[Exodus as Post Hoc Memorial Narrative]], and [[Putting Dogma Over Data]].
