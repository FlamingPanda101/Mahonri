# Discover the Book of Mormon — Did Horses Exist in Pre-Columbian America?

A conversation between host [[Kirk Magleby]] and guest [[Daniel Johnson]] (Atlanta-area LDS researcher) on the **horse anachronism** — the most-cited objection to the [[Book of Mormon]], that "everyone knows... there were no horses in the Americas before the Spanish arrived," so a horse in a text published in 1830 must be a 19th-century blunder.[^1] The episode assembles four lines of reply: pre-Columbian horse remains from Yucatán caves and cenotes; the methodological complaint that finds are dismissed by circular dating; Native American testimony and the recent *Science* reappraisal; and the broader paleontology of late Holocene horse survival.

## Summary

Johnson opens by conceding the mainstream picture as the critic frames it — Mesoamerica had "no beasts of burden, no draft animals" — then qualifies it with Maya iconography (Kerr vase #196, a deer bearing a creation-stone "as a draft animal," implying the *concept* of loading a large animal) and the wheeled-effigy "toys" from Tabasco, Veracruz, and elsewhere that show the wheel was known even if not used for transport.[^2] He argues the discipline reasons in a circle on horses: petroglyphs that look like horses are ruled "post-contact" *because* they look like horses, and researchers have written in print that "if it looks like a horse then it must be post contact... and if it's pre-contact it can't be a horse."[^3] See [[If It Looks Like a Horse It Must Be Post-Contact - Circular Dating of New World Horses]].

A second thread is the **documented horse remains** that, Johnson says, "no one really seems to be talking about." Edward Mercer's *The Hill Caves of Yucatan* (excavations from the late 1800s) recovered horse teeth and bones that Mercer, assuming the consensus, labeled modern *Equus caballus*; later paleontologists reidentified them as extinct species (*Equus conversidens*, *E. mexicanus*). The Loltún Cave system and a cenote at the late-Classic Maya capital Mayapán (occupied c. AD 1020–1440) yielded horse remains, the Mayapán find published in the *Journal of Mammalogy* in 1957 (Carnegie material) and read as "pre-Maya."[^4] He then points to the central-Mexico stratigraphy near San Luis Potosí — the [[Wade Miller]] / Rosario Gómez *Texas Journal of Science* study (2022), six years and several expeditions of work — without re-arguing its details here. See [[Horses Are Trending Not Settled as an Anachronism]].

A third thread is **testimony and reappraisal**. Johnson stresses that at every European point of contact (Blackfoot 1754, Ute c. 1600–1700, Missouri/Arikara 1682, Tejas/Sonora 1567) explorers found nations *already* possessing full horse cultures — breeding stock, horsemanship, trade — and argues the Spanish expeditions (stallion-heavy, e.g. "550 stallions, two mares," horses dying at sea, eaten, or killed in battle) were poorly suited to seed those populations.[^5] He cites Native American origin myths ("we've never not had the horse"), Yvette Running Horse Collin's University of Alaska Fairbanks dissertation deconstructing a "Eurocentric mythology," and the 31 March 2023 cover of *Science* reporting horses among Plains tribes "at least a century" earlier than historians had supposed (c. 1600), opening the door to still earlier dates. See [[Native American Testimony That Horses Were Always Here]].

Magleby supplies the framing thesis: horses are "anomalous" only because scholars defend an "established canon" — that megafauna "did not survive the ice age." He marshals Alaskan permafrost DNA for late-Holocene survival of horses (and mammoth), the genetically anomalous Bashkir/North American Curly horse, and a Copernicus analogy for how slowly consensus turns.[^6] He closes by naming the "obvious logical fallacies" he sees in the slam-dunk-fraud inference: appeal to tradition, bandwagon, begging the question, circular reasoning, and motivated reasoning.[^7] See [[Late Pleistocene-Holocene Survival of New World Horses]] and [[Only Two Percent of the Americas Has Been Excavated]]. The episode's repeated refrain — "if you're not looking for something, you'll never find it" — doubles as its argument and its caveat: much of the cited material is indirectly dated, single-study, or testimonial, and the hosts themselves call it "trending," not closed.
