# The Stick of Joseph — From Jew to Latter-day Saint: Avraham Gileadi's Conversion Story

[[Avraham Gileadi]]'s first in-person on-camera interview, in which the Isaiah scholar narrates an improbable spiritual journey — Dutch wartime Catholicism, a New Zealand rock band, an Israeli kibbutz, formal conversion to Judaism, and baptism into the [[Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints]] in the Pool of Siloam — before previewing the Isaiah scholarship (literary-structural "apocalyptic Isaiah," the Hebraisms of the Book of Mormon) that the hosts plan to explore in follow-up episodes. The hosts repeatedly liken his life to "an LDS version of Forrest Gump."

## Summary

- **Wartime Catholic childhood (Holland).** Born 1940 in Maastricht; earliest memories are of WWII — planes crashing, his street bombed, a priest-uncle killed by a shell while ministering to the dying. His father, a Dutch-army captain, joined the underground and helped New Zealand pilots escape, leading the family to emigrate to New Zealand when Gileadi was ~10. As an [[altar boy]] (ages 7–10) taught English by monks, he developed an early "deep testimony of Jesus… as if I knew him all along."[^1][^2]
- **Seminary, rock band, and the turn toward Israel.** He briefly entered a Catholic seminary to become a priest but left after six months — "too much ritual and not enough holy spirit." He led a popular rock-and-jazz group (the Spotlights) but gave it up after reading in the Apocryphal Books of Adam and Eve how music "ravished the soul," seeing the parallel on his own dance floor. Convinced the Jewish return to Israel was fulfilling prophecy, he earned passage and emigrated at 27.[^3][^4]
- **The kibbutz and the Book of Mormon.** Pointing blindly at a biblical map, he chose the Jezreel Valley — which turned out to be the one kibbutz in Israel for Jewish immigrants from New Zealand, Australia, and South Africa. There a Jewish librarian (a South-African immigrant) pressed a brown Book of Mormon on him — "this book is for you, you must read this." Reading it, he "felt filled with light," though puzzled by details like Zion on the American continent. The book had been left by an anonymous American Mormon volunteer "in case somebody might pick it up."[^5][^6][^7]
- **Conversion to Judaism (the schoolmaster).** Prompted that he "can have the Holy Ghost before being baptized" but must first "learn Judaism, the law of Moses, [the] schoolmaster for the gospel," he studied with a rabbi, applied to convert ("they don't want converts"), and after a year and a half was accepted before a court of three rabbis — mikvah, symbolic circumcision, Israeli citizenship (which he still holds). He went "fully into Judaism" while still privately believing in Jesus, kept a [[Nazarite vow]] (long hair and beard, "like Moses"), and even appeared on a national feast-day poster. He insists he was "never asked sensitive questions" about Jesus — "it's the Lord's doing."[^8][^9][^10]
- **The rabbi's Isaiah tradition.** At a Jerusalem yeshiva he was "entranced with Isaiah" under an American rabbi-professor. The rabbi taught the key tradition: "the book of Isaiah applies to Isaiah's day and to the end time simultaneously" — "we have no proof, but it's a tradition among us." Gileadi later "found the proof" in literary structures. See [[Isaiah Is Apocalyptic and Applies Simultaneously to Isaiahs Day and the End Time]].[^11][^12]
- **Baptism in the Pool of Siloam.** Reading a Jerusalem Post ad, he wrote to [[David Galbraith]], head of the pre-Jerusalem-Center "Israel group," and walked five miles each way to Bethany for eight weeks of questions. Recognizing in Galbraith "a man full of the spirit of the Lord" — "unlike all the people I'd been with… who did not have that spirit" — he was baptized in the Pool of Siloam, "the first [Jewish citizen] that I know of" before Israel barred Jewish baptisms. President Harold B. Lee, then visiting with Elder [[Gordon B. Hinckley]], let him keep his Nazarite vow: "he'll grow as he grows in the gospel."[^13][^14][^15]
- **The temple felt like coming home.** Coming to the LDS temple, neither his Catholic nor his Jewish background found anything "foreign" in the endowment or even the older symbolic penalties (which he defends as the necessary "curses" half of the covenant). "Learning the gospel is like coming home… the Catholic life and the Jewish life had graduated toward the next step, the fullness of the gospel." He testifies "anybody who… truly follows God… will eventually end up a member of the church."[^16][^17]
- **Translating the Book of Mormon into Hebrew.** Commissioned to revise the Church's Hebrew Book of Mormon (after a draft by Hebrew-University adjunct Jonathan Shunary), he found the book "very sophisticated": dozens of chiasms — including one spanning the entire book of Mosiah and culminating in [[Isaiah 53]] (the Ancient America Foundation / [[Kirk Magleby]]), and two classic chiasms in 3 Nephi 20–21 on the house of Israel and the Gentiles. The project was abandoned when Church budget shifted to newly opened Eastern Europe. See [[Latter-day Saints Are the Ephraimite Gentiles in the Book of Mormon]].[^18][^19]
- **Hebraisms and the King James question.** He affirms the Hebraisms are "absolutely… real" — e.g., the five-word "and it came to pass" is one prefixed Hebrew word (a *wayyiqtol* connector). On translation, he notes the Book of Mormon "borrows the King James translation of Isaiah," and that Joseph Smith's occasional added phrases — which Gileadi "knew from the internal context of Isaiah" were not original — were attempts "to make sense of the… King James version" for an audience that already used it. See [[Joseph Smith Added Phrases to Isaiah to Clarify the King James English]].[^20][^21]
- **Covenant theology as vassal treaty.** He frames the Book of Mormon's covenant theology on the ancient Near Eastern emperor–vassal treaty structure (lord/servant, lord of hosts rallying his armies for a vassal under mortal threat), noting the Lamanites adopted vassalage as a *political* claim of seniority over the Nephites but not as a spiritual system — connecting to the vault's [[The Covenant Idea Is Borrowed From Assyrian Vassalage]] and [[Deuteronomy Was Patterned After the Vassal Treaty of Esarhaddon]].[^22][^23]
- **The apocalyptic Isaiah project.** He spent a year translating Isaiah in a basement (12 modern translations, lexicons, concordances, every word but prepositions checked), then a decade on a synchronous literary structure [[William Brownlee]] discovered in the Dead Sea Isaiah scroll, producing *The Literary Message of Isaiah*. He cites [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus]]'s words in 3 Nephi 23 ("all things that he spake hath been and shall be") and Ecclesiastes 1:9 as confirming the Hebrew "typological," not logical, mindset.[^24][^25]
