# Dan McClellan — The Bible Says We Will Become Gods

[[Dan McClellan]], a credentialed Hebrew Bible scholar working from [[Critical Scholarship|academic-biblical-studies]], does a stitch-in commentary on the [[Allie Beth Stuckey - Are Mormons Christians - Jacob Hansen Debate|Allie Beth Stuckey vs. Jacob Hansen debate]] over whether the Bible teaches that humans become gods. His thesis is that **deification is genuinely biblical — more radically so in the first century than in the later creedal tradition — but that both debaters argue past it**, each subordinating the ancient data to a modern dogma. He is therefore a **foil to both sides at once**: he faults the evangelical ([[Allie Beth Stuckey]]) for [[Putting Dogma Over Data|putting dogma over data]] on monotheism, and faults the Latter-day Saint ([[Jacob Hansen]]) for arguing the way "Mormons talk to each other" instead of engaging the text — while himself building a strong scholarly case that the biblical and early-Jewish/Christian worlds affirmed humans becoming gods.

Most of the individual exegetical moves here are already documented in the vault's theosis / divine-council cluster from McClellan's sibling videos and from LDS sources (see the wikilinks below); the **distinctive contributions of this video** are the four-constellation method, the diluted-patristics warning, the John-10-not-Revelation-3 coaching, and the Litwa case that created/non-creating gods were ordinary ancient theology.

## Summary

- **Four constellations, not two.** McClellan insists the debate conflates four distinct, internally varied "constellations of belief" — Mormon, evangelical, biblical, and early-Jewish/Christian — and that the two living constellations are arguing with each other while neither represents the ancient ones.[^1] This is the deification-specific form of his [[Putting Dogma Over Data|dogma-over-data]] critique. New note: [[The Four Constellations of Deification Belief]].
- **Patristic theosis is watered down — don't proof-text it.** The deification of Athanasius and Cyril is "heavily qualified… passive participation," "divested of a lot of the content" of the 1st–2nd century; per his own 2007 paper, patristic, biblical, and Mormon deification are "three very distinct ideals," so citing the fathers answers neither the evangelical's question nor the LDS case.[^2] New note: [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]] (a foil to the affirmative [[Christ Became Man That We Might Become God - The Exchange Formula]]).
- **Lead with John 10, not Revelation 3 or the fathers.** Revelation 3:21 and the acorn/oak analogy "[are] not the passage you should lead with"; the defensible biblical proof text is [[John 10.34-36|John 10:34-36]], and Hansen's failure to use it was "probably the biggest shortcoming in his argument."[^3] New note: [[LDS Should Lead With John 10 Not Revelation 3 or Patristics]].
- **Created and non-creating gods were staple ancient theology.** Quoting [[David Litwa]], divinity in the first century meant immortality and power, *not* the power to create; "most gods in the ancient Mediterranean basin were not considered to be creators," and "created or generate gods" were "not… unusual before the 4th century CE."[^4] New note: [[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)]].
- **John 10:34-36 carries a divinization soteriology.** Reading Psalm 82 not in the Hebrew but in Jesus' mouth, McClellan (with [[Andrew Byers]] and [[Hugo Mendez]]) argues John makes the human/divine boundary "porous": "oneness is deification as well as divine association."[^5] Already covered: [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology]], [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]], [[Image Christology in the Gospel of John]].
- **Psalm 82 in the Hebrew is a divine-council scene — don't appeal to it directly.** It "is a divine council court scene where the patron deities of the nations are being condemned to mortality… not about humans becoming gods."[^6] Already covered: [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]; entities [[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]], [[Psalm 82.1|Psalm 82:1]].
- **John 10:33 — "a god," not "the God of Israel."** The anarthrous *theos* parallels the anarthrous *anthrōpos*: "you are a human… making yourself a god or divine," and first-century blasphemy was broad enough to cover that.[^7] Already covered: [[John 10.33|John 10:33]].
- **"God is one" is not monotheism.** *Adonai echad* (the Shema) asks how many *Adonais* there are, not how many gods; the parallel is Song of Songs 6:9 ("my perfect one is one") — exclusivity of relationship, not of existence.[^8] Already covered: [[The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence]]; entities [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deuteronomy 6:4]], [[Song of Songs 6.8-9|Song of Songs 6:8-9]].
- **Of five "monotheistic" slogans, only three are in the Hebrew Bible — and they assert incomparability, not non-existence.** "No other gods exist" is "never stated anywhere in the Bible"; [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43:10]] ("no god *formed*") is anti-idol polemic; "none like me" is ANE incomparability rhetoric.[^9] Already covered: [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]], [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]].
- **Early Judaism and Christianity affirmed a "second God."** Philo reads [[Genesis 31.13|Genesis 31:13]] / the Logos as a "second God"; Justin Martyr calls the Logos "another God" and reads Psalm 82 as humans becoming gods; Origen distinguishes *ho theos* (the God) from *theos* (gods by participation).[^10] Already covered: [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]], [[The Messenger of Yahweh Explicitly Identifies as God]], people [[Philo of Alexandria]], [[Justin Martyr]], [[Origen]], [[Arius]].

## LDS Relevance

The video is a sharp illustration of a Tier-B, non-confessional scholar who is **both a corrective and a resource** for Latter-day Saint apologetics. *Against* the LDS side: it forbids the lazy moves of citing 4th-century fathers or "talking like Mormons talk to each other" as if these settled the biblical question (see [[Patristic Theosis Was Watered Down From the Biblical Version]], [[LDS Should Lead With John 10 Not Revelation 3 or Patristics]]) — the same [[Putting Dogma Over Data|dogma-over-data]] error the apologist rightly throws at critics. *For* the LDS side: McClellan's positive case is that the biblical and early-Jewish/Christian worlds affirmed a graded divine plurality into which humans could be elevated, that "[[Created and Non-Creating Gods Were Staple Pre-Nicene Theology (Litwa)|created gods]]" were ordinary theology, and that John carries a real divinization soteriology — results that sit closer to the [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead]] and [[Human Deification in Mormonism|LDS deification]] than to strict creedal monotheism. The honest fault line remains the [[Same-Species-as-God as the Core LDS Distinctive in Theosis|same-species ontology]]: McClellan describes ancient *categories* of divinity, not the King Follett cosmology, and reads John 10 as Johannine divinization rather than as exaltation to the Father's own status. Net: the apologetic discipline this video teaches is to **argue the first-century data, not the modern dogma** — see [[Start From the Critical Reading Then Negotiate]] and [[Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain]].
