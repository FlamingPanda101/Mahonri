# Thoughtful Faith — Sola Scriptura Debate

A formal moderated debate hosted by [[Jacob Hansen]] on his **Thoughtful Faith** channel, on the thesis: *"The Bible, meaning the Protestant 66-book canon, is the ultimate rule of faith to which all other sources of faith are to be tested or examined by to derive doctrinal truth."* [[Kelly (Brain Perspective)|Kelly Powers]] (evangelical, *Berean Perspective*) argues the **affirmative**; [[Robert Boland]] (Latter-day Saint, *Scriptural Mormonism*) argues the **negative**. The format runs 20-minute openings, 10-minute rebuttals, 20-minute cross-examinations each, and 5-minute closings.

The debate is unusually **asymmetric in shape**. Powers spends much of his case on *Mormonism* — the Joseph Smith Translation, the priesthood/lineage problem, the pre-1978 priesthood ban, praying about the Book of Mormon, Muhammad and other claimed prophets — arguing that without scripture as the standard, one cannot test any prophet. Boland refuses the frame entirely: he **concedes Mormonism is false for the sake of argument** and presses a tight *internal critique*, insisting the burden is wholly on Powers to demonstrate the **formal sufficiency** of the 66 books from the biblical text itself. Most of Boland's arguments are versions of material already heavily mapped in the vault's sola-scriptura cluster; this note captures only the genuinely **new, distinctive moves**.

## Summary
- **The burden / formal vs material sufficiency.** Boland's organizing move: conceding Mormonism, Catholicism, and Orthodoxy could all be false does not make Protestantism the *default*; Powers must positively prove that the 66-book canon is *formally sufficient* and the *sole* infallible rule, not merely useful. (Generic versions: [[Material vs Formal Sufficiency - The Home Depot Analogy]]; [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]].)
- **2 Timothy 3:16 — "profitable," not "sufficient."** Boland's lexical deep-dive on ὠφέλιμος (*ōphelimos*) as a weak/"fractional" quality-word (BDAG, EDNT), distinct from the vault's existing *theopneustos* note. → [[Profitable Is a Fractional Word Not a Claim of Sufficiency]]; cf. [[Theopneustos Means Life-Giving Not Infallible]].
- **Hezekiah and Josiah's liturgical reforms.** 2 Chronicles 29:25 and 35:4 show kings binding worship by a command "of the LORD by his prophets" (David, Gad, Nathan, dead ~250 years) that is *nowhere in the Bible* — a binding "word of God" outside scripture. → [[Hezekiah and Josiah Bound a Word of God Never in the Bible]].
- **James's use of Amos 9 at the Jerusalem Council.** Acts 15 rests James's ruling on the *Septuagint* form of Amos 9:11-12 (which diverges from the MT and is absent at Qumran), filtered through Peter's *modern revelation* — not scripture alone. → [[James Read Amos 9 Through the Septuagint at the Jerusalem Council]].
- **The canon-as-article-of-faith dilemma.** Using Jude's citation of 1 Enoch as inspired prophecy and the now-missing first Corinthian epistle (1 Cor 5:9), Boland forks the Protestant: either the canon is *not* an article of faith (so you cannot bind Jude), or it *is* (but then a teaching binds that is absent from the text) — either way *tota scriptura* fails. → [[The Canon-as-Article-of-Faith Dilemma]]; cf. [[Book of Enoch - Quoted as Scripture in Jude]].
- **Conscience as the real ultimate authority.** Citing Charles Hodge, Boland argues the Protestant's *individual conscience* (judging what is "biblical") plays the ultimate normative role — so the rule of faith is really the private interpreter, not the text. → [[Conscience Is the Protestants True Ultimate Authority]].
- **Powers' affirmative** is the standard evidential case: scripture self-attests and was treated as authoritative by Jesus, the apostles, and the early fathers; the Bereans ([[Acts 17.10-11|Acts 17]]) tested even an apostle by scripture; Galatians 1 lets us test "an angel from heaven"; the burden is on anyone claiming *additional* scripture. (Mapped at [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]], [[Scripture as the Test of Prophets and Revelation]].)
- **The Melchizedek-priesthood / "ἀπαράβατος" exchange.** A side-thread: Powers argues the Bible teaches the Melchizedek priesthood for no one but Melchizedek (Gen 14) and Jesus (Heb 7, "untransferable"); Boland replies the lexicon (BAG/TDNT) gives ἀπαράβατος the sense "permanent/unchangeable," not "without successor," so Hebrews does not bar participation in Christ's priesthood.
