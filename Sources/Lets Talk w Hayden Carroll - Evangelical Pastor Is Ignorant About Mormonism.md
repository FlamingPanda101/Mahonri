# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Evangelical Pastor Is Ignorant About Mormonism

[[Hayden Carroll]]'s point-by-point rebuttal of clips in which [[Mark Spence]] of [[Ray Comfort]]'s **Living Waters** ministry interviews a self-described new member of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Carroll's thesis is that Spence both **misrepresents** what Latter-day Saints actually believe (especially on grace and salvation) and **abuses scripture** (sola scriptura, Deuteronomy 18), while manipulating an under-catechized convert — whom Carroll repeatedly suspects of being a "paid actor." Nearly every argument here is already developed elsewhere in the vault; this note wikilinks the existing cluster and adds one genuinely new concept on Deuteronomy 18.

## Summary
- **The "penguin" / "believe it enough" caricature.** The interviewed convert says God will let you become whatever you sufficiently want (a penguin). Carroll: the Church teaches no such thing — "you'll get what you want in the next life" has obvious limits and excludes reincarnation/becoming an animal — so the clip paints Latter-day Saints as absurd via a misinformed new member.[^1]
- **Salvation "after all we can do" is NOT works-righteousness.** Spence quotes [[2 Nephi 25.23|2 Nephi 25:23]] to charge LDS works-salvation. Carroll rebuts at length with grace-saturated Book of Mormon texts ([[2 Nephi 2.8|2 Nephi 2:8]], Alma 22:14, Helaman 14:13, 2 Nephi 10:24, 31:19) and prophetic statements from Oaks, Ballard, McConkie, and Uchtdorf that salvation "cannot be earned." See [[LDS Salvation Is by Grace Not Earned by Works]]; works are a *manifestation* of faith, [[Works Are the Fruit of Faith Not the Price of Salvation|not its price]].[^2]
- **The "despite all we can do" reading.** Carroll plays a clip of Dan McClellan (the transcript's "Dan Mullen") summarizing his 2020 *Journal of Book of Mormon Studies* paper: across 18th–19th-century usage, "after all X can do" means "**despite** all X can do," so 2 Nephi 25:23 teaches grace, not residual works. Fully covered by [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]].[^3]
- **The prooftext turnabout.** Carroll argues critics could just as dishonestly weaponize [[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7:21]], Hebrews 5:9, and [[James 2.24|James 2:24]] to "prove" Protestants believe in works-salvation; the fix is context, applied evenly. See [[The Prooftext Double Standard on Grace and Works]].[^4]
- **Sola scriptura / the 66-book canon.** Spence calls the Bible the "sole infallible rule of faith and practice." Carroll's standing challenge: no prophet ever said the present 66 books are that authority; the idea traces to the creeds (which the First Vision calls an abomination), and a fallible author cannot produce an infallible text. See [[The Three Unjustified Protestant Canon Presuppositions]], [[Sola Scriptura - Scripture Self-Attests Its Authority]], and [[A Text Cannot Be Infallible Because Language Is Fallible]].[^5]
- **"Should you pray about adultery/murder?"** Spence equates praying about the Book of Mormon with praying about whether to sin. Carroll: a category error — Latter-day Saints do not hold the Bible as final authority, and moral absolutes are not parallel to seeking confirmation of scripture; he also flags a suspicious video cut. On the "burning in the bosom" charge, Carroll denies LDS rely on "feelings alone," weighing evidence like anyone else. See [[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]] and [[Burning in the Bosom vs Self-Evidencing Light]].[^6]
- **Modern prophets / apostles after the Resurrection.** Against the claim that God no longer calls prophets, Carroll notes Paul was called an apostle after the Resurrection, so "Christ was the final revelation" fails. See [[Paul the Apostle]] and the canon-closure critiques in [[Closing the Canon Was Used to Declare Prophecy Ended]].[^7]
- **Deuteronomy 18 misused against Joseph Smith.** Spence invokes [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:22]] as a mechanical false-prophet test. Carroll triages Joseph Smith's prophecies into conditional / already-fulfilled / not-yet-fulfilled, then argues Deut 18 condemns the *presumptuous* prophet, not one with an unfulfilled prediction — Jonah ([[Jonah 3.4|Jonah 3:4]]) being the disproof. **New concept:** [[Deuteronomy 18 Condemns the Presumptuous Prophet Not the Mistaken One]].[^8]
