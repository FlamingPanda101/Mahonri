# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Evangelical Christian Admits Mormons Are Christian

LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] sets up a table at Arizona State University and converses with **Titha**, a non-denominational Evangelical ("I just like to read the Bible") who grew up Baptist and attended Assemblies of God. The conversation runs the familiar gauntlet — the Trinity-makes-God-evil argument, scriptural subordination texts, and the [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God|Hellenization charge]] — but its distinctive payoff is the **title concession**: after Carroll asks Titha to *define* "Christian," she lands on a minimal experiential-and-relational test, and once Carroll pins "God" to the Creator of [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]] she says outright, "you're a Christian. Mormons are Christian." [^7] The encounter is a clean case study in the **definitional handshake** tactic — secure agreement on a low, scripture-only definition of "Christian" first, then ask whether the Latter-day Saint meets it — and in the **heart-posture-over-doctrine** soteriology Titha uses to justify the concession (Solomon worshipping "on the high places" yet still met by God). [^5]

The intro also previews Carroll's recurring venue: he plans to set up a table at the Mesa Temple Lights and the Easter pageant to engage the Evangelicals who come to evangelize Latter-day Saints, on "the implications of the Trinity" and "the idea that Mormons are Christians." [^1]

## Summary

- **Setup and intro (00:01–02:01).** Carroll thanks [[Jacob Hansen]] for featuring the channel on *Thoughtful Faith*, announces a Google Drive mirror for missionaries who cannot access YouTube, and floats a future table at the Mesa Temple Lights / Easter pageant to debate the Trinity and whether Mormons are Christians. [^1]
- **Defining terms (02:30–03:30).** Titha agrees the word "Trinity" never appears in the Bible but affirms God is three persons. Carroll restates the traditional definition (one being, three persons, *homoousios* / [[Consubstantiality|consubstantial]]); Titha agrees that is the claim, and Carroll isolates *consubstantiality* as the part he rejects — tracking [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]].
- **Carroll's four arguments (03:30–05:30).** He previews four lines against the Trinity: (1) it makes God evil; (2) New-Testament contradictions; (3) the [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God|Hellenization]] / perfect-being-theology history; (4) the logical problem of one being as three persons. [^2]
- **Subordination texts (06:00–07:31).** Carroll lists the Father as "the only true God," "greater than I," "none is good but God," the Son's limited knowledge, and the divergent will in Gethsemane ([[Luke 22.42|Luke 22:42]]), which he labels *subordination*, "a common understanding... in the early church" put away "at the council of Nicaea in 325."
- **The God-is-evil argument (07:31–18:31).** Via the torture-a-baby hypothetical, Carroll presses that a God who creates people *ex nihilo* foreknowing they will go to hell cannot be good — the [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity|constrained-God reply]]. Titha resists with [[Divine Command Theory Is Metaethics Not Ethics|divine-command]] moves ("who are we to put our morals on God?", the cookie-baker / flood analogy) and the free-will defense (sin, not God, causes suffering). [^3]
- **The LDS solve (18:31–21:00).** Carroll offers pre-existence, co-eternal intelligences, an embodied God, and a God [[God Is Constrained by Eternal Law Not Unconstrained|bound by eternal law]] (unwritten, "inherent in reality," like gravity) as the reason God cannot just "snap his fingers" — and notes God "can cease to be God if he breaks moral law." [^4]
- **Heart over doctrine (21:00–24:30).** Titha pivots to epistemic humility about hell and a heart-posture soteriology: she recounts a sermon on Solomon worshipping "on the high places" yet still met by God — "his actions were out of line, but his heart was for God." [^5]
- **The Protestant adjudication problem (21:30–24:01).** Carroll raises who adjudicates the "correct interpretation of scripture" among non-creedal Protestants (Presbyterian vs. Baptist on infant baptism; "where's the list of fundamentals?") — the [[No Final Interpreter - The Protestant Adjudication Problem|no-final-interpreter]] line.
- **The definitional handshake and concession (24:30–26:30).** Titha's working definition of Christian: agreement "that Jesus is the son of God, that he died on the cross for our sins. To me, the other stuff doesn't really matter." [^6] She affirms one need not be a trinitarian to be a Christian, then — after Carroll asks what she means by "God" and she answers "the all-knowing being who created Genesis... 1:1," to which Carroll assents — she concludes: "then... you're a Christian. **Mormons are Christian.**" [^7]
- **"I'm not the judge" (26:30–27:31).** She immediately softens it — her father studied and rejected Mormonism as self-contradictory, and "I'm not the judge... God knows" — landing on a sincere-effort standard that Carroll matches: people "doing their best," "not fighting against what they know is truth," "are going to be okay." [^8]
