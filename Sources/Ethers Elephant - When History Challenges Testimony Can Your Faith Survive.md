A long-form [[Ether's Elephant]] livestream (host [[Cardon Ellis]] with lay apologist [[Luke Hansen]]) whose central segment — "Trunk Treasures: What if Joseph Smith were a murderer?" — asks how a faithful Latter-day Saint should hold **difficult Church history** against a **personal testimony**. The episode is deliberately exploratory: Ellis spends much of it "getting into the shoes" of a member who has found something in the historical record that genuinely bothers them (Fanny Alger, Brigham Young and slavery, the David Whitmer "leave the Church" revelation, endowment and garment changes), and asks where the line really is between *imperfect-but-still-a-prophet* and *disqualifying*.

The discussion frames the problem as an **epistemology** problem, not a data problem. Three distinct arguments do the work: (1) a **gold-pill parable** illustrating C.S. Lewis's *bulverism* — rejecting a cure because the man who gave it may be a murderer; (2) **falsifiability** (Karl Popper) — faith claims do need a falsifiable boundary, but Church *history* is the wrong instrument to falsify the Restoration; and (3) a scientist's case that **history is structurally bad data** ("what survives is not random… every model is wrong, some are useful"), so the appropriate posture toward almost any historical claim is calibrated skepticism. A closing segment defends the **fruits argument** against the Warren-Jeffs "bad fruits" objection, and a temple segment argues that an ordinance "has to change in order to stay the same."

## Summary

- **The gold-pill parable (the heart of the episode).** Ellis: a terminal patient is healed by a gold pill from a kind stranger; later a nurse claims the stranger murdered her brother. "Certainly, it would be a mistake to claim that because of that knowledge, the gold pill didn't work."[^1] Applied to Joseph: even *if* he did something immoral, "we should not reject the message because of the messenger" — to do so is "a textbook example of bulverism."[^2] Hansen supplies the scriptural twin in the man born blind ([[John 9.1-3|John 9]]): "I'm not even going to talk about that 'cause that's above my pay grade. What I can speak to though is the fact that I can see."[^3] → [[The Gold Pill Parable - Bulverism Against the Messenger]]

- **Falsifiability, used both ways.** Hansen invokes Karl Popper — a thesis you have rigged so it can never be falsified "can't tell you anything about reality."[^4] Ellis concedes the point: a testimony is *not* unfalsifiable — "if Jesus came to you and said this church is not true," a faithful member should follow Jesus.[^5] But the crux is the *instrument*: "I don't think you can use church history as a way to falsify the claims of the restoration."[^6] The critic's "what if we *proved* X" hypotheticals are answered: "maybe just present your argument instead of us just pretending that you have a really good one."[^7] This is the counterpart to [[Heads Faith Wins, Tails Reason Loses - The Unfalsifiability Charge]] and [[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]].

- **History is structurally bad data.** Ellis, "as a scientist," argues the record is "both highly biased — because what survives is not random — and highly discrete," with gaps that widen the further back you go.[^8] "Every model is wrong, some are useful" applies even more to history than to statistics, so "whatever you hear, have a healthy level of skepticism."[^9] The disciplined complement is the source-criticism method of [[Source Criticism in Mormon History]]; the pastoral complement is [[Living With Uncertainty Is a Faith Skill]]. → [[History Is Bad Data - The Wrong Instrument to Falsify Faith]]

- **Testimony is not built on history in the first place.** Citing Jacob Hansen's epistemological framework, Ellis insists "the reason you have your faith is not because you studied church history… ideally you had some kind of spiritual witness," and "church history really only kind of fits into one of those areas."[^10] So a historical shock attacks a load it was never carrying — the same diagnosis as [[Inverted Testimony - Why Faith Built Top-Down Collapses]] and [[Worldview as the Prioritization of Witnesses]].

- **Where is the actual line?** Prompted by a viewer ("Is there anything Joseph could have done that would dissuade you?"), Hansen answers "serial axe murder of children" — and notes that the questioner's hypothetical is symmetrical and unfalsifiable in the opposite direction (you also can't *prove* a husband isn't secretly pretending to love you).[^11] The honest concession: "I think it is a mistake to claim that nothing could possibly damage your faith."[^5] What *would* shake faith is an attack on the **epistemology** used to gain it — e.g., if it rested on the false premise that "church history is perfect."[^12]

- **The fruits argument vs. the Warren-Jeffs objection.** A viewer presses: doesn't Warren Jeffs count as a *bad* fruit of Joseph Smith? Hansen: the exception is not the rule — David Koresh, Jonestown, Weinstein, Epstein "pulled those things off just fine without Joseph Smith," so Jeffs is "a really good appeal to" pathos, "but it's not logos."[^13] Ellis adds the statistician's point: you cannot read the rule off a cherry-picked outlier — "is the average height 5'8 because this guy's 6'6?" — the fruits argument only works **in aggregate**.[^14] This is the steelman-and-limit companion to [[Correlation Is Not Proof - The Honest Limit of the Fruits Argument]], [[Fruit Versus Theology - Are They Separable]], and [[Where Are the Fruits - Following the Critics' Narrative to Its Conclusion]]. Hansen notes the fruits test is "one of the only things that Jesus pointed to as an epistemology" ([[Alma 32.27-43|Alma 32]]).[^15]

- **"It has to change in order to stay the same" (temple segment).** Responding to the Salt Lake Tribune on endowment and garment changes, Hansen distinguishes form from covenant: "The ordinance is not the form. The ordinance is the covenant."[^16] But the deeper point is paradoxical — *even if the words and clothing never changed, context would change their meaning*, so "if it's going to stay the same, it has to change."[^17] His example: Nauvoo washings were a literal bath because pioneers had not bathed in a week, a significance no modern rite can reproduce. → [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]]

- **Argue from intellectual consistency (method).** Hansen's recurring tactic is to grant the critique's force, then surface a parallel inside the critic's own theology: the unevangelized "1,600-year gap" answers the apostasy objection; conditional/unfulfilled prophecy (Ezekiel on Tyre) answers the "false prophecy" attack. "A lot of the way that they explain their faith is the same way that I would explain my faith."[^18]
