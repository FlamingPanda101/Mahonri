# PragerU — Reason Is Not Enough to Make Us Good

A short PragerU video, narrated by [[Dennis Prager]], attacking the
Enlightenment assumption that **reason alone** produces good people and a good
world. Prager's central claim is that reason is morally **neutral** — a tool
that can justify evil as readily as good — and that the moral commitments most
people prize (the value of liberty, the preciousness of human life) are
**faith-based**, not products of reason. The video is a clean popular statement
of the position that ethics requires a transcendent foundation beyond
rationality, making it a useful foil for the Level 1 ethics sub-section on the
basis of right and wrong.

## Summary
- **The Enlightenment premise under attack**: since the 18th-century "Age of
  Reason," educated people have assumed reason alone leads to goodness — that we
  need neither God nor religion, only reason [^1].
- **Evil is not irrationality**: dismissing dictators (Stalin, Pol Pot, Mao) and
  hostile regimes as "mad" or "irrational" is a category error; their acts are
  evil, not insane, and calling them irrational is wishful thinking that
  conflates reason with goodness [^2].
- **Reason can justify wrongdoing**: cheating on a test, a shady-but-lucrative
  deal, or infidelity can all be perfectly *rational* if you can get away with
  it and the benefits outweigh the risks — so rationality does not track
  morality [^3].
- **Moral heroism is not rational**: a non-Jew risking death to hide a Jew in
  Nazi Europe was morally great but not rational; "you can't get much more
  rational than self-preservation," and rescuers never cited reason as their
  motive [^4].
- **Reason is just a tool**: like a knife that can murder or, in a surgeon's
  hand, heal, reason serves whatever end you already want; it can equally defend
  liberty or a totalitarian dictatorship [^5].
- **The value of liberty is not derived from reason**: many rational people
  prize security, order, territory, or theocracy over liberty — reason alone
  does not tell you which to choose [^6].
- **The Singer example**: a Princeton philosopher ([[Peter Singer]]) said he
  would kill a disabled baby if it were in the family's best interests; Prager
  claims no *purely rational* rebuttal exists — only the faith claim that humans
  bear the image of God [^7].
- **Athens vs. Jerusalem**: reason-based Greece thought it reasonable to expose
  sickly babies; faith-based Jerusalem taught the world to keep them alive — so
  the preciousness of human life is a *belief*, not an assertion of reason [^8].
