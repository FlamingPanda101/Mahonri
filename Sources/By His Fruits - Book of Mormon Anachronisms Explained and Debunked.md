# By His Fruits — Book of Mormon Anachronisms EXPLAINED & Debunked

A short, illustrated explainer that reframes the standard "the Book of Mormon is full of anachronisms" attack. The presenter opens with a friend's challenge (steel swords before steel existed) and then introduces a three-part taxonomy that controls the whole video: anachronisms are *verified*, *alleged*, or *reversed*. His thesis is that archaeology has been progressively moving items from the "alleged" column into the "reversed" column — the opposite of what a 19th-century forgery should produce — and he uses Matt Roper's quantified inventory plus the steel-sword and cement cases to show the trend.

## Summary

- **Three kinds of anachronism.** *Verified* = demonstrably out of period (his illustration: a 1781 "telegram," impossible before the 1830s telegraph). *Alleged* = looks out of place on current evidence but unproven (a 1911 critic's claim that Nephi's "most precious steel" sword is centuries too early). *Reversed* = once-alleged, now confirmed in period. See [[The Three Types of Anachronism - Verified Alleged and Reversed]]. [^1]
- **The steel sword is a reversed anachronism.** An iron, carburized-steel sword dated to ~600 BC (Nephi's era) was excavated at Jericho in 1982 — about 17 miles from where Nephi grew up — moving the "steel" objection from alleged to reversed. See [[The Jericho Steel Sword Reverses the Nephi Steel Anachronism]]. [^2]
- **Roper's inventory shows a steep reversal curve.** Matt Roper (Scripture Central) tracked alleged anachronisms over time: only ~7% reversed in 1830–1844; ~22% by 1965; ~77% by 2024 — with the remaining ~9% the only items critics can still press. The reversals come from non-LDS archaeology, in the footnotes. See [[The Anachronism Reversal Rate Trends Toward the Book of Mormon Over Time]]. [^3]
- **Science can neither prove nor disprove — by design.** The presenter quotes a [[Neil A. Maxwell|Neal A. Maxwell]] aphorism: scripture "will remain in the realm of faith," with "enough plausible evidence... to prevent scoffers from having a field day, but not enough to remove the requirement of faith." (He notes there is likewise no archaeological evidence for Abraham or Joseph in Egypt, yet few doubt they existed.) See [[Enough Evidence to Prevent Scoffing Not Enough to Remove Faith]]. [^4]
- **The cement case and the Heber J. Grant story.** A young skeptic told Heber J. Grant the Book of Mormon's cement houses (Helaman 3:7) disproved it; Grant replied his faith rested on having read and prayed about the book, and trusted that descendants would find the cement. They did — pre-Columbian cement expertise is now confirmed in the Americas. See [[Nephite Cement Construction Was Confirmed at Teotihuacan]]. [^5]
- **The inversion of the argument.** The very items once "used to attack" are now "providing evidences." A genuinely fraudulent ancient record should accumulate problems under scrutiny; the Book of Mormon's problem-list shrinks, which the presenter treats as a mark of authenticity. [^6]
