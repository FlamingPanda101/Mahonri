Catholic apologist [[Trent Horn]] presents what he calls *the best moral argument against atheism*, and — unusually — has the script vetted in advance by non-theistic PhD candidate [[Joe Schmid]] (*Majesty of Reason*) "to make sure all the philosophical ground is covered properly." Horn's central move is to **lower the ambition and sharpen the target**: rather than argue "you can't have morality without God," he argues that God is the *best explanation* for a cluster of *specific, puzzling* moral facts that secular naturalism strains to account for. He frames it as "more of an argument about the moral poverty of secularism that opens the door for God than a strict argument for God from morality."[^7]

The essay has a clean arc. (1) **Clear the bad versions.** "You can't be good without God" is a strawman; the real claim is "you can be good without God, but you can't have *good* without God" — and even then, not that morality is *impossible* without God (you can have morality the way you can have football, as agreed-upon rules), but that you can't have *objective* morality without God.[^1] (2) **Survey the standard argument and its weaknesses.** [[C.S. Lewis]]'s *Mere Christianity* version, the reply that God isn't the *best* explanation of moral truths (Platonism, brute necessary moral facts à la Wielenberg), and [[Sam Harris]]'s well-being naturalism — all of which can "fall flat" because the atheist just calls the moral fact *self-evident* or a *brute fact*.[^2] (3) **Horn's better approach — the modest argument from particular acts.** Zero in on the *most* puzzling cases: the intrinsic dignity of *non-rational* humans (the disabled, infants, the brainless organ-farm reductio) versus animals, and the wrongness of *painless, consensual* perverted sex acts (bestiality, necrophilia, incest, rape-fantasy). These are exactly what we'd expect if God designed humans for dignity and ordered sexuality, and exactly what a "as long as everyone consents / don't cause unnecessary pain" ethic cannot explain.[^8] (4) **Answer the Euthyphro/voluntarism comeback** with classical theism: God's commands are good not because he is powerful but because he *is* perfect goodness by nature ([[God Is Perfect Goodness Itself]]; [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]; [[Evil Is a Privation Not a Thing]]).

Much of the *abortion / consciousness-criterion* material recurs from Horn's other vault videos and is wikilinked rather than re-noted — see [[The Brainless Organ-Farm Reductio Against the Consciousness Criterion]], [[You Can Harm Someone Without Hurting Them]], and [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]]. The two genuinely *new* contributions here are the organizing thesis ([[The Modest Moral Argument from Particular Puzzling Acts]]) and the sexual-faculty argument ([[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]]). **LDS divergence:** Horn's Euthyphro reply leans on divine *simplicity* (God *is* Goodness Itself / pure act); the Restoration instead holds that God perfectly *conforms to and embodies* eternal moral reality he himself honors — a different third-horn answer that reaches the same anti-voluntarist conclusion (cf. [[God's Nature as the Standard - The Third Horn of Euthyphro]]).

## Summary

- **There is no single moral argument.** Just as there is no one cosmological argument, there is a *family* of moral arguments agreeing only that something about good and evil points to God; so there are better and worse versions. The "Reddit-level" worst is "you can't be good without God."[^1]
- **The three reframings.** (a) Move from conduct to ontology: "you can be good without God, but you can't have good without God." (b) You *can* have morality without God — as mutually-agreed rules, like football — so the claim isn't impossibility. (c) The real thesis: **you cannot have *objective* morality without God.**[^1]
- **Lewis's popular version.** *Mere Christianity* (radio addresses for wartime Englishmen, avoiding [[Frederick Copleston|Copleston]]-style contingency arguments): people quarrelling over a bus seat appeal to a standard above their opinions; cross-cultural moral *disagreement* presupposes a real morality to disagree *about* ("if your moral ideas can be truer and the Nazis' less true, there must be some real morality… as New York is a real place"). Lewis concedes this gets nowhere near "the God of Christian theology" — only "something directing the universe."[^3]
- **Survey result.** In Horn's prior episode polling 150 Christian thinkers, the *cosmological* argument won as best, and Horn agrees it best supports a being with divine attributes — but regular people's "eyes glaze over" at contingency and infinite-past paradoxes, so the moral argument is more *useful* pastorally.[^2]
- **Weakness 1 — God isn't obviously the best explanation.** John Beversluis lists rivals (Platonism, stoicism, natural law, the utilitarianisms, deontologies, virtue ethics); **Erik Wielenberg** (*Value and Virtue in a Godless Universe*) proposes *necessary moral truths* — "it's wrong to cause pain for pain's sake" is just always true, like 2+2=4 — needing no lawgiver.[^2]
- **Weakness 2 — the brute-fact wall.** When Christians ask "why is rape/murder objectively wrong?", critics answer "because it's bad to cause pain without good reason, and bad to do bad things," looking at the Christian as if he's *missing a self-evident fact*. Horn illustrates with [[Sam Harris]]'s *Moral Landscape* ("values are a certain kind of fact… about the well-being of conscious creatures"), the Charlie Kirk / Kyle Beavon exchange, and the Dean Withers caller exchange ("why is child rape wrong?" → "because you're raping a child… I'm not giving you more than that").[^4] Horn reads the Withers caller charitably: not defending Trump, but trying to "pull the rug out" — Withers as an atheist can't ground *why* it's objectively wrong in the first place.[^5]
- **The better, modest approach.** Be *modest* (don't claim proof beyond doubt) and *zero in on the most puzzling moral facts* for naturalism. It's "more an argument about the moral poverty of secularism that opens the door for God." See [[The Modest Moral Argument from Particular Puzzling Acts]].[^7]
- **Puzzle A — dignity of non-rational humans.** Suffering is a *species-neutral* category; murder-by-suffering can't explain why we treat humans with *less* rationality (and so less capacity for suffering) as full murder victims with intrinsic dignity, while dogs more rational than some disabled humans are not. The Destiny debate: Destiny conceded his worldview couldn't condemn *in principle* modifying fetuses into "brainless organ farms" or "toddler sex dolls"; Jeff McMahan grants it would be permissible to kill a healthy orphaned newborn to harvest organs for three sick children ("most will find this intolerable… I cannot embrace it without misgivings"). Secular anti-infanticide reasons (near-persons, adoptability, social respect for life) all apply to the unborn too, so the consistent defenders "bite the bullet" on infanticide. See [[The Brainless Organ-Farm Reductio Against the Consciousness Criterion]] and [[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]].[^8]
- **The potentiality dialectic (steelmanned).** Atheist: *potential* for rationality grounds value → explains infanticide's wrongness. Horn: but not the *severely handicapped* who will never be rational. Atheist: merely *possessing a rational nature* suffices. Horn: then be **pro-life**, since every embryo has a rational human nature — yet 91% of atheists are pro-choice and don't seem to recognize this immaterial property in their own godless worldview. It "makes sense if all humans are made in God's image."[^9]
- **Puzzle B — perverted but painless/consensual sex acts.** Just as murder reduces to puzzling killings, rape reduces to puzzling *sexual* evils. Cases the consent/harm ethic can't condemn: Andrew Wilson's twin-brother **incest** (Naima: "gross" but can't call it immoral; family all die in the example to remove emotional harm); the genetic-deformity rebuttal fails (we don't ban pregnancy over 35; doesn't apply to same-sex incest or adult twins or *genetic sexual attraction* between separated relatives / sperm-donor half-siblings); **bestiality** (consent objection fails since we eat animals and use police canines without consent — Destiny/Jasmine bite the bullet; **Peter Singer** and David Benatar flirt with permissibility); **necrophilia** with prior consent; BBC "drugged consenting partners for a rape kink." See [[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]].[^10]
- **What the wrongness really tracks.** Not the harm to recipients (pain/infection can be eliminated) but "the wrongness of *misusing a particular biological process* that has much more value than any other mere biological process." [[Joe Schmid]] concedes: a better account than bare natural-law theory "cites God specifically setting aside the sexual faculty as distinctively sacred… an icon of the Trinity," and admits non-theistic accounts "can get strained."[^11]
- **The evolutionary-debunking twist.** It gets "too coincidental" that the universe's brute moral truths happen to repel exactly what repels humans. Evolutionary debunking arguments say a mind*less* process tuned for *survival* can't be trusted to track abstract necessary moral truths about acts that cause *no* identifiable harm — but instead of abandoning moral realism, take comfort that God gave us moral senses to "rise above our merely animal natures." See [[Evolution Cannot Ground Objective Morality]].[^12]
- **[[J.L. Mackie]]'s concession.** Objective, *intrinsically prescriptive* properties "supervening upon natural ones" are "so odd a cluster… most unlikely to have arisen… without an all-powerful god to create them" — which is *why* Mackie denied objective morality and adopted **error theory** (all moral claims are false). Horn turns the queerness the other way: oddness under naturalism is evidence *for* theism. See [[J.L. Mackie]].[^13]
- **The pastoral offer.** Even if the puzzles don't move the atheist to God, let them push him past a "stunted… as long as everyone consents / don't cause unnecessary pain" ethic — e.g. toward virtue ethics and [[Alasdair MacIntyre]]'s *After Virtue*, which "for an intellectual atheist might be more effective… than all the apologetics books in the world."[^14]
- **The Euthyphro comeback.** Atheist: theism creates *worse* problems — would it be right if God commanded Adam to marry his dog rather than Eve? If still wrong, the wrongness has nothing to do with God. Horn: God *won't* command such things — not because a moral law sits above him, but because his commands are good "not because he's powerful but because he is perfect by nature." The Catechism: "God's almighty power is in no way arbitrary… In God, power, essence, will, intellect, wisdom, and justice are all identical."[^15]
- **Sinnott-Armstrong's worry, answered by classical theism.** Walter Sinnott-Armstrong: even if *a* god exists, that god might not be loving or just. Horn: God *by definition* has all great-making properties; this isn't arbitrary but flows from his identity as the foundation of reality. [[Thomas Aquinas]]: God is the cause of all existence because God *just is* existence / pure actuality (the locomotive that *is* motion; creatures are boxcars receiving it). A perfect being lacking nothing — including lacking no virtuous response to any state of affairs — *must* be perfectly good, since **evil is a privation**, an absence of good. See [[God Is Perfect Goodness Itself]] and [[Evil Is a Privation Not a Thing]].[^16]
- **Full circle to Lewis.** Lewis: "goodness is, so to speak, itself; badness is only spoiled goodness," presupposing a prior good. Lewis's moral argument is *underappreciated* because *Mere Christianity*'s later chapters show the moral law lives not in "unforgiving Platonic forms" or impersonal karma but in "supreme love itself, the triune God." Recommends Christopher Shock's article and David Baggett & Jerry Walls's *God and Cosmos*.[^17]
- **Bottom line.** When using moral arguments, focus on atheism's difficulty explaining the wrongness of *very particular* acts — "objectifying non-rational humans or engaging in perverted, painless sexual acts" — and how that wrongness is just what we'd expect if God had a plan for the most important parts of human life.[^18]
