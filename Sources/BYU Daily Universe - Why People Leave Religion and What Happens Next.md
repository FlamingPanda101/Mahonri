# BYU Daily Universe - Why People Leave Religion and What Happens Next

**Outlet:** BYU Daily Universe (student newspaper) | **Reporter:** Spencer Adolphson | **Researchers:** [[Sam Hardy]] (BYU psychology) & [[Justin Dyer]] (BYU religious education), with positive-psychology commentary from [[Jared Warren]] | **Date:** 2025

This article reports a June 2025 study by [[Sam Hardy]] and [[Justin Dyer]] on *religious deidentification* — what predicts young people leaving religion, and what happens to their mental and emotional functioning afterward. The work began in 2016 with 652 religious 12–14-year-olds from California, Arizona, and Utah (about half affiliated, or formerly affiliated, with The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints), surveyed every two years for nine years on positive attributes (self-esteem, sense of purpose) and negative ones (sexual risk-taking, suicidal ideation).

## Summary

- The headline finding overturns the intuition that leaving religion brings relief: youth who left religion showed **increased negative functioning** — notably a weaker sense of purpose that *worsened* after they left, rather than the emotional improvement people expect. See [[Leaving Religion Does Not Bring the Relief People Expect]].
- The downturn was **markedly steeper for those leaving the LDS Church** than for those leaving several other faiths. Hardy attributes this to its being a "high-cost religion" — one demanding more commitment, so that exiting it produces a more dramatic impact. See [[High-Cost Religion Makes Leaving More Costly]].
- Hardy frames the study against the "rise of the nones": the share of Americans claiming no religion grew from single digits in the 1990s to roughly 30 percent, paralleled by a significant rise in youth mental-health problems. See [[The Online Echo Chamber Accelerates Disaffiliation]] and [[Younger Generations Less Religious Forecasts Continued Decline]].
- Hardy and Dyer describe a **bidirectional, self-perpetuating cycle**: poorer mental health (suicidality, depression) makes people more likely to struggle with faith, and loss of faith in turn removes a source of connection and comfort, deepening the difficulty. See [[A Bidirectional Cycle Links Faith Struggle and Mental Health]].
- [[Jared Warren]], a BYU positive-psychology professor, notes that belonging to a religion cultivates purpose and gratitude, and asks where a young person who leaves will find these instead. He insists religion is not the *only* source and calls the constructive alternative "honoring the search" — overcoming dissonance to find one's own path to flourishing. See [[Honoring the Search - Finding Purpose After Leaving]].
- The researchers will keep collecting data (next survey in 2026), hoping to trace deidentification's effects into adulthood, not just the short term.
