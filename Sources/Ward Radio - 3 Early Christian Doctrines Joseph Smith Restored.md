# Ward Radio — 3 Early Christian Doctrines Joseph Smith Restored

A Ward Radio episode hosted by [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Luke Hansen]], answering [[Pat Madrid]]'s old Catholic-apologetics challenge to "find the proto-Mormon Church in the first centuries." Hansen draws three doctrines from [[Barry Bickmore]]'s *Restoring the Ancient Church: Joseph Smith and Ancient Christianity* (FairMormon) — plus a bonus — where, he argues, the 1830s farmer "just got it right": he restored a belief held in early Christianity that later mainstream Christianity dropped.[^1] The three: (1) the coming of Elias/Elijah as a forerunner before the Second Coming, (2) a literal thousand-year millennial reign, and (3) creation *ex materia* rather than *ex nihilo*.

## Summary
- **The framing is the [[Apostasy as Gradual Falling Away Not Single Event|Great Apostasy]] as a doctrinal-history claim.** Hansen concedes the proto-Mormon church "had to have been there for at least a little bit," and presents three doctrines critics assume Joseph invented that were in fact early and later lost.[^1] See [[The Coming of Elias Before the Second Coming Was an Early Christian Doctrine]].
- **Doctrine 1 — the coming of Elias.** [[Malachi 4.5-6|Malachi 4:5]] (quoted to Joseph by [[Angel Moroni]]) and [[Joseph Smith History 1.68-69|JS-History]]/[[D&C 110]] place Elijah's return before the great and dreadful day; the panel argues "Elias" is a *role/title of a forerunner* (per Luke 1:17), so multiple forerunners are possible, harmonizing [[Matthew 17.10-13|Matthew 17:11-13]] where Jesus both identifies John the Baptist as Elias and says Elias "shall come and restore all things." [[Hippolytus of Rome]] (c. 200), [[Justin Martyr]] (to Trypho), plus Victorinus, [[Methodius of Olympus]], [[Cyprian of Carthage]], Lactantius, [[Jerome]], [[Augustine]], and Theophylact all expected Elijah to come before the Second Coming — a belief now essentially extinct in mainstream dogma. See [[The Coming of Elias Before the Second Coming Was an Early Christian Doctrine]].
- **Joseph put Elijah's coming on Passover.** [[D&C 110]] records the Kirtland Temple appearance of Elijah on 3 April 1836 — Passover — the very day Jews keep an open chair/cup for Elijah; the panel calls this an undesigned coincidence Joseph "struck upon."[^2]
- **Doctrine 2 — a literal millennial reign.** Before the Reformation, Catholic and Orthodox tradition read the millennium "spiritually," but [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] called the literal thousand-year reign an "indispensable part of orthodoxy," quoting [[Papias]]. The panel notes medieval scribes deleted the millennial passages from manuscripts of *Against Heresies*, and [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] dismissed Papias as "a man of very small intelligence." Joseph wrote the literal reign into the Articles of Faith (AoF 1:10). This restates the vault's existing chain.[^3] See [[Early Christian Premillennialism Was the Earliest Millennial View]], [[Augustine Recast the Millennium as the Present Age of the Church]], [[Three Millennial Positions Premillennialism Postmillennialism Amillennialism]], [[Clement and Origen Allegorized Away the Physical Second Coming]].
- **The millennium fell out via Montanism and Gnostic anti-materialism.** The literal millennium fell into disrepute partly through its embrace by the [[The Montanists Were an Apocalyptic New Prophecy Movement|Montanists]] and a Gnostic disdain for physical things; the panel ties the canon's closure ("no more revelation") to the same anti-prophecy reaction.[^4]
- **Doctrine 3 — creatio ex materia, not ex nihilo.** Joseph's [[Abraham 4.1|Book of Abraham]] organizing of unorganized matter restores the older view; [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE|creation ex nihilo is a late-2nd-century invention]] arising "in its fierce battle with Gnosticism" (citing [[Peter Hayman]], Edwin Hatch on Basilides as its ultimate author, and [[Jonathan Goldstein]]'s concession). [[Blake Ostler]]'s 2005 *Out of Nothing* argues the apologists adopted ex nihilo because middle Platonism required it — answering [[William Lane Craig]] in *The New Mormon Challenge*. This restates the vault's existing creation cluster.[^5] See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Is Not Biblical]], [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference]], [[Greco-Roman Jewish Creation View Was Material Substrate]], [[2 Maccabees 7.28 Does Not Teach Creation Ex Nihilo]].
- **Ex nihilo is the Trinity's linchpin.** The panel argues ex nihilo is essential to the [[The Uncaused Cause Is Aristotle Not Scripture|uncaused-cause/unmoved-mover]] God and [[Divine Simplicity - God Has No Parts of Any Kind|divine simplicity]]: if eternal matter exists outside God, "there is stuff that doesn't trace back to the one," collapsing the Neoplatonic Trinity. Hence rejecting ex nihilo (affirming ex materia) "destroys the Trinity."[^6] See [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].
- **LDS Christology distinguished from Arianism.** When critics charge that LDS make Jesus "a created being" (the Arian heresy), the panel answers that on ex materia Christ is *eternal* — "and so are you" — which is what "blows their mind."[^7]
- **Bonus — premortal existence.** [[Origen]] argued from the consent problem of being "spawned into existence" in an evil world, and the 2nd-century Clementine Recognitions speak of man "whose internal species is older" than his birth — a pre-mortal-life seed less widely attested than the other three.[^8] See [[Origen on the Preexistence and Fall of Souls]], [[Pre-Existence in Mainstream Christian Thought]].
