# Modern Scholarship Confirms What Joseph Smith Said in 1844

Alex Arnold of *Moroni's Standard* argues that across physics, ancient Near Eastern studies, Hebrew Bible scholarship, and archaeology, independent and overwhelmingly **non-LDS** researchers keep arriving at conclusions that [[Joseph Smith]] reached in the 1830s–40s — and that several of these conclusions actively *contradicted* the scholarly consensus of Joseph's own day. The hinge example is the April 1844 [[King Follett Discourse]]: Joseph claimed the Hebrew *bara* in Genesis 1:1 means "organize," not create from nothing, and that matter and intelligence are co-eternal with God. 134 years later the German Protestant theologian [[Gerhard May]] established that creation *ex nihilo* was a second-century invention, not a reading of the Hebrew Bible. Arnold's thesis is not that this proves the Church true, but that "zero evidence" stopped being an honest description, and the trend line keeps moving toward corroboration rather than away from it.

## Summary

- **Eternal matter (1844) vs. modern physics.** Joseph taught the elements are eternal ([[D&C 93]]:33) and that God organized pre-existing material. Arnold notes this lines up with energy conservation (first law of thermodynamics), the non-empty quantum vacuum, and the Big Bang model's refusal to describe something emerging from *absolute* nothing — whereas *ex nihilo* requires energy coming into existence at creation, defensible only by exempting God from physics [04:01]–[06:00]. See [[Modern Physics Is Consistent With Eternal Matter Not Ex Nihilo]].
- **Bara and the late origin of ex nihilo.** Gerhard May traced *creatio ex nihilo* to the late-2nd-century anti-Gnostic polemic of Irenaeus and Tertullian, "not from the Hebrew text and not from the Greek New Testament." The Brown-Driver-Briggs lexicon does not define *bara* as creation from nothing — "Joseph Smith read the Hebrew correctly in 1844" [06:32]–[07:30]. See [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]].
- **Deification and the divine council.** [[Michael Heiser]] (PhD, Hebrew Bible, Wisconsin; Protestant) documented in *The Unseen Realm* that the original Hebrew worldview had a hierarchical cosmos of multiple divine beings and genuine deification language ([[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]] "you are gods," quoted by Jesus in John 10:34; the Dead Sea Scrolls reading "sons of God" at Deuteronomy 32:8) [08:30]–[10:01]. This matches the [[King Follett Discourse]] and the Lorenzo Snow couplet ([[Lorenzo Snow]]). See [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]].
- **The First Temple and Josiah's reforms.** [[Margaret Barker]] reconstructs a pre-exilic First Temple tradition containing deification, divine council, sacred garments, and ascent rituals — all reshaped by Josiah's 7th-century reform. Encountering LDS temple practice she said, "Here... is someone who knows about the First Temple" [10:01]–[11:02]. Because Lehi left Jerusalem ~600 BC carrying pre-reform brass plates, the Book of Mormon's "mismatch" with the post-reform Masoretic Bible is what authenticity, not fraud, would predict [13:00]–[14:01]. See [[Josiahs Reform and the First Temple Make Restoration a Coherent Category]] and [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]].
- **The pattern and its cost.** None of these scholars (May, Heiser, Barker, Friedman) coordinated or aimed to vindicate Joseph; following the scholarship is "too costly" for traditions built on a particular reading of religious history, which is why the popular evangelical world lags the academy. Recognizing the parallels without converting is legitimate — Barker and Heiser read LDS theology as genuinely ancient but interpret that antiquity differently — "they see us as ancient and consider it a limitation. We just consider it the credential" [14:01]–[15:30], [23:31]–[25:00]. See [[Recognizing the Parallel Is Not the Same as the Theological Conclusion]].
