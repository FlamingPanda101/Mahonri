# The Stick of Joseph - New Evidence the New Testament Was Shaped by Stoicism

Independent commentator [[Brian Thomassen]], with [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]] and Brad Whitbeck, argues that **Stoic thought is embedded in the New Testament from the very beginning** — so the usual "Hellenization-as-corruption" model of the Great Apostasy mis-assigns blame. He frames Stoicism as a theoretical "preparatory gospel," parallel to how Freemasonry served Joseph Smith.

## Summary
- **A revelation model where language colors the message.** Against the "funnel" model (Plato's *Ion*: the revelator is a mere channel), Thomassen reads D&C 1:24 and Alma 29:8 to say revelation comes "after the manner of their language" — so the Greek-writing New Testament authors are unavoidably shaped by their Greco-Roman, predominantly Stoic, intellectual world.[^1] See [[Revelation Is Colored by the Revelators Language and Culture]].
- **Three Hellenization models are all post-apostasy.** Tertullian (reject Athens), Clement of Alexandria (quilt the good bits in), and Augustine (baptize neoplatonism) are men living in the ashes of an apostasy that had already happened; none appeals to new revelation.[^2]
- **John 1:1 deploys the Stoic Logos.** "In the beginning was the Logos" would read to any literate Greco-Roman as the Stoic binding principle of the cosmos — God and reason are one — used as a familiar bridge to introduce Christ, like Ammon teaching King Lamoni.[^3] See [[John 1-1 Deploys the Stoic Logos as a Bridge]].
- **Four marks of Stoic ethics in the New Testament.** (1) the *open/unbounded* ethic (universal *agape*; "love your enemies," with no Old Testament precedent, paralleling Seneca/Epictetus); (2) the *adverbial* ethic (the widow's mite; intent over the size of the sacrifice); (3) *natural law* (a Stoic invention, downstream to Aquinas and the Founders); (4) the *sage as exemplar* (Socrates for Stoics, Moses for Philo, Christ for Christians).[^4][^5] See [[The Open Ethic - Stoicism Anticipated Love Your Enemies]], [[The Adverbial Ethic - Intent Makes the Sacrifice Acceptable]], [[Natural Law Is a Stoic Invention Downstream to the Founders]], [[The Sage as Exemplar Parallels the Imitation of Christ]].
- **The agape/phileo exchange in John 21.** Christ asks Peter "*agapas* me?" twice; Peter answers "I *philo* thee"; Christ descends to *phileo* the third time — illustrating that *agape* (the Stoics' word) was too abstract/"anemic" for an ordinary audience.[^6] See [[The Agape-Phileo Exchange in John 21 Reflects Stoic Vocabulary]].
- **Philemon and the Stoic adiaphoron.** Paul sending the runaway slave Onesimus back is intelligible because for Stoics outward status (slave or free) is *adiaphoron* — indifferent — so the letter entered the canon without friction.[^7] See [[Philemon Reads Coherently Through Stoic Indifference to Status]].
