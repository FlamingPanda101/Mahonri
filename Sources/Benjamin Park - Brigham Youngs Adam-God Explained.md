# Brigham Young's "Adam-God" Explained

Historian [[Benjamin Park]]'s entry in his "So You Want to Know More About X" Friday series, giving the **academic-history account** of [[Brigham Young]]'s Adam-God teaching: what Young actually taught, the key primary sources, how the doctrine was received and disputed among the apostles, why it was eventually abandoned, and how to think about whether it was ever "doctrine." Park frames it as "probably the most substantive example of how some core doctrines of the Latter-day Saint tradition, including the Godhead, evolved in the first century of the faith's existence" [^1].

- **Opening hook:** As a missionary in Washington DC, Park carried a photocopied 1852 Brigham Young sermon declaring Adam "our God," and one night couldn't tell who he was praying to — he lacked the historical tools to understand the text, which the video supplies [^2].
- **What Young taught:** Adam = Michael the archangel = the ancient of days; Adam and Eve lived, died, and were exalted on another world, then came to organize and people this earth; Adam is "our father and our God, and the only God to whom we have to do." The doctrine is "distinctly tethered" to Young's theology of family, polygamy, and procreation [^3].
- **Primary sources surveyed:** the April 9, 1852 *Journal of Discourses* sermon; an 1854 general conference sermon Wilford Woodruff called "the greatest sermon" ever delivered to the Saints; an February 1857 sermon showing Young's "wiggle room"; and the 1877 **lecture at the veil** at the St. George Temple, preserved in John Nuttall's diary [^4].
- **Reception:** preached and defended by [[Wilford Woodruff]], [[Orson Hyde]], and [[Heber C. Kimball]]; rejected publicly by [[Orson Pratt]], who published rebuttals and was forced to revise his remarks in 1860 [^5].
- **Abandonment:** after Young's 1877 death, apostles grew comfortable opposing it; 1890s RLDS missionaries and newspaper series forced a stance; [[Joseph F. Smith]] (1902) and [[James Talmage]]'s 1915 *Jesus the Christ* codified a Godhead theology that moved past Adam-God [^6].

## Summary

Park opens with "Brigham Young the theologian" — a description, he notes, "you don't frequently hear," since Young is remembered as organizer, leader, and colonizer rather than a revelator like [[Joseph Smith]]. Park argues that anxiety over not being seen as a scriptorian/revelator weighed on Young, and that he "saw the Adam God doctrine as one way for him to make his claim to that type of revelatory prophet" [^7].

A key untangling move: in the 19th century **Elohim and Jehovah did not necessarily mean Heavenly Father and Jesus.** You must "uncouple those terms and titles" for Young's scheme to make sense. The reconstructed teaching: Adam (identified as Michael in LDS scripture — itself a revision of Protestant trends) and Eve were "born raised and died on another planet," exalted as gods, created spirit children, and then Michael "took on the form of Adam" to inaugurate the human series from his own lineage [^3].

Park stresses this was **not off-hand.** Young "taught it a lot," especially in the 1850s, most famously in the **April 9, 1852** sermon published in the *Journal of Discourses*: "He is Michael the Archangel, the ancient of days... He is our father and our God, and the only God to whom we have to do." The same sermon teaches that "the father had begotten" Jesus "in his own likeness... not begotten by the Holy Ghost," with Young's "classic Brigham Youngism" quip that if a spirit had impregnated Mary, the missionaries' own conduct would produce "illegitimate children across the globe" [^8]. A second mass quote comes from an **1854** general conference sermon — "Every world has an Adam and an Eve" — which [[Wilford Woodruff]] reported as "the greatest sermon that has ever been... delivered to the Latter-day Saints" [^9].

Park's interpretive frame: Adam-God was a **"frontier theology that divonized humanity."** Adam serves as "type A for what humans want to become through exaltation," and the doctrine was Young's way of "trying to understand polygamy in the eternities." Young said he received it "both through revelation and also learning it from Joseph Smith"; Park notes scholars find only "kernels" in Joseph (God's other name as Amen, Nauvoo exaltation sermons, an exalted Adam) — see [[How Brigham Young Extrapolated Adam-God from Joseph Smith]] [^10].

On **reception and dispute**, Park describes an "apostolic bash over core doctrines": [[Wilford Woodruff]], [[Orson Hyde]], and [[Heber C. Kimball]] preached and defended it; it appeared in church periodicals, "especially in Europe." [[Orson Pratt]] "outright rejected the doctrine," published rebuttals, and disagreed at a general conference; Hyde countered Pratt with the argument that God "would remove" a prophet rather than "suffer his mouthpiece to go in error." In 1860 the two "fought over it again and Orson Pratt was forced to revise his remarks." Yet Young himself showed "wiggle room" in a February 1857 sermon: "whether Adam is the personage that we should consider our heavenly father or not... I do not care for one moment how that is" [^11].

The doctrine became "sacralized" most fully in the **temple**: when the St. George Temple was completed in 1877, Young and Woodruff integrated Adam-God into the endowment via the **lecture at the veil**, recorded in temple worker John Nuttall's diary ("Adam was an immortal being when he came on the earth... had begotten all the spirits... when this earth was organized by Elohim, Jehovah, and Michael, who is Adam, our common father") — see [[Lecture at the Veil Required Adam-God]]. Some members and Pratt objected, and a few faced disciplinary proceedings over their opposition [^12].

Park then answers the recurring question — **was Adam-God ever doctrine?** — by distinguishing two definitions, the historian's and the believer's. This is the academic payoff of the video; see [[A Historian's Definition of Doctrine vs the Believer's]] and the existing [[Was Adam-God Official Binding Teaching]] [^13].

On **abandonment**: shortly after Young's 1877 death, more apostles spoke against it ([[Joseph F. Smith]] among the uncomfortable). It faded in the 1880s, then in the 1890s **RLDS missionaries** (followers of [[Joseph Smith III]]) and anti-Mormon **newspaper series** (1897-1899) made it a public liability — especially after polygamy receded post-1890 — forcing leaders to take a stance. Joseph F. Smith's 1902 *Improvement Era* statement and [[James Talmage]]'s 1915 *Jesus the Christ* (which placed Jesus over Adam in eternal seniority), followed by the 1916 "The Father and the Son" statement, formally moved the church past it; later leaders, including [[Joseph Fielding Smith]] and Spencer W. Kimball (1976), denied Young ever taught it. See [[Newspaper Pressure Forced the Codification That Ended Adam-God]] [^14].

Park's closing lesson: the episode "shows how not everything in LDS theology can be taken for granted and has changed in some ways fundamentally over decades on topics as crucial as the godhead," and reveals "the diversity among LDS leaders." He warns the doctrine is "usually reduced into a caricature or funhouse mirror of what it actually was" [^15].

**Recommended further reading (per Park):** Thomas Alexander, "The Reconstruction of Mormon Doctrine" (Sunstone); Gary Bergera, *Conflict in the Quorum* (chapter on the Pratt–Young Adam-God fights); Stephen Taysom, *Like a Fiery Meteor* (biography of [[Joseph F. Smith]]); and — his top pick — [[Jonathan Stapley]], "Brigham Young's Garden Cosmology," *Journal of Mormon History* (January 2021) [^16].
