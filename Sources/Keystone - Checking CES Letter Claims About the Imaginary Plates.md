# Keystone — "Checking CES Letter Claims About the Imaginary Plates"

A [[Keystone]] episode rebutting a cluster of sources the [[Jeremy Runnells|CES Letter]] marshals to argue that the Book of Mormon witnesses never physically saw the plates but described only a vision, "entrancement," "imagination," or sight "with the eye of faith." Keystone works through each cited source — [[Anthony Metcalf]] on [[Martin Harris]], [[Zenas Gurley]] on [[David Whitmer]], [[Stephen Burnett]]'s 1838 letter, and [[John A. Clark]]'s "eye of faith" report — and argues that on inspection they are late, second- or third-hand, internally garbled, or blend Martin's *pre*-translation handling of the covered plates with his *post*-translation angelic witness.[^1]

## Summary
- The two "entrancement" quotes the CES Letter presents as separate are from the **same paragraph** of one 1880 book by disaffected member [[Anthony Metcalf]], who dated his Martin Harris interview to winter 1875–76 — which "would have been an interview with a corpse," since the real interview was likely 1873; "entrancement" appears in no other source and is read as Metcalf's embellishment.[^2] See [[Anthony Metcalf Entrancement Quote Is a Late Doubled Embellishment]].
- Calling the experience a **"vision" does not make it unreal**: Latter-day Saints call the First Vision a vision while affirming it was a real physical event; [[David Whitmer]] answered the objection by telling Edward Thurman the vision "was as factual as" seeing a nearby apple tree.[^3] See [[Calling the Witness Experience a Vision Does Not Make It Unreal]].
- Even hostile critic [[Dan Vogel]] concedes the word **"imagination"** in [[Stephen Burnett]]'s report of Martin "likely did not come from Martin but was an embellishment by Burnett himself."[^4]
- [[Stephen Burnett]]'s "saw a city through a mountain" line is **garbled**, and Burnett conflates Martin's covered-box hefting during translation with the later uncovered angelic showing; [[Richard Lloyd Anderson]]: "Martin's candid denial of seeing the plates while translating was sometimes exaggerated into a denial of ever seeing the plates."[^5] See [[Critics Conflate Martin Harriss Pre- and Post-Translation Plate Experiences]].
- The [[John A. Clark]] "eye of faith" quote is **third-hand** (Clark quoting an unnamed Palmyra gentleman quoting Martin), and the CES Letter double-counts it as two separate Palmyra witnesses when the two references trace to one copied-and-pasted anti-Mormon web source — a sourcing red flag.[^6] See [[The CES Letter Double-Counts a Single Copied-and-Pasted Source]].
- The methodological payoff: the **mere existence of contradictory sources is not enough**; weighed against the witnesses' lifelong, consistent testimony and everything else they did and did not do, these critical quotes "lose their bite."[^7] See [[Contradictory Sources Must Be Weighed Not Merely Counted]].
