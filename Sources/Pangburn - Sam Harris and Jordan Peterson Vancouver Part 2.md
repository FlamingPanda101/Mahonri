# Pangburn — Sam Harris & Jordan Peterson, Vancouver (Part 2)

The second night of a Pangburn Philosophy event in Vancouver, moderated by evolutionary biologist [[Bret Weinstein]]. [[Sam Harris]] (New Atheist, author of *The Moral Landscape*) and [[Jordan Peterson]] (clinical psychologist, *Maps of Meaning* / *12 Rules for Life*) debate whether human values must be grounded in religious tradition and story, or can be derived from facts about conscious well-being. The conversation opens with each man **steelmanning the other's position** from night one, then converges on a single fault line: the role of *metaphorical truth* and whether ethical orientation requires an a priori value structure that facts alone cannot supply.[^1] Assigned as **preface** material for *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1 — a model of charitable, high-altitude disagreement about religion.

## Summary

- **Steelmanning as a thinking discipline.** Weinstein has each participant restate the other's view as strongly as possible before rebutting. Peterson frames this as basic intellectual hygiene: "you want to take arguments that are against your perspective and you want to make them as strong as you possibly can so that you can fortify your arguments against them... if you make them weak that just makes you weak."[^2] See [[Steelmanning Is the Discipline of Strengthening Your Opponent's Argument]].

- **Metaphorical truth — the load-bearing concept.** Weinstein defines it: a claim "literally false" yet such that "if you behave as if [it] were true you come out ahead." The shared example is *treat every gun as if it is loaded* — a useful fiction more useful than the literal truth, yet (Harris insists) intelligible *only* by contrast with the literal truth, and one you'd happily bet a million dollars against at a casino. Religions, on this view, are encapsulations of such fictions. See [[Metaphorical Truth as a Useful Fiction]].[^3]

- **Peterson's evolutionary genealogy of sacrifice.** Behavior with survival value (delayed gratification) was selected for, then *self-consciously represented* and dramatized into story; "sacrifice" = "the discovery of the future" — give up something valued now to gain more later. Pushed to its archetypal extreme this yields child sacrifice; Peterson reads its psychologizing (Abraham/Isaac, circumcision-as-substitute, finally abstract parental sacrifice) as moral progress *within* the tradition. See [[Sacrifice as the Discovery of the Future]].[^4]

- **Harris's reply: the genetic fallacy.** Even if delayed gratification and child sacrifice were historically *united*, it is the **genetic fallacy** to think the shared origin obliges us to venerate or retain the abhorrent part. "Delayed gratification is fully separable from a notion that it might ever be rational or good to sacrifice a child." He charges that Peterson's method — uplifting "barbaric, ignorance-derived beliefs" into something palatable — could equally rehabilitate witchcraft. See [[The Genetic Fallacy Against Religious Origins]].[^5]

- **The Moral Landscape and the well-being navigation problem.** Harris brackets "morality" and grounds value in consciousness: the "lights are on," experience runs from needless hellish suffering to flourishing, and ethics is just *navigation* in that space. There are right/wrong directions even if multiple irreconcilable "peaks" exist. See [[The Moral Landscape - Grounding Value in Conscious Well-Being]].[^6]

- **Peterson's central objection: the is/ought gap and the a priori structure.** You cannot derive values from facts in your own lifetime — "there's too many facts, you need a structure to interpret them." Where is the value of an antique or Elvis's guitar located? Not in the material substrate but in the socially constructed hierarchy of value, in "interpersonal space." Harris's own move (extract value from facts) faces the *same* multiplicity-of-interpretation problem he levels at scripture. See [[You Cannot Derive Values From Facts Without an A Priori Structure]].[^7]

- **Faith vs intuition.** Harris denies his foundational moves are "faith": math and ethics rest on *intuitions* (e.g., Riemann curving Euclidean space) that "can run afoul of other discoveries," not on dogmatic propositional claims like "Jesus rose from the dead." Peterson presses that all perception requires an uninspected, partly biological, partly cultural a priori frame with "a dogmatic element." See [[Foundational Intuitions Are Not the Same as Religious Faith]].[^8]

- **Dogmatism: bug and feature.** Harris concedes resistance-to-update is "a bug and a feature" — a terrible tension, because most new ideas are stupid and dangerous, yet a vital subset must be incorporated or "we're all going to perish." The Western tradition's highest value, he argues, is *respect for the process that updates moral judgment* — which religion's "decided for all time" posture obstructs.[^9]

- **Empathy doesn't scale (Slovic / Kahneman & Tversky).** Harris cites Paul Slovic's finding that compassion *drops* when a second needy child is named — a "moral illusion." He argues moral progress means correcting story-driven, protagonist-shaped intuitions with quantification. Peterson replies the heuristics evolved because they "produce a reliable result," and being gameable into contradiction doesn't make them "deeply flawed." See [[Empathy Does Not Scale - The Identifiable Victim Effect]].[^10]

- **Evil as deflated by neuroscience.** Harris: a complete brain-level account of evil would dissolve the category — every Saddam becomes "unlucky," a Charles Whitman with a tumor on his amygdala — unless there is libertarian free will (a factual disagreement). Buddhist/Hindu framings already map "evil" onto *ignorance* (Angulimala, Ravana), so the judeo-christian good/evil frame is not universal. Peterson holds the bad-life/good-life distinction is universal even if good/evil is reframable.[^11]

- **Harris's bottom line: religion gives bad reasons to be good.** The contemplative experience and the ethical life are "babies" we can keep without believing anything "on insufficient evidence." Belief in paradise is "ruinous for prioritizing what we should be prioritizing in this life," and Pascal's-wager-style willed belief is incoherent — "you can't decide to believe something for which you have no evidence simply because of the good effects."[^12] See [[Religion Gives Bad Reasons to Be Good]].

- **Peterson's closing.** He grants Harris's aim (ground ethics in something solid) and the hell/heaven distinction, but maintains the decisive decisions along the way are impossible without the a priori value structure — "I think the evidence is absolutely overwhelming that we can't" navigate without it.[^13]
