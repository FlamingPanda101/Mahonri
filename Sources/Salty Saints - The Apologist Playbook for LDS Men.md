# Salty Saints — The Apologist Playbook for LDS Men

A panel episode of **Salty Saints**, hosted by [[Hayden Holm]], featuring three Latter-day Saint content creators — campus apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] (*Let's Talk with Hayden Carroll*), [[Roy Anderson]] (*Iron Rod Cast*), and [[Sam St. Laurent]] (*Through the Mist*). The framing question is pastoral rather than polemical: how should an ordinary LDS man *engage* a faith challenge without panicking — "stop freaking out, do this instead."[^1] Most of the substantive method restates positions [[Hayden Carroll]] develops elsewhere (know the other worldview, disarm rather than dodge, make the critic justify his standard, let the Spirit do the converting), so those threads are wikilinked to existing notes below rather than duplicated. Two genuinely new specifics — the ethics of monetizing the gospel, and the self-defeating "the plates were too heavy" argument from [[Bill McKeever]] — plus a fresh pastoral claim about youth, are catalogued as new concepts.

## Summary
- **Don't put the gospel behind a paywall.** The cold open (a "Think Celestial" merchandise line, the old "ponderize" sale scandal) crystallizes into an ethic: monetizing or paywalling what the prophets or God have given "gives me the ick." Carroll relays [[Jacob Hansen]]'s commitment to "never put my content or my testimony behind a paywall," distinguishing legitimate donations/early-access from members-only gospel content. Grounded in "make not my Father's house a house of merchandise" ([[John 2.19-21|John 2:16-21]]).[^2][^3][^4] See [[Never Put the Gospel Behind a Paywall]].
- **Know the other person's worldview first** — the recurring thesis. Carroll "won't talk or debate or even discuss something with somebody unless I know what their worldview is," because the question *"what denomination are you?"* lets you stop dodging and start disarming.[^5][^6] He cringes at missionaries who "try to dunk on other religions when they don't know anything about other religions," recounting an elder who got "wiped" by an Apologia preacher who "sneaked in sola scriptura" the missionary couldn't see.[^7] Restates [[Genuine Seekers vs Bullet-Shooters]] and the audience-pairing of [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]].
- **Disarm, don't dodge** — [[Greg Bahnsen]]'s gun illustration is quoted directly: critics keep us "dodging bullets," and "the actual best way to deal with those people is to simply take the gun out of their hand." Against "Joseph Smith was a polygamist," you don't justify the polygamy — you ask "don't you believe in the Bible? What about the whole house of Israel?"[^8][^9] See [[Disarm vs Dodge - Bahnsen's Two Apologetic Strategies]].
- **Make the critic justify his standard.** Evangelical/Catholic/Orthodox apologetics against the LDS reduces to "we're right, and so by our standard you're wrong" — valid but unsound, because it "presupposes you're correct when you're actually not." The fix: "shed your presuppositions and critique us internally," and "you have to justify your standard before you're going to attack us with it." Examples: the closed-canon demand ("where does the Bible say it's a closed canon?") and sola scriptura.[^10][^11][^12] Restates [[I'm Right So You're Wrong - The Hidden Presupposition Pattern]] and [[Internal Critique - Two Questions for Every Critic]].
- **Tailor the move to the worldview.** For an *atheist* who objects to Joseph Smith's conduct, Carroll asks "do you believe Joseph Smith had free will?… so could he have done any differently?" — usable on consistent atheists and Calvinists alike. He credits the "four horsemen" ([[Richard Dawkins]], [[Sam Harris]], [[Christopher Hitchens]], [[Daniel Dennett]]) with handing apologists "so much content" on morality, free will, and consciousness, and admits Judaism is the one he still doesn't know well enough, so he approaches Jewish interlocutors with "teach me about this."[^13][^14] Connects to [[The Four-Level Conversation Structure of Apologetics]].
- **Keep your cool because your position is strong.** "A lot of the anxiety in us goes away when we better understand what the other person believes"; once you can "disarm them and tease them a little bit about their own worldview," you stop being anxious — "I got the strongest position here and I know how to articulate it."[^15][^16] Carroll describes early "anxiety attacks," resolved by realizing "no one is going to tell you anything you've never heard before" and "my faith is in Jesus Christ."[^17]
- **You must have a testimony before you step into the space.** [[Roy Anderson]]: if your testimony "was lying on Joseph Smith's shoulders," an attack will make it "crumble." "You have to have a testimony of the gospel and a testimony of Jesus Christ before you step into that space." (He also rebuts the "sex with a 14-year-old" slur as unevidenced.)[^18][^19]
- **Don't justify the talking point — disarm the standard; and don't repeat secondhand attacks you can't source.** Carroll studied "all the anti" (the CES Letter, the ex-Mormon subreddit) "to know how to engage," finding "99.9% is just garbage" with a few "fair" hard issues. The "weird" gambit ("doesn't this sound weird to you?") is met by conceding it *and* turning to Balaam's donkey or "[[2 Kings 3.26-27|Elisha and the she-bears]]" — "incredulity" is not an argument.[^20][^21] Echoes [[Dive Into the Primary Sources Before Repeating the Talking Point]] and [[The Amen Brother Apologetic Strategy]].
- **The "plates were too heavy" argument is self-defeating.** [[Bill McKeever]] spent "30 of his 60 minutes" telling evangelicals the golden plates "would have been too heavy for Joseph Smith to run through the woods with." Carroll's rebuttal: assume the prophet for a second — "if God wanted Joseph Smith to be able to carry those plates… do you think God could have allowed him to do that?" A critic who believes Moses parted the Red Sea cannot rest a case on Joseph being physically weak.[^22][^23][^24] See [[The Plates Were Too Heavy Is a Self-Defeating Anti-Mormon Argument]].
- **The "demonic possession" charge has real-world consequences.** Because the LDS case for Joseph "is so strong," critics retreat to "he must have been demonically possessed" — a move [[Trent Horn]] is set to debate Carroll on ("the real-life consequences of calling a group demonic"). A superchat sharpens it: New-Testament demons "always confessed the true Jesus," so calling Mormons demon-inspired *and* worshippers of "a different Jesus" is incoherent.[^25][^26] Connects to [[Apologetics as Identity Politics Over Evidence]].
- **Apologetics is a shield for a developing testimony.** [[Roy Anderson]]: apologetics "works as a shield for weak or doubting testimonies… I can take the lashings while you go figure out if Jesus Christ is your savior," cutting "the heads off" the accusations (e.g., [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]], [[Romans 5.12|Romans 5:12]]).[^27] Carroll grounds it in [[Jeffrey R. Holland]]'s FAIR address: the Spirit converts (the "heart"), apologetics is the "mind" part ([[D&C 8|D&C 8:2]], "in your heart and in your mind"), and "in the absence of a defense, some people believe mistakenly that it is indefensible." [[D&C 64.30-33|D&C 64]]: some are given to know, some to believe. He parallels [[Alex O'Connor]]: what changes a mind is "a personal experience with God," not a proof.[^28][^29][^30] Restates [[Spirit Over Argument - The Limits of Apologetics]].
- **The church wants this — but it must come from the members, not the apostles.** Carroll recalls a Seventy telling content creators "the church loves what you're doing… but it has to come from you guys. It can't come from the top. The role of the apostles is not to be apologists." He frames the whole effort as a return to the early-church era of "formal debates," lost to a "nicety culture," now revived "from the members."[^31][^32]
- **The pastoral core: most youth are gauging your confidence, not demanding an answer.** A leader approached by a youth "shouldn't carry the burden alone" — use the church's resources, don't rush an answer. Crucially, "most of the youth who come to adults with questions… actually just want to know if you're okay with it." They want to see whether you "cower" or say "absolutely he did — do you want to talk about it?" — so "exude some confidence," because the position is strong.[^33][^34][^35] See [[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]].
- **Don't dunk — least of all at church.** Carroll's tabling is "not there to destroy them"; he saves the teardown for an *apologian*, not a curious student — the public/private distinction of [[Tear Down Worldviews in Public Debate Not Private Ministry]]. In elders quorum, don't parrot a single obscure quote from one general authority (the [[Bruce R. McConkie]] / "Kolob" speculation problem); ask instead "what's the *official* statement from the brethren?" — "unless there's an official statement, we're not held to anything."[^36][^37] The reverse is how critics ("God Loves Mormons") quote-mine an apostle's private opinion out of context, smuggling in prophetic-infallibility ([[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:20-22]], read to the end).[^38][^39]
- **Should every LDS dad do apologetics?** Consensus: "they should know it; they don't necessarily need to go out and do it" — a "Defense Against the Dark Arts," in-case-of-emergency-break-glass skill for when a spouse or child "is falling prey to some of these arguments." Carroll's closing logic for staying: "if I'm going to leave Mormonism, where am I going to go?" — atheism is "absurd," the other Christian traditions "fall apart," Eastern religions have "no truth claims," so the alternatives collapse.[^40][^41][^42]
