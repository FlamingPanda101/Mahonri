# Christianity's Creedal Doctrine Doesn't Come from the Bible. Here's Where It Actually Comes From.

[[Alex Arnold]] of *Moroni's Standard* — a self-described former outsider "with no stake in the answer" — argues that the creedal definitions critics use to disqualify Latter-day Saints (the [[Council of Nicaea|Nicene Creed]], the Chalcedonian Definition, the Athanasian Creed) do not derive from the Bible but from **Greek Neoplatonic philosophy**, formalized at councils convened by Roman emperors and ultimately enforced by imperial decree. The whole episode follows a single thread: not whether creedal theology is *true*, but whether it can function as the "neutral, ancient, apostolic baseline" by which all other Christian claims are measured. His repeated finding: the doctrines presented as the most ancient — God as formless/immaterial/absolute, *ex nihilo*, the Trinity as one indivisible substance — keep turning out to be *late*, and the sources closest to the apostles look least like Nicaea.

Arnold stresses that his authorities are the tradition's own: [[Jaroslav Pelikan]] (Lutheran → Orthodox), [[Rowan Williams]] (Archbishop of Canterbury), [[R.P.C. Hanson]] (Church of Ireland bishop), [[Adolf von Harnack]] (German Lutheran), [[Lewis Ayres]] (Catholic), and [[Henry Chadwick]] (Anglican) — "the people that creedal Christianity's own institutions trained, employed, and cited."

## Summary
- **The creeds are late and philosophical, not apostolic.** Creeds began as short baptismal confessions ("Jesus is Lord"; the threefold name) centered on what *Christ did* — died, buried, rose, appeared ([[1 Corinthians 15.1-4]]). The defining Nicene term *homoousios* ("of one substance") appears nowhere in the Greek NT; it is borrowed from the Neoplatonic tradition. The Nicene Creed in current form dates to 381, Chalcedon to 451, the Athanasian Creed to the 5th–7th c. (and not by Athanasius). *Ex nihilo* (per [[Gerhard May]]) is a late-2nd-c. response to Gnostic cosmology read backward into Genesis — see [[Bara Means Organize and Ex Nihilo Is a Second-Century Invention]].
- **Nicaea was imperial, not apostolic.** [[Constantine]] — an unbaptized layman — convened Nicaea (325), presided personally, made his preference known, and exiled bishops who refused to sign. [[Eusebius of Caesarea]], theologically closer to [[Arius]] than [[Athanasius]], wrote to his congregation admitting *homoousios* was unbiblical and that he accepted it on the emperor's explanation — primary-source evidence of signing under pressure. The argument is about the *mechanism of authority*: binding doctrine in scripture comes through prophets/apostles with direct revelation (Acts 15: "it seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us"), not imperial council. See [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]].
- **The post-Nicene record is oscillation, not consensus.** In the 56 years to 381, Athanasius was exiled five times; Constantius II and Valens backed Arian/semi-Arian formulas; the Council of Sirmium (357) produced a formula [[Jerome]] said bore "the faith of Nicaea in darkness." Nicene orthodoxy stabilized only when Theodosius made it the official religion by decree and outlawed Arianism — "a criminal statute," not spiritual discernment.
- **Greek philosophy supplied what the text lacks.** The Cappadocians ([[Basil the Great]], [[Gregory of Nyssa]], [[Gregory of Nazianzus]]), trained in Greek philosophy, introduced "absolute simplicity — without body, parts, or passions" and a timeless/spaceless/immaterial nature from [[Plotinus|Neoplatonism]] — against a Hebrew God who walks ([[Genesis 32.24-28|wrestles Jacob]]), speaks face-to-face, and has a back ([[Exodus 33.20-23]]). [[Adolf von Harnack]] called this "the acute Hellenization of Christianity." Paul in [[Acts 17.28-29]] used Greek vocabulary as an *entrance* then introduced anti-Greek content (resurrection of the body) — see [[Translation Versus Transformation - The Order of Operations in Hellenization]].
- **The pre-Nicene fathers were subordinationist, and the trend runs the wrong way.** [[Justin Martyr]] ("a second God"), [[Origen]] (subordinate Son, posthumously condemned 553), [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] (Son and Spirit as the Father's "two hands"). The sources *closest* to the apostles are the *most* subordinationist — fatal for any "recovery of original doctrine" narrative. See [[The Trend Line Runs the Wrong Way for the Recovery Narrative]].
- **The creeds cannot be the neutral baseline (circularity).** Even granting the theology might be correct, a historically-situated, philosophically-shaped, politically-conditioned claim "can't function as that neutral baseline." Using it to disqualify others is circular: orthodoxy is defined by the councils, and anything outside is rejected — without ever accounting for where the councils got binding authority. See [[The Creeds Cannot Be the Neutral Baseline - The Circularity of Creedal Disqualification]].
- **Chalcedon's schism indicts the standard.** The 451 definition produced an unhealed 1,500-year schism with the Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, and Syriac churches — communities that *predate* Chalcedon and trace to the apostolic era. A standard that rules out the oldest continuous Christian communities (over a split 20th-c. ecumenists call a terminology misunderstanding) reveals its own inadequacy. See [[The Chalcedonian Schism Refutes the Standard It Defines]].
- **LDS theology fits the older picture; the spirit-guidance defense is symmetric.** Distinct embodied persons, a divine council, and human-divine participation ([[2 Peter 1.4]] *theosis*, attested in [[Irenaeus of Lyons]] and [[Athanasius]], documented by [[Norman Russell]] and conceded by [[C.S. Lewis]]) match the pre-Nicene landscape — confirmation the restoration "describes something historically real." The "the Spirit guided the imperfect councils anyway" defense applies with equal force to [[Joseph Smith]]; the LDS evidential case (Nahom, Dead Sea Scrolls, chiasmus) at least points to something *external* to break the loop.
