# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Street Preachers Refuse to Talk with a Mormon

[[Hayden Carroll]] releases footage, captured "several years ago" at the **Mesa Temple Christmas lights**, of his attempts to engage a team of evangelical open-air preachers — chiefly a man named **[[Diego (Apology Church)|Diego]]** from "Apology Church." Carroll frames it self-critically, noting he was "a little less tactful" then than now.[^1] The encounter is a case study in the *manner* of apologetic engagement: one preacher refuses dialogue outright, claiming superior spiritual standing ("I got the Holy Spirit living inside me. You don't… and that's the end of the conversation"),[^2] while Diego engages but, in Carroll's reading, never answers the question actually asked. The substantive threads are catalogued below and connect to [[Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue]], the [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture|circularity of "the Bible alone"]], and [[Are Mormons Christian (Beshears)|whether Latter-day Saints count as Christians]].

## Summary
- **The refusal to engage a *prepared* interlocutor.** A preacher who will talk to passers-by declines Carroll, who notes the asymmetry: "I just think it's funny that you guys will talk to people who aren't prepared to talk, but when somebody's prepared and ready to chat about it, you say, 'Sorry, I don't want to chat about it.'"[^3] The same preacher closes by asserting privileged spiritual status to end the exchange.[^2] See [[Street Preachers Engage the Unprepared but Refuse the Prepared]].
- **"Why the Bible only?" pressed to a circle.** Carroll's central question is not whether the Bible is inspired (he affirms it is) but *why it should be the **only** source* — "Where does the Bible say we should only use the Bible?" The preacher answers from 2 Timothy 3:16 ("all word is inspired by God"), and Carroll counters that the verse does not say *the Bible alone*, nor could "this Bible" name a canon that "didn't exist when that was written."[^4] When pressed on *how* he knows scripture is God's word, the preacher loops — "Because it's the word of God… written by the Holy Spirit… it's in scripture" — and Carroll names the [[The Circularity Charge Against Self-Attesting Scripture|circular reasoning]].[^5] Both later concede knowledge of God comes "by faith."[^6]
- **The infant-damnation reductio.** Carroll runs a Socratic dilemma: *if* all are "born sinners" *and* one must consciously "accept Jesus Christ" to be saved, *then* every infant who dies is damned. The preacher is forced toward "It could be a possibility… we don't know," and Carroll states the fork: "you have to either deny that we're all born sinners or deny that we have to accept Jesus Christ."[^7] The preacher's fallback — an "age of accountability… at age eight" — Carroll notes is not in the Bible the preacher insists is sufficient (it is in the Doctrine and Covenants).[^8] See [[The Infant-Damnation Reductio Against Inherited Guilt Plus Required Profession]].
- **The "different gospel" / Galatians 1 charge, and "define the gospel."** The preacher invokes Revelation 22:18 and Galatians 1:8 to brand the Restoration "a different gospel"; Carroll asks him to *define* the gospel, gets "the good news," and replies "I agree. Mormonism agrees."[^9] This is the recurring methodological demand to **define terms before condemning**.
- **The Book of Mormon "named the disciples" objection.** Diego's stated reason for rejecting the Book of Mormon is that Nephi names New-Testament disciples "600 years before," whereas Old-Testament prophets "never name them by name." Carroll's rejoinder presses the hidden premise — *why* the Bible's pattern is the standard a *non*-biblical book must meet — and notes the parallel incredulity toward any miracle ("Do you think it's hard for someone to accept that a person came back to life?").[^10]
- **Foreknowledge-and-hell exchange.** A long stretch turns on whether a God who foreknew the lost yet created them is just, and what becomes of those who "never heard of Jesus Christ" — the preacher first says missionaries reach everyone, then concedes "there are some people who haven't heard," then "they are going to hell," a tension Carroll keeps surfacing.[^11] (Documented here as context; the soteriological reductio is captured in the infant concept above.)
