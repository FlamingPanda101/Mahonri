# Ward Radio - Proof the CES Letter Origin Story Was a Lie

A [[Ward Radio]] live stream in which host [[Cardon Ellis]] — joined by [[Jonah Barnes]], Brad Witbeck, and [[Luke Hansen]] — interviews researcher [[Michael Peterson]] about a self-published 70-page report arguing that the *origin story* of [[Jeremy Runnells]]'s [[CES Letter]] ("an honest, distraught member trying to get official answers, asked by a CES director") was fabricated. Peterson, with co-author Jacob Hess, marshals dated forum and Reddit posts to argue Runnells was already an active, adversarial critic of the Church for roughly six to nine months *before* he wrote the letter — and that the "honest seeker" framing was a marketing device (a "shiny wrapper") wrapped around recycled anti-Mormon material to entice faithful members into reading it.

The episode is heavily laced with running satire (fictional "Super Chats," mock photos of Runnells "under sniper fire," self-deprecating "Boomer / Google Slides" jokes), but the substantive spine is Peterson's timeline of pre-letter activity and his reading of Runnells's own later statements.

The argument is steelmanned and answered here: see [[The Shiny Wrapper Was the Bait of the CES Letter]], [[Adversarial Church-Bashing Predates the CES Letter by Months]], and the methodological reply [[Exposing a Public Deception Is Not an Ad Hominem]].

## Summary

- **The contested origin story.** The hosts play Runnells's own clips (from [[Mormon Stories]]-style interviews) in which he says the letter's only intended audience was "the CES director, my family, and a few people on Reddit" for feedback, that it "just caught on" with "exactly zero marketing dollars," and that he was "a distraught and very frustrated member trying to get official answers... in hopes of restoring my testimony." Peterson's thesis: "all of the evidence demonstrates that that is false."[^1]
- **The published timeline of the letter.** Runnells dated his faith crisis to February 2012; he began writing the letter ~March 2013, posted drafts on ExMormon Reddit, and emailed it to a CES director ~April 20, 2013 — so the writing was a roughly month-long deliberate project, not a spontaneous reply to an unanswered query.[^2]
- **Pre-letter adversarial activity (the core "receipts").** Peterson presents ~11 dated posts under the pseudonym "kabot" (a play on *Kolob*) and the flair "I'm on a tapir" (mocking Book of Mormon horse apologetics). Examples: July 2012 "coming out" on ExMormon Reddit; an "Open Letter to Quentin L. Cook" on Mormon Curtain (Oct 10, 2012) whose topic list closely prefigures the CES Letter; coaching a young man to lead his father out of the Church; "how do I save my kids from the church"; linking [[Grant Palmer]] videos; using denigrating acronyms (TSCC, TBM).[^3]
- **The "shiny wrapper" mechanism.** Peterson argues the body of the letter is largely recycled from [[Sandra Tanner|Gerald and Sandra Tanner]], MormonThink, and Grant Palmer's *Insider's View of Mormon Origins* — material a faithful reader would normally set aside — so the sympathetic "honest seeker" introduction was the hook that got people to read it. Reactions online (e.g., commenter "Miss Scrivinsky") responded to the wrapper-story more than the content.[^4]
- **Crowdsourcing and denial.** Runnells posted drafts asking critics for feedback/arguments (not just grammar), invited readers to "personalize it for your TBM loved ones," allegedly helped post it to MormonThink, and later denied involvement in its viral spread — and much of his FAIR rebuttal was reportedly co-written by a Redditor ("mithryn").[^5]
- **"Not an ad hominem."** The hosts repeatedly press whether exposing the author's motives is just attacking the person rather than the letter. Their reply: the letter's whole thesis is that the Church is *untrustworthy/dishonest*, so the trustworthiness of the accuser — and the truth of the origin claim he himself markets on the front page — is squarely "the issue, not an issue."[^6]
- **Named converts to the thesis.** They cite [[Daniel C. Peterson]]-adjacent figures and specifically BYU's Stephen Harper, who said at a recent FAIR conference that he had "wanted to wrap my arms around Jeremy Runnells" but concluded "that's just not what happened, that's not the truth."[^7]
- **The CES director as "cipher."** Peterson argues real "official answers" run through bishops, stake presidents, area authorities, and ultimately the First Presidency — not a single CES coordinator (one of ~150+ teaching staff, not an official spokesman). Citing Brian Hales, he says naming the letter after a CES director lent it "a false sense of credibility."[^8]
- **The questions-as-cudgels claim.** The hosts contend the ~175 "questions" are mostly declarations, not genuine inquiries — a rhetorical knife rather than an open invitation to dialogue; Peterson notes the introduction's irenic tone "collides" with the no-holds-barred attack that immediately follows.[^9]
