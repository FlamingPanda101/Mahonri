# Pangburn Philosophy — Peterson vs Harris on God, Morality, and Religion (Vancouver Night 1)

A roughly two-hour recorded public debate staged in Vancouver, with [[Bret Weinstein]] moderating a discussion between [[Jordan Peterson]] and [[Sam Harris]]. Used as **preface / primer** material in [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* course (Level 1, Fundamentals): a non-LDS philosophy explainer on how one grounds morality, what is wrong with dogmatism, and whether religious narratives carry truth. Despite the adversarial framing, much of the exchange is Peterson and Harris *agreeing* — and then locating precisely where they diverge.[^1]

## Summary

- **The shared problem: two catastrophes to avoid.** Both want to ground ethics so as to escape *both* dogmatic moral absolutism (which Harris ties to "fundamentalism," Peterson to a "pathology of order") *and* moral relativism / nihilism (a "pathology of chaos"). They agree the suffering of Auschwitz is a fixed point — and that the perpetrators were *wrong*, not merely disliked. See [[Two Catastrophes - Dogmatic Absolutism and Moral Relativism]].[^2]

- **The real enemy is dogmatism, not religion per se.** Harris locates the danger in *dogmatism* — believing things strongly without evidence, which "cannot be revised in real time through conversation" and so must be enforced by force. Peterson presses that secular totalitarianisms (USSR, Nazism) share this feature without belief in God, so the common denominator is unrevisable claims of absolute truth, not theism. Both then agree free speech is the corrective mechanism and belongs "at the pinnacle of the hierarchy of values." See [[The Problem Is Dogmatism Not Religion]], [[Free Speech as the Corrective to Dogmatism]].[^3]

- **Tribal violence is deeper than religion.** Citing [[Jane Goodall]]'s chimpanzee raiding-party observations at Gombe, Weinstein and Peterson agree that group-based atrocity is an inherited primate capacity that predates religion — so religious or political dogma is an *amplifier*, not the root, of human aggression. See [[Tribal Violence Predates Religion - The Chimpanzee Evidence]].[^4]

- **Harris's case against scripture: the texts can't be edited.** Harris argues the books endorse slavery, sex slavery, and brutal injunctions (e.g. cutting off a thief's hand) "for all time," that fundamentalists honestly read the plain sense, and that ISIS is closer to the text than liberal believers — who are quietly importing modern ethics from *outside* the text. The exit door from fundamentalism "is bashed open from the outside" by secularism and science. See [[Scripture Cannot Be Edited - The Fundamentalist's Honesty Advantage]].[^5]

- **Peterson's reply: narrative and metaphorical truth.** Peterson concedes barbaric sentences exist but argues a complex *narrative* must be read holistically (the Bible's beginning read "in light of the end"), and defends **metaphorical truth** — claims literally false yet adaptive to act upon. Harris counters that the more you flee the literal text, the more "unconstrained" you become — "playing tennis without the net." See [[Metaphorical Truth and Narrative Reading]].[^6]

- **The metaethical core: can you get values from facts?** Harris grounds morality in the claim that "the worst possible misery for everyone is bad" — a fixed point that ends G.E. Moore's infinite regress and which he calls a *factual* claim. Peterson agrees it is bad but denies it is a *fact*; he calls it a foundational moral intuition, and argues (with Kant) that facts are never unmediated — an evolved, story-shaped interpretive structure always sits between us and the data. See [[The Worst Possible Misery Is Bad - Harris's Moral Floor]], [[You Cannot Get From Facts to Values Without an Interpretive Structure]].[^7]

- **Religious texts as evolutionary heuristics.** Weinstein reframes scriptures as *heuristics* that let people make calculations they cannot otherwise perform, encoding fitness-enhancing behavior from past environments. All three converge: such stories may have been adaptive, but because they are "responsive to past environments," they cannot be trusted as timeless on faith — "useful fictions have to be retired; useful truths stay true." See [[Religious Texts as Evolutionary Heuristics]].[^8]

- **Peterson's definition of God.** Pressed by Harris, Peterson reads a list: he "acts as if God exists," and God is the highest value, the voice of conscience, "that which eternally dies and is reborn in the pursuit of higher being," "that which selects among men," and truthful speech embodied (the "Word"). Harris objects that this is not what most believers mean and so the word is being emptied of its traditional content — comparing it to redefining "ghost." See [[God as the Highest Value and Truthful Speech]], [[Redefining God Empties the Word - Harris's Deepak Objection]].[^9]

- **The masturbation/prayer test case.** To make the heuristic argument concrete, the three analyze why "God might care if you masturbate" as a fitness-enhancing adaptation (channeling release toward early mating/reproduction) and what a "prayer that is answered" might mean mechanistically (penitent self-examination), with no supernatural mechanism required. See [[Religious Texts as Evolutionary Heuristics]].[^10]
