Catholic apologist [[Trent Horn]] argues that medically assisted suicide (and euthanasia) has crossed from a marketed mercy for the terminally ill into a **dystopia already arrived** — a slippery slope on which the sick, disabled, poor, and mentally ill are nudged toward death. The video pairs a **slippery-slope argument** (Where do you draw the line? Once "reduce suffering" is the standard, the category cannot stop at the dying) with a **dossier of systemic-abuse cases** (housing, veterans' care, insurance, a Catholic hospital's lawsuit, a "suicide pod"), then rebuts the three standard pro-assisted-suicide arguments (the animal-mercy analogy, "death with dignity", and "do you just want people to suffer?"). It closes by recommending [[Stephanie Gray Connors]]' book *Start with What: 10 Principles for Thinking About Assisted Suicide*.

This is a **Catholic** source, but its sanctity-of-life ethic aligns closely with the LDS position (the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints opposes elective euthanasia and assisted suicide, while permitting withdrawal of *extraordinary* life support and pain relief that may incidentally hasten death). It complements the vault's existing euthanasia/abortion material — [[Abortion and Euthanasia as Pagan Not Christian]] (Scrivener, on the *provenance* of the practice) and Horn's own [[The Counsel of Trent - My Abortion Debate Youve Never Seen]] (the *what-we-are* grounding of equal rights, which reappears here against the dignity-by-function objection).

## Summary

- **The slippery-slope argument — "Where do you draw the line?"** Horn argues (per the summary) that legalization inevitably expands. Data: the Netherlands assisted a **physically healthy 29-year-old autistic woman** in dying for *mental* suffering (May); in **Canada**, assisted death went from **1 in 50 deaths (2019) to 1 in 25 (2022)** — a leading cause of death, surpassing car accidents and influenza. The logic: society normally uses physical force to stop a person jumping off a bridge, so permitting assisted suicide declares **"some lives are worth saving from suicide, but others are not."** If the justifying goal is *reducing suffering*, it cannot logically stop at the terminally ill — it extends to chronic pain, disability, and depression, "which is happening now." He likens the underlying judgment to Nazi Germany's **lebensunwertes Leben — "life unworthy of life."** See [[Assisted Suicide Cannot Draw a Principled Line]].[^1]
- **Systemic-abuse dossier — the vulnerable are pushed.** Horn marshals cases (per the summary): (1) a **St. Catharines, Ontario** man sought MAID because he was about to lose his housing — $1,200/month disability could not prevent homelessness — and his doctor signed off; (2) a **Canadian veteran** seeking treatment for combat PTSD and traumatic brain injury was instead **offered assisted death** by Veterans Affairs; (3) an **Oregon** woman denied coverage for a life-prolonging chemo drug received an insurer's letter offering to pay for **physician-assisted suicide** instead; (4) **Providence Healthcare**, a Catholic hospital in British Columbia, is sued for requiring MAID-seeking patients to **transfer out**; (5) **Switzerland** approved a 3D-printed nitrogen **"suicide pod"** — Horn notes the hypocrisy that activists champion the pod while calling **nitrogen executions of death-row inmates** cruel and unusual. See [[The MAID Abuse Cases Show Death Offered as Cost-Saving]] and [[The Suicide-Pod Nitrogen Hypocrisy]].[^2]
- **Rebuttal 1 — the animal-mercy analogy fails.** To "we put suffering animals down — why not the same mercy for humans?" Horn answers (per the summary) that we **do not** euthanize humans *because* we owe them **more** mercy than animals: animals are routinely euthanized for being **unwanted or too expensive**, whereas human dignity is intrinsic and **not** set by being wanted or by medical cost. See [[We Show Humans More Mercy Than Animals Not Less]].[^3]
- **Rebuttal 2 — "death with dignity" misunderstands dignity.** Horn argues dignity concerns **how** one confronts death, not the **manner** of dying; losing bodily function or feeling pain does not strip dignity. Catholic teaching permits **painkillers that may unintentionally hasten death** (the principle of double effect), but *actively choosing suicide* offends human dignity itself. See [[Dignity Is How You Face Death Not the Manner of It]].[^4]
- **Rebuttal 3 — "do you just want people to suffer?"** Horn answers (per the summary) that Catholic teaching does **not** require **extraordinary, burdensome, or disproportionate** treatment to prolong life artificially — discontinuing overzealous treatment is licit. And, citing [[Karol Wojtyla (John Paul II)|John Paul II]], suffering is **not inherently pointless**: it can be united to Christ's suffering for a greater spiritual good. See [[Refusing Extraordinary Means Is Not Choosing to Suffer]].[^5]
- **Recommendation.** Horn closes by recommending [[Stephanie Gray Connors]]' *Start with What: 10 Principles for Thinking About Assisted Suicide*.[^6]
