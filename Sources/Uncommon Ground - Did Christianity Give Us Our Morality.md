# Uncommon Ground — Did Christian Faith Give Us Our Morality?

A roughly 2.5-hour episode of *Uncommon Ground with Justin Brierley* (Think Faith / Premier), recorded at Yarnton Manor, Oxford. Atheist philosopher [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] (CosmicSkeptic) stress-tests Christian apologist [[Glenn Scrivener]]'s "air we breathe" thesis — a popularized, confessional version of [[Tom Holland]]'s *Dominion* argument — that modern Western values (compassion, equality, freedom, consent) are historically indebted to, and "unthinkable without," Christianity.[^1]

## Summary

- **The thesis on trial is "unthinkable without," not "blossoming of."** Scrivener distances himself from Holland's stronger "blossoming" framing, claiming only that Western developments "are unthinkable without Christianity" — the cross is "the stone that drops into the pond" sending out "ripple effects." O'Connor presses that this stronger claim is the one that fails. See [[Unthinkable Without Christianity vs Blossoming Thesis]], [[The Dominion Thesis - Western Values Are Christianized]].[^2]

- **O'Connor's core counter: the values predate or arise independently of Christianity.** He repeatedly produces pre-Christian or non-Christian instances — Hammurabi's prologue (protect the weak from the strong), the Hippocratic Oath ("rich or poor, male or female"), Aristotle's just-war limits, Buddhist compassion, Magna Carta as barons with swords — to argue the values cannot be *uniquely* indebted to Christianity. See [[Western Values Predate or Arise Independently of Christianity]], [[The Hippocratic Oath Predates the Christian Medical Ethic]], [[Rights as Self-Interest Asserted Against Power Not Christian Blossoming]].[^3]

- **The Bible problem and the Marcion dilemma.** O'Connor argues Christianity's "attachment to Judaism" is fatal: the Old Testament God commands brutalities (Canaanite conquest, Exodus 22:29's firstborn, chattel slavery in Leviticus 25). The Christian must either become a [[Marcion|Marcionite]] (jettison the OT) or own those texts. Scrivener answers that Christians read Scripture narratively, as a story culminating in Christ crucified, not as isolated proof-texts. See [[Christianity's Attachment to Judaism Undermines the Thesis]], [[The Narrative Reading of Scripture vs Proof-Texting]], [[The Binding of Isaac as a Type of Christ]].[^4]

- **The falsifiability impasse.** O'Connor demands a falsifying condition — something the West could do that Scrivener could *not* trace to Christianity. Scrivener offers "industrial-scale abortion" and euthanasia as genuinely *pagan*, non-Christian practices, but O'Connor charges that any value gets retrofitted as "Christian-ish language for pagan ends." Scrivener replies that history has no control group, so falsifiability (a scientific tool) is the wrong test. See [[The Falsifiability Demand Against the Air-We-Breathe Thesis]], [[Abortion and Euthanasia as Pagan Not Christian]].[^5]

- **Slavery, Exodus, and abolition.** O'Connor argues the Exodus is Israelite-specific liberation (the freed Hebrews immediately take foreign slaves), that the Bible at best condemns transatlantic *brutality* not slavery as such, and that abolition tracks material conditions (Haitian revolt, French Revolution, technology) more than Christian conviction. Scrivener counters that abolitionism is historically unthinkable — [[Gregory of Nyssa]] (c. 379) is the first wholesale condemnation in history — and that Christianity abolished slavery "twice." See [[The Exodus Is Israelite-Specific Not Universal Abolition]], [[Abolition Tracks Material Conditions Not Christian Conviction]], [[Christianity Abolished Slavery Twice]], [[Own the Anti-Abolitionist Christianity Too]].[^6]

- **Consent in the bedroom — the sharpest exchange.** Scrivener cites 1 Cor 7:3-5 (mutual ownership of bodies; abstain only "by mutual consent") as Christianity "thrusting consent into the bedroom." O'Connor reads it as the *opposite*: the only permitted abstention is by *mutual* consent, so a spouse cannot unilaterally refuse — a structure that arguably licenses marital rape. See [[1 Corinthians 7 Consent Is to Abstain Not to Have Sex]], [[Paul Pioneered Mutual Bodily Ownership]].[^7]

- **O'Connor's own metaethics: emotivism.** He affirms ethical emotivism — moral statements are expressions of emotion with no truth-value — but stresses this is "tangential," since he is offering an *internal critique* of the thesis, not a rival grounding for Western values. Scrivener notes the three interlocutors share moral intuitions despite radically different metaethics. See [[Ethical Emotivism as O'Connor's Metaethic]], [[Shared Intuitions Despite Different Metaethics]].[^8]
