# Is Secular Humanism Just Bastardized Christianity?
[[Jacob Hansen]] argues that secular humanism is not a self-standing worldview but an attempt to keep the moral fruits of Christianity — universal human dignity, equality, rights, and the priority of the weak — after cutting away the Christian root that produced them. Drawing heavily on Tom Holland's *Dominion* (with supporting voices from [[N.T. Wright]], [[Steven Pinker]], [[Daniel Dennett]], and [[Pope Benedict XVI]]), he frames secular humanism as a "faith position" that lives on borrowed Christian capital and risks collapse once severed from its source.

## Summary
- The historian Tom Holland's *Dominion* argues Christianity set off "ripple effects" of revolution through Western history (the 11th-century papal reformation, the Reformation, the Enlightenment) that moderns now "take for granted" without seeing their origin.[^1]
- The conviction of a *universal common humanity* with inherent dignity does not come from the Greeks or Romans but from Genesis ("God creates man and woman in his own image"); humanism is an attempt to "abstract this notion from the matrix of Christianity" and let it live without its spiritual framework.[^2]
- Holland (in dialogue with atheist humanist [[Daniel Dennett]]) presses that humanism is therefore "a faith position... no less a faith position than that of a Protestant or a Catholic," "bred of the marrow of Christian civilization."[^3]
- Much of modern moral philosophy — including Marxism — is people trying to "hold on to their Christian moral inheritance without rooting it in Christian theology"; Marx's indignation at the suffering of the poor draws on the Jewish-Christian conviction that "the first shall be last and the last shall be first."[^4]
- The Christian axiom of universal human dignity "lights a flame of social reformation," framing social progress toward a society where that dignity is realized — a goal "totally alien to the Romans, the Hindus, the Greeks, the Aztecs, the Mongols, the Vikings," and essentially every non-Western civilization.[^5]
- Hansen anticipates the objection from atrocities done in Christianity's name: Holland does not see history as a linear line of progress; the Christian ideal "had to claw its way out of the default darkness of the human condition," contending against those who mingled un-Christian interests with Christian teaching.[^6]
