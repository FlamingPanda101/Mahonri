# Ward Radio — Apocryphal Isaiah Reveals the True Identity of Abinadi

A Ward Radio episode in which host [[Cardon Ellis]], apocrypha author [[Jonah Barnes]], Scripture Notes owner [[Oak Norton]], and [[Brad Whitbeck]] argue that the prophet [[Abinadi]] was the lost heir of the royal Nephite (Jacobite) line, and that his much-attacked "sting of death" language is not a Pauline anachronism but a quotation of an *apocryphal Isaiah* — the same lost Isaiah verse that survives in the Gospel of Nicodemus and that, they argue, stood on the brass plates.

## Summary

- **The "sting of death" is apocryphal Isaiah, not borrowed Paul.** Critics call it an anachronism that [[Abinadi]] in [[Mosiah 16.7-8|Mosiah 16]] uses "the sting of death is swallowed up in Christ" — language from [[1 Corinthians 15.55|1 Corinthians 15:55]], written centuries later. The panel answers that Paul was himself *quoting Isaiah*: the Gospel of Nicodemus (Descensus ad Inferos 16:11) has Isaiah declare "O death, where is thy sting? O hell, where is thy victory?" So Abinadi, [[Aaron]] (Alma 22), [[Mormon]] (Mormon 7), and Paul are all quoting a lost Isaiah text — preserved on the brass plates — and the parallel becomes evidence *for* the Book of Mormon. See [[The Sting of Death Is Apocryphal Isaiah from the Brass Plates Not a Pauline Anachronism]].
- **Abinadi as the vanished Jacobite prince.** Reading the book of Omni, the panel notes that [[Amaleki]] (son of "Abinadom"/[[Abadom]]) had a brother who "went with [[Zeniff]]" to the land of Nephi and vanished ([[Omni 1.30|Omni 1:30]]); the name "Abinadom" ≈ "Abinadi"; and [[Ammon]]'s party "mourned the death of Abinadi" decades later. The reconstruction: Abinadi was a trained prince of the record-keeping line who knew the brass-plates Isaiah by heart — which is why he could quote esoteric, otherwise-lost prophecies. This corroborates the existing note [[Abinadi Enacts the Suffering King While Quoting the Servant Song]].
- **Apocrypha as a recurring "player three."** The hosts treat extra-canonical texts (Gospel of Nicodemus) as an independent witness that vindicates Book of Mormon content the critics call anachronistic — the same move the channel applies elsewhere. The episode is heavily sponsored by Scripture Notes ([[Oak Norton]] demonstrates its search and AI features at length; this is not load-bearing).
