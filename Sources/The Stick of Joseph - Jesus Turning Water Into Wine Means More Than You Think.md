# The Stick of Joseph - Jesus Turning Water Into Wine Means More Than You Think

A personal essay read on the Stick of Joseph channel — written by the author as a 22-year-old returned missionary still in recovery from a teenage pornography addiction — that re-reads the Cana miracle ([[John 2.1-11|John 2:1–11]], "More Than Water and Wine") through the lens of Jewish purification ritual. Its distinctive exegetical move: the six stone water-pots "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews" were not empty but held the *used, dirty* washing-water into which dozens of wedding guests had rinsed their impure hands — so when Christ turned that water to the best wine, he symbolically transformed the *accumulated sins* of everyone present. From there the author argues a typological lesson about [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]]: he does not need a vineyard to make wine, only dirty water brought to him, and he can turn faith-destroying sin into faith-promoting nourishment within the kingdom of God.

## Summary
- **The personal predicament.** The author frames the essay with his own teenage state at 17 — addiction, drugs, lying in church, "a willing slave to… the master of lies" — and the peculiar bind that he knew what was right but lacked the *desire* to do it: "I not only needed help doing, but something even more difficult, wanting to do." He needed not just to change, but to *want* to want to change.[^1][^2]
- **The text.** He reads [[John 2.1-11|John 2:1–11]]: at a wedding feast Mary ([[Mary (mother of Jesus)]]) tells the servants "whatsoever he saith unto you, do it"; Jesus has them fill six stone water-pots "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews"; the drawn-out water has become wine, and the governor of the feast judges it the best wine of the night.[^3]
- **How Jewish purification worked.** For Jews then and Orthodox Jews now, ritual hand-washing was constant — after sleep, before and after meals, before prayer. Water was kept in large basins; one poured water over each hand three times with a cup, and the dirtied water drained *back into the basin* to be reused. After dozens of guests had washed their impure hands, that reservoir water would be filthy.[^4]
- **The dirty-water reading.** From the language of verses 6–7 the author infers the pots were already *partly full* (washing-water returned to them), so Jesus merely had them "top them off" — "and they filled them up to the brim." Because John specifies the pots were *for purification*, the state of the water is part of the story: it was "dirty water, unclean, unfit." Even if the pots had been refilled with clean water, the symbolism holds — those pots "symbolically held the sins of all those in attendance that night." See [[The Cana Purification Jars Held the Guests' Sins]].[^5][^6]
- **The faith of the servants.** Drawing out apparently-dirty water and bearing it to the most important guest "was no small faith" — yet "they bear it," and the governor declares it the best wine of the feast.[^7]
- **The transformation, applied.** Mapping the miracle onto his recovery: he reluctantly opened up, began to pray, study scripture, and attend seminary, and "little by little… he started to transform dirty water to wine." Dreaded duties "became delicious to me"; he "was being given desires and attributes that were not mine," adopting the *logos* of [[John 1.1|John 1:1]] by surrounding himself with [[Jesus of Nazareth|Christ]]. The lesson: "Jesus does not need a vineyard to make wine. Dirty water will do, but you have to bring it to him… day by day, pot by pot, from grace to grace." See [[Christ Makes Wine From Dirty Water Not From a Vineyard]].[^8][^9][^10]
- **The wedding-feast typology.** Throughout the New Testament the wedding feast symbolizes the kingdom of God / the celestial kingdom; all who attended "symbolically washed their hands clean of their impurities, as must we if we intend to enter the kingdom of God."[^11]
- **Sin becomes nourishment.** The "beautiful symbolism doesn't end there": once repented of, sins "may become something that nourishes us." "The sting of our sins may be that which leads us to him," and the faith gained in forsaking old sins "may nourish us in the very kingdom we forsook them to enter." "Christ can turn faith-destroying sin into faith-promoting experience" — he "does not need a good man to make a saint. Give him dirty water." See [[Christ Makes Wine From Dirty Water Not From a Vineyard]].[^12][^13]
