# Mormonism with the Murf — Joseph Smith's Top 20 Fulfilled Prophecies

Larry "the Murf" (channel *Mormonism with the Murf*, which "explores church history and the church's truth claims") walks through what he considers the twenty strongest fulfilled prophecies attributed to [[Joseph Smith]], drawing material from Brian Stutzman's book *A Timeline of Joseph Smith's Prophecies* and a Jeff Roundy video on *Larry Saints Q&A*.[^1] The episode is notable for its candor: although the host is a believing Latter-day Saint, he repeatedly puts on his "skeptic hat" and concedes that most of these accounts are late reminiscences written decades after the fact.[^2]

- The host frames the whole list with a methodological caveat — "the vast majority of them are written years after the supposed prophecy happened, later reminiscences," and he suspects that in many cases "maybe something was said from Joseph Smith and then when it was later fulfilled they're then writing it down perhaps making it more specific, more miraculous." This is the recurring theme of [[The Late-Reminiscence Problem in Joseph Smith Prophecy Claims]] and [[Retrofitting a Prophecy After the Fulfillment]].[^2]
- He presents apparently strong cases — the [[Falling of the Stars Prophecy]] (the 1833 Leonid meteor storm landing on the 39th of 40 prophesied days) and the [[Stephen A. Douglas Presidency Prophecy]] — while flagging the [[Absence of Contemporaneous Sources Undermines a Prophecy]] for each.[^3]
- He distinguishes genuinely impressive claims from ones easily fulfilled by the prophet's own agency, articulating the [[Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Objection]] (e.g. Joseph could send Orson Pratt to Jerusalem himself; the Saints could simply choose to migrate to the Rocky Mountains).[^4]
- He treats some claims as weak even on believing grounds — the [[Kirtland Safety Society Bank Failure Prophecy]] he finds "not very remarkable" and more useful as an apologetic than as evidence, applying the [[Apologetic vs Evidence Distinction for Prophecy]].[^5]
- He concedes the believer can still hold these as real prophecies later embellished, while urging caution against presenting them as unanswerable evidence, the posture of [[The Charitable-but-Critical Believer Posture]].[^6]
