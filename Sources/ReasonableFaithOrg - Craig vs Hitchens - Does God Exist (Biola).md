# Craig vs Hitchens — Does God Exist? (Biola)
A formal, classically-structured debate held at Biola University in 2009 before roughly 3,000 people, with overflow audiences across the U.S. and abroad. [[William Lane Craig]] argues the affirmative — defending two theses: that there is *no good argument that atheism is true*, and that there are *five good arguments that theism is true* — while [[Christopher Hitchens]] argues the negative. The format runs opening speeches, rebuttals, cross-examination, closing statements, and student Q&A, moderated by Hugh Hewitt.

## Participants
- [[William Lane Craig]] (affirmative) · [[Christopher Hitchens]] (negative)

## Summary
- **Craig's cumulative case.** Craig advances five arguments: the cosmological (Kalam) argument, the teleological (fine-tuning) argument, the moral argument, the resurrection of Jesus, and the immediate experience of God. He frames the first four as deductive, insisting Hitchens must identify a *false premise* to defeat them.[^1]
- **Hitchens' core strategy.** Hitchens declines to argue *that God does not exist*; he says the burden lies on the believer and that he simply finds every argument for God "fallacious or unconvincing." He presses "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence" and the gap from a bare deistic First Cause to the caring, prayer-answering God of Christianity.[^2]
- **Craig's reply on atheism's burden.** Craig repeatedly notes that disbelief without a positive argument yields only *agnosticism*, not atheism, so Hitchens has given "no argument that God does not exist."[^3]
- **The fine-tuning / "wastefulness" exchange.** Hitchens calls the cosmos cruel, capricious, wasteful, and headed for "nothingness" (heat death), denying design; Craig replies that temporal duration is irrelevant to design and that "we're headed for nothingness" is an *atheistic* assumption that ignores the resurrection and immortality.[^4]
- **The moral argument exchange.** Both agree that without God morality is just an evolutionary by-product; Hitchens challenges anyone to "name a moral act a believer can do that an unbeliever cannot," while Craig insists the issue is not *knowing* but *grounding* objective moral values.[^5]
- **The problem of evil (student Q&A).** Asked about children with AIDS, Craig distinguishes the intellectual from the emotional problem of evil and argues no atheist can show God lacks morally sufficient reasons; Hitchens largely concedes he is "not one of those atheists" who demands God owe an explanation, but indicts specific scriptural injunctions (e.g., the Amalekites) as evil.[^6]
