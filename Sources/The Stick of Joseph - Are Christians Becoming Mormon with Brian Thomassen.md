# The Stick of Joseph - Are Christians Becoming Mormon with Brian Thomassen

Independent commentator [[Brian Thomassen]], in conversation with hosts including [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)]], flips the perennial "Are Mormons Christian?" question on its head and argues the reverse: over the last ~200 years the trend line of broader Christianity has been moving *toward* historically Latter-day Saint positions, not away. Building on a paper by his former boss [[Truman Madsen]] (1974) as expanded by [[David Paulsen]] (2006), he walks through seven doctrines, giving for each Joseph Smith's original position, the 19th-century divergence (the hostile reaction against it), and a 20th–21st-century convergence (mainstream Christianity drifting back).

## Summary
- **The thesis: convergence, not assimilation.** Against online claims that the Church is "cozying up" to creedal Christianity for acceptance, Thomassen argues "our creedal Christian brothers and sisters are coming closer to becoming like Latter-day Saints in both doctrine and practice," so the better question is "are the rest of Christians becoming more Mormon?" The answer he offers: "they want to be. They just don't know."[^1][^2] See [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]], [[Seven Restoration Doctrines the Broader Church Is Drifting Toward]].
- **Point 1 — Charismata and a reopened canon.** Joseph Smith produced 872 pages of new scripture with 99 "thus saith the Lord" declarations and 1831 instructions on gifts of the Spirit, against a cessationist consensus that "the heavens are closed." The convergence: Pentecostalism (65M in the US, 600M+ worldwide) recognizes ongoing revelation, and new-text discoveries (Dead Sea Scrolls, Nag Hammadi) plus the lack of a settled canon (Protestant 66, Catholic 73, Orthodox 76–81 books) undercut the closed, inerrant-canon model. "If you recognize revelation, ipso facto, new scripture is on the board."[^3][^4][^5]
- **Point 2 — A personal, passible God.** Joseph's God weeps (Moses 7) and feels (Alma 7:12); creedal "logic of perfection" rejected passibility as "profane." The convergence: from von Hügel (1912) forward, "theology has no falser idea than that of the impassibility of God," and a suffering, loving God is now common in mainstream theology — contradicting the "without body parts or passions" formula.[^6][^7] See [[Passibility - Mainstream Theology Now Affirms a God Who Suffers]], [[God Is Impassible - Acting Without Being Acted Upon]], [[Without Body Parts or Passions Is a 1530 Protestant Creed]].
- **Point 3 — A social, embodied Godhead.** Joseph taught the Father has "a body of flesh and bone as tangible as man's." The convergence: social trinitarianism ("God is not a solitary being but a living society"; "personality is a necessity in God"), a return of Cappadocian-style relational models, and open theism — though the embodiment piece remains rejected.[^8][^9] See [[The Superlative God Deflection Collapses Into Nothing]], [[Embodied God in Christian Scholarship]].
- **Point 4 — Deification / theosis.** Joseph: "they are gods, even the sons of God" (D&C 76); "God himself was once a man." Augustine was tentative; later critics called deification "blasphemous." The convergence: Paulsen's bibliography found 222 publications on deification — 88% since 1950, nearly half since 1990 — and the 1993 Catholic Catechism quotes Athanasius ("He became man that man might become gods"). Eastern images (three blending lamps, *perichoresis*, the heated sword) illustrate participation; Blake Ostler's fusion analogy pushes toward genuine becoming.[^10][^11][^12][^13] See [[The Deification Publishing Boom Since 1950]], [[Divine Council and Deification Were Native to the Hebrew Bible]], [[Human Deification in Mormonism]].
- **Point 5 — Divine feminine (Joseph-adjacent).** No explicit canonized Joseph Smith statement, but first-presidency statements (1909 Origin of Man; 1995 Family Proclamation) and firsthand reports (Zina Huntington, Eliza R. Snow) affirm Heavenly Parents. The convergence: scholarship now treats "ancient Israel knew a goddess" (Asherah) as near-consensus — Raphael Patai's once "wild-haired" *Hebrew Goddess*, the Kuntillet Ajrud and Khirbet el-Qom inscriptions, the Judaean pillar figurines with their large "wisdom gives sight" eyes — though pulpit acceptance is still "embryonic."[^14][^15][^16] See [[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship]], [[Wisdom Personified as the Mother in Heaven]], [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight]].
- **Point 6 — A self-surpassing God in time.** Joseph: "there is no end to my works, neither my words" (Moses 1) — God is eternally creating and self-surpassing. The divergence: Plato, Philo, Aquinas, and Calvin describe an immutable, timeless God outside time. The convergence: process theology — William James ("God himself may be growing"), Whitehead ("as true to say that God creates the world as that the world creates God"), and Hartshorne (the "self-surpassing" God) — rejects classical immutability.[^17][^18][^19] See [[Open Theism and a Self-Surpassing God]].
- **Point 7 — The fate of the unevangelized.** Joseph: the dead "will possess the same privilege" via priesthood ordinances and Christ preaching to the spirits in prison (1 Peter 3). The divergence: Augustine, Aquinas, Luther, and Calvin held there is "no repentance" after death. The convergence: across traditions (evangelical, open theist, Arminian, mainline, Eastern Orthodox) figures now hope God mercifully saves those who never heard — a "sentimentality" that, Thomassen argues, tacitly admits their revelation is incomplete and points back toward an open canon.[^20][^21][^22] See [[Salvation Is Binary In Christ or Out of Christ Not a Spectrum]], [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **The payoff.** All seven are now live topics in contemporary Christianity, "however, only with Joseph Smith are all of these brought together in one modeling" — he "hit all seven, and he did them before it was cool." The trend lines, Thomassen concludes, favor the Restoration: "we are in a strong theoretical position and do not have to feel apologetic on any level."[^23][^24]
