# Alex O'Connor — There Are Multiple Gods in the Bible, Not Just One (Dan McClellan)

A wide-ranging interview in which atheist YouTuber [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] presses Hebrew Bible scholar [[Dan McClellan]] on whether the Bible actually teaches one God. McClellan argues that strict monotheism is a modern, post-biblical category read *back* into the text; the Hebrew Bible instead presupposes a tiered divine council (a high god, his consort, and the *bene elohim*), and the famous "there is none besides me" passages are rhetoric of incomparability, not metaphysical inventory. O'Connor plays a sympathetic but probing FOIL, repeatedly voicing the Christian/Trinitarian reply (the "us" of Genesis is the Trinity; "no other gods" means strict monotheism) so McClellan can answer it. The conversation also detours into creation *ex nihilo* (McClellan: a late-2nd-century-CE innovation) and the *elohim*-as-judges question in Exodus.

## Summary

- **The plural "let us make man" is the divine council, not the Trinity or a royal "we."** McClellan reads the cohortative of Genesis 1:26 (and the plurals in Gen 3:22, 11:7) as God addressing the deliberative body of gods; the *bene elohim* recur in Gen 6, Job, Deut 32, Psalm 82. The Trinity reading fails because the plural appears only here, not in every divine self-deliberation, making it an arbitrary selective harmonization. See [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]]. [^1]
- **Monotheism is a 17th-century value-laden category, not a biblical one.** "Monotheism" arose as an identity marker (monotheists = good); imposing it on the Bible is an attempt to presuppose ideological continuity with the text, not an analytic tool that helps us read it. See [[Monotheism Is a Modern Identity Marker Not a Biblical Category]]. [^2]
- **Creation *ex nihilo* is a late-2nd-century-CE innovation, absent from Genesis.** Gen 1:1 (*bereshit* as a construct/temporal clause: "when God began to create") describes pre-existing tohu-wa-bohu, darkness, and the deep; *bara* means to fashion/separate, not to make from nothing. Ex nihilo emerges ~170 CE in Christian debates with Gnostics over eternal matter and bodily resurrection; even 2 Maccabees 7:28 and the Greek "from what is not" trade on the Greek convention that unformed matter is "non-being." See [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE]] and [[Bereshit Is a Construct Form Forcing a Dependent-Clause Reading]]. [^3]
- **The earliest poetry preserves the council most clearly.** Narrative prose post-dates ~9th c. BC; older embedded poetry (Judges 5's stars fighting; the "host of heaven" as a divine army) shows the pantheon. Deut 32:8–9 distinguishes Elyon (the Most High) from Adonai, who receives Israel as his apportioned inheritance among the divine sons. See [[Deuteronomy 32 Dead Sea Scrolls Monolatry]]. [^4]
- **The Dead Sea Scrolls + Septuagint reconstruct "sons of God" against the Masoretic "sons of Israel."** The MT's "sons of Israel" in Deut 32:8 is a later softening; the LXX reads "angels of God" (its habit for *elohim*), and a Qumran Deuteronomy fragment preserves "sons of God" — "about as close as you can get to a slam dunk." The same editing shows in Deut 32:43 ("worship him all you gods," recovered from Qumran/LXX, lost in the MT/KJV). See [[Septuagint Angelic Reinterpretation Is Anachronistic]]. [^5]
- **Yahweh in this stratum is a territorial deity who is later universalized.** The Deut 32 picture has each nation assigned to a patron god, Israel to Adonai; Psalm 82 then deposes the negligent gods so Adonai inherits all nations — a post-exilic universalization. See [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]]. [^6]
- **"There is no other" is incomparability/territorial rhetoric, paralleled by Babylon's own boast.** Nathan McDonald (Cambridge) shows "none besides me" is scope-limited ("I'm the only one *for you*," like the Song of Songs' "my dove is one"). Decisively, Isaiah 47 and Zephaniah/Nineveh put the *identical* "I am, and there is none besides me" in the mouth of *Babylon* — which obviously isn't claiming to be the only nation. So the formula can't prove sole existence. See [[Negation Texts Assert Incomparability Not Non-Existence]] and [[Babylon Taunt Formula Shows Negation Is a Boast of Superiority]]. [^7]
- **The "they were not gods, just idols" reply (2 Kings 19) misreads the idol polemic.** The polemic denigrates the idol while still presupposing the deity it indexes: Laban calls his stolen teraphim "my gods," yet separately swears by "the God of Nahor" (a real, non-idol deity, with a *plural* verb "let them judge"); even Paul says idols are nothing yet pagans sacrifice "to demons." See [[Idol Polemic Denies the Idol Not the Deity It Indexes]] and [[Laban's Teraphim and the God of Nahor Distinguish Idol From Deity]]. [^8]
- **In Exodus 21–22, *ha-elohim* means "the gods," not "judges."** McClellan: *elohim* never means human judges; that gloss is post-biblical (2nd–3rd c. CE). The plural verb in Exod 22:8–9 forces a plural-gods reading (the household gods indexing deities before whom oaths are sworn); the NET note even cites Cyrus Gordon and Anne Draffkorn — who argued *against* "judges" — while drawing the opposite conclusion. See [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]] and [[Exodus 21-22 Ha-Elohim Means the Gods Not the Judges]]. [^9]
- **A myological-nihilist / framing aside on Genesis as separation.** O'Connor floats his friend's "God is a mereological nihilist" idea (creation as organizing and labeling), and McClellan notes Ellen van Wolde's argument that *bara* means "to separate" — Genesis as drawing dividing lines (light/dark, waters above/below). Adam's first task is to *name* (taxonomize) the animals. See [[Bara Means to Separate and Genesis Is Organization Not Creation From Nothing]]. [^10]
