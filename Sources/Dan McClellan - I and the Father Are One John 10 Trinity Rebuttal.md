# Dan McClellan — "I and the Father Are One" (John 10) Trinity Rebuttal

A short-form video in which biblical scholar **[[Dan McClellan]]** rebuts a Trinitarian apologetics "content creator" who reads Jesus' statement "I and the Father are one" ([[John 10.30|John 10:30]]) as a declaration of the Trinity. McClellan argues that the Trinity did not exist when the Gospel of John was written, that John itself defines the oneness as a **mutual indwelling** extended even to Jesus' followers, and that Jesus' appeal to **[[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]** only makes sense if the accusation against him is claiming to be *divine* (a god), not claiming to be the very God of Israel. He corrects the apologist's "corrupt human judges" reading of Psalm 82, offers two scholarly alternatives, and frames the whole exchange through his **image christology**.

## Summary
- The Trinity — three co-equal, co-eternal, consubstantial persons of one divine essence — did not exist at the close of the 1st / start of the 2nd century CE; it only takes its formal shape in the 4th century, so John 10:30 cannot be read as asserting it [^1]. See [[Trinity Did Not Exist When John Was Written]].
- John itself explains the oneness as a **mutual indwelling** between Jesus and the Father ([[John 14.9|John 14:9]]), which is then *extended to Jesus' followers* in the prayer that "they may be one as we are one" ([[John 17.20-23|John 17:20-23]]) — i.e. divinization, not consubstantiality [^2][^3]. See [[I and the Father Are One Means Mutual Indwelling]] and [[Johannine Divinization Soteriology]].
- The "they picked up stones, so he must have claimed to be God" argument fails: *any* speech mocking, deriding, or diminishing God counted as blasphemy, so a claim to be "one with the Father" could be blasphemous without being a claim to be Yahweh [^4]. See [[Blasphemy Did Not Require Claiming to Be Yahweh]].
- The apologist's claim that **[[Psalm 82.6|Psalm 82:6]]** addresses corrupt human judges is rejected as a post-biblical (2nd–3rd century CE) renegotiation; *elohim* in the Hebrew Bible is never used of human judges, and Psalm 82 condemns the **patron deities of the nations** in the divine council, deposing them to mortality so that Yahweh can inherit all nations ([[Deuteronomy 32.8-9|Deuteronomy 32:8-9]]) [^5][^6][^7]. See [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges]] and [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]].
- The grammar of [[John 10.33|John 10:33]] is decisive: the anarthrous *theos* ("you, being human, make yourself a god / divine") makes Jesus' Psalm-82 reply coherent; if the charge were "you make yourself the very God of Israel," that reply would be an irrelevant straw man — so the text itself defeats the Trinitarian reading [^8][^9]. See [[Anarthrous Theos Defeats the Trinitarian Reading of John 10]].
- McClellan judges the apologist's *qal va-homer* ("lesser-to-greater") reading weak because it would *lessen* Jesus' divinity, and prefers the earlier rabbinic reading of Psalm 82 as **Israel at Sinai** — made immortal/divine by receiving the word of God, then consigned back to mortality at the golden calf — which fits John's logos christology, since Jesus *is* that word made flesh [^10][^11]. See [[Psalm 82 as Israel Divinized at Sinai]] and [[Lesser-to-Greater Reading Weakens Johannine Christology]].
- Finally, the whole passage is best read through **image christology**: Jesus bears, manifests, and mediates the divine presence and authority, so "whoever has seen me has seen the Father" and Thomas can say "my Lord and my God" — not because Jesus *is* the Father, but because he is a divine image [^12][^13]. See [[Image Christology in the Gospel of John]].
