# The Woman in the Wilderness - The True Story of Eve: Linguistic Archaeology

A conversation between podcast host Joselyn ([[The Woman in the Wilderness]]) and historical linguist [[J Richard Lambert]] arguing that the traditional reading of Eve — a duped, subordinate woman who "messed up" — is a thousand-year mistranslation that inverts what the underlying Hebrew actually says. Lambert reads Genesis 2–3 as *proto-Hebrew*, a "soft" (conceptual, polysemic) language rather than the "hard," one-word-one-meaning English we project onto it.[^1] Through "diachronic linguistics" he claims to peer through the script to where and when later translators reshaped it.[^2]

The central moves: Adam (not Adam-and-Eve) is the one commanded; the cliffhanger structure of the oral tradition leaves Adam "stuck"; Eve is introduced not by name but by her role — *ezer kenegdo* — a word for salvation, making her Adam's deliverer.[^3] The serpent (*nachash*) is read as a symbol of the Messiah, so "the serpent beguiled me" becomes "I foresaw the Savior, and so I partook."[^4] Eve is "found," not surgically built from a rib; she is taken out of humanity and recreated in Eden as a Heavenly-Mother figure.[^5] Lambert grounds the whole project in *concordism* and an [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context|accommodating God]], and frames learning to abandon old readings via [[Steven Pinker|Pinker's]] cognitive psychology of "truth towers."[^6]

## Summary

- **The traditional narrative is a mistranslation.** Lambert sets up the inherited story — obedient Adam, "poor dumb Eve" who breaks the law and "that's why women are horrible" — and says it is sexist and "frankly just not true," contradicted by the text itself.[^7]
- **Persian, not just Hebrew, shaped the Bible.** The Hebrew alphabet/Bible was written down only after Cyrus the Great freed the Jews; concepts like heaven/hell entered via Zoroastrianism (Ahura Mazda / Ahriman), whereas the older view (*Sheol*) maps onto the LDS paradise/prison intermediate state.[^8]
- **Josiah's monotheistic reform was confusion, not conspiracy.** After the boy-king's father was assassinated, counselors read it as "we've lost our god" and purged the temple; Lambert (citing [[Margaret Barker|Barker's]] *Mother of the Lord*) calls the LDS view honestly polytheistic and blames "Stockholm syndrome" for Latter-day Saints assimilating to mainstream Christianity.[^9]
- **Adam alone is commanded.** Using paragraph markers (*pe*/pilcrow/*tifcha*) that mark where the oral unit ended, Lambert argues Gen 2:17–18 is a statement ("if you do this, death will enter") not a threat, and ends on a cliffhanger: Adam must multiply but cannot, with no solution.[^10]
- **"Help meet" is a degrading transliteration.** *ezer kenegdo* was rendered as a transliteration ("help-meet") rather than translated; *ezer* appears only ~25 times in the Bible and every time is tied to salvation (the Messiah, Yahweh, and Eve). Best paralleled by *Ebenezer* ("stone of help/salvation," 1 Sam 7).[^3]
- **Eve was "found," recreated in Eden, not built from a rib.** *wayyiqach* ("took") carries a sense of going out to search and find; surrounding grammar says they "went out and searched for Eve and did not find her." Joseph Smith's [[Abraham 5.14-21|Abraham 5:21]] changes one word so that Eve was *found* for Adam. She is sanctified — taken out of humanity and recreated in Eden in the image of Heavenly Mother.[^5]
- **Eve is named only after the Fall.** She receives the name *Chavah* (rooted in "life / everlasting life," the mother's name) only after partaking; God's question is "what will you *call* her" (rhetorical, like Job 38), a new-name conferral upon the wisdom she has gained.[^11]
- **The serpent is the Messiah.** *nachash* breaks every Hebraic snake-naming pattern; in the Midrash the serpent is the most humble creature, and Moses' staff-into-serpent and [[John 3.16|John 3:14–16]] show the serpent as the symbol of Christ. So Eve "beguiled" = tempted/led = *she foresaw the Savior and chose the necessary Fall*.[^4]
- **The Fall as wisdom, taught by the Church.** Lambert quotes [[Russell M. Nelson|President Nelson]] (2022: Eve acted with "courage and wisdom… she did what had to be done") and Sister Dennis (2023: "a transgression but eternally a glorious necessity") to show this is LDS doctrine, not "the book of Jared."[^12]
- **Divine feminine as the outsider.** Joselyn links Eve to Rahab, Ruth, and Mary Magdalene — women cast as harlots by tradition but not by the linguistics — outsiders who facilitate salvation history.[^13]
- **Concordism and "truth towers."** Lambert holds that pure revelation carries pure truth through the "cracks," even when the surrounding text is sexist; abandoning false "truth towers" is neurologically painful (per [[Steven Pinker|Pinker]]) but yields a foundation without fear.[^6]
