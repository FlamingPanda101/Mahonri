# Informed Saints - The Hoax That Should Have Fooled Joseph Smith

**Channel:** Informed Saints | **Panel:** [[Jasmine Rapley]], Neil, and Steven Smith | **Topic:** the [[Kinderhook plates]] hoax and whether [[Joseph Smith]] "fell for it"

A round-table episode on the **Kinderhook plates** — six double-sided bell-shaped brass plates etched (with acid and beeswax, not engraved) by three Illinois men, planted in an "Indian" burial mound near Kinderhook, dug up before witnesses in May 1843, and paraded into Nauvoo in the hope that Joseph Smith would "translate" them and produce a sequel to the Book of Mormon. The critic's claim is that Joseph took the bait and thereby proved himself a fraud. The panel, drawing on the work of [[Don Bradley]] and [[Mark Asher McGee]], argues the opposite: Joseph never used a seer stone or any revelatory instrument on the plates, "translated" at most a single character by comparing it against his secular tools, sought outside academic authentication, produced no revelation, and quietly let the plates go — behavior that, they argue, both demonstrates his sincerity and is conspicuously unlike a charlatan's.

## Summary

- **The plates were an admitted forgery, and probably a prank, not an entrapment.** Wilbur Fugate, Robert Wiley, and the blacksmith Bridge Whitten cut the brass, etched fake "inscriptions" with acid, and aged the plates. Fugate later confessed it began as "a joke" after reading [[Parley P. Pratt]]'s *Voice of Warning* ("truth will spring forth out of the earth"); the long-assumed *nefarious* anti-Mormon motive is, on Bradley/McGee's reading, overstated.[^1] The dig-it-up-before-witnesses method was the standard 19th-century forger's playbook, and pre-scientific Americans widely believed one could simply happen upon ancient artifacts in the ground.[^2] (On chain-of-custody as the real authentication test, cf. [[Provenance Is the Standard for Authenticating Book of Mormon Artifacts]].)
- **The Saints' excitement was reasonable in context, and not theirs alone.** [[John Taylor]] published facsimiles; Pratt wrote optimistically; but non-Mormons were curious too — the *New York Herald*, the *Quincy Whig* (sarcastically), and the non-LDS observer Charlotte Haven all took interest. Hindsight, not gullibility, is what now exposes the trick.[^3]
- **What Joseph actually did is the crux: no revelatory instruments.** Per Joseph's journal and eyewitnesses, he sent for his **Hebrew Bible and lexicon** (he had studied Hebrew) and had his **"Egyptian alphabet"** brought over — he never called for his seer stone or the Urim and Thummim. A self-identified "Gentile" eyewitness in the *Herald* says Joseph compared the plates to his Egyptian alphabet (the same one tied to the Book of Abraham papyri).[^4] See [[Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation]].
- **The famous "translation" quote is secondhand and reconstructible from one character.** The line that Joseph "translated a portion" identifying the buried man as "a descendant of Ham through the loins of Pharaoh" comes from [[William Clayton]], not Joseph directly. Bradley and McGee match it to a single boat-shaped character on the facsimiles whose entry in the [[Grammar and Alphabet of the Egyptian Language]] reads "honor by birth, kingly power by the line of Pharaoh... possessor of heaven and earth" — so Joseph's whole "translation" plausibly amounts to spotting one familiar glyph and inferring the rest.[^5]
- **Using the Egyptian alphabet does not mean Joseph authored it or used it to "translate" Abraham.** Treating it as a lexical tool (as reliable as his Hebrew lexicon) is a non sequitur from authorship; [[W. W. Phelps]] is the likely primary compiler. This blocks a predictable critic counter.[^6]
- **Joseph sought outside, secular authentication.** Fugate's late letter claims Joseph wanted the plates sent to the Antiquarian Society of Philadelphia (and France/England) *before* translating — and Joseph never moved to acquire the plates, unlike the Egyptian papyri he raised money to buy. See [[Joseph Smith Sought Secular Authentication of Artifacts He Believed Ancient]].
- **The apologetic payoff — and the "game recognizes game" twist.** [[Richard Bushman]]: "The trap did not quite spring shut." No sequel, no revelation, no acquisition. The panel adds that a con man who faked his own plates would have been instantly suspicious of someone else's dug-up plates — yet Joseph took them sincerely, which argues *for* his sincerity. See [[Joseph Smith Did Not Translate the Kinderhook Plates by Revelation]] and [[A Hoaxer Would Recognize a Hoax]].
- **Prophets can be sincerely mistaken; the Church itself broke the forgery story.** The panel compares the episode to the [[Mark Hofmann]] forgeries (the Church bought them precisely to have them authenticated) and notes Stanley B. Kimball's 1981 *Ensign* article was among the first to call Kinderhook a forgery — later confirmed by metallurgical testing of the one surviving plate (19th-century brass). On prophetic fallibility cf. [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]] and [[Prophets Are Fallible Today]].
