# Let's Get Real with Stephen Jones - Joseph Smith's Biggest Discovery (Don Bradley E0039)

**Show:** Let's Get Real (Episode E0039) | **Host:** [[Stephen Jones]] | **Guest:** [[Don Bradley]] (historian, *The Lost 116 Pages*) | **Title:** "Joseph Smith Expert: 'My Biggest Discovery Happened While Out of The Church' | Don Bradley E0039"

A long-form, intensely personal interview in which Bradley calls his reading of **[[Joseph Smith]]'s 1813 childhood leg surgery** "the most foundational of all of the insights" he has found — a discovery he made while an ex-Mormon atheist. The episode interweaves three strands: deep historical context for the [[Word of Wisdom]]; an abductive argument about Joseph's character; and Bradley's own grief over the recent death of his son Donnie, which reframes the whole discussion around the father-son bond and the problem of evil.

- **The puzzle.** Why did seven-year-old Joseph refuse the brandy and wine offered as the only anesthetic for an excruciating experimental bone surgery — at the time of *highest per-capita alcohol consumption in U.S. history* (~7 gallons/adult/year), two decades before the Word of Wisdom existed and before any temperance movement?[^1] See [[Joseph Smith's Refusal of Alcohol Cannot Be Explained by the Word of Wisdom]] and [[Joseph Smith's Childhood Leg Surgery Was a Rare Experimental Operation]].
- **Bradley's answer (abductive).** Joseph didn't merely refuse the alcohol — he *paired* the refusal with "I will have my father sit on the bed and hold me." Read against [[Joseph Smith Sr]]'s documented "weakness for wine," the best explanation is that the boy was *parenting his parents*: protecting his exhausted mother (sent far from the house) and setting a sober example for his father — "if I can do this without alcohol, so can you."[^2] See [[Joseph Smith Refused Alcohol to Set an Example for His Father]] and [[Abductive Reasoning Is How Historians Infer the Best Explanation]].
- **Word of Wisdom context.** The 1833 revelation came less than four months after [[Emma Smith]] nearly died bearing [[Joseph Smith III]]; its promises of health, marrow, and strength to "run and not be weary" answered the household's real need, and the code reads as positive counsel (favor plants, eat meat sparingly), not just prohibitions.[^3] See [[Emma's Difficult Childbirth Is the Immediate Context of the Word of Wisdom]] and [[The Word of Wisdom Is Positive Health Counsel Not Just a List of Prohibitions]].
- **Character and identity.** Citing the principle "we become what we sacrifice for," Bradley argues the surgery both *reflected* and *cemented* Joseph's identity as one who works to "save the fathers" — flowering later in baptism for the dead and the Malachi "hearts of the fathers" mandate. He reframes his old ex-Mormon thesis (Joseph the self-serving opportunist) as the product of a single slanted question ("what was in it for him?").[^4] See [[We Become What We Sacrifice For]], [[A Question Is a Lens That Shapes What the Historian Sees]], and [[Joseph Smith's Father-Son Bond Seeded the Doctrine of Saving the Fathers]].
- **Grief, the problem of evil, and hope.** Bradley recounts losing his faith largely over the problem of evil (child suffering, mental illness), and his return; in the wake of Donnie's death he says he heard himself say "I trust you, God" while screaming. He grounds hope in the **resurrection as the beginning of a new creation** and in doing works for the dead.[^5] See [[Resurrection Is the Beginning of a New Creation]], [[The Universal Desire to Fulfill the Dead's Wishes Witnesses to Objective Moral Truth]], and [[Grace as Self-Compassion for the Sake of Those Who Love You]].
