# Ward Radio — Fake Book of Mormon Artifacts!!!

A [[Ward Radio]] live stream hosted by [[Cardon Ellis]] with [[Jerry Grover]] (geologist/linguist) and [[Josh Gailey|Josh Galey]], debunking forged artifacts misused as Book-of-Mormon evidence. The thrust is unusual for the channel: the LDS panel is the one *attacking* claimed artifacts — Michigan Relics, the Bat Creek Stone, the Los Lunas Decalogue Stone, the Padilla plates — arguing that ruling out fakes is necessary to defend the genuine case. All four artifact debunkings are **new to the vault**; the methodology (provenance, forensics) connects to the existing artifact/provenance cluster.

## Summary

- **The Michigan Relics are 100% forgeries — and Latter-day Saints proved it.** ~3,000 objects from a Scotford-Soper scheme (1890s–1920) made of hot-rolled commercial copper with modern saw marks, unfired clay, a forger's nail-slash signature; debunked by apostle-geologist [[James E. Talmage]] and LDS archaeologist Richard Stamps. [[Wayne May]] still promotes some. See [[The Michigan Relics Are 100 Percent Forgeries]]. [^1]
- **The Bat Creek Stone is probably fake and irrelevant regardless.** Found by the later-implicated forger [[John Emmert]]; inscription matches a Masonic encyclopedia plate; the carbon date dates the mound, not the carving; and even if genuine, its Hellenistic Paleo-Hebrew script post-dates Lehi. See [[The Bat Creek Stone Is Probably a Fake and Unrelated to the Book of Mormon]]. [^2]
- **The Padilla plates were forged to target Latter-day Saints.** Modern jeweler's saw marks, glyphs copied from the Book of Mormon "characters document," art lifted from Chichén Itzá; the related lock stone/stele (held by [[Jerry Ainsworth]]) are unprovenanced and almost certainly from the same forger; a Brazilian hoax even bore a [[Mark Hofmann]] glyph. See [[The Padilla Plates and Related Stones Were Forged to Target Latter-day Saints]]. [^3]
- **Method: forensics can override provenance, and defending faith means ruling out fakes.** The Grolier Codex was vindicated by forensics despite no provenance and [[Michael Coe]]'s skepticism; the panel's rule is to withhold disputed objects and let evidence discipline belief. See [[Forensics Can Override Poor Provenance]] and [[Defending Faith Requires Ruling Out the Fakes]]. [^4]
