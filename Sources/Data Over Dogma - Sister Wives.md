# Data Over Dogma — "Sister Wives"

A *Data Over Dogma* episode in which [[Dan McClellan]] and [[Dan Beecher]] walk through two clusters of "weird stuff in the Bible." The first segment ("taking issue") treats the thrice-told **wife-sister deception**, where a patriarch passes his wife off as his sister to a foreign king — [[Abraham]] and Sarai in Egypt ([[Genesis 12]]), Abraham and Sarah at Gerar ([[Genesis 20]]), and [[Isaac]] and Rebekah at Gerar ([[Genesis 26]]).[^1] The second segment ("what is that?") treats the **teraphim** — household divine images that appear, unproblematically, in the stories of David's wife Michal ([[1 Samuel 19]]), Rachel ([[Genesis 31.19|Genesis 31:19]]), and Micah the Ephraimite ([[Judges 17]]–[[Judges 18]]).[^9]

## Summary
- The same wife-sister story is told **three times** — twice of Abraham and once of Isaac — which McClellan presents as the consensus marker of independently circulating patriarchal traditions later stitched into one lineage, with the arbitrary name-changes (Abram/Abraham, Sarai/Sarah) flagged as editorial seams.[^2][^3] See [[The Wife-Sister Story Is a Tripled Doublet]].
- Within these narratives **polygyny is treated as normative**, but two men claiming the same woman is the actual problem: a single man taking multiple wives passes without comment, yet Pharaoh's and Abimelech's near-marriages to a patriarch's wife trigger divine plagues.[^4] See [[Polygyny Was Normative but Wife-Sharing Was the Transgression]].
- The deception is framed as an **etiology of patriarchal wealth** — each telling ends with the patriarch enriched (Abraham's flocks, slaves, and donkeys; Isaac's hundredfold harvest), the same "get rich by deceiving the powerful" pattern McClellan sees running through all the patriarchal narratives, including Jacob's dealings with Laban.[^5]
- The escalating detail across the three tellings (Genesis 12 is terse; Genesis 20 adds the dream, the half-sister excuse, and the closed wombs; Genesis 26 adds the "Philistine" setting and drops the abduction) illustrates how **traditions accrete explanation over time**; the half-sister rationalization in Genesis 20 is read as an attempt to make Abraham "not a liar," and the divine-name shift to the Tetragrammaton in Genesis 20:18 as a later editorial addition.[^3][^6]
- **Teraphim** were normal anthropomorphic divine images — possibly tools of healing or oracular oath-taking ("bring them to *ha-Elohim*," Exodus 22) — kept in ordinary and even pious households; their physical size varies wildly across stories (life-size in [[1 Samuel 19]], pocket-size in [[Genesis 31.19|Genesis 31:19]]).[^7][^8] See [[Teraphim Were Normative Divine Images Never Condemned]].
- Crucially, in none of these narratives are the teraphim **condemned** by God or the narrator — they are simply a normal, even desirable, feature of early Israelite folk religion (Micah's private shrine, ephod, and homemade priesthood), with no later editorial gloss explaining them away.[^8]
