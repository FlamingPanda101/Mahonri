# Mormonism with the Murph — Is Nahom Archaeological Evidence for the Book of Mormon?

A long-form interview in which host "Murph" (a non-believing former Latter-day Saint) has researcher [[Neal Rappleye]] lay out the case that [[Lehi]]'s Arabian journey in First Nephi converges with real Arabian geography — the [[The Frankincense Trail Fits Lehis Wilderness Journey|Frankincense Trail]], the [[Nahom and NHM Converge with Nephis Eastward Turn|NHM tribal region]], and [[Bountiful and Khor Kharfot Match the Book of Mormon|Bountiful]] — and then point-by-point answers the criticisms raised by [[Dan Vogel]] in his "debunking Nahom" video and by the LDS Discussions episode.

## Summary
- Rappleye frames the whole project the way Arabian-studies scholars treat *any* ancient travel account (e.g. Strabo's Roman expedition): you cannot trace an exact route, so you correlate the text's directional data with the known logical trade routes. By that method, Lehi's trail fits the Frankincense Trail. [^1]
- He stresses that the predictions came *first*: [[Hugh Nibley]] and the Hiltons (incl. [[John Hilton III]]'s parents) argued Lehi followed the incense trail and placed Bountiful in Dhofar, and interpreted [[1 Nephi 16.34|1 Nephi 16:34]] ("the place which was called Nahom") as a *pre-existing* name to be found where the trail turns east — all before the Nehem maps and altars were known. [^2]
- The crucial clarification: the NHM votive altars (Bar'an temple, ~Marib) were *not* found at Nahom; they name a dedicant "from the region of Nehem **west of Marib**," dating the *tribal region* (~5,000 sq km around the Wadi Jawf) to the 6th–7th century BC — see [[Nahom Names a Tribal Region Not the Altar Findspot]]. [^3]
- He argues there is essentially zero chance [[Joseph Smith]] used a Niebuhr/d'Anville map: the nearest known copy was ~300 miles away, it spelled the name differently, and it carried no trails, eastward turn, or fertility cues — see [[Joseph Smith Could Not Have Used a Nehem Map]]. [^4]
- Against [[Dan Vogel]]'s Valley-of-Lemuel reading, Rappleye invokes the exegetical principle that the detailed narrative governs the chapter summary, not vice versa — see [[The Detailed Narrative Outranks the Chapter Summary in First Nephi]]. [^5]
- Against Vogel's strongest argument (that the text keeps the party on the Red Sea coast all the way to Nahom), he shows the last reference to "the borders near the Red Sea" is at Shazer, ~1,000 miles north of Nahom, and the broken-bow/Nahom account never mentions the sea — see [[Nahom Need Not Lie on the Red Sea Coast]]. [^6]
- He answers the secondary criticisms: the "no other people mentioned" objection (a spiritual abridgment written decades later — cf. [[Brant Gardner]]'s "record of ethnogenesis"), the "steel bow" anachronism (a composite bow with metal fittings), burials at Nahom (extensive turret tombs along the Wadi Jawf), Bountiful's timber (tamarind heartwood / coconut palm), and iron ore (geologist William Revell Phillips' samples). [^7]
- On the Frederick G. Williams "19th degree" statement, Rappleye notes it is almost certainly *not* a Joseph Smith revelation ([[Stephen Smoot]]'s *Uncanonized Revelations*; [[Don Bradley]]'s work attributing it elsewhere), and that its latitude is wrong for the maps anyway. [^8]
- His overall verdict: not a "slam-dunk" or "bullseye" in the strict sense — that standard is unfair for any ancient text — but a multi-line convergence "as strong as I think it could possibly be," which he finds extraordinary; he holds [[Convergence Not Proof Is the Standard for Book of Mormon Historicity|convergence, not proof]], is the right standard, and that bias is unavoidable on both sides. [^9]
