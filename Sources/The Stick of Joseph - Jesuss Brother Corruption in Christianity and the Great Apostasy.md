# The Stick of Joseph — Jesus's Brother, Corruption in Christianity, and The Great Apostasy

A [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Stick of Joseph]] episode with [[Mike Day]] and [[David Butler (Stick of Joseph)|Dave Butler]], hosted by the channel's Marine hosts [[Hayden Paul|Hayden]] and [[Jackson Paul|Jackson]] (recording around a military deployment). It is a shorter, more conversational companion to the much longer [[Ward Radio - The Great Apostasy Deep Dive with Mike Day and Dave Butler|Ward Radio Great Apostasy deep dive]], revisiting the same thesis: that primitive Christianity was plural and contested from the start, that [[James the brother of Jesus|James]] — not [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]] — led the Jerusalem church, and that the [[Great Apostasy]] was a gradual corruption culminating when imperial power married religion.

## Summary

Day opens on the "money text" [[2 Thessalonians 2.3|2 Thessalonians 2:3]], noting *apostasia* (*apo* + *histēmi*, "standing away") and the early-Christian expectation of an imminent Second Coming that Paul has to pull back. He recounts Robert Eisenman's radical Dead Sea Scrolls thesis and its effect on James scholarship — see [[Paul Invented Christianity as a Cloak for James's Nazirite Judaism]] — and reaffirms (as in the companion video) that [[Acts 15 Shows James Deciding Not Peter|James, not Peter, renders the verdict in Acts 15]] and that [[Paul vs James and the Judaizer Conflict|Paul and James were in open conflict]] (the [[Galatians 5.12|castration wish]]).

The conversation then turns to canon and authorship: the gospels post-date Paul and "put out fires" (à la Bart Ehrman), and over half the New Testament is attributed to a man who never walked with Jesus — with several letters likely pseudonymous. See [[Over Half the New Testament Comes From a Man Who Never Walked With Jesus]]. Day sketches the post-apostolic progression — the apostolic fathers (authority by [[Authority in the Early Church Was Proximity to the Apostles|proximity to the source]]), then the apologists who defended the faith to Greek thinkers by identifying Christ with Aristotle's [[Unmoved Mover Reshaped God the Father|unmoved mover]] and the *logos* — which he argues began [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God|Hellenizing the doctrine of God]]. He catalogs the rival first-century christianities ([[Docetism and Early Christological Diversity|Docetists]], adoptionists, [[Docetism and Early Christological Diversity|Ebionites]], Gnostics).

Day locates the decisive corruption not in any single creed but in the marriage of imperial power to Christianity — see [[Marrying Imperial Power to Religion Is the Decisive Corruption]] (citing Joseph Fielding Smith, Philip Jenkins's *Jesus Wars*, and the Bruce Shelly history). Butler closes with the restorationist frame that Christianity and Judaism were meant to be a single ancient tradition, so their very separation is itself evidence of apostasy — see [[Christianity and Judaism Were Meant to Be One Religion]].
