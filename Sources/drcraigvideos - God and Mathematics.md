# God and Mathematics
An animated [[William Lane Craig]] explainer arguing that the "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics" in describing the physical universe is best explained by God. Drawing on Eugene Wigner, Galileo, and Philo of Alexandria, it contends that naturalism can only call the applicability of math a "happy coincidence," while theism explains it: the same Mind that designed the cosmos on a mathematical model also built it on that model.

## Summary
- Abstract, causally inert mathematical entities (numbers, sets, equations) nonetheless describe the physical world with stunning precision — the laws of physics are all expressed as mathematical equations.[^1]
- A track record of math *predicting* physical reality (Newton's gravity, Maxwell's radio waves, Einstein's relativity confirmed at the 1919 eclipse, the Higgs boson found 48 years later) shows math reflects real aspects of the world, not mere bookkeeping.[^2]
- Nobel physicist Eugene Wigner called this effectiveness "a miracle... which we neither understand nor deserve"; philosophers split into naturalists and theists in answering why.[^3]
- Naturalism's "the world just happens to have a mathematical structure" fails twice: much applied math (imaginary numbers, infinite-dimensional spaces) cannot be physically realized, and it never explains *why* the structure is so elegant.[^4]
- Theism explains it via Philo's architect analogy and yields the argument: if God does not exist the applicability of math is just a happy coincidence; but it is not just a happy coincidence; therefore God exists.[^5]
