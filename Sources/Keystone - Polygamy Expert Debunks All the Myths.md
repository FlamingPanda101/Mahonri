# Keystone — "Polygamy Expert Debunks ALL the Myths"

A [[Keystone]] interview in which Latter-day Saint women's-history specialist [[Britney Chapman Nash]] works through a list of misconceptions about nineteenth-century plural marriage one at a time. Her governing method is to resist "painting polygamy with a broad brush": every plural marriage "is different," so the corrective to caricature is the documented diversity of real people's lived experience in their own words.[^1]

## Summary
- **Men were not primarily motivated by sex.** Sexual gratification "may have been true for some," but the records show many men "dragging their feet," because plural marriage carried heavy temporal and emotional responsibility; "to invent a theology just to entitle a man to more sexual partners just doesn't fully make sense" when easier paths to sex existed.[^2] See [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]].
- **Neither "all men loved it / all women hated it."** Experience spanned a spectrum; some women resented it lifelong, some thrived and loved their sister wives, and it was "not uncommon for a first wife or second wife to seek out an additional wife for her husband." Utah's unusually liberal, near-"no-fault" divorce laws functioned as a "pressure release valve."[^3]
- **Doctrine was not uniformly understood.** Unlike the correlated modern Church, the 19th-century Church "was not united in the same way"; plural marriage began privately/secretly (1841), was not announced publicly until 1852, and "folk doctrine seeped in," producing genuine confusion — even contradiction within a single sermon — about its eternal implications.[^4]
- **Exaltation did not require polygamy** (despite real period confusion that it might). After the 1890 Manifesto, messages clarified; [[Marcus Nash|Elder Marcus B. Nash]]'s 2015 article confirms (from [[Orson Pratt]]'s carefully recorded ceremony) that "the covenants and blessings are identical between both polygamous and monogamous marriages."[^5] See [[The Covenants and Blessings Are Identical in Monogamous and Plural Sealings]].
- **Coercion is a per-story question — including for first wives.** Some felt social, religious, or theological pressure (men too, for ecclesiastical example); women fiercely rejected the "duped sheep" stereotype (May Talmage's 1883 *Woman's Exponent* letter). But [[Doctrine and Covenants 132|D&C 132]]'s "law of Sarah" could pressure a *first* wife, whose objection without "good reason" could be disregarded.[^6] See [[First Wives Could Be Coerced by the Law of Sarah]].
- **The missionary-trafficking panic was a caricature.** Lurid English/Scandinavian cartoons of lecherous Mormon men luring chained women to Utah misrepresent missionary work, though some converts did meet future spouses abroad.[^7]
- **Most Utahns were not polygamous, and the practice declined steadily.** Using [[Kathryn Daynes]]'s study of Manti, plural-family membership (husband, wife, or child) peaked at 43.1% (1860) and fell to 36% (1870), 25.1% (1880), and 7.1% (1900); most polygamous men had just two wives.[^8] See [[Polygamy Was a Minority Practice That Declined Steadily]].
- **Monogamy was not "fair" by contrast.** 19th-century monogamous American women often could not own property, vote, escape domestic violence, or keep custody on divorce; Utah women — *because of* polygamy's accommodations — could own property, voted from 1870, divorced while keeping children, and pursued higher education.[^9] See [[Nineteenth-Century Utah Women Had More Rights Than Monogamous American Women]].
- **Modern questions.** No indication the Church would reinstate polygamy if legalized (agency is central); men can still be sealed to multiple women while women since 1998 can be sealed to multiple men after death ("seal them all and we'll figure it out"); belief that 19th-century polygamy was "right" is not a test of faithfulness — the temple-recommend question on polygamy was required only in 1856.[^10] See [[Belief in Polygamy Was Required for the Temple Only in 1856]].
