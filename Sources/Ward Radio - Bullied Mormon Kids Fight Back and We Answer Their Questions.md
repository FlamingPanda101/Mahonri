The hosts of [[Ward Radio]] — [[Cardon Ellis]] (host), [[Jonah Barnes]] ("associate professor of all things apocryphal"), and guest [[Ryland Hutchinson]] of *Ether's Elephant* — run a livestream built around a real **teachers-quorum activity**: Latter-day Saint teenagers wrote down the questions they get "brigaded" and "peppered" with at school by peers (often, the hosts argue, coached by pastors to confront their Mormon classmates), and the panel fields them live. The format is a rapid-fire Q&A — each host gives a 60-second answer, then the chat is invited to out-answer them via super-chat — interleaved with a long opening monologue on recent anti-LDS hostility (the Grand Blanc, Michigan chapel shooting; the *Daily Wire* / Matt Fradd episode; Mark Driscoll's *Why Mormons Aren't Christian* rushed out after the shooting) and heavy comedy. The stated mission: equip youth to *not* "run away scared" the way their classmates' pastors predict they will.

Most of the runtime is a **grab-bag of already-familiar objections**, and the substantive theology is asserted at speed rather than developed — so the episode spawns *no* new doctrinal concepts. Its genuine contribution is **methodological**: two reusable tactics for fielding a hostile question, isolated below, plus a near-verbatim restatement of the vault's existing confidence-over-recall thesis.

## Summary

The objections raised by the teachers quorum, each already covered in the vault and wikilinked rather than duplicated:

- **"Do Mormons wear weird underwear?"** → garments / sacred clothing. The hosts compare the garment to priests' vestments, a graduate's cap and gown, the Jewish *yarmulke*, and a Christian's cross. Covered in [[The Garment Is the Covering of Christ]] and [[We Wear the Divine Name - The Tetragrammaton on the High Priest and the Garment]]. [^1]
- **"Did Joseph Smith marry a 14-year-old?"** → [[Helen Mar Kimball]]. The hosts' whole reply is the *sealing ≠ marriage* distinction. Covered in [[Joseph Smith and Helen Mar Kimball Sealing]] and [[Plural Marriage Was Sealing Not Sex]]. [^2]
- **"Do Mormons get their own planet?"** → exaltation / deification, sourced loosely to D&C 130 (the white-stone Urim-and-Thummim passage) and dismissed by Jonah as a "speculative" extrapolation people "draw out" of a single statement. The doctrine is covered in [[Human Deification in Mormonism]] and [[D&C 130.20-21]]; Cardon frames "you get your own planet" as a pejorary on par with "sky daddy" (captured in the new method note below). [^3]
- **"Do Mormons worship frogs?"** → the panel traces this to the [[Mark Hofmann]]-forged **salamander letter** (with "frog" a garbled mutation), and Cardon delivers the strongest factual answer of the episode: Hofmann was a *bomber and forger*, the salamander letter was a fraud, and its only evidentiary value is as proof of how far anti-Mormons will go. Covered in [[Anti-Mormon Tropes Trace Back to a Few 1830s Forged Affidavits]]. [^4]
- **"Were the early prophets racist?"** → [[Brigham Young]], slavery, and the curse of Cain. The hosts argue Joseph Smith ordained Black men; Brigham took in, freed, and raised an enslaved girl, abolished the Native American slave trade, and was "less racist than the people of his day"; and that LDS racism was a *later* borrowing from Protestant neighbors. Covered in [[Brigham Young Personally Opposed Slavery]], [[Brigham Youngs Slavery Law as a Transition to Freedom]], [[The Curse as Division of Labor Not Punishment]], and [[Native American Slavery and the Buy-or-Watch-Them-Die Dilemma]]. (Cardon also recycles the vault's point that viral "racist Brigham Young quotes" have been caught fabricated by ChatGPT — see [[Journal of Discourses Misrepresents Brigham Young]].) [^5]
- **"Why does the Book of Mormon contradict the Bible?"** → the hosts split: deny it touches anything that matters (Christ as Savior), *and* cheerfully grant the Bible contradicts *itself* (two creations, two floods, Joseph's genealogy, Judas's death) so the Book of Mormon correcting it is a feature. Covered in [[Bible Contradictions - McClellan Example Catalog]] and [[Doctrinal Development Is Not Doctrinal Contradiction]]. [^6]
- **"Is there a secret Mormon bunker with artifacts?"** → the Granite Mountain Records Vault. Played almost entirely for laughs ("it's next to the Ark of the Covenant and the Stargate to Starbase Colob"); the only serious content is that the Church preserves genealogical/temple records "because it cares more about genealogy than money." No standalone vault note exists or is warranted — it is folded into the premise-validation method note below as the clearest example of *conceding* a loaded frame. [^7]
- **"Why can't you date before 16?"** → the hosts note there is no hard doctrinal rule, only a cultural guideline, and pivot to the joke "are you asking me out right now?" No dedicated note needed.

A **recurring theological foil**, not an objection from the youth: Jonah Barnes's anti-**Deuteronomist** position resurfaces (a super-chatter's "#down with the Deuteronomists"), and he steelmans the *standard* defense of Deuteronomy before reaffirming his view that the Deuteronomistic reform corrupted the gospel. Already thoroughly covered — see [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament]] and [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology]]; spawns nothing new here.

### New concept notes spawned (method, not doctrine)

The episode's two original contributions are both about *how* a Latter-day Saint youth should field a hostile question:

1. **[[Deny the Loaded Premise Before You Explain]]** — the running "you validated the premise again" correction. A *true* answer ("yes, we have sacred clothing") loses if it ratifies the question's smuggled pejorative ("*weird* underwear," "*secret* bunker," "*married* a 14-year-old"); deny the loaded word first, then substitute the accurate description. Ryland is run as the live foil.
2. **[[Lean Into the Meme - Humor as Apologetic Disarmament]]** — for the insincere drive-by question, own the caricature ("my planet's gonna have rings, it's gonna be lit"). The joke answers the *real* question ("are you someone I can ask, or will you run away scared?") and plays the long game with the silent "bleachers."

A third pillar of the segment — Jonah's "decision tree" (is the asker *sincere*? what they really want is to see if you'll *cower*) and the three reminders ("play the long game," "don't forget the bleachers," "it's okay to have questions") — is a near-verbatim restatement of the vault's existing **[[Youth Raising a Concern Are Gauging Your Confidence Not Demanding an Answer]]** and the missionary-triage logic of **[[Missionaries Are Tour Guides Not Question Answerers]]**, so it is wikilinked rather than re-noted.
