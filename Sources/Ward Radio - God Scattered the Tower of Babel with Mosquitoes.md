# Ward Radio — God Scattered the Tower of Babel with Mosquitoes

Host [[Cardon Ellis]] interviews researcher [[Jerry Grover]] (geologist/engineer), who applies
scientific and historical-linguistic reasoning to the Jaredite "confounding of the language,"
proposing it was not the instantaneous birth of all human languages but a **local, gradual,
physically-caused** breakdown of speech — most plausibly a **mosquito-borne encephalitis** amid an
extended Euphrates flood.

## Summary
- Grover argues the traditional "all languages came from Babel ~2500 BC" reading conflicts with
  historical linguistics (Egyptian already existed by ~3000 BC; language families don't radiate from
  one Mesopotamian point), so the event must be understood differently. See
  [[Ether Reframes the Tower of Babel as a Local Mosquito-Borne Encephalitis Event]].[^1]
- The Book of Ether clarifies the *scope*: the brother of Jared's people prayed both not to be driven
  out and not to be "confounded," implying a *spreading* condition they could see affecting others —
  a worry about mutual unintelligibility among a specific group, not a global language origin.[^2]
- Grover's mechanism: viral encephalitis (spread by mosquitoes/ticks) causes slurred speech, anomia,
  echolalia, perseveration, impaired comprehension, hearing loss, and muteness — matching a breakdown
  in *understanding and producing speech*. Extended flooding both forced abandonment of the city ("the
  driven out") and bred the mosquito vector.[^3]
- He adds a linguistic flourish (reconstructed water/saturation roots behind "Babel," per Abarim
  Publications), while noting the Book of Ether never calls it "Babel" — that name is a later Hebrew
  addition. He frames the whole as God "working through natural means," like the plagues.[^4]
- Honest caveats voiced on-air: the proposal is "new," "has not gone through peer review," and is "not
  supported in the archaeological record, but not disproven either." Cardon presses the strongest
  objection — that disease-aware ancients would have noted a plague, yet scripture mentions none; Grover
  answers that the mosquito-virus link was imperceptible before the 1800s.
