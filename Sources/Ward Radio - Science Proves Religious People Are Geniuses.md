The hosts of [[Ward Radio]] — host [[Cardon Ellis]] with guests [[Jonah Barnes]] and [[Austin Falter]] — set out to debunk a trope Cardon ranks among his "top 10 commandments of anti-Mormonism": *you're stupid if you believe.*[^1] Barnes presents data he had dropped in the show's Discord, sourced to political scientist [[Ryan Burge]]'s site **graphsaboutreligion.com**, which crosses educational attainment against irreligiosity. The headline finding inverts the trope: in the U.S. survey data, **the more educated a person is, the *more* likely they are to be religious and to attend services**, not less.[^2] The episode walks through several of Burge's charts — the aggregate education-vs-"none" bar graph, the same trend broken out by race, the inverse weekly-attendance chart, an attendance-by-age cut, and an LDS-only education-vs-attendance chart — then closes on a Pew chart of daily prayer and out-of-services scripture reading. Much of the runtime is the channel's usual comedy (spoofing "graphsaboutreligion" spelling for "the anti-Mormons," a running "statisticians are paid off by the Church" conspiracy bit, and a Dallin H. Oaks "have more babies" tag), but the substantive spine is a single sociological inversion worth isolating.

## Summary

**1. The core claim — educated people are *more* religious, not less.** Reading Burge's main bar graph (x-axis = education from "no high school" to "postgraduate"; bar height = share identifying atheist/agnostic/nothing-in-particular), Barnes notes the irreligious share *falls* as education rises: 39% (no high school) → 34% (high-school grad) → 36% (some college) → 33% (2-year) → 33% (4-year) → 31% (postgraduate).[^3] He stresses this is the "complete opposite" of the trope, and adds an interpretive aside that finishing postgraduate work takes a "diligence and persistence" that "something deeper than consumerism" — i.e. religion — helps supply.[^4] Captured in the new note [[Educated People Are More Religious Not Less]].

**2. The trend is robust across demographics — it is signal, not an immigrant artifact.** Anticipating the objection that "very religious Hindu immigrants" or a single faith might skew the aggregate, Barnes shows Burge broke the data out by race (white, Black, Hispanic, Asian, all-others) and the downward trend in irreligiosity persists in every group — steepest for Black respondents (42% → 21%), flattest for Hispanic (34% → 32%, because they "come in being better believers" with low baseline irreligiosity).[^5] An *inverse* chart (share attending weekly, trending *up* with education) holds across the same racial cuts, and an attendance-by-age chart holds across every decade (20s–70s).[^6] Barnes makes the methodological point that a boring, flat-everywhere result is exactly what a "very, very strong and robust correlation" looks like. The robustness-as-signal logic parallels the vault's [[Variance Between Groups Is Real Signal Rebuttal]].

**3. The pattern holds inside the LDS Church.** Burge's LDS-only chart (years of education vs. share "always" attending, split by sex) shows attendance climbing with education for both LDS men and women; LDS women out-attend men at every level until the most-educated band (17+ years), where postgraduate men edge ahead.[^7] This localizes the general finding to the hosts' own tradition.

**4. Closing Pew data — LDS lead daily prayer and scripture reading.** The episode ends on a Pew chart where Latter-day Saints (with evangelicals) top *daily prayer*, and where **76% of Latter-day Saints report reading scripture outside of religious services**, against a second-place 66% tie (historically Black churches and evangelicals).[^8] This devotional-superlative material overlaps and reinforces the vault's existing [[Latter-day Saints Are the Empirical Archetype of American Religiosity]] and so spawns no new concept here.

**Caveats and what does *not* become a note.** Every figure is read off screenshots; the hosts never open Burge's or Pew's underlying data on-air, and they repeatedly concede the relationship is *only correlation* — explicitly floating "a solid family," married parents, and good community as a possible confounding common cause of both education and religiosity.[^9] The "religion supplies the diligence to finish a PhD" aside is a causal gloss the data cannot bear. The political-polarization observation (educated → more liberal → less religious, which the hosts note runs *opposite* to their chart and concede is where data "corruption or misinterpretation" is likeliest) restates the vault's [[Politics Predicts Religious Disaffiliation]] and is wikilinked rather than duplicated. The steelman of the secular critic is developed in the concept note below and leans on [[Survey Data Is Unreliable Objection]].
