# Dan McClellan — "John 1:1 Does Not Say Jesus Is God"

A short-form video in which biblical scholar **[[Dan McClellan]]** responds to a Trinitarian apologetics "content creator" who had critiqued McClellan's earlier four-part series arguing that [[John 1.1|John 1:1]], [[John 8.58|John 8:58]], [[John 10.30|John 10:30]], and [[John 20.28|John 20:28]] do **not** assert that Jesus is the God of Israel. McClellan's central move is that **[[Consubstantiality|consubstantiality]]** — three persons sharing one substance/essence/being — is a **later philosophical framework** unavailable in the first century CE, so it cannot be read out of John. He then dismantles the creator's two specific arguments: (1) that the on-screen Greek of John 1:1c proves "the Word was the God of Israel," and (2) that because [[Genesis 1.1|Genesis 1:1]] makes God the creator and [[John 1.3|John 1:3]] makes the Logos the creator, the Logos *is* the God of Israel. See [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]] and [[Trinity Did Not Exist When John Was Written]].

## Summary
- "Consubstantiality of three persons occupying one substance, one essence, one being that is God is a philosophical framework that did not exist in the first century CE," so it is anachronistic to find it asserted in John [^1]. See [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]].
- **Greek word-order blunder.** The creator read the *interlinear* gloss as if it were the actual Greek of John 1:1c. It is not: an interlinear reorders the Greek to track the English. The real order is *theos ēn ho logos* — **theos comes first**, not last ("*hina logos theos*" / "*ho logos theos*" is the creator's mistaken sequence). Not knowing this signals the creator "doesn't really know Greek." The substantive grammar of the anarthrous pre-verbal *theos* is treated elsewhere [^2][^3]. See [[John 1-1c Anarthrous Theos and Subordinate Divinity]] and [[John 1-1 - Qualitative Theos (And the Word Was God)]].
- **The creation argument misreads John 1:3.** The syllogism "Genesis 1:1 = God created; John 1:3 = the Logos created; therefore Logos = the God of Israel" fails because John 1:3 says all things came to be *dia* ("through / via") the Logos. The Logos is therefore the **intermediate / instrumental** agent of creation, **not the primary originating** agent (who is God the Father) [^4][^5]. See [[John 1-3 - The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation Not the Originator]].
- McClellan documents the *dia* reading from four standard reference works he shows on screen — **BDAG** (sense 4 of διά: marker of personal agency, "Christ as intermediary in the creation of the world, John 1:3"); **[[Lydia Novakovich]]**'s *John 1–10: A Handbook on the Greek Text* (Baylor; "secondary, intermediate agency... the primary ultimate agent of creation is God"); **[[Daniel Wallace]]**, *Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics* (1996; ultimate agency → Father with *hypo*, intermediate → Christ with *dia*, impersonal means → Spirit with *en* / the dative); and **[[John McHugh]]**'s ICC volume (the text says "through him," *not* "by him") [^4][^5][^6][^7]. See [[John 1-3 - The Logos Is the Intermediate Agent of Creation Not the Originator]].
- Conclusion: nowhere in the Gospel of John is it asserted that the Word is "the very God of Israel"; the creator's argument "betrays... abject ignorance of the Greek of this passage" [^8].
