# Data Over Dogma - How the Trinity Was Invented

In this episode of the Data Over Dogma podcast, [[Dan McClellan]] and co-host [[Dan Beecher]] argue that the doctrine of the Trinity was not revealed but **constructed** between the 2nd and 5th centuries CE as Greco-Roman-educated Christian apologists tried to harmonize a diverse New Testament under one philosophically defensible framework. McClellan traces the doctrine from the early "Logos as a second god" speculation of [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]] and [[Justin Martyr]], through the [[Council of Nicaea|Council of Nicaea (325 CE)]] where [[Arius]] was defeated and *homoousios* (consubstantiality) adopted, to the [[Chalcedon Hypostatic Union as Mystery Defense|Council of Chalcedon (451 CE)]] and the hypostatic union. His central thesis: the Trinity survived only because the institutional church, backed by Roman imperial coercion, enforced it — "without the Roman Empire... it would have gone the way of the dodo long, long ago."

- **The Trinity is a product of canonization plus institution.** Once diverse texts (Mark, Paul, Matthew, John) were bound into "one book" governed by one authoritative social institution, harmonization became mandatory, and Greek philosophy supplied the unifying framework.[^1]
- **Two non-negotiable features of deity drove the construction:** deity must be eternal (no time when it did not exist) and unchanging (no hierarchy, no alteration). [[Arius|Arius']] subordinate, created Son violated both, which is why subordinationism — standard in the 3rd century — became heresy in the 4th.[^2]
- **Imperial might, not coherence, kept the doctrine alive.** [[Constantine]] convened Nicaea to force unity ("I don't care how you fix it, just fix it"); dissenters like Arius were exiled, and [[St. Nicholas]] is reputed to have slapped him.[^3]
- **The hypostatic union "shut up, it's a mystery."** Chalcedon's 100% God / 100% human formula is, McClellan argues, an admission that the framework is held together by authority rather than intelligibility.[^4]
- **Creatio ex nihilo was likewise invented (170-180 CE)** to answer Greek-philosophical objections to the resurrection of decomposed bodies — a parallel case of doctrine generated by apologetic pressure.[^5]
