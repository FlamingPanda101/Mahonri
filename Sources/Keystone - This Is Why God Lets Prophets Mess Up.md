# Keystone - This Is Why God Lets Prophets Mess Up

A short Keystone video for a Latter-day Saint audience that takes prophetic fallibility as a given ("we all know by now that prophets can make mistakes — that's old news") and asks the deeper question: if prophets speak for God yet still err, what does the God–prophet *relationship* actually look like, and why would God permit the mistakes? The host rejects his boyhood "Neo from the Matrix" picture (God directly uploads the play; the prophet executes flawlessly) in favor of a football analogy — God as head coach who sets vision and philosophy, the prophet as offensive coordinator who calls the individual plays and is sustained even when plays fail.

## Summary

The argument runs through several beats, all developed via the coaching metaphor:

- **Vision vs. play-calling (delegation).** The head coach owns "overall team strategy and philosophy" but does not generally call each play; the offensive coordinator (OC) does, trying to bring the coach's vision to life. The coach "isn't going to micromanage every decision" and "has confidence that the OC is going to do his job," overruling only occasionally. Applied: God supplies unchanging overarching principles (e.g., "our bodies are gifts from God"); prophets work out what that looks like "on the field." The Word of Wisdom is the worked example — early leaders advised moderation in alcohol, coffee, and tea; later leaders changed the play to full abstention. Different OCs read the same vision and "approach some things differently." See [[God as Head Coach and the Prophet as Offensive Coordinator]].

- **God puts the ball back in the prophet's court.** The host's favorite supporting case is the brother of Jared (Ether 2–3). When the barges have no light source, God does not solve it; He asks "what will ye that I should prepare for you?" — declining to dictate the play and inviting the prophet to propose one, which God then sustains. See [[God Puts the Ball Back in the Prophet's Court]] and [[Ether 2.23-25]].

- **Sustaining while disagreeing.** "Some past and present prophets have called for plays that I have sometimes disagreed with. But I still sustain and respect those leaders" because of the authority the head coach gave them. This is folded into [[God as Head Coach and the Prophet as Offensive Coordinator]].

- **Why not just dictate every play?** Because the coach "is more interested in seeing his players learn and improve," and "you can't do that without practice and opposition. Without opposition, there is no game." This is the agency/soul-making rationale already developed in [[Learning by Experience over Book Learning]] and [[Lehi's Theodicy - Opposition in All Things]] and [[God Works Through Fallible Prophets]].

- **The players who get hurt.** People are sometimes hurt when leaders run certain plays. The host's example is Peter denying Christ three times — a painful learning experience for Peter that "could have also hurt a lot of other people's testimonies." God "might not always prevent it, [but] he is always prepared for it," with "a really great medic" — Jesus Christ and the Atonement — ready to care for the injured.

- **Closing pivot.** If the head coach is patient with the OC and refines the playbook over time, the host can be patient too; he points viewers to a companion video on how different prophets have "run different plays at different times."
