# The Cult Label — Interview with Dr. Lorne Dawson

Ward Radio interviews Dr. Lorne Dawson, a retired professor and leading scholar
of new religious movements (NRMs) and radicalization. Dawson was a key figure in
the "classical period" of NRM scholarship (1980s–early 2000s) and his empirical
work was instrumental in courts rejecting "brainwashing" as non-scientific.

## Key Topics Covered

### 1. History of the Word "Cult"
- Originally a **neutral scholarly term** for ritual-focused religious practice
  (e.g., "the cult of the Virgin Mary" within Catholicism).
- Weber and Troeltsch (early 20th c.) developed church/sect/cult as sociological
  categories — "cult" was a fuzzy residual category for small, mystical groups.
- **Mid-20th century hijacking**: American evangelical theologians applied "cult"
  as a **pejorative** against Latter-day Saints, Jehovah's Witnesses, Christian
  Scientists, and Seventh-day Adventists — because these groups were growing and
  threatening mainstream Protestantism. This is where the negative connotation
  originates.
- The "Great American Cult Scare" (late 1970s–80s) revived the pejorative usage
  when Hare Krishna, Scientology, and the Unification Church recruited university
  students.

### 2. The Brainwashing Myth — Empirical Refutation
- **Self-appointed "cult experts"** charged money to testify in court that NRMs
  brainwashed members. Courts initially didn't know how to handle these cases.
- **By the late 1990s**: Courts worldwide (especially US) ruled that these
  "experts" were **not scientific** and their testimony inadmissible.
  - US v. Fishman (1990) was a key precedent.
- **The data**: Two massive independent studies (one psychiatric, one sociological,
  same year, no coordination) found:
  - Fewer than **1% of people contacted** by an NRM ever join.
  - **90% of those who join leave voluntarily within 2 years.**
- Dawson: "If brainwashing exists, why aren't way more people joining these
  groups? And why aren't you staying?"
- The American Psychological Association and American Sociological Association
  filed amicus briefs stating brainwashing theories were not scientifically viable.

### 3. Who Actually Joins NRMs
- **Overwhelmingly middle-class, educated, white** — university students from
  privileged backgrounds.
- **Disproportionately from secular/non-religious homes.** Secular Jews joined at
  rates vastly out of proportion to population.
- Contradicts the "weak, vulnerable, gullible" stereotype — these were capable
  young people with unmet existential needs.
- Dawson: "People often come to extreme positions through very normal processes
  — curiosity, inquisitiveness, doing their own research."

### 4. Why People Join: Identity & Meaning
- Core driver: **need for certainty** in a confusing world with too many choices.
- Dawson: "People want some clarity. They want to know what's right and wrong.
  They want to feel good about themselves and morally aligned."
- More extreme perspectives offer more clarity — this is a feature, not evidence
  of pathology.
- "If someone's engaging in something you think is outlandish, the answer is
  rarely that they are outlandish or psychologically damaged. The answer is
  almost always: here's a person with some real need that's not getting met."

### 5. The Sociology of Religious Distinction
- **Church/Sect/Cult** continuum: Churches are inclusive, low-tension; Sects
  are high-tension, high-demand; Cults are new, small, loosely organized.
- Dawson: "Churches that become too inclusive and dilute their standards too much
  fail ultimately. Sectarian religions are strong because they have strong
  behavioral requirements and barriers."
- On LDS temple openness: The tension between transparency and maintaining
  distinctive identity is sociologically normal and healthy.

### 6. The Irony of Discomfort
- "Being uncomfortable about your faith is in part actually a marker that
  probably you've got something worthwhile going on."
- If you can explain every aspect of your faith freely to anyone on the subway,
  there's probably nothing particularly distinctive about it.
