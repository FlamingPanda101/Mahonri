On the *For All The Saints* podcast, host [[Ben Hancock]] interviews LDS scholar [[Stephen Smoot]] about a newly studied Gestapo dossier — roughly 500 pages held in the German Federal Archives (Bundesarchiv) in Berlin — that documents how the Nazi state and its leadership viewed the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in 1930s Germany. Smoot, working for Josh Coates at the [[BH Roberts]] Foundation, translated and analyzed the material (memoranda, surveillance reports, confiscated literature, photographs) spanning 1933–1939, publishing it on the BH Roberts website, on Mormonr.org, and in the Spring 2025 issue of *Latter-day Saint Historical Studies*.[^1][^2]

## Summary

The dossier originates with the Sicherheitsdienst (SD) — the Nazi party/SS intelligence service tied to the Gestapo — and shows that Nazi surveillance of the Saints was **systematic, coordinated, and Reich-wide**, not a one-off.[^3] From the scattered reports Smoot reconstructs *why* the regime distrusted the Church: its American foreign connections; its centralized tithing system (money flowing to a church rather than the state); perceived "Jewish influence" on its theology (suspicion of Old Testament–rooted concepts like **Zion**, **Sabbath**, and the very word *tithing*, plus a Jewish-owned Berlin mission building); a theology judged "bizarre" and "deviant"; and suspicion that polygamy was still secretly practiced. A 1938 report to Reichsleiter [[Alfred Rosenberg]] explicitly calls the Church's tenets "incompatible with the national socialist worldview." See [[The Nazis Deemed Latter-day Saint Teachings Ideologically Incompatible with National Socialism]] and [[The Gestapo Dossier Reveals Systematic Nazi Surveillance of the Latter-day Saints]].[^4][^5]

Smoot frames the Church's response as one of three options open to a religious minority in 1933: open resistance (Jehovah's Witnesses → concentration camps), enthusiastic pro-Nazi conformity (Ludwig Müller's Reich Church → loss of integrity), or **accommodationism** — recognizing and submitting to the regime's laws while neither endorsing nor opposing it, grounded in the 12th Article of Faith and Doctrine and Covenants 134. He argues from the documents that this was a deliberate **survival strategy**, not cowardice or secret sympathy, citing an explicit Gestapo threat of "the strictest state police measures." See [[The Church's Accommodationist Survival Strategy in Nazi Germany]].[^6][^7]

The episode contextualizes the infamous photograph of President [[Heber J. Grant]] preaching before a swastika flag (Frankfurt, July 1937): a public venue owned by local Nazis, the swastika then the national flag by law, with Gestapo informants planted in the audience, and Grant's content apolitical (an exposition of the Articles of Faith). See [[Heber J. Grant Before the Swastika Flag Is a Decontextualized Cheap Shot]].[^8] It surveys individual Saints — the heroes [[Helmut Hübner]] and [[Paul Herbert Schick]] who refused the regime, Max Roschka who helped Jews during Kristallnacht, and the minority (~2.5% vs. a ~10% national average) who joined the Nazi party, including the pro-Nazi branch president Arthur Zander.[^9][^10] Smoot reconstructs the **posthumous excommunication of Hübner** as a local survival measure under Gestapo pressure — urged by Zander but consented to by anti-Nazi district president [[Otto Berndt]] to signal the boy acted alone — later reversed by the First Presidency in 1948, with Hübner's blessings restored. See [[Helmut Hubner's Excommunication Was a Local Survival Measure Later Reversed]].[^11][^12] Other documented details: the July 1936 banning of Talmage's *The Articles of Faith* (likely for its pro-Israel/pro-Zion chapters) by Goebbels's Reich literature chamber; LDS missionaries training Germany's 1936 Olympic basketball team as a proselytizing bridge; Alfred C. Rees's 1939 *Völkischer Beobachter* article cozying up to the regime; and high-level discussions (in the Ribbentrop bureau) about whether to ban the Church outright — which never happened, partly because of the accommodationist posture and partly because the Church was small (~8,000 members in 1933, ~13,000 by 1939).[^13][^14]
