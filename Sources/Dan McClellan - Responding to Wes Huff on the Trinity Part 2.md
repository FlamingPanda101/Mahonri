# Dan McClellan — Responding to Wes Huff on the Trinity (Part 2)

This is a **companion to [[Dan McClellan - Responding to Wes Huff on the Trinity]]** (video `ZuOnruwfjMM`): a second, differently-edited [[Dan McClellan]] response to the same [[Wes Huff]] interview, sharing the title and the core thesis — that the Trinity is a **post-biblical philosophical framework** imposed on texts that the [[Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images|logic of divine images]] explains better. The opening Yahweh-God exchange and most arguments (the [[The HANDS Acronym Does Not Prove Deity|HANDS acronym]], [[Name-Bearing Agency in Exodus 23|"my name is in him"]], [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology|Metatron / Sanhedrin 38b]], [[Genesis 19.24|Genesis 19:24]]) **substantially overlap** the sibling note and are wikilinked rather than re-extracted.

What this cut adds is a sustained, technical walk-through of **how** the Trinity's vocabulary was built over centuries — see the one new concept, [[Prosopological Exegesis Evolved Into Hypostasis Over Centuries]] — plus McClellan's framing of the competing **multiple NT christologies** (logos, adoptionist, image, name-bearing angel) and his memorable "keys to drive God's car" image for the divine name. The data remains congenial to the LDS rejection of the creedal Trinity. See also [[Subordination Within the New Testament]] and [[Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity]].

## Summary

- **The "algebra problem," restated.** The Hebrew Bible says only Adonai may do certain things; the NT shows Jesus doing them and claiming Adonai's exclusive titles. "How do we solve for X?" There are "several different ways to answer this question," and the NT itself records several attempts — the Trinity is *not* the answer that was already in circulation.[^1]
- **The answer already on the table: divine mediators bearing the name.** Late Second Temple Judaism's solution was "divine mediators who bore the presence and the power and the authority of God… authorized to speak as and to be identified with God. Not because they were God," but via "a communicable vehicle of divine agency," chiefly the [[Divine Name Theology|divine name]].[^2] See [[Divine Agency and the Divine-Name Bearer]].
- **"Keys to drive the car."** McClellan's signature image: "If you have the divine name in you… then you get to drive God's car and you get to act like you're God." This is what the [[The Messenger of Yahweh Explicitly Identifies as God|angel of Adonai]] does.[^3]
- **The messenger's muddied identity.** Citing Genesis 16, 22, 31, [[Exodus 3.6|Exodus 3]], Judges 2, 6, 13, the messenger's identity is "muddied up with the identity of God" and can say "I am the God of Israel" because "God has endowed them with the divine name" — "the logic of divine images that we see in ancient West Asia," the messenger "a walking talking divine image."[^4] See [[The Angel of Yahweh Bears the Divine Name]].
- **King-as-image background.** The same logic grounds humanity "created in the image of God" and the ancient Egyptian/Mesopotamian "king as the body of God, the image of the god, the *ṣalmu* of the god."[^5]
- **The Son of Man is worshiped — without being the Father.** "In 1 Enoch 48 the Son of Man will be worshiped by everyone on earth. Not because the Son of Man is God the Father, but because" he has been endowed with a communicable vehicle of divine agency and so, "like a divine image, can facilitate worship of the God."[^6] See [[1 Enoch]].
- **Being vs person — and why it came too late.** "Being describes what you are. Person describes who you are." Early Christians did develop *ousia* (being) and *hypostasis* (person) to relate Jesus to God, "however, the framework… simply did not exist when the New Testament was being written," not before "the late 2nd century CE at the very earliest."[^7]
- **The multiple, competing NT christologies.** McClellan enumerates them: **John** adopts a [[Image Christology in the Gospel of John|logos / emanation christology]] (a separate being closely connected to God as the expressed *logos*, leaning on [[Philo of Alexandria|Philo]] and middle Platonism) plus *mutual indwelling* ("anyone who has seen Jesus has seen the father… not because Jesus is the father, but because Jesus manifests the presence"); **Mark** presents an **adoptionist** christology; **Paul** "hints at adoptionist christologies but also… image christology and… Jesus as the name-bearing angel." "A variety of different ways to account for how Jesus can both not be God but also manifest God."[^8]
- **The vocabulary was built step by step (NEW).** From prosopological exegesis → [[Justin Martyr]]'s "distinct in number, united by the same will" → *ousia*/*hypostasis* used interchangeably then disentangled → a middle-Platonic *vertical* (subordinationist) gradient, not a horizontal co-equal one → [[Origen]] still subordinationist → the 4th-century endpoint. "A framework that takes centuries to develop."[^9] This is the dedicated new concept: [[Prosopological Exegesis Evolved Into Hypostasis Over Centuries]].
- **No retroactive concept-smuggling.** "There's absolutely no case to make that the New Testament authors already had this concept in the bag, but just couldn't figure out how to express it… The concept took centuries to develop."[^10]
- **[[Matthew 28.19|Matthew 28:19]] is name-bearing, not Trinitarian.** Baptizing "in the name" (singular) "of the father, son, and the holy spirit" returns to "the possession of the divine name as we see in [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20–21]]" — the son "authorized to bear and manifest the presence… of the father. That is image christology," not the Trinity.[^11] Reading Trinitarianism into it "is a product of your dogmatism, not of the available data."[^12]
- **The HANDS acronym, again.** Father, Son, and Spirit each receive the **H**onors, **A**ttributes, **N**ames, **D**eeds, and **S**eat of God — but this is just "the algebra problem that has multiple resolutions," and image christology is "probably the most common" NT resolution.[^13] See [[The HANDS Acronym Does Not Prove Deity]].
- **Apologists hallucinate the Trinity in the Hebrew Bible.** Second Temple Judaism had divine-name mediation but "did not have a concept of anything remotely approximating the Trinity, nor is Jesus anywhere in the Hebrew Bible at all." Alleged Trinity references are really "the divine council and the other gods," or apologists "hallucinating references to distinct Adon[ai] which are not in the Hebrew Bible."[^14] McClellan points to his open-access book *Adonized Divine Images* (Googleable PDF).[^15]
- **The ark is a divine image.** "The ark of the covenant was a divine image. It bore and manifested the power, the presence, the agency of God."[^16]
- **[[Genesis 19.24|Genesis 19:24]] is not two Yahwehs — and Huff added words.** "The phrase 'on earth' is simply not in that verse… Wes is not only altering but also adding to the passage." A hyper-literal rendering ("from with Adonai, from the heavens") plus the doubled *min* preposition marks Adonai as the **source** of the sulfur and fire and locates Adonai "in the heavens." "The overwhelming majority of scholars… agree this is not a reference to one Adonai on earth and one Adonai in heaven."[^17]
- **"Complex unity" and "the Jews understand this" — both rejected.** "No, Jewish folks do not understand this this way." Reading the pre-incarnate Christ / complex unity in is "the interpretive framework that we impose… to read our ideologies into the text"; on their own terms the texts "do not arrive at those conclusions."[^18]
- **Huff's Hebrew slips corrected.** There is "no such word in Hebrew" as Huff's alleged *chevro* for God's omnipresent spirit; McClellan suspects he means the **sefirot** — "the 10 emanations from God in Kabbalah, which is later mystical Judaism," not rabbinic.[^19]
- **Sanhedrin 38b: Metatron is the "Adonai" of Exodus 24.** The heretic before Rav Idit asks why Exodus 24 says "Come up to *Adonai*" rather than "come up to me"; Rav Idit answers the *Adonai* there "refers to [[Metatron]], not to Adonai," who "has the same name as his master," quoting "my name is in him" ([[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20–21]]). Name-indwelling mediation "begins way back in the early post-exilic period and goes down centuries beyond Christianity."[^20] See [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology]].
- **"Two powers in heaven."** McClellan accepts the substance while disliking the label: in academia "that's what it's called" — "God is ruling and reigning in heaven and still has a presence on earth. Only one God, but there's something else going on."[^21]
- **Deuteronomistic name-theology is the key.** It is "the same thing that's going on in Deuteronomy… where God's self is limited to the heavens, but God's presence is made available in the temple by virtue of God causing their name to dwell in the temple." Whether ark, temple, messenger, or Jesus, "God's presence is accessible… via the bearing of that name by some kind of material medium" — including in [[John 20.28|John 20]] when Thomas says "my Lord and my God" and in [[John 14.9|John 14]].[^22]
- **Data or dogma?** Taking the texts "on their own terms," the logic of divine images is "the best available explanation"; the alternative is to "bring a bunch of frameworks… that presuppose inerrancy and inspiration and univocality and historicity." "The question is, what's the priority? The data or the dogma?"[^23]
