# Dan McClellan — "David Didn't Kill Goliath"

A YouTube video in which [[Dan McClellan]] walks through one of the most famous contradictions in the Bible: who killed Goliath. [[2 Samuel 21.19|2 Samuel 21:19]] credits [[Elhanan]] the Bethlehemite, while the famous story of [[1 Samuel]] 17 credits [[David]]; [[1 Chronicles 20.5|1 Chronicles 20:5]] "fixes" the clash by having Elhanan kill *Lahmi the brother of Goliath* — a later scribal harmonization preserved in the KJV. McClellan argues from Hebrew script history, naming conventions, and the text-critical principle *lectio difficilior* that the Samuel reading is earlier and that the David tradition appropriated Elhanan's victory. This source feeds [[Elhanan Killed Goliath - Chronicles Harmonized the Contradiction]] and [[Lectio Difficilior - The Harder Reading Is Usually Earlier]].

## Summary
- **The setup.** 1 Samuel 17 has David kill Goliath, describing his spear shaft as "like a weaver's beam" (17:7). But 2 Samuel 21:19 says **Elhanan** the Bethlehemite "slew Goliath... the shaft of whose spear was like a weaver's beam" — reusing the same description, so it is unmistakably the same giant.[^1]
- **The Chronicler's fix.** Centuries later, 1 Chronicles 20:5 says Elhanan killed "Lahmi the **brother** of Goliath," removing the contradiction with the David story.[^2]
- **Script-history argument.** The two Hebrew verses differ by tiny strokes that resemble each other **only in the Aramaic square script** adopted during the Babylonian exile, not in older paleo-Hebrew — so the confusion could only have arisen at the time Chronicles was edited (the exile or later), not when 2 Samuel was written.[^3]
- **Naming argument.** "Lahmi" would mean ≈ "bread," an unattested naming convention; and it is a *Semitic* name, whereas a brother of Goliath would be a Philistine and in this early period would bear a Philistine name — so "Lahmi" looks like a textual artifact, not a person.[^4]
- ***Lectio difficilior*.** By the principle that scribes change texts from harder to easier readings, the contradiction-bearing 2 Samuel text is the likely **earlier** reading; the smoother Chronicles text is the likely later one.[^5]
- **Conclusion.** The earliest tradition has Elhanan killing Goliath; the David-tradition editors **appropriated** that victory for David. 1 Samuel 17 is the later retelling.[^6]
- **Commentary survey.** McClellan reads a chain of scholarship from 1899 onward — Henry Preserved Smith, Hugh Williamson, Roddy Braun, Sara Japhet, Ralph Klein, Graeme Auld, A. A. Anderson, [[Marvin Sweeney]], and Joel Baden — all agreeing Chronicles is the harmonized/corrupted text and 2 Samuel the more original. Sweeney: the view that David took credit for a warrior's victory "is the overwhelming academic consensus." Williamson and Japhet add that the Samuel text is itself slightly corrupt and the change may have entered the source text rather than being the Chronicler's own deliberate act.[^7]
- **Thesis.** "The Bible is not univocal and it very frequently contradicts itself" — this being one of the most famous examples — and the solution is to recognize different authors, audiences, and purposes, with editors smoothing contradictions as they go.[^8]
