# What Is Morality? (Part 3) — The Roots of Nearly All Disagreement

Part 3 of [[Jacob Hansen]]'s *Thoughtful Faith* metaethics series picks up where [[Thoughtful Faith - The Nature of Morality from the Ground Up (Ethics)]] left off. Having argued that the universal desire to escape one's own suffering grounds objective morality, Hansen answers the obvious rebuttal — *isn't well-being subjective?* — by relocating nearly all moral conflict to disagreement about the **nature of being itself**: because we disagree about what reality is, we disagree about what ultimate well-being looks like and which road leads there. He then defends moral objectivity against the charge that it collapses into [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzschean]] / [[Ayn Rand|Randian]] rational egoism, arguing that only a God who **connects the well-being of all** can rescue love and obligation from pure self-interest.

## Summary
- **Different notions of well-being don't make all paths equal.** A critic says the "desired end" is mere preference, hence subjective; Hansen replies that even if people disagree about what most reduces suffering, "all roads will [not] indeed actually lead us equally away from suffering" — some systems (communism, fundamentalism, utilitarianism, humanism) move us farther from the worst conceivable suffering than others. [^1] See [[Some Paths Reduce Suffering Better Than Others]].
- **The real root of moral disagreement is disagreement about the nature of being.** "The issue is that we don't agree about the nature of being itself, so we cannot agree about what ultimate well-being looks like." Beliefs about reality (eternal life for a Latter-day Saint vs. an 80-year timeline for an atheist) "frame the entire way that we view morality." [^2] See [[Moral Disagreement Is Really Disagreement About the Nature of Being]].
- **Suffering is law-governed and objective, like physical health.** Disagreement about what brings better health doesn't make health subjective — visit a hospital ward for obesity and diabetes. Likewise "suffering doesn't care about your arbitrary opinions": put your hand on a hot stove and suffering follows. [^3] See [[Some Paths Reduce Suffering Better Than Others]].
- **Love is willing the good of another; apathy is its opposite.** "Love is to will the good of the other... to will that they move away from suffering," which is why a person who loves you tells you that you *ought not* harm yourself. Apathy is the opposite of love because it does not care about another's moral direction. [^4] See [[Love Wills the Good of the Other; Apathy Is Its Opposite]].
- **Pure rationality makes morality selfish — only God connects all well-being.** Hansen rejects [[Sam Harris|Harris]]-style humanist utilitarianism: rationally, "the only well-being you experience is your own," which (citing [[Ayn Rand]] and [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s will to power) makes all morality selfish. The "saving factor" is a metaphysical Force — God — through whose justice you "can never ultimately on the Eternal time scale increase your well-being by harming others," so that all mankind may "be one" ([[John 17.20-23]]). [^5] See [[Pure Rationality Makes Morality Selfish - Only God Connects All Well-Being]].
