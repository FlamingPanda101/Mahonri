# The Stick of Joseph — Race, Curses, and the Book of Mormon (Marvin Perkins)

LDS apologetic video on the [[Stick of Joseph]] channel: host [[Jackson Paul]] introduces and re-airs a presentation (originally c. 2012) by Black Latter-day Saint educator [[Marvin Perkins]] answering the charge that the Book of Mormon is **racist** — specifically that the "curse of the Lamanites" teaches that dark skin is a divine curse. Perkins' method is to survey *every* scriptural use of "black" and "white" applied to people across the Old Testament, New Testament, and Book of Mormon and argue the language is **idiomatic** (gloom, mourning, spiritual state), not racial; and to redefine *curse* itself as a **separation from God because of sin**. This overlaps the vault's [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]] and [[White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning]], and adds Perkins' distinctive curse-as-separation thesis.

## Summary
- **"Black" is used of people only eight times in the OT/NT, and the footnotes gloss it as gloom.** Of 44 occurrences of *black*, only eight describe a person; Perkins walks Job 30:30, Song of Songs 1:5-6, Jeremiah 8:21 ("Hebrew idiom meaning gloomy"), Jeremiah 14:2 ("dejected"), Lamentations 4:8, Joel 2:6, and Nahum 2:10 — all marked as figurative for grief or dejection, paralleling the anguished Job. See [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]] [^1][^2].
- **The 1981 edition added footnotes redirecting the Book of Mormon "skin" passages to spiritual darkness.** Before 1981 [[2 Nephi 5.21|2 Nephi 5:21]] ("skin of blackness") and [[2 Nephi 30.6|2 Nephi 30:6]] ("scales of darkness") carried no footnote on *skin*/*scales*; the post-revelation edition added footnotes pointing to the Topical Guide entries "Darkness, spiritual" and "spiritual blindness." Perkins argues "every scripture in the Book of Mormon that made you believe the Lamanites had darker skin... has a new footnote leading you back to these two" [^3][^4].
- **"White and delightsome" was changed to "pure" — and the *mix* footnote points to Interfaith marriage.** The 1981 *white → pure* change (2 Nephi 30:6) tracks the same redirection; Perkins notes Joseph Smith already made the change in 1840. The new footnote on *mix* in 2 Nephi 5:23 reads "Topical Guide: Marriage, Interfaith," so the "cursed seed of him that mixeth" is about Interfaith, not interracial, marriage — among people who shared the same father, Lehi. See [[White to Pure Word Change Does Not Alter the Meaning]] [^5][^6].
- **A curse is a *separation from God* because of sin, not a skin color.** Perkins' central thesis: the dictionary gives 15 senses of *curse* (incl. excommunication / driving away); the scriptural sense is loss of access to God's Spirit, temple, and presence through sinful living. He marshals D&C 29:41, Moses 5 (Cain "driven out... from the face of the Lord"), Alma 3:18-19 ("every man that is cursed brings upon himself his own condemnation"), and the Anti-Nephi-Lehies whose "curse of God did no more follow them" once they lived the gospel. New concept: [[A Curse Is a Separation From God Not a Skin Color]] [^7][^8][^9].
- **External anchors: McKay, the Second Article of Faith, and 1 Samuel 16:7.** Perkins quotes [[David O. McKay]] ("there is not now and there has never been a doctrine in this church that Negroes are under a divine curse"), the Second Article of Faith (punished "for their own sins, and not for Adam's transgression"), and 1 Samuel 16:7 ("the Lord looketh on the heart") to argue the judgment imagery (Jacob 3:8, "their skins will be whiter than yours... before the throne of God") cannot be literal skin [^10][^11].
- **The 1978 revelation gave knowledge, not just policy.** Perkins frames Official Declaration 2 as a *revelation* (a giving of light) that did far more than lift the ban: it tied the Book of Mormon and Bible together and erased "all the views and all the thoughts of the past" (quoting an Elder Maxwell-style charge to CES teachers to "forget everything... said in the days past"). *Steelman the channel itself voices:* the verses do exist and were read racially for over a century by LDS leaders, and the edition changes track the lifting of the ban — so the text is at minimum susceptible to a racial reading (see [[White to Pure Edit Tracks the Priesthood Ban Not Revelation]]) [^12].
