# What Serious Atheists Get Right and What They Miss

[[Jacob Hansen]] and a co-host set aside the weak atheist arguments and steelman the two most serious forms of atheism: [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]'s honest embrace of meaninglessness and [[Sam Harris]]'s attempt to ground morality objectively in the well-being of conscious creatures. They grant that Harris correctly identifies what moral language *describes* — a creature's movement toward or away from suffering — but argue he cannot ground an obligation toward *others'* well-being, and that only a Christian world, where ultimate justice means "you can't get away with anything," closes that gap.

## Summary
- The strongest atheism is Nietzsche's: he admits it's all arbitrary power and "it is what it is" — rationally consistent, even though no one actually lives that way, which itself signals they don't really believe it.[^1]
- Sam Harris gets the closest of any atheist by defining good and bad as descriptions of a conscious creature's movement toward well-being or toward suffering.[^2]
- Harris's account falls short because well-being is experienced privately: enslaving your neighbor might raise *your* well-being while wrecking theirs, and nothing in his system grounds why you ought to prefer their good to your own.[^3]
- The hosts agree morality *describes* a thing's relationship to suffering — even calling God "good" only because He saves us from suffering, and calling the Cross "good" only because it ends in salvation rather than universal eternal death.[^4]
- In a Christian world your well-being is bound up with everyone else's under ultimate justice, so "you can't get away with anything"; in the atheist world you sometimes genuinely can get away with harming others.[^5]
