The hosts of [[Ward Radio]] (Jonah, Carton, and [[Britney Ward Radio|Britney]]) react to a *Christianity Today* article by editor-in-chief [[Russell Moore]] titled "Mormons Expect More of the Next Generation. Why Don't We?" Moore — quoting Stephen Bullivant's book *The Nonverts* and his own son's fascination with Salt Lake City — concedes that the LDS missionary program is a transformative good even when it produces no converts, and warns that evangelicals fail their youth by expecting too little. The hosts embrace the "high-demand religion" label as a compliment ("anything worthwhile is high demand," "the ultimate 75 Hard") and then turn Moore's own argument back on him: if creedal Christians tolerate everything from River-Jordan immersion to a baptismal "spray bottle" while still counting one another as Christians, why is a different view of the Trinity disqualifying for Latter-day Saints?

## Summary

The episode advances three distinct arguments worth isolating from the generic "strict church grows" thesis already in the vault.

First, the **missionary program as self-formation**: Moore grants (via *The Nonverts*) that missionaries who never baptized anyone still did not regard their missions as failures, because the experience is "character building" — echoing Elder Charles Callis's 1911 line that trudging in the rain "might not be an efficient way to do missionary work, but it's the greatest way to build missionaries." The hosts pair this with an X thread citing a study that the mission raised most returned missionaries' self-esteem by pushing them out of their comfort zone, and reframe the two-year mission as "the ultimate 75 Hard." This is captured in [[The Mission Builds the Missionary Even Without Converts]].

Second, Moore's own **entrustment thesis**: too many young people leave "not because they've concluded that they don't need the church, but because they've concluded that the church doesn't need them." The LDS answer is to hand youth real responsibility — a host recounts being made branch president as a teenager in Siberia. See [[Trust the Next Generation With Real Responsibility]].

Third, a **tu quoque on creedal latitude**: the hosts argue creedal Christianity permits enormous variance on the mode of baptism (a point Christ explicitly commanded) yet treats a non-creedal view of the Trinity (a word absent from scripture) as disqualifying — "it is not our excellence but your pride that is keeping your youth program down." See [[Creedal Latitude on Baptism but Not the Trinity]]. The bulk of the runtime is comedic riffing (an extended bit caricaturing the "I hate Mormons too" disclaimer evangelicals attach before praising the Church), which the notes set aside in favor of the substantive arguments.

The episode's empirical claims are asserted, not sourced on-air; the underlying retention and well-being data live in the vault's existing notes ([[High-Demand Religion Retains More Members]], [[The Fruits of the Church - Do Critics Have a Better Program]]).
