# Glenn L. Pace Memo — The Mormon Satanic Panic

## Summary

- **The 1980s–90s national satanic panic took root in Utah more intensely than anywhere else.** Out of 384,000 sex-abuse reports referred to law enforcement in 1989, fewer than 1% (3,400) ended in a trial and prison sentence; the McMartin trial, the missing-children campaign, Dungeons & Dragons, and horror films had paralyzed rational thinking nationwide before investigators could intervene. [^1]
- **Therapist Dr. Barbara Snow was the single common denominator in Utah's ritual-abuse cases.** Starting in Lehi in 1985, her leading-question, no-recording, isolation interview methods produced ever-multiplying accusations (40 adults in one ward) with no physical evidence; a judge's affidavit noted the only link between unconnected cases was Snow's involvement and the total absence of corroboration. [^2]
- **The Allen Hadfield trial (1986) became the panic's signature miscarriage of justice.** Hadfield, originally a Snow supporter, was the only adult charged; Snow conveniently dated the alleged abuse to the one night she knew he had no alibi (April 9, 1986, when she had phoned him). Convicted narrowly, he always maintained innocence and a later court-ordered therapist (Dr. Robert Card) found him "vibrantly normal." [^3]
- **Official investigations vindicated the skeptics but lagged public opinion by years.** A $250,000 state ritual-abuse crime unit under investigator Mike King examined ~300 cases; only "three or four" proved out (~99% wild goose chases). Sociologist and LDS critic Anson Shupe concluded there was no abuse ring and that the Church did not pressure investigators — "the scandal collapsed of its own weight." Yet a 1992 Deseret News poll found 90% of Utahns still believed satanic ritual abuse, including infant sacrifice, was real. [^4]
- **The episode is framed as the prelude to the Glenn L. Pace memo,** which would remain a fixture of the Utah religious landscape for 40 more years; a subsequent installment promises a line-by-line analysis of the memo's origins and findings. [^5]
