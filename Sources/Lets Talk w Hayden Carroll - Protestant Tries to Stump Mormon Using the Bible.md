A campus-table conversation in which [[Hayden Carroll]] fields a young non-denominational Protestant student ("Archer") who walks up curious about Mormonism and then pulls up a series of Bible proof-texts to challenge it. Despite the title, the exchange is friendly and exploratory rather than a debate; the student repeatedly concedes Carroll's points and leaves with a Book of Mormon. The verses raised — Galatians 1:8, 2 Corinthians 11:13–14, Philippians 2:5–11, Deuteronomy 18, 1 Peter 3, John 17:3 — are all classics already worked elsewhere in the vault, so this note's value is as a compact tour of the LDS responses and as the source for one genuinely new framing: an *offensive* anti-Trinity argument from the New Testament's differentiated worship vocabulary.

## Summary

The conversation moves through the standard LDS-vs-Protestant terrain. On **continuing revelation and the canon**, Carroll argues that Scripture never says God has stopped speaking, that the closed-canon assumption "puts a limit on God that he didn't put on himself," and that demanding "we already have Scripture" mirrors the Jewish response to the first-century apostles — see [[Sola Scriptura Muzzles God]], [[Sola Scriptura Could Not Have Been True During the Apostolic Age]], and [[Closing the Canon Was Used to Declare Prophecy Ended]]. He pre-empts the [[Revelation 22 - Add or Take Away Rebutted|Revelation 22]] objection by noting most Protestants have abandoned it (John wrote about his own vision on Patmos before any Bible existed). He frames the **infallibility of Scripture** as incoherent: prophets and the people who wrote Scripture are not infallible, therefore Scripture cannot be — see [[Sola Scriptura Is Not Word-for-Word Literalism]] and the interpretation problem in [[All Doctrine Requires Interpretation Rebuttal]] and [[The Three Problems with Sola Scriptura - Interpretation Logic Canon]].

When the student raises **[[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]]** ("though an angel preach another gospel") and **2 Corinthians 11:13–14** (Satan as an angel of light) against Joseph Smith's angelic visitations, Carroll replies that the "gospel" Paul defends is the death-and-resurrection of Christ — which the LDS also preach — and that the existence of false apostles does not entail that God has stopped sending true ones (he cites Paul's "now revealed through the apostles and prophets"). On **Deuteronomy 18 and false prophecy**, he gives the Jonah defense and a three-category taxonomy of Joseph Smith's prophecies (conditional / fulfilled-but-unrecognized / not-yet-fulfilled) — fully covered by [[All Prophecies Are Conditional]], [[Deuteronomy 18.20-22|Deuteronomy 18:20–22]], and [[Apologetic vs Evidence Distinction for Prophecy]].

The longest thread is **christology**. Carroll defends a [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead]] of three separate beings, one in purpose, and presses the student on **Philippians 2** (the Son "did not count equality with God" / emptied himself), **[[Matthew 26.24|Gethsemane]]** ("not my will but thine"), and the Son's ignorance of the hour of the Second Coming — arguing these show two distinct beings/wills, not one substance. He rejects the [[Hypostatic Union|hypostatic union]] / two-natures doctrine as an unbiblical "theological solve" required only to make the Trinity work — see [[Gethsemane Two Wills Refute Same-Being Trinity]], [[One Mind Dilemma Against Two Natures]], [[Hypostatic Union Creates Two Jesuses]], and [[Ephesus and Chalcedon Defined the Two Natures of Christ]]. The student's strongest push — "how can he die if he's not fully man?" — Carroll parries with the Yahweh-cannot-die move (the spirit doesn't die, so being able to die doesn't prove a second nature any more than it makes saved humans God); see [[Yahweh Cannot Die Objection Rebutted]] and [[Yatsar Formed in Isaiah Targets Manufactured Idols]] for the related divine-nature questions.

He grounds the whole picture in the **Hellenization** thesis ([[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]]): the first four centuries read Greek (Platonic/Aristotelian) metaphysics — a "greatest conceivable being," omniscient, omnipotent, unbound — into the biblical text, producing the creedal doctrines of God; the Book of Mormon exists to correct that corruption. See [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]] and [[Trinity Is a Scholarly Creation Not a Revealed Doctrine]]. On the **afterlife**, he lays out the LDS [[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem|spirit world]]: everyone goes there at death; those who had no chance to hear the gospel wait in "prison" to be taught (Christ preaching to the spirits in [[1 Peter 3.18-20|1 Peter 3]]); paradise (the thief on the cross, "today thou shalt be with me") is *not* the Father's heaven, since the risen Christ tells Mary he has "not yet ascended to my Father" ([[John 20.17|John 20:17]]) — exactly the argument catalogued under [[Degrees of Glory vs Heaven]]. On **accountability**, only those who reject a genuine witness are condemned; God will be just with those who never heard.

The note's one new contribution is christological-liturgical: Carroll asks why the New Testament uses **differentiated forms of worship** (*proskyneo* for the Son, *latria* "reserved only for the Father") if Father and Son are one being — turning the Trinitarian's own Greek-worship distinction into an argument against the Trinity. That argument is developed in [[Differentiated Worship Vocabulary in the New Testament Refutes the Trinity]].
