# What Is Morality? (Part 2) — Is Morality Subjective?
This is the sequel to [[Thoughtful Faith - The Nature of Morality from the Ground Up (Ethics)]], where [[Jacob Hansen]] grounded objective morality in the universal desire to escape suffering and then closed on the cliffhanger "isn't well-being subjective?" Here he answers that objection by drawing a single load-bearing distinction — between *subjective perception* (how we interpret a reality outside ourselves) and *subjective preference* (a mere mental construct like liking a color). Suffering, he argues, falls on the perception side: it is an objective feature of reality even though it is experienced through perception, so "you ought not put your hand on that stove" is more than a statement of preference. The constructive frame closely tracks [[Sam Harris]]-style well-being realism, reframed inside Latter-day Saint thought.

## Summary
- **Subjective perception ≠ subjective preference.** The objection that suffering and well-being are "experienced subjectively" trades on an ambiguity. *All* of reality is known through subjective perception, but perception "is the way that we subjectively interpret a reality that exists outside of ourselves," whereas preference ("I like green") is "purely a mental construct without a right or wrong answer because it is not a description of anything in objective reality." [^1]
- **Suffering is objectively real, not merely a matter of preference.** Suffering and its opposite are "arguably the most real thing that exists," the "ocean in which we swim as sentient beings"; place your hand on a hot stove and the effects "objectively exist neurologically physiologically psychologically." So suffering is interpreted *through* perception but is not *created by* it. [^2]
- **"You ought" appeals to objective reality; "I prefer" appeals to arbitrary preference.** Because burns and the suffering they cause are real, "we rightly say that you ought not put your hand on that stove." Morality gives "a rational basis for saying you ought to do X" rather than the merely arbitrary "I prefer that you do X" — an appeal to "the objective reality of suffering and the inherent desire of all sane creatures to avoid their own suffering." [^3]
- **A new cliffhanger.** If we all desire to avoid our own suffering, why is there "so much disagreement about morality"? Hansen defers that to the next video. [^4]
