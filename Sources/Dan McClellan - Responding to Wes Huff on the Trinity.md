# Dan McClellan — Responding to Wes Huff on the Trinity

[[Dan McClellan]], a credentialed Hebrew Bible scholar writing from [[Critical Scholarship|critical-scholarship]] rather than apologetics, responds point-by-point to Christian apologist [[Wes Huff]]'s claim that the Trinity is a biblical and coherent framework. McClellan's thesis is that the Trinity is a **post-biblical conceptual and philosophical framework** — articulated only in the 4th century CE — that is *imposed* on the biblical texts to unify them, rather than a doctrine the biblical authors held. Along the way he dismantles the apologetic "HANDS" argument (see [[The HANDS Acronym Does Not Prove Deity]]), substitutes the cognitive [[Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images|logic of divine images]] for the Trinity as the better explanation of divine mediation, and rebuts Huff's "two Yahwehs" reading of [[Genesis 19.24|Genesis 19:24]].

His data is congenial to the LDS rejection of the creedal Trinity, though McClellan is not himself making an LDS argument. See also [[Subordination Within the New Testament]] and [[Trinity Built to Satisfy Greco-Roman Philosophy]].

## Summary

- **"Christianity before Trinitarianism."** Huff holds that Christianity is impossible without the Trinity; McClellan counters that "we had Christianity for almost 300 years before Trinitarianism as it is understood today was first articulated," and that even NT Christianity "was not a monolith… characterized more by pluriformity than by uniformity."[^1]
- **The "word X is not in the Bible" parry fails.** Huff analogizes: the word "monotheism" is not in the Bible either, yet the Bible is monotheistic. McClellan rejects the premise — "there are a lot of scholars who would say the Bible is not monotheistic, including myself" — and points to his published Oxford Research Encyclopedia essay and a Brown University volume on why "monotheism" is a problematic category for the ancient world.[^2]
- **No tetragrammaton in the NT.** Huff says Father, Son, and Spirit are each "described as Yahweh God." McClellan: the tetragrammaton "does not occur a single time anywhere in the New Testament," least of all combined with *theos*. *Kyrios* ("Lord") substitutes for it, but the secular-vs-substitutionary ambiguity is *deliberate rhetoric* the NT authors use to explore Jesus' "not clearly ironed out relationship to God."[^3]
- **The Shema is about how many Adonai, not how many gods.** The Shema does not say "the Lord is one God"; it says "Yahweh our God, Yahweh is one." The first-common-plural suffix on *Elohim* ("*our* God") presupposes *Elohim* is "a generic class with multiple members," and the verse counts how many *Adonai* there are, not how many gods exist.[^4] See [[The Shema Asserts Exclusive Relationship Not Sole Existence]].
- **The coherence demand smuggles in univocality, inerrancy, and inspiration.** Insisting the data be assembled into something "theologically and philosophically valid" presupposes "that every author… was expressing the exact same opinion… They're not allowed to disagree." The texts could be "ad hoc… intuition… whatever was rhetorically useful"; demanding philosophical validity is "responding to the interrogations of Greco-Roman thought leaders," not reading the Bible "on its own terms."[^5] See [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]].
- **The HANDS rebuttal.** Huff deploys Bowman & Komoszewski's acronym — Father, Son, and Spirit each receive the **H**onors, **A**ttributes, **N**ames, **D**eeds, and **S**eat of God. McClellan answers (a) inferring identity-of-being from shared prerogatives again presupposes univocality across centuries, and (b) those very prerogatives are *already granted* to non-God entities — the messenger of Yahweh, Yahoel, Metatron, and (in Revelation/1 Enoch) glorified humans who are enthroned, worshiped, and bear the divine name.[^6] See [[The HANDS Acronym Does Not Prove Deity]].
- **The messenger of Yahweh is and is not God.** In Exodus 3 "the angel of the Lord" says "I am the God of your father," and narrators identify the messenger "simultaneously as God and as the messenger." This paradox needs no Trinity; it is "the logic of divine images," a widespread ancient West Asian framework.[^7] See [[The Messenger of Yahweh Explicitly Identifies as God]].
- **Partible personhood and the headstone analogy.** McClellan grounds the divine-image logic in a cognitive theory: "personhood is partible and permeable… vehicles of agency are communicable and can inhabit other entities." His everyday proof: a mourner "speaking to a headstone as if they were speaking to the deceased loved one." Kings in Mesopotamia are called the *ṣalmu* (image) of a deity; the angel is "a sentient walking-talking divine image."[^8] See [[Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images]].
- **"My name is in him."** [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20-21]] explains the mechanism: the messenger can do what only God does — "he will not forgive your transgressions" (a phrase used elsewhere only in Joshua 24:19) — because "my name is in him." The name is "a communicable vehicle of divine agency, authority, and presence."[^9] See [[Name-Bearing Agency in Exodus 23]].
- **Second Temple and rabbinic parallels.** The Apocalypse of Abraham's Yahoel is "the namesake of the mediation of my ineffable name"; in b. Sanhedrin Rav Idit identifies the "Adonai" of Exodus 24:1 as Metatron, "the little Adonai," quoting "my name is in him." These show name-bearing mediation circulating independently of any Trinity.[^10] See [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology]].
- **The same prerogatives are democratized to humans.** Revelation grants "the one who overcomes" a seat on Christ's throne and worship before them; the forehead bears God's name; the Son of Man is worshiped in the Parables of Enoch; Ezekiel the Tragedian's Moses is given a throne, scepter, and diadem. The honors/attributes/names/deeds/seat are "communicable to other entities that are not God."[^11]
- **Jesus is the authorized possessor of the divine name.** Philippians 2's "name above every name," John's "your name which you have given me," and the "I am" (*egō*) of John 8:58 are read as Jesus signaling "I have the divine name" — the NT "accounted for Jesus's relationship to God by appealing to this explanation that was already in circulation."[^12] See [[John 8.58|John 8:58]].
- **Genesis 19:24 is not two Yahwehs.** Against Huff's "Yahweh on earth rained fire from Yahweh in heaven," McClellan reads the redundancy as marking that "Adonai is no longer on earth… is in the heavens now" — a likely Deuteronomistic redaction concerned with divine presence (cf. the Shekinah on the ark while God reigns in heaven). "There aren't really critical scholars who would say… this is evidence for the Trinity."[^13] See [[Genesis 19.24|Genesis 19:24]].
- **John 1:18's "one and only God" is a disputed variant.** Huff leans on "no one has ever seen God" + "the one and only God… has made him known." McClellan: that reading is contested; "one and only *son*" is the phrase John uses elsewhere, "one and only god" occurs nowhere, and the *lectio difficilior* argument fails because amid Trinitarian debates "one and only god" was "a more helpful reading," not a more difficult one.[^14] See [[John 1.18|John 1:18]].
- **Homoousios was known early but rejected as heretical.** *Homoousios* ("consubstantial") "was already known in the second and third century CE but was rejected as heretical," because *ousia* and *hypostasis* had not yet been distinguished; prosopological exegesis existed by the 2nd century "but it wouldn't result in the Trinity until the 4th."[^15]
- **The Trinity had to overcome subordinationism.** "Subordinationism is explicit in the New Testament," and was "the final hurdle that had to be overcome before they landed on the expression of the Trinity… at Nicaea in 325 CE." The pre-325 concept "is a slightly different concept… developing between the 2nd and the 4th centuries."[^16] See [[Subordination Within the New Testament]] and [[Nicaea Did Not Settle the Trinity]].
- **Tradition, not the Bible, is the real authority.** That a doctrine can "overrule the Bible" on Jesus' subordination shows "the Bible is not the ultimate authority. The tradition is the ultimate authority… The Bible is just the proof text" for "the authority of your own tradition."[^17] See [[God Himself Not a Book Is the Ultimate Authority]] and [[Literalism Collapses Into Authority of Tradition]].
- **"Use non-biblical language — fine; impose a unifying framework — not fine."** McClellan grants the obvious point that one must use extra-biblical words (e.g., "hypostatic union") to *describe* scripture. His objection is narrower: the Trinity is "a post-biblical conceptual and philosophical framework… developed precisely to function as a unifying framework that could be imposed upon the Bible to make the Bible say what the tradition needs it to say."[^18]
