# Ether's Elephant — Don't Listen to ExMormon Influencers

A brief, fast-cut video in which a faithful Latter-day Saint creator (Ether's Elephant) splices together short clips from popular ex-Mormon "influencers" and argues that, taken together, they systematically misrepresent what members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints actually believe. The format is itself the argument: a believer watching the montage is invited to "cringe" at claims they do not recognize as their own faith, and to comment with their own examples of misrepresentation. The video's distinctive contribution to this vault is a single diagnostic — that much ex-Mormon content blurs the line between idiosyncratic personal/local experience, LDS *culture*, and official *doctrine* — capped by a teaser that the deepest misrepresentation concerns the very nature of God.

## Summary
- **The core charge: misrepresentation, not disagreement.** The thesis is not that ex-Mormons are entitled critics who reach different conclusions, but that their clips state, as LDS belief, things the host (an active member) flatly does not believe — e.g. "those who go to the celestial kingdom do not have reproductive organs," or that "girls are taught that they should always say yes... the first commandment of Mormon dating."[^1] The host pre-empts the obvious rebuttal ("you're taking clips out of context") by conceding these are clips and inviting viewers to watch the originals, while insisting the misrepresentation "happens all the time."[^2]
- **Conflating the anecdote, the culture, and the doctrine.** The video's one genuinely new analytic move is to sort the misrepresentations into kinds: some ex-Mormons describe "their unique experience with family or local church leaders," which "might seem like the norm when in fact they are not"; others are "talking about a culture rather than a religion." To an outsider, both register as official doctrine.[^3] See [[Conflating Anecdote and Culture With Doctrine Misrepresents the Church]].
- **Affective editing as a tactic (already catalogued).** The host notes that "even when they do say somewhat accurate things, they do it in the most creepy way with odd wording and even sometimes placing creepy music in the background to subtly make you feel uncomfortable."[^4] This is the same affect-engineering charge the vault already develops against Johnny Harris — see [[The Enlightened Ex-Member Frame Treats Believers as Zoo Animals]] and [[The Critics' Manipulation Tactics and Logical Fallacies Toolkit]].
- **The influencer-economy frame.** By repeatedly labeling the critics "ex-Mormon *influencers*" and contrasting them with "active members," the video casts deconstruction content as a genre with its own incentives and audience — the same media-asymmetry terrain the vault maps in [[The Internet Rewards the Short Lie Over the Long Truth]]. Its prescription is parallel: if you want to understand the Church, "include some discussion with active members of the faith."[^5]
- **The cliffhanger: did they leave the wrong God?** The host says the "most egregious misrepresentation comes from ex Mormons failing to understand fundamental LDS theology about the nature of God," and defers the demonstration to a sequel — closing with the provocation, "when ex Mormons left the church, could they have just left the wrong God?"[^6] The substantive theology is not argued here; the vault's account of the LDS doctrine of God is developed elsewhere (e.g. [[The Intuitive Personal God Points Back to the Family of Heaven]]).
