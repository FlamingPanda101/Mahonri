# LDS historian debunks myths about Adam & Eve

Latter-day Saint Bible scholar [[T. Benjamin Spackman]] argues on *Keystone* that Saints have "a very strong tendency to read Genesis augmented by the temple movie as this is a documentary history of what really happened" — and that this is "almost certainly wrong," not because the events are fictional but because the demand for documentary history is *anachronistic*. He traces that demand to three post-1500 intellectual shifts (the Reformation's privileging of the literal sense, the scientific revolution, and the Enlightenment), then dismantles a chain of "myths" about the Fall: that disproving a literal Adam disproves Christianity, that there was no death or reproduction before the Fall, that the two commandments in the garden conflict, and that the temple preserves the pristine original of Genesis. His refrain is that scripture presents the Fall "more as a process than as an event," that very little is doctrinally "at stake" in the details, and that the load-bearing rope is "the birth, death, and resurrection of Jesus, the son of God."

## Summary

- **The "historically accurate?" question is itself anachronistic.** Spackman backs up to the 1500s: the Protestant Reformers threw out three of the four traditional senses of scripture and kept only the literal; the scientific revolution made mechanistic, mathematical description "the primary way we describe reality"; the Enlightenment prioritized reason over (religious) authority. The result is a modern chain — "scripture is inspired → that means true → truth means facts of science and history" — that "is not how Israelites would have read." Genre is the real question, and ancient Near Eastern creation accounts "were rarely concerned with creation itself" but used it as a framework to make some other point [00:00]–[03:30], [07:00]–[08:32]. See [[Genesis Is Theology Not Science - Sears's Reconciliation Journey]] and [[Divine Accommodation Means Revelation Meets Humans in Their Context]].
- **The "no Adam → no Christianity" syllogism is a flawed atheist inheritance.** The argument ("if Adam and Eve weren't real then there was no Fall, no need for atonement, no need for Christ") "goes back to a British atheist" — Robert Blatchford, *God and My Neighbour* (1903) — and was answered almost immediately by G. K. Chesterton and others. Ironically the syllogism was then picked up by Seventh-day Adventist [[George McCready Price]], "the father of young earthism," as an argument *against* evolution. That intelligent, committed Christians who accepted evolution (Francis Collins, various popes, [[C.S. Lewis]], Billy Graham) exist shows in practical terms that the logic fails [03:30]–[07:00]. See [[The Blatchford-Price Syllogism - No Adam No Atonement]].
- **The Fall was a change of degree or scope, not an absolute reversal of cosmic law.** Reading Genesis closely, death, reproduction, choice, and experience are *already present in the garden* on a smaller scale: Adam and Eve eat (a kind of death of plants); nothing says they were immortal apart from eating the tree of life ([[J. Reuben Clark]] allowed limited death in Eden); a 1973 church-magazine article by correlation-chair Eldredge taught they were "physically capable of having children" but "would not" while innocent; Eve "perceives that it is good for food" and "pleasant to the eyes," presupposing judgment and aesthetics. So "the fall... is a change of degree or scope, not an absolute monumental shift in the laws of the universe" — "scripture seems to present fall more as a process than as an event" [12:30]–[19:31], [33:32]–[34:32]. See [[The Fall Was a Change of Degree or Scope Not an Absolute Reversal]].
- **"No death before the Fall" is a recent interpretation, not settled doctrine.** The 1931 First Presidency said the Church "has no doctrine one way or the other on death before the fall or preadamites" and to "leave science to the scientists"; that same year it approved a [[James Talmage]] talk affirming death and reproduction had gone on "for a very long time" and publishing it as an official pamphlet [19:31]–[21:32]. See [[No Death Before the Fall Is a Twentieth-Century Interpretation Not Settled Doctrine]] and [[Nothing Has Been Revealed Concerning Evolution]].
- **What must we be dogmatic about?** President Gordon B. Hinckley's answer: "the only thing we really have to be dogmatic about is that Adam and Eve existed and we descend from them." Spackman pairs this with the **genealogical Adam** proposal: because of pedigree collapse, the most recent common ancestor of everyone now living lived only ~6,000 years ago — so a literal first couple can be ancestral to all humanity without being the *only* humans then alive [22:00]–[25:01]. See [[The Genealogical Adam and Universal Common Ancestry]].
- **The "conflicting commandments" problem is an artifact of the temple's reconciliation, not Genesis.** The command to "multiply and replenish" is in Genesis 1, where humanity (not couples) is created and there is no tree to avoid; there is "no hint of this in Genesis 2." Critics' charge that God set Adam and Eve up to fail "comes from conflating the two accounts." Joseph Smith, solving the double-creation problem, "tak[es] the contents of Genesis 2 through 3 and put[s] them into the structure of Genesis 1," which is why the two commandments sit juxtaposed *in the temple* — "but that's not actually how we have it in Genesis, Moses or Abraham." Solving the double-creation problem thereby created the conflicting-commandments problem [27:00]–[31:30]. See [[The Conflicting Commandments Are an Artifact of the Temples Double-Creation Reconciliation]].
- **"Knowing good and evil" means maturation, not an instant download.** Elsewhere in the Old Testament the phrase marks the transition from childhood to adulthood (Isaiah 7's child "before he knows to refuse the evil and choose the good"; Deuteronomy 1's "children, who... had no knowledge between good and evil"). So eating the fruit is "less of a snap of the fingers download than a maturing into adulthood," consistent with the Fall-as-process reading [31:30]–[33:32]. See [[Irenaeus on the Fall as Childlike Growth Not Corruption]].
- **Hold the rope.** "When you first swim out to the deep end of the pool you... hold on to the rope at the side." That rope is "the birth, death, and resurrection of Jesus, the son of God." Once Spackman saw "there wasn't a whole lot at stake here," it freed him to explore possibilities; "how we got here is less important than how we're getting out" [34:32]–[37:00]. Echoing Joseph Smith, the creation and Adam and Eve are not in the "fundamental principles of our religion" — "everything else is an appendage" to the atonement.
