# Life After Life

Raymond Moody's 1975 study that introduced the term **"near-death experience" (NDE)** and founded the modern field of near-death studies. Drawing on roughly 150 cases of people who were pronounced clinically dead, or came very close, and were revived, Moody abstracts a recurring **composite experience** — a loosely ordered sequence of common elements — and weighs naturalistic against survivalist explanations without claiming to have proven survival.

## Summary
- Abstracts a **~15-element composite NDE** (ineffability, peace, the noise, the dark tunnel, leaving the body, meeting others, the being of light, the life review, a border or limit, and the return) that recurs across demographically and religiously diverse subjects; no one reports every element, but most report many in a stable order [^1].
- **Methodologically modest by design:** Moody disclaims statistical rigor and frames the book as a prompt for serious study, not proof. The 2001 Preface surveys later, more controlled research (Kenneth Ring, Bruce Greyson, James Whinnery's centrifuge pilots, Melvin Morse's pediatric study) as corroboration [^2].
- Surveys **parallels** in the Bible, Plato, the *Tibetan Book of the Dead*, and Emanuel Swedenborg, then catalogs **objections** and candidate **explanations** — supernatural, physiological/pharmacological, and psychological — arguing none yet defeats the data [^3].
- **For Latter-day Saint apologetics:** an empirically-flavored corroboration of **conscious survival of bodily death**, resonant with LDS spirit-world theology, a **life review / judgment**, and a **being of light**. Serves as a Thoughtful Faith Level-1 *afterlife* source.
