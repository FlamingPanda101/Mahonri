# Wes Huff - Comparing the Quran with the New Testament

A solo presentation by **[[Wes Huff]]** ("Two Books: Looking at the New Testament and the Quran") on the comparative **transmission history** of the two texts — his actual academic specialty (textual criticism of NT and Christian-origins documents, with graduate work on the Quran). He frames four questions: how was the NT transmitted, how was the Quran transmitted, how do they compare, and what does it mean for Christian-Muslim dialogue.

## Summary
- **Free vs. controlled transmission.** The New Testament was copied without central control across the ancient world (24,000+ manuscripts in Greek, Latin, Coptic, Syriac, etc.), producing 400,000-600,000 variants — but precisely *because* no authority controlled it, those copies can be compared to recover the autographs. The Quran was an oral/memorized text standardized and pruned by [[Uthman]], who destroyed variant codices — a controlled transmission. See [[The Quran Is a Controlled Transmission and the New Testament Is a Free Transmission]].
- **Multivocality and tenacity.** The NT's many independent lines of transmission (multivocality) and the survival of early readings (tenacity) let scholars trace the text back like assembling a 100,000-piece puzzle with extra pieces. He illustrates variants with the "Wicked Bible" (1631 "thou shalt commit adultery"), the *movable nu*, and *homoeoteleuton* in 1 John 3:1. See [[Textual Multivocality and Tenacity Trace the New Testament to the Autographs]].
- **The Quran has variants too.** Against the claim of a flawless single Quran (e.g. Qazi), Huff cites the 1972 Sana'a palimpsest, the 2015 Birmingham Quran, the two living textual traditions (Hafs ~97% vs. Warsh ~3%), and Paris MS 328 (Surah 3:158, "gathered" vs. "not gathered"). [[Fred Donner]] is quoted on the "disarray" of Quranic studies; [[Andy Bannister]] and Quran Gateway are noted as a nascent attempt at Quranic text-criticism. See [[The Quran Has Its Own Manuscript Variants]].
- **The Quran's appeal to the prior scriptures.** Huff closes with [[Quran 3.3]], [[Quran 5.47]], and [[Quran 10.94]]: the Quran calls the Torah and Gospel "sent down" by God and tells Muhammad to consult them — but the Gospel of Muhammad's day (e.g. Codex Ambrosianus, Syriac) reads essentially as ours does and contradicts the Quran, so the appeal backfires. Compare [[Surah 10-94 Makes the Bible the Standard for the Quran]] and [[The Quran's Testimony to the Previous Scriptures]].
