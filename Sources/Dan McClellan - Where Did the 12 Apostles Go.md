# Dan McClellan — Where Did the 12 Apostles Go

A duet-style response to a viral evangelistic video that narrates how each of the Twelve "scattered across the ancient world" and was martyred — Peter crucified upside down, Andrew on an X-cross at Patras, and so on — building to the apologetic punchline that "nobody lays down their life like that... for a lie that they made up." [[Dan McClellan]] answers on two independent fronts: (1) **historically**, almost every one of these martyrdom traditions traces to apocryphal *Acts* written one to four centuries after the apostle's death, texts whose historicity the Christians citing them otherwise reject; and (2) **logically**, the "they died for it" inference is a straw man that confuses sincere belief with the truth of the thing believed. Because the video is itself framed as a witness-of-the-Resurrection argument, McClellan's rebuttal cuts directly against the popular minimal-facts apologetic — and, by extension, against the LDS use of the same template for the [[Apostle Analogy - Sincere Belief unto Death|Book of Mormon witnesses]].

## Summary

- **The historiographical thesis: the martyrdom stories are late legend.** "The majority of what we heard was overwhelmingly not reality, but much later traditions that were created by people writing apocryphal texts that the majority of Christians entirely reject anyway." For most of the Twelve the *only* source for *how* they died is a second-to-fifth-century apocryphal *Acts*. See [[Apostolic Martyrdom Traditions Are Late Apocryphal Legend]]. [^1]
- **Peter.** The upside-down crucifixion comes from the late-2nd-century *Acts of Peter*, "otherwise entirely rejected by most Christians." The earliest reference to Peter's death — [[Clement of Rome|1 Clement]] 5 — speaks only of his "many labors" and "testifying" before going "to his appointed place of glory," which *may* imply martyrdom but does not state it. [^2]
- **Andrew.** The crucifixion tradition is from the late-2nd/early-3rd-century *Acts of Andrew*, again rejected as historical except for the martyrdom detail it supplies. [^3]
- **James the Greater.** The "preached in Spain" tradition originates in 6th–7th-century CE Spain; per Acts 12 he was executed by Herod Agrippa I in the early 40s CE, leaving no time for a Spanish mission. *(His death is the one martyrdom actually attested in the New Testament.)* [^4]
- **John.** The "exiled to Patmos, died naturally" account depends on identifying the Beloved Disciple with John of Patmos with John son of Zebedee — an identification scholars widely reject on linguistic grounds (the Greek of Revelation vs. the Gospel rules out common authorship), and the Gospel "of John" was not written by anyone named John. See [[Beloved Disciple Was Not John the Son of Zebedee]] and [[Gospel of John Is Pseudepigraphal Not Eyewitness]]. [^5]
- **Philip.** Crucifixion traditions come from apocryphal texts; the earliest notice, from [[Polycrates of Ephesus]] (late 2nd century), says only that Philip died and was buried somewhere — no martyrdom. [^6]
- **Bartholomew, Thomas, Matthew, Thaddaeus/Jude, Simon the Zealot, Matthias.** Each martyrdom tradition derives from an apocryphal *Acts* of the 3rd–5th centuries CE (the *Acts of Bartholomew*, *Acts of Thomas*, *Acts of Matthew*, the *Acts of Andrew and Matthias*, etc.), elaborated further in medieval legend. Earlier non-martyrdom notices (e.g. Bartholomew going to India) are themselves late but predate the death-stories. [^7]
- **James the Less is conflated with James the brother of Jesus.** Later Christians merged James son of Alphaeus with [[James the brother of Jesus]] — and it is the *brother* who has "probably the best historical argument for martyrdom," his death being mentioned in [[Josephus]]. James the Less *as* son of Alphaeus has no martyrdom tradition until the 4th–5th centuries. See [[The James the Less Conflation Inflates the Martyr Count]]. [^8]
- **The logical rebuttal: dying for a belief is a straw man for truth.** McClellan grants that people die for sincerely held convictions constantly — "but nobody themselves make up a lie and then give their life like that. I don't know anybody who is suggesting that Jesus's 12 apostles made up the lie of his resurrection." Critical scholars do not claim the disciples lied; they hold the disciples "probably sincerely believed that Jesus was resurrected" — and "there is an enormous difference between sincerely believing something that's not true and making up a lie." See [[Dying for a Belief Proves Sincerity Not Truth]]. [^9]
- **People die for things that are false — even things they merely imagine or fabricate as true.** Throughout history people have given their lives "for things that they believed that were not true, and sometimes even for things that they imagine to be true," because they valued the claim or its benefits — family, community, identity, nation, legacy — more than safety. "It's exactly human nature." [^10]
- **The witness-argument is rhetoric, not logic.** Countless people have died for Islam, Mormonism, Buddhism, and secular nationalism; the bare fact of dying for a claim cannot establish the claim's truth. "The notion that the giving of one's life indicates that they must have known something to be incontrovertibly true... is just nonsense. That's not logic. That's just rhetoric." The whole edifice is "a claim that is not supported by any data being propped up by the accumulation of a number of other claims that are not supported by any data." [^11]
- **McClellan's closing exhortation.** Against the video's altar-call ("Who do you say that Jesus is?"), he urges viewers "to think more critically about the claims that you come across rather than just excitedly and uncritically repeating them to the internet." [^12]
