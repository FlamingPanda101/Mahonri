# Wireless Philosophy — The Epistemic Regress Problem (Kevin McCain)

A short, neutral philosophy explainer by epistemologist [[Kevin McCain]] (University of Alabama at Birmingham), used as a non-LDS general-philosophy segment within the [[Thoughtful Faith - Apologetics Course Overview|Thoughtful Faith]] Level 1 course, sub-section 1.1 (epistemology). McCain lays out the **epistemic regress problem** — the demand for "a reason for your reasons" that can be pressed against any belief — and surveys the four standard responses to it: foundationalism, infinitism, coherentism, and skepticism. The framing is deliberately diagnostic: the regress's main value is not as a road to skepticism but as a way to "get clear about the structure of epistemic justification."[^1][^2]

## Summary

- **The regress problem is old, and its real payoff is structural.** McCain traces the problem to Aristotle and notes [[Sextus Empiricus]] used it to argue for skepticism, but insists its "most important role" is clarifying how reasons must relate to one another and to our beliefs for justification to obtain. See [[The Epistemic Regress Problem]].[^1][^2]

- **A child's "why?" models the regress of reasons.** Just as a child can keep asking "why?", an interlocutor can keep asking for the reason behind each reason you offer (president-at-the-White-House → saw it on the news → news is accurate → …). Applied to *every* belief, this is what let skeptics argue for global skepticism. See [[The Epistemic Regress Problem]].[^3][^4]

- **The chain has only three structural options.** A chain of reasons can (1) end, (2) continue infinitely, or (3) circle back on itself. The skeptic says none yields justification; most epistemologists instead read the trilemma as a map of the available theories — plus skepticism as a fourth, negative option.[^5][^6]

- **Foundationalism (the chain ends) is dominant.** Justification bottoms out in basic beliefs — often justified by experience, which gives a reason without itself needing one — that "stop the regress." The most popular view, arguably since Aristotle. See [[Foundationalism Ends the Regress in Basic Beliefs]].[^7]

- **Infinitism (the chain runs forever) is the new minority view.** The idea that an endlessly continuable chain of reasons just *is* justification; a late-20th-century arrival that "pretty much everyone" historically dismissed and that remains relatively unpopular. See [[Infinitism Accepts an Endless Chain of Reasons]].[^8]

- **Coherentism (a web, not a chain) is the runner-up.** Coherentists reject the linear setup entirely, picturing justification as a system or web of beliefs that justifies when its parts are interconnected "in the right ways." Second-most-popular view. See [[Coherentism Replaces the Chain with a Web of Beliefs]].[^9][^10]
