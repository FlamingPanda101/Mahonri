# Let's Talk w/ Hayden Carroll — Curious Student Can't Fathom Protestant Christian Teachings

An unscripted campus conversation in which LDS apologist [[Hayden Carroll]] engages a student, **"S,"** who is **agnostic and religiously open** ("I believe in something, not anything in particular ... I'm willing to learn about everything") and who reports being repeatedly told by Protestants that unbelievers will "burn in hell," die early, or have a bad life. [^1][^2] The student finds these teachings — eternal hell for those who never heard, and a God who *creates* people he foreknows will be damned — morally **incomprehensible**, and Carroll agrees, positioning Latter-day Saint doctrine as the set of revealed "solves" that dissolve each puzzle: **postmortem opportunity** for the never-evangelized, **premortal consent** that removes God's culpability for suffering and predestination, and an **open/living canon** against the Protestant claim that "God doesn't speak anymore." [^3][^4][^5] Carroll frames Mormonism as keeping "the good of Christianity" while discarding, by revelation, "all of the horrible takes." The dialogue recurs to the same doctrinal cluster Carroll handles elsewhere — compare [[Lets Talk w Hayden Carroll - Student Says God Started All Religions]] and his Ratio Christi presentation [[Hayden Carroll - Ratio Christi Campus Talk]].

## Summary

- **Restoration framing — the one disagreement with "traditional" Christianity.** Carroll grants that on the broad theology "we're totally on board," then locates the divide: mainstream Christians "use the Bible alone" and have "cut themselves off from God" by denying public revelation after the apostles, "developing what they call a canon ... the 66 books." The LDS counter is to "question that presupposition that the Bible is all we need." This is the closed-vs-living-canon dispute developed in [[A Living Canon Is a Feature Not a Bug]] and [[Sola Scriptura Muzzles God]]. [^6][^7]
- **The hell objection — universal damnation for the never-evangelized.** The student relays being told "if you don't believe in Jesus ... you're going to burn in hell." Carroll: "Latter-day Saints, we don't believe that. We are judged according to what we know ... Is God going to make you burn in hell forever? Is that a good and fair God? We don't think so." Those without a witness in this life "will actually have an opportunity in the next life." This is the postmortem solve in [[Postmortem Salvation Resolves the Soteriological Problem]] and [[The Soteriological Problem of Evil - The Inconsistent Triad]]; cf. the Protestant problem in [[Problem of Hell and Creation]]. [^3][^8]
- **Accountability is preserved.** Carroll guards against a "do whatever you want and repent later" reading: "Does that mean you can go live your life however you want and just say I'll do it later? The answer is no. There is accountability." Ultimate salvation still comes "through somebody who knows Jesus Christ and accepts him." [^9]
- **The hypocrisy objection — and why it cannot disprove the faith.** The student's main reason for rejecting Christianity is that adherents are hypocrites (she cites Joel Osteen allegedly not opening his church during a Texas flood). Carroll concedes the pastoral force but severs it from the truth-claim: "Either Jesus Christ ... was risen again or he wasn't ... if I'm a hypocrite, that doesn't impact the historical event of the resurrection," and "on that standard you can't be a part of any organization ... you're going to find hypocrites in every organization." See [[Adherent Hypocrisy Does Not Disprove the Religions Historical Claim]]. [^10][^11][^12]
- **"God creates people knowing they'll go to hell" — the creation/predestination puzzle.** Carroll returns to an off-screen interlocutor who "agreed that God creates people knowing they're going to go to hell," calling the implication incoherent: "Why create those people? Just create only people who are going to go to heaven." He ties the Protestant inability to escape this to their closed canon and lack of "salvation after death." See [[Problem of Hell and Creation]] and [[The Three Facts About God Entail Predestination]]. [^13][^14]
- **The premortal-existence solve — consent, predestination, and the problem of evil.** Carroll's signature move: the doctrine of the **pre-existence** ("we believe people have existed in some form forever ... the word we use is intelligence"). "Our doctrine is that you and I actually chose to come here. ... God said, 'I have a plan for you ... you might have cancer or you might die early ... I want you to make the choice.'" This consent "solves a lot of the suffering problem" and answers predestination — "God knows who was going to go to hell before he created them. Our solve is yes, but we chose it." See [[LDS Premortal Consent Removes Gods Culpability]], [[Pre-Existence Solves the Problem of Evil]], and [[Pre-Mortal Existence Dissolves the Problem of Hell]]. [^15][^16][^17]
- **The canon argument — no scripture says prophets stopped.** Carroll runs the prophetic-continuity line from [[Adam]], [[Noah]], [[Abraham]], [[Enoch]], [[Moses]], [[Jesus of Nazareth|Jesus Christ]], Peter/James/John, and [[Paul the Apostle|Paul]], to "and the Christian world says, 'And that was it.'" His logical point: "there's no scripture that says there's no more prophets ... no verse that says God is done speaking ... So even if you do accept the Bible, it doesn't necessitate the stop of prophets." A "prophet" means only "someone authorized to speak for God." See [[Hebrews 1-1-2 - Jesus the Last Prophet Rebutted]]. [^18][^19]
- **A living prophet, dated in real time.** Carroll notes the conversation is October 14th: "our previous prophet just passed away. He was 101 years old. They just announced a new prophet 30 minutes ago" — he plans to watch the livestream after. (This dates the dialogue to the death of [[Russell M. Nelson]] and the announcement of his successor.) [^20]
- **Islam compared.** Asked whether only Christianity holds that God no longer speaks, Carroll offers a tentative parallel for Islam: they "have the Quran ... Muhammad in the 600s, the final prophet ... I don't think they have prophets today," while disclaiming expertise. See [[Muhammad]]. [^21]
- **The "stuck in a 2,000-year-old book" theme.** Both interlocutors stress that "our world has changed so much," so a faith confined to an ancient closed canon "can't solve all your issues." Carroll balances this against the value of being "traditional or conservative" amid a culture he thinks is "seeing too much change." [^22][^23]
- **Where it lands.** Carroll explicitly disclaims a hard sell ("I'm not here to put in a plug for Mormonism"), while still commending Latter-day Saint service (LDS "give X amount of service on top of any other American") and offering resources. The student, previously unsure about Mormons, comes away finding them "very open-minded" and reiterates her plan to "talk to a little bit of everyone" before committing. [^24][^25]
