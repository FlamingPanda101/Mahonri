# Informed Saints — The Biggest Mistake People Make About DNA and the Book of Mormon

The [[Informed Saints]] hosts — [[Stephen Smoot]], [[Neal Rappleye]], and [[Jasmine Rapley]] — work through *Misconceptions about DNA in the Book of Mormon* by [[John Butler (DNA scientist)|John M. Butler]] and [[Ugo Perego|Ugo A. Perego]], a population geneticist who specializes in Native American DNA and a forensic geneticist. The episode catalogues the popular online claim "no Israelite DNA in the Americas, therefore the Book of Mormon is false" and dismantles it misconception by misconception, arguing the objection rests on a simplistic picture of population genetics and on a particular (and long-abandoned) hemispheric, empty-continent *interpretation* of the text rather than on the science itself. See [[The Empty Continent Assumption Underlies the DNA Objection]], [[Founder Effect and Genetic Drift Erase a Small Founding Signal]], and [[The Philistine DNA Study Shows Genetic Signals Vanish into Host Populations]] for the existing vault cluster this episode reinforces.

## Summary
- **"The biggest mistake" is a category confusion.** The hosts argue most of the DNA objection is really a criticism of how *some* Latter-day Saints have read the Book of Mormon (a hemispheric, empty-continent reading), not a scientific finding. Once the limited-geography / other-peoples reading is granted, DNA is "defanged considerably."[^1]
- **We have no reference point for Lehi's DNA.** There is no preserved Lehite/Mulekite/Jaredite genome to compare against; ancient Israelites were not genetically uniform, and a small founding group may carry rare, hard-to-detect markers rather than "representative" ones — a [[Founder Effect and Genetic Drift Erase a Small Founding Signal|founder-effect]] point.[^2]
- **The empty-continent assumption is false on the text and on archaeology.** Even internally, the Jaredites overlap Lehi's arrival, so the continent is not empty when Lehi lands; and archaeology shows the Americas were already populated. See [[The Empty Continent Assumption Underlies the DNA Objection]].[^3]
- **Limited geography is not goalpost-moving — it predates the DNA debate.** Latter-day Saint thinkers (Anthony Ivins in the First Presidency, later Bruce R. McConkie, authors as far back as the 1920s, antecedents to the 1840s per [[Matt Roper]]) had already abandoned the strict hemispheric reading *before* DNA was discovered. The 1981 introduction's "principal ancestors → among the ancestors" change is a clarification of a modern editorial framing, not a revealed text. See [[The Limited-Geography Reading Predates the DNA Debate]].[^4]
- **The Neanderthal objection is apples-to-oranges.** "If we can sequence 70,000-year-old Neanderthals, why not Lehi?" fails because Neanderthal remains are first *identified anthropologically* and then sequenced, whereas there are no identifiable Nephite bodies, and "Nephite/Lamanite" are cultural-political labels, not anthropological categories. See [[The Neanderthal DNA Disanalogy]].[^5]
- **"DNA can't just disappear" misunderstands genetics.** Specific markers can become diminishingly difficult to detect via genetic drift, the founder effect, and the difference between *genealogical* and *genetic* descent — you have ancestors who contributed none of your DNA. Y-chromosome and mitochondrial lineages can vanish through a single break in male-to-male or female-to-female descent. See [[Genealogical Descent Is Not Genetic Descent]].[^6]
- **Selection and admixture favor the host population.** Colonists' genes, not adapted to the new environment, get selected against; in documented ancient cases the *settled* population's genes flow into the colonizers, not the reverse — illustrated by the [[The Philistine DNA Study Shows Genetic Signals Vanish into Host Populations|Philistine study]] where Aegean DNA vanished within ~200 years.[^7]
- **The genetic bottleneck compounds the loss.** Cataclysmic die-offs (war, famine, disease) shrink the gene pool: the Nephite extermination, and especially the ~90% post-Columbian population collapse from disease, would obscure any small founding signal. See [[The Genetic Bottleneck Compounds the Loss of a Founding Signal]].[^8]
- **Haplogroup X2A does not prove the Book of Mormon either.** The hosts warn faithful "Heartlander" enthusiasts off X2A: it entered the Americas ~18,000 years before present (far too old) and is a *sister* lineage of Middle Eastern X, not descended from it. See [[Haplogroup X2A Is Not Valid Evidence for the Book of Mormon]].[^9]
- **The hypothesis is essentially untestable.** [[Michael F. Whiting]] (who reviewed scientific grant proposals) concluded that "did a small group of Hebrews reach the Americas and contribute genetically" cannot be framed as a falsifiable scientific hypothesis with the available data — none of the genetic data was gathered with that question in mind.[^10]
- **God did not "change the Lamanites' DNA."** Reading the "skin of blackness" curse as a divine edit of the genome is a category error — a theological/miraculous claim smuggled into a scientific argument — and is unnecessary to reconcile the text with population genetics. See [[God Did Not Change Lamanite DNA]] and [[Book of Mormon Skin of Blackness Is Not About Race]].[^11]
- **The honest verdict: DNA neither disproves nor proves the Book of Mormon.** It does falsify a hemispheric, empty-continent reading. The hosts stress that Butler and Perego are full believers with every motive to cite real evidence, and that the debate has now shifted from science to hermeneutics — the informed critics (Thomas Murphy, Simon Southerton) themselves moved from genetics to "but the text doesn't teach limited geography."[^12]
