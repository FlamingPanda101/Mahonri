A **WARD RADIO** interview with historian **[[Don Bradley]]** (author of *The Lost 116 Pages*), hosted by **[[Cardon Ellis]]** with **[[Brad Whitbeck]]** and **[[Jonah Barnes]]**, reconstructing **what the Liahona actually looked like and how it functioned**. Because the surviving Book of Mormon gives only sparse description, Bradley triangulates the text with two documented 19th-century sources to recover the device's design, its second spindle's purpose, and why it was eventually retired.

## Summary

- **The textual puzzle: a second spindle with no explained function.** [[1 Nephi 16.13|1 Nephi 16]] describes a round ball ("compass") with **two spindles**, one of which "pointed the way we should go," but the abridgment never says what the *other* spindle did — the gap Bradley sets out to fill [^1].
- **Source 1 — Fayette Lapham (via Joseph Smith Sr.).** [[Fayette Lapham]], a relative of [[Martin Harris]]'s mother, visited the Smith home (~1830, the Book of Mormon at press) and reported the Liahona had "two pointers, one pointing steadily the way they should go, the other the way to where they could get provisions and other necessaries" [^2].
- **Source 2 — Francis Gladden Bishop (via Martin Harris).** [[Francis Gladden Bishop]], a close Kirtland associate of Martin Harris, published a detailed description traceable to Harris: a small **brass ball about three inches in diameter**, two steel points (spindles) in opposite directions, with **12 squares around each point** and, between the 24 squares, **figures representing things on the earth — vegetation, animals, running streams, water** [^3].
- **The reconstructed mechanism: directional + pictographic + verbal.** Synthesizing the three sources: the **first spindle points the direction**, the **second spindle points around the rim to a pictographic symbol** of what they are heading toward (e.g., a picture for wild game), and the changing **writing on the spindles** ([[1 Nephi 16.13|1 Nephi 16]]) adds verbal communication — combining three modes in one device, which Cardon likens to the **Antikythera mechanism** — see [[The Liahona's Second Spindle Pointed to Pictographic Symbols]] [^4].
- **Three inches is tiny.** The hosts note the three-inch diameter overturns the usual two-handed-globe depictions — it is "golden snitch" / pocket-watch sized [^5].
- **A magic-8-ball analogy for the writing.** Bradley entertains that the changing writing could be a *technological* mechanism surfacing pre-existing text (like a magic 8-ball) rather than purely miraculous appearance, fitting the ancient practice of **casting lots** to determine God's will [^6].
- **Why the Liahona stopped working — succession to the interpreters.** From Lapham's account, after arrival in the New World the Liahona led its bearer (Bradley argues [[Mosiah]] the first, during the exodus to Zarahemla) to an object he was told to put on his face — the **interpreters** — and once it had led him to its own replacement, the Liahona ceased to work. This single lost-pages narrative answers *two* gaps the surviving text leaves open: how the Nephites got the interpreters, and why the Liahona fell out of use — see [[The Liahona Was Succeeded by the Interpreters]] [^7].
- **The Nephite sacred cache parallels the Ark of the Covenant.** The Liahona was likely in the stone box (per Katherine Smith Salisbury). Bradley draws a point-by-point parallel between the Nephite relic-cache and the biblical Ark: gold plates ↔ stone tablets; interpreters ↔ Urim and Thummim on the high priest's breastplate; the Liahona (a round ball giving sustenance) ↔ the pot of manna (a round ball giving sustenance); the [[Laban|sword of Laban]] ↔ the sword of Goliath kept in the tabernacle — see [[The Nephite Sacred Cache Parallels the Ark of the Covenant]] [^8].
- **Does the Church know?** Bradley notes the Church has no "chief archaeologist" but consults historians and Book of Mormon scholars who are aware of his work; he expects a "gradual trickling" of this material into future productions [^9].
