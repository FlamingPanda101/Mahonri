# Exposing the Incoherence of Critics Like Bill Reel — The Sin of Inconsistency

[[Jacob Hansen]]'s second entry in a *Thoughtful Faith* series on "intellectual sins" — following arbitrary standards with **the sin of an inconsistent or incoherent worldview**. Drawing on presuppositional apologists [[Greg Bahnsen]] and [[Cornelius Van Til]], Hansen argues that a worldview whose claims contradict each other "cannot be true... it's disqualified from the start," and turns this test on the prominent critic [[Bill Reel]], whose denial of free will (he argues) collapses the very moral condemnations Reel levels at the Church.

## Summary
- **Logical vs. everyday "inconsistency."** Hansen first clears up terms: in ordinary speech "consistent" means reliable/predictable, but in logic a *set of claims* is consistent only if all can be true at once; an inconsistent set entails a contradiction (an "a and not-a"), which "is false in all logically possible worlds" — like saying 2 + 3 = 7.[^1] A worldview is incoherent when its own claims contradict each other.[^2]
- **The Bahnsen method: arbitrariness, presuppositions, inconsistency.** Citing [[Greg Bahnsen]]'s lectures (and [[Cornelius Van Til]]), Hansen lays out a three-step engagement: (1) look for *arbitrary* opinions with no basis; (2) surface the unargued *philosophical presuppositions* a person takes for granted; (3) expose the *internal inconsistencies* ("dialectical tensions") in those presuppositions. Bahnsen's worked example is Epicurean empiricism — "all knowledge is perceptual" is itself not known by perception, and Epicurus's imperceptible atoms could never have been perceived, so the view "refutes itself."[^3][^4]
- **Bill Reel's double standard on free will.** In a recorded exchange, [[Bill Reel]] grants that humans have no free will/agency (all behavior is determined by genetics, epigenetics, and upbringing) — yet still wants to condemn racism, pedophilia, and the Church as "morally reprehensible." Hansen presses: "we don't hold people morally accountable for things they don't choose to do," so Reel's worldview is internally inconsistent. Reel visibly "struggles with the contradiction."[^5][^6]
- **Atheism's self-refutation.** A second sample inconsistency: if the atheist is merely the product of "chance plus matter plus time," then his own reasoning — including his belief in atheism — "is itself a product of chance" and "completely unreliable." The worldview undercuts the rationality it needs to argue for itself.[^7]
- **Belief-vs-behavior incoherence (Peterson).** Hansen adds the "most common" inconsistency — the gap between professed belief and lived behavior — illustrated with a [[Jordan Peterson]] clip: "what you act out is much more what you are than what you say about yourself," so a person's real, operative "religious" axioms are read off their actions.[^8][^9]
- **Use it in public, not in private ministry.** Because logically "ripping apart" someone's worldview "usually does more harm than good," Hansen counsels restraint one-on-one; but in public debate with an audience, a "calm, civil, and unrelenting deconstruction" can help build the kingdom — "wise as serpents but harmless as doves." His payoff: the believing worldview "has its challenges" but is "relatively consistent," and "without logical consistency a worldview cannot be true."[^10][^11]
