# Mathematical Challenges to Darwin's Theory of Evolution
A *Uncommon Knowledge* panel hosted by Peter Robinson in Fiesole, Italy, in which mathematician [[David Berlinski]], Yale computer scientist [[David Gelernter]], and philosopher of science [[Stephen C. Meyer]] argue that neo-Darwinism cannot account for the origin of new biological form. The case is built on a probabilistic core — functional proteins are vanishingly rare in sequence space — and on the claim that producing new life requires new genetic *information*, which in every other experience traces back to a mind.[^1] Gelernter, who once accepted Darwin's "beautiful" theory, explains his public defection after reading Meyer's *Darwin's Doubt*.[^2]

## Participants
- [[David Berlinski]] · [[Stephen C. Meyer]] · [[David Gelernter]]

## Summary
- **Macroevolution, not microevolution, is the problem.** All three grant Darwin's mechanism explains small adaptations (beak shape, fur density) but deny it explains the *origin of species* and higher body plans.[^3]
- **The Cambrian explosion is abrupt.** Dozens of animal body plans appear in roughly 5-10 million years with no precursors — a "blink of an eye" against the waiting times population genetics would predict.[^4]
- **New form requires new information.** A new protein means a new gene means new code; the Cambrian is "an explosion of biological information," and information in our uniform experience always comes from a mind.[^5]
- **Combinatorial inflation makes random search hopeless.** For a 150-amino-acid protein the ratio of functional to non-functional sequences is ~1 in 10^77; even ~10^40 organisms across Earth's history can search only a trillion-trillion-trillionth of the space, so chance is "overwhelmingly more likely to be false than true."[^6]
- **Early developmental mutations are fatal.** Mutations that could remake a body plan must act early in development, where they are almost invariably lethal — a "destructive dilemma" (late = no effect, early = fatal).[^7]
- **Darwinism functions as a worldview.** The panel frames Darwin alongside Marx and Freud as a materialistic worldview-builder, which explains the "bitter" academic reaction to ID.[^8]
