# John Anderson Media — Love Isn't Enough: Gay Marriage, Surrogacy & Divorce (Katy Faust)

A third conversation between [[John Anderson (media)|John Anderson]] and [[Katy Faust]], founder of *Them Before Us*, recorded in Sydney. The throughline is a **child-centric reframing of every family debate**: instead of asking what adults want or what makes adults happy, ask what children have a *right* to — and Faust's answer is that children have a natural right to be known and loved by their own biological mother and father. From that single premise she re-reads same-sex marriage, surrogacy and donor conception, no-fault divorce, the "no-difference" parenting studies, and even the dating crisis and the political gender split. Anderson supplies a living test case throughout: his mother died when he was three, and he was raised by his unremarried father.

This note indexes the **new** arguments in the episode; claims already developed elsewhere in the vault — the value of mother-father complementarity ([[Complementarity Is Not Specificity - The Salt-Crystal Confusion]] treats the underlying logic), family as the locus of meaning ([[Evolution Points to Family as the Locus of Meaning]]), the IVF/conception question ([[The IVF Reductio Against Conception-as-Ensoulment]]), the misuse-of-the-sexual-faculty argument ([[Misusing the Sexual Faculty Is Wrong Apart from Harm or Consent]]), equal worth grounded in being not doing ([[Equal Human Rights Come From What We Are Not What We Can Do]]), and the family-structure "fruits" data ([[Utah and Idaho Top the Family Structure Index]], [[The Law of Chastity as a Family-Stability Engine]]) — are wikilinked rather than re-created.

## Summary

- **The child's right reframes the slogan.** Faust's foundational move: if children have a right to their mother and father, then "love makes a family" *functionally means* "child loss makes a family," because a child must lose a biological parent to be placed in a motherless or fatherless home. She tests this against her own life — the premise of redefining marriage implies "two men could have replaced my mom" with zero impact, which she calls "not just statistically a lie" but demonstrably false from lived experience.[^1] See [[Love Makes a Family Means Child Loss Makes That Family]].
- **Loss by intentionality wounds differently than loss by tragedy.** Faust and Anderson develop a careful distinction: a child who loses a parent to *death* is mourned-with by a whole community that remembers the parent fondly, whereas a child who loses a parent to *divorce or by design* is often isolated in grief — sometimes the only one who looks back on the lost home. Modern medicine and the end of mass-casualty wars mean we now *create* mother-loss "in the name of progress" that we used to label tragedy. Studies are cited (secondhand) that children who lose a parent to death fare *better* than those who lose one to divorce.[^2] See [[Loss by Intentionality Wounds Differently Than Loss by Tragedy]].
- **The "no-difference" studies are weak; the consensus they overturned is robust.** Faust argues the 2005–2015 glut of "kids fare no different with two moms or two dads" studies used recruited (non-random) samples, tiny n, inadequate controls, and — crucially — measured no real outcomes and never waited for the children to grow up. The gold-standard, population-based work (she credits **Paul Sullins**) found major deficits. She sets this against three pre-2005 "truisms" of family-structure research: biology matters, gender matters, and losing a biological parent is harmful.[^3] See [[The No-Difference Same-Sex Parenting Studies Are Methodologically Weak]].
- **You become what you behold.** Children default to the family patterns they witness — children of divorce are ~45% more likely to divorce; daughters of single mothers more likely to become single mothers — so escaping a pattern requires *beholding* a healthier one. Faust tells her own and her husband's story of being "discipled" into a new way of marriage by healthy couples at church; Anderson presses the role-modeling gap for boys with no father (or no mother) to copy.[^4] See [[You Become What You Behold - Modeling as the Engine of Family Transmission]].
- **Child rights confront sexual autonomy as the highest value.** Faust (in "Jordan Peterson speak") argues the reason child-rights claims provoke a "violent reaction" is that they dethrone what she calls "the ultimate god of the left," sexual identity and autonomy: if children have a right to their parents, then adult rights "to marry, to parenthood, to happiness" must "bow down." Anderson adds the anti-egoism rejoinder — others sacrificed for you at both ends of life — and Faust grounds it in a theology of human vulnerability.[^5] See [[Child Rights Confront Sexual Autonomy as the Highest Value]].
- **Bring back casual dating.** Faust diagnoses a Christian over-correction: reacting to the hookup culture, courtship culture taught that one should not date unless the other is already "marriage material," so young adults knock most candidates out before a first coffee. Dating, she argues, is a *skill* nobody practices anymore; dating apps make it worse by elevating "the hotties" and swiping past the 89% who are quality but not a "10." Her fix: low-stakes casual dating, and a matchmaker (an older married woman) in every church.[^6] See [[Bring Back Casual Dating - The Courtship Overcorrection]].
- **Surrounding observations (not separately noted).** The episode also touches the depopulation/"population winter" theme; the squandering of peak fertility under career-first messaging ("your career shouldn't look just like a man's"); the political gender split (young men toward traditionalism via responsible male models, young women leftward via empathy-for-the-underdog social contagion, Faust citing [[Jordan Peterson]]'s "not everything that cries is a baby"); IVF as not-pro-life and not-actually-healing (the Trump administration's stalled IVF executive order); and a reading of the UK "rape gang" controversy as empathy misdirected toward victimizers. These are framed as applications of the child-centric lens rather than free-standing arguments.[^7]
