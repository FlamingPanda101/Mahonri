# Ward Radio — Meet the 7th Century Apocryphal Priest Who Inspired Lehi

A Ward Radio episode in which host **[[Cardon Ellis]]** interviews **[[Jonah Barnes]]** ("the associate professor of all things apocryphal") about **Rechab** and the **Rechabites** as a candidate for the unnamed "prophets of old" that [[Lehi]] invokes in [[1 Nephi 1.8-19|1 Nephi 1:19-20]]. Barnes argues the apocryphal *History of the Rechabites* (the *Zosimus* narrative), read alongside [[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35]], describes a "proto-Lehi" — a righteous Jerusalem-era family driven out for preaching the Messiah who flee across a great water to a promised land, yielding a six-point parallel to the Book of Mormon.

## Summary
- **Rechab as the "Proto-Lehi."** Barnes proposes the otherwise-unnamed "prophets of old, whom they had cast out and stoned and slain" of [[1 Nephi 1.8-19|1 Nephi 1:19-20]] include **Rechab**, whose descendants the *History of the Rechabites* portrays fleeing doomed Jerusalem for messianism — a story Barnes calls "just like the story of Lehi," listing six parallels too close (he argues) to be coincidence. See [[The Rechabites Are the Prophets of Old Who Inspired Lehi]].
- **Jeremiah 35 fixes them in Lehi's era.** [[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35]] presents the Rechabites as contemporaries of Jeremiah (and thus Lehi), bound by their forefather **Jonadab son of Rechab** to a nomadic life — no houses, vineyards, or settled cities — which Barnes reads as "explaining why Lehi was so willing to leave the land of his inheritance," and ties to the Kenites who accompanied Israel.[^1]
- **The *Zosimus* apocryphon mirrors the flight.** In the *History of the Rechabites*, the monk Zosimus is led by an angel across an impassable body of water to a righteous people who say "we are the children of Rechab," driven out of Jerusalem for "worshiping God the Son of God himself."[^2]
- **A perpetual-posterity promise echoing Enos.** [[Jeremiah 35|Jeremiah 35:19]]'s reward — Jonadab "shall not want a man to stand before me for ever" — parallels the covenant that a righteous father's seed "will not be utterly destroyed" ([[Enos 1.1-2|Enos 1]]); later traditions (the Mishnah's "sons of the water-drinkers," Joseph Wolff's 1839 report of Yemeni Rechabite-claimants near Sinai) extend the line.[^3]
- **A deuteronomistic suppression motive.** Barnes folds this into the vault's broader thesis that a record of Jerusalem's establishment murdering Messiah-preachers was "an inconvenient truth" later editors removed — fitting the southern-edition / [[Deuteronomistic Corruption of the Old Testament|deuteronomistic-corruption]] pattern and the claim that the Book of Mormon presupposes a richer, partly-lost pre-exilic record.[^4]
