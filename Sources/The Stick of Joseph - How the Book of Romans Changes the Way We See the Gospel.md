# The Stick of Joseph — How the Book of Romans Changes the Way We See the Gospel

A conversation hosted by [[Dave Butler|David D. John Butler]] ("Big Mustard") with guests [[Brian Thomassen]] and Brad Whitbeck, arguing that the **Epistle to the Romans** — "the most theologically impactful book within Western Christianity" — is badly misread because Latter-day Saints (and everyone else in the West) inherit an **Augustinian lens** that is anachronistic to Paul's first-century setting. Thomassen's reconstruction reads Romans as a *Greco-Roman rhetorical performance* addressed to a Hellenized Roman elite: grace is reciprocal *charis*, Paul's "I" is a fictive persona, the famous Romans 7 dilemma is a *Medea* allusion, and the letter's real thesis is **self-mastery (*enkrateia*) through Christ, not the law**. It is a companion to the channel's [[The Stick of Joseph - Are Christians Becoming Mormon with Brian Thomassen|earlier Thomassen episode]].

## Summary

- **Romans is huge, but under-read in the church.** It drove the conversions/theologies of [[Augustine]], Luther, Melanchthon, Zwingli, Bucer, and Calvin, yet gets little LDS pulpit attention — and what attention it gets filters it "through Protestant theology" and proof-texting rather than reading the text on its own terms. Coheres with [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]].[^1]
- **The Augustinian lens is anachronistic.** [[Augustine]] (late 4th c.) read Romans (c. 55 CE) through problems that did not exist for Paul: a settled Jewish-Christian split (the temple still stood in the 50s; the parting came after 70 AD), his fights against **Pelagius** and the **Manicheans**, his invention of **original sin**, and a Latin, *versified* text he could parse for dogma. Crucially, **Augustine did not know Greek**, cutting him off from the classical tradition and the roots of the Jesus movement. The Eastern church never made him a saint, rejecting inherited sin. Coheres with [[The Pelagian Controversy on Grace and Human Ability]] and [[The Pelagianism Erratum]].[^2][^3]
- **Versification distorts the argument.** Chapter/verse divisions are not in Paul's continuous Greek; e.g. the "therefore" that opens Romans 2:1 belongs to the end of Romans 1, and the split makes readers miss the point. The text becomes "a vehicle for establishing dogma" rather than rhetoric/dialectic.[^4]
- **Grace is reciprocal *charis*, not a one-way boon.** On the Augustinian reading, grace is hierarchical, a free "boon" given from above to a *passive* recipient. But to a Greco-Roman audience **charis** was a *reciprocal* relationship (depicted as the three Graces in a circular dance — one gives, one receives, one returns); there is no passivity. Parallels: Japanese *on-gaeshi*; Sophocles' *Ajax* "favor begets favor." "Justified *freely*" (Romans 3:24) means God was *not compelled* (cf. *xenia*/guest-friendship), not that nothing is returned. See [[Grace in Romans Is Reciprocal Charis Not a One-Way Boon]]; cf. [[Grace Is Unconditional Love Not an Earned Wage]], [[Luther Inserted Allein Faith Alone Into Romans 3-28]], and the vault scripture note [[Romans 3.23-25]].[^5][^6]
- **The audience is a Hellenized Roman elite.** Paul writes *in Greek* to *Latin*-speaking Romans — analogy: Joseph Smith writing to Londoners *in French* would imply a specific educated audience. Greek literacy marked the upper social strata. So the readers are a classically educated, *paideia*-trained class.[^7]
- **The hook is self-mastery (*enkrateia*).** Why would Romans care about a conquered, "irrelevant" people (the Jews)? Because the Jews *persisted*, and Romans (a martial people prizing discipline) respected what endures: the **Mosaic law** as a vehicle for *self-mastery*. The earliest Gentile converts (Cornelius the centurion; the "god-fearers") were drawn to that disciplined regimen — the same move [[Philo of Alexandria]] made for a Gentile audience. See [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law]].[^8]
- **Romans 7 is a Medea allusion.** The core trope — "the good that I would I do not, but the evil which I would not, that I do" ([[Romans 7.19|Romans 7:19]], also 7:15) — would register to any Greco-Roman like "to be or not to be," a quotation from **Euripides' *Medea*** (430 BCE), the deliberating sorceress who murders her children: the stock example of **acrasia** (weakness of the will), the opposite of *enkrateia*. Thomassen documents the same line echoed by Ovid, Epictetus, and **Seneca** (whose *taliporos anthropos* Paul quotes at [[Romans 7.24|Romans 7:24]]). Medea is the "dangerous barbarian other"; Rome used her (and Cleopatra as a Medea figure against Antony) to define itself as civilized. See [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law]].[^9][^10]
- **Paul's "I" is a fictive persona (*prosōpopoeia*).** The first-person "I" of [[Romans 7.7-9|Romans 7:7-9]] is not autobiography but *prosōpopoeia* — a "fictive eye," a *persona* (Greek *prosōpon*, "face/mask") the reader inhabits. Paul adopts the character of a Roman trying (and failing) to take on the Mosaic law, just as Origen later faulted Celsus for botching the *prosōpon* of a Jew. See [[Paul's I in Romans 7 Is a Fictive Persona Not Autobiography]].[^11][^12]
- **The flip: Romans are the real barbarians; mastery comes through Christ.** Paul's clever inversion: "you Romans present yourselves as the civilized masters of reason — but you are slaves to your passions, and a borrowed, alien fealty to the law will only make you fail." Real *enkrateia* is found "within Christ" ([[Romans 7.4|Romans 7:4]] — "married to another, even to him who is raised from the dead"), through baptism, not the law. The gift of Christ is *civilization in the kingdom of God*. See [[Romans Argues Real Self-Mastery Comes Through Christ Not the Law]].[^13][^14]
- **Recovering the source-material is its own apologetic.** The whole exercise — recovering the *Medea* reference, *charis*, the fictive "I" — shows how far later tradition drifted from Paul's world "because Augustine didn't speak Greek," a drift LDS readers inherit unawares. The takeaway: reread Romans treating every "I" as *prosōpopoeia* and the theme as self-mastery vs. *acrasia*.[^15][^16]
