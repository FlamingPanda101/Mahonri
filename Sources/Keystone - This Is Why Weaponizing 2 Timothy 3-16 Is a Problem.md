# Keystone — "This Is Why Weaponizing 2 Timothy 3:16 Is a Problem"

A [[Keystone]] explainer arguing that [[2 Timothy 3.16-17|2 Timothy 3:16]] ("all scripture is God-breathed") does not support biblical inerrancy. The host stacks "bigger fish" in sequence: the verse cannot refer to the Bible (which did not yet exist), "inspired" does not mean "perfect," and — leaning on [[John C. Poirier]]'s monograph — the Greek *theopneustos* most plausibly meant "life-giving / vivifying," not "inerrant," in the era 2 Timothy was written.

## Summary
- **The Bible cannot make claims about itself.** 2 Timothy is a single first-century letter written before many biblical books and centuries before the canon was compiled; reading "all scripture" as "the Bible" is like reading one song's lyrics as a description of an entire Spotify playlist that did not yet exist. "All scripture" (*pasa graphē*) would, for the author, have largely meant traditional Jewish scripture. See [[Theopneustos Was Used for Sandals Ointments and Streams Not Inerrancy]].
- **Inspired does not mean perfect.** Quoting scholar [[John C. Poirier]]: "if I say I'm inspired, no one thinks I'm claiming to be inerrant," yet when scripture claims inspiration the inerrancy claim is treated as "loud and clear" — "the rules are clearly flexible." The word *inspired* has been used to smuggle a system of ideas into the text.
- **Theopneustos meant "life-giving."** Poirier's study (Library of New Testament Studies) finds *theopneustos* in the relevant period described sandals, burial ointments, and streams of water — none of which are "inerrant." The sense is *vivifying / life-giving / life-extending*: scripture is good and spiritually life-giving, an endorsement, but not a claim of inerrancy.
- **"Inspired" is a third-century reading.** The inspirationist understanding arose with [[Origen]] in the third century; per [[Dan McClellan]], Origen had rhetorical incentive to read *theopneustos* as marking a divine source for a unified, verbally inspired scripture, and that reading stuck.
- **Outside corroboration.** Keystone reports that an anonymous published non-LDS scholar, Rev. Dr. [[Angela Parker]] (New Testament and Greek), and Dr. Anna Cwikla all agreed the verse is not a proof text for inerrancy / that Poirier's translation is sound.
- The episode closes by pointing to [[Galatians 1.8|Galatians 1:8]] as the next commonly weaponized verse.
