# Data Over Dogma — The Gospel of John: A New History (with Hugo Méndez)

Hosts **[[Dan McClellan]]** and **[[Dan Beecher]]** interview **[[Hugo Mendez]]**, associate professor of religious studies at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, about his book *The Gospel of John: A New History*. The conversation ranges over the existence (or not) of the "Johannine community," the authorship and unity of John, the two major textual additions, John's high Christology, its pervasive symbolism, and his thesis that John is fundamentally a pseudepigraphal work akin to the apocryphal gospels.

## Summary
- Méndez argues there was **no "Johannine community"** — the similarities between the Gospel and the letters of John are best explained not by a shared community transmitting tradition but by later authors *copying and imitating* an earlier text; his 2020 open-access article "Did the Johannine Community Exist?" answers, in effect, "no" [^1].
- He holds that **1, 2, and 3 John are pseudepigrapha** written to look like supplemental works of the Gospel's author, and that the **Gospel of John itself is pseudepigraphal** — a single author presenting himself as an eyewitness ("the disciple whom Jesus loved") he was not [^2].
- The **Beloved Disciple is not John the son of Zebedee**: Acts 4 presents that John as illiterate, a Galilean fisherman with no tie to Jerusalem's high-priestly circles, whereas the Beloved Disciple is literate, theologically sophisticated, and a friend of the high priest [^3].
- John was likely written by **one author** who developed his manuscript in drafts/stages (hence minor "seams/skips"), with **two later additions**: the *pericope adulterae* (7:53–8:11), absent before the 5th century, and **chapter 21**, after the original ending at 20:30–31 [^4].
- John **knew the Synoptics** and deliberately changed them; it gave Christianity its strong sense of Jesus' **pre-existence and divinity**, and deploys **pervasive symbolism** turning nearly every story into a "parable" of Jesus' teaching — making John, Méndez argues, more like the Gospel of Thomas/Mary than the Synoptics [^5].
