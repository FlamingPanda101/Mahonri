# Dan McClellan — The Trinity Is Not Unavoidably Biblical

[[Dan McClellan]], a credentialed Hebrew Bible scholar working from [[Critical Scholarship|academic-biblical-studies]], does a point-by-point stitch-in response to a content creator who promises to prove "really quickly and very concisely that the Trinity is biblical and is unavoidably so." McClellan's thesis is that the Trinity is **a post-biblical philosophical framework imposed on the texts to unify them**, not a doctrine the biblical authors held — so calling it "unavoidable" begs the question. The biblical data are diverse and predominantly [[Subordination Within the New Testament|subordinationist]]; "unavoidability" is an artifact of having already decided the answer and then reading it back in.

Because this video covers the very same terrain as the sibling [[Dan McClellan - Responding to Wes Huff on the Trinity]] from the same round, almost every individual move here is already documented in the Trinity cluster (see the wikilinks in the Summary). Its one distinctive contribution — repeated roughly half a dozen times as the through-line — is the explicit naming of the **editorial procedure** univocality forces: [[Univocality Forces Centering Aligned Texts and Marginalizing the Rest]].

The data are congenial to the LDS rejection of the creedal Trinity, though McClellan is not making an LDS argument. See also [[Trinitarianism Is Anachronistic to the Biblical Period]] and [[Canonization Forced the Harmonization That Produced the Trinity]].

## Summary

- **"Christians before Trinitarianism."** Against "if you deny the Trinity you deny the God of the Bible," McClellan notes "there have always been many Christians who have denied the Trinity; in fact there were generations of Christians before the idea of the Trinity was ever created."[^1] See [[Latter-day Saints Are Christians Just Not Creedal Christians]].
- **A post-biblical synthesis imposed to unify the texts.** "God is one being but three distinct persons" is "a post-biblical philosophical innovation that was imposed upon the Bible to try to synthesize and unify all the different texts, the majority of which distinguish Jesus quite clearly from God."[^2] See [[Trinity Was Constructed Not Revealed]] and [[The Bible Is a Library Not Univocal]].
- **Univocality is what required inventing the Trinity.** Once the whole NT is "treated as a single inspired corpus that is univocal and inerrant, then we have to come up with one explanation that works — and that is why the Trinity had to be invented."[^3] See [[Pan-Canonical Univocality Is Invalid Interpretation]] and [[Canonization Forced the Harmonization That Produced the Trinity]].
- **The conceptual substructure is entirely 2nd–5th century.** No text "provides the conceptual substructure and explanation for how Jesus is God that remotely approximates" the framework of three persons "co-equally, co-eternally and consubstantial comprising the one being that is God," which "was developed between the second half of the 2nd century and the fifth century."[^4] See [[Trinity - Historical Development]] and [[The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible|The Trinity and Consubstantiality Are Not in the Bible]].
- **John 1:1c is qualitative, not "the Word was God."** Anarthrous pre-verbal *theos* for a known individual "should be understood as qualitative… describing qualities, so 'divine' would be a more accurate translation."[^5] The upset comes from wanting "the God of Israel to exhaust the category of deity," itself "a post-biblical concept" anachronistic to 1st-century Judaism.[^6] See [[John 1-1c Anarthrous Theos and Subordinate Divinity]] and [[Ancient Judaism Affirmed a Divine Spectrum]].
- **Colossians 1:15 is image christology.** Pseudonymous Colossians calls Jesus the "image of the invisible God" — a *divine image* (cf. an idol/cult statue) that makes the deity's power and presence accessible and can be called both the deity and not the deity; the same author calls Jesus "firstborn of all creation," i.e. created.[^7] See [[Divine Images Explain Jesus Relationship to God]] and [[Partible Personhood and the Logic of Divine Images]].
- **Creation is "through"/"in" Jesus, not by Jesus as primary agent.** Colossians' *en autō / di' autou* make Jesus "an instrument or a secondary agent, but not the primary agent of creation."[^8]
- **Prayer through the Son is not proof of the Trinity.** Pseudonymous 1 Timothy 2:5 has "one God and one mediator… the human Jesus Christ"; praying "to the Father through the Son by the power of the Holy Spirit" simply names three entities and "does not mean the Trinity."[^9] See [[Divine Mediation Predates the New Testament]].
- **Genesis 1:26 "let us" is the Divine Council.** "That's God speaking to the other gods of the Divine Council, as the majority of scholars agree"; Genesis 3:5/3:22, the start of ch. 6, and 11:7 all reference *plural gods*, and "if you've got multiple gods we're not talking about the Trinity."[^10] See [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We]].
- **Genesis 1:1 is a temporal clause.** *Bərēʾšît* is in the construct: "when God began to create the heavens and the earth," the consensus of recent commentaries, NRSV, Alter, and the JPS Tanakh — not a standalone absolute beginning.[^11]
- **Elohim is grammatically plural, semantically determined by its verbs.** The creator's "plural word occasionally used in a singular case" is muddled; *Elohim* is "a grammatically plural word that can be used in either a semantically plural or singular sense," decided by the verbs/pronouns — and the singular *bara* yields a singular sense, not a "plurality-with-singularity" smuggling in persons.[^12] McClellan calls the persons-in-Elohim move "laughable eisegesis."[^13] See [[Elohim Never Means Human Judges]].
- **The "I am" sayings invoke the divine *name*, not the tetragrammaton.** Exodus 3:14's *ʾehyeh* ("I am") "is not the tetragrammaton… spelled aleph-yod, not yod-vav"; the LXX *egō eimi* renders *ʾehyeh* and *ʾanî hûʾ* ("I am he," Deuteronomy/Deutero-Isaiah). Jesus' *egō eimi* (John 8:24; 8:58; Mark 14:62) is "an indirect, coded reference to a version of the divine name," i.e. "guess who's got the name of God," not a claim to be Yahweh.[^14] See [[John 8.58|John 8:58]], [[The Divine Name Yahweh Was Given to Jesus]], and [[Divine Agency and the Divine-Name Bearer]].
- **The name is a communicable vehicle of divine agency.** [[Exodus 23.20-21|Exodus 23:20-21]]: the messenger can withhold forgiveness "because my name is in him." Yahoel (Apocalypse of Abraham) and the later Metatron ("the little Adonai") bear the name; the Son of Man and Jesus (John, Philippians) are repeatedly associated with possessing it — all "in existence within Judaism… for centuries prior to the birth of Jesus."[^15] See [[Name-Bearing Agency in Exodus 23]], [[The Angel of Yahweh Bears the Divine Name]], and [[Metatron and the Divine Name Are the Taproot of Christology]].
- **John 10:30 + John 17 reductio.** "I and the Father are one" is true as *one being? no — one connection*; in the John 17 intercessory prayer Jesus prays his followers be one "just as" he and the Father are one, so a same-substance reading folds believers into the Trinity's very being.[^16] See [[John 10-30 - I and the Father Are One Rebutted]] and [[John 17 Oneness Is Indwelling Not Consubstantiality]].
- **Philippians 2 is a strike against, not for, deity.** Scholars are in "widespread agreement" that *morphē theou* "does not mean Jesus shared one essence with God" but had "the outward appearance or filled the role of a deity"; reading it as shared essence is "interpreted wrongly by Trinitarians."[^17] See [[Philippians 2 Kenosis - The Son Emptied Himself of Divinity]].
- **Matthew 28:19's singular "name."** The single *onoma* shared by Father, Son, and Spirit is "the communicable vehicle of divine power… given to Jesus" (Philippians 2; John 17:12) — which is exactly why opponents charge *blasphemy* when Jesus winks at possessing it, "not a claim to be God."[^18] See [[Blasphemy Did Not Require Claiming to Be Yahweh]].
- **Jude 5 does not call Jesus "the God of Jacob."** Jude says Jesus "saved a people out of the land of Egypt"; Exodus 23 has Yahweh send the *messenger* ("my name is in him"), and "in this time period Jesus was identified with the angel of Adonai," which "is not the very God of Israel."[^19] See [[The Messenger of Yahweh Explicitly Identifies as God]].
- **Subordination is explicit and was a hurdle, not a footnote.** "Neither I nor the angels but only the Father knows" means "the Son is subordinate to the Father and lacks knowledge, which does not comport with the Trinity"; like the passages where Jesus "says he's less than the Father," these "have to be reinterpreted" under the imposed lens.[^20] See [[Subordinationism Was the Early Church View]] and [[Kenotic Christology - Jesus Emptied Himself and Did Not Know the Hour]].
- **Modalism and subordinationism were live early options, rejected by philosophers + empire.** Both "would be rejected as heresies later on… not because the Bible says so but because philosophers decided that this didn't fit within their particular soteriological models," enforced "once they had the weight of empire behind them."[^21] See [[Modalism Monarchianism and Sabellianism]] and [[Trinity Survived Only Through Imperial Coercion]].
- **The Shema is monoyahwism, not Trinity.** *YHWH ʾĕlōhênû* "just means 'Yahweh our God'… an appositional phrase," and *YHWH ʾeḥād* "is just monoyahwism"; "absolutely none of [the takes on the Shema] have anything to do with the concept of the Trinity."[^22] See [[Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge]].
- **"Deny the Trinity = not a Christian" is boundary maintenance.** The gatekeeping is "just petty and naive boundary maintenance"; "there is no person… on Earth that has authority over who gets to be a Christian."[^23] See [[Boundary Maintenance Drove Doctrinal Systematization]] and [[Trinity Is a First-Rank Salvation Issue]].

## LDS Relevance

McClellan's data — diverse, subordinationist texts; Jesus as the authorized bearer of the divine name rather than Yahweh himself; "one" as unity not substance; the creedal synthesis as a 2nd–5th-century imposition enforced by empire — line up squarely with the Latter-day Saint distinction between the [[The Godhead Model vs the Trinity Model|Godhead and the Trinity]]. His "petty and naive boundary maintenance" verdict directly answers the standard charge that denying the Trinity puts Latter-day Saints outside Christianity (cf. [[Are Latter-day Saints Christians]]). He is not making the LDS case, but the historical-critical floor he lays is the same one [[Blake Ostler]] builds the Godhead argument on.
