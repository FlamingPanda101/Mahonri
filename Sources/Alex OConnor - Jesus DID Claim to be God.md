# Alex O'Connor — Jesus DID Claim to be God: The Evidence

A long-form [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] ("Within Reason") interview with Catholic New Testament scholar [[Brant Pitre]] on Pitre's book *Jesus and Divine Christology*. O'Connor argues, as a charitable agnostic foil, the mainstream-critical position that the **historical Jesus never explicitly claimed to be God** (a separate question from whether he *was* God); Pitre contends that Jesus *did* claim divinity — but "in a Jewish way," through riddles and scriptural allusion — and that this is the historical fire from which the early church's high Christology rose.

## Summary
- **The paradox the book solves.** Historical-Jesus research has assumed since the 18th century that the historical Jesus made no divine claims, yet the earliest (all-Jewish) Christians treated Jesus as divine almost immediately ([[1 Corinthians 8.4-6|1 Cor 8:6]] folds Jesus into the *Shema*). Pitre's thesis: that "smoke" of early divine Christology comes from the "fire" of Jesus's own divine messianism, made obliquely via riddles, parables, and allusions to the *Shema*, the Decalogue, and key prophetic texts.[^1] See [[Jesus Claimed Divinity in a Jewish Way Through Riddle and Allusion]].
- **Forgiving sins (Mark 2).** O'Connor reads "your sins are forgiven" as a delegated/priestly prerogative later given to the disciples ([[John 20.28|John 20]]); Pitre argues the divine passive in [[Leviticus 4]] and the monotheism of [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43]] make forgiving sins an exclusively *divine* prerogative, so the scribes ("who can forgive sins but God alone?") are not misreading him.[^2] See [[Forgiving Sins Was a Divine Prerogative in Second Temple Judaism]].
- **Walking on the sea (Mark 6).** Pitre's strongest case: Jesus takes the divine name (*ego eimi*, "I am") plus the theophanic "do not be afraid," alluding to [[Exodus 3.14|Exodus 3]] *and* [[Isaiah 43.10|Isaiah 43]], while doing what [[Job 9]] says only God does — tread the waves. He shows the move is granted even by non-Christian scholars (the Jesus Seminar).[^3] See [[The Walking on the Sea Is a Theophany Claiming the Divine Name]].
- **The *ego eimi* qualification.** Both agree *ego eimi* often just means "it's me" (the blind man in John 9; Jesus's own warning in Mark 13). O'Connor presses the Septuagint counter — Exodus 3 LXX reads *ego eimi ho on*, so a Greek author wanting the divine name would write *ho on*, not bare *ego eimi*. Pitre answers that Isaiah 43 LXX renders the same Hebrew with bare *ego*, so the allusion still lands; meaning is fixed by context.[^4] (Cross-ref [[John 8-58 - Before Abraham Was I Am (Divine Name Agency)]].)
- **O'Connor's unifying counter-reading.** Jesus is a disciple of John the Baptist who *delegates* his powers (forgiving sins, walking on water, judging) to his disciples ([[John 17.20-23|John 17]]), so each "divine" act is something believers will one day share — a theosis-shaped low Christology. Pitre grants the theosis point but says some acts (taking the divine name; demanding super-parental love) are *not* delegated.[^5] See [[Disciples Share the Throne and Divine Glory]].
- **Worship (*proskyneo*) is deliberately *not* his strongest argument.** Both agree *proskyneo* ranges from bowing before a superior to divine worship (cf. the Septuagint, Alexander bowing to the high priest bearing the divine name), and that cultic *latreuo* is never given to Jesus in the Gospels (except an elusive Daniel-7 echo). Pitre concedes this argument "can't bear the weight."[^6]
- **Super-parental love (Matthew 10).** Pitre's subtle case: Philo and Josephus rank honoring parents (the Decalogue's *kabod*) as second only to God; so Jesus's demand to be loved *more than father and mother* ([[Matthew 10.34-36|Matthew 10:37]]) is, per Rabbi Jacob Neusner, "only [what] God can ask of me."[^7] See [[Jesus's Demand for Super-Parental Love Is an Implicit Divine Claim]].
- **The blasphemy charge (Mark 14).** The only cause of death all four Gospels agree on is blasphemy. Claiming to be Messiah is not a capital crime; claiming to be the heavenly [[Daniel 7.13-14|Daniel 7]] Son of Man + the enthroned Lord of [[Psalm 110.1|Psalm 110]] is. Pitre: the divine Jesus is "the most crucifiable Jesus." O'Connor counters with the "two powers" timing problem and Stephen's stoning ([[Acts 7.55|Acts 7]]).[^8] See [[The Blasphemy Charge Makes the Divine Jesus the Most Crucifiable Jesus]] and the existing [[The Sanhedrin Blasphemy Charge Presupposes the Enochic Son of Man]].
- **Three questions kept distinct.** Throughout, Pitre separates (1) whether Jesus *was* God, (2) whether the historical Jesus *claimed* it, (3) whether the Gospels/evangelists *portray* it — locating his book on (2), with (3) as confirmatory (e.g. Mark's theophanic "passing by" in the sea narrative).[^9]
