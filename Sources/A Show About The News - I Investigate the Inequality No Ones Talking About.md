# A Show About The News — I Investigate the Inequality No One's Talking About

A secular, comedic explainer (host "Trace," interspersed with an "Abraham Lincoln" sketch) on **location inequality** — why a handful of cities (New York for finance, Silicon Valley for tech) capture an outsized share of economic growth, and why governments that spend billions trying to copy them mostly fail. The video has no religious or apologetic content; it is catalogued here only for completeness and for its single portable idea about how behaviors cluster by place.

## Summary

- **Clustering creates self-reinforcing hubs.** Talent and customers attract more firms and talent, a virtuous cycle (New York/finance, Silicon Valley/tech, Boston/biotech). But clustering alone doesn't explain why a *super*cluster ignites in one spot and not another with equal assets (Boston, Dublin). [^1]
- **"Small area variation": behavior clusters by location, often subconsciously.** A cardiologist who moves from Boulder to Buffalo quickly adopts local treatment rates; Vietnam-veteran heroin addiction largely evaporated on return home (~5% relapse). Specific behaviors concentrate in specific places, and newcomers absorb them. (Drawn from Gladwell's *Revenge of the Tipping Point* and the medical small-area-variation literature.) [^2]
- **Behavior clusters are nearly impossible to build inorganically.** Dublin's tax incentives drew Apple/Meta/Amazon yet it doesn't crack Europe's top 10 for startups; East Palo Alto stays poor despite sitting *inside* Silicon Valley, a legacy of redlining that persists as a behavioral pattern even after the policies ended. [^3]
- **The lesson for policymakers: double down on existing advantages.** Switzerland's 1500s Calvinist jewelry ban funneled craftsmanship into watches, and centuries of focus (the Swatch Group rescue) built a ~$32B/yr industry — cultivating a local strength rather than chasing the economic fad of the day. [^4]
