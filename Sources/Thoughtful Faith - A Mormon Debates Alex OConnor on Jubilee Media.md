# A Mormon Debates Alex O'Connor on Jubilee Media

[[Jacob Hansen]] (Thoughtful Faith) breaks down his and fellow Latter-day Saint [[Hayden Carroll]]'s appearance on Jubilee Media's "Surrounded" format, where ~25 Christians took turns challenging the atheist philosopher [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]]. Hansen frames the episode as a "bloodbath" in which O'Connor "dismantled" the creedal (Protestant and Catholic) Christians, then argues that the two Latter-day Saints fared better because the restored gospel's *constrained, finitist* conception of God answers the problem of evil that creedal Christianity cannot.[^1] After being voted out, Hansen was invited back by O'Connor for a one-on-one and made the claim that "the restored gospel... provides a conception of God that is more rationally defensible than any other credal Christian sect."[^2]

## Participants
- [[Hayden Carroll]] (Latter-day Saint, suffering/consciousness exchange) · [[Jacob Hansen]] (Latter-day Saint, main debater) · [[Alex OConnor|Alex O'Connor]] (atheist)

## Summary
- **Hayden's gambit — suffering presupposes consciousness.** Hayden grants that, given a material world, atheism better accounts for suffering, then reverses: suffering is "a manifestation of consciousness," so if atheism cannot account for consciousness it cannot account for suffering. O'Connor concedes consciousness "may seem to imply the existence of a conscious creator" but accuses Hayden of "shifting the goalposts" and pivots to the *evil-god hypothesis* and gratuitous animal suffering.[^3] See [[Suffering Presupposes Consciousness Which Atheism Cannot Explain]] and [[The Evil God Hypothesis Neutralizes the Argument from Suffering]].
- **Jacob's cross-exam — "Jesus never claimed to be God."** Jacob walks O'Connor from "only the Gospel of John unambiguously portrays Jesus claiming divinity" to conceding the synoptic authors "were trying to portray Jesus as God," using the *ego eimi* of John 9, the blasphemy charge, judging the world, worship, and the virgin birth. O'Connor grants the gospels are the best source for the historical Jesus yet says he simply does not trust them (cf. legends of Alexander the Great).[^4] See [[Walking OConnor From One Gospel to All Four on Jesuss Divinity]].
- **Jacob's main claim — a constrained God is more rationally defensible.** O'Connor chose Jacob for a one-on-one. Jacob argues the real problem is *injustice*, and that the LDS finitist God — who creates within co-eternal law rather than *ex nihilo* — could not bring humans (and animals) to their divine potential without a fallen world of genuine injustice freely passed through. He grounds this in premortal consent and "opposition in all things" ([[2 Nephi 2.8|2 Nephi 2]]).[^5] See [[A Constrained God Solves the Problem of Evil Better Than Creedal Christianity]].
- **The compensation dispute and animal suffering.** O'Connor presses that compensating suffering does not justify causing it (the Burlington Club analogy: paying off a trashed restaurant), and that animals never consented. Jacob counters with the "create cancer to have chemotherapy" reframing — suffering is justified only when *necessary* to a greater good that could not be had otherwise — and argues a fall-without-atonement world (O'Connor's implied picture) gives no reason for life to continue.[^6] See [[Compensating Suffering Is Not the Same as Justifying It]].
- **Aftermath.** Hansen reports O'Connor afterward praised Latter-day Saint theology as "a fascinating religion," singled out Mormonism when asked which denomination is most internally consistent, and quipped that because the restoration is so recent, "we have better eyewitness testimony to Moroni and the plates than we do... to the resurrection of Jesus" — even as O'Connor "remains a firm atheist."[^7]
