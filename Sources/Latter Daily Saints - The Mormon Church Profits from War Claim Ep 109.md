# Latter Daily Saints — "'The Mormon Church Profits from War' Tucker Carlson Guest Claims | Ep. 109"

The hosts respond to a ~15-minute segment of Tucker Carlson's interview with documentary filmmaker Nathan Apffel, in which Apffel charges that the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints "profits from war" because its investment arm, Ensign Peak Advisors, holds stock in defense contractors such as Lockheed Martin. The panel concedes the factual holdings while dismantling the inference of malice, supplying context on the size and nature of broad-market investing, the 2023 SEC settlement, the Church's screening of "sin" stocks, and the supply-chain problem that makes "profiting from war" an unbounded charge.

## Summary
- **The claim and its source.** Apffel, a filmmaker who "audits" mega-churches and operates from a worldview that "any organized institution of religion... [is] corrupt," tells Carlson the Church sits on "like 350 billion in net assets," is the second-largest private landowner in the US, and is "heavily invested in machinery of war" — concluding "the LDS church profits from war."[^1][^2] The hosts identify this as conflation: applying the lens used on Kenneth Copeland-style prosperity preachers to a worldwide church with a stated mission to build the kingdom of God.[^3][^15]
- **Index-fund holdings are not endorsement.** Ensign Peak holds ~$25 million in Lockheed Martin out of a roughly $3.5 billion portfolio — "very little," and a defense contractor reportedly ranks around the 92nd-largest holding.[^7][^9] The hosts argue this is ordinary broad-market exposure (S&P 500-tracking), not an attempt to "lobby" for war; if the Church were steering members toward its own investments, it would be promoting Microsoft, Apple, and Nvidia, which "dwarf our investment in Pfizer."[^11]
- **The supply-chain / "where do you draw the line" problem.** Jonah Barnes, who worked for a defense contractor, grants the cynical reality that the industry knows "war is profitable," but argues culpability cannot be traced cleanly: parts flow through steel fabricators and sub-vendors who "don't know" the end use — "at what point do you say all of you are benefiting from war?"[^4][^5] A *Good Place* analogy (an apple whose purchase drops your moral score because of child labor and a warlord's oil) illustrates that in a complex economy "nothing is as black and white as you think."[^12]
- **The SEC settlement, contextualized.** The hosts recount the ~$5 million SEC settlement (Church $1M, Ensign Peak $4M) over filing Form 13F through subsidiary entities rather than one consolidated filing — done "at the advice of their legal counsel," a fine "not any greater or less than" typical for a fund that size, after which Ensign Peak now files a consolidated 13F (latest cited February 2026).[^6] See the existing [[The SEC Settlement Does Not Prove the Church Hid Its Wealth]].
- **Intentional ethical screening.** A source present at Ensign Peak's founding under Gordon B. Hinckley described screening out "sin" stocks — alcohol, tobacco, gambling, pornography (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Netflix, Starbucks, even Bath & Body Works are excluded per widowsmite.org). The hosts note the irony: instead of credit for *not* profiting from tobacco, the Church is attacked for incidental defense exposure.[^8][^10]
- **Tithing and the "forced to pay" myth.** The hosts rebut Carlson's assumption that members must pay tithing to belong: it is a minority who pay, no bank statements are checked, the temple-recommend question asks whether you are "a full tithe payer" (which can be true at zero dollars), and non-members benefit from humanitarian disbursement (1.4 billion in 2025 aid cited).[^13][^14]
- **The rhetorical move named.** The panel frames Apffel's argument as attributing *malice* — that the Church has "an incentive to promulgate more wars" — where the evidence is at most a small, ethically-screened holding; Carlson's "they're probably lobbying to declare war" is called out as having "zero evidence... actually contrary evidence."[^15][^16] See [[Profiting From War Charge Confuses Passive Holdings With Endorsement]] and [[Universal Complicity Proves Too Much - The Supply Chain Objection]].
