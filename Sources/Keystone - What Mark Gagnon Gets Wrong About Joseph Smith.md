# Keystone — "What Mark Gagnon Gets Wrong About Joseph Smith"

A [[Keystone]] review of comedian and podcaster [[Mark Gagnon]]'s video (on his channel *Religion Camp*) about Joseph Smith and early Mormonism — "three things he got right and five things he got wrong." Keystone grades Gagnon "90 to 95% accuracy" and praises his respectful, non-hostile approach, while correcting several specifics.[^1]

## Summary
**Got right:**
- **Joseph Smith and polygamy** — Gagnon (joking) grants that if Joseph were merely "after sexual gratification, there would have been other easier ways," since historical plural marriage "came with responsibility... it was commitment," echoing [[Britney Chapman Nash]].[^2] See [[Men Often Dragged Their Feet on Polygamy]].
- **"Mormon" as an organic nickname** after the Book of Mormon; using the full Church name is "a big deal among Latter-day Saints" but Gagnon "nailed" the respectful framing.[^3]
- **The mission as character-builder** (his closing tribute to Latter-day Saint missionaries).[^4]

**Got wrong:**
- **Treasure-digging scope.** Gagnon correctly calls it "folk Christianity," not nefarious, but overstates that the Smiths abandoned farming for treasure-hunting "as their primary source of income." [[Lucy Mack Smith]]'s memoir explicitly denies it: "we never... suffered one important interest to swallow up every other obligation," and they did not stop work "to win the faculty of Abrac, drawing magic circles, or soothsaying."[^5] See [[Joseph Smith Treasure Hunting Extent]].
- **The "necromancy" charge.** Gagnon says Joseph was "arrested... and charged with practicing necromancy" (tied to [[Alvin Smith]]). Keystone: enemies called Joseph many things, but he was never charged with necromancy — "I don't even think you could specifically be charged with necromancy at that time"; Gagnon is conflating it with the 1826 "disorderly person" matter.[^6] See [[Joseph Smith Was Never Charged With Necromancy]].
- **Hill Cumorah / geography.** Gagnon treats the New York hill as the Book of Mormon Cumorah and the site of a 400 AD battle. Keystone: early Saints did not call the NY hill "Cumorah" until the mid-1830s (a likely misreading of [[Mormon 6.6|Mormon 6:6]]); "Book of Mormon geography doesn't match up with the New York location," and Mormon 6 indicates the plates were *not* buried where the final battle occurred.[^7] See [[The New York Hill Is Not the Book of Mormon Cumorah]].
- **Translation timeline.** Gagnon portrays a multi-year translation. In fact, after the 116 pages were lost, "virtually everything in the published Book of Mormon... was translated between April and the end of June 1829" — [[John W. Welch]] found it took roughly 60 full-time working days, dictated "looking at a rock and a hat" before witnesses without significant revision.[^8] See [[Dictation Speed and Method Argue Against Fabrication]].
- **Why Moroni took the plates back.** Gagnon says "no one really knows why." The Book of Mormon itself answers in [[2 Nephi 27]]: to "seal up the book again, and hide it up unto me," preserving the sealed portion "not yet read."[^9] See [[Moroni Took the Plates to Preserve the Sealed Portion]].
- **The witnesses underweighted.** Given the video's title, Gagnon should have spent more time on the 11 official witnesses who claimed to see and handle the plates — testimonies that "significantly complicate any naturalistic explanation."[^10]
