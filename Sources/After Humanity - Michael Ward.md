# After Humanity

Michael Ward's 2021 scholarly **guide and commentary on C.S. Lewis's *The Abolition of Man*** (1943), Lewis's compact philosophical defense of the objectivity of value. Ward supplies the reception, occasion, context, background, and legacy of Lewis's lectures, then a chapter-by-chapter gloss on the text itself. The title — *After Humanity* — foregrounds Lewis's warning that a civilization which debunks objective value and seeks to "conquer Nature" (including human nature) ends by abolishing humanity itself.

## Summary
- **Lewis's thesis (the *Tao*):** objective value is real and broadly self-evident — a "doctrine of objective value" attested across the great moral traditions, which Lewis calls the *Tao*. To deny it is not to advance beyond morality but to fall outside it [^1].
- **"Men Without Chests":** an education that teaches pupils all value-judgments are merely subjective produces people without the trained sentiments ("the chest") that mediate between reason and appetite — and then ironically complains that they lack virtue [^2].
- **"The Abolition of Man":** the drive to subject all of Nature to human will collapses into some men conditioning all others; with the *Tao* rejected, only arbitrary power and impulse remain. Ward shows Lewis's prescience regarding eugenics, conditioning, and (today) transhumanism [^3].
- **Apologetic use (TF Level-1 ethics):** the **definitive defense of objective morality / natural law**, and the direct rebuttal to naturalist/relativist ethics such as [[The Moral Landscape - Sam Harris]]. A strong ally for the Latter-day Saint affirmation of eternal, objective moral law (cf. natural-law reasoning), even though Lewis argues on purely philosophical, non-confessional grounds.
