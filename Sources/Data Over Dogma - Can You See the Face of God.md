# Data Over Dogma - Can You See the Face of God

In this "It's the Law" segment of the *Data Over Dogma* podcast, [[Dan McClellan]] argues to co-host [[Dan Beecher]] that the thrice-yearly pilgrimage commandment ([[Deuteronomy 16.16|Deuteronomy 16:16]], [[Exodus 34.24|Exodus 34:24]], [[Isaiah 1.12|Isaiah 1:12]]) was originally a command to **see the face of God**, not to "appear before" him. He shows that the underlying Hebrew consonantal text reads the verb *ra'ah* ("to see") in the **active** voice, but the [[Masoretes|Masoretes]] added vowel points that force a **passive** reading ("be seen / appear"), and that every standard English translation follows the passive Masoretic vocalization. McClellan ties this to a broader thesis that early Israelite religion understood God as **corporeal and visible**, that the temple housed a **standing stone (massebah)** that mediated the divine presence, and that the later anti-image (aniconic) polemic was a secondary development.

- **The pilgrimage commandment was knowingly mistranslated.** The Hebrew of Deuteronomy 16:16 literally says "three times in a year will see all your males the face of Adonai your God"; the consonants want the active "you will see the face of God," but the Masoretic vowels twist it into the passive "appear before God."[^1] See [[Pilgrimage Commandment Was to See Gods Face]].
- **Isaiah 1:12 is the decisive case.** It uses an infinitive construct, a form whose active and passive **do not share the same consonants**; the consonants *lir'ot panai* can only mean "to see my face," yet the Masoretes vocalized it as the passive *lehera'ot*. The NRSVUE relegates "or see my face" to a footnote.[^2] See [[Infinitive Construct in Isaiah Proves Active Reading]].
- **Early Israelite religion held God to be corporeal and locally present.** Seeing the face of God was a ritual **beatific vision** in the temple, where the worshipper gazed on a divine image — most plausibly a standing stone like the one excavated in the Judahite temple at [[Arad Temple Standing Stone|Arad]].[^3] See [[Standing Stone Massebah as Material Medium of Divine Presence]], [[Beatific Vision in the Israelite Temple]].
- **The anti-idol polemic is a later development.** Divine images existed in earlier Israel and Judah; the prohibition ("you heard a voice but saw no image") emerges later, especially in [[Deuteronomy 16.16|Deuteronomy]]. [[Trygve Mettinger]] argues for a *de facto aniconism* — non-representational imagery driven by market and resource limits, not institutional opposition.[^4] See [[Israelite Aniconism Was Later Development]], [[De Facto Aniconism Mettinger]].
- **The "cannot see God and live" tradition coexists with traditions that he was seen.** [[Exodus 33.20|Exodus 33:20]] insists no one can see God's face and live, yet [[Exodus 24.10|Exodus 24:10]], [[Numbers 12.8|Numbers 12:8]], and [[Genesis 32.30|Genesis 32:30]] all describe people seeing God — evidence of competing, conflicting conceptions in the text.[^5] See [[Conflicting Traditions on Seeing Gods Face]].
- **The commandment fused vision with obligatory offerings.** The same verse forbids appearing "empty-handed," so the beatific-vision pilgrimage doubled as a recurring tithe/offering demand and a form of costly signaling tying males to the temple.[^6] See [[Seeing Gods Face as Costly Signaling and Offering]].
- **Smoke and possibly cannabis incense shaped the experience.** Two incense altars beside the Arad standing stone tested positive for three kinds of cannabis burned ~2,700 years ago; the smoke both obscured the image and likely heightened the mystical character of the encounter.[^7] See [[Cannabis Incense and the Mystical Temple Experience]].
