# Data Over Dogma — Deuteronomy's Secret Source

A *What's That?* episode in which [[Dan McClellan]] and [[Dan Beecher]] argue that the book of [[Deuteronomy]] was patterned after the **Vassal Treaty of [[Esarhaddon]]** (early 7th-century BCE Assyria), and that the biblical idea of "covenant" is itself a borrowing of the Assyrian institution of vassalage. The "secret source" of the title is this Assyrian treaty template, not the "Book of the Law" found in the temple — though the episode ties the parallel to the standard critical dating of Deuteronomy to [[Josiah]]'s reign (late 600s BCE), which makes the dependence a problem for a Mosaic authorship.

## Summary
- **Esarhaddon and the vassal treaty.** Esarhaddon (reigned c. 681 BCE, ~12 years) did not invent but "mastered" the Assyrian vassal treaty — a protection-racket arrangement in which a dominant power demands tribute and loyalty from a subjugated nation in exchange for not destroying it. McClellan frames vassalage as geopolitical-scale extortion: "you pay us… and we will protect you from us."[^1][^2]
- **The treaty's form.** Surviving versions open with a preamble titling Esarhaddon "King of the World, King of Assyria, son of [[Sennacherib]]," then invoke dozens of divine witnesses (Ashur, Anu, Sin, Shamash, Adad, and "all the gods of Nineveh… Calah… Arbela"), then stipulate obligations.[^3][^4]
- **"You shall love" the suzerain.** Section 24 commands the vassal to "love" the crown prince [[Ashurbanipal]] "like yourselves" — the same covenant-love idiom as Deuteronomy 6:4's Shema ("you shall love the LORD your God") and the "as yourself" of the second great commandment.[^5][^6]
- **Stipulation parallels.** "You shall not seek any other king or any other lord" / "do not place any other king or any other lord over you" (sections 17, 25) mirror "you shall have no other gods before me" ([[Deuteronomy 5.7|Deut 5:7]], Exodus 20).[^7][^8] The treaty's appeal to "all the gods of heaven and earth" as witnesses parallels Deuteronomy calling "heaven and earth to witness" ([[Deuteronomy 30.19|Deut 30:19]]).[^9]
- **The curses, including cannibalism.** Deuteronomy 28's siege curse — that you will "eat the fruit of your womb, the flesh of your own sons and daughters" ([[Deuteronomy 28.53|Deut 28:53]]) — and the "destroy your name and your seed" curse (Deut 29) match the treaty's curses (section 47: "in your hunger eat the flesh of your sons… may your ghost have nobody to take care of… libations"), down to the deprivation of an afterlife.[^10][^11]
- **Dating problem.** Deuteronomy purports to be dictated by [[Moses]] (its last chapter narrates his death) c. 1200–1400 BCE, but "was probably brought together under King Josiah toward the end of the 600s BCE." Its dependence on a treaty template from 500–700 years later "is such a problem for folks who think it is historical."[^12]
- **Covenant = vassalage.** McClellan's payoff: "the idea of covenant comes from this" — when the Bible speaks of covenant, it is borrowing Assyrian vassalage as "the metaphor for your relationship with God," with God cast in Esarhaddon's role.[^13]
- **The "stick" outweighs the "carrot."** Like the treaty, Deuteronomy is mostly threat: the curses dominate, the blessings are a "token carrot." The hosts connect this to modern eternal-conscious-torment apologetics ([[Kirk Cameron]] is cited), and note Assyria's notorious brutality (the Lachish reliefs; the book of [[Jonah 3.4|Jonah]] trades on Assyrian cruelty).[^14][^15]
