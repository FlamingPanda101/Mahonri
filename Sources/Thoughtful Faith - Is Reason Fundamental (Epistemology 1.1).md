# Thoughtful Faith — Is Reason Fundamental? (Epistemology 1.1)

This is the capstone video of the **[[Thoughtful Faith - Apologetics Course Overview|Thoughtful Faith]]** Level 1, sub-section 1.1 (Epistemology) sequence. The unnamed host narrates a single sustained argument — that **the root of human knowing is not rationality but direct, intuitive experience with reality, which (he argues) is fundamentally spiritual** — and illustrates each step by splicing in teaching clips from academic philosophers and psychologists. It is framed as his "most tentative" video, ideas he is "still trying to work out." [^1]

The host's method is a *regress descent*: he starts from his five "witnesses" (authority, outcomes, reason, intuition, sense data) and argues each rests on a more fundamental one, descending authority → outcomes → reason → intuition → experience, until he reaches a "self-justifying foundation." [^2]

- **The regress problem motivates the search for a foundation.** [[Kevin McCain]] explains the ancient epistemic regress problem (Aristotle, Sextus Empiricus): any justified belief invites "why?", and the reasons themselves invite "why?", like a child's endless questioning. The dominant answer is *foundationalism* — the chain of reasons terminates in basic beliefs that are themselves justified (often by **experience**) but need no further reason. [^3][^4] See [[The Epistemic Regress Problem]], [[Foundationalism Ends the Regress in Basic Beliefs]].
- **Reason itself rests on intuition.** [[Jennifer Nagel]] (following [[John Locke]]) describes intuition as immediate, non-inferential, a-modal judgment — we just *see* that no round thing is square, or that 5×11=55 — and notes that every step of explicit demonstration is itself intuitive. The host concludes reason is "emergent from more basic intuitions." [^5][^6] See [[Reason Is Emergent From Intuition]].
- **Sense data is not foundational either — it must be intuited by a valuing self.** Using [[Jordan Peterson]]'s account of the brain filtering an over-complex reality, and the *Monkey Business / invisible gorilla* selective-attention experiment, the host argues raw sense data never arrives in a vacuum: it is filtered through a self/consciousness oriented by a "hierarchy of values." [^7][^8] See [[Sense Data Is Filtered Through a Valuing Self]].
- **Reason is the rider; intuition (the "elephant") is in control.** [[Jonathan Haidt]]'s rider-and-elephant metaphor: conscious reasoning is small and post-hoc, while ~99% of the mind (the intuitive elephant) drives belief and behavior; you persuade by speaking to the elephant. The host identifies the elephant with what scripture calls the **spirit / the Hebrew heart (levav)**. [^9][^10] See [[The Rider and the Elephant Model of the Mind]], [[Persuasion Must Address the Elephant First]], [[The Elephant Is the Spirit - The Hebrew Heart]].
- **The foundation is spiritual experience, and it can yield perfect certainty.** [[Blake Ostler]] (FAIR talk) argues the heart — *levav*, the unified seat of cognition and affect — is the basis of religious knowledge, and that one must not *argue* spiritual experience to be valid, since that would imply something more basic than it. The host adds the saber/conocer (know-about vs. know-by-acquaintance) distinction and argues that while rational certainty is impossible, one can be 100% certain *that an experience happened* even if one misinterprets it. [^11][^12][^13][^14] See [[The Heart Levav Is the Basis of Religious Knowledge]], [[Do Not Argue Spiritual Experience to Be Valid]], [[Saber vs Conocer - Knowledge by Acquaintance]], [[Perfect Certainty of Experience Not of Interpretation]].

The synthesizing thesis: intuition, sense data, rationality, outcomes, and authority "are not ultimately at odds but are meant to be integrated into a complete system that, when properly calibrated, allows us to see reality in its full depth." [^15] See [[Integrated Epistemology - Calibrating the Faculties]].
