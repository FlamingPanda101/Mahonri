# The Kalam Cosmological Argument — Part 1: Scientific
A short animated [[William Lane Craig|Craig]] explainer that lays out the Kalam cosmological argument — *(1) whatever begins to exist has a cause; (2) the universe began to exist; (3) therefore the universe has a cause* — and then marshals the **scientific** case for premise 2. It argues from the second law of thermodynamics, from the Big Bang discovery chain (Einstein's general relativity, the Friedmann–Lemaître expanding-universe prediction, Hubble's redshift measurements), and from the Borde–Guth–Vilenkin theorem that any on-average-expanding universe must have an absolute past beginning. It concludes that the cause must be spaceless, timeless, immaterial, uncaused, and unimaginably powerful — "much like God."

## Summary
- **The argument is stated as a deductive syllogism**, and premise 1 is defended from ordinary experience: believing something can pop into being uncaused is "more of a stretch than believing in magic," and if it could, we would see it happening "all the time."[^1]
- **The thermodynamic argument:** the second law shows the universe is "slowly running out of usable energy"; had it existed forever, it would already have run out — so it "points us to a universe that has a definite beginning."[^2]
- **The Big Bang discovery chain:** Einstein's 1915 general relativity let us speak meaningfully about cosmic history; Friedmann and Lemaître predicted expansion from his equations; Hubble's 1929 redshift measurements confirmed the universe is expanding and "sprang into being from a single point in the finite past."[^3]
- **The Borde–Guth–Vilenkin theorem:** Borde, Guth, and Vilenkin proved any universe on average expanding throughout its history "cannot be eternal in the past, but must have an absolute beginning" — even a multiverse — so "there is no escape" from a cosmic beginning.[^4]
- **The conclusion:** since both premises stand and the universe "can't cause itself," its cause "must be beyond the space-time universe" — spaceless, timeless, immaterial, uncaused, and "unimaginably powerful, much like... God."[^5]
