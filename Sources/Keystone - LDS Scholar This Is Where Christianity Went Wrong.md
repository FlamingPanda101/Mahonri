# Keystone — LDS Scholar: This Is Where Christianity Went Wrong

A ~44-minute interview in which Keystone hosts Latter-day Saint scholar **[[Terrell Givens|Terryl Givens]]** for a wide-ranging conversation on "probably the most fundamental core difference between Latter-day Saints and other Christians — the nature of God." Givens narrates his own "reconstituting of Christian history," locating the points at which he thinks mainstream Christianity "went off the rails" and arguing that the Restoration recovers a God "worthy in every way of our adoration."

## Summary
- **Givens's master thesis** is that the God of [[John the Evangelist|John]]'s Gospel and first epistle — a God whose defining attribute is the self-emptying love shown when Christ washed the apostles' feet ([[1 John 4.8|God *is* love]]) — was progressively displaced by the impassible, sovereign Absolute of Greek philosophy, and that [[Moses 7|Moses 7]]'s weeping God restores the original. See [[The Weeping God of Moses 7 Is Anthropocentric Not Theocentric]] and [[Greek Philosophy Corrupted the Christian Concept of God]].
- He identifies **two datable "wrong turns"**: the 6th-century anathematization of [[Origen]] (premortality, universalism) and the early-5th-century condemnation of [[Pelagius]], which subordinated free will to grace and contracted salvation "from universal to a limited few." See [[The Two Great Wrong Turns - Overthrow of Origen and Pelagius]].
- He argues the **privileging of sovereignty over love** is not accidental but emerged "simultaneously with the development of [[Creation Ex Nihilo Originated Late 2nd Century CE|creation ex nihilo]]" — a craftsman-God who fashions from pre-existing material was traded for a sovereign who commands "light be." See [[Sovereignty Was Privileged Over Love and Creation Ex Nihilo Drove It]].
- His **"most scathing critique"** is the doctrine that "God's goodness is not our goodness" / "his justice is not our justice" (he attributes the latter to [[Augustine]]) — which he says made crusades, inquisitions, and the damning of infants morally sayable. See [[Gods Goodness Is Our Goodness - Against the Moral-Universe Divide]].
- He **steelmans [[Marcion]]**: the second-century heretic was wrong about much but "right" that a God of pure love cannot be reconciled with a literally-read Old Testament — and that the appeal of a God who is "only love, not retribution" is "innately appealing." See [[Marcion Was Right That a God of Pure Love Cannot Be Reconciled With a Literal Old Testament]].
- On the **Trinity**, Givens affirms a [[Social Trinitarianism Better Fits the Distinct Persons of Scripture|social trinitarianism]] (citing [[Jurgen Moltmann]]) and answers the polytheism charge by accepting "a plenitude of gods, but we worship one." He flags the embodied risen Christ and Father/Son impassibility-asymmetry as unsolved creedal problems (see [[Embodied Risen Christ Strains the Trinity]]).
- On **convergence**, he claims mainstream Christianity has already moved toward LDS positions on universalism, divine embodiment, and divine passibility — but **never toward premortality**, which he finds inexplicably off-limits "even [to] the most progressive." See [[Premortality Is the One LDS Doctrine Mainstream Christianity Will Not Move Toward]] and the existing [[Convergence of Mainstream Christianity Toward LDS Doctrines]].
- On **Heavenly Mother**, he roots the "feminine divine" in ancient Israel ([[Shaddai Is the Breasted God and Points to the Mother|Shaddai]], Asherah, "Did God Have a Wife?"), reading it as an "irrepressible yearning" for a nurturing maternal aspect of God (the Catholic cult of Mary as one expression). See [[Feminine Divine in Ancient Near Eastern Worship]].
- On the **divine attributes**, he argues a [[A Self-Limiting God - Reinterpreting Omniscience Omnipotence and Omnipresence|self-limiting God]] need not know the future (if free will is real "the future doesn't exist yet"), limits his own power to allow agency, and is omnipresent only through the Spirit, not the body.
- He closes with an **eschatology of continued struggle**: the next life "is going to look a lot more like this one," a heaven where there is "no God who's going to serve as referee" and where, per Joseph Smith, "if you have no accusers, you will enter heaven" — every grievance must be reconciled. See [[Heaven Is an Arena of Continued Struggle and Mutual Reconciliation]]. He ends by endorsing [[Dieter F. Uchtdorf|Elder Uchtdorf]]'s point that "theology matters" because "what we believe about God shapes how we act."
- He references his own related Keystone interview with [[Casey Paul Griffiths]] on the [[King Follett Discourse]].
