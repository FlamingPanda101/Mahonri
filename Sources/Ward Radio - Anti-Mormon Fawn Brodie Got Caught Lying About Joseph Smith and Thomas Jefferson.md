## Summary
- Thesis: [[Fawn Brodie]]'s *No Man Knows My History* (1945) is "the CES Letter of its generation" — a **psychobiography** (her own term) that read Freudian motive into Joseph Smith and "presented conjecture as fact." Latter-day Saints flagged its errors for decades (Hugh Nibley's *No, Ma'am, That's Not History*), but the academy ignored it — until Brodie applied the *same* method to [[Thomas Jefferson]].
- The Jefferson case study: Brodie revived the old Adams-campaign slander that Jefferson fathered [[Sally Hemings]]'s children. A 1998 *Nature* headline ("Jefferson fathered slave's last child") seemed to confirm it — but within weeks the lead researcher [[Eugene Foster]] published that the DNA proved only that *a* Jefferson did, and better DNA + historical records point to brother [[Randolph Jefferson]]. The hosts note Monticello still asserts Thomas's paternity while citing Foster's contrary report.
- Historiography points: historian [[Gary Wills]] savaged Brodie's Jefferson book *before* the DNA episode ("the author's industry and her ignorance"). The hosts generalize to a "veneer of academia" critique of psychohistory and "woke history" ([[Howard Zinn]]), arguing controversial figures require **inference to the best explanation** and source-credibility judgment — and that hostile sources (then YouTube comments, now) make terrible affidavits.
