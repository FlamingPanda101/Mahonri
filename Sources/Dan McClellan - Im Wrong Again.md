# Dan McClellan — I'm Wrong AGAIN?!?

A sarcastic reply to a commenter who claimed McClellan was "wrong again" for saying no syllable of the Bible condemns slavery, citing [[Exodus 21.16|Exodus 21:16]] ("Whoever steals a man and sells him, and anyone found in possession of him shall be put to death"). McClellan argues the verse is a **mistranslated, in-group-only ban on man-stealing** that does nothing to question, condemn, or prohibit the institution of slavery — and that the anti-slavery reading is a 19th-century renegotiation imposed on the text from the outside. This is a companion piece to [[Dan McClellan - Biblical Slavery and the Reformation Red Pill Rebuttal]] and reuses its core findings while adding three new, specific points.

## Summary

- **The proof-text comes from a mistranslation (the ESV's "and").** Rendering the second clause as "**and** anyone found in possession of him" would absurdly make it a capital crime to *unknowingly buy* a kidnapped person; the correct disjunctive "**or**" shows both clauses describe the one kidnapper — liable whether he has already sold the victim or still holds him. The ESV does, however, correctly render "steals a **man**" (Hebrew *ish*).[^1] See [[The ESV Mistranslates Exodus 21.16 to Criminalize Unwitting Buyers]].
- **The Covenant Code's lexical precision limits the law to freeborn Israelite men.** Exodus 21 carefully distinguishes *ish* (Israelite man), *ish o isha* (man or woman), *eved/amah* (male/female slave), and *ben o bat* (son or daughter), because penalties track social status. The kidnapping law names only an *ish* — an adult male Israelite — confirmed by the explicit parallel at [[Deuteronomy 24.7|Deuteronomy 24:7]] ("from his brothers, from the sons of Israel").[^2] (Already catalogued — wikilinked, not duplicated.) See [[Covenant Code Lexical Precision on Social Status]], [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]].
- **Every complex slave society banned kidnapping its own free members.** Just as it was illegal to kidnap a free white adult male in the antebellum American South without that in any way undermining chattel slavery, a ban on kidnapping a free Israelite says nothing against the slave trade.[^3] See [[Kidnapping Prohibition Protects Israelites Not the Slave Trade]].
- **Even a real kidnapping ban couldn't touch slavery, because kidnapping wasn't a major slave source.** The actual supply channels named in the text — purchase from families, from other Israelites, from surrounding nations, and conquest ([[Leviticus 25.44-46|Leviticus 25:44-46]]) — were the load-bearing ones; banning a marginal channel leaves the institution intact.[^4] See [[Kidnapping Was Not a Primary Means of Sourcing Slaves]], [[Hebrew Bible Endorses Chattel Slavery of Foreigners]].
- **The surrounding law code heartily endorses slavery.** Exodus 21 opens with self-sale and a father selling his daughter into perpetual enslavement as a concubine/sex slave who, unlike a male debt slave, does not go free after six years — so [[Exodus 21.16|v.16]] is plainly not undermining a practice the same chapter approves.[^5] See [[Hebrew Bible Endorses Slavery from Beginning to End]].
- **Abolition was read into the Bible from the outside.** The conviction that slavery is wrong "was developed independent of the Bible" and retrojected onto it in a 19th-century "renegotiation we all agreed to accept"; no syllable was *written* with the intent to condemn enslavement.[^6] See [[Abolition Was Read Into the Bible From the Outside]], [[The Bible Was Wrong and Consensus Determines Morality]].
