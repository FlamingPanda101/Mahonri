# Philosophy Tube — Deduction and Induction

A short *Gentleman Thinker* explainer from [[Philosophy Tube]] — "philosophical concepts explained in brief in a posh English accent" — distinguishing the two basic forms of reasoning, **deduction** and **induction**. [^1] It is used in the *Thoughtful Faith* Level 1, sub-section 1.1 epistemology track as a neutral, non-LDS primer on how arguments move from premises to conclusions and on the differing kinds of support each form provides.

- **Two sorts of reasoning: deduction (top-down) and induction (bottom-up).** "Generally speaking there are two sorts of reasoning; deduction and induction." Deduction "takes premises and reduces them down to conclusions"; induction "takes premises and draws probable conclusions based on what those premises provide evidence for." [^1][^2] See [[Deduction Is Top-Down and Induction Is Bottom-Up Reasoning]].
- **Deductive conclusions are logically certain; inductive ones are only probable.** With the classic syllogism — "All men are mortal. Earl Grey is a man. Therefore Earl Grey is mortal" — "we can be logically certain of the conclusion. Given the premises the conclusion must be true." By contrast an inductive conclusion "is not certain": every Briton you have met being polite does not guarantee the next will be. [^1][^2] See [[Deduction Is Top-Down and Induction Is Bottom-Up Reasoning]].
- **Science runs on induction, and uncertain induction is still a fantastic guide to truth.** Watching a cannonball fall 99 times lets you be "fairly but not completely certain that it will fall the 100th time." "Much of science is built on inductive reasoning despite the fact that its conclusions are uncertain. It is a fantastic guide to truth." [^3] See [[Deduction Is Top-Down and Induction Is Bottom-Up Reasoning]].
- **Deduction is "complete" but it is not clear what makes a good induction.** "It is clear what makes a good deduction. Namely that the premises entail the conclusion. But it isn't really clear what makes a good induction." The cannonball inference is plainly good induction; "all swans ever observed are white, therefore all swans are white" is plainly bad — yet the video offers no general criterion separating them. This is the classic **problem of induction**. [^3][^4] See [[No Clear Criterion Separates Good Induction from Bad]].
