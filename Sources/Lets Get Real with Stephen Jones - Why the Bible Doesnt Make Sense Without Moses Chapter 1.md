# Why The Bible Doesn't Make Sense Without Moses Chapter 1 (Don Bradley E0050)

An episode of *Let's Get Real with Stephen Jones* in which host [[Stephen Jones]] interviews LDS historian [[Don Bradley]] about **Moses Chapter 1** of the [[Book of Moses]] (Pearl of Great Price). The thesis: Moses 1 is God's lost **preface** — a revelatory "key" or "lens" — that was meant to be read *before* Genesis and that reframes the whole Bible. Given to [[Joseph Smith]] in June 1830, just before he began the Joseph Smith Translation, the chapter answers two questions Genesis leaves unanswered — *who am I?* and *what story am I living in?* — by establishing first who God is and then that the human being is God's child.

The conversation is also unusually personal: Bradley narrates his own arc from believing Latter-day Saint, to ex-Mormon atheist, to deist, and back to the Restoration, and closes by applying Moses 1 to the deaths of his brother Charles and his son Donnie.

## Summary

- **The preface frame.** Genesis opens with commandments and creation but no introduction of the characters or their relationship to God, so it can read as legalistic or "task-oriented" — "if you do this, I'll give you that." Moses 1 is the missing author's preface that supplies the relational frame. (See [[Moses 1 Is the Lost Preface and Hidden Key to the Bible]].)
- **Who is speaking.** Unlike Genesis's anonymous third-person voice ("In the beginning God…"), Moses 1 opens in the first person: God speaks, names Moses, locates the scene on a mountain, and explains that Moses is **transfigured** so he can endure God's presence — cueing a temple context. (See [[Moses 1.1-2]], [[Discernment Comes From Prior Experience of God's Glory]].)
- **"I am God, you are my son."** God's first words establish his own nature (Lord God Almighty, endless) and then declare Moses his son — identity before instruction. Bradley shows the *same opening pattern* at the head of every dispensation: Adam, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus (at his baptism), and Joseph Smith. (See [[Every Dispensation Opens With God Declaring Divine Sonship]].)
- **The priority inversion.** Because Moses is "in the similitude of mine Only Begotten" ([[Moses 1.6]]), Jesus is not merely "a prophet like unto Moses" ([[Deuteronomy 18.15]]); rather Moses is a son of God modeled on Christ. Chronology (Moses first) distorts the eternal priority (Christ first). (See [[Moses Is a Son of God in the Likeness of Jesus Not Jesus a Prophet Like Moses]].)
- **The completed parallel.** A biblical scholar (via [[Robert Millet]]) noted the life of Moses parallels the life of Christ at many points but lacks a temptation-in-the-wilderness; Moses 1 *is* that missing temptation narrative, completing the typology. (See [[Moses 1 Supplies the Missing Temptation in the Moses-Christ Parallel]].)
- **Creation as a model for living.** Drawing on [[Mircea Eliade]], Bradley argues a culture's creation story models how its people live. Because the Father creates "by the word of my power, which is mine Only Begotten Son," and humans are made in the Son's similitude, we are to do all that we do "in the name of the Son" — Christ-centered consecration. (See [[The Creation Story Is a Model for How to Live]].)
- **Against Calvinism.** Moses 1:39's purpose ("this is my work and my glory…") rebuts the Calvinist/Puritan background of Joseph Smith's own family (the [[Presbyterian]] [[Lucy Mack Smith]]): God does not create vessels of wrath for his "greater glory" but children for eternal life. (See [[Moses 1.39]], [[The Calvinist God Cannot Love Because He Controls Everything]].)
- **Discernment.** Moses resists Satan-as-angel-of-light precisely because he has already experienced God's true glory; filling one's life with experiences of the good prepares one to recognize evil. (See [[Discernment Comes From Prior Experience of God's Glory]].)
