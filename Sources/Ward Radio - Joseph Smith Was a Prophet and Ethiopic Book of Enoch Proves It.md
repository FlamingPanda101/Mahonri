# Ward Radio — Joseph Smith Was a Prophet and Ethiopic Book of Enoch Proves It

A Ward Radio episode in which host **[[Cardon Ellis]]** asks **[[Jonah Barnes]]** for his single strongest apocryphal proof of [[Joseph Smith]]; Barnes answers "Enoch" and walks through parallels between Joseph Smith's 1830 Book of Moses ("Enoch Zero") and Enochic texts translated only after Joseph's death.

## Summary
- **"Enoch Zero" = the first complete English Book of Enoch.** Barnes calls [[Moses 6|Moses 6-7]] "Enoch Zero," claiming it was "the first start-to-finish English Book of Enoch in existence," predating the full Ethiopic (1 Enoch) translation. He rejects the "Pan-Enochic" theory (that Joseph needed no Enoch source) as a cope. See [[Moses 6-7 Restores Enochic Content Unavailable to Joseph Smith in 1830]].[^1]
- **The "lad" bullseye.** Joseph uses the word "lad" exactly once ever — Enoch calling himself "but a lad" ([[Moses 6.31|Moses 6:31]]). The Mandaean Enoch tradition (French translation 1856, never in English) repeatedly calls Enoch *Petit Anush*, "little Enoch, the lad"; the Bet ha-Midrash has Enoch "small (*katan*)… a lad among them." See [[Joseph Smiths Lone Use of Lad Matches the Mandaean Little Enoch]].[^2]
- **The calling-scene paraphrase (Slavonic Enoch).** The 2 Enoch (Slavonic, discovered 1896) calling of Enoch — God lamenting that the people "denied my unity… rejected my commandments" — matches Moses 7's calling scene; the hosts liken it to running one passage "through a thesaurus."[^3]
- **The Son of Man vision.** Enoch sees "the Son of Man" lifted into the bosom of the Father (Moses 7), a title the canonical Old Testament never applies to the Messiah but which the Ethiopic 1 Enoch's Parables do. See [[Book of Enoch - Enoch as the Son of Man]] and [[Son of Man - Development of the Title]].[^4]
- **Mahijah / the Book of the Giants.** The name Mahijah (Moses 6-7) appears as Mahawai/Mahujah in the *Book of the Giants*, first translated 1971; Barnes relays [[Hugh Nibley]]'s "someday we'll find out where he got it" anecdote (attributed to Dead Sea Scrolls translator [[Matthew Black]]). See [[The Book of the Giants Names Mahijah Matching Joseph Smith's Enoch]].[^5]

## Notable framing / rhetoric
- Repeated "Joe Stradamus predicts" / "Daily Mail reports" bit, and "he was a prophet or we're all crazy… that math don't make no sense."[^5]
- The "Gettysburg Address" analogy: a paraphrase translated across a language and a millennium would still be recognizable as the same scene without being word-for-word.[^2]
- Heavy sponsor reads (Scripture Notes; Go and Do Travel's "Gospel on the Nile") and promotion of *The Lost Gems of Genesis* (coupon code "ward radio").
