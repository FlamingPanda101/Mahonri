# Moroni's Standard — The Oldest Belief About God Was Not Monotheism

[[Alex Arnold]] (Moroni's Standard) argues that the *oldest* documented picture of God — in the Hebrew Bible and across every literate ancient civilization — is a populated heaven with a presiding supreme God and a deliberative council of divine beings, **not** strict philosophical monotheism. Strict monotheism, on his telling, always shows up later and is always traceable to a datable philosophical or political moment ([[Council of Nicaea|Nicaea]], 325 AD; the [[Deuteronomistic Reform Explains the Book of Mormon Theology|Deuteronomistic reform]]). The argument is built almost entirely on non-LDS scholarship — [[Michael Heiser]], [[Frank Moore Cross]], [[Margaret Barker]], [[Mark Smith]], [[Martin Noth]], [[Gerhard May]] — and is offered as a falsification of the claim that [[Joseph Smith]] invented divine-plurality cosmology in 1830s Palmyra.

## Summary

- **The Hebrew text itself is the foundation, not the earliest church.** The [[Echad and the Shema as a Loyalty Pledge|Shema]] uses *echad* (composite unity), not *yachid* (absolute singularity), so [[Deuteronomy 6.4|Deut 6:4]] asserts covenant loyalty, not that only one divine being exists. The divine council then runs through [[Psalm 82 Is About Divine Council Not Human Judges|Psalm 82]], [[1 Kings 22|1 Kings 22]] (Micaiah's vision), [[Isaiah 6.1|Isaiah 6]], Zechariah 3, [[Job 1|Job 1–2]], and [[The Plural of Genesis 1 Is the Divine Council Not the Trinity or Royal We|Genesis 1:26]] ("let us make man"). [^1] [^2] [^3]
- **Psalm 82's threat only works on divine beings.** "Die like men" is a *demotion* — you cannot threaten humans with human mortality; and Jesus deploys the psalm as a live legal defense in [[John 10.34-36|John 10:34–35]]. [^4]
- **The text was edited.** [[Frank Moore Cross]] (refining [[Martin Noth]], 1943) documented the Deuteronomistic History: Josiah-era scribes (late 7th c. BC) centralized worship, enforced Yahweh-only monotheism, and suppressed the divine council and the divine feminine — [[Asherah Pillar Iconography Depicts Wisdom Who Gives Sight|Asherah]], named ~40 times almost always in condemnation, and remembered in the ~800 BC Kuntillet Ajrud inscription "Yahweh and his Asherah." [^5] [^6]
- **The Book of Mormon window.** [[Margaret Barker]]'s First Temple theology + Lehi leaving Jerusalem ~600 BC (only ~20 years post-reform, which was geographically uneven) means the [[Brass Plates Bypassed the Textual Corruption Chain|brass plates]] preserve a pre-reform priestly stream. [^7]
- **The cross-cultural convergence (the new centerpiece).** ~17 unconnected civilizations — Sumer (Anunnaki), Babylon (Igigi/Anunnaki + Enuma Elish "let us create mankind"), Canaan (El + 70 sons), Egypt (Ennead), Hittites (Pankus), Greece, Norse, Vedic India, Aztec, Maya (Popol Vuh), China, Shinto, Aboriginal Australian — independently produced the same tiered divine-council architecture. The trilemma (coincidence / psychological universal / fragments of one original revelation) is developed in [[Cross-Cultural Convergence on a Divine Council Is the Oldest Picture of God]]. [^8] [^9]
- **Monotheism's late, datable arrival.** [[Creatio Ex Nihilo Is the Lynchpin Difference|Creation ex nihilo]] (2nd c., per [[Gerhard May]]) and *[[Homoousios Means Same Essence Which Mormons Extend to All Humanity|homoousios]]* (Nicaea, 325) are Greek-philosophical imports absent from scripture. The only foundationally monotheistic traditions are Islam (7th c.), Sikhism, Baha'i, and arguably Zoroastrianism — so a Christian critic invoking "the monotheistic standard" borrows a bar his own [[Trinitarianism Is Anachronistic to the Biblical Period|Trinitarian]] tradition does not straightforwardly meet ([[If You Want Strict Monotheism Be a Muslim]]). [^10] [^11]
- **The timeline forecloses cultural osmosis.** The Ugaritic tablets (1929), Dead Sea Scrolls (1947), Nag Hammadi (1945), Heiser's synthesis (2015), and Barker's work (1980s–90s) all postdate Joseph Smith — so the "he absorbed it from his environment" theory collapses, because the relevant scholarship did not yet exist to be absorbed. The LDS texts (1 Nephi 1:8, Moses 4:1–4, D&C 121:32, all published 1830–1835) already describe the council. [^12] [^13]
- **Objection-handling segment.** Rebuts "you're just pagan" (architecture ≠ borrowing — Sumer also had flood narratives), "Heiser would reject the LDS use" (scholarship's accuracy is separable from the scholar's approval), and the [[Infinite and Eternal Are Not the Same - The Regress Objection Rebutted|infinite-regress "who made God" objection]] via the infinite-vs-eternal distinction. [^14] [^15]
