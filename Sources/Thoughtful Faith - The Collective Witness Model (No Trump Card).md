# Thoughtful Faith — The Collective Witness Model (No Trump Card)

The epistemology installment (Level 1, §1.1) of [[Jacob Hansen]]'s Thoughtful Faith course. Building on prior videos in the series — where Hansen argued that knowledge is ultimately *confidence in the reality of something*, and that such confidence is gained through five "witnesses": **intuition, sense data, outcomes, reason, and authority** — this video attacks a single error he sees recurring in both religious and secular life: the assumption that **one witness trumps all the others** or is **infallible**, supplying 100% certainty.[^1]

Hansen names two mirror-image versions of this error as "heresies" and then sets out his constructive alternative, the **collective witness model** — a system of "checks and balances" or "triangulation" in which independent witnesses mutually validate (or undercut) a claim, with no single witness reigning supreme.[^9]

## Summary
- **The framing error: treating one witness as infallible / a trump card.** Knowledge is confidence in reality, gained through five witnesses (intuition, sense data, outcomes, reason, authority). The false idea Hansen targets is that any one of these "trumps all of the others" and gives 100% certainty.[^1] See [[The Collective Witness Model of Knowing]].
- **The heresy of authority alone.** Drawing on his debate with Calvinist friends at Apologia Church over *sola scriptura* — the claim that every word of the Bible is infallible because God placed it there, so "if the Bible says it, it's true" regardless of eyes or reason — Hansen says some Latter-day Saints hold a structurally identical view, treating living or dead prophets as the trump card. This "blind obedience," he argues, "is not our Doctrine."[^2][^3] He cites [[George Q. Cannon]]'s warning against "blind self-security" — settling one's eternal destiny in leaders' hands without inquiring of God for oneself.[^3] See [[The Heresy of Authority Alone]].
- **Fallible does not mean unreliable.** Hansen warns against over-correcting: it would be "stupid" to distrust twelve top brain surgeons merely because they are fallible. **Every** authority, secular or religious, is fallible — but that does not make them untrustworthy. Authority is established by **trust**, and trust is built on **outcomes**; Christ taught we know a prophet "by the fruits" ([[Matthew 7.16-20|Matthew 7]]).[^4][^5] See [[Fallible But Reliable - Why Authority Still Counts]].
- **The heresy of feelings alone.** The mirror error grants ultimate authority to personal intuition — "personal feelings that we think are coming from God" that trump every other witness. People holding this often reject Church authority simply because their intuition says otherwise.[^6] But intuitions can be shown mistaken by reason, the words of prophets, sense data, or outcomes; hence prophets teach that personal revelation should *align* with prophetic teaching. Hansen quotes [[Dallin H. Oaks]]: "the personal line does not function independent of the priesthood line," and adds his own gloss that it should also align with reason and empirical evidence.[^7][^8] See [[The Heresy of Feelings Alone]].
- **The constructive model: triangulation, domain-weighting, and walking by faith.** When witnesses conflict, "the truth is simply less clearly manifest" and one should be cautious; when many align "in unison," confidence rises.[^8] The tools are domain-weighted: sense data dominates the physical world; intuition dominates beauty and moral obligation; authority weighs more in institutional religious practice.[^10] Even science contains such conflicts (empirical experience vs. the mathematics of quantum mechanics/relativity), and scientific consensus is reliable but not infallible.[^10] There is "no magic formula, no trump card"; as Paul said we "see through a glass darkly" ([[1 Corinthians 13.12]]) and "walk by faith" ([[2 Corinthians 5.7]]).[^9] See [[The Collective Witness Model of Knowing]].
