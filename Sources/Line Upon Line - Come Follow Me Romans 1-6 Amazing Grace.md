# Line Upon Line - Come Follow Me Romans 1-6 (Amazing Grace)

An animated "Come Follow Me" overview (Living Scriptures' *Line Upon Line*, Season 4 New Testament) framing the August 7-13 study of **Romans 1-6** around the question that "ignited one of the biggest debates in Christianity": **are we saved by faith or by works?** Opening on young Martin Luther's despair at feeling "always unclean before God," it surveys Paul's Roman argument — sin and the law condemn, no one keeps the law perfectly, Abraham's faith was the foundation of his righteousness, and Christ's sacrifice supplies the righteousness we lack — landing on the synthesis: "righteousness is given through faith and we are justified by his grace." It closes on John Newton's hymn *Amazing Grace*. [^1] [^2]

The doctrinal content restates the vault's existing grace cluster ([[LDS Salvation Is by Grace Not Earned by Works]], [[After All We Can Do Means In Spite of All We Can Do]], [[Works Are the Fruit of Faith Not the Price of Salvation]], [[Paul Redefined Charis as Unmerited Gift Against Greco-Roman Reciprocity]]); its one distinctive contribution is a five-point heuristic for reading Pauline epistles — see [[Reading Paul - A Five-Point Toolkit for the Epistles]].

## Summary
- **The great debate.** Luther grew up believing "all God sees are our sins" with "no way to earn a place in heaven," until Paul's Romans freed him; the verses "the righteous will live by faith" and "all are justified freely by his grace through the redemption that came by Christ Jesus" (Romans 1:17; 3:24) "totally rocked Christianity." The framing question: can we "earn our place in Heaven by working really really hard to be perfectly obedient," or is heaven "a gift from God to people who really really believe in Jesus?" [^1]
- **What an epistle is.** Quick map of the New Testament: four Gospels, Acts, 21 Epistles (handwritten apostolic letters to early-church congregations), Revelation. The Pauline epistles are Paul's letters; combining "Paul's great detail-focused brain, ancient cultural issues, and the archaic King James language" makes them hard to read. [^1]
- **The five-point toolkit for reading Paul** (the source's distinctive teaching): (1) Paul uses **examples from his audience's culture** — Old Testament to Jews, sports to Gentile Corinthians; (2) he asks **rhetorical questions** to make points, not to seek answers; (3) he **sets up an idea readers think they understand, then overturns it**; (4) he uses **big chunks of verses to hit one idea from many angles**, so meaning isn't in a single line — "keep reading"; (5) you are reading a **translation** (the 400-year-old KJV especially), so consult other trusted versions. [^1] Captured as [[Reading Paul - A Five-Point Toolkit for the Epistles]].
- **The Romans 1-6 "case file."** Paul opens on how horrible sin is and the misery of breaking God's law; praises the fairness of God's judgment (Romans 2); teaches that "the law can't save us because not a single one of us can keep the law perfectly" — addressed to Jews in Rome relying on the 613 commandments of the law of Moses to distinguish themselves from Gentiles; goes back to **Abraham**, "whose faith was the foundation of his righteousness" (ch. 4); then proclaims that "the sacrifice of Jesus overcomes our lack of righteousness." [^1]
- **But do the commandments still matter?** "Does that mean we just forget about the Commandments? God forbid" — "of course we need the Commandments; we can't just sit around believing and not doing anything." The resolution: "righteousness is given through faith and we are justified by his grace"; "Jesus gives us his righteousness... our best efforts don't get us far, but humble hearts now that's something he can work with." This is the vault's [[Works Are the Fruit of Faith Not the Price of Salvation|faith-and-its-fruits]] position. [^1]
- **Faith activates grace; commandments demonstrate faith.** Citing Enos ("thy faith hath made thee whole," Enos 1:8) and "if you love me keep my commandments" (John 14:15): faith "allows him to flood our lives with grace," and the commandments "help us come unto Christ and help us become the kind of people who understand, want, and even give grace" — "we value God's gift of forgiveness when we forgive others." [^1] [^2]
- **Amazing Grace.** The hymn was written by **John Newton**, a slave-ship captain who reached out to God in a storm, repented on realizing "the horror of his sins," and "devoted the rest of his life to God... working to abolish slavery." [^2]
