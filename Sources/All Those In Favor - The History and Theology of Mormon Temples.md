# All Those In Favor — The History and Theology of Mormon Temples (Jonathan Stapley)

An interview with historian [[Jonathan Stapley]] about his Oxford University Press book *Holiness to the Lord: Latter-day Saint Temple Worship*. Stapley offers a "third way" between insider devotional discourse and outsider exposé: a respectful, historical account of how the temple developed and what it means, aimed at both Latter-day Saint and non-LDS readers.

## Summary
- **The temple originates in the "endowment of power" of Luke–Acts.** Before the church was a year old, Joseph Smith revealed that the Saints would go to Ohio to be "endowed with power from on high" — language taken straight from Luke 24 / Acts 1 (the disciples told to "tarry in Jerusalem"). Biblically literate early members expected a Pentecostal outpouring, which they understood the Kirtland visions to fulfill.[^1] See [[The Endowment of Power Recapitulates Luke-Acts and Pentecost]].
- **The ceremonies are drawn from the Hebrew Bible's priestly consecration.** Joseph Smith revealed washings, anointings, priestly robes, and (in [[Revelation 7.9-14|Revelation's]] vision of robed worshippers) a vision of becoming the kings/queens and priests/priestesses who surround God's throne — John of Patmos's vision "made a reality," with "queens and priestesses" added by Joseph Smith.[^2] (Cross-ref the existing [[Ordinance Creation Recontextualizes Ordinary Actions]] and [[The Heavenly Temple and Christ as Great High Priest]].)
- **Heaven is *constructed* relationally; hell is disconnection.** Stapley's headline theological claim: in the Nauvoo cosmology heaven is not a destination or reward but "the durable relations formed through expanded temple ceremonies" — a material network of kinship. "Where those relationships don't exist, there's simply no heaven," and hell is "being separate and alone" (King Follett: "I don't fear hellfire... it don't exist").[^3] See [[Heaven Is a Constructed Relational Network and Hell Is Disconnection]].
- **The adoption sealing.** After Joseph Smith's death the apostles practiced "adoption" — sealing converts to church leaders rather than to their own parents — to build a connected kinship network *in the present* before multigenerational family chains existed.[^4] (Cross-ref [[Women Were Once Sealed to Multiple Living Men]].)
- **"Ordinance" is a Protestant loanword; LDS rites function more like sacraments.** Protestants coined "ordinance" (vs. Catholic/Orthodox "sacrament") to deny that baptism does real metaphysical work; the early church borrowed the term, but LDS ceremonies — believed to "do a thing," conveying grace — resemble sacraments. Hence Joseph Smith called *faith, repentance, baptism, and the Holy Ghost* the "first ordinances," because faith and repentance are "laws" no less than baptism.[^5] See [[Ordinance Was a Protestant Loanword for Rites That Function as Sacraments]]. (Cross-ref [[Ordinance Meant Law Not Liturgy When Joseph Smith Said Ordinances Cannot Change]].)
- **Liturgy changes; the laws/ordinances of the gospel do not.** What "cannot change" is the plan of salvation, not the ceremony's form — which is why temple worship can be updated (actors → video; instantaneous worldwide updates; any-language accessibility) without violating Joseph Smith's claim that ordinances are unchanging.[^6] (Cross-ref [[An Ordinance Must Change to Stay the Same]].)
- **Exaltation, theogony, and the "underappreciated distinction."** Distinguishing *salvation* from *exaltation*, Stapley reads the King Follett sermon's becoming-God language as "becoming kings and priests to God," and stresses that Joseph Smith drew a *distinction* between Jesus/the Father and our path that later speculation blurred.[^7] (Cross-ref [[God Was Once a Man Is Esoteric Not Core Doctrine]] and [[Gods of Their Own Planets Is a Caricature of Exaltationist Theosis]].)
- **Pioneer speculation that did *not* survive.** First-generation leaders (the Pratts, Brigham Young, B.H. Roberts) speculated publicly and disagreed; some ideas — Brigham Young's [[Adam-God Doctrine - What Brigham Young Taught|Adam-God]], "spirits were born," Roberts's tripartite intelligence — were later set aside as "not reflecting the truth." Stapley frames this as a reflective-equilibrium process, like early Christianity's Christological debates and like science.[^8] (Cross-ref [[Was Adam-God Official Binding Teaching]].)
- **Radically non-dogmatic / strong orthopraxy.** The temple-recommend "creed" is minimal (assent to God, Christ, the Spirit, the Restoration, leaders' authority); within that there is "a huge diversity of belief," so critics cannot categorize "Mormons" as a doctrinal monolith. LDS orthodoxy is thin but its *orthopraxy* is strong.[^9] See [[Latter-day Saints Are Radically Non-Dogmatic With Strong Orthopraxy]].
- **Race and the temple.** Stapley's chapter on the priesthood/temple restriction (building on Paul Reeve, Matt Harris, Ed Kimball) argues Joseph Smith revealed *no* racial restriction (Black men were ordained, participated in Kirtland), that the one priesthood restriction Joseph did pronounce was against the *Missourians* and was descriptive (war/expulsion forfeits priesthood access), and that Brigham Young recast non-scriptural curse-of-Ham narratives in temple/cosmological terms — "racist and wrong."[^10] (Cross-ref [[Priesthood Ban Origins]] and [[The Priesthood Ban Was the Development Not Its Reversal]].)
