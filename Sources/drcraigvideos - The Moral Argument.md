# The Moral Argument

A short animated [[William Lane Craig|Craig]] explainer that lays out his signature moral argument for God: *(1) if God does not exist, objective moral values and duties do not exist; (2) objective moral values and duties do exist; (3) therefore God exists.* It distinguishes the grounding of moral **values** (God's nature as the objective reference point) from moral **duties** (God's commands), and answers the Euthyphro dilemma with a third option — God wills something because he *is* good. The piece reframes "can you be good without God?" from the easy question (without *believing* in God) to the real one (without God *existing* as the foundation of morality).

## Summary
- The real question is not whether one can be good without *believing* in God, but whether objective good and bad can exist without God *existing* as the foundation; without an objective reference point, morality reduces to mere subjective preference ("like a preference for strawberry ice cream").[^1]
- **Values** are grounded in God's *nature* as the standard, while moral **duties** are grounded in God's *commands* (e.g., the command to love one's neighbor), which let us affirm generosity as objectively good and condemn abuse and discrimination as objectively evil.[^2]
- Euthyphro is answered with a third option: God wills something *neither* because it is independently good *nor* arbitrarily, but because he *is* good — God is the standard, "just as a live musical performance is the standard for a high-fidelity recording."[^3]
- The second premise is defended from moral experience: just as sense experience convinces us the physical world is real, moral experience convinces us morality is objective ("hey, that's not fair!"); denying it makes the man who says raping children is acceptable "just as mistaken as the man who says two plus two equals five."[^4]
