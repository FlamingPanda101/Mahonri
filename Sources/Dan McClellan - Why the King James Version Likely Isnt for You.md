# Dan McClellan — Why the King James Version Likely Isn't for You

A short explainer in which McClellan argues that the King James Version is most valuable as a **literary artifact**, not as a tool for understanding what the biblical authors were trying to communicate. He gives three reasons — archaic language, an inferior manuscript base (the Textus Receptus), and an outdated, overly literal translation method — and recommends the updated New Revised Standard Version (NRSVue) instead. The video is useful in the vault as mainstream-scholarship support for the Latter-day Saint claim that the manuscript and translation questions are real and consequential (see [[The Manuscript Question Determines Your Bible]]).

## Summary

- **Reason 1 — the KJV's English was already archaic in 1611.** It is "a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision of a very conservative revision" reaching back almost a century to [[William Tyndale]] and [[Miles Coverdale]]; it was criticized for outdated language even at publication, and 400+ years have made it worse. Example: [[Jude 1.22|Jude 22]]'s "making a difference" meant *making a distinction* in 1611, not "having a positive impact" (that sense arose ~1900). See [[The KJV Was Already Out of Date in 1611]]. [^1]
- **Reason 2 — the KJV rests on inferior manuscripts (the Textus Receptus).** The KJV used the third edition of [[Erasmus]]' *Textus Receptus* to revise the Bishops' Bible. We now have far more, far earlier, far more reliable manuscripts and a more refined textual criticism, yielding a more reliable source text. About 16 passages in the KJV are absent from the earliest manuscripts and are simply omitted by many modern translations. Even within Jude 22, the participle is accusative, not nominative — so the verse is about *on whom* to have compassion (the doubting), not *how*. See [[The KJV Rests on the Inferior Textus Receptus]]. [^2]
- **Reason 3 — the KJV used an inadequate, overly literal method.** It "frequently punted to just reproducing the form of the source language," yielding indecipherable English. Example: [[2 Corinthians 2.5|2 Corinthians 2:5]] ("he hath not grieved me but in part that I may not overcharge you all") is incomprehensible because it follows Greek word order in a caseless language; the NRSVue renders the *sense* clearly. See [[The KJV Used an Inadequate Overly Literal Translation Method]]. [^3]
- **Recommendation.** For understanding the authors, McClellan recommends the updated edition of the New Revised Standard Version — current on scholarship, translation philosophy, and the manuscript evidence. [^4]
