# Ward Radio — 3 Mormon Apologists Ask Them Anything

A long Ward Radio livestream and ask-me-anything. Host [[Cardon Ellis]] convenes three online Latter-day Saint apologists — Devin Barnes of Latter-day Logic, [[Braden Gospel Guardian|Braden]] of Gospel Guardian, and [[Dalton Williams]] of Unashamed Truth — for two intertwined activities: a running "cursory review" of [[Walter Martin]]'s countercult classic *The Kingdom of the Cults* (6th ed., found on the shelf at Barnes & Noble), and live answers to super-chat questions about how Latter-day Saint apologists actually argue. Between the comedy bits (tacos, snakes, boomers) sit several reusable apologetic exchanges on sources, the "cult" label, the Church's wealth, and how to engage critics.

## Summary

- **The Kingdom of the Cults as a case study in bad sourcing.** Reading Martin's Mormonism chapter aloud, the panel hits the line praising **E. D. Howe** as a "contemporary of the Smiths who did tremendous research." They trace Howe's *Mormonism Unveiled* (1834) back to **Dr. Philastus Hurlbut** — "doctor" being his given name, not a credential — an excommunicated dissenter who collected ~62–80+ affidavits in Palmyra, was found in court to have made death threats against Joseph Smith, and (per the *Saints* history and Wikipedia they read on air) likely **wrote the statements himself and had illiterate neighbors sign them**, then sold the collection to Howe. Cardon repeatedly likens citing this as "tremendous research" to citing *Mein Kampf* as scholarship on Jewish people. The panel openly corrects itself mid-stream: it first conflated [[Philastus Hurlbut|Hurlbut]] with **John C. Bennett** (Nauvoo, 1840+, spiritual wifery) before clarifying that Hurlbut/Howe (Kirtland/Ohio, 1834) are the actual source chain. See [[Anti-Mormon Tropes Trace Back to a Few 1830s Forged Affidavits]].[^1][^2][^3][^4]
- **How to weigh historical criticism — primary sources and multiple attestation.** Answering "how do you distinguish legitimate historical criticism from weak or circular sources?", the apologists give a journalism/forensics analogy: prefer **primary over secondary over tertiary** sources, demand **multiple attestation** (two independent verifiable witnesses), and prefer accounts **closest in time** to the event. The first diagnostic question is simply *"Do you have a source for that?"* — if not, the person is parroting. They note that many CES Letter citations, when actually read, are misinterpreted, taken out of context, from unqualified sources (e.g. non-Egyptologists on the Book of Abraham), or simply don't exist. They flag the double standard of accepting hostile "a guy heard from a guy" hearsay (Fanny Alger in the barn) while rejecting the witness accounts that support the plates. See [[Weigh Sources by Proximity and Multiple Attestation]].[^5][^6][^7]
- **"Is it a cult?" — make them define the term.** On Martin never actually explaining *why* the Church qualifies as a cult, the panel's prescription is to **ask the accuser what they mean by "cult."** Whatever definition they give (commonly the **BITE model**, whose author allegedly cites his own studies as the corroborating studies) either (a) doesn't fit the Church, which emphasizes agency and choice, or (b) fits so loosely that it would also catch Jesus's church, Moses and the Israelites, the military, sports teams, MLMs, and "the Dallas Cowboys fan base." The label hinges on *control*, "but the church doesn't control." See [[Make the Accuser Define Cult]].[^8][^9]
- **Engaging critics — steelman vs. straw man, and refusing the Gish gallop.** Asked how a fair critique should be structured, Braden contrasts the **straw man** (dumb down, re-word, decontextualize the argument to weaken it) with the **steel man** (put yourself in the opponent's shoes and state their case as strongly and honestly as possible). The clearest tell of a bad-faith critic is the **Gish gallop**: throwing one accusation after another without pausing for an answer — "what this book is doing, what the CES letter does." The remedy is to take control of the conversation, address point one fully before moving on, ask questions (the questioner controls the conversation), and end or block the exchange if the other side ignores answers. A sincere critic responds to your answer with "okay, interesting — what about this?" See [[Steel Man Versus Gish Gallop in Apologetic Dialogue]].[^10][^11][^12][^13]
- **The Church's wealth as evidence, not scandal.** Against Martin's framing of "shrewd business practices," the panel reframes tithing and reserves: ~$1.58B in humanitarian aid in a recent year, wealth held mostly in land/assets rather than cash, and a welfare program "done unimpeachably well" that kept running through a government shutdown that toasted SNAP and other charities. They argue the *absence* of yachts, jets, and luxury watches (unlike megachurch pastors) is evidence Christ's church, and that the Church teaches **self-reliance** ("teach a man to fish") and rigorously **audits** the charities it funds rather than just "throwing money at an issue." See [[Church Wealth and Welfare Are Evidence Not Scandal]] and the related [[The SEC Settlement Does Not Prove the Church Hid Its Wealth]].[^14][^15][^16][^17]
- **Book of Mormon "plagiarism" and "lacks history."** On Martin's charge that the Book of Mormon plagiarizes the KJV and "betrays a great lack of information on world history and the history of the Jewish people," the panel answers that the book's stated purpose (per the apostles and Nephi's small-plates statement) is to **testify of Christ and the more spiritual things**, not to be a political-military history — so demanding secular historical detail **imposes an external standard the book never set for itself**. (Complements [[Read the Book of Mormon as a Translated Ancient Text]] and [[The Book of Mormon Is Measured Against the Wrong Period]].)[^18]
- **Lighter tangents (not load-bearing).** Large stretches are comedy and chat management: whether a taco is a sandwich; how to "shut down boomers" over 3 Nephi 11:29 (they actually agree with the verse — contend *for* the faith, not *with* contention); Devin's pest-control snake stories; super-chat shout-outs; merch and coupon codes; a divine-command-theory tangent prompted by a chat question (Cardon admits he is "the least qualified person on the planet" to address it). These are skipped here.
